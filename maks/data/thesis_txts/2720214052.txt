Abstract
The following thesis examines the development process of a software-based home test de-
signed for a non-clinical setting, aimed at improving early detection of Fatty Liver Disease (FLD).
Through a state-of-the-art literature review and the following development process, several de-
sign aspects key to the success of this specific home test were identified and prioritized. By
applying these prioritized design aspects to a User-Centered Design (UCD) development ap-
proach, focused on the development and user-testing of prototypes, a final design was created.
The final design consist of a multi-platform application which utilizes a machine-learning algo-
rithm, developed by the company Evido, to calculate the users risk of suffering from FLD, and
present the results and recommended actions to the individual. The final design had very high
usability with a System Usability Scale (SUS) score of 88, rating it as excellent, suggesting that
applying the prioritized design aspects to a UCD process lead to high product performance. A
lack of trust in the efficacy of the algorithm and distributor of the application remains a lacking
aspect however, and is highly dependant on external factors, such as a general lack of trust in
technology amongst parts of the population and the potential propagation strategy.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Fatty Liver Disease (FLD) is a global liver pandemic and a leading cause of death and disability
[1]. Approximately 25% of the world’s population suffers from fatty liver [2], and complications
from FLD currently cause around 1 million deaths each year globally. A large contributing factor
is the lack of accurate screening in primary care. Fatty Liver Disease is a condition in which ex-
cess fat in the liver, can cause scar tissue to form over many years until it reaches it’s end-stage,
called Cirrhosis. FLD remains an asymptotic until it reaches this end-stage causing 2/3 of FLD
cases in Denmark to be diagnosed when the condition is already fatal [3]. Today, the patient
pathway is characterised by inaccurate screening that leads to undiagnosed high-risk patients
and redundant referral of healthy patients. This is mainly due to General Practitioners relying
on outdated and inaccurate liver tests to make their clinical decisions.
LiverPro is a clinical decision supporting software which enables timely detection of FLD patients
at first point-of-contact (in primary care). LiverPro utilises regularly used blood samples and is
based on state-of-the-art algorithms that translate simple blood samples, which are taken by a
personal physician into an accurate risk assessment of the patient with 92% accuracy based
on current testing [4]. The company behind the product, Evido, have developed a simplified
version of the algorithm, called LiverCheck, which doesn’t require blood samples, and instead
only requires the user to input data such as their age, sex, height weight and more.
This project will focus on developing the self-test[5] User Experience (UX), utilizing LiverCheck,
designed for a non-clinical setting, with the goal to reach as many potential patients as possible.
The desired outcome of this project is a user-tested high-fidelity prototype of a software appli-
cation for individuals to assess liver condition at home or outside of a clinical setting. The goal
of which is to assist with early suspicion notification for potential development of a Fatty lever
disease.
The self-test will be developed for the danish market, and is shaped significantly by this context.
Denmark is a highly digitized country, with a strong public health sector, but no existing national
policy regarding FLD exist. While The European Association for the Study of the Liver (EASL)
presented a framework [6] in 2021, specifically aimed at reducing the growing number of FLD
cases in Europe, none of the recommendations have yet to be implemented on a national level.
Some competing solutions in the clinical space exist [7], and have proven effective, but no direct
competitor using standardized blood samples taken at the personal physician exist for LiverPro,

and no product currently exist which allows for self-testing in a non-clinical setting such as in the

What design aspects should be prioritized when designing a software for non-clinical

How can the prioritized design aspects be applied for the development of a non-clinical
self-test of fatty liver disease detection?
The research questions are addressed through the following chapters:

1.1 Definition of Concepts
The following section consists of a short definition of concepts of particular importance to this
investigation and design process.
Fatty liver diseases
Fatty liver disease (hepatic steatosis) means an individual has extra fat in their liver [9][10].
There are two specific types, alcoholic related liver disease (ARLD) and non-alcoholic fatty liver
disease (NAFLD) based on cause. An illustration of the different stages of NALFD can be
seen in figure 1.1. The end result of both types is irreversible damage to the liver (cirrhosis),
which can permanently reduce or completely destroy function of the liver. The disease develops
through a buildup of fat within the liver cells, causing them to burst and scar tissue forming
within and around the liver, reducing the amount of functioning cells. The disease is usually
asymptomatic until the liver has been significantly damaged, making detection difficult in most
cases. Diagnosis of the disease is usually done through several different tests, including a blood
test called a liver function test (LFT)[11], an ultrasound scan of the liver (Fibroscan)[12], with
some individuals requiring a liver biopsy.
Figure 1.1: The different stages of Non-Alcohol Fatty Liver Disease
Alcoholic Related Liver Disease
Alcoholic Related Liver Disease (ARLD) is a term used to define liver conditions directly caused
by a high alcohol intake [13]. Treatment includes reducing or completely removing all intake of
alcohol. The disease develops in 3 stages:
1. Alcoholic Fatty Liver Disease (AFLD): Buildup of fat in the liver, resulting from drinking
large amounts of alcohol over a short period of time (days).
2. Alcoholic hepatitis: Persistent inflammation caused by a longer period of high alcohol
consumption.
Page 3
==PAGE:9==
3. Cirrhosis: Permanent damage due to years of inflammation, causing the liver to shrink,
and can lead to liver failure and liver cancer.
Non-Alcoholic fatty liver disease
Non-alcoholic fatty liver disease (NAFLD) is the term used to define conditions resulting from fat
building up in the liver over a longer period of time, caused by general sources, and not limited
to alcohol [14]. The disease develops in 4 stages:
1. Simple fatty liver (Steatosis): Harmless buildup of fat within the liver cells.
2. Non-alcoholic steatohepatitis (NASH): Inflammation occurs due to liver cells bursting from
fat buildup.
3. Fibrosis: Persistent inflammation causing large amounts of scar tissue to form, but the
liver is still able to function as intended.
4. Cirrhosis: Permanent damage due to years of inflammation, causing the liver to shrink,
and can lead to liver failure and liver cancer.
Non-alcoholic fatty liver disease has a genetic component, but is primarily a lifestyle disease
caused by unhealthy habits, such as obesity, type 2 diabetes, smoking, high blood pressure,
high cholesterol etc. Treatment involves removing unhealthy habits and introducing healthy
ones. The disease takes many years to develop, and is therefore mainly found in individuals
over 50.
Clinical vs Non-Clinical setting for tests
A clinical setting in regards to testing refers to tests being performed specifically at a clinic,
such as a general practitioner’s office, hospital or any other healthcare facility [15]. Non-Clinical
settings refer to any setting outside of these confines.
Self-test
The primary type of self-test carried out over the last few years, have been COVID-19 self-tests,
and current EU regulations defines devices used for self-testing as:"any device intended by the
manufacturer to be used by lay persons, including devices used for testing services offered to
lay persons by means of information society services"[5]. This definition is different than the
definition provided for devices used near-patient testing which refers to "any device that is not
intended for self-testing but is intended to perform testing outside a laboratory environment,
generally near to, or at the side of, the patient by a health professional"[5]. This report will NOT
use the definition used in regards to computer software testing, as defined by the U.S. National
Institute of Standards and Technology stating that self-testing refers to "Testing within a system,
device or process during normal operation to detect misbehavior"[16][17].

Software as a Medical Device (SaMD)
The International Medical Device Regulators Forum (IMDRF) defines Software as a Medical
Device (SaMD) as "software intended to be used for one or more medical purposes that perform
these purposes without being part of a hardware medical device." [18]. This differentiates itself
from the two other types of software related to the medical field, which is software in a purpose-
built medical device, and software used in the manufacturing or maintenance process in regards
to medical devices. In the context of this report, the software is specifically designed for non-
medical hardware devices, such as smartphones and PCs.
Prototype
Prototype is a wide term, which covers many different types of methods designed to help test
ideas. This project uses the definition presented in Design Thinking:” A prototype is a simple
experimental model of a proposed solution used to test or validate ideas, design assumptions
and other aspects of its conceptualization quickly and cheaply, so that the designer/s involved
can make appropriate refinements or possible changes in direction.” [19]. The form a prototype
can take may vary, both in fidelity and medium, can be quick and rough or fully formed, and
be used to test part of a solution or the entirety of it. For this project, several different types of
prototypes will be used at different stages of the process. These types of prototypes involve a
low fidelity Click-Through prototype and a high fidelity functional Prototype.
Digital Literacy
The ability, for an individual, to understand and use digital information as defined by Paul Gilster
[20]. When discussing digital literacy, the subterm digital inclusion describes the ability for an
individual or group to access technological resources. Groups such as the elderly, low-income
groups, lower educated, unemployed and language learners experiencing greater digital exclu-
sion [21].
Data Visualization
This report will use the definition of data visualization presented by IBM:"Data visualization is
the representation of data through use of common graphics, such as charts, plots, infographics,
and even animations. These visual displays of information communicate complex data relation-
ships and data-driven insights in a way that is easy to understand." [22]. The visualized data is
not limited to the results of the LiverCheck test, but can also include aspects such as tracking
features and more.

The Situation Globally
Around 2 billion individuals consume alcohol globally, and around 2 billion adults are obese or
overweight. Approximately 25% of the world’s population suffers from fatty liver [2], and Cir-
rhosis complications currently cause around 1 million deaths each year globally, ranking as the
11th most common cause of death. Fatty Liver Disease (FLD) can also lead to liver cancer,
which is the 16th most common cause of death worldwide. Combined these two liver diseases
(FLD and liver cancer) account for 3.5% of all deaths globally [1]. Cirrhosis is amongst the most
common causes of life years lost and years of disability in individuals, accounting for 2.1% and
1.6% of the global burden. Given most causes of these liver diseases are preventable, and are
lifestyle related, multitudes of initiatives exist globally to increase the general health of popula-
tions [23][24]. Non-Alcoholic Fatty Liver Disease (NAFLD) is the most widespread liver condition
in the west, estimated to affect 17-46% of all adults, differing based on diagnostic method and
factors such as age, sex and ethnicity [25].
In Europe, the region of the world with the highest alcohol consumption, and a high prevalence
of obesity, liver disease has now become the second most common cause of working life lost,
following ischaemic heart disease [6]. Chronic liver diseases accounted for almost 300.000
deaths in Europe in 2019, equating to a 25% increase from its 1990 number, when accounting
for population sizes. It has been estimated that if early detection and prevention were to be
properly implemented across Europe, these almost 300.000 deaths could be prevented [6].
From an economic perspective, NAFLD results in annual direct medical costs of 103 Billion
Dollars in the U.S. and 35 Billion Euros in Germany, France, Italy and the UK combined[26]. The
burden on these nations increase significantly when societal costs are included, balooning the
annual costs of NAFLD to 292 Billion Dollars and 227 Billion Euros respectively. These costs are
expected to increase in the future proportionally to the increasing amount of individuals suffering
from NAFLD in the west, unless new treatment methods can be found, or the general health of
the population improves.

The Situation in Denmark
In Denmark the number of individuals drinking and smoking has been steadily declining over
the last decade, and the amount of individuals drinking more than 10 units of alcohol each
week has reduced from 24.6% in 2010 to 15.7% in 2021 [27]. The prevalence of alcoholic liver
disease (ALD) fell from 343 per 1 million population in 2006 to 311 per 1 million population in
2011, keeping in line with the falling alcohol consumption in the general population throughout
this period of time [28]. The number of obese individuals over the age of 16 (BMI > 25) and
heavily obese (BMI > 30) have increased significantly from 46.8% of the population in 2010 to
52.6% of the population in 2021 [27]. Rates of obesity were highest for older and lower educated
individuals. A general correlation can be found between individuals suffering from obesity and
NAFLD. Approximately 50% of individuals suffering from obesity [29] will as a result of their
obesity and other lifestyle factors also suffer from NAFLD. With more individuals in Denmark
becoming obese, and a larger prevalence of other lifestyle diseases such as type 2 diabetes,
a similar increase in NAFLD is expected over the coming years. A majority of Danes asked
in 2021, expressed a desire to improve their health, both through healthier eating habits and
increase in exercise [27]. A direct correlation between weight loss and improvement of liver
health has been widely documented [30], which can be done by the individuals themselves, or
with the assistance of the public health sector.
The Patient Pathway
The current patient pathway for individuals being determined to be at risk in Denmark is as
follows:
1. A full blood package is drawn during an annual checkup, after which blood samples are
transported to a centralized lab for analysis.
2. Test values are sent from the lab to the general practitioner, who then interpret the results
and consult the patient, depending on said results.
3. The patient can then be referred to secondary care, of which 54% currently are redundant
[3].
4. Of those referred, who have been correctly diagnosed with Cirrhosis, 2/3 have been iden-
tified too late, resulting in the disease being irreversible and fatal[3].
There is currently no pathway for individuals who are not deemed to be at risk by the general
practitioner, or widespread testing amongst the general population.
The Company, Evido
Evido [4] is a Danish startup, spun off from FLASH - Center for Liver Research at Odense Uni-
versity Hospital [31], where liver research and large scale population screening of liver health
Page 7
==PAGE:13==
has been underway for many years. The startup is based around the product LiverPro, a clini-
cal decision tool which enables timely detection of FLD patients at first point-of-contact (in pri-
mary care). In Denmark this would be your personal doctor. The tool provides specialized
liver knowledge and recommendation on demand for all clinicians, and utilizes regularly used
blood samples and the existing test practice environment in order to allow for easy implementa-
tion and scalability. LiverPro is based on state-of-the-art algorithms that translate simple blood
samples into an accurate risk assessment of the patient with 92% accuracy for patients in the
high-prevalent group, based on current testing done by the company 6.1.
The company behind the product, Evido, has developed, and is currently optimizing, a simplified
version of the algorithm, which doesn’t require blood samples. This simplified algorithm only re-
quires data which can be input by the user, such as age, weight, waist circumference and more.
The accuracy of this algorithm is still being determined, and heavily depends on what stage of
the disease the algorithm will be set to detect. If the algorithm is set to detect all individuals who
could suffer from FLD, without taking into account the severity of the disease, then the accuracy
could be significantly higher, than if it were targeted towards identifying users who are in the
later stages of the illness. Since a large number of individuals suffering from FLD in Denmark
are in the benign early stage, and many years away from reaching the more dangerous stages,
a decision within the company has been made to adjust the algorithm. This adjustment filters of
individuals in the benign stages to only include individuals in the more dangerous later stages.
The resulting accuracy of the current algorithm is approx 75%, but is expected to be increased
to more than 80% if the definition of individuals who would be deemed at risk of suffering from
FLD were to be broadened. Investigating what the potential effects of such a choice will be done
in the literature review section on "Overdiagnosis".
This project will focus on developing the self-test UX, to reach as many potential patients as
possible. The project is focused on an application for the Danish market. It is the goal of
the company to expand beyond this market in the future, but all data collected throughout this
design process, and all resulting insights will be specific to the Danish market. Some preliminary
investigations have been carried out by the company to assess interest from potential users, with
current results being promising. There are currently no existing products on the market focused
on increasing ease-of-access and improving diagnostic accuracy of liver health screenings using
this type of algorithm. Several liver health initiatives currently exist, both in the clinical space,
and in regards to education and awareness, However, no direct competitor to either LiverPro or
LiverCheck currently exists on the market. These policy initiatives and existing and competing
solutions in the clinical space will be expanded upon in the following literature review chapter.

2.1 Digital Public Service Channels in Denmark
When developing any product, the context in which it will be released is crucial to its design.
Denmark is one of the most digitized countries in Europe, and the percentage of individuals
using the internet for interacting with public authorities and obtaining information from public
authorities is 30% higher than the EU average (approx 90% of the the population)[32]. Den-
mark scores well in regards to the implementation of The European Interoperability Framework,
which is a framework focused ensuring access to digital public services for all citizens within the
EU[33]. The Danish Governments Digital developed a Health Strategy 2018–2022 focused on
enabling all stakeholders to cooperate in an integrated network centered around the patients.
This strategy has resulted in the regions developing and implementing electronic health record
systems[34]. A National Strategy for Artificial Intelligence, launched in 2019 marks the health-
care sector as one of the focus areas for artificial intelligence (AI)[35]. One of the signature
projects involving AI focuses on utilizing it to support general practitioners by allowing them to
9
==PAGE:15==
diagnose diseases quicker and assist in identifying abnormal test results.
The existing digital infrastructure with the danish healthcare sector and high level of digital lit-
eracy in Denmark, suggests that citizens would be more willing than in most other countries to
accept a software based test such as LiverCheck. It also stresses the importance of keeping the
integration options open to allow for easier implementation in the existing digital infrastructure.
2.2 Policy Initiatives
When looking at initiatives aimed specifically at combating challenges resulting from the grow-
ing number of individuals suffering from Fatty Liver Disease (FLD) in the west, one can look at
both governmental and non-governmental organisations. Countless patient groups and inter-
est organisations focused on liver health can be found around the world, such as: ELPA [36],
GlobalLiver [37], Liverpatients International [38] and in Denmark; Leverforeningen [39]. These
organizations focus on increasing awareness, influence national policy around the world and
assisting patients. Specific strategic action plans have been developed by the World Health Or-
ganization (WHO) for combating viral hepatitis [40], with a lack of focus on FLD. Current public
health campaigns by the danish health authorities focus primarily on vaccination and hygiene in
order to prevent transmission of Covid-19 [41], along with long running campaigns focused on
reducing drug use and smoking. The European Association for the Study of the Liver, (EASL-
Lancet Liver Commission) has proposed a framework for tackling the health threats associated
with the rising occurrences of liver diseases across Europe [6]. The framework consists of 10
recommendations, of which half are focused on health policy and half are focused on health
care providers. Two of these recommendations (#1 & #2) are focused on increasing awareness
and early stage testing amongst the general population, by investing in advertisement, educa-
tion regarding the disease and population-specific outreach programs. It must be noted that this
framework was published in late 2021, and has yet to be implemented.
This policy framework aligns with the ambitions of Evido, and increasing awareness and early
detection are the specific purpose for which LiverCheck will be developed. So while this project
will not be utilizing the framework, and a public health campaign may not be a solution for
this project, a synergistic relationship is more likely due to the recent increased focus from
policy makers in tackling the rising FLD issue in Europe. Any solution must involve investigating
existing methods for tackling this issue in both the clinical and non-clinical space.
2.3 Clinical Solutions
In the clinical space, several newer clinical tests have been developed utilizing machine learn-
ing with the FibroTest [42] being the most prominent, with a mean applicability rate of 97% [43].
The FibroTest utilize several different biomarkers of a large blood sample and must be carried
out in a medical test lab, which is compliant with their requirements. Newer tests also using
blood samples can be found on the market, such as the Enhanced Blood Fibrosis (ELF) test
Page 10
==PAGE:16==
by Siemens [44] which also utilizing different biomarkers and have proven effective [45]. When
comparing the Fibrotest and ELF test to a liver biopsy, regarded as the gold standard, and dif-
ferent indirect markers, on individuals suffering from alcohol related fatty liver, the Fibrotest and
ELF test both accurately detected advanced liver fibrosis in patients in approx 90% of cases [7].
Both tests proved significantly more accurate than diagnosing using indirect markers. A group
of Scottish researchers utilized nonlinear mixed effect models on a standard liver function test
(LFT) results, increasing the accuracy of the test by more than 43% (to over 90%) [46]. LFT is
the standard blood sample method currently ordered by primary physicians when liver disease
is suspected. FibroTest, ELF and the previous tests however all require a specific type of blood
test. These protocols can be further developed to be applicable to a standardized type of blood
test, as in the case of LiverPro, or even function completely independent of a blood test as in
the case of LiverCheck.
Attempts at further optimizing standard blood samples are ongoing with researchers from both
FLASH (Odense University Hospital) and other universities identifying a new potential biomarker
for detection of individuals with Non-alcoholic steatohepatitis (NASH) [47].
Researchers at
Odense and Copenhagen University have used machine learning to detect proteomic (proteins)
biomarker panels, which were able to accurately predict future liver events of individuals suf-
fering from alchohol related liver disease (ARLD) [48]. A group of Nordic researchers have
developed a risk prediction model, using parameters such as age, sex, alcohol use, waist-hip
ratio, diabetes, smoking and blood sample information [49]. The risk prediction model is based
on parameters similar to the one developed by Evido. The model was validated using data from
Finland, Denmark and The UK, using the same protocol and showing similar results. The risk
prediction model is however focused on predicting the risk for future advanced liver disease
over the next 15 years, as opposed to LiverPro, which focuses on determining if the individual
suffers from Fatty Liver Disease at this moment. The system used by LiverPro, in regards to
placing individuals as either in low, medium or high risk of suffering from the disease at this mo-
ment in time, is also the same principle LiverCheck will be developed from. Since LiverCheck
is focused on funneling at-risk individuals to their personal doctor to be tested immediately, a
"current liverstatus" result was deemed more usable for users.
While these tests all uses similar technology, none of the methods utilizing standard blood sam-
ples which can be collected at ones personal doctor are currently implemented on the danish
market, meaning no existing competitor on the market exists to either LiverPro or LiverCheck.
2.4 Self Testing
When looking at the non-clinical space, the lack of tests similar to LiverCheck, means widening
the scope for this section was neccesary in order to generate usable findings. A well doc-
umented and researched self-test, the canadian home fitness test, developed in the 1970s,
designed to screen the national health of the population through a self-report system was found
to be an adequate predictor for aerobic capability [50]. However, it was noted regarding the test
Page 11
==PAGE:17==
that when screening a large asymptomatic population using a survey or self-report system, a
large amount of false positives and negatives will occur, due to incorrect data being recorded
[51]. A similar result should be expected from LiverCheck, and must be accounted for. This
aspect will be discussed further in the section on "Overdiagnosis".
Age related usability testing was found to be necessary when developing home testing devices,
as older individuals in general perform poorer with electronic devices and applications. Instruc-
tions greatly assisted in reducing this discrepancy, but the type of instruction was found to be
critical, as older individuals who received video training performed at the same level as their
younger counterparts [52]. This study highlights the need of taking digital literacy into account
when developing a product for the general population, and will be discussed in the "Digital Liter-
acy" section of this literature review. Web-based Direct-Access Testing (DAT) was utilized in the
USA to allow individuals to bypass a physician, if they had suspicions of suffering from celiac
disease [53]. This self-diagnosis of celiac disease was found to provide the same amount of
legitimacy as a medical diagnosis, but resulted in significant relief and some behavioral changes
in individuals. Researchers noted when looking at the motivation and experiences of self-testers
regarding cardiovascular risk factors, that 3 types of distinct users existed: those who actively
looked for the self test themselves, and those who engaged in the self-test when offered, either
with or without previous knowledge of the disease [54].
This research highlights the importance of doing usability testing on a diverse group of indi-
viduals, as LiverCheck is aimed at all adults (in particular those above 50). It also highlights
the importance of clear instructions and ensuring that individuals with different motivations are
included in the testing, in order to develop a UX which achieves the goal of funneling at-risk
individuals towards clinical testing.
Another aspect to consider when assisting users with the test and its results is Artificial intelli-
gence (AI), which is increasingly being utilized to develop chatbots as a replacement for consul-
tation with a doctor [55]. These chatbots heavily rely on the AI algorithms and training data and
have generally been designed for specific populations. Barriers preventing widespread adop-
tion of the chatbots primarily include usability and credibility. Such chatbots could be utilized to
assist older individuals who are struggling to test themselves, in order to improve the UX of the
test. In the psychological space, existing products such as BetterHelp [56] have been providing
mental health services to consumers for several years. When analyzing what effectiveness and
motivations amongst individuals who used such programs, researchers found that factors such
as functionality, content, legitimacy and the perceived need for it by the user influenced the per-
ceived effectiveness of the programs [57].
These factors are key to keep in mind when developing LiverCheck, and investigating the ex-
isting methods and frameworks utilized by designers of medical devices, can assist in shaping
the process used to develop LiverCheck.

2.5 Designing and developing a Medical Device
The current medical device development process is characterized by large complexity, the in-
volvement of multiple engineering disciplines and increasingly stringent requirements from gov-
ernments in regard to safety [58]). The User-Centered Design (UCD) approach is one of the
most prevalent in the medical industry, with large amounts of research done to support it [8].
Medical device user error, caused by poor human factor design during development has been
shown to be significant factor in the effectiveness of a medical product, as well as the number of
injuries and fatalities sustained by its users [59]. One way to combat this problem has been to
implement a UCD approach focused on a carrying out a comprehensive scientific literature re-
view and ensuring involvement from potential users at multiple stages of the design process [8].
Government agencies such as the FDA have also developed medical standard guidelines to as-
sist producers with the application of usability engineering principles for their devices to improve
safety, reduce amount of misuse, and improve the general usability [60]. Specific methods used
in Human Factors Engineering (HFE)-based medical device design, a form of UCD, includes
contextual inquires, interviews, functional analysis, prototyping and testing [59].
These methods are not only key to the development of medical devices, but form foundational
aspects of all UCD. Applying a general UCD process, with an emphasis on reducing misuse by
users and reducing the amount of overdiagnosis any solution can lead to, should result in a more
optimal product. For this project, a UCD process shaped around the Double Diamond will be
utilized. An aspect unique to the development of medical devices is the amount of regulations
applied to them, and the extensive approval process any product must go through. These guide-
lines, which are covered in the next section of the literature review, are frequently updated, and
guides designed at navigating them can be used to assist one during the product development
process [61].
2.6 Regulations Regarding Medical Devices
When developing any medical device, certain Medical Device Regulations (MDR) apply, based
on what type of product is being developed. For Denmark, these are determined by the EU and
apply across all member states [62]. Any design developed through the process must consider
which Class it falls under [63], since different rules apply to medical devices based on factors
such as the invasiveness of the device, the temporal extend of its effects, the requirement of
continuous use etc. The partner company for this project, Evido, uses a Class I license [64],
which allows them to carry out the approval process internally for new software or devices if
that software falls under the same license. This factor means that any tests which are less in-
vasive and more anonymous than the LiverPro blood sample test, would not require applying
for a higher license than the existing Class I. Examples of other medical devices which falls
under Class I and can therefore be self-certified are: Manual wheelchairs, corrective glasses,
Stethoscopes, Personal Protection Kits and many more.
Page 13
==PAGE:19==
This aspect of whether application for a higher license is required will be kept in mind throughout
the design process and will form part of the evaluation process for the concepts developed. Any
product which requires a classification higher than Class I, will be deemed more bothersome to
realize as the approval process will be far more complex.
2.7 Digital Literacy
Several factors determine the design of a product. For this specific context, Digital Literacy, the
visualization of the result and the potential for overdiagnosis are of critical importance. When
developing any piece of software, digital literacy must be kept in mind, and in this situation where
the target demographic consists of older individuals, even more so, due to the rising average
age in the EU, as a result of increased life expectancy and falling birth rates [65]. Several ini-
tiatives intended to analyze and develop solutions to this problem have been carried out by the
EU, in order to increase social inclusion and quality of life. The DIGITOL [65] report notes that In
general Nordic countries such as Denmark, Sweden and Finland score significantly higher than
their European counterparts, with 77% of individuals in Finland having basic software skills, with
younger individuals scoring significantly higher than older. The report also makes it clear that
the diverse profiles of older individuals within society makes a “one-size-fits-all” training method
or approach in order to reach all of these individuals impossible. Internet usage amongst the
older population (45 and older) is also usually more focused on social and information seeking
activities, such as maintaining contact with old friends, or looking up information regarding public
services [66], which differs from younger individuals who use it for activities such as shopping,
banking and other practical activities. Focus group research has also shown that older indi-
viduals are apprehensive towards newer devices such as smartphones and tablets, despite a
willingness to learn the technology [67]. A lack of clear instructions and support were noted as
the key reasons, further cementing the need for a greater focus on the user experience when
developing a product for an older generation as compared to a younger demographic.
This research highlights the need for ensuring that the platforms on which LiverCheck is launched,
are compatible with the target demographic. Whether or not multiple platforms must be utilized
in order to cast as wide a net as possible, or if it can be narrowed down to one remains to be
investigated.
2.8 Data Visualization
When presenting results of a test, the visualization of the data is key to ensuring understanding
amongst users. Data visualisation has long been used in the medical industry, and has been
found to have a clear effect in particular in regards to continuous glucose level monitoring [68].
Several experiments concerning the effect of different types of data visualization have been
carried out. In an experiment testing the effect of social norms on behaviour, a large group of
households in the U.S. were informed via written notes of their households energy consump-
tion and how it related to other individuals within the local community [69]. While users who
Page 14
==PAGE:20==
used more energy than average generally reduced their energy usage, a "boomerang effect"
or "rebound effect" would occur amongst users were informed that they used less energy than
their peers, leading to a higher energy usage in these individuals. This rebound effect could
be negated by including a happy smiley for households which consumed less than average,
and a sad smiley for households consuming more in their written note. For the purpose of Liv-
erCheck, ensuring that individuals who score "well" do not "boomerang" is key, and so utilizing
social norms to ensure this could be beneficial. In visualizing results in longer term tracking
software (such as fitness apps like Google Fit, or Apple Fitness+), researchers utilized gami-
fication and data visualization by giving users of a health app a virtual avatar[70], in this case
a fish, which grew as they exercised, meaning no negative reinforcement were included. This
experiment underscored the importance of having visual indicators, and focusing on encourag-
ing positive user behaviour. Studies have also found that individuals are more likely to achieve
goals or tasks if a high-low range is set, as opposed to a single number, meaning that they are
more likely to lose weight if the monthly goal is 2-4kg instead of 3kg [71].
This principle is transferable to LiverCheck both in regards to how recommendations to seek
clinical help or carrying out lifestyle changes should be presented, but also how the length of
the test should be presented to the users before they commence it.
2.9 Overdiagnosis
When developing any device or software which could result in an increased workload for gen-
eral practitioners and other parts of the Danish healthcare system, a key aspect which must
be investigated is the concept of overdiagnosis. Singh et al[72] note that: “It is extremely im-
portant to recognize and increase awareness of the phenomenon of overdiagnosis, as it is one
of the most common and unavoidable consequences of screening and early detection of any
disorder.” The psychological burden on the individual and financial effects on society can be se-
vere, and Overdetection, referring to: “the identification of abnormalities that were never going
to cause harm, abnormalities that do not progress, that progress too slowly to cause symptoms
or harm during a person’s remaining lifetime, or that resolve spontaneously.[73]” is only propa-
gated by self-testing. A recent study has shown that the benefit-harm ratio of early screening
for breast cancer has deteriorated with over the past 30 years, and will continue to do so due
to increasingly effective treatment [74]. Overdiagnosis is more likely to occur in individuals with
slow progressing diseases such as Fatty Liver Disease (FLD) [72], adding to the challenge of
developing a tool which is effective but does not contribute to overdiagnosis.
Determining when to refer them to a clinical test at the personal physician, and how to express
the risks of FLD they may face is of utmost importance for any design. Some aspect of this
comes down to the algorithm, which is outside the scope of this project, while others come
down to communication, information delivery and product design.

3.1 Overall project structure
The project will follow the double diamond structure [75] while utilizing agile development [76]
throughout the process, and particularly once an initial prototype has been developed to test it
and generate further insights into the user group and product requirements. This structure has
previously been utilized for projects of a digital nature, with high ambiguity in the user group [77],
and provides this project with both a user-focused launch point in the double diamond, and the
tools to allow for rapid development and testing through agile development, and more precisely
SCRUM sprints [78]. These tools were selected, based on the findings from the literature review
concerning the need for User Center Design (UCD), and previous experience with utilizing these
methods in UCD projects. The overall project structure and timeline is show in figure 3.1.
Figure 3.1: Overall project structure and timeline, illustrated using the Double Diamond frame-
work
The Double Diamond framework consist of four different phases namely: ’Discover’,’Define’,’Develop’,
and ’Deliver’[93], each having either a converging focus or diverging focus. The Double Dia-
mond allows for testing and iterating of the design, in particular during the ’develop’ and ’deliver’
phases. The structure of the framework allows for returning to a previous stage if a re-evalution
Page 20
==PAGE:26==
of the design or underlying assumptions foundational to the development of the product is necce-
sary. For this project, the ’develop’ and ’deliver’ phases are repeated for each iteration of the
prototype in order to create and test it. This process is best illustrated by the cycles each iter-
ation of the prototype went through, shown through the agile development framework in figure
3.2.

State-of-the-art literature review
The initial desk research consisted of a literature review, with the purpose of understanding the
disease, the context in which it exists, Evido’s position and current research into dealing with
the rising rate of fatty liver disease in the west.
Proto-Personas
No scope was given by Evido, and no detailed investigations into the user group had been
performed by them. They use two very broad proto-personas in their sales material, one of a
middle-aged woman and one of a middle-aged man, which can be found in Appendix A (A).
Expanding on these two based on the initial desk research, using the proto-personas method
allowed for the creation of a more diverse field of personas. The purpose of this process was
to both gain an initial understanding of the diversity within the group of individuals potentially
suffering from liver health issues, before carrying out the contextual interviews. The method
suggested a framework in which you fill out 4 boxes containing:
• A fictional name
• Biographical Information
• Character Traits
• Values

Archetypes were then be formed based on these four dimensions, which encapsulated aspects
of the general population, so that as encompassing a net were cast in regards to individuals
who could potentially suffer from fatty liver disease.
Actor Worlds and Development Arenas
The ’Actor Worlds and Development Arenas’-method is used to describe the arena of develop-
ment in which a product, service or company exists, and the worlds of actors which surrounds
it and interacts with it. The purpose of the method is to detail the surrounding environment and
predict implications which might arise with the introduction of a new actor or product. For this
project, the method was used to describe the stakeholders surrounding the LiverCheck project,
in order to gain a greater understanding of all actors involved.
Contextual Interviews
Contextual interviews were conducted on 10 individuals. 5 were citizens who had been invited to
a liver health screening at FLASH - Center for Liver Research at Odense University Hospital. 5
were individuals from the general populous with no existing liver health issues, in order to gain a
more nuanced image. The individuals invited to the health screening, were interviewed following
their screening, while waiting for their results. The questions asked to each group were identical
and the interview structure was the same, starting with a a structured pre-interview followed by
a semi-structured contextual interview. The pre-interview consisted of demographic questions
allowing me to compare the subjects with eachother, the desk-research data and the existing
proto-personas. The contextual interview section were focused on gauging what individuals
would want in a product focused on improving their liver health without clinical intervention,
and aspects connected to this, such as estimated effect, ability to change behaviour, preferred
method of introduction and more. An example of an interview can be found in Appendix E (E).
Personas
The previously created proto-personas were modified based on insights from the contextual
interviews and newer insights gained from the continuous literature review. The goal of this
modification of the previously created personas, was to more accurately present the user group
and gain a greater understanding of the diversity within it.
Wallwalk
A wallwalk was carried out to create an overview of key input from the literature review, contextual
interviews and the creation of the development arena and personas. Thereby allowing for a
greater understanding of the problem area. The purpose of this process was to further scope
the problem, and helping define the key question any solution should attempt to answer.

The ’Function Means Tree’-method, was applied to define, what basic function the system should
provide. Based on the defined key question that a solution should answer, a descending series
of structured questions further details the system. The purpose of this method, was to ensure
the key question would allow for an open ideation process in the Develop stage, and sketch out
different approaches to answering this key question.

KJ-Technique
The ’KJ-Technique’-method aimed at structuring the often chaotic ideation process, by separat-
ing the idea generation from the synthesizing stage. This was done by having 1 5 minute brain-
storming session, 1 5 minute break, and another 5 minute brainstorming session. Once these
sessions were completed the design ideas were clustered based on what aspect of a home-test
they fit thematically. Additional post-its were added during this step if a cluster seemed to miss
a key aspect. These clusters formed the columns of the following morphology.
Page 24
==PAGE:30==
Morphology
The ’Morphology’-method served as a framework for the concept-creation, and was based on
the design ideas and clusters generated by the KJ-Technique. The design ideas were distributed
into 5 columns, corresponding to the transferred 5 clusters. 10 concepts were drawn from the
existing ideas focused on creating coherent solution oriented concepts. The concepts were then
developed, sketched out and organized in order to prepare for evaluation with the Evido CEO.
Weighted Objectives Method (WOM)
The ’WOM’-method was applied in order to evaluate the concepts from the morphology, based
on quantitative measures of the concepts ’goodness’. The ’WOM’ method ranks concepts based
on weighted parameters, meaning different amount of importance is applied to the designs per-
formance across different parameters. The parameters and weights of the matrix were deter-
mined through dialogue with the company CEO, in order to ensure the selected concept would
fulfill their requirements. Scoring is done from 1-5, and is done for each concept independently
of one another. The purpose of this method was to select a general concept which could be
further developed through prototyping, testing and iterating, and to create alignment between
the designer and the company.
Lean Canvas
A Lean Canvas was then developed, creating a 1 page overview of the concept consisting of;
the background for why it is needed, it’s intended user group and key design aspects. The
purpose of this step was to gain a clear overview of the concept before sketching out the User
Story Map and developing the click-through-prototype.
User Story Map
The ’User Story Map’ method sketches out the different point at which a user interacts with a
system, and the necessary features required to facilitate these interactions. The method was
applied in order to get a general overview of the user interaction with the software, and sketching
out the system flow, to ensure a blueprint was available for the development of the click-through
prototype in Figma.
Click-Through Prototype
A click-through prototype allows users to interact with a product as if it existed, without the
designer or developer writing the required code. For this project, the click-through prototype
was developed in Figma [94], in order to generate an initial prototype which could be tested
on users. A substantial number of digital visual software prototyping tools exist on the market,
and Figma was selected due to previous experience with the tool. Products such as Adobe XD,
Justinmind or any other click-through prototyping tool could be equally effective for this project.

State-of-the-art literature Review
The literature review, presented in an earlier chapter, served as the scientific foundation upon
which the development process could commence. Key takeaways from this investigation which
formed the design process and product design included but were not limited to:
No policy initiatives in Denmark, specifically targeting Fatty Liver Disease (FLD), which
LiverCheck could be implemented as part of, currently exist. Meaning that systemic ap-
proaches, such as top-down initiatives cannot be a solution for this project.
A policy framework has been developed by the European Association of the Study of
the Liver, which could potentially be implemented in the future, allowing for a synergistic
relationship with LiverCheck and LiverPro.
Existing clinical solutions require more complicated tools than LiverPro, and no competing
solutions to the LiverCheck algorithm currently exist on the market.
Compliance with a large amount of regulations when developing medical devices, and in
the case of Evido, the current Class I license allows for them to carry out the approval
Page 32
==PAGE:38==
process internally, meaning any design concept developed throughout this process must
keep in mind which Class of medical product it would fall under.
A User-Centered Design (UCD) approach has proven highly effective when developing
medical products and is widely used in the idustry. As stated previously in the report, the
Double Diamond framework, with large amounts of user testing, has been used throughout
the process as the primary UCD approach.
Age related usability testing is crucial when developing self-tests, in particular tests depen-
dant on users fully understanding instructions. Usability testing of prototypes developed
throughout this project must include different age groups in order to validate functionality.
Digital literacy is generally high in Denmark, but a significant difference between gen-
erations still exist, with different preferences for intermediary when interacting with soft-
ware. Designing a product which can work cross-platform, or utilizes a platform the largest
amount of the population can interact with and feel exited to use must be kept in mind dur-
ing the development process.
Factors such as functionality, content, legitimacy and perceived need by the user were key
in influencing the perceived effectiveness of medical software. These factors will be kept
in mind when developing LiverCheck.’
Effective data visualization can be used to ensure any communication of test results, risk
or information will lead to the desired response. Utilizing tools such as high-low ranges
and social norms can be advantageous for LiverCheck.
Overdiagnosis is a problematic aspect of any medical testing, in particular early screenings
of slow progressing diseases such as FLD. When developing LiverCheck, adjusting design
to reduce the amount of overdiagnosis which could potentially result from releasing it must
be kept in mind throughout the process.

against which the design concepts were evaluated, were established based upon the 3 aspects
of the mindset model from Design Thinking [97], expanded to 5 which include; viability, respon-
sibility, desirability, sustainability and feasibility. Additional criteria were added in cooperation
with Evido to ensure aspects such as estimated impact, benefit to customer and potential for
monetization were included. The criteria were weighted (with the total adding up to 100 or 1) in
accordance with the principles of WOM. The requirements and criteria can be found in the table
in figure 4.17.

Figure 4.17: Table of Requirements and Criteria designs must adhere to
The reasoning behind the selection of requirements is as follows:
1. GDPR regulations must be complied with (consent must be given before sharing data):
Any product which requires sharing of data within the EU must comply with current General
Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) rules. A full overview of GDPR can be found on their
website [98].
2. Must connect to LiverPro:
One of the purposes of the product being developed is to function as a funnel tool to
hopefully increase the amount of individuals interested in their liver health, and through
that go to their doctor and get a blood test. The product does not necessarily have to utilize
LiverPro results (for tracking or similar purposes), nor does it need to directly recommend
users to get their liver health checked if they are perfectly healthy.
3. Near patient testing regulations (MDR forordning) must be complied with:
Medical Device Regulations (MDR), presented in the literature review, must be complied
with. A full overview of MDR regulations can be found on their website [99].
4. Must be launch-ready by Q4, 2023:
LiverPro is scheduled to be launched in Q4, 2023 and LiverCheck is required to be launch
ready at the same time.
What the different criterias entails is as follows:
1. Scalability (Viability):
How scalable is the concept, is it something which can be used by a small group of indi-
viduals in Denmark, or can it be exported to the rest of Scandinavia, Europe, the world?
2. Credibility (Responsibility):
How credible is the format, distributor, data handling etc. Would a user trust the source,
Page 47
==PAGE:53==
the algorithm (if one is used), the test method? What information are we asking of the
user, and what responsibilities does the company have in regards to this information?
3. Usability (by target segment) (Desirability):
How usable is the product by the target segment, is it a format that all users in the segment
know how to use and like to use? A formula-1 car and a standard sedan have different
user groups and therefore different requirements in regards to usability. Older individuals
are more used to computers, while younger individuals are more used to mobile devices
etc.
4. Benefit to users / Impact:
How much does this product benefit our users, how great of an impact on their lives would
it have?
5. Benefit to customer:
How much does this product benefit our customer, both from a financial, workload, effi-
ciency perspective. Does it fulfill the requirements they have for a product of this sort, and
is it even something they would be willing to pay for? The customer will vary depending
on the concept, sometimes they will be the user, sometimes a larger organization.
6. Direct monetization of livercheck:
How much money can Evido make directly off this product (excluding LiverPro).
7. Societal impact (Sustainability):
How great of a societal impact would this product have. This differentiates itself from
benefit to user, in that a product can be highly beneficial to a small user group without
affecting the larger society in general.
8. Development effort (Feasibility):
How much work would it take to develop this product, is it possible to produce a finished
product within our timeframe (Q4, 2023), and how demanding would it be?
The weights assigned to each criteria, represent a clear focus on ensuring high usability, cred-
ibility and benefit to both users and customers, while assigning less value to aspects such as
the potential for direct monetization of LiverCheck. With the weights assigned, evaluating each

Some specific design choices made during the prototype development phase of note are:
The primary colour chosen for the prototype was blue, due to its trust generating effect,
according to color psychology, and general association with existing medical institutions,
such as the Danish regions and hospitals.
The test questions were split into multiple screens to allow screen text to be large and easily
readable on any device, in order to increase accessibility for users with sight issues.
Text was used throughout the application to improve accessibility for individuals with lower
levels of digital literacy, which had been identified as a key aspect to tackle in the Discover
and Define phase.
All buttons were large, with significant difference in between in order to reduce the potential
for miss-clicks and increase usability and accessibility for individuals with reduced motor
skills.
Information regarding FLD was included, in accordance with the key design aspects pre-
viously defined and to ensure any result from the test would be useful to users.

In addition to a written recommendation, a visualization of the resulting score on the final
page was implemented, in order to clearly visualize the context of the users, in accordance
with one of the key design aspect defined earlier in the process.
A direct recommendation to go see ones personal doctor if the user was deemed at risk of

to LiverPro and making the product more valuable to Evido.
No design is perfect from the start, and these design choices, will later be evaluated through
user tests. The principles used for the design of the prototype are generally true, but are heavily

Prototyping
The final prototype development consisted of addressing feedback from users, aimed at minor
improvements without changing the fundamental user experience of the software. Based on
feedback from users during the quantitative prototype testing, several aspects of the prototype
were addressed. These were specifically:
1. Test information screen was modified.
To increase user trust, specific figures regarding the number of individuals tested by FLASH
before developing the algorithms behind LiverPro and LiverCheck.
This change was
based on specific feedback from some users who felt like the algorithm could not be ac-
curate, based on these 7 parameters, and would like to know how it was created.
2. Test result output text made unique.
A user felt that the text prompts on the results screen was misleading for users who did not
fulfill any risk criteria, and so instead of all users receiving the message of "The risk has
been determined based on you fulfilling the highlighted criteria", users at no risk would
instead receive the message "You are not at risk because you do not fulfil any of the
following criteria"
3. Including text on result screen notifying users the test is over
Some users were unsure whether or not the test was complete and if they could close the
program, so a text message was added to adress this.
Several aspects were harder to address, either due to it being outside our control, the format of
the input required for the algorithm, or a lack of large scale testing. These will be discussed in
the overall analysis section.

amounts of feedback in order to identify issues not detected during qualitative testing. The

extremely high, some aspects can still be optimized. A few aspects which could not be directly
adressed through a re-design include:

temically, is crucial. The systematic aspects (Inside Application), initially identified through the
state-of-the-art literature review and contextual interviews but expanded upon throughout the
design process, were concretely addressed in the following ways:
1. Informs about FLD.
The pre-test information screens with references to Sundhed.dk allows users to increase
their awareness of FLD.
2. Present information and results effectively, without resulting in over diagnosis.
This aspect was tackled in multiple ways. The results were worded as individuals being
"at risk" of suffering from FLD, instead of directly stating they suffered from it.
Illustrations showing the different stages of FLD was omitted from the pre-test and post-
test screens, as these could cause emotional stress to users not suffering from FLD.
The specific risk criteria individuals fulfilled was included on the final page, to allow them to
better understand the results, and reduce unnecessary fear or confusion amongst users.
Information on the application itself was kept to a minimum, and links to Sundhed.dk where
more information can be accessed was instead selected.
3. High usability, regardless of level of digital literacy.
This aspect was also tacked in multiple ways, presented throughout the analysis and re-
sults section. A few ways of note include:
Implementing UX Design principles, based on the UX-Honeycomb, the gestalt principles
and color psychology, were implemented throughout the design.
Text size was kept large, and amounts of text was kept to a minimum throughout the ap-
plication to assist visually impaired individuals, and reduce reading burden of users.
The test was designed to take as little time as possible, while limiting the number of ques-
tions per page to 1, to reduce the chances of users being overwhelmed.
4. Establish high level of trust in product and potential test.
This aspect was directly addressed in the application by text explaining the origins of the
research resulting in the test, and logos of the research institutes from where it originates.
5. Communicate clearly.
This aspect became apparent during the testing, and focuses around the modifications to
the waist circumference question, described throughout the analysis and results section.
Feedback from users made it clear, that communicating clearly is essential, in particular
when presenting instructions. Changing this question, and in particular the formulation of
the question resulted in a significantly better performance during the quantitative testing
of the 3rd prototype.
Several changes to the language used throughout the test also occurred throughout the
development and testing, as a result of user feedback, and illustrates why this aspect is so
important, in particular when user input is necessary for the product to have the intended
effect.

5.1 Expansion Potential
The current version of LiverCheck consist of a limited test, and while user feedback indicates a
high level of satisfaction with the current product, and high usability, some potential avenues of
expansion still exist as shown in figure 5.1.
Figure 5.1: Potential avenues of expansion for LiverCheck
Two primary lanes of expansion exist, one focused on creating a product which not only can
evaluate an individuals risk of suffering from FLD, but expanding into other lifestyle diseases
such as diabetes or heart diseases. This type of expansion would be heavily dependant on
large amounts of research similar to that carried out at FLASH - Center for Liver Research at
Odense University Hospital, which forms the foundational research required for the develop-
ment of the algorithm used in LiverCheck and LiverPro. While it is highly likely that this type
of research have been carried out for well known lifestyle diseases such as diabetes or lung
cancer, an investigation into this research and the development of algorithms to detect these
would need to be developed. An expansion of included parameters and as a result questions in
the test would most likely be necessary, which could increase the length of the test and reduce
usability for users. A second concern with this type of expansion would be that the impact for
each individual disease may be diluted. This is due to users would receiving results in regards
to many different diseases, with no general practitioner to explain these results.
75
==PAGE:81==
The second lane of expansion focuses on increasing features included in the product only tar-
geting liver health. One way would be for mobile device users to store LiverCheck results of
their phone, as a way to view progress. Users attitude towards this feature was evaluated dur-
ing the testing of the 1st prototype. Users expressed a positive attitude towards the feature,
but did not find it necessary for the application, which resulted in it originally being deselected
for further development. Introducing this feature would be relatively simple, but would require
some testing in order to ensure similar levels of usability as in the rest of the application. This
feature could be expanded upon by allowing users to store results from clinical tests, such as
LiverPro, an ultrasound scan, a liver biopsi and other diagnostic methods. These clinical tools
all present their results in different formats, and no universal "figure" or "number" exist which
would allow users to directly compare results from different diagnostic methods. This challenge
means that communicating the meaning of the results to users would be a large challenge, in
particular in a situation such as a test-log where no medical professional would be available to
assist the user. These challenges means that implementation of any features beyond its core
should not be done without extensive testing, in order to ensure that usability and user trust in
the core product does not diminish.
5.2 Propagation Strategy
No specific propagation strategy has currently been selected by Evido. Based on the external
design aspects presented in the analysis and results chapter, a few different concrete propa-
gation strategies which could work individually or in conjunction. These potential methods of
distribution can be seen in figure 5.2, and will be elaborated upon further on.
A clear aspect which must be taken into account, before evaluating the potential methods of
distribution for LiverCheck, is the inherent levels of trust different distributors provide. Test-users
and interview subjects expressed significantly different levels of trust in the product, depending
on who the distributor and source was. A simplified overview of these levels of trust can be
seen in figure 5.3. When looking at feedback from users, a personal connection and direct
connection to the danish healthcare authorities resulted in higher levels of trust. The distributor
mentioned by most users as favored was a personal physician, and a general distrust existed
amongst users to unknown individuals or companies. Entities not directly connected to the
danish healthcare service were in general less trusted.

Expanding upon, and evaluating the different distribution methods presented in figure 5.2, is
necessary in order to compare estimated effectiveness of each. These distribution methods
include:
(A) Doctors send out LiverCheck as a pre-consultation tool.
This method would involve doctors sending out LiverCheck as part of a pre-consultation
notice, in which they often ask users to fill out a form or carry out a short test. During
the interview with the general practitioner, he noted that this method would work well in
the existing workflow. A clear strength of this method of distribution is the high levels of
Page 77
==PAGE:83==
trust the user would have in the test, due to the distributor. This method would also allow
users to have their blood sampled at their upcoming consultation, reducing the need for
them to see the doctor on a separate occasion. A clear weakness with this strategy is the
limited number of individuals this method would target, since it requires doctors to already
deem a patient potentially at risk. It also involves larger amounts of work for the general
practitioners, who would need to assess many patients, in order to determine who should
receive LiverCheck.
(B) LiverCheck is send out to all patients in a doctors general practice.
This method would involve general practitioners offices across the country sending out
LiverCheck to patients. This method has several key strengths, the first of which is that if
successful, this method would target all adult citizens in Denmark. A second strength is
once again the high levels of trust the user would have in the test, due to the distributor. A
key weakness with this method is that general practitioners offices may be overwhelmed
by patients all requesting blood work done at the same time. A second weakness is that
this method would require convincing each general practitioners office to be a distributor,
since these work as independent units.
(C) LiverCheck is advertised in general practitioners office or on website.
This method would involve physical advertisements being placed in offices, or digital ad-
vertisements/recommendations being placed on the general practitioners office’s website.
The interviewed general practitioner stated that similar test recommendations already ex-
ist on their website and that implementing LiverCheck into the existing system would be
relatively easy. He did not mention the possibility of physical advertisements in the waiting
room. A key strength of this method is once again the inherent trust in the distributor. A
second strength is that an advertisement in the waiting room, would allow users to test
themselves while they wait for their consultation, meaning blood work could be done in-
stantaneously if they are deemed at risk. A weakness in regards to this method, would be
once again to convince each individual office to advertise the product. A second weak-
ness is a current lack of knowledge on the willingness of general practitioners to physically
advertise in their waiting room, and regulations regarding such advertisement.
(D) LiverCheck is send out to citizens by health authorities (over e-boks or mail).
This method would involve the Danish Health Authority (Sundhedsstyrelsen) or other au-
thorities such as the danish regional governments, mainly focused on healthcare, to send
out an invitation to try out LiverCheck to citizens. The key strength of this method would be
a matter of reach, and general simplicity, as it would only involve one or a few institutions
to send out a standardised mail. This method however is dependant on convincing health
authorities to carry this out, and since Fatty Liver Disease (FLD) is not a disease which in
well known publicly, convincing authorities to carry out a national awareness campaign is
highly unlikely. Public research institutions in Denmark, such as FLASH, can currently buy
"access" to send out mails regarding tests to citizens, but the current costs of this method
makes it highly unlikely to be feasible for LiverCheck.
Page 78
==PAGE:84==
(E) LiverCheck is advertised outside of general practitioners office.
This method would involve advertising LiverCheck on digital platforms or physically in
public locations, and could utilize NGO’s or other groups to reach as many individuals as
possible. The key strenght of this method is the speed at which it can be implemented.
Buying advertisement space on social media platforms such as Facebook or Instagram
is a relatively simple process, and would reach a large number of individuals. Utilizing
NGO’s focused on liver health and public health could further strengthen the campaign,
and increase the credibility of the product, by involving trustworthy partners. The clear
weakness of this method is a matter of cost, and while LiverPro (the clinical version of the
algorithm), is monetized, no such monetization currently exist for LiverCheck. This means
that any investment into large scale advertisement campaigns would have to be finansed
by profits generated from LiverPro, and would most likely not be able to be implemented
until LiverPro is already widespreadly used. This essentially creates a loop of LiverCheck
is suppose to guide individuals towards LiverPro, but if LiverPro isn’t in widespread use,
then we can’t finance the tool designed to guide users towards LiverPro.
These different distributions method all have unique challenges attached, and further research
would be necessary in order to determine which one, or multiple ones, would be optimal. The
original design concepts utilizes distribution method B, and while it is still recommended to heav-
ily involve general practitioners in the propagation strategy, deselecting options at this stage is
unnecessary, since the product design allows for any distribution method to be viable.
5.3 Sources of Error
Throughout the development process sources of error exist. A clear source source of error is
the inherent lack of testing on IOS, and lack of testing on a large array of android smartphones
in general. One test-user of the 3rd prototype noted that the browser version of the application
performed poorly on her smartphone, as previously stated in the analysis and results chapter.
While the browser version has been tested on both PC, android and IOS by the designer with
no identified problems, testing on a larger sample of devices would be necessary before launch.
Testing of the native IOS application would also be necessary, but is expected to function equally
well to the android version.
The lack of interviews with a greater number of general practitioners, means that any opinions
expressed during the single interview conducted cannot be extrapolated to represent all general
practitioners in Denmark. Takeaways from the interview, such as workflow, procedure and pre-
consultation tests are however standard across the country. The interview conducted served
mainly as a method of gaining a deeper understanding of the patient pathway. While this project
does not directly deal with the implementation of the product and is therefore not heavily affected
by this lack of large scale interviewing with physicians, further investigation and interviews would
be necessary in order to select the appropriate propagation strategy.

5.4 Revisiting the research questions
The prioritized design aspects, identified primarily through the initial literature-review and con-
textual interviews were applied throughout the design process to shape the final prototype. As
shown in figure 4.46 some aspects were possible to address within the design of the applica-
tion itself, while others required external actions. When evaluating the success of the process,
how well the final product realize these aspects is the only current barometer usable, since the
product has not been launched. In regards to these aspects, the feedback from the quantita-
tive testing indicate that the design is highly usable, and relatively trustworthy. Scepticism still
remain however in regards to the effectiveness of the algorithm, due to the simple nature of the
test and a general technology scepticism amongst many individuals.
The context of development for this product, and specific nature of the disease it tackles makes
generalization of the results to all lifestyle disease difficult. It however means that the solution
produced is more likely a better fit for the context in which it was designed, as opposed to a more
general solution. The design has been formed in cooperation with Evido, and is therefore heavily
influence by their vision for the product, its position in the patient pathway, and connection to
LiverPro. These aspects interfere with the scientific purity of the project, but also increases the
likelihood that the resulting application is of use to Evido, and eventually being realized and
implemented.

5.5 Conclusion
Through the state-of-the-art literature review, and initial phases of the process, several design
aspects key to the success of a software-based home test for early detection of Fatty Liver Dis-
ease (FLD) were identified, which should be prioritized throughout the following development
process. The aspects were expanded upon later on in the process, and grouped based on
whether or not they could be tackled through the design of the product, or were reliant on exter-
nal factors such as the propagation strategy.
The prioritized design aspects, influencing the design of the final prototype include: 1) The appli-
cation should inform users about FLD. 2) The application should present information and results
effectively, without resulting in overdiagnosis. 3) The application should have high usability, re-
gardless of users levels of digital literacy. 4) The application should establish high levels of trust
in the product and a potential test (if it includes one). 5) The application should communicate
clearly. This aspect is especially crucial if a test is involved.
Through the contribution of the LiverCheck prototype it was investigated which external aspects
influence the success of a design in in this particular context. These aspects, which could not be
tackled through the design of the prototype include: 1) Existing Channels of distribution should
be utilized for propagation of LiverCheck. 2) A high level of trust in the product and potential
test should be established external of LiverCheck. 3) General practitioners should be educated
in regards to FLD and LiverCheck.
The final product consist of a multi-platform application which allows users to test their liver
health by inputting their age, sex, height, weight, alcohol intake, waist circumference and whether
or not they suffer from, type-2 diabetes. The application calculates, through the use of a pro-
prietary algorithm developed by Evido, the users risk of suffering from FLD. Quantitative testing
of the prototype indicate high levels of usability and satisfaction with the design amongst users,
with a SUS-score of 88, and a confidence interval of +/- 2. Scepticism still exist in regards to
the effectiveness of the algorithm, the simplicity of the test, and the distributor.
It can therefore be concluded with a reasonable degree of certainty, that applying the identified
and prioritized design aspects to a User-Centered Design approach of a product development
process for a non-clinical self test of FLD resulted in a high performing design. Further investi-
gation and testing is however necessary in order to select the optimal propagation strategy, and
determine the potential impact of implementing LiverCheck.
