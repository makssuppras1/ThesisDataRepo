Abstract
Reading effectiveness is shaped by varying environmental and ergonomic factors ┬¡ light┬¡
ing, screen glare, font size, and device type ┬¡ that alter eye┬¡tracking ease, fatigue, and
comprehension. This study quantifies how text┬¡formatting styles (line spacing, font size,
text width) interact with usersÔÇÖ eye and reading conditions, such as myopia, astigma┬¡
tism and dyslexia, by measuring participantsÔÇÖ eye┬¡movement metrics and then comparing
these results with participantsÔÇÖ preferences.
Among the 21 participants, font sizes of 12 to 16, line height 1.0┬¡1.5 and text width normal
to widescreen were tested across 3 experiments. Results indicate that the participants
prefer a minimum font size of 16 and line height of 1.5 points, with the text width pref┬¡
erence varying. 86% of participants matched their style preference with actual reading
speed. Reading conditions of the participants were not found to significantly affect the
style preference.

1
Introduction
1.1
Motivation
Reading is an important form of communication that the majority of people do daily to
get new information and understand the world around them. The ability to read can be
affected by various conditions, such as bad eyesight, poorly readable font, poor reading
skills and learning differences, such as dyslexia.
For people with reading and learning differences, the primary help so far has been to
adjust the font and text size. However, even that is not sustainable for all scenarios, such
as fatigue or external distractions.
Reading the Reader is a project that investigates adaptive text presentation based on indi┬¡
vidual reading behaviors. It aims to use machine learning techniques to analyze real┬¡time
measurements, such as eye movements, pupil dilation, and posture to identify distinct
reading patterns. By integrating approaches from data science, vision research, typo┬¡
graphic design, and software engineering, the project focuses on tailoring typographic
settings to the needs of each reader, thereby improving comprehension and accessibility,
particularly for individuals with reading difficulties.[1]
The project is managed by an associate professor and head of study at Technical Univer┬¡
sity of Denmark, Per B├ªkgaard, together with professor Sofie Beier who specializes in
Management, Strategy, and Organization (MSO) and is the head of the Centre for Visibility
in Royal Danish Academy. Collaborators include the aforementioned school researchers
and masterÔÇÖs students, and Novo Nordisk Fonden for funding. [1, 2, 3, 4]
1.2
Initial Problem Statement
Dyslexia is a common learning difference that affects reading comprehension, often due
to difficulties with text processing, eye movement control, and sustained focus. For indi┬¡
viduals with dyslexia, traditional text presentation formats amplify these challenges. While
adaptive technologies have shown promise in mitigating these difficulties, there remains
a gap in understanding how real┬¡time adjustments to text layout, font, and spacing can
enhance the reading experience.
This thesis aims to contribute to Reading the Reader project by designing, prototyping,
and evaluating multiple reading interfaces that adapt to different reading patterns using
eye tracking. The thesis aims to leverage existing research on typography and user ex┬¡
perience (UX). By exploring various interface designs and conducting usability tests, this
work will provide actionable insights to improve the ÔÇØReading the ReaderÔÇØ project and
create a foundation for adaptive text systems.[5]
1.3
Initial Research Questions
The initial research questions were based on the assumptions of the author of what is
feasible to achieve within this thesis, without detailed research about the specifics. The
questions are listed below.

2.2
Eye Tracking
Eye tracking is a technology that uses specialized cameras or sensors to monitor and
record the movement and focus of a personÔÇÖs eyes. It allows for tracking where an indi┬¡
vidualÔÇÖs gaze is directed, the patterns of visual attention, and potentially how the content
was perceived. This information can be used in a variety of applications, such as user
interface design, market research, psychology studies, and assistive technologies for in┬¡
dividuals with disabilities.[14]
For the brain, visual attention helps individuals direct mental resources to sensory inputs
so that the mind can effectively process the surrounding environment.
The book by Duchowski [14] describes four types of eye tracking methods: electro┬¡oculography
(EOG), scleral contact lens search coil, photo┬¡oculography (POG), video┬¡oculography
(VOG), and video┬¡based combined pupil and corneal reflection.
Electro┬¡oculography (EOG) tracks eye movements by measuring the voltage difference
between the cornea (positive) and the retina (negative). Small electrodes around the eyes
pick up the signal, which typically remains linear for moderate gaze angles. Although fac┬¡
tors such as head motion, muscle interference, and skin┬¡electrode contact can add noise,
careful setup and signal processing can reduce errors. EOG supports identifying sac┬¡
cades, fixations, blinks, and other movements, making it useful for different applications.
Researchers often apply classification methods (such as support vector machines or Hid┬¡
den Markov Models) to interpret EOG signals more accurately. [16]
Figure 2.2: A visualization of the electro┬¡oculography method by attaching electrodes on
the face. [17]
The scleral contact lens search coil method records eye movements by embedding wire
coils in a scleral contact lens covering both the cornea and sclera. The lens connects to the
eye using suction, and the lens moves with the eye. When placed in a magnetic field, the
coils generate a voltage due to electromagnetic induction, enabling precise tracking of eye
movements in horizontal, vertical, and rotational directions around the line of sight. This
method provides high accuracy and a wide range of motion tracking, making it ideal for
physiological and neurological research. However, it requires anesthesia, head fixation,
and specialized equipment. Extended use may lead to blurred vision, discomfort, pain,
4

or visual distortions. Despite these limitations, the scleral search coil remains one of the
most precise methods for eye movement recording [18].
Photo┬¡oculography (POG) is a method of tracking eye movements by capturing photos or
videos of corneal reflections, pupil position, or limbus tracking. It relies on images taken
at regular intervals to analyze eye movement patterns. The technique can be applied us┬¡
ing high┬¡speed cameras or specialized optical devices to record eye fixations, saccades,
and smooth pursuit movements. While it provides detailed movement analysis, it often
requires head stabilization or correction methods to separate head movements from eye
movements [18].
Video┬¡oculography (VOG) uses infrared or visible light cameras to track eye movements
by detecting the position of the pupil, corneal reflections, or both. It allows for contact┬¡
free measurement of gaze direction and eye motion, making it more comfortable than
methods that require contact lenses. VOG can operate with a free head or include head┬¡
tracking mechanisms for improved accuracy. It has been widely used in vision research,
vestibular testing, and human┬¡computer interaction due to its ease of use and ability to
provide real┬¡time tracking data [18].
Video┬¡based combined pupil and corneal reflection (PCCR) is a subset of VOG. This
method combines tracking of the pupil center and corneal reflections to determine the
point of gaze. A light source creates a reflection on the cornea while a camera captures
both the pupil and reflection. The difference in position between the pupil and the corneal
reflection is used to calculate the direction of gaze, allowing for accurate eye tracking
even with minor head movements. This approach is commonly used in commercial eye┬¡
tracking systems for applications such as cognitive research, visual attention studies, and
user interface control [18].
This thesis uses Tobii Eye Tracker 4C (IS4 Large Peripheral), a PCCR┬¡type eye tracker.
It is a portable device that can be mounted to different screens using adhesive pads.
Once mounted, it is connected to a computer via USB 2.0 and is able to track eyes and
head (including position and recognition) [19, 20]. In this thesis, only the eye┬¡tracking
capabilities are used via TobiiÔÇÖs API.

2.3
Eye Feature Extraction
When deciding which algorithms to use for data analysis, previous work by the author was
considered [22]. In that research, it is noted that I2MC and NSLR┬¡HMM were perceived
to be the best for eye movement analysis due to their amount of detail and accuracy to
the data. On the other hand, Andersson et al. [23] concluded I┬¡HMM and I┬¡VT to be the
best algorithms. It was noted that when one algorithm ranks the best, the others tend to
perform poorly, depending on the evaluation method, suggesting that different algorithms
are best for different datasets and evaluations [23, 22].
The Identification by Two┬¡Means Clustering (I2MC) algorithm is designed to detect fixa┬¡
tions in eye movement data, even when recordings contain high levels of noise or inter┬¡
ruptions. It first fills in brief gaps in the recorded eye positions, then analyzes segments
of the data to separate fixations from saccades. By comparing changes in eye position
across small time windows, it determines which parts represent stable fixations. Finally,
it combines fixations that occur very close together and removes very short events that
are unlikely to represent true fixations. This makes the algorithm effective for accurately
identifying fixations in challenging eye┬¡tracking scenarios, such as research with infants
or patients, while also working for simpler use cases like this thesis [24].
NSLR┬¡HMM is a method for classifying eye movements that combines Naive Segmented
Linear Regression (NSLR) ÔÇö a technique for simultaneous denoising and segmentation
of gaze signals ÔÇö with Hidden Markov Models (HMM)[25] for event classification. The
algorithm segments gaze data into linear (straight┬¡line) segments, fitting these directly
to the raw data without prior filtering and dynamically adjusting to measurement noise.
Each segment, defined by the gaze data itself, is then classified into specific eye move┬¡
ment types (fixations, saccades, smooth pursuits, post┬¡saccadic oscillations) based on
statistical properties such as velocity and direction. By integrating denoising into the seg┬¡
mentation step, the method enables robust classification across both controlled laboratory
conditions and real┬¡world settings with higher noise levels [26]
When analysing experiment data in paragraph 5, the author found that aforementioned
algorithms were not sufficiently suitable for this kind of data ┬¡ multiple readings of texts by
multiple people, as opposed to 10┬¡second segments or single reading of an article, which
was done in the authorÔÇÖs personal course.[22]
Therefore the author instead used the I┬¡DT algorithm, which is also mentioned in the
research paper.[27]
Dispersion┬¡Threshold Identification (I┬¡DT) is a method of discerning fixations when, within
a sliding time window, the gaze points are grouped together in a certain location. It slides
a moving window across the raw eye┬¡tracking sequence, starting with enough samples
to cover a minimum duration (typically 100ÔÇô200 ms). For each frame, it measures how
far the gaze dots spread by adding the horizontal width and vertical height. If this spread
is below a set limit, it keeps adding the next point to the frame. When the spread finally
goes over the limit, it records the frameÔÇÖs center as a fixation, throws out those points,
and keeps scanning. If the very first check is already over the limit, it just shifts the frame
forward by one point.[23, 27]
The method has two adjustable parameters ┬¡ the dispersion threshold (how wide the points
may spread, often between 0.5┬¡1 degrees of vision) and the duration threshold (how long
the first set of points must stay in the same area). I┬¡DT runs quickly enough for real┬¡
time use, and its explicit time check helps it ignore fleeting noise spikes that fool purely
speed┬¡based methods. The produced fixation maps are said to be almost identical to

the Hidden┬¡Markov (I┬¡HMM) approach, while being simpler to code. The main caution
is careful parameter tuning: because dispersion and duration thresholds are dependent
on each other, poor choices may create too large or small segments of fixations. When
thoughtfully configured, however, I┬¡DT offers a robust balance between efficiency and
accuracy.[27]

The ease of reading is determined by two key factors: legibility and readability. Legibil┬¡

Readability describes how easily entire words, sentences, and paragraphs can be com┬¡

Typography is the technique of making written language both legible and readable while

2.5
Digital Reader Design
User experience, in the context of reading texts, can take inspiration from the physical
e┬¡book readers.
Kostick [36] article about the digital reading experience elaborates on some of the aspects
that are ÔÇØwrongÔÇØ about eBook readers compared to physical books. Kostick starts by
describing that the experience of reading an e┬¡book should be equivalent to a physical
one in terms of physical and digital properties. UX experts Steve Portigal and Whitney
Quesenbery note that digital reading is inherently not equivalent to physical papers, and
hence should aim to stay distraction┬¡free while offering novel technological features [36].
Mod [37], a writer and designer, claims that the problem is purely in the software, high┬¡
lighting problems with over┬¡used real┬¡life imitation (such as book shape in the interface),
too many visible interface elements, lack of hyphenation and alignment options, rigid mar┬¡
gins [37, 36].
Research by Huang, Chen, and Ho [38] investigates how tablet reading systems differ
in user behavior and context of use compared to other digital platforms, and it seeks to
redesign their interactive interfaces to enhance usability and user acceptance [38].
Huang, Chen, and Ho [38] used the technology acceptance model (TAM) [39] ┬¡ an online
survey, followed by in┬¡person observation and interview. The data was collected and

analyzed using affinity diagrams[40], and brainstorming was conducted to evaluate the
required adjustments. Interface prototypes were created using iterative design [41].
The design phase resulted in an improved reading interface for tablets that incorporated

2.6.1
Audiobooks and text┬¡to┬¡speech
As described in paragraph B.1, the most obvious way dyslexia affects daily activities is
the difficulty in understanding written text. One way to mitigate that is to avoid having to
read altogether, or reducing the amount of text one has to read. This can be done with
text┬¡to┬¡speech tools and audiobook players.
Rachel Melegrito describes how to enable the built┬¡in text┬¡to┬¡speech option in Windows,
macOS, iOS, Android, Amazon Kindle and Microsoft Word platforms [43].
Other popular tools include Learning Ally ┬¡ audiobooks for the learning impaired [44] and
Natural Reader [45] ┬¡ a natural┬¡sounding speech┬¡to┬¡text engine [46, 47, 48].
2.6.2
Writing, grammar and spelling
For individuals with dyslexia, writing can be a challenging task due to difficulties with
spelling, grammar, and sentence structure. Assistive tools, such as spell checkers, gram┬¡

Many browsers have a spell┬¡checker built┬¡in [49]. Text editing software like Microsoft Word
and LibreOffice additionally have a grammar checker [50, 51].
Ginger [52] and Grammarly [53] have browser extensions, online and downloadable tools

2.6.3
Customized fonts and styling
As it is not always practical or possible to use text┬¡to┬¡speech tools, there are also ways to
adjust the text itself to improve its readability. Dyslexia┬¡friendly fonts improve readability by
enhancing letter distinction, while features like adjustable spacing and size further support
comprehension.
It is recommended to use sans┬¡serif fonts, no italic or underlining. Common sans┬¡serif
fonts are Arial, Verdana, Tahoma, Trebuchet, Calibri and Helvetica, notably also Comic
Sans [54, 55, 35].
More targeted fonts include OpenDyslexic [56] and Dyslexie [57]. These fonts are specifi┬¡
cally designed to look distinct and be thicker at the bottom of the character to avoid getting
inverted by the reader.
Additional suggestions include a font size minimum of 12 points, large line┬¡spacing, align┬¡
ing text left instead of justification, high color contrast with a simple background [55, 35].
2.6.4
Mind mapping
Mind mapping tools provide a visual approach to note┬¡taking, helping users break down
complex ideas into clear, interconnected concepts, while at the same time requiring less
text input than conventional text forms.
MindMeister [58] and Ayoa [59] are popular examples of online visual mind mapping
tools [47, 46, 60, 61]. Ayoa also promotes itself as ÔÇØneuro┬¡inclusive,ÔÇØ [59] suggesting
that it is designed to be accessible for individuals with neurodivergent conditions, such as
dyslexia.

2.8
Final Problem Statement
Reading is rarely performed under ideal circumstances═¥ factors such as ambient lighting,
screen glare, font size, and device type constantly vary, creating different conditions that
12
Prototyping and Evaluating Adaptive Reading Interfaces for Improved User Experience
==PAGE:19==
can either hinder or help comprehension. These environmental and ergonomic variables
shape how easily the eyes track lines of text, how quickly fatigue sets in, and ultimately
how well information is understood and retained.
The goal of this study is to investigate how different text formatting styles influence reading
performance and user preference depending on the presence or lack of reading condi┬¡
tions. By analyzing participantsÔÇÖ reading speed and accuracy across various styles, the
research measures the impact of formatting on comprehension. The study also examines
whether peopleÔÇÖs personal preferences match the eye tracking data, essentially asking if
readers can naturally tell which text format works best for them.
The findings contribute to the development of adaptive reading systems in the Reading
the Reader project.

The final research questions are based on the research done in paragraph 2.7 and the
specifics of the experiment as explained in paragraph 3. The questions are listed below.
ÔÇó Within the selected amount of formatting styles, which text formatting style was pre┬¡
ferred by the participants?
ÔÇó How impactful were the differences of text styles on the reading speed?
ÔÇó What is the correlation between user preference, reading speed and eye tracking
data?
ÔÇó Do eye/reading conditions affect the preference?

5.6.1
Research Question Answers
Within the selected amount of formatting styles, which text formatting style was
preferred by the participants?
Answer is inconclusive.
On Figure 5.15, it can be seen that the majority of experiment 1 participants preferred the
largest font size. On experiment 2, there was a split between font size 14 and 16 points,
normal and extended width. On experiment 3, only font size 16 was tested and the user
preference was split between normal and slightly wider width. The second experiment
had a reading data┬¡based difference towards increased font size and width, in the third
experiment the width preference was equal by participant choice and almost equal by
reading data.
Therefore, it is known that the participants preferred a minimum font size of 16 and line
height of 1.5 points, but the specific text width preference varies.
How impactful were the differences of text styles on the reading speed?
Generally significant.
86% of participants (18 people) had a lower mean fixation duration on the two preferred
text styles. There were 3 outliers in experiments 1 and 3 who had higher mean fixation du┬¡
ration on their preferred text styles. The difference between preferred and non┬¡preferred
style durations varied significantly person by person ┬¡ for some the difference was as high
as 58.3 ms (experiment 3 participant 3), for others it was as low as 0.3ms (experiment 3
participant 6).
It must be noted that each new experiment used shorter texts than the previous, to ensure
that it fits on screen entirely within the increased font size and adjusted style. Therefore
experiment 1 has naturally the longest total reading durations (maximum 4m 24s) and
experiment 3 the shortest (maximum 1m 51s).
What is the correlation between user preference, reading speed and eye tracking
data?
For the most part, user preference does match userÔÇÖs reading speed and eye tracking
data.
As noted in previous two questions, there was a case of a tie in preferences for exper┬¡
iment 2 and 3, with the eye tracking data uncovering a significant (2) and insignificant
(3) differences in participantsÔÇÖ reading efficiency. Reading speed was faster for preferred
style for 86% of participants.
Do eye/reading conditions affect the preference?
No, the analysis could not find differences in preference based on eye conditions, except
for minor differences in fixation rate and duration for the third experiment, which require
more research to verify.
5.7
Limitations
In the survey, participants were the most unsure about their proficiency level, especially
because some of them were born in Denmark and therefore not subjected to the same
English language tests (such as IELTS[88] or TOEFL[89]) to join the university. That
said, the university does expect a language level between B2 and C2 (average C1) for
all Danish and non┬¡Danish citizens, bachelorÔÇÖs and masterÔÇÖs programmes.[90, 91, 92, 93]
Therefore it is believed that all participants entered their level accurately.

The experiment was designed for screens with 1920x1080 resolution, also known as ÔÇØFull

6
Conclusion
This study set out to clarify how simple typographic differences influence what readers like
and how efficiently they read. Across three sequential experiments a pattern occurred:
most participants did not prefer font sizes smaller than 16 pt or line heights smaller than
1.5 lines. The best column width, though, was not the same for all readers═¥ some liked
the usual width, while others liked a bit wider text. The difference may have been affected
by eye or reading conditions.
Eye tracking confirmed that text style preference usually implies their reading perfor┬¡
mance. In 86% of cases the style a participant chose also produced shorter mean fixation
durations, with time savings that ranged from negligible (0.3 ms) to substantial (58 ms).
Only three outliers read more slowly in their chosen text format.
Eye and reading conditions such as myopia, astigmatism and dyslexia did not directly
influence text style preferences, though a slight variation in reading performance was
discovered in experiment 3. Because each successive experiment used shorter texts
to accommodate larger fonts, total reading times are not directly comparable across all
experiments═¥ nevertheless, the consistent pattern of faster fixations on preferred styles
supports the conclusion that increasing font size and line spacing can measurably improve
on┬¡screen reading for most users, while column width should remain selectable by the
user.
The analyzed data and experiment format can be used by the next researchers working
on the Reading the Reader project.
