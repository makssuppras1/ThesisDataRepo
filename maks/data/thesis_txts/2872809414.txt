Graphical Abstract
This graphical abstract summarizes the core components of the study: the integration of prior
biological knowledge into gene­gene co­expression network inference, using multiple methods
applied to real­world scRNA­seq data. The impact of prior knowledge varied across methods and
was strongly influenced by data limitations and integration strategy. Evaluation focused on struc­
tural changes, as biological and statistical validation were constrained. The study highlights the
need for careful handling of priors, method selection, and realistic expectations when incorporating
prior information into network inference.

1
Introduction
Understanding how genes interact and co­regulate one another is a major focus in
systems biology and transcriptomics. The increasing use of single­cell RNA sequencing
(scRNA­seq) has enabled researchers to study gene expression with remarkable detail.
However, this high­resolution data also presents new challenges in identifying consistent
and biologically meaningful patterns within complex and often noisy datasets.
This chapter provides the foundational context for this thesis. It begins by introducing
the principles of systems biology and the importance of using network­based approaches
for gene expression analysis. It then outlines the key challenges associated with co­
expression network inference, highlights the motivation of exploiting the integration of prior
knowledge, defines the main research questions, and presents the specific objectives of
this work. Finally, it offers an overview of the thesis structure.
1.1
Background & Motivation
Systems biology is an interdisciplinary approach in biomedical research that seeks to
understand biological systems as integrated wholes—at the level of the organism, tissue,
or cell—rather than focusing solely on individual molecular components. Traditional re­
ductionist1 methods, such as microarrays,2 protein chips,3 and single­gene knockout ex­
periments,4 have provided valuable insights into specific genes [J. M. Stuart et al. 2003].
However, these techniques often fail to capture the complexity of biological systems,
which are characterized by extensive molecular interactions and regulatory feedback.
Systems biology addresses this limitation by emphasizing the networks, interactions, and
properties that arise from these complex relationships [Tavassoly et al. 2018]. It combines
mathematics, engineering principles, computational modeling, and large­scale biological
data to develop a quantitative and predictive understanding of biological phenomena.
A key feature of systems biology is the interactome, which is a complex representa­
tion of functional interactions between molecules either within a cell or within the organism
as a whole [Vidal et al. 2011; Luck et al. 2016]. By integrating omics data, which refer to
large­scale datasets used to study molecular processes, systems biology provides a com­
prehensive view of cellular function, allowing researchers to study how genes, proteins,
and regulatory molecules work together in health and disease [Pinu et al. 2019]. This
approach has been particularly valuable in fields such as cancer research [Hernández­
Lemus et al. 2024], immunology [Chu et al. 2021], and infectious diseases [Ward et al.
2021], where predicting system­wide behavior is essential for therapeutic development.
Building on the systems­level perspective, modern research increasingly employs net­
work based approaches to analyze gene function. Among these, gene­gene co­expression
networks (GGCNs) are widely used to identify patterns of coordinated gene expression.
A GGCN is a graph where nodes represent genes and edges represent statistically sig­
nificant co­expression relationships — meaning that the genes show similar expression
patterns across a set of samples or cells. Co­expression itself refers to the correlated ac­
tivity of genes, suggesting potential functional or regulatory relationships between them.
By analyzing large­scale transcriptomic data, GGCNs help reconstruct functional gene

One critical area where systems biology has made a significant impact is the study of
gene­gene interactions. Rather than functioning independently, genes are co­regulated

Constructing meaningful co­expression networks is therefore essential for interpreting

is common in computational biology, especially when integrating expression data with
protein­level information like PPI networks. For instance, this is common in tools like the
STRING database, which takes gene input and builds protein interaction networks by rep­
resenting each gene with a single, representative protein [Szklarczyk, Gable, et al. 2021].
1.1.1
Challenges in Constructing Co­Expression Networks
Despite their potential, constructing biologically meaningful GGCNs remains a signifi­
cant challenge due to multiple factors related to data availability, network inference limi­
tations, and biological complexity. These challenges impact the reliability, interpretability,
and overall usefulness of inferred networks in functional genomics and disease research.
Key obstacles include:
1. Lack of ground truth
A major limitation in co­expression network inference is the absence of validated
ground truth networks, meaning there is no universally accepted benchmark to con­
firm whether inferred gene­gene interactions truly reflect biological reality [Smet et
al. 2010; Milano et al. 2022]. Co­ expression relationships are inferred based on sta­
tistical dependencies rather than direct physical interactions. As a result, evaluating
network accuracy requires indirect assessments, such as:
• Biological interpretability – checking whether identified gene modules corre­
spond to known biological functions.
• Sparsity – Ensuring that the network is neither too dense nor too sparse, bal­
ancing sensitivity (detecting true interactions) and specificity (avoiding false
positives). A network that is too dense may contain many false or irrelevant
connections, making it difficult to identify meaningful biological patterns. On
the other hand, a network that is too sparse may overlook important interac­
tions, limiting our understanding of the system’s full complexity and regulatory
relationships.
• Alignment with known pathways – Comparing inferred interactions with well­
established pathway databases.
Since no gold standard exists, the reliability of inferred networks depends on these
indirect validation metrics, which may introduce biases or limit interpretability.

2. Data Sparsity and Noise
Gene expression datasets typically contain thousands of genes, leading to high di­
mensionality and sparse expression profiles, which together complicate network in­
ference. This increases the risk of:
• Overfitting – Algorithms may detect random correlations that appear statisti­
cally significant but lack true biological meaning [Smet et al. 2010; Milano et al.
2022].
• False discoveries – Weak signals may be intensified by noise, leading to incor­
rect gene­gene associations [Smet et al. 2010; Milano et al. 2022].
Additionally, technical artifacts such as batch effects (systematic variations between
experiments), sequencing errors (incorrect base calling or expression quantifica­
tion5), and dropout events in single­cell RNA sequencing (scRNA­seq) data (missing
gene expression due to inefficient mRNA capture6), introduce further noise making
it difficult to distinguish true biological interactions from false patterns.
3. Complexity and Heterogeneity of Biological Networks
Biological systems are naturally dynamic and context­dependent [Smet et al. 2010;
Milano et al. 2022]. The same gene can participate in different regulatory processes
depending on:
• Cell type – Gene expression patterns vary significantly across tissues and de­
velopmental stages.
• Environmental conditions – Stress, disease states, and drug responses alter
gene interactions.
• Species differences – While gene functions are often preserved across species,
network structures can differ.
Traditional co­expression inference methods often assume static relationships, fail­
ing to capture the context­specific nature of biological networks. This can lead to
missing dynamic or condition­specific interactions and incorrectly assuming that all
detected interactions are biologically relevant across different contexts. To improve
networks, inference methods should consider cell types and conditions, rather than
assuming interactions are universally stable.
4. Limited Integration of Prior Biological Knowledge
Most network inference approaches do not integrate prior biological knowledge,
such as protein­protein interactions (PPI), pathway data7 , or cell­type annotations,
limiting their interpretability and reliability [Milano et al. 2022]. Integrating such in­
formation can enhance network robustness and provide deeper biological insights
by:
• Leveraging PPI networks – Ensuring that co­expressed genes also encode
proteins that physically interact.
• Using pathway databases – Verifying whether inferred gene modules align with
known biological pathways.
• Incorporating cell­type­specific expression data – Distinguishing universally ac­

This process is closely linked to dataset­specific annotations, including time

However, many co­expression methods overlook these additional biological layers,

• False interactions – Some inferred connections may lack biological relevance.

1.1.2
Motivation
While co­expression networks are widely used to extract insight from single­cell data,
they often struggle to produce biologically meaningful results. As outlined in the previous
section, challenges such as technical noise, sparsity, context­dependence, and the ab­
sence of ground truth limit the reliability of purely data­driven inference methods [Zitnik
et al. 2024; Milano et al. 2022]. These problems aren’t just technical—they also make it
harder to understand the biology behind the networks. For example, if we don’t include
biological context or known information, the networks might show false connections or
miss important ones.
This motivates the exploration of inference strategies that incorporate prior biological
knowledge to improve the quality of GGCNs. Integrating such information offers the po­
tential to reduce noise, filter biologically unlikely connections, and enhance robustness
and biological interpretability. It also helps uncover gene modules involved in shared
functions, which might be missed by data­driven methods [Zitnik et al. 2024; Mongardi
et al. 2024; Zhuang et al. 2022].
Incorporating prior knowledge therefore offers a more reliable framework for construct­
ing co­expression networks, particularly in the context of complex and noisy transcriptomic
data. This thesis investigates how such integration can support more accurate and mean­
ingful biological discovery [Mongardi et al. 2024; Zhuang et al. 2022].
1.2
Scientific Question & Research Goals
To address the above challenges, a wide range of computational strategies have been
proposed for gene­gene co­expression network (GGCN) inference. These include classi­
cal methods, such as those relying on mutual information or correlation measures, which
are widely used due to their simplicity and scalability [Song et al. 2012]. In addition, they
include Bayesian models that capture uncertainty and potential causal structure [Lee et
al. 2009], graph­based methods that emphasize the modular and topological properties
of gene networks [Alcalá­Corona et al. 2021], and Lasso­based approaches that pro­
mote sparsity and interpretability through regularized regression [Galindez et al. 2023].
However, their strengths and limitations, particularly in capturing biologically meaningful
networks remain unclear.
Notes on terminology:
1. In this thesis, as mentioned before, the term classical approach refers to well­
established co­expression inference methods that rely on statistical measures such

2. On the other hand, the term graph­based methods refers specifically to integra­

Understanding these computational methods is not only of academic interest but also
of significant biological and medical importance. By identifying key gene interactions, re­

experimental validation, eventually improving our understanding of complex biological
systems.
Given the limitations of current co­expression inference methods—such as lack of
ground truth, data sparsity, technical noise, and insufficient biological context—this the­
sis investigates whether integrating prior biological knowledge can improve the inter­
pretability and reliability of inferred networks. Specifically, the aim is to construct GGCNs
that incorporate biologically validated data such as PPI, in order to better capture func­
tional gene relationships in complex systems and produce more biologically meaningful
networks.
The central hypothesis is that integrating prior biological knowledge will enhance the
interpretability and reliability of inferred networks compared to methods that rely solely on
scRNA­seq expression data, which can be noisy, sparse, and lacking in biological context.
The primary research question this thesis seeks to answer is:
”Does the incorporation of prior knowledge improve gene­gene co­
expression network inference across different network inference
strategies?”
This thesis will focus on human data, aiming to explore how GGCNs can provide in­
sights into biological processes and improve our understanding of their functional organi­
zation.
In addition, this thesis also reflects on the relative strengths and limitations of the four
inference strategies — classical, Bayesian, graph­based, and Lasso approaches — of­
fering insights into their practical advantages in different biological contexts. Therefore, a
secondary research question addressed in this thesis is:
“How do different inference strategies compare in terms of their
ability to produce biologically meaningful and interpretable gene­
gene co­expression networks?”
1.2.1
Thesis Objectives
To address these research questions, the thesis will explore different strategies for
constructing GGCNs that integrate prior biological knowledge. Specifically, the project
aims to:
• Investigate the potential use of simulated scRNA­seq data and focus on real­world
datasets to evaluate whether meaningful biological insights can be recovered.
• Incorporate PPI data as a source of prior biological knowledge to enhance network
interpretability.

• Apply and assess four distinct inference strategies — a classical approach, a Bayesian
approach, a graph­based approach, and a Lasso­based approach — to study how

• Evaluate the inferred networks using a range of available analytical techniques to

1.3
Structure of the Thesis
The remainder of the thesis is organized into eight chapters, each focusing on a specific
component of the research.
In Chapter 2, a literature review is presented, covering transcriptomics, co­expression
networks, simulated scRNA­seq data, the role of prior biological knowledge, classical
strategies, Bayesian, graph­based, and Lasso for network inference.
Chapter 3 describes the datasets used in this thesis, including real­world scRNA­seq
data and prior biological knowledge in the form of PPI networks. It also briefly discusses
initial attempts using simulated data and explains why they were ultimately not included
in the final analysis.
Chapter 4 addresses the first inference strategy, categorized in this thesis as the clas­
sical approach. This includes a well­established method based on mutual information and
correlation.
Chapter 5 first introduces the Bayesian modeling approach, detailing the model struc­
ture and construction of the initial GGCN. Building on this, the chapter then describes a
graph­based integration method that refines the Bayesian­inferred network by incorpo­
rating additional biological knowledge, resulting in a biologically informed and integrated
GGCN.
Chapter 6 presents the last inference strategy, based on Lasso regularization tech­
niques, and outlines how prior biological knowledge is incorporated to enhance network
inference.
Furthermore, Chapter 7 presents the evaluation framework and results from the real­
world data analyses. Finally, Chapter 8 discusses the main findings of the thesis, re­
flecting on the strengths and limitations of the different inference approaches. It also
summarizes the overall contributions of the study and proposes directions for future re­
search.

Literature Review
The Literature Review Chapter discusses how GGCNs serve as a powerful tool to study
gene interactions and regulatory pathways. It also explores the types of biological data
used for network­based analysis, with a particular focus on transcriptomics and scRNA­
seq.
In addition, the Literature Review covers some approaches for simulating scRNA­seq
data, which help generate realistic gene expression patterns for testing computational
methods.
Furthermore, this chapter highlights the potential role of prior biological knowledge
in improving network inference, discussing how integrating external information can en­
hance the biological relevance of the networks. Finally, this review examines existing
methodologies for GGCN inference, including classical approaches, Bayesian models,
graph­based integration techniques, and Lasso­based regularization methods. Each of
these frameworks offers distinct advantages and limitations in capturing gene interac­
tions, and their effectiveness can often be enhanced by incorporating external biological
knowledge into the inference process.
2.1
Co­expression Networks & Transcriptomics
2.1.1
Gene­Gene Co­expression Networks
GGCNs are powerful tools for understanding the functional relationships between genes.
A GGCN is a model that represents the functional relationships between genes based on
similar expression patterns across multiple biological conditions. Genes that exhibit sim­
ilar expression profiles are likely to be involved in related biological processes, forming
clusters or modules [J. M. Stuart et al. 2003].
By assembling co­expression relationships into a network structure, researchers can
uncover biological modules related to core processes. Additionally, these networks pro­
vide important insights into gene function, making them essential for systems biology and
multi­omics integration.
To be more specific, GGCNs are valuable for predicting gene function, identifying core
biological processes, and revealing conserved gene modules across species. They have
been widely applied in areas such as disease research, plant biology, and evolutionary
studies, where they help uncover regulatory mechanisms and functionally related gene
groups [J. M. Stuart et al. 2003; Serin et al. 2016].
However, GGCNs present challenges in interpretation due to their high interconnec­
tivity, which can hide meaningful biological insights. GGCNs are correlational and do
not establish causal relationships, meaning they indicate gene associations but not direct
regulatory influence (cause­effect interactions). They lack directionality and they do not
indicate whether one gene activates, represses, or has no effect on another. Additionally,
their accuracy heavily depends on high­quality transcriptomic data and robust statistical
analysis, as noise or biases can lead to misleading connections [Serin et al. 2016].

scale datasets generated from high­throughput biological studies that systematically an­

researchers can construct network­based models that uncover functional connections be­
tween molecular components.

2.1.5
Challenges/Limitations of scRNA­seq
Despite the numerous advantages of scRNA­seq, there are several challenges and
limitations [Kharchenko 2021]. Some of them are the following:
• High technical noise and uncertainty: Single­cell measurements have significant

• Sparse and variable data: scRNA­seq data often contains a large number of zero­

• Computational complexity: Data from scRNA­seq are high­dimensional and re­

• Batch effects: The sensitivity of scRNA­seq measurements to technical differences
between experimental batches can introduce systematic biases, making data inte­
gration and interpretation challenging.

2.3
Prior Knowledge
In the context of systems biology and gene network inference, prior knowledge refers
to existing biological information that can guide or constrain computational models. This
includes well­established datasets such as PPIs, biological pathways, transcription factor
binding data3 , and functional annotations (e.g., Gene Ontology terms [Ashburner et al.
2000]). It may also include experimental annotations such as cell types, disease condi­
tions, and time­series information [Stock et al. 2025].
2.3.1
Types and Sources of Prior Biological Knowledge
To be more specific, various sources of prior biological information have been devel­
oped and widely adopted in gene network inference studies. These databases provide
high­quality, verified information about molecular interactions, functional roles, and cellu­

• Protein­Protein Interactions (PPIs) — Databases such as STRING provide inter­
action data with confidence scores derived from experimental, computational, and
text­mining evidence [Szklarczyk, Gable, et al. 2021].

2.3.2
Advantages of the Prior Knowledge integration
Incorporating prior biological knowledge has emerged as a powerful strategy to ad­
dress some of the fundamental challenges in gene network construction, such as data
sparsity, noise, and the lack of ground truth [Zitnik et al. 2024; Milano et al. 2022]. By
grounding statistical models in known biology, we can:
• Enhance biological interpretability: Incorporating known pathways, gene func­
tions, or regulatory relationships helps ensure that inferred networks align with es­
tablished biology, making the results easier to interpret and validate.
• Reduce false positives: By filtering out gene­gene interactions that are biologically
implausible, prior knowledge helps limit false connections, leading to more precise
and meaningful networks.
• Increase robustness and reproducibility: Guided models are less sensitive to
noise and variability in the data.
• Support discovery of functionally coherent modules: Prior knowledge enables
the identification of gene clusters that participate in shared biological processes,
improving functional annotation and interpretation of modules.
• Reduce computational burden: By narrowing down the search space to biologi­
cally plausible interactions, models can likely operate more efficiently and focus on
the most relevant relationships.
Studies have shown that integrating prior knowledge improves the accuracy and rele­
vance of inferred gene­gene co­expression networks, especially when working with noisy
or high­dimensional transcriptomic data [Mongardi et al. 2024; Zhuang et al. 2022].
2.3.3
Challenges and Considerations
While integrating prior biological knowledge into gene network inference offers numer­
ous benefits, it also introduces several challenges that must be carefully addressed to
ensure reliable results.
One major concern is the potential for bias and overfitting. When prior knowledge
is excessively relied upon, especially from outdated or incomplete databases, the result­
ing networks may promote pre­existing assumptions. Moreover, while constraining the
search space improves computational efficiency, it can unintentionally limit the explo­
ration of novel gene interactions. This may introduce confirmation bias, where models
favor known biology over uncovering unexpected or emerging patterns [Stock et al. 2025].
A second challenge is the conflicting or noisy nature of biological priors. Differ­
ent sources provide inconsistent or overlapping interaction evidence. These conflicts can
misguide the inference process or weaken biologically relevant signals. Thus, it is impor­
tant to select high­quality priors [Stock et al. 2025].

Furthermore, prior knowledge is often context­specific. Interactions that are valid
in one tissue type or biological condition may not generalize to others. Applying priors
without considering the biological context can introduce false associations [Stock et al.
2025].
Another important consideration is the quality and confidence of the prior informa­
tion. Not all priors are supported by equally strong evidence. Databases like STRING
offer confidence scores to guide integration, but some sources may lack such quantifi­
able reliability, making it difficult to weigh interactions appropriately in model construction
[Stock et al. 2025].
From a practical perspective, different algorithms handle prior knowledge in different
ways. Some treat it as hard constraints, meaning certain connections are fixed and can­
not be changed. Others use soft weights, giving more importance to known interactions
without forcing them, or include prior knowledge directly in their mathematical models. As
a result, the way prior knowledge is integrated—and how flexible it is—depends on the
specific method being used [Stock et al. 2025].
Finally, there is lack of benchmark frameworks that isolate the impact of prior knowl­
edge from the inference algorithm itself Without such benchmarks, it is difficult to deter­
mine whether improvements in network accuracy come from the quality of the priors or
the computational method itself [Peng et al. 2025].
Therefore, these considerations highlight the importance of thoughtful and context­
aware integration of prior knowledge in gene network inference [Stock et al. 2025], par­
ticularly when working with noisy and high­dimensional datasets such as scRNA­seq.

knowledge, the next step is to explore how GGCNs are actually inferred. The following
sections review some key methodological approaches.
2.4
Classical Methods
Classical methods for constructing GGCNs primarily rely on statistical measures of
association, especially correlation­based and mutual information­based techniques [Song
et al. 2012].
2.4.1
Correlation­Based Methods
Correlation­based methods, such as the Pearson and Spearman correlation coeffi­
cients, quantify the similarity between the expression profiles of gene pairs. The Pearson
correlation captures linear dependencies, while Spearman’s rank correlation assesses
monotonic relationships, making it more robust to outliers and non­normal distributions.
These methods are straightforward to compute and commonly applied due to their in­
terpretability and computational efficiency, which has led to their widespread use in co­
expression analysis. However, they are inherently limited to detecting linear or monotonic
relationships and may fail to uncover more complex associations [Song et al. 2012].
2.4.2
Mutual Information­based methods
Mutual Information (MI)­based methods provide a more general measure of statis­
tical dependency, capable of detecting both linear and non­linear relationships between
gene expression profiles. Unlike correlation, MI captures more complex associations by
quantifying how much knowledge of one gene’s expression reduces uncertainty about
another. This flexibility makes MI­based methods a powerful alternative in co­expression
analysis. However, MI estimation is more computationally intensive and often relies on

techniques like discretization or k­nearest neighbor density estimation, which can intro­
duce variability in performance, especially in studies with small sample sizes [Song et al.
2012].
2.4.3
Construction of the GGCN
The construction of a co­expression network typically begins with computing a similar­
ity or dependency measure (correlation or MI) for all gene pairs. This yields a symmetric
similarity matrix, often referred to as the adjacency matrix of the network. A thresholding
step is then applied to determine which gene pairs are considered co­expressed and thus
connected by an edge in the network. This threshold can be hard (binary network) or soft
(weighted network), though the choice significantly affects network structure and down­
stream analysis [Song et al. 2012].
Further mathematical details for these methods and the construction of the GGCN are
provided in the Appendix A.1.
2.4.4
Advantages of the Classical Methods
These methods offer several advantages. Their simplicity makes them easy to imple­
ment and interpret, which is especially valuable in large­scale genomic studies. These
methods are also computationally efficient, allowing for rapid analysis of datasets contain­
ing thousands of genes. Moreover, they are widely applicable and effective in detecting
general patterns of gene co­expression, making them a standard choice in many biologi­
cal and mathematical studies [Song et al. 2012].
2.4.5
Limitations of the Classical Methods
Despite their usefulness, classical methods come with some limitations. Correlation­
based techniques are subject to linear bias, meaning they primarily detect linear or mono­
tonic relationships and may miss more complex dependencies. They are also sensitive to
noise in the data, such as outliers or measurement errors, which can distort results. Addi­
tionally, these methods do not provide directionality, so they reveal associations without
indicating cause or regulatory influence. Furthermore, mutual information­based methods
require larger sample sizes for reliable estimation, making them less effective in studies
with limited data. Finally, the choice of threshold for defining network edges can greatly
affect the resulting network structure, introducing subjectivity and variability into the anal­
ysis [Song et al. 2012].
2.4.6
Representative Methods Based on Classical Approaches
There are several well­established methods built upon classical co­expression strate­
gies. One of the most widely used is Weighted Gene Co­expression Network Analysis
(WGCNA), which constructs weighted networks using soft­thresholded correlation mea­
sures. Its popularity comes from its ability to identify biologically relevant gene modules
and relate them to phenotypic traits, making it both easy to interpret and statistically reli­
able.[Langfelder et al. 2008].
Another notable approach is INfORM, which extends correlation and mutual information­
based inference by incorporating an ensemble of algorithms and provides the possibility
of integrating prior biological knowledge. It facilitates the detection of functionally coher­
ent modules and provides an intuitive interface for non­expert users, making it particularly
effective for practical applications in transcriptomic data analysis [Marwah et al. 2018]. In
addition, a more recent extension builds upon the INfORM framework by simplifying its
implementation and explicitly incorporating prior biological knowledge into network con­
struction [Federico et al. 2022].
Finally, recent work continues to emphasize the practical advantages of co­expression

2.5
Bayesian Strategies
Bayesian inference is a statistical framework that models uncertainty by updating the
probability of a hypothesis as new data are observed. It offers a framework for modeling
complex biological systems, particularly where uncertainty and variability are inherent,
as is common in gene expression analysis [Gao et al. 2024]. In recent years, Bayesian
approaches have gained significant attention in computational biology for their ability to
incorporate data­driven modeling within a probabilistic structure.
2.5.1
Bayesian Networks
Bayesian networks are a class of probabilistic graphical models that represent vari­
ables and their conditional dependencies using directed acyclic graphs (DAGs) (e.g., see
Figure 2.2) , where nodes correspond to genes and edges indicate potential regulatory
relationships [Gao et al. 2024]. These models are especially useful for inferring causal
structures, as they capture the directionality of influence between genes.

A key theoretical advantage of Bayesian strategies is their probabilistic foundation,
which enables explicit modeling of uncertainty. Unlike classical correlation­ or mutual
information­based methods, Bayesian networks provide a principled way to represent
conditional dependencies and potential causal relationships. This makes them particularly
suitable for exploring gene regulatory mechanisms, where directionality and conditional
structure are biologically meaningful [Gao et al. 2024, Scutari 2009].
Moreover, Bayesian approaches allow for the incorporation of prior biological knowl­
edge which can guide and constrain network inference. This is especially beneficial in
high­dimensional settings like transcriptomics, where data are often noisy and sparse.
Bayesian models also exhibit high flexibility, which allows for integration with scR­
NAseq data and other omics sources, making them valuable tools for identifying candidate
regulators, studying cell­type­specific interactions, and supporting downstream analyses
such as gene clustering or functional enrichment [Gao et al. 2024, Scutari 2009].

2.5.4
Other Bayesian Approaches
Several other Bayesian methods have also contributed meaningfully to gene network
inference. For example, Bayesian Gene Regulation Model Inference (BGRMI) is a model­
based approach designed to infer gene regulatory networks from time­course gene ex­
pression data. It combines a Bayesian framework with heuristic search strategies to effi­
ciently evaluate alternative regulatory models. BGRMI also allows the integration of prior
biological knowledge, which enhances both the biological relevance and accuracy of the
reconstructed networks [Iglesias­Martinez et al. 2016].
Another recent Bayesian approach is the ”Bayesian Optimized Networks Obtained
by assimilating Omic data” (BONOBO), which infers GGCNs for individual samples. It
assumes gene expression follows a multivariate Gaussian distribution and combines each
sample’s data with information from other samples to estimate sample­specific networks
that capture individual variability [Saha et al. 2024].
2.6
Graph­Based Methods
Graph­based methods refers, here, to approaches that integrate multiple graphs—for
instance a data­driven GGCN and a network derived from prior biological knowledge.
Instead of analyzing expression data in isolation, these methods aim to combine comple­
mentary sources of information, each represented as a graph, to construct more informa­
tive and biologically meaningful networks.
One widely used example is Similarity Network Fusion (SNF), which constructs sepa­
rate similarity networks for each data type and then combines them into a unified graph
through an iterative message­passing process. SNF has proven effective in revealing
shared patterns across diverse omics layers and improving the robustness of downstream
analyses [B. Wang, Asif M Mezlini, et al. 2014].
In addition, one recent strategy involves graph neural networks to integrate multi­omics
data. Specifically, Valous et al. propose a graph­based approach where each omics
dataset is first modeled as a separate graph, and then integrated either by fusing the
graphs into a single network or connecting them in a multi­layer structure [Valous et al.
2024]. This method allows for capturing both within­ and across­omics relationships, en­
hancing biological interpretability and predictive performance. A key advantage is the
ability to incorporate prior knowledge and complex interactions, but it also requires care­
ful graph construction and can be computationally demanding.
However, relatively few methods focus specifically on constructing or leveraging gene–
gene co­expression networks. Most existing approaches operate on sample­level or het­
erogeneous graphs, where nodes represent patients, omics layers, or other modalities
rather than genes. As a result, they often overlook detailed interactions at the gene level.
This suggests a clear opportunity for further research and the development of new meth­
ods that explicitly model and integrate GGCNs within multi­omics frameworks.
2.7
Lasso Approaches
Another strategy for gene network inference is based on regularized regression tech­
niques, particularly the Least Absolute Shrinkage and Selection Operator (Lasso). Lasso
is especially well­suited for high­dimensional gene expression data, where the number of
genes (features) far exceeds the number of samples. By introducing an ℓ1­norm penalty,
Lasso encourages sparsity in the model, effectively selecting only a subset of relevant
predictors while shrinking the rest towards zero [Zhuang et al. 2022].
This sparsity­inducing property makes Lasso particularly interesting for constructing
GGCNs. In practice, Lasso identifies regulatory relationships by modeling the expres­

2.7.3
Limitations of Lasso­Based Methods
Despite its advantages, Lasso­based methods also face several limitations [Zhuang
et al. 2022]. One major challenge is the method’s sensitivity to correlated features; in
cases where genes are highly correlated, Lasso may arbitrarily select one gene from the
group, potentially overlooking key regulators. Additionally, the performance of Lasso is
highly dependent on the regularization parameter (λ), which needs careful tuning and can
vary across datasets.
Another limitation is that Lasso assumes linear relationships between genes, which
may not capture more complex regulatory interactions that exist in biological systems.
Finally, Lasso methods may struggle when applied to noisy or sparse scRNA­seq data,
where dropout events and technical noise can degrade performance unless combined
with robust preprocessing or modified frameworks that account for these challenges.

logical structures, or integrate prior knowledge.

et al., where Lasso was used to select principal components from co­expression modules
predictive of salt stress response in rice. This approach enabled the identification of key
genes and modules associated with salt tolerance [Du et al. 2018].

In addition to gene expression data, prior knowledge data were incorporated to en­

3.1
Simulated Data
Several simulation strategies were explored to generate benchmark scRNA­seq data
for evaluating network inference methods, including the SymSim package [X. Zhang et
al. 2019], a sparse hierarchical Bayesian factor model [Sekula 2020], and a zero­inflated
negative binomial model via the NORTA algorithm [Cario et al. 1997]. However, each ap­
proach presented limitations: SymSim focused more on cell­to­cell variability than gene­
gene interactions; the Bayesian model risked introducing bias due to overlap with methods
under evaluation; and the NORTA­based approach, while more general, involved techni­
cal constraints. As a result, none of the simulated datasets were used in the final analysis.
Full details are provided in Appendix B.1.
Limitations of Simulated Data
Although simulated data can be useful for benchmarking purposes, after careful evalu­
ation, they were not used for the main analysis in this thesis. Several important limitations
were identified.
First, it is difficult to meaningfully incorporate real biological prior knowledge, such as
protein­protein interaction (PPI) networks or pathway data, into simulated datasets. Since
simulated data do not reflect real biological systems, using real­world priors would not be
appropriate. Even if a simulated prior were generated to match the synthetic data, as
proposed in Lingjærde et al. [Lingjærde et al. 2021], the results would still not correspond
to any known biology.
Moreover, even if prior biological knowledge could be smoothly integrated into simu­
lated datasets, the artificial nature of the data would limit the biological interpretability of
any inferred networks. For this reason, and to ensure the biological relevance of the final
results, only real­world scRNA­seq data were used for network inference in this thesis.

First Preprocessing Step
To ensure a fair and consistent evaluation across all network inference strategies, the

Specifically, the Bayesian inference method used in this thesis is based on the sparse
hierarchical Bayesian factor model (HBFM) introduced by Sekula et al.[Sekula 2020]. This
model is designed to work directly with raw scRNA­seq count data, as it incorporates
overdispersion and zero inflation through a latent factor structure. Therefore, no normal­
ization or log­transformation was applied to the input expression matrix.
The input matrix for HBFM must be a non­normalized gene­by­cell matrix of integer
counts, with no missing values. The structure of the data should reflect true biological
heterogeneity to allow the model’s latent factors to capture meaningful gene­gene asso­
ciations.

3.4
Second Preprocessing Step
The classical approach (INfORM [Marwah et al. 2018]) and the Lasso based method
[Lingjærde et al. 2021] used in this thesis, are designed to operate on bulk RNA­seq data1
rather than single­cell data. To ensure that all methods operate on the same underlying
data, the previously preprocessed single­cell datasets were converted into pseudobulk
expression matrices. This approach aggregates single­cell expression profiles into cell­
type­level summaries, generating bulk­like data suitable for methods that assume bulk
measurements while preserving meaningful biological variation.

3.6.1
Selection of Prior Knowledge Type
As explained in Subsection 2.3.1, various types of prior biological knowledge can be
incorporated into gene network inference. These include pathway databases (e.g., KEGG
[Kanehisa et al. 2021], Reactome[Gillespie et al. 2022]), gene ontologies (e.g., GO [Ash­
burner et al. 2000]), transcription factor binding data, disease and drug associations (e.g.,
Open Targets, DrugBank [Wishart et al. 2008; Ochoa et al. 2021]), and PPI networks (e.g.,
STRING [Szklarczyk, Gable, et al. 2021]). Such prior knowledge can also take the form of
dataset­specific annotations, including time points, cell types, or differentially expressed
genes.
In this thesis, PPI data from the STRING database [Szklarczyk, Gable, et al. 2021]
was selected as the source of prior knowledge due to its broad biological coverage, con­
fidence scoring system, and widespread use in network biology. The STRING database
[Szklarczyk, Gable, et al. 2021] is a widely used resource that collects evidence from
experimental studies and established databases.
PPI data represent experimentally validated or computationally predicted physical or
functional associations between proteins. These interactions often reflect underlying bio­
logical processes and regulatory mechanisms. STRING provides a weighted interaction
network, where each edge between proteins is assigned a confidence score based on the
strength and reliability of supporting evidence. The scores range from low to high (0 to
1000), with higher scores indicating more reliable interactions.
Using the combined score provides a robust and simplified way to evaluate the relia­
bility of protein­protein interactions. It allows researchers to leverage the full spectrum of
evidence available in STRING without relying on a single data source. In this study, it also
served as a practical basis for filtering high­confidence interactions for further analysis.
To be more specific, the combined_score is specifically designed to integrate multiple
types of supporting evidence into a single, unified metric [Szklarczyk, Gable, et al. 2021]
. These include:
• Co­expression — Genes or proteins that show correlated expression patterns across
multiple datasets.
• Experimental data — Interactions validated by laboratory experiments.
• Curated database annotations ­ Information from databases documenting known
interactions.
• Text mining — Associations extracted from scientific literature by automated algo­
rithms that scan published articles.

• Gene fusion, neighborhood, and co­occurrence — Genomic context clues, such as
genes being fused, located near each other, or consistently co­existing across dif­

To conclude, while other forms of prior knowledge—such as pathway annotations, tran­
scription factor binding data, or cell­type–specific information—could also be valuable for
network integration, only PPI data were used in this thesis due to time constraints and the
need for a focused, manageable workflow. Importantly, the STRING PPI dataset provided
a clear and standardized structure, making it straightforward to preprocess and integrate
into downstream analyses. Its tabular format, accompanied by well­defined scoring met­
rics, allowed for efficient filtering and incorporation into the network inference framework.
As a potential direction for future work, incorporating different/additional types of prior
knowledge could offer complementary biological context and enhance the interpretability
of inferred networks. Comparative evaluations of different prior knowledge types may also
help determine which sources are most informative for specific biological questions.

the eye dataset, candidate marker genes such as CRX, RHO, and PAX6 are known to

However, due to a practical oversight during the initial data acquisition phase — specif­

maintain consistency across all three datasets and ensure methodological alignment, the
strategy was adjusted accordingly. A general pipeline was implemented, in which a subset
of the STRING network was created for each dataset by intersecting the full PPI network
with the gene expression matrix, as mentioned previously.

Using dataset­dependent PPI subsets offers several practical advantages. By retain­
ing only those interactions involving genes that are actually expressed in the dataset, the
approach helps reduce noise and improves the biological relevance of the network. This
filtering reduces computational complexity, as smaller and more focused networks are

helps maintain the overall topology of the PPI network, such as key hubs and connectivity

Nonetheless, using a dataset­independent PPI network can introduce several impor­

network may not be expressed or even present in a given scRNA­seq dataset. As a re­

or disconnected subgraphs and reducing the overall utility of the prior knowledge. This
also undermines the biological relevance of the analysis, as including interactions for non­
expressed genes may introduce noise rather than meaningful signal. Additionally, many
network inference algorithms (such as the Lasso­based approach that I am implement­
ing) assume that genes in the expression matrix directly correspond to nodes in the prior
network; mismatches can break these assumptions, causing invalid matrix operations or
incompatible model structures.
Therefore, given the numerous drawbacks associated with using a dataset­independent
PPI network, the choice to use dataset­specific PPI subsets emerges as both practical and
methodologically sound. Tailoring the PPI network to each dataset by filtering only those
interactions between genes that are present in the expression data ensures compatibil­
ity, improves computational efficiency, and maintains biological relevance. This strategy
guarantees that the prior knowledge is directly applicable to the actual data under study
and avoids potential issues from forced integration of incompatible features.

4.1

The INfORM (Inference of Network Response Modules) framework is an R­Shiny ap­

4.1.1
Reasoning for Choosing INfORM
INfORM was selected as the classical network inference method in this thesis due to
its comprehensive, robust, and biologically oriented design. INfORM integrates multiple
inference algorithms through an ensemble approach, combining correlation and mutual
information­based methods to generate a consensus GGCN. This strategy helps mini­
mize algorithm­specific bias and enhances the stability of the inferred network structure
[Marwah et al. 2018].
Although this thesis does not take advantage of INfORM’s module detection and op­
timization features, it is worth noting that the framework includes well­established com­
munity detection algorithms (e.g., Walktrap [Pons et al. 2005], Louvain [Blondel et al.
2008]) and supports biologically meaningful interpretation through tools like GO­based
heatmaps and radar plots. These components contribute to INfORM’s broader utility in
modular network analysis [Marwah et al. 2018].
Finally, INfORM has demonstrated strong practical performance in real­world case
studies, successfully identifying known disease markers and novel candidates in disease
contexts such as psoriasis [Marwah et al. 2018]. This evidence underscores its utility and
reliability for hypothesis­driven, biologically relevant gene network analysis.

4.3.1
Integration Strategy and Pipeline Modifications
The preprocessed PPI data, containing high­confidence interactions, were loaded as

Integration
To incorporate this prior into the INfORM workflow, the following was applied to the
standard script­based inference process:
The binary prior matrix APPI was incorporated during the consensus network con­
struction step, specifically as part of the ensemble of edge weight matrices used by
the get_ranked_consensus_matrix() function. It is treated as one additional network
alongside those inferred from data­driven combinations of algorithms, estimators, and
discretization methods.
The prior contributes equally during rank aggregation, with its edge scores ranked in
the same way as those from other configurations. This setup allows the prior to influ­
ence the final consensus network by promoting biologically supported edges, while still
preserving the contributions from data­driven results.
The rest of the INfORM pipeline remained unchanged, including the use of all method
combinations and the median as the default aggregation function at both Level I and Level
II.
4.3.2
Rationale and Impact of Incorporating Prior Knowledge
This integration strategy was selected because it is practical, straightforward to im­
plement, and fully compatible with the existing INfORM framework. It avoids the need
to modify core inference algorithms and allows smooth incorporation of prior knowledge
without disrupting the standard workflow.
This approach offers several advantages. First, the prior knowledge can be added or
removed without altering the core inference process. Also, it is flexible, as it supports
any type of binary prior—such as PPI or pathway­based links—provided it aligns with the
genes in the expression dataset.
Finally, although the prior network is integrated equally, its influence may be decreased
due to the large number of data­driven configurations involved (e.g., dozens of combina­
tions across algorithms and parameters). However, even with limited relative weight, the
inclusion of prior edges allows biologically validated interactions to strengthen their pres­
ence in the consensus network—especially when they align with patterns observed in the
expression data. This strengthening potentially improves interpretability and biological
relevance while maintaining the influence of data­driven results.

5.1
Hierarchical Bayesian Factor Model
Second model to infer GGCNs from scRNA­seq data, is the Sparse Hierarchical
Bayesian Factor Model (HBFM) developed by Sekula et al.[Sekula 2020]. This method
is specifically designed to handle key characteristics of single­cell data such as overdis­
persion and zero inflation.
Specifically, overdispersion refers to a situation where the observed variance in a
dataset is greater than what is expected based on a specific statistical model. In HBFM,
this is addressed by marginalizing over log­normal latent factors, which induces overdis­
persion in the marginal distribution of the observed counts despite the Poisson likelihood.
Furthermore, zero inflation arises due to a large number of zero counts across genes and
cells, and is treated by the model structure. The resulting sparsity of the data is acknowl­
edged in the paper as a major motivation for developing a sparse factor model.
The model is implemented in R, with the source code publicly available at github/mnseku­
la/hbfm. It performs inference using Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) sampling and
includes a stochastic Expectation­Maximization (EM) initialization procedure to improve
convergence. As explained in the paper [Sekula 2020], this initialization helps the MCMC
chains to start from a region of higher posterior probability and thus avoid poor local op­
tima, enabling better mixing and convergence, meaning the chains can more effectively
explore the parameter space and produce samples that are representative of the under­
lying distribution.
Given these characteristics, this model was chosen because it effectively handles
some of the challenges of scRNA­seq data while providing clear and interpretable re­
sults. Its sparse factor structure helps focus on the most important gene relationships,
reducing noise and complexity. In addition, the Bayesian framework allows for a natural
way to account for uncertainty in the data. These features, along with its strong perfor­
mance in previous studies, made it a potentially good choice for inferring reliable GGCNs
in this thesis.
5.1.1
Comparison to Standard Factor Models
Traditional factor models, as described in Bartholomew et al. [Bartholomew et al. 2011],
are used to explain the correlations among observed continuous variables in terms of a
smaller number of unobserved latent variables (factors). These models assume that the
observed data follow a multivariate normal distribution and can be expressed as:

• x is a vector of observed variables,
• µ is the mean vector,
• L is the factor loading matrix,
• f is the vector of latent factors (typically assumed standard normal),
• ϵ is a vector of normally distributed residual errors.
This formulation assumes linear relationships, continuous data, and often dense factor
loadings.
In contrast, the HBFM developed by Sekula et al. [Sekula 2020] is designed for dis­

served gene expression counts and models latent biological variation using log­normally
distributed latent factors.
Importantly, the model introduces sparsity through discrete
gene­factor associations (using discrete­valued variables with three possible states αgf ∈
{−1, 0, 1}), making it more interpretable and suitable for high­dimensional, sparse data.
This structure allows HBFM to handle overdispersion and zero inflation, which standard
factor models are not equipped to manage.
Although HBFM differs from traditional factor models, it remains conceptually rooted in
the same framework. Like classical factor models, it explains observed variability through
a lower­dimensional set of latent variables, making it an extension of the factor modeling
approach, which better suits the characteristics of single­cell gene expression data.

5.2.2
Latent Factors
Each factor represents a shared signal across a subset of genes and cells. The use
of αgf ∈{−1, 0, 1} allows the model to capture both positive and negative co­expression
patterns:
• αgf = 1: gene g is positively associated with factor f,
• αgf = −1: gene g is negatively associated with factor f,
• αgf = 0: gene g is not influenced by factor f.
This discrete structure enforces sparsity and interpretability in the model by explicitly
specifying the direction and presence of gene­factor associations.
Latent factors f are hidden variables that are not directly observed but influence gene
expression levels. In the context of scRNA­seq data, these latent factors can capture a
variety of signals, including:
• Biological processes (e.g., cell cycle, immune activation, metabolic states)
• Cell types or states (e.g., stem cells, differentiated cells)
• Technical variation (e.g., batch effects, dropout noise)
How Latent Factors Operate in HBFM
In the HBFM framework, each gene’s expression level is influenced by a combination
of latent factors. For instance, consider a scenario with three latent factors:
• Factor 1 may represent cell cycle variation,
• Factor 2 may capture immune­related activation,
• Factor 3 may reflect technical variation.
Each gene is associated with a subset of these factors via the discrete loading variable
αgf. For example, a gene might be positively influenced by Factors 1 and 2 but not by
Factor 3. Another gene may be linked only to Factor 2. This structure allows the model to
group genes with similar regulatory patterns and identify modules of co­regulated genes
across cells.
Rather than modeling each gene independently, HBFM captures the shared structure
in gene expression by learning these latent factors directly from the data through Bayesian
inference. It estimates which genes are influenced by which factors and the strength (via
ϕf) and direction (via αgf) of each gene–factor relationship.
After the latent factors are learned, a gene co­expression network is constructed based
on factor sharing. Genes that share similar latent factor profiles are considered co­expressed,
as they respond similarly to the same underlying biological or technical signals.

5.2.4
EM Initialization and MCMC Sampling
Stochastic EM Initialization
To improve convergence behavior and provide a better starting point for posterior infer­
ence, the HBFM model employs a stochastic EM initialization procedure. This initialization
helps the model parameters begin in regions of relatively high posterior probability before
full MCMC sampling begins, improving practical mixing and reducing burn­in time.
The EM initialization iteratively alternates between the following steps:
• E­step: Sample the latent factors λif from their conditional posterior distribution,
given the current values of the other model parameters. This stochastic step ap­
proximates the expectation step in classical EM by drawing samples rather than
computing expectations analytically.
• M­step: With the sampled λif values fixed, update gene­specific parameters such
as αgf and βg by maximizing their conditional posterior distributions. Some of these
updates, particularly for βg, can be computed in closed form because the Gamma
prior and Poisson likelihood are conjugate, resulting in a posterior distribution that
can be derived analytically.
Although this initialization does not guarantee avoidance of poor local optima, it often
improves the initial position of the MCMC chains in practice.
The authors recommend running the EM initialization for a several hundred to a few
thousand iterations prior to launching the full posterior inference.
MCMC Sampling
Following the stochastic EM initialization, model parameters are inferred using a MCMC
sampling procedure.
The MCMC procedure iteratively samples from the full conditional posterior distribu­
tions of all model parameters. Specifically, it updates:

5.2.5
Model Selection: Choosing the Number of Latent Factors
An important hyperparameter in HBFM is the number of latent factors F, which con­
trols the model’s capacity to explain gene expression variability. To select an appropriate
value for F, the Deviance Information Criterion (DIC) is used. DIC is a model selection
criterion commonly used in Bayesian inference. It balances model fit and complexity by
penalizing models with a higher number of effective parameters. Formally, it is defined
as:
DIC = ¯D + pD.
¯D represents how well the model fits the data on average, based on the posterior samples.
The term pD is a penalty for model complexity and it reflects how many parameters the
model is effectively using. Models with lower DIC values are preferred, as they achieve
better trade­offs between goodness of fit and model simplicity [Berg et al. 2004].
5.2.6
Model Properties and Practical Considerations
Having described the inference procedure and model selection, some statistical prop­
erties of the model and practical considerations, that affect its application to real scRNA­
seq datasets, are explained.
After marginalizing over the latent factors λif, the observed counts Ygi exhibit overdis­
persion, meaning their variance exceeds the mean. This occurs despite being modeled
conditionally as Poisson, because the Poisson rate µgi depends on the log­normally dis­
tributed latent variables λif ∼Lognormal(0, ϕf). This behavior is described in the original
HBFM paper by Sekula et al. [Sekula 2020], where the authors state that the model re­
sults in a Poisson­lognormal formulation. Here, “marginalizing” refers to integrating out
the latent variables λif, which are not observed but influence the Poisson rate µgi. This
integration introduces extra variability, yielding a marginal distribution for Ygi with heavier
tails and greater variance than a standard Poisson. As a result, the model captures the
high dispersion and technical noise often present in scRNA­seq data more effectively than
models relying solely on the Poisson assumption.
Additionally, while the model is scalable to datasets with a moderate number of genes
(G) and cells (I), practical applications are typically limited to around 100 genes and 3000
cells. The main computational bottleneck emerges from the estimation of the full gene–
gene correlation matrix, which has a quadratic complexity of O(G2). Furthermore, the
use of MCMC sampling for parameter inference contributes significantly to runtime due
to its iterative nature and the need to generate many samples to accurately estimate the
uncertainty in the model parameters. Although a stochastic EM initialization is used to

5.3
Attempts of PPI Integration in HBFM
The primary objective was to incorporate prior biological knowledge into the HBFM
method in a way that preserved the Bayesian structure of the model, without changing its
underlying assumptions or formal inference process. However, due to mistakes during
the implementation and significant time loss, the integration attempt was not successfully
completed. Consequently, this direction was abandoned. Instead, the networks inferred
by HBFM without prior knowledge were used in a graph­based integration approach, as
described in Section 5.4.
Some initial attempts involved strategies that unintentionally changed the Bayesian
nature of the method. These approaches resembled graph­based methods rather than
Bayesian inference, and therefore they are not considered appropriate for prior integrated
Bayesian analysis.
Another attempt involved modifying the initial factor loading α matrix after the stochas­
tic EM initialization by integrating the PPI prior knowledge. Gene pairs with known inter­
actions were probabilistically encouraged to share common latent factors, aiming to start
the model from a biologically informed configuration.
However, this adjustment did not contribute to generating a more biologically meaning­
ful network. Since the standard EM step already provides stable initialization, modifying α
post­EM may only (in the best case senario) accelerate convergence rather than improve
inference. Importantly, because the HBFM model is fully Bayesian and infers parame­
ters based on the observed data, altering initial values does not affect the final results—
provided that the data remain unchanged and the MCMC convergence is consistent. In
this case, the changes had no impact on the outcome, though in theory they could lead to
a different optimum and thus alter the results. Additionally, such modifications risk degrad­
ing the initialization compared to the EM­derived starting point. Therefore, this approach
was not kept in the final pipeline.

informative network.

5.4.1
Rationale for Selecting SNF
SNF was selected because it is specifically designed to integrate multiple heteroge­
neous data types by operating at the network level rather than the raw data level. This
makes it particularly robust to noise, differences in data scale, and data heterogeneity—
common challenges in biological datasets [B. Wang, Aziz M Mezlini, et al. 2014]. SNF
captures both shared patterns and complementary signals across data sources by build­
ing similarity graphs ­where similarity reflects how alike two entities are based on the
information captured in each dataset­ for each network and gradually combining them
through an iterative process that lets the networks influence each other [B. Wang, Aziz M
Mezlini, et al. 2014].
A key strength of SNF is its ability to preserve local network structures during the fusion
process. This is achieved by retaining only the most relevant local connections through K­
nearest neighbor sparsification and by controlling the information exchange during fusion
with localized transition matrices. These mechanisms ensure that subtle but meaningful
relationships unique to each network are maintained, while shared patterns are gradually
aligned. The result is a fused network that is not only more informative but also more
interpretable [B. Wang, Aziz M Mezlini, et al. 2014].
In the context of this thesis, where direct inclusion of prior biological knowledge into
the Bayesian model proved challenging, SNF offers a powerful alternative for integrating
prior knowledge with data­driven inferences at the graph level. SNF was selected also
because, even though it was introduced over a decade ago, it remains widely regarded
as one of the most effective and frequently recommended methods for network­level data
integration in computational biology, as emphasized in recent studies [W. Jiang et al.
2025]. It has also been extended to emerging settings such as multi­modal and missing­
data scenarios (e.g., miss­SNF [Gliozzo et al. 2025]).

6
Lasso Approach
In this chapter, the final GGCN inference method applied in this thesis is presented,
which is a Lasso­based approach. The rationale for selecting this approach is first ex­
plained. The chapter then describes the implementation methodology, including both the
standard graphical Lasso (GLasso) (without prior integration) and the tailored graphi­
cal Lasso (TGL), which incorporates prior biological knowledge into the inference process
[Lingjærde et al. 2021].
6.1
Rationale for Method Selection
The study by Lingjærde et al. [Lingjærde et al. 2021] introduces a methodological
framework for reconstructing GGCNs by estimating gene interaction structures from ge­
nomic data, under the assumption of multivariate normality. The framework centers on
Lasso­based approaches, namely:
• Graphical Lasso (GLasso): a standard method that estimates sparse precision
matrices by applying an L1 penalty to encourage sparsity. It is used to infer network
structure purely from data, without incorporating external information.
• Weighted Graphical Lasso (WGL): an extension of GLasso that incorporates prior
information through a weight matrix, assigning different penalties to different edges
based on external evidence. However, this method does not adjust for the potential
unreliability of the prior data.
• Tailored Graphical Lasso (TGL): a further extension that applies a non­linear trans­
formation to the prior weights, allowing the method to adaptively control the influ­
ence of prior information depending on its usefulness. It generalizes both GLasso
and WGL, and is designed to be robust when the prior information is uncertain or
noisy.
In this thesis, only GLasso and TGL are applied. The WGL is not included due to
its limitations: it does not account for the accuracy of prior information and may enforce
misleading connections if the prior is unreliable. In contrast, TGL provides a more flexible
and reliable approach to integrating prior knowledge [Lingjærde et al. 2021], while GLasso
serves as a baseline method for comparison.
Finally, this framework was selected because it directly addresses two key challenges
in GGCN inference: the high dimensionality of gene expression data and the need to
incorporate prior knowledge without over­relying on its accuracy. As demonstrated in the
original study, TGL achieves an effective balance by adaptively integrating moderately
informative priors, making it well­suited for the current analysis.
6.2

in the GitHub repository: tailoredGlassoAnalysis.

7.1
Data Quality and Design Trade­offs
Although great care was taken in preprocessing the three scRNA­seq datasets used in
this thesis, several limitations occured that likely impacted the quality and interpretability of
the resulting GGCNs. These limitations come from certain decisions made during prepro­
cessing, including stratified downsampling, pseudobulk aggregation, and prior knowledge
integration.
7.1.1
Methodological Constraints Introduced by Bayesian Inference
The most significant preprocessing constraints in this thesis were due to the decision to
include the Bayesian network inference method (HBFM) proposed by Sekula et al. [Sekula
2020]. Although this model offers appealing theoretical advantages—such as the ability
to handle zero­inflation and overdispersion inherent in scRNA­seq data—it imposed sig­
nificant limitations on data dimensionality.
To ensure computational feasibility, the authors recommend using fewer than 100
genes. This recommendation was followed in this thesis, resulting in each dataset being
reduced to only 100 highly variable genes. Consequently, a vast majority of biologically
informative features were excluded. For instance, Dataset 1 originally contained nearly
30,000 genes, but only 100 were retained for inference. In addition, the number of us­
able cells was also restricted due to the computational cost of MCMC sampling, leading
to downsampling to 2,000–3,000 cells per dataset.
While this dimensionality reduction was necessary to apply the HBFM model, it comes
at a substantial cost. In the context of real­world scRNA­seq analysis, where biological
heterogeneity and rare cell populations are encoded across thousands of genes, such
aggressive filtering inevitably results in the loss of valuable biological signal. Critical path­
ways, regulatory modules, or tissue­specific expression patterns may be entirely missed
if their genes are excluded during preselection.
Although the model performs well in small, carefully chosen settings and simulated
benchmarks, its practical applicability to large­scale or exploratory scRNA­seq studies is
questionable. Real biological systems are highly complex and often require the integra­
tion of thousands of genes to accurately infer gene­gene regulatory relationships. The
imposed gene limit fundamentally restricts the interpretability and generalizability of the
inferred networks, especially when compared to more scalable approaches like Lasso or
graph­based methods.
Furthermore, the inclusion of this specific Bayesian method also constrained the de­
sign of the overall preprocessing pipeline, affecting all subsequent methods and limiting
the total number of observations and genes. The downstream effects of these constraints
on the other preprocessing decisions are discussed in the following subsections.

7.1.2
Stratified Downsampling and Signal Reduction
To ensure balanced representation across cell types, stratified downsampling was per­
formed in all datasets. However, this step may have accedentally suppressed important
biological signals. As highlighted in recent discussions on best practices in scRNA­seq
analysis [Maan et al. 2024], aggressive downsampling—especially when cell types are
unevenly distributed—can substantially reduce the power to detect co­expression rela­
tionships, particularly in rare subpopulations.
This trade­off poses a major limitation: although downsampling reduced cell­type im­
balance, it likely destroyed some of the inherent structure in the data, reducing delicate
co­regulation patterns.
7.1.3
Pseudobulk Aggregation Bias
Pseudobulking was performed by summing raw expression counts within annotated
cell groups. While this approach is used for compatibility with bulk­based methods, it
introduces a strong dependency on group size. Unlike mean or median aggregation,
summing expression counts scales the resulting values directly with the number of cells
per group. This raises the concern that some of the observed signal may be driven by
group abundance rather than expression levels themselves, making comparisons across
groups less meaningful—especially when some clusters contain many more cells than
others.
7.1.4
Size and Coverage of the Prior Network
The final prior networks used for integration into the GGCN inference process were
derived from filtered STRING PPI data and intersected with the set of 100 highly vari­
able genes selected per dataset. As shown in Table 3.4, the resulting networks were
extremely small: only 31, 17, and 10 unique genes were retained for Datasets 1, 2, and
3, respectively. Correspondingly, the number of edges available for use as priors was
also limited—ranging from 108 in Dataset 1 to just 12 in Dataset 3.
This sparse overlap between prior knowledge and the gene expression space signif­
icantly constrained the potential impact of the priors in downstream inference. In partic­
ular, when only 10–30% of the genes in the model are represented in the prior network,
any regularization or weighting based on prior knowledge is confined to a small subset
of genes, potentially reinforcing known interactions among well­studied genes (introduc­
ing a bias toward established biology) while leaving the broader network structure largely
unconstrained.
Given this limited gene coverage, the actual contribution of these priors to the inferred
GGCNs is likely minimal—especially in methods where prior edges must compete with
strong data­driven associations, such as in TGL or ensemble filtering. In such settings,
priors may serve more as weak confirmations of known biology than as meaningful con­
straints guiding novel structure learning.
7.1.5
Considerations for Methodological Evaluation
Therefore, the quality of the input gene expression data and the limitations of the prior
biological knowledge raise concerns regarding the interpretation of the results. While
these constraints do not invalidate the findings, they introduce potential biases and reduce
the resolution at which gene–gene relationships can be reliably detected.
For this reason, it is important to interpret the resulting networks with these factors in
mind, particularly when comparing the performance of different inference methods.

7.6
Challenges and Limitations in Evaluation
In typical studies of GGCNs, a range of quantitative and biological evaluation strate­
gies are used to assess the quality and biological credibility of the inferred networks.
These include statistical validation, network topology analysis, community detection, and
functional enrichment. However, due to the methodological and data­driven constraints
discussed throughout this thesis, many of these standard evaluation approaches were
either infeasible or inappropriate in this study. This section outlines common evaluation
strategies and explains the specific reasons they could not be meaningfully applied here.
1. Topological Network Property Analysis Studies often compute properties such as
degree distribution, betweenness centrality, clustering coefficients, and assortativity to
assess structural features of the inferred networks [Alcalá­Corona et al. 2021]. However,
these metrics are only meaningful in sufficiently large and connected networks. In this
study, many of the inferred networks—especially those from the Lasso­based method—
contained numerous disconnected components or isolated nodes. As discussed in recent
literature, topological measures in disconnected or sparse graphs can produce misleading
results and are therefore not reliable in this context [Alcalá­Corona et al. 2021].
2. Community Detection and Modularity Analysis Algorithms such as Louvain [Blon­
del et al. 2008] or Leiden [Traag et al. 2019] are commonly used to detect modular struc­
tures within networks. However, these methods rely on graph connectivity and assume
that the network forms a single, or mostly connected, component. Given that many of
the networks inferred here were fragmented, applying community detection would result
in trivial (every node ends up in its own group, or all nodes are lumped into one) or un­
interpretable groupings (there’s no clear biological or structural meaning — the grouping
seems arbitrary or noisy). Thus, modularity­based evaluation was considered inappropri­
ate.
3. Gene Set Enrichment Analysis (GSEA) GSEA is a powerful technique for evaluating
biological relevance by testing whether gene modules are enriched for known functional
Inclusion of prior knowledge into gene­gene co­expression networks
71
==PAGE:82==
pathways [Shi et al. 2007]. However, GSEA requires a ranked list of genes—typically
based on correlation strength, differential expression, or some continuous metric. In this
thesis, the networks were derived from binary adjacency matrices, without continuous
edge weights or gene rankings. Therefore, standard GSEA procedures could not be ap­
plied.
4. Functional Enrichment of Modules or Hubs Another common approach is to identify
hub genes or clusters and test for enrichment ([Huang et al. 2009]) in known biological
processes or pathways (e.g., via GO [Ashburner et al. 2000]). However, due to the severe
dimensionality reduction required by the Bayesian method (limited to 100 genes) and
the low overlap between the datasets and known pathway databases, any such analysis
would suffer from low statistical power and potential bias toward well­studied genes. As
a result, no reliable enrichment analysis could be conducted.
5. Statistical Significance Testing of Structural Changes In principle, one could sta­
tistically test whether prior integration significantly alters the network structure—e.g., by
comparing the distribution of overlap scores or edge differences across many random
prior networks. However, only a single fixed prior network was used per dataset. Without
replicates or alternative priors, it was not possible to estimate a null distribution or conduct
hypothesis testing. Future work could look at ways to test how reliable or consistent the
network changes are.
6. Biological Validation Lastly, biological validation—whether through pathway enrich­
ment, functional module analysis, or manual comparison with established gene–gene
interactions—would also not be approriate. Given the small number of retained genes
(only 100 per dataset) and the sparse coverage of priors, such comparisons would be
speculative and potentially misleading.
Given the outlined limitations, no additional evaluation procedures were applied in this
study. Applying standard biological or statistical tools under these constraints would risk
producing unreliable or misleading results. Rather than forcing inappropriate validation,
this thesis acknowledges the limits of the available data and methods, and avoids over­
interpreting findings that cannot be reliably supported.

Conclusions & Future Work
This final chapter concludes the thesis by summarizing the key findings, reflecting on
the two central research questions, and highlighting the broader implications of the work.
It also outlines limitations and proposes directions for future research.
8.1
Summary of Key Findings
This thesis’s goal was to investigate whether the integration of prior biological knowl­
edge improves GGCN inference across different computational strategies, and how the
choice of inference method affects the structure and biologically interpretability of the re­
sulting networks. To this end, four distinct approaches were applied—classical (INfORM),
Bayesian (HBFM), graph­based (SNF integration), and Lasso­based (TGL)—to three real­
world scRNA­seq datasets, both with and without the incorporation of PPI data as prior
knowledge.
The findings can be summarized as follows:
• Effect of Prior Knowledge: The impact of prior knowledge on GGCN structure var­
ied substantially across methods. In the classical INfORM approach, prior integra­
tion typically led to modest structural changes—sometimes reducing and other times
increasing network density—while preserving overall connectivity. In contrast, the
graph­based approach resulted in substantial edge pruning, reflecting the filtering
effect of prior data, yet still maintained a fully connected network. The Lasso­based
approach exhibited the least change, with only small numbers of edges added or
removed; however, it consistently reduced the number of disconnected nodes, sug­
gesting improved network connectivity.
• Method­Specific Characteristics:
– The classical approach showed moderate sensitivity to prior knowledge. In
some datasets, prior integration led to edge pruning, while in others, it intro­
duced new connections. The networks remained fully connected, but structural
overlap with the baseline varied, indicating that prior knowledge influenced the
networks, though not in a consistent direction. Due to evaluation constraints,
no claims can be made about whether the resulting networks were more inter­
pretable.
– The Bayesian method was severely constrained by computational limitations,
requiring aggressive dimensionality reduction that likely excluded important bi­
ological signals. Its practical applicability in real­world scRNA­seq contexts
remains limited.
– The graph­based approach was more sensitive to prior information and exhib­
ited less structural consistency after prior integration. The substantial changes
in network structure—often involving extensive edge removal—suggest that
the prior acted as a strong filter. While this may help emphasize known biol­
ogy, it also risks excluding novel or weaker data­driven associations.
– The Lasso­based method preserved sparsity while incorporating prior knowl­
edge smoothly, showing high structural stability and improving connectivity.
Although the structural changes were minimal, the method demonstrates how
prior knowledge can act as a gentle regularizer rather than a disruptive force
which overrides the underlying data­driven structure.
• Cross­Method Comparison: Edge overlap scores and connectivity analyses re­
vealed that both the classical and Lasso methods incorporated prior knowledge in
a way that reinforced or refined the network structure rather than radically altering

==PAGE:84==
it. In contrast, the graph­based approach often led to more pronounced changes,
suggesting it is more sensitive to the influence of priors.
• Evaluation Challenges: Standard biological validation techniques such as gene
set enrichment, community detection, or hub analysis were not applicable due to
methodological constraints (e.g., small gene sets, lack of continuous edge weights,
and sparse prior coverage). Instead, a structural comparison approach was adopted
to provide a basis for evaluating the potential impact of prior knowledge across meth­
ods.
8.2
Addressing the Research Questions
RQ1: ”Does the incorporation of prior knowledge improve gene­gene co­expression net­
work inference across different network inference strategies?”
The results suggest that the incorporation of prior knowledge influences the structure of
inferred networks in a method­specific manner, but no definitive statement can be made
about whether it “improves” inference in a general sense. Due to the lack of biological
ground truth and the inability to apply enrichment or validation techniques, this question
could not be fully resolved.
What can be observed is that:
• The classical method showed moderate sensitivity to prior knowledge, with varying
effects across datasets.
• In the Bayesian setting, prior integration was attempted but ultimately unsuccessful,
reflecting implementation challenges rather than inherent limitations of the method
itself.
• In the graph­based approach, prior knowledge had a strong filtering effect, substan­
tially altering network structure.
• In the Lasso­based method, prior integration led to minor structural changes, sug­
gesting that prior knowledge was incorporated in a restrained and stable manner.
Overall, the effect of prior knowledge depended heavily on the integration strategy and
the sparse overlap between prior and data features. Whether these changes improved
GGCN inference remains unresolved.
RQ2: “How do different inference strategies compare in terms of their ability to produce
biologically meaningful and interpretable gene­gene co­expression networks?”
This question could not be directly addressed, as the thesis did not include biological
validation due to the small gene sets, limited prior coverage, and constraints on enrich­
ment analysis. As a result, no conclusions can be drawn about the biological meaningful­
ness or interpretability of the networks.
Contributions to the Field
Although this thesis does not introduce new methods or biological discoveries, it pro­
vides practical insights into the challenges of integrating prior knowledge into gene­gene
co­expression network inference.
By comparing four common inference strategies under realistic constraints, it highlights
how method choice, data limitations, and sparse prior coverage can significantly affect
outcomes.
The work raises awareness about the need for careful handling of data, thought­
ful selection of inference methods, and cautious interpretation of structural changes—
encouraging future studies to combine prior integration with robust validation.

8.3
Limitations and Future Work
Several limitations emerged from this work:
• Dimensionality Constraints: The necessity to limit the datasets to 100 genes (due
to the Bayesian method) likely excluded many biologically relevant signals, reducing
the realism of the inferred networks.
• Sparse Prior Coverage: The STRING­derived prior networks had limited gene
overlap, diminishing their potential impact on inference.
• Lack of Quantitative Validation: The inability to perform any commonly used eval­
uation technique limited the depth of biological interpretation.
Future research could address the limitations identified in this study by exploring alter­
native strategies for both inference and prior integration.
One direction would be to explore alternative techniques for incorporating prior knowl­
edge into the current Bayesian framework (HBFM). The inability to integrate prior informa­
tion in this thesis was not due to limitations of the model itself, but rather the challenges
in implementing a suitable integration mechanism. Investigating more effective ways to
incorporate prior structure within the HBFM model may allow for successful integration
without compromising the Bayesian nature of the model.
In addition, scaling up the number of cells could improve the robustness and stability of
the inferred networks. However, the strict limit of 100 genes—required by HBFM for com­
putational feasibility—remains a major limitation. This bottleneck severely restricts the bi­
ological richness of the input data and diminishes the potential impact of prior knowledge,
as many informative genes are excluded upfront. Given this, it may be more productive
to explore alternative Bayesian inference methods that are inherently more scalable and
capable of handling larger gene sets without requiring such aggressive filtering. Advanc­
ing Bayesian approaches with more flexible prior incorporation and improved scalability
could lead to more meaningful applications in real­world scRNA­seq analysis.
Another important direction for future work involves improving the pseudobulk aggre­
gation strategy used during preprocessing. In this study, raw counts were summed within
annotated cell groups, which probably introduced biases related to group size. More ro­
bust approaches—such as using the mean or median instead of the sum, or applying
more biologically informed grouping criteria—could reduce these biases and better pre­
serve the underlying expression patterns. Refining pseudobulk techniques may enhance
the quality of the input data and, in turn, improve the reliability of the inferred networks.
Moreover, the quality and handling of prior knowledge itself need further attention. A
large portion of prior information was lost during intersection with the selected gene sets,
limiting its impact. Future work could experiment with more refined filtering or weighting
schemes that might improve the relevance of the retained prior edges. Additionally, using
different types of priors (e.g., tissue­specific interactions, regulatory networks, or pathway­
based priors) and more sophisticated preprocessing pipelines could help retain a greater
amount of informative biological signal.
Overall, improving the scalability of inference, rethinking pseudobulk creation and in­
tegration strategies, and optimizing the construction and use of priors could lead to more
informative and biologically grounded network models in future studies.
8.4
Final Reflection
This thesis highlights the practical and methodological complexities involved in inte­
grating prior knowledge into gene­gene co­expression network inference. The outcomes
showed that prior integration does not uniformly benefit all inference strategies and is
strongly shaped by factors such as data dimensionality, method­specific constraints, and
the quality of available prior information. These findings underline the importance of align­

ing inference approaches with the structure of the data and the nature of the prior, rather
than assuming that integration will automatically improve results. By highlighting these
challenges and trade­offs, this work provides a foundation for future studies to make more
careful decisions when constructing gene­gene co­expression networks under real­world
constraints.

Appendix: Data
B.1
Simulation Attempts and Limitations
Several approaches for generating simulated scRNA­seq data were initially explored to
provide additional benchmarks for evaluating network inference methods. However, after
careful consideration, multiple limitations were identified that ultimately prevented their
use in the final analysis. This section outlines the simulation attempts and the reasons
why the simulated data were not incorporated into the main results.
B.1.1
SymSim R Package
At the beginning, the SymSim package was explored as a potential tool for simulating
scRNA­seq data. Comparison studies have shown that Symsim provides high realism
in capturing biological and technical variability and has a very good performance, even
though it is relatively slow and computationally intensive [Cao et al. 2021]. SymSim is
a simulator that generates realistic single­cell data, while also introducing biological and
technical noise [X. Zhang et al. 2019]. The simulator separates the simulation into two
steps: generation of true gene expression levels, and subsequent simulation of technical
noise such as dropout events, amplification bias1, and sequencing depth2. This dual ap­
proach makes SymSim highly flexible and effective for benchmarking tasks like clustering
and differential expression.
However, after initial experimentation, SymSim was found to be less suitable for the
goals of this thesis. The tool is primarily designed to simulate variability between cells,
focusing on modeling complex cell­to­cell relationships and population structures, rather
than capturing gene­gene relationships that are critical for co­expression network infer­
ence. Because the inference methods in this work emphasize accurate modeling of gene
interactions, the focus on cellular heterogeneity in SymSim did not align with the goal of
the thesis.
Therefore, the SymSim­based approach was eventually rejected.
B.1.2
Simulation Attempt Using a Sparse Bayesian Factor Model
As a second approach, a simulation strategy based on the sparse hierarchical Bayesian
factor model (HBFM) introduced by Sekula et al.[Sekula 2020] was explored. In their
study, Sekula et al. generated 12 simulated datasets. The first six (Sim 1–6) were pro­
duced directly using the HBFM model. This model simulates gene expression data by
modeling latent factors that influence gene activity across cells. Specifically, expression
values are sampled from a Poisson distribution, where the mean is determined by gene­
specific baseline levels and contributions from shared latent factors. These factors are de­
signed to capture the key characteristics of single­cell RNA­seq data, including high spar­
sity, overdispersion, and zero inflation. The method organizes genes into structured mod­
ules by assigning them shared factor loadings, and generates gene­gene co­expression
patterns through these shared associations.
Although this simulation approach produced biologically realistic data and was effec­
tive for recovering latent structure, it was ultimately rejected for use in this thesis. This is
because the simulation framework closely mirrors the architecture of the Bayesian infer­
ence model used later in this project. Using simulated data that is inherently structured

Simulation Attempt Using a Zero­Inflated Negative Binomial Model

Protein Names in STRING: In the STRING database [Szklarczyk, Gable, et al. 2021],
proteins are primarily identified by Ensembl Protein IDs. These identifiers follow the format

• 9606 is the NCBI taxonomy3 ID for Homo sapiens (human)
• ENSP indicates that the identifier refers to an Ensembl Protein
• The numeric string uniquely identifies a specific protein
Because STRING focuses on protein­protein interactions, it lists connections between
protein products, not directly between gene symbols.
The Ensembl Database: Ensembl is a widely used genomic database that provides
comprehensive information on genes, transcripts, and proteins across many species. The
database ([Ensembl 2024]) is used for mapping between different biological entities (e.g.,
linking a protein to its corresponding gene), enabling standardized annotation in bioinfor­
matics workflows.
Each gene in the Ensembl database is assigned a unique Ensembl Gene ID, typically
starting with the prefix ENSG (e.g., ENSG00000139618).
HGNC: HGNC stands for the HUGO Gene Nomenclature Committee, which is respon­
sible for assigning unique and standardized gene symbols to human genes ([HUGO Gene
Nomenclature Committee 2024]).
HGNC names are designed to be:
• Unique (no two genes share the same symbol)
• Stable (avoiding frequent changes)
• Widely recognized across biological research, clinical studies, and databases.

2006].

Association Measures and Discretization
To estimate the strength of gene­gene relationships, INfORM allows the use of both
correlation­based and mutual information­based measures. These metrics quantify how
gene expression levels vary together, either linearly or more generally. For mutual infor­
mation methods, discretization of continuous expression data is often required to improve
estimation accuracy. This subsection describes the roles of these measures in the net­
work inference process.
The following provides a brief overview of each of these measures and their relevance
to network inference:
• Correlation Measures: Correlation measures assess the degree to which two vari­
ables change together. In the context of gene expression, they are used to quantify
the similarity in expression patterns between gene pairs. While these methods are
easy to interpret and computationally efficient, they primarily capture linear or mono­
tonic relationships and may overlook more complex, non­linear associations [Song
et al. 2012].
• Mutual Information: Mutual information measures the statistical dependency be­
tween two variables (in this case, gene expression levels). It quantifies how much
knowing the expression of one gene reduces uncertainty about the expression of an­
other gene. High mutual information suggests a strong association between genes,
even if the relationship is non­linear[Kraskov et al. 2004].
• Discretization Strategies: Since mutual information is typically calculated for dis­
crete variables, continuous gene expression data must first be discretized—i.e.,

D.1
HBFM ­ Sampling Strategies
The sampling strategies used in the MCMC procedure are explained below:
• Metropolis–Hastings: Applied to αgf and ϕf, which do not have conjugate priors.
In Metropolis­Hastings, a new candidate value is proposed (e.g., by proposing a new
candidate value near the current one), and accepted with a probability that depends
on the ratio of the posterior probabilities of the new and current values. This allows
sampling from distributions that are difficult or impossible to compute analytically —
i.e., without closed­form expressions [Chib et al. 1995].
• Gibbs Sampling: A method where each parameter is updated by sampling from
its full conditional distribution while keeping others fixed [Casella et al. 1992]. Used
for βg, which has a conjugate Gamma prior. Because the posterior distribution of
βg is also Gamma, it can be sampled directly in closed form from its full conditional
distribution.
• Adaptive Rejection Sampling: Used for λif, which is modeled with a log­normal
distribution restricted to positive values. The adaptive rejection sampling is an effi­
cient method for sampling from distributions that have a smooth, peaked shape. It
works by creating a simple approximation of the target distribution and improving it
step by step to draw accurate samples, without needing to define a separate pro­
posal distribution [Gilks et al. 1992].

The Louvain Method
The Louvain method is a popular algorithm for detecting commu­
nities in large networks by optimizing a quality function known as modularity. It works in
two main phases: (1) nodes are greedily grouped into communities to locally maximize
modularity, and (2) communities are aggregated into super­nodes to build a reduced net­
work, and the process repeats iteratively [Blondel et al. 2008].
Modularity Q measures the difference between the observed edge density within com­
munities and what would be expected at random:

The algorithm is highly efficient, scales well to large networks, and is widely used in
biological network analysis to uncover modular structure [Blondel et al. 2008].
Connectivity and Modularity
Specifically, modularity reflects how well a network is di­
vided into clearly separated groups, where nodes within the same group (community) are
more densely connected to each other than to nodes in other groups. A high modularity
score indicates strong internal connectivity within communities and weaker links between
them. In biological networks, this often corresponds to functional modules—sets of genes
that participate in similar biological processes or interactions [Alcalá­Corona et al. 2021].
Connectivity, on the other hand, refers to whether the network is fully connected—that
is, whether there is a path between every pair of nodes/genes. A disconnected network
may contain isolated components that constrain global analysis and interpretation [Alcalá­
Corona et al. 2021].
Therefore, only fused networks that were fully connected and exhibited high modularity
were considered.
Convergence Tracking
SNF was applied to the selected affinity matrices using a mod­
ified implementation that includes convergence tracking. At each iteration, the similarity
networks exchange information by updating each matrix based on the others. The algo­
rithm continues this process until either the maximum number of iterations is reached or
the similarity values across iterations stabilize, in which case it stops early. This helps
improve efficiency while ensuring that the fused network has converged. This approach
was based on the open­source R package provided at SNFtool GitHub repository.

StARS Selection Criterion
StARS is used to select the optimal regularization parame­
ter λ based on model stability under subsampling [Liu et al. 2010, Lingjærde et al. 2021].
For each λ, networks are estimated across multiple random subsamples of the data, and
the consistency of edge selection is evaluated.
In this context, instability refers to how frequently a given edge appears in some sub­
samples but not in others. If an edge is either consistently present or consistently absent
across all subsamples, it is considered stable. However, if it appears only in a fraction of
the subsamples, it is viewed as unstable. The total instability for a given λ is calculated
by averaging these inconsistencies over all edges.
The final λ∗is chosen as the smallest value for which the overall instability remains
below a user­defined threshold β. A commonly used value is β = 0.05, which means that
up to 5% edge disagreement across subsamples is considered acceptable. This default
is also adopted in the TGL paper for all analyses [Lingjærde et al. 2021].
Range of Steepness k
Although the paper by Lingjærde et al. [Lingjærde et al. 2021]
mentions the range k ∈[0, 150], their code analysis uses k ∈[1, 150]. This is because
at k = 0, the logistic transformation assigns a uniform transformed weight qij = 0.5 to all
edges, regardless of the prior weight wij. This removes the effect of the prior information.
The Extended Bayesian Information Criterion (EBIC)
EBIC was used to select the
optimal k by balancing model fit and complexity. The EBIC is defined as:
EBIC( bΘ) = −2 · ℓ( bΘ) + |E| · log(n) + 4 · |E| · γ · log(p),
where ℓ( bΘ) is the log­likelihood, |E| is the number of edges in the network, n is the num­
ber of samples, p is the number of genes, and γ is a user­defined parameter controlling
the strength of the penalty on model size [Chen et al. 2012; Lingjærde et al. 2021].
The value of γ was set to 0.6, following the recommendation from Lingjærde et al.[Lingjærde
et al. 2021].

Master Thesis Description:
This project investigates the integration of prior biological knowledge into gene­gene co­expression
network (GGCN) inference using real­world single­cell RNA sequencing (scRNA­seq) data. The fo­
cus is on evaluating how different inference strategies—specifically, a classical correlation and mu­
tual information­based method, a Bayesian latent factor model, a graph­based integration approach,
and a Lasso­based regularization method—respond to the inclusion of prior knowledge derived from
protein­protein interaction (PPI) networks. The study compares networks inferred with and without
prior knowledge across three datasets and evaluates structural changes using edge­level analysis
and overlap scores. Due to methodological constraints, biological validation was limited, and the
emphasis was placed on understanding the practical implications and trade­offs of prior integration
across different modeling approaches.
