Abstract
The application of data-driven machine learning continues to advance the possibilities of
modern computational abilities. While the potential of these models is vast, the models
can still be viewed as somewhat immature. Many of these techniques are covered in
research papers, but not fully propagated into textbooks with clear theoretical details
and implementation guidelines. For these techniques to be implemented in practice in
modern engineering, the capabilities and limitations need to be transparent with acces-
sible implementations.
This thesis investigates the intersection of deep generative modelling and Scientific Ma-
chine Learning (SciML). First the abilities of Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs)
and Variational Autoencoders (VAEs) to generate synthetic data from dynamical sys-
tems is tested. The results of this examination showed the VAE to be the more reliable
method of the two, producing better and more robust samples. A literature review of
selected methods within SciML and deep generative modelling examines the current
state-of-art. This review illuminates the potential of Physics-Informed Neural Networks
(PINNs) as grey-box models i.e solving problems with physical domain knowledge and
data measurements. The versatility of the GAN framework is brought to light in many
different applications, with proposed modifications of the GAN architecture. Through-
out the project the methods are benchmarked on common ”toy problems” or through
reproduction of public results.
The project proceeds to investigate the abilities of PINNs and the capabilities and lim-
itations of Automatic Differentiation (AD), which is used to enable physics-informed
loss terms. Tests of the Wasserstein-GAN (WGAN) and the WGAN-Gradient Penalty
(WGAN-GP) are conducted, in effort to improve the initial benchmark GAN results.
These methods are found to help mitigate some of the known GAN issues, but does not
help produce significantly more accurate results.
Multiple proposed Physics-Informed GAN (PI-GAN) methods are implemented and
tested. The Physics-Informed Generator GAN (PIG-GAN) and Physics-Informed Dis-
criminator GAN (PID-GAN), were deemed to have the ability to produce uncertainty
quantification and handle noisy data. A Navier-Stokes GAN (NSGAN) is implemented
and tested, showing the ability to generate data for fluid flow problems, potentially allow-
ing for a faster and more accurate method within computational fluid dynamics in the
future. A simplified version, the Physics-Informed-(Navier-Stokes) GAN (PI-(NS)GAN),
is proposed and tested, showing promising results as a grey-box model.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
The recent rise of powerful computing resources has fundamentally changed the abilities
of computers, and led to advances in areas such as machine learning enabling many
new technologies. Advances in computing capabilities and improved availability to large
data sets, has pioneered the area of data-driven machine learning across science and en-
gineering areas. Data-driven machine learning has the ability to inform decision making
and problem solving using data as the primary source of information. These machine
learning techniques has the ability to do predictive modeling, classification, data min-
ing, generative modeling, and more. All these techniques are increasingly applied in
fields such as computer science, finance and medicine. As the abilities of these methods
becomes more and more evident, the need for even more sophisticated models increase.
Innovative methods are constantly proposed as data science continuous to grow, while
these new techniques promise greater potential, the limitations and usefulness can be
diﬀicult to grasp. The field of deep generative modelling is a currently trending topic
with ChatGPT’s [1] presence in the media being an example of this, and is a result of
new innovation in the technical foundations relying on recurrent neural networks, trans-
former based language models and attention mechanisms.
Deep generative modelling uses neural networks to learn an underlying complex distri-
bution from a given dataset to generate new samples from this learnt distribution. Deep
generative modelling is an important and growing scientific area and has the ability
to create realistic synthetic data, within the domain of the training data. Synthetic
data can potentially be used to train machine learning models, particularly when it
is diﬀicult to collect a suﬀicient amount of real-world data.
In addition, generative
models can be used in a variety of applications, this includes image generation, natural
language processing, anomaly detection etc. There exists various types of generative
models, some popular examples being variational autoencoders (VAEs), generative ad-
versarial networks (GANs) and diffusion models. This thesis will focus on VAEs and
GANs. While the potential abilities of these models are vast, they can still be viewed
as somewhat immature and lacking robustness. Many of these techniques are covered
in research papers and not fully propagated into textbooks with clear theoretical details
and implementation guidelines.
Another emerging field within machine learning is scientific machine learning (SciML)
coined by George Karniadakis in 2019 [2], which is the practices of combining classic
scientific computational methods with machine learning. These methods aim to reduce
the amount of data necessary for data-driven techniques to be effective, by adding do-

main knowledge of the physical system of interest to the model. A recent example of
this, is the physics-informed neural network (PINN), which can produce solutions of

data from experiments, predict behavior of systems and estimate properties of materials

The purpose of this project is to investigate the intersection of deep generative mod-

deep generative modelling has the potential to reduce the cost of data collection, and
improve performance of data-driven models. For the generation of synthetic data to be
a useful tool, their ability to learn the underlying distribution of a dataset, without need-
ing massive amount of training data, is essential. The motivation of combining SciML
and deep generative modelling is therefore clear, as SciML aims to help with exactly
this problem. Furthermore, the combination of SciML and deep generative modelling
enhances the capabilities of the models, and allows for whole new problems to be solved
with these novel methods. The motivation for this project is also to investigate these
emerging methods, and examine their functionality and limitations. This will be carried
out through a detailed literature review and implementation of a number of the methods
and benchmarking these on common ’toy problems’ or through reproduction of public
results.

1.2
Novel contributions
The novel contributions of this work are the application and study of generative models
combined with differential equations describing dynamic systems. The focus has been to
test various models and describe their abilities, and additionally discuss their advantages
and limitations. In general, the tests throughout the project have been carried out in
tutorial style, to enable simple recreation of the presented methods.
To our knowledge, the novel contributions are:
• A combination of various generative models with differential equations describing
dynamic systems, and a thorough testing of capabilities and limitations.
• An extensive literature review, investigating the progression of selected generative
techniques all the way from their origins to the current state-of-the-art.
• The development of a simpler version based on an existing framework describing
a Navier-Stokes GAN - referred to as the PI-(NS)GAN.
• A comparison of the performance of the Navier-Stokes GAN to the original PINN
describing the same system.
• Implementation of data-driven discovery for the physics informed GAN frameworks
tested in this thesis, to approximate unknown parameters of differential equations.
Finally, the work in this thesis contributes to the knowledge of emerging scientific ma-
chine learning methods to solve complicated engineering problems.

CHAPTER 2
Fundamental Theory
This chapter covers some of the fundamental theory needed to implement neural net-
works and in extension of this, physics-informed neural networks, variational autoen-
coders and generative adversarial networks. First we explain the universal approxima-
tion theorem that give backing to the training of neural networks.
Next, we cover
automatic differentiation (AD) that is the basis for eﬀicient and accurate differentiation
via the chain rule for such neural networks. From here, we can establish the basic PINN,
VAE and GAN architectures and their capabilities.
2.1
Universal Approximation Theorem
One the main reasons why neural networks are such a powerful tool is the Universal
Approximation Theorem (UAT), which in essence means that a neural network can
compute any function. This does not mean it is an exact fit, but it does mean that by
using enough hidden neurons, there exists a neural network
f(x) = N(x; w, b),
(2.1)
whose output f(x) can approximate g(x) by satisfying |f(x) −g(x)| < ϵ, for all inputs
x and with ϵ < 0 [4]. A neural network N(x; w, b) consists of node layers that can be
divided into; an input layer, a hidden layer and an output layer. If there are multiple
hidden layers it is called a deep neural network. Each node, also called artificial neuron,
is connected to all other nodes in the next and previous layer, and have an associated
weight (w) and bias (b). Each node has an activation function that uses linear or non-
linear functions like sigmoid, hyperbolic tangent or ReLU. The input values flow through
the weights, they are added to the bias and are activated by the activation functions. If
a node is activated, its output flows to the next layer in the network. The activation of
the final layer in vectorized form is written as
al = σ

wlal−1 + bl
,
(2.2)
where al is the activation of the last layer. It is calculated by applying the weight matrix
wl to the activation of the previous layer al−1, adding the biases vector bl and finally
applying the non-linear activation function σ.
The first and most simple form of neural network is the feed forward neural network [5]

seen in Figure 2.1. It works by feeding labeled data, i.e. training data into the input
layer and forward through the network. The output layer will either; classify the data
into a chosen number of classes, or predict an output if regression is the goal. At first
these classifications will be very poor, but this can be solved by training the network. To
do this, we need a loss function to measure the distance between the network’s output
and the true values. Typical loss functions could be the mean squared error (MSE) or
the cross entropy loss. To minimize the loss, the backpropagation algorithm, proposed
by Rumelhart et al. [6], is used. It works by computing the gradient of the loss function
with respect to each weight by the chain rule. This is done iteratively backwards from
the output layer. Each weight can then be updated accordingly to these derivatives. One
full iteration of all this is called an epoch. Several epochs are usually needed, in order
to achieve a useful trained network. This simple approach is also called a black-box
approximation, meaning it is purely data-driven and approximates a function without
any explicit knowledge of the function itself.
Figure 2.1: Illustration of a deep neural network [7].
There a multiple universal approximation theorems, which each imply that neural net-
works can represent a wide variety of functions, when given appropriate weights. These
can be divided in mainly two categories: The arbitrary width case (meaning arbitrary
number of artificial neurons) and the arbitrary depth case i.e an arbitrary number of
hidden layers, each layer containing a limited number of artificial neurons. One of the
first proofs of the arbitrary width case, was proven by George Cybenko [8] using sigmoid
activation functions. It has later been expanded upon by many authors. Hornik [9]
showed that the choice of activation function is insignificant, instead it is the multi-layer
feed-forward architecture that enables the method to be a universal approximator. Both
the arbitrary depth case and bounded depth and bounded width case, have been proven
in many instances, which we will not cover here. The important idea behind these proofs
are mainly that the specific architecture of a neural network is problem-specific, but the
framework is so flexible that it can approximate any type of functions.

2.2
Automatic Differentiation
In order to train neural networks one thing is completely essential, automatic differentia-
tion (AD). It allows for minimizing loss functions and do backpropagation. This section
will give an overview of AD and the techniques that make it possible [10][11][12]. In
order to perform gradient descent to optimize loss functions, differentiation is necessary
to calculate gradients. This can be done in four different ways: Manual, numerical,
symbolic and automatic differentiation. Manual differentiation means working out the
derivatives by hand or mathematical tools by programming them manually.
This is
of course too time consuming for deep neural networks. Numerical differentiation uses
finite difference, so it is easy to implement. The problem is that it is a subject to round-
off and truncation errors, and it requires at least two function evaluations per input
dimensions. Symbolic differentiation uses algebraic manipulations based on the chain
rule for function differentiation to obtain an expression for the derivative. The problem
here is that it suffers from ”expression swelling”. This means, that the result is overly
complicated symbolic representations, which require expensive evaluations to calculate
the gradient.
This leaves AD, which is a game changer in the world of deep learning. AD has two
modes; forward mode differentiation and reverse mode differentiation. Before going into
details on the modes, dual numbers need to be introduced.

In summary, forward mode AD has the time complexity O(ninputs) as it needs to run
once per input, in order to calculate the full Jacobian matrix needed for all the partial
derivatives. The memory usage is constant O(1). The reverse mode has time complexity
O(noutputs), but has memory complexity O(noperations) as all intermediate values must be
stored, since it is recursive. The importance of AD in deep learning is indispensable,
as without backpropagation it would not be possible. In addition to this AD has the
ability to differentiate neural networks, which allows physics to be expressed in the loss
term. More on this in section 2.5.

Variational Autoencoders
Variational Autoencoders (VAEs) was proposed by Diederik P. Kingma and Max Welling
in 2014 [13], which enables eﬀicient approximate inference trough optimization of neural
networks.
VAE has a similar architecture to Autoencoders, but differs in terms of
objective. The aim of VAEs is to learn the underlying distribution of data and encode
it over a latent space, this allows eﬀicient inference and enables generative modeling by
sampling from the learnt distribution.
The architecture of VAE consist of an encoder, a latent space and a decoder.
This
is similar to a standard autoencoder. The objective of an autoencoder is to compress
input data into a small point in the latent space, and then be able to reconstruct the
original input from the encoded latent space. This is useful for encoding unlabeled data,
denoising, data compression etc [14]. In order to use this network structure for generative
modeling, the composition of the latent space needs to be changed from deterministic
(a regular autoencoder) to probabilistic. The latent space also needs to be organized in
a way that will enable sampling.

2.4
Generative Adversarial Networks
Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) were first introduced in 2014 by Ian J. Good-
fellow and co-authors [18]. A GAN is a model architecture for training a generative
model. It consists of two sub-models: a generator model for generating new data in-
stances from the problem domain and a discriminator model for authenticating whether
the generated instances of data is real (from the training set) or fake (produced by the
generator).
So, the generative model is set against an adversary, the discriminative
model, which learns to determine whether a sample is from the model distribution or
the data distribution, thereby adversarial. The generative model can be thought of as
a counterfeiter, attempting to create fake currency and use it. While the discriminative
model can be thought of as the police, attempting to detect the counterfeit currency.
The competition then drives both sides to enhance their procedure until the counterfeit
currency is indistinguishable from the authentic currency.
Figure 2.7: Illustration of the adversarial part of GANs, which can be represented as
competition between a counterfeiter and the police [19].
The generator model is a neural network that takes a fixed-length random vector as
input. The vector is denoted z, it is drawn from a simple distribution e.g. uniform, and
is used to seed the generative process. The generative network transforms this vector to
a point in a multidimensional vector space, thereby creating a compressed representa-
tion of the original problem domain, as this process is performed repeatedly for different
random vectors. The compressed representation is referred to as a latent space. After
==PAGE:21==

problem domain that follows the targeted distribution.
The discriminator model is simpler than the generator model.
It only takes a data
sample, either generated or from the real data distribution, and outputs the binary clas-
sification label ”real” or ”fake”. The combined representation of the generator model
along with the discriminator model can be seen in Figure 2.8.
Figure 2.8: High-level architecture of a GAN [20].
Now that we have established the basic architecture of a GAN, we will look into the
mathematical details defining GANs. It essentially requires us to define one additional
item, which is a loss function.
The generative network is defined as: G(z; θG), where z is fixed-length random vec-

distribution over data, a prior pz(z) on the input variables is defined.
The discriminative network is defined as: D(x; θD), where x can either be a real or
generated vector. If x is a real vector, we then say it comes from the original data dis-
tribution pdata(x), however if it is a generated vector, it then comes from the generated
distribution pg(x).
D(x) represents the probability that x is from the original data distribution pdata(x)
rather than from the generated data distribution pg(x). θD is the network parameters
for the network D.

This section is a brief introduction of the physics-informed neural networks (PINNs)
framework introduced by Raissi et al.[23].
This is a quick overview of the original

Physics-Informed Neural Networks are a universal function approximator that uses prior
knowledge of physical laws in the training of neural networks. They are able to general-

by improving the information content in the given data by using the governing laws of
physics.

CHAPTER 5
Literature Review
This chapter is a literature review of the current state of the art methods within
physics-informed neural networks and deep generative modeling.
We will cover the
original introductions of the methods and further extensions/improvements upon
the original propositions.
This literature review mainly focuses on incorporating
information about physics in VAEs and GANs, but will also highlight other relevant
and current methods within deep generative modelling and physics-informed neural
networks.
This literature review will research the abilities of these methods as well
as their limitations. It will investigate applications in different settings and look into
where some of these methods are more advantageous than others.
The field of ML is rapidly expanding, as the amount of data and computational power is
continuously increasing. As the capabilities of ML is growing, so is the need for increas-
ingly more sophisticated methods and innovations. One of the the new fields emerging
from ML is Scientific Machine Learning (SciML) coined by George Karniadakis in 2019
[2]. SciML is the practices of combining classical scientific computational methods with
machine learning. This joint approach can excel in scenarios, where ”dynamics are com-
plex and multiscale, and/or where data is sparse and expensive to acquire” [32]. The
potential of SciML is great, but since the field is new, it can be diﬀicult to see where
the methods can surpass the standard methods. Some of the promising research areas
are, but not limited to: Surrogate modeling and model reduction, interpretable machine
learning, Bayesian inverse problems, physics-informed deep learning and more [32]. A
major part of SciML is based on the idea of encoding the physical laws described by
differential equations using AD. The next section contains a brief introduction of the
original PINN paper, followed by subsequent advancements.
5.1
Physics-Informed Neural Networks (PINNs)
Physics-informed neural networks were first presented in 2017 by Raissi et al.
[23]
(box 2 Figure 5.1).
The theory behind one of the paper’s main concepts has been
described in Section 2.5. The training of PINNs is simulation-free as it does not require
any training set obtained from numerical PDE solvers. Instead, it only requires the
physical problem description, this includes: The governing laws of physics, domain
definition, initial and boundary conditions and material properties. Therefore, PINNs
in its simplest form is considered a white-box approach, since it is purely model based
==PAGE:48==
40
5 Literature Review
and not fed any data (only information about the physical system). However, a PINN
of this plain configuration does not provide any advantages in terms of accuracy and
computational effectiveness compared to the ordinary methods.
It should thereby
not be viewed as replacements of the classical numerical methods for solving partial
differential equations (e.g., finite elements, spectral methods, etc.) [23].
If instead the PINN is ”fed” some data points from the solution of the PDE describing
the physical system (or real observations), the PINN can be viewed as a grey-box
approach, as it is now, not completely data-driven (black-box) nor purely model based
(white-box).
This addition enhances the robustness of the PINN’s ability to learn
useful solutions of differential equations, by incorporating information about physics
in the loss function. It relies on sampling in the parameter space in training. This
means it can use the inputted data points as guidance, but still heavily relies on the the
governing laws of physics. This means that PINNs have the potential to solve inverse
problems with small amounts of data by using problem domain knowledge, which can
be very diﬀicult for ordinary methods. This is, according to George Karniadakis, one
of the huge potentials of PINNs, as no eﬀicient methods exist to solve problems with
known governing equations (even without knowing all parameters of the equations) and
limited collected data [33].
From the introduction of PINNs, the concept has been vastly expanded upon.
A quick search on DTU Findit produces around 2,000 results and on Google
Scholar 12,400 (18/11/2022).
This means that it is diﬀicult to recap all the
research,
different approaches and results.
According to the CRUNCH group
(https://www.brown.edu/research/projects/crunch/home), the research team of
Professor George Em Karniadakis, ”a whole alphabet of PINNs now exists: cPINNs
(conservative); vPINNs (variational); pPINNs (parareal); nPINNs (nonlocal); B-PINNs
(Bayesian) etc”.
A collection of the papers can be found at [3].
Similar for all
these methods, are that they combine innovations in machine learning and scientific
computing with the original PINN concept to improve upon or solve problems, which
can be diﬀicult with classical methods. The major advantages for most of these PINNs
are their ability to solve grey-box problems.
One example of these PINNs is the B-PINNs (Bayesian Physics-Informed Neural
Network).
Several variations of this framework are used in different applications in
Meng et al. [34] for solving multi-fidelity data problems and in Yang et al. [35] for
forward and inverse PDE problems with noisy data.
These are good examples of
PINNs versatility, in Meng et al. B-PINN uses three different neural networks; a DNN
for low-fidelity data, a Bayesian neural network (BNN) for cross-correlation and a
PINN used to encode the physical laws from the PDE. The paper demonstrate two
major advantages ”the approach is capable of capturing both linear and nonlinear
correlations between the low- and high-fidelity data adaptively, and can quantify both
aleatoric uncertainty associated with noisy data and epistemic uncertainty associated
with unknown parameters”. An example of solving real world problems with this, is
using the framework to approximate sea surface temperatures using low-fidelity satellite
temperature data combined with high-fidelity temperature sensor placed in the ocean.
==PAGE:49==
5.1 Physics-Informed Neural Networks (PINNs)
41
This method results in better accuracy using multi-fidelity data compared to using
single-fidelity.
In Yang et al. [35], a different B-PINN framework is proposed, here two neural networks
are used, one BNN and one PINN for ”solving forward and inverse PDE problems
with noisy data associated with the solution”. The authors demonstrate good accuracy
and robustness using a Hamiltonian Monte Carlo (HMC) B-PINN-HMC. This method
outperforms the standard PINN as it is less prone to overfit the noise data. It should be
noted that for data with high amount of noise, perhaps a PI-GAN framework is more
advantageous [33] (NB NB NB PI-GANs for noisy data and uncertainty quantification
is covered in Section 5.5).
These are just a few examples from the ”alphabet of PINNs”.
It is clear that the
potential to solve conventionally diﬀicult grey-box problems is large, but some potential
issues are that these PINNs are quite problem specific, and it can be diﬀicult to quickly
survey the best suited method for a given problem. This means that, if straightforward
templates of implementations are not available and ”math-professor-level expertise” is
necessary to design problem specific frameworks, it is reasonable to assume that these
methods will not be implemented as standard in the industry in the foreseeable future.
Another of PINNs abilities worth discussing is surrogate modeling. Surrogate modeling
is a promising application of PINNs, it allows PINNs to be an approximation of a
solution to a system, where all the time consuming calculations are done when training
the network. Once this is complete, a full solution is then obtained with just one forward
pass through the network. Implementations of this can be found in Haghighat et al. [36],
where they use surrogate modeling in solid mechanics and in Nakamura et al. they test
PINNs with variable initial conditions [37]. Much of the promise lies in implementing
PINNs, where parameters of the governing equations or initial conditions can be variable
input for the network. This means that a simple surrogate model can be implemented
with very little computational power required after it is trained, as the solution of a
complicated system is now just one forward pass. This could allow automatic control
in applications such as drones with very little CPU needed or be used for very fast
approximations of finite element methods (FEM) cases such a mechanics. These ideas
or similar are explored in articles by the CRUNCH group and others.
Since the loss function in PINNs is composed of multiple loss functions, it often will be
beneficial to weigh the components individually (instead of an equally-weighted linear
combination). As the importance of each loss function could be different, thus the com-
ponents should be assigned weights as per their contribution to the learning. Heydari
et al. in 2019 [38] address this by proposing a family of methods, called SoftAdapt,
that dynamically change function weights of the loss function composed of multiple
loss functions based on iterative updated performance statistics of the component losses.
The article proposes three variants.
The first one is the original variant (Soft-
Adapt), that uses the heuristic, ”it is better to favor the gradient of a function according
==PAGE:50==
42
5 Literature Review
to its recent performance”. SoftAdapt is a Softmax function where the rate of change is
the input, which serves as a performance measure of each part. Which is useful when
the components of the loss function have the same order of magnitude.
The next variant is the loss weighted, where the Softmax function is modified to
account for the current values of the losses, as well as their rates of change. It uses the
magnitude of each part of the loss function as well as the rate of change, which gives
the most improvement when the values of the objective functions are on different scales.
The advantage of using this variant, is in assigning smaller weights to functions that
are close to their minima, even if rates of change stay constant or positive.
The third variant is the normalized variant.
This has the effect of sharpening the
distinction between small rates of change and softening it between large ones. It uses
normalized rates of change to ensure a better distribution of weights, when the slopes
possess vastly different scales.
It can be combined with the previous loss weighted
variant.
Various experiments is conducted to evaluate the performance of the three
SoftAdapt variants in different test cases.
This method improves the learning process of multi-part loss functions, which would be
beneficial for many of the methods addressed in this chapter. However, we have not
seen it been applied in the various research papers read for this literature review.

5.5
Physics-Informed GAN (PI-GAN)
After these advancements in the robustness for training of GANs with WGAN and
WGAN-GP, multiple different approaches to make the GAN architecture physics-
informed have been taken.
These are built on the work of the original PINN work
by Raissi et al.[23] (box 2 in Figure 5.1) explained in Section 2.5. Some of the exact
details of how this is incorporated in the GAN framework will be examined in Chapter

PI-GANs.
In Yang et al.[35] (box 11 in Figure 5.1) a PI-GAN method is introduced to solve stochas-
tic differential equations (SDEs) based on a limited number of scattered measurements.
This is done by incorporation of the governing physical laws, here in the form of SDEs us-
ing automatic differentiation. The PI-GAN framework uses the WGAN-GP method and
consist of a discriminator/critic as a normal feed forward deep neural network (DNN).
The generator are made up of a combination of feed forward neural networks DNNs, and
a neural network induced by the SDE. This means that the generator is now physics-
informed. It will not only try to ”fool” the discriminator, but also uphold the governing
physics. The PI-GAN method demonstrated by Yang et al.[35] produces some promising
results: The method was able to solve forward, inverse, and mixed problems without
changing the framework of PI-GANs. The framework could produce means, standard
deviations and diffusion coeﬀicient of the stochastic solutions in good agreement with
benchmarks. The method can also produce these good approximations even when noise
dimension was mismatched between the input noise dimension and the one for the target
stochastic processes. The authors highlight that the PI-GAN method could effectively
solve very high dimensional problems given more sensor data with low-polynomial growth
in computational cost. In terms of issues and limitations several are presented: One is
that overfitting occurs in the generator, in addition to the discriminator pointed out by
[45]. Furthermore, it is concluded that: The standard GAN are not suitable for approx-
imating stochastic processes with deterministic boundary condition. Finally, that the
computational cost for PI-GAN is high and more consuming than comparable PINN
methods.
Building on the work done in the aforementioned article, Yibo Yang and Paris Perdikaris
propose adversarial uncertainty quantification in physics-informed neural networks [47]
(box 12 in Figure 5.1). The premise of the idea is by making the generator physics
informed by using a residual loss function it is possible to make ”a class of proba-
bilistic physics-informed neural networks that are capable of approximating arbitrary
conditional probability densities, while being constrained to generate samples that ap-

A different approach to make GANs with physical knowledge, is presented by Golany

train a DNN and is shown to significantly improve heartbeat classification compared to
just training on the actual data samples. These results improve the accuracy of state of
the art classifiers, and the framework is not specific to ECG, but can be applied to any
system described by differential equations.

5.6
Diffusion Models and Flow-based Models
This chapter has until now covered some of the current research withing deep generative
modeling and the practises of making VAE and GANs physics-informed. Before summing
up these models, two other popular methods within deep generative models is worth
giving a brief introduction, namely Diffusion models and Flow-based models.
The diffusion technique, introduced by Sohl-Dickstein et al. [56], works by gradually
adding Gaussian noise to an image until the image consist completely of noise, the
network then learns to recover the data by reversing the noising process [57]. To generate
new data when training is complete, randomly sampled noise is passed through the
learned denoising process. Diffusion models are latent variable models as they map their
latent space by using a fixed Markov chain. The fixed Markov chain adds noise gradually
to the data to approximate the posterior q(x1:T | x0), x1...xT are latent variables with
the same dimensionality as x0, which is the input data. The aim of the training process
is then to learn the reversal of this process i.e. pθ (xt−1 | xt). The trained model can then
traverse backwards along this chain to generate new data. Diffusion models have some
advantages to VAE and GAN: They produce very high quality images, and does not
include adversarial training, which can be diﬀicult as descried in this chapter. Diffusion
models also have good scalability and parallelizability [57].
Flow-based models, proposed by Danilo Jimenez Rezende and Shakir Mohamed [58],
works by explicitly learning the probability density function of the real data set. The
is in contrast to VAE and GAN, which does not do this as it is very diﬀicult and
intractable.
However, Flow-based models can do this with the help of normalizing
flows. Normalizing flow is a power full statistical tool for distribution approximation.
It works by applying a series of invertible transformation functions, creating a chain
of transformations that obtains the probability distribution of the data set. Using this
technique the log-likelihood of the input data is no longer intractable, meaning the loss
function is simply the negative log-likelihood over the training data set

that all this work is new, and the implementation of some of the aforementioned
methods is cumbersome and not easily done with already implemented functions in
PyTorch/Tensorflow etc.
In contrast, VAEs often requires significantly less training time than training GANs,
because VAEs have a simple end-to-end structure, similar to feed forward neural
networks. Yet, it is still able to achieve satisfactory results. This has also been our
experience so far.
However, the VAE framework is less flexible as it is more locked
in its plain structure. Since, the model consist of simpler neural network components
i.e.
one network for reducing the dimensionality of the data and one for increasing
the dimensionality of the data.
This leaves most of the approaches to be changes
in the latent space topology, which can be rather complex and challenging to implement.
It can be a challenging task to understand in which problem scenarios these models are
advantageous to use, and select the most suitable approach. From the research done in
this chapter, generally PINNs can be used for inverse problems and surrogate modelling.
If the problem is very noisy or stochastic in nature, we start to move into GAN/VAE
territory. This means, if the problem needs to work on noisy data measurements, the
abilities of GANs/VAEs as a grey-box model should be considered [33]. The distinctions
between surrogate models and generative models in the context of differential equations,
can be hard to discern. Typically when the problem is clear and predefined, and fast
execution speed is a main concern, PINNs and surrogate modelling are the better ap-
proach. If expansion of data sets or sampling is the concern, then GANs/VAEs are the
preferred choice.

CHAPTER 7
Improving GAN
In Chapter 4 the abilities of the standard GAN framework to learn dynamics of physical
systems were tested. The results was not impressive leaving a lot to be desired. In
the litterateur review, Chapter 5, methods of improving upon the standard GAN ar-
chitecture were presented, namely the WGAN and the WGAN-GP. In this section we
will investigate if WGAN and WGAN-GP can produce better results than their original
version. To do this we will examine the damped harmonic oscillator.
7.1
WGAN
The major difference in WGANs compared to the standard GAN framework, is that the
discriminator is changed to a critic. This means that the critic does not use the sigmoid
function in the output layer to output a probability, but instead outputs a scalar score
that does not have to be between 0 and 1. This score can be interpreted as how real the
input are. It affects the value function, which means that (2.30) is changed to
min
G max
D
V (D, G) = Ex∼pdata (x)[D(x)] −Ez∼pz[D(G(z))].
(7.1)
In order for this to work, D is constrained to be a 1-Lipschitz function. A Lipschitz
continuous function is limited in how fast it can change, this essentially means that a
real number exists such that the gradient of the function is smaller than this real number.
To enforce the constraint, WGAN applies a very simple clipping to restrict the maximum
weight value in D. This means that the weights of the critic must be within a certain
range controlled by some tunable clip value such as [−0.01, 0.01].

The WGAN-GP offers an alternative way to enforce the Lipschitz constraint. A dif-
ferentiable function is 1-Lipschtiz if and only if it has gradients with norm at most 1
everywhere, so the gradient norm of the critic’s output is directly constrained with re-

penalizes the model if the gradient norm moves away from its target norm value 1. The
penalty is enforced on the gradient norm for random samples ˆx ∼pˆx. The new objective

CHAPTER 8
Physics-Informed GANs
This chapter will cover the theory, implementation and results from the PI-GAN
frameworks proposed by Yibo Yang & Paris Perdikaris [47] and Daw et al.[48]. First,
we will try to recreate some of the uncertainty quantification results produced by Daw
et al.[48] using both the PIG- and PID-GAN methods. Uncertainty quantification is
an important end-goal for real-world applications of machine learning, as it allows for
better understanding of results and enables proper analysis of confidence of predictions.
This is essential as uninterpretable machine learning can be a barrier for actual
adaptation of machine learning.
Following this, inspired by the work of Cuevas et al. [49], we will try to use these PI-
GAN methods for synthetic data generation, and see if it can improve the results from
Chapters 4 and 7, and thus be a better and more robust alternative to the standard
GAN framework in the context of physical systems.
8.1
Model Description

informed-generator GAN (PIG-GAN). Daw et al. call their work the physics-informed-
discriminator GAN (PID-GAN), hence forward on we will use the same abbreviations.
Daw et al. also share their work implemented in PyTorch both for the PIG-GAN and
for the PID-GAN. We will base our implementation on both frameworks.

CHAPTER 9
Navier-Stokes GAN
To further showcase the versatility and potential of the GAN framework, this chapter
will examine the proposed Navier-Stokes GAN (NSGAN) by Wu et al. [50]. The NSGAN
is a proposed model to take advantages of the excellent fitting ability of PI-GANs and
its performance of numerical prediction and data generation, within computational fluid
dynamics (CFD). The aim of the method is to generate flow field data at low cost with
high precision. PI-GANs have excellent potential for this as it can learn a mesh free
solution and generate data for arbitrary resolution quickly with high precision. During
this chapter we will first cover the details of the proposed method, then apply the idea to
the damped harmonic oscillator problem, before finally testing it on a fluid flow problem.

The generator is defined as fully connected neural network, in order to better use the
automatic differentiation. It uses the swish function as activation function, given as
swish(x) = x · sigmoid(βx) =
x
1 + e−βx,
(9.4)
with β = 1. The coordinates x, y and the time step t are input to the neural network,
p,u and v are then generated. The lower half of Figure 9.1 expresses this in detail.
The discriminator of the NSGAN is defined as a convolutional neural network (CNN),
as CNNs have great performance on feature extraction [50]. However, since the input
of the discriminator consists of the coordinates x and y of and the time step t, with the
generated/real p,u,v it is quite different from a normal CNN case. To remedy this, a
special rearrange module is introduced. It duplicates x,y and t and reshapes it into a
3 × 3 grid, which can be interpreted as a low-resolution image. This is illustrated in
Figure 9.2.

9 Navier-Stokes GAN
Figure 9.2: Illustration of the low-level discriminator architecture [50].
Since the goal of discriminator is to determine a probability, which describes the
authenticity of the input, based on the conditions or prior x, y, t. The coordinates
and time step are chosen as duplicated variables rather than pressure and velocity
components.
This is the method proposed by Wu et al. [50] for fluid flow based on the Navier-Stokes
equation. In order to test this method on the damped harmonic oscillator we needed
to make a few changes. First, the generator input is simply the time step t and the
generated output is u, this can be seen in Figure 9.3. The discriminator was changed
to a simple fully connected network with the input being t and u/ˆu. We assessed that
the rearrange module was not as well suited for a simple ODE, due to the solution not
being multi-dimensional. Some version of a CNN could probably work, but was saved
for the fluid flow problem, after some very promising results using just a simple fully
connected network. When implementing this method for the pendulum we refer to is as
the Physics-Informed-(Navier-Stokes)-GAN (PI-(NS)GAN).

9.3
PI-(NS)GAN Results
To test the potential of the PI-(NS)GAN, we continue to use the example with a damped
harmonic oscillator. In the first test of the PI-(NS)GAN, it is trained on only 10 training
points, and it uses 1,000 collocation points to compute the residuals. The result can be
seen in Figure 9.4 and Table 9.1. We can see that the PI-(NS)GAN performs very well,
both in terms of accuracy and CPU time. The PI-(NS)GAN achieves a MSE of 9.96e-07,
which is evaluated over 500 test points. Considering that it only uses 10 training points,
this is a an excellent result as we have not been able to achieve similar accuracy using
PINNs. This result was the first attempt using the framework, and from experimenting
with hyperparameter tuning after this result. We concluded that this result was not just
a coincidence, but rather excellent robustness of the PI-(NS)GAN.

9.4
NSGAN Implementation
In this section, we will examine the abilities of the NSGAN to learn a solution of an
incompressible fluid flow described by the Navier-Stokes equations. We will test on the
same example used by Raissi et al. [24] in their original PINN paper. This enables us
to easily compare the NSGAN to the PINN example as Raissi et al. has made their
data publicly available https://github.com/maziarraissi/PINNs. The example used
in the paper is the two dimensional Navier-Stokes PDE given by
ut + λ1 (uux + vuy) = −px + λ2 (uxx + uyy) ,
(9.5a)
vt + λ1 (uvx + vvy) = −py + λ2 (vxx + vyy) .
(9.5b)
u(t, x, y) is the x-component of the velocity field, v(t, x, y) is the y-component and
p(t, x, y) is the pressure. λ = (λ1, λ2) will both be known and unknown in the following
tests. The solutions used by Raissi et al. are in the set of divergence-free functions,
meaning
ux + vy = 0
(9.6)
with the assumption
u = ψy,
v = −ψx,
(9.7)
for some latent function ψ(t, x, y). The training data used for this section is generated
by Raissi et al.
using the spectral/hp-element solver NekTar [65].
They assumed
”a uniform free stream velocity profile imposed at the left boundary a zero pressure
outflow condition imposed at the right boundary located 25 diameters downstream of
the cylinder, and periodicity for the top and bottom boundaries of the [−15, 25] ×
[−8, 8] domain” [24]. The corresponding steady-state solution is divided into training
and test data and used for the following sections. The problem can be seen in Figure 9.8.

9.5
NSGAN Results
To test the NSGAN on the Navier-Stokes problem from Raissi et al.
[24], a few
modifications were needed. The problem by Raissi et al. does not include p in their
loss function, but only uses v and u for both physics and MSE loss. For the NSGAN
implementation p needs to be included in the loss function for the ”rearrange module”
to be effective. This means that the NSGAN and PINN is not as easily comparable.
Another problem is that in the paper by Wu et al. [50] they use ten hidden layers with
256 neurons per layer. Using this large networks were not feasible for the computational
resources available to us. It should be noted that Raissi et al. only uses nine layers
with 20 neurons in each layer in their PINN. As the implementation of the NSGAN was
done late in the process, not much time for large computations and fine tuning were
left. However, the results still show promise even with little available data and limited
training time.
If Figure 9.9, 9.10 and Table 9.4 the result of training the NSGAN on 2,000
training points can be seen. Using 5,000 training points same as Raissi et al. lead to,
too slow training times. As seen the NSGAN does capture most of the pattern and
the values of the true pressure. The error is quite low for the predicted v and u, (not
plotted) around a few percentage, while the error is around an order of 10 larger for p.
This is likely doe to the setup of the PI-loss function. As seen in Listing 9.11 the PI-loss
function only calculates residual loss directly for v and u, this means the pressure is
only approximated from the MSE loss term in the generator training loop Listing 9.13.
This is clearly a limitation and probably the reason for the much larger p error. With
more time available, we would investigate whether p could be integrated into the PI
residual loss term more optimally. Taken all these considerations intro account, the
results produced by the NSGAN are still an indication that with further improvements
and more computational resources, the NSGAN would be able to generate accurate
results. Whether the NSGAN can produce better results on this NS problem, than the
PINN, is not possible to conclude from this test.

CHAPTER 10
Conclusion
The primary contribution of this thesis is an in-depth investigation, examining the
combination of the deep generative models GANs and VAEs, and scientific machine
learning. First a series of benchmark experiments using simple ”toy problems”, were
conducted on the GAN and VAE. Testing VAE’s and GAN’s ability to learn the
dynamics of differential equations, and potential for synthetic data generation.
The
investigation established that the VAE was more reliable and robust in recreating these
dynamics. However, it still struggled with more complicated and non-linear systems
such as the Lotka-Volterra equations. The GAN was not able to generate satisfactory
results without noise and suffered from common GAN issues such as mode-collapse and
long training times.
To improve these initial results, an extensive literature review was conducted to
investigate the progression of selected generative techniques all the way from their ori-
gins to the current state-of-the-art. From this research, a wide range of physics-informed
techniques were presented. Generally, PINNs showed great potential as grey-box models
i.e. for problems where the governing physics is known and data is collected. VAEs
showed potential for adding physical knowledge to the model, however due to its more
rigid architecture these changes are restrictive and comprehensive to implement. The
literature review revealed the potential of GANs, illuminating the flexibility of the
GAN architecture and the power of using the adversarial training.
Novel proposed
GAN frameworks proved useful for uncertainty quantification, stochastic differential
equations, synthetic data generation and computational fluid dynamics, and handling
noisy data as grey-box models. This literature review was the basis for the decision to
study the PIG/PID-GAN frameworks’ capacity to improve the initial GAN results, and
investigate the abilities of the NSGAN.
Before implementing physics-informed versions of GANs,
an initial implementa-
tion and test of PINNs was carried out, to investigate the inner workings and
constraints of the incorporation of physics into neural networks. These tests found the
PINN framework to be a powerful tool to aid the training of neural networks, in settings
with small quantity of data and for data-driven discovery of differential equations. A
simple experiment displayed that higher order derivatives had lacking accuracy, when
calculated using automatic differentiation, disclosing a potential constraint in PyTorch’s
current automatic differentiation algorithm.

126
10 Conclusion
In order to address some of the encountered problems during the benchmark
GAN tests, the WGAN and WGAN-GP were introduced.
A test of these revealed,
that while both methods did remedy the effect of mode-collapse, neither method
ensured that the GAN could produce satisfactory samples, when subjected to the same
experiments as the standard GAN.
Testing the two physics informed GAN frameworks, the PIG- and PID-GANs.
It
was concluded that these models have promising capacity for adding uncertainty quan-
tification to neural networks, hence improving the interpretability of machine learning.
Additionally, both models were able to handle noisy data well. The PIG/PID-GAN
frameworks were deemed to be unsuitable for synthetic data generation, if the training
data was composed of data from dynamical systems with a wide range of solutions.
The versatility of GANs were further expressed by the PI-(NS)GAN and NSGAN. These
models showed great promise combining the idea of physics-informed loss functions with
the adversarial training process. This was highlighted as the PI-(NS)GAN was able to
robustly and quickly approximate a solution to a damped harmonic oscillator using
little data with high accuracy. In addition, the PI-(NS)GAN had promising capabilities
for data-driven discovery. Testing the NSGAN on data from an incompressible fluid
flow described by the Navier-Stokes equations, the NSGAN was able to predict the
pressure and velocity components and perform data-driven discovery. The accuracy of
these predictions were not great, however with more time and computational resources
available improvements seems likely.
10.1
Future Work
The work presented in this thesis investigates selected state-of-the art methods within
scientific machine learning and deep generative modelling.
While this project has
showcased some of the vast potential of these emerging method, many problems and
limitations still exist. Considering the aim of creating realistic synthetic data, the work
presented does not offer a reliable option for dynamical systems. Therefore, based on
the literature review, the apparent future work would be to follow the methods proposed
by Golany et al. [51] [52]. Since, the authors claim their framework to be versatile and
have proven results incorporating physical domain knowledge in a real-world application.
The PI-(NS)GAN and NSGAN proved to be potential powerful grey-box models.
This was best showcased on the damped harmonic oscillator, leading to the obvious
future work to be the test of more complex systems and further improvements of the
NSGAN results. Furthermore, to examine GANs’ ability to handle noisy data, it would
require us to test this for all the implemented physics-informed models, in order to
