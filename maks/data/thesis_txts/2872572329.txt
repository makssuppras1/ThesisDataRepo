Abstract
This thesis presents the design, modeling, and experimental validation of a high power­
density, low­voltage, high­current multi­phase Series­Capacitor Buck Converter with a 6­
phase coupled inductor, intended as a voltage regulation module pre­stage for data center
applications. The converter is designed to operate from a nominal 48 V input and deliver
2 V at up to 800 A, within a compact form factor compatible with vertical power delivery.
To achieve this, four Series­Capacitor Buck Converter modules operate in parallel, each
comprising six partially interleaved phases, enabling the use of 25 V­rated MOSFETs.
A detailed steady­state analysis is carried out to derive key design equations and iden­
tify operating modes. Core components—including MOSFETs, capacitors, and magnetic
elements—are selected based on figure­of­merit comparisons and electrical loss evalua­
tions. The coupled inductor employs a ladder­type topology that enables near­complete
DC flux cancellation and is modeled using a reluctance­to­resistance transformation to
simplify analysis. Optimized inductance values are validated through magnetostatic sim­
ulations.
Due to the use of series­capacitors, conventional high­side bootstrap methods are unsuit­
able, thus a cascaded bootstrapping scheme is implemented. A 16­layer prototype PCB
is designed with 2 oz/ft2 copper weight and optimized for high­current handling using
paralleled layers. Multi­layer ceramic capacitors (MLCCs) are used exclusively to meet
capacitance, ripple, and current requirements.
Two prototypes are built: one full assembly with four modules for performance testing,
and another single­module build for current verification. Testing confirms a close match
between measured and modeled inductor currents. The measured inductance was ap­
proximately 30 % lower than expected, attributed to manufacturing inaccuracies, which
were supported by simulation. The converter achieved a peak efficiency of 93.06 % at
320 A, and a near full­load efficiency of 90.73 % at 720 A, resulting in a power density of
86.35 W/cm3 (1415.6 W/in3). Following the failure of one module, the remaining three
delivered 600 A at a measured per­module full­load efficiency of 89.81 %, corresponding
to a theoretical maximum power density of 95.8 W/cm3 (1570.4 W/in3).

Introduction
1.1
Background
With the rapid increase in processing power of modern AI processors and high­performance
computing (HPC) applications, the requirements for the power delivery systems must fol­
low suit. While the required supply voltages typically remain low, in the range of 1 V to
2 V, the required current must reach several hundred amperes to maintain the required
power consumption, in some cases upwards of 2 kW for a single system [1].
To supply the required current, Voltage Regulation Modules (VRMs) must be able to de­
liver hundreds of amperes while fitting into small footprints and maintaining high efficiency—
thus achieving high power densities. To comply with modern requirements, as well as to
facilitate the increasingly popular shift to 48 V datacenter supply networks—replacing the
outdated 12 V distribution—the current VRM topologies and methods must be subject to
constant development to further increase efficiency while reducing size.
The workload for AI and HPC applications is not constant and may vary greatly over short
periods of time. Thus, for the converter to maintain a well­regulated output voltage and
avoid instability—even at current changes of hundreds of amperes—it must achieve a
fast transient response, in order to regulate and counter any transients during operation.
This often leads to converters with high closed­loop bandwidths and, consequently, high
switching frequencies—reducing the efficiency of hard­switching converters.
Currently, two methods for converting the 48 V distribution bus to the required Point­of­
Load (PoL) voltage are used: single­stage and multi­stage conversion[2, 3].
In a single­stage PoL converter, the input bus voltage is converted directly to the required
PoL voltage without intermediate steps, whereas the multi­stage solution uses multiple
cascaded converters connected through intermediate bus voltages. For the multi­stage
solution, the first stage—the pre­stage—is often operated in an unregulated manner at a
reduced switching frequency, in order to increase system efficiency. The pre­stage sup­
plies an intermediate bus voltage to a closed­loop, low conversion­ratio, high­frequency,
high­efficiency PoL converter. This way, only the final stage of the converter is tightly
regulated, enabling a fast transient response[4].
For the single­stage solution, the entire converter must operate in a regulated closed­
loop setup while maintaining a high switching frequency and, consequently, a wide control
bandwidth. The elevated switching frequency can lead to increased losses—especially
in hard­switching topologies.
An up­and­coming method for supplying modern processors is by utilizing Vertical Power
Delivery (VPD)[5, 6], in which the VRM is placed vertically below the processor, supplying
the current through shorter paths, by way of direct PCB vias, thus reducing the copper
losses incurred from transporting the current horizontally. However, VPD implementations
lead to stringent size requirements, which must be complied with.
This project aims to design and validate a high­current, high­efficiency unregulated power
converter suitable as a pre­stage for VPD­based multi­stage VRM applications.

1.2
Overview of Thesis Structure
The structure of the thesis is as follows:
• Chapter 2 investigates the series­capacitor buck converter topology, its operating
principles, including a steady­state analysis and derivation of key voltage and cur­
rent design equations.
• Chapter 3 presents the magnetics design, including a modeling methodology based
on reluctance­to­resistance transformation, and verification against simulation.
• Chapter 4 describes the gate driving challenges for the series­capacitor buck con­
verter, and introduces a cascaded bootstrapping method.
• Chapter 5 focuses on the MOSFET selection, based on figure­of­merit selections,
and loss estimations.
• Chapter 6 covers the selection of input, output, and series­capacitors, as well as
the PCB specifications and design.
• Chapter 7 contains verification of the designed motherboard, power converter, and
designed magnetics.
• Chapter 8 concludes the thesis by discussing key challenges encountered, potential
directions for future work, and summarizing the main findings.

1.3
Converter Specifications
To make the switch­mode converter suitable for data center applications, predefined spec­
ifications and constraints have been selected.
To support modern AI processors, the converter must be capable of supplying a substan­
tial current. Therefore, the output current for the converter in this project is set to 800 A.
The converter will not serve as a direct PoL converter but rather as a PoL pre­stage, step­
ping down the input voltage to a constant intermediate voltage that will supply the final
PoL converter. The intermediate voltage is chosen to be 2 V to reduce the voltage stress
on the PoL converter by lowering its required conversion ratio.
The converter will be designed to comply with the increasingly popular 48 V data center
supply bus voltage [7], with a nominal input voltage of 48 V. However, to account for input
voltage variation caused by the battery voltage of uninterruptible power supplies (UPS)
used in data centers, the converter must tolerate an input voltage range of 40 V to 60 V
[7].
The converter will be designed for VPD, where it is placed on the back of the proces­
sor PCB, delivering current vertically through vias. This contrasts with the more common
Lateral Power Delivery, where the converter is located beside the processor. VPD helps
reduce copper losses by minimizing the path length between the converter and the pro­
cessor. To align with VPD requirements, the converter footprint will be limited to 50 mm ×
50 mm, with a maximum height of 6 mm. On top of this, the converter will be designed for
a switching frequency of 400 kHz, in the hopes that the components can be minimized,
without being dominated by the switching losses.
The objective of this project is not to identify the most efficient converter topology through
comparison, but rather to evaluate whether a specific topology, namely the series­capacitor
buck converter, can be optimized and deemed viable for data center applications. This
will involve a comprehensive analysis of the converter, including MOSFET optimization,
capacitor selection, and magnetic component design.
The series­capacitor buck converter is chosen for its ability to reduce MOSFET voltage
stress, thereby enabling the use of smaller MOSFETs. This can potentially reduce the
overall converter volume and increase power density.

Topology
The multi­phase series­capacitor buck converter (SCBC) is made up of n synchronous
buck converters, in which a series­capacitor is inserted in place of the MOSFET inter­
section of a normal buck converter, in all but the last phase. In addition, the adjacent
phases are connected to the source terminal of the high­side MOSFET of the previous
phase, instead of being placed in parallel, as is customary for a traditional multi­phase
buck converter. The schematic for an n­phase SCBC is shown in figure 2.1.

The objective of the series­capacitor is to act as a voltage source for the following phase.
This way, the voltage stress across the MOSFETs will be decreased, allowing for the use
of low­voltage devices, which often exhibit superior characteristics, such as reduced drain­
source conduction resistance (RDS(on)), and overall parasitic charges and capacitance,
which is expected to mitigate various losses.
2.1
Steady­State Analysis
In order to analyze the topology waveforms, it is assumed that the converter has reached
steady­state. For a switch­mode converter, steady­state implies that the currents and
voltages have settled into their final, periodically repeating values, with no start­up or
noise transients present. Therefore, the waveforms and equations will be based on the
steady operating condition of the converter.
2.1.1
Modes of operation
One of the constraints of the SCBC operation is that no adjacent phases must have their
high­side MOSFET enabled at the same time, as this would invalidate the use of the
series­capacitor, thus increasing the voltage stress across the MOSFET, invalidating the
main benefit of the topology. Therefore, the method of operation must be analyzed. When

phase, and a recurring state in which none of the phases are energized.
In Mode II operation, the duty cycle of the converter is too high to allow for sequential
energization, which would force the adjacent phases to be energized at the same time,
thus invalidating the series­capacitor and the topology as a whole. Therefore, some other
order of energization must be used. For this mode, non­sequential switching must be
used, in which non­adjacent phases overlap. During Mode II operation, the converter will
exhibit 2n distinct switching states. Mode II implementation can be seen in Figure 2.2b.
In Mode III operation, the duty cycle is ideally exactly the correct length, which allows for
sequential energization of the phases. However, due to nonidealities of real circuits, the
waveforms will exhibit rise and fall times, thus this mode of operation cannot realistically
be achieved. The mode of operation would lead to n distinct switching states, one for
each of the phases, as the ’dead­time’ from Mode I has been eliminated. An examples of

2.2
Derivation of Design Equations
Throughout the derivations of the design equations, the MOSFETs will be assumed ideal
and will thus be represented as a short circuit when enabled and an open circuit when
disabled. As the AC current components in the converter are highly dependent on the
inductor topology, they will be assumed to be zero in the current chapter. Thus, both the
inductors and capacitors are assumed to be near­infinite in size. The series­capacitors
will therefore act as perfect DC voltage sources, and the inductors as perfect DC current
sources. As the inductor current is estimated to be pure DC, the RMS current estimations
for the MOSFETs and capacitors can be performed using simple piece­wise analysis, in
which the current waveforms are divided into smaller elements and analyzed. The method
is further described in [8].

3
Magnetics Design
For this project, the output inductors of all phases will be combined into a coupled inductor
structure with a single common core. The use of a coupled inductor, compared to that of
discrete inductors, has a range of benefits, which, depending on the utilized magnetics
topology, exhibits varying characteristics, such as the possibility of reduced current ripple,
reduced overall magnetics footprint, partial­to­near­full DC flux cancellation, and reduced
required number of turns, due to the addition of the reluctance of neighboring phases.
The magnetics will be implemented in a planar fashion, in which the core is inserted into
the PCB, and copper tracks will make up the core windings. This will allow for a reduction
in overall converter height, without compromising on the height of the magnetics.
3.1
Magnetics Modeling Methodology
Coupled inductors can be designed based on a broad range of magnetic geometrical
topologies, which all exhibit different characteristics. A common topology utilized for multi­
phased topologies is the ’ladder’ structure [9, 10], as shown in figure 3.1. The phase
windings for the converter will be wound around the vertical ’rungs’ of the core, which are
connected using the horizontal top­ and bottom­plates. This topology is popular due to its
lack of a common flux return path, which, assuming all phases will experience the same
DC current component, can lead to a high degree of DC flux cancellation, thus allowing
for reduction of size. Due to the ’2D’ structure of the core, the modeling process is greatly
simplified, compared to ’3D’ core structures, such as matrix topologies, in which the rungs
are placed in a 3D grid. The ladder topology also allows for an intuitive placement of the
surrounding converter components, such as the capacitors and MOSFETs, thus reducing
the complexity of the converter layout process.
Figure 3.1: Coupled inductor ladder core structure.
The magnetics design method utilized in this project will be based on the commonly used
resistance­reluctance model[11, 12], in which the magnetics are piece­wise transformed
from magnetic reluctance into electrical resistance. The windings in the magnetics model
will be converted into voltage sources in the electrical model conversion, with a voltage of
VWx = NxIx
(3.1)
in which VWx is the voltage of winding x, when transformed to the electrical domain, Nx is
the number of turns of winding x, and Ix is the current through winding x. In this project,
the number of turns will be the same for all phases, thus Nx will be written as N.

By comparing the scenarios in which the currents are balanced, but the permeability dif­
fers, in figures 3.5 and 3.7, it can be seen that a higher permeability leads to reduced
current ripple. This is the expected behavior, as the increased permeability leads to an
increase in inductance, thus reducing the change in current. However, by analyzing the
flux densities for the two scenarios, it can be seen that the permeability, and thus the
inductance, has virtually no impact on the flux density.
By introducing a current imbalance between the phases, it’s clear to see that the DC flux
density is heavily influenced, but the peak­to­peak ripple remains the same. However, by
comparing the flux density between the two scenarios in which the current is imbalanced
and the permeability differs, given in figures 3.6 and 3.8, it can be seen that the reduced
permeability leads to less DC flux spread, but will have an increased current ripple.
From this, it can be concluded that materials with high permeability will have reduced
current ripple compared to materials with lower permeability, while the flux remains the
same, as long as the currents are balanced. If the current isn’t balanced between the
phases, a lower permeability material will be better suited, as it reduces the DC flux density
spread. The increased current ripple will increase the AC copper loss of the core, while
increased flux density can cause the core to become saturated. Thus, to mitigate the
chances of saturation, low permeability materials will be favored for this project.

Core loss
The second loss mechanism of the converter magnetics is the core loss, which in turn can
be divided into two loss types, namely magnetic hysteresis loss and eddy current loss,
both of which are an effect of exposing the magnetic material to a time­varying magnetic
field.
The magnetic hysteresis loss occurs as a result of B­H hysteresis of magnetic materials,
in which energy is expended to realign the magnetic domains when the magnetic field
changes.
The eddy current loss occurs due to circulating currents being induced into the magnetic
material, which in turn leads to resistive loss due to the conductivity of the material in use.
A commonly used method for estimating the core loss is by way of Steinmetz’s equation,
which estimates the volume­relative core loss based on the frequency, peak magnetic flux
density, and three Steinmetz coefficients, k, α, and β. Steinmetz’s equation is given as
3.20.

3.4.2
Core design constraints
For the design of the coupled inductor, some constraints are set. These include that the
PCB will be split into four equal squares, each with a size of 25 mm × 25 mm. The number
of turns for each phase is pre­selected to be one, so as to reduce the copper loss. On top
of this, at most one third of the PCB will be dedicated to the magnetics, the rest of which
will be for the capacitors and MOSFETs. Lastly, the core will be produced as a 6­legged
’E­core’, in which two of such will make up the magnetics for a single module.
The PCB will have to be produced with a maximum thickness of 2.8 mm, which will allow
for the ’rung’ of an ’E­core’ to have a set length of 1.5 mm, to allow for a slight margin. To
be within the total height constraint of 6 mm, the top and bottom plate thickness will also
be limited to 1.5 mm. The PCB will be produced with 16 layers, each with a copper weight
of 2 oz/ft2. The windings of the phases will be interleaved, leaving 8 layers of copper for
each phase.
To allow for production tolerances, the copper width will be reduced by 0.5 mm, to allow
for 0.2 mm between the copper and core cutout, and 0.05 mm between the core and the
PCB edge.
Based on these constraints, the parameters which can be optimized are the width and
length of the core rungs. During the optimization, the main focus will be on the copper loss,
and the minimum and maximum flux density, instead of the core loss. Even though higher
core loss will decrease the efficiency of the converter, the peak flux density is deemed
more important, as flux density saturation can inhibit the operation of the converter.
A sweep of the width and length of the core rungs is performed. At each step in the sweep,
the model estimation from section 3.1 is performed, and the DC and AC copper losses
from section 3.3 is estimated. The length sweep was performed with a range between
0.5 mm and 8.5 mm, whereas the width was swept between 0.5 mm and 3.6 mm. This
sweep was performed at an input voltage of 40 V, 48 V, and 60 V, to make sure that the
designed core exhibits acceptable characteristics in the full input voltage range. On top
of this, the frequency used for the sweep is 400 kHz, as this is the expected converter
switching frequency.

4
Gate drivers
To turn on a MOSFET, the voltage difference between the source and gate terminals must
exceed a set threshold voltage to allow current to flow. Although MOSFETs are voltage­
driven, they often exhibit large parasitic capacitances, which must be charged and dis­
charged to turn the MOSFET on and off. For very low­frequency systems, in which the
MOSFET is turned on and off at most a few times every second, a digital signal pro­
cessor (DSP) or signal generator might supply enough current to adequately operate the
MOSFET. However, as the MOSFET switching frequency increases, the few milliamperes
provided by the DSP become insufficient to charge and discharge the gate­source capac­
itance within each switching period.
Therefore, an intermediate driving mechanism must be introduced between the DSP and
the MOSFET to enable faster gate charging and discharging, and thus, faster switching.
For this, the gate driver circuit is introduced. The gate driver is commonly made up of a
level­shifting circuit, which is driven by the DSP, and an external power supply. As the
output of the level shifter is driven by the external power supply, it will be able to supply a
higher current.
The level shifter will be used to drive a complementary N­ and P­channel MOSFET half­
bridge, the output of which will drive the MOSFET for the converter. This way, when the
DSP signal is high, the external supply of the gate driver will be connected to the gate of
the MOSFET, allowing for a high­current source. When the DSP signal is low, the gate will
be connected to ground through the gate driver, allowing for a high­current sink. A sketch
of the gate driving circuit can be seen in Figure 4.1, which includes a current­limiting
resistor ’Rext’.

High­side bootstrapping

the high­side MOSFET’s source terminal experiences a voltage offset during part of the
switching cycle.

A common way to avoid this problem is to use a bootstrapping circuit in conjunction with
the gate driver, as shown in figure 4.2. The bootstrapping circuit consists of a diode
connected between the external power supply and a capacitor, with the other terminal
of the capacitor connected to the switching node of the half­bridge. When the low­side

4.3
Component selection
When selecting the gate driver for the project, there are numerous parameters that should
be accounted for. This includes: voltage rating, maximum sink and source current, num­
ber of channels, isolation, propagation delay, allowed switching frequency, rise and fall
time into a known load, and many more.
For this project, the rise and fall time will be used as a basis for selecting the gate driver.
It has been decided that the number of channels is irrelevant, and multiple gate drivers
can be used if required. On top of this, the gate driver must have isolation between
the input and output sides in order to be used in a cascaded bootstrapping setup with­
out the need for multiple external isolated power supplies. Based on these criteria, the
’MAX22702EASA+’ gate driver from Analog Devices has been selected. This selection
was mainly based on the very low rise and fall times of 3.6 ns and 1.8 ns respectively, into
a 200 pF load. On top of this, the gate driver is isolated and has a wide input voltage range
on the isolated converter side, which makes it a good candidate for the cascaded boot­
strapping setup, in which the supply voltages decrease for each phase. It also features
low high­ and low­side resistance, and thereby high current source and sink capabilities.
The one disadvantage of the gate driver is the fact that it only has a single channel, which
means that two drivers are required for each phase.
The bootstrapping/supply capacitor has been selected in accordance with the gate driver
datasheet to be a 22 µF and 1 µF capacitor in parallel. For the bootstrapping diode, the
’VSS8D3M10’ Schottky diode from Vishay has been selected due to its low forward volt­
age of 0.58 V, high current capability of 3 A, and high reverse blocking voltage of 100 V.
The low forward voltage is important to reduce the bootstrap voltage difference between
the first and last phase. It must be able to handle a high current, as the current to supply
all the high­side gate drivers and bootstrapping setups will flow through the diode of the
last phase. Finally, the diode must be able to block nearly the full converter input voltage
and must therefore be able to block a high reverse voltage.

Semiconductors
The semiconductor is the heart of any switch­mode converter, regardless of topology, as
switching cannot occur without it. Therefore, selecting the semiconductors, in this case
the MOSFETs, is a critical task to avoid under­ or over­sizing, minimize losses, and meet
size constraints. In this section, the loss mechanisms of the MOSFETs are analyzed and
used to select the most suitable candidate for the project.
5.1
MOSFET loss estimation
In order to select the best MOSFET for the project, their losses must be estimated and
compared for various models. In this project, the gate driver losses will not be included
as converter losses, but will be seen as external. Thus, the MOSFETs will exhibit three
types of power loss, namely switching loss, conduction loss, and output capacitor loss.
Switching losses
The switching losses occur during the turn­on and turn­off of the MOSFETs, in which
the gate capacitance must be charged/decharged. During this time, the MOSFET will
experience both the bus voltage across it, as well as the peak conduction current through
it, simultaneously, thus experiencing a power loss for a short period of time. By increasing
the switching frequency of the converter, more turn­on and turn­off events will occur in the
same time frame, which in turn will increase the switching losses of the converter. The
estimation of the switching losses can be performed by way of equation 5.1, in which
VDS is the ’blocking’ voltage which the MOSFET experiences while off, ISW is the current
through the MOSFET at the switching event, ton and toff are the rise and fall time of
the MOSFET, which leads to the voltage and current overlap, and fsw is the MOSFET
switching frequency.

PCB design
In this project, two PCBs will be designed: one for the power converter and one for the
DSP and signal routing. To reduce the power loss between the converter output and the
output power connectors, they will be placed on the same PCB as the converter, whereas
to decrease the size of the power converter PCB, the gate drivers will be placed on a
’motherboard’, which the power board will be connected to.
6.1
Converter PCB
As mentioned in Section 3.4.2, the PCB specifications are pre­defined as: 16 layers,
2 oz/ft2 inner and outer layer copper weight. On top of this, the vias will be copper filled
to allow for increased current­carrying capability. Each of the four SCBC modules will be
allotted an area of 25 mm × 25 mm. The converter size constraint of 50 mm × 50 mm
× 6 mm is only meant for the power stage, meaning that any MOSFET gate drivers and
input/output power connectors won’t count towards the size constraint.
6.1.1
Series­capacitor selection
The series­capacitor plays a crucial role in the SCBC operation and must thus be selected
carefully. To avoid large deviation from the idealized circuit analysis performed in Section
2, the maximum allowed series­capacitor voltage ripple is selected to be 15 % of the DC
voltage across them when running the converter at the nominal input voltage of 48 V. To
decrease the size of the series­capacitors, multi­layer ceramic capacitors (MLCC) will be
used due to their high energy density and small footprint, which will allow for reduced
voltage ripple without compromising the power density of the converter. One major dis­
advantage of MLCCs is the significant loss of capacitance that occurs during increased
DC voltage bias, which must be taken into consideration when selecting the capacitors.

allel, thus increasing the capacitance. Due to the high per­phase current, multiple parallel
series­capacitors are already expected in order to spread the per­capacitor current, thus
decreasing the severity of the capacitance loss.

6.1.2
Input­ and output­capacitor selection
Due to the expected length between the designed converter and the external power supply
supplying it, the wires will have an increased undesirable inductance, which can affect
both the current and voltage waveforms, increasing noise in the system. To counter this
and create an input current buffer, input capacitors are placed as close to the input of the
converter as possible, to negate the undesired inductance and decrease the AC current
loops. The input capacitors must be able to handle the full input voltage range and are thus
selected to be the same kind as the phase 1 series­capacitor ’C2012X5R2A475K125AC’,
as it can handle a DC voltage up to 100 V, while having a compact 0805 footprint. Like that
of the series­capacitor of phase 1, eight capacitors will be placed in parallel to increase
the capacitance, reduce the ESR, and decrease the per­capacitor current stress.
On the output of the converter, capacitors are also needed in order to decrease/remove
the voltage ripple, which will occur due to the triangular current shape. To decrease the
ripple, the capacitance should be as high as possible, while the ESR should be as low as
possible. A low ESR also reduces the capacitor conduction loss. As the output voltage
will be a near­constant 2 V, the output capacitors will be selected to have a voltage rating
of 6.3 V, so as to allow for the use of 0603 capacitors. No capacitance and ESR targets
are set, but as many 0603 capacitors as possible will be placed in parallel on the output
of the converter. From the finished design, it was found that it will be possible to fit 40
0603 capacitors side­by­side in parallel for each module. For the output capacitor, the
KGM15CR50J226xx has been selected, due to its high capacitance of 22 µF, small 0603
footprint, and relatively low ESR of 2.42 m Ω.
6.1.3
Converter layout
Due to the ’straight’ geometry of the ladder core, the layout of each module of the PCB will
be divided into three sub­groups: the inductor; the capacitors; and the MOSFETs, so as to
effectively use the space. To minimize the module current imbalance, each module will be
routed and designed exactly the same, but mirrored, in order to minimize the impedance
variance.
The module will be designed with the MOSFETs towards the edge of the module, in order
to ease the placement and decrease the length of the gate signals between the MOS­
FETs and the drivers. The inductors will be placed close together near the center of the
converter, and the capacitors between the inductor and MOSFETs. To better utilize the
size constraints, capacitors and MOSFETs will be placed on both sides of the PCB.

6.2
Motherboard PCB
The motherboard PCB will be used to route the signals from the DSP, through the gate
drivers, into the MOSFET gate terminals. The motherboard will be designed based on
the pin header positions of the converter PCB, so it can slot into it without the need for
soldering. This will allow for easy separation of the board in case component replacement
is required. As no power from the converter PCB will be routed on the motherboard, the
stackup and copper weight aren’t important. The PCB will be made up of four layers, in
which the outer two will be for the signal routing, and the inner two for pure ground planes,
in the hopes of reducing noise and loop size, as well as increasing the ground coupling of
the DSP signals. The gate driver circuitry is designed to be as compact as possible, while
utilizing both sides of the PCB for components. The gate driver IC itself will be placed on
the backside of the PCB so as to avoid thermal conflict with the converter components, as
the gate driver is expected to increase somewhat in temperature. Two voltage connectors
are placed on the motherboard: one 5 V for the input side of the gate driver, and a 12 V
– 15 V for the output and bootstrapping setup side of the gate driver. The schematic for

losses dominate at higher loads. This is caused by the decrease in flux density—and

For the 350 kHz case, the core loss is expected to dominate throughout the whole current
sweep, thus limiting the peak efficiency.

the design frequency of 400 kHz, which leads to the best full­load efficiency but reduced
low­power efficiency, and 450 kHz, which has slightly lower full­load efficiency but better
low­power efficiency.

efficiency is expected to remain constant with the change from four to three modules.
However, a noticeable drop in efficiency was observed with the three­module converter,
compared to that of the four­module converter. By comparing the two measurements at
the same frequency of 450 kHz, the three­module converter has a per­module loss which

8
Discussion and Conclusion
8.1
Challenges and Practical Considerations
Throughout the project, several practical challenges arose. The first of which was the
unavailability of the desired MOSFET, which led to the use of a non­ideal part. On top of
this, the core and PCB tolerances were worse than expected, which led to the need for
filing the PCB and core edges to make them fit.
Due to time constraints, the core was designed and ordered before the PCBs were. With
the cores in production, it was found that the PCB manufacturers were unable to meet the
target specifications of a PCB with a thickness less than 2.8 mm, and the original desired
copper weight of 3 oz/ft2. Thus, the cores could not be re­optimized, and the ordered core
was used. With a copper weight decrease from 3 oz/ft2 to 2 oz/ft2, a significant increase
in resistance, and thereby copper loss, is observed.
On top of this, in the very last stage of converter testing, it was found that the PCB man­
ufacturer had made a mistake, and the vias weren’t copper filled, as had been expected
throughout the design and testing phases. This, like the copper weight, increases the
resistance and the losses. Without the copper fill, the number of vias is greatly under­
sized for the amount of current passing through them. It is expected that this is part of the
reason for the high increase in temperature in the copper surrounding the MOSFETs, as
the via density is lower than other parts of the converter. The last major challenge in the
project was measuring and validating the current through the inductor phases. Without a
proper inductor test­bench, the measurements had to be performed on the converter PCB.
Due to the high component density, any modifications were hard to make and could intro­
duce parasitics, which could skew the results from what is observed in the performance
converter PCB.
8.2
Future Work
With a good foundation for the multi­module, multi­phase series­capacitor buck converter,
further improvements could be made to the converter.
The first point of improvement is the use of the originally selected MOSFETs to increase
the semiconductor performance.
A full­load condition test, on a converter which has all four modules working, should be
performed and validated.
On top of this, the magnetics could be re­optimized to account for the reduced copper
weight. Other core materials could be explored to better optimize and decrease the ob­
served core loss. New magnetic geometries could be investigated, such as combining
the four coupled inductors into a single core with 24 legs. This could open the possibility
for ensured current sharing between the modules, depending on the magnetics topology.
Lastly, a more accurate in­depth analysis of the converter copper track loss could be
performed, resulting in better component placement, for increased performance.

8.3
Conclusion
The objective of this project was to design, produce, and validate a high current, low volt­
age, high power­density switch­mode converter, based on a six­phase series­capacitor
buck topology with a common coupled inductor, for use as a PoL pre­stage. Throughout
the thesis, the SCBC topology steady­state operation was analyzed, from which voltage
and current design equations could be derived. The magnetics for each module was com­
bined into a single coupled inductor, which was analyzed and optimized based on size,
flux density, and copper loss. The MOSFETs used were selected based on a FOM analy­
sis, after which loss estimations were performed to select the final candidate. Due to the
nature of the topology, ordinary bootstrapping could not be utilized for high­side switching,
resulting in the use of a cascaded bootstrapping system. The converter was manufac­
tured as a two­PCB system, of which one was a motherboard containing gate drivers and
bootstrapping circuitry, whereas the other was the converter PCB, containing the MOS­
FETs, capacitors, and magnetics. Tests and measurements were performed, resulting in
waveforms which match the theoretical estimations and values. Efficiency tests were per­
formed at varying switching frequencies and load currents. Although the converter could
not be tested at the full output current of 800 A, thus achieving full­load conditions, it was
tested at currents upwards of 720 A, while maintaining an efficiency of 90.73 %, resulting
in a power density of 86.35 W/cm3 (1415.6 W/in3). With three modules operating, the
converter was able to reach an output current of 600 A, resulting in per­module full­load
conditions, at which the converter reached a peak efficiency of 89.81 %, resulting in a
theoretical four­module full­load power density of 95.8 W/cm3 (1570.4 W/in3).
During testing, a rise in temperature upwards of 120 °C was observed. This is expected
to be in part caused by the PCB manufacturing mistakes, as well as the poor, uneven
airflow caused by the utilized power connectors.
The multi­module, multi­phase SCBC topology with coupled inductors has proven to be
a viable solution for use as a high­current VRM pre­stage, and although not tested, is
expected to also work efficiently as a direct single­stage PoL converter. The converter
operated as theoretically expected while maintaining a high degree of efficiency, thus
marking the project as a major success.
