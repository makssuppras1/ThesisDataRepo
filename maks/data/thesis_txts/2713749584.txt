Abstract
High quality randomness is valuable and constantly sought after in modern society. It
finds ubiquitous applications ranging from cryptography to fundamental sciences [1, 2].
Random numbers shall be uniform as well as unpredictable to make no naive assump-
tions on the adversary’s capability. A quantum random number generator (QRNG) gen-
erates truly random numbers based on a quantum system’s measurements that are well-
defined inherently random processes [3]. One of such systems of interest is a vacuum
state, an inherently pure state that cannot be correlated to anything in the universe and it
is thus trustworthy against controls by potential attackers [4]. With homodyne detection,
amplitudes of vacuum fluctuation with a Gaussian distribution are accessed and trans-
formed to uniformly distributed random numbers. Previously, it has been demonstrated
that such a QRNG can achieve 2.9 Gbps [2], which is firmly among the fastest to date.
Such a high-speed generation is ideal in cloud or for quantum key distribution.
In this thesis, I have implemented a QRNG with an even simpler setup - a vertical cav-
ity surface-emitting laser (VCSEL) and a silicon-pin photodiode, both are off-the-shelf
components thanks to a recently developed protocol with a single photodiode [5]. The
non-unity photodiode quantum efficiency is equivalent to a beamsplitter with a vacuum
input which is harnessed as the entropy source of this QRNG. The protocol is accompa-
nied with a robust security analysis against quantum side information that has removed
numerous conventional assumptions. It encompasses non-Gaussian noise processes and
temporal correlations between samples due to a realistic photodetection bandwidth.
I have built a device-dependent QRNG using the 16 bit analog to digital converter (ADC)
with a ready field-programmable gate array (FPGA) for readouts. The stochastic model,
derived from the QRNG’s physical model, determines the min-entropy of the measure-
ments, which in turn decides how many bits from 16 bits are truly random so to extract
randomness. System characterization is crucial in the QRNG’s certification as it bounds
the three parameters determining the stochastic model. Having drawn careful corre-
spondence between experiment and theory, I have characterized the detector to add des-
ignated electronics, studied the saturation behavior of the photodiode to achieve truthful
measurements for determining the conditional variance of the vacuum and carefully de-
termined the optimal gain of the amplifier to avoid raw samples falling outside of the
ADC range. The highlight of my work is on revealing how much true randomness we
shall be able to extract from this QRNG. I have increased the final min-entropy to 4.71
bit per 16-bit samples, which is an increase by 34% and will result a generation rate in
Gbps regime with future online extraction and application to QKD. I have implemented a
method to characterize the nonlinearity of the ADCs based on IEEE standard that worth
looking for a new quantization method to cut min-entropy reduction due to ADC digiti-
zation error. This work is also one of the first examples of VCSEL technology employed
for multi-Gbps real-time randomness generation.

Introduction
Random numbers are crucial and ubiquitous in a modern world built upon a massive
computational and communications infrastructure. However, true and verifiable ran-
domness is not easy to come by [6, 7, 1].
Arithmetically generated random numbers can appear with the statistics of a uniform
distribution. Those pseudo random number generators (PRNG) are useful. For example,
a random number generator based on XOR shift is popular with Monte Carlo simulations
with over 200 million numbers per second generated[8]. However, such random num-
bers are not feasible for cryptography. It has been shown that quantum key distribution
(QKD) is not immune to attacks if the measurement bases and the states are not truly ran-
dom [3]. Random numbers shall be uniform as well as unpredictable to avoid relying on
assumptions on the adversary’s capability. Thus people have resorted to quantum sys-
tems, which are known as inherently probabilistic. Randomness from a quantum system
differs from a classical system in that even a quantum system with complete description
can still stay probabilistic - it is impossible to deterministically predict the outcomes of
all measurements [9]. Further, there is no hidden variable that controls the generation of
randomness according to Bell’s inequality [10].
A quantum random number generator (QRNG) generates truly random numbers based
on a quantum system’s measurements that are well-defined inherently random processes
[3]. Vacuum states are of particular interest. Abundantly supplied, a vacuum state is
an inherently pure state that cannot be correlated to anything in the universe [4]. It
sounds ideal until imperfection in the physical realization is introduced. Side informa-
tion that can be exploited by attackers arises from both quantum or classical correlations
are caused by such imperfection. To lower bound the reduction of the true randomness
considering such side information depends on the assumptions made on the physical
devices. This gives rise to a category of QRNGs named device-dependent QRNGs [11].
Homodyne measurements of vacuum fluctuations are the forerunners in this category
[5]. Besides the security and unpredictability of the random numbers, the generation rate
is of the additional interest for it often imposes a bottleneck for applications built upon
a random number generator. Previously, it has been demonstrated that such a QRNG
can achieve 2.9 Gbps [2], which is firmly among the fastest to date. Such a high-speed
generation is desired for QKD as well as cloud applications.
Within QRNG realizations, there are both Discrete-Variable (DV) and Continuous-Variable
(CV) domains. The outcomes of a measurement determines if it is DV or CV. There are
in general two stages in realizing a quantum random number generator (QRNG), utiliz-
ing a quantum entropy source and postprocessing. In the first stage, a physical setup is

prepared and measured to generate a raw bit stream. Such a bit stream will enter the
postprocessing stage, where randomness extraction is performed, to eventually generate
trustworthy random numbers. During my thesis, with a CV protocol, exploring vacuum
fluctuation as an entropy source with quadrature measurement. An amplitude quadra-
ture measurement projects a quantum state onto a continuous quadrature amplitude
eigenstate |q⟩with an eigenvalue as the measurement outcome. In this specific work,
such a quantum state is a vacuum state and the outcome is a Gaussian distributed ran-
dom variable. Let Ψ(q) be the wave function that represent the vacuum state, q denotes
the amplitude quadrature, the probability density |Ψ(q)|2 is then Gaussian distributed.
Such a continuous distribution is discretized with an analog-to-digital converter (ADC)
into N consecutive integer numbers for the purpose of a random number generator.
The setup of this QRNG is simple and commercially favorable - a vertical cavity surface-
emitting laser (VCSEL) and a single silicon-pin photodiode, both are off-the-shelf compo-
nents. A composed 16 bit analog to digital converter (ADC) with a ready high through-
put field-programmable gate array (FPGA) are used for reading out. The continuous
measurement is read out as the alternating current (AC) and sampled by the 16-bin
ADC at a frequency of 1 GHz. Here, we have Gaussian distributed random numbers
Xt ∈[−215, 215]. The non-unity photodiode quantum efficiency can be modelled as a
beamsplitter with a vacuum input. This vacuum state is accessed and utilized as the en-
tropy source of this QRNG. The protocol is accompanied with a robust security analysis
against classical as well as quantum side information contributed by excess noise. It en-
compasses non-Gaussian noise processes for both laser and detector noise processes. It
also includes the realistic scenario that photodetection with a finite bandwidth creates
temporal correlations between samples. Therefore, no filters have to be implemented to
attempt the removal of temporal correlations to fit into an ideal case.
The stochastic model, derived from the QRNG’s physical model, determines the min-
entropy of the measurements, which in turn decides how many bits from 16 bits are truly
random so to extract randomness. System characterization is crucial in the QRNG’s certi-
fication as it bounds the three parameters determining the stochastic model. As a starting
point, I have studied the protocol and the method to arrive at the min-entropy to draw
the correspondence to accessible physical quantities in the experiments. The conditions
implied by crucial steps in the security analysis specifies experimental apparatus includ-
ing the sampling rate and units of data readout as well as signal processing toolkits, for
instance, the parameters used for a estimation of power spectrum density (PSD). I have
characterized the detector to add designated electronics, studied the saturation behavior
of the photodiode to achieve truthful measurements for better determining the total sys-
tem gain as well as carefully tune the optimal gain of the amplifier to avoid raw samples
falling outside of the ADC range. I have increased the final min-entropy to 4.71 bit per

Chapter 3 gives the prediction of the detectable electronic signal transformed from opti-

Background of quantum mechanics
In this section, the aspects of quantum mechanics that are closely related with the quan-
tum random number generator are presented. In particular, I have described how phys-
ical quantities and measurements are represented in quantum mechanical formalism.
Measurable physical quantities can be defined by a Hermitian operator with real eigen-
values. With such introduction, I have quantized a simple harmonic oscillator, which is
to be extended to a quantized electromagnetic field in the following section.
The formulation and presentation on this topic are inspired by the following [12, 13, 14].

Theoretical background of the QRNG
This whole section include short introductions to quantum optics and informatics to pre-
pare a theoretical background on a continuous-variable quantum random number gener-

3.1.2
Gaussian states and their statistical properties
A quantum system is continuous-variable if it has an infinite-dimensional Hilbert space
described by observables with continuous spectrum of eigenvalues, or, eigenspectra. A
quantized electromagnetic field is a prime example. Gaussian states are continuous-
variable states that have representations in terms of Gaussian functions [21].
An ideal laser shall generate an optical field described by coherent states. Stimulated
emission coined the name for the phenomena that an incoming photon of a specific fre-
quency interacts with an electron in its excited state that causes the electron’s drop to a
lower energy level and the freed energy transfer to the electromagnetic field for creating
a new photon. This is the working principle of a laser which has laid the foundation for
modern world infrastructure including optical communications and many more. Since
the laser was made available, it has as well showcased phenomena that urged explana-
tions beyond classical description. One of such examples will be evident when a laser
field experiences photon counting.

3.4.1
From discrete random variables to stochastic processes
The notion of measuring information was introduced by Shannon in the late twentieth

3.5
A QRNG protocol with a single photodiode
A protocol to generate quantum random numbers from measuring amplitude of vacuum
fluctuation is developed, based on the described theory background, which states as fol-
lows: a laser light goes through a beam splitter and is detected by a single photodetector.
The measurement outcomes are transformed to uniformly distributed random numbers
by a randomness extraction procedure whose parameters were to be determined before-
hand during system characterization.
This protocol is mainly developed by Tobias Gehring and complemented by a security
proof by Cosmo Lupo. The security proof removes the accustomed requirements on the
involved noise processes to be Gaussian and stays robust against even quantum side in-
formation. Furthermore, instead of having to physically remove correlations between
consecutive samples with designated filters to fit in an i.i.d. analysis, temporal correla-
tions between samples imposed by a realistic detection bandwidth are taken into account
throughout the security analysis.
An another novelty that out stands is that this protocol takes the advantage of the loss
during the photodetection process as a quantum resource. The vacuum to access is no
other than the non-unity photodetection quantum efficiency η < 1 equivalent to the
beamsplitter model.
Since a vacuum state is symmetric with respect to all reference
phases in a homodyne detection picture, a single photodetector is enough to measure
its amplitude quadrature. Traditionally, QRNGs with vacuum fluctuation quadrature
measurements are based on the balanced homodyne scheme, whereas the beam splitter
is 50 : 50 and each output port of the beam splitter is detected by one of a pair of identical
photodetectors [2][4]. The two electrical outputs is subtracted and the resultant electri-

cal signal is ideally free of classical noise and proportional to a quadrature of the vacuum
state. Having successfully demonstrated verifiable randomness on such a simple entropy
source, however, maintaining the balancing can be a challenge and cancelling all classi-
cal noise usually remains in an ideal realm. In addition, all the efforts to find or make an
exact 50 : 50 intensity beam splitter are spared, since the beam splitter required by our
protocol is merely virtual.
Not only does one have the freedom to choose an arbitrary photodiode (as long as it is not

6
Conclusion
During my thesis, I have implemented a quantum random number generator (QRNG)
based on an amplitude quadrature measurement on the vacuum state using a single
photodiode. The setup consists mainly of two off-the-shelf components: a vertical-cavity
surface-emitting laser (VCSEL) and a photodiode. The readout of the vacuum fluctuation
measurement is completed by a programmed, high throughput field programmable gate
array (FPGA) consisting of ADCs with 16 bits. I have performed system characterization
to determine the total system gain, or conditional variance of the vacuum in ADC units,
the fraction of measurements outside of an empirical ADC range as well as the ADC dig-
itization errors. According to the min-entropy derived from a robust security analysis
that this QRNG is based on, I will be able to extract 4.7 uniformly random bits from the
16 bits, which implies a generation rate within Gbps realm.
In the near future, I would like to further work on how to tightly bound the characteri-
zation of the ADC nonlinearity. The current method that I am using is a bit paranoid, for
it has combined the ADC nonlinear imperfection together with the noise. It will signifi-
cantly improve the generation rate if I will be able to reduce the min-entropy loss due to
the characterization of ADC digitization errors. On top of that, I would like to use this
QRNG for the application of a quantum key distribution. The setup of this QRNG is quite
simple and economical, which may have the potential to be industrially integrated.
