Abstract
Android smartphone, developed by Google, has become a vital part of the
daily routine. Today, there are numerous available applications in the oﬃcial
and alternative marketplaces. Although there are many security mechanisms
to scan and ﬁlter these marketplaces for malicious applications, malware still
reach the device of the end-user. In this project, we introduce the SafeDroid
v2.0 framework.
SafeDroid v2.0 is a ﬂexible, robust, iterable and versa-
tile solution for statically analyzing Android applications. The main goal
of our framework is the automated production of fully suﬃcient prediction
and classiﬁcation models in terms of maximum accuracy scores and mini-
mum negative errors. SafeDroid v2.0 oﬀers optimized features and enhanced
functionality, compared with the ﬁrst version of the framework. There are
3 main architectural components in the perimeter of the framework: the
feature extraction, the data analysis and feature vector creation as well as
the classiﬁcation and model training module. Outside this perimeter, the
framework provides a reporting server and an Android application for the
testing of the produced models in real-life scenarios.
The update feature
enhance its functionality, while the parameterization ability of the execution
allows the use of the framework for diﬀerent scenarios. Our results show that
SafeDroid v2.0 produces highly accurate scores even on highly unbalanced
dataset inputs.

Introduction
The concept of mobile telecommunication is a thought that has been around
for almost a century. The ﬁrst trials, which are mapped around the begin-
ning of the 20th century, made clear the need for wireless communication.
The ﬁrst successful commercial use of mobile telephony took place in 1926
and it was provided as an extra service to the ﬁrst class passengers of the
German railway tracks, in the route between Berlin and Hamburg. Soon
after and by the outburst of 2nd World War, the opposite sides strived to
improve the technology of mobile communication, as the headquarters had
to communicate with the battle front.
As years passed, scientist community started to experiment on the idea
of mobility. In 1974 engineers Bell Labs proposed Cellular Concept. This
idea was based on the use of hexagonal cells for mobile phones on vehicles.
Two decades later and after many experiments and modiﬁcations, Porter[8]
proposed that the cell towers use the now-familiar directional antennas to
reduce interference and increase channel reuse as well as methods to reduce
wasted channel time.

History Of Android
The birth of the idea of Android devices is the product of two components.
The ﬁrst one is the evolvement of technology. Although the idea of powerful
portable devices that would be easy to carry around pre-existed, the feasibil-
ity of such development was conﬁned. The technological advancements of our
era made smart phones a dream-come-true for all. The second component
is the modern lifestyle. The society has turned to a model where everyday
life is spinning at high speeds, in a reincarnation of Benjamin Franklin’s
proverb “time is money”. This pace dictates faster responses to the emails,
pre-arrangement of one’s routine and constantly keeping up with the social
and personal life. By the time Android was introduced to the public, all
these needs were only possible to be accomplished through personal comput-
ers.Today, the proper use of the technological assets made possible to have
tiny super computer in our pockets.
Smartphones breakthrough was the combination of diﬀerent technologies
in a small machine, able to carry it around. As an ascendance of the mobile
phones, it oﬀers the ability of making phone calls and sending text messages
to other users around the world. The employment of more powerful CPUs
allowed not only the addition of new hardware assets, such as camera, GPS
and network interface, but the access to software that was only accessible
from personal computing stations.
The combination of telephony and internet is the ingredient that gave
smartphones such a huge success. This allowed the mobile market to ex-
pand and attract more demanding client categories. Smartphones are used
from people that want to be in constant communication but located in iso-
lated spaces, businessmen that need to keep track of sales, teenagers that
care about video recording and status updates on the social networks and
travellers that want to virtually explore new places.

1.2
Aim of the project
The aim of this project is to provide a complete framework for producing
profound machine learning models to discriminate benign applications from
malicious ones. Consecutively, these models will form the base of real time
malware detection service for any Android device. The main target audiences
of this framework are both the security research community and individuals.
The solution introduces a universal system for application analysis, data
extraction and storage, diﬀerent machine learning classiﬁers and algorithms
as well as visualization of the produced results. The tuning parameters, which
are identiﬁed after the procedure, are fed to the machine learning service in
order to produce a state-of-the-art trained model. The framework should be
fast, scalable and easy to use. It will be evaluated here on its diﬀerent results
and respective accuracy scores.

Contribution - Novelty
The project is the continuation of research on the SafeDroid malware detec-
tion application, which was introduced on September 2016 [14]. Although
the application was able to produce real-time results, it suﬀered from high
complexity and some bottlenecks that slowed down the whole procedure and
thus the user experience. As our ﬁrst steps, we produced a utilized version
of both front and back ends that oﬀered hotﬁx to increase robustness of the
application.
SafeDroid v2.0 framework is an elegant open-source solution designed to
provide a detailed overview of diﬀerent classiﬁcation algorithms and ﬁnally
choose the best available model. Its novelty lies on the automation of the
procedure that includes application analysis, statistical analysis, feature vec-
tor extraction, service update and feedback presentation to the user. The
framework supports user parameterization in the sense of choosing exactly
the conditions the data set has to fulﬁll. Eventually, the produced model can
be tested in an integrated back-end server that comes along a light-weight
Android application.
Our contribution is summarized as follows:
• Optimization of the malware detection system of SafeDroid v1.0. The
new version of the framework requires 0.1 of the memory overhead and

Theory
3.1
Android Architecture
The Android operating system, developed by Google, is an open source soft-
ware based on Unix. It is organized in 6 main layers, as shown in ﬁgure
3.11.
Linux Kernel: Top bottom layer of the Android software stack is the
kernel which is based on the Linux 2.6 kernel. It has been formed to intro-
duce a notion of abstraction for the diﬀerent hardware components. Linux
kernel has been chosen because of its robustness. It provides multitasking
and application management, combines diﬀerent core system services such as
memory, process and power management modules in addition to providing
networking stack and device drivers for hardware.
Hardware Abstraction Layer: HAL layer provides standard interfaces
that expose device hardware capabilities to higher level Java API frame-
work. Each of the multiple library modules implements a diﬀerent interface
for a speciﬁc hardware component. These libraries are fed loaded when an
application asks for access to the device hardware assets.

Android Runtime: The main component in the Android runtime layer
is the Dalvik virtual machine. It was designed speciﬁcally for Android to
meet the standards of runtime procedures under limited amount of assets,
like CPU, memory and energy. Dalvik virtual machine runs DEX ﬁles which
compressed Java byte codes, optimized for minimal memory footprint. The
main advantage of converting application’s source code to DEX is that it
becomes much more lightweight and, thus, runs well on small processors
with limited access to energy and CPU. Next level up encloses core libraries
written in Java.

3.1.1.2
Security in Kernel Space
As discussed earlier, Android is organized in layers that build up the model
as a whole. Each stack component assumes that the components below are
properly secured.
The underlying Linux kernel provides a multitasking execution environ-
ment to allow multiple processes to run concurrently. Although this proce-
dure is normal for operating systems used by personal computing stations,
here things are diﬀerent. Each application running on Android device runs
its own instance of the Dalvik virtual machine rather than running a pro-
cess directly in the kernel space.
The kernel oﬀers a secure inter-process
communication facility to enable secure communication and data exchange
between diﬀerent applications running on diﬀerent processes. This feature at
kernel level ensures that even native code is constrained by the Application
Sandbox.
Application Sandbox assigns a unique user id to each application and runs
it as that user in a separate process, a diﬀerent approach from other operating
systems where multiple applications run with the same user permissions.
As a result, each application deﬁnes its own ﬁlesystem permissions, which
ensure that one user-process cannot read or alter another one’s ﬁles, unless it
explicitly shares the locally created ﬁles. The sandbox is simple and easy to
control. Application Sandbox takes care of the plausible memory corruption
15
==PAGE:26==
Chapter 3. Theory
errors that may occur from time to time, resulting in an establishment of a
security wall able to repel arbitrary code execution.
This security model extends to upper layers. The software at the stack
above the kernel, such as application framework and runtime , operating sys-
tem and standard libraries as well as the application themselves run within
the Application Sandbox. The well deﬁned model allows no security restric-
tions on how an application can be written by isolating each diﬀerent code
pieces at runtime.

3.2
Machine Learning
According to the Oxford Dictionary, machine learning, also referred to as
data mining or predictive analysis, is the capacity of a computer to learn
from experience, i.e. to modify its processing on the basis of newly acquired
information 2. The goal of machine learning is to teach machines to carry
out tasks by providing them with examples. These tasks are characterized
by frequent repetition and by the complexity of the decision making scheme.
A relevant parallelism for real life machine learning example is to analyze
the procedures and rational thoughts that lead the human brain to take
decisions and act. Some rules will be neglected while others will be over-
speciﬁed. The same mindset applies for machine learning; we try to deﬁne
as clear and distinct rules as possible on a set of samples that are used to
train the model. After training phase, the acquired model is used to spot this
patterns and rules on real life data; when the data fulﬁll a certain amount of
these rules then a decision can be taken.

Supervised learning
is the task of inferring a function from a pre-
deﬁned training (input) data set. This set requires proper labeling of the
observations and is structured in pairs; one is the input object and the other
is the desired output value. The supervised learning algorithm analyzes the
input training data and produces an inferred function, called classiﬁer or a
regression function. The desired functionality is to predict the correct output
value for any valid input object. This is only feasible when the algorithm is
able to generalize from the training set to unseen data.
Due to its nature and feasibility of deployment, supervised learning is
employed more extensively than the unsupervised one. In our experiment we
used supervised techniques so we are going to elaborate on its use.
The model creating procedure has to perform the following steps :
1. Determining the type of training set. This is inferred from the type of
the results that we want to produce.
2. Collecting the training set. The set needs to be as representative as
possible.
3. Formatting the data set to reﬂect model needs. The better the forma-
tion of the training data, the more accurate the predictions.
4. Examining the produced results and the corresponding learning algo-
rithm. This a pre-training phase where the learning algorithm runs ”in
the dark”.
5. Determining what are the best hyper parameters or validation parame-
ters and execute the learning algorithm of the training. Later attempt
to ﬁne tune the parameters that will aﬀect the ﬁnal prediction model.
6. Evaluating the results. This examination is based on diﬀerent aspects
such as confusion matrix, F1 values, bias, variance, eﬃciency etc.

The authors of [16] created a tool to monitor and collect relevant system

Hybrid
Sharid et al. in [2] planed their research around both analyzing applications’
native code as well as monitoring and detecting for abnormalities during
runtime. Native code refers to machine code that is executed directly by the
main processor, opposed to bytecode executed in the virtual environment of
the Android JVM. Their static analysis focuses on deﬁning certain patterns
in the control ﬂow. It mainly targets on ostensibly insigniﬁcant incidents that
could pass otherwise unobserved. Their tool, DroidNative, is also able to
analyze bytecode by making use of the Android runtime. ART is the managed
runtime used by applications and system services to compile bytecode and
executes the Dalvik Executable format and DEX bytecode speciﬁcation. In
this case, ART is used to compile bytecode into native code for the purpose
of analysis. Their experiment data set consists of 5490 applications, 1758 of
these malwares. The two experiments produced contrasting results; the ﬁrst
one had 98.5% accuracy with a variation of 1% while the second ranged from
70% to 80%. The false positive rates showed high values for both of them,

3.3.4
Remarks
All three approaches have high and low points. The static approach produces
the most accurate results and is the least expensive one; all the computation
are executed in a dedicated server, while the Android device has the role of
providing only the executable of the application - which most of the cases
is the size of KB. It is a lightweight approach whilst eﬃcient and is per-
formed at installation time and only for once. From the negative side, there
are techniques that can obfuscate the results such as code encryption or dy-
namic load of code. Dynamic analysis techniques are more sophisticated, but
add a serious amount of computation overhead. They surpass static analy-
sis in being resilient to evasion, code obfuscation and polymorphism. They
run as daemons processes, constantly monitoring the device for abnormal

more resources, in terms of power and bandwidth, which leads to the degra-
dation of the user’s experience. The observation of vague patterns produces
high false positive rates, which makes dynamic analysis infeasible in real-time
scenarios.While it seems the most promising approach for future detection
mechanisms, hybrid analysis is still in premature stages of development.

6.2
Feature Extraction
In order to train and create our machine learning based decision model, we
need data to be used as input. The feature extraction phase is the translation

of Android applications to useful data, representing the functionality of each
of the applications. As so, the ﬁrst structural component of our framework
is the data extraction or feature extraction and it’s carried out by employing
the functionality of AndroWarn.
Inspired by relevant studies for static feature extraction, our solution’s
ﬁrst step lies on the derivation of applications relevant data.
This data
will be later formed properly to be fed as an input to the diﬀerent machine
learning classiﬁers and algorithms. Examining the aforementioned data we
can inspect and learn about the nature of each application separately. The
available data after extraction can be categorized as follows :
• APIs. It is a generic way of inheriting functionality. APIs are language,
context and platform speciﬁc and they consist of all the libraries and
modules that an application loads before or during the execution of its
code. It’s the way that functionality is carried and re-used in diﬀerent
application and to get distinct results. By examining the API calls,
one can get a thorough overview of what an application tries to achieve
without the need of execution. Such calls can invoke Android oﬀered
modules, Java standard libraries as well as third-party code. While
most of the applications share the same standard calls, some of them
might use more controversial ones that are tightly linked with malicious
behavior.
• Permissions.
These component represents the acknowledgement of
agreement to use speciﬁc services between an application and the end
user. This services are security critical and thus user approval is needed
to be granted. They are presented to users in list form before the ap-
plication gets installed at the device. Users have to consent or else the
application aborts the installation procedure. Individual permissions
are characterized by the security level. There are several categories of

==PAGE:69==

levels, such as installer, normal, dangerous, privileged etc. and each
one employees its own protection level. All the requested permissions
are listed in the AndroidManifest.xml ﬁle that comes along the APK
ﬁle.
• Software & Hardware Components. Following the same mindset as the
requested permissions, the application needs to be granted access to
use speciﬁc h/w and s/w features. For hardware, this is translated in
getting access to physical assets like the camera and the GPS. Soft-
ware speciﬁc permissions are those presented in section 3.1.1.3, namely
content providers, broadcast receivers , services and activities. As dis-
cussed earlier, an application needs access to the aforementioned com-
ponents to invoke foreign code functionality as well as communicate
with other applications and use the common storing space.
The presence of any of the above does not clearly indicate that an application
is malicious or benign. In order to obtain knowledge that would allow us to
predict the nature of an application, structured analysis is performed.

SafeDroid v2.0 framework uses an integrated database to store the incoming
extracted data to the disk. As discussed earlier, SD v1.0 relied on ﬁle system

we employed a database. Databases have many advantages over storing data
to a ﬁle as they can handle race conditions and allow multiprocessing. This
is very important when the software needs to provide results fast and avoid
deadlocks. Moreover, databases are fault tolerant and can handle very large
datasets while scaling smoothly without introducing much overhead. This

Another advantage, compared to storing into ﬁle is that databases minimize
data inconsistency. Thus, we don’t to worry about potential duplicates in
our data set. After the creation of the database, querying and computing the
properties of the diﬀerent feature vectors is more straightforward and can be
done with any tool able to carry out this task1

6.5.1
Feature Set
The most important step in the establishment feature set procedure is to
highlight those APIs that could determine best the nature of any application.
SafeDroid’s v1.0 approach was to form a set of packages imported by 60% of
malicious and 40% of benign applications in the data set. The percent count
of the employment of each API was calculated based on the unbalanced data
set. Then, the diﬀerence in the count of usage among malicious and benign
applications was calculated. Finally, the framework chose the ones with a
count diﬀerence of more than 35% [14]. This approach managed to produce
a set of 743 features.
SafeDroid v2 follows a slight alternative and simpler resolution method.
63

The most important amendment between SD v1.0 and v2.0 is that in v2.0 the
whole set of APIs that were deﬁned after the analysis phase are considered
as the source for the formation of the feature set. The motivation behind
this decision is the hypothesis that every API call has the same contribu-
tion on both the categorization and the identiﬁcation of the nature of an
application. For example, an API which is undoubtedly benign, such as the
java.lang.String API, which represents string literals, wouldn’t be ﬁltered out
of the feature set just because is considered to be benign. Instead, we rely
on the presence of malicious APIs to take the role of ﬁltering it out of our
list: the more malicious applications we have in our initial dataset, the less
the occurrence of benign APIs in the feature set. Additionally, we are not
positively conﬁdent that seemingly benign APIs cannot be used for malicious
purposes. For the same example, the String API could work together with
Android NDK Toolset3 and be used for buﬀer overﬂow, or work together
with the Reﬂextion API which allows the invocation of another method by
providing only the name of the function as a string, as was shown by Dolberg
et al. in [9].
Another reason that reviles the motives for this decision is that we wanted
to allow full control to the user of the framework. Providing a standardize
ﬁltering of the API set would contradict the parameterization feature of our
framework, which allow the user the free will on taking decisions. The user

Discussion
8.1
Feasibility of SafeDroid
The main goal of our framework is the automated production of fully suﬃ-
cient prediction models in terms of maximum accuracy scores and minimum
negative errors. This can be considered as a concatenation of diverse param-
eters, each one adding a piece on the same procedure. Our framework goes
deep in exploring and deﬁning these parameters. The orchestration includes
an eﬃcient reverse engineering module, proper data categorization, discrim-
ination and selection of the features and testing session. After the end of
the described procedure, the framework makes an estimation on which data
it needs to proceed with and which algorithms to use for the classiﬁcation
and model production. As seen in the results presented in Chapter 7, SD is
capable of executing all the tasks in suﬃcient time while the accuracy results
are kept high.
The novelty of our solution lies on the parameterization ability it pro-
vides. SD framework acts like a toolbox for security researchers by taking
their hands oﬀof the technical implementation and the need of code devel-
opment and allowing them to concentrate on any further evolutionary ideas.
Moreover, it can be adjusted to fulﬁll diﬀerent intentions of execution.

The use of a database for storing the extracted data has many advantages.
First, it allows iterability without the need of executing the extraction proce-
dure over and over, which rises the eﬃciency of our framework by decreasing
the execution time. Second, it supports updating. A third thing is that it
allows categorization of malwares and features based on their metadata. For
example, one could use this feature to create datasets of malwares based on
the year they were produced or released. This type of categorization allows
the observation of patterns in malware evolution and it is the ﬁrst step of
predicting how the malware could evolve in the future.
Another valuable feature of our framework is the provision of feedback to
the users. As so, the users are informed about the outcome of the procedure
either during the execution or after its completion.
The outcome is the
enhanced communication between user and framework in order to jointly
reach on the best decision, depending on the context of the execution.
Each one of the classiﬁers represents a diﬀerent approach of solving the
same problem, works under diverse tunes and has its own advantages and
disadvantages, as presented by Liu et al. in [17] and Srikanth et al. in [22]:
• K Nearest Neighbor: Works well for small to medium sized number
of observation but does scale well for over-populated datasets. It oﬀers
suﬃcient distance measurements for weighted observations.
• Support Vector Machine: It is able to generalize on huge datasets by
maximizing the distance between the features (margins) on the hyper-
plane.
It is an eﬀective approach for binary classiﬁcation but it is
generally slow.
• Decision Tree: Is based on traditional statistical disciplines. Its ad-
vantages are the low algorithmic complexity O(logn) and the eﬀective-
ness when the oﬀered decision are limited. Another advantage is that

• Random Forest: Uses fully grown Decision Trees in a more eﬃcient

8.2
Limitations
As every solution, ours comes with some limitations. As it relies on static
analysis to curry out its task, our framework would not produce suﬃcient
results in cases where evasion techniques are employed by authors of malware.
These techniques are employed in an attempt to conceal the true nature
of their apps and evade security scans. Here we present some well known
techniques.

==PAGE:94==
Chapter 8. Discussion
Dynamic Loading
This is another technique to avoid static detectors.
The application dy-
namically loads malicious code during execution time and thus evades the
identiﬁcation procedure. There are some certain ways to that would force an
app to load code during execution, which are embedding malicious payload
within the native code and loading classed for within the jar and apk ﬁles.
The outcome of both cases would be the execution of uninstalled code.
Update Attacks
In this occasion, a seemingly benign application is installed on the phone.
Being benign, the app passes all security tests and is marked as safe. The
same app will later connect to a remote server and and download malicious
code[26]. After the update, the app installs the newly obtained code and
transforms itself to malicious one. A measure against technique is the re-
examination of seemingly harmless apps.

Today, there are approximately 1.6 billion active Android devices.
This

As application production companies fail to serve the public and soothe

with valuable insights.
SafeDroid version 2.0 framework employs static analysis for the extraction
of applications’ identiﬁcational features, a solid technique for the creation of
the feature set vector (section 3.3.1), diﬀerent approaches for the formation
of testing and training subsets, a variety of classiﬁcation algorithms for the
production of the prediction model as well as a back end server working
together with an Android app.
