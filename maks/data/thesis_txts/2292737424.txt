Abstract
A new statement from the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC)[1] have
been published which recommend a large reduction in the emissions of carbon dioxide
(CO2) and other long-lived greenhouse gases to reduce global warming to below 2 degrees
relative to pre-industrial. This leads to a rise in the global demand for natural gas (NG)
since it is a clean burning and low pollution fuel. One of the future excavation of NG
is expected to be from deep oﬀshore gas ﬁelds which will require the construction of a
Floating Liqueﬁed Natural gas (FLNG) vessel.
The purpose of the thesis is to identify which liquefaction processes are appropriate to be
included in a FLNG vessel, with respect to the cost, volume, exergy eﬃciency and potential
of thermal energy recovering, and selection parameters as for example equipment count,
ﬂexibility.
Eight liquefaction processes proposed to be suitable for oﬀshore application on a FLNG
vessel are simulated, and evaluated.
The proposed processes are the single mixed re-
frigerant (SMR), a CH4 and N2 (Niche), dual mixed refrigerant (DMR), Propane mixed
refrigerant (C3MR), Nitrogen Methane expander process (N2CH4), triple reverse Brayton
process (Tricycle), a multi-stage Brayton-Joule-Thomson process (N2-C2-C3) and the Cas-
cade process. The liquefaction processes were modelled with the assumption of steady state
and optimized through Aspen Hysys with Hypertech SQP optimization tools with respect
to three scenarios. The three scenarios were minimizing of the costs and the volume, and
maximizing of the exergy eﬃciency, and in all case with respect to identical parameters to
ensure that each simulated liquefaction process was comparable. The gas plant in where
the liquefaction process was modelled with respect to the literatures was used to identify
the composition of the NG stream and the need of external heating and cooling was iden-
tiﬁed through a Pinch analysis. The liquefaction processes were then implemented in the
gas plant and optimized with respect to volume minimization.
The analysis shows that DMR process is the best all-round liquefaction process. This is
because the optimization cases show that the DMR process has a volume rate of 1.27 m3
per kg Liqueﬁed Natural gas (LNG) per second, a cost rate of 14.19 million USD per kg
LNG/s and an exergy eﬃciency of 46.52 percent and a payback time of approximately 0.79
years for a natural gas composition of 87.5 C1, 5.5 C2, 2.1 C3, 0.3 i-C4, 0.5 n-C4, 0.1 i-C5
and 4 mole percent Nitrogen and a mass ﬂow of 135 kg LNG/s. The Niche process was
found to be the most eﬃcient process with respect to minimizing of the volume consump-
tion. It is because it has a volume rate of 0.97 m3 per kg LNG per second, a cost rate of
24.94 million USD per kg LNG/s and an exergy eﬃciency of 28.27 percent and a payback
time of approximately 1.34 years when applying the same natural gas composition and
mass ﬂow as for the DMR process.
i
==PAGE:8==
Through implementation of the liquefaction processes with the gas plant, the liquefaction
process was providing cooling duty to the Natural Gas Liquids Extraction (NGLE)system
and then liquefaction of the NG stream. The cost rate was then found to be of 17.4 million
USD per kg LNG per second and the volume rate to be of 1.48 m3 per kg lng per second
for the DMR process. In case of the Niche process the cost was increased to 43.75 million
USD per kg LNG per second, and the volume rate increased to 1.341 m3 per kg LNG per
second. In addition, it was found that the DMR process had a consumption of 145.5 MW
of electricity, which is lower than the consumption of the Niche process with 344.6 MW.
The pinch analysis shows that both liquefaction processes were suitable for implementa-
tion with respect to utilization of waste heat. In case of the DMR process, supplementary
ﬁring would be needed to provide enough thermal energy to the system. In case of the
Niche process, 259.13 MW of thermal energy from the gas turbine would be released to
the environment.
By taking in account achieved results and the selection parameters the DMR process is
found to be the most suitable candidate for application on a FLNG vessel, as long as there
is enough space for it’s slightly higher volume consumption and if the impact of motion of
the vessel proved to have negligible eﬀect.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Natural gas (NG) poses an essential role in the the supply of primary energy worldwide
where it covers 21.3%. With the higher green awareness in the society. A new statement
from the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC)[1] have been published. The
statement says that a large reduction in the emission of carbon dioxide(CO2) and other
long-lived greenhouse gases is recommend to limit the global warming to below 2 degrees
relative to pre-industrial. Due to this, it is excepted that there will be made more regula-
tions to reduced emissions from the diﬀerent sections in the society. This leads to a rising
in the global demand for NG since it is a clean burning and low pollution fuel.
The future excavation of NG is expected to be divided between the remaining conventional
ﬁelds and unconventional gases as shale gas, coal bed methane and deep oﬀshore ﬁelds
[2]. In connection with the deep oﬀshore ﬁelds, it will require the construction of ﬂoating
Liqueﬁed natural gas (LNG) facilities as example a Floating Liqueﬁed Natural gas (FLNG)
vessel or a LNG ﬂoating production, storage and oﬄoading unit (LNG FPSO). The reason
for it is, that the ﬁelds is too far away from the shore or too deep, which make pipeline
delivery to onshore liquefactions plants infeasible [3] compared with the LNG concept as
shown in ﬁgure 1.1.
Figure 1.1: Overview and comparison of the cost for diﬀerent transport methods,

Today in the oﬀshore production site the NG is drawn from the gas well at the bottom

gas plant have to be constructed and new subsea pipelines have to be constructed to the

1.1
Overview of FLNG
The liquefaction process on a FLNG vessel does only poses of a small part of the whole
process as shown in ﬁgure 1.3. The processes in a FLNG is much similar to an oﬀshore
gas plant with the diﬀerences that some of the processes can be omitted depending on the
quality of the well and gas steam.
The whole process starts with the NG/water/oil mixture will be extracted from multiple
sub-sea wells and then collected in a sub sea production manifold from where it will be
lead to the FLNG vessel through some ﬂexible risers. At the FLNG vessel the mixture
is ﬁrst lead through a slug catcher, then a separation process system in where the con-
densate(oil) and water is being separated from the gas components as NGL and NG. The
condensate will then be lead into a condensate stabilizer. The separated condensate and
water is then stored in a tank for later retrieval. The gas components is then entered
the Pretreatment process system which consist of 4 systems. First CO2 is removed in the
Acid Gas Removal System and compressed and re injected through the CO2 Compression
System. The Gas components is lead to the Dehydrate System in where the water content
is removed/reduced, and then lead to the Mercury Removal System in where the Mercury
content is removed and stored. Then the gas components enters the liquefaction process
system which consist of 3 sub system. The Natural Gas Liquids (NGL) Extraction System,
The Main Liquefaction System and ﬁnal the End Flash System. In the NGL Extraction
system the NGL is separated from the NG and lead to the Fractionation Process system
3
==PAGE:13==
Figure 1.3: Overview of the processes in a gas plant
where the NGL is separated into C2, LPG, C3, C4 and heavier hydrocarbons by the Ethane
Distillation System and the Propane Distillation System and the Butane Sistillation Sys-
tem and stored. The NG from the NGL Extraction system is lead to the Main liquefaction
system in where the NG is cooled down to approximate 110 K and expanded to a mixture
of LNG and gasses by a valve. The mixture will then enter the End Flash System in where
a gasses will be ﬂashed and the LNG mixture will be stored in a LNG tank.
Sub-sea
The wells will be completely sub-sea and unlike traditional drilling platforms no wellhead
platform is required. The depth of the wells is depending on the location and the depth
of gas reservoir but in the case with Shell Prelude LNG, each well will be on approximate
5.400 meters depth. The Gasses extracted from the wells will be collected in the production
manifold and then lead trough ﬂowlines to the riser base manifolds. The Riser base man-
ifold is connected to all wells and the FLNG by umblilicals which carry electrical power,
hydraulics, chemicals and cables to transmit signals which allow the wells to be controlled
by the FLNG vessel. Final the gases are moved to the FLNG vessel through ﬂexible risers
which is designed to accommodate the motion of the FLNG Vessel on the ocean surface[4].
Slug Catcher
The purpose of the slug catcher is to ensure steady ﬂow into the treatment section. It is
a vessel with a larger buﬀer volume to cope with possible slugs of water which could be
formed in the ﬂexible risers. The slug catcher is designed as two vessels on top of each
others with an open connection and by using the density diﬀerent the purpose of the top
vessel is to separate the gasses from the liquid and the bottom vessel is going to separate
4
==PAGE:14==
condensate from water/Monoethyleneglycol(MEG). The Gases are then send further to the
separating process.
Separating process
On its way from the Slug Catcher the gases are heated if needed to prevent the formation
of hydrates, and at the separating process the particles and residual condensate is removed
from the gasses by a gas separator. From the separating process the gases continues to
the acid gas removal system. The condensate from the separating process is led to the
condensate treatment process.
Condensate treatment process
The received condensate from the Slug Catcher and the Separating process is heated to
inhibit hydrate formation and then it enters a three phase separator to remove residual
gases and water/MEG mixture from the condensate. After that the condensate ﬁrst enters
a liquid column where possible MEG residues are removed. Then it enters a stabilisation
column where the metane, ethane, propane and butanes from the condensate and the
residual gases are separated, compressed and returned to the gas treatment process. The
condensate is then stored in a tank. The water and MEG from the condensate treatment
process is then send to the MEG recovery process.
MEG recovery process
The water and MEG mixture from both the slug catcher and the condensate treatment
process is treated in a column where the MEG is removed from the water. The MEG is
then the recycle back to the well and reused while the water is ﬁltered through a biological
treatment system and then discarded to the sea.
Acid Gas Removal System
To prevent the CO2 from freezing to dry ice during the liquefaction process the CO2
content must be reduced to less than 50 pmm [4] in the treated gas steam. That is done in
a absorber column where the inlet for the gas steam is in the bottom and the steam ﬂows
up agains a counter current ﬂow of aqueous amine solution which binds to the CO2 in a
chemical reaction. The amine solution is then drawn from the bottom of the column and
in order to dissolved the CO2 from the amine, the ﬂuid is expanded to a lower pressure,
heated and then separated in a distillation column. The CO2 content is then transferred
to the CO2 Compression System while the amine is cooled and recycle in the absorber.
CO2 Compression System
To reduced the FLNG eﬀect on the global warming the removed CO2 from the Acid Gas
Removal System is compressed and injected into a CO2 well.
Dehydrate System
After the gas sweetening the water content have to be removed in order to prevent ice in
the liquefaction processes. There are many diﬀerent ways to remove the water content.
One of them is to use an absorption process in where NG which contain water enters the
bottom of a column and ﬂows upward in counter-current contact with an aqueous glycol
solution which will absorb the water vapour. The water content can be reduced further
5
==PAGE:15==
by using an adsorption process in where the gas will be send through a ﬁxed bed vessel
ﬁlled with alumina silicate crystals which will absorb the water residuals. To regenerate
the ﬁxed bed, a reverse ﬂow of heated dry gas is lead through the vessel to remove the
water content. To prevent the vessel from being out of operation during the regeneration
there have to be two vessels in service operating on a rotation cycle, where one vessel is on
regeneration while the other is in operation. After the dehydrate system the water content
in the gases is reduced to less than 0.5[5] ppmV or less than 1 ppmV [4].
Mercury Removal System
To prevent possible corrosion of the aluminium materials in the liquefaction process the
mercury content have to be reduced to less than 10 ng/SM3 [5] [4]. This can be achieved
through diﬀerent processes such as using a regenerable molecular sieve, or a Non-regenerable
absorbent or an combination of both. In the Molecular sieve the mercury will forms amal-
gam with the silvers impregnated in the sieve. Later the mercury can be desorbed into a
regeneration steam during the heating cycle of the drying vessel.
Natural Gas Liquids Extraction System
On the way to the Natural Gas Liquids Extraction System, the gases are typically send
through the pre cooling part of the liquefaction process or cooled down through a seawater
heat exchanger to force condensing of some of the heavier gas components by decreasing
the temperature. Then the removal of NGL from the NG occurs in a fractionation column
in where the components are separated by density and the amount of removed NGL is
depending on the sales speciﬁcations.
Ethane Distillation System
The Ethane Distillation system consist of a fractionation column. In where the residual
methane and ethane is removed and either returned to the natural gas stream, used for
power production,used as make-up refrigerant,ﬂared or storage for export.
Propane Distillation System
After the Ethane Distillation system another fractionation column is going to separate the
propane and butane from the condensates. The separated propane and butane are then
either, stored in a tank for export, ﬂared or used as make-up refrigerant.
Main Liquefaction System
The purpose of the liquefaction system is to decrease the temperature of the NG to ap-
proximate 110 K and then expand it to 1.03 bar at which point it turns liquid. The Main
liquefaction system is examined in this study since there exist many diﬀerent processes to
achieved the low temperature, and the main liquefaction system typically accounts for 30
to 40 percent of the overall plant cost.
End Flash System
The end ﬂash system consist of a separator or a drum in where the excess nitrogen content
is removed from the LNG.

Storage
The produced LNG is then stored in a LNG tank in the hull of the vessel at 1.03 bar and
110 K.

Prico process
The Prico process shown in ﬁgure 1.6 is a Single mixture refrigerant (SMR) process used
at the liquefaction plant Marsa el-Brega in Libya. The Prico process is selected to be
investigated in this study because it is one of the simplest processes since it consists of
only one cycle and has a low equipment count.
The mixed refrigerant is compressed through a set-up consisted of several compressors and
inter-coolers up to a pressure level which allow the mixed refrigerant to give enough cooling
duty after expansion. The numbers of compressors depending on the pressure diﬀerence
between the lowest and highest pressure level, and the speciﬁc restriction on the pressure
ratio. In this case the compressions set up is decide to consist of 2 compressors with one
inter-cooler and one after-cooler. The purpose of the inter-cooler is to reduce the tempera-
ture of the gas stream in the compression process and then reduce the power consumption
of the compressor. The purpose of the after-cooler is to reduce the outlet temperature of
the compressor which will reduce the cooling duty in the heat exchanger due to a lower
inlet temperature of the hot refrigerant stream. After the after-cooler the refrigerant is led
through a heat exchanger network. There should be noted that due to the single cycle, the
Prico process had to provide enough cooling duty for cooling the NG and the hot stream
refrigerant from the after-cooler. When the refrigerant has been cooled down to an appro-
priate temperature, the refrigerant is been expanded to a appropriate pressure in where it
contains liquids. Then the refrigerant liquid re-enters the heat exchanger as a cold stream
and provide cooling duty to cooling of the NG and hot refrigerant stream.
The composition of the refrigerant is shown in table 1.4 and the composition is chosen
based on the boiling temperature to match the temperature grid between the cold and hot
streams in the heat exchangers. The pinch temperature in the heat exchanger should takes
in account, since a low pinch temperature in the heat exchanger will increase the exergy
eﬃciency but also the size of the heat exchanger.
Due to the diﬀerence in feed gas composition and design parameters in this project and in
the reference case [11] the Prico process is restored in Aspen Hysys and some adjustments
have been performed as shown in table 1.5. There should be noted three major diﬀerences
between the reference model and the restored model. The major diﬀerences are the cooling
water temperature, the NG pressure and more important, the reference model include
pressure losses at the heat exchangers. The lower cooling water temperature in the reference
case decrease the demand for cooling duty in the heat exchanger. It is because both the
NG and the hot refrigerant stream will have a lower temperature at the inlet of the heat

Niche process
The Niche process shown in ﬁgure 1.7 is included in this study because it is expected to
be used in the FLNG designed by Hoegh LNG [12]. The Niche process is a dual cycle
process in where the ﬁrst cycle provided the pre cooling duty, the second cycle provided
the liquefaction and sub cooling duty. Each of the cycles are similar the Prico process
since each cycle are going to provide cooling duty to itself and the NG. The only diﬀerence

expansion turbine is used. Through the expansion turbine, the extracted power is used to
supply a part of the compressors power consumption. Worth to be noted is, that there do
not occurs any condensation of refrigerants in the cycles in the Niche process. This make
the process much more stable for motions since there is no risk of liquid-vapour maldistri-
bution. Some other advantages are that a cycle remaining in gaseous phase reduces the
risk of leakage, eliminates the need for liquid refrigerant storages, drums and separators.

Tricycle process
The Tricycle process shown in ﬁgure 1.9 is claimed [15] to be suitable for FLNG and is
patented by Technip [16]. The Tricycle process consists of 3 independent reverse Brayton
cycles but can be used in diﬀerent conﬁgurations in where the cycles integrated.
One
conﬁgurations could for example be that the ﬁrst cycle is pre cooling the second cycle
before compression, but in this project the most simple conﬁguration is used with no heat
exchanges between the cycles.
The ﬁrst cycle is pre cooling the NG by ﬁrst compressing the refrigerant mixture to a
desired pressure through a 3 stage compression with intercooling. Then the refrigerant
is cooled through an after-cooler to decrease the temperature. After the after-cooler the
refrigerant is expanded through an expansion turbine with power recovery and enters the
heat exchanger. At this point, the refrigerant have a lower temperature than the pre-cooled
NG to avoid temperature crossovers in the heat exchanger.
The second cycle is responsible for condensing and the third cycle for subcooling of NG.
They are slightly diﬀerent from the ﬁrst cycle since there will be a heat exchanger between
the refrigerant intake to the ﬁrst compressor and the hot ﬂuid at the outlet of the after-
cooler. There should be noted that nowhere in the system, condensation of refrigerant
exists and the refrigerant composition in each cycle can be found in table 1.10. There
should be noted that all the refrigerant will stay gaseous which as in case with the Niche
process made it much more stable for motions.

2.1
Equally design parameters
To make an evaluation of the diﬀerent liquefaction process possible they have to operate
on the same terms, otherwise it will not be straightforward to make a comparison. Due
to the diﬀerence in, for example, NG feed pressure, temperature and composition of the
diﬀerent proposed natural gas liquefactions processes in the diﬀerent articles, a number
of design parameters are selected and used as a base for modelling all the liquefaction
processes considered in this project. The selected design parameters are shown in table
2.1. The diﬀerences in NG feed compositions are shown in table 2.2 in where the selected
design composition of NG used for all liquefaction processes are label as ’new’.
Natural gas composition
The Feed NG composition is selected based on the majority of the investigated liquefac-
tions process to make sure that the majority of the liquefaction process have the right
refrigerant composition since the selection of refrigerants are based on the NG composition
and subcooling temperature.
Sub cooling of Natural Gas
The subcooling temperature of the NG is based on an Aspen Hysys model in which the
temperature before the expansion valve is adjusted to ﬁnd the optimum between a high
purity and a high liquefaction rate. The optimum was found to be at approximately 110
K at 65 bar since it gives a 94 percent liquefaction rate and the LNG will consist of 89.2
percent methane with the proposed NG composition.
Compressor eﬃciency
In case of the compressors, a maximum pressure ratio on 4 are selected per compressions
stage, since the outlet temperature of of the compressor increase with the pressure ratio.
Since the power required per compressor is directly proportional to the inlet temperature,
the compressions stage will include inter-cooling. Regarding the eﬃciency, the compressors
are modelled as a centrifugal compressors in where the polytropic eﬃciency usually are used
[18]. It is recommended to select an eﬃciency at between 70 and 85 percent[18], and a
polytropic eﬃcency of 82 is used in this project.
23
==PAGE:33==
Expander turbine eﬃciency
The expander turbines are selected to have an adiabatic eﬃciency of 87% which is inside
the range of 85 to 90 percent [19].
Pressure loss
To simplify the models, the assumption of no pressure loss for all components in the
liquefaction process are used.
Pressure limitations
Regarding optimization of the liquefaction process, the maximum pressure is selected to
be at 80 bar to reduce the amount of compressors needed, and to reduce the time needed
for optimization. 80 bar is due to the maximum pressure found in the the reference cases
of the selected liquefaction processes.

Heat exchanger type
The heat exchanger is the main component in cryogenic process, and poses of between 20
and 30 percent of investment cost of the liquefaction process [20]. Since the liquefaction
process can represent 27 to 28 percent of the total investment cost, in which the hull cost
accounting for approximate 20 to 46 percent of the total cost. Then the heat exchanger will

the total investment cost are due to all the other processes on the FLNG as gas treatment,
oﬄoading, power generation, turret, utilities and more. [21] [22].
There exist many diﬀerent type of heat exchangers as for example shell and tube, plate-ﬁn,
double pipe, spiral tube, spiral plate and coil wound heat exchangers. All mentioned heat
exchangers are capable to cooling or heating streams, but in case of liquefaction process,
some criteria have to be met. The heat exchangers have to operate with large temperature
ranges since the temperature at the outlet can be much higher than the temperature at
the inlet. An example of this is the Prico process, since this process only has one heat
exchanger to cooling the ﬂuid from the ambient to -163 degrees. In connection with oﬀshore
NG processing then the space usage has to be minimized, which increases the demand for
a compact heat exchanger design. Another criterion is that the selected heat exchanger
has to allow multiple streams inside the same heat exchanger. Because, in liquefaction of
NG, it is common to have multiple streams inside the same heat exchanger where both NG

In where M is the ﬂuid ﬂow rate in the layer, H is the enthalpy, Qinternal is the heat gained

streams, it has been assumed that the temperature of the hot streams in all intervals should

exchanger which allows multi streams to be used in same heat exchanger, is that steady
state mode do not include the physical layout. Because of this, the order of the streams
given to the exchanger is not important and the UA value is calculated based on the heat
curves.
2.3
Optimization scenarios
When the liquefaction processes were selected and matched a new feed gas composition and
the design parameters, then the operating parameters were no longer at the optimum. To
be able to compare the liquefaction systems on same terms, they all have to be optimized
after the same target, since some of the liquefaction processes as the DMR process were
better optimized because of having the same gas composition before as after they were
matched to the design parameters. These targets will be exergy eﬃciency maximizing,
minimizing of the investment costs and minimizing of the space utility. The liquefaction
models will be optimized with Aspen Hysys optimization tool Hyprotech SQP which will
be applied on 3 diﬀerent cases, in where each of the targets are the optimizing parameters.
The optimization variables are in all cases the mass ﬂow, pressure, pressure ratio in the
compressors, split ratio in the tee, the NG temperature between the heat exchangers and in
case of the Niche and the Tricycle, the refrigerant temperature after the heat exchangers.

Hyprotech SQP
Hyprotech SQP is a sequential quadratic programmings (SQP) algorithm which is avail-
able through Aspen Hysys for optimization. SQP is used for nonlinear optimization, in
which the objective function is non linear. By non linear it means that the objective func-
tion is not directly proportional to the input value. Hyprotech SQP allows selection of the
step size restriction, multi optimizations variables, and allows optimization on both uncon-
strained, equality and inequality constrained problems. An other feature is that it allows
the inclusion of penalty value on the variables to highlighting one variable over another.
In the optimizer, the objective function will be selected. In case of minimization of the
objective function the optimizer will create the following function:
min(f(y1, y2, y3, y4, yn))
(2.3)
In where y is the process variable which typically is indirect a function of the primary
variable. In the optimizer, each primary variable, which the optimizer is going to varied,
are speciﬁed as:
x1,minimumallowablevalue ≤x ≤x1,maximumallowablevalue
(2.4)
In where x is the primary variable and all primary variables are normalized based on the
upper and lower bounds:
(,0)norm =
x −xminimum
xmaximum −xminimum
(2.5)
Due to this, it is important to select reasonable bounds since too high or too low variable
bounds can result in problems when scaling the primary variable by the optimizer. At last
the equality and inequality constraints are speciﬁed:
z1,minimumallowablevalue ≤z ≤z1,maximumallowablevalue
(2.6)
In which z is the constraint, and the optimizer is not allowed to violate the speciﬁed
interval, if the founded solution has to be feasible. Through optimization, the primary
variables will be adjusted and every time a step failing to be feasible the step length is
reduced on the last optimized primary variable.
Constraints
To ensure that the optimizer is only evaluating feasible points, constraints have to be
deﬁned. These constraints have to for example avoid temperature cross overs in the heat
exchangers or liquid in the compressors. The constraints applied in all models are shown
in table 2.4 and it should be noted that in case of the Niche process and the Tricycle
process a constraints on the vapour faction is applied on all points in the system to avoid
condensing of refrigerants.

One way to evaluate a system is based on the system exergy eﬃciency, since it deﬁnes how
large a percentage of the fuel exergy provided to the system is converted into the product

percentage of the compressor consumption including power recovery is used to cool down
the NG from 300K to 110K. Because of this, in one of the optimization cases, the objective
will be to maximize the exergy eﬃciency. The exergy eﬃciency of a liquefaction process
can be calculated with equation:

the cooler is not included in the exergy eﬃciency calculations of the liquefaction process

2.4.4
Acid Gas Removal System
The process for removal of the CO2 content is typically divided into adsorption and ab-
sorption processes. The adsorption process is a physical-chemical phenomenon in where
the the CO2 content is concentrated/bound on the surface of a solid object, which can
be a grate or example a regenerable molecular sieve. By using this process two systems
in parallel have to be used, to avoid shut-down of the whole system through desorbing
of the captured gas which is going on by blowing hot air or steam through the column.
One of the disadvantages of this process is that the eﬃciency depends on the size of ad-
sorption surface, and since particulate matter or condensation of liquids as water in the
column can blurs the adsorption surface, it will have a large eﬀect on the column eﬃciency.
In the absorption process the process depends only on the physical solubility and chemical
reactions between the feed gas and the feed absorbing media. In the chemical absorp-
tion process, NG with contain CO2 enters the bottom of a column and ﬂows upward in
counter-current contact with an aqueous amine solution. Depending on the size and ﬂow
and type of aqueous amine solution, temperature and pressure the NG at the top out-
let of the column should have a largely reduced CO2 content. The rich amine solution
with CO2 is stripped from the bottom of the column, and lead to a regenerator in where
the CO2 content is desorbed from the amine solution.
The lean amine solution is the
recycled in the absorption column. The advantage of this process is that it is a continuing
process, so there are no need to force a shut-down of the system to regenerating the amines.
The absorption process is selected to be used in this project due to the option of continu-
ing process and the fact the amine process is used in approximate 95 percent of U.S. gas
sweetening operations [35].
As shown in ﬁgure 2.8 the acid gas removal system consists of one absorption column, one
expansion valve, a gas/liquid separation column, one heat exchanger, a distillation column
and ﬁnal a pump, a cooler and a mixer. The absorption ﬂuid is typically an amine mixed
in water and the main alkanolamine products used in the gas sweetening industry are [36]:

20 weight percent MEA dissolved in water and in case of the 15 wt% MEA the
solution have a freezing temperature of -5 degrees. The MEA solution loading (the

mole CO2 per mole of pure MEA, a concentration higher than 0.35 is not preferred

Fractionation operations
The fractionation operations include the Natural gas liquids extraction system, the de-
ethanizer, the de-propanizer and the de-butanizer shown in ﬁgure 2.11. In all three cases,
the fractionation is modelling as three distillations column in where the NGL content is
divided into Ethane, Propane, Butane and heavier hydrocarbon components. The concept
of the distillation column is that the hydrocarbon mixture enters into the column in the
mid trays which is known as the feed tray. From this tray the liquid part of the feed ﬂows
down the column, and at the bottom the liquids is collected in a reboiler. By supplying
heat to the reboiler some of the liquid is vaporized and re-enters the column and some of
the liquid exits the column for further processing. The recycled vaporized liquid moves
up through the column and enters a condenser at the top. At the condenser the vapour
is cooled and the condensate and some of the vapour is recycled back to the top of the
column where the condensate ﬂows down the column again.
By the repeated counter
current contact between the vapour and liquid mixtures, parts of the liquids vaporize and
parts of the vapours condense which results in that the higher part of the column will be
richer in lighter hydrocarbons and the lower part of the column will be richer in heavier
hydrocarbons. It should be noted that the extracted gas from the condenser will not consist
one pure component but a mixture in where for example Ethane will pose a larger part of
the mixture in the de-ethanizer.
Natural Gas Liquids Extraction System
The Natural gas liquids extraction (NGLE) system is shown in ﬁgure 2.30. The purpose of
the NGLE system is to increase the purity of the LNG, to adjust the wobbe index and the
gross caloriﬁc value by removal of the heavier hydrocarbons. The Caloriﬁc value speciﬁes
the amount of energy generated by combustion of the gas and is diﬀerent from location
to location but it is typically between 34 and 45 MJ per scm [33] [42]. The wobbe index
measures the heating eﬀect that a burner is exposed to through combustion of the gas
and is depending on the location as the caloriﬁc value. The wobbe index is for example
in Belgium between 52,56 and 56.02 for LNG and between 48.24 to 53.34 in France but
world wide a range from 45 to 55 is not unusual [43]. The higher the wobbe index is, the
greater a thermal load is exposed on the burner. By adjusting the gas to be inside the
preferred ranges of both HHV and the Wobbe index, then combustions problems suck as
ﬂame lifting, ﬂashback and excessive NOx and CO generation can be avoid. Another thing
to be noted through design of the NGLE system is that the temperature of the condenser

integration by maximizing the energy recovery within the process or to minimize the use
of external energy sources. Through the pinch analysis, all streams to be heated or cooled

using the assumption that, there is only used counter ﬂow heat exchangers, no pressure
drop in the heat exchanger, enthalpy of each stream is only temperature depending, no
changes of potential and kinetic energy, no heat lost to the surroundings and ﬁnal the
assumption of steady state is used which make the analysis not time dependent.

cess, and there should be noted the streams where energy is released into the environment
which can be recovered if needed. The next step is to decide the minimum temperature

2.6
Challenges and Selection criteria
The selection criteria for liquefaction process for a FLNG will be signiﬁcantly diﬀerent
from the onshore types because of other challenges as motion due to the sea conditions,
reliability, safety, weight and space limits, ﬂexibility, eﬃciency and corrosion due to salt
water spray.
Motion
A FLNG vessel in operation will be aﬀected by the motion of the sea, although this is
minimizing by the vessel design and the stability control system it is not eliminated. If the
vessel is inclined more than 1.5◦due to motion of sea the capacity for LNG production can
be reduced by 10% [22]. Because the motion will aﬀect the liquid part of the process ﬂow
and reduce the performance cause to liquid-vapour maldistribution. There will also occur
mechanical stress due to the motion and liquid sloshing. There should be noted that this
are not a problem for liquefaction process operating only in the gaseous phase.
Reliability
Reliability for liquefaction processes is typically based on the results of previous projects.
For example the C3MR cycle is one of the most commonly used in onshore applications
and this means that this cycle is the most reliable cycle in the world since it has been in
operation for years.
Safety
In connection with the liquefaction processes the size of ﬂammable components is an im-
portant aspect of the safety issue since the limitation of space will make it diﬃcult to
separate the locations occupied by personnel with sections containing ﬂammable invento-
ries. Accordingly liquefactions process minimize or eliminate ﬂammable components are
67
==PAGE:77==
preferred. Another safety aspect is the size of toxic refrigerant and the leak detection of
these.
Weight and space limits
Compared to a land based LNG facility, the available area for liquefactions process is much
smaller on an FLNG and then a compact design of the liquefaction process is preferred.
A compact design should be interpreted as a low volume consumption, a low equipment
count and a low weight. Furthermore a compact design of the liquefaction process will
reduce the costs, the weight and the impact of motion.
Corrosion
Opposite land based facilities the topside equipment on the FLNG vessel will be exposed
to continuous salt water spray which must be taken into account in material selection since
the exposed equipment must not be constructed from aluminium but from other chloride
resistant materials as stainless steel.
Flexibility
Since the environmental temperature and composition of NG can vary during the lifetime
and is diﬀerent from well to well then the liquefaction process have to be tolerant of a
variety of process conditions.
Eﬃciency
As in case with the onshore liquefaction facilities the thermal eﬃciency of the liquefaction
process are desired to be as high as possible.

Discussion
4.1
Discussion
The discussion will be separated in diﬀerence parts. First the results from the modelling
of the total gas system will be discussed. Then simple models of the liquefactions process
will be compared with the full scale liquefaction process, and discussed with respect to for
example the push factor, liquefaction pressure and the change in composition. After that,
the recovery of internal thermal energy and how well the liquefaction processes ﬁt with the
overall system will be discussed. At last, the primary results for each liquefaction process
will be discussed with respect to for example the selection criteria.
4.1.1
Plant model
Based on the literature, the design parameters selected for the modelling of the pretreat-
ment operations and fraction columns are reasonable. The operating pressure of the system
was selected to be at 50 bar, [33] states that a pressure between 30 and 50 bar is a rea-
sonable value for the ﬁrst separator. The selected value is therefore reasonable based on
the literature. Due to the inlet pressure in the manifold on 80 bar, a higher operating
pressure could be selected. By selection of a higher operating pressure, both the cost and
volume consumption of the liquefaction process will be lower, than in case of a operating
pressure of 50 bar. This is because, a higher pressure in the liquefaction process will be
achieved without implication of an compressor. The consequence of using a higher op-
erating pressure is, that after some years the pressure in the well will be lower than the
operating pressure, and it will be harder to separate the lighter hydrocarbons from the
heavier. This leads to either a compressor/pump at the manifold inlet has to be applied or
using of gas lifting to maintain the higher operating pressure and that a higher amount of
lighter hydrocarbons will remain in the condensate. If a higher pressure at the slug catcher
were used then it should be excepted that the power consumption of the compressor in the
separating stage would increase. This is because of a larger amount of lighter hydrocar-
bons have to be returned, and the higher pressure ratio. The overall eﬀect of increasing
the operating pressure has not been investigated but this could lead to a higher amount
of the condensate will go through the fractionation columns. Since pure Octane has a
larger economical value than condensates, then it could be worth to increase the operating
pressure.
In modelling of the acid gas removal and dehydrate systems, both an absorption process
and a molecular sieves has been used which increase the amount of liquid content in the
138
==PAGE:148==
system. With 600 tonne/h liquid for the acid gas removal system as shown in ﬁgure 3.34
and with 28.13 tonne/h for dehydrate system as shown in ﬁgure 3.42, a better choice could
be to remove the absorption process, and only using a molecular sieves in both cases. This
is because of the stabilization of the vessel will be improved due to the reduced sloshing
of liquid. In the LNG FPSO designed by CB& I and in Shell Prelude FLNG the acid gas
removal system is modelled as an amine absorption system as in case of the model, but
the dehydration column uses a molecular sieve[46] [4]. The advantages of not using only a
molecular sieves for the dehydrate system is, that the molecular sieve can be in operation
for a longer time before it has to be regenerated.
Three fractionation columns have in the model been implicated to divide the NGL stream,
into a C1/C2 mixture, a C3/C4 mixture, a pure C4 and a pure C8 mixture as shown in
ﬁgure 3.49, 3.52 and 3.53. This is as in case of Prelude FLNG, where the NGL stream
will be fractionated into C2, C3, C4 and a light condensate product. The number of frac-
tionation columns could have been reduced to only consisting of the butanizer since the
overhead gas stream then will consist of a mixture of C1,C2,C3 and C4 in where C4 possess
the largest share of the stream. Then the overhead gas stream could be sold as LPG while
the bottom liquid stream would be pure C8. The solution of only using a butanizer would
require less space than the modelled fractionation process.
Through modelling of the pretreatments and fractionation operations, such as the separa-
tion stage and dehydration stage, all the loss and ﬂow of material was found as shown in
ﬁgure 3.54. This shows that a small loss of methane of only 0.02 tonne per hour occurs
through the separation stage. This was excepted since the objective of the separating stage
was to recover as much Methane as possible without violating the constrain on maximum
5 percent Octane in the treated gas. The 0.15 tonne/h of methane loss though the acid
gas removal system was unexpected. This is because the average methane emission from
an acid gas removal unit is at 6000 cubic feet per day which is approximate 71 tonne/day
[47] by assuming a density of 422 kg per cubic meters. With a hourly loss of 0.15 tonne
methane, only 3.5 tonne/day is lost from the system and this could be recycled back to
the separating stage or the inlet of the absorber column. The small loss of methane in the
dehydration system only possess of 0.72 tonnes per day, which can be recycled back to the
inlet of the dehydration column or earlier in the system. A similar trend for C2, C3 and
C4 is found in the acid gas removal system and the dehydration system in where a small
amount is lost and can be recycled. The largest loss of 10.34 tonne/h methane was found
to be in the liquefaction process, after the expansion valve due to nitrogen rejection. The
large share of loss of methane is an indicator of that the liquefaction process should be
cooling the LNG to a lower temperature than 110 K. Another option is to include a ni-
trogen expander cycle after the liquefaction process for condensing of the methane content
at the ﬂash gas outlet or the nitrogen and methane gases could be recycled back to the
inlet of the liquefaction process. Due to the large power consumption of the liquefaction
process, the gases could be used to supply some of the gas turbines.
The model is modelled with an intake gas stream deducted the water stream at the man-
ifold at 17.38 million standard cubic meters per day (scmd). The WTG ratio and gas
composition used in the model comes from the hammerfest LNG plant with a intake gas
ﬂow of 20.8 million scmd. Though modelling of the plant, it was found that by using the
used settings, the vessel will be capable of producing 4.28 mtpa LNG, 0.68 mtpa conden-

4.1.2
Linkage of the liquefactions model
At the NGLE system, it has been examined if it could pay oﬀto increase the pressure before
the liquefaction process. In this study the DMR liquefaction process was used. The studies
shows that an increased pressure, results in an increase in the total cost and a decrease in
the total volume as shown in ﬁgure 3.57. It was excepted that the volume would decrease
since the latent heat decreases with a higher pressure resulting in a lower refrigerant stream
needed. In case of the location of the compressors, it was found that, a location at the
NG node, will give the largest increase at the lowest cost as shown in ﬁgure 3.58. The
cause of why the volume consumption and cost of a TC location is much higher than a NG
location, is the larger mass ﬂow through the compressor. With a location at the TC node,
the mass ﬂow though the compressor will be at 243.8 kg/s which is almost twice as much
as in case of a location at the NG node. It should also be noted that by compressing the
stream with an inlet temperature of approximate 20 degrees the power consumption and
outlet temperature will be much higher than in the case of the NG location where the inlet
temperature is at only -34.15 degrees. In the further analysis of the liquefaction processes,
a liquefaction pressure of 50 bar is used. At the time of the investigation of the liquefaction
process, it could not be decide if a cost increase on 81.06 mio USD could justify a reduction
in volume consumption on 5.4 m3 in case of a compressor at TC. In case of a compressor
at the NG node, the cost increase will be on 24.24 mio USD for a volume reduction on
8.1 m3. As perspective, the volume consumption at 50 bar is calculated to be at 197.24 m3.
It can be concluded that if a reduction in the volume is desired, then the location of the
compressor should be at the NG location rather than at the TC location.

4.1.3
Optimization scenarios
One of the the aims for this study is to investigate which liquefaction processes are appro-
priate to be including in a FLNG/FPSO vessel. It is relevant to evaluate the models based
on the achieved results though the optimization scenarios. In case of the optimizations
results, the costs and exergy scenario did in most case provide a similar cost and exergy
eﬃciency. This make sense since the only purpose of the Hyprotech SQP optimizer in case
of the exergy scenario was to reduced the power consumption of the system since the power
needed for liquefaction is constant throughout the optimization. Hyprotech SQP optimizer
will then reduce the mass ﬂow through the compressors or the pressure ratio. Because of
the fuel and compressor costs represent approximately 40 percent of the total costs each
as shown in ﬁgure 3.9 then a low cost rate will be achieved. In case of the cost scenario,
the Hyprotech SQP will optimizing the process with respect to minimizing of the costs,
and when the fuel and compressor costs represent 40 percent of the total cost each, then
then mass ﬂow and compressors will be reduced, and the results will be similar the exergy
optimization.
The volume optimization scenario was used for the further analysis because of the limita-
tion on the available space at the vessel. There should be noted that through a combination
between the cost and volume scenario, an optimum could be found in where both the cost
and the volume were low. This have not been done in the project because a simulating of
this combination results in computer crashes.
An important aspect to be noted in use of Hypertech SQP optimizations tools is that
there is no guarantee that the founded optimum is the global optimum, in some case, a
better optimum was found by modifying the start values of the optimization variables. An
example of this is shown in ﬁgure 3.4, ﬁgure 3.6 and ﬁgure 3.8. In this cases the cost of
the exergy optimization is lower than in the cost optimization, but it should be noted that
the results is much similar, so it should not be excepted that the problem will change the
results signiﬁcant. It could be discussed if all combinations of the optimizations variables
were investigated that the global optimum would then be found with certainty, but the time
needed by the optimizer would be enormously since in some of the liquefactions process,
there exist more than 10 optimizations variables.
Other studies [48] have noticed this
problem too.
Cost distribution
Through the cost optimization scenario it was found that the N2-CH4 has a cost more
than twice as high than all the others liquefaction processes as shown in ﬁgure 3.9. Since
only 20.8 percent of the transmitted energy is used for cooling of NG as shown in ﬁgure
3.4 the high amount of self-cooling is the primary reason to the high costs. This is because
a larger mass ﬂow of the refrigerant is needed to provide the same cooling duty to the
NG stream, which aﬀect the fuel cost, drivers cost and compressors. In case of the other
liquefaction processes the Niche and Tricycle have a signiﬁcant higher cost than the other
mixed refrigerant processes and the N2-C2-C3 process. This is because these two processes,
operates with pure refrigerants as gases through the whole liquefaction process. Due to no
condensing of the refrigerants, a lower heat transfer coeﬃcient exist, and a lower exergy
eﬃciency is achieved due to the temperature grid through the heat exchanger as shown in
ﬁgure 3.3 and 3.2. This increase the amount of refrigerant needed in the process, which
aﬀect the cost of the compressor and demand for fuel. That the cost of DMR, C3-MR and
cascade process is lower than the other processes is excepted due to the higher eﬃciency,
141
==PAGE:151==
and lower refrigerant ﬂows. Other studies have noticed that the capital cost of the mixed
refrigerant system should be excepted to be approximate 10 percent lower than the reverse
brayton systems[49], and that the mixed refrigerant systems would have a lower energy
consumptions due to the lower mass ﬂow [21]. The N2C2C3 process was found to has a cost
rate of 6 million USD higher than the Cascade, DMR and C3MR process. The analysis
shows, that if the cost should be reduced then the size and consumption of the compres-
sors should be given special attention since a reduction of the compressor consumption will
reduced the cost of the drivers and fuel.
Though analysis of the cost rate it was found that the cost rate per kg LNG/s was very
depending on the selected NG ﬂow. Figure 3.11 shows that by using a realistic LNG ﬂow
of example 135 kg/s rather than the push factor, the cost rate of the DMR process reduced
with approximate 34 percent. A similar reduction in the cost rate can be found in case of
all the liquefaction process except the Prico process where the cost rate is much sensitive
for change in mass ﬂow. Since the cost rate is more or less stable at mass ﬂows from 10 to
150 kg lng/s, the cost rate without the push factor is much more reliable for estimating the
cost of a liquefaction process. It should be noted that the cost of the two-phase separators
have not been included in the cost analysis which may have a signiﬁcant eﬀect on the cost
rate of the C3MR process with four two-phase separators. In case of the DMR process,
only one two-phase separator is used and this should be held in mind when comparing
the liquefactions processes. Through pinch analysis the NG composition is changed to be
the same as at the gas outlet of the NGLE system. By comparing ﬁgure 3.11 with table
3.10 the diﬀerence in cost rate is not signiﬁcant in case of the DMR, C3MR, Prico and the
Cascade process, but the cost increase a lot in case of the Niche and the N2CH4 process.
This means that these models are very sensitivity for shift in NG compositions.
The payback time of the liquefaction process shows that most of the liquefaction process

years for the N2CH4 process based on ﬁgure 3.11. In case of the Prico process, the cost
was very sensitive for changes in mass ﬂows, so the payback time is assumed to be kept at
1.79 years. An other study in where a economic analysis of a LNG FPSO have been made
by using probabilistic approach, it was found that the LNG FPSO would have a payback
time between 1.45 and 5 years with 55.89 percent certainty [50]. Since the liquefaction
process represent approximate 27 percent of the total capital cost [22], the payback time
from the liquefaction process must be near the calculated payback time in this model if

process, the cost per annual tonne LNG is lower than a onshore liquefaction plant. This
is expected since cost of an oﬀshore liquefaction vessel will be able to be constructed at
a shipyard and then be sailed to the location. In case of an onshore liquefactions plant
which typically is located in an isolated place, then the workforces and materials have to
be transported to the location, which is much more expensive [21]. An other economic
analysis of a FLNG FPSO vessel using probabilistic approach has estimated the total cap-
ital cost to be at 2.2 billion USD [50]. By assuming the liquefaction process poses of 27
percent of the total cost [22], a annual cost per tonne LNG can be calculated to be at
114 USD which is half so high as the DMR process. Regarding other FLNG projects, the
1.2 mtpa Petronas FLNG 1 which is under construction is estimated to has a capital cost
of 1500 mio USD[52]. By assuming that the liquefaction process poses 27 percent of the
capital cost, then Petronas FLNG 1 vessel has a cost of 337.5 dollar per annular tonne
LNG. In case of the 1.5 mtpa Petronas FLNG 2 vessel the capital cost is estimated to be
at 2000 mio USD which gives a cost of 360 dollar per annular tonne LNG which is inside
the range of the modelled DMR, C3MR and Cascade process.
It can be concluded that DMR, C3MR and Cascade process can be recommend if a low
cost is desired.
Volume distribution
Figure 3.12 shows that the volume of the coolers possess the largest share of the volume
consumption for all liquefactions process except the Prico and Tricycle process. The Prico
and Tricycle process were found to have the largest volume consumption in where the heat
exchanger possesses the largest share. Regarding the Tricycle process one of the primary
reason is that the heat transfer coeﬃcient of a gas is lower than a ﬂuid, and since the
Tricycle process operates only in the gas phase, a larger heat exchanger is needed. In case
of the Prico process, ﬁgure 3.1 shows the temperature grid through the heat exchanger. It
shows that only 22 percent of the applied cooling duty goes for cooling of the NG stream
which increase the needed mass ﬂow. Then the optimizer has found a balance between
the volume of the compressors and drivers with the volume of the heat exchanger. The
liquefaction process with the lowest volume consumption rate was found to be the Niche
process with approximate 1 cubic meters per kg lng per second followed by the N2-C2-C3
and DMR process with 1.14 and 1.27 cubic meters per kg lng per second. The volume
consumption rate was found to be constant for all mass ﬂows, so long the ratio between
the refrigerants and NG ﬂow was kept. With a mass ﬂow of 135 kg/s the total volume of
the Niche process is found to be at approximate 131 m3. It should be noted that piping,
refrigerant storage and other safety components is not included. In case of the volume of
the DMR process and especially the C3MR process it will be at 171.5 m3 and 239.12 m3
and it should be excepted that the volume will be larger since the volume of the two-phase
separators is not included. By comparing ﬁgure 3.12 with table 3.10 the changes in NG
composition don’t aﬀect the volume consumption signiﬁcant for the DMR, Cascade and
the C3MR process. It is found that the Niche process and particular the Prico and N2CH4
process is sensitive for changes in NG compositions since the volume increase to 181.9 m3
in case of the Niche process, 554.3 m3 in case of the Prico process, and 468.3 m3 in case
of the N2CH4 process. There should be noted that the volume needed for storage of the
refrigerants is not included in the calculations.

Exergy destruction and eﬃciency
In case of the exergy eﬃciency table 3.9 shows as excepted that the mixed refrigerant
liquefaction processes have the largest exergy eﬃciency. This is because of the option to ﬁt
the temperature grid of the cold refrigerants to the temperature grid of the hot refrigerants.
The N2CH4 process have the lowest exergy eﬃciency. This is as expected since ﬁgure 3.4
shows the large temperature diﬀerences between the hot and cold composition stream. An
other study [14] has emphasized that a low exergy eﬃciency in the N2CH4 process should
be excepted due to the large temperature diﬀerence in the heat exchangers. Regarding the
Prico process, ﬁgure 3.13 shows that the largest loss in energy in the system is due to the
coolers, compressors and heat exchangers. This is conﬁrmed through an other study [53]
in where the compressors possess 31.55 percent, the coolers possess 32.19 percent and the
LNG heat exchangers possess 27.13 percent of the total exergy destruction. An other study
[54] found an exergy eﬃciency between 39.6 percent and 42.3 percent through minimizing
of the power consumption which is comparable to the eﬃciency of 40.69 achieved through
exergy optimization scenario. In connection with the exergy eﬃciency of the Niche and
Tricycle process the reason of the low exergy eﬃciency is obvious shown in ﬁgure 3.3 for
the temperature grid through the heat exchanger for the tricycle process. This shows that
the temperature diﬀerence between the hot and cold stream increases through the heat
exchanger due to no condensing of refrigerants. Since the tricycle operates with only single
phase gases as refrigerants then a temperature grid through the heat exchanger can not
be achieved. The low eﬃciency of the Tricycle has been noted in an other study[15]. The
exergy analysis shows that the largest exergy destruction are in most cases due to the
compressors and the coolers and in some case due to the LNG heat exchangers. This is
conﬁrmed in other studies [55] [56] in where both the compressor and heat exchangers and
in some case the coolers [13] were found to poses the largest exergy destruction.
It can be concluded that the DMR, C3MR, Cascade and Prico process which is mixed re-
frigerants processes, has the highest eﬃciencies, which results in a lower power consumption
as shown in ﬁgure 3.10. In addition, it can be conclude that the largest exergy destruction
in most cases is found in the compressors and coolers.

recovered. The optimum is to design the heat exchanger network for the whole system on
the same time if maximum thermal energy recovery is desired.
Recommendation
In connection with the recommendation of the most suitable candidate for application on
a FLNG vessel, the selection criteria should be taking in account. Most of the criteria to
be considered are shown in table 3.10 and 3.9. Further should the ﬂexibility, reliability of
the process, and the option to merge the thermal energy demand with the overall system
also be considered.
Through the analysis, it is found that the N2CH4 process is not recommend for oﬀshore
application, since this process had one of the worst volume rate of 1.87 m3 per kg LNG
per second through the volume optimization, and a volume rate of 3.452 m3 by matching
146
==PAGE:156==
the composition with the NGLE system. The N2CH4 is found to have cost of 1398.7 USD
per annual tonne LNG which is higher than the typical value of an onshore plant [51]. In
addition it has a large size of liquid content due to the low eﬃciency of the system (a larger
amount of refrigerant is needed to provide the needed cooling duty). At last, through the
pinch analysis it was found that the energy recovering of the waste heating can not justify
the high power consumption of the process, since a large fraction of the waste heat from
the gas turbines is released to the environment. The advantages of the N2CH4 process is
the low component number, which make it more simple to operate.
Since the volume consumption is one of the most important criteria because of the space
restriction, the Tricycle process is not recommended since it has the largest volume con-
sumption and one of the largest component numbers, and is not a proven technology in
liquefaction of NG. The advantages are that the cost rate of the tricycle process is a slightly
lower than the Niche process, and that it do not have any liquid content.
The Prico process has the lowest component number, but in both table 3.10 and table 3.9
the volume consumption was found to be very high together with the cost rate compared
to the other mixed refrigerant processes. The power consumption in table 3.10 and the GC
curve in ﬁgure 3.78 indicates that a larger part of the produced LNG will be used for power
generation and supplementary ﬁring compared to the other mixed refrigerant processes.
Since the volume of the prico process is much higher than the Niche process and the cost
is slightly lower then the Prico process is not the best suited liquefaction process.
The N2-C2-C3 has not been analysed completely, and has in table 3.9 the second lowest
volume rate with a volume rate of 10 percent lower and a cost rate 1.35 times higher
than the DMR process. Since the liquefaction process is unproven and it has a very large
component number and a higher cost it can neither be rejected as a suitable process for
oﬀshore application, but likely it will not be suitable compared with the alternatives.
The Niche process was found to have the lowest volume consumption in both the optimiza-
tion scenario and when matching with the NGLE system. The advantages of this system
are that no liquid content is used, and the low component number which make it simple
and easy to operates. The cost analysis shows that it is much more expensive than the
other mixed refrigerant processes. The pinch analysis and the cost analysis shows that it
has a higher fuel consumption but through matching of the thermal energy requirements,
no addition of supplementary ﬁring was needed. If the volume is the most important cri-
teria then the Niche process is recommend to be suitable for oﬀshore application.
The Cascade, DMR, C3MR processes are much similar, all having a low cost and volume
and a high eﬃciency. The C3MR process has the highest volume consumption of these
three processes and highest component number, and due to it’s use of four two-phase
separators the system is more aﬀected by motion and a larger risk of liquid sloshing exist,
which makes it less suitable compared with the other two. The DMR process and Cascade
process are both suitable for oﬀshore application. Since the Cascade process use Ethylene
as refrigerant which is not able to recovery from the NG stream and has a higher volume
consumption and costs rate through the optimization the DMR process should be selected
rather than the Cascade process due to the lower volume and use of refrigerants, which
exist in the NG stream.

Chapter 5
Conclusion
5.1
Conclusion
The comparison of the power consumption, volume consumption and costs of the DMR,
Niche, Prico, N2CH4, Cascade, C3MR, Tricycle and N2C2C3 liquefaction process, indi-
cates that the DMR process is the best all-round liquefaction process. This is because the
optimization cases show that the DMR process has a volume rate of 1.27 m3 per kg LNG
per second, a cost rate of 14.19 million USD per kg LNG/s and an exergy eﬃciency of 46.52
percent and a payback time of approximately 0.79 years for a natural gas composition of
87.5 C1, 5.5 C2, 2.1 C3, 0.3 i-C4, 0.5 n-C4, 0.1 i-C5 and 4 mole percent Nitrogen and a
mass ﬂow of 135 kg LNG/s. The Niche process was found to be the most eﬃciency process
with respect to minimizing the volume consumption. This is because it has a volume rate
of 0.97 m3 per kg LNG per second a cost rate of 24.94 million USD per kg LNG/s and an
exergy eﬃciency of 28.27 percent and a payback time of approximately 1.34 years when
using the same NG composition and mass ﬂow as for the DMR process.
Matching the liquefaction processes with the gas plant, in where the liquefaction process
provide cooling duty to the NGLE system and the liquefaction of a NG stream consist of
88.5 C1, 5.48 C2, 2.58 C3, 0.7 C4, 2.74 N2, it was found to have a signiﬁcant impact on the
cost and volume rate. In this case, the DMR process has a total cost rate of 17.4 million
USD per kg LNG per second and a volume rate of 1.48 m3 per kg lng per second. In case
of the Niche process the cost was increased to 43.75 million USD per kg LNG per second,
and the volume rate increased to 1.341 m3 per kg LNG per second. In addition, it was
found that the DMR process, has a consumption of 145.5 MW of electricity, which is lower
than the consumption of the Niche process with 344.6 MW.
By matching the liquefaction processes with the gas plant it was found that the gas plant
will be capable of producing 4.28 mtpa LNG, 0.68 mtpa condensate, 0.0035 mtpa C1/C2
mixture, 0.017 C3/C4 mixture, 0.064 iC4/nC4 mixture and 0.65 mtpa pure Octane. In
case of the DMR liquefaction process, the capital cost was found to possess of 230.39 USD
per annual tonne LNG. In case of the Niche process the capital cost was found to possess
of 623.38 USD per annual tonne LNG by assuming the demand of fuel is equal to the DMR
process. Because of this, the capital cost per annual tonne LNG is expected to be higher
for the Niche process due to a higher power consumption.
Through the Pinch analysis it has been concluded that both liquefaction processes was
suitable for implementation with respect to utilization of waste heat.
With respect to
maximum recovering of thermal energy the DMR process should be selected rather than
148
==PAGE:158==
the Niche process. This is because 259.13 MW of thermal energy from the gas turbine in
case of the Niche process will be released to the environment. In case of the DMR process,
supplementary ﬁring will be needed to provide enough thermal energy to the system.
Bases on this, the DMR process is found to be the most suitable candidate for application
on a FLNG vessel, as long as there is enough space for it’s slightly higher volume consump-
tion and if the impact of motion of the vessel proved to have negligible eﬀect.
Through modelling of the gas plant and the liquefaction processes, potentials for improve-
ments were found. The production capacity of LNG can be improved with 0.09 mtpa by
recovering the loss of methane from the two-phase separator after the liquefaction pro-
cess. The volume of the liquefaction process can be decreased with 8.1 m3 by addition
of a compressor after pre-cooling of the NG and compressing the stream to 68 bar. The
largest loss of energy in the liquefaction processes was found to be the compressors and
coolers which in most cases each possess of above 30 percent of the total exergy destruction.
5.1.1
Suggestion on futher work
For further work, the dimensions and layout of the liquefaction processes should be con-
sidered with respect to the available space on a FLNG vessel. In addition it could be of
interest to investigate how changes of the pressure through the gas plant will aﬀect the
overall power consumption and purity of the produced LNG stream, and the options to
recover loss of materials found in this master thesis. In addition a part load analysis should
be conducted to investigate the sensitivity of the plants with respect to changes in mass
ﬂows, temperature, pressure and compositions of the intake gas at the manifold. Since it
was found that the compressors and coolers possesses the largest exergy loss in the liq-
uefaction processes it could be interesting to investigate how these components could be
improved.
