Abstract
Microplastic (MP) is an environmental contaminant of emerging concern due to suspected
negative eﬀects on soil ecosystems and human health. MP pollution of the soil compart-
ment has arguably been overlooked by the massive attention directed towards the marine
environment in recent years. However, the declining health condition of soils has begun
to receive increased scientiﬁc and political attention. Soils receive large inputs of MP
from agricultural practices, abrasion of tyres, washing of synthetic textiles and littering.
Atmospheric deposition has furthermore been postulated to be an important pathway for
long-range transport of MP to soils. Determining the extent of MP pollution in soil is
a prerequisite for the assessment of potential consequences for soil ecosystems and hu-
man health. This project aims to investigate the inﬂuence of anthropogenic activities on
MP abundance and distribution in soil. Soil samples from areas of low and high human
activity were collected. An experimental protocol for MP analysis was developed based
on a literature review on analytical methods to extract, quantify and identify MP from
soil. The protocol consisted of 1) pretreatment with 10% hydrogen peroxide for 7 days
at room temperature for removal of organic matter, 2) density separation of MPs and
soil minerals using zinc chloride and 3) using the novel analytical method of laser direct
infrared (LDIR) chemical imaging for MP quantiﬁcation and identiﬁcation. MP analysis
was coupled with radioisotope analysis using Cs-137 and Pb-210 for determining the age
of soil layers. Soil dating analysis showed that a depth of 10-15 cm could correspond to
the beginning of the 1960s for both sites. Below 25 cm depth it was estimated that soil
layers were minimum 100 years old. The distribution of isotope Cs-137 for the area of
high human activity was approximately uniform indicative of a disturbed soil, while the
distribution for the area of low human activity resembled that of an undisturbed soil.
The results showed a decreasing trend in MP concentration with depth for the area of low
human activity. For the area of human activity the highest concentration was observed
beneath the top soil at 12-14 cm depth (68-338 particles g–1dw). MP was detected in
45-50 cm depth for both locations, however the identiﬁed plastic types were similar to
those detected from background contamination. Thus, it was not possible to conﬁrm if
the presence of MP was due to contamination during laboratory work, disturbance of
soil layers or vertical migration of MP. More polymer types were detected in the area of
high human activity (12 types) compared to the area of low human activity (6 types)
indicative of multiple sources of pollution. The results did not provide suﬃcient evidence
to diﬀerentiate areas of high and low human activity with regards to MP abundance.
However, the larger diversity of plastic types found in the area of high human activity
provided clear indications of multiple sources of MP pollution. This project contributes
with important insights to methodological challenges related to MP analysis and provides
ﬁrsthand knowledge on the MP abundance of Danish soils.

Sammenfatning
Mikroplastik (MP) er et miljøfremmed stof mistænkt for at have negative eﬀekter på
økosystemer og menneskets sundhed. Forurening med MP i jordmiljøet har stået i skyggen
af det store fokus, der har været på det marine miljø. I de sidste par år er der kommet
større politisk og videnskabeligt fokus jordmiljøet sundhed. Væsentlige kilder til MP i
jord er landbruget, slid af bildæk, vask a syntetiske tekstiler og henkastning af aﬀald.
Atmosfærisk deposition er mistænkt for at være en vigtig transportmekanisme for MP.
Bestemmelse af MP forurening er nødvendigt før konsekvenserne kan undersøges. For-
målet med dette projekt er at undersøge menneskers indﬂydelse på indholdet af MP i
jord. Jordprøver fra områder med hhv. høj og lav menneskelig aktivitet blev analyseret.
En laboratorie protokol for MP-analyse blev udviklet baseret på eksisterende litteratur
omhandlede analytiske metoder til at ekstrahere, kvantiﬁcere og identiﬁcere MP. Frem-
gangsmåden bestod af: 1) oxiderering af organisk materiale ved brug af 10 % hydrogen
peroxid i syv dage ved stuetemperatur, 2) densitet separering ved brug af zinc klorid til
at adskille MP fra jord partikler og 3) kvantiﬁcering og identiﬁcering af MP ved brug af
analyseredskabet Laser Direct Infrared (LDIR) Chemical Imaging. Analyse af MP blev
koblet med analyse af de radioaktive isotoper Cs-137 og Pb-210 for jordaldersbestem-
melse. For begge områder viste radioaktivitetsanalysen, at jorddybde ved 15 cm svarede
til begyndelsen af 1960’erne. Jordlag dybere end 25 cm blev vurderet til at være omkring
100 år gammel. Fordelingen af Cs-137 for lokationen påvirket af høj menneskelig aktivitet
var uniform, hvilket indikerer at jorden er forstyrret. Fordelingen for lokationen påvirket
af lav menneskelig aktivitet lignede en uforstyrret jord. Resultaterne fra MP-analysen
viste en tendens med koncentration af MP med jorddybde for området påvirket af lav
menneskelig aktivitet. Anderledes var det for området påvirket af høj menneskelig ak-
tivitet, hvor den højeste koncentration af MP blev målt i 12-14 cm dybde (68-388 partikler
per gram tørstof). MP kunne måles i det dybeste lag (45-50 cm) for begge lokationer.
Dog i så lave koncentrationer, at det ikke kunne vurderes om MP stammede fra jorden,
forurening under laboratoriearbejdet eller vertikal transport af MP gennem jorden. Flere
plastiktyper blev identiﬁceret i området påvirket af høj menneskelig aktivitet (12 typer)
sammenlignet med området påvirket af lav menneskelig aktivitet (6 typer). Dette kan
ses som en indikation for hvordan mennesker påvirker MP i jorden. Dette projekt tilve-
jebringer vigtig viden omkring udfordringer vedrørende analyse af MP og kendskab til
fordeling af MP i danske jorde. Forurening med MP er et miljøproblem af global karak-
ter og vi opfordrer til mere forskning inden for dette område. Vi vil også understrege
vigtigheden af politisk handling for at miniminere udledningen af MP.

Introduction
Plastic has become a valuable material for humankind and has even achieved status as a
consumer necessity. It has gained immense popularity across practically every industry
due to its variety of applications, low cost and long durability. These highly valued prop-
erties are ironically also the reason why plastic is considered a widespread and persistent
environmental pollutant of growing concern. According to the latest OECD report on
the global plastic lifecycle 9 % of plastic is recycled, 19 % is incinerated, while 50 %
is landﬁlled and 22 % is unmanaged plastic waste, where a large share ends up in the

results in continuous accumulation in the environment (Geyer et al. 2017; OECD 2022).
Plastic can be degraded biotically and abiotically, albeit the process occurs rather slowly.
Degradation of plastic depends strongly on plastic type and ambient environmental con-
ditions (Chamas et al. 2020). The physical breakdown of plastic material results in the
formation of small plastic fragments known as microplastics (MP). The classiﬁcation
scheme of plastic fragments according to size is quite ambiguous in literature (Hartmann,
Hüﬀer, et al. 2019). This report accepts the deﬁnition of MP having a size of <1mm. A
large proportion of research within the area of MP has focused on aquatic environments in
the last years, while the soil compartment only recently has gained increasing attention.
The presence of MP have been detected practically everywhere. Sources of MP in soil
includes ﬁbres generated from washing of synthetic textiles, tyre abrasion, use of plastic
mulch in agriculture and littering. Pathways of MP to the environment include applica-
tion of sludge and compost and use of untreated irrigation water, which are among the
major culprits for MP pollution of agricultural land (Li et al. 2020; Jia et al. 2022; Bläsing
et al. 2018). Air has also been identiﬁed as an important pathway enabling long-range
transport and subsequent atmospheric deposition of MPs (Dris et al. 2016; Bläsing et al.
2018).
MP pollution is suspected to negatively eﬀect soil functions, alter compositions of soil
microbial communities and reduce plant performance. The risk associated with pollu-
tion MP of soils is furthermore not constrained to the presence of the polymer itself.
Plastic usually contains additives, which have potential to leach into the surroundings
and exert toxic eﬀects on soil organisms. In addition, MP can function as sorption sites
for hydrophobic contaminants leading to the immobilization of contaminants or mitigate

MP analysis of soil is still an emerging ﬁeld of research and no standard laboratory protocol

1.1
Aim and Hypothesis
This project has the overall aim to expand our knowledge on MP in soil focusing on
currently available methods for soil sampling, sample preparation and laboratory analyses
for extraction and identiﬁcation. Methodological challenges during the experimental work
will be discussed including suggestions for improvements.
Three locations are selected for MP analysis. For the purpose of the hypotheses listed
below, the sampling locations are classiﬁed according to their presumed degree of human
activity. One sampling location is chosen for each of the following categories: The soil
is from an area of 1) low human activity, 2) low human activity near an area of high
human activity or 3) high human activity. High human activity is deﬁned as heavily
populated areas such as urban areas or areas highly aﬀected by humans such as highways,
industrial districts and event sites.
Low human activity is deﬁned by areas receiving
minimal human intervention, such as undisturbed forests, areas that have little to no
infrastructure or constructions. The following hypotheses will contribute to ﬁlling the
knowledge gaps concerning microplastic contamination of soils caused by human activity:
1. The abundance of microplastic in soils is higher in areas characterized by high human
activity than in areas with low human activity as microplastics are generated from
production, use and disposal of plastic products
2. The abundance of microplastic in soils decrease with increasing soil depth reﬂecting
the onset of industrial plastic production in the 1950s followed by an exponential
growth in plastic production, use and disposal
3. Microplastic is detected in low concentrations in areas with low human activity as
microplastic can be transported by air and subsequent atmospheric deposition is
expected to be a non-negligible pathway into the environment

Plastic production and plastic types
Plastic is a material made of synthetic polymers known for being extremely versatile and
inexpensive. The properties of plastic include long-term durability, resistance towards
heat and degradation and being non-conductive (Thompson et al. 2009). The raw material
used for production of plastic is primarily derived from fossil fuels and to a lesser extent
from biomass (Geyer 2020). Synthetic polymers are distinguished from natural polymers
in the way they are created.
Natural polymers are the result of biological processes,
whereas synthetic polymers are artiﬁcially produced and in most cases non-degradable.
A common misconception about plastic derived from biomass is that it automatically
inherits the biodegradable property of the raw material used (Geyer 2020). The name
only refers to the material used and is therefore not necessarily biodegradable.
The
numerous types of plastic each exhibits diﬀerent characteristics making the materials

packaging industry is responsible for the largest share of plastic used with 36%. Next is
the construction sector with 16% and thirdly the textile industry with 14% (Geyer 2020).

2.1.1
Plastic additives
Chemical additives such as plasticizers, ﬂame retardents, stabilizers, pigments, antioxi-
dants and ﬁllers are typically combined with synthetic polymers to improve the function-
ality of the ﬁnal plastic products that are to serve a speciﬁc purpose (Hahladakis et al.
2018). Plastic producers are not legally obliged to inform what additives they use in their
products and in what amounts (Geissen 2022). In addition, they do not necessarily know
what the additives they use actually contain (Rillig 2022). Additives are either present as
free phase in the polymer or they are reactive, i.e. they become an integrated part of the
polymer. Most additives are present in the free phase, meaning they have the undesirable
ability to migrate towards the plastic surface and subsequently leach into the surround-
ings (Billings et al. 2021). Some additives are known to cause ecotoxicological eﬀects in
both plants and living organisms depending on the level of exposure (Billings et al. 2021;
Hahladakis et al. 2018; Bläsing et al. 2018). Additives released from plastic products in

2.2
Degradation of plastic
Plastic is mainly considered inert because of its large chemical structure. The resistance to
degradation is ironically also one of the reasons why plastic is considered an environmen-
tal pollutant. However, through wear and tear, either during use or in the environment,
or inﬂuence by environmental factors, plastic products can undergo physical deformations
and chemical alterations resulting in the release of small plastic fragments. These frag-
ments are typically referred to as microplastics in the literature. Even though the term
MP has been recognized for decades dating back to the earliest mentioning in 1990 (Ryan
1990), there is still no oﬃcially accepted deﬁnition of its characteristic dimensions. Most
refer to MP as having a size between 1 μm and 5 mm, while others prefer to narrow
it down to 1 μm to 1 mm and refer to the interval 1-5 mm as mesoplastic (Hartmann,
Hüﬀer, et al. 2019). MP is classiﬁed as primary or secondary. Primary microplastics are
produced intentionally for use in certain products, whereas secondary microplastics are
fragmented pieces of larger plastic items (Hale et al. 2020).
Plastic can be degraded both biotically and abiotically. Abiotic processes often dominate
the initial stage of plastic degradation, while biotic processes occur in the ﬁnal stage
(Zeenat et al. 2021; Chamas et al. 2020).
The actual degradation rate of individual
plastic types depends on several environmental factors, the content of additives and the
shape and size of the plastic fragment which makes it diﬃcult to estimate (Chamas et al.
2020).
Abiotic
Abiotic degradation processes are either physical or chemical. Physical processes include
mechanical degradation, which refers to weakened mechanical properties of the plastic
material as a result of a loss in molecular weight leading to fragmentation, embrittlement
or cracking (Chamas et al. 2020). The resulting fragments have an increased surface-to-
volume ratio which can further enhance degradation rates as many reactions occur at the
plastic surface (Gewert et al. 2015).
Chemical processes refer to reactions which cause alterations of the polymer structure
on a molecular level (Chamas et al. 2020). Environmentally relevant pathways of degra-
dation include 1) photodegradation caused by exposure to UV-radiation resulting in 2)
oxidative degradation, where degradation on the plastic surface is initiated by light (photo-
oxidative) or the entire polymer structure by heat (thermo-oxidative) in the presence of
Page 7 of 79
==PAGE:23==

oxygen and 3) hydrolysis requiring the presence of water (Chamas et al. 2020; Gewert
et al. 2015). Plastic can also be degraded thermally. However, the temperatures in the
environment rarely or never reach the temperatures required for plastic degradation to
proceed this way. A potential exception from this is for example landﬁlls or composting
units, where temperatures can become signiﬁcantly high. Not all of the aforementioned
reaction types apply to all plastic types. The polymer structure decides what degradation
pathways are possible, while the initiation of the reactions greatly depends on factors such
as UV-radiation in particular as well as water and heat (Zeenat et al. 2021; Chamas et al.
2020; Gewert et al. 2015).
Biotic
Biotic degradation commonly referred to as biodegradation is caused by microorganisms.
Microorganisms are a broad term encompassing both bacteria, actinomycetes, and fungi.
Biodegradation of plastic usually proceeds abiotic processes, as the latter is responsible
for degrading larger polymer structures into more manageable sizes for microorganisms
to degrade and mineralize (Gewert et al. 2015).
The formation of functional groups
containing oxygen as a result of abiotic processes additionally makes plastic material
more susceptible to biological degradation (Gewert et al. 2015).
Plastic types are not equally susceptible to microbial attack due to diﬀerences in polymer
structure. Biodegradation rates are strongly dependent on ambient conditions including
temperature, pH, moisture, and nutrient availability. Additionally, microorganisms may
even exhibit diauxic growth, as synthetic polymers may not represent the most desired
source of energy (Meixner et al. 2020).

Air
MP can be transported by air and be subjected to dry and wet atmospheric deposition.
This can explain why MP is found in areas far from known sources of emission. The
ability of a MP particle to be transported by air depends on polymer type, morphology
and size as well as environmental factors, including wind speed (Kallenbach et al. 2022).
Fibers are more likely to be transported by air than fragments, and the same is true for
smaller and larger particles, respectively. MP ﬁbers with a length up to 400-500 μm and
fragments with the diameter below 350 μm have been reported to be found in atmospheric
deposition (Wright et al. 2020). Several polymer types have been detected as MP in air,
including ﬁbers of PET and polyamide (PA) and fragments of PP, PE, PET, PVC and
PS (Kallenbach et al. 2022; Wright et al. 2020).
Dris et al. (2016) investigated the atmospheric deposition of MP in an urban site near
Paris. They found an atmospheric deposition ﬂux of 110 ± 96 particles m–2day–1. The
fallout particles were mainly ﬁbers and at least 29% were partially of plastic origin. This
study highlights that atmospheric deposition should be considered an important pathway
for MP to enter the environment (Dris et al. 2016). Measuring atmospheric deposition in
rural areas far from MP sources can be used to create a baseline for atmospheric deposition
of MP and as such quantify atmospheric input of MP to soil. As an example, Ambrosini

Surface water
MP has been detected in surface waters including lakes, rivers and streams. Rivers and
streams can transport MP over long distances and ﬂooding of surface waters is a possible
pathway for MP to enter soil (Bläsing et al. 2018). An estimated 4,200 to 6,600 t year–1
of particles were generated from wear and tear of tires in Denmark. Tyres are composed
of rubbers, additives, and polymer types such as polyester and polyamide (Lassen et al.
2015). Tire particles were assessed to be the largest contributor of secondary MP to the
aquatic environment in Denmark. However, as the particles generated are mainly in the
size range from 2.5-10 μm, it is also likely that some of these particles enter the soil vi
street run-oﬀor via air (Lassen et al. 2015).
Agriculture
Some agriculture practices count the major source of plastic to the terrestrial environ-
ment including plastic mulching, irrigation and biosolids (Bläsing et al. 2018). In plastic
mulching large areas of agricultural land is covered by plastic ﬁlms to increase soil tem-
perature and thereby improve agriculture outputs. It is a practice used throughout the
world and generally expected to increase in the future. The polymer type commonly used
for this mainly include PE (Bläsing et al. 2018).
Irrigation with untreated wastewater can also introduce MP to soil. It is expected that
the use of untreated wastewater for irrigation will increase in the coming future due to
climate change and population growth (Bläsing et al. 2018).
Biosolids are another signiﬁcant source of MP in agricultural soil. Biosolids are treated
sewage sludge from wastewater treatment plants (WWTP) and are applied to land as a
fertilizer and soil improvement. However, WWTPs have been found to retain the majority
of plastics entering the plant. This plastic ends up in the biosolids and then in soils. It has
been estimated that 63,000 to 430,000 ton of plastic particles are added to agricultural
land annually with biosolids in Europe. This is more than what has been estimated to
enter global oceans. The most common plastic types found in biosolids are PE, PP, PVC,

2.3.2
Published data on microplastic abundance in soils
An increasing number of studies investigating MP in soils are being published. However
there are still challenges related to the geographical representativeness. Studies mainly
takes place in Central Europe and China. This gives some idea of MP concentration
in soils, but it is likely that global diﬀerences exist. It is therefore necessary to obtain
data covering the entire world (Weber et al. 2022). Majority of studies only investigate
the top layer in agricultural soils (Weber et al. 2022). Concentrations in agricultural soil
on a global scale have been found to vary from 0.34 up to 190,000 MP particles per kg
soil (Jia et al. 2022). Investigating only concentrations of MP in agricultural soil gives
limited information of environmental burdens of MP. Various units are used to report
MP results making comparisons between studies challenging. In majority of studies the
unit "particles per kg" is used (Dorau 2022). However, also units such as particles per
gram, particles per 10 g, varying in dry and wet weight, and particles per m2 have been
reported.
2.3.3
Eﬀects and potential consequences
It is has become increasingly evident that soils receive large inputs of MPs both directly,
such as application of sludge and compost on land, and indirectly, such as from atmo-
spheric deposition. Furthermore, degradation rates of plastic in soil are considerably low
compared to other environmental compartments due to lack of abiotic factors accelerating
degradation such as UV-radiation and higher temperatures (Chamas et al. 2020). As a
result, MP will continue to accumulate in the terrestrial environment if current practices
remain status quo (D. He et al. 2018). The consequences related to the presence of MP
in soil ecosystems are however still not well-researched (Rillig et al. 2019). But it may be
problematic to wait until research has a deﬁnitive answer, since investigating long-term

eﬀects correspondingly takes a long time. In the following sections the current knowledge

Soil functions
A healthy soil provide a myriad of valuable functions. They provide the foundation for
food production, store and ﬁlter water, act as carbon storage, enable nutrient cycling,
and provide habitats for soil organisms (Vogel et al. 2019). Improper management or
pollution of soils can have detrimental eﬀects on these functions. The introduction of
MPs in soil have been suggested to decrease soil bulk density and water holding capacity
potentially aﬀecting plant performance and microbial diversity (Rillig et al. 2019). While
some eﬀects can be considered positive, such as improved aeration, the presence of MPs
is still undesirable (Rillig et al. 2019). Liang et al. (2021) investigated how MP ﬁbers
aﬀected soil aggregate stability. They found that MP ﬁbers reduced aggregate stability
and enzymatic activity with addition of organic matter (OM), which could result in a
shift in microbial communities and lowered mineralization rates ultimately aﬀecting soil
functions.
However, they compared their results with similar studies and the eﬀects
seemed to be dependent on soil type (Liang et al. 2021).
MP can furthermore leach
additives that might exert toxic eﬀects on soil organisms and serve as a potential sorption
site for hydrophobic contaminants due to the hydrophobic properties of many plastic
types (Billings et al. 2021; Teuten et al. 2007; Hartmann, Rist, et al. 2017).
Uptake and accumulation in plants
If there is growing evidence that majority of soils are contaminated with plastic, a logical
concern would be that plants could be subjected to contamination as well. Some studies
have investigated the uptake of plastic from soil in terrestrial plants, more speciﬁcally
NPs . NPs have shown to be able to enter certain plants species through the root system
and leaf surface (Hua et al. 2023). However, the probability of MPs entering plants and
subsequently be transported through the plant vascular system are postulated to be lower
than for NPs since entry is, among other factors, dependent on particle size (Hua et al.
2023; Larue et al. 2021). Not enough information on uptake of MPs in plants currently
exist to provide insight into the risk of accumulation in plant tissues and subsequent
transfer to higher trophic levels (Larue et al. 2021). Another yet probably more concerning
risk factor concerns uptake and accumulation of plastic additives in plants. Wang et al.
(2015) investigated the content of phthalates in vegetables and found that there was in
fact a human health risk associated with dietary exposure, especially when considering
the continuous exposure over the long term.

Exposure to soil organisms
Ding et al. (2022) investigated the ecotoxicological eﬀects of ﬁve types of MP and their
extractable additives on a common terrestrial worm. The additives included a number of
heavy metals, polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons (PAHs) and other organic compounds.
They found that the presence of MP and the exposure of the worms to extractable addi-
tives negatively aﬀected the growth rate and the survival rate of the worms. This suggests
that extractable additives are responsible for negative consequences of soil fauna. Fur-
thermore, the eﬀects on soil fauna are speciﬁc to polymer types, probably due to the
diﬀerent additives used in diﬀerent polymers. The authors also found that MP changed
the gut microbiota, which could be one of the mechanisms of toxicity of MP in the soil
fauna (Ding et al. 2022).
2.4
Policy developments, regulations and public
awareness
The public is generally unaware of the immensely important environmental, societal and
economic values that are attributed to soil ecosystems. If current trends of soil degradation
continues, it could have disastrous consequences in the form of unprecedented declines in
ecosystem services, which society heavily relies on. In 2021 the European Commission
adopted the The EU Soil Strategy for 2030, which was created speciﬁcally to counteract
this alarming trend. Its purpose is to create a legal framework to prioritize soil protection
across the EU with the long-term goal of achieving healthy soils by 2050 also known as
the EU Soil Health Law (Commission 2021).
In the EU Soil Strategy, the European
Commission (2021) admits that:
"... the EU has so far not been able to equip itself with an adequate legal
framework granting soil the same level of protection as water, marine envi-
ronment and air. Yet, the need has become more pressing, and the knowledge
about soils and the recognition of their value has advanced signiﬁcantly over
the past years. The pressures, expectations and claims on soil have intensi-
ﬁed, while the climate and biodiversity crises are aggravating the situation.
We need healthy soils now more than ever."
According to the European Commission (2021), they will: "... by 2024, develop an EU
priority list for contaminants of major and/or emerging concern that pose signiﬁcant risks
for European soil quality." As signiﬁcant knowledge gaps exist on the impacts of MP on
the terrestrial environment, it is not clear whether MP will be considered a priority in
this regard.

Developing a regulatory framework for plastic materials is a complicated matter as plastic
cannot be viewed as a singular compound. The term plastic comprises several synthetic
polymer types combined with a variety of additives. The synthetic polymers and addi-
tives that make up a plastic product are regarded as separate compounds according to
REACH and thus subjected to diﬀerent regulations (Mitrano et al. 2020). While additives
typically needs to be registered under REACH, the physiochemical characteristics of most
synthetic polymers meet the criteria for polymers of low concern exempting them from
most regulations (Mitrano et al. 2020).
The European Chemicals Agency (ECHA) came with a proposal in 2019 to restrict the
use of primary MP added in products. The proposed restriction has to date not yet been
adopted (ECHA n.d.). Despite the lack of EU restrictions on MPs, some countries have
already taken action by banning products that contain intentionally added MP (ECHA
2019).
The environmental burden of plastic pollution is undoubtedly obvious to most people as
visible plastic litter is a common sight practically everywhere. Increased media awareness
on the consequences of plastic pollution, especially in the marine environment, has also
been a huge driver to incentivize political action with regards to combating the excessive
plastic use and amounts of generated plastic waste in our modern day society (Mitrano
et al. 2020). The prospect of turning current trends of increasing global plastic use around
is however still doubtful highlighting that demands for intensiﬁed political action are of
utmost importance.

This chapter provides theoretical insight into the preliminary steps of soil MP analysis,
and use of radioactive nuclides for soil dating.
3.1
Soil sampling for microplastic analysis
Majority of studies investigating MP contamination of soils focus mostly on laboratory
methods to extract the MP, while minor attention is given to the soil sampling process.
The importance of planning, performing and reporting the soil sampling is simply not
recognized. The absence of comprehensive information concerning the very ﬁrst step of
investigating MP contamination of soils complicates any future attempts to replicate and
compare results.
3.1.1
Planning of soil sampling strategies
An important aspect of planning a soil sampling campaign is to ensure spatial represen-
tativeness for a speciﬁed geographical area according to the research question (Möller
et al. 2020). The main obstacle of mapping out the MP contamination of soils is that
a homogeneous distribution cannot be assumed (Türk et al. 2022). Spatial variations in
MP concentrations in soils can be observed over shorter as well as longer distances, both
vertically and horizontally. This important issue has also been highlighted by Vollertsen
et al. (2019) in a recent report addressing the current knowledge gaps in MP research.
This further complicates the process of upscaling the results, as it is practically unknown
whether the soil sample is representative for the area in question or not. This problem is
especially true for small samples, as the variability can be too high to draw any conclusions
(Dorau 2022).
A common strategy for soil sampling of MP has yet to be reported. In this context, Möller
et al. (2020) argue that the ISO 18400-102 Standard can be applied for MP sampling.
This procedure provides: "...guidance on the development of site investigation strategies
and detailed guidance on the development of sampling strategies..." (standard 2017) with
regards to determining soil properties. Most studies do, however, not follow this standard.
Multiple sampling techniques are available for soil sampling including random, systematic,
Page 15 of 79
==PAGE:31==
Microplastic in Soil
CHAPTER 3. SOIL SAMPLING AND CHARACTERIZATION
stratiﬁed and compositing sampling (Junhao et al. 2021).
Random sampling involves
randomly selecting sampling sites within the area of interest (Jacobsen n.d.). Systematic
sampling is when the sampling is based on a deﬁned criteria. Stratiﬁed sampling involves
dividing the sampling into sub-groups, e.g. dividing the ﬁeld into smaller areas, which
are then sampled using random sampling. Lastly, composite sampling is taking multiple
samples close to each other at a site and then mixing them before analysis, so they
represent an average of that site (Möller et al. 2020). Each of these sampling approaches
has advantages and limitations and should be considered before choosing one of them.
Once the sampling technique is decided, the number of sampling points should be de-
termined. The research question should still be kept in mind to make sure the number
of sampling points ensure good geographically representativeness. There are no direct
guidelines as to how many sampling points should be included and this is also where large
variation is seen. Junhao et al. (2021) compared articles sampling agriculture soils for
MP analysis. Here the number of sampling points varied from 60 to 435.
Sampling depth is another parameter with large variation between articles. It is generally
accepted that MP is present in the highest concentration the top layer of the soil. The
sampling depths vary from only sampling the top 5 cm of the soils to 40 cm below the
surface. Most articles focus on the upper 30 cm and therefore there is limited information
on the MP content below 30 cm (Dorau 2022). It can be argued that MP contamination
should not exist in the deeper layers of the soil.
Lastly, how much soil to collect should also be considered. There are no clear guidelines
on how much mass is suitable for MP analysis. Junhao et al. (2021) reviewed papers
investigated MP in agriculture soils and found sampling masses ranging from 5 gram to 4
kg. This is a huge variation which of course will aﬀect the results of the study. According
to ISO Standard 18400, 500 g should be the minimum sample amount.
3.1.2
Performing soil sampling
Some aspects need consideration before initiating the physical execution of the soil sam-
pling. Equipment and containers used for sampling and storage of environmental samples
are not designed to suit the needs for MP investigations in particular. They are often
made of plastic materials, which can be suspected to contaminate samples during use. It
is therefore recommended to opt for equipment and containers made of other materials,
such as glass and metal. The sampling device should also be thoroughly cleaned between
usages to minimize the risk of cross-contaminating samples.

Soil characterization
Description of soil characteristics in relation to MP investigations is important but sel-

enables possible adaptation of the extraction method to the type of soil investigated. Im-

content, bulk density and soil texture (Thomas et al. 2020).

Using radioisotope analysis for dating of soil
This section describes the basic theoretical background for using radioactive nuclides for
soil dating.
3.3.1
Radioactivity in the environment
The geosphere contributes signiﬁcantly to natural background radiation. Radioactivity
originates from the decay of the primordial radionuclides Uran-238 (238U), Thorium-
Page 17 of 79
==PAGE:33==
Microplastic in Soil
CHAPTER 3. SOIL SAMPLING AND CHARACTERIZATION
232 (232Th), and Potassium-40 (40K) found in soils and rocks (International Atomic
Energy Agency 2003). Radionuclide concentrations depend on the type of soil and rock.
Human disturbance of natural environments has potential to enhance the decay rate
by increasing the availability of radionuclides to the biosphere. Human activities such
as mining or other extraction related processes can enhance radioactivity (International
Atomic Energy Agency 2003). Background radiation additionally stems from artiﬁcial
sources, which can be dated to back to speciﬁc historical events and occurrences that
have resulted in uncontrolled release of radioactive material into the atmosphere. This
includes tests of nuclear weapons and nuclear accidents (Foucher et al. 2021).
Naturally occurring radionuclide: Lead-210
Products from the decay chains of primordial radionuclides are considered natural ra-
dionuclides. Lead-210 (210Pb) is part of the decay chain for the primordial radionuclide
238U. 210Pb has a half-life of 22.3 years and is produced from 226Ra following the decay
of the short-lived gas 222Rn (Foucher et al. 2021; He et al. 1997). 210Pb is distinguished
between the terms supported and excess, where the former refers to the formation in rocks
and soil and the latter refers to the formation in the atmosphere following the escape of
the 222Rn gas. While the term excess is used as a reference for 210Pb used for dating
purposes in this project, both excess and unsupported are terms used interchangeably in
literature. The excess 210Pb is subjected to atmospheric deposition and deposited on the
surface of the soil. The distribution of excess 210Pb in soil is capable of determining the
age of soil layers that are up to 100-150 years (Foucher et al. 2021).
Artiﬁcially created radionuclide: Cesium-137
As a result of the nuclear weapons tests starting in the 1950’s the artiﬁcially created
radionuclide Cesium-137 (137Cs) was released into the atmosphere and deposited onto soils
around the world. Nuclear testing reached its peak in 1963, where the atmosphere received
the highest inputs of 137Cs (He et al. 1997). In the following decades, the atmosphere
received much lower inputs with the exception of short-term, local releases of 137Cs caused
by nuclear accidents in Chernobyl (Ukraine) in 1986 and in Fukushima (Japan) in 2011
which are mostly detected in Europe and the Northern Hemisphere (Foucher et al. 2021).
137Cs has a half-life of 30.2 years and if no new events cause the release of the isotope, it
will decay and completely disappear (He et al. 1997).
Distribution of radionuclides Cesium-137 and excess Lead-210 in soil
The presence of radioactive isotopes 137Cs and excess 210Pb in soils originate primarily
from wet atmospheric deposition. The initial distribution in soil is dependent on both the

precipitation rate and the adsorption mechanisms of the particular soil (He et al. 1997).
Several physical, physiochemical, and biological processes that occur in a soil system are
responsible for the subsequent redistribution of radionuclides. Examples include diﬀusion,

4.1.1
Density separation
Density separation (DS) is commonly chosen as a method for isolating MP from the
bulk matter of soil (Radford et al. 2021). The principle of the method is based on the
density (ρ) diﬀerence bwteen the heavier mineral fraction of the soil (ρ > 2.0 g cm–3) and
the lighter plastic fraction (ρ < 1.6 g cm–3). A salt solution functions as a separation
medium in which the MP particles will be isolated. The density of the solution should
ideally be below that of the soil fraction and higher than that of the plastic fraction to
achieve an eﬀective extraction. Table 4.1 summarizes typical salts used for DS and related

polymers like PVC and PET.

Oil separation
Another commonly used approach for MP extraction is oil separation (OS), that provides
a more cost eﬀective and safe alternative to DS, as DS often rely on expensive and envi-
ronmentally hazardous substances (Prosenc et al. 2021; Junhao et al. 2021). OS exploits
the oleophilic properties of several plastic types (Prosenc et al. 2021; Radford et al. 2021).
Multiple studies have investigated various oil types’ aﬃnity for plastic to identify which
achieves the highest recovery eﬃciency of MP from soil. Using olive oil and castor oil
obtained high recovery eﬃciencies of above 90% (Thomas et al. 2020).

4.1.3
Magnetic separation
Grbic et al. (2019) developed a method to extract MP using magnetic nanoparticles.
They modiﬁed iron (Fe) nanoparticles by adding a hydrophobic tail. The hydrophobic
properties of plastic caused the nanoparticles to bind to MP. The binding of nanoparticles
to the MP is related to the surface area to volume ratio. This makes the method ideal
to separate small MP (> 20 μm) as more Fe-nanoparticles can bind per unit mass. This
is one of the methods main advantages. Once the nanoparticles are attached to the MP,
the MP can easily be separated using a magnet. Grbic et al. (2019) used this method
to isolate MP from diﬀerent environmetal samples, but not soil. Ramage et al. (2022)
further developed this method to be eﬀective for soil samples by including a step that
ﬁrst removed any magnetic matter in the soil before adding the nanoparticles.
They
tested the method on four diﬀerent soil types (loam, high-carbon loamy sand, sandy loam
and high-clay sandy loam) and investigated recoveries by spiking the samples with three
diﬀerent types of MP. They report average recoveries ranging from 87-97% for the four
soil types with the highest recovery for high-clay sandy loam and the lowest recovery for
high-carbon loamy sand (Ramage et al. 2022). This method has potential to overcome
some of the limitation of DS. However, it is a novel method and needs further development
before it can be widely used.
4.1.4
Froth ﬂotation
Froth ﬂotation is another novel method to isolate MP. The principle builds on hydrophobic
properties the MP. The sample is aerated in water, so that bobbles are generated. MP
will stick to the air bobbles and then ﬂow upward, where they can be ﬁltered oﬀ. The
methods is fast and no pretreatment of the sample is necessary. Its main advantages are
that it a very cheap, non-hazardous and sustainable method that require no chemicals
(Jiang et al. 2022). So far this method have been applied to isolate MP from seawater
and from sediment. It still needs to be tried out on soil samples, however, it should be
applicable (Y. Zhang et al. 2021; Jiang et al. 2022). Yet, froth ﬂoation is still in its early
stage of development and more research is needed to explore the potential of this method.

Raman Spectroscopy
Raman Spectroscopy is named after the Indian physicists C.V. Raman (Koenig 1999b). It
relies on the ability of a molecule to scatter light when the sample is exposed to monochro-
matic light (laser). The interaction between the laser’s photon and the vibrational energy
levels of the molecule causes the molecule to scatter light in a characteristic way which is
unique for every molecule (Koenig 1999a). This is called Raman scattering and is most
pronounced for non-polar molecules, as the it relates to the polarizability of the molecule
(Koenig 1999a). The wavelengths used for the laser is in the visible light or near-IR range
(380nm-2500nm). The change in the photons energy is detected and based on this a
molecule speciﬁc Raman spectrum is generated (Agilent Technologies Inc. 2023). Raman
spectroscopy is able to detect and analysis particles down to 1 μm which is very desirable
in MP analysis (Käppler et al. 2016).
A limitation of Raman spectroscopy is the undesirable eﬀect of ﬂuorescence, which can
interfere with the Raman spectrum and make particle identiﬁcation impossible. Addi-
tives in the polymers can cause ﬂuorescence, which is a disadvantage when using Raman
spectroscopy in identifying MP(Käppler et al. 2016).
Fourier Transform Infrared Spectroscopy
Fourier Transform Infrared Spectroscopy (FTIR) relies on infrared (IR) spectroscopy for
quantiﬁcation and identiﬁcation of particles. IR light have wavelengths from 700 nm to 1
mm placing it between the visible light and radio waves (Moore 2016).
IR frequencies in the range of 4000-600 cm–1 (Mid-IR) correspond to the frequencies of
bond vibration of organic molecules. There are two types of molecular vibrations: Stretch-
ing and bending. Each functional group absorbs IR at certain frequencies corresponding
to the chemical bonds present in the group. This means all organic molecules exposed to
mid-IR light generate a unique IR spectrum (Bruice 2017).
The IR-spectrum can be divided into two sections; 4000-1400 cm–1 is called the functional
group area and 1400-600 cm–1 is called the ﬁnger print region (Bruice 2017). The func-
tional group area is similar for many organic compounds containing the same functional
units, whereas the the ﬁngerprint area is unique for individual molecules and depends on
the exact placement of the functional group (Bruice 2017). Synthetic plastic polymers
are produced from organic compounds meaning FTIR is suitable for quantiﬁcation and
identiﬁcation of MP.
IR spectroscopy depends on the dipole moment of a molecule and therefore is the most
distinct spectra produced for polar molecules. Some information will be present both in
the IR and the Raman spectrum of the compound. However, IR and Raman can be used
complementary as bond with high IR intensities might have low Raman intensities and

==PAGE:41==

vise versa (Käppler et al. 2016).
In FTIR the sample is exposed to all wavelengths simultaneously. Some wavelengths will
be absorbed while others will be reﬂected. These waves interfere with each other before
hitting the detector. The result from the detector is then Fourier transformed producing
a spectrum. This spectrum can then used for identiﬁcation of the MP (Käppler et al.
2016).

MP. The system is fully automated and requires only minimal sample preparation in the
form of transferring the extracted MP to a Kevley Low-emissivity (Low-e) glass slide.
The Low-e slide does not interfere with the absorption in wavenumber range from 4000
to 400 cm–1, however it does reﬂect 95 % of mid-IR (Kevley Technologies 2022). The
LDIR contains a Quantum Cascade Laser (QCL) which emits light at a wavelength from
5555.54 nm to 10256.41 nm which is in the mid-IR area (Agilent Technologies Inc. 2021a).
This means the LDIR operates in what is referred to as the ﬁngerprint area of organic
compounds in the IR spectrum (Bruice 2017). According to Agilent Technologies Inc., this
interval is more than adequate for most plastic types found in the environment (Agilent
Technologies Inc. and Robey n.d.).
The LDIR sends IR rays through the particle, the rays are reﬂected at the slide and will

pass trough the particle again. Depending on which particle it is diﬀerent wavelengths
will be absorbed. The reﬂected IR will reach a mercury cadmium telluride detector and
based on this the LDIR generates a absorbance spectrum of the particle over the spe-
ciﬁc wavelengths. The LDIR has two modes of action. The ﬁrst mode, the selected area
is scanned at one wavelength very fast ( approximately 2 min). The second mode, the
QCL run through all wavelengths to produce a particle spectrum (Alwan et al. 2022).
The providers claim it takes 8 seconds to generate a spectrum per particle (Agilent Tech-

proportional to the number of particles.
The LDIR is equipped with two high performance cameras that creates visual images of
identiﬁed particles allowing the user to inspect the particle shape. The Clarity Software
has an integrated library containing spectra of several plastic types and common natural
impurities which is used for the identiﬁcation of the particles. Additional spectra can
be added to the library (Agilent Technologies Inc. 2019) which is a useful feature, when
searching for a speciﬁc plastic type, for example when performing recovery tests. The
successful identiﬁcation of unknown plastic types from environmental samples is therefore
highly dependent on the available spectra in the LDIR Library.
This highlights the
importance of having an adequate library to achieve the best results.
The LDIR is provided with a microscope grade objective lens which allows for high-
magniﬁcation visible image and making it possible to manually detect particles down to
1 μm (Agilent Technologies Inc. 2019). However, a 1 μm particle can not be identiﬁed
using IR as a reliable spectrum cannot be generated. Using the automated workﬂow, the
limit of detection (LOD) and reliable identiﬁcation of particles is 20 μm. In the range of
10-20 μm, the LDIR might be able to generate a spectrum, but it depends on mulitple
parameters, such as the polymer type, particle morphology, how well the IR light can
focus on the particle, etc. Based on this, the minimum size of particles identiﬁed using
the LDIR is 20 μm (Agilent Technologies Inc. 2019).
Pyrolysis Gas Chromatography Mass Spectrometry
An alternative to spectroscopy methods is Pyrolysis Gas Chromatography Mass Spec-
trometry (PyrGCMS) . This method combines three "steps" to determine the amount of
MP in the sample, 1) pyrolysis which fragmentates the compounds, 2) gas chromatogaphy
(GC) separates the fragments and 3) mass spectroscopy (MS) detects the fragments.
During pyrolysis the sample is heated up to high temperatures (> 500 ◦C) under an
inert atmosphere (Steinmetz et al. 2020). At these temperatures the chemical bonds are
broken and diﬀerent fragments of the molecule are formed. These fragments are called
pyrolysates (Steinmetz et al. 2020). The weakest bonds in the molecule and bonds which
Page 27 of 79
==PAGE:43==
Microplastic in Soil
CHAPTER 4. METHODS FOR MICROPLASTIC ANALYSIS
yield the most stable fragments breaks ﬁrst (Bruice 2017). Next, GC is used to separate
the pyrolystes. This is done by injecting the sample through a column. Based on the
physical properties of the pyrolysates and the settings of the column, the pyrolysates are
retained in the column. This results in the fragments arrive at the end of the column at
diﬀerent times. Hereafter, MS is used to detect the pyrolysates. This generates a mass
spectrum, which is unique for each molecule (Bruice 2017). Based on this is it possible
to determine the mass of individual plastics in a sample containing a mix of diﬀerent
polymers. The detection limit for PyrGCMS depend on the plastic type. For example,
the detection limit range for PS is 3 - 200 ng and for PE, PP and PET the detection
range is 0.5 - 50 μg (Steinmetz et al. 2020).
The advantages of this method is that it only require simple sample preparation and gives
exact information of the mass of the polymer in the sample (Matsui et al. 2020). The
limitation of the method is that it is destructive and does not give any information of the
morphology of the MP (Steinmetz et al. 2020).

Sampling method used at Roskilde Dyreskueplads
The sampling method used for collecting soil samples at Roskilde Dyreskueplads is here
described. The sampling took place the 28th October 2022. The exact sampling location
was chosen based on the undisturbed appearance of the soil. No vehicle tracks or other
visible changes were observed. The soil sample was collected using a metal tube with
an adjustable horizontal metal strip on the outside of the tube indicating the sample
depth. The tube was washed with water and dried with compressed air between every
use to reduce cross contamination. In the interval from 0 to 20 cm below ground, the
soil sample were divided in 2 cm fractions, whereas from 20 to 50 cm below ground the
sample were collected in 5 cm intervals. This adjustable metal strip ensured that correct
sample (2 or 5 cm) were taken. For the 2 cm fractions, a small metal shovel was used at
the bottom of the tube when lifting the tube. Otherwise the sample would not stay in
the tube. For the 5 cm fractions the friction in the tube was enough for them to stay in
the tube. All samples were stored in aluminium foil trays covered with aluminum foil.

6.9
Trial and error
This section presents methods considered for the experimental protocol accompanied by
an explanation as to why they were disregarded.
Centrifugation
Implementation of centrifugation during Step 2 (DS) was considered to reduce settling
time of the soil mineral fraction. An experiment with plastic centrifuge tubes was per-
formed to investigate if usage contaminated samples with PP. Contamination could be
the result of plastic particles present in the tubes from the beginning or due to corrosion
caused by friction with soil particles during centrifugation. However, due to problems
with upscaling, these results were not useful. Literature highlights the importance of
avoiding laboratory equipment of plastic. It was therefore decided not to proceed with
centrifugation using plastic tubes Glass centrifuge tubes were proposed as an alternative
to plastic tubes to remove the source of potential contamination. The sturdiness of the
glass tubes were tested with only water for multiple rounds of centrifugation. The glass
tubes showed a seemingly arbitrary tendency to break during centrifugation even at rela-
tively low RPM (<3000). During test rounds with ZnCl2 and soil it was observed that the
tubes broke more frequently than with water. Is was discovered that a possible cause for
the breakage was the lack of rubber-stoppers under the glass tubes. However, as DTU did
not have rubber-stoppers ﬁtting the glass tubes, it was not possible to use centrifugation
for Step 2.
Separatory funnel
The advantages and disadvantages of using a separatory funnel (Figure 6.9) instead of
glass vials in Step 2 (DS) were investigated. The separatory funnel enables removal of
the settled soil mineral fraction from the bottom removing the possibility of the soil and
supernatant mixing. The separation solution and soil sample were added to a beaker
and agitated using a magnetic stirrer for 15 min at 300 rpm prior to entering the funnel.
The main disadvantage experienced was clogging of the bottom valve.
Even though
the fraction >2mm had been removed, there were still incidents with clogging. It was
furthermore diﬃcult to control the outﬂow rate. The rate could change in an instant if
the supernatant suddenly created a passage through the soil layer. If the valve is the
slightest resistant, it can be diﬃcult to close in time before all supernatant is lost and the
experiment would have to be repeated. The disadvantages of using the funnel for Step 2
outweighed the beneﬁts, and the use was therefore discontinued.

7.1.1
Interpretation of 137Cs
The distribution of 137Cs for KAL resembles the expected distribution curve for an undis-
turbed soil with a peak near the soil surface and a decreasing concentration with depth
(Figure 7.1a). KAL shows a deﬁnitive peak in 137Cs in 10-12 cm depth (8.35 Bq kg–1)
which can be attributed to the intense nuclear testing in 1963, after which the concen-
tration experiences a steep decline down to 20-25cm depth. As the commercial plastic
production started simultaneously with the nuclear testing in the 1950s, it was not ex-
pected to detect MP below 25 cm. From 20-25 cm and down to the deepest sampled layer
(45-50 cm) the concentration is consistently below 1 Bq kg–1. The distribution of 137Cs
for ROS is more uniform compared to KAL. From the top layer to a depth of 30-35 cm
the concentration vary between 1.4- 2.1 Bq kg–1, whereafter a decline is observed reaching
approximately 0 at 40-45 cm depth. This suggests soil from ROS is disturbed thereby
posing a challenge to estimate age of soil layers. MP is expected be found in similar
concentrations from 0-35 cm depth and non-detectable below 35 cm.
Measured low concentrations of Cs-137 for FRH
Very low concentrations of 137Cs were measured for FRH and no clear peak in concen-
tration was observed near the soil surface. Measurements were below the LOD from 20
cm depth and below. The measurements were compared with measurements from 2014
(Figure 7.2). The concentrations in 2014 were signiﬁcantly higher compared to the sample
a decade later. A subtle peak of 137Cs is observed between 10-20 cm (6.4 Bq kg–1) where-
after the concentration decreases. The concentration below 30 cm was low (1.4 Bq kg–1)
and MP is therefore not expected to be detected in these layers.

Discussion
This chapter provides a discussion on how our results relate to our hypotheses.
The
experiences using the experimental protocol for MP analysis developed in this project
will also be assessed. It furthermore provides recommendations for how the protocol can
be improved. The potential of combining soil dating and MP analysis will be reviewed.
Lastly, mitigation measures and options for remediation of MP pollution of soil will be
touched upon.
8.1
Accounting for background contamination
in results
Normal laboratory practice prescribe that background contamination is subtracted from
the measured value.
This presents a challenge for MP analysis, as the plastic types
measured as background contamination might not be represented in the small sample mass
taken for analysis in analytical equipment such as LDIR. The plastic types detected in the
investigated sources of background contamination are instead qualitatively assessed during
interpretation of results from the soil samples. This works for small scale experiments
with few samples, but it may be too time consuming when interpreting numerous samples.
The problem can furthermore be limited by using larger sample masses.
This would
likely result in higher observed concentrations of MP and would consequently reduce the
signiﬁcance of background concentration.
The plastic types identiﬁed as sources of contamination were the same plastic types de-
tected in the deeper soil layers (45-50cm) of FRH and ROS, including PET, PE-C, PU,
and POM. While PE and PS were also sources of background contamination, these are
detected in larger amounts in only some of the soil samples indicating that the MP might
be true pollution.
8.1.1
Unknown source of contamination with polypropylene
PP was the only polymer type detected in all analyzed subsamples from both locations
and was identiﬁed as the dominating polymer type in most samples. An arbitrary sub-
sample from KAL was analyzed to determine if the presence of PP in FRH and ROS was a
coincidence. Out of 60 particles identiﬁed as MP in the KAL subsample, 56 of them were
identiﬁed as PP. This conﬁrmed our suspicion that a contamination might have occurred

during laboratory work. The investigated sources of background contamination contained
negligible amounts of PP and could therefore not be the source of contamination. This
suggest that PP might originate from the chemicals used, either H2O2 or ZnCl2. Further-
more, the spectrum quality of particles identiﬁed as PP in ROS and FRH samples were
> 0.9. High qualities are not expected for MP originating from environmental samples

8.2
Evaluation of hypotheses
In this section our three hypotheses are evaluated based on the results from Chapter 7.
8.2.1
Hypothesis 1
The abundance of microplastic in soils are higher in areas characterized by high human
activity than in areas with low human activity as microplastic are generated from produc-
tion, use and disposal of plastic products
The total MP count over depth for FRH and ROS shows no clear tendency that could
be indicative of low and high human activity. The abundance in the top soil for FRH
and ROS is similar, where the maximum value is observed for FRH. The large interval
for the total MP count in 12-14cm depth for ROS is attributed to an extremely high par-

assume that the measured concentration of up to 338 MP g–1dw is representative for the
soil layer.
A clear diﬀerence between FRH and ROS is the composition of polymer types over depth.
12 plastic types in total were identiﬁed for ROS compared to 6 for FRH. The activities
related to the site at ROS can be considered a point-source pollution with plastic. ROS
arguably receives a more diverse input of MP than FRH due to events like Roskilde
Festival, where especially various single-use plastic items are carelessly discarded on the
ground and subjected to fragmentation. This coincide with the observation that only soil
samples from ROS contained visible meso- and macroplastic (See Appendix A.6). Other
plastic products are also sources of MP due to wear and tear. The uniformity of plastic
types over depth observed for FRH indicates that FRH likely receives MP from continuous
sources. It should be noted that the detection of some plastic types in soil samples can
be attributed to sources of background contamination.
The results presented in Chapter 7 do not provide adequate evidence for accepting or

Hypothesis 3

8.3
Coupling of soil dating and
microplastic analysis
To our knowledge, this is the ﬁrst project trying to couple dating of soil layers with MP
analysis. One study used gamma analysis to estimate the age of a sediment core from a
pond in London, United Kingdom, and coupled this with MP accumulation rates (Turner
et al. 2019). Sediments present a less disturbed environment compared to soil and can
therefore provide more clear evidence used for determining the age of layers. However,
sediments are sinks for MP that originate from or has entered aquatic environments.
Turner et al. (2019) found a trend of decreasing MP abundance with increasing sediment
depth which is in good agreement with our ﬁndings.
Gamma analysis for soil dating is a useful addition to MP analysis as it provide information
Page 57 of 79
==PAGE:73==
Microplastic in Soil
CHAPTER 8. DISCUSSION
on disturbance of soil layers. Performing a gamma analysis is straight-forward and does
not involve the use of hazardous or expensive chemicals. A large sample size enhances
gamma signals thus improving measurements and reducing analysis time.
For future
investigations, we suggest to take one soil core for soil dating in close proximity to the
soil core used for MP analysis to ensure suﬃciently large sample sizes for both analyses.
Integration of soil dating analysis with MP analysis in soil in this project aided in the
understanding of MP distribution over depth. The distribution of 137Cs and 210Pb over
depth for FRH obtained from soil dating analysis conﬁrmed the site was undisturbed.
However, low amounts of MP was still present in soil layers that were estimated to be
from before the 1950s. This creates the suspicion that MP are able to transport vertically
in soil. Finding MP in low depths of an undisturbed soil should be seen as a ﬁrst indication
of MP transport in soil. At this point this can only be considered speculative and more
research is needed to survey the possible mechanisms responsible for transport of MP in
soil. Soil dating analysis of ROS veriﬁed the suspicion that the site was highly disturbed
since the distribution of 137Cs was more uniform and the distribution of excess 210Pb
was neither exponential nor showed any logical trends with depth. This observation was
in coherence with the results from MP analysis.
The results showed the highest MP
concentration was detected in 12-14 cm depth and not in the top layer. However, the
deepest layer (40-50 cm) had in fact the lowest concentration of MP compared to the
other investigated soil layers.
It is important to determine a baseline value for MP in soil and secondly the accumulation
rate of MP. If MP in soil is indeed increasing this would be incentive for policy markers
to realize the huge challenge we face regarding MP pollution in soil. We would therefore
recommend to accompany MP analysis in soil with soil dating analysis to gain insights to
the soil history that can be relevant for understanding the distribution of MP in soil.
8.4
Soil sampling
The inherent uncertainty of small sample sizes questions the representativeness of the
results obtained in this project as they could be coincidental. Analyzing soils with low
concentrations of MP creates additional uncertainty, as it is practically impossible to
diﬀerentiate between MP originating from soil and MP contamination from laboratory
work. Quantifying the extent of MP pollution of a particular site cannot therefore not be
extrapolated from sampling only one place with samples of the sizes used in this project.
The uncertainty related to the heterogeneity of MP distribution in soil furthermore com-
plicates the soil sampling strategy. We recommend sampling a larger soil mass for MP
analysis than what used here. In general we would recommend to follow the ISO Standard
18400.

8.5
The importance of soil characteristics
Soil characteristics might play an important role inﬂuencing the extraction of MP (Section
6.2). Selected characteristics of soil suspected of interfering with the extraction eﬃciency
of extraction methods of MPs were measured, including pH and grain size analysis. The
initial plan was to use the recovery tests to investigate how the soil parameters aﬀect
the extraction process. However, as the recovery tablets did not turn out completely as
desired, it was not possible to correlate recovery percentages with soil characteristics. We
suggest further investigation in this topic. Optimized recovery tablets could provide a
valuable tool for assessing the soil parameters inﬂuences on extraction.
8.6
Optimization of laboratory protocol
This project aimed to investigate correlations between abundance and distribution of MP
in Danish soils in three locations with varying levels of human activity. An experimental
laboratory protocol for extracting MP from soil was prepared inspired by existing methods
in literature, including density separation with ZnCl2 and oxidation of OM with H2O2
with the addition of testing a new method for quantiﬁcation and identiﬁcation of MP in
soil using LDIR.
Multiple attempts to optimize the experimental setup and enhance the eﬃciency of the
individual steps were made during this project before ending up with the ﬁnal laboratory
protocol. Section 6.9 contains a detailed description of the attempts that were prone to
error, were deemed too laborious or ineﬃcient for us to proceed with.
One of the objectives for the ﬁnal laboratory protocol was to have a minimalistic exper-
imental setup while striking a balance between eﬀort and desired output. However, the
results from the LDIR showed that only 0.02-2.5% of particles in a puriﬁed sample was
identiﬁed as MP and up to 93% were discarded prior to data treatment due to being
below the acceptance quality of 0.8 in this project (Figure 7.3). While no studies have
Page 59 of 79
==PAGE:75==

reported using LDIR for soil analysis of soil and thus can provide comparable results, the
levels of MP puriﬁcation are seemingly inadequate. A discussion of potential adjustments
for improving the experimental protocol is therefore provided in the following section.
8.6.1
Degrading agents selected for removal of soil organic
matter
Samples were treated with H2O2 in a low concentration (10%) for a long period of time (7
days) at room temperature instead of a higher concentration (30-35%) for a short period
of time (4-24 hours) at elevated temperatures (40-70 degrees Celsius). This choice was
made as H2O2 had reported high OM removal eﬃciencies while minimizing the potential
for MP degradation especially with regards to PA. The morphological changes of MP
particles were however not investigated in this study. The sensitivity of the OM removal
method can therefore not be assessed.
The acceptance criteria for ﬁnalized digestion were lack of activity and a shift from a
brownish to a greyish color of the soil.
After ﬁltering samples in the end of Step 1,
larger, brownish particles assessed to be OM residues could however still be observed
especially in top layers. As the proceeding Step 2 provided no means of OM removal,
we suspected that the ﬁnal results would show high contents of OM. This was indeed
conﬁrmed by LDIR analysis. The presence of OM may hamper correct identiﬁcation of
MP as mentioned in Section 4.2 and therefore presents a signiﬁcant source of potential
error. This single oxidation step was therefore considered inadequate in reducing the OM
content of especially cellulose to an acceptable level.
Recommendations and considerations
Future recommendations for achieving higher removal eﬃciencies include employing a
sequential approach taking advantage of the beneﬁts of multiple degrading agents. Relying
on multiple steps to achieve a suﬃcient purity of the ﬁnal sample can keep non-plastic
particles from interfering with the results of the ﬁnal analysis.
While oxidation with
H2O2 is still considered an eﬀective method for OM removal in soil samples, enzymatic
treatment with cellulase, hemicellulase and protease would be a viable addition to the OM
removal scheme. We recommend including measurements of the OM removal eﬃciency
prior to initiating the actual extraction process in order to assess the suitability of the
method for a particular soil type. Introducing additional steps to the puriﬁcation process,
such as multiple rounds of OM removal, simultaneously enhances the risk of losing MP
from samples.
Especially if these steps introduce a higher degree of interaction with
samples, including changing of glassware and ﬁltration. MP is hydrophobic meaning it
has a tendency to stick to glassware, which was experienced during this project. Fibers

8.6.2
Method selected for physical separation of soil and
microplastic
DS was selected to separate the bulk matter of soil consisting of sand, silt and clay from
MP. After ﬁnalization of Step 2 the isolated particles on the ﬁlter was visually inspected.
It was observed that a signiﬁcant amount of soil particles were still present on the ﬁlter.
While the majority probably was OM, we suspected that some soil mineral particles were
still left as well. Step 3 was initiated despite of this observation due to time limitations.
None of the reviewed articles using LDIR for soil analysis have reported any issues with
identiﬁcation of MP due to contamination with mineral particles. The main disadvantage
can therefore be argued to be prolonged analysis time.
While this deﬁnitely can be
considered a source of annoyance, we do not recommend adding an additional DS step or
similar physical extraction method with the potential of losing more MP in the process.
Choice of salt for separation solution
Multiple salts used for preparing a separation solution have been tried and tested in lit-
erature. Most frequently mentioned arguments for discouraging the use of speciﬁc salts
include that 1) a NaCl solution has an insuﬃciently low density for extraction of higher
density synthetic polymers 2) most higher density salt solutions are environmentally haz-
ardous and 3) price. Meixner et al. (2020) found that CaCl2 generated a signiﬁcantly
higher MP count compared to ZnCl2 when using the same soil, which the suspected to
be due to MP contamination in the bottle. The use of CaCl2 was therefore discontinued.
This observation has however not been reported elsewhere in the literature reviewed. The
issue regarding contamination with MP in industrially produced chemicals used for the
experiments is however acknowledged as a potential source of error.
The main beneﬁt of using ZnCl2 as separation solution is that it is suitable for extraction
of higher density plastic types. The results conﬁrmed that plastic types such as PVC and
PET in fact had been isolated and identiﬁed. The main drawbacks of using ZnCl2 are
the cost and that it is environmentally hazardous. The ability of ZnCl2 to corrode metals
became clear, when the aluminum foil used for covering a sample had been in contact
with the separation solution. The foil had become severely corroded and foil fragments

8.6.3
Using LDIR for quantiﬁcation and identiﬁcation of
microplastics
This section discuss the main limitations and advantages of using LDIR for MP analysis.
Time of analysis
The time required for a full analysis of a whole slide varied between 3 and up to 48 hours
depending on the total particle count. The claim from Agilent that LDIR uses 8 seconds
to identify a particle is too optimistic in our opinion.
From our personal experience,
we observed that it could take up to 30 seconds to ﬁnd a matching spectrum. It was
challenging to plan the experimental work properly analysis times were unknown.
LDIR library
The correct identiﬁcation of MP depends on the available spectra in the LDIR Library.
While the LDIR Library is based on work by Primpke et al. (2018) using reference spectra
in the 3600 to 1250 cm–1 wavelength range, the LDIR only performs analysis in the
wavelength range from 1800 to 975 cm–1. Agilent has not provided information on how
the spectra from Primpke et al. (2018) were modiﬁed to ﬁt the higher wavelengths used
by LDIR thereby questioning the representativeness of the spectra for LDIR analysis.
Secondly, Primpke et al. (2018) base their library on pure materials supplied mainly from
industry. However, plastic materials rarely consist only of the pure synthetic polymer,
as plastic can be composites and contain additives (Section 2.1.1), which can disturb the
IR-spectrum of the synthetic polymer resulting in wrong or low quality identiﬁcation of
MP particles.
During the last stage of this project Agilent released version 1.5 of the Clarity software.
According to Agilent, the new version comes with multiple improvements, including faster
analysis, an updated microplastics library, advances in spectra matching and the possi-
bility for analysis directly on the ﬁlter (Agilent Technologies Inc. 2022). While this could
have positively aﬀected the eﬃciency of MP analysis for this project, it was decided to
continue using the original library to identify remaining samples.
MP libraries should be continuously improved and expanded to cover a broad range of
plastics that might be found in the environment.
This includes degraded plastics, as
it is a valid assumption that some form of degradation of the MP particle can have
occurred in the environment. This makes it challenging to correctly identify MP that has
Page 63 of 79
==PAGE:79==

been exposed to environmental factors accelerating degradation, such as UV-radiation,
oxygen, heat and water (Section 2.2) resulting in alteration of the molecular structure
and thus the spectrum. It is also encouraged to include other materials likely to be found
in environmental samples. Sharing of MP spectra between researches are furthermore
encouraged so all have updated MP libraries.
Sources of erroneous particle identiﬁcation
Automated identiﬁcation of particles using LDIR introduces some uncertainty in the re-
sults. Particles that physically overlap on the low-e slide can be interpreted as one particle
leading to the LDIR generating a mixed spectrum thus resulting in a faulty identiﬁcation
or the particle being labelled as unknown (Cheng et al. 2022).
Degradation of polymers induces structural changes altering the molecular spectrum from
that of the original synthetic polymer (Cheng et al. 2022). Jia et al. (2022) investigated the
MP content of agricultural soils in China using LDIR for quantiﬁcation and identiﬁcation.
They chose a quality criteria of 0.65 (compared to 0.8 in this project) for the initial
data sorting process of results obtained from the automated particle analysis with the
argumentation that MP might have been degraded. However, we found that spectra for
MP at lower quality often resembled spectra for naturally occurring material. Our ﬁndings
therefore represent the best case scenario, as we might have discarded true MP particles
using a high quality criteria of 0.8.
Presence of OM in the puriﬁed sample is suspected to reduce the certainty of the MP
identiﬁcation (Cheng et al. 2022). This further illustrates the necessity of eﬃcient removal
of organic impurities. Though our samples contained relatively high amount of organic
impurities, it is still reasonable to assume that the LDIR functions satisfactorily.

8.7
Mitigation and remediation
To our knowledge, no remediation technologies currently exist that produce satisfying re-
sults within a reasonable price range. This further highlights the importance of preventing
discharge of both primary and secondary MP to the environment in the ﬁrst place.
Bioremediation using microorganisms has been proposed as a potential solution to combat
MP pollution of soils (Zeenat et al. 2021). However, the physiochemical properties of
diﬀerent plastic types, and the size and morphology of the plastic fragments have large
inﬂuence on the biodegradability of MPs (Zeenat et al. 2021).
Farmers can become reluctant to receive compost and sludge to use on their land, as these
residues are typically contaminated with MP which accumulates in the soil. Complete
avoidance does however not provide a viable long-term solution. Instead, focus should be
on improving the quality of sludge and compost. Ni et al. (2020) conducted a lab-scale
test on the dependency of temperature on the treatment of sewage sludge by pyrolysis to
reduce the content of MPs with densities > 1.1 g cm3. They showed that the MP concen-
tration decreased signiﬁcantly already at relatively lower temperature. However several
risk factors were associated with lower pyrolysis temperatures, such as formation of new
polymer types and production of PAHs remaining in the solid residues. Thus they recom-
mended a minimum pyrolysis temperature of 450 ℃. The resulting solid pyrolysis product
called biochar is shown to be nutrient rich and applicable for use on agricultural land as
a substitute for the sludge itself (Ni et al. 2020; Ghodke et al. 2021). Further research in-
vestigating this technology is encouraged even though it might not be economically viable
as a universal solution.
8.8
Overall discussion and recommendations
The attention and interest in MP pollution of soils are growing. However, based on pro-
jections of plastic production combined with slow degradation rates of plastic in soils, MP
is expected to continuously accumulate in soils endangering soil ecosystems and human
health. This calls for immediate actions from policy makers if current trends are to be

==PAGE:81==

turned around. A prerequisite for assessing environmental consequences of MP pollution
is the ability to determine the content of MP in soils.
This project aimed to assess the inﬂuence of anthropogenic activity on the MP abun-
dance in Danish soils using an experimental protocol developed based on currently avail-
able methods in literature. The results showed no clear distinction between the total
abundance of MP in areas of low and high human activity respectively. A decreasing
concentration of MP with depth was observed for the area of low human activity (FRH),
while the trend was less clear for the area of high human activity (ROS), since the top soil
(0-2 cm) contained less MP than the deeper soil layer (12-14 cm). However, an overall
decrease in concentration with depth was observed. Low concentrations of MP in the
deepest soil layer investigated (45-50 cm) were observed for both areas (5-88 MP g–1dw).
Based on soil dating analysis using 137Cs and 210Pb a depth of 10-15 cm is estimated to
correspond to the beginning of the 1960s. Below 25 cm depth it was estimated that soil
layers were minimum 100 years old. The presence of MP in the deepest soil layer cre-
ates the suspicion, that MP can transport vertically in soil. However, the results did not
provide suﬃcient evidence to conﬁrm this, as measurements from sources of background
contamination showed detection of the same plastic types (PET, PE-C, PU and POM).
While the outcome of this project presented limited data on MP abundance, the results
highlighted that MPs are detectable even in areas far from emissions sources.
Several improvements to the experimental protocol developed in this project were identi-
ﬁed. Prioritization of further OM removal would have been preferred if we had not been
limited by the predeﬁned project duration, as the content of cellulosic material in puriﬁed
samples was quite high constituting up to 74%. MP analysis using LDIR was furthermore
shown to be time consuming when samples contained high particle counts. Additional
steps to remove remaining soil constituents prior to analysis are therefore considered nec-
essary to minimize potential interfering with MP identiﬁcation and reduce total analysis
time.
With focus on developing an eﬃcient and reliable protocol for determining the content
of MP in soil it can be argued that a quantitative method like Pyr-GC/MS generat-
ing an output of mass could accompany methods like LDIR or FTIR to achieve further
information on MP size and morphology.

Conclusion
This project investigated the inﬂuence of human activity on MP pollution of Danish soils
from two locations using an experimental protocol for MP extraction and identiﬁcation
developed as part of the work. The original plan was to investigate three locations, but due
to time limitations, this was not possible. MP analysis was combined with radioisotope
analysis to determine approximate age of soil layers. This enabled estimating approximate
time of MP deposition and assessing the ability of MP to transport vertically in soil. The
experiences with the experimental protocol were the following:
• The level of puriﬁcation of samples obtained by Step 1 (OM removal) and 2 (DS)
using the experimental protocol was unsatisfactory. Only up to 2.5% of extracted
particles was identiﬁed as MP based on a quality criteria of 0.8. The remaining
particles were mainly identiﬁed as naturally occurring material including cellulosic
and coal
• LDIR is a novel analytical instrument marketed as being capable of MP analysis
of environmental samples. Advantages of LDIR include automated workﬂow, ease
of use, and faster analysis time compared to analytical equivalents. Disadvantages
we encountered during this project included the inability to diﬀerentiate between
natural and synthetic polyamide, and unpredictable analysis times
• The method used for quantifying the recovery of the experimental protocol did not
succeed. The ability of the protocol to eﬃciently extract and identify MP could thus
not be determined. This prohibited us in investigating the inﬂuence of measured
soil characteristics (TOC, carbonate content, pH and soil type) on the extraction
eﬃciency
The results from MP analysis using the experimental protocol and radioisotope analysis
using Cs-137 and Pb-210 are the following:
• The distribution of 137Cs over soil depth for ROS showed a uniform distribution with
no clearly deﬁned peak generated by the intense nuclear testing in 1963 indicating
that the soil is disturbed.

indicative of an undisturbed soil

Perspective
Research concerning MP in soil is still in its infancy with limited original research being
published. Signiﬁcant research gaps still exist for all aspects of MP pollution of soil. This
includes mapping out sources and pathways, soil sampling strategies, methods for MP
analysis, and consequences for soil ecosystems, groundwater and human health.
Several methods of extracting MP from soil are described in literature. While some of
the results sound promising, the prospect of developing a standard protocol seems dis-
tant. The heterogeneous nature of soil already bring about experimental challenges in
itself. Adding to this, the numerous types of plastic that exist and that could possibly be
present in soils complicates any attempt to develop a universal approach for the successful
extraction and correct identiﬁcation of MP. We are hopeful that the scientiﬁc community
continues to work towards solving this obstacle. We are aware of ongoing research inves-
tigating MP abundance in agricultural and undisturbed areas in Denmark performed by
Aalborg University in collaboration with the Danish EPA. Their ﬁndings will hopefully
provide further insight into MP pollution and potentially establishing a baseline value for
MP in Danish soils.
Prioritization of monitoring soil conditions and focusing on reducing emissions sources of
MP are encouraged, which require a substantial eﬀort from both policy makers and the
scientiﬁc community. In this regard, we urge to apply the precautionary principle and
put it to use in eﬀective regulations. Regulations that address both the upstream and
downstream problems related to plastic applications are needed. The global extent of
plastic pollution furthermore calls for global action rather than only on national levels.

B.4
Preparation of separation solution
The separation solution was prepared from scratch to obtain a desired density of 1.7-1.8
g cm–3. To a 1L glass bottle 0.7 kg of MilliQ water was added. The reaction of ZnCl2
with water is exothermic causing the solution to heat up. The ZnCl2 was therefore added
over time (total of 1.05 kg) ensuring the solution did not become overheated. The solution
was stirred on a magnetic stirrer during mixing and overnight.

Figure D.4: Measured concentrations of the radionuclides a) 137Cs and b) excess 210Pb
over soil depth from the sampling locations FRH (FRH), Kalø (KAL) and ROS (ROS)
used for investigating microplastic abundance
The distribution of excess 210Pb for FRH decrease from the top soil (19.68 Bq kg–1) and
reaches zero in 15-20 cm depth. This is in good coherence with the measurements of
137Cs and it indicates that the soil beneath this layer is minimum 100 years old and
minimal mixing of the top layers has occurred. The highest measured concentration of
==PAGE:114==
excess 210Pb for ROS is in the top soil (19.49 Bq kg–1) with a similar value to that of
FRH. However, the distribution of excess 210Pb for ROS shows no clear trend with depth
as ﬂuctuations are observed all the way down to the deepest sampled and measured soil
layer.
The soils from KAL are quite diﬀerent from the others. There is no clear peak in the
137Cs concentration and in fact the concentration is also substantial lower than what is
measured at the two other locations. We were able to compare with measurements from
2014 and here the 137Cs concentration was approximately 10-20 Bq/kg for the top 10 cm
layer. This indicates that maybe a mistake occurred during the measurements of 137Cs.
However, double checking with the persons reasonable for the results conﬁrmed that no
mistake happened. There are multiple other reasons the 137Cs could be weaken: Firstly,
137Cs is decaying which result in a continuously lower concentration in the environment.
Secondly, soil erosion and uptake by plants are other factors that could explain why the
concentration is so low now. Unfortunately, this makes it impossible for us to estimate
the age of this soil. The radio activity from excess 210Pb decreases with increasing depth
indicating. It is similar to what is found for FRH, which indicates that the soil has not
been very much disturbed and that the deeper soil at KAL is also minimum 100 years old.
However, as KAL has the most uncertain result in soil dating and due to time limitations,
it was decided not to perform any MP analysis on this soil.
