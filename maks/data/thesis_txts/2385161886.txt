Sandwich Arch Bridges
Abstract
This project investigates the sandwich arch bridge concept. This is done by simulating dif-
ferent loads on both a 3 hinge arch and a truss structure.
The inuence of the height above the crown, hoc, the wall placements, load placement and
bridge length were investigated. An increase hoc resulted in a decrease of all forces, except the
force in the arch. An increased number of vertical walls resulted in an increase in the force
in the walls and a decrease in the force in the lling. The inuence of wall skewness proved
to be less clear. It was shown that there was not a single load positioning, that simultane-
ously resulted in the maximum forces in all elements of the bridge. Increased span lengths
was shown to result in larger forces and the conclusions for the other geometrical parameters
proved to be the same for diering span lengths, with a few exceptions.
The presented ways of constructing a curved sandwich arch bridge must be considered either
inadequate, when it comes to bearing capacity or infeasible, when considering the construc-
tion process.
3 sandwich arch bridge designs are presented, one with a gravel lling (design 1), one with
cement stabilized gravel lling (design 2) and one with a pervious concrete lling (design 3).
It was found that design 3 was the most optimal due to its lower complexity than design 1
and higher frost resistance than design 2.

Introduction
The sandwich arch bridge is a concept introduced by Professor Kristian Hertz at DTU. The
primary advantage over the conventional closed-spandrel arch bridge is the utilization of the
entire height of the bridge, and not just the arch, to take the moment. This results in smaller
forces in the arch and deck and means that instead of simply adding compression to the arch,
additional lling material works more eectively in the sandwich bridge design. The eect of
this is that the sandwich arch bridge can be thinner than a conventional arch bridge, with a
lower material consumption as a result.
Two dierent sandwich arch bridges are investigated, the non-reinforced sandwich arch bridge
and the reinforced sandwich arch. Both concepts will be investigated in this report, the non-
reinforced sandwich arch as a simple 3 hinge arch and the reinforced sandwich arch as a truss
structure. For the truss structure the force in the arch, deck, lling and vertical walls will
be calculated. Furthermore a case study of a 30 m long bridge is performed. The inuence
of dierent geometrical parameters on the forces in the bridge will be investigated.
The
investigated parameters are the height above the crown, the wall placements, load placement
and bridge length.
Dierent ways of constructing a curved sandwich arch bridge will also be investigated.
3 dierent sandwich arch bridge design suggestions will be presented, each with a dierent
lling material.
Lastly the construction of the bridge will be considered.

Theory
3.1
Closed-spandrel arch bridge
The closed spandrel arch bridge is a concept extensively used by the Romans at least as
early as the 2nd century B.C. [11]. The concept is simple but eective: An arch of either
concrete, masonry or stone is erected and a lling material of either earth, gravel of concrete
is placed on top.
The lling material helps stabilize the structure, causing compression
in the arch. A uniformly distributed trac load can also be benecial, provided that the
compressive strength of the arch is sucient [1]. The most hazardous loads on an arch bridge
are concentrated loads, especially if placed at the 1/4 point of the span. This will result in a
large moment in the arch and due to the limited thickness of the arch, a large tensile stress.
In a traditional arch bridge this is counteracted by either increasing the thickness of the arch
or the height above the crown, thus increasing the amount of lling material and reducing
the tensile stress by adding compression.
3.2
Pearl-chain arch bridge
The pearl chain arch bridge is a new technology invented by Professor Kristian Hertz at DTU
[12]. While it is not necessary for a sandwich arch bridge to utilize pearl-chain technology it
confers signicant advantages during construction and will therefore be used in project. This
section will give a brief overview of the pearl-chain technology.
The principle of the pearl chain reinforcement is to chain pre-cast concrete elements together
using post-tensioning cables [13]. The elements are cast with a curved duct through which, the
post-tensioning cable is lead. For pearl-chain arches, the elements are cast with inclined ends,
resulting in an arch with the same curvature as the reinforcement duct. This is illustrated
in gure 3.1. The pre-cast concrete arch elements are transported to the construction site
where they are chained together, mortar is applied to the joints between the elements and
the arch is then lifted in place. This eliminates the need for scaolding, resulting in a faster
and cheaper construction process.

The arch elements are pre-cast with inclined ends and ducts for the post-tensioning cables. A

Discussion
9.1
Inuence of geometric parameters
This section discusses the inuence of the height above the crown hoc, the wall placement and
bridge length on the forces in the bridge as well as the placement of trac loads.
The inuence of the height above the crown is the one most clearly described as an increase
in hoc will result in a decrease in all forces, with the exception of the force in the arch. This
was expected as an increased hoc will result in a larger moment arm, thus leading to a smaller
force couple. hoc was also noted to inuence the location of the worst load (with regards to
the normal force from the positive moment), as a large hoc moves this location closer to the
bridge quarter point. This is because the relative dierence in the moment arm on dierent
locations on the bridge decreases for large hoc, thus giving a load positioning akin to that for
a conventional arch bridge.
The load positioning, however, proved to be more complicated than initially expected. As
shown in the truss analysis there is not a single load positioning that simultaneously results
in the maximum forces in all the elements in the bridge. While the maximum forces in the
arch occur for approximately the same load placements, and close to location found in the
simple arch analysis, this is not the case for the other forces.
The inuence of the placement of the walls is not as clear as that of hoc, though some
general relations were found. An increase in the number of walls seemed to increase the force
in the walls and decrease the force in the lling. The decrease of the wall force was due to
the change in angle for the force in the lling.
The inuence of skewness was also investigated, showing that for large spans (30-50 m) the
force in the lling could be reduced by mid skewing the walls. For the force in the walls, there
proved to be an advantage of abutment skewness for spans of 40 and 50 m. The somewhat
chaotic tendencies might be because the results shown in gures are the maximum forces. It
is not certain that the sections with the highest force is the same for dierent geometrical
parameters.
These ndings are interesting when compared to open spandrel arch bridge, which, like the
Zhushanlu Road Bridge [22] have mid skewed walls. The open spandrel arch bridges, how-
58
==PAGE:65==
Andreas Elbrønd s123785

ever, does not take the forces in the same way as the sandwich arch bridge and it is therefore
dicult to compare the two designs.
An increase in span length will result in a general increase in forces in the bridge and with
the exception of the inuence of skewness and the inuence of hoc on the force in the walls,
does not seem to impact the conclusions given above. For the inuence of hoc on the force in
the walls, the increased span lengths only seem to accentuate the trends.
When choosing the bridge geometry, a balance must be struck between the height above
the crown and the use of steel for post-tensioning of the deck and walls as an increased hoc
will require extra lling material. A detailed cost analysis should therefore be performed for
each bridge as there can be on-site conditions, for instance the possible length of the ramp,
that can impact the optimal design.
9.2
Comparison of bridge designs
When comparing the reinforced sandwich arch bridge with the unreinforced, it is clear that
tensile strength requirements of the unreinforced sandwich arch bridge are so high that it re-
quires regular concrete lling, something that is much more expensive than the post-tensioning
required for the reinforced version.
The main disadvantage of design 1 is the requirement of side walls to contain the gravel.
These are more dicult to construct than simply applying one of the bound lling materials
in a form, and require additional reinforcement. It could be argued that since the compres-
sion strut in the side walls are sucient to take the compression force otherwise taken in
the gravel. If the gravel is removed, so could the tensile reinforcement in the side walls, as
they then no longer have to take a moment. The removal of the gravel lling, however, will
also have the eect that bridge deck are no longer continuously supported, meaning that it
requires a moment capacity, leading to the requirement of tensile reinforcement in the deck
and/or extra walls in the length direction of the bridge to reduce the span. While the require-
ments for the extra reinforcement or the extra walls have not been calculated, the additional
steel and concrete necessary are deemed more costly than the benet of leaving out the gravel
lling material.
Designs 2 and 3 are quite similar with the major dierence being the frost resistance, tensile
strength and cement content of the lling material.
The pervious concrete contains con-
siderably more cement than both the cement stabilized gravels but exhibits much better
freeze-thaw durability [3]. The poor freeze-thaw resistance of the cement-stabilized gravel is
problematic for its use as it will be exposed on the bridge sides. Because the bridge sides are
vertical and not horizontal as the test setup in [3], the water will most likely run down the
sides and not form pools on the surface. This means that the poor freeze-thaw resistance of

the cement-stabilized gravel might not be so problematic in the current design. While the
application of an outer layer of frost resistant concrete might prevent surface damage of the
concrete, the interplay between inner and outer frost damage is a debated topic [23], and the
application of an outer layer might not prevent internal damage. The prevention of internal
damage must be considered important as it can lead to greatly reduced strength [24].
The dierence in tensile strength is of importance when it comes to the maximum eccen-
tricity of the curved bridge, but the achievable eccentricity is so low that this should not
factor into the considerations for the lling material. For both torsional shear taken in the
lling and side walls, the possible eccentricity is so poor that this method is considered in-
adequate for construction of a curved arch. The inclusion of shear reinforcement could of
course improve the torsional capacity but could only be placed in concrete strong enough
not to be crushed before the reinforcement was activated. Of course the inclusion of shear
reinforcement in strong concrete provides an alternative way of transferring the shear forces
in the bridge, though this is not investigated in this report.
From the considerations presented above, design 3 is deemed to be the most optimal due
to its lower complexity than design 1 and higher frost resistance than design 2. It is possible
that a detailed cost analysis could show other results, but such an analysis is out of scope for
this project.

Conclusion
The sandwich arch bridge concept have been investigated in this report and a case study
of a 30 m long bridge performed. This resulted in the mapping of the inuence of dierent
geometrical parameters on the forces in the bridge as well as 3 dierent design suggestions.
Additionally dierent ways of constructing a curved sandwich arch bridge were considered.
The bridge was analysed both as a simple 3 hinge arch bridge and as a truss structure. For
the truss structure the force in the arch, deck, lling and vertical walls were calculated. Lastly
the construction of the bridge was considered.
The inuence of the height above the crown, hoc, the wall placements, load placement and
bridge length were investigated. An increase hoc resulted in a decrease of all forces, except the
force in the arch. An increased number of vertical walls resulted in an increase in the force in
the walls and a decrease in the force in the lling. The inuence of wall skewness proved to
be less clear. It was shown that there was not a single load positioning that simultaneously
resulted in the maximum forces in all elements of the bridge. Increased span lengths was
shown to result in larger forces and the conclusions for the other geometrical parameters
proved to be the same for diering span lengths, with a few exceptions.
The presented ways of constructing a curved sandwich arch bridge must be considered either
inadequate, when it comes to bearing capacity or infeasible, when considering the construction
process.
When comparing the 3 presented designs, design 3 is deemed to be the most optimal due to
its lower complexity than design 1 and higher frost resistance than design 2.
