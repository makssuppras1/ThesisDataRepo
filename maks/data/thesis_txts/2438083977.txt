Summary (English)
Along the promises of eﬃciency, electronic and online election protocols require
both security and privacy in order to be implemented. It has become clear over
the last decade that one needs to formally specify the goals and assumptions of
such voting systems and formally verify them. Of particular interest is here the
speciﬁcation and veriﬁcation of privacy goals.
We consider in this thesis the approach of (α,β)-privacy that is based on speci-
fying two formulae α and β in ﬁrst-order logic with Herbrand universes, where α
reﬂects the intentionally released information and β includes the actual crypto-
graphic messages the intruder can see. The thesis will study how (α,β)-privacy
can be used on an entire voting protocol, and as the main case study, we use
the known secret voting protocol “FOO 92”.
This involves formalizing a transition system of (α,β)-formulae, and verifying
that every reachable state satisﬁes (α,β)-privacy. Moreover, we shall formalize
other privacy-related goals such as receipt-freeness.

1.1 Objectives
3
It appears we need strong guarantees towards electronic voting.
The areas
to study are abundant. When reading the previous report, sort of validation
control of voting machines are needed.
This work has higher-level concerns
though. Before thinking about the implementation, the transition to electronic
voting requires to model electoral systems as voting schemes and protocols in
order to formally analyze them.
1.1
Objectives
This project aims to analyze a speciﬁc election protocol, the FOO92 voting
scheme [21]. Numerous formal methods have been developed to analyze security
protocols, including election protocols which are a relevant case. Approaches
based on static equivalence of frames have been thoroughly explored. We use a
newly developed approach called (α,β)-privacy [28]. It consists of a novel and
declarative way to specify privacy goals.
We specify the goals our voting scheme should verify. Various goals have been
traditionally associated with election protocols. We showed that the accuracy of
the voting scheme is crucial for the protocol to fulﬁll its objectives. This latter
goal is part of veriﬁability goals. In this work, we are not concerned with this
category of properties, even though we will mention previous analysis of FOO92
that treated them. We focus on privacy goals. We will formally specify voter
privacy and receipt-freeness goals as α and β formulae.
This involves formalizing the voting scheme as a transition system of (α,β)-
formulae and verifying that every reachable state satisﬁes (α,β)-privacy. For
the purpose of its analysis, we will construct an interpretation that is consistent
with the formulae and their models to show the respect of (α,β)-privacy.
1.2
Literature
We will give some background on the study on voting schemes. This includes
precising what is a voting model. This allows us to put the FOO92 protocol
into a global context. The most common security properties of a voting scheme
will be stated and we will deﬁne the scope of our analysis.
(α,β)-privacy is a logical approach and before formalizing it we need to state
an appropriate logic. We strongly rely on Herbrand Logic [22], that is to say a

First-Order Logic with Herbrand universes. We will establish the form of the
exchanged messages of the protocol we study. We also encode a notion of frames
into Herbrand logic so (α,β)-privacy will be also compared to the classical static
equivalence of frames.
We oﬀer a thorough presentation of the FOO92 voting scheme. We introduce its
original design, including the security properties. Previous analysis are of some
interest to be compared later to our own analysis. Finally, a variant directly
built on the protocol will be looked into since the ideas they introduced can be
related to our own modiﬁcations.
The voting scheme analyzed in this work has a long tradition.
It has been
developed in 1992 and since diverse variants have been built on it. The protocol
has already been formally analyzed, markedly with methods based on static
equivalence of frames. We evoked one of these analysis by Steve Kremer and
Mark Ryan [25] based on the applied pi calculus.

Chapter 2
E-voting: challenges and
solutions
The need for secure, reliable and trustworthy e-voting scheme has been ex-
pressed. Yet, the technical challenges surrounding the design of a voting proto-
col have to be explicated. What does it mean for voting schemes to be secure?
Specifying the goals is not enough. How can it be proved that the designed
protocol veriﬁes these properties? Developing analysis techniques for security
protocols is a preponderant concern for the research community in logic and
program veriﬁcation. Among these security protocols, voting schemes hold a
peculiar place. The high stakes of some elections create unique challenges.
We brieﬂy mentioned in Chapter 1 the importance of the electoral system, that
is to say the set of rules that determines how elections and referendums are
conducted and how their results are determined. We do not claim that e-voting
can improve democracy, election turn-outs, etc. E-voting scheme can only reﬂect
the quality of a the electoral system they model.
The question of the quality of these voting schemes is not easy to answer. To
evaluate it, clear properties have to be stated. Two substantial property families
are commonly considered in formal veriﬁcation: veriﬁability and privacy prop-
erties. The latter is of crucial importance in the analysis we present in Chapter
6. Some properties as usability will not be addressed further in our analysis.
==PAGE:16==
6
E-voting: challenges and solutions
2.1
Voting models
Before introducing the protocol that will be studied in this work, we think it
is of some interest to shortly allude the diﬀerent sort of voting schemes. Some
works summarize the diversity of the voting schemes that have been developed
the last decades, e.g. [24] or [7].
2.1.1
Voting machines and online voting
E-voting covers various means of voting. It is possible to class them into two
categories: voting machines and online voting. Voting machines have already a
long tradition, especially in the United States. Their accuracy and their speed
are the main arguments when they are publicly advertised. In the commercial
movie [36], the main argument was already their exactitude: “The machine never
makes mistake”. Nevertheless, voting machines still require the voters to come
out at a polling stations. Then, the voting machines are simply an interface
for the voters to cast their votes and to automatically process and count the
ballots.
On the contrary, online voting does not require the voters to go to a partic-
ular place. They require more a less just a personal terminal from where the
voters can perform the voting actions. It could be the own voters devices as
for instance their computers. However, on-line voting schemes raise even more
challenges. Polling stations have the advantage to isolate the voter from the
outside environment. Ballot boxes also provide a convenient way to anonymise
ballots and it is easy for whoever wants to watch to prevent frauds. To repro-
duce the contributions from these elements, voting schemes use among others
cryptographic systems.
2.1.2
Using homomorphic encryption
Homomorphic cryptosystems, such as [33], encrypt ballots in a way one can
operate on the encrypted ballots. For instance, it is possible to operate on these
encrypted ballots and to obtain another cryptogram representing the sum of the
votes from all voters. It is useful to maintain the voter anonymity and ballot
privacy during the whole election.

2.1.3
Using Mix-net
The mix-net has been conceptualized in [13] by David Chaum.
It provides
an anonymous channel that mixes the cast votes and destroys the correlation
of their order. This anonymous channel is implemented with a set of mixing
servers. Each server permutes the order of the votes and transform them by
encrypting them for instance. Mix-nets are the basis for onion routing. It is not
considered suitable for large-scale election schemes. Two examples of electronic
voting systems based on mix-nets are [35] and [34].
2.1.4
Using blind signatures and commitments
Chaum introduced the concept of blind signatures in [11]. The idea is to digitally
sign data, in our case a ballot, without revealing its content.
Commitment
schemes were ﬁrst formalize in [10]. A protocol player, here a voter, chooses
a value from a set and commits to their choice, for instance a candidate. The
choice can be revealed only if the voter release the value. Blind signatures and
commitments are the two basis for the protocol we analyze in this thesis.
2.2
Properties
Two categories of properties are introduced: veriﬁability properties and privacy
properties. Only the latter are studied in our analysis. However, as a conse-
quence of the importance of the former in the design of a voting scheme, we still
present them.
2.2.1
Veriﬁability properties
Most voting schemes produce winners and losers. Thus, for an election to be
accepted as legitimate, the results must be convincing to all: voters, authorities
and candidates. However the voting schemes are implemented, whether it is in
a traditional manner or through an electronic process, the possibility of errors
or fraud makes accuracy assurances necessary.
Lately, several veriﬁability properties have been gathered in the so-called end-
to-end veriﬁability (E2E-V). The following deﬁnition is taken from [5].
==PAGE:18==
8
E-voting: challenges and solutions
Definition 2.1 (End-to-end verifiability) A voting system is end-
to-end veriﬁable if it has the following three kinds of veriﬁability:
• Cast as intended: Voters can independently verify that their selections
are correctly recorded.
• Collected as cast: Voters can independently verify that the representa-
tion of their vote is correctly collected in the tally.
• Tallied as collected: Anyone can verify that every well-formed, collected
vote is correctly included in the tally.
□
E2E-V voting systems consist in directly providing to the voters means to check
and monitor the integrity of the elections. In these systems, errors and frauds
in the process of voting and tallying must be detectable. When designing such
systems, one should keep in mind, as explained in Section 2.2.2, that a total
transparency is nearly never desirable.
This veriﬁability property puts a great responsibility on the voter. Indeed, what
good would be the system if nobody is exercising their veriﬁability properties?
Fortunately, in most cases, only a small portion of the voters who is performing
these checks is required to ensure compelling evidence.
The usability and the skills required to perform these checks are also question-
able. Ordinarily, software can be provided to perform them. However, it then
raises the question of if the checking software can be trusted and of who is
checking the checking software.
Some also add to the previously cited properties eligibility veriﬁability. Only
legitimate voters must be able to successfully cast a vote. This is done in order
to prevent ballot stuﬃng.
2.2.2
Privacy properties
Privacy properties are at the core of this work. In most protocols, “privacy”
and “conﬁdentiality” are interchangeable. However, when it comes to voting
schemes, privacy is a more subtle property. Privacy is an end per se for election
==PAGE:19==
2.2 Properties
9
systems. If a voter is not certain that their privacy is going to be respected,
they might not express their true preferences during the election. This would
be a direct threat to democracy. We develop in this part the diﬀerent privacy
properties and class them intro three types: conﬁdentiality, receipt-freeness and
coercion-resistance.
2.2.2.1
Conﬁdentiality
Conﬁdentiality means that it should be impossible to link a voter with their
vote. In other words, it means that the system should not leak any information
about how a voter voted that cannot be deduced from the published results.
Sometimes, the conﬁdentiality property is deﬁned in terms of vote swaps. An
intruder should not be able to notice anything when two voters swap their
votes. In the rest of this work, when we simply refer to “privacy”, it will mean
“conﬁdentiality”.
2.2.2.2
Receipt-freeness
In voting schemes, not only a voter privacy should be ensured, but also a voter
must not be able to yield their vote. That is to say that a voter must not be able
to prove how they voted. We call this property receipt-freeness. One of the ﬁrst
to address receipt-freeness in Josh Benaloh in [6]. However, as pointed in [15],
no formal deﬁnition of receipt-freeness was given. This led to ﬂawed protocol
designs. We give an informal deﬁnition of receipt-freeness taken from [7]:
Definition 2.2 (Receipt-freeness) A voting system is receipt free if
a voter is unable to prove how she voted even is she actively colludes with a
coercer and deviates from the protocol in order to try to produce a proof.
□
One of the main contribution to this work is to give a formal deﬁnition of
receipt-freeness. We encode this property into Herbrand logic in Section 6.3.
2.2.2.3
Coercion Resistance
An even stronger privacy property has been deﬁned: coercion-resistance. This
distinction between receipt-freeness and coercion-resistance in not always clear
in the literature. The major diﬀerence that is usually pointed out is that for
==PAGE:20==
10
E-voting: challenges and solutions
coercion-resistance, the intruder is able to actively coerce the voter during the
election. As for receipt-freeness, the coercion commonly takes place after the
publication of the results. We give the deﬁnition from [15]:
Definition 2.3 (Coercion-resistance) A voting system veriﬁes coercion-
resistance if a voter cannot cooperate with a coercer to prove to him that she
voted in a certain way.
□
We do not develop a formal deﬁnition of coercion-resistance in this thesis. It is
left as future work.
2.2.2.4
Everlasting privacy
We will not consider this property in this work but we mention it to the reader.
In our model, we do not consider the strength of the encryption systems. As
explained in Section 3.4, we use the classical Dolev-Yao intruder model where
cryptanalysis of messages is impossible. But the current encryption systems
could be broken in the future. Moran and Nor expressed a concern about en-
crypted votes that could be later linked to the voters. They deﬁne in [27] a new
security property called everlasting privacy.
Definition 2.4 (Everlasting privacy) A voting scheme has everlast-
ing privacy if its privacy does not depend on assumptions of cryptographic
hardness.
□
They, for instance, introduce a solution using commitments and homomorphic
encryption that makes the integrity of the elections rely on cryptographic hard-
ness rather than its privacy. This would be a problem only if an intruder can
act during the limited time of the election, as once the results are published, it
is of little use to try to break the encryption systems.
2.2.3
Other relevant properties
The voting scheme we analyze in this work has been design for large-scale elec-
tions. Even if it is not the purpose of this thesis, one has to consider that the
amount of computational power needed to perform the election was a crucial cri-
terion for this voting scheme. Therefore, it guided the choice of the paradigm.

That is why, for instance, mix-net was not a possible choice for the FOO92
voting scheme.
Usability is also consider important. Indeed, the users, here the voters, have to
be able to perform the actions easily. But in our case, this property has been
eluded.

Chapter 3
Preliminaries
Before formalizing our approach — (α,β)-privacy — in Chapter 4, we need to
state an appropriate logic. We follow the work of Sebastian Mödersheim and
Luca Viganò in [28]. We discuss Herbrand Logic in Section 3.1, i.e. First-Order
Logic with Herbrand Universes.
We then specify in Section 3.2 the form of
messages in our framework with a typical black-box algebraic model that consists
of an arbitrary set of cryptographic operators and their algebraic properties. We
then introduce in Section 3.3 the notion of frames encoded in Herbrand logic
that we widely use throughout this work. We ﬁnally evoke in Section 3.4 the
intruder model and the assumptions about the network.
3.1
Herbrand Logic
First-Order Logic (FOL) could have been a choice for this work. However, it
is not well-suited for computer science. Indeed, the interpretation of constants
and of the cryptographic operators are not easy. We need to interpret them in
the free algebra or the algebra induced by a set of algebraic equations. It is then
not possible to express everything we need only with FOL. Instead, it is possible
to use Herbrand Logic proposed in [22] as a plain and a higher expressive logic.
Note that most of the deﬁnitions that we give come directly from the speciﬁca-
==PAGE:23==
3.1 Herbrand Logic
13
tion of Herbrand Logic found in [28]. They are well-suited for the (α,β)-privacy
approach. Indeed, the authors adapt the work of [22] to the context of (α,β)-
privacy.
We successively give the deﬁnitions of the syntax of Herbrand Logic in Deﬁnition
3.1, of Herbrand universe and algebra in Deﬁnition 3.2 and of the semantics of
Herbrand Logic in Deﬁnition 3.3.
Definition 3.1 (Syntax of Herbrand Logic) Let Σ = Σf ⊎Σi ⊎Σr
be an alphabet that consists of
• a set Σf of uninterpreted function symbols,
• a set Σi of interpreted function symbols and
• a set Σr of relation symbols,
all with their arities. We write f n and rn to denote a function symbol f and a
relation symbol r of arity n, respectively.
We write f(t1, . . . , tn) when f ∈Σf and f[t1, . . . , tn] when f ∈Σi, and we
denote the set of considered cryptographic operators by the subset Σop ⊆Σf.
Constants are the special case of function symbols with arity 0; for an uninter-
preted constant c0 ∈Σf, we omit the parentheses and write simply c instead

3.4
The intruder model and the network
Our intruder model is the henceforth standard Dolev-Yao intruder model [18].
It markedly means that the intruder controls the entire network. We do not
rely on the network to oﬀer any protection. However, as explained just after,
we will introduce the notion of secure channel on which the intruder has limited
or no control. From now on, we consider that the intruder can read messages,
block them, divert them to a diﬀerent recipient and insert new messages. Every
user or entity has a pair of public and private keys. All the public keys are
known to every one. The private keys are, at least initially, only known by their
owners.
We do not consider that all the participants are necessarily honest
people, or at least that they are ﬂawless. As it is standard, the intruder can
use all public cryptographic operations but he cannot further analyze messages
with cryptanalysis for instance.
We will use to model the FOO92 protocol the notion of channels. We use a
precious notation for channels called the bullet-calculus [26]. It was initially
an abstract way to model cryptography as the properties of a communication
channel. There are three kinds of channels. On an authentic channel A • →
B, B is certain that the message comes from A. On a conﬁdential channel A
→• B, only B can read the message. A secure channel A • →• B is both
an authentic and a conﬁdential channel. Only B can read the message that he
knows for sure is coming from A. We will only use the later kind of channel to
model our protocols.

(α,β)-privacy
As mentioned before, a number of works already focused on specifying privacy
goals in a formal way. However, such a formal deﬁnition is not that easy to come
up with. Among formal methods, one could roughly think of the approaches
based on static equivalence of frames in the applied pi-calculus. We introduced
it in Deﬁnition 3.10 of the previous chapter and we explained how to express it
in our logical framework. The static equivalence of frames approach consists of
the possibility for the intruder to distinguish between two given worlds. But the
problem of specifying the set of needed pairs of worlds to deﬁne the identiﬁed
goals arises. A more intuitive way of deﬁning privacy goals could improve this
deﬁnition.
This problem has been identiﬁed and addressed in [28] by Sebastian Mödersheim
and Luca Viganò.
They introduced a novel and declarative way to specify
privacy goals called (α,β)-privacy. α and β are two formulae in ﬁrst-order logic
with Herbrand universes — Herbrand logic has been introduced in Chapter 3. α
is the intentionally released information, i.e. the “non-technical” information. β
represents the actual cryptographic messages that the intruder observes, i.e. the
“technical” information. Intuitively, (α,β)-privacy holds where β does not bring
more “non-technical” information than α. In other words, the intruder should
not be able to derive “non-technical” information from β that he cannot derive
from α. The authors prove that a close relationship between static equivalence of
frames and (α,β)-privacy holds. They build on this relation to obtain a decision

4.1
Basis on (α,β)-privacy
4.1.1
Context
Is is rather straightforward to understand the notion of secrets. A secret is for
instance a piece of information that must be known only to clearly identiﬁed
parties. Following this intuitive deﬁnition, it is trivial to state secrecy goals.
In a protocol, it could consist in keeping secret data between identiﬁed parties.
For instance, traditionally private keys should be known only to their owners.
However, privacy goals are more subtle than secrecy goals. A number of formal
notions of privacy have been proposed, e.g. [2], [9], [14], [16], [19], [37], [38],
[40] and [1]. Despite some diﬀerences in their deﬁnitions, they all outline that
privacy goals are usually not deﬁned in terms of secrecy. Our case is a good
example: Votes and candidates are not secret, they are typically known to every
parties. However, the relation between these values have to be kept secret. For
instance, who voted for whom is not a public information.
4.1.2
Payload and Technical Information
The authors based their model on zero-knowledge proofs for privacy. Indeed, in
these proofs, the prover intentionally releases some information to the veriﬁer.
This information is called the statement and in our model it corresponds to
the payload α. For instance, in a voting scheme, the list of candidates is made
public from the beginning of the vote. When exchanging messages, other types of

zero-knowledge proofs. The latter kind of information is called, in our model,

4.1.3
Privacy on Messages
As it is standard, the frames we introduced formalize the intruders concrete
knowledge.
But for our approach, we also need to formalize the structural
information the intruder has about messages.
Most of the protocols in real
environments have a public structure made public in standards. To quote Claude
Shannon, “the enemy knows the system” or even “one ought to design systems
under the assumption that the enemy will immediately gain full familiarity
with them ”. This structural knowledge also takes the form of a frame, but with
variables in messages. The concrete knowledge is then a peculiar instance of
this structural knowledge. In (α,β)-privacy, the intruder can use this structural
knowledge for his deductions.
Example 4.1 An intruder can observe a message commit(a, b) for instance.
This is part of his concrete knowledge. He knows that the term is the application
of the cryptographic operator commit to two terms, but he does not know which
terms. His structural information has then the form of commit(v, r) with two
variable v and r that can be instantiated but he does not know how.

(α,β)-privacy has been extended to transition systems. We use a slightly sim-

details as a transition system in Chapter 7.
The original deﬁnition deﬁned a state as a triple (α,β,γ) of formulae, where γ
represents the truth. We replace γ by the previously mentioned θ being a model
of α and acting as a substitution. Along the same lines, θ represents the truth.
We aim to prove that (α,β)-privacy holds in every reachable state (α,β,θ).
Definition 4.10 (Transition systems) A state is a triple (α,β,θ), where
α and β are as before and θ ∈LΣ0(V) is such that θ |= α and θ is true in exactly
one model of α (with respect to Σ0 and the free variables of α). We also call θ
the truth and may also apply it to Σ0-terms like a substitution.
Let S denote the set of all states. A transition system is a pair (I,R) where I ∈S
and R ⊆S × S. As is standard, the set of reachable states is the smallest set
that contains I and that is closed under R, i.e.: if S is reachable and (S,S′ ∈R,
then also S′ is reachable.
We say that a transition system satisﬁes privacy iﬀ(α,β)-privacy holds in every
reachable state (α,β,θ).

The FOO92 protocol proposed by Atsushi Fujioka, Tatsuaki Okamoto and Kazuo

Widely studied throughout the literature, it has served as the basis for more

5.1.2
The security of the voting scheme
In [21], the security properties for the FOO92 protocol have been deﬁned as
follows:

• Completeness: All valid votes are counted correctly.
• Soundness: The dishonest voter cannot disrupt the voting.
• Privacy: All votes must be secret.
• Unreusability: No voter can vote twice.
• Eligibility: No one who isn’t allowed to vote can vote.
• Fairness: Nothing must aﬀect the voting.
• Veriﬁability: No one can falsify the result of the voting.
A secret voting scheme is said to be secure if it veriﬁes this properties. The

requirements are omitted by this simple protocol.

5.2.2
Analysis by Kremer and Ryan
Steve Kremer and Mark Ryan proposed a formal veriﬁcation of the FOO92
scheme in [25] using the applied pi calculus [2]. It is a language that allows
to describe concurrent processes and their interaction. It has been base on the
pi-calculus. However, it is easier to use as it is less pure. In their analysis, the
authors use both manual techniques [2] and automated tools like ProVerif [8].
ProVerif is able to check privacy properties as reachability properties and is also
able in some cases to prove that processes are observationally equivalent.
We will not go into details concerning the applied pi calculus.
The authors
introduce three notions for their proof: observational equivalence (≈), static
equivalence (≈s) and labelled bisimilarity (≈l). Many properties of security pro-
tocols are formalized in terms of observational equivalence between processes.
Unfortunately, the ability of ProVerif to prove observational equivalence is in-
complete. We already evoked static equivalence in Chapter 3. Basically, two
frames are statically equivalent if, when considered as substitutions, they agree
on the distinguishability of terms. The authors mention that for frames static
equivalence agrees with observational equivalence, while for general extended
processes, observational equivalence is ﬁner. Labelled bisimilarity is similar to
usual bisimilarities except that at each step processes need to be statically in-
dependent. When ProVerif cannot prove observational equivalence, the authors
then prove at each step bisimilarity and static equivalence.
The veriﬁcation is not restricted to a bounded number of sessions. The authors
do not need to explicitly deﬁne the intruder.
In ProVerif, the channels are
automatically consider anonymous. They deﬁne a signature and an equational
theory in a process, similar to what we deﬁnes in Chapter 3. They also deﬁne
a main process for the environment. They ﬁnally deﬁne three more processes:
one for the voter, one for the administrator and one for the counter.
This analysis is concerned with three properties: fairness, eligibility and privacy.

8.2
No respect of the phases
Steve Kremer and Mark Ryan emphasize in [25] the importance of the respect of
the three phases. Every voter has to complete the current phase before the next
can start. Let us assume that the scheme is not enforcing it so that the intruder
can ask a coerced voter not to open his vote until the end of the counting phase.
We ask the following question: what happens if a voter chooses not to open
his ballot at the end, that is to say, not to send their commitment value to the
bulletin board? What happens if no voters choose to open their vote?
8.2.1
A voter chooses not to open a vote
Let us assume it is Dan that refuses to open his vote. It means that Dan did
not send his commitment value to the counter. Then, the counter is unable
to print on the bulletin board the corresponding commitment value. On the
bulletin board, there is now an unopened ballot. Dan is sending as part of the
set of lying recipes a commitment value. He cannot just pick any value from
the bulletin board anymore because the intruder is expecting to use the value
Dan sends to open the ballot.
In this case, Dan has to send his true r[D] to the intruder. Therefore, receipt-
freeness is not veriﬁed, the intruder succeeded to coerce Dan and he has a
==PAGE:80==
70

proof of how he voted. This emphasizes the fact that in FOO92 all voters must
complete all steps to verify the security properties.
8.2.2
Several voters choose to open their vote
Now, we assume that several voters did not have sent their commitment values.
Only Dan has been coerced in this way. It is not relevant that the other has been
or not coerced by the intruder. However, this brings us back to the previous one.
The intruder expects Dan not to have opened his vote. So, he still has to give
to the intruder a commitment value that correctly opens one of the unopened
ballot. As the voters to whom belong the other unopened ballots did not send
their commitment values neither, Dan cannot give a correct commitment value
for these ballots.
He is forced to give his correct commitment value to the
intruder. Therefore, receipt-freeness is not veriﬁed, the intruder succeeded to
coerce Dan and he has a proof of how he voted. This emphasizes again the
fact that in FOO92 all voters must complete all steps to verify the security
properties.
8.3
The intruder coerces several voters
An interesting case arises when the intruder tries to coerce several voters. We
deﬁned the intruder’s hypothesis θI as a model of α. However, when the intruder
is trying to coerce voters, he is giving them instructions to follow. For instance,
if the intruder asked 10 voters to vote for 1 but at the end 1 only gets 8 votes,
he can conclude that some of the voters did not vote as he asked. This way,
there are two cases to consider: whether the concrete situation itself does not
contradict the intruder’s hypothesis or not. For the ﬁrst case, it is interesting
to wonder if the coerced voters collaborate together or not.
8.3.1
The coerced voters collaborate
Suppose there are enough votes for the candidates the intruder tried to favour.
Then, if the coerced voters collaborate, they can divide correct votes up. For
instance the intruder coerced 8 voters to vote for 1 and on the bulletin board
there are 10 votes for 1. The coerced voters collaborate and they make sure
that they all pick a diﬀerent ballot from the bulletin board to exhibit to the
intruder. Then, we get back to the case of our analysis in Chapter 7.

71
Therefore, receipt-freeness is veriﬁed, the intruder did not succeed to coerce
the voters. The voters were able to ﬁnd collectively a consistent story with an
arbitrary intruder’s hypothesis.
8.3.2
The coerced voters do not collaborate
Let us consider for instance exactly the same case. The intruder coerced 8 voters
to vote for 1 and on the bulletin board there are 10 votes for 1. The coerced
voters did not collaborate. It is then possible than two or more of them pick the
same ballot from the bulletin board to exhibit to the intruder. If the intruder
gets several times the same ballot in his knowledge, he can conclude that some
of the coerced voters tried to lie.
The voters were not able to ﬁnd collectively a consistent with an arbitrary
intruder’s hypothesis. Therefore, receipt-freeness is not veriﬁed. However, that
does not mean that privacy is not veriﬁed, the intruder still does not know how
each voter voted.
8.3.3
The coerced voters cannot come up with a consistent
shared story
Suppose there are not enough votes for the candidates the intruder tried to
favour. It means that the coerced voters will obviously not be able to come up
with a consistent story collectively.
8.4
Future work
This discussion leads the way to future work.
First, it would be interesting
to come up with a formal deﬁnition of coercion-resistance. That is to say, an
encoding into Herbrand logic of the coercion-resistance property. One of the
diﬀerence we make between receipt-freeness and coercion-resistance is that with
coercion-resistance, the intruder tries to coerce the voter at every step of the
voting scheme. The intruder is not just asking for a proof of how the voter voted
at the end of the protocol.
One could also extend our approach to model the variants based on the FOO92
voting scheme to test the veriﬁcation on them. Some of these variants have been

an interesting challenge to show whether an approach based on (α,β)-privacy
can catch these errors. Another extension would be to state formally the cases
evoked during the discussions.

Chapter 9
Conclusion
I’ve arranged with my executor to
be buried in Chicago. Because
when I die, I want to still remain
active politically.
Mort Sahl
This satiric sentence, attributed to Mort Sahl, expresses the need for trustworthy
elections. Some argue that the answer could be electronic voting. These systems
must be veriﬁable so that people can be certain of their outcomes. However,
as we explained in the introduction of this work, even if it is relatively easy to
ensure privacy with physical means in traditional election systems, it is a proper
challenge for electronic voting.
This work is not concerned with veriﬁability properties. We use a novel and
declarative way to specify privacy goals, (α,β)-privacy [28]. We wanted to ex-
pend this framework to receipt-freeness properties. Our case study is the FOO92
voting scheme [21]. We aimed to model this protocol as a transition system of
(α,β) formulae. We then had to check that the modeled voting scheme was
verifying the privacy properties we speciﬁed.
In Chapter 2, we brieﬂy introduce the context of electronic voting schemes and
==PAGE:84==
74
Conclusion
the challenges their design is facing. We markedly give an informal deﬁnition of
privacy goals.
Chapter 3 states an appropriate logic for our approach, namely Herbrand Logic
[22]. It also speciﬁes the form of the exchanged messages, especially introducing
the algebraic model. We also encode a notion of frames in Herbrand Logic.
Chapter 4 introduces the (α,β)-privacy approach developed by Sebastian Möder-
sheim and Luca Viganò [28]. It raises the need for a new approach to formalize
privacy goals, comparing it the classical static equivalence of frames. We encode
the basic privacy goals in the form of a message-analysis problem. We also give
a model-theoretical approach to guide our proofs and deﬁne transition systems.
Chapter 5 describes the FOO92 voting scheme [21].
We browse a previous
analysis of the voting scheme. We also introduce some attempts to improve
the protocol. Some of them can be related to the augmentation or additional
assumptions we made in our analysis.
In Chapter 6, we present the scope for our analysis. We expend some deﬁnitions,
such as the structural information and the concrete knowledge as frames. We
introduce the intruder’s hypothesis θI and the intruder’s interpretation I. We
show how this interpretation models some of our formulae introduced earlier to
model privacy goals. We also encode formally the receipt-freeness problem into
(α,β)-privacy.
We model in Chapter 7 the FOO92 voting scheme into a transition systems of
(α,β) formulae. We prove that every reachable state in this transition system
respects (α,β)-privacy. For this purpose, every interpreted symbol is modeled by
the interpretation I. We ﬁrst study the respect of basic privacy properties and
we introduce the receipt-freeness problem at the end of the transition system.
We discuss in Chapter 8 the results of our analysis.
We come back to the
augmentation and the additional assumptions we made. We also discuss about
possible deviations or limits to the original voting scheme such as voters not
being enforced to rigorously following the diﬀerent phases of the protocol.
In conclusion, this work successfully applies the novel and declarative (α,β)-
privacy approach to a full voting protocol. Privacy goals for election systems
are subtle and we reﬁne their model. The message-analysis problem can model
basic privacy goals and we give a formalization and an encoding in Herbrand
Logic of the receipt-freeness property. We then model an entire protocol, the
FOO92 voting scheme, into a transition system of (α,β)-privacy and we are able
to verify the properties.

5 variants of the protocol. These variants are more complex to model in (α,β)-
privacy but it could be of some interest to compare their analysis to this one.
