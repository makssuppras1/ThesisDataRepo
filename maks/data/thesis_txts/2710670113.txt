Abstract (English)
As glaciers melt and permafrost thaws due to climate change, large amounts of dormant
microbes are being released into the environment. This raises several obvious questions
like; What kind of microbes are being released? Are any of the released microbes po­
tentially harmful? Do any of the released microbes carry genes we should be concerned
about, like novel antimicrobial resistance genes?
This thesis presents a study of the microbial communities found within kitchen middens
of the Inuit and Norse cultures that inhabited Greenland throughout history. Reasons for
the collapse of these cultures are not yet fully understood, and it cannot be ruled out that
pathogens played a role. By combining metagenomic approaches based on analyzing
reads, assembled contigs, and metagenome­assembled genomes, it was found that the
microbial community composition varied a lot between sample locations. Furthermore,
it was found that genes related to virulence and antimicrobial resistance are part of the
natural metagenome in this environment. One location in particular, Sermermiut, had ele­
vated levels of several genes related to antimicrobial resistance and virulence compared
to samples from other locations.
Resistance to a wide array of antibiotics was observed, but resistance to tetracycline and
fluoroquinolone was most common. The high presence of genes related to efflux pumps,
suggest this as a main mechanism of resistance.
Although, genes related to virulence was found within all metagenome­assembled genomes,
some of these genomes had especially high amounts of these genes and represent po­
tential candidates for pathogens.

1
Introduction
Spillover of emerging infectious diseases from the environment causes a serious threat to
global health, clearly demonstrated by the ongoing coronavirus pandemic. In the case of
coronavirus, bats served as a reservoir for the virus which then spilled over into humans
[1]. Soil hosts some of the most complex and diverse microbial communities [2], and like
bats, soil can serve as a reservoir for pathogens including Bacillus anthracis (B. anthracis)
[3] and Pseudomonas aeruginosa (P. aeruginosa) [4]. Spillover of disease from soil can
happen either by direct transmission or through animal vectors [3]. Soil is also found
to be a reservoir of antibiotic resistance genes (AMR), which can be spread between
populations through horizontal gene transfer (HGT) [5]. Understanding complex microbial
soil communities can help prevent future spillover of diseases and the spread of AMR.
Currently little is known about the microbial soil communities in remote and harsh en­
vironments [4]. Here, microbes have evolved to withstand harsh conditions, and thus
these environments can potentially serve as source of novel pathogens and AMR genes
[4]. Large amount of mostly dormant microorganisms are hidden in permanently frozen
environments like glaciers and permafrost. It has been estimated that ∼4 × 1021 microor­
ganisms are released annually from these environments and enter local ecosystems [6].
The hypothesis, that pathogens which have been gone from the face of the earth for thou­
sands or millions of years are now being released, seems to be supported by anthrax
outbreaks in Siberia. The cause of these outbreaks has in part been attributed to the
thawing of permafrost and reactivation of B. anthracis [7].
Despite the harsh conditions, humans have been settling along the coast of Greenland for
thousands of years. History there has been characterized by local reoccurring extinction
events and at least three distinct ancient cultures have collapsed in Greenland throughout
time [8] [9]. Although, these extinction events are likely explained by the harsh conditions,
change in climate and disappearance of food sources, it cannot be ruled out that disease
also has been a factor [9]. Middens of human settlements in Greenland contains large
amount of well preserved organic material, that are now being released due to erosion
and permafrost thawing [10]. Moreover, settlements in Greenland are typically located in
coastal areas with good hunting and fishing opportunities, like Disko bay [9], areas that
today are important for commercial fishing.
This thesis aims to profile the microbial community of Greenlandic middens using metage­
nomic sequencing techniques. Furthermore, a workflow is proposed to identify potential
pathogens within complex microbial communities.

Theory
2.1
Ancient cultures of Greenland and the content of their
middens
Human history in Greenland has been molded by harsh Arctic conditions, where small cli­
matic changes can have severe impact on the survival of its inhabitants [8]. People have
migrated to Greenland, through a gateway to North America in the high Arctic, and settled
along a narrow band of habitable islands along the coast [9]. Migration, often followed by
local extinction events, has happened recurrently since the first humans arrived in Green­
land 4500 years ago [8]. This has led to at least three distinct ancient cultures, the Saqqaq
Paleo­Inuit culture (2500­800BC), the Dorset Paleo­Inuit culture (800BC­1300AD) and a
Norse culture (985­1450AD). All of these cultures have eventually collapsed and are now
replaced by the Neo­Inuit Thule culture (1200AD­today)[8] [9].
Figure 2.1: Foto of erosion front of Qajaa midden showing bones and rocks. Foto from
Elberling et al. [10]
To gather information about the ancient cultures of Greenland, excavation of midden de­
posits have shown to be a useful tool. Especially middens at archaeological sites around
Disko bay have given valuable insight into Paleo­Inuit cultures. Middens are mounds of
soil, peat, bones, stones, tools and other remnants of human habitation. At the right con­
ditions with quick deposit rates and permafrost moving quickly into the midden, organic
material and archaeological artifacts can be preserved for thousands of years [10]. On
the picture 2.1, it can be seen how erosion of a midden at the archaeological site in Qajaa
have revealed bones and rocks deposited in the midden throughout time. Midden deposits
have been excavated throughout the last 100 years with the outcome of some interesting
findings. For instance, well preserved human hair found in a midden at Qeqertasussuk

ally, a metagenomic study of eukaryotic DNA content in middens from both, Paleo­Inuit
and Norse settlements has given insight into the diet of early Greenlandic cultures and no­

Climate change, which is expected to be more extreme in the Arctic [12], now poses
a threat to the conditions favourable for preservation of organic artifacts in the middens.
Due to increases in air temperature and change in precipitation patterns, it is expected that
there will be an increased ground thawing. This leads to drainage and erosion, which in
turn increases subsurface oxygen availability. Furthermore, heat generation by increased
microbial activity is shown to cause a positive feedback loop with the warming effect of
climate change [10].
2.2
Metagenomics
Like genomics is the study of genetic information of an organism, metagenomics is the
study of genetic information of all organisms in an environment. Through advances in
high­throughput sequencing techniques and bioinformatic tools, metagenomics has be­
come an important part of microbial ecology [13] [14]. Microbiome sequencing can be
divided into two different approaches, marker gene sequencing and whole­genome shot­
gun (WGS) sequencing. Marker gene sequencing is based on sequencing of a specific
region to investigate diversity and composition of specific taxonomic groups. To investi­
gate bacteria and archaea communities the 16S rRNA gene is commonly used as marker.
For fungal communities the internal transcribed spacer (ITS) region is often used and for
other eukaryotes the 18S rRNA is a useful marker gene [14]. The main advantage of the
marker gene approach is that only a single gene is sequenced which allows for in­depth
community profiling with minimal sequencing effort. WGS on the other hand requires a
higher sequencing effort as it aims to capture the entire genetic information within a sam­
ple. With this information, WGS sequencing allows functional analysis of the microbial
community. Furthermore, WGS sequencing allows complete community profiling includ­
ing, archaea, bacteria, eukaryotes, viruses and plasmids [14].
As microbial communities variate significantly in structure and composition depending
on their environment, no single optimal method exists for analysing metagenomic data.
Choosing methods, tools and databases can be a challenge as these have to fit the data
and research questions of the study [14].
2.2.1
Assembly
Current sequencing technologies require fragmented DNA. Reassembling these fragments
is non­trivial in metagenomic samples, as they contain genomes of many different organ­
isms [15]. Metagenomic assembly can be done with the help of reference genomes, this
requires large databases and cannot assemble genomes of novel species. Because of
this, metagenomic assembly is often done de novo [15]. Continuous sequences assem­
bled from multiple reads are called contigs. Scaffolds are not continuous sequences but
two or more contigs known to be close to each other because of paired end information

[15]. Although, other methods exists, the currently most popular assemblers like metaS­
PAdes [16] and MEGAHIT [17] uses de Bruijn graphs for assembly. Reads are split into
k­mers. k­mers are then placed into the de Bruijn graph in a way that each unique k­mer
is only represented once. With this method, assembly is done by solving the optimization
problem of finding the optimal path through the graph. As the optimal k­mer size is depen­
dant of the data, it needs to be found iteratively. Assembly is a memory intensive task,
as complex de Bruijn graphs are stored in memory. Currently MEGAHIT is probably the
most memory efficient assembler [15] and is able to assemble complex metagenomes,
like soil metagenomes, with relatively few resources. This efficiency is due to a unique
data structure, succinct de Bruijn Graphs [18], and the filtering of k­mers only found once
[15].
2.2.2
Binning
Binning is the grouping of contigs or reads into individual genomes [19]. Binning is typi­
cally done with three different approaches. The first approach is taxonomic dependant and
uses alignment to reference genomes, this approach is limited by the reference databases
and cannot be used for binning sequences from unknown species within the sample [19].
The second approach clusters sequences with the assumptions that sequences belonging
to the same organism is similarly abundant within a sample, and species will have sim­
ilar abundance across multiple samples. The last approach is based on compositional
traits like guanine­cytosine (GC) content and tetranucleotide frequencies (TNF), which
has been proven to be distinct among species [20] [21]. A downside of compositional
based binning is that short contigs do not have enough information to be binned accu­
rately and are therefore often not included in the binning [22]. Some metagenomic bin­
ners, like CONCOCT [22], VAMB [23] and MetaBat2 [24] uses a hybrid approach, were
both coabundance and compositional information is utilized.
VAMB
VAMB is among the top performing binning tools [25], it utilizes a combination of TNF and
abundances to cluster DNA sequences into genomes.
VAMB has five overall steps of which four are mandatory and one is optional, these can
be seen in Figure 2.2.
a. FASTA file with DNA sequences is parsed and TNF computed and stored in a N×103
matrix.
b. BAM files of sample specific read mappings to all sequences are parsed and abun­
dances are calculated as reads per kilobase per million mapped reads (RPKM).
These are stored in a N × S matrix where each column represents a sample.
c. The TNF and RPKM matricies are concatenated encoded with a variational autoen­
coder (VAE). This results in a N ×L latent representation (µ), which retains weighted
information about TNF and RPKM, while being smaller.
d. Using an adaptive iterative medoid clustering method, the latent representation is
clustered. Sequences are suggested as medoids, and a histogram is made of cosine
distances from medoid to all other sequences (d1). When distances are separated
in the distribution by a valley, a radius r is set to the valley minimum. Sequences
that lies within r are then clustered (d2). This step is repeated until all sequences
have been clustered.
e. The final optional step is to split clusters up into sample specific bins.
Clustering contigs from all samples together, allows for better coabundance analysis,

increases strain level purity [23].

2.2.3
Dereplication
Dereplication is a step included in many metagenomic pipelines, as a way to handle
highly similar metagenome assembled genomes (MAGs) [26]. Dereplication requires pair­
wise comparison between MAGs, which becomes computationally demanding for large
genome sets [27]. A tool like dRep [27] uses the fast but less accurate MASH algorithm
[28] for initial comparison and clustering of the MAGs. Then the slower but more accu­
rate gANI algorithm [29] is used, for pairwise comparisons within each cluster. Because
MASH performs poorly on incomplete genomes CheckM [30] is used to estimate genome
quality and completeness. MAGs with < 50% completeness are removed prior to genome
comparisons. Genomes of high sequence similarity, default is 99% average nucleotide
identity (ANI), are then dereplicated by retaining only the highest quality genome [27].
As arguments both for and against the inclusion of a dereplication can be made, it really de­
pends on the study design, data and the biological questions investigated. Dereplication
reduces redundancy within the dataset [26], and removes low quality genomes. Mapping
reads to several highly similar MAGs, will result of a random distribution of reads mapped
to these MAGs. One could conclude that these MAGs represent smaller co­occouring
populations, while in reality they may in fact represent one large population.
On the other hand, highly similar MAGs assembled from multiple samples can contain
valuable information that would be lost during dereplication [26].

2.2.4
Community profiling and taxonomic annotation
Read mapping
To get an overall picture of the composition and abundance within a microbial commu­
nity, reads are often mapped to a reference database. An advantage of using reads for
community profiling is that it requires less sequencing depth, compared to profiling based
on MAGs. Furthermore, to get abundance estimates from the MAG based community
profiling read mapping to the MAGs needs to be included.
There are several different tools and databases which can be used for read based com­
munity profiling. Read mapping tools like Kraken2 [31], Kaiju [32], and MetaPhlAn3 [33]
all try to accomplish fast and accurate read mapping but work in different ways. Kraken2
works by associating k­mers, represented as compact hash codes, to a lowest common
ancestor found in a reference database of assembled genomes. By counting how many
k­mers mapped to each taxon, a classification tree is constructed, and the leaf with the
highest scoring root­to­leaf path is then used to classify the input sequence [31]. Kaiju
starts by translating reads into six potential reading frames and then aligns these to a
reference database of annotated proteins. Then reads are clustered into taxonomic units,
which are then classified based on the alignment to the database[32]. MetaPhlAn3 uses
a third approach, where reads are aligned to a database of marker genes [33] instead of
entire genomes.
Taxonomic annotation of bins
A challenge with metagenomic pipelines, that use de novo assembly and then taxonomic
independent binning, is that it is unknown what species each MAG represent. To over­
come this challenge, MAGs are compared to reference genomes for taxonomic annotation.
A tool for annotating MAGs is GTDB­Tk, which uses reference genomes from the NCBI
assembly database [34]. GTDB­Tk works by first predicting genes with Prodigal [35] and
from these identifies a set of marker genes with HMMER [36]. Query genomes are then
assigned to either the Archaea or Bacteria domain, based on the proportion of marker
genes found for each. Marker genes specific for the selected domain, are then put into a
single multiple sequence alignment. Then the query genome is placed into a reference
tree, based on posterior probability and maximum likelihood, with pplacer [37]. Taxonomic
classification is then decided based on location in the reference tree. Where there is am­
biguity in the taxonomic rank assignment, relative evolutionary divergence (RED) is used
to resolve it. This is followed by ANI to assign the query genome to the species of the
closest reference genome. If no reference genomes have >95% ANI the query genome is
classified as a novel species within the genus. RED is a measure of the relative placement
of a node in a tree, between its root and tip [38].
2.3
Pathogens
Pathogenic microorganisms cause disease by invading, colonizing and harming its host
[39]. These microorganisms include bacteria, viruses, fungi, and protozoa. As hosts
and pathogens have co­evolved, pathogens have modified their virulence mechanisms
adapting to the hosts defence [39]. Pathogenicity is the ability of a microorganism to
cause disease and virulence is the degree of pathogenicity. Virulence can be broken into
two overall properties; toxicity and invasiveness. Toxicity is the degree of harm caused to
the host by substances of the pathogen, and invasiveness is the ability of the pathogen
to invade and colonize its host [39].
2.3.1
Virulence factors and antimicrobial resistance
Virulence factors have co­evolved with host defence mechanisms over millions of years.
Due to the use of antibiotics the evolution and spread of antimicrobial resistance (AMR)

has increased rapidly over the last 50 years [39]. Host defence systems and antibiotics,
both represent bottlenecks for survival of the pathogen population. Virulence­ and resis­
tance factors are similar in that they can be transmitted between species through horizon­
tal gene transfer (HGT). Mobile genetic elements (MGE) are probably the main mecha­
nism of spreading virulence factors and AMR between populations [39].
Virulence factor is a broad term that covers many different traits of the pathogen. These
traits include regulators, siderophores, secretion mechanisms and cell surface structures
[40]. An important subgroup of virulence factors are bacterial toxins, which can be fur­
ther divided into two broad categories; exotoxins and endotoxins. Exotoxins are typi­
cally polypeptides and proteins, released to the environment by pathogens, that triggers
a range of host responses through enzymatic modulation or interaction with cell receptors
[40]. Endotoxins are lipopolysaccharides (LPS), a main component of the outer cell mem­
brane in gram­negative bacteria. LPS are released when bacteria multiply or die, and are
a major factor of toxicity in gram­negative bacterial infections [41].
Even though AMR alone is not considered a virulence factor [39], AMR uses similar
kinds of systems as virulence factors. These are systems like, cell wall alterations, efflux
pumps and porins. Because of this, selective pressure of antibiotics make opportunistic
pathogens able to colonize by having or acquiring AMR [40][39].
Although, the evolution and spread of AMR, as well as the rise of multiresistant bacterial
pathogen is largely contributed to the extensive use of antibiotics throughout the last 50
years [39], AMR predates anthropogenic antibiotic use [5]. As a competitive mechanism,
soil bacteria naturally produce antibiotics, as a response to this, a range of AMR mecha­
nisms have co­evolved [5]. Studies in remote areas like Antarctica [5] [4] have shown a
large microbial diversity with several antibiotic resistant bacteria, including Pseudomonas
with resistance to 10 or more compounds [4]. Both studies of Antarctic soil resistomes [5]
[4] have found that while the resistome is diverse, many of the resistance genes are efflux
pumps. The more recent of the two studies, [4] has found parts of the resistome encoded
in predicted MGE allowing HGT of potential novel resistance genes.
2.4
Statistics
Statistical analysis of metagenomic data is often complicated by the amount of data, vari­
ation in sequencing depth, sparsity and read counts not conforming to theoretical distri­
butions [42] [43]. In this section some of the statistical methods used in this project are
presented.
2.4.1
Non­parametric comparison between groups
Wilcoxson rank­sum test
When no parametric model is apparent, testing for differences between two groups is typ­
ically done with Wilcoxon rank­sum test [44]. Wilcoxon rank­sum test is a non­parametric
alternative to a two­sample t­test, and does not assume normal distribution [45]. Despite
of this, Wilcoxon rank­sum test can still be used for normally distributed data, but is less
sensitive to outliers. This is because it is based on the ranks of observations rather than
their values [45].
Distance measures and ordination
Analysing dissimilarities between samples, for example beta diversity of species, can be
done by calculating distances between each sample [46]. Several distance measures
can be used to describe dissimilarity between samples. In general, ecological distance
measures, like Bray­Curtis or Unifrac, works better for ecological data, than euclidean
distances. Bray­Curtis similarity is a distance measure based on species count in each
sample, while Unifrac is a measure of phylogenetic distances between samples. This
8

means that Bray­Curtis does not take evolutionary distance into account, while Unifrac
does not take abundances into account. A compromise between these two could be a
weighted Unifrac distance measure, wherein the phylogenetic distance is weighted by the
abundance of each species[46].
For analysis and visualization of large distance matrices ordination is usually performed.
As the ordination method principal component analysis (PCA) only allows the use of eu­
clidean distances, principal coordinate analysis (PCoA) is used for any other distance
measure [46].
Ordination plots like PCoA can be accompanied with a Permutational Multivariate Analy­
sis of Variance (PERMANOVA), to test for significant differences in cluster centroids[47].
PERMANOVA is non­parametric and determines significance by running several permu­
tations of the data. For each permutation a statistic F is calculated, the p­value is then
calculated based on how many times F ≥than the same statistic from the original data
[47]. The resulting p­value is an indication, that there is a significant difference between
groups, but it does not explain which groups are significantly different from each other. To
test for this PERMANOVA can be run pairwise on all possible group pairs [47].
Adjusting for multiple testing
When using multiple statistical tests to reach a conclusion, the risk of a false discovery
(false positive) increases with each subsequent statistical test [48]. To counteract this, p­
values can be adjusted according to how many tests have been performed. The simplest
way to do this, is to divide the confidence level with the number of tests performed, this
is called the Bonferroni correction. However, adjusting for multiple testing comes with
increased risk of declining a true discovery (false negative). The Bonferroni correction is
quite conservative and increases the risk of false negatives by a lot. The Holm­Bonferroni
method also adjusts for multiple testing but with a lower increase of false negatives, as it
includes information about the distribution of p­values [48] [49].
2.4.2
Differential abundance analysis
A common statistical analysis for read counts in microbiome research is differential abun­
dance analysis (DAA). DAA is often complicated by zero­inflated read count matrices,
overdispersion and highly variable library sizes [43]. There are several different methods
of performing DAA, each method tries to account for the challenges in different ways.
One method used by the tool DESeq2 [50], detects and corrects dispersion estimates
which are too low. This is done by a model pooling information across genes, which
then analysis the dependence of the dispersion on the average expression strength for
all samples. DESeq2 performs well compared to other DAA methods, in regards to false
discovery rate (FDR) and retrieval power [43].

3.3.1
Read based analysis
FastQC [54] was used, with default settings, to evaluate data quality. The output from
FastQC was concatenated into a single report with MultiQC [56]. Sequencing depth of
each sample was estimated using Nonpareil3 [57] in kmer mode with default settings on
forward reads.
Profiling of the microbial community was done using MetaPhlAn3 [33] with the unknown
estimation and add virus options enabled. As MetaPhlAn3 was unable to classify a large
proportion of the reads, the reads were mapped against a human genome with BWA [58]
to check for human DNA of the samples.
3.3.2
Contig based analysis
Assembly of reads into contigs was done with Megahit [17] using the meta­large preset,
designed for complex metagonomic samples. Megahit was picked as the assembly tool
of choice after metaSPAdes [16] ran into memory issues, even when assigned 1500GB
of memory per sample on a High­Performance Computing (HPC) cluster. To evaluate the
assembly, QUAST [59] was used.
Gene prediction was done with Prodigal [35] with procedure (­p) set to meta. The amino
acid sequences of predicted genes were then clustered with MMseqs2 [60] using the
easy­linclust workflow at 95% sequences similarity identity. A representative amino acid
sequence from each gene cluster were then mapped against three different databases to
identify AMR, bacterial toxins and virulence factor genes. The database used to identify
AMR genes was CARD 3.1.4 together with its Resistance Gene Identifier (RGI) software
[61]. DIAMONDS blastp was used with e­value threshold of 1 × 10−5 and set to only
output the best hit when mapping to the Virulence Factor Database (VFDB) [62] protein
sequence core dataset, containing experimentally verified virulence factors only. To iden­
tify bacterial toxin genes, a hidden markov model (HMM) database from the tool PathoFact
[40] was searched, using HMMER 3.2.1 [36] hmmsearch at minimum sequence score of
40.
To estimate gene abundances, preprocessed reads were mapped against hits from the
three different databases using Minimap2 [63] and then counted with Samtools [64]. This
workflow was setup with Snakemake [65]. Differential abundance analysis (DAA) of gene
counts was performed with R [66] in Rstudio [67] using the DESeq2 package [50].
3.3.3
Binning based analysis
Clustering of contigs was done with VAMB, [23] following the recommended workflow
described on their Github [68] with the exception of the first step, where Megahit [17]
was used for assembly instead of metaSPAdes [16]. CheckM [30] was used to check
bin quality i.e. contamination and completeness with the linage workflow. Taxonomic
annotation was assigned to the bins with GTDB­Tk’s [69] classify workflow with default
settings.
Dereplication of highly similar bins was performed with the tool dRep [27] at 99% ANI.
However, it was later decided to retain all bins, as to not dereplicate bins originating from
different samples.
Prodigal [35] was used to identify and translate open reading frames (ORF) of the bins.
Protein sequences were then mapped to the three databases described in section 3.3.2,
to check for virulence, AMR and bacterial toxin genes in each of the bins.

3.4
Statistical analysis and data visualisation with R
Output from different parts of the workflow 3.2 was imported into R [66] for plotting and
statistical analysis, here the following packages were used. When possible, the data was
wrangled into tidy format with the Tidyverse package [70] and visualized with ggplot2 [71].
Plotting of nonpareil sequencing depth estimates was done with the Nonpareil package

multiple color schemes to tree­plots was enabled through the ggnewscale package [78].

5
Discussion
5.1
Data quality and the complexity of midden metagenomes
The data set (Table 3.1) consists of samples taken from middens at three different loca­
tions. Middens in Greenland consists of peat soil, animal bones, tools and other remnants
of human habitation [9] [8]. In areas where permafrost could move into the midden rela­
tively quickly organic material is well preserved, as seen in the Qajaa midden [10].
Soil likely contains some of the most challenging metagenomes to sequence and analyse,
due to the size and diversity of soil microbial communities [79]. It has been estimated that
one gram of soil contains 2000 to 18 000 unique prokaryotic genomes [79]. Furthermore,
DNA extraction from soil samples can be challenging due to the presence of humic acids,
lysis­recalcitrant bacteria, and DNA absorbing soil compounds like clay [80]. Despite
these challenges the data in this project had overall good sequence read quality as evi­
dent by the high phred scores. It has been suggested that even 300Gbp is not enough
data to thoroughly cover the metagenome of a small soil sample [2]. Although, the mean
sequencing effort was 6.75Gbp, the estimated coverage within each sample in this project
was between 50% and 95%. An important distinction is that in this project only trimmed
reads of sufficient quality was included, while the 300Gbp estimate is for unfiltered data.
Another distinction between a given soil sample and a sample from a midden is that mid­
dens also contain remains of large animals. Another study [8] of Greenland middens,
focused on vertebrae and helminths (parasitic worms), has found 42 different vertebrate
taxa. Samples from Inuit settlements, like the one at Qajaa, showed a large proportion of
Canis lupus (Dog/Wolf) DNA together with DNA from seal species like Pagophilus groen­
landicus and Pusa hispida, Monodon monoceros (Narwhale), Rangifer tarandus (Caribou)
and even Balaena mysticetus (Bowhead Whale). Samples from Norse settlements, like
the one at Nuuk, showed primarily vertebrate DNA from domestic animals like Bos (Cow),
Ovis (Sheep) and Capra (Goat). With this it has to be considered that vertebrate DNA
are competing with microbial DNA for sequencing reads. Using the microbe specific tool
MetaPhlAn3 [33] for profiling the DNA composition, resulted in a large proportion of the
reads to be unclassified. To answer which of these unknown reads can be attributed to
vertebrates, plants or microbes not included in MetaPhlAn3, requires additional analysis.
Mapping of reads against a human reference genome showed a considerate amount of
human DNA in the samples. Especially Qajaa samples had a large proportion of human
DNA with some samples containing more than 50%.
A large proportion of DNA from a few vertebrate species will likely increase redundancy
within the data, which is used by Nonpareil to estimate average coverage [72] [57]. Of the
three locations, Qajaa had the highest percentage of duplicate reads with 26.27%. The
average percentage of duplicate reads in the data was 18.25%.

Microbial composition
Aside from unknown and human DNA a variety of microbes was identified. Bacteria and
Virae was found in all samples while Archaea was mainly found in the Qajaa samples.
Most of the viral DNA was classified as belonging to bacteriophages. In many samples
a high relative abundance of Proteobacteria was found. The generally fast growing Pro­
teobaceria [81] prefers nutrient rich soil environments while the generally slower growing
Actinobacetria prefers nutrient limited envirionments. The high ratio of Proteobacteria to
Actinobacteria indicate high nutrient availability within the middens [81]. Included in the

Proteobacteria found in the samples, are Pseudomonadaceae and Enterobacteriaceae.
Pseudomonas, which belongs to Pseudomonadaceae, are abundant in soil and play an
important role in promotion of plant growth and pathogenicity control [82]. Some Pseu­
domonas species like P. aeruginosa are known to cause disease in humans. Furthermore,
they are known to be resistant to a broad spectrum of antibiotics due to antimicrobial ef­
flux systems [83]. Several pathogenic members of the Enterobacteriaceae family have
been found in the samples. These include Raoultella terrigena (R. terrigena) a human
pathogen with high mortality rates [84], Escherichia coli (E. coli), Enterobacter as well as
the plant pathogen Erwinia billingiae (E. billingiae).
In samples where Proteobacteria are not abundant Firmicutes tended to be the domi­
nating phylum. Many Firmicutes are endospore­forming and resistant to extreme condi­
tions and desiccation [85]. Firmicutes can not only be found in soil, together with Bac­

not endospore­forming, were only found in one sample [85]. Another common gut bacte­
ria is Akkermansia mucinophila (A. mucinophila) which was found only in a single Nuuk
sample.
Looking at the Shannon diversity index Nuuk had the lowest diversity while Qajaa and
Sermermiut had a similar median diversity value. These observations were supported by
the p­values, although it cannot be concluded that Nuuk was significantly different than
the other two locations with a confidence level of 95% after adjusting p­value for multiple
testing.
Although Qajaa and Sermermiut had similar alpha diversities they had the largest differ­
ence in composition based on a PCoA analysis of Bray­Curtis distances, while Nuuk and
Qajaa were more similar. From Figure 4.6 it could be assumed that Qajaa had the highest
distances to the other two locations due to the large relative abundance of Archaea and
their absence at the other locations. A reason why this was not the case could be that the
Bray­Curtis distance metric does not include taxonomic distances [46].
5.3
Differential abundance
5.3.1
AMR
Looking at the difference of resistome between the three locations it was shown that Ser­
mermiut had the least in common with the other two locations. A DAA showed that the
nine genes with the most significant difference between the locations were all adeF efflux
pump genes. When looking at other significant AMR genes, these were typically also
related to efflux pumps. All of these were more abundant at Sermermiut compared to the
other locations. In general more resistance to all drug classes was found at Sermermiut.
Resistance towards tetracycline and fluoroquinolone was especially high. Resistance to
these two drug classes can be acquired through efflux pumps [86] [87], like the ones found
in high abundance within the samples.
As described in section 5.2 Proteobacteria were the most relatively abundant phylum in
all Sermermiut samples except one. Among the Proteobacteria several Pseudomonas
species were identified. Pseudomonas have several genes coding for efflux pumps [83]
that cause multidrug antimicrobial resistance.
Thus our results are in line with a re­
cent study investigating microbial composition and resistance genes in soil samples from
Antarctica [4] In this, they also found that many of the resistance genes were efflux pumps.
Furthermore, they identified several multidrug resistant Pseudomonas.

5.3.2
Bacterial toxins
The PCoA of bacterial toxins, showed three fairly distinct clusters, supported by the Ado­
nis tests. The genes with the most significant difference between locations had differ­
ent functions, unlike what was seen for the most significant AMR genes. The bacterial
toxin gene found most significant by the DAA was tlh. This gene is considered a marker
gene for Vibrio parahaemolyticus (V. parahaemolyticus) [88], which is the leading cause
of gastroenteritis in humans all over the world [89]. V. parahaemolyticus is usually found
in marine and coastal environments and typically causes disease through undercooked
seafood [89]. tlh was found at all three locations, but was significantly more abundant at
Sermermiut. Although, tlh is a marker gene for V. parahaemolyticus, this species was not
found in the microbial composition analysis. Due to the broad annotation, several of the
bacterial toxin genes found, might not be related to bacterial toxins at all. Protein tyrosine
phosphatase, as an example, is a super­family of proteins that plays an important role
in biological processes [90]. It assists in maintaining the cellular equilibrium of tyrosine
phosphorylation, which can lead to disease when out of balance [90]. This might be the
reason for its inclusion in the bacterial toxin database, but it is unknown if its presence in
the samples are due to this pathogenic ability or its basic regulatory function.
5.3.3
Virulence factors
The PCoA of virulence factor genes show a similar trend as seen for bacterial toxins.
There are three distinct clusters, although some overlap between Nuuk and Qajaa was
seen. The Adonis test confirmed that the clusters indeed were significantly different.
A DAA showed many virulence factor genes that had a significantly different abundance
between locations. The most significantly different virulence factor gene was Hemagglu­
tinin protease (HAP). HAP was found in Vibrio cholerae (V. cholerae), which has been
responsible for at least seven different pandemics throughout history [91], and causes
approximately 95 000 annual deaths according to the Centers for Disease Control and
Prevention (CDC) [92]. HAP was found at all locations but was most abundant at Sermer­
miut. The same trend was seen with the most abundant bacterial toxin gene tlh (section
5.3.2). Both tlh and HAP are related to diseases caused by species from the Vibrio genus,
but just like no V. parahaemolyticus was found in the microbial composition the same was
true for V. cholerae.
Several of the other significant virulence factor genes, were Type IV pili genes. Type
IV pili are appendages on the cell surface that serves a variety of functions, like motility,
DNA uptake and microcolony formation [93]. Type IV pili are found among several known
pathogens like P. aeruginosa, V. cholerae and Neisseria gonorrhoeae (N. gonorrhoeae).
In these microorganisms the pili increase pathogenisis by assisting with host cell adhesion
[93]. However, in other microorganisms the existence of pili might not have a pathogenic
function. The problem with the database used is that it does not take into consideration
in which organism the gene is expressed in and therefore if it really is a virulence factor.
5.4
Binning and identification of potential pathogens
5.4.1
Bin similarity and quality
Binning of genes resulted in 2150 distinct bins, of these 1292 where annotated with GTDB­
Tk. Comparing this to the read­based composition profiling where 578 different taxa was
found, it would seem that binning is better for taxonomic profiling. However, such a simple
conclusion cannot be drawn. One reason is that a large amount of information is lost
during binning, as discussed in section 2.2.2, current binners including VAMB struggles
to cluster short contigs accurately. Because of this, contigs shorter than 2000bp are not
The microbiome of Greenlandic middens
39
==PAGE:50==
included in the binning. The median N50value was slightly above 1000bp, and only a
single sample had an N50 value of ∼2000. This means that only in the very best case,
half of the assembly is retained when binning. Another reason is that VAMB clusters are
made up of contigs from all samples, these contigs are then split into sample specific bins
[23]. This means, that several bins can arise from each genome cluster, making many
of the bins very similar. Because of this, it can be considered to include a dereplication
step into the workflow. Dereplication of the 2150 resulted i 165 dereplicated genomes,
showing that many of the bins are highly similar. There are arguments both for and against
dereplicating the genomes [26]. However, in this project it was chosen to include all 2150
bins in the downstream analysis.
The quality of bins also have to be taken into consideration. To do this, contamination
and completeness of the bins were estimated with CheckM [30]). Y. Yue et al. [94] has
defined high quality bins as having < 5% contamination and completeness ≥90%. In this
project it was decided to include medium quality bins defined by contamination < 10% and
completeness ≥50%. Bins that failed to meet these quality thresholds were deemed of
low quality and not included in downstream analysis.
While some bins were highly contaminated the majority of rejected bins were rejected
because of low completeness. This can in part be explained by the complexity of the
metagenomes and the loss of information during binning, resulting in many incomplete
bins representing low abundance species in the samples. In total more than 1500 bins
were deemed of low quality.
The 1292 annotated bins can be seen in the two trees (Figure 4.23 & 4.24). There seemed
to be some clustering on the trees based on bin quality, which was especially apparent
among Archaea. This clustering can be explained in two ways. The first is that some
clades were harder to bin than others. The second is that some clades had a low abun­
dance making it difficult to reach high completeness of their genomes.
5.4.2
Identification of potential bacterial pathogens
Many bins contained a large amount of pathogen associated genes.
Like what was
discussed in section 5.3, many genes found within the used databases can have both
pathogenic and non­pathogenic functions. Additionally, bacterial toxins can be consid­
ered a subcategory of virulence factors, and it is not unlikely that some genes are counted
both as virulence factors and bacterial toxins.
From Figure 4.25 it can also be seen that there was phylogenetic clustering of bins with
high abundance of pathogen related genes. The largest cluster of potentially pathogenic
bins were Proteobacteria. Several known pathogens are found within the Proteobacteria
phylum [95]. Proteobacteria was found to be the most abundant phylum in many of the
samples. Addtionally, tlh and HAP, both genes known from pathogenic Proteobacteria
[88] [91], were found at all locations but especially at Sermermiut.
5.4.3
Identification of pathogen related genes in Archaeal bins
Although Archaea have a close association with both plants and animals, no pathogenic
Archaea have yet been identified [96] [97]. Studies have not been able to prove that
Archaea are unable to acquire pathogenic traits [96] [97]. Because of this, it cannot be
concluded that pathogenic Archaea do not exist. In this project it was found that the
Archaeal bins had fewer virulence factor and toxin genes than Bacteria, and no AMR
genes were found. Two bins had especially many toxin and virulence factor genes. These
two bins was annotated as Methanosarcina and Methanosphaerula.

5.5
Potential of the workflow and possible alterations
The workflow presented in this thesis can be split into three different parts. The first part
uses reads and allows for an overall profiling of the microbial community. The second part
which uses contigs translated into protein level information explores the overall genetic
potential for AMR and virulence within the samples. The third part uses MAGs to identify
species with high potential for resistance and virulence.
The initial idea for this thesis was to use a specific tool, PathoFact [40], to predict AMR,
virulence factors and bacterial toxin genes. The PathoFact pipeline uses RGI/CARD [61]
combined with DeepARG [98] to identify AMR genes. This is then combined with a pre­
diction of MGE, using PlasFlow [99] for plasmids and DeepVirFinder [100] and VirSorter
[101] for phages.
For prediction of virulence factors PathoFact uses a random forest classifier together with
a HMM database of potential virulence factor genes. This is coupled with prediction of
signal peptides using SignalP [102] to further group the potential virulence factor based
upon if they are secreted or not.
The prediction of bacterial toxin genes by PathoFact is similar as for virulence factors, but
it relies only on a HMM database. Again predicted bacterial toxins are grouped by the
presence or absence of predicted signal peptides [40].
Although, the idea of using PathoFact as a whole in this project was eventually discarded,
the PathoFact pipeline has served as inspiration for the workflow used in this project. Two
steps from PathoFact, which was not implemented due to the time constraints of a masters
thesis, could be added to the workflow to gain additional information about pathogenicity.
The first of these two steps is the prediction of MGE to combine information about AMR
genes with the potential for HGT. The second of these steps is the prediction of signal
peptides to distinguish between secreted and non­secreted virulence factors and bacterial
toxins.
One reason why PathoFact was eventually not used in this thesis, was the lack of meta­
data for the virulence factor HMM database. When this database was used to search for
potential virulence factor genes within each binned genome, the result was that all 2150
MAGs had a very large number of predicted virulence factor genes, often several thou­
sands. Comparing this result to the fact that one E. coli genome has around 5000 genes
[103], it became apparent that the result probably was full of false positives, and without
any information about the genes included in the database, it was unfeasible to distinguish
these false positives from true discoveries of virulence factor genes.
This highlights one of the limitations of the workflow, which is the reliance on databases.
Instead of the virulence factor database used by PathoFact, the VFDB [62] core database
was used instead. This database only contains genes associated with experimentally veri­
fied virulence factors, and the number of potential virulence factor genes found within each
bins using this database was lower. However, every single bin still contained multiple vir­
ulence factor genes even bins annotated as Archaea species known to not be pathogenic.
Thus the presence of virulence factor genes alone is not enough to determine if a MAG is
pathogenic, instead the amount of virulence factor genes compared between MAGs can
be used to identify which MAGs represent the best candidates of potential pathogens.

6
Conclusion
This thesis presents a workflow to profile the microbial community of midden samples
obtained from Greenland, and to investigate the potential for pathogenicity and AMR of
the species found within these middens.
The input data was of overall good quality and had a decent coverage, of what can be
considered a very complex community. A large proportion of the sequence reads were
not assigned taxonomy during the overall profiling of the microbial community. Some of
the unknown reads were found to be of human origin, especially samples from Qajaa had
large amounts of human DNA. In the samples from Qajaa it was also found that Archaea
made up a large proportion of the microbial community, this was in contrast to the other two
locations were Archaea was almost not detected at all. In general there was shown to be
a significant difference between the microbial composition of the three locations. In many
samples the Proteobacteria phylum was of high relative abundance. This was especially
the case in the Sermermiut samples, in these elevated levels of many genes associated
with AMR and virulence compared to the other two locations were found. Many of the AMR
genes found were related to efflux pumps that often causes resistance to several different
drug classes. Resistance to tetracycline and fluoroquinolone were the most common, but
there was found resistance to multiple other drug classes as well.
A DAA showed significant differences between locations in terms of AMR, virulence fac­
tors and bacterial toxins. The amount of virulence factor related genes that were found,
was highly influenced by the choice of reference database, and the functional annotation
of the genes therein is quite broad. Because of this, pathogenicity cannot be concluded
by the presence of a virulence factor gene, but instead the amount of virulence related
genes could be used to identify potential pathogenic MAGs. MAGs with high amounts of
genes related to pathogenicity were found to often belong to the same clades.
Assembly and binning of soil metagenomics data is hard, and many bins were of low
quality. Despite of this the presented workflow shows promise in regards to identifying
potential pathogens, even in complex microbial communities. It can be further expanded
upon to also include prediction of MGE and signal peptides.
