Abstract
This research investigates the properties of a sampling­based auralization method for re­
constructing binaural signals from room impulse responses. We found that the number of
sampling points, the order of spherical harmonics, and the edge length of sampling sig­
nificantly influence the efficacy of binaural reconstruction. Through comparative analysis
of different room acoustic simulations, we identified strengths and weaknesses of each
simulation and revealed that the auralization error at high frequencies does not equate
to the sum of the theoretical deviations from room impulse response and auralization
method. This study marks a significant advancement in understanding the intricacies of
sampling­based auralization methods and their implications in room acoustic simulations.

1
Introduction
Since the introduction of auralization, it has established itself as an indispensable instru­
ment in the realms of architecture and the automotive industry. It serves as an intuitive
medium for demonstrating the outcomes of acoustic design [1]. Numerous software solu­
tions catering to room acoustics offer auralization results, thus streamlining the acoustic
design process. These software applications employ varied auralization techniques, how­
ever, due to proprietary restrictions, these methods often remain undisclosed. Disparities
between the simulated and actual measured data can arise from these methodological
variations. To address this issue, a uniform approach was implemented, enabling com­
parisons of outputs from various software, thereby reducing their influence and permitting
an evaluation of the efficacy of each auralization method.
Until now, the majority of auralization methods have been derived from the Geometric
Acoustics (GA) approach due to its intuitive and straightforward application [2].
This
method typically involves convolving the signal with the Head­Related Transfer Func­
tion (HRTF) corresponding to the specific angle. This area has seen significant research
exploration. For instance, Lokki et al. investigated the evaluation results of a binau­
ral auralization system, modeled on image sources and edge diffraction. The evaluation
demonstrated that the spatial characteristics of applied auralization closely mirrored those
of real head recordings [3]. Schroder proposed a RAVEN framework for simulation and
auralization centered on geometrical acoustics. This framework simulates acoustic scat­
tering and diffraction, yielding highly accurate auralization results [4]. Taylor et al. put forth
a novel methodology for adjusting geometrical acoustic simulations based on ray tracing.
This method calculates the sound propagation paths from the source to the receiver and
leverages the independence of visibility tests and verification tests to dynamically guide
the simulation towards enhanced accuracy and performance [5].
Nonetheless, methods relying on geometric acoustics can exhibit substantial errors in
the presence of diffraction [6]. Torres et al. ventured into modeling edge diffraction, us­
ing a time­domain model that has undergone rigorous verification to calculate early room
impulse responses inclusive of edge diffraction. This approach made strides towards
achieving more accurate indoor acoustic auralization [7]. To hasten the computation of
auralization, Savioja et al. established a GPU framework, aiming to supersede CPU com­
putation, and significantly amplified the computation speed [8].
In addition to geometric acoustics, several researchers have devoted their efforts to devel­
oping auralization methods grounded in wave­based approaches. Georgiou et al. intro­
duced an auralization technique for a vehicle pass­by, utilizing two­dimensional binaural
impulse responses derived from the Pseudo­Spectral Time­Domain method [9]. Subjec­
tive evaluations indicated that while the differences were nuanced, they indeed existed.
Hargreaves et al. presented an auralization framework based on the Boundary Element
Method (BEM), achieving results that closely mirrored the measured data in certain con­
texts [10]. Southern et al. explored the impact of dispersion errors within the Wave­Based
(WB) method on auralization [11]. In their work, Yoshida et al. proposed a streamlined
binaural auralization technique for indoor acoustics, deploying a large­scale parallel wave­
based indoor acoustics solver rooted in the dispersion­optimized explicit time­domain fi­
nite element method, thereby facilitating swift auralization of rooms [12]. Bleda et al. con­
templated the feasibility of auralization using synthesized impulse responses obtained
from the finite­difference time­domain method within a wave field synthesis reproduction

system [13]. Stofringsdal et al. introduced a technique to convert grid data into planar
wave components, leveraging a circular virtual source array centered at a reference posi­
tion. The resulting plane wave decomposition could be directly processed for auralization

This research implements an auralization approach proposed by Sheaffer et al., grounded
in spherical array signal processing. The technique incorporates local sampling of the

scrutinizes the sources of error. The sixth chapter encapsulates the entire paper.

Theoretical background
2.1
Room acoustics
Room acoustics refers to the study of the behavior of sound in enclosed spaces, such as
rooms or concert halls. It is a branch of acoustics, which is essential in architecture and
audio engineering. The purpose of room acoustics is to create environments that support
the nature and quality of sound required for their intended activities, whether it be speech
clarity in a classroom or pleasing reverberation in a concert hall.
In room acoustics, factors such as room size, shape, materials, and furnishings play sig­
nificant roles in how sound propagates and is perceived within the space. Sound can be
reflected, absorbed, or diffused by different surfaces, leading to a wide variety of acous­
tical characteristics.
For instance, hard and flat surfaces, like concrete or glass, reflect sound, causing echo
and reverb. Conversely, soft and porous materials, like carpets or drapes, absorb sound,
reducing echo and background noise. Therefore, understanding room acoustics allows for
the creation of spaces that enhance the auditory experience, whether in terms of speech
intelligibility, musical quality, or noise control.
There are two main simulation techniques widely employed in room acoustics: geometri­
cal acoustics and wave acoustics.
Geometrical acoustics treats sound as rays and is particularly effective for simulating high­
frequency sound behavior. By simplifying the complex acoustic field into manageable
geometrical models, it enables a fast and straightforward analysis. However, it may not
fully capture phenomena like diffraction and interference, leading to limitations in certain
scenarios.
Contrastingly, wave acoustics adheres to the wave equation and models sound propa­
gation in a more comprehensive manner, including wave effects such as diffraction and
interference. Although more accurate, especially in low­frequency situations or smaller
spaces, the computational load can be significant due to the intricate nature of the method.
In essence, each method has its advantages and trade­offs, and the choice between them
hinges on the specifics of the scenario and the objectives at hand.
2.1.1

Geometrical acoustics is especially effective in situations where the sound frequencies
are high and the dimensions of the objects in the room or the size of the room itself are
large compared to the wavelength of the sound.

Energy­based models or pressure­based models are commonly used in the propagation
of sound. An energy­based model takes into account the energy of each sound wave,
the propagation delay, and the visibility of the contribution observed at the receiver. The
key property of this approach is that it ignores interference effects due to energy summa­
tion, which is useful for determining reverberation time and other energy­based acoustic
parameters.
In the pressure­based model, the phase of the sound pressure is recovered by construct­
ing a complex sound pressure amplitude for each wave number contribution. This method
allows the introduction of interference effects between sound waves, since in this tech­
nique the sound pressure is summed up instead of the energy. Thus this method pre­
serves the information about the phase.
Reflection and absorption of materials
The absorption coefficient and reflection coefficient are critical parameters in room acous­
tics, each signifying different aspects of how sound interacts with surfaces.
The absorption coefficient is a dimensionless quantity ranging between 0 and 1, indicating
the amount of incident sound energy absorbed by a surface material. A coefficient of 1
implies that the surface absorbs all the sound energy incident upon it, essentially acting
as a perfect absorber. Conversely, a coefficient of 0 suggests that no sound energy is
absorbed, and all the incident sound is reflected.
The reflection coefficient, also a dimensionless quantity between 0 and 1, quantifies the
proportion of incident sound energy reflected by a surface. In reality, most materials have
absorption and reflection coefficients that lie somewhere in between, meaning they both
absorb and reflect a portion of incident sound energy.

There are two main approaches to modeling geometric acoustics: the image source
method and the ray tracing method.
Image Source Method (ISM)
Also known as the mirror source method, the ISM is a classic, intuitive technique based
on the principle of sound reflections. This method involves the creation of an ’image’ of
the sound source for each reflecting surface. Essentially, an image source is constructed
behind each surface relative to the original sound source, mirroring its position. The paths
between these image sources and the receiver then correspond to possible direct or re­
flected paths of the sound. Initial calculations start with the direct path from the source to
the receiver, followed by paths reflected once, twice, and so on. This method is particularly
effective for small, enclosed spaces with a limited number of reflections, such as rooms
with simple, box­like shapes. However, as the order of reflections and the complexity of

Ray Tracing Techniques
Ray Tracing is a computational method widely used in both acoustics and computer graph­
ics to simulate wave propagation. In acoustics, this method is often used to calculate room
impulse responses, evaluating the distribution of sound in a space. The basic principle
involves casting a large number of rays from the sound source, and tracing their paths
as they bounce off and are absorbed by surfaces. This ’bouncing’ of rays continues until
a specified order of reflection or an energy threshold is met. The results can provide a
detailed visualization of how sound energy disperses and where it concentrates, which is
particularly useful in acoustically complex spaces. High­frequency sound, where diffrac­
tion effects are negligible, is especially well­suited for ray tracing simulations. However,
due to the high computational demands, ray tracing is often used with other techniques
or simplifying assumptions.
2.1.2
Wave­Based method
Wave­based methods in room acoustics provide a direct and physically accurate solu­
tion to the wave equation, which describes the propagation of sound waves in a medium.
These methods model the way sound waves interact with room surfaces and obstacles,
considering factors such as wave diffraction, interference, and phase shifts. This makes
wave­based methods particularly suitable for scenarios where wave phenomena signif­
icantly affect the sound field, such as in small rooms or at low frequencies where the
wavelength of the sound is comparable to the room dimensions. However, wave­based
methods can be computationally intensive, particularly for larger spaces or higher fre­
quencies. They are often used in concert with geometrical acoustics methods to provide
a complete acoustical simulation of space.
There are several methods for wave­based acoustic simulation as follows.
Finite Difference Time Domain (FDTD) Method
The FDTD method involves discretizing both time and space into finite steps and then
solving the wave equation at each point. By propagating these calculations forward in
small time steps, it is possible to simulate the behavior of the wave field over time. The
FDTD method is capable of handling complex boundaries and materials, but it requires
significant computational resources, especially for large spaces or high frequencies. This
is due to the Courant condition, which states that the time step must be less than or equal
to the spatial step size divided by the speed of sound.

Boundary Element Method (BEM)
The BEM is a method that solves the wave equation by treating the boundaries of a room
as a mesh of discrete elements. By defining the pressure and velocity at each of these
elements, it is possible to calculate the pressure field in the entire room. The BEM is
computationally intensive, but it can provide highly accurate results, especially for low­
frequency sound propagation. It is also well­suited to handling complex room geometries.
Finite Element Method (FEM)
The FEM is similar to the BEM, but instead of only discretizing the boundaries of the
room, it discretizes the entire volume. This allows for greater detail in the modeling of
the sound field, but it is even more computationally intensive than the BEM. The FEM is
particularly well­suited to modeling sound propagation in materials and structures, such
as the vibration of a building due to sound.
2.2
Auralization
Auralization, a concept parallel to visualization, stands at the forefront of advancements in
acoustic sciences. This process, built upon the principles of physics, acoustic engineer­
ing, psychoacoustics, and electroacoustic sound reproduction, endeavors to transform
acoustic effects, primary sound signals, or the mechanisms of sound reinforcement or
sound transmission into an audible manifestation. The auralization process is predicated
upon the generation, processing, and reproduction of signals, ultimately yielding perceiv­
able sound.
Within the domain of acoustic virtual reality, auralization augments traditional acoustic
analysis, synthesis, prediction, and rating tools. Contrary to conventional numerical or
analytical approaches, auralization actively engages the listener, facilitating an enhanced
understanding of acoustic phenomena without the requirement for verbal explanation.
This direct involvement offers an auditory experience that transcends the limitations im­
posed by averaged or approximated numerical data, bringing us nearer to the subjective
auditory experience.
Historically, auralization was first introduced in the context of the acoustic modeling of
sound fields within rooms. The capabilities of auralization have considerably evolved
from the early efforts to process signals from scaled room models to contemporary digital
computation methodologies. Despite existing constraints regarding the precise reproduc­
tion of subjective auditory impressions, the consistent research and development within
this field continue to validate its credibility as a robust design tool.
Emerging technologies such as transaural reproduction, room equalization, and active
noise control, when integrated with auralization, offer promise in expanding the applica­
tion spectrum of this technology beyond the confines of laboratories and basic headphone
reproduction. Prospective areas of interest extend to information dissemination, educa­
tion, and entertainment, offering exciting avenues for future exploration and development.
2.3
Spherical Harmonics
Spherical Harmonics is a set of special functions defined on the surface of a sphere that
forms an orthogonal basis for the space of square­integrable functions. They play a vital
role in many scientific fields.
In acoustics, especially in the context of room acoustics, spherical harmonics are often
used for the analysis and representation of sound fields. They can be viewed as the three­
dimensional counterpart of the Fourier series, providing a spatial frequency decomposition
of a sound field. In essence, this allows for the breakdown of complex sound fields into

2.3.1
Properties of spherical harmonics
Spherical harmonics exhibit several significant characteristics that lend themselves par­
ticularly useful in the realm of acoustics:
Orthogonality
Spherical harmonics of distinct order and degree are orthogonal when integrated over the
sphere. This property implies that any complex sound field can be uniquely decomposed
into a series of spherical harmonics.
Completeness
Spherical harmonics form a complete set of basis functions, meaning any function de­
fined on a sphere can be precisely represented through a linear combination of spherical
harmonics.
Rotational Invariance
Spherical harmonics are rotationally invariant, meaning their shapes remain unchanged
under a rotation of the coordinate system.

Higher order and degree spherical harmonics correspond to higher spatial frequencies,

Analysis of the unified approach to
auralization
The methodology of unified auralization employed within this thesis is extrapolated from
Sheaffer’s paper [15], with the programmatic implementation facilitated by Jens Ahrens.
A succinct depiction of the procedure is provided in Figure 3.1. The process commences
with the sampling of various spatial positions for the purpose of calculating Room Im­
pulse Responses (RIRs). Following this, spherical harmonic decomposition is invoked
in concert with the HRTF corresponding to the associated angle, thereby culminating in
the rendering of the binaural signal. A significant attribute of this technique is its univer­
sal applicability in the realm of auralization, encapsulating both wave­based simulation
methodologies as well as those based on geometric acoustics.
Figure 3.1: Process of auralization of sampled sound pressure fields. The image was
created by Jens Ahrens.
The arrangement of sampling points can be executed via numerous strategies. For the
purposes of this research, a cubic configuration is chosen, illustrated in Figure 3.2. It is
important to recognize that the nature of this arrangement can significantly impact the ac­
curacy of binaural reconstruction. Consequently, an exploration into the effect of several
key parameters on the signal reconstructed is warranted: the quantity of sampling points
(P), the sampling edge length (L) (which concurrently determines the sampling volume
and the distance between the sampling points (dx), and the number of spherical harmonic
orders (O) utilized during the reconstruction process. The quality of the reconstructed sig­
nal can be evaluated from various angles, including time and frequency domain analyses
at specific sampling points, error estimation in the overall reconstructed signal, and as­
sessment of the condition number.

Perceptual evaluation of spherical
harmonic order in binaural reproduction
The spherical harmonics order directly impacts the quality of the reconstructed binaural
audio, prompting us to examine the required SH orders to achieve audibly indistinguish­
able sound with L = 0.14m and P = 1000. The aim was to identify a suitable scenario for
subsequent comparisons in the simulation software. Consequently, a subjective listening
experiment was designed to assist in this determination.
4.1
Experimental design
The experiment consists of two modules, ’Front’ and ’Side’, as these two scenarios rep­
resent the smallest and largest deviation from the ground truth, respectively, thus encap­
sulating all possible angles. The stimuli for the experiment were generated by convolv­
ing the reconstructed BRIR with drum sounds. Drums were selected due to their robust
transients and wide bandwidth, traits that help expose even the slightest discrepancies
between stimuli. All stimuli were normalized to maintain consistent loudness.
The listening test incorporated the ABX and 2­up­1­down methods, both of which are
prevalent in psychoacoustics. In the ABX experiment, participants were asked in each
trial to discern whether stimulus X was identical to either stimulus A or B. The sequence
of stimuli A and B was randomized in every trial. The UI of the experimental program is
shown in Figure 4.1.
Figure 4.1: UI of the hearing application.
The 2­up­1­down method is an adaptive algorithm that automatically modifies the test’s
difficulty based on participants’ responses. As suggested by its name, if a participant
18
Unified auralization of room simulations
==PAGE:27==
answers correctly twice consecutively, the difficulty level for the following question is in­
creased; however, if a participant answers incorrectly, the difficulty level for the subse­
quent question is decreased. A change in the direction of difficulty is termed a ”reversal.”
The test duration can be controlled by setting the number of reversals, with more rever­
sals generally leading to more precise results. However, overly lengthy experiments can
cause participant fatigue, leading to decreased result accuracy; therefore, the number of
reversals should be set according to the specific circumstances. In this experiment, the
number of reversals was fixed at four.
The experiment was structured with eight difficulty levels, ranging from 6th order (easi­
est) to 20th order (hardest). To avoid deadlock due to consistently correct or incorrect
responses, the program was configured to automatically terminate after 25 trials.
This experiment was carried out at Chalmers University of Technology. Ten participants,
comprising six males and four females aged between 23 and 38 with no reported hearing
impairments, partook in the experiment.
4.2
Results and discussion
The outcomes of the experiment are presented in Figure 4.2. To process the data, we
averaged the last two reversals down to a round number, which was used as the partici­
pant’s threshold. Analysis of the results indicated that the thresholds were not influenced
by the age or gender of the participants. Regarding the front direction, the majority of
participants’ thresholds were clustered from the 12th to the 16th orders. In the case of
the side direction, half of the participants were consistently able to distinguish the stimuli
from the ground truth, aligning with the results shown in Figure 3.11

scenario includes several RIRs measured at various locations, and BRIRs measured at

5.4
Result and discussion
Simulations of RIR and BRIR across the three scenarios were conducted utilizing three
distinct software applications. However, the material absorption coefficients supplied by
BRAS could not be directly applied to TREBLE, necessitating their conversion into the
impedance, which inevitably infused errors into the process. Additionally, a manual trun­
cation of RIR and BRIR outcomes was undertaken to eliminate erroneous reflection com­
ponents deriving from the walls.
In the ensuing analysis of results, for the sake of simplicity, the signals will be referred to
solely as BRAS, RAVEN, TREBLE, and ODEON.
5.4.1
RIR
The outcomes derived from the simulation software and BRAS were subject to Fourier
transformation for the extraction of their spectra. Given that each software utilizes a dis­
tinct sound source during the simulation and yields a different ratio of direct to reflected
sound energy, a mere normalization of the signal energy does not adequately calibrate
the amplitude. As a result, a manual alignment of the simulation results with the spectral
amplitude of BRAS was executed through the introduction of a constant gain value, a pro­
cess that leaves the spectral shape of the simulation outcomes unaltered. The primary
function of this gain value is to equalize the peak value of spectral amplitudes between
150­300 Hz, premised on the assumption that all software simulations provide absolutely
accurate amplitude results at low frequencies.
Absorption scene
The absorption scenario results are visualized in Figure 5.5. The spectral differences of
the three software post­gain adjustment and the BRAS spectrum were calculated, with
the outcomes displayed in Figure 5.6.

In an endeavor to demonstrate that the spectral frequency shift in ODEON results from

positive and negative values. During spectral computation, these positive and negative
values counteract each other, with a more substantial negative value at both peaks. Con­
sequently, the amplitudes of the direct sound and the first reflection both result in negative
values after the cancellation, suggesting that they share the same phase. Conversely, the
ODEON results reveal differing signs for the amplitudes of the two components, thereby
indicating a phase discrepancy of 180 degrees.

5.4.4
Summary
In the overall analysis of RIRs, RAVEN demonstrates excellent performance at mid­to­
low frequencies in both the Absorption and Rigid scenes. Although there is a frequency
offset at high frequencies, the overall amplitude aligns closely with that of BRAS. TREBLE
performs admirably below 2 kHz in all three scenarios, especially in the Diffusor scene, the
only one in which it excels, thanks to the utilization of the WB simulation method. ODEON
maintains a consistent amplitude trend with BRAS across all three scenarios, but there is
a frequency offset because it employs an energy­based GA simulation, which results in a
loss of phase information.
Based on all the figures and analysis presented in this chapter, it can be deduced that the

sources of error in room simulation auralization primarily originate from two aspects: the
simulation itself and the auralization method employed.
In both RAVEN and ODEON, the errors of the built­in and unified auralization methods
are relatively small at low and medium frequencies, implying that the RIRs’ errors are the
predominant factor. Beyond 9 kHz, the error of the auralization method starts to become
notable, and the room’s auralization error stems from both the RIR and the auralization
simulation. It is crucial to note that these errors, when manifested in the BRIRs, do not
align perfectly with the theoretical values. Instead, they are accompanied by shifts in
frequency and amplitude, but the underlying trends remain consistent. For TREBLE, the
unified method works well at low frequencies. However, due to the lack of a built­in method
for comparison, further analysis was not pursued.
The deviation between BRIRs and BRAS at higher frequencies from the theoretical pre­
dictions can be attributed to several potential sources. These include errors introduced
during the acquisition of BRAS measurements, the alignment process of RIR and BRIR
curves, wherein low­frequency data was used as a baseline, and the truncation applied
during the processing of RIR and BRIR signals. Each of these factors might contribute to
the introduction of additional discrepancies.
As for the auralization methods, it’s challenging to definitively say which of the two meth­
ods is more efficient when the sound source is directly in front. However, when the sound
source is at a 45­degree angle, the built­in method at the right ear (the ear distant from
the sound source) of both ODEON and RAVEN performs better than the unified method.

6
Conclusion
This investigation explores the properties of a sampling­based auralization method for re­
constructing binaural signals from room responses. The findings demonstrate that the
number of sampling points, the order of spherical harmonics, and the edge length of
sampling significantly influence the efficacy of binaural reconstruction. Specifically, an
increase in the number of sampling points, a higher order of spherical harmonics, and a
decrease in sampling edge length correlate with reduced physical errors in binaural re­
construction. However, objective errors may not fully represent subjective perceptions.
Consequently, subjective auditory experiments were conducted on reconstructed audio
in specific scenarios, establishing the auditory threshold of spherical harmonics for these
conditions.
BRAS was selected as the benchmark for comparing and analyzing the room impulse
responses and binaural room impulse responses generated by RAVEN, TREBLE, and
ODEON. The RIR comparison indicated exceptional accuracy of TREBLE within the fre­
quency domain of wave­based methods, especially in the diffuser scene—an accuracy
unmatched by other simulations utilizing geometric acoustics methods. RAVEN demon­
strated solid performance in absorption and rigid scenarios within mid to low frequencies,
although high­frequency deviations were observed. As ODEON utilizes energy­based
simulation, discrepancies were observed across all frequencies; however, the spectral
amplitude trend aligned with that of BRAS.
Regarding BRIR comparisons, when the sound source was positioned directly ahead,
the inherent auralization methods of RAVEN and ODEON produced results comparable
to the unified method. However, at a 45­degree azimuth of the sound source, the ear
closest to the source yielded comparable results between the two auralization methods for
frequencies below 10kHz. In contrast, the ear furthest from the sound source displayed
superior auralization performance with the software’s inherent method over the unified
method in mid to high­frequency ranges.
The analysis further disclosed that both RAVEN and ODEON delivered commendable
results at low frequencies, irrespective of the auralization method employed. At this junc­
ture, the auralization error primarily originates from the acoustic simulation itself. At high
frequencies, the auralization error of the unified method did not equate to the sum of the
theoretical deviations from RIR and the auralization method. This discrepancy may result
from cumulative errors accrued during measurement or signal processing.
