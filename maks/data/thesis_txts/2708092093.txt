Abstract  
 
 
In this thesis, the theory and methods of structural biology was used to investigate enzymes 
relevant for biofuel and medicinal production.  
 
Biofuel production is currently too costly, so in this project a new enzyme, arabinoate 
dehydrogenase (ADH), was investigated. The enzyme is potentially involved in the conversion 
of lignocellulosic biomass and would thereby increase the yield of biofuel and biomaterial 
production. An AlphaFold 2 model was used to obtain initial structural information, but 
unfortunately it was not possible to obtain crystals of sufficient quality for diffraction 
experiments. However, the AlphaFold model showed that the enzyme can possibly act as a 
glyoxylate reductases since the residues of the active sites are very similar compared to a 
homologous enzyme.  
 
Furthermore, an enzyme potentially involved in more efficient Artemisinin production was 
studied, namely the 9530 enzyme. Artemisinin is one of the most effective anti-malaria drugs, 
but unfortunately it is currently not possible to produce it in an efficient manner. To enable 
utilization of this enzyme, a more thorough understanding of its three-dimensional structure is 
needed. The X-ray crystal structure of the 9530 enzyme from the Artemisia annua L plant is 
reported here with a resolution of 1.51 Å. The space group of the crystal was P 3121 and it 
contained one molecule in the asymmetric unit. In order to solve the crystal structure of 9530, 
the molecular replacement method was used since a template model with 76% amino acid 
sequence identity was present in the Protein Data Bank. The final model of 9530 showed that 
259 of the 262 amino acids could be fitted in the electron density map, as the information of 
three amino acids of the C-terminal was absent. After several refinement rounds, the final Rwork 
was 0.157 and Rfree  0.183. The structure consists of 12 alpha helices without any beta sheets. 
The comparison of the 9530 structure with its homolog, an ascorbate peroxidase, revealed that 
several key changes occurred, both in the morphology and the key residues in the heme and 
ascorbate binding sites. The changes indicate that the 9530 enzyme cannot possess peroxidase 
activity. Despite this, there is still a possibility for redox-regulated activity by Cys126.  
 
Overall, this thesis report presents the investigation of enzymes for use within biofuel and anti-
malarial drug treatment, and it was found that the ADH protein can act as glyoxylate reductases 
and that the 9530 enzyme could be involved in redox-regulation activity.

1. Introduction  
 
1.1 Structural biology 
 
Understanding the structure and function of biological molecules is essential in a variety of 
areas, e.g. for public health, discovery of drug targets1 and also for renewable energy carriers 
such as biofuel. Having a structure of the sub-cellular and molecular objects of biology with a 
high resolution (atomic level) provides vital knowledge of how biological processes occur 
inside macromolecules. Structural biology is the science of determining macromolecule’s 
structure, which helps researchers realize the design of biological molecules and how they 
work1.   
 
In the 1950s, structural biology received special attention2.  In 1953, with X-ray ﬁber 
diffraction, the DNA double helix hypothesis2 was proposed. In 1958, the ﬁrst X-ray 
crystallographic structures of myoglobin were discovered3 and in 1960 the hemoglobin 
structure 4 was understood.   
 
All these revolutionary studies are evidence of how important structure determination is for the 
understanding of biology. By revealing the genetic code and determining the protein structure, 
we learn about nature and how we can use the knowledge in a large number of applications.   
Today, the techniques that researchers apply within structural biology include cryo-electron 
microscopy (cryo-EM), X-ray crystallography, nuclear magnetic resonance (NMR) 
spectroscopy and more recent methods which are based on in silico such as AlphaFold 2 and 
RoseTTAFold. The majority of the proteins deposited at the Protein Data Bank, 85%, are 
obtained by X-ray crystallography5. 
 
Cryo-EM is mostly applied to protein-protein complexes, deoxyribonucleic acid (DNA) 
complexes, and membrane proteins, as obtaining crystals from such proteins is difficult due to 
their flexibility6. Previously, Cryo-EM could only provide images with low to medium 
resolution (3.5-6 Å)7, but recently it has become possible to reach an atomic level resolution 
(1.2 Å)8. NMR spectroscopy is another method which can also help the researcher understand 
the structures, dynamics and functions of macromolecules9. This method can typically be 
applied jointly with other methods, including cryo-EM10 and X-rays crystallography11. In this 
project, the X-ray diffraction technique is applied for determining the protein structure, so this 
technique will be elaborated on in more detail in section 1.2. According to a report from 2017, 
more than 120,000 protein structures from the Protein Data Bank (PDB) have been determined 
using X-ray crystallography12. 
 
The more recent methods, AlphaFold 2 and RoseTTAFold, are based on machine learning 
which can predict a structure model for macromolecules13.  With the AlphaFold 2 method, the 
amino acid sequence of the protein of interest is used as an input to query a protein database, 
such as the PDB, to find similar sequences. Then, using neural network algorithms, the 
information of the similar sequences is extracted and used to develop a new structure model14.

1.2  X-ray crystallography 
 
There is a broad range of applications for X-ray crystallography within genetic engineering, 
drug development, and deep understanding of biological systems, such as enzyme catalysis15. 
The following sections give a general overview of methods for use in crystallography 
experiments (Figure 1). Generally, the crystallization part is one of the most challenging steps, 
as it typically requires various optimization strategies to obtain useful crystals for data 
collection. In the following chapter, the importance of each step is elaborated on.

1.2.1 Purification  
 
The level of purity of a protein has an impact on both the possibility of obtaining crystals as 
well as the quality of the crystal. A crystal is defined as a symmetric arrangement of molecules 
in a three-dimensional lattice16. Thus, if the protein solution is heterogenous (includes both the 
protein and other materials), the undesired material may intrude into the packing of the protein 
molecules and prevent the creation of a well-shaped crystal. Generally, a crystal is better able 
to diffract if the crystal packing is more ordered17. The most common steps of the purification 
process consist of affinity, ion exchange (IEX) and size exclusion chromatography (SEC). For 
these techniques, the protein of interest is extracted from a mixture based on its ability to bind 
the chromatography column, its size and its total charge18. 
 
Affinity chromatography consists of two phases, one mobile phase (e.g. the solution including 
the protein of interest) and one stationary phase19. The stationary phase contains ligands that 
can interact with a specific part of the protein of interest, for instance polyhistidine tag (His-
tag). After loading the mobile phase into the column, the target proteins are bound to the 
stationary phase and the undesired materials pass through the column. The bound proteins are

In IEX, the charged protein residues interact with a matrix in the column which has the opposite

In SEC, the molecules in a solution (the mobile phase) can be separated into their different 
sizes. The column consists of beads (the stationary phase), which have pores with different 
sizes. The molecules with larger size do not pass through the pores, while the smaller molecules 
do. This operation causes the small molecules to pass through the column slower than the 
molecules with larger size25. In this manner, molecules with different sizes are separated.  
 
1.2.2 Crystallization 
 
Crystallization is a method which starts by supersaturating the protein of interest in a solution, 
which then leads to nucleation and lastly crystal growth26. 
 
The protein crystallization phase diagram indicates different phases which are dependent on 
the protein concentration and the precipitate concentration (Figure 2). In an unsaturation phase, 
proteins are completely dissolved in the solvent and no crystals can nucleate. In the 
supersaturation phase, nucleation or crystal growth may take place, but it depends on the extent 
of supersaturation of the protein solution. This phase zone consists of three sub zones: a 
nucleation zone, a metastable zone and a precipitation zone where the protein aggregate27. In 
the nucleation zone the primary process for formation of crystals takes place, which means the 
birth of new crystal nuclei and then growth. The crystal nuclei can also grow in the metastable 
zone, but crystal nuclei cannot form. This zone indicates the suitable level of supersaturation 
that can be used for crystal optimization. In the precipitation zone the proteins aggregate due 
to high supersaturation level. As a result, neither crystal growth nor nucleation occurs in this 
phase zone.

1.2.3 Screening methods  
 
The screening step is about discovering a suitable condition to obtain crystals. For this purpose, 
several conditions can be designed by manipulating the chemical, biochemical and physical 
parameters. Generally, the adjustable parameters are temperature, pH, protein concentration 
and precipitant type (reservoir solution). If the protein is pure, stable, and homogeneous, there 
is a higher likelihood of this protein to form the crystals. 
 
There are two main types of screens: Sparse-matrix screens28 and systematic screens29. A 
sparse matrix screen is based on the conditions that have provided crystals in the past. This 
screening method was introduced in 1991 by Jancarik and Kim28. Nowadays, the screens are 
commercially available through many companies such as Hampton Research, Molecular 
Dimensions and Rigaku.  
 
In systematic screening, typically two of the crystallization parameters are manipulated while 
the other parameters are unchanged. The parameters that may be adjusted are buffer pH, 
precipitant concentration, salt, and protein concentration.

1.2.4 Crystallization methods  
 
The commonly applied crystallization methods include liquid diffusion, dialysis liquid 
diffusion, free interface diffusion (FID), and batch and vapor diffusion. In this project the vapor 
diffusion technique is applied, due to the ease with which the process can be automated.  
 
In vapor diffusion method, many variables can be manipulated to get satisfactory results30. In 
general, three vapor diffusion techniques are usually used: sitting drop, sandwich drop, and 
hanging drop. The sitting drop technique will be described in more detail in the following. 
  
The solution volumes used with this method are typically in the range between nanoliters and 
milliliters. To start the crystallization, a crystallization drop (e.g. protein solution + screen) is 
pipetted into a well and then it is sealed with a reservoir solution next to the protein solution. 
The well must be airtight. The difference in vapor pressure over the reservoir and in the 
crystallization drop drives a force for the equilibration process30. It is important to note that the 
used reservoir solution has a lower vapor pressure of water than the crystallization drop31. The 
existing water in the drop evaporates and then condenses on the surface of the reservoir 
solution. This process will continue until the vapor pressure of water in the drop is the same as 
the vapor pressure of water in the reservoir solution. 
 
There are many kinds of plates and drop-support surfaces that have recently been designed and 
used, such as 24-well, 46-well and 96-well, which are more compact when stored.  
 
In the hanging drop experiment, the protein solution for crystallization is placed on a coverslip. 
To create a strong vacuum inside the crystallization well, a small amount of vacuum grease is 
used on the upper edge of the well.  Then the coverslip, on which the drop was placed on, is 
flipped and the drop is then hanging over the sealed reservoir (Figure 3A). 
 
In the sitting drop experiment, the protein solution for crystallization is placed on a micro 
bridge inside the well (Figure 3B). The micro bridge is surrounded by a reservoir solution. To 
prevent evaporation the well is sealed. The sealing of the well must be done quickly, as the 
protein drop has a small volume and can quickly evaporate. In comparison with the hanging 
drop method, the sitting drop method has some distinct advantages. For instance, it is easier to 
automate as there is less manual manipulation. Additionally, the drop is more stable since it 
does not risk dropping from the micro bridge into the reservoir solution. Lastly, the seeding 
experiments can also be applied if desired.

1.2.5 Crystal optimization by microseeding 
 
Seeding is a process to reproduce and to increase the chance of getting proper crystals. This 
technique allows for growing crystals in the metastable zone, where nucleation cannot occur, 
but crystals will grow from the seeds. Firstly, a seed/solution of seeds must be added to a drop, 
which is saturated so as to reach the metastable zone. Then the seed(s) can grow to larger 
crystals. If we know the number of seeds that are added to the metastable zone, we can in theory 
control the number of crystals grown32. However, in practice this task is difficult, as counting 
the seeds is not possible. To solve this problem, serial dilutions from the seed stock can be 
made. In this project the micro seeding method is used for optimizing the crystal. 
 
The micro seeding technique is based on transferring a small number of microcrystals to a drop 
in the metastable state33. As the conditions for nucleation in microcrystals is optimum, the 
nucleation takes place quickly33. To transfer the microcrystals, either streak seeding or a 
transfer syringe may be used. 
 
In streak seeding, a seed stock, which is made by smashing crystals, is transferred to the new 
drop using a seeding wand. The seeding wand must be soaked in the seeding stock and then 
touch the new drop. It is important that the drop is in the metastable zone to be seeded. The 
metastable zone can be reached by decreasing the concentration of the precipitant or protein, 
or both, in the drop.  
 
1.2.6 Synchrotron beamline 
 
In protein X-rays crystallography, the use of synchrotron radiation is a common technique34.  
Synchrotron beamlines are able to provide tunable wavelengths and a much higher X-ray flux, 
which can provide high resolution images35. Thus, this technology has received considerable 
attention from many crystallographers34 and after its invention it quickly became an important 
technique in structural biology.  
 
The first synchrotron radiation was observed at the General Electric particle accelerator in 1947 
in USA36. A synchrotron is a cyclotron where the charged particles are accelerated in bent 
trajectories by applying magnetic fields (Figure 4)37. By generating the curved motion for the 
charged particles, they release electromagnetic radiations that is called synchrotron radiation37. 
Nowadays, synchrotron radiation has a broad spectrum of applications such as in medical and

The charge-coupled detectors (CCDs)42 are the most frequently used detectors for synchrotron

reducing radiation damage by flash freezing crystals at -173 ℃.  As a result, the data collection 
may be conducted from a single crystal, while earlier, the data had to be collected from multiple 
crystals.  
 
1.2.7 Data collection  
 
The instruments for collecting X-ray diffraction data and the software for processing the data 
have been upgraded continuously since the beginning of structure determination of 
macromolecules43. Today, due to many improvements within the technology, both the data 
collection and data processing can be carried out automatically and remotely. However, there 
are a number of strategies that one needs to take into account for setting up the data collection.  
As protein crystals have weak diffraction, radiation with a high intensity is required to collect 
usable data44. Additionally, the primary beam should have low divergence to avoid 
overlapping of the diffraction spots. To obtain a high-resolution dataset, the shorter

wavelength (high energy) can be helpful, but the total exposure time must be minimized in 
order to reduce the effects of radiation damage44. As mentioned earlier in section (1.2.6), 
cryogenic freezing of crystals can also prevent the crystals from being damaged by the 
radiation, but it is important to soak the crystal in a cryo-protectant solution before freezing 
it. This solution acts to prevent ice formation both in the solvent inside the crystal and on the 
surface of the sample45.   
 
Crystallographic symmetry also has an impact on the data collection. For instance, if the crystal 
system is hexagonal, which is a high symmetry system, it is only required to collect diffraction 
data within 35°, because the crystal has a 6-fold axis. On the other hand, if the crystal system 
has a lower symmetry, such as a monoclinic symmetry system, data collection for a full 180° 
is needed43. As a monoclinic crystal has two fold axis, rotating the crystal by 180° can provide 
a full dataset 46. Today, as X-ray data collection is relatively quick, most datasets include data 
from 180° of the crystal rotation. 
 
The most frequently used structure determination packages are Collaborative Computational 
Projects 4 (CCP4) and PHENIX47. PHENIX was developed by one of the supervisors of this 
project, Prof. Adams, and is the software of choice for this project.  
 
1.2.8 Data processing  
 
After the collection of X-ray data it is time to process it. The processing of the diffraction data 
is very complex, but there are many software packages and programs that are able to do so. 
The most commonly used software packages are XDS48, BEST49, HKL-2000 and xia250.  
The main step in processing the data is understanding and defining the crystal system and the 
unit cell dimensions of the crystal. Additionally, the orientation of the crystal in the beam must 
simultaneously be determined51. The first diffraction image is often the best resource for 
determining the orientation as the image has the highest quality.  
The next stage is to index the diffraction pattern. Every single spot on the image (Figure 5) is 
given an index, cited as three integers: h, k, and l (Miller indices). The autoindexing by the 
software programs can be done by predicting (using mathematical calculations) the diffraction 
image, as the cell dimensions and orientation are known from the former stage. Subsequently, 
the program matches the real image with the predicted image. However, to know that the real 
and predicted images are fitted properly, a check-up is required to prevent any errors43.

After autoindexing, the intensity of each diffraction spot is measured. Protein crystals contain 
small molecules that diffract weakly in a large unit cell. In other words, if the volume of a 
protein crystal is larger, it diffracts strongly. The intensities of each diffracted spot depends on 
the size of the amplitude and the phase information of the diffracted waves52. The intensities 
of all the images in the dataset can be correlated and a scale factor with the highest value of 1 
must be given. The scale factor for the first image is set to 1 as it has the highest quality. All 
other images in the dataset are scaled to the first image52. Therefore, collecting a complete 
dataset from one single crystal is beneficial as one reference image is essential for the scale 
factor distribution. However, it must also be considered that sometimes, due to vulnerability of 
the crystal to radiation damage, it is required to collect data from more than one crystal.

increases by one.  The intensities are defined as a function of the amplitude of the diffracted 
waves and the phase angle between each wave. If there is a constructive interference, the phase 
difference is zero, and if there is a destructive interference, there is a phase difference of 180°52. 
The amplitudes can be calculated by a computer program such as the CCP4 suite55, while the 
phase information cannot be determined from the data collection. This concept is called the 
phasing problem and the “problem” can be solved in an indirect way using certain techniques 
such as isomorphous replacement and molecular replacement. 
 
The isomorphous replacement method56 is useful in the case where there is no availability of a 
homologous model for the protein of interest. This method needs at least two data sets57. The 
data set that excludes heavy atoms from the protein crystal is called the negative data set and 
the dataset that includes heavy atoms is called the positive data set. When heavy atoms such as 
mercury, silver, platinum or gold are present in a crystal, the reflections are more intense than 
the reflections of a protein crystal which contains only lighter (e.g. carbon, nitrogen and 
oxygen). As heavy atoms have more electrons and their electrons are bound tightly to their 
nuclei, proteins with attached heavy atoms have reflections with higher intensity. The 
difference between the intensities of reflections of the two datasets gives information about the 
positions of the heavy atom in the protein molecules. Subsequently the position information 
can be used to calculate the phase angle information of the native crystal and hence its electron 
density map.  
==PAGE:18==
 
18
 
The molecular replacement (MR) method can be used when a suitable related protein structure 
is already determined and published in PDB. For instance, two closely related viruses or an 
enzyme originating from two different sources58. Generally, at least a sequence identity match 
of 25% is needed between the protein of interest and its homolog for the MR model59. This 
method uses the phase information of a known protein structure to estimate the phase 
information of the unknown protein structure60. In the case where a homolog model is not 
available, the predicted model from AlphaFold can be used for MR61.    
 
MR checks all the possible positions and orientations of the homologous model in the crystal 
unit cell of the unknown protein. The homologues model can be translated and rotated  in the 
unit cell of  the unknown protein by a translation function62 and a rotation function63. After the 
correct position of the model in the unit cell of the unknown protein is found, the phase of the 
model is borrowed to calculate the phase for the unknown protein. There are several computer 
programs available for searching for MR solutions such as AMoRe64,  SOMoRe65, Phaser66, 
MOLREP67 and PHENIX47. 
 
1.2.10 Refinement and model building 
 
During the refinement phase the calculated electron density map can be improved68.   In the 
model building stage, the amino acid sequence of the unknown protein is matched into the 
calculated electron density maps. The matching depends on whether the phase problem is 
solved. If the MR method is used, some fragments of the protein backbone is already in the 
density map, but it depends on the completeness of the homologous model. Thus, it is better to 
do refinement before matching the rest of the unknown protein in the map. In the case the 
electron density map is computed with the use of the isomorphous replacement, the protein 
backbone matching must be done from the start. 
The purpose of refinement is to determine the smallest difference between the calculated and 
the observed structure factors69 and it is possible by adjusting the model parameters. 
R-factors (equation 1.3) is key for evaluation of the progress and the quality of the refinement 
approach70.  If the refinement strategy is chosen properly, the R factor value decrease as the 
result of further refinement70.

The R-factor calculates the difference between the amplitude of the calculated structure factor 
(𝐹𝑐𝑎𝑙) and of the experimental (observed) dataset (𝐹𝑜𝑏𝑠). There are also two other factors which 
are used by crystallographers; Rwork and Rfree. The experimental dataset (reflections) is divided 
into two sets: a work set and a test set. 
 
The Rwork, which is based on the work dataset, is used for calculation of the density map, 
refinement and model building etc. The Test set contains some random data of reflections that 
are never used for model optimization. Those random reflections are used to calculate Rfree. 
Rfree provides a cross validation tool which can be used to confirm that the diffraction data is 
not overfitted71. It is important to note that both Rwork and Rfree factors must drop in parallel as 
a consequence of refinement to avoid overfitting68.

quality of the refined model. These two parameters can be calculated through certain software 
programs such as Molpropity72.

2. The arabinoate dehydrogenases enzyme 
 
2.1  The aim of the study  
  
The future large-scale production of biofuels and biomaterials will very likely be based on 
lignocellulosic material. This will reduce both fossil fuel dependence and greenhouse gas 
emissions, as the alternative is sugar and grain, which could instead be used as food for humans 
and animals73.  
 
For producing cost-effective biofuel and biomaterials, it is essential to create techniques with 
the capacity to convert all the sugars in the lignocellulosic material. Lignocellulosic biomass 
consists of around 40-50% cellulose, 25-30% hemicellulose and 15-20% lignin74.  
 
With a pretreatment process, the hemicellulose in lignocellulosic materials can be hydrolyzed 
into a mixture of glucose, xylose, and other sugars such as arabinose, which is a pentose 
sugar75. Bioconversion of these sugars leads to a range of bioproducts, such as platform 
chemicals, polymer precursors and biofuels76. Therefore, there is growing interest in 
optimizing the conversion of such sugars.  
 
Arabinose can be found in nature in an L-configuration and only 3–15% L-arabinose from the 
existing lignocellulosic materials can be extracted77 . Therefore, there is a need for discovering 
new techniques and enzymes to increase the extraction of this sugar. The first protein of interest 
in this project belongs to the dehydrogenase family. Dehydrogenase enzymes are able to 
catalyze oxidation and reduction reactions depending on coenzymes. The main coenzymes of 
this enzyme family are nicotinamide adenine dinucletide, (NAD+), nicotinamide adenine 
dinucletide phosphate (NADP) and flavin78. The reaction mechanism of dehydrogenases is 
based on  transferring hydrogen atoms from an organic compound to the electron acceptors 
(the cofactors)78. Then, the enzyme oxidizes the organic compounds and energy can be 
released. Generally, dehydrogenase enzymes can be found in all kinds of microorganisms in 
the soil and they have an essential role in biology79.  
 
Pentose sugars are metabolized by microbes through different routes80. Usually, metabolism in 
microbes happens through glycolysis and the pentose phosphate pathways81-82. However, these 
pathways include more than ten steps to reach tricarboxylic acid (TCA), which explains the 
complex and relatively slow production rate83. Fortunately, there is an alternative to these 
pathways, i.e. the nonphosphorylative pathway. This pathway ensures high maximum 
theoretical yields, because xylose or arabinose flows to the C5 compounds without excretion 
of CO284 (Figure 6). Lately, several studies have been carried out with a focus on the 
nonphosphorylative pathway from D-xylose83, but there is still a need for more investigation 
of L-arabinose for chemical synthesis via the same pathway.

2.2 Materials and methods  
 
In the following the materials and methods employed in the project regarding the ADH 
protein are described. 
 
2.2.1 Protein purification (affinity chromatography) 
 
ADH gene-containing Escherichia coli BL21DE3 on plasmid was cultured in a 2 x 1 L Terrific 
broth (pH 7.2) at 18 °C. The dilution ratio was about 1:100 dilution. When an optical density 
of 600 nm was obtained, the protein expression was induced with the addition of 0.5 mM 
isopropyl β-D-1-thiogalactopyranoside (IPTG). The protein expression was continued for 16-
18 hours at 18 °C. Then the biomass was recovered by centrifugation (Avanti J-10 XP; 
Beckman culture, USA, Rotor ID: JLA 9.1000, 5000 RCF, 20 minutes, 4 °C). The yield of 2 L 
culture was 16.1 g. The cell pellets are kept at -80 °C until the purification.  
 
The 16.1 g harvested cells were thawed. To re-suspend and lyse the cells, 130 mL of buffer 
was added to the 16.1 g harvested cells. The buffer consisted of 4-(2-hydroxyethyl)-1-
piperazineethanesulfonic acid (HEPES) (conc. 25mM), pH 7.4, sodium chloride NaCl (conc. 
250mM), Dithiothreitol (DTT) (conc. 1mM)), magnesium chloride MgCl2 (conc. 2mM), 
lysozyme (conc. 0.1 mM), deoxyribonuclease I and 1x ethylenediaminetetraacetic acid free 
protease inhibitor cocktail (conc. 0.3 𝜇g/mL) from CalBiochem. Then the cells were disrupted 
with an Emulsiflex C3 at 18 kpsi. The lysates were centrifuged (Avanti J-10 XP; Beckman 
culture, Rotor ID: JLA 25.50, 40000 RCF, 40 minutes, 6 °C) to yield a crude extraction of 
soluble proteins. The soluble protein volume was 140 mL and then 0.56 mL of imidazole (conc. 
5 M) was added to the protein (conc. 20 mM). 
 
For purifying the protein an ÄKTA pure chromatography system was used. Two buffers were 
prepared. One binding buffer: HEPES (conc. 25mM), pH 7.4, NaCl (conc. 250mM), DTT 
(conc. 1mM). And one elution buffer: HEPES (conc. 25mM), pH 7.4, NaCl (conc. 250mM), 
DTT (conc. 1mM) and imidazole (conc. 500 mM). The soluble protein was then loaded onto 
two freshly charged 5 mL HisTrap High Performance (Cytiva) with highly cross-linked 6% 
agarose (ligand: Ni2+). The column had been pre-equilibrated with the binding buffer. The 
ADH protein was eluted with the elution buffer from 4% to 65% with a linear gradient. 
Subsequently the fractions were analyzed with sodium dodecyl sulfate-polyacrylamide gel 
electrophoresis (SDS-PAGE).  The used SDS-PAGE gel was Mini-PROTEAN TGX (Tris-
Glycine extended) gels (Bio-Rad). The gels have resolving gel from 8-16% and they are 
designed for the Mini-PROTEAN family of vertical electrophoresis cells.

2.2.7 Data collection of the X-ray diffraction 
  
To see to how the crystal from the previous step (section 2.2.7) can diffract, X-ray diffraction 
data collection was conducted on beamline 8.2.2 of Berkeley Center for Structural Biology 
(BCSB) at Lawrence Berkeley National Laboratory (LBNL). BCSB has five macromolecular 
crystallography beamlines in total at the Advanced Light Source (ALS). Beamline 8.2.2 is 
equipped with a superconducting bend magnet source87.  The sample mounting for the 8.2.2 
beamline is carried out using Rigaku ACTOR sample mounting robots87. 
 
Before collecting the X-ray diffraction data, some well-shaped crystals were fished and frozen. 
To fish the crystal, a Mounted CryoLoop with 20-micron diameter nylon loop (Hampton 
Research) was used. The nylon loop was sticked to hollow, stainless steel, MicroTubes. The 
length of the MicroTube is originally 24 mm (can be notched to a desired length) but in this 
experiment its length was chosen to 18 mm as it fits beamline 8.2.2. After the CryoLoop was 
ready a crystal with a reasonable size and shape was chosen to be fished. The fished crystal 
was soaked in cryoprotectant which consisted of 80% reservoir solution and 20% glycerol. 
Cryoprotectant keeps the crystals away from damaging and ice formation, as data collection 
takes place under cryogenic temperatures. The ice formation can occur due to PEG in the 
crystallization solution. Then the crystal was placed in a puck which was cooled down in liquid 
nitrogen (Error! Reference source not found.).  
 
 
 
Figure 13) A picture of a liquid nitrogen container used to freeze the fished crystals before the beamline. 
At beamline 8.2.2, the crystals were transferred from the liquid nitrogen to a goniometer 
automatically. During collection of data, the crystal was cooled using a stream of gaseous 
nitrogen. The phi angle was set from 0 to 180º to collect data from the entire crystal.

2.4 Discussion 
 
As the main focus of the project has been to solve the structure of the protein using X-ray 
crystallography, firstly some further optimization strategies for obtaining high quality crystals 
are discussed. Lastly, a computational model of the ADH protein using AlphaFold 2 as a way 
to obtain structural information of ADH is discussed too.   
 
To optimize the ADH protein crystal, a number of chemical and biochemical factors were 
varied in this project. Changing the chemical factors, such as the concentration of the protein 
and the different screens, were the first steps to take to know whether the protein would 
crystallize. The biochemical factors, such as removing His-tag, was also done. Additionally, 
microseeding has been applied to induce the nucleation.  
 
Besides the modifications that were done for the ADH protein in the project, there are many 
other strategies to pursue as future work. In this project work, many crystals have been 
obtained, but none of them have been diffraction quality crystals. 
 
To approach an enhancement of the crystallization it is a good idea to start with as many 
different screens as possible. If that approach does not succeed, employing additive screens, 
changing the protein buffer (different pH and salt concentration), changing the temperature and 
using other crystallization techniques, not only sitting drop vapor diffusion, could also be 
beneficial.   
 
Due to time limitation of the project, only 6 different kinds of screens have been used:  Shotgun 
(CSIRO), PEG ion, PEG Rx, INDEX (Hampton Research), Berkeley (Rigaku) and 
MCSG1(Anatrace), so in total 576 different conditions. There are more than 22,000 
crystallization conditions available commercially90.  
 
Additionally, applying additive screens could also help to improve the crystals. Additives are 
small molecules that may be bound by the protein molecule and make some favorable changes 
in the physical or chemical properties or conformation of the protein17. These changes may 
lead to interactions within the crystal and then the formation or optimization of the lattice91.  
 
The applied protein buffer for the ADH protein was HEPES (conc. 25mM, pH 7.4), NaCl 
(conc. 150mM). A new buffer that contained Tris-HCl (conc. 25 mM. pH 8) and NaCl (50 
mM) was prepared in order to change the pH and salt concentration of the protein buffer. This 
new buffer replaced the buffer used so far, and the initial crystallization was repeated. This 
specific modification did not lead to any crystals, but it is still worth it to try other protein 
buffers with different pH and salt concentrations.   
 
The other parameter that has an impact on the crystallization is temperature.  The crystallization 
experiment for the ADH protein were performed at room temperature (23-24 ℃). Manipulating 
temperature is a common strategy in crystallographic experiments, as this factor affects protein 
solubility, protein nucleation and supersaturation92. The enhancement of the crystallization can 
occur at both lower and higher temperatures than room temperature92.  
 
The crystallization method used in this project is the sitting drop vapor diffusion, while one of 
the ADH protein homologs (43% sequence identity),  glyoxylate/hydroxypyruvate reductases 
(GRHPR) (PDB ID: 5aov) was crystallized with hanging drop vapor diffusion93. So, another 
==PAGE:43==
 
43
alternative to approach a promotion in the ADH crystal formation may be to try the hanging 
drop vapor diffusion technique.  
 
Genetic modification in the protein sequence, such as surface entropy reduction, can also be an 
alternative path to optimize the quality of the crystals. This strategy is based on replacing small 
clusters with two to three surface residues of Aln, Tyr and Thr94 . The replaced residues must 
be characterized by a high conformational entropy such as Lys (positive charged), Gln(polar) 
and Glu (negative)95. If theses hydrophilic residues are localized on the surface of the protein, 
the entropy of the surface will increase due to the random motions of the residues96.  The higher 
entropy on the surface decreases the formation of lattice contact points, and as a result, this can 
prevent one from obtaining diffraction quality crystals94-96.  
 
For this purpose, the SER prediction (SERp) webserver was used. The SERp software indicated 
3 cluster candidates that comprise residues which could be suitable for a mutation designed 
with the aim of improving the crystallization (Figure 25) ( 
 
Table 11). Normally, only one cluster is preferred for each construct, as many mutations on a 
protein surface can impact its interaction with other proteins96. According to the SERp server, 
an ideal cluster candidate for mutation typically has a SERp score of 3.0 or higher. Therefore, 
cluster 1 could be a good option for mutation in future work with the ADH protein.

3.1 The aim of the study  
 
Malaria as a global epidemic is an enormous challenge which takes the lives of nearly half a 
million people yearly98, primarily in Africa and Asia99. In the 1970s, an anti-malarial drug, 
artemisinin, was discovered by Tu Youyou’s team and this has been one of the most effective 
antimalaria treatments ever since100. Artemisinin can be extracted from Artemisia annua L (A. 
annua), which is a sweet worm-wood plant101. The A. annua plant has a low content of 
artemisinin, so there is an unmet demand of the drug to decrease the mortality rate of malaria. 
Artemisinin can also be synthesized by organic chemistry, but the current synthetic processes 
for the production of artemisinin are costly and thus the access to the drug is a challenge for 
poor countries99. Therefore, it is urgent to find a low-cost, sustainable and scalable production 
platform for artemisinin-based therapies.  
 
The chemical formula of artemisinin is C15H22O5 and it contains a peroxide linkage 
(endoperoxide bridge) which is essential for its antimalaria activities99. As artemisinin has four 
main derivatives (Figure 30), the whole drug family is called artemisinin-based combination 
therapies102.  
 
 
     
  
 
 
 
Figure 30) An overview of the chemical structures of artemisinin and its main derivatives. The endoperoxide 
bridges are highlighted by red color. The illustration of the chemical structures is reused from Jing Yang et 
al.98. 
Here follows a brief description of how malaria infection occurs. When a person is infected by 
the malaria parasite, the parasite enters the liver via the blood stream. Afterwards, when the 
parasites are matured (merozolite) in the liver, they infect the red blood cells (erythrocytes)103. 
In the blood cells, the parasite breaks down immense amounts of  hemoglobin from the blood 
to get nutrients104. The consumption of hemoglobin by the parasite leads to the release of a 
large amount of heme.  If the infected person is treated with artemisinin, it too enters the red 
blood cells, and the heme cleaves the endoperoxide bridge of artemisinin105. By breaking the 
endoperoxide bridge, an electron rearrangement occurs and a reactive carbon-centered radical 
is made106. The reactive carbon (C-radical) alkylates the membrane protein of the parasite, 
damage its mitochondria and produces reactive oxygen species (ROS)104-107.  The produced 
ROS, such as hydrogen peroxide H2O2, hydroxyl radicals OH∙ and superoxide anions O2
∙−, cause 
DNA damage and this results in the death of the parasite 108.

The first step in the structure determination of the 9530 enzyme was testing the quality of the

of the dataset by finding abnormality in a dataset113. The input file in the Xtriage program is in 
MTZ format which contains the experimental diffractions data. Xtriage has many outputs 
where the most important of them for this project are explained in the following:

number of identical molecules in each asymmetric unit. When the percentage of the

how many copies of the molecule must be selected for molecular replacement.

which are absent systematically. The tool tells you which space groups are excluded 
and it helps you find the most likely space groups.   
• “Data strength and completeness” provides the data completeness in different 
resolution ranges. The outputs are two graphs of the signal-to-noise ratio.  One of the 
graphs is the signal-to-noise and the resolution range, and the other one links the signal-
to-noise and the completeness.

Wilson plot presents the decrease in intensity as a function of resolution. The plot can 
be used for calculation of the overall B-factor. Generally, if the variation of a unit cell 
to another unit cell is higher in one case than another, then the overall B-factor is larger 
and the Wilson plot falls off faster.  
• “Diagnostic tests for twinning and pseudosymmetry” include an analysis of 
intensity distribution statistics to identify any unusual data. In most cases the unusual 
data is a sign of twinning. The twinning commonly happens if the two identical crystals

Phenix.phaser is the main program used in phenix to perform MR47. To run the program the 
experimental diffraction data of the 9530 enzymes (in mtz file format), the atomic model from 
the PDB with the highest homology model (in the pdb file format), the sequence of the 9530 
enzyme (.SEQ) and an estimate of the number of molecules in the crystal from the output of 
phenix.xtriage were used. The program applies maximum likelihood algorithms to determine 
the rotation of the model with respect to the unit cell (rotation function), and then the translation 
of the rotated model within the unit cell (translation function)115. The “simple one-component 
interface” from the MR menu in Phenix was chosen. The result of the process was a file in mtz 
format and a file in pdb format. The mtz file contains coefficients for the electron density map 
using the homolog model of the 9530 enzyme and the experimental amplitudes. The output 
pdb file is the initial model.  
The AutoBuild function in Phenix did sets of density modification, model building and 
refinement to yield a new model and improve the map for the 9530 enzyme.  The AutoBuild 
program contains a model completion algorithm, which can build structure loops of a protein, 
the  edges between the chains of a structure and lastly it is also able  to optimize the chain side 
of the structure116. The inserted files were the sequence of the 9530 enzyme (in seq file format), 
the experimental diffraction data (in mtz file format), the initial map, which was the output of 
the previous step (phenix. phaser) (in mtz file format), and the starting model which was also 
the output of phenix. phaser (in the pdb file format). After the AutoBuild was done, the built 
model was refined using phenix.refine117. The phenix. refine can be run as many times as 
needed and the refinement strategy can be changed according to the need. In this project the 
most common refinement strategy was designed as follows.   
• XYZ coordinates is geometrical refinement of the model that tries to find an agreement   
to the experimental data diffraction.  
• Real-space is the refinement of the position of each residue which must be fitted to the 
density map. Real-space refinement can be run both locally and globally. Locally means 
each residue can be refined individually to the map density after the global refinement 
is done.  
• Individual B-factors refines individual atomic B-factors. B factor or temperature 
factor is connected to the atomic movement causes by static disorder in a crystal118. 
• Occupancies is a parameter which could be helpful if the X-ray data has a high 
resolution. This refines the alternative conformation of the residues. As the X-ray data 
of the 9530 protein has a high resolution, some residues have two conformations. 
•  Automatically add hydrogens to model can help to get more precise model geometry 
runs.

3.4 Discussion  
 
The coordinates of 9530 were compared to TL29 using the PDBeFold server. The results 
indicate that the 9530 and TL29 are 86% identical in terms of the secondary structure and 75% 
identical with ascorbate peroxidase-ascorbate complex (APX-ascorbate complex) (PDB 
ID:6XV4). Since the 9530 enzyme is predicted to be a peroxidase (section Error! Reference 
source not found.), the analysis is started by looking at the areas with the possible peroxidase 
activity site for all three enzymes. Firstly, the 9530 enzyme is compared with the APX-
ascorbate complex enzymes and then with TL29. The coordinates of the TL29 and APX-
ascorbate complex enzymes are downloaded from the PDB website. Note that the PDB file of 
TL29 from the PDB website includes two copies of the molecule in the asymmetric unit. 
Therefore, the downloaded PDB file has been modified to one molecule of TL29. Secondly, 
the oligomeric state and the possible functionality of the enzyme are discussed.  
   
3.4.1 Structural comparison of 9530 with APX-ascorbate complex 
 
The APX-ascorbate complex is a heme and ascorbate dependent peroxidase124. The function 
of the enzyme can be defined briefly as catalyzing the cleavage of H2O2 using an ascorbate as 
an electron donor 122. As a result, monodehydroascorbate and H2O are produced124.  
The structures of the two enzymes are superposed in the following Figure 41. Non-superposed 
areas of both the 9530 and APX-ascorbate complex enzymes, where the significant differences 
are marked by different colors, can be seen in Appendix B. Compared to the APX-ascorbate 
complex, the N-terminal of 9530 is shorter, but its C-terminal is larger than the APX-ascorbate 
complex. The C alpha helix and the loop from C to D alpha helices are longer in the 9530. The 
D alpha helices in both enzymes start from the same position, but the D-helix of the 9530 
enzyme is longer and it is followed by two extra helices, D2 and turning alpha helix D2’, shown 
in dashed purple in Figure 41.   
The D alpha helix in the APX-ascorbate complex is followed by a short loop, which links it to 
the D2 helix. The short loop has a cysteine amino acid which is located on the outer side of the 
enzyme and is exposed to solvent. The cysteine may play an important role for enzyme activity 
and it is discussed in more detail later in this section. On the other hand, the APX-ascorbate 
complex has some regions which are missing in the 9530 enzyme, such as an H alpha helix, 
two small beta strands and loops which embrace the potassium ion. Both the two beta strands 
and the loops interacting with the metal ion are positioned between the F and G alpha helixes, 
shown with dashed green color (Figure 41). The longer D-helix of the 9530 enzyme has 
significant impact on its feature. This is because the alpha helix inhibits binding of heme to the 
heme binding site due to strict hindrance. Additionally, the loop which connects the D2’ helix 
to the E helix in the 9530 passes through the middle of the heme binding site (Figure 42). This

Again, due to the high similarity, both in terms of sequences and the secondary structures (76%

was suggested that the enzyme could be involved in redox-regulating activity through its 
cysteine 122. Generally, redox regulation activity of an enzyme takes place when cysteine 
residues of the enzyme react with ROS such as H2O2 and superoxide (O2•)128. TL29 has two 
cysteines which are located far away from each other. These two cysteines are conserved in the 
9530 enzyme too.  In the 9530 enzyme, Cys 126 is located at the end of the D helix and Cys 
248 is located in C terminal ( 
Figure 45). Cys 126 is located on the other edge (surface) of the enzyme, therefore it could 
more easily be involved in redox regulation activity. However, Cys 248 is positioned within 
the enzyme and it is difficult to access122.

Conclusions  
 
For the ADH enzyme, the growth of a diffraction quality crystal was not achievable in this 
project within the timeframe. Even though the main purpose of the project originally was the 
determination of the structures of proteins through X-ray crystallography, an AlphaFold model 
of the ADH protein was applied instead. The rationale behind this approach was that an 
AlphaFold model can provide knowledge about the enzyme, both in terms of its structure and 
functions. Based on the model of the ADH protein, the enzyme consists of two domains, the 
substrate binding domain and the cofactor binding domain. The comparison of the ADH protein 
with its homolog glyoxylate/hydroxypyruvate reductases revealed that their active sites contain 
similar residues (expect for one residue). This similarity between the active sites means that 
the ADH protein may be involved in the catalysis of the reduction of glyoxylate to glycolate, 
even though the function of the enzyme was predicted as arabinoate dehydrogenase. On the 
other hand, having a larger tunnel gate in the ADH protein could enable larger substrates like 
L-arabonate to enter into the active site.  
 
 
The 9530 structure was determined with a 1.51 Å resolution. The best diffraction quality 
crystals of the 9530 enzymes were grown in PEGRx screen: 0.1 M HEPES pH 7.5, 30% w/v 
PEG 1,000. The X-ray diffraction data collection was collected on beamline 8.2.2 of BCSB at 
LBNL. The highest resolution in the dataset was 1.51 Å and the space group for the crystal was 
P3. The quality of the data was evaluated using Phenix. Xtriage software. The software did not 
detect any twinning in the data or translational NCS. The electron density map of this structure 
was solved by the MR method and the used research model had 76% amino acid sequence 
identity with the 9530 enzyme. The phenix.phaser program found a solution of one molecule 
in the asymmetric unit of the crystal. To refine the enzyme polypeptide residues inside the 
electron density map, the phenix.refine program was applied over several rounds. In addition, 
one calcium ion, five glycerol ligands and 350 water molecules were also added to the 9530-
electron density map.  The refinements ended with a final Rwork of 0.159 and Rfree of 0.184. The 
two geometric restrains, RMSD Bonds and RMSD Angles, were 0.007 Å and 083°, 
respectively. After solving the structure of the 9530 enzyme it was compared with two other 
known enzymes, TL 29 and APX- ascorbate complex. The comparison of the 9530 with APX-
ascorbate complex showed that the 9530 enzyme most likely do not binds to heme nor to the 
ascorbate, due to the crucial differences in its active site. Subsequently, the 9530 enzyme was 
compared to the most similar enzyme from PDB, namely TL 29.  The comparison indicated a 
high similarity in their secondary structure. As TL 29 can bond with trxA, it was assumed that 
the 9530 enzyme can bond in the same manner, as the cysteines are conserved at the same place 
in both enzymes. Since TrxA has redox-regulated activity via disulfide-dithiol exchange with 
its target, the 9530 enzyme could be involved in this activity too. In this project it was also 
investigated whether the 9530 enzyme can be a dimeric enzyme and the dimerization area is 
interestingly close to the cysteine of each monomer. This could point to the 9530 enzyme being 
able to form a disulfide bond between two monomers. Then the cystine of TrxA would make 
a nucleophilic attack on the disulfide bond and one monomer of the 9530 would make a new 
disulfide bond with TrxA and the other monomer of the 9530 could be converted to a thiolate 
anion. Despite the crystallographic analyses in this work, more biochemical experiments are 
needed to determine the function of the 9530 enzyme.
