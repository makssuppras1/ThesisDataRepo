Abstract 
Preclinical drug studies have been carried using animal and simplistic in vitro models 
for many years. Nevertheless, this established paradigm has started to change with the 
arrival of the organs-on chips or micro physiological systems (MPS). These are in vitro 
micrometric replicas that resemble better the native human tissue. HiPSc-CM have 
started to be used to develop cardiac MPS. However, if trespassed to 2D conventional 
surface cultures these cells loose their natural fibril alignment, among other 
characteristics. This loss of structure is then reflected in their function, therefore 
cancelling their potential to model human disorders to screen new drugs. In this work, 
a user-friendly protocol is presented to print patterned soft hydrogel substrates that 
are able to promote the arrangement of an anisotropic skeletal muscle tissue. Besides, 
the printed hydrogels are compatible with MEA devices to record electrical cell 
activity. This proof of principle shows the ability of the proposed methodology to 
potentially arrange hiPSc-CM in their native structure and therefore, obtain a relevant 
cardiac tissue suitable for drug safety and toxicity screening, more precise diagnostics 
or more sensitive biomarkers.

1 Introduction 
1.1 Motivation of the project 
The primary electrical disorders are a group of inherited cardiac ventricular 
arrhythmias that are a major cause of sudden cardiac death in young individuals. 
Inherited ventricular arrhythmias result from mutations in genes encoding cardiac ion 
channels or their modulatory subunits [1]. Even though some treatments are available 
now a days, none of these is effective enough. In consequence, patients have many 
difficulties to carry a common lifestyle [2].   
The development of a drug is an arduous and long path that comprises many phases, 
which require large economic investments. For that reason, it is crucial to develop 
novel tools that allow the successful test of the effectiveness and safety of drug 
candidates 
before 
these 
reach 
human 
trials.  
If a drug candidate is not going to work, the sooner it is discovered, the more money 
will be saved and the less fatal consequences it will have. Even though in silico tools 
are being developed, it is still needed to test thousands of drug candidates in 
preclinical trials before these make it to the first in human. 
Different models are used during preclinical trials. Animals, cell free systems or simple 
in vitro systems are some examples. Even though these are very useful, still are far 
from closely resemble the native human cardiac tissue where the drug is going to act.  
Therefore, there is a need to develop preclinical tool platforms that mimic the native 
human cardiac tissue and that allow the screening of large amounts of drug candidates 
in a cost-effective way. 
Tissue engineering is a very relevant field that combines different disciplines to 
recreate organ-like environments that can be used for many purposes. New hiPSc 
protocols can be used to generate differentiated cardiomyocytes that are useful to test 
possible dug candidates. Besides, genetic engineered tools like Crispr Cas9 can help to 
generate mutants to simulate diseased tissues.  Furthermore, by using cytocompatible 
soft patterned substrates combined with hiPSc it is possible to generate physiologically 
relevant anisotropic cardiac tissues[3].  If this is coupled with an integrated sensor, an 
ideal micro physiological system that resembles very closely a human native cardiac 
==PAGE:7==
 
7 
tissue can be used in the screening of drugs. Thus, in vitro tissue recreations like micro 
physiological or organs-on-chips promise to accelerate biomedical and pharmaceutical 
research by providing accurate in vitro replicas of human tissue[3] . 
1.2 Hypothesis and objectives 
In this project, it is hypothesised that 3D printed soft hydrogel microstructures can be 
used to culture hiPSc derived cardiomyocytes to allow them self organize and generate 
an anisotropic laminar tissue in which an action potential will propagate as in the 
native cardiac tissue.  
The objective of this project is to develop a simple, yet effective procedure to generate 
anisotropic cardiac tissues in different platforms like conventional petri dishes or 
microelectrode arrays (MEA). C2C12 cells are used as a cell line model to test the 
arrangement potential of these hydrogels. Besides, these cells are used to test the 
functionality of a MEA once the 3D printed hydrogel structures are found between the 
recording electrodes and a cultured engineered tissue. 
The addressed sub-objectives in this project are: 
- 
Develop ink formulations and DIW protocol suitable to generate 3D printed 
hydrogel micropatterns. 
- 
Study the self-organization ability of C2C12 cells on 3D printed hydrogel 
micropatterns. 
- 
3D print gelatin patterns on MEA’s and perform electrical read-outs of C2C12 
cultures. 
- 
Prove HIPSC adhesion and alienation in gelatin substrates

2 Background 
2.1 General need for improved in vitro tissue models 
2.1.1 Drug development: Key challenges  
The development of a drug is an arduous and long path that comprises many phases, 
which require huge economic investments (average 1,8 Billion dollars). After a drug 
discovery phase, many initial candidates enter a selection process in which different 
tools like animal models, cell free systems, in silico analysis are used to select the most 
suitable candidate that will enter the clinical phase in humans. If the clinical phase is 
passed successfully and other marketing steps are also overcome, the drug will reach 
the market, around 15 years after its discovery, to provide health solutions and 
socioeconomical benefits. The latter, is a very established model that has been 
working for nearly the last century. Nevertheless, the biopharma sector is facing 3 
major challenges that offer the opportunity to develop novel tools to keep on 
providing quality drugs for the society: 
- 
One of these is the increased cost of the drug development process. 70 years 
ago one drug reached the market with an average cost of 14 million dollars, 
nowadays the cost to develop one single drug is 1.8 billion dollars. 
- 
Another challenge is the molecular and physiological difference between the 
preclinical used models and humans that can make irrelevant its use [4], which 
is directly linked to the prior challenge as it increases the cost of developing 
new drugs. In 2015 11.3 billion dollars were expended in animals to test 6000 
drug candidates. From these, around 30 % made it to the clinical trials and less 
than 1 % reached the market.  
- 
The last challenge is the failure of many drugs that have to be withdrawn from 
the market. The main reasons for that are apparently safety and toxicology, 
being cardiotoxicity a major issue. In fact, 71 drugs have been withdrawn from 
the market between 1953 and 2013 due to cardiotoxicity, which accounts for 
≈142.2 billion dollars.  Even approved drugs in the market present potential 
risks for specific patient populations and thus, are tagged with adverse drug 
==PAGE:9==
 
9 
reaction notifications. That increases the chances to harm these groups of 
patients if they would consume these drug products [5]. One example of this 
are drugs which induce patients to develop long QT syndrome in the long term 
[6]. 
Thus, there is an unmet need to develop novel tools that can mimic more closely the 
physiology of the native tissue where the final product is going to be used. Ideally, 
these technologies will help to reduce the high amount of wasted money from drugs 
that have to be withdrawn from the market.

2.1.2 Personalized medicine, personalized tissue models 
Drug response can change depending on different patient characteristics and 
circumstances like diet, microbiome, age, weight, drug-drug interactions and 
genetics[7]. Thus, it is important to take into account as many of these factors to 
obtain an accurate definition of a patient profile to develop and select treatments that 
minimize harmful side effects and guarantee the best outcome. This concept of 
tailored therapies that provide a better response with the highest safety margin to get 
the best patient care has been named as personalized medicine (PM). The progression 
of biological understanding pushes to develop new tools and methodologies. 
Therefore, the actual drug development paradigm is not useful to successfully take PM 
into practice. Until now, very simplistic in vitro models or animals have been used 
during the preclinical stages of drug development. These simplify way too much the 
real picture, in which millions of different patients are going to consume a unique 
product that has been developed based on a generalist model that misses many 
factors. In this overall scenario, PM gives the opportunity to manufacturers and 
physicians to take better clinical decisions for every single patient by avoiding the one-
size-fits-all model to develop and prescribe specifically tailored treatments. Besides, 
these personalized models are arising to bring more accurate diagnostics or find better 
biomarkers[5].

wild type and isogenic cells.   In both cases, the tissues built in vitro could be used to develop a drug that 
will be further used in a population that share the genetic profile that leads to the studied disease[5].

2.2 Heart, 
cardiomyocytes 
and 
electrophysiology 
in 
homeostasis. 
The heart is the pump of the body in charge of producing a continuous flow of blood to 
provide oxygen and the necessary nutrients to all tissues. It is the principal organ of 
the cardiovascular system. Furthermore, It is composed, between others, of cardiac 
muscle cells, which contract due to an electrophysiological phenomenon named 
cardiac action potential (AP)[8] . 
The cardiac AP is a change in the voltage of a cardiomyocyte membrane, that is caused 
by the movement of ions through protein ion channels placed across the cell 
membrane, which leads to cell contraction [9]. Ion channels are membrane protein 
that can allow or restrict the passage of ions through the cell membrane depending on 
different stimuli. Within the heart, it is possible to find two different types of 
cardiomyocytes: 
- Pacemaker cells can be found in the sinoatrial (SA) node. The SA node is the 
aggrupation of pacemaker cells where the action potential starts spontaneously. Then 
the AP travels through the electrical conduction system of the heart to reach working 
CM and trigger the contraction of the heart (Figure 2).  
- Cardiomyocytes which form the atria and the ventricle chambers. As these are non-
pacemaker cells, they cannot generate a cardiac action potential, but they can conduct 
it[8]. 
Another characteristic that is different from these types of cells is the ion channels that 
these express. Besides, all cardiac cells are linked via gap junctions. This gap junctions 
are protein channels that electrically connect cardiac cells allowing the action potential 
to travel from one cell to another [9]. 
==PAGE:12==
 
12 
 
Figure 2: Cardiac conduction system. A single impulse begins in the SA node that will send a wave of excitation 
through the atria that will cause an atrial systole. This initial impulse reaches the AV node with a slight delay to 
allow the full contraction of the atria and fill the ventricles of blood. Then, the impulse will travel through the 
bundle of Hiss to the smaller branches that will send a wave of excitation through the ventricles that will cause a 
ventricular contraction[10]. 
Initially, in a healthy individual, a non electrically stimulated cardiomyocyte is found in 
resting membrane potential, around -90 mV, being the inside of the cell more negative 
than the outside. Inside of the cell, the major ion that can be found is potassium (K+), 
in the outer part of the cell it is possible to find in higher amounts sodium (Na+) and 
chloride (Cl-). The depolarization of a ventricular cardiomyocyte normally starts with 
an influx of positive charges through the gap junctions found in the intercalated disks. 
This activates voltage gated sodium channels, resulting in the first phase of the action 
potential. After, the Na+ channels are rapidly inactivated reducing its movement to the 
inner cell. At this point the potential is in a plateau phase. L- type calcium channels 
open to allow the Ca+ to get into the cell.  This Ca+ binds to calcium-activated channels 
that are present in the sarcoplasmic reticulum. Then Ca+ is released increasing its 
concentration in the cytoplasm. This Ca+ is the main responsible of the contractile 
activity of the heart. During this phase, K+ and Ca+ channels work to maintain the 
action potential slightly constant. Finally, the repolarisation phase happens due to a 
opening of voltage gated potassium channels that leads to a progressive loss of K+ 
from the inside of the cell. The voltage gated K+ channels close when the membrane

13 
potential arrives to -90 mV approximately. The propagation of this series of events 
trough all the cardiomyocytes leads to a single contraction of the heart [8,11].

2.2.1 Heart arquitechture/structure 
The heart is able to function this elegant and coordinated way due to an integration of 
structures at different hierarchies. 
The organization of the heart tissue starts with the embryogenesis, through a process 
that has to be coordinated at several size and time levels to finally obtain a complex 
network of tissues in which function and structure are tightly related. 
The cardiomyocytes that build the muscle tissue of the heart have an elongated shape 
with a diameter of 10-15 μm and 100 μm of length approximately [12]. This is critical 
for the alignment of the contractile cytoskeleton [13,14].  These cells are connected via 
gap junctions from the ends in the longitudinal direction and also from the sides 
yielding a laminar anisotropic muscle tissue that is 4 cell diameters wide. The 
alignment of the intracellular fibres combined with this organisation of interconnected 
cells gives rise to a perfect field that allows a rapid electrical spread [13]. 
This laminar anisotropic cardiac muscle tissue is the basic structure of many layers that 
have different orientations and are separated by connective tissue that assembles 
them to build the ventricular chambers (Figure 4)[12]. Besides, this cardiac muscle 
==PAGE:14==
 
14 
tissue is developed and coupled at the same time with the neural and vascular tissues, 
which all together form the heart. 
 
Figure 4: Schematic representation of the structure breakdown of the heart’s left ventricle [15]. 
Integrins are a type of adhesion cell receptors that are found in the membrane of 
many kind of cells that allow them to adhere to certain proteins that form the cardiac 
extracellular matrix (ECM). Integrins are heterodimeric receptors. Thus, each integrin 
will have higher specificity for a certain ECM protein depending on the combination of 
domains that form each cell receptor. The integrin expression profile of a given cell 
depends on the type of cell and also on the development or maturity state that this 
cell is found. I1α1β, I5α1β and I7α1β are the most expressed integrins in adult 
cardiomyocytes. These integrins bind to collagen, fibronectin and laminin respectively 
[16]. Therefore, the presence of diverse ligands in the ECM allows the attachment of 
the different cells that organize in 3D to form the heart. Nevertheless, the ECM does 
not act only as a simple scaffold. It is an organ feature that evolves at the same time as 
the whole heart tissue since the beginning of its formation (embryogenesis). Besides of 
coupling the heart tissue, the ECM maintains its structure and guides the orientation of 
CM by disposing structural cues. Furthermore, it is stiff enough to maintain the 
structure and shape of the heart and, at the same time, the ECM is soft enough to 
provide the necessary elastic support during the mechanical heart activity[12].

2.3 Emerging tools for advanced in vitro cardiac tissue models 
New tools have been proposed to develop new advanced in vitro systems that can be 
used to increase drug development success, biomarker sensitivity or get better 
diagnostics. The next are some of these tools. 
2.3.1 HiPSc 
Human induced pluripotent stem cells result from the reprogramming of a somatic cell 
extracted from a donor. By providing different factors, a somatic cell is brought back to 
an embryonic-like state from which it is possible to obtain a wide variety of cell types, 
including cardiomyocytes (CM). These cells have shown to be a promising tool to build 
tissues in vitro for many purposes [17]. HiPSc are chosen over other cell types due to 
many reasons: 
- 
They are very fast proliferating, in contrast to human primary CM, which are 
very difficult to isolate and purify when extracted. Therefore, when cultured, 
the other cell types present in the extracted sample proliferate faster, 
complicating the generation of a cardiac tissue.  
- 
Can be maintained for months on culture without showing spontaneous 
differentiation. 
- 
They can be expanded to create large cryobanks of cells with the same genetic 
profile. This overcomes the problems that genetic variability can generate 
when carrying experiments. Besides makes more easy to generate banks with 
specific genetic profiles [5]. 
- 
Overcome some ethical concerns related to embryonic stem cells (ES) as there 
it no need to handle embryos to generate them. 
2.3.1.1 HiPSc differentiation and maturation 
During fertilisation, two haploid progenitor cells fuse and form the first diploid cell that 
will develop into a pluricellular individual. Thus, this initial cell has the potential to 
differentiate into all possible cell types (totipotent) that are required for the normal 
function of a living healthy organism. In the early embryonic development, 3 germ 
layers are formed, the endoderm, the mesoderm and the ectoderm. These 3 different 
layers will give raise to different cell types (Figure 5) [18]. 
==PAGE:16==
 
16 
 
 
Figure 5: Different germ layers that develop during embryogenesis and the cell types that arise from them[18]. 
This differentiation happens in vivo due different stimuli that cells receive like 
molecular signals, cytokines, cell-cell contact, mechanical… 
Novel differentiation protocols have been developed and can be used to obtain 
differentiated hiPSc derived CM in vitro. For example, it is possible to induce the 
formation of mesodermal cells by boosting the WNT pathway to later halt it using 
inhibitors and induce cardiac differentiation [19,20]. Finally, it is possible to increase 
CM purity by metabolic selection using lactate in substitution of glucose as a carbon 
source [21]. 
However, it is a challenge to culture these cells in 2D using common in vitro techniques 
and platforms, CM loose many physiological properties questioning its relevance when 
providing answers to the addressed initial problem such as drug safety and efficacy 
testing [22].  
 It is important to point that cells are not simple building blocks that behave 
mechanically the same all the time. They are constantly interacting with their 
surrounding to modify their mechanical behaviour by altering their cytoskeletal 
structure [23]. Therefore, when CM are found outside their natural environment miss 
many types of stimuli that ‘’confuses’’ them and makes them deviate from their 
natural appearance and function. 
Culturing CM into conventional rigid flat surfaces like tissue culture polystyrene (TCPS) 
lacking microstructural cues and that are far from resembling the stiffness of the 
native myocardium (around 18 kPa [13]) denies the possibility of developing the 
required cell shape, the alignment of the cytoskeleton and the anisotropic organisation 
to propagate an AP as the native tissue does. Basically, CM just grow and spread 
through these surfaces without any organisation in the cell or tissue level. 
Furthermore, the stiffness of the surface also influences the CM organisation. It has 
==PAGE:17==
 
17 
been studied how the mechanical signals from the substrate elasticity are a critical 
factor to define cell fate. For example, the excessive rigidity can affect negatively the 
development and assembly of myofibrils[24]. Thus, when CM are cultured in vitro in 
conventional flat plates the rigidity of the surface to which they attach does not make 
possible to the cells to fully contract as the surface is not elastic at all. This rigidity is 
mechanically transduced and makes the CM to shift their structure and organisation 
into a different fate. Also, CM tend to form clusters searching for a softer substrate to 
attach and therefore be able to fully contract. 
Therefore, the cardiac tissue cultured with these conventional techniques is not 
mature enough[12–14]. Besides of the deviation of the morphological and structural 
framework, this is reflected in the activity of the cells, which show deviations on 
electrophysiology, calcium handling, cell cycle and contractile activity[25]. 
2.3.1.2 C2C12 cells as a preliminary study model 
Myoblasts are the embryonic predecessors of muscle cells or myocytes. Therefore, 
myoblasts are able to fuse into multinucleated myotubes in a differentiation process to 
form myocytes [26]. 
The C2C12 is a muscular cell line derived from mouse with myoblast characteristics. 
This cell line has the advantage that can differentiate into contractile fused myocytes 
very easy and rapidly. Moreover, C2C12 are a cheap, simple yet versatile cell line, 
which facilitates its handling and the performance of the experimental work. Besides, 
using a simple staining protocol, it is possible to observe the F-actin fibres that form 
the cellular myotubes to analyse the general alignment of the culture. Furthermore, it 
has been already shown that C2C12 are able to align and form a contractile tissue in 
2D when using soft enough substrates [27]. 
Therefore and even though C2C12 differentiated myocytes present many differences 
with hiPSc differentiated CM, due to their features, C2C12 are used as a preliminary 
study model to optimize the printing protocol and test whether an electrical read-out 
can be performed when a soft substrate is printed on top of an MEA chamber surface. 
The final preparation of the MEA chamber will be further tested on hiPSc derived CM.

2.3.2 Cardiac Microphysyological systems (MPS) 
According to the ORCHID a micro physiological system or an organ on chip is “a fit-for-
purpose microfluidic device, containing living engineered organ substructures in a 
controlled microenvironment, that recapitulates one or more aspects of the organ’s 
dynamics, functionality and (patho) physiological response in vivo under real-time 
monitoring”[28]. Organs on chips present many advantages respect conventional cell 
cultures like automated cell cultures, better media-cell ratios or the possibility of 
coupling individual organs on chips to create bodies on chips [17]. 
 
Figure 6: Microphysiological system example. Heart on chip that consist of a closed chamber on top of an MEA. The 
cultured CM lie on micromolded gelatin deposited on top of the MEA microelectrodes. The chamber has a tubing 
system that allows automated media renovation[17]. 
Very recently, different studies have combined the use of integrated sensors with 
engineered CM cultures to generate advanced in vitro systems that can help to 
generate replicas of the native cardiac tissue suitable for many analysis and studies 
[3,29,30]. 
2.3.2.1 Microelectrode array (MEA) 
A MEA is an electronic device, made of glass, which contains multiple microelectrodes 
that can sense voltage changes in the extracellular space of a cell culture (Figure 7). 
The voltage change in the intracellular space, caused by the change of ion 
concentration when an action potential occurs, can be correlated with the voltage 
==PAGE:19==
 
19 
change in a local extracellular space, and that can be detected and transduced into an 
electrical signal. Moreover, using a specialized software and hardware it is possible to 
send electric pulses through the same microelectrodes and induce the cells to 
generate a response that can be recorded as well [31–33]. It is important to remark 
that a MEA has the advantage of being useful since the beginning of the culture until 
the end, allowing to record any events that happen during the experiment that is 
performed. 
 
Figure 7: MEA device. Blue circles on the lower left square represent the single electrodes from where it is 
possible to detect and record field potentials from cell cultures. Besides, it is possible to apply electric 
stimuli through them [34].

2.3.2.2 Engineered laminar (2D) tissues 
 
Figure 8: Aligned neonatal rat ventricular myocyte (NRVM) fibres. This is the typical basic engineered 2D structure 
of an anisotropic cardiac tissue. Scale bar 10 μm [35]. 
Even though the heart tissue of the human body is organised in 3D, 2D platforms can 
help to disassemble organs and break them into basic pieces to understand better 
their structure and function. CM cultured as monolayers present some advantages 
over other kind of cultures. First, CM cultured on 2D have one side which is not 
interacting with the plate surface or the neighbour cells and thus, it is in constant 
contact with the culture media which contains all the necessary factors required for 
the cell’s activities. Thus the vascularisation of the tissue, which is always a limiting 3D 
cultures, is avoided and the transport is simplified [36].  
Furthermore, the ‘’free’’ tissue surface Is exposed to perform different kind of analysis 
[37]. For example, it is possible to stain the actin fibres and analyse the structure of the 
tissue using a fluorescence microscope [14]. Also, it is possible to immunostain specific 
markers that might be expressing the cultured cells, and therefore recognize in which 
phase they are found [38]. 
Imaging analysis can be also used to asses cardiomyocyte functionality taking 
advantage of the exposure of a 2D cardiomyocyte culture. For example, it is possible to 
visually detect the electrophysiological activity of the engineered tissue by adding a 
calcium fluorophor [39]. The inconvenience it that a phototoxic wavelength is required 
to excite the fluorophor and therefore the cultured cells get damaged[40]. For the 
same purpose, a multielectrode array (MEA) can be used. Both methods can be used 
to generate a propagation map to asses function-structure correlation of the in vitro 
generated cardiac tissue. 
==PAGE:21==
 
21 
The cardiac tissue presents a laminar anisotropic organisation at the 2D level (Figure 
8). That kind of structure confers the ability to the action potential to spread rapidly 
and very co-ordinately. This conformation of the cells is achieved due to specific 
mechanical signals that come from the neighbour tissues and ECM. Thus, these signals 
have to be emulated if the objective is to build this type of tissue in vitro. Traditionally, 
soft lithography technique has been used to make microcontact printed protein 
patterns (Figure 9) to induce changes in the organisation of cultured cells as a 
collective and generate anisotropic cardiac tissues [29,41,42]. This technique allowed 
to obtain, for example, brick wall patterns, being each brick 20 μm wide and 100 μm 
long [42]. Besides, topological microcues have been generated using gelatin molding 
methodology (Figure 9) with the same purpose [17,39]. The use of this technique 
allowed to produce, for example, ridges lower than 2 μm wide, 3 mm high and grooves 
down to 20 μm wide [17]. The achieved structural changes using these methodologies 
can be also reflected in an improvement of the tissue functionality [4,13,14,22,43–45]. 
 
Figure 9: Commonly used techniques to induce anisotropic tissue organization. (A) Microcontact printing. First, a 
predesigned pattern in PDMS is coated with the protein that will be printed. Second, the stamp is dried. Once the 
stamp is ready, it is manually put in contact with the surface where the culture will be performed. Finally, cells are 
seeded and its culture is run. (B) Hydrogel micromolding. First, the chosen hydrogel is placed on the desired surface. 
Second, the patterned stamp is placed to micromold the hydrogel mixture and left while the hydrogel is crosslinked. 
Then, the stamp is removed. Finally, cells are seeded and its culture is run[30]. 
This successful works show how the engineered cardiac tissue can be further 
improved, when grown on softer substrates instead of TCPS, to further identify and 
find treatments for arrhythmic diseases, other heart conditions or screening of

secondary effects on the heart of drugs used for other indications. Nevertheless, the 
stiffness of the resulting substrates, using these common techniques, is too high 
compared to the native cardiac tissue (23 to 114 kpa when using micromolded gelatin 
and up to MPa scale when microcontact printing is performed on PDMS vs 18 kPa of 
native myocardium [13]). Besides, micromolding and microcontact printing require 
manual handling of the stamps to generate the designed patterns, which affect the

used to resemble the stiffness and elasticity of the heart. Besides, bigger soft hydrogel 
microcues could be generated to culture CM on them being possible to obtain better 
anisotropic tissues while simplifying the experimental protocol.

2.3.3 3D printing 
3D printing is a computer-based technology in which a pattern generating-mechanism 
is controlled and guided to build pre-designed structures layer by layer. During the 
printing process, different materials can be used to obtain the desired object. Some 
examples are resins, dissolved powders or polymers[46]. This technology was 
introduced 4 decades ago to meet the needs of rapid model and prototype making 
that many industries required.  
In tissue engineering, 3D printing evolves to the so-called bioprinting technique, when 
cells are added to the used ink or the already printed structure. Therefore, this 
methodology, can be combined with the use of cells to conventional culture tools to 
resemble better the native tissue environment [47]. Thus, the biologic scientific 
intention when using this technology is to control very accurately as many printing 
parameters as possible to obtain defined substrates compositions to build designed 
structures with very specific mechanical and functional properties[46]. 
 
2.3.3.1 3D printing hydrogel substrates

Depending on the tissue type that wants to be generated, a different stiffness has to 
be reproduced. Hydrogel substrates, like gelatin, represent a good choice due to their 
biocompatibility and their versatility, as the stiffness of the final material can be 
==PAGE:24==

adjusted just by changing its concentration[4]. The most suitable 3D printing technique 
will be defined by the properties of the used material. Gelatin is prepared by dissolving 
dry powder at a temperature higher than its gelation temperature. Furthermore, it is 
found as a solution when it is being printed and becomes a gel after that, when the 
temperature lowers below the gelation temperature point. Therefore, a pattern-
generating device that allows a controlled deposition of this liquid ink through a nozzle 
is required (Figure 11). Among many available 3D printing methods, the option that 
allows to work that way is the direct ink writing (DIW) technique [48]. DIW can be 
performed using a continuous ink approach in which the formulated material is 
extruded either using a pneumatic or a volumetric system. In the pneumatic system, a 
plunger travels through the loaded cartridge moved by an applied air pressure while 
the nozzle slides through a surface disposing soft mater (Figure 10). Therefore, in this 
kind of system the only parameters that can be controlled are the air pressure that will 
lead to the material extrusion and the feed rate or velocity at which the nozzle will 
travel through the printing surface. As the exact extruded volume is not known, it is 
not possible to concrete the exact thickness of the printed layers. 
In a volumetric system, the plunger that travels through the loaded cartridge to 
extrude the soft matter is a piston. The velocity of the piston is a parameter that can 
be directly controlled, as well as the feed rate of the nozzle. Thus, as the dimensions of 
the cartridge are also known, it is possible to estimate the dimensions of the final 
prints, including the thickness. The next formula can be used:

Hydrogel solutions might need defined strategies to be successfully printed in a 
desired manner. The gelation temperature decreases as the concentration of the 
gelatin solution increases. Therefore, when a higher gelatin concentration is used, a 
temperature control device will be needed to avoid nozzle clogging and allow its 
extrusion. However, if the concentration of the gelatin solution is too low the 
formulation will behave as a liquid that will drip of the nozzle and spread through the 
surface where the hydrogel structure wants to be generated. Thus, to control the 
extrusion of a low concentrated gelatin formulation might be needed to add a 
component to increase the viscosity of the preparation. By doing that, it will be 
possible to control the extrusion by regulating the shear that is applied to the loaded 
formulation. 
Even though the hydrogel preparations gel after certain time, the rise of the 
temperature when incubating the cultures at 37 oC would dissolve the gelatin again. 
For that reason a crosslinker agent is required to ensure the stability of the hydrogels. 
Transglutaminase enzymes work forming a peptide bond between the carboxamine 
groups of the glutamine side chain and the amino groups of the lysine side chains[49].

Using a MTG solution after the gelation of the prepared gelatin will ensure its stability 
even when exposed to a temperature raise. 
2.3.3.2 DIW of MPS 
DIW is an interesting tool that can be used as a facile manufacturing route to produce 
MPS of different complexities. 
Previous work demonstrated how it is possible to combine different biocompatible 
inks and build an entire micro physiological system using a multimaterial three-
dimensional approach (Figure 12). The developed device permitted to perform 
continuous electronic and non-invasive readout of an engineered cardiac tissue 
cultured on a foldable cantilever. One of the components of this device was a tissue-
guiding layer made of patterned silicone that induced the formation of an anisotropic 
cardiac tissue. Due to this anisotropic organisation, the cantilever was able to deflect 
with every tissue contraction. Neonatal rat ventricular myocytes (NRVM) were used for 
that. Besides, dose-response studies were performed on NRVM and HIPSC derived CM 
tissues using drugs that influence contraction strength or beat rate. Finally, the 
suitability of the device for studying the development of a hiPSc proved extended 
studies derived CM during 28 days. It was shown how the contractile stress increased 
over time and the decrease of the spontaneous beating rate that indicates the 
increased maturity [35]. In this work, bigger cues (60 μm spaced grooves) were used to 
shift the organisation of the engineered cardiac tissue. Therefore, it is hypothesized 
that these cues could be even bigger and still induce the formation of an anisotropic 
tissue. It is important to highlight that the printing time increases very rapidly when 
generating smaller features. Furthermore, it is critical to reduce the printing time if a 
hydrogel substrate is used as uncoupled gelation and dehydration are not desirable. 
Thus, the advantage of printing bigger structures would be the simplification of the 
protocol to engineer these cultures.

3.3.2 DIW Prints 
Either 4 % LB and 7.5 % HB gelatin solutions were prepared by dissolving dry gelatin in 
PBS or DMEM at 45 oC. The solutions had to be flipped from time to time to ensure the 
uniform distribution and solution of the gelatin. 
When Xanthan gum was added to the mixture, dissolving the dry gum in PBS at 60 oC 
made a previous 1% stock solution. The solution had to be stirred vigorously from time 
to time to ensure the total solution of the gum. Besides, the volume used to dissolve 
the gelatin was adjusted to obtain the desired concentrations of both Xanthan gum 
and gelatin. Once both solutions were ready, the adjusted volumes were mixed 
vigorously using vortex. The final solution was centrifuged at 1000 rpm for 1 min at RT 
to remove any air bubble. 
4% LB and 7.5 % HB gelatin were softly loaded, in a pressurized cartridge and a 
volumetric cartridge respectively, using a Pasteur pipette minimizing the formation of 
air bubbles. The plungers of the cartridges were compressed until all possible air was 
removed. After, a 70 μm nozzle was assembled to the cartridge and the plunger was 
further compressed until the mixture started to be extruded through the nozzle tip.

The 4% LB cartridge was placed in the pressurized holder at RT and the 7.5 % HB 
gelatin was placed in the volumetric heated holder at 32 oC to avoid its gelation. 
A needle length measurement was then performed for both nozzles. The following 
step was to measure the surface height in which the print would be deposited. To 
validate the needle length and surface height measurements, both nozzles were 
manually guided close to the Z=0 coordinate to perform a visual assessment. Using a  
camera it was possible to see when the nozzle was touching the surface for the first 
time. At that point, the Z coordinate was checked. The maximum error allowed 
between the Z coordinate and the visual observation was of 30 μm. If the difference 
from what was observed in the camera and the Z coordinate that stated the software 
was higher than 30 μm both the needle length and surface height measurement were 
repeated after ensuring the proper cleaning of the material.

3.3.3 Crosslinking 
MTG solutions were used to crosslink the casted and printed gelatin formulations. 
First, dry MTG was vigorously dissolved either in DMEM or PBS using vortex. Second, 
the solution was sterile filtered using a 0.45 μm pore diameter filter and kept on ice 
right before use. 
Right after casting or printing on a plate, these were kept on ice for 15 minutes. After, 
the cold MTG solution was added keeping the plates on ice, for 15 minutes more. Then 
the plates were parafilmed on a plastic box and kept 1 h at 37 oC. After the incubation 
time, the gels were washed 3 times with PBS and kept in the fridge until further use. 
3.3.4 Gelatin adhesion to glass 
Rounded glass coverslips were cleaned and treated chemically with the objective of 
improving gelatin adhesion to the glass. First, the glass coverslips were plasma treated 
for 10 minutes to remove all organic residues. Second, the glasses were immersed in a 
2 % (3-Aminopopyl) trimethoxilane solution in 96 % ethanol for 15 minutes. Then, the 
coverslips were washed with 100 % ethanol 3 times. After, the rounded glass was 
baked at 105 oC for 15 minutes. Finally the coverslips were placed on the 6 well plates 
where the prints would be made later.

4.1.1 Xanthan gum concentration selection 
Gelatin is found in a liquid solution appearance when it is printed. If the gelatin 
concentration is not high enough, the blend tends to spread through the surface 
where it is deposited before reaching the gelled state. For that reason, viscous gelatin 
compositions had to be formulated to print filaments controlling their geometry 
without compromising the stiffness once gelled. 
Xanthan gum (Figure 15) was used as an additive to formulate the 4% LB gelatin inks. 
This polysaccharide has been extensively studied and used in many industries as it 
presents good solubility in water, it is biocompatible, and it can modify the rheological 
characteristics of the solution to which is added [50]. 
 
Figure 15: Molecular structure of Xanthan Gum[50]. 
A sweep was performed using concentrations from 0.2 to 1 % of xanthan in 4 % LB and 
7.5 % HB gelatin. As the xanthan gum concentration increased it was possible to 
visually assess the increase of the blend viscosity, which allowed a higher control of 
the extrusion. Nevertheless, an increase of the gum concentration was also reflected in 
a higher change of the gelatin extrusion properties. Therefore, the formulation with 
0.25 % Xanthan gum was chosen as it already showed an increase in their viscosity that 
allowed enough control while it was extruded. Besides, a higher concentration was not 
desirable to avoid unexpected biological behaviours. Moreover, it was decided to only 
add this gum to the secondly printed layer of 4 % LB gelatin. This decision was made as 
it was observed that when printed on glass surfaces, the formulations that included 
xanthan gum detached more easily. Also, it was possible to observe unusual 
conglomerations and cracks on the gelled formulation that contained the gum. These 
disruptions were more notable when the concentration of xanthan gum was increased 
==PAGE:40==
 
40 
(Figure 30).  These inconvenients were overcome by using DMEM instead of PBS to 
better dissolve the polysaccharide in the 4 % LB gelatin solutions. It was thought that 
the divalent cations present in the DMEM would help solubilize the gum by creating 
ionic bonds between the gelatin peptide and gum charges. 
4.2 C2C12 cultures on casted plates 
C2C12 cells were cultured on plates casted with different gelatin concentrations 
containing xanthan gum to test the biocompatibility of the blends and choose the 
appropriate one that would be further used for the printing protocol. Besides, 
different MTG concentrations were tested. 3, 4 and 5 % LB gelatin 0.25 % xanthan gum 
were used. All of them were cross-linked with 1, 3, 5 or 10 U/ml MTG solutions during 
one hour at 37 oC. Moreover, the gel thickness was minimized as much as possible 
(420 μm) to test its mechanical sensitivity when reduced. 
After gel preparation, C2C12 cells were cultured under standard conditions. The gels 
crosslinked with 1 and 3 U/ml MTG dissolved overtime. The 3 % gels with 0.25 % 
xanthan gum crosslinked with 5 and 10 U/ml MTG broke very easily while handling for 
culture maintenance (Figure 16: A and B). 4 and 5 % gels with 0.25 % xanthan gum 
crosslinked with 5 and 10 U/ml MTG were the only ones that showed to be stable 
enough to maintain the C2C12 cultures. Besides, spontaneous contractile activity was 
observed under the microscope in those gels at day 17 of the differentiation protocol 
(Figure 16: C, D, E and F).

4.3 C2C12 induced alignment 
Once an optimized reproducible printing protocol was developed, different patterns 
were printed in sterile conditions in conventional TCPS 6 well plates to later perform 
C2C12 cultures on them. The objective of this experiment was to test whether it was 
possible to shift from a random organization that can be observed on rigid flat 
conventional culture surfaces to a defined alignment of the C2C12 fused myotubes. 
Three patterns were tested. The first consisted on a 4 % LB gelatin vertical meander on 
top of a 7.5 % HB gelatin square with a separation between the centre of contingent 4 
% LB gelatin strips of 400 μm. The second pattern had the same structure with the 
difference that 0.25 % xanthan gum was added to both HB and LB gelatin formulations. 
The third pattern, also included 0.25 % xanthan gum in both gelatins, but the spacing 
between contingent 4 % LB gelatin strips was 300 μm. Moreover, single flat square 
layers of 7.5 % HB, 4% LB, 7.5 % HB 0.25 % xanthan gum and 4 % LB 0.25 % xanthan

The alignment of the C2C12 fused myotubes was quantitative assessed using an 
alignment score ranged from 0 to 1, being 1 the maximum alignment score possible 
and around 0.200 for a random alignment. The minimum score (0.206, SD 0.007) was 
obtained for the 4 % LB gelatin 0.25 % xanthan gum flat square (Figure 17 E). The other 
printed flat square scores were also around 0.2, never rising more than 0.225. Even a 
higher score was obtained with the cultures on TCPS (0.230, SD 0.055)(Figure 17: B). 
Nevertheless, significantly higher scores were achieved with the cultures on patterned 
gelatin. A mean alignment score of 0.450 (SD 0,062) was obtained for the cultures on 4 
% LB gelatin meander on 7.5 % HB gelatin base layer with 400 μm between the centre 
of contingent 4 % LB gelatin filaments (Figure 17 G). The same spacing design that 
included xanthan gum in its formulations yielded a score of 0.671 (SD 0.049) (Figure 17 
H). Finally, a mean alignment score of 0.655 (SD 0.037) was obtained for the cultures 
on 4 % LB gelatin 0.25 % xanthan gum meander on 7.5 % HB gelatin 0.25 % xanthan 
gum base layer with 300 μm between the centre of contingent 4 % LB gelatin filaments 
(Figure 17 I). 
The fact that no major differences were observed between the cultures performed on 
TCPS and flat hydrogel substrates suggest that having a flat soft substrate might not be 
enough to induce the alignment of the C2C12 fibres in a micrometric scale. However, it 
seems that the softness of the substrate has a major effect when micrometric cues are 
included in the culture surface. This can be seen in the score difference between the 
gelatin patterns that did (≈0.650) and did not (≈450) contain xanthan gum. The 
xanthan gum present on the gelatin formulations might interfere with some of the 
peptide interactions, which make the final gelled structure more porous and therefore 
softer that the pure gelatin. Due to these results, it is thought that the patterned 
filaments are a critical factor to induce the alignment of the fibres, and a minimized 
stiffness of those patterns helps to improve this alignment. 
To further test this hypothesis, it was printed a 7 % HB gelatin meander on top of a 7 % 
HB gelatin base layer with a distance between the centre of contingent filaments of 
400 μm (Figure 18 and Figure 31). This formulation is the most stiff of all the ones that 
were used to perform cell cultures. The cultures were run in the same conditions and 
the alignment score was calculated following the same standards. The obtained mean 
score was 0.391 (SD 0.076). This score is higher than the ones obtained with the flat 
==PAGE:47==
 
47 
gelatin prints. That confirms the critical role of the micrometric cues to induce fibre 
alignment. Moreover, this score is even lower than the patterns made with a 4 % LB 
gelatin meander, which also shows the importance of minimizing the stiffness of the 
culture patterned surface to improve the alignment of the engineered tissue.

Additionally, it was possible to observe that the orientation of the anisotropic cultures

different hypotheseis were proposed. The first is related to the 3D printer set up. It is 
known that the used 3D printer has a tilt of few μm. Therefore, it is possible that the 
printed ridges were able to acquire an inclination while gelating. This supposed 
inclination would offer the myotubes a direction preference to develop and fuse. It is 
also possible that the cells adapted differently to the distinct shapes of the patterns. 
These consisted of a flat surface in which rounded ridges were placed on top. 
Therefore it could be that when the myoblasts were seeded, these could start to 
organise parallel to the printed filaments, when attached to the flat areas. However, 
the ones that attached to the higher ridges could acquire an inclination to ‘’climb’’ 
through these. Thus, when developing and fusing, the overall alignment started to shift 
towards the orientation that the myoblasts required to attach and proliferate in the 
printed ridges.

4.4 Improvement of gelatin adhesion to glass 
First, single flat square layers of 4 % LB and 4 % LB 0.25 % xanthan gum were printed 
on glass to test the adherence of the gelatin to this material while being under culture 
conditions. 3 samples of each were printed on separate glass slides that were placed 
on 60 mm petri dishes. C2C12 cells were seeded once the prints were ready. The 
cultures were kept under standard conditions.  The prints made with the formulation 
containing xanthan gum detached after the first day in the incubator and came off 
before it was possible to start with the culture differentiation. The prints made with 
plain gelatin were stable on glass until the differentiation. Nevertheless, as days were 
passing the prints started to detach slowly from the sides until these completely 
detached on day 16 of the differentiation protocol (Figure 19).

4.6 HiPSc cultures on gelatin casted plates 
During this experiment, it was attempted to culture immature hiPSc on petri dishes 
casted with LB gelatin at 4 % and 8 % concentrations. The main objective was to prove 
whether the hiPSc were able to proliferate on the hydrogel substrates. This 
experiment was run twice. In the first attempt, a contamination was detected in all 
casted plates. In the second attempt the gels were covered once with a 1% 
chlorophorm solution. After, the casted plates were washed thrice with PBS before cell 
seeding. On a separate experiment, cells were also cultured on a conventional flat rigid 
6 well plate coated with matrigel. When the immature cells reached 80 % confluence 
the differentiation protocol was applied. 
No hiPSc adhesion was observed in the gelatin casted plates; therefore it was not 
possible to follow any culture progression in those conditions (Figure 26: C, D, E and F). 
For the matrigel coated rigid plates cell adhesion and proliferation was achieved, being 
possible to start the differentiation at day 7. Around day 6 of the dedifferentiation

switched from a monolayer to form local cellular aggregations that started to contract

differentiation protocol (Figure 26: A and B).

4.7 HiPSc cultures on gelatin 3D printed patterns 
After the failure trying to culture immature hiPSc on casted plates with “raw” gelatin, it 
was attempted to culture this cells on 3D printed patterns coated with matrigel. Two 
different matrigel coatings were applied. The first coating was performed during the 
crosslinking of the printed pattern. 1% matrigel solution was added after 30 minutes of 
crosslinking at 37 oC and was left for 30 more minutes. After, the gels were washed 
thrice with PBS and were kept in the fridge until further use. The second one consisted 
on a regular passive matrigel coating at 37 oC during 1 hour before cell seeding. 
Immature hiPSc cells were seeded on a 4 % LB gelatin 0.25 % xanthan gum meander on 
7.5 % HB gelatin base layer 20 μm  thick with 400 μm between the centre of 
contingent 4 % LB gelatin strips.

crosslinking protocol. Neither in the print areas nor the TCPS surface. However, cell

The initial low cell adhesion on the passively matrigel coated prints suggested that 
there was something present or missing in the prepared plates that difficult the 
survival of the immature hiPSc. One possibility is that one hour coating with matrigel at

components of gelatin and matrigel are collagen and laminin respectively. Thus, it is 
required that the seeded cells show an integrin expression profile that is able to 
adhere to the combination of ECM ligands that are found as a substrate. Furthermore, 
it is known that the integrin expression profile of CM changes depending on their 
development stage [16,52]. Thus, it is hypothesised that due to the immaturity stage 
of these cells, the integrin expression profile is not able to adhere the ECM ligands 
pesent in the culture, and therefore the cells are not able to attach and proliferate 
optimally the first days after seeding. As said, cells seemed to proliferate at the same

Another possible explanation to the low proliferation rate of the hiPSc culture is that 
some of the newly introduced components directly perturbed the seeded cells. Even 
though the LB gelatin, the HB gelatin and the xanthan gum did not affect negatively 
the C2C12 cells it is possible that their concentrations would have to be adjusted to 
allow hiPSc to normally expand, as these cells are more sensitive. Nevertheless, 
previous works have already used gelatin in similar concentrations to culture this cell 
type [4,17,38]. For that reason it is not very likely that this component of the 
formulation is disturbing cell viability. From all the materials used to prepare the 
prints, MTG is the one that could potentially have a toxic activity for the cells due to its 
origin and activity. Even though 3 PBS washes were applied after crosslinking, it could 
be that some traces of the enzyme stayed attached to the porous gel being released 
overtime and disturbing the cell growth. That is also thought as most of the cell 
adhesion was observed on the PS areas and very few cells were found on the gelatin 
prints. 
As said, the crosslinked gel is a porous structure that gets soaked with the solution in 
which it is found. And it takes some time until it is exchanged with the freshly added 
media. Consequently, the seeded cells have a direct contact with the solution in which 
the gel patterns are kept, which is always PBS. Even though PBS is a saline solution that 
might not harm at all any cell type, hiPSc are very sensitive and tricky cells. For that 
reason it would be worthy to previously acclimate the environment by adding some 
culture media to the plates prior to cell seeding. However, that can generate an 
advantage for any microbial contamination that can be present on the prepared plates 
and therefore, could ruin the culture.

5 General Discussion 
Lind et al. 2017 showed how it is possible to induce the alignment of NRVMs and 
hiPSc-CM to generate anisotropic cardiac tissues. This was achieved by culturing these 
cells on 3D printed parallel polydimethylsiloxane (PDMS) filaments with 60 μm spacing 
between them. Jensen et al. 2020 found that the use of soft enough hydrogel 
substrates allowed C2C12 skeletal muscle to improve their alignment. Thus, in this 
work, it was aimed to combine both approaches to print patterned hydrogel filaments 
with the minimum spacing possible to optimize cardiac cell alignment. To start, a 
preliminary study was carried using C2C12 cells. 
Previously, Carmen from the TMAT group was able to print alternating lines of 10 % 
and 2.5 % LB gelatin on a PS surface, being more than 500 μm the space between the 
centre of contingent filaments made of the same gelatin concentration (Figure 28). 
 
Figure 28: 5 X magnification microscope picture of alternating 2.5 % and 10 % LB gelatin filaments. Distance 
between the centre of contingent filaments 500 μm. Scale bar: 500 μm. 
To optimize a user-friendly 3D printing protocol it was necessary to re-formulate the 
gelatin mixtures needed to generate small enough yet still soft patterned gelatin 
substrates. This was achieved by controlling the temperature of extrusion of the 7.5 % 
HB gelatin. Besides, it was necessary to add xanthan gum in a concentration that 
allowed controlling the extrusion and geometry of the deposited 4 % LB liquid gelatin 
while printed on top of a 7.5 % HB base layer. After optimizing a reproducible printing 
==PAGE:59==
 
59 
protocol it was proved the possibility to improve skeletal muscle alignment by 
combining the benefits of micrometric cues and minimized substrate stiffness. Besides, 
the designed patterned hydrogel placed on a MEA did not interfere to record the field 
potentials generated due to cell contractions. The further objective was to extrapolate 
the developed protocol to culture hiPSc, however, it was not even possible to obtain a 
homogenous monolayer of an immature culture. To continue in this direction would 
be worthy to study the viability of a culture starting with cells on a more mature stage. 
It is important to remember that the main integrins expressed by adult CM are I1α1β, 
I5α1β and I7α1β, which have higher affinities for collagen, fibronectin and laminin 
respectively [16]. Therefore, it would be interesting to try different combinations of 
these ECM ligands to analyse the adhesion capacity of both hiPSc and hIPSc-CM. 
Additionally, the integrin expression profile of these specific cells could be studied to 
find possible protein ligands that would be included in the structural substrate and 
improve the culture conditions. 
The approached methodology using a 3D printer presents the advantage of being 
relatively affordable for mostly any laboratory involved in this research field [53]. 
However, the main disadvantage of the used protocol is the processing time that 
significantly increases due to the crosslinking step. Moreover, the remaining traces of 
the crosslinking enzyme (MTG) in the porous gelled structure might harm cells with a 
higher grade of sensitivity like hiPSc. 
Finally, this work carried with C2C12 represents a proof of principle of how soft 
hydrogel substrates can be 3D printed to engineered muscular tissues and analyse 
their electrical activity when cultured on MEA devices. As a future perspective, this 
hydrogel substrates formulations would have to be further developed to be included in 
a multimaterial approach to build improved micropysiological systems suitable for 
preclinical cardiac drug screening, diagnose development or finding new biomarkers.

6 Conclusions 
The main findings of this project are: 
- 
Hydrogel substrate formulations for 3D printing of patterned structures 
suitable for C2C12 cultures have been defined. 
- 
These novel formulations have allowed to reduce the distance from more than 
500 μm to 300 μm  (200 μm without controlling the print geometry). 
- 
A soft flat substrate is not enough to induce C2C12 fibre alignment in the micro 
scale and induce the formation of an anisotropic skeletal muscle tissue. 
- 
Micro scale topological cues combined with minimized substrate stiffness has 
been proved to highly improve C2C12 fibre alignment and induce the formation 
of an anisotropic skeletal muscle tissue. 
- 
Field potentials originated from a C2C12 culture electrical activity can be 
recorded in presence of a hydrogel layer between the recording 
microelectrodes  and the cultured cells on an MEA. 
Further studies will have to be carried to achieve hiPSc adhesion and proliferation in 
soft hydrogel susbtrates.

Reduced distance between contingent gelatin strips 
The distance between the centre of contingent 4 % LB gelatin strips was reduced to 
200 μm. However, a good control of their geometry was not achieved. The size of the 
used nozzle (70 μm), the reduction of the pressure to avoid the merge of the 4 % LB 
0.25 % xanthan gum filaments, and the need to print from a closer distance to achieve 
the deposition of the gelatin represented a series of limitations that prevented the 
smooth deposition of this substrate (Figure 29).

Ink and 3D printing protocol optimisation 
 
First, it was attempted to print alternating LB and HB gelatin formulated filaments. This 
approach was not reproducible as in many occasions the second layer overlapped the 
first one instead of being deposited were it was designed initially (Figure 30 A). 
Therefore, it was decided to change the strategy to first print a flat surface of HB 
gelatin to later print a LB gelatin meander on top. Besides, a sweep with different 
xanthan gum concentrations was performed to choose a formulation that could be 
printed controlling its extrusion. The final concentration was 0.25 % dissolving the final 
blend in DMEM as explained (4.1.1). Higher xanthan gum concenrations dissolved in 
PBS yielded filaments with visible conglomerations and cracks (Figure 30).

Disease: Different mutations in ion channels 
 
Sudden cardiac death is a fatal event that happens in 3 of every 100.000 people under 
40 years in Germany and North America[2]. From these cases, 70 % are found to be 
caused by an inherited arrhythmic syndrome. 
The ion channels present in any cell membrane are proteins encoded by genes from 
the DNA. If this DNA contains the ‘’correct’’ sequence, the expressed protein will 
execute its homeostatic function. Nevertheless, mutations in these sequences can lead 
to dysfunctional ion channels that can cause the disruption of the normal electrical 
activity of the cell, the so called primary electrical disorders. If these mutations occur 
in the germ lines, the genotype can be passed from one generation to the next. The 
combination of both situations results in an inherited cardiac ventricular arrhythmia. 
There are different ion channels involved in the phases of the action potential process 
that results in the heart contraction (2.2). Thus, alterations in different ion channels 
can result in different disease conditions.
