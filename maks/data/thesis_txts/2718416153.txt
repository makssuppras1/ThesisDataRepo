Abstract
The Bayesian approach to machine learning provides a promising alternative to the clas-
sical approach. However, scaling Bayesian methods to modern neural network models
presents many challenges. One approach is the variational inference, which performs
posterior inference on the model’s parameter space by optimizing a variational objective
over a family of variational distributions. These variational distributions should scale well
to the high dimensionality of the posterior. For this reason, a common variational family
is the mean-field Gaussian. However, it tends to underperform wrt non-variational meth-
ods - e.g., SWA-Gaussian proposed in Maddox et al. [2019]. One interpretation [Farquhar
et al., 2020a] blames the tendency of their probability mass function to condense in a shell
around the mean as dimensionality increases. Farquhar et al. [2020a] claims this causes
the variational objective to exhibit a high degree of variance. Farquhar et al. [2020a] pro-
poses the radial variational distribution, which shifts probability mass towards the mean
by separately sampling the direction and distance to the mean over the parameters in
each weight and bias of the neural network model.
In this thesis, we seek to investigate further the radial variational distribution and how it
compares to the mean-field Gaussian. We further propose a radial variational distribution
over the entire parameter space. In order to test whether further centering of variational
samples around the mean reduces gradient variance and improves performance. We
further show how to change the distance distribution and test several choices.
We investigate the claims in Farquhar et al. [2020a], by testing the radial variational distri-
butions against mean field Gaussian on a series of variational Gaussian linear regression
problems, where we test the convergence of the variational parameters to the analytically
optimal solution.
We further test all variational distributions on a collection of variational Bayesian deep
models on multiclass classification problems, on a range of metrics. We also test perfor-
mance on data corruption, and variational continual learning.
We find our proposed radial further reduces gradient variance but does not generally
improve performance.
In variational Gaussian linear regression, comparing the two: we found the mean field
Gaussian converges better to the optimal mean parameter, whereas the radial converges
better to the optimal variance parameter.
Across all problems and metrics, we see that the radials only tend to outperform the mean
field Gaussian in accuracy. However, the mean-field Gaussian is more resistant to noisy
inputs in addition to having better calibration, Which is contrary to the finding in Farquhar
et al. [2020a].
We found that changing the distance distribution improves performance, particularly thicker-
tailed distributions generally improve accuracy, coinciding with lower gradient variance.

Introduction
Machine Learning is a rapidly advancing field, both in industry and academia, for good
reasons. It seems to be at the eye of a perfect storm. Where our information processing
capabilities have exploded as prescribed by Moore’s law [Mack, 2011]. Along with a cor-
responding explosion in available data [Qiu et al., 2016]. These discoveries give machine
learning scientists both the fuel and the machines to create groundbreaking models that
can ”understand” the complexities of our world maybe even better than we can [Grace
et al., 2018].
Machine Learning - more specifically, supervised machine learning which we will focus
on in this thesis - can generally be thought of as modeling relations between two sets of
variables x, y. In classical machine learning the goal is to find a map from x-space to
y-space that correctly predicts y for some new x.
In Classical Machine Learning: Given that some samples from the joint distribution over
x, y - colloquially called ”data” - we can then find the member of the model family, which
makes the data as likely as possible this is also called the maximum likelihood estimate.
This approach has proven very useful, but it has certain drawbacks. It tends to overfit
when we have a small amount of data compared to the model’s flexibility. That is, it
overly fits the noise in the data and not the underlying relation. However, some methods
seem to alleviate these issues by penalizing overly flexible members of the model family,
or rather, it favors simple explanations. This general approach is called regularization
[Bishop, 2006, sec. 1.1].
Another issue is that of model calibration, i. e. the model should be accurate in how likely
it is to be right/wrong.
Example - Calibration: Say we have a binary classifier that aims to correctly classify
whether images contain some object - i.e., assigns images how likely they are of contain-
ing said object by a probability. As such, a desirable property would be that the images
assigned the probability α would - on average - have α fraction of them containing said
object. If satisfied, we would say that the model is perfectly calibrated. End
Unfortunately, modern deep learning models do poorly on calibration metrics and even
worse than earlier deep learning models despite improved accuracy - Guo et al. [2017].
This finding is particularly disconcerting as machine learning spreads to critical sectors
such as medicine and health care, where risk assessments are critical.
Bayesian Machine Learning takes a different approach than classical machine learn-
ing. This branch takes its basic approach from the seminal Bayesian equation, which
includes two terms - excluding the normalizing constant, see (1.1). These are the likeli-
hood P(y|x, f) and the prior P(f) - where f maps x to y. The likelihood is conceptually
similar to the objective function found in classical machine learning. The prior can be seen
as a generalization of the regularization term mentioned above, allowing us to bias the
model toward our prior ”expectations.” As such overfitting is inherently not as big an issue
in Bayesian machine learning [Bishop, 2006, p. 9].
Bayesian machine learning can be formulated as the problem of estimating the posterior

The Bayesian approach seems to alleviate some of the pathologies encountered in clas-
sical machine learning Brosse et al. [2020], Bishop [2006].
• Over-fitting: As the prior increasingly biases toward our prior ”expectations” as
data decreases, or in other words, we require an increasing amount of data for us
to consider an increasingly unlikely a priori model.
• Calibration: The distribution over the model family - inherent to the Bayesian ap-
proach - can be propagated into the function space over data-space by calculating
the predictive distribution via marginalization over model-space. Intuitively: This
should alleviate the issue as this distribution in function space should ideally align
our confidence in our predictions with the probability of our predictions being accu-
rate.
An additional advantage of the Bayesian approach in practice is that generally the chosen
model family is often under specified by data. As such, any single model in the family can
only capture curtain aspects of the relation. This can be alleviated by a weighted averag-
ing over multiple models - weighted based on how well they relate to the likelihood and
the prior - which captures different aspects of the relation leads to greater generalisation
than any single model could Wilson [2020].

Deep Learning is the practice of using a class of model families called deep neural net-
works in machine learning. These neural networks are parametric model families - ie
families of neural network models can be associated with a parameter space, and, indi-
vidual models with elements of the parameter space. These parameter spaces can be
of arbitrary dimension, and in some modern applications exceed millions of dimensions
ie millions of parameters. This endows them with incredible flexibility and allows them to
capture amazingly complex relations between an enormous number of variables.
Neural networks consists of a series of alternating affine and nonlinear transformations.
They have in many ways become the ”go to” general model due to their universal ap-
proximation property [Scarselli and Tsoi, 1998]. Further, the depth of these models - ie
the length of the transformation sequence - seems to give deep neural networks a very
attractive structural bias toward realistic relations [Wilson and Izmailov, 2020].
Why a Bayesian Approach to Deep Learning ?: In deep learning we often have under
specified models as we often fit deep model families which are vastly more flexible than
the data we fit them to, as such there are often a diversity of good models each fitting
the data equally well. In other words the deep model family contains a diversity of good
models each explaining the data equally well. Making it is desirable have representation
of this diversity. As such we could argue and its has been done so in Wilson [2020] that
in deep learning - at least intuitively - the case for the Bayesian is especially strong as this
diversity could ideally be represented by a distribution over the deep model family.
It further seems that the Bayesian approach provides robustness in tasks such as clas-
sifying images with imposed noise Wilson and Izmailov [2020], giving credence to the
intuition presented.

2.1
Opportunities
A Bayesian approach to deep learning presents both challenges as well as opportunities.
The illumination of the opportunities associated with a Bayesian approach to deep learning
informs what properties we expect or aspire to in Bayesian deep learning models. In the
following we present a collection of observations made in the literature on the opportunities
presented by a Bayesian approach to deep learning.
The Bayesian Approach Better Accounts for Epistemic Uncertainty:Wilson [2020]
argues based on the properties of deep neural network posteriors that we will often have
a plurality of diverse models capable of explaining the data. As such picking just one
of these would lead to an unfounded degree of model confidence when compared to a
Bayesian approach which attempt to capture this plurality. This is however a highly intu-
itive argument which focuses mostly on the theoretical merits of the Bayesian approach.
A more practical argument leveraged in the same paper - [Wilson, 2020], points out the
existence of flat regions in parameters-space - wrt the posterior, containing a large volume
of models. These flat regions are - reported to, contain a plurality of models which gener-
alize well. Due their large volume, point estimates are particularly unhelpful in capturing
this plurality, where as integration - via Monte Carlo methods, are theoretically much more
apt. This is a more practical argument as these flat region are compact, which current in-
ference methods are capable of capturing to some extend.
The Bayesian Approach Explains Generalization: Wilson and Izmailov [2020] further
argues the merits of marginalisation - via Monte Carlo methods, for achieving better gen-
eralization. As well as providing results supporting the Bayesian approach’s generaliza-
tion abilities on progressively corrupted data. Wilson and Izmailov [2020] Further argues
that deep ensemble methods should be seen as an approximate Bayesian approach as
opposed to being entirely non Bayesian.
In Wilson and Izmailov [2020] it is further argued that the Bayesian approach can illuminate
the discussion on deep learning models’ ability to learn randomized labels on images,

as well as the double decent phenomenon. Wilson and Izmailov [2020] argues that the
prior’s support over function space of deep learning models is extensive however there is
a structural preference - which can be strengthened by an appropriate prior in parameter-
space, toward maps that naturally captures preferable relations. Ie these functions have
greater support wrt the prior over function space. However the prior still has support for

This probabilistic perspective is then further validated by providing results supporting that

Being Even a Little Bayesian Improves Calibration: Kristiadi et al. [2020] provides

et al. [2020] further provides results both in binary and multi-class classification supporting
the preceding analytical analysis, as well as these findings carrying over to multi-class
classification.
2.2
Challenges
From a Bayesian approach to deep learning emerges many challenges due to the ne-
cessity of the approximations we must make to enable large scale posterior inference.
As such it is important to analyze and explore the implications of these approximations.
In order to inform the development of the Bayesian deep learning. In the following we
present a collection of observations made in the literature on the challenges associated
with variational inference.
Variational Inference in Bayesian Deep Learning Limits our Choice of Objective
Functionals: Dhaka et al. [2021] investigates Monte Carlo methods ability to correctly
estimate important quantities for various variational objectives, as the dimensionality of
the problem increases. Here they found that only mode-seeking variational objectives
were viable for high-dimensional variational inference.
Note that: Dhaka et al. [2021] only provides results for problems whose dimensionality
range form 0 - 50. This is an entirely different scale than the one we are dealing with in
this thesis where the dimensionality is vastly higher.
Dhaka et al. [2021] further provides a cheap diagnostic for the accuracy of the Monte Carlo
estimates, as well as methods for reducing the variance of the Monte Carlo estimates.
Both of these are based around the idea that the ratio between the posterior and the
variational distribution - ie the variable in the variational objectives, follows a generalised
Pareto distribution. As such we can fit a generalised Pareto distribution to the ratios. The
parameters of this distribution then gives us a proxy for the accuracy of our Monte Carlo
estimates as well as allowing for ”smoothing” of extreme rations by using a method called
Pareto Smoothed Importance Sampling which reduces the variance of the ratio.
Dhaka et al. [2021] also provides results supporting the claims made above as well as
Pareto Smoothed Importance Sampling’s ability to improve the accuracy of variational
distributions.
Last Iteration Optimization Poorly Approximates Posterior Statistics: Dhaka et al.
[2020] provides results supporting: That last iteration optimization methods does not ac-
curately capture posterior statistics even when the posterior is a member of the variational
distribution family, and shows that averaging over the last iterations improves accuracy.

2.3
Mean Field Variational Distributions
The mean field approach to variational inference allows us to scale posterior inference up
to large scale Bayesian deep learning models. As such the implications of this approach
are of great interest to us in Bayesian deep learning community, allowing us to understand
its shortcomings such that we may engage in well informed application, as well as allowing
for improvements to current mean field methods. In the following we present a collection
of the observations made in the literature on the implications of the mean field approach.
Mean Field Methods are Asymptotically True: Wang and Blei [2019] shows that as
data goes to infinity the optimal mean field model - wrt the KL-divergence, coincides with
the true posterior as well as the mean of the optimal mean field model being asymptotically
normally distributed around the true parameter value.
Wang and Blei [2019] further shows that the model error asymptotically dominates the
variational approximation error. Pointing out: That the most significant source of error is
the choice of model and not the choice of posterior inference scheme. They further provide
results supporting mean field models asymptotic predictive performance is less one-sided
than the asymptotic posterior approximation - due to the error incurred by the mean field
assumption being fractional of total predictive error, while constituting the entirety of the
posterior approximation error.
Mean Field Models Perform Similarly to Models with Covariance on Sufficiently
Deep Neural Network models: Farquhar et al. [2020b] develops a method for analyzing
the local covariance between parameters in the posterior over compact regions in input
space. They then use this method to show that mean field Gaussian variational distri-
butions induce covariance over these regions, and that deeper models allows for more
complex covariance.
They further prove that - given curtain assumptions, for any predictive posterior distribution
there exist a deep neural network with a mean field distribution over its parameters which
induces the same predictive posterior.
Farquhar et al. [2020b] further provides results supporting: that for neural networks of
increasing depth, mean field and covariance variational distributions perform increasingly
similar.
These findings are very much in line with the conclusions of Wang and Blei [2019], as
they proved that model error generally dominates posterior inference error. And therefore
suggested that using better models will generally give better results than better posterior
inference.

4.6
Variational Continual Learning
As a last test we wish to see how well these variational distributions perform in the context
of continual learning. Here we use that same set up as in Farquhar et al. [2020a] - which
i based on a methodology proposed in Nguyen et al. [2017]. The methedology goes as
follows: We split the FashionMNIST data set up into 5 sets such that the first set contains
the numerical labels: 0, 1, and the second set: 2, 3, and so on up to labels: 8, 9, we
further split these sets 0.9 / 0.1 for validation. We then start out with the deep dense
network - introduced in section 4.3, but with only one output dimension. We then train
the model on the first set as a binary classification task with 1 the positive class using
the one dimensional out out put. After this we set the variational distribution as prior and
leave the variational parameters in the solution current solution - ie same as the new
prior. We then add an output dimension to the model, where the additional parameters
are initiated using the Kaiming He initialization [He et al., 2015]. We then train the the next
binary classification task on the second set using the new output dimension, and set the
variational distribution as prior as before. We continue in this manor until we have trained
on all five tasks. We then evaluate our model on the left out validation set for fine tuning
the training procedure. All variational distributions start out using an isotropic Gaussian
prior and then after the first task transfers to it’s own variational distribution family as prior.
As a result it starts using the cross entropy associated with that prior, which we derived
in section 3.4.3.
As this is a fairly light model in terms of the computational resources required for train-
ing. We decided to run a grid search over batch sizes [512, 1024], number of epochs
[1, 2, 4, 8, 16], learning rates [0.1, 0.1/3, 0.1/32], and initial prior isotropic standard deviation
[1, 10, 100]. For each variational distribution we then chose the training procedure which
minimizes the mean square error rate, ie:

5.1
Discussion on Implementation
Here we discuss our implementation of the variational distributions, test setup, and deep
neural network models used in this thesis.
5.1.1
variational distributions and Gaussian linear regression
As we implemented the factorized Gaussian radial ourselves, we first needed to verify that
our implementation worked as intended. We did this as a preparatory step in implement-
ing the factorized Gaussian radial distribution and the Gaussian linear regression setup -
see section 4.3.1. The results indicated in figure 4.1 where we verify that these implemen-
tations work as intended due to: Firstly, the implementation of the factorized Gaussian
radial from Farquhar et al. [2020a] converges to the optimal mean and empirical vari-
ance, which informs us that our implementation of Gaussian linear regression works as
it should. In addition, our implementation of the Gaussian radial converges similarly to
the factorized radial, which we expected in these instances. As the factorized radial only
factorizes over the weights and the one-dimensional bias. We see, of course, also that
our implementation of the mean-field Gaussian likewise converges.
The further implementation of the alternative radial profiles was relatively simple. As it
only required changing the base distribution and in the case of the variational continual
learning setup - see section 4.6, it required changing the cross entropy due to the change
in prior - see section 3.4.3.
5.1.2
Deep Neural Network Models and Data Sets
We then implemented a deep, dense network on FashionMNIST and MNIST. In addition
implemented ResNet18, Resnet50 and PreResNet50 as described in section 4.3.2, which
we used on CIFAR10. To verify this implementation, we chose to implement a regular-
ized maximum likelihood - described in section 3.1, to act as a baseline as it was easy
to compare its performance to other implementations. However, we did not get as good
results as those we compared it to. Nonetheless, it was not the purpose of it. The dis-
crepancy is likely due to us using a too-high learning rate and not a decreasing learning
rate schedule which is common for the implementations which achieve good results. We
believe this is the case, as, during their initial training, their learning rates are comparable,
and they get similar results to ours. We could have further verified this had time permitted
it. Regardless, as our best variational bayesian networks got results close to the best of
performing models we found, we decided to leave it at that. We also found that using data
augmentation - see section 4.3.2, before each new epoch helped significantly to get our
results up to par with the implementations we found.
For our variational distributions, we found that it helps to increase accuracy and negative
log-likelihood by leaving batch normalization on evaluating the model and one variational
sample at a time. This result is contrary to common practice in classic deep learning. We
did not explore what causes this, but we hypothesize: That it might be due to the vari-
ational distribution increasing its variance in direction which only causes scaling - which
is removed by the batch normalization, during training to reduce the variational objective.
However when we turn off the batch normalization these different scalings start to have

==PAGE:60==
an effect, and that evaluation with multiple variational samples causes normalization over
each layer output over several models which means that we no longer evaluate each
model independently as well as likely causing unwanted shifts in the batch normalization.
We used the batch normalization implementation in PyTorch. As such, its parameters
are not included in the variational distribution. As, at least to our knowledge, they should
be viewed as transformations that minimize internal covariance shifts between layers.
As such, it makes little sense to perform variational inference on them. We could have
looked closely at the literature to see the best practice for dealing with batch normalization
in Bayesian neural networks. Nevertheless, we choose to leave it at that.
Concerning the number of variational training samples, learning rate, batch size, and the
number of epochs, we choose to keep them constant. With a slight exception in the
number of epochs for the ResNet models and the deep dense network model, we rand
for 50 and 20, respectively, as we found this to be generally enough for convergence. It
also allows us to compare better the variational distributions regarding the computational
resources used to train them. We use one variational training sample to estimate the
quantities in eq. (3.71), and a batch of 128.
We see in table 4.2 as well as in the figures 4.3 and 4.6 that the Rre/ResNet50 models
seems to underperform wrt ResNet18 across all variational distributions and regularized
maximum likelihood. We hypothesize that it might be due to the increased depth of these
networks, in terms of layers, which can cause a greater degree of covariance shift - [Ioffe
and Szegedy, 2015]. We might have counteracted this if we had used a larger batch size,
as this would have decreased the variance of the batch normalization.
For these model data pairs, we decided to test them on various Gaussian isotropic priors
with different levels of standard deviation in order to better tune this hyperparameter. It
would have been interesting to test them on a more refined range of standard deviations
to better see the impact on the performance. However, as the larger models require
considerable training time, this would have been quite time and resource-consuming.
5.2
Data Corruption and Variational Continual Learning
We implemented a data corruption setup - see section 4.5, by using the trained models
and evaluating their test sets with increasing levels of Gaussian noise. Here it would
have been interesting to try out different types of corruption, such as in Ovadia et al.
[2019] rather than just Gaussian.
We implemented the variational continual learning setup - see section 4.6, by splitting up
the ten labels of the FashionMNIST data set into five pairs. And then sequentially training
on each of the five tasks on the deep dense network, using the variational parameters
found in the previous task prior to the current - see section 4.6 for further details. It would
have been interesting to try this out on one of the larger models, such as ResNet18.
However, to get good results for the deep dense network, we had to - similarly to Farquhar
et al. [2020a], perform an extensive grid search on the training parameters. That would
be prohibitively time and computationally resource-consuming for a model like ResNet18.
It could also have been interesting to try training continual learning on smaller sets of data
containing all classes. And then see if the models would obtain similar results to those of
the normal setup, which would hopefully not require the same degree of grid search as
we would include all classes.

Question II
Due to the factorized Gaussian radial outperforming the mean-field Gaussian variational
distributions in Farquhar et al. [2020a], we decided to test this further on metrics and
settings not included in Farquhar et al. [2020a]. We expected to see the superiority of the
radials generalize to other metrics due to the hypothesis raised in Farquhar et al. [2020a]
that: the high gradient variance causes the mean field Gaussian variational distribution
to not converge. However, testing convergence for variational Bayesian neural networks
is difficult, as we do not generally know what the variational parameters are supposed
to converge on. Nevertheless, there are more analytically tractable settings where this
is possible. One such setting is the Gaussian linear regression setting. In which the
posterior, as well as the optimal mean field approximation - wrt the KL divergence, are
tractable. That allows us to study their convergence directly. It should however be kept in
mind that the posterior of a variational Bayesian neural networks generally looks nothing
like that of a Gaussian linear regression, and thus we should be cautious not to draw too

estimate the variance better. These two tendencies seem to grow stronger as dimension-

This deprecation in the mean-field Gaussian performance compared to the radial, as the
model size increases, is corroborated by the results in table 4.1.
It would therefore be interesting to further test this hypothesis. A simple first test could be
to gradually reduce the variance of the mean field Gaussian to see if performance would
increase. We could also try to estimate empirical variance of the variational samples for
the radials and mean field Gaussian respectively to see if the empirical variances are con-

try gradually increasing the variational samples used in evaluation in the mean field Gaus-

where we would - based on the hypothesis, expect the radials to quickly converge due to
the smaller volume covered by their variational distribution, where as the mean field Gaus-
sian would require much more extensive variational sampling in order for its performance
to converge.
The most important takeaway how ever is that we need to understand why we get different
results from those in Farquhar et al. [2020a], which should be understood first, before
testing the hypotheses we proposed.

5.4
Conclusion
In this thesis, we further analyze and test the methods and arguments leveraged in Far-
quhar et al. [2020a]. We did this by further comparing the radial and the mean-field Gaus-
sian approaches. In this endeavour we further explore the radial approach by proposing
a defactorization over the individual weights and biases. We then tested these variational
distributions on variational Gaussian linear regression - see section 4.3.1. We further
show how to change the radial profile and tried an exponential and a Student’s t profile
- see section 3.4.3. We then tested all of these variational distributions on a collection
of variational Bayesian deep neural networks on a range of metrics - see section 4.4.1.
As well as a data corruption test and variational continual learning - section 4.5 and 4.6
receptively.
The goal of this thesis was to pursue the research questions below.
5.4.1
Question I: Which radial variational distribution is the best based on
our metrics
Here we expected that defactorizing the radial from Farquhar et al. [2020a] would further
decrease gradient variance and improve performance.
In table 4.1 we see that defactorizing did reduce gradient variance, and did not seem to
depend much on the model size, which we see it does for the factorized Gaussian radial.
We further see that defactorization improves accuracy. However, we do not see a general
trend that defactorizing improves performance on larger models, which is surprising, as
defactorizing did significantly reduce gradient variance for larger models - see tabel 4.1.
We do not see that defactorizing improves calibration, which was further surprising, as
well as mixed results on expected log predictive density. We likewise did not find any
improvements in the data corruption test.

In figure 4.10 for variational continual learning. We would again have expected improve-

These results raise further questions as to how gradient variance impacts model perfor-

5.4.2
Question II: Does the radial variational distributions out perform
Gaussian mean field across all of our metrics
Here we expected the mean-field Gaussian to underperform generally, just as we saw in
Farquhar et al. [2020a].
In the variational Gaussian linear regression set up (section 4.3.1), we found that as we
increase dimensionality, the mean-field Gaussian better estimates the meanwhile being
worse at estimating variance than the radials. These results might hint at a general dif-
ference between these two approaches.
The results in table 4.1 show that, as expected, the mean-field Gaussian underperforms
in accuracy and that this discrepancy - wrt the radials, gets worse as the sizes of the
models increase. However, we generally see that it outperforms the radials across the
other metrics, including calibration - see figure 4.7, which is contrary to what was found
in Farquhar et al. [2020a]. In figure 4.10, we see that the mean-field out performs the
factorized Gaussian radial, contrary to what we see in Farquhar et al. [2020a].
The data corruption results - section 4.5, are further interesting as we see that the mean-
field Gaussian is significantly more robust than the radials.
Thus, based on the results from variational Gaussian linear regression and the tests and
metrics on the variational Bayesian deep models, it can be hypothesised that the mean
field Gaussian might generally be good at finding good means. In contrast, the radials
might generally be better at finding good variances. This finding might explain why the
mean field Gaussian seems more robust to data corruption, as well as getting good results
on expected log predictive density, expected calibration error, and performing decently
in variational continual learning, while still generally underperforming when it comes to
accuracy.
However, we should see this hypothesis in the shadow of the two general contradictions
between our results and those of Farquhar et al. [2020a], which stand as our main con-
clusions to question II.

best on 4 / 5 of the variational Bayesian deep models and the best results on expected
log predictive density - compared to the other radials. We further see that the Student’s

on the findings in Farquhar et al. [2020a].

Beyond Question I-III
The results and conclusions from this thesis are rather mixed, as we both have results that
concurred with Farquhar et al. [2020a], such as the low gradient variance and generally
good performance of students t radial. However, many surprising results are seen through
the lens of Farquhar et al. [2020a], and others directly contradict their results.
So while our results open many opportunities for further study, we must first be able to
reconcile them with the results of Farquhar et al. [2020a] before conducting further studies.
