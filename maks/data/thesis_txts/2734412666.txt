Abstract
Increasingly more automation is being integrated into manufacturing processes in the
pharmaceutical industry to alleviate the burden on workers tasked with repetitive and
strenuous duties, thereby enhancing efficiency, safety, and overall productivity. This the-
sis will investigate the applicability of deep reinforcement learning for bin packing with a
robotic arm in cases when the packages need to be put inside a bin immediately upon
arrival and arrive in an unknown order. The aim is to develop an automated solution for
the clinical trials shipping area at Novo Nordisk.
We present a solution combining a vision system for identifying incoming packages and a
Deep Reinforcement Learning decision-making algorithm that determines optimal pack-
age placement based entirely on the visual information. The primary goal of this algo-
rithm is to maximize space utilization by efficiently packing each package in a bin without
knowledge of future package arrivals. The system was designed and validated in virtual
environments with synthetic data, followed by a test with a physical setup to evaluate the
simulation-to-reality transfer.
The findings indicate that automation of this task is feasible, with performance slightly
varying according to the specific scenario. In a use case with a bin of size 40 x 40 x
32 centimeters, the agent was able to utilize on average 84.29% of the space and was
able to pack all the packages that could physically fit inside the bin. Moreover, the results
suggested that neural networks can compensate for measurement noise from the camera
sensor and thus reduce the need for a more complex physical setup.

Introduction
During the period of Industry 3.0, the automatization of many factories and production
lines was done with robots doing the repetitive or dangerous work previously done by
human workers. Followed by the rise of Industry 4.0 and the increasing computational
power available, more and more of the data became available to train various Artificial
Intelligence (AI) models to aid robots and to automate processes [1]. However, the limits
have not been met yet and the research of the applicability of AI in automation is an
ongoing work.
Even now it is often the case, that robots used in industry have predetermined rule-based
trajectories and while they provide an effective solution for many static use case scenar-
ios, they often fail in dynamic and unpredictable environments as they usually require a
full mathematical model of the problem [2]. Moreover, such an approach often requires
a technician to reprogram the robot every time there is a change in the robot’s tasks. To
be more specific, for classical robotics, it is immensely difficult to solve tasks that require
sequential decision-making. In such a setup, each action may have an impact on the fol-
lowing actions which influence the following actions, and so on. Such a scenario requires
non-trivial algorithms which makes it infeasible for classical robot systems to solve.
These reasons are why some problems such as bin packing still require human decision-
making abilities. For instance, in bin packing scenarios, packages of varying sizes often
arrive one by one over time and need to be packed right away or with only a small buffer
space available. Therefore, it is not possible to use classical optimization algorithms to
put the packages inside the bin in an efficient way as not all information is available im-
mediately. This is an NP-hard (Nondeterministic Polynomial time) problem and it requires
decisions to be made online as the packages arrive, without having full knowledge of fu-
ture package arrivals. Such behavior in robots is hard to engineer and requires a certain
degree of intelligence. This could be achieved by using the Reinforcement Learning (RL)
paradigm, which allows a robot to learn how to make decisions in the given problem by
itself [3].
However, RL comes with its own set of challenges such as determining a good strategy for
learning, constructing policy functions, defining reward signals, and a need for the agent
to interact with its environment as well as other design choices [4] [5]. Some of these
problems can be solved by introducing Deep Neural Networks (DNN) as nonlinear function
approximators [6], which on the other hand adds another dimension to the complexity.
There have been many efforts to create a solution for this problem in the context of 3
Dimensional Bin Packing Problems (3D-BPP). For instance, using the classical approach
as bin discretization into a grid shows promising results [7] [8]. Moreover, some new
approaches such as the utilization of configuration trees to reduce state space have been
explored [9]. However, even today using Deep Reinforcement Learning (DRL) to solve
3D-BPPs is a non-trivial task that deserves attention.
1.1
Problem Statement
The major challenge is that the 3D-BPP with objects incoming over time as they are be-
ing packed, is an NP-hard problem from the family of combinatorial optimization problems
and the pre-programmed robots can not handle such scenarios. That is also due to the
fact that without the full sequence of the incoming objects, the classical optimization al-
gorithms can not be applied [10, p. 8]. It is therefore preferable to have a system that is

more flexible and can potentially learn how to make decisions from experience. In Novo
Nordisk’s clinical shipping area, human workers continue to manage tasks due to the
operation’s specific requirements. Specifically, individuals are responsible for receiving
incoming packages, strategically placing them inside bins while trying to utilize space and

The purpose of this project is to explore the possibilities of automating such tasks by em-

Research questions:
• How can deep learning be leveraged for function approximation in robotic 3D bin
packing?
• Can RL be used to tackle decision-making problems in the use case given (e.g.
space optimization and other use case requirements)?
• What types of uncertainties may arise in 3D-BPPs?
1.2
Research Goals
The purpose of the project is to create a solution to automate the bin packing problem in
Novo Nordisk’s clinical trials shipping area. The goal is to enable a robotic arm to reach
its target object by detecting it and afterward estimating its position and rotation using
computer vision algorithms. Moreover, the aim is to make the robot capable of intelligent
decision-making about where the target object should be placed in the bin by applying
reinforcement learning. Finally, the results of experiments will be used to answer the
question about whether it is possible to automate the use case given, as well as whether
Nvidia’s Omniverse platform can be effectively used to conduct such experiments. There-
fore, the main research activities are:
• Set up a virtual environment by following the use case scenario so the solution can
be initially developed without a physical robot.
• Design and implement a perception system for object detection and its pose esti-
mation.
• Review, select, and implement a reinforcement learning algorithm that will enable
the robotic arm to make intelligent decisions regarding the bin packing problem.
• Analyze the results of the experiments and determine whether the solution can be
used for automation of the use case given.
• (optional) Transfer and test the solution with a physical robotic arm and evaluate its
performance.

2.1
Background
Because the problem consists of an agent (robot), state (bin and packages) and the agent
is able to interact with the environment (manipulate packages), the Markov Decision Pro-
cess (MDP) can be used to model this problem. MDP is a mathematical framework for
modeling decision-making problems where the outcome is partially random [11] and is
characterized by a tuple (S, A, P, R). Here S is a state space which is a set of possi-
ble environment states. A is the action space and is a set of possible actions the agent
can take. P represents the transition probability function that describes the probability of
entering one state from another given some action is taken. Mathematically, it is repre-
sented as P(s′|s, a), where s is the current state, a is the action taken, and s’ is the next
state. Finally, R is a reward function and it defines the immediate reward emitted by the
environment as a result of taking some action a in a state s which resulted in a transition
to a new state s’ usually noted as R(s, a, s′) [10, p. 48].
Such a way of modeling the environment and the interactions with it is the underlying
mechanism in many RL algorithms. RL algorithms are a group of Machine Learning (ML)
algorithms that learn to take actions based on the observation of the current state. This
is achieved by learning from experience gained from the interaction with its environment
while utilizing a reward signal (Figure 2.1). The ultimate goal is to learn the mapping of
states the agent can encounter to actions that are most suitable in the situation - policy. In
the standard setup, the agent interacts with the environment for N time steps until a certain
criterion or terminal state is met (Figure 2.2). A terminal state is a state from which it is
not possible to continue the sequence. The time span from the start until the termination
is called an episode. A trajectory is a sequence of so-called transitions which record at
each time step n the current state sn (sometimes referred to as a frame), action that was
taken an, the next state sn+1, reward rn+1 and an information about whether the new state
is terminal.

There are multiple ways to categorize RL algorithms and one of them is to consider the
method they use to learn decisions. There are three main methods for implementing RL
algorithms. The first one is a value-based method where the aim is to estimate the value
of different states or state-action pairs and use these estimates to make decisions. The
second is a policy-based method where the goal is to learn a policy that will define the
agent’s behavior. The last method is the actor-critic method which combines the elements
of both value-based and policy-based methods. Another way to categorize the algorithm
is based on the relationship between the policy used for collecting data and the policy that
is updated and improved during the learning process. Based on this distinction there are
two groups: on-policy and off-policy algorithms. The off-policy strategy allows the agent
to learn from data that was generated by a different policy. The policy used for learning
(target policy) is distinct from the policy used for data collection (behavior policy). In an on-
policy strategy, the agent learns and updates its policy based on the data it collects while
following its current policy. The learning and data collection policies are the same. Thus,
past experience is not used which can be less sample efficient compared to off-policy
methods which often reuse the past experience.
Finding optimal policy is not an easy task because each action that is taken can impact
all the future rewards which result in so-called delayed reward [10]. In other words, the
immediate reward can e.g. be a small negative value but the long-term effect in the future
might be a high positive reward. Therefore it can be difficult for the algorithm to learn
which action actually led to the high reward which is an issue known as the credit assign-
ment problem [12]. Moreover, the more actions and states are available, the more difficult
it becomes to keep track of the mappings between the states and suitable actions. For-
tunately, approaches using deep neural networks for approximations of policy functions
demonstrated solid capabilities [6].
2.1.1
Neural Networks in Reinforcement Learning
Deep Learning (DL) is a sub-field of ML that uses Neural Networks (NNs) for function
approximation - mapping of inputs to outputs. NNs are a class of algorithms inspired by
the structure and functioning of the brain that are designed to recognize patterns and
learn from data. The core idea is to enable a computer to build complex concepts out of
simpler concepts [13, p. 5]. The most common type of a NN is Feedforward Neural Net-
work (FNN) where the information flows only forward from the input through intermediate
computations to the output [13, p. 164]. The building block of FNNs is an artificial neuron
which is a function that performs a weighted sum of the inputs, adds a bias, and then
applies an activation function to produce an output. Weights and biases are learnable
Deep Reinforcement Learning for Robotic Arm Control within 3D Packing Problems
5
==PAGE:18==
parameters while the activation function is a ﬁxed nonlinear function and its purpose is to
allow the FNN to learn nonlinear functions. The parameters of NNs are usually learned by
using gradient-based optimizers. Optimizers are algorithms that minimize or maximize a
mathematical function by iteratively moving toward the minimum or maximum of the func-
tion. Gradient-based optimizers use the first-order derivative of a function with respect to
its parameters to update those parameters [13, p. 172]. In [14] it was shown that RL algo-
rithms can indeed benefit from DL approaches. This led to many architectures exploiting
this principle, for instance: ACKTR [15] or Proximal Policy Optimization (PPO) [16].
Deep Q-Network (DQN) [14] was the first success in merging the paradigms of deep learn-
ing and reinforcement learning. DQN uses an action-value function (Q-function) which is
a function that gives the expected reward for taking a specific action in a given state (Q-
value). The main goal is to maximize the total expected reward which is an estimate of
action-value extended by further following the optimal policy afterwards. Another standard
algorithm is ACKTR which stands for Actor Critic using the Kronecker-Factored Trust Re-
gion which is a method that incorporates a technique to approximate a natural gradient
update. Natural gradients are an alternative to the aforementioned traditional gradient
which takes into account the geometry of the parameter space.
PPO is an on-policy, actor-critic algorithm known for its increased stability, a feature
achieved by constraining the policy updates [16]. This constraint prevents the algorithm
from making overly drastic changes to the policy, contributing to smoother and more reli-
able learning. PPO follows from the TRPO (Trust Region Policy Optimization) algorithm
[17] and falls under the broader category of policy gradient methods. Policy gradient meth-
ods, such as PPO, are characterized by their direct optimization of the policy to maximize
the expected cumulative reward. The focus on directly optimizing the policy makes PPO
well-suited for scenarios with complex action spaces or stochastic environments. One
distinctive feature of PPO is its promising performance even in cooperative multi-agent
settings [18]. Despite being an on-policy method, PPO showcases the ability to handle
cooperative interactions among multiple agents efficiently. This observation challenges
the common notion that on-policy methods inherently suffer from sample inefficiency.
Another way the neural networks can improve the performance of RL algorithms is by
exploiting the capabilities of Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) to extract spatial in-
formation from the input [13, p. 326]. These networks are biologically inspired algorithms,
which are able to imitate certain functions of the mammalian primary visual cortex. For
instance, one of the primary advantages is that CNNs exhibit feature transformation in-
variance [13, p. 359]. In the context of the Bin Packing Problem (BPP), the idea of using
CNN for processing the agent’s observations was explored e.g. by Zhao et al. in [8] where
the actor-critic agent’s policy and value functions were approximated by a CNN.
2.1.2
Further consideration in RL
Despite many variations and different approaches to reinforcement learning, one concept
that all algorithms have in common is the importance of their reward functions. These
functions serve as feedback and provide information about how good or bad an action
taken was. Unfortunately, up to this date, there is no clear general solution, and engi-
neering them by trial and error seems to be still the dominant approach [19]. Ongoing
research is being conducted on this matter and there are multiple promising results e.g.
in [20] Eureka algorithm, where a combination of a large language model and evolutionary
optimization managed to achieve human-level reward design even for low-level manipula-
tion tasks. A different approach was explored in [21] where a set of expert demonstrations
was used to learn the optimal driving strategy using inverse reinforcement learning (IRL)
6

==PAGE:19==
where the agent learns from a teacher who is usually a human expert. One of the ap-
proaches to IRL is using the maximum entropy principle [22] where the goal is to maximize
the function that maps states to rewards. This idea was explored from the probabilistic
perspective in [23] and showed it can recover the reward function even from noisy data.
An additional factor to be considered is the exploration/exploitation trade-off dilemma
when the agent chooses between exploring new possibilities to discover potentially better
actions and exploiting known actions to maximize immediate rewards. There are multiple
approaches to tackling this problem. For instance, PPO introduces an additional term
to the loss function that penalizes insufficient exploration to increase the entropy [24].
However, this stochastic nature usually decreases over the duration of training. Another
approach to tackle this problem is e.g. RPO [25] which uses perturbed distribution with
the aim to sustain a higher level of randomness in the choice of actions during the training.
Another approach to influence states explored by the agent is by limiting which states
the agent can encounter. This idea was exploited in [26] where the so-called curriculum
learning was explored. Curriculum learning is a concept in which the problem is initially
simplified and once the agent reaches a certain level of ability to solve the problem, the
task is made more complex. This way the task gradually transitions from being oversim-
plified to the full problem and the point is to guide the agent during the training toward a
better final solution. This theory was e.g. applied in [27] where it was demonstrated on
the walking task of a quadruped robot.
A further consideration that needs to be made is the impact of memory on algorithms.
Some RL algorithms use experience replay which is a method where the agent remem-
bers its past experiences and can reuse them [28]. Moreover, some off-policy algorithms
can even use memories of other agents to improve their own policy [29]. Such approaches
can be more data efficient but might also result in a less stable learning process. Even
though algorithms such as PPO are usually not keeping massive amounts of memories,
the memory size can still play a vital role in the learning process [30], as it does in humans
and animals [31]. In the classical implementation, PPO uses only a small memory buffer
which is often overwritten by new experiences. This introduces a risk of over-fitting to the
most recent experience and forgetting previous but potentially useful experience. Fur-
thermore, due to the use of neural networks in PPO and algorithms alike, there is a risk of
catastrophic forgetting that is a non-negligible phenomenon [32]. Such issues often arise
when an agent has to perform multiple tasks sequentially and forgets previously learned
policies as it learns new ones. This occurs due to the nature of back-propagation when
the weights of the model are being constantly updated. Similarly to experience replay,
pseudo-rehearsal is a method that can potentially help prevent this problem by artificially
generating additional training data to supplement the real experiences [33].
2.2
Literature Review
The multi-dimensional Bin Packing Problem (BPP) has long been a significant subject
of study due to its practical applications in fields such as logistics, manufacturing, and
transportation. The complexity of the problem increases with the number of dimensions,
with the two-dimensional and three-dimensional cases being the most commonly exam-
ined scenarios. Martello and Vigo [34] were pioneers in this area, presenting the first
exact method for the 2D-BPP. Building upon this, Martello et al. [35] extended the theory
and proposed methods for tackling the more complex 3D-BPP. These studies primarily
utilized heuristic algorithms, which are designed to find good enough solutions in a rea-
sonable time frame with a known dataset of packages. Over the years, researchers have
contributed to the evolution of these methods, focusing on enhancing the stability and

optimization of the algorithms. In our work, we will utilize DRL to approximate packing
strategies that could exceed the performance of heuristic solved packing in more chal-

2.2.1
Online bin packing
The online version of the bin packing problem introduces a dynamic element where pack-
ages arrive in a sequence, unknown ahead of time. This lack of foresight makes it highly
challenging to achieve an optimal solution for packing. Characterized by its vast decision
space, the online bin packing problem is recognized as NP-hard, presenting a complexity
that makes it impossible to solve the problem optimally [36].
2.2.2
Online 3D-Bin Packing with DRL
Research in DRL for the 3D-BPP has started fairly recently and the topic has not been
studied much yet. The studies that have explored the topic have found fairly good results
which resemble space utilized by human packers [7]. It is a common approach like in [7],
[8] and [37] that auxiliary tasks are added to the baseline RL algorithms because they can
help the agent learn useful features of the environment that are beneficial for the primary
task (bin packing). By learning to solve related auxiliary tasks, the agent may discover
features that capture important underlying structures or dynamics of the environment. For
instance, in [7] the auxiliary task was to learn a feasibility map that dictated how feasible it
is to place a package in a specific location given the current state of the bin. However, the
focus of this work will be to apply only a standard RL algorithm to solve 3D-BPP without
any auxiliary tasks as they often require to be designed for a specific problem by a domain
expert.
Something that helps humans with packing is the ability to see multiple incoming pack-
ages. With this information, a strategy can be made according to the packages seen. In
[7] they call this look-ahead which means the agent can look at k>1 number of packages.
This expansion made the agent pack more efficient, especially with their addition of using
Monte Carlo Tree Search (MCTS) on the look-ahead packages optimizing the decision-
making of the agent. Our agent will only have the possibility of seeing one package at
a time. The priority of our agent was to make sure the reward function directed it to-
ward a feasible packing strategy before moving further to other additions to the algorithm.
Therefore the agent has no look-ahead.
In real-world packing scenarios, human packers frequently adjust the position of already-
placed packages to maximize space utilization. The concept of a ’buffer zone’ has been
introduced to enhance this flexibility. This buffer zone, with a variable size, acts as a
temporary holding area for packages. When the agent identifies a package that has been
inefficiently placed, it can relocate this package to the buffer zone. This relocation makes
room for the incoming packages, allowing for a more optimal arrangement. In [38], it was
found that a buffer capacity of 12 packages resulted in the most efficient use of space.
Interestingly, increasing the buffer beyond this capacity did not further improve space
utilization, indicating an optimal buffer size for maximizing packing efficiency. In our setup,
no buffer zones are introduced. This work will focus on maximizing the agent’s efficiency
without such an option as it is not always physically possible to include buffer zones in the
production environment.
Ensuring reliability in real-world bin packing applications is crucial for their adoption in
commercial environments. Previous research primarily concentrated on the decision-
making capabilities of the packing agent, often overlooking the real-world challenges of
physical constraints, as well as assuming perfect perception and manipulation by robots.
However, in [39], the emphasis shifts towards enhancing the reliability of bin packing in

practical settings. This improvement is achieved by integrating a Robot Movement Prim-
itive (RMP) into the system. The Robot Movement Primitive (RMP) is created to carefully
avoid any collisions in the working area. This is a key step in making sure these bin pack-
ing systems can be used effectively in real-world situations. By focusing on preventing
collisions with RMPs, the study makes an important advance in making automated bin
packing systems both practical and reliable. NVIDIA Isaac sim offers the possibility of us-
ing RMP-flow for robot manipulation. RMP-flow is therefore used to manipulate the robot
from picking position to placement position while avoiding collisions.
The unpredictable sequence of packages in the online 3D-BPP poses a challenge in pre-
dicting when an agent might fail. Previous solutions have shown good average perfor-
mance in packing efficiency, but focusing solely on average results overlooks the poten-
tial worst-case scenarios, which are critical to avoid in practical applications. In real-world
settings, the packing agent needs to function flawlessly every time. The study [40] in-
troduces an innovative approach: an Adjustable Robust Reinforcement Learning (AR2L)
algorithm. This algorithm fine-tunes robustness weights, striking a balance in the agent’s
policy performance, particularly in these worst-case situations. This adjustment aims to
ensure consistent and reliable performance across all scenarios, not just on average,
enhancing the system’s applicability in real-world operations. Implementing a robust rein-
forcement learning agent requires the knowledge of which parameters in the environment
need robustness. This is a deep research in itself and would be too time-consuming to
implement. Robustness enhancement is therefore not included in our project.
This work will examine the application of standard algorithms like PPO without auxiliary
tasks to avoid adding unnecessary complexity to the solution. Moreover, the focus will be
put on physically constrained environments where it is not always possible to use buffer
spaces or the look-ahead method to get information about future packages. In all studies,
the bin is discretized to simplify the action space significantly. Discretizing the bin involves
splitting the packing area (bottom of the bin) in a grid. With this setup, the precision of the
system is lowered but precision is not the first priority in packing a bin. For our project,
the bin is discretized as well.

Bin Packing Behavior
The bin packing process is controlled by a state machine enabling the robot to execute
four primary actions:
• PickItem(): The robot moves to an package on the conveyor to pick it up.
• PlaceItem(): The robot places the picked package in the designated packing area.
• make_go_home(): The robot returns to a safe home position.
• DoNothing(): The robot waits for the next actionable package.
The Cortex World continuously scans for packages ready for pickup. Once a package is
within reach, it’s marked for action, and the robot aligns its end effector for optimal grasp
and placement. The alignment is essential to get the orientation of the package correct
when placing it in the bin. Here’s a step-by-step breakdown of the process:
1. Package Pose Transformation: The item’s pose in the world is retrieved, providing
its position (item_p) and orientation (item_R) as a transformation matrix (item_T).
2. Orientation Adjustment: The rotation matrix (item_R) is unpacked to obtain the
item’s axis orientations (item_ax, item_ay, item_az). These axes represent the
item’s orientation in 3D space relative to the world frame.
3. Target Axis Calculation: The target orientation for the end effector is decided by
matching the rotation of the package while making sure the end effector is perpen-
dicular to the package’s flat side. To ensure the end effector points downwards in
the world, the end effector x-axis rotation in the world has to be pointing in the neg-
ative z-direction of the world xtarget = −zworld. The y-axis orientation of the end
effector is set to the x-axis orientation of the package ytarget = xitem. And the z-axis
orientation can then simply be calculated by taking the cross-product of the xtarget
and ytarget, ztarget = xtarget × ytarget. A visualization can be seen in Figure 3.6
4. Adjusting for Package Pickup: A margin is introduced to position the end effector
slightly above the package before descending to pick it up, to ensure the end effector
does not push through the package.
5. Final Target Pose: The target pose (target_T) for the end effector is assembled
from the calculated orientation and adjusted position, readying the robot arm for
package pickup.
This process ensures that the robot arm approaches and aligns with the package correctly
for successful pickup, taking into account the item’s orientation and position within the
simulation environment.

taking the cross product.

long sides perpendicular to the bin’s sides), we adjust these vectors using a rotation
operation. Done by rotating the target orientation with rotation matrix (R_z_90). This
rotation matrix rotates the orientation 90 degrees in the z-axis.
3. Creating the Orientation Matrix: After defining the vectors, we compile them into
a rotation matrix (target_R).
4. Final Placement Pose: The last step combines the item’s placement location with
the end effector’s rotation matrix. This gives the robot a complete set of instructions
on where and how to place the item, including any necessary rotation.

Computer Vision Pipeline
As the position and orientation of the packages to be packed is needed for the completion
of the task, an algorithm was constructed for this purpose. This algorithm estimates the
width, length and height of the packages and consists of the following pipeline steps:
1. Image acquisition - in the first step, a color image is captured by the RGB-D camera
placed above the conveyor belt.
2. Cropping - all information in the image is not necessary, therefore cropping is done
to focus on the area where the package can be detected.
3. RGB channel extraction - in the case of our setup the packages are red and the
background is white. In this case, the blue channel provides the highest contrast for
red objects against light backgrounds.
4. Grayscaling - the image is then converted to grayscale for preparation of segment-
ing
5. Segmentation - background is segmented out by filtering out high values (bright
background pixels) and keep low values (dark package pixels). With this, we cre-
ate a binary image with ones resembling the package and zeros resembling the
background.
6. Package detection - next the package is detected in the segmented image by first
finding the contours in the blurred image and then by counting the number of points
that approximate the contour. If the contour is of rectangular shape (the shape can
be recovered from 4 points), it is considered a rectangle.
7. Pose and size estimation - The angle of rotation θ is calculated from the central
moments, using the OpenCV function moments(), which computes moments of the
binary image that can be used to find the centroid and the orientation of the package.

4.1.1
Package Classification
Detecting the package is only the first step; accurately determining its pose and size,
especially its height, is crucial for a successful packing strategy. However, using the
RealSense camera in the setup presents a challenge for measuring package height ac-
curately. At a mounting distance of 89cm, the camera’s depth measurement has an error
margin of ± 2%, translating to an error of up to ± 1.78cm. This level of inaccuracy is
problematic since the packages used differ in height by as little as 1cm, making it difficult
to distinguish between them based solely on height measurements. Since we know the
dimensions of all package types, a better approach is to classify the packages based on
size characteristics other than the height measurement from the camera. Using classifi-
cation not only helps in identifying the correct package height but reduces measurement
noise for the package’s other dimensions as well.
One issue with relying on camera measurements is that they can vary depending on
the package’s position relative to the camera. For instance, when a package is directly
under the camera, as shown in figure 4.3a, the top of the package is visible and can
be easily separated from the background. However, if the package is not centered and
is closer to the edge of the camera’s view, as in figure 4.3b, the side of the package
becomes visible. This can mislead the measurement algorithm into overestimating the
package’s size due to the side’s visibility. This problem is illustrated in figure 4.4, where
the measurement values for the package’s surface area (red pixels) of different package
types, such as type 5 and 6, overlap, making it hard to tell them apart based on area
alone. Introducing additional features for classification, as shown in figure 4.5, helps in
differentiating between package types more effectively. Therefore, a neural network was
implemented for the task of classification. Neural networks are well-suited for this task
because they can handle multiple input dimensions, adapt to varying data, and learn to
recognize the unique measurement deviations associated with each type of package.

Hand-eye calibration is a crucial process in robotics and computer vision, aiming to de-
termine the spatial relationship between the robot’s end effector and a camera. This cali-
bration is essential when the world position of the camera is unknown and it is necessary
to estimate the pose of objects in relation to the robot base.
28
Deep Reinforcement Learning for Robotic Arm Control within 3D Packing Problems
==PAGE:41==
The objective of hand-eye calibration is to find a rigid transformation that aligns one set of
3D points (set A) with another (set B). This transformation includes rotation and translation
but excludes scaling. Set A contains n number of 3D positions of the end effector relative
to the UR5e robot’s base frame. Correspondingly, each point in set A is paired with a
point in set B, observed by the camera.
The point sets are generated by moving the end effector, equipped with a visible tip, within
the camera’s field of view. Each point represents a unique end effector pose. The end
effector’s pose, as recorded by the UR5e, populates set A. The corresponding points
in the camera’s view are obtained using the ‘realsense-viewer‘ software. This software
helps identify the pixel location of the end-effector’s tip in the color image, along with its
associated depth in the depth image. Utilizing the intrinsic parameters of the color camera,
the estimated position of the end effector relative to the camera pose is computed and
inserted into set B. The calibration points are detailed in Appendix Section A.1.
The process of finding a rigid transformation involves the following steps:
1. Centralizing Points: The process starts by computing the centroids of the two 3D
point clouds. By subtracting these centroids, the point sets are translated to the
origin, facilitating the discovery of a rotation around the origin.
2. Correlation Matrix (H): The matrix H, an approximation of the covariance matrix
of the two sets, is computed by multiplying the centralized point cloud Am with the
transpose of the centralized matrix Bm (H = AmBT
m).
3. Singular Value Decomposition (SVD): The SVD of H decomposes the matrix into
three matrices (H = USV T ), where U and V are orthogonal, and S is a diagonal
matrix of singular values. This decomposition is crucial for identifying the optimal
rotation.
4. Finding Rotation: The rotation matrix R is derived from the SVD results (R =
V U T ). This matrix represents the best rotation aligning Am with Bm.
5. Checking for Reflection: If the determinant of R is negative, indicating a reflection,
this is not a valid rigid transformation for our purposes. Correct this by flipping the
sign of the last row of V before recalculating R.
6. Computing Translation: The translation vector t is calculated next.
Since the
points were initially centralized, t is the difference between the rotated centroid of A
and the centroid of B (t = −R · centroidA + centroidB).
7. Assembling the Transformation Matrix: Finally, a 4x4 transformation matrix is
constructed, incorporating both the rotation and translation. This matrix can be ap-
plied to any set of points to achieve the calculated transformation.

Note on RF6
The main idea behind the RF6 is that it terminates the episode every time an undesired
behavior occurs (e.g. placing a package outside the bin, inside a full bin, or at an unstable
position). The consequence is that it limits the state space exploration only to preferable
states and states that should be physically feasible. For instance, it will not explore spaces
where the current package will be placed with a gap between packages placed before as
it would lead to a waste of space.
Other Approaches
Beyond the various reward functions, two different approaches were tested. First, RF6
was trained with dropout in its hidden layers with a rate of 20% to test whether the model
was overfitting given the agent’s exposure to a limited subset of the state space. The
second approach was adopted from OpenAI paper [49] where a low amount of epochs
and high size of memory with a larger batch size was used. These parameters were used

Proximal Policy Optimization
PPO was chosen as the main tool for intelligent decision making by the robotic arm in the
3D-BPP as it is still considered a state-of-the-art algorithm with valuable traits such as im-
proved training stability and ease of implementation. The specifics of the implementation
and the PPO’s relation to Gymnasium is displayed in figure 4.14 below.
Figure 4.14: Implementation of the PPO agent and its interaction with the environment.
As can be seen, the observations of the environment’s state are retrieved from the Gym-
nasium environment and collected inside the agent’s memory. The memory consists of
individual transitions, recording states s, actions a, rewards r, and values v. Based on
the observation, the actor outputs a policy distribution which is a categorical probabil-
ity distribution over the actions. Categorical distribution is used due to the discretization
of the bin’s action space. Next, an action is sampled and it is used to interact with the
environment, which starts a new cycle of interaction. When the memory is full, a mini-
batch sample is taken and fed to the critic network which outputs an estimated value in
response. The difference between the actual reward observed and the estimated value
is calculated and used to compute the advantage of the action which is a measure of how
much better the action is compared to others. The implementation used computes Gen-
eralized Advantage Estimation AGAE(λ) which balances the trade-off between bias and
variance and then this value is used to update the critic network.

6
Discussion
This chapter will discuss the results and observations obtained during the experiments
related to both vision and packing system. Moreover, it will examine the diverse range of
possible applications of the system that has been developed, shedding light on how it can
be utilized across various industries and sectors. It will explain what the limitations are by
providing an understanding of the system’s boundaries. Finally, it will propose ideas for
future research and debate the future development that could extend the current system’s
capabilities.
6.1
Vision System
The results from the experiments showed that including a simple neural network for pack-
age classification can be a solution to the limited capabilities of physical sensors. Thus,
employing deep learning for the interpretation of visual information has the potential to
improve the overall performance of a packing agent. During the runs of experiments,
the algorithm misclassified only a single package which was a consequence of expos-
ing the network to previously unseen data. This happened because the data collected
for the training of the classifier were focused on a package that is placed in the central
area of the camera’s field of view. Therefore, if that package was placed at the edge of
the camera’s view during the test, the package could become misclassified. However,
this problem could be further improved by introducing a higher variety in the training data
set. Similarly, the problem was when the camera could see one or more of the sides of
a package. As the sides have the same color as the top, the camera has a problem dis-
tinguishing between these two areas. As a result, the algorithm’s estimate of a centroid
was sometimes misaligned from the actual centroid. This led to a few collisions when the
robotic arm lifted a package by an incorrectly estimated center and then tried to place it
next to other packages. This could be improved in the future by not using the NN only for
classification but for the estimation of centroids as well.
6.2
Bin Packing System
The experiments demonstrated solid capabilities of the agent to pack bins. The agent
was able to learn to rotate packages in suitable situations and to demonstrate a strategy
to utilize space despite the fact that the packages were incoming in random order. The
agent was able to both stack items on top of each other and use the space in lower layers if
the incoming package could fit. The experiments revealed that the agent is consistent with
its performance which means that the policy found by the agent can deal with randomness
inherent to the problem. It was also found that using only PPO without any auxiliary tasks
is able to pack a bin with a base of size 40cm x 40cm up to the height of 32 centimeters in
a manner that resembles a human packing style. When increasing the bin’s height over
this point, the complexity of the task increases enough for the agent to start suffering from
catastrophic forgetting. Interestingly the agent that was trained with curriculum learning
was able to find a better local optimum compared to the regular training approach even for
the agent trained on a bin with a height of 40cm. This can be explained by regular training
converging to sub-optimal solution because of the complexity of the state space and also
because the PPO algorithm does not guarantee that it will converge to the global optimum.
The solution was to use curriculum learning to push the policy in the right direction from
the local optimum toward the global optimum which resulted in a better policy. Despite the
fact that the agent suffered from catastrophic forgetting from a certain height of the bin,
58
Deep Reinforcement Learning for Robotic Arm Control within 3D Packing Problems
==PAGE:71==
the curriculum learning agent still managed to find a better policy compared to the regular
agent, because it packed almost the same number of packages but in a smaller bin. For
a larger bin - with a height above 32cm, some way of handling the catastrophic forgetting
is required. Further improvements can be achieved in the future by exposing the agent
to simpler environments during the entire training to prevent catastrophic forgetting or by
keeping the older experience from the simple tasks and then sample from them during
the training of more complex tasks.
It is worth noting that different architectures heavily impacted the performance of the
model. For instance, introducing a CNN made it easier for the agent to understand spa-
tial relationships inside the bin which allowed for more sophisticated policy. Moreover, it
seems that different reward functions led to different local optimums, thus finding different
packing strategies. This behavior is expected but it implies that properly engineering the
reward function remains one of the most important tasks in RL. The model also requires a
certain degree of tuning of a wide range of parameters and the starting point for choosing
parameters for the final model was obtained from a parameter sweep. The parameter
sweep gave a good direction and was close to optimal parameters but since the architec-
ture slightly changed and due to the time and resource restrictions, there is still a need
for a more thorough parameters sweep to ensure the model has the optimal parameters.
However, the architecture of the model and the parameters remain problem specific and
a general more universal solution has yet to be found.
The agent showed promising results even in a physical setup where it was able to pack
a bin with a height of 12 centimeters. Despite the fact that the agent was trained without
any simulation of physics the agent still managed to converge to a solution that places
packages at physically stable positions most of the time. An unexpected issue that was
encountered during the physical tests was when a package was defected. If the surface
of the package was bent inwards, the vacuum gripper had difficulties lifting the package
from the pick-up area. This could be fixed e.g. by detecting anomalies and discarding
damaged packages.
6.3
Possible Applications
This project was developed in collaboration with Novo Nordisk, specifically as a proof
of concept for bin packing in the clinical trials shipping area. However, the solution is
not restricted to clinical trials and can easily generalize to other use cases where the
goal is to utilize space inside a bin or on a pallet and the packages arrive in unknown
order with varying dimensions. This could be used e.g. in warehouses and logistics
where objects need to be loaded on pallets and trucks, food industry where packages with
food/beverages need to be packed before shipping, or in retail where effective packing
can reduce shipping costs. Furthermore, this solution was developed with a focus on only
the immediate package that needs to be packed, meaning that it can be implemented in
physical setups where it is not possible to e.g. have a conveyor belt with an option to get
information about the next incoming packages. This could for example be in case when
the packages arrive all at once in no particular order and the agent needs to start packing
them in random order.
6.4
Limitations
The vision system was not the main scope of this project and therefore the precision of
detection and pose estimation can still be further improved. For instance, the variation of
natural lighting was not considered and the vision system had to be manually calibrated
before the run. Also, the RealSense RGB-D camera that was used has depth estimation
Deep Reinforcement Learning for Robotic Arm Control within 3D Packing Problems
59
==PAGE:72==
error = ± 2% which would impact the packing if NN classifier was not introduced. With
more precise sensor a higher accuracy could be reached. Moreover, in the physical setup,
there are many extra considerations like the material the packages are made of which can
impact packing. For example, if the surface of packages is slippery it leads to unexpected
movements e.g. slight rotation upon picking the item with a vacuum gripper or slipping
when placed on top of each other. The system was not developed for such use case
scenarios and assumes that once the orientation of the package is detected, it will not
change as well as it assumes that once a package is placed to the target position it will
never move. Finally, the agent was trained in an environment that does not consider
physical forces like gravity. Therefore, the agent is capable of deciding where it is possible
to place a package only from the spatial viewpoint. This could be further improved by
including physical forces during the training.
6.5
Future Work
As discussed, solving the catastrophic forgetting could potentially allow the agent to solve
even more complex tasks. Thus, the most interesting future work would be the elimination
of this problem by applying some of the recent solutions like pseudo-rehearsal [33]. This
could potentially not only increase the agent’s performance but make it more robust and
general as well. Another natural extension would be introducing more actions to manipu-
late the packages, e.g. store temporary packages at some buffer, repack already packed
packages, or introduce a look ahead where the agent would get information about not
only the current package but n future packages that are arriving on a conveyor belt.
Currently, the reward function is hand-engineered and one of the possible future improve-
ments could be to let the agent learn the suitable reward function on its own. That way the
solution could become more general and the need for fine tuning of the reward function
would be eliminated. This could be achieved for example by introducing inverse rein-
forcement learning to extract the reward function from observations of a human worker
and afterwards training the agent to learn policy using this reward function. Such an agent
would not only learn how to choose a good action but it would learn what a good action
is as well.
Another way to continue the work would be to introduce a second degree of rotation. As
mentioned, currently the agent can rotate the packages only around the package’s z-axis
meaning that the package can be turned 90 degrees to the right / left on the pick-up table.
Adding the ability to rotate packages around the x / y axis could potentially further improve
the performance and result in a more human-like packing approach.
A combination of these ideas could contribute to the development of an efficient and more
transparent AI solution that could aid human workers in handling logistic tasks.

7
Conclusion
In this thesis, a PPO DRL model has been developed to optimize space utilization within
the context of Online 3D-BPP. This model was first validated within a simulated environ-
ment, providing not only a visual demonstration of its decision-making capabilities but a
platform for in-depth analysis of its strategies as well. The transition from a simulated to a
physical system allowed for real-world testing, critically assessing the model’s robustness
against the challenges presented by sensor noise and other practical uncertainties.
Exploring various reward functions and training methodologies highlighted the complex-
ities of solving Online 3D BPP. Notably, the reward function RF5 was found to be most
effective, achieving a mean space utilization of 79.63% within a 40 cm high bin.
Curriculum learning showed promising results in enhancing the model’s performance.
Moreover, it provided insights into the underlying issue of regular training converging
to suboptimal solutions. This strategy effectively pushed the policies toward the global
optimum and achieved superior results compared to the regular approach. In addition,
it showed that from a certain level of problem complexity, a problem with catastrophic
forgetting arises which needs to be further researched.
The thesis also evaluated a neural network for package classification in a physical setup.
Achieving an accuracy of 99.82%, this classifier showcased the feasibility of using low-
cost cameras for reliable package classification in production environments, highlighting
the potential for integrating machine learning techniques into operational workflows.
The system’s performance evaluation was conducted through three experiments, each
assessing different aspects of the agent’s functionality, the system’s performance in sim-
ulation and in a physical environment.
The first experiment dived into the model’s ability to comprehend rotation and predict
future package arrivals, focusing on a single layer of packing within the bin. The RF5
agent showcased a wide range of space utilization outcomes, from 47.08% to 92.86%,
across 80,000 samples. Such variability underscored the inherent uncertainty associated
with the order of incoming packages. Despite the fluctuations, we saw the model’s ability
to understand rotation and strategic foresight of package arrivals.
By extending the bin’s height to 20cm in the second experiment, we observed a notable
improvement in the model’s performance consistency. The confidence interval tightened
significantly, reflecting a mean space utilization that surpassed previous results from the
first experiment. This enhancement was attributed to the model’s capability to execute its
packing strategy over a larger volume, across a broader range of options, thus providing
more flexibility. Such findings suggest that increasing the bin’s dimensions could facili-
tate more effective space utilization strategies by the DRL agent (e.g. placing the same
package types on top of each other).
The final experiment focused on the physical implementation of the system, examining
its performance with a bin height of 12cm. This phase tested the DRL agent’s packing
efficiency, the accuracy of the package classification, the precision of the computer vision
system in estimating package centroids, and the effectiveness of the vacuum gripper.
Across five trials, the DRL agent consistently achieved high packing rates, with a slight
variance in the number of packages packed. The package classifier performed exception-
ally well, with minimal misclassifications. However, the pose estimation algorithm faced
Deep Reinforcement Learning for Robotic Arm Control within 3D Packing Problems
61
==PAGE:74==
challenges in accurately determining package centroids, primarily due to not accounting
for detection of package sides. Despite this, the vacuum gripper exhibited remarkable
reliability, with only a single failure noted.
In summary, the experiments conducted affirm the potential of DRL models in automating
and optimizing bin packing processes. By transitioning from simulation to physical setup,
this research contributes valuable insights into automated bin packing solutions, paving
the way for future advancements in the field.
