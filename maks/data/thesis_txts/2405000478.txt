Testing of a multicomponent vaccine against MRSA in a Sus scrofa
domesticus skin model
by Iman Chehade
Methicillin-resistant Staphylococcus aureus (MRSA) has been a worldwide health con-
cern for over a decade which has seen recent and rapid growth in prevalancy. The major
shift in the epidemiology of MRSA became a point of concern when community-associated
methicillin-resistant Staphylococcus aureus (CA-MRSA) was recognized as a frequent cause
of skin and soft tissue infections (SSTIs). Given the high incidence of life-threatening,
antimicrobial-resistant infections caused by MRSA, researchers have a large incentive to
generate preventative measures such as vaccines. Unfortunately, a lack of understanding
about the immunologic interplay between S.aureus and the host has hampered eﬀorts to
develop a vaccine.
To help provide an avenue to study this interplay, this study has established a potential
skin model for human S. aureus skin infection in Sus scrofa domesticus by using MRSA
USA300 and has identiﬁed four potential antigen candidates which should be further
investigated in the development of a multicomponent vaccine against MRSA infection.

1 Introduction
Staphylococcus aureus (S. aureus) is recognized as an important human pathogen in a
range of infections [1]. It is both a commensal bacterium and a well recognized pathogen
which is known to be carried intermittently in 60 % of the population [2]. Carriage of S.
aureus has been displayed as an important key player the epidemiology and pathogenesis
of infections caused by the microorganism [3].
The global epidemic of S. aureus has been a global problem since Penicillin resistance
occurred in the 1900’s followed by fast occurring Methicillin resistance [2].
Methicillin-resistant Staphylococcus aureus (MRSA) has been a worldwide health con-
cern [1]. However the epidemiology of MRSA is now changing and community-associated
methicillin-resistant Staphylococcus aureus (CA-MRSA) has become increasingly more im-
portant since infections caused by MRSA no longer are conﬁned to hospital setting only
[4]. CA-MRSA has been acknowledged as a predominant cause of skin and soft tissue
infections (SSTIs), particularly abscesses, impetigo and cellulitis [5]. A bacterial abscesses
can emerge in all layers of the skin. It is a localized accumulation of pus which appears
as a result of inﬂammatory reaction by the host against invasive microorganisms such
as S.aureus [2]. One predominant cause of abscess progression caused by CA-MRSA, is
the bacterial isolate CA-MRSA strain USA300 [2; 5]. Even though abscesses can be self
limiting, formation of deep and large abscesses in diﬀerent compartments of the body can
be life threatening [4].
The increasing number of CA-MRSA SSTI incidences [2] has become alarming, and a call
for immediate attention.
Given its high incidence of causing life-threatening, antimicrobial-resistant infections, sev-
eral studies have prompted the necessity to introduce non-antimicrobial actions such as
vaccines [6]. Currently vaccines aiming to protect against S.aureus infections have failed
in human trials [7]. In order to develop a vaccine the knowledge of the correlates of pro-
tective immunity against S.aureus infection is crucial [8]. Unfortunately little is known
about the factors that contribute to the protective immunity against S.aureus between
the host and the invasive S.aureus agent [7; 8].
In addition, the absence of representative preclinical models of skin infections such as
abscesses presents an obstacle to design a rational vaccine against S.aureus [8]. Vaccine
approaces so far have been able to display that single antigen vaccine approaches are not
suﬃcient and that eﬀorts of developing multicomponent vaccine would potentially become
more successful in eradicating S.aureus [7].

Chapter 1. Introduction
The outcome of this thesis is to establish a Sus scrofa domesticus skin model for MRSA
infections in humans using the CA-MRSA strain USA300. The study involves a multi-
component subunit vaccine which contains eight diﬀerent proteins that have previously
demonstrated protection in a lethal S.aureus sepsis mouse model. In this study we aimed
to elucidate the applicability of the vaccine in a potential Sus scrofa domesticus skin
model caused by S.aureus MRSA USA300. Moreover, the vaccine will also be tested for
its eﬃcacy in elimination of carrier status of S.aureus.

2 Literature Review
2.1
Staphylococcus Aureus
Staphylococcus aureus is a major human opportunistic pathogen [5]. It is a gram-positive
bacteria capable of causing a wide range of infections, and it has plagued man for centuries
[9]. Staphylococcus was ﬁrst described as Staphylococcus by the surgeon Sir Alexander
Ogston in 1880 in Scotland. He observed grape-like clusters of bacteria in stained pus
obtained from abscesses in patients, and classiﬁed them according to the greek expression
staphyl´e (grape) [9; 10]. A few years later, the German physician, Friedrich J. Rosenbach,
described and named the organism Staphylococcus aureus. He grew and isolated the bac-
teria from abscesses and proposed the nomenclature Staphylococcus aureus, (S.aureus),
from the Latin expression “aureus” meaning golden. The observation was based on the
appearance of the colonies as pigmented yellow-orange or “gold” [10].
S.aureus is a widespread commensal bacterium but also a commonly isolated human
pathogen [5]. S.aureus, is found as part of the normal ﬂora, and approximately 50-60
% the human population carry the organism [2]. Almost 20% carry the organism per-
manently, whereas 30-50% carry it intermittently [11]. It is most frequently carried in
the nose but can also be found on the skin and mucosae in general [5; 12].
While it
has been found as an predominant cause of skin and soft tissue infections (SSTIs), it
has also been a leading cause of, bacteremia, pneumonia and endocarditis [5; 13]. It is
also a predominant cause of device-related infections and nosocomial infections (hospital-
acquired infection) most frequently seen in cases such as post surgical wound infections
and intravenous catheter-associated infections [5; 8].
The past two decades, we have witnessed a great increase in invasive infections caused by
S. aureus, in particularly skin and soft tissue infections. A surveillance study conducted
in the USA demonstrated nearly 11.6 million cases of skin infections caused by S.aureus
and the mortality rate in severe cases, remain to increase despite the availability of highly
eﬀective antibiotics [8; 14; 1]. Given the great number of skin infections per year, which
is currently still increasing, it is now seen as major concern for the public health. Recent
studies have demonstrated that S. aureus is not alone the only leading cause of skin
and soft infections. With the emergence of antibiotic resistant S. aureus strains, such as
Methicillin-resistant S. aureus (MRSA), the eﬀectivity of antibiotics is decreasing and the
diﬃculty in treating these infections is increasing. In Addition, the study demonstrated
that S. aureus was the predominant cause of bacterial skin and soft tissue infections,
accounting for about 76 %, where MRSA was responsible for 78 % of these infections [1].

Chapter 2. Literature Review
2.1.1
MRSA
S.aureus’ successful attempt of being one of the most known infectious agents is based
on its versatility. It is known to be extremely adaptable, especially in the antibiotic era
[9]. The remarkable epidemiological transition has evolved since the organism acquired
its ﬁrst resistance mechanism against penicillin, shortly after the drug was introduced
clinically. In order to overcome the resistance to penicillin, Methicillin was introduced
in 1959 unfortunately only two years after Methicillin was introduced hospitals began
to report resistance at a rapid and increasing rate [9; 7]. Methicillin-resistant S.aureus
(MRSA) has been a major health concern ever since as it is nearly resistant to all ß-
lactam antibiotics and a multiple classes of non ß-lactam antibiotics, such as vancomycin
[2; 15]. MRSA infections, were usually limited to nosocomial infections and were termed
healthcare-associated MRSA, (HA-MRSA), as the infected patients would originate from
the healthcare environment, hospitals and individuals in contact with these facilities [1; 15]
. However, in the mid-1990s, a major shift in the epidemiology of MRSA became a point
of concern, when a major increase of the infectious burden became apparent [13; 15]. The
increase was associated with community-associated MRSA, (CA-MRSA). These strains
were most frequently associated with SSTIs, and were most likely seen in individuals who
were healthy and had no previous exposure to the health care environment [13; 16].
CA-MRSA is a major concern today since it is fast-spreading and responsible for progres-
sive and fatal diseases in seemingly healthy individuals [7]. A study conducted in the US
emergency departments estimated that the number of registered SSTIs nearly doubled af-
ter CA-MRSA was introduced [13]. USA300 is one of the predominant CA-MRSA isolate,
which contributed in the increase of SSTIs in USA as well as creating a public health crisis
[4]. It was ﬁrst recognized in the USA, and later found in countries such as Denmark,
Germany and Australia [16]. USA300 is commonly found as a cause of skin and soft tissue
infections. accounting for an increase in registered SSTIs from 4 to 42 % between 2000
and 2005 [15; 16; 13].
In addition, evidence have also suggested that animals may occur as an important reser-
voir for MRSA, especially CA-MRSA [13]. In the beginning of 2000, the emergence of
individuals being infected with MRSA carried by animals rapidly increased. These kinds
of MRSA were termed livestock-associated MRSA (LA-MRSA) [17]. The majority of cases
were associated with MRSA strain CC398, which is known to colonize animals, especially
conventional pig farms. In Denmark alone, the number of individuals infected by the pig
MRSA CC398, increased from 14 cases in 2007 to 1249 in 2016 [17; 18].
In general MRSA infections represent a major public health concern with its increasing
frequency and widespread emergence.
MRSA strains are becoming more virulent and
multi-drug resistant, leading to complicated treatments and need for non-antimicrobial
actions [1; 6].
2.2
Skin and soft tissue infections
Skin and soft tissue infections (SSTIs) involve inﬂammatory microbial invasions of the
diﬀerent layers of the skin. It aﬀects the epidermis, dermis and subcutaneous tissues,
leading to relatively superﬁcial infections and highly invasive and threatening infections
==PAGE:19==
2.2. Skin and soft tissue infections
5
[19; 20]. SSTIs are quite frequent, they can occur as a single incidence or as intermittent
incidences. They can occur in seemingly healthy individuals hereby children, young adults
and middle-aged adults, however certain conditions may also predispose to SSTIs, such
as immune suppression, trauma (from previous surgeries), pro-longed hospitalizations,
diabetes mellitus and pre-existing skin conditions [19; 21].
SSTIs are divided into two types, uncomplicated and complicated SSTIs. Bacterial SSTIs,
which are clinically presented as mild and self-limiting, are recognized as uncomplicated
SSTIs. The most common uncomplicated SSTIs are infections such as, impetigo, cellulitis,
abscesses and ecthyma [2; 20]. Complicated SSTIs on the other hand characterize a diﬀer-
ent clinical presentation involving deep tissues, indicating a more severe and progressive
infection such as deep abscesses and necrotizing fasciitis [2; 21].
A variety of microorganisms contribute to the colonization of the skin, however S.aureus
strains including resistant strains, have been known as the key contributor to SSTIs for
over a decade [5]. S.aureus has been isolated in impetigo, which is a bacterial skin infection
aﬀecting children. It is a simple infection characterized by a breach of the inter-epidermal
vesicles, which lead to crusted lesions without presenting systemic symptoms [5; 21]. In
addition S.aureus has also been isolated in necrotizing fasciitis and surgical site infections
(SSIs).
Necrotizing fasciitis is a rapidly progressive soft tissue infection, aﬀecting the
fascia and leading to necrosis of subcutaneous tissue. Necrotizing fasciitis may appear as
a consequence of surgical procedures [20]. SSIs, can both be superﬁcial, and severe. Severe
outcomes of SSIs, usually involve infections of tissues deeper than dermis such as, organs,
or implanted material after surgery [21]. It has been indicated that the occurrence of SSIs
is almost 2-3 % after all surgeries, and that S.aureus accounts for 30 % of them [5].
While being the manifesting pathogen in the above mentioned skin infections, the hallmark
of SSTI infections caused by S.aureus is actually abscesses and cellulitis [5; 4]. A large
study has demonstrated that over 60 % of all SSTIs due to S.aureus has been clinically
presented abscesses and cellulitis as the most common SSTIs, with abscesses being the
most frequent ones [2; 4].
2.2.1
Pathogenesis of Staphylococcus aureus Skin Abscesses
An abscess is a localized accumulation of pus which appears as a result of an inﬂammatory
reaction by the host against invasive microorganisms. Abscesses can appear anywhere in
the body, and the most frequent ones are skin abscesses, anal abscesses and throat abscesses
[5; 21]. This section will however only deal with the bacterial skin abscesses caused by
S.aureus in general.
A bacterial skin abscess can emerge in all layers of the skin, and is characterized by the
appearance of erythema (redness) and swelling with drainage of purulent material. Mi-
croscopically, the matured abscess is composed of necrotic polymorphonuclear leukocytes
(PMNs), primarily neutrophils, extracellular DNA, live bacteria and viable PMNs. In
addition, the matured abscess is surrounded by a ﬁbrous capsule [2; 1].
Skin abscess may develop as a result of punctured skin, inﬂamed hair follicles, burn wounds
or even after surgical procedures [1]. A skin abscess can be superﬁcial and resolve on its
own without any treatment [4]. However individuals with certain factors such as immuno-
suppression, severe burns and diabetes are at high risk of developing severe abscesses

which are life-threatening and in need for certain treatments such as antibiotics, incision
and drainage, or even surgical procedures for deeper abscesses [2; 21]. In addition, if the
bacteria is able to escape the abscess itself, it can enter the bloodstream and cause further
severe damages if not treated [21].
As CA-MRSA strains have been described as a major cause of SSTIs and in particularly
abscesses, it is important to acknowledge the increasing threat of antibiotic resistance
and the lack of eﬃcient treatment towards complicated SSTIs including severe and deep
abscesses [4].
In order to help combat S.aureus related complicated SSTIs with treatments such as
vaccines, it is essential to understand the immune response which is activated once S.aureus
encounters and invade the host. The upcoming subsections will thus deal with the key
factors of the innate immune responses against S.aureus, including the recognition of
S.aureus on the skin by the host, neutrophil recruitment to the site of infection, and as
well highlight the signiﬁcance of antimicrobial peptides and proinﬂammatory cytokines in
combating S.aureus upon encountering the host defense.
The barrier of the skin
The skin is the largest organ in the body, which is well-known to be the primary barrier
between the host and the environment [20]. It comprises a physical and chemical barrier,
which protect the body from invasive microorganisms such as S.aureus by the ﬁrst-line
defense system also known as cutaneous immunity [22]. The skin consists of a complex
network with a variety of cell types and several layers within the skin, which all together
maintain processes such as disease prevention and inﬂammation. Thus each layer has a
particular and important role in the course of cutaneous immunity [1; 23]. The stratum
corneum layer (SC) in the epidermis, also known as the outermost layer of the skin, is
the most important protection barrier. It is a waterproof lipid layer which is comprised of
dead diﬀerentiated keratinocytes [1; 22], see ﬁgure 2.1. During the course of diﬀerentia-
tion, which arise in stratum basale (bottom layer of epidermis), the keratinocytes migrate
upwards in the epidermis [24], producing compounds and proteins along the way leading to
a strong physical and chemical barrier. They are known to produce antimicrobial peptides
(AMPs) along the way, which can be found in SC [22].

The cutaneous immune response against S.aureus
Beside the structural character keratinocytes provide the outer layer of the skin, they
also contribute as an important factor in the early cutaneous immune responses against
S.aureus [22]. Recent ﬁndings, have demonstrated that keratinocytes are capable of pro-
ducing important AMPs, including β-defensins and cathelicidin (LL-37), which have been
observed to exhibit a direct bacteriostatic response against S.aureus [1; 23].
In addi-
tion, keratinocytes express pattern recognition receptors (PRRs), which recognize S.aureus
pathogen-associated molecular patterns (PAMPs) [26]. They express surface PRRs, Toll-
like receptors (TLRs), TLR 1, 2 and 6 and cytoplasmic PRRs, nucleotide-binding oligomer-
ization domain, NOD-like receptors (NLRs), in particular nucleotide-binding oligomeriza-
tion domain containing 2, NOD2 [1].
S.aureus speciﬁc PAMPs such as peptidoglycan (composing the cell wall of S.aureus),
lipopeptides (present on the inner membrane of S.aureus) and lipoteichoic acid (LTA) are
recognized by surface keratinocyte PRRs, whereas NOD2 recognizes muramyl dipeptide,

The result of
PRR recognition is essential for a variety of factors upon S.aureus invasion. Aside from
producing AMPs, keratinocyte also promote the production of cytokines and chemokines
through PRR activation and signaling pathways, which in return promote the recruitment
of neutrophils to the site of infection [23].
Neutrophils are arguably the essential immune mechanism that is required against skin
infections due to bacterial invasion such as S.aureus [2]. A great number of studies have
been able to demonstrate the importance of neutrophil recruitment, through mice as well
as humans with defects in neutrophil function, or conditions which display a lower num-
ber of present neutrophils. A study conducted in mice demonstrated that impairment in
neutrophil recruitment establishes severe skin lesions with a further deﬁciency in bacterial
clearance [1; 27]. In addition, several human studies have revealed that deﬁciency in neu-
trophil functions, such as oxidative burst, phagocytosis, and chemotaxis lead to a higher
susceptibility to S.aureus [2; 1].
In regards to abscess formation, neutrophils play a central role, they indeed represent a
hallmark for early defence in invaded tissue by S.aureus [28; 29]. In addition, the accumu-
lation and persistance of neutrophils during abscess formation and resolution, contribute
to the overall pathology of S.aureus SSTIs [2].
Following an insult in the skin, neutrophils are rapidly recruited from the circulation. The
recruitment of neutrophils is initiated by the cells residing in the skin [28]. Keratinocytes,
mast cells, DCs and macrophages are capable of recognizing PAMPs, that activate signal
transduction pathways, which in return lead to the release of proinﬂammatory cytokines
such as, tumor necrosis factor-α, (TNF-α), interleukin (IL) IL-1α/β, IL-6 and IL-17 [26;
28].
Speciﬁc PAMPs of S.aureus such as LTA/peptidoglycan and muramyl dipeptide,
are recognized by TLR2 together with co-receptor CD14 and NOD2, respectively [2; 1].
The recognition will in return activate signaling pathway including NF-κB, which induce
transcription of pro-inﬂammatory cytokines [29], see ﬁgure 2.3. Moreover, the combination
of PRR recognition and the proinﬂammatory cytokines, induce the secretion of signaling
molecules, chemokines, which contribute to the attraction and large inﬂux of neutrophils
to the site of infection in the skin [28].
Chemokines such as CXC-chemokine ligand 1, CXCL1, CXCL2 and CXCL8 facilitate an
attraction signal for neutrophils, by activating the CXC chemokine receptor 2 (CXCR2)
expressed on neutrophils speciﬁc for the expressed chemokines [1]. These all participate in
neutrophil recruitment via the leukocyte adhesion cascade [30]. The leukocyte adhesion
cascade is responsible for the entry of the neutrophils in to the dermis to combat the
invasion of S.aureus in the skin [1]. The recruitment of neutrophils occurs in postcapillary
venules, through rolling, adhesion and diapedesis [28; 30]. When an inﬂammation is taken

Like any other immune cell, neutrophils express PRRs such as TLR2/6 which are im-

The PRR recognition also participate in
general activation of the cell and stimulation of phagocytosis, however phagocytosis is
best stimulated by opsonins [2].
Neutrophil phagocytosis is the process in which the cell engulf the bacteria e.g. S.aureus
into phagosomes leading to sequestration and elimination. The uptake of the bacteria relies
heavily on the opsonization of bacteria with speciﬁc immunoglobulins (Igs) or complement
[29]. Opsonization is a process which can be proceeded by Igs or complement in order to
mark the pathogen and trigger the phagocytic machinery by phagocytes [2]. Neutrophils
express speciﬁc Fc receptors (FcRs), which recognize antibodies bound to epitopes on the
surface of S.aureus. The FcRs, FcγII (CD32) and FcγIIIB (CD16) recognize speciﬁc IgG
decorating the outer surface of S.aureus [29; 30]. In addition, the speciﬁc IgGs bound
on the surface of the bacteria, mediate the complement system [24]. Neutrophils express
complement receptors which recognize complement factors. They express CR1 (CD35)
and CR3 (CD11b/CD18) which recognize the surface-deposited complement factor C3b
and/or iC3b which is fragmented C3b [2; 29]. In addition, neutrophils also express C5a
receptors, which contribute in neutrophil migration, cell activation and ROS production
[29].
Upon recognition the neutrophil will engulf the bacteria into phagosomes, which are
membrane-bound vacuoles [30]. The bacterial killing is initiated within the phagosome
with multiple mechanisms. Phagosomes fuse with lysosomes, primary granules (Azurophil
granules) and secondary granules (peroxidase-negative granules), leading to the formation
of phagolysosome [24; 29].
Upon fusion, the NADPH oxidase will be activated in the
membrane of the phagolysosome and lead to the generation of highly toxic ROS [29]. The
generation of ROS by NADPH-oxidases is also referred to respiratory burst which is known
to participate in direct bacterial killing of the engulfed bacteria, see ﬁgure 2.5 [2; 1].

In addition, the fusion will result in a great enhancement in antimicrobial peptides partic-

ingested bacteria [1; 29].
Moreover, recent studies demonstrated that the neutrophils are capable of releasing NETs
which are web-like structures composed of chromatin and granule proteins, which intend
to trap, neutralize and kill non-ingested S.aureus [29; 31]. NETs are released via two
independent pathways, the ﬁrst is through cell death termed NETosis and the second is
through a non-lytic NETosis [31].
NETosis is a process where the neutrophil undergo
cell-death in a diﬀerent pathway than apoptosis, which leads to destruction of the plasma
membrane followed by the release of NETs [29].
However, neutrophil using non-lytic
NETosis do not need to go through cell-death upon invasion, instead they release NETs,
but result in an anucleated cytoplast which is still capable of ingesting S.aureus [31].
Once the neutrophils have fulﬁlled their task in combating S.aureus they go through a
programmed cell death termed apoptosis [24]. Apoptosis is important, as it ensures that
the release of toxic content is maintained, and thereby preventing further tissue damage
[32]. As neutrophils are rapidly migrating to the site of infection and undergoing apoptosis
right after, the accumulation leads to the formation of pus [2; 24].
The importance of neutrophils in abscess formation has been implicated based on S.aureus’
ability to evade the host response of neutrophils by multiple mechanisms [1]. Abscesses
develop as a result of the ﬁght between S.aureus and neutrophils, therefore it is also im-
portant to understand the virulence of S.aureus in order to get the full understanding of
abscess formation and its content [2].
S.aureus is unfortunately no bystander, which means that a number of virulence factors
have been documented as contributors to destruction of neutrophils as well as provide
longer survival rate for the bacteria itself [29]. The virulence factors are able to alter the
neutrophil recruitment, aﬀect every step involved in phagocytosis, and even cause cell lysis
of neutrophils (necrosis) [2].
S.aureus secretes inhibitory chemotaxis proteins termed CHIPS, which interferes with the
migration of neutrophils [1]. CHIPS are capable of inhibiting the chemotaxis towards C5a
(which is an anaphylatoxin and a chemoattactrant) and formylated peptides (N-formylated
peptides released by S.aureus) by binding directly to the C5aR and the formylated peptide
receptor [1; 29]. Moreover, they produce extracellular adherence proteins (Eap), which
interfere with the adhesion of neutrophils on the endothelium, by binding to ICAM-1,
hence hindering diapedisis and further migration to the site on infection [2].
In addition, S.aureus has developed defense mechanisms against phagocytosis. Protein
A (SpA), is a surface protein, which is able to inhibit phagocytosis by binding to the
FC region of IgG [22]. The binding to the FC region will thereby impede binding by
opsonizing antibodies as the antibody will bind in an incorrect orientation on the surface
of S.aureus [1]. Moreover, clumping factor A (ClfA) has as well been associated with the

pathogenesis of abscesses [2]. ClfA is capable of binding to ﬁbrinogen, which may form a

Furthermore, studies have demonstrated that S.aureus can aﬀect apoptotic pathways,
comprise macrophage eﬀerocytosis, and even alter cytokine production [33].
As men-
tioned previously, neutrophils undergo natural occurring cell lysis, however S.aureus is
capable of expediting this process with its secreted toxins leading to programmed necrosis
[2; 33]. α-Hemolysin (Hla) has been closely related to the pathogenesis of S.aureus in
abscess formation through processes such as cell necrosis [2]. Hla, is a cytotoxin which
is secreted by S.aureus generating pores in the plasma membrane of a variety of immune
cells [1]. The pores will eventually result in an uncontrolled ion inﬂux such as calcium ions
inﬂux (Ca2+) leading to cell necrosis [2]. They have also been associated with activation
of caspase 8 and 9 [32]. Caspases are known as crucial factors for apoptosis, and the
activation of caspases 8 and 9 by Hla has been demonstrated to further activate caspase
3 leading to DNA degradation and cell death [2; 34]. In addition to caspases, Hla is able
to elicit neutrophil recruitment by inducing the production of IL-1β through downstream
activation of caspase 1 by activating NLRP3 inﬂammasome which is a member of the NLR
family [35]. Moreover, the importance of Hla in abscess formation has been demonstrated
in animal infection models, where deletion or immunzation against Hla decreased the size
of abscesses [2].
Eﬀerocytosis is an important termination step once neutrophils undergo apoptosis [2; 36].
Eﬀerocytosis is initated by macrophages by recognizing an ”eat me” signal, which ensures
that bacteria within phagosomes, and cytotoxic molecules found in neutrophils do not
cause any further tissue damage upon cell death [36]. However, S.aureus is able to intrude
this process upon phagocytosis, by increasing the level of CD47 which stands for ”don’t
eat me” signal, hindering macrophages from clearing neutrophils [33].
Another virulence factor which has been strongly associated with abscess formation is
coagulases. S.aureus coagulase (Coa) is known for its contribution to form the ﬁbrous
capsule surrounding the abscess through conversion of ﬁbrinogen to ﬁbrin [2]. The ﬁbrous
capsule is also aﬀected by the activation of prothrombin (coagulation factor II) by Coa
and accumulation of the virulence factor, von Willebrand factor binding protein (vWbp)
[2].
Another virulence factor which is worth mentioning is the leukotoxin, Panton-Valentine
leukocidin (PVL) [1]. It has been associated with direct lysis of leukocytes, and as well
been associated with abscess formation caused by CA-MRSA [5; 2]. Notably PVL, has
also been applied as a marker for CA-MRSA strains in particularly USA300 which is by
far the most important virulence strain in SSTIs [5].

understand the adaptive immune response. In contrast to innate immunity, the adaptive
immunity creates an immunological memory after combating a pathogen, which is capable
of stimulating the host defense to subsequent encountering of that particular pathogen [8].
Hence, a vaccine is desired to elicit a response which is similar to the natural response upon
encountering. However, it is especially important to develop vaccines that are capable of
providing suﬃcient protection against reinfection, thus inducing adaptive immunity [7].

The humoral and T cell mediated immunity against S.aureus
Adaptive immune responses are highly speciﬁc, and comprise B lymphocytes (B-cells) and
T lymphocytes (T-cells) [24]. The humoral mediated immunity involves antibodies, which
are secreted by antibody-secreting plasma cells [8]. The B-cell will bind to a free S.aureus
antigen, and further be activated by T-helper cells (Th cells) upon presentation through
an antigen:MHC class II complex [24; 1]. The antibodies have three diﬀerent roles, neu-
tralization, opsonozition and complement activation [24]. Neutralization by antibodies is
initiated towards toxins such as Hla and PVL [1]. Phagocytosis on the other hand as previ-
ously mentioned, is highly relied on opsonins such as antibody and complement, which also
indicate the importance of B-cell responses against S.aureus [2]. Furthermore, the impor-
tant role of antibodies in the course of infection is also highlighted through the virulence
factors which can avoid phagocytosis, e.g. SpA [8]. One may argue, that development of
vaccines enhancing B-cell responses thereby focusing on the humoral response, could lead
to protective immunity against S.aureus. However, a number of studies have been able to
demonstrated that S.aureus diﬀers from typical encapsulated bacteria [1; 8]. It has been
clearly established that patients with B-cell deﬁciencies do not have a higher risk to more
severe S.aureus infections in contrast to regular encapsulated bacterial infections caused
by pathogens such as Streptococcus pneumoniae or Hemophilus inﬂuenzae b [8]. Even
though antibodies are important for initiating phagocytosis, vaccines aiming to elicit a
humoral response alone has not been proven to be eﬃcient [7]. Previous vaccine attempts
such as the capsular polysaccaride vaccine, StaphVax R
⃝, aimed to elicit antibody-mediated
opsonization [37]. The vaccine succeeded in clinical trial I and II demonstrating high an-
tibody titers, unfortunately the eﬃcacy became limited in clinical trial III and resulted in
recurrence in one third of the patients [37; 38]. It was further discovered that strains which
were isolated from the patients lacked a capsule, including USA300, indicating that not
all S.aureus isolates have a polysaccaride capsule, thus limiting the eﬃcacy of facilitated
opzonization [8].
In contrast to the antibody-mediated immune responses against S.aureus, T-cell dysfunc-
tion has demonstrated a higher risk for S.aureus skin infections [1]. T-cell are capable of
proliferating and diﬀerentiate into three diﬀerent eﬀector cells, helper T-cells (Th cells),
cytotoxic T-cells (CTL) and regulatory T cells (Treg), depending on the type of pep-
tide:MHC complex presented [39]. Th cells are further divided into Th1 cells, Th2 cells
and Th17 cells each one of them contributing with an important role in cutaneous host
immune responses against S.aureus [8]. Th1 cells are mainly associated with macrophages
through secretion of IFN-γ, whereas Th2 cells are responsible for antibody-mediated im-
mune responses through production of cytokines such as IL-4 and IL-13 [39]. With the
production of IL-17, including IL-17A and IL-17F, Th17 cells have been able to demon-
strate an important role within neutrophil recruitment and abscess formation [1; 31]. The
studies of Th cells in general have been able to establish how the innate immune response
might collaborate with the adaptive immune response in order to ﬁght S.aureus infections
[1]. It has been suggested that the production of cytokines such as IL-1α, IL-1β and IL-23
mediate Th17 cell formation [1; 26; 39]. IL-17A and IL-17F are cytokines which are se-
creted by Th17 cells, which in return upregulate the production of important AMPs such
as LL-37 by keratinocytes through binding of IL-17 present on keratinocytes [28; 1]. In

Vaccine strategies aiming to induce protective Th17 cells have been proposed. The vaccine
NDV-3 has demonstrated protective immunity in phase I trials through rapid antibody
response and as well through IL-17 mediated responses [8; 40]. The vaccine is composed of
a recombinant adhesin/invasin, albicans agglutinin-like sequence 3 (Als3), the N-terminus,
which is found on the surface of Candida albicans, hence the name (rAls3p-N) [40; 41].
It has been established to have a structural homolog to S.aureus adhesins, and is also
known to bind and invade human endothelial cells [40]. Through abscesses in mice, NDV-

of infection [8; 40]. It was further discovered that the absence of protective antibodies did
not have any signiﬁcant diﬀerence in combating S.aureus in the skin, however the induc-
tion of protective Th1 cells and Th17 cells was essential decreasing the size of abscesses [8].
Other vaccines approaches have also tested the possibility of a multivalent vaccine, which
comprises a vaccine with multiple S.aureus antigens [8]. The aim of producing such a
vaccine is to elicit a broad range of responses, i.e. the humoral response and the adaptive
immune response [7]. Even though humoral responses have been proven ineﬃcient alone,
it has been noted that in combination with cell-mediated responses it will be possible to
target a broad array of S.aureus strains [8]. The most advanced vaccine today which is
composed of more than one antigen is the SA4Ag development by Pﬁzer [7; 42]. The vac-
cine is currently in phase II, and has four diﬀerent vaccine candidates which aim to elicit
both the humoral and cell-mediated immune response. It involves the virulence factor
ClfA important for ﬁbrogen binding, two capsular polysaccharides which have an impact
on phagocytosis, and lastly a manganese transport protein C (MntC), which contributes
in the coagulation cascade, and functions as an ion-scavenging factor [8; 42]. The vaccine
has demonstrated a robust immune response in healthy individuals, and were maintained
through 12 months. The study included 454 healthy individuals, and did not report any
serious adverse events [42].

involve both the humoral and cell-mediated immune response, most importantly secretion
of IL-17 to mediate the recruitment of neutrophils and production of AMPs, initiating a
robust antibody production, which can help mediate phagocytosis by neutrophils and as
well contribute to neutralization of S.aureus toxins.

3 Experimental strategy
The purpose of this thesis is to establish a pig skin model for MRSA infections in humans.
The study involves a multicomponent subunit vaccine which contains eight diﬀerent pro-
teins which possibly provide protection against MRSA.
The vaccine is based on a study carried out by Rasmussen et al.
and the associated
research group. They identiﬁed 35 possible vaccine candidates by screening the MRSA252
strain using the in silico platform VacFinder. Selected S.aureus proteins were tested in
a lethal sepsis mouse model, where 13 proteins demonstrated protection. In addition to
this, the same study conﬁrmed that 12 out of these 13 antigens were highly conserved
across 70 completely sequenced S.aureus strains.
This thesis examines eight of the twelve conserved proteins identiﬁed in the previously
named study, namely protein 1-8. Additionally, a pilot study tried to establish skin ab-
scesses in nine pigs and demonstrate eﬃcacy of the multicomponent vaccine mentioned
above. The animals were inoculated with 108 cfu/0.1 mL MRSA USA300 suspended in
PBS. Unfortunately the pilot study failed to demonstrate a signiﬁcant diﬀerence between
vaccinated and non-vaccinated animals since no major signs of infection were detected. It
was argued that the pigs had some natural capability to clear MRSA USA300 from the
injection site.
The current study attempts to improve upon the pilot study by reﬁning the number of
participants, the monitoring of the in vivo experiment, and the in vivo bacterial challenge.
In contrast to the pilot study, the number of pigs examined in this study is 30. Twelve pigs
are vaccinated, sixteen pigs are left non-vaccinated, and two pigs are used as controls. As
the pilot study failed to establish a reliable infectious skin model, the 28 pigs are further
divided into four diﬀerent subgroups depending on the type of inoculum and injection site
see table 4.1. In order to investigate the interplay between MRSA and host immunity
during skin infection and asses the pathogenesis of S.aureus in regards to inoculation
depth, the inoculum will either be administered i.c. or s.c.. Since S.aureus is capable of
causing both superﬁcial and severe infections in the skin it is convenient to inoculate in
the diﬀerent compartments of the skin.
In order to investigate if the vaccine provides protective immunity against MRSA skin
lesions, two diﬀerent types of inoculums are tested. The ﬁrst inoculum is identical to
the pilot study. The second inoculum is composed of MRSA USA300 and Xanthan gum.
Xanthan gum is added in order to prolong the presence of MRSA USA300 at the injection
site. In addition to this, Xanthan gum is expected to function as a matrix, simulating a
possible bioﬁlm environment and hereby enhance the pathology of S.aureus compared to

18

the ﬁrst inoculum. The choice of Xanthan gum is further elaborated in section 5.1.2 and
6.1.
The pigs are monitored over a period of 10 days. The monitoring starts on day zero, which
is the day of inoculum and ends on day 10, the day of termination. The monitoring will
involve daily measurement of temperature, photos of the injection site to monitor possible
lesion formation and collection of blood samples on day zero, one, three, six and day ten.
In vitro assessments are also added to the following study. In order to investigate the
eﬃcacy of the vaccine, Enzyme-Linked Immunosorbent Assays (ELISA) are conducted.
This particular technique is applied to detect if the vaccine elicited an immune response
compared to the natural immune response against S.aureus and to detect if the vaccine is
capable of inducing the antibody response post inoculation.
Furthermore, it is well acknowledged that S.aureus is mostly carried in the nose and on
the tonsils, so in order to investigate if the vaccine is capable of eliminating the carrier
status, nose and tonsil swabs are collected from all the pigs. Askylgaard is acknowledged
as a LA-MRSA positive farm. The samples are collected in total three times throughout
the study. They are collected once they are moved from the farm, on the day of inoculum
and the day of termination.
We aim to look at x hypothesis throughout the study:
1. The multicomponent subunit vaccine will eliminate the S.aureus carrier status
2. The multicomponent vaccine will prevent the progression of skin lesion post inocu-
lation
3. The depth of inoculation play a crucial role in the progression of skin lesions
4. Adding Xanthan gum to the inoculum creates a bioﬁlm-like environment which en-
hances the pathology in skin lesions compared to pure S.aureus MRSA USA300
inoculum
5. The vaccine will elicit a greater response compared to natural immunity
The upcoming section, chapter 4, will deal with the relevant experiments and describe the
procedure of the current study in detail.

4 Materials and Methods
4.1
Animal Immunization
4.1.1
In vitro
Preparation of vaccine
The vaccine is composed of eight available recombinant proteins, which are potential pro-
tective S.aureus antigens, namely protein 1-8, see appendix 4, tabel 8.2 for protein info.
The vaccine contains an adjuvant, Alhydrogel, which is an aluminium hydroxide wet gel
suspension. This particular adjuvant is also referred to as Alum and is well known to stim-
ulate local recruitment of antigen presenting cells (APCs) and the migration to draining
lymph nodes [24]. Moreover, it has also been discovered to activate the NLRP3 inﬂam-
masome complex [24]. Since the vaccine is a recombinant subunit vaccine, the use of an
adjuvant is necessary to improve the immunogenecity of the vaccine.
Each vaccine injection dose (1 mL) is a mixture of 50 µg of protein 1-8 absorbed on to 40 µL
alhydrogel, see appendix 1, section 8.1.1. The proteins are transferred to a tube and mixed
with alhydrogel. The tube is incubated and rotated for an hour at room temperature (RT)
on an end-over-end tube rotator. The tube is centrifuged for two minutes at 200xg, and
the supernatant is then discarded leaving 50 % of injection dose in the tube, see appendix
1, section 8.1.1 for detailed steps. Subsequently physiological saline is transferred to the
suspension until the desired injection volume is reached. The vaccine is kept at 4◦C until
use.
4.1.2
In vivo
Immunization of piglets
A group of 12 female pigs, approximately four weeks old, were immunized subcutaneously
with a 1 mL vaccine mixture consisting of protein 1-8 and alhydrogel, and boosted once
with the same vaccine solution after 21 days. The pigs were immunized in Askylgaard,
and were transferred to Lindholm 13 days after the ﬁrst boost. In order to determine
the protective advantages of the vaccine, 18 non-vaccinated pigs from Askylgaard were
included in the study and transferred to Lindholm as well, see appendix 5, table 8.4 for
piglet info.

4.2
Bacterial Challenge
4.2.1
In vitro
Foreign substances which can retain S.aureus USA300 at injection site
The purpose of this experiment was to ﬁnd a foreign substance which can retain the
S.aureus USA300 inoculum in the pigs once injected.
Two diﬀerent experiments were conducted in order to simulate a possible bioﬁlm environ-
ment for S.aureus USA300 upon inoculation.
A chitosan based bioﬁlm-like environment
A study conducted by Stewart et al. [43] demonstrated the use of Chitosan in developing
an artiﬁcial bioﬁlm. They were able to construct an artiﬁcial bioﬁlm which resembles the
mechanics of Staphylococcus epidermis natural bioﬁlms [43]. It was therefore of interest to
reconstruct the matrix from the scientiﬁc article and observe whether S.aureus will take
advantage of the bioﬁlm construct and survive.
The bioﬁlm matrix was composed of a mixture including chitosan, bovine serum albumin
(BSA) and λ-DNA [43]. The three components were supposed to resemble the extracel-
lular polymeric substances (EPS) produced by cells within a bioﬁlm, i.e. extracellular
polysaccharides, extracellular proteins and DNA, respectively.
S.aureus USA300 was plated directly from a -80◦C stock onto blood agar (BA) plates and
grown O/N at 37◦C. On day two, a single colony was cultured in tryptic soy broth (TSB)
and left shaking O/N at 37◦C at 150 rpm.
On day three, a stock solution of 1 wt% chitosan ((Sigma Aldrich, St. Louis, MO), 448877-
50G) was dissolved in 0.3 M Acetic Acid (Merck KGaA, Germany, 1.00063.1011) (pH=3.0)
according to the protocol presented in Stewart et al. [43]. The artiﬁcial EPS was prepared
in TSB containing 0.6 wt% chitosan from the prepared stock solution, 1% BSA (Sigma
Aldrich, St. Louis, MO, A9418-100G) and 60 µg/mL λ-DNA (Thermo Scientiﬁc, USA
SD0011), see appendix 2, section 8.2.1. Vacuum ﬁlration with a Bioﬁl vacuum bottle with
a polyethersulfone (PES) membrane pore size 0.22 µm was applied in order to sterilize the
solution.
Before mixing the S.aureus USA300 culture with artiﬁcial EPS, an OD600 = 1.8 for the
cells had to be adjusted, please see appendix 2, section 8.2.1 for detailed steps of OD
adjustments. Previous work has demonstrated that OD=1.8 equals 109 cfu/mL and 108
cfu/0.1 mL. Once the OD was adjusted the pellet was resuspended in the EPS solution
and plated onto BA plates. A dilution row from 100 to 10−12 is prepared and plated onto
BA plates and left O/N at 37◦C. Please see ﬁgure 8.2 in appendix 2, section 8.2.1 for
setup. The plating is initiated within sterile conditions.
A Xanthan gum based bioﬁlm-like environment
A second experiment involving Xanthan gum which is a polysaccharide with powerful
thickening properties was carried out.
It is also an agent which is frequently used as
a stabilizer, preventing ingredients from separating. In this case, it may be used as an

This paragraph will deal with the ﬁnal preparation of S.aureus USA300 inoculum used

substrates leading to detectable color output that can be read by a spectrophotometer.

wells was measured with an ELISA reader at OD 450 nm with 650 nm as a reference.

6 Discussion
6.1
Evaluation of artiﬁcial EPS
The choice to investigate xanthan gum and chitosan as potential artiﬁcial extracellular
matrix components was based on both their physical properties (viscous and sticky) and
their presence in literature pertaining to artiﬁcial extracellular matrices and promoting
material retention or cell survival.
As outlined in Stewart et al. [43], chitosan could be used as a component in an artiﬁcial
EPS. Additonally, xanthan gum has been extensively researched for its pharmaceutical
properties, and is known to be non-toxic and does not irritate the skin [46]. Xanthan gum
and its derivatives have also been investigated in regards to its ability to retain various
compounds [47], including bacteria [48], in biological environments [49].
The choice of Xanthan gum was based on its ability to retain the growth of S.aureus
compared to the control (S.aureus + PBS 108 cfu/mL) in all dilutions provided in the
experiment. Chitosan alone resulted in reduced bacterial growth regardless of the concen-
tration of chitosan compared to the control (S.aureus + PBS 108 cfu/mL). In addition to
this, prolonged suspension in chitosan and O/N cultures in chitosan reduced the bacterial
growth even further. The matrix did not result in the same degree of reduction in growth
caused by chitosan alone, but it should be noted that the matrix still negatively impacted
S. aureus growth compared to Xanthan and the control. Furthermore, xanthan is cheap,
easy to aquire, easy to dissolve and easy to work with. Additionally, as mentioned earlier,
Xanthan gum is safe to use and would not cause any further harm to the pigs. More
importantly, we would be assured that the immune responses would not be reacting to the
foreign particle injected with S.aureus.
As can be seen in results section 5.1.1, ﬁgure 5.1, chitosan had a negative eﬀect on the
growth of S.aureus USA300. The eﬀect of chitosan on S.aureus growth was not shown in
Stewart et al., but other literature clearly shows its eﬀect on bacterial growth. Chitosan
has displayed antimicrobial activity against a variety of microorganisms including S. aureus
[50]. It is thought to achieve its antimicrobial activity through its polycationic nature,
which interferes with the permeability of the bacterial cell wall leading to cellular lysis
[51]. A study conducted on the behaviour of S.aureus in the presence of chitosan displayed
a reduction of growth colonies [50]. It was highlighted that the reduction of bacterial
growth in liquid medium was strongly associated with the concentration of chitosan. The
maximum activity of chitosan alone against S.aureus in liquid cultures was attained at 1
g/l. It displayed a reduction of approximately 96% after 12 hours [50].
Though a diminished antibacterial eﬀect was noted when chitosan was mixed with λ-DNA
and BSA, it is not recommended that chitosan be investigated further as a EPS component
==PAGE:58==
44

due to its antibiotic properties.
As literature pertaining to the use of xanthan gum in i.c. and s.c. injections was lacking,
working concentrations had to be developed. As retention of bacteria at the injection site
was needed, it was of interest to apply the highest possible concentration of Xanthan.
The highest initial working concentration was dependent upon the syringe used to inject
inoculum, i.e. the solution had to be able to be taken up into the syringe. Concentrations
above above 8mg/mL were not able to be aspirated.
Additionally, the bacterial survival was of speciﬁc interest. As can be seen in section 5.1.2
ﬁgure 5.2, CFU increased with xanthan gum suspension. As S. aureus is known to gen-
erate and grow in bioﬁlms [52], the more bioﬁlm-like environment of xanthan gum could
promote survival. Xanthan gum has been eﬃciently used in studies as an encapsulation
procedure for protection of external environments. Xanthan gum derivative has for in-
stance been investigated to encapsulate and promote survival of bone marrow cells, which
could indicate something similar might be occurring [53] with the combination of xanthan
and S. aureus. In addition to this, patents have been ﬁled and approved for the use of
xanthan as encapsulation material of probiotics in animals in order to protect them from
unfavourable environments such as temperature [54]. The patent was ﬁlled subsequent
to a study demonstrating the beneﬁcial application of xanthan gum to probiotics against
gastrointestinal infections in animals [54].
Due to its characteristics and status as a well-researched pharmaceutical material, xan-
than gum has a high potential for further use in the generation of an artiﬁcial EPS to
retain presence of S.aureus.
6.2
MRSA carrier status detection
Unfortunately, the selective plates used to determine MRSA carrier status in nose swabs
were changed after the ﬁrst sampling due to lack of availability. MRSA selective plates
are composed of a chromogenic mix and antibiotic mix which are proprietary, but are
known for being highly sensitive of screening for MRSA (99.6% sensitivity) [55].
The
S.aureus ChromoSelect Agar plates which were applied for the two remaining sampling
procedures are selective for coagulase positive S.aureus. These diﬀerences in selection are
the likely cause of the massive diﬀerence in number of colonies between the ﬁrst and the
remaining two carrier status samples see section 5.2 table 5.3. The large increase in S.
aureus CFU from the ﬁrst to second samples indicates that MRSA was not screened for in
the second and third carrier status of nose samples. Therefore, MRSA carrier status can
not be identiﬁed past the ﬁrst nose sample. If the plates were available for the other two
samplings we would have been able to monitor for transmission of and change in MRSA
carrier status.
Presence of MRSA could not be determined in the tonsil samples due to the selective
plates used. Like nose swab plating, selective plates were changed to ChromoSelect plates
after the ﬁrst sample. Plates used for the ﬁrst sample, Mannitol Salt Phenol Agar plates,
select for S. aureus in general and not MRSA. However, more virulent staphylocooci

data.
S.aureus carrier status has been implicated as a risk factor for infections like SSTIs.
In particular, reoccurring skin infections have been shown to be more likely in MRSA
carriers [57]. MRSA is capable of being transmitted through direct physical contact [58].
However it has recently been implicated that transmission can also occur between animals
and humans [59]. Pigs in particular are considered as a reservoir for MRSA capable of
infecting humans through occupational exposure such as farmers [60]. The importance of
MRSA carrier status has been studied in surgical patients, which demonstrated that the
elimination of MRSA carrier status resulted in a reduction in infection rates at hospitals
[61]. Thus, strategies aiming to eliminate virulent S.aureus from commensal populations
is attractive and should be further optimized.
6.3
Evaluation of inﬂammatory response
The inﬂammatory response is a fundamental sign given by the body upon disease or
injury, and it is an invaluable tool to measure the body’s response against invasion. Clin-
ical evaluation of inﬂammation is usable, but when developing disease models empirical
measurement are needed.
The ﬁrst measure used to evaluate inﬂammatory response was temperature.
Fever is
usually associated with inﬂammatory responses upon infection. However, monitoring of
temperature alone is not suﬃcient to empirically determine the level of inﬂammatory
response. Temperature measurements include a degree of uncertainty, as variations in
homeostatic temperature.
According to table 5.5 almost all pigs displayed an average
temperature of 39, which could indicate fever, before inoculation. In addition to this, the
==PAGE:60==
46
Chapter 6. Discussion
temperature did not change drastically from before inoculation to after inoculation which
further reduces the reliability of temperature as an indicator of inﬂammatory response.
However, the acute-phase proteins measured seem to be much more reliable indicators of
a developing innate, non-speciﬁc immune response. Both CRP and haptoglobin have been
used as markers for acute inﬂammation in a rat model [62]. CRP is known to increase
upon stimuli caused by a variety of factors such as tissue damage and infections in general.
All pigs, vaccinated and non-vaccinated, demonstrated increased levels of CRP on day 1
compared to day 0. This indicates that upon inoculation, an inﬂammatory response was
induced in the pigs.
More interestingly it was possible to observe a correlation between the increase of CRP
responses and the degree of irritated skin and redness at the injection site in pigs on
day 1. Decreased level of CRP on day 6 and 9 also corresponded to less severe clinical
signs of skin lesions. Acute phase proteins such as CRP are widely used in humans as a
diagnostic tool [45], and has also been demonstrated to increase in pigs upon inﬂammation
[]. The second acute-phase protein measured, haptoglobin, has also been shown to be an
eﬃcient marker for inﬂammatory responses in pigs []. An increase in haptoglobin should
indicate an inﬂammatory response. However, a consistent increase in haptoglobin levels
on day 1 compared to day 0 was not seen. Moreover, it was diﬃcult to correlate the
increase/decrease with the clinical presented inﬂammatory signs compared to CRP.
CRP display a great measurement of inﬂammation in this study, and should deﬁnitely be
implicated as an empirical indicator in future studies.
6.4
ELISA
A general issue which was seen in the ELISA procedure as well as within the pilot study
and in this study was the high level of background signal in the negative controls. Some
proteins would demonstrate minor diﬀerences in background noise from plate to plate
run. In this study we added Equus caballus serum (Horse serum) in the blocking buﬀer to
ensure proper blocking before proceeding with the ELISA procedure. Adding horse serum
resulted in both lower background noise and lower plate to plate variation in regards
to blank samples. Since horse serum does no not share the same species source as the
secondary antibody, non-speciﬁc binding was less likely.
However, it should be noted that there is room for further optimization. The ELISA for
each pig sample had to be performed at least twice because of high background levels.
High background levels can appear for a variety of reasons. One highly important factor
which can lead to high background noises is the chance/ability of an antibody binding to
bind solid surfaces of the plate. This can be monitored and improved by leaving a number
of wells on the plate, non coated to ensure that the antibodies from sample specimens do
not bind to the plastic surface of the plate. In case the antibody does bind to the plastic
surface, it can be subtracted from the OD value of the antibody response once OD values
determined in the blanks is subtracted. This will provide the opportunity for more reliable
OD measurements of the antibody response. In addition, washing steps are important for
reducing background levels, adding more washing steps to each step could be eﬃcient for
background levels.

Moreover, the plate development time could be adjusted. The plates were developed within
3-5 minutes. In practice, this was challenging if more than two plates were run simultane-
ously, leading to diﬀerences in development times and possible variation in ELISA readout.
A lower concentration of the secondary antibody would prevent this. Lastly, the serum
sample could be adjusted, as the OD for vaccinated animals was consistently high. The
”ﬁrst ELISA setup” demonstrated clearly that in low dilutions against speciﬁc proteins
it was not possible to compare OD antibody response in vaccinated animals compared to
non-vaccinated animals.
The reliability of the ELISA procedure displayed in this study could also be further im-
proved by adding controls which could monitor the plate to plate variation. In this study
we displayed two ELISA set-ups which were conducted diﬀerently. The ﬁrst ELISA setup
included two control samples in order to display the eﬃcacy of the vaccine compared to
the pilot study. The control samples included one vaccinated animal which showed high
antibody response against protein 3, 5 and 7 and one non-vaccinated animal which dis-
played a constant low response against 3, 5, and 7. The second setup on the other hand did
not apply any controls other than comparison of blanks across plates and the pilot study.
It is therefore diﬃcult to conﬁrm if the OD values are reliable and if they are actually
reproducible. Since the last ELISA setup was the most eﬃcient one for running a great
number of pigs at once, ﬁnding control samples for that particular setup is important to
ensure reliability of results.
6.5
Pig skin model for S. aureus infection
Compared to the pilot study, S.aureus retention was greatly improved. The pilot study
did not display any sign of abscesses upon MRSA USA300 infection evaluation. In the
current study we were able to develop well-deﬁned skin lesions which fulﬁlled the clinical
signs of abscesses. The abscesses were well-deﬁned and contained purulent material which
indicated a host response against S.aureus. In general, i.c. injection of inoculum resulted
in more severe lesion clinically. The inoculum containing xanthan gum resulted in the
most severe lesions in non-vaccinated pigs, with Xanthan i.c. being the most successful
inoculation method. In addition to this s.c. xanthan gum inoculum also displayed almost
the same size of lesions in vaccinated animals compared to non-vaccinated. The use of PBS
alone resulted in abscesses, but the most severe abscesses were associated with Xanthan.
The injection site played a signiﬁcant role in this study. The pilot study did not inject on
the inner thigh of the pigs. The injection on the inner thigh provided us with a thicker
layer of skin thus a better control of proper i.c. and s.c. injection. The i.c. injection was
in particular highly improved by the choice of the inner thigh as the place for injection.
This diﬀerence is highlighted by observing the size of the skin lesions caused by MRSA
USA300 + PBS in this study, which was deﬁnitely improved compared to the pilot study
(data not shown). Moreover, the use of xanthan has been able to show that retaining
S.aureus may be possible at injection site, and that a foreign particle could participate in
more severe lesions.
The experiments conducted throughout this study has been able to demonstrate the fea-
sibility of using pigs as a model for skin abscesses in humans. To our knowledge pigs have

6.6
Vaccine success
Several proteins of interest were identiﬁed through ELISA. When selecting antigens for a
potential vaccine, the production of high levels of antibodies in the vaccinated animals is
desired. Additionally, low levels of antibodies against the same protein in naturally chal-
lenged animals also ideal, as this allows for discrimination between vaccinated individuals
and individuals which have been challenged by the disease that is the vaccine target.
There were some notable diﬀerences seen in the antibody levels of vaccinated animals in
the pilot study and this study.
Two proteins, three and ﬁve, showed very high levels
of antibody response in the pilot study while not generating much of a response in this
study. This could be due to diﬀerences in vaccine formulation between the studies, as
freund’s incomplete adjuvant (FIA) was not included in the vaccine used in this study.
As subunit vaccines require adjuvants in order to elicit a strong immune response, one
could argue that the avoidance of using FIA in this current study may have lead to the
low responses against protein 3 and 5. Indeed FIA is known to induce predominantly
Th2-biased response as well as promote stimulation of antibody producing plasma cells.
It should be noted that since we intend to produce a vaccine which can provide protective
immunity in humans we would need to be cautions in the choice of adjuvants. Alum is
FDA approved for use in humans, therefore an immune response enhanced by alum only,
would be more favourable in this case.
As can be seen in ﬁgure 5.13a, 5.14b and 5.13b, proteins 1, 6, and 8 fulﬁll this criteria. In
contrast, high levels of antibodies speciﬁc for protein 4 were found in both vaccinated and
non-vaccinated animals. A vaccine based on this protein would not allow for identiﬁcation
of a vaccinated individual among those naturally challenged. However, this protein could
still be included in a multicomponent vaccine, as the presence of antibodies against non
naturally occurring antigens could still be detected. Another measure of which proteins
are the best candidates for future study is the consistency of the antibody response in
individual animals. Proteins one and eight were fairly consistent in the amount of antibody
generated against the protein, while protein six showed increased antibody levels from
inoculation to termination. The increase in antibody levels against protein 6 after bacterial
challenge is evidence for clonal expansion having occurred, meaning that this antigen is

found in MRSA USA300 and that this protein has been exposed to the immune system.
With this taken into consideration, proteins four and six are good candidates for further
study, due to the high antibody response along with an increase in levels of antibody
generation.
It was hypothesized that vaccine status would relate to abscess size. Within both the
vaccinated groups, there is a large variation in the size of abscess when compared to
antibody levels across all proteins see supplementary sheet (variation in subgroups). Pig
no. 31 displayed the biggest lesion among vaccinated animals. Looking at the antibody
response, it displays a great antibody response against protein 1, 2, 4, 6 and 8.
The

not a good predictor of abscess size. This also display an uncertainty in the mechanism of
immunity of the proteins used in the vaccine.
Since we do not have any knowledge about the proteins used in the vaccine, one could
argue that they do not represent MRSA virulence factors which would participate in the
formation of abscesses. Furthermore it could be argued that the humoral response alone
is not suﬃcient in combating infections caused by S.aureus. Previous vaccine approaches
against abscesses has demonstrated that absence of protective antibodies did not display
any diﬀerence in lesion size, whereas protective T-cells, in particular Th17 cells were proven
essential in the decrease of size of abscesses in mouse models. This could indicate that
in order to make a good multicomponent vaccine against infections such as abscesses, a
combination of the two is necessary to elicit the best response possible [8; 7].

7 Tying things
together/Conclusion
A number of parameters were investigated in this study. Firstly, evaluation and improve-
ments on the pilot study was conducted. We tried to establish a pig skin model resembling
human skin abscesses through improvements on the unsuccessful pilot study. We tested
diﬀerent injection sites and types of inoculum in order to establish skin lesions and ab-
scesses. We investigated the application of a foreign particle to the inoculum in order to
retain S.aureus at the injection site longer than S.aureus inoculum suspended in PBS.
In addition to this, we evaluated the eﬃcacy of the multicomponent vaccine in pigs. We
investigated if the vaccine was capable of displaying smaller lesions compared to non vac-
cinated individuals. We also investigated if the vaccine was capable of elicitng an immune
response which does not occur naturally upon S.aureus invasion.
This study was successfully able to generate abscesses in the skin of infected pigs. There
was a diﬀerence between the four subgroups. In general, injections with xanthan displayed
more severe abscesses in vaccinated and non vaccinated individuals. Inoculate injected i.c.
showed the most clear clinical signs of abscess formation. Inoculum injected s.c. did not
generate visible abscesses on the surface of the skin, but did however result in abscesses
with a high bulk density like in Pig no. 37, 38, 54 and 55. This could indicate that for
clinical evaluation of abscess progression, i.c. injection would be preferred, as s.c. injection
generates deeper, less visible abscesses which are more challenging to evaluate clinically.
Abscesses generated in the pilot study were minor and did not show reliable sign of skin
lesions for clinical evaluation. Thus, the work in this study has displayed outstanding
results which could to a step closer to building a ﬁnal pig skin model for human s. aureus
skin infections.
It was not possible to evaluate the success of the vaccine in this study based on clinical
parameters. Without more data pertaining to abscess size with this method of bacterial
challenge, it is hard to separate natural variations in abscess size from vaccine-related
abscess reduction.
A good example is pig no.
29 and 31, which both displayed high
antibody response but two diﬀerent skin lesion sizes. Hence, antibody response was not a
good metric for abscess size.
Two factors made it challenging to determine if vaccine eﬀectiveness could be determined
by looking at clinical signs of inﬂammation and abscess size. The ﬁrst is the fact that eight
proteins were tested at once. This increases the diﬃculty of identifying proteins which had
the largest aﬀect, as diﬀerent pigs displayed diﬀerent levels of reactions against individual
proteins. Another confounding factor was the variations in the skin model tested, making
it more diﬃcult to compare.
==PAGE:66==
52
Chapter 7. Tying things together/Conclusion
However, this study was able to identify four antigens which could be interesting to in-
vestigate further. Protein 1 and 8 displayed consistency in antibody response from day 0
to day 10 in vaccinated animals. Protein 6 displayed an increase in antibody response in
vaccinated animals post inoculation compared, but not in non vaccinated animals. Pro-
tein 4 displayed an increase in antibodies speciﬁc for protein 4 in both vaccinated and non
vaccinated animals, which also could be consider a good candidate for a multicomponent
vaccine.
In contrast to vaccine success, it was not possible to evaluate the vaccines eﬀect on MRSA
carrier status due to the complications explained in the discussion. Furthermore, no cor-
relation between antibody response in the individual pigs and colony count of tonsil and
nose swabs from collection day two and three was found.
Interestingly, if the carrier
status of S.aureus strains in general wanted to be eradicated, this particularly may not
be feasible with this particular vaccine. S.aureus is known for its commensal and op-
portunistic character. Due to this it is diﬃcult to diﬀerentiate between colonizing and
disease-causing strains. One could argue that it could have eliminated the carrier status
of virulent S.aureus strains such as MRSA, but not commensal S.aureus as it might not
express the same proteins. On the contrary if non virulent S.aureus was eradicated, one
could think that it may alter a natural balance of the nasal micro-ecology with potentially
undesired results.
In summation, this thesis has established a potential skin model for human s. aureus
skin infection in pigs and has identiﬁed four potential antigen candidates which should
be further investigated in the development of a multicomponent vaccine against MRSA
infection.
7.1
Future work
In general it has been challenging to correlate all the diﬀerent parameters included in
this study. This study suggests that in further investigation the parameters are to be
divided more broadly. The i.c. injection with xanthan should be examined individually
in a greater number of pigs for proper statistics as both vaccinated and non vaccinated
pigs demonstrated a big variation in abscess progression. Moreover, it could be of interest
to investigate the four potential proteins against MRSA USA300 in this skin model and
examine eﬃcacy compared to the multicomponent vaccine with a total of eight proteins.
Potentially, it could also be used to identify antigens that are potential factors for abscess
formation expressed by MRSA. This could be done by studying the 13 potential antigens
presented by Rasmussen et al. [6] which could present antibody speciﬁc response in order
to reduce the size of abscesses. Lastly, further investigations of xanthan gum should be
carried out. It could be of interest to investigate the potential of BSA and λ-DNA in
combination with Xanthan to observe if a better matrix could be constructed. Further
investigation of xanthan gum for use in inoculum, should be carried out in order to achieve
easier working concentrations/formulations.

The following protocol is adapted by Gitte Erbs’ inoculum preparation protocol with a few adjustments.
Day 1:
Plate the bacteria directly from the −80◦C S.aureus USA300 stock onto a blood agar plate, and leave the
plate O/N at 37◦C.
Day 2:
Make four liquid cultures of the bacterium by taking a single colony into 10 mL tryptic soy broth (TSB).
Leave the tube shaking O/N at 150rpm at 37◦C.
Day 3
Preparation of Chitosan matrix construct (Artiﬁcial EPS) :
The chitosan matrix is prepared in a two-time concentration in order to reach the actual end concentration
of the matrix, chitosan 0.3 wt%, 0.5% BSA, and 30µg/mL λ-DNA upon resuspension with S.aureus USA300
inoculum.
1. A stock solution of 1 wt% Chitosan was prepared in 0.3 M Acetic acid. The Acetic acid used in the
lab is Acetic acid (Glacial) 100 %.
2. 3.4 mL Acetic acid is mixed with 100 mL MiliQ water. In order to adjust pH = 6.0 sodium hydroxide
(5 M) was used. Once the pH was reached, the solution was added MiliQ water to reach a total
volume of 200 mL, and hereby reach a concentration of 0.3 M. The pH is considered based on the
ﬁndings of Stewart et al. [43].
3. 1 g of Chitosan is mixed with 100 mL Acetic Acid 0.3 M, in order to make a 1 wt% Chitosan stock
solution. The pH is adjusted again to pH = 6 once chitosan is added.
4. Chitosan, BSA and λ-DNA were mixed in TSB, with a total solution of 10 mL with the following
end concentration, 0.6 wt%, 1%, and 60µg/mL respectively.
5. The solution is transferred to a vacuum bottle with a PES membrane, pore size 0.22 µm in order
to sterilize the solution.
Preparation of Xanthan matrix construct:
We prepared a solution of 16 mg/mL Xanthan gum, in order to reach an end concentration of 8 mg/mL
Xanthan gum upon mixing with S.aureus USA300 inoculum.
A solution of Xanthan gum 16 mg/mL is dissolved in 7 mL PBS buﬀer in MiliQ water.
1. Weight 0.112 g Xanthan Gum (by Doves Farm) and add 7 mL PBS.
2. Stir the solution with very low heat and keep an eye on the solution.
3. When the solution becomes jelly, remove it and keep it in the fridge at 4◦C until use.

5. Re-suspend pellet in 10 mL PBS in each and measure OD600 in a cuvette. (Example: OD600 for
USA300 2.063)
6. Make 10 mL of cells in each tube adjusted to OD = 1.8 using the formula demonstrated below:

Inoculum preparation of S.aureus USA300
This protocol is based on a previous protocol made by Gitte Erbs and edited according to the new additional
steps of the experiment. The bacterial challenge includes 28 pigs, and two control pigs. As we need to
inoculate 28 pigs with three diﬀerent injection spots on the inner thigh, we would need 3x0.05 mL of
inoculum at 2 × 109 cfu/mL per pig. So we need at least 7 mL of inoculum at 2 × 109 cfu/mL.
An S.aureus culture resuspended in PBS with an OD600 = 1.8, equals 109 cfu/mL.
Day 1:
Plate the bacteria directly from the −80◦C S.aureus USA300 stock onto a blood agar plate, and leave the
plate O/N at 37◦C.
Day 2:
Make a liquid culture of the bacterium by taking a single colony into 10 mL tryptic soy broth (TSB). Leave
the tube shaking O/N at 150rpm at 37◦C. To ensure we have enough material we should make 6 tubes,
each with 10mL TSB.
Day 3 - Day of inoculum preparation
1. Pour the six tubes with bacteria into two 50 mL falcon tube.
2. Centrifuge the tube in 10 min at 1600 rpm
3. Combine the cells by pouring out the supernatant and add 15 mL PBS in each tube. Combine 30
mL in one tube.
4. Centrifuge the tube in 10 min at 1600 rpm
5. Re-suspend pellet in 20 mL PBS and measure OD600 in a cuvette. (Example: OD600 for USA300
2.063)
6. Make 20 mL of cells adjusted to OD = 1.8 using the formula demonstrated below:
Example = 20 × 1mL×1.8
2.063
= 17.45 mL + 2.55 mL PBS
7. Centrifuge the tube in 10 min at 1600 rpm
8. Resuspend the inoculum in 50 % of the volume above (10 mL PBS), make sure to resuspend the
tube centrifuged in step 7. This will give a solution of 2 × 109 cfu/mL. 3.5 mL of the inoculum was
mixed with 3.5 mL 16 mg/mL Xanthan gum* and 3.5 mL of the inoculum was mixed with 3.5 mL
PBS in order to obtain 108/0.1 mL injection dose.

ELISA experimental protocol
The following protocol is inspired and adapted based on Gitte Erbs’ protocol with a few additional changes.

Coat a Maxisorp plate or a Polysorp plate with 1 µg/mL protein. Protein 1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 6, 8 are coated
onto Maxisorp plates, and protein 7 is coated on Polysorp plates.
1. The plates are coated with 100 µL/well of the desired protein,1 µg/mL, by using a multi-channel
pipette.
2. The coated plates are placed o/n at 4◦C.
Each protein has an individual concentration, as demonstrated below:
Table 8.2: Protein 1 - 8 info
Day 2:
1. Wash the plates three times with PBS - 0.05% Tween. Remember to pound the plate after each
washing step.
2. Block the plates by adding 200 µL/well by using an eight channel multi-pipette, and leave the
plate shaking for 30 minutes at room temperature. Protein 1, 3, 4, 6 and 8 are blocked with PBS -
Tween - 1% BSA and protein 2, 5 and 7 are blocked with PBS - Tween - Casein.
3. Wash the plates three times with PBS - 0.05% Tween.
4. Sample is added 100 µL/well. The samples are diluted 1:1500 and 1:7500 depending on the coated
protein. The samples are diluted in blocking buﬀer. Please see ”ELISA Plate Setup” to ensure
where and what to add the diﬀerent samples. Leave the plate shaking for 2h at room temperature.
5. Wash the plates three times with PBS - 0.05% Tween.
6. Conjugate by adding 100 µL/well of the Goat anti-swine IgG (H+L) secondary antibody. The
secondary antibody is diluted in blocking buﬀer. Leave the plate shaking for 1h at room temperature.
7. Wash the plates three times with PBS - 0.05% Tween.
8. Develop the plate by adding 100 µL/well of TMB plus. Leave the plate under aluminum foil, and
keep an eye on the plate. Develop for approximately 2-5 minutes.
9. Stop the plate by adding 100 µL/well 0.5M H2SO4.
10. Read the plate with an ELISA reader at 450/650 nm.
