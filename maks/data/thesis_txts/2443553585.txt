Abstract
The objective of this study was to explore in practice how combinations of indoor
environment factors affect occupants in an open plan study environment in the
DTU Smart library. In particular, the study will focus on the effect that controllable
LED lighting may have on the noise level and occupant perception. The study was
based on measurements of illuminance, correlated colour temperature (CCT), sound
level and temperature, and also observations of occupant behaviour and occupant
feedback.
The measurements of temperature, sound level and illuminance were carried out
in a period of ﬁve weeks during the heating season. During the period, occupants
could answer a questionnaire about the indoor environment in the library.
There was not found any relations between the measured noise level, illuminance
and temperature proved in the study.
Compared to the current (2018) Danish standards and regulations in the temperature
and noise level were in short periods above the requirements from the Danish
Working Environment Authority in short periods. The illuminances were in 5 out of 6
areas signiﬁcantly lower than the required 500 lx. Based on occupant responses the
overall evaluation of the indoor environment in the library was rated satisfying. The
lighting was the factor with the highest satisfaction rate, while the noise level had
the lowest satisfaction rate, and was mainly caused by noise from conversation.

1
Introduction
Indoor environment is a broad subject and it is characterised by many different
parameters, which can have different effects on the indoor climate and the perception
of it. Two of these parameters are lighting and noise. Lighting includes several
aspects, i.e. daylight and artiﬁcial lighting, brightness and colour temperature, and
might have a relation to sound level. In a time where the open plan ofﬁce layout
is used more and more often in both work and study environments, the acoustical
conditions are even more relevant. Therefore, the study investigates the relation
between lighting and sound level in an open plan environment which in this study,
is the DTU smart library.
The DTU (Technical University of Denmark) Smart Library is a very popular place
to sit and study among the students, and is used by many through the year. The
students use the facilities for group work, but also individually, and they often stay in
the library for a long period of time, which is why the indoor environment has to be
good. The last couple of years, the library has done a lot to create and improve a good
study environment for the students, such as updating the interior, technical systems
and indoor environment. Just last year, the library went through a comprehensive
renovation where the ventilation and lighting system were changed, among other
things. One of the purposes of the renovation was to transform the library into a
smart library, bringing a lot of interesting technology into the space of the library
that can help improve the user experience. After the renovation, the employees of
the DTU Smart Library expressed their great interest in having students using the
library facilities as a part of their projects.
1.1 Objective
The objective of this study was to explore in practice how combinations of indoor
environment factors affect occupants in an open plan study environment in the DTU
Smart library. In particular, the study will focus on the effect that controllable LED
lighting may have on the noise level and occupant perception. The study was based
on measurements of illuminance, correlated colour temperature (CCT), sound level
and temperature, and additionally, observation of user behaviour and user feedback
received from a questionnaire.

The data from the measurements will be analysed using relevant statistical calcula-
tions, with the aim of describing any relations between the indoor environmental
factors, and compare the data to the current standards and regulations in the Danish
building industry.
1.2 Project Structure
The project went on over the course of ﬁve months and the project process was
divided into ﬁve phases as shown in ﬁgure 1.1
Figure 1.1 Project phases.
In the ﬁrst phase, literature related to indoor environment, speciﬁcally, lighting
and noise were studied. The literature review gave an understanding of the theory
and previous studies in this ﬁeld, and provided an insight on how to approach the
experiment.
The second phase was the preparation phase. In this phase the frame of experiment
had to be decided and planned, what should be included and how should it be
done. This involved a lot of communication with all the involved parties: the staff
of the library and CAS (Campus Service) employees. Also, the instruments and
questionnaires were prepared. The third phase was the actual experiment and
measuring period, and the transition from preparation and execution period was not
very clear. Since a lot of things had to be prepared and not all things were ready at
the same time, the setup of the experiment was done in many small steps.
The fourth and ﬁfth phases mostly consisted of data processing and documentation.
The data from the measurements and questionnaires were analysed, statistical tests
were performed and checked if any general conclusion was found.

2.1.2 Correlated Colour Temperature
Correlated colour temperature describes the colour tone of white light sources, which
are other sources that are not black body radiators, such as LEDs and ﬂuorescent
sources [41]. The scale for CCT goes from warm tones to cool tones, as shown in
ﬁgure 2.1, and is measured in kelvin (K).
Figure 2.1 Correlated colour temperature scale (Elemental LED) [9].

Lighting with a CCT below 3200 K is considered warm and above 4000 Kvit is
considered cool [41]. A CCT around 3000 K is equivalent to incandescent lighting
and 6500 K is the same tone as an overcast sky.
2.1.3 Colour Rendering Index
Colour rendering index is a measure that describes how well the light source shows
the colours of objects compared to a reference source, such as incandescent light or
daylight. The CRI varies from 0 to 100, where a CRI of 100 means that the colours
illuminated by the given light source can be expected to be shown exactly as if they
were illuminated by a reference light source, either incandescent light or daylight
[40].
2.1.4 Daylight
Daylight is the direct and reﬂected light coming from the sun, and it enters the room
through openings in the building, such as windows and skylights. The illuminance
of daylight is also measured with a lux meter and in the unit lux (lx). Another way
to analyse the amount of daylight on an indoor surface is by calculating the daylight
factor. Daylight factor is a measure, that compares the amount of daylight on an
indoor plane surface with the amount of daylight on an outdoor plane in overcast
sky conditions [43]. The daylight factor (DF) is expressed in percentage as [8]:
DF = Ein

The higher the percentage, the more daylight is available on the measured surface.
If the average DF is around 2%, the room is considered daylit [8].
2.2 Noise
2.2.1 Sound Pressure Level
When a sound wave is travelling in a sound ﬁeld through a medium, the medium
particles are moving backwards and forwards repetitively, which are causing a
neighbouring particle to be displaced. The moving particles produce vibrations and
these are transmitted from particle to particle in the medium. At a certain point
the medium particles are gathered, which is creating a high pressure, while at a

caused by a sound wave and the ambient pressure of the medium the sound wave is

2.2.2 Sound Level
Sound level, also known as weighted sound pressure level, is the sound pressure
level measured with a frequency weighting and exponential time weighting, and
averaged over a period of time [25].
Frequency Weighting
There are three commonly used frequency weightings A, C and Z. A- and C-weighting

2.4 Atmospheric Indoor Environment
Atmospheric environment is mainly described by the air quality. The inhaled air
should not contain pollutants that cause any health risks for the occupants, and
simultaneously be perceived as fresh and not stuffy. The indoor space contains many
pollution sources, i.e. building materials, furniture, equipment, the outdoor air, and
even the occupants themselves, and they emit chemicals into the air, which at certain
concentrations and long exposure time, can be fatal. Therefore, it is important to
ventilate the indoor spaces at sufﬁcient air rates. The most common pollutant in
the air in occupied spaces is carbon dioxide (CO2). CO2 is emitted into the air from
the human body and the concentration is measured in ppm (parts per million). In
rooms with many occupants and non sufﬁcient ventilation rates, the concentration
will quickly exceed the recommended limits (see 2.5.4). As many other pollutants,
CO2 is not detectable by humans, but the concentration is often used as an indicator
of the air quality and contents of bio efﬂuents in the air. Furthermore, the CO2
concentration is typically used to determine the ventilation rates [12].
2.5 Regulations and Standards
In Denmark, when a new building is built, it has to comply with the Building Regula-
tions (Bygningsreglementet), that apply at the year of construction. The Building
Regulations consist of requirements regarding energy, indoor climate, ﬁre safety etc.,
and also guidelines for how to comply with the requirements. Every few years, the
requirements are modiﬁed, and the current one is the 2018 version, shortened BR18.
Another authority, which is inﬂuencing the indoor environment is The Danish Work-
ing Environment Authority (AT). AT decides the requirements the Danish workplaces
have to comply with for the safety and well-being of the workers. Besides being a
study place, DTU library is also a working place for the staff members and therefore
it has to comply with the requirements.
Globally and nationally, there are a lot of standards regarding this subject, and
despite not being legally binding, some standards are frequently used as guidelines
for design.
This section is about the current regulations and standards regarding the indoor
environment in workplace environments, speciﬁcally ofﬁces where the main activity
is desk work.

Literature Review
Studies about general indoor environment in ofﬁce environments were performed
by Galasiu and Veitch in 2006. The researchers did a literature review of several
studies investigating the preference and satisfaction of lighting and daylight related
control systems in ofﬁces. The review resulted in these ﬁndings [22]:
• The preferred light level was lower for people working much of the time in
front of computers, than for others.
• The preferred light level depended on the daylight level. When the daylight
was low, higher light levels were preferred and the other way around.
• when the thermal conditions were not accepted by the occupants, the lighting
conditions were very likely to be perceived as unpleasant.
• The lights were kept on for the whole day until the occupants went home, and
when the daylight level was low the lighting was turned on, but when the
daylight level was high the lighting was kept on
Another study focused on chinese open plan university research ofﬁces were per-
formed by Kang et. al in 2017. The aim was to get the young researchers to evaluate
their own perception of the indoor environment quality (IEQ) and their comfort
in the ofﬁce. The data showed that IEQ correlated with ofﬁce productivity, and
that the acoustic environment had the biggest inﬂuence on productivity, especially
conversational noise was most disturbing, which may be a problem in the library.
Furthermore, the study showed that the occupants’ perception depended strongly on
the individual factors such as, age, gender, work activity, etc. [29].
Combination of Lighting and Noise
Limited number of studies investigated artiﬁcial lighting and sound level. Instead,
studies focus more on artiﬁcial lighting and its inﬂuence on occupant productivity
and how it affects occupants’ perception of the indoor environment.
However, a recent study from this year done by the Department of Electrical Engi-
15
==PAGE:32==
neering at the Technical University of Denmark in collaboration with Henning Larsen
Architects and the Royal Danish Academy of Fine Arts investigated the correlation
between noise level and artiﬁcial lighting type. The study took place in four class-
rooms at a Danish elementary school, and the results showed that the noise level
was strongly affected by the lighting type. The focused light distribution reduced
the noise level by 1 to 6 dB in 75% in the problem solving sessions, and in 25% of
these sessions, the reduction was between 3 to 6 dB, these were in comparison to
the standard ambient light distribution [7].
Liebl et al. did not ﬁnd signiﬁcant effects of combined noise and lighting distrac-
tions on cognitive performance. In the experiment test-persons were exposed with
combinations of visual and acoustic distractions in an ofﬁce environment, and their
cognitive performance and well-being were evaluated. The visual distraction was
created by a video projector, projecting light in the occupants’ ﬁeld of vision. The
projector either consistently illuminated a projection screen, or projected a travelling
beam on the projection screen which moved with a random frequency, creating two
scenarios. A background noise at 40 dB(A) with high and low speech intelligibility
was added as a distraction, creating four test parameters. The study concluded that
the speech sounds were the most affecting factor on the performance and well-being,
good intelligibility being worse than poor intelligibility [31]. This indicates that
speech or conversation can be a disturbing factor that should be considered when
designing the open plan ofﬁces or study halls.
CCT and Illuminance Inﬂuence on Occupant Perception
CCT was also a research topic back in 1941, when Kruithof created a curve that
showed combinations of colour temperature and illuminance of a lighting source that
were pleasant to look at and be in. He found out that low illuminance is preferred in
low colour temperature and vice versa [30]. A couple of decades later, Wang et al.
in 2017, looked into the comfort and preference of LED lighting. The researchers
tested subjects in scenarios with twelve CCTs in the range of 2000 K - 100000 K and
three illuminances between 350 lx and 1000 lx. The study showed that lower CCTs
were preferred for relaxing, and higher CCTs were preferred for working [44].
Other researchers have been investigating the effect of CCT on occupant perception
and performance in ofﬁce environments. One of them was Juslén in 2006, where he
concluded that industrial workers preferred lower illuminances when CCT was high
and higher illuminances with low CCT, and that high CCT also caused an increase in
the productivity of the workers [28]. The same results were found in 2007 by Mills et
al., when they experimented with high CCT ﬂuorescent lighting in a call-centre. Two
CCTs of 2900 K and 17000 K were used, and the high CCT showed improvement of

the workers subjective report of their well-being and productivity [34].
Earlier this year, Toftum et al. investigated how correlated colour temperature (CCT)
of white LED lighting affects the perception of thermal environment and performance.
The CCT varied between 2700 K to 6300 K, and subjects answered questionnaires
and did performance tests in three temperatures: 19°C, 22°C and 27°C. The study

study by Ju et al. [27]. As the studies show, CCT in working environments can affect

Summary
The main points from the literature review are summarised here:
• There is a signiﬁcant difference between noise levels in focused light vs. ambi-
ent light.
• Background noise affects the evaluation of the indoor environment negatively,
especially speech and conversational noise.
• Preference of low illuminance when CCT is and high illuminance when CCT is
high.
• High CCT is preferred in work environments and increases the performance
and perception of well-being and comfort.
• There is a correlation between high CCT and perceived brightness.

depending on the parameter that is measured. Only one parameter per Modcam,

TrackMap
First, there is the TrackMap conﬁguration. This tracks the movement of the occupants
in the library. The TrackMap sensors are mounted in the lighting railings, with the
camera pointed directly downwards, and equipped with a ﬁsh-eye lenses, which
covers a rectangular area of 3m x 6m. The sensor constantly records a video of
the area, the built-in processor goes through the feed and every time there is some
movement in the area, it is marked as a disturbance, which is processed as movement.
The processed data is then sent to the database, where heatmaps are generated. To
get a complete representation of the library, the trackmap sensors are placed all over
the library. The locations of the sensors are marked in ﬁgure 5.2.
People Counter
The Modcams used for counters are not equipped with ﬁsh-eye lenses, as the
trackmap Modcams did. They are mounted in the ceiling near doorways, also
pointing downwards. When conﬁguring the settings for the sensors, a rectangular
area at the door is marked and the sides of the rectangle is deﬁned as in/out. Every

5.3 Data Processing
At the end of the measurement period, the instruments were taken down and the
data was collected and examined. The illuminance data were divided into six data
sets, one for each location (A1-A6). The sound level were divided into four sets, for
each location (B1,B2), and ﬁnally the data from the temperature logger, which were
placed at 9 different locations (T1-T9).
Before the data was illustrated and analysed, the data sets were sorted. Some of
the illuminance data sets contained some extreme maximum and minimum values.
Therefore, it was decided to cut off the upper 1% quantile of each data set and the
2% quantile for A5. All values below 100 lx were cut off as well since illuminances
below 100 lx only occurred when the light sensor was covered. All data sets were
sorted after the usage hours of the library, which were determined after looking at
the Modcam data, see section 6.1.
After the sorting process, the data was ready to use for calculations and analyses.
For the calculations of statistics and plotting of graphs, the program R Studio were
used. R Studio were used to calculate the mean and standard deviation, and plot
box plots and histograms. Also used in the later part of the report is the equivalent
sound pressure level, which were calculated using the equation 2.3. The equivalent
SPL was calculated over the occupancy hours of the library.

6.6.5 Summary of Results
Temperature
The measured indoor temperatures were all between 20-26.1°C, and more than half
of the temperatures were above 23°C. Some areas had periods where the tempera-
ture was above 25°C and 26°C, which is higher than the recommended temperature
by AT and ISO 7730 standard. Among all the participants, 29% were dissatisﬁed
with the temperature and 23% wanted lower temperatures.
Lighting
The means were in the range of 306-583 lx. Based on the mean values of the
illuminances, only one of the six areas complied with the requirement of minimum
500 lx. In ﬁve of the areas, illuminances below 500 lx occurred in at least 80% of
the total period, which is quite a lot. Despite the low illuminances, the lighting was
perceived as satisfactional by 91% of the participating occupants.
Noise
The sound level was measured in four areas, 3 study areas and 1 quiet zone, and
the sound level was between 24-90 dB(A) in the period. The mean values for the
three study areas were between 43-49 dB(A), and 39.6 dB(A) in the quiet zone.
According to AT, the sound levels in the study areas should not exceed 60 dB(A),
which happened in around 5% of the period. For the quiet zone, the recommended
maximum was 50 dB(A), which was exceeded in 1-2% of the period. Since the
recommended maximums were not exceeded in really large periods, the sound level
in the library was acceptable by AT standards. The equivalent sound pressure level
for all of the areas were below 56 dB(A) in the usage hours, which were far below
the required maximum of 85 dB(A). The occupants’ opinions about the noise level in
the library were mixed. A small majority of 58% of the occupants were satisﬁed with
the noise level, while the rest was either dissatisﬁed or neutral. The biggest source
of noise were conversational noise.
Analyses of relations
Out of all the investigated parameters, none seemed to be affected by the other
parameter. There was no proved relation between the noise level and illuminance,
or sound level and temperature.
The occupant responses did not show clear tendencies, but a noticeable amount of
people expressed their dissatisfaction with the temperatures and noise levels. The
library were too warm and they were affected by the conversing neighbours.

7
Discussion
During the measurement period, a few minor faults/error occurred with the instru-
ments, which could have affected the results of this study. In the following chapter,
these errors will be discussed and the results of the study will be evaluated.
Measurements
Before the measurements began, the instruments were placed in the library, and
it was a bit tricky. The main problem was the lux meters. The light sensors had to
be placed on the table surfaces to get representative data, which meant that the
rest of the instrument had to be fastened under the table, so it would not disturb
the occupants. The only solution was to tape it to the table leg and because of the
position, sometimes buttons were pushed and the measurement was stopped. This
happened a few times, and because of that, there were some rather large periods
without any measured data. Another obstacle of the lux meters was the battery
issue. The lux meters ran on battery the whole period and when the power ran out,
there was no data logged until the batteries were changed. The periods without data
were sometimes longer than a day. Since the light sensors were placed on the table
surfaces, the occupants covered them up and cast shadows on them while working.
This affected the measurements and made it difﬁcult to divide the "real" data from
the "false".
The measurement of the correlated colour temperature in the library contained some
uncertainties, and the results did come out as expected. The lighting system was
divided into smaller zones on each ﬂoor, which acted as one unit and within each
zone the lighting had the same brightness and CCT. However, when the CCT was
measured some of the zones had two different CCTs for some unexplainable reason.
Also, during the measurements there was one zone that had a constant CCT of 3500
K, the whole day. The lighting was supposed to vary over the day, starting with low
CCTs in the early morning and increase over midday until the afternoon where it
decreased again. Only the lighting on the ﬁrst ﬂoor were working after this schedule,
while the CCT on the second ﬂoor was more constant troughout the day.
Questionnaire
The questionnaire were developed in collaboration with fellow master’s students,
and were only accessible electronically with the URL of the website. The only method
55
==PAGE:72==
to get the occupants in the library to answer the questionnaire was trough ﬂyers
and by asking them up front, which was problematic, because the ﬂyers would be
removed by the cleaning staff the next day and it was not possible to visit the library
every day. Compared to the 2000+ daily visits, the number of participants in the
questionnaire was quite low. If the participation were higher, maybe it was possible
to detect clearer tendencies.
Results
The mean of the temperatures on both ﬂoor were around 23°C, considering that this
was measured in the winter season, it might have been a bit high. The mean of the
outdoor temperature was 6.4°C in the same period. In a couple of the areas, the
temperature were higher than 25°C, which was the maximum required by AT. If the
maximum was already exceeded in the winter season, what would the temperatures
be like in the summer. The reason for the high temperatures were most likely the
occupants. With approximately 2000 visits per day and the library being ﬁlled up
to max. capacity from 7:00 to 22:00 every day, it is not difﬁcult to imagine the
temperature increase. The problem now lies in how this generated heat is removed,
and if the ventilation rates are efﬁcient enough.
Five out of six areas had means below 500 lx, and were not complying with the
BR18 and AT regulations. Those were also the areas where things were placed in
front of the windows preventing the daylight to enter these areas. In some places
the windows were completely covered by the group room cubicles, not allowing
the occupant sitting in the study area to get a clear view out. Even though the
illuminances in the library were not acceptable compared to the regulations, the
occupant response was not affected by these illuminance levels. 91% of the occupants
were satisﬁed with the lighting, which was a signiﬁcantly higher satisfaction rate
compared to the temperature and noise level. The positive reaction could be caused
by the fact that all the occupants in the library was working on computers and
therefore preferring lower illuminances, which Galasiu and Veitch concluded in their
literature review [22]. Another reason could be because the newly installed lighting
spots are placed in a way, that creates a well-lit ambient light that covers the whole
area, which could affect the perception of the lighting.
The noise levels were all within an acceptable range, and complied with the AT
requirements in almost the whole period. Yet, 42% of the occupants were not satisﬁed
with the noise levels. The results from the questionnaire indicates that the most
disturbing factor was noise from other peoples conversations. As previous studies
by Liebl et al. [31] and Kang et al. [29] also concluded that conversational noise
were one of the most disturbing noise sources in ofﬁces, which affected the ability to
concentrate and the occupants’ well being.

8
Conclusion
The objective of this study was to investigate how combinations of indoor environ-
ment factors affect occupants in an open plan study environment, in particular what
effect controllable LED lighting have on the noise level and occupant perception.
The data from the measurements and the occupant response was investigated in the
study. However, it was not possible to prove any relations between the measured
noise level, illuminance and temperature.
The indoor environment was assessed and compared to the 2018 standards and
regulations in the Danish building industry. The temperature and noise level were
in short periods above the requirements from the Danish Working Environment
Authority. The illuminances were in 5 out of 6 areas signiﬁcantly lower than the
required 500 lx.
Based on the occupant responses the overall evaluation of the indoor environment
in the library was rated satisfying. The lighting was the factor with the highest
satisfaction rate, while the noise level had the lowest satisfaction rate, and was
mainly caused by noise from conversation.
