 II 
Abstract 
Palmaria palmata is an edible macroalgae in high demand and with rising interest. Natural 
supply is limited and vulnerable to overuse, but commercial cultivation is still in its infancy. 
Efficient and sustainable cultivation techniques from hatchery to grow-out are still needed. 
Integrated multitrophic aquaculture (IMTA) has been put forward as a means of co-
cultivating fed and extractive species with increased sustainability compared to monoculture. 
In IMTA the cultivation of extractive species, like P. palmata, act as a means of 
bioremediation through incorporation of excess nutrients, nitrogen (N) and carbon (C) from 
fed species like finfish. This project investigated different aspects of P. palmata cultivation, 
with a focus on land based aerated tumble cultivation in IMTA systems.  
 
The life cycle was covered through experiments on spore release and seeding, lab scale 
seedling growth and pilot scale long term growth resulting in thalli >70 cm. Integrated 
cultivation, using water from salmon recirculation aquaculture systems (RAS), was tested at 
both lab and pilot scale at varying irradiances. Cultivation potential of salmon RAS water 
was tested against standard growth media enriched seawater. Performance was primarily 
assessed through growth rates and productivity, but also in combination with chlorophyll 
fluorescence measurements of photosynthetic performance. Bioremediation potential was 
estimated based on P. palmata productivity and contents of C and N, and potential for human 
consumption was investigated by tissue analyses of undesired substances like Arsenic. 
Contamination issues were addressed through an experiment using chemical disinfection on 
seedlings with epiphytic fouling.  
 
The spore release and seeding yielded highly variable and overall low results, with spore 
densities ranging between 634-2351 g-1 (fresh weight, FW) and seeding densities ranging 
between 1.2-7.2 cm-1 substrate. But the seeding technique proved capable of providing evenly 
seeded substrates using only 5 g of sorus tissue during just 24 hours of seeding. The specific 
growth rates varied between 4.8 - 14.7 % d-1 and -1.6 - 6.9 % d-1 in the lab and pilot scale 
experiments, respectively. The highest yields were in the pilot scale experiment which had a 
productivity of 84.8 g m-2 d-1 (FW) corresponding to 13 g m-2 d-1 (dry weight, DW). 
Chlorophyll fluorescence measurements revealed early insights into high stress levels and 
lowered photosynthetic performance only shortly prior to mass incidence of bleaching and

degradation of tissues, seen in the pilot scale experiment, likely due to a combination of high 
water temperatures and high irradiances. However, summer growth of P. palmata was 
maintained in spite of water temperatures measured above 20 °C by shading which reduced 
irradiance levels by 90%. Tissue analyses revealed no accumulation on undesired substances 
if used for human consumption. Estimates of bioremediation potential for nitrogen and 
carbon indicate P. palmata  as a suitable species as long as high productivity is ensured. 
Epiphytic contamination was observed in all experiments, but the chemical treatment of 
epiphytic contamination revealed promising results, as 10 seconds exposure to 0.05% sodium 
hypochlorite repressed initial epiphytic growth, without reducing dry weight yields.  
 
To ensure viable cultivation of P. palmata it is important to obtain efficient hatchery 
techniques capable of providing a continuous and reliable supply of material for on-growth. 
Closed agitated seeding systems capable of producing both evenly seeded substrates and un-
attached spores for use in aerated tumble cultivation could be worth investigating further. The 
cultivation of P. palmata in Salmon RAS water proved capable of sustaining high growth 
rates, comparable to standard growth media enriched seawater, and the growth could be 
maintained throughout the summer by application of strong shading. IMTA cultivation of P. 
palmata in salmon RAS, could potentially provide bioremediation of excess nutrients for a 
more sustainable production, and at the same time diversify production.

 IV 
Preface 
This master thesis presents four experiments investigating cultivation of Palmaria palmata in 
integrated multitrophic aquaculture systems – from spore to harvestable resource. The 
experiments were carried out between 3rd of April 2019 and 16th of January 2020. Cultivating 
new sources of food and feed is important in a world with growing demand, especially if they 
can help in moving towards more sustainable production and mitigate environmental issues.  
The first section is an introduction to the topic to put the experimental findings into context. 
Here I briefly summarize overall macroalgal uses and cultivation, then I focus specifically on 
P. palmata, its biology and current cultivation practices as well as some topics relevant to the 
thesis including bioremediation potential, chlorophyll fluorescence, undesired substances and 
contamination issues. The second section introduces aims, objectives and hypotheses of the 
experimental work. The third section describes materials and methods used in the four 
experiments. Here the four experiments are presented according to life stage and not how the 
experiments were conducted in chronological time order. First the spore experiments are 
presented, moving on to seedling cultivation then presenting cultivation of larger thalli and 
ending with the contamination treatment as it can take place during any life stage. This order 
is repeated during the rest of the thesis. Section four describes the results, section five 
presents the discussion and section six summarizes the conclusions. Section seven contains 
all references cited during the previous sections.

1. Introduction 
1.1 Background 
The global demand for marine food products is increasing for the past decades and is 
expected to continue in the future (FAO 2018). The capture and harvest from natural sources 
has proven a stagnant, insufficient supply for some time now and has been replaced by 
aquaculture as the main source in recent years (FAO 2018).  
 
The growth in fed aquaculture is largely dependent upon external feed inputs like fishmeal 
and fish oil supplied from fisheries but also feed ingredients from terrestrial farming (Tacon 
and Metian 2015). Fed aquaculture in open sea cages is also a direct source of nutrients like 
nitrogen (N) and phosphorus (P) to the aquatic environment (Wang et al. 2012) of which the 
dissolved part is directly available for algal growth and may cause eutrophication (Skogen et 
al. 2009).  
 
These days, there is concern and skepticism regarding the sustainability and environmental 
impacts of industrialized fed aquaculture (Olsen and Osmundsen 2017). In Denmark, open 
water aquaculture has been restricted by nutrient emission regulations for decades (Holdt and 
Edwards 2014).  
 
Several solutions are in play in order to ensure future growth and improve sustainability of 
the aquaculture industry including research in feed sources, reducing water usage and 
increasing discharge control (Lekang 2013).  
 
Recirculating aquaculture systems (RAS) have been put forward as a possible solution and is 
a closed production method where system water is treated and re-used, which enables highly 
intensive systems with minimal water use and high discharge control (Lekang 2013).  
 
Integrated multi-trophic aquaculture (IMTA) as opposed to monoculture is a production 
method incorporating organisms from different trophic levels in a system where waste 
products from fed species are used by extractive species, thus resulting in more sustainable 
systems with less environmental impact (Chopin et al. 2012).  
 
==PAGE:13==
 
 
 
8 
Macroalgae represent a large group of extractive species that utilize photosynthesis and 
dissolved inorganic nutrients, thus representing a sustainably renewable resource, and several 
studies have investigated the possibilities of using macroalgae in IMTA systems (Chopin et 
al. 2001; Corey et al. 2014; Figueroa et al. 2006; Grote 2016; Kim et al. 2013; Troell et al. 
2009).  
 
1.2 Global production and applications of macroalgae 
The global production of macroalgae is a growing industry with an annual market value 
exceeding 6 billion USD (FAO 2018). The large array of possible uses of macroalgae have 
led to a demand far exceeding what harvest from natural sources can supply (McHugh 2003).  
 
Today the vast majority, more than 95%, of the total macroalgae production volume is 
supplied by cultivation (Chopin 2014; FAO 2018). Macroalgae cultivation eases harvesting 
pressure on natural sources whilst also satisfying the rising demand for macroalgae 
production (Lüning and Pang 2003).  
 
The majority of the global macroalgae production, more than 95%, is concentrated in Asia, 
with China, Indonesia, the Philippines, the Republic of Korea, Japan and Malaysia as the 
largest producing countries (Clements and Chopin 2017).  
 
In Asia, production is focused around macroalgae like Eucheuma spp., Saccharina Japonica, 
Undaria pinnatifida, Pyropia tenera and Kappaphycus alvarezii (FAO 2018). Pyropia 
tenera, Saccharina Japonica and Undaria pinnatifida are mostly used as a food source, while 
Eucheuma spp. and Kappaphycus alvarezii are used for extraction of carrageenan (Bixler and 
Porse 2011).  
 
European production of macroalgae is primarily based on harvesting from natural sources, 
and is almost negligible when compared to the global production, accounting for about 1% 
(FAO 2018). The main European species are Laminaria spp. and Ascophyllum which are 
used for extraction of alginates and the main producing countries are Norway, France and 
Ireland (FAO 2018). There is a rising interest and involvement in the cultivation and 
utilization of macroalgae in Europe (Monteiro et al. 2019). Most commercial cultivation 
focus in Europe has so far been on Saccharina latissima (Stévant et al. 2017).

Globally, macroalgae cultivation is the fastest growing aquaculture sector and has seen a 
doubling in production in the past decades (FAO 2018). Historically, macroalgae have been 
used and consumed mostly in Asia and the trend continues today (Mouritsen et al. 2013).  
 
There is still a living tradition for consuming macroalgae in some coastal areas of Europe, 
namely Ireland but also Iceland and Scotland (Mouritsen et al. 2013). Today, the interest in 
macroalgae for human consumption is rising in the western world where it is viewed not only 
as a means of eating more sustainably and healthily but also as interesting and tasty 
(Mouritsen et al. 2013).  
 
Palmaria palmata is a local European macroalgae and with its attractive taste it is one of the 
more interesting species for human consumption (Mouritsen et al. 2013). It can be eaten 
fresh, dried or roasted but is not well suited to extensive boiling and it can be incorporated in 
a wide variety different foods and dishes (Mouritsen et al. 2013). Personally I find that P. 
palmata is very tasty when pickled or fermented and also excellent when still very young and 
eaten fresh after a quick rinse in regular tap water.  
 
Due to rising demands it is now important to focus on improving cultivation methods of this 
species to ensure a dependable, sustainable supply whilst protecting natural populations that 
can only supply a limited and seasonal supply (Grote 2019).  
 
With P. palmata retail prices around 245 EUR kg-1 dry weight (DW) (Schmedes et al. 2019) 
and 2250 DKK kg-1 DW (Dansktang.dk) and with fresh macroalgae retailing around 200 – 
620 DKK kg-1 fresh weight (FW) (Dansktang.dk) it is also a species with actual economical 
value.

1.3 Biology of P. palmata 
P. palmata is a red macroalgae common in the North- and North-East Atlantic Ocean 
(Lüning 1990). It grows in cold waters in the intertidal and shallow subtidal zone down to 20 
m (Irvine and Guiry 1983) where it is attached to hard substrates like rocks or larger 
macroalgae (Irvine and Guiry 1983; Lüning 1990).  
 
P. palmata is a perennial species whose morphology is highly dependent on environmental 
conditions (Werner and Dring 2011). Naturally it grows up to 50 cm long and 8 cm wide 
(Braune and Guiry 2011) from a small round holdfast with upright thallus and it has a soft 
and leathery texture in colors of brownish-red, but in aquaculture it can assume other shapes 
(Mouritsen et al. 2013). It is a species attributed with the ability to withstand high-energy 
environments (Sanderson 2006).  
 
The life cycle of P. palmata was completed in culture in 1980 by Meer and Todd (Fig. 1). 
They revealed a strange life cycle including a sexual gametophyte generation with extreme 
dimorphism and a single asexual tetrasporophytic life stage.  
 
Diploid tetrasporophytes release tetraspores that give rise to haploid female and male 
gametophytes in a 1:1 ratio but with vastly different morphology and size (Werner and Dring 
2011). Tetraspores are immobile and relatively large with a diameter around 30 µm (Le Gall 
et al. 2004).  
 
Female gametophytes are microscopic and their sexual maturation is achieved just a few days 
after tetraspore release (Meer and Todd 1980). Male gametophytes have a morphology and 
size similar to their tetrasporophytic origin and separating the two is only possible after about 
a year when they mature (Werner and Dring 2011).

There is a large difference in timing of maturation between the two sexes and female gametes 
are fertilized by male gametes of the previous year (Grote 2019). Tetrasporophytes develop 
on the fertilized females, overgrow them and become independent (Grote 2019). Mature 
tetrasporophytes form tetrasporangial sori containing spores which can be seen as irregular 
and elevated dark-red areas (Werner and Dring 2011).

1.4 Cultivation of P. palmata 
The increasing demand and limited natural supply have led to several studies on various 
cultivation methods of P. palmata ranging from open sea growth trials and land based tank 
cultivation in monoculture to IMTA systems both in sea and on land (Corey et al. 2014; 
Edwards and Dring 2011; Le Gall et al. 2004; Martínez et al. 2006;Pang and Lüning 2006; 
Pang and Lüning 2004; Sanderson et al. 2012; Schmedes et al. 2019; Titlyanov et al. 2006).  
 
Cultivation methods are often divided into land-based tank cultivation and open sea growth, 
and usually they both require a hatchery phase for cultivation of spores and sporelings (Grote 
2019).  
 
Tank cultivation is generally more expensive than open sea growth (Sanderson 2006) but has 
the advantage that optimal culture conditions can be applied and controlled (Grote 2019).  
 
Land-based cultivation in combination with efficient hatchery techniques may provide the 
possibility of year round production of P. palmata (Pang and Lüning 2006).  
 
The following sections will cover aspects of cultivation from spore to harvestable resource 
divided in three parts; hatchery phase, open sea growth and land-based tank cultivation. 
 
1.4.1 Hatchery phase 
Tetrasporophytes of P. palmata naturally mature and develop tetrasporangia under winter 
conditions (Werner and Dring 2011) which correspond to a temperature of 10 °C and short-
day light conditions with a 8:16-h light:dark regime (Pang and Lüning 2006). They can be 
collected from wild populations during the reproductive season (Werner and Dring 2011) or 
their maturation can be artificially induced in culture by manipulating temperature and light 
to provide spores around the year ( Pang and Lüning 2006).  
 
When collecting wild P. palmata they need to be kept moist and cool at around 10-15°C to 
prevent stress-induced spore release (Werner and Dring 2011), and only clean fertile sori of 
tetrasporophytes should be chosen (Edwards and Dring 2011).  
 
==PAGE:18==
 
 
 
13 
Spores are mainly released under complete darkness or very low light conditions at 5-10 
µmol photons m-2s-1 at temperatures varying from 1-10°C (Grote 2019). Controlled 
desiccation in darkness and at low temperature (5°C) is often applied to induce spore release 
(Le Gall et al. 2004; Schmedes and Nielsen 2019). Highly variable maximum spore release 
yields from 5000-25000 g FW-1 to 67906 ± 11303 g FW-1 of sorus tissue have been reported 
(Le Gall et al. 2004; Schmedes et al. 2019).  
 
Spores are generally seeded straight onto substrates or kept in tumble culture with a seeding 
duration around three days, but settled spores can be seen under microscope already after 24 
hours (Grote 2019). Common substrates used for the settling of spores include various types 
of strings like 2 mm Kuralon or 1-2 mm polyester (Grote 2019) and also 5-7 mm 
polypropylene can be used (Schmedes et al. 2019). Spore densities of 50 spores cm-1 with an 
even distribution should be the aim for on-growth (Werner and Dring 2011).  
 
The currently most common seeding method relies on gravity with a 1:1 sori:substrate ratio 
and is not very efficient as it has a high use of sorus tissue, high spore mortality rate and 
uneven spore distribution (Schmedes et al. 2019).   Only the male gametophytes, representing 
just half of the spores, will develop into a harvestable resource because fertilization of female 
gametophytes is not included (Schmedes et al. 2019).  
 
Research into new hatchery techniques by Schmedes et al. (2019) has revealed a more spore 
efficient seeding method using a germination-maceration-agitation (GMA) method. In 
addition, Schmedes and Nielsen (2019) have shown that the consecutive use of sori in 
agitated flow-through systems with effluent filtration and inclusion of a female gametophyte 
fertilization step can provide both seeded substrates as well as plenty of spores for GMA- and 
tumble culture.  
 
Following spore release, cultures are usually kept at 10°C in UV treated and filtered seawater 
at low irradiances from 15-50 µmol photons m-2s-1 in 12:12-h light:dark regimes with 
moderate aeration (Le Gall et al. 2004; Werner and Dring 2011). Tumble cultures of spores 
can similarly be kept at 10°C under a 12:12-h light:dark regime at 10 µmol photons m-2s-1 
during the first month and increased to 40-60 µmol photons m-2s-1 for another month before 
transfer to on-growth according to Pang and Lüning (2006).  
==PAGE:19==
 
 
 
14 
Low irradiance levels inhibit the growth of unwanted epiphytes (Grote 2019) and high 
irradiance levels are also believed to inhibit the growth and survival of sporelings (Edwards 
and Dring 2011). When using tumble cultivation, a relatively high stocking density can help 
prevent epiphytic growth (Pang and Lüning 2006). 
 
1.4.2 Open sea growth 
Cultivating P. palmata at sea successfully requires careful site selection (Grote 2019). An 
ideal location has a water current of about 5-10 cm s-1 to provide ample nutrient and CO2 
exchange (Werner and Dring 2011) as well as inhibiting the growth of epiphytes (Grote 
2019). Water depth should be between 1-10 m to avoid strong wave action (Werner and 
Dring 2011) and have low particle loads to avoid coverage and reduction of photosynthesis 
(Grote 2019). Water temperature should be between 6-17°C (Morgan and Simpson 1981b) 
and never above 20°C as it is lethal to P. palmata (Grote 2019).  
 
High temperatures and irradiance levels can lead to photoinhibition causing bleaching 
(Sanderson et al. 2012) and disintegration of tissues (Edwards and Dring 2011). These 
temperature effects along with strong growth of epiphytes limit on-growth at sea in summer 
and early autumn in many locations (Edwards and Dring 2011).  
 
Seeded substrates or sporelings attached to substrates, which in either case are usually strings 
or nets, can be used for on-growth at sea (Grote 2019). Sporelings are typically 1-5 months 
when deployed at sea (Werner and Dring 2011) where it has been shown that larger, older 
sporelings have a greater survival rate (Edwards and Dring 2011). Cultivation can be done in 
various longline systems using weighted droppers or net-like structures (Edwards and Dring 
2011).  
 
According to Edwards and Dring (2011) harvest can take place after about 3-5 months, which 
effectively limits on-growth at sea to one crop annually due to seasonally unfavorable growth 
conditions during summer. However, Sanderson et al. (2012) suggests the possibility of 
multiple cropping if growth rates are sufficiently high. 
 
P. palmata tissues become tougher as they age and should generally be harvested when they 
are still palatable and not overgrown by epiphytes (Edwards and Dring 2011). There are large 
==PAGE:20==
 
 
 
15 
geographical and seasonal variations in the chemical composition of P. palmata (Hagen 
Rødde et al. 2004) which should also be taken into consideration when choosing the time of 
harvest.  
 
Yields varying between 510 – 2000 g FW m-1 longline (Browne 2001; Werner and Dring 
2011) and up to 940 g FW m-1 using droppers (Sanderson 2006) have been reported.  
 
1.4.3 Land-based tank cultivation 
To achieve optimal growth of P. palmata in a land-based tank culture, the water should have 
full salinity with 32 parts per thousand (ppt) reported as optimal (Morgan et al. 1980). 
However, the cultivation of P. palmata at lower salinities is not well investigated according 
to Schmedes et al. (2019)  who suggests cultivation potential down to 15 ppt.  
 
Temperature should be between 6-17°C (Morgan and Simpson 1981b). High growth rates are 
often often reported at 10°C (Corey et al. 2013; Grote 2016; Manríquez-Hernández et al. 
2016; Pang and Lüning 2004) although some studies find highest growth at 6°C (Corey et al. 
2012; Kim et al. 2013). High productivity has also been reported above 17°C but with 
degeneration of tissues occurring above 21°C (Matos et al. 2006). 
 
Optimal light intensity is reported between 125 and >140 µmol photons m-2s-1 at a 
photoperiod of 16:8-h light:dark (Manríquez-Hernández et al. 2016; Martínez and Rico 2004; 
Werner and Dring 2011), but P. palmata has been cultivated at surface irradiances as high as 
1600 µmol photons m-2s-1 at a photoperiod of 16:8-h light:dark (Pang and Lüning 2004).  
 
Aeration, inherent to tumble culture, ensures tissue growth and health and is essential for 
nutrient and CO2 uptake (Grote 2019) as well as evening out exposure times to surface 
irradiation (Pang and Lüning 2004). However, Caines et al. (2014) showed that aeration can 
be stopped during the dark phase, with no negative effects on growth or nutrient uptake, thus 
cutting electricity costs.  
 
Depending on water source, nutrients may need to be added in tank culture systems to sustain 
high growth rates, both macro- (N, P, kalium and magnesium) and micronutrients (Andersen 
et al. 2005). Carbon (C) can also become limiting to the growth of P. palmata (Bidwell and 
==PAGE:21==
 
 
 
16 
McLachlan 1985). The average optimal nutrient ratio for cultivation of macroalgae is 30:1 
N:P (Atkinson and Smith 1970), but high growth rates and productivity have been achieved 
for P. palmata at ratios of 10:1 (Pang and Lüning 2004).  
 
When cultivated in tumble culture, the circulation time, stocking density and tank depth 
affect the tissue exposure to light and thus growth, and high surface irradiance levels can lead 
to higher growth rates (Pang and Lüning 2004). However, in nutrient-rich waters, high 
irradiance levels may cause growth of unwanted epiphytic fouling (Grote 2019). Supplying 
nutrients in the dark phase can limit epiphytic growth (Demetropoulos and Langdon 2004). 
 
Stocking can be done with seedlings from the hatchery or young material collected from 
natural populations (Pang and Lüning 2004). Vegetative cultivation using meristematic 
fragments, typically the apical tips of fronds, has been completed in tank culture and 
suggested as feasible due to high growth rates (Morgan and Simpson 1981a, 1981b; 
Titlyanov et al. 2006).  
 
Optimal stocking density for growth and the prevention of epiphytic growth has been 
reported varying from 2-4 kg FW m-2 (Kim et al. 2013) to 6-8 kg FW m-2 (Pang and Lüning 
2006). Even though stocking densities of 10 kg FW m-2 have been shown to limit growth of 
P. palmata (Pang and Lüning 2004, 2006) it has also been suggested as optimal in high 
irradiance setups (Manríquez-Hernández et al. 2016). Overall, optimal stocking density is 
highly dependent on differences in system setup like irradiance, water exchange rate, nutrient 
supply as well as tank size and shape (Grote 2019).  
 
In tank culture, harvest can be done regularly at scheduled intervals depending on growth 
rates (Grote 2019). Maximum growth rates for P. palmata in different systems have been 
reported as high as 9-12% FW d-1 (Corey et al. 2014; Grote 2016; Pang and Lüning 2004; 
Sagert and Schubert 2000) and maximum yields of 143 g FW m-2 d-1 (Werner and Dring 
2011) and 609 g FW m-2 d-1 (Pang and Lüning 2004) have been reported.  
 
1.5 Bioremediation potential and cultivation of P. palmata in IMTA 
P. palmata is regarded as a well suited species for biofiltration in IMTA systems (Corey et al. 
2014; Grote 2016; Kim et al. 2013; Lüning 2001). Many studies have investigated P. palmata 
==PAGE:22==
 
 
 
17 
bioremediation at lab scale (Corey et al. 2012, 2013; Grote 2016; Manríquez-Hernández et al.  
2016) or at large scale during limited time, like one month or less (Caines et al. 2014; Corey 
et al. 2013; Matos et al. 2006; Tremblay-Gratton et al. 2018) and less have completed long 
term studies over several months (Corey et al. 2014).  
 
P. palmata has a higher affinity for ammonium (NH4+) than for nitrate (NO3-) leading to 
increased tissue N content under elevated NH4+ concentrations (Corey et al. 2013; Grote 
2016; Morgan and Simpson 1981a). However, it has a higher specific growth rate when 
provided with NO3- than with NH4+ (Grote 2016; K. C. Morgan and Simpson 1981a).  
 
Intensive RAS use bacterial biofilter nitrification of NH4+ to NO3. Fish excrete NH4+ through 
the gills which enters into a pH dependent equilibrium with ammonia (NH3) and even low 
concentrations of NH3 is highly toxic to most fish (Handy and Poxton 1993). These systems 
operate at neutral pH where total ammonia nitrogen (TAN, NH3 + NH4+) is almost entirely 
present as NH4+. Thus, bacterial biofilters used in RAS provide system water with high 
concentrations of NO3- and smaller concentrations of NH4+.  
 
Palmaria has a C concentrating mechanism, thought to promote increased growth rates under 
elevated CO2 levels (Demetropoulos and Langdon 2004; Kübler and Raven 1995) and P. 
palmata has been shown to be more efficient at C removal than N removal (Corey et al. 
2014). Sebök et al. (2017) found that P. palmata growth rates are positively affected by 
elevated CO2 concentrations up to a certain limit. Intensive RAS system water has elevated 
CO2 levels due to fish and biofilter respiration and utilizes degassers to strip CO2 from the 
water because it limits fish growth.  
 
When selecting P. palmata for cultivation and bioremediation in IMTA it should be 
considered that different strains can be adapted to different environmental conditions 
(Manríquez-Hernández et al. 2016). P. palmata is interesting for bioremediation purposes in 
IMTA systems because it is a valuable species with increasing demand especially as a food 
source, which could convert excess nutrients into a useable product.

1.6 Chlorophyll fluorescence 
Measurements of chlorophyll fluorescence is a fast technique for the determination of 
photosynthetic activity which is one of the most extensively monitored parameters in plant 
stress research (Schreiber et al. 1986). Pulse amplitude modulated (PAM) chlorophyll 
fluorescence measurements associated with photosystem II (PSII) can be used to assess 
photosynthetic capacity and stress-dependent changes in both higher plants (Schreiber et al. 
1986) and macroalgae (Andersen et al. 2013; Bates and Snow 1987; Beer and Axelsson 2004; 
Cordi et al. 1999; Figueroa et al. 2003, 2006, 2009, 2019;Franklin and Forster 1997; Häder 
and Figueroa 1997; Hanelt and Nultsch 1995; Hanelt et al. 1997; Holzinger et al. 2004; 
Kerrison et al. 2016; Kübler and Raven 1995, 1996; Nielsen and Nielsen 2008; Quintano et 
al. 2019; Sagert and Schubert 1995, 2000; Sarker et al. 2013; Sebök et al. 2017) 
 
When light energy is absorbed by chlorophyll molecules, the result is chlorophyll excitation 
followed by de-excitation through one of three competing pathways: thermal dissipation 
which is often referred to as non-photochemical processes or non-photochemical quenching 
(NPQ), chlorophyll fluorescence which is the re-emission of red photons or it can be used to 
drive electron transport and photosynthesis which is referred to as photochemistry (Maxwell 
and Johnson 2000). The three competing pathways of de-excitation imply that any change in 
one will affect the other two and as such, fluorescence measurements can be used to calculate 
both photochemistry and thermal dissipation efficiencies (Maxwell and Johnson 2000).

Overall, testing for undesired metals and compounds should be done if macroalgae are to be

IAs is usually transformed into an organic non-toxic form of As when taken up in marine 
organisms (Holdt and Kraan 2011), and quantities of bio accessible As, like iAs, in most 
macroalgae except a few brown algae, appear too low to pose a risk to human health (Wells 
et al. 2017). Even so, data on the total amount of As is important to better characterize 
potential intraspecies variability (Monteiro et al. 2019). Contents of As vary strongly 
depending on location and age of the specimen (Mouritsen et al. 2013). Young tissues from 
Denmark are reported to have concentrations of 0.1 mg kg-1 DW whereas older tissues had 
thrice the amount at 0.3 mg kg-1 DW (Mouritsen et al. 2013) but Sloth et al. (2012) found 
much higher levels at 10.7 mg kg-1 DW.  
 
The concentrations of substances like heavy metals and other elements depend on the 
surrounding environment, they can vary locally and undesired substances should therefore be 
tested if macroalgae are to be used for human consumption (Holdt and Kraan 2011).

1.8  Contamination issues in the cultivation of macroalgae 
The growth of epiphytic fouling organisms on cultivated macroalgae is a major problem 
(Lüning and Pang 2003). Growth of epiphytes compete with the cultivated species for light, 
substrate and nutrition and have a negative impact on their photosynthesis and growth 
(Titlyanov and Titlyanova 2010). Furthermore, heavy biofouling can render cultivated 
macroalgae unsuitable for human consumption (Marinho et al. 2015) and as such drastically 
reduce its potential value. 
 
Self-shading by high stocking densities and resulting low light conditions suppres the growth 
of fouling algae (Grote 2019). Maintaining a high stocking density, 8 kg FW m2, effectively 
reduced irradiance at 0.5 m depth to 0.05% of surface irradiance in a P. palmata cultivation 
system and provided a high areal-specific yield despite lower growth rates (Lüning and Pang 
2003). Growing cultured algae at high densities, approximately 10 kg FW m-2, can control the 
growth of epiphytes in aerated tank tumble cultivation (Bidwell et al. 1985).  
 
Modifying day length to species specific optimum even outside of the natural growth season 
has been shown to increase the growth rates of perennial macroalgae and may help prevent 
the growth of epiphytes (Gomez and Lüning 2001).  
 
Mechanical cleaning by hand is relatively effective but time consuming (Baweja et al. 2009). 
Sodium hypochlorite (NaClO) is a common disinfectant, and it has been used in disinfection 
the of macroalgae (Kerrison et al. 2016; Rimstad 2019). Concentration suggestions for 
NaClO disinfection vary from 0.1-1% (Kerrison et al. 2016; Kientz et al. 2011). The 
bleaching of chemically treated macroalgae tissues is seen with different treatments agents 
and may be related to undesired tissue degradation (Kientz et al. 2011).

2. Study aim, objectives and hypotheses 
This study aims to investigate land-based cultivation of P. palmata from spore to harvest 
sized sporophytes. The main experiment was a pilot scale growth trial conducted at Atlantic 
Sapphire, a land-based salmon RAS farm in Hvide Sande, Denmark. Here, growth and 
performance of P. palmata was tested as a function of light using growth and chlorophyll 
fluorescence measurements. The potential use of the cultivated P. palmata as a human food 
source was investigated by looking at biochemical components and the bioremediation 
potential was estimated using P. palmata tissue nutrient content.  
 
Additionally, experiments looking into spore release and seeding, lab scale growth of 
seedlings as a function of light and growth media, and chemical treatment of epiphytic 
fouling on seedlings were conducted at The Danish Shellfish Centre (DSC) in Nykøbing 
Mors, Denmark. The study combines work at different scales, from pilot scale to lab 
experiments covering different life stages of the species covered by specific learning 
objectives. 
 
2.1 Objectives 
 
• Apply knowledge of the life cycle of P. palmata to land based cultivation. 
• Investigate the cultivation potential of P. palmata in system water from a land based 
salmon RAS farm and estimate the bioremediation potential. 
• Investigate the summer growth of P. palmata in system water from a land based 
salmon RAS farm. 
• Evaluate the potential of P. palmata cultivated in salmon RAS farm system water as a 
human food source based on tissue analyses. 
• Explore the potential for optimizing the growth of P. palmata in land based 
cultivation using for instance different light, nutrient and density regimes. 
• Plan, design and conduct laboratory experiments investigating spore release, seeding 
and seedling growth.

3.1.2 Experimental setup and measurements 
The experiment was divided in two parts: spore release and seeding. This was to investigate 
both the spore density released from wild P. palmata collected at Fornæs Fyr and to test 
spore seeding in a closed vertical seeding setup modified and adapted from Schmedes and 
Nielsen (2019). 
 
3.1.3 Spore release 
The spore release was carried out using two different treatment methods: direct spore release 
over 24 hours and drying for 24 hours prior to 24 hours spore release.Tetrasporophytic sorus 
tissue was identified under stereo microscope and samples measuring 1x1 cm were cut out for 
each treatment method (n = 3) totaling 6 samples. Then samples were washed in filtered 
seawater (1µm) and dried using clean paper towels. Samples were divided in two according 
to treatment method and the drying was done in a black plastic bag for 24 hours at 5°C.  
 
Spore release was conducted over 24 hours in sterilized glass beakers placed on a shaker 
Table (300 rpm) to prevent spore attachment and wrapped in aluminum foil to provide dark 
conditions using 50 ml of sterile sea water per sample. Initial counting was done by taking a 
5ml subsample from a beaker where the volume had been shaken to provide a homogenous 
result representative of the total volume. This provided a very low spore count (Fig. 2).  
 
When noticing the low spore densities the holding bucket was investigated and it became 
clear that sporulation had already started here as the water volume as well as the sides of the 
bucket had a distinct red tint and the presence of spores was confirmed (personal 
observation).  
 
To manage a low spore density the samples were given an hour for spores to settle after the 
24 hours’ release and the supernatant was gently removed. The remaining volume was shaken 
to allow for a sampling of the total remaining volume which was assumed to contain all the 
released spores. Counting was done manually (n = 4) using a digital inverted microscope and 
spore release was estimated (g FW-1 and cm-2).

3.2.3 Chlorophyll fluorescence measurements  
Circular tissue samples (⌀ = 2cm) were cut out and acclimatized in darkness during a 
minimum of 15 minutes in containers with system water to maintain a steady temperature. 
Fluorescence was measured using PAM (Dual-PAM-100, Waltz, Effeltrich, Germany). The 
absorption coefficient (AC) of each sample was calculated as follows: 
 
 
AC = 1 – Et / E0 
(2) 
 
with E0 representing ambient light and Et being the light transmitted through the sample 
measured in µmol photons m-2 s-1 using a planar LiCOR sensor.  
 
During measurements, each tissue sample was placed in a clamp, at a fixed distance from the 
measuring fiber optics, which allowed no ambient light to reach the sample. Each run took 6 
min and consisted of 12 measurements at 12 levels of illumination ranging from 0-1194 µmol 
photons m-2s-1 each lasting 30 sec. Fluorescence measurements provided numerical values of 
the following parameters: 
 
• F = fluorescence measured briefly before the application of a saturation light pulse.  
• Fm  = maximal fluorescence of a dark-adapted sample with all PSII centers closed. 
• Fm’ = maximal fluorescence of illuminated sample with all PSII centers closed. 
• F0 = minimal fluorescence of dark adapted sample with all PSII centers open. 
• F0’ = minimal fluorescence of illuminated sample with all PSII centers open. 
 
Numerical values of the following parameters could be obtained directly from the PAM-
software, but to increase the accuracy of some parameters the measured AC values had to be 
incorporated instead of a generic one, and for consistency all parameters were recalculated.  
 
The above parameters (F, Fm, Fm’, F0 and F0’) were used according to Klughammer and 
Schreiber (2008) to calculate both photochemical and non-photochemical quenching 
parameters. 
 
 
 
 
==PAGE:37==
 
 
 
32 
The quantum yield of PSII was calculated as: 
 
 
Y(II) = (Fm’ – F) / Fm’ 
(4) 
 
which is the efficiency of PSII photochemistry or the proportion of light absorbed by 
chlorophyll associated with PSII used in photochemistry and an informative photochemical 
quenching parameter. 
 
The quantum yield of light energy dissipated through non-regulated non-photochemical 
quenching expressed by: 
 
 
Y(NO) = F / Fm 
(5) 
 
which is the energy that is passively dissipated as heat and fluorescence mainly due to closed 
PSII reaction centers. 
The quantum yield of light energy dissipated through regulated non-photochemical 
quenching expressed by:  
 
 
Y(NPQ) = (F / Fm’) / (F / Fm) 
(6) 
 
which is the energy dissipated as heat via the regulated photoprotective NPQ-mechanism. 
 
The overall photosynthetic capacity expressed by the electron transport rate (ETR) was 
calculated according to Sebök et al. (2017): 
 
 
ETR = PAR x Y(II) x  0.5 x AC 
(7) 
 
Where PAR is the photosynthetic active radiation (µmol photons m-2 s-1), 0.5 represents the 
proportion of energy split between PSII and PSI and AC is the absorption coefficient of the 
sample.  
 
Plotting ETR at varying irradiance levels and fitting curves according to Platt et al (1980) as 
described by Ralph and Gademann (2005) provided photosynthetic activity estimates of 
==PAGE:38==
 
 
 
33 
maximum electron transport rate (ETRmax), the maximum light harvesting efficiency (alpha) 
and the saturating irradiance level (Ik).  
 
The dark adapted ratio of variable to maximal fluorescence was calculated according to 
Schreiber et al. (1986): 
 
 
Fv / Fm = (Fm - F0) / Fm 
(8) 
 
which reflects the potential quantum efficiency of PSII and is a sensitive indicator of 
photosynthetic performance, with optimal values around 0.83 in unstressed green plants and 
lower values indicating stress and photoinhibition in particular (Maxwell & Johnson 2000). 
Values of Fv/Fm  are known to be lower especially in red algae (Franklin and Forster 1997).

3.3 Pilot scale growth experiment 
3.3.1 Sampling 
P. palmata was collected at Middelfart (55°50’71.14”N, 9°72’89.05”E) by divers in the 
intertidal zone on the 1st of April 2019. Water temperature and salinity at the location is 
around 4 °C and 15-21 ppt respectively, at that time of year (National monitoring survey data, 
NOVANA), but was not measured at sampling time. The sampling time was deliberately 
early in the season to provide immature and small individuals of P. palmata with a high 
potential for growth. This resulted in a limited amount of available material, which was dealt 
with in the setup. The samples were kept in a white plastic bucket containing locally sampled 
seawater during transport to temporary holding facilities at DSC, Øroddevej 80, 7900 
Nykøbing Mors, Denmark. Here they were kept at 10 °C and low light in UV treated and 
filtered seawater (1 µm) prior to transport to the experimental site at Atlantic Sapphire, 
Langsand 34, 6960 Hvide Sande, Denmark. 
 
3.3.2 Experimental setup and measurements 
The system was designed to test growth and performance of P. palmata collected from 
natural populations cultivated in salmon RAS system water in a low maintenance land based 
outdoor flow through tank tumble culture system under different light conditions over several 
months during spring, summer and fall 2019. An additional aim was to estimate the 
bioremediation potential of P. palmata in the system and to test the samples for unwanted 
substances with human consumption in mind.  
 
The setup consisted of 12 conical tanks (polyethylene, 30L with a surface area of 0.1075 m2), 
from now on referred to as growth tanks, set up in two identical holding tanks (Fig. 7). Each 
growth tank was set up with air bubbles from the bottom to provide aeration and mixing of 
the water volume, whilst a steady flow was ensured by a water inlet and outlet (Fig. 7), 
resulting in an approximate water volume of 25L.

from being flushed, but it was then deemed unnecessary as well as providing a possibility of 
unwanted tissue collection and removed. The water flow in each growth tank was initially set 
to approximately 1.5 l min-1 resulting in a water exchange rate of about 16 min-1. The high

Differences in spore release and seeding densities were analyzed using Welch’s t-test.  
 
The effect of irridiance levels, water type and time on SGR and productivity was tested by 
three-way analysis of variance (ANOVA) 
 
Fluorescence measurements and estimates from the lab growth experiment were examined as 
functions of irradiance levels and water type using two-way ANOVA.  
 
SGR, productivity and tissue analyses from the Hvide Sande experiment were divided into 
subgroups which were examined individually. Results from May were examined as a 
function of treatment using one-way ANOVA. Results from the two treatments were 
examined individually as a function of time using one-way ANOVA.  
 
Fluorescence measurements and estimates from Hvide Sande were also divided and analyzed 
in subgroups. Results from May were examined as a function of treatment and tissue type 
using two-way ANOVA. Results from the two treatments were examined individually as a 
function of tissue type and time using two-way ANOVA and finally the middle segments 
from the shaded treatment were examined as a function of time using one-way ANOVA.  
 
SGR and productivity from the disinfection experiment were examined as a function of 
duration using one-way ANOVA as the concentration treatment only yielded viable results 
for the lowest concentration.  
 
When ANOVA indicated significant effects post hoc analysis using Tukey’s honest 
significant difference test was used to make pairwise comparisons of treatment means.  
 
A normal distribution of data and homogenous variance in data were ensured prior to 
analyses with diagnostics plots and data was transformed when required to achieve 
homogeneity and normality.  
 
All data analyses were done in R (R Core Team 2019).

5. Discussion 
5.1 Spore release and seeding experiment 
The results of this experiment demonstrated the previously reported unpredictable and 
variable nature of P. palmata spore release (Irvine and Guiry 1983) and settlement 
(Sanderson 2006; Werner and Dring 2011). Spore release and seeding densities were low 
when compared to previous studies (Browne 2001; Le Gall et al. 2004; Schmedes et al. 2019 
Werner and Dring 2011). The spore release densities were quantified after 24 h spore release. 
A study by Schmedes et al. (2019) used 72 h and found a higher spore yield (per gram sori), 
still with high variation, which indicates a positive effect of using long spore release time. 
The present findings support the idea of long-term spore release as being more efficient.  
 
The initial confirmation of fertile tetrasporophytic sorus tissue and observations of 
tetrasporangial densities seemed promising. Some samples were observed to have dense 
clouds of loose tetraspores already visible in the water film covering the sorus tissue, yet all 
experimental results yielded low spore densities. The sampling from the beach face at Fornæs 
Fyr and the subsequent handling during transportation and storage prior to the experiment 
presumably had a high impact on the observed results. The normal procedure is to collect 
fresh material by diving and use controlled desiccation followed by rewetting to induce 
sporulation (Schmedes et al. 2019; Schmedes and Nielsen 2019). Samples collected at the 
beach face is very likely to have experienced several drying and rewetting cycles and each 
sampled individual may have washed up at the beach face at different times, thus collection at 
the beach face introduces a lot of unknown variability into the sampled material. When 
placing the collected material in seawater during transport and storage it is essentially 
receiving a treatment quite similar to the controlled desiccation followed by a rewetting 
sporulation induction treatment. Red spore aggregates seen in the storage bucket (personal 
observation) confirms that spore release took place during storage, which together with the 
unknown variability induced by sampling at the beach face could help explain the low spore 
densities observed in the experiments.  
 
The counting of spore seeding in this experiment was done after just 24h release and only 
represents initial spore density prior to any mortality. Schmedes and Nielsen (2019) report 
initial spore seeding densities using 5g of seeding sori  after three days of spore release within 
==PAGE:79==
 
 
 
74 
the same range as found in this study, but differences in system setup (flow through vs. 
closed), spore seeding durations (72h and 24h) and likely differences in seeding sori quality 
due to sampling at the beach face in this experiment complicate direct comparisons.  
 
Mortality rates are reported as high as 60–90% (Werner and Dring 2011), but other studies 
report germination success rates in the range of 50 – 80% (Le Gall et al. 2004; Schmedes and 
Nielsen 2019) which is representative to lower mortality rates. When accounting for a mean 
of the above reported mortality rates, the number of germinated seedlings in this experiment 
would be in the range of 0.6 and 3.6 cm-1 for net and string substrates respectively. These 
numbers are far from the ideal number of 50 cm-1 suggested by Werner and Dring (2011) and 
low compared to the 9 cm-1 found by Schmedes and Nielsen (2019).  
 
With limited sori available for spore seeding, it is crucial to increase the spore seeding 
efficiency. A way to increase the spore seeding efficiency is to increase the amount of growth 
substrate per gram sori, however, this requires a method to secure good spore dispersal within 
the seeding tank. Schemdes and Nielsen (2019) showed that by using high agitation in flow-
through tanks the growth substrates was evenly seeded. Nets and strings provided seeding 
substrate equal to 27 m and 37.5 m linear length in each tank respectively. The linear lengths 
could probably be increased for both net and string substrates by using a longer piece of net 
or by placing more string coils in each tank. An extrapolation of the estimate of germinated 
seedlings would provide 1620 and 6750 germinated seedlings in each tank using net and 
string substrate respectively. These numbers assume that the whole net surface could be 
seeded and that only half the string surface could be seeded due to self-shading by the coils, 
which seems reasonable based on previous findings (Schmedes and Nielsen 2019) and since 
no difference (p > 0.05) was found between the bottom and the top for the two substrates. 
This indicates that string coils could be a more efficient substrate in terms of hatchery space 
required.  
 
No significant differences in treatment methods were found for either spore release or spore 
seeding, though large numerical differences were found both between treatments and within 
treatments between replicates. The varying spore yields between treatments, although not 
statistically different, hint at a possible positive effect of using a 24h controlled desiccation 
step to induce spore release and of using string as opposed to net as seeding substrates. 
Further, the observed spore release during storage indicates that the 24h controlled 
==PAGE:80==
 
 
 
75 
desiccation step to induce spore release might be more effective if used on freshly collected 
fertile material. 
 
The number of treatment replicates was low (n = 3) compared to the observed variation, and 
more replicates could have increased the precision of the estimates to help find statistical 
differences. Alternatively, a more precise counting, by counting more times to reduce the 
variability and possibly including an automated counting step like a Coulter Counter, 
providing an additional count to compare with, could have lowered the observed variation.  
 
This experiment shows that initial seeding of spores can be verified even after low spore 
seeding times using a small amount of sorus tissue in a high agitation closed vertical seeding 
system. Schmedes and Nielsen (2019) has previously shown that seeding with 5g of sorus 
tissue yields spore densities similar to seeding with 10g and 15g of sorus tissue during the 
nursery stage. They also found overall even spore dispersal, presumably due to agitation of 
the water column causing turbulent resuspension of spores and increasing settlement 
likelihood. 
 
Future research should work with fresh sorus tissue with no previous exposure to drying and 
rewetting cycles, and could focus on longer seeding times, varying substrate densities and 
level of water agitation. Spore aggregates could be collected and used in GMA seeding 
according to Schmedes et al. (2019) or cultivated as un-attached seedlings in tumble culture. 
 
5.2 Lab scale growth experiment 
Specific growth rate and productivity was higher in the 100% F/2 water compared to RAS 
water. Irradiance levels alone had no significant effect on SGR or productivity. Time overall 
had a negative effect on SGR and a positive effect on productivity, which is correlated to 
system stocking densities and likely linked to epiphytic growth as well. High stocking 
densities have previously been found to have a negative effect on growth rates and a positive 
effect on productivity (Pang and Lüning 2004, 2006; Kim et al. 2013). When comparing 
growth rates found in this experiment with growth rates from previous studies, system 
parameters like temperature, stocking density, irradiance, light regime and nutrients which all 
affect the growth rate of P. palmata (Grote 2019) have to be taken into account.  
 
==PAGE:81==
 
 
 
76 
During this experiment the maximum SGR was 14.71 % d-1 (FW) over 14 days (grown at 100 
PAR in 100% F/2 water) and overall SGR was 12.72 % d-1 for all irradiances and water types 
during the first 14 days which is higher than previously reported (Table 19). Maximum SGR 
in RAS water was 13.47 % d-1 (FW) over 14 days at 100 PAR.  
 
Comparable reports of high growth rates (Grote 2016; Pang and Lüning 2004) had shorter 
water exchange, weighing and restocking intervals than this experiment. Pang and Lüning 
(2004) added nutrient media daily and Grote (2016) added nutrient media semi-weekly. 
Shorter intervals ensure less fluctuations in stocking density and nutrient concentration 
providing more stable growth conditions and the high SGR in this experiment was found in 
spite of likely less favorable intervals. Some reports of high P. palmata growth rates (Pang 
and Lüning 2006; Sagert and Schubert 2000; Titlyanov et al. 2006) measured growth as 
elongation rates, surface area increase or measured growth of fragments of marginal 
meristems and submeristems complicating direct comparisons with the present experiments.  
 
Dry weights during this experiment were generally low and comparable to spring values 
found in previous studies (Hagen Rødde et al. 2004; Martínez, and Rico 2002). Strong 
growth of P. palmata has been suggested to be associated with lower DW (Martínez, and 
Rico 2002).

The growth of epiphytes increased during the experiment, which has a negative effect on both 
growth rate and productivity of the basiphyte through reducing the supply of irradiance, 
nutrients and C (Lüning and Pang 2003). Epiphytic growth was first observed at 230 PAR in 
100% F/2 water, and later in all samples at varying levels, but with the strongest growth at 
higher PAR and in 100% F/2 water (Fig. 21). These findings correspond well with previously 
established knowledge that epiphytic growth is enhanced by strong light and high nutrient 
availability (Grote 2019).  
 
Stocking densities in this experiment varied from ~ 0.5 – 13.5 g FW L-1 over the course of the 
experiment, with the highest SGR found between 0.5 and 4 g FW L-1 which corresponds well 
with previous findings (Table 19) except for one study, (Pang and Lüning 2004) which 
successfully employed a combination of high irradiances and stocking densities in achieving 
a good balance between surface exposure time and a high degree of self shading ensuring 
good growth conditions.  
 
Both water types in this experiment contained high amounts of N as NO3-, which has 
previously been found to promote high growth rates in P. palmata (Table 19). The observed 
difference in tissue coloration between samples grown in 100% F/2 and RAS water (Fig. 22) 
could be partly due to the RAS water containing NH4+ which, when supplied as the sole 
source of N, is known to promote increased tissue N content (Corey et al. 2013; Grote 2016; 
Morgan and Simpson 1981a). Corey et al. (2013) even report enhanced NO3- uptake when 
supplying low concentrations of NH4+, but conflicting results (Grote 2016; Morgan and 
Simpson 1981a) showing a suppresion of  NO3- uptake by NH4+ suggest that the relationship 
between N uptake rates is complicated. P. palmata has previously been shown to use N 
reserves when cultivated at high irradiances, leading to paler tissues (Manríquez-Hernández 
et al. 2016; Morgan and Simpson 1981b) which could also help explain the difference in 
color seen between the two water types in this experiment.  
 
The F/2 media is enriched in both macro- and micronutrients (Guillard 1975; Guillard and 
Ryther 1962), whereas the RAS water only contains an excess of the nutrients supplied by 
fish excretion and feed spill. The content of P and micronutrients in the RAS water was not 
analyzed and it is a possibility that the observed differences in growth and productivity are 
due to lacking nutrients, other than N, which become limiting when water is only exchanged 
approximately every 14 days. Salmon feed contains high levels of P, but the efficiency of P 
==PAGE:83==
 
 
 
78 
removal in RAS systems is generally high and it is a possibility that the N:P ratio in the RAS 
water was unfavorable for the growth of P. palmata. Iron has also been put forward as a 
potentially limiting nutrient in RAS IMTA systems as it limits the conversion of NO3- to 
NH4+ at low concentrations (Manríquez-Hernández et al. 2016).  
 
The irradiances used in this study are within the range of what has previously been found to 
promote high growth rates in P. palmata (Table 19). The lacking effect of the varying 
irradiances may be partly due to the setup of the system. Starting material was provided from 
a dense culture purposefully grown in low light, low nutrient conditions as it has been shown 
as an effective means of biomass storage with a low incidence of epiphytes (Bidwell et al. 
1985). The system was set up with no acclimatization period and all samples experienced a 
sudden increase in both light and nutrient levels when the experiment was started. 
Acclimatization prior to experimental startup is a well-established procedure to avoid large 
initial variability in measured parameters (Grote 2016; Morgan and Simpson 1981b; Sebök et 
al. 2017). Low initial stocking densities provided little in terms of self-shading and when 
stocking densities became high, they were all reduced again by harvesting of tissues. It is a 
possibility that all light intensities caused stress when the experiment was started and that the 
experimental duration was not long enough to capture any significant light induced 
differences in SGR. Although not statistically detectable, there were some signs of light 
induced treatment differences. Consistent, numerically lower, initial SGR of samples grown 
at 230 PAR could indicate light induced stress which has previously been reported for P. 
palmata at irradiances of 290 – 570 µmol photons m-2 s-1 (Sagert and Schubert 2000). The 
discoloration of tissues first observed in the 230 PAR treatment, later in the 180 PAR 
treatment, both in 100% F/2 water (Fig. 22) could be a combination of tissue N reduction due 
to growth, as previously mentioned, and chronic photoinhibition as described by Franklin and 
Forster (1997) leading to onset of bleaching as described by Sanderson et al. (2012).  
 
Fluorescence measurements of both photosynthetic performance and stress parameters reveal 
that all samples from all treatments overall had low photosynthetic performance and showed 
some signs of stress, which supports that the lacking effect of irradiance levels on growth 
may be due to overall high stress in all treatments. The low photosynthetic performance is 
exemplified by low ETRmax and alpha compared to previous findings for P. palmata 
collected in Grenaa, Denmark and cultivated in tumble culture (Sebök et al. 2017). The 
==PAGE:84==
 
 
 
79 
material used in this experiment originated from a low light setting and combined with the 
relatively short duration of the experiment could perhaps explain the low photosynthetic 
performance. However Ik values indicate adaptations to increased irradiances as described by 
Sagert and Schubert (1995), with a pattern of higher Ik for tissues grown in RAS water, 
although not significant. The low photosynthetic performance could also be due to chronic 
photoinhibition as described by Franklin and Forster (1997). Alpha decreased with increasing 
PAR, indicating a photo inhibitory effect (Holzinger et al. 2004). Fv/Fm was lowered at higher 
irradiances which indicates increased stress levels (Cordi et al. 1999). The cultivation in RAS 
water also resulted in lowered alpha and a tendency towards a lowered Fv/Fm compared to the 
100% F/2 water. This connects the observed lower growth rates and productivity to a 
decreased photosynthetic performance and increased stress levels despite healthier looking 
tissue color.  
 
Rapid and high photochemical quenching (Y(II)) indicates that only a small fraction of 
incoming light was used in photosynthesis which is also reflected in low ETR values. All 
samples had a larger proportion of unregulated stress (Y(NO)) than regulated stress 
(Y(NPQ)) which, according to Klughammer and Schreiber (2008), reflects the inability of the 
specimen to protect itself against damage by excess illumination and furthermore, that high 
values of dark adapted Y(NO) are indicative of photodamage. Samples grown in 100% F/2 
water at 180 PAR had significantly higher Y(NO) and samples grown at 230 PAR had 
numerically higher dark adapted values of Y(NO). 
 
All this indicates that the cultivation of P. palmata in a setup like this should be done at 
irradiances lower than 230 and 180 PAR and that cultivation in water containing both NO3- 
and NH4+ might alleviate bleaching issues. RAS water contains very high levels of N and the 
lower growth rates for P. palmata cultivated in RAS water compared to 100% F/2 water, are 
likely due to other limiting nutrients like P or Fe but this needs further research. The 
cultivation of un-attached P. palmata seedlings provided from dense storage cultures yields 
tumble friendly tissues with high growth rates and has potential as a means of rapid on-
growth and biomass increase which should be studied at a larger scale.

5.3 Pilot scale growth experiment 
5.3.1 Growth rate and productivity 
The specific growth rate and productivity of P. palmata was higher in the ambient treatment 
during April-May than during June where both parameters were negative. The negative SGR 
and productivity coincide with high irradiance and maximum water temperatures above 20°C 
(Fig. 10) which has previously been found to cause bleaching (Sanderson et al. 2012) and 
disintegration (Edwards and Dring 2011) of P. palmata. The growth rate and productivity 
were negatively affected by the shaded treatment during May when compared to ambient, 
most likely due to lowered daily light hours (Table 3) and irradiance (Fig. 10). During May, 
daily light hours in the shaded treatment corresponded to a light:dark regime of 12.5:11.5-h 
with a daily mean PAR during light hours of 26±13 µmol photons m-2 s-1, which is far from 
optimal conditions according to previous studies (Tables 19 and 20).  
 
The maximum SGR and productivity for P. palmata occured in the ambient treatment 
between April 3rd and 30th  and was at 6.95 % d-1 and 84.85 g m-2 d-1 (FW), respectively. This 
was followed by a less productive period between April 30th and May 27th with SGR and 
productivity at 4.57 % d-1 and 73.93 g m-2 d-1 (FW) respectively. Growth rate and 
productivity in the shaded treatment were highest between April 30th and May 27th at 2.1% d-1 
and 25.95 g m-2 d-1 (FW) respectively. Irradiances and light:dark regimes in the shaded 
treatment were generally unfavorable for optimal growth when compared to ambient (Fig. 10, 
Table 3) or previous studies (Tables 19 and 20).

not easily overgrown, corresponds well with previous findings (Gomez and Lüning 2001).  
 
The reduction in growth rate seen from April to May without markedly poorer system 
parameters could be explained by the inverse relationship between the size of an organism 
and its growth, as was proposed by Manríquez-Hernández et al. (2016). They found that 
smaller P. palmata fronds have greater growth potential than larger fronds (Manríquez-
Hernández et al. 2016), which has also been documented by Corey et al. (2012). Declining 
growth rates for larger plants has also been described by Morgan and Simpson (1981a, 
1981b). P. palmata thalli were small avg. < ~5cm when stocked on April 3rd (personal 
observation) compared to April 30th up to ~40cm (Fig. 26) and very small compared to May 
27th up to ~70 cm (Fig. 12). 
 
System parameters in the ambient treatment during April-May (stocking density: ~1.8-3.5 g 
L-1, temperature: 11.5-13.9 °C, Irradiance: 65-98 µmol photons m-2 s-1 at ~15:9-16:8-h 
light:dark regime, weighing interval: 27 days, growth unit: 25 L and nutrient concentration of 
~2900-3000 µM NO3- and ~55-76 µM NH4+) are comparable with previous studies of P. 
palmata growth rates (Table 19) and productivity (Table 20). Salinity in this experiment 
during April-May was ~15-18 ppt, which is lower than previous studies (Tables 19 and 20) 
and lower than what is generally viewed as optimal conditions (Morgan et al. 1980). Nutrient 
levels in this experiment were about three times higher than what has previously been 
reported (Tables 19 and 20), but only with regard to NO3- and not for NH4+ which has 
previously been found to be toxic to P. palmata (Grote 2016; Morgan and Simpson 1981a). 
 
Surprisingly, the growth rate and productivity of the shaded treatment were never negative 
during the summer and neither bleaching nor disintegration of tissues was observed despite 
several incidences of water temperatures above 20°C (Fig. 10) which it should not be able to 
survive (Grote 2019). However, temperature was not measured directly in the growth tanks, 
==PAGE:87==
 
 
 
82 
but in the larger holding tanks (Fig. 9). During interruptions in water flow, the water in the 
holding tanks became stagnant and especially on sunny days, it is very likely that they 
developed a temperature driven stratification (thermocline) with the highest temperatures in 
the surface water. Due to the construction of the system only a small part of the holding tanks 
received incoming light, just where the sensors were placed. The growth tanks had 
continuous aeration providing circulation and could not develop this thermocline. 
Furthermore, the growth tanks were submerged in the waters of the holding tank, which were 
presumably still quite cold in the deeper waters, due to both their light grey color, absorbing 
little warmth and shading from the growth tanks. It is a distinct possibility that during water 
interruptions, especially on sunny days, temperatures measured by the sensors overestimate 
actual temperatures in the growth tanks. However, the bleaching and disintegration of tissues 
from the ambient treatment during June shows that temperatures were indeed lethal, at least 
in combination with high irradiances of the ambient treatment which probably caused higher 
water temperatures there, relative to the shaded. But throughout the summer, even with 
shading, both mean and maximum water temperatures were measured above 20°C many 
times in the shaded treatment (Fig. 10). Between June 26th and August 21st  the mean 
temperature was 15.6-16.8°C overall, with seven days above 18°C and two of them being 
consecutive days at 21.7°C and 24°C. Maximum temperatures were measured at or above 
20°C a total of nine days varying between ~20-26.8 °C, and at or above 18°C a total of 25 
days. Comparatively, between May 27th and June 26th, when bleaching and disintegration 
occurred in the ambient, the mean overall temperature was ~15.1°C. Furthermore, the mean 
temperature was only above 18°C during one day and there were just three days with 
maximum temperatures at 20.8°C, 21.3°C and 24.7°C. Whether growth tank temperatures 
were actually above 20°C becomes speculative, but measured temperatures which proved 
harmful to P. palmata in the ambient treatment were harmless in the shaded treatment, 
presumably due to lower irradiances. 
 
The growth rate and productivity values in this experiment are comparable with previous 
findings (Tables 19 and 20). But the duration of ~7 months and ~monthly measuring intervals 
presented here are longer than most. The only real long scale growth trial of P. palmata to my 
knowledge is by Corey et al. (2014). They found markedly lower growth rates (1.1 % d-1, 
FW), but similar overall mean productivity (~20.9 g m-2 d-1 (FW)) as was found in this 
experiment at ~19.7 g m-2 d-1 (FW) when pooling both treatments and including the negative 
productivity of June.  
==PAGE:88==
 
 
 
83 
Previous studies of seasonal variations in P. palmata contents have reported highest dry 
weight contents during late summer and fall and lower values during winter and spring 
(Rødde et al. 2004; Martínez, and Rico 2002). These studies attribute high growth rates 
during spring with lower dry weight content and the summer reduction in growth rates with a 
subsequent increase in dry weight content. The overall trend in the shaded treatment fits this 
pattern of increasing dry weight content and decreasing growth throughout the summer when 
looking at the development from May 27th and onwards. However, during the first month of 
the shaded treatment its dry weight content decreased ~30%. This lowered dry weight content 
at lowered irradiance is in line with a previous study of P. palmata in cultivation (Morgan 
and Simpson 1981b) who found that dry weight contents increased with increasing 
irradiances. It is a possibility that the sudden reduction in irradiance and daily light hours 
caused the drop in dry weight in the shaded treatment and also that the increase in dry weight 
content of the ambient treatment during April could be explained by relatively high 
irradiances. 
 
Studies presenting higher growth rates than this experiment report the use of higher 
irradiances (Table 19), although they present surface irradiances. In this experiment, 
irradiances were measured just below the water surface to try and measure what actually 
reached the algae (Fig. 9), but this complicates direct comparisons. Furthermore, the growth 
of fouling algae reduced irradiance measurements shortly after each weighing interval as was 
seen by visual inspection (Fig. 9) and is evident by peaks in irradiance coinciding with 
measurement dates (Fig. 10). Thus, measured values likely underestimate the actual 
irradiances. 
 
A light attenuation profile conducted in the growth tanks (Fig. 16) reveal somewhat similar 
profiles for a fouled and a cleaned growth tank with ~12% of surface irradiance at 24cm 
depth. Irradiance just below the surface is ~3% higher in the cleaned growth tank which is 
not a large difference when considering the ~monthly cleaning intervals. Fig. 16 also shows 
that ~19-22% of surface irradiance penetrated the water surface of the fouled and cleaned 
growth tanks respectively. These measurements strengthen the evidence that irradiance 
measurements presented in Fig. 10 likely underestimate surface irradiances. According to the 
attenuation profile, the mean daily surface irradiance during light hours would be in the range 
of ~325-490 µmol photons m-2 s-1 during April and May respectively. Furthermore, the 
==PAGE:89==
 
 
 
84 
maximum daily surface irradiances during April and May would be in the range of 7500-
10500 µmol photons m-2 s-1. These are higher values than what reaches Earth at the top of the 
atmosphere, and presumably the real values are somewhere in between. The extrapolation of 
an attenuation profile conducted during a single sunny day to cover two months of varying 
weather conditions is questionable at best, and should only be viewed as a further argument 
that irradiances during this experiment were most likely underestimated, and that direct 
comparisons with earlier studies are not meaningful. 
 
Studies presenting higher productivity always had higher stocking densities (Table 20). Pang 
and Lüning (2004) used almost ten times higher stocking densities, very high surface 
irradiance, cultivated only small tissues of 7-10cm and used weighing intervals of three days 
(nine times more frequent) allowing for a much more precise resolution in terms of growth 
rates and productivity while utilizing the high growth potential of small tissues as stated by 
(Manríquez-Hernández et al. 2016). Matos et al. (2006) achieved yields close to three times 
higher with only twice the stocking density at ambient light conditions during May, and they 
used growth tanks ten times bigger.  
 
More frequent weighing intervals would allow for better management of stocking densities 
and for bleaching issues to be detected and handled earlier and thus reducing the likelihood of 
bleaching and tissue disintegration leading to negative growth rates and productivity. Bigger 
growth tanks would alleviate problems with large tissues disrupting optimal tumbling leading 
to bleaching issues (Fig. 12) and poorer growth conditions. Furthermore, they would act as 
better buffers against unwanted temperature fluctuations due to their larger volume. The 
combination of more frequent weighing intervals and bigger growth tanks would allow for 
higher stocking densities, as they could be maintained at a steady level through frequent 
harvests and the tumbling would not be blocked even by large fronds. Higher stocking 
densities would in turn provide more self-shading, leading to less bleaching issues even at 
high irradiances while at the same time reducing growth of unwanted epiphytes.  
 
This experiment has shown that P. palmata growth rates and productivity comparable to 
previous studies can be achieved in a low maintenance system even at low salinities, but high 
yields and high dry weight content require high irradiances. Summer growth of P. palmata is 
possible even at temperatures measured above 20 °C, although its growth is low and survival 
==PAGE:90==
 
 
 
85 
is presumably linked to low irradiances. Epiphytic growth is very low as long as high growth 
rates are maintained or strong shading is applied. Flow through systems using high water 
exchange rates provide nutrient supply in excess when compared to closed systems, 
especially the ones with infrequent water exchanges like the 14 days used in the lab 
experiment. P. palmata collected from wild populations develops very large thalli which can 
obstruct tumbling if growth tanks are sized too small.  
 
Further research, using weekly to semi-weekly weighing intervals with a focus on higher 
stocking densities, larger tank size and/or possibly on-growth of un-attached seedlings with a 
tumble friendly morphology should be conducted. Furthermore cost effective cultivation by 
intermittent aeration as proposed by Caines et al. (2014) as well as growth induction and 
reduction of epiphytic fouling by short day treatment as proposed by Gomez and Lüning 
(2001) could be looked into. 
 
5.3.2 Fluorescence measurements 
During May there was a clear and strong effect of the shaded treatment on photosynthetic 
performance and stress parameters. The photosynthetic performance was overall comparable 
to previous findings for P. palmata collected in Denmark (Sebök et al. 2017). The lowered 
irradiance levels in the shaded treatment resulted in lowered alpha, Ik and Fv/Fm as well as 
lowered Y(NO) indicating less photoinhibition (Holzinger et al. 2004) and lowered stress 
levels (Cordi et al. 1999) compared to ambient. ETRmax was statistically similar for both 
treatments, but had very large variation within especially the ambient treatment which 
effectively eliminated the numerically observable pattern of lowered ambient ETRmax. 
Growth and productivity data clearly shows that the ambient treatment had a higher growth 
rate and productivity than the shaded and underlines that at least during May stress levels in 
the ambient treatment were not high enough to have a negative effect on growth. 
Furthermore, the increased photosynthetic performance of the shaded treatment was not close 
to outweighing the negative effects of lowered irradiances and daily light hours on growth.  
 
When looking at the different segments during May, it is evident how basal tissues are more 
similar between treatments in terms of photosynthetic performance and stress levels, and also 
how tissues diverge more and more in terms of these parameters when moving towards the 
distal part. Photosynthetic adaptations to different irradiances occur within weeks in P. 
palmata, and saturation irradiance values can be used as an indicator for light acclimation 
==PAGE:91==
 
 
 
86 
status (Sagert and Schubert 1995). When examining the pattern of higher Ik in ambient 
compared to shaded tissues, and increasing Ik from basal to middle and distal tissues for all 
treatments during May, their varying light acclimation status becomes evident and is in line 
with the findings by Sagert and Schubert (1995). But despite the highest light adaptations 
exemplified by highest Ik in ambient distal tissues during May, they still had the lowest 
photosynthetic performance and were the most stressed exemplified by the overall lowest 
ETR, ETRmax, Y(II), Y(NPQ), alpha, Fv/Fm and highest Y(NO). Furthermore, initial high 
values of Y(NO), as seen especially for middle and distal tissues from ambient treatment, 
indicate photodamage to PSII (Klughammer and Schreiber 2008). 
 
When looking at fluorescence data, it is not surprising that the ambient treatment crashed 
during June at higher water temperatures combined with high irradiances. The tissues already 
expressed high levels of stress on the 27th of May and it seems likely that just a slight 
increase in stress levels could tip the balance from survivable to lethal. Lowered Fv/Fm and 
concomitant bleaching of tissues have previously been observed in red macroalgae cultivated 
in finfish effluent at ambient light (Figueroa et al. 2006). When looking at the shaded 
treatment throughout the season there are very few changes in fluorescence parameters with 
only a tendency towards lowered ETRmax in September. This supports the shaded treatment 
growth data and shows that shading provided acceptable growth conditions for P. palmata 
throughout the season, even during summer.  
 
However, when looking at the growth data, it is clear that the shading treatment caused too 
much of a reduction in productivity when compared to ambient and previous studies (Tables 
19 and 20). Ambient sunlight contains UV radiation which can damage both photosynthetic 
components and DNA of marine macroalgae (Franklin and Forster 1997). Previous studies 
have shown that even small doses of UV radiation decrease photosynthetic performance and 
cause increased stress in P. palmata (Cordi et al. 1999; Holzinger et al. 2004). Future 
experiments should preferably focus on a treatment with UV filters to avoid their deleterious 
effects and minimize shading to avoid the negative effects seen on growth rates and 
productivity when compared to ambient.

5.3.3 Tissue analyses 
The contents of all measured elements were in line with previous findings for P. palmata 
from Denmark (Mouritsen et al. 2013; Sloth et al. 2012). The only element found to increase 
during the experiment was As which was highest in the samples from October 29th. Contents 
here were even at a level above the maximum allowed levels for iAs in France (Fig. 29). This 
finding that As increases as tissues age is in line with previous findings (Mouritsen et al. 
2013). However, the percentage of iAs in total As has been reported in previous studies to 
vary from 3.3-17.2% with most values of <5% (Mouritsen et al. 2013; Sloth et al. 2012). 
Assuming the highest percentage with an added safety margin of 100% the content of iAs 
would be 1.47 mg kg-1 (DW) on October 29th which is  ~50% of the French maximum 
allowance. These findings indicate that the cultivation of P. palmata in salmon RAS water 
does not release an excess of undesirable substances.  
 
P. palmata tissues generally thickened, darkened and became tougher throughout the 
experiment as growth rates decreased. Furthermore, as growth abated, concomitant growth of 
epiphytic fouling was increasingly observed. When taking several parameters into account, 
like the increasing contents of As, though presumably still at levels with no risk to human 
health, the dwindling or even negative growth rates and the increasing epiphytic fouling, then 
the optimal harvest time could be after 1-2 months in the experimental setup used. This 
would alleviate the negative effects of prolonged cultivation and focus on the most 
productive period. Future cultivation studies should still ensure low levels of unwanted 
substances according to Monteiro et al. (2019)  and should preferably include more 
substances like kainic acid as it is a substance known to accumulate in P. palmaria .

Several bioremediation studies report removal rates g-1 d-1 (DW) which give a direct measure

(DW) and -0.12-3.34 mgC g-1 d-1 (DW) and are comparable although in the low end. The rate 
of nutrient uptake in macroalgae is directly, through active and passive transport, or 
indirectly, through photosynthesis and growth, affected by factors like surface area to volume 
ratios, temperature, water velocity, irradiance level, nutrient concentration and nutritional 
history (Caines et al. 2014). These factors can complicate direct comparisons between 
studies, especially as they are not always described in detail.

expressed per unit area, then stocking densities become important. Previous studies have 
shown that P. palmata N and C removal rates depend more on growth rates and less on tissue 
N and C contents, as these vary insignificantly when provided with ample nutrient supply 
(Caines et al. 2014; Corey et al. 2012).

for P. palmata cultivated at ambient light. They report N removal rates ~5 times higher and 
productivity ~3 times higher than this experiment. They calculated N removal based on 
reductions in water concentrations and not based on tissue contents, which leaves room for N 
to be removed by other means than just incorporation into P. palmata biomass. Furthermore, 
they did not acclimatize the initial stocking material to the high nutrient concentrations of the 
culturing system, presumably leading to increasing tissue N contents, thus increasing the 
removal rates. These differences could help explain the discrepancy seen between 
productivity and removal rates when comparing with this experiment. But the study reveals 
how higher stocking densities (6 vs 1.8-3.5 g L-1) and high productivity are essential to a high 
bioremediation capacity. Future studies should primarily focus on completing tissue analyses 
for contents of C and N, secondly on setups using higher stocking densities with more 
frequent harvests to maintain younger, smaller tissues with a higher nutrient uptake and 
growth potential.  
 
5.4 Chemical treatment of epiphytic seedling fouling 
The results of this experiment support that NaClO treatment can reduce unwanted epiphytic 
fouling of macroalgae as has previously been shown (Kerrison et al. 2016). It demonstrates 
that a careful dosing of chemical treatment is necessary in order to eliminate epiphytic growth 
without damaging the cultivated species as previously stated by Kerrison et al. (2016). The 
concentrations and treatment times used in this study (0.05 - 0.45% at 10-30-60s) were lower 
than the 0.1-1% for 1-10 min which was suggested as effective treatments by Kerrison et al. 
(2016). Even 10s treatment with 0.45% NaClO proved lethal to P. palmata seedlings in this 
experiment, whereas all treatments with 0.05% ensured 100% seedling survival.  
 
The fact that NaClO treatment damages both the epiphyte and the basiphyte (P. palmata), 
thus repressing growth for weeks onwards as stated by Kerrison et al. (2016), was evident in 
this experiment when comparing growth rates and productivity between exposure times.  
The repression of seedling growth in combination with a very long experimental duration 
with no water exchange allowed the growth of green algae to establish itself in all beakers. 
Green algae then presumably outcompeted the bleached and growth repressed P. palmata 
seedlings in the 0.45% NaClO treatments resulting in a lethal outcome for the seedlings. P. 
==PAGE:95==
 
 
 
90 
palmata treated with 0.05% NaClO were less bleached and less growth repressed thus 
managing to survive and grow despite the competition with the green algae.  
 
The initial seedling epiphyte was a filamentous brown algae (Fig. 17). No treatments left 
visible traces of this epiphyte when the experiment ended, indicating that all treatments 
effectively reduced the growth of this epiphyte.  
 
When comparing growth rates and productivity between this experiment and seedlings grown 
under similar conditions in the lab scale experiment, it is clear that these are lower for 
treatments with 0.05%  NaClO during 30s and 60s. The 10s treatment growth rate was lower, 
and yield (FW) was lower, but dry weight was higher resulting in similar dry weight yields. 
The water exchange rate and nutrient supply in the two experiments were very different. 
Water was exchanged roughly every 14 days in the lab experiment, but there was no water 
exchange in the disinfection experiment for 33 days. When taking the different water 
exchange rates into account, a difference in growth performance could be expected. But, 
similar dry weight yields for P. palmata, treated with 0.05% NaClO during 10s using no 
water exchange, and untreated P. palmata, grown under similar conditions with water 
exchange twice as frequent, indicate that the treatment was effective.  
 
Dry weight contents were higher in the seedlings treated with NaClO, and more so for longer 
treatments, compared to seedlings grown under similar conditions, which could be related to 
their lower growth rates, as it is suggested that high growth rates promote low dry weight 
contents in P. palmata (Martínez, and Rico 2002).  
 
The fact that treatment with just 0.05% NaClO during 10s reduced the growth of initial 
epiphytes on P. palmata seedlings in this experiment, with no negative effects on dry weight 
yields, should be further investigated in more controlled trials with higher water exchange 
rates. This could be coupled to flow through systems, and should include control treatments 
conducted simultanously and not displaced in time. Using a treatment with low dose and low 
exposure time minimizes expenses to chemicals as well as reduces the labour required. At the 
same time it has much less negative effect on growth and productivity of P. palmata 
compared to treatments with higher doses and exposure time.

6. Conclusions 
In the present study, I have investigated different aspects of P. palmata cultivation with a 
focus on land-based aerated tumble cultivation and grow out. I have used a method to 
perform hatchery seeding based on controlling early life history traits, specifically spore 
release and settlement. Further, I have conducted lab-scale assessments of seedling growth 
and pilot scale long term growth resulting in thalli >70cm. Finally disinfection of epiphytic 
fouling of seedlings was tested at lab-scale. 
 
I have tested the growth of small sporelings at lab-scale in a controlled setup testing salmon 
RAS water against conventional growth media enriched water. In addition, I have examined 
growth and performance of P. palmata in the IMTA concept through integrated cultivation of 
seaweed and finfish, by cultivation in the system water from a salmon RAS unit. In both 
studies, I manipulated irradiance levels. Measurements of growth and productivity were the 
primary evaluation criteria, in combination with chlorophyll fluorescence measurements of 
photosynthetic performance. 
  
Epiphyte growth is a challenge in land-based grow out of seaweeds. I addressed this issue by 
comparing the effect of using different concentrations and exposure times of a chemical 
disinfection agent attempting to clean small P. palmata seedlings which suffered from 
epiphytic fouling prior to a growth experiment. 
 
Fertile tetrasporophytes collected at the beach face at Fornæs lighthouse provided low spore 
release yields in the lab, presumably because of several uncontrolled factors including air 
exposure, rehydration from tides and spore release during storage. The low spore release 
yield presumably resulted in low spore settlement densities on the growth substrates, which 
may be improved by increasing the spore release time. Despite low spore yields and short 
seeding times a low amount of sori proved capable of seeding the growth substrates, using 
agitated water conditions in a closed seeding tank, with initial seeding counts comparable to 
previous studies. Previous studies have shown that spore release in P. palmata is 
unpredictable and highly variable according to ripeness of sori. The spores are large and 
immobile which pose a challenge in obtaining an even settlement density on the growth 
substrates. Future research in closed agitated seeding systems, using small amounts of sorus 
==PAGE:97==
 
 
 
92 
tissue and long seeding times may provide a viable alternative to standard gravitation seeding 
systems. Furthermore, excess spores should be used in GMA seeding or cultivated as un-
attached seedlings. 
 
Lab scale growth experiments proved high growth potential of un-attached P. palmata 
seedlings, even higher than in comparable previous studies, likely due to the small seedling 
size and low initial stocking densities. The lab experiment showed that, seedlings cultured in 
RAS system water yielded lower growth rates than cultivation in conventional 100% F/2 
enriched water, yet still higher than in comparable previous studies. Irradiances above 230 
PAR caused the first bleaching of seedlings cultivated in conventional growth media, 
followed a few weeks later by bleaching of seedlings at 180 PAR. Seedlings cultivated in 
RAS water maintained their red color throughout the experiment. The fluorescence 
measurements of seedlings grown in the lab experiment revealed overall low photosynthetic 
performance for all tissues. All samples had high proportions of non-regulated non-
photochemical quenching (Y(NO)) indicating photodamage. Tissues cultivated at higher 
irradiances had the lowest photosynthetic performance exemplified by lowered alpha and 
Fv/Fm in tissues cultured at 230 and 180 PAR compared to 100 PAR. Furthermore, P. 
palmata seedlings grown in RAS water showed a tendency towards higher levels of 
photosynthetic stress parameters when compared to seedlings grown in 100% F/2 enriched 
water despite healthier looking tissues. Growth of epiphytes was observed throughout this lab 
experiment, seemingly increased by higher irradiances and 100% F/2 enriched water.  
 
The pilot scale growth experiment provided valuable insights in low maintenance flow-
through systems using ambient light and salmon RAS water. Ambient light conditions 
resulted in the highest growth rates but also caused bleaching, disintegration of tissues and 
negative growth rates and productivity when combined with high temperatures. The shaded 
treatment with 90% reduction of ambient light had significantly lower growth rates and 
productivity compared to the ambient treatment, but was able to survive high summer 
temperatures with constant positive growth rates and productivity.  
 
Fluorescence measurements revealed a clear adaptation/phenotypic acclimation in the two 
light groups and pointed at lowered photosynthetic performance and high stress levels in 
ambient tissues shortly prior to their bleaching and disintegration. Once the growth abated in 
==PAGE:98==
 
 
 
93 
the ambient group, epiphytic fouling of fronds occurred. The amount of epiphytes was lower 
in the shaded treatment.  
 
Tissue contents of tested substances (As, Cd, Pb, Hg) proved to be within acceptable limits 
regarding food safety. This suggests that  P. palmata can be cultivated in IMTA with land-
based salmon RAS. Only the content of arsenic increased over time, indicating that 
cultivation time should be lower than the experimental duration. Thus, future studies should 
investigate a cultivation strategy using shorter culture periods, by regular restocking with new 
young tissues. Such a method might secure high growth rates during the whole year, which 
potentially increases the bioremediation capacity of C and N. The present estimates of C and 
N removal rates reveal that P. palmata may be useful but is depended on high growth rates 
and productivity.  
 
Chemical disinfection using a concentration of 0.05 % NaClO in a 10-second exposure 
treatment proved effective in repressing the initial growth of epiphytes. This decontamination 
treatment showed only limited negative impact on growth rate and no negative impact on dry 
weight yield when compared to untreated seedlings grown under similar conditions.  
 
Overall, the present study showed that by utilizing ambient light, IMTA of P. palmata in 
salmon RAS is a potential method to propagate P. palmata biomass, but requires further 
research. The present study provided good results for further development of P. palmata 
IMTA in land-based fish production. Focus on efficient hatchery techniques is required, aside 
from seeded substrates these should provide cultures of un-attached seedlings which have 
high growth potential and a tumble friendly morphology. These un-attached seedlings could 
be kept at low light low nutrient conditions to provide a constant supply for flow through 
growth trials using RAS water. Growth trials should focus on stocking densities, optimal 
growth tank size and harvest time as well as possibilities of light regime manipulations and 
UV-filtration with weekly to semi-weekly weighing and maintenance intervals. Chemical 
treatment with low dose low and exposure times should be implemented in growth trials to 
further examine its effects on growth, productivity and reduction of epiphytic fouling. 
Fluorescence measurements could provide valuable insights in photosynthetic capacity and 
stress responses to several factors like irradiance levels, light regimes, temperature, UV-
radiation as well as effects of chemical treatments and help reduce issues with tissue 
bleaching and disintegration.
