Abstract
Despite the continuous growth of the renewable energy sector, the world’s energy demand is rising,
pushing the oil and gas industry towards the exploration of more extreme environments encompassing
high pressure and high temperature (HPHT) conditions. This comprises a need for high-performance
anti-corrosive epoxy coatings to withstand the hostile environment. In the present study different
silicone derivatives were used to chemically modify the curing agents of an amine-cured epoxy novolac
system and the performance and degradation of the resultant epoxy-silicone hybrid system at HPHT
conditions were investigated. Recent studies have revealed a softening effect of the hydrocarbon
phase in an HPHT environment on the epoxy novolac network, caused by high afﬁnity between the
aromatic novolac system and the aromatic hydrocarbon phase [1]. The aim of this study was remove
some of the aromatic content in the coating network by replacing the DGEBA currently used for
adduction of the curing agent with a silicone derivative, which has the potential to further enhance
the thermal stability, chemical resistance, and ﬂexibility of the system. Three different silicone deriva-
tives were tested for the adduction, i.e. one long-chain siloxane and two short-chain silanes. The
silicone adducted systems were applied to a steel substrate, cured, and then exposed to an HPHT
environment simulated by a closed batch reactor comprising three distinctive phases (a gas phase of
N2 and CO2, a hydrocarbon phase of p-xylene and an artiﬁcial seawater phase). After exposure, the
coating degradation was analysed by Digital Microscopy (DM), Scanning Electron Microscopy (SEM)
coupled with Energy-Dispersive X-ray analysis (EDX), Attenuated Total Reﬂection-Fourier Transform
Infrared Spectroscopy (ATR-FTIR), and Differential Scanning Calorimetry (DSC). The addition of a
siloxane adduct to the system resulted in a Tg increase of 9 °C compared to the conventional DGEBA
adducted epoxy novolac system. No considerable change in Tg was found for the silane adducted
samples. The gas phase exposed zone remained predominantly intact for all systems after exposure
to the HPHT environment. Thus, the high-pressure N2 and the supercritical CO2 did not have any
signiﬁcant detrimental effect on the system. Previous research showed a decrease in Tg of 20 °C in
the hydrocarbon zone for a standard DGEBA adducted system, resulting from softening of the epoxy
network due to resin-hydrocarbon interactions [1]. Replacing DGEBA was expected to reduce the
afﬁnity between the coating network and the p-xylene in the hydrocarbon phase. However, SEM
revealed the formation of micro pinholes in this zone, suggesting that some interactions had taken place.
Despite this, the DSC analysis presented no signiﬁcant Tg depression in the hydrocarbon exposed zone,
suggesting that the softening had been mitigated by the addition of a silicone adduct. A slight decrease
in Tg was, however, found in the sea water exposed zone for both the siloxane and the silane adducted
systems. The aforementioned study reported a formation of iron oxide in the hydrocarbon-seawater

but a trace amount of iron were detected by the EDX analysis. Thus, the underﬁlm corrosion was not

Problem description
Although the renewable energy sector is growing, fossil fuels are still of great importance to satisfy
the world’s energy demand. The rapid depletion of resources in the oil and gas industry has forced
the industry to explore more hostile environments comprising high pressure and high temperature
conditions (HPHT). The extreme conditions and corrosive species present in these environments
create great challenges for the materials utilised in these processes. This constitutes a demand for
high-performance anti-corrosive coatings to protect the material from degradation and avoid early
replacement of equipment. High-performance epoxy-based coatings are the most commonly used
coating system for these purposes, typically epoxy novolac or the diglycidyl ether of bisphenol F
(DGEBF). However, previous studies performed at CoaST, DTU, has shown some deﬁciencies in the
coating system both for novolac and DGEBF, when exposed to HPHT conditions [1]. Although both
systems showed some defects, superior properties was observed for the novolac system, compared
to the DGEBF system under HPHT conditions. The DGEBF system showed loss of adhesion in the
seawater phase when exposed to HPHT, but only blisters are observed for the novolac system and
the coating was still intact. Both systems showed corrosion in the interface between the hydrocarbon
phase and the seawater phase. The observed corrosion is presumably caused by softening of the epoxy
network due to afﬁnity towards the solvent p-xylene, which subsequently allows corrosive species to
enter the coating system and corrode the substrate [1]. This present study aims at improving the water
and solvent resistance of the epoxy resin system, as well as the thermal stability of the system, by
formulating a silicone-epoxy hybrid system. Previous studies have stated that silicone shows promising
performance when added to an epoxy resin system, due to its ability to enhance the thermal stability,
chemical resistance and ﬂexibility of the system. Additionally, replacing some of the aromatic groups
in the formulation with silicone derivatives may reduce the interactions between the hydrocarbon phase
in the HPHT conditions and the resin system. Chemical modiﬁcation of the epoxy system can be
performed in two different ways, either by modifying the epoxy resin or the curing agent. The present
study, aims at chemically modifying the curing agent, also known as adduction. The silicone derivative
will be pre-reacted with an amine curing agent, resulting in a silicone adducted amine. Adding the
silicone derivatives as an adduct can enhance the compatibility between the silicone and the epoxy resin,
which can further lead to a more homogeneous distribution of silicone derivatives in the epoxy network
system. The purpose is to formulate a silicone-epoxy hybrid coating system for a wide variety of HPHT
applications including combustion chambers, waste incinerators, heat exchangers, and reformer catalyst
tube, in addition to the oil and gas industry. The performance of the silicone-epoxy hybrid system
will be tested using an HPHT batch reactor fabricated at DTU, which simulates the HPHT conditions.

Introduction
The world’s energy demand is increasing and will continue to be satisﬁed primarily by fossil fuels,
despite the vast growth in renewable energy technologies. Many years of oil drilling has lead to
a gradual depletion of resources which pushes the industry to explore new oil and gas reservoirs
with more hostile downhole conditions, often referred to as high pressure and high temperature
(HPHT) wells. Although the oil and gas industry has many years of experience in working with
elevated temperatures, there are limited studies concerning the interrelationship between pressure and
temperature [2]. Exploiting these wells create challenges regarding the materials and technologies used
for the drilling. The harsh environment signiﬁcantly accelerate the degradation and corrosion of the
involved materials, which can highly reduce the lifetime of the equipment. To avoid this phenomenon,
high-performance epoxy-based coatings have been widely used for the protection of metal substrates.
The extensive utilization of epoxy resin systems in the coating industry is attributed to its high thermal
resistance, moderate solvent and chemical resistance, and excellent adhesion properties. However, the
effects of the harsh HPHT conditions on the epoxy coating system remains largely unexplored.
Previous studies performed in CoaST, DTU, [3] conﬁrmed that introducing silane chemistry into the
epoxy network increased the resin-substrate interactions, thus improving the adhesion properties of
the system. The addition of silicone derivatives to an epoxy system can have a key role in expanding
the range of both physical and chemical properties of the conventional epoxy system [4]. The focus
of this study is to research the effect of these silicone-epoxy hybrid systems in terms of resistance
towards water permeation, hydrocarbon interactions and thermal stability under exposure to HPHT
conditions. More speciﬁcally, the focus will be on pre-reacting the amine curing agent with different
silicone derivatives. A method commonly known as adduction. These silicone adducted amines should
provide great homogenity of silicone throughout the epoxy network, providing enhanced properties for
the complete silicone-epoxy hybrid system. The applicability and durability of these high performance
hybrid systems at HPHT conditions will be investigated, and the degradation pathway will be assessed
by different characterisation techniques.

2.1
Coatings Technology
Coatings have been used for centuries both for aesthetic purposes and to provide surface protection.
Although, coatings are an ancient technology, new products are constantly being developed with new
and more advanced functionalities. In general, a coating can be described as a thin covering layer that
is applied on the surface of an object, often referred to as a substrate. However, in reality this thin
covering layer is a complex mixture of chemical substances, each providing different functionalities
to the coating system [5]. These substances can be divided into the following broad categories:
binder, solvent, pigments, extenders, and additives. The chemical, physical and mechanical properties
of a coating system can be manipulated by careful considerations of the choice of each of these
substances [6].
The binder or resin works as the ﬁlm-forming agent which solidiﬁes through either physical or chemical
processes and binds together the other components in the coating [7]. The choice of binder plays an
extensive role in determining both the chemical and physical properties of the coating. Some of the
most commercially used binders are epoxy, polyurethane, and acrylic polymers. The solvent is added
to the system to ensure the desired viscosity of the coating. It is a volatile component that evaporates
during and after application. Typically used solvents are aromatic and aliphatic hydrocarbons, esters,
ethers, or alcohols. For some applications, water can be used as the volatile component (water-borne
coatings), which is becoming more desirable as it reduces the environmental impact of the volatile
organic compounds (VOCs). The pigments are insoluble solid particles ﬁnely dispersed in the coating.
The main functionalities of the pigment are to provide colour and opacity to the coating [5]. However,
pigments can also be added for other reasons, such as enhancing corrosion and heat resistance or
providing strength and barrier properties. The pigments which are added to improve the mechanical
properties of the coating system are often called extenders or ﬁllers. Additives are a component which
is added to the system in very small quantities to give the coating desirable characteristics. Examples
of additives include anti-foaming agents, plasticisers, UV absorber, or heat stabilizers.
As previously mentioned, coatings are widely used to prevent corrosion on metallic substrates by
blocking the permeation of ions. Anti-corrosive coating systems are usually composed of multiple
layers of coatings with different functionalities and purposes. The system typically consists of a primer,
one or more intermediate coats, and a topcoat, where the individual layers can be metallic, inorganic, or
organic. The protective mechanism of an anti-corrosive coating can be classiﬁed into three categories:
barrier effect, inhibitive effect, and galvanic effect. The barrier effect is acquired by applying a coating
2
==PAGE:14==

system with low permeability of ions, thus hindering contact between the aggressive species and the
substrate. Barrier coatings are characterised by an inert pigmentation, with a low volume concentration
of pigments such as titanium dioxide, micaceous iron oxide, or glass ﬂakes [6]. This low pigment
volume concentration provides a dense and cohesive coating with low permeability towards aggressive
species. The inhibitive effect involves passivation of the substrate by creating a protective chemical
conversion layer of pigments. The inhibitive pigments are generally inorganic salts consisting of
cations such as phosphates, chromates, molybdates, nitrates and silicates [6]. The pigments act as a
barrier by ensuring sufﬁcient leaching from the coating. The galvanic, also called sacriﬁcial, effect
occurs when the substrate is protected by a metal or alloy which is electrochemically more active than
the metal substrate. Metallic zinc powder is often used to provide this galvanic effect for corrosion
protection of steel. These coatings are only applied as primers, where the zinc act as an anode and
sacriﬁce itself for the protection of the metal, which becomes a cathode [6]. The performance and
robustness of an anti-corrosive coating system exposed to HPHT conditions are difﬁcult to assess, as
there are several external and internal factors involved. These factors will be further discussed in the
following sections.
2.2
High Pressure High Temperature Conditions
As previously mentioned, the search for hydrocarbons are becoming more extreme in terms of depths,
pressure, and temperatures. These harsh conditions can accelerate corrosion and degradation of the
exposed equipment and thereby highly affect the structural integrity of the material. The term HPHT is
mainly employed when referring to oil drilling of wells under extreme conditions. There are currently
no industry-wide accepted standards deﬁning HPHT conditions and the related interrelationship
between temperature and pressure. The term might also be applied in reference to wells that only
have one of the extreme conditions, either high pressure or high temperature. In previous years,
HPHT was attributed to any condition with pressure or temperature above the atmospheric conditions.
Currently, various deﬁnitions depends on the engineering purpose or the geographical location of the
well. The most common HPHT deﬁnition is when the pressure exceeds 10,000 psi (680 bar) and/or the
temperature exceeds 300 °F (149 °C) [8]. Another commonly used classiﬁcation system has segmented
the operations into three main tiers. An example of this approach, made by Schlumberger is shown
in Figure 1. The division is based on commonly encountered technology thresholds, related to the
behavior of standard elastomeric seals [9].

2.3
Epoxy Resin Systems for HPHT
The hostile and corrosive environment in the HPHT wells makes corrosion a pervasive challenge in
the oil and gas industry. The speciﬁc requirements for an anti-corrosive coating system are highly
dependant on the environment that the coating may be exposed to during service. The application of
highly cross-linked anti-corrosive epoxy coatings has shown great performance in this matter, due to
excellent adhesion to metals, high heat resistance, moderate water and chemical resistance, and good
mechanical properties [6].
Epoxy resins are known as a highly versatile material and are used in a wide variety of industries
due to its unique properties. The most common applications are as adhesives, coatings, or composite
materials. It is a high-performance thermosetting resin, usually characterised as a group of oligomeric
materials which contains one or more epoxy group. The epoxy group is a three-membered ring, also
known as epoxide or oxirane, shown in Figure 3, which may lie within the backbone but is usually
placed as an end-group [11].
Figure 3: The chemical structure of an epoxy functional group.
The three-membered epoxy ring is highly strained which raises its internal energy, thus increasing the
reactivity towards many chemical species, especially proton donors. In order to obtain a completely
cross-linked structure, the precursor resin need to have two or more functional groups per molecule.
The cross-linking occurs by a condensation mechanism with a curing agent, also known as a hardener,
typically an amine or an acid anhydride, resulting in a thermoset structure. This process is formally
known as curing. A disadvantage of this process is that many precursor materials requires heat for
completion and the curing rate may be signiﬁcantly slower than for example a free radical curing
system [11]. In addition to the reaction with the curing agent, further cross-linking may occur either
by epoxy reacting with the formed hydroxyl groups or with themselves in the presence of elevated

curing.
The epoxy base usually consists of an aliphatic, cycloaliphatic, or aromatic backbone. The choice of
backbone structure highly affects the properties of the resin. The resin can be categorised into different
types based on the molecular structure and properties, speciﬁcally glycidyl epoxy and non-glycidyl
epoxy. Non-glycidyl epoxies can be either aliphatic or cycloaliphatic, where the aliphatic has a linear
structure with an epoxy group attached and the cyclo aliphatic is a closed structure [12]. The most
common glycidyl epoxy resin is the glycidyl ethers, which has the chemical structure shown in Figure

in many commercial products. Glycidyl ethers have shown great performance for anti-corrosive
purposes and will be the emphasis of this present study.

The most commercial and commonly used epoxy resin is diglycidyl ether of bisphenol A (DGEBA).
DGEBA is synthesised from the reaction of bisphenol A and an excess of epichlorohydrin in the

2.3.2
Types of Curing Agent for Epoxy
As mentioned, the epoxy functional group is highly reactive towards various chemicals, which enables
the ability to readily transform from a thermoplastic liquid to a hard thermoset solid. This conversion is
achieved by the addition of a curing agent. The choice of curing agent can highly affect the properties
of the cross-linked product. Curing agents can participate both directly in the reaction and chemically
bond to the resin system or act as a catalytic promoter. The curing process can either happen at room
temperature with heat from the exothermic reaction, or with external heating, depending on the curing
agent.
Typical curing agents used for epoxy curing are amines, amides, acid anhydrides, and mercaptants.
Amines are one of the most frequently used curing agents and possesses the best properties in regards
to HPHT application. One explanation for this is that the other curing agents contains a carbonyl
group which can create challenges due to the existence of CO2 in the gas phase of HPHT conditions.
Additionally, anhydrides and amides are unstable at high temperature conditions and will therefore not
perform optimal in an HPHT environment. Another explanation is the highly cross-linking nature of
amines. The curing reaction of epoxy with an amine happens in two steps. Firstly, the primary amine
reacts with an epoxy group, forming a secondary amine and a hydroxyl group. In the second step, the
secondary amine reacts with another epoxy group and forms a tertiary amine and another hydroxyl
group. The mechanism for this reaction is shown in Figure 7.

The primary amines are more reactive than the secondary amines, due to steric hindrance, and will
therefore react ﬁrst. During the previously mentioned post-curing process, tertiary amines can work as
a catalytic promoter which together with external heating enables the reaction between epoxy and the
formed hydroxyl groups, see Figure 8. This process creates a tightly packed network structure, which
shows excellent chemical resistance and heat resistance, making it ideal for HPHT conditions.
Figure 8: The reaction between epoxy and an hydroxyl group in the post-curing process [11].
Amines can come in many different structures, either aliphatic, cycloaliphatic, or aromatic. The choice
of structure highly affects the performance of the ﬁnal coating. Many different variables need to
be considered when selecting the optimal curing agent for a system. Aliphatic amines has a high
reactivity (fast curing) at ambient temperature, due to the lack of steric hindrance in the molecular
structure of the compound. However, the aromatic and cycloaliphatic amine curing agents contains
bulky groups which increases heat resistance and mechanical strength of the system. Aromatic amines
are found to be toxic and therefore the use of these amines have been restricted. Cycloaliphatic amines
have, however, shown great performance, and in addition to heat resistance, it also shows a superior
chemical resistance compared to aliphatic amines [15]. All of these parameters need to be carefully
considered when selecting the optimal system for HPHT applications. The different factors affecting
the anti-corrosive behaviour of the epoxy system in an HPHT environment will be further discussed in
the following section.

2.4
Anti-corrosion Behavior of Epoxy Systems at HPHT
For the purpose of understanding the anti-corrosive behavior of an epoxy system under HPHT condi-
tions it is important to consider each factor involved in the exposure environment. High-performance
epoxy-based coatings exhibits excellent resistance towards corrosion and heat as well as great me-
chanical strength. However, during oil and gas extraction the process equipments are exposed to
several different factors which can impact the performance of the coating. In addition to the elevated
temperature and pressure, highly corrosive components are present in these wells which can accelerate
the chemical degradation of the system. These components are a combination of three different phases,
including a gas phase and two liquid phases i.e. hydrocarbon and saline liquids (seawater). There are
very limited research on the combined effect of these phases on the epoxy coating system at HPHT
conditions. Therefore, the effect of each individual factor will be discussed in the following section, in
order to obtain a broader understanding of the degradation mechanism of the coating system and the
parameters involved.
2.4.1
Effect of Temperature and Pressure
Both temperature and pressure has an immense effect on the degradability and durability of a coating
system. The thermal degradation of an epoxy network is highly connected to the glass transition
temperature (Tg) of the material. Tg represents the temperature at which there is an increase in the
thermal expansion coefﬁcient. In more physical terms, Tg is the temperature where atoms in the
molecules start vibrating with increasing energy as a result of heating [5]. The vibrations increase
the thermal expansion coefﬁcient which generally leads to an increase in speciﬁc volume, giving a
higher degree of freedom. For highly cross-linked thermoset systems the movements in the polymer is
restrained even at high temperatures. Thus, the polymer will not melt, but endure a slight softening
transition due an increase of molecular mobility. This softening will highly impact the functional
properties of the coating system, such as the chemical and water permeation resistance. When coatings
are exposed to such complex systems as found in the HPHT wells there are many factors which need to
be considered. Due to the elevated temperatures present in these systems a high Tg would be preferable.
A higher Tg can be manufactured by increasing the degree of cross-linkage and by the choice of epoxy
resin, curing agent and other components in the system. However, by increasing Tg the ﬂexibility of
the system will decrease, making it more brittle. Both of these factors needs to be carefully assessed
when deciding on the optimal parameters.
There are very few studies researching the effect of high pressure on epoxy-based coatings. However, a

study performed by Liu et al. [18] showed that increasing the pressure from 1 atm to 35 atm accelerated

It was also found that an increase of cross-link density of the epoxy system decreased the diffusion

the brittleness of a high Tg system can lead to cracking during pressure build up and release. Thus,
elevated pressure can affect the water permeation, chemical resistance and might lead to cracking of
the coating network.
2.4.2
Effect of Seawater
Immersion of the process equipment in seawater is another important factor to consider. Seawater
contains a high content of dissolved salt, primarily sodium chloride, which possesses highly aggressive
corrosion promoting properties towards metals [6]. Besides, the salinity of the water may lead to
degradation such as osmotic blisters and thereby affect the adhesion or mechanical properties of the
coating [20]. If water permeates through the coating system, corrosion may occur on the metal surface
and directly affect the adhesion of the coating system. Jie et al. [21] investigated water transport
behaviour of epoxy coating at hydrostatic pressure of seawater. From the study it was observed that
higher pressure accelerated the diffusion rate of seawater into the epoxy coating, as well as accelerating
physical failure of the coating network. However, no changes were observed in the chemical structure
as a result of seawater permeation. Increasing hydrophobicity and cross-link density of the coating
system are examples of measures which could lead to enhanced water barrier properties.
2.4.3
Effect of Hydrocarbons
One of the aforementioned liquid phases present in oil and gas extraction under HPHT conditions is
hydrocarbons. Apart from oil and gas applications, organic solvents are present in many industries
and can thereby affect the durability of the anti-corrosive coating system. In this study, the main
emphasis will be on the aromatic hydrocarbon p-xylene, which is a common component in the oil
and gas industry. A previous study performed by Subramanian et al. [20] examined the effect of the
aliphatic hydrocarbon (n-parafﬁn) and the aromatic hydrocarbon (p-xylene) on a DGEBF and a novolac

epoxy system at HPHT conditions. The study showed that p-xylene had a more blistering effect on
the coating system than n-parafﬁn, which might be caused by a strong afﬁnity between the aromatic

organic solvents in different cured epoxy systems. The study revealed that in addition to the effect of
afﬁnity and hydrogen bonding, the Tg of the epoxy network had an immense effect on the sorption of

to diminish the hydrocarbon interaction.
2.4.4
Effect of CO2
The presence of CO2 in HPHT wells can have a detrimental effect on the epoxy coating network. A
previous study investigated the corrosive effect of CO2 on an epoxy-steel interface at HPHT conditions.
Accelerated corrosion was observed due to the presence of CO2 [23]. At HPHT conditions CO2
is expected to be present in its supercritical state due to its low critical temperature (31.1 °C) and
pressure (75 bar) [24]. In the supercritical state, the component possesses the density of a liquid,
while maintaining the ﬂow properties of a gas. This increases the barrier penetration ability leading
to a reduction of the Tg of the cross-linked network [25]. In addition to the effects caused by the
supercritical properties, the presence of CO2 in water can increase the hydrogen ion concentration by
the formation of carbonic acid [26], as shown in Equation 1, 2 and 3.

2.4.5
Rapid Gas Decompression
Rapid gas decompression is a phenomenon that occurs under depressurisation to ambient conditions.
This phenomenon will typically take place in the oil extraction process at HPHT conditions and it can
lead to different failures such as volume change, crack initiation, and crack growth of the cross-linked
network. The rapid gas decompression process can be divided into two steps, the pressurisation step
and the depressurisation step. In the pressurisation step two effects occurs. One is the plasticisation

matrix due to high pressure and high temperature. This can result in increased backbone movements
and an increase in free volume, thereby leading to a decrease in Tg. The other effect is the compression
effect from the high pressure gasses, which has the opposite behaviour compared to the ﬁrst effect. In
the second step, the system is depressurised to ambient conditions, resulting in a high volume change
of the components [27]. The occurring failures are mainly caused by this quick release of the gases
dissolved in the coating system, leading to stress in the material and rupture of the component.

2.5
Silicone Derivatives for Epoxy Hybrid Coatings
The harsh environment present under HPHT conditions requires high-performance coatings with
superior mechanical, thermal, and anticorrosive properties. Although epoxy-based coatings acquire
excellent characteristics for these environments, there is still potential for improvement. The shortcom-
ings of epoxy coatings include low fracture energy [28], poor hydrophobicity [29], and low weathering
resistance. Additional components can therefore be added to modify the epoxy system and improve
these properties. Such components are typically polyurethane, silicone, or other thermoplastics.
Silicone derivatives used for epoxy hybrid coatings have attracted considerable attention in recent
years. A silicon molecule has dual reactivity where both organic and inorganic reactive groups can be
synthesised into one molecule, see Figure 9. The application of organic-inorganic hybrid polymers
is of increasing interest as it offers a unique combination of properties. Epoxy-silicone polymers
offer a variety of unique properties including superior thermal and thermo-oxidative stability, lower
water permeability, enhanced ﬂexibility, UV resistance, chemical resistance, improved weatherability,
and higher corrosion resistance [30–32]. Addition of silicone derivatives will also replace a certain
percentage of aromatic structure in the network system, resulting in a lower compatibility between the
resin system and the hydrocarbons in the HPHT environment. Decreasing this compatibility could be
a promising measure for avoiding Tg depression resulting from hydrocarbon-resin interactions. The
following sections will further explore the use of silicone derivatives for epoxy hybrid coatings.
Figure 9: Illustration of the dual reactivity of silanes [33].

substituents attached to a single silicon atom. This monomeric silicon compound is commonly known
as silane and it typically consists of three inorganic-reactive alkoxy groups, either methoxy or ethoxy,
and one organic group, as shown in Equation 4.

The organic group, R, can be either reactive (epoxy, amino, methacrylate, etc.) or unreactive (methyl,
butyl, phenyl, etc.). The OR’ group is the reactive methoxy or ethoxy group. For this study, the
emphasis will be on silanes attached to an organofunctional group, primarily an amine or epoxy
group. The inorganic reactivity of the silicon atom is caused by its electropositive nature. Com-
mon silane substituents such as amine, methoxy, or hydroxyl can react with inorganic materials
which contain hydroxyl groups, such as −Si −OH and metal −OH [33]. Silanol, −Si −OH, or
silylamine, −Si −NH2, are highly unstable and will quickly condense to siloxane, Si −O −Si, or
Silazane, −Si −NH −Si−, respectively [33]. This gives silanes excellent functionalities as adhesion
promoters or coupling agents for coating systems. An organic spacer group will typically separate the
organofunctional group from the silicon atom [33]. The organofunctional group can bond with the
resin and the silane group can bond to the substrate. This reaction involves four steps as shown in
Figure 10. In the ﬁrst step, the silane reacts with water to hydrolyse the three labile alkoxy groups.
This is then followed by condensation to oligomers, which further creates hydrogen bonds with the
-OH groups on the substrate. Finally, a covalent linkage can be formed with the substrate during drying
or curing and water is liberated [34].
Figure 10: Schematic illustration of the silane coupling agent reaction with an inorganic substrate [35].
Through this reaction, silanes form a strongly absorbed polysiloxane ﬁlm on the metal surface. The
chemical and mechanical integrity of the ﬁlm can be manipulated by changing parameters such as
solution concentration, pH, drying time, and temperature. Silane as adhesion promoters increases
the durability by increasing the initial bond strength and stabilise the surface, making the bonded
joint more permanent in moist aging environments [36]. The silanes can be applied directly to the
substrate, as a conventional primer, or mixed into the polymer matrix. Amine functional silanes or
epoxy-functional silanes can be added either to the curing agent or to the epoxy resin and thereby
participate in the curing mechanism. When mixed into the coating, the silane coupling agent will
migrate to the interface between the substrate and coating [37]. The addition of silane into the epoxy
matrix can therefore enhance the properties of the network system, making it more suitable for HPHT
conditions.

2.5.2
Amine Functional Silane
Silanes, as discussed, can be used to modify epoxy resin to obtain desired properties such as better
adhesion, hydrophobicity, weather resistance, and ﬂexibility. For this purpose, amine functional
silanes are frequently employed as they are highly reactive with various resins and polymers. An
organo-functional alkoxysilane with an amine functional group such as 3-Aminopropyl-triethoxysilane
(APTES) is commonly used for coating systems, see Figure 11.
Figure 11: The chemical structure of an 3-Aminopropyl-triethoxysilane (APTES) [38].
APTES is a highly attractive compound for silicone epoxy hybrid systems. The three reactive metal
groups lie closely together which enhances both reactivity and ﬂexibility of the molecule. Subsequently,
the organic spacer group has a critical length of three methylene units providing limited interfacial
ﬂexibility [38]. APTES can easily react with an epoxy group in the resin, forming a silicone epoxy
hybrid system, as illustrated Figure 12.
Figure 12: A schematic illustration of the reaction between an epoxy and the amine functional silane, APTES
[29].
Amine functional silanes can be used in several different ways e.g. pretreatment of resin, hardener
or ﬁllers, or blended with the hardener, creating a two-part curing agent. The treatment of ﬁllers
helps improve the dispersibility of the ﬁllers as well as enhancing other properties such as ﬂexibility
and water resistance. There are numerous different amine functional silanes available on the market,
providing different functionalities to the coating network. APTES is one of the most common, but other
mono-, di- and triamine functional silanes are also used in coating systems. For certain applications, a
blocked amine functional silane is preferred due to excellent storage stability in resin systems. The
blocked amine group can be activated by the presence of moisture [39].

Epoxy Terminated Silicone Resin
Epoxy terminated silicone resins generally include a higher molecular weight siloxane chain with a

2.5.5
Silicone Epoxy Hybrid Resin
As mentioned, there are multiple ways of formulating silicone epoxy hybrid systems. Several studies
have investigated the effect of adding silicone derivatives into epoxy resin systems. Ananda Kumar
et al. [42] researched the effect of adding polyhedral oligomeric silsesquioxane amine (POSS-NH2)
into a phosphorus-containing polyurethane epoxy resin. POSS-NH2 is a cage-like nano-structured
organosilicon with an amine functional group. The result showed an improved corrosion resistance
when POSS-NH2 was present in the cured system. This improvement may be attributed to an increase
in cross-link density due to the nano-structured cross-linking sites of POSS-NH2. Similar results
were demonstrated by Jang et al. [43] where steel corrosion was suppressed in both hot and humid
conditions by the addition of an n −b-Aminoethyl aminotriomethoxy silane (AAPS) coupling agent
to DGEBA. This study also investigated the addition of an epoxy-functional silane GPTMS and an
organosulfur compound with triethoxysilane terminal groups, namely Bis[3-(trimethoxysilyl)-1-phenyl-
propyl]tetrasulﬁde (RC-2). The epoxy/GPTMS/steel and epoxy/RC-2/steel systems showed a lower
corrosion protection than the epoxy/AAPS/steel system. This was explained by the catalytic effect of
the tertiary amine functional group within the structure of AAPS. This amine group acted as a catalyst
and accelerates the hydrolysis and condensation of −Si −O −CH3 to form disiloxane bonds. AAPS
then made Fe −O −Si−bonds on the steel surface and promoted water resistance by the formation

2.6
Chemical Modiﬁcation of Epoxy Resin System for HPHT conditions
The market for coatings technology is highly competitive and is currently facing the dual task of
improving the performance of the coating as well as following strict VOC regulations. Amine cured
epoxy coatings have satisfactorily served the industry for many years. However, new technology is
still required to reach the high performance necessary to meet industrial standards and withstand the
HPHT conditions. Chemical modiﬁcation of the already utilised epoxy resin system provides the
opportunity to add new and desired properties to the existing product. The use of silicone derivatives
for chemical modiﬁcation of the epoxy resin was discussed in the previous section. This section will
focus on modiﬁcation of the curing agent, often referred to as adduction of the curing agent. One of
the main objectives of performing an adduction of the curing agent is to lower the curing temperature.
This allows for an extension of the painting season because the coating application period can continue
even at lower outside temperatures. Subsequently, adduction leads to a lower toxicity and volatility of
the curing agent, and it can enhance the compatibility between the curing agent and the epoxy resin.
There are numerous ways to chemically modify the curing agent and some of these different adduction
methods will be presented and discussed in this section.
2.6.1
Adduction Chemistry for Curing Agents
The curing agent plays a pre-eminent role in determining curing conditions, processing, and properties
of the ﬁnal epoxy product. Adduction of curing agents can be deﬁned as the process in which an excess
of curing agent is pre-reacted with a polymer resin [44]. One of the most common adduct is formulated
by the adduction of an amine curing agent using an epoxy functional system. For the reaction of
an epoxy-amine adduct, the epoxy will react completely, but the adduct will contain residual amino
groups [44], as illustrated in Figure 17. These amino groups can then further react with epoxy resin to
form a thermosetting polymer.
Figure 17: A schematic illustration of the reaction between an amine and an epoxy, forming a epoxy-amine
adduct [44].

Adduction can be performed with a wide variety of different curing agents and polymer resins, depend-
ing on the desired properties. Adducted curing agents are generally less corrosive and less volatile than
unmodiﬁed curing agents, due to the resulting increase in molecular weight [45]. Furthermore, they are
more resistant to blush formation, which will be further clariﬁed in the next section. However, adduc-
tion also leads to an increase in curative viscosity. Therefore, the adduct is typically formulated with
solvents or plasticisers such as benzyl alcohol to reduce viscosity, which then can act as accelerators
for the curing reaction at ambient or sub-ambient temperatures [46].
2.6.2
Adduction of Amine Curing Agents
Owing to the high functionality of amines, amine adducts are one of the most widely used modiﬁed
curing agents. Some of the more prevalent aliphatic and cycloaliphatic amines often used for adduction
includes: diethylene triamine (DETA), triethylene tetramine (TETA), isophorone diamine (IPDA), and
1,2-diaminocyclohexane (1,2-DACH) [45]. A variety of different compounds can be used to adduct the
amine, wherein the most occurring is the epoxy-amine adduct shown in Figure 17. Another example is
the Mannich base, prepared by reacting amines with phenol and formaldehyde, illustrated in Figure 18,
or phenolic amidoamines, illustrated in Figure 19. These adducts appear highly effective for ambient
temperature curing.

phenol and formaldehyde [45].
Figure 19: A schematic illustration of two different phenolic amidoamine adduct [44].
In addition to allowing low-temperature curing and modiﬁcation of the curing rate, amine adducts are
generally less prone to blushing relative to unmodiﬁed amine curing agents. Blushing is a phenomenon
occurring when a primary amine reacts with atmospheric CO2 and water to form carbamate salts that
can exude to the surface and produce blush [45], see Figure 20. Blush formation has a detrimental

2.6.3
Previous Research on Amine Adducts
Low temperature curing agents are highly desired in many industries. An example of this is cy-
cloaliphatic diamine curing agents which are used extensively for ﬂooring applications due to their
low temperature and fast curing properties, low viscosity, good adhesion, low toxicity, and excellent
colour stability. For this application amine adducts are often employed, owing to their superior curing
performance. A study performed by Gorman et al. [46] investigated the performance of three different
amine adducts i.e. the cycloaliphatic diamines, methylcyclohexyl diamine (MCDA) and isphorone
diamine (IPDA), and the arylyl diamine m-xylylene diamine (MXDA), all adducted by DGEBA.
Nine adducts were synthesised, containing different mixing ratios of DGEBA and diamine. Benzyl
alcohol (BnOH) was added to some of the adducts, to allow for lower mixing ratios. The synthesised
DGEBA:diamine mixing ratios were 1:16, 1:8, and 1:2.5, where the latter was mixed with additional
BnOH. The results showed that although the MXDA-based adduct had a lower viscosity than the
MCDA-based adduct, the viscosity of the MCDA-based adduct was progressing more slowly, giving it
a longer pot-life [46]. When investigating the thermal properties it was observed that the adduction of
the diamines led to a slight increase in Tg for the DGEBA cured with the adducted amines, compared
to pure diamines. The following order was observed for Tg, MCDA > IPDA > MXDA > adduct-BnOH
mixtures. The additional methyl group separating the amine group from the aromatic core in MXDA,
resulted in a more ﬂexible backbone and thereby a lower Tg. Furthermore, MCDA-based adducts
showed the highest stability against carbamate formation, with the 1:2.5 MCDA-BnOH giving the best
result, due to the decreased content of primary amine. Another study performed by Zubkova et al. [47]

2.6.4
Adduction by Epoxy Base
Modiﬁcation of the curing agent by epoxy base imparts many desirable functionalities to the curing

It can enable low-temperature curing, increase or decrease curing rate, extend pot life, reduce reactivity
to CO2, and improve compatibility with the resin [48,49]. Furthermore, the increased molecular weight
of the adduct results in a less volatile and less toxic product [48]. DGEBA is one of the most common
epoxy base for adduction. Owing to the high reactivity of the epoxy group, a variety of different
curing agents can be used for this adduction. DGEBA can be used for adduction of both aliphatic and
cycloaliphatic amines, shown in Figure 21a and 21b. Additionally, epoxy-imidazole adducts, shown in

long pot life, rapid curing rate, and a high heat resistance of the ﬁnal product [48].

2.6.5
Adduction by Silicone Derivatives
Silicone derivatives are used to enhance the cross-link density, heat and corrosion resistance and
the mechanical properties of a coating system and provide the system with a dual organic-inorganic
reactivity, as discussed in Section 2.5. Epoxy functional silanes could be an alternative novel technique
for the adduction of curing agents, providing the combined effects of adding silane chemistry into the
coating system, as well as the desired properties from the adduct. Incorporation of a siloxane polymers
could, however, provide other desirable properties such as enhanced ﬂexibility and thermal stability of
the cross-linked network. Pre-reacting the silicone derivatives with the curing agent can also increase
the compatibility between the silicone and the epoxy resin due to the addition of highly reactive amine
groups and potential similarities in chemical structure between the curing agent and the epoxy resin.
This may result in an enhanced homogenity of the silicone derivatives in the epoxy network system.
Very few studies have investigated the effect of curing agents adducted by silicone derivates. A
study performed by Gârea et al. [51] investigated new epoxy-amine adducts, including an epoxy
functional PDMS, as modiﬁer agents on the epoxy-montmorillonite nanocomposite structure, to
enhance the compatibility between inorganic and organic phases. The modiﬁer agent was used to
add a hydrophobic character to the clay and thereby increase compatibility. Several epoxy-amine
adducts were examined, including adduction by epoxy terminated PDMS (EP-PDMS), DGEBF,
Diglycidyl ether of 1,4 cyclohexanedimethanol (DGECHM), and Triglycidyl ether of triphenylol
methane (TGETPM). The results from the analysis showed that the best compatibility was observed
for the adducts containing aromatic groups i.e. DGEBF and TGETPM. For the systems modiﬁed by
the siloxane polymer EP-PDMS, the formation of intercalated structures were conﬁrmed, but the large
molecular weight distribution of EP-PDMS, conﬁrmed from gel permeation chromatography (GPC),
resulted in various distances between the silicate layers [51]. A slight decrease in Tg was observed
when EP-PDMS was added, which in the case of montmorillonite can be explained by exfoliation of
the composite.
Although there is limited research on curing agents adducted by silicone derivates there are several
patents where amino-silane adducts are presented for different purposes. A patent by Oertli et al. [52]
displays the use of amino-silane adducts for moisture-curing compositions, based on the hydrolysing
effect of the silane group when it is in contact with moisture. This hydrolysis leads to the formation
of organosilanols (-Si-OH groups), followed by a condensation reaction to organosiloxane (Si-O-Si).
Thorlaksen et al. [53] has patented an amino silane adduct curing agent for an amine cured epoxy
based tie-coat composition. The patent states that the use of high molecular weight amino-silanes
can enable good adhesion at low-temperature curing. No available studies have reported the effect

2.7
Role of Silicone Adducted Curing Agents in HPHT
The complex nature of HPHT conditions creates a need for high-performance coatings with speciﬁc
protective properties. Modiﬁcation of a coating system by addition of silicone derivatives can enhance
the performance of the coating by manipulating the properties of the system. There is limited research
on the speciﬁc performance of silicon adducted curing agents for anti-corrosive epoxy coatings.
However, the purpose of this section is to obtain a broader understanding of the effect of adding
silicone adducts to epoxy systems at HPHT conditions.

2.7.2
Thermal Stability of Silicone Adducted Amine
The extreme environment emerging under HPHT conditions require a coating system with excellent
thermal stability. The incorporation of silicone adducted amines can enhance the thermal resistance of
the coating network and thereby suppress the degradation process. This delay in degradation may be
caused by the partial ionic nature, high bond energy, and thermal stability of the Si-O-Si linkage [54].
The aforementioned study by Gârea et al. [51] stated that modifying montmorillonite by a EP-PDMS
based amine adduct gave a signiﬁcantly higher thermal stability than observed for the unmodiﬁed
montmorillonite. Thermogravimetry analysis (TGA) showed that the onset temperature, at which
the weight loss is 5%, went from 162 °C for an unmodiﬁed network [55], to 200 °C for the network
modiﬁed by EP-PDMS based amine adduct [51]. Further, it should be noted that the incorporation of
silanes can lead to a slight decrease in Tg caused by the presence of the highly ﬂexible Si-O-Si chain.
However, the strong bond energy of the Si-O-Si linkage may increase Tg. Thus, parameters such as
reactive groups and chain length need to be carefully considered to obtain optimal thermal properties
for HPHT conditions.
2.7.3
Solvent Resistance of Silicone Adducted Amine
For oil and gas applications, the petrochemical solvents are one of the main contributors to degradation
of the cross-linked epoxy network. One of the solvents typically present in these operations is p-xylene,
which can have a detrimental effect on the epoxy network. A reason for this is its ability to soften

==PAGE:38==

January 31, 2021
the cross-linked system [1]. Softening occurs when the solvent penetrates the network, resulting in
an increase in free volume and thereby a lowering of Tg. This resin-solvent interaction can further
enable easier penetration of other corrosive species, thus lowering the resistance performance of the
cross-linked anti-corrosive coating. It is therefore highly important to understand the degradation
pathway caused by resin-solvent interactions, and whether these interactions are chemical or physical.
By replacing a certain amount of aromatic structure in the epoxy system with silicone derivatives, the
afﬁnity between the solvent and the resin will be reduced, which may mitigate the solvent penetration.
Limited studies are investigating the effect of silicone derivatives on solvent interaction. However, there
are studies investigating silicone’s effect on water absorption, which will be further discussed in the
upcoming section. For unmodiﬁed epoxy resin, the effect of different solvents has been investigated,
but not in regards to HPHT conditions. Kaplan [22] examined the diffusion behavior of seven different
solvents on DGEBA cured with four different curing agents, i.e. triethylenetetramine (TETA), m-
phenylenediamine (MPDA), dicyandiamide (DICY), and nadic methylanhydride (NMA). The examined
solvents included water, methanol, acetone, acetate, ethyl, isopropanol, 1.2-dichloroethane, and toluene.
A sorption analysis was conducted on the cured epoxies by immersion in the seven solvents. 1.2-
dichloroethane and toluene are the most similar solvents to p-xylene, owing to their chemical structure
and non-polar nature. The results showed that the choice of curing agent highly affected the absorption
of the solvents. The epoxy cured with MPDA showed superior solvent resistance, compared to the
other curing agents, as shown in Figure 22. Additionally, it was seen that a lower absorption of water
and methanol was observed for the NMA cured epoxy, despite the expected hydrogen bonding from
the different functional groups (i.e. esters) [22]. The NMA cured epoxy had a higher Tg than the
others, which might explain the good water and methanol barrier performance. This indicated that
both the physical factors, such as Tg, and the chemical factors, such as afﬁnity towards curing agent,
could have a dominant effect on the solvent absorption.

2.7.4
Hydrophobicity of Silicone Adducted Amine
A major challenge for epoxy coatings in HPHT environments is the permeation of seawater and
corrosive species, which can deteriorate the adhesion and lead to corrosion of the substrate. It is
therefore necessary to create a coating system with properties to withstand this phenomenon to avoid
corrosion of the equipment material. A measure to obtain a better water resistance is to fabricate a
coating system with a more hydrophobic nature. Modiﬁcation of the epoxy-based system by silicone
derivatives can provide improved water resistance of the protective coating due to its hydrophobic
nature, compared to the relatively more hydrophilic epoxy amine network, and due to the formation of
strong bonds across the interface between the polymer and the substrate. This formation of Si-O-Si
bond on the steel surface can block water from penetrating through the interface between the substrate
and the coating.
Jang et al. [43] reported the correlation between the formation of siloxane network on the steel surface
and water resistance. The aforementioned study tested different types of silane-modiﬁed epoxies on
a steel surface and the result showed that the more hydrolysed and condensed silanes gave a better
water resistance. A study performed by Wang et al. [56] examined the performance of a pure novolac

system and an epoxy-silane hybrid system. The water barrier properties of the two epoxy systems were
analysed and it was found that the epoxy-silane system showed far superior water resistance compared
to the pure epoxy. Although water still penetrated into the ﬁlm, the internal water fraction in the bulk
ﬁlm was extensively reduced for the silane incorporated epoxy. This low water absorption implies good
adhesion between the coating and the substrate. Additionally, the coating was completely recovered to
its previous state after drying, which implies that the water was only physically absorbed [56]. The
silanes were reacted both with the epoxy network, serving as a curing agent, and on the substrate
surface, as an adhesion promoter. This resulted in a more dense and less hydrophilic bulk ﬁlm,
compared to neat epoxy novolac, and a dense hydrophobic layer on the interface between substrate
and coating. A combination of these factors led to enhanced water barrier properties, caused by the
addition of silane into the epoxy system. Thus, modiﬁcation by silicone adducted curing agents may
prevent the seawater in HPHT environments from permeating into the coating system and corrode the
substrate.
2.7.5
Improved mechanical properties of Silicone Adducted Amine
Highly cross-linked epoxy systems impart excellent stiffness, strength, chemical resistance, and heat
resistance. However, these network systems tend to be rather brittle in nature, with a poor crack
resistance. The quick realease of gases in the coating network during the rapid gas decompression
occuring in HPHT environments can cause material stress and crack formation if the system is too
brittle. Incorporating highly ﬂexible silicone derivatives may therefore enhance the mechanical strength
of the network by reducing the brittleness of the system.
Silanes used as a coupling agent for epoxy composites have shown excellent performance in enhancing
the mechanical properties of the composite. This is due to its ability to produce compatibility and
miscibility between the composite ﬁllers and the epoxy matrix. Wan et al. [57] investigated the
tensile and ﬂexural strength and modulus of epoxy modiﬁed with different loadings of graphene oxide
(GO) ﬁllers and silane functionalised GO (silane-f-GO) ﬁllers. It was observed that the composites
containing the silane-f-GO exhibited higher strength and modulus than the composites containing GO
without the silane functionalisation for all ﬁller loadings, as shown in Figure 23. Both tensile and

2.8
Characterisation Methods
Characterising long-chain polymer networks is a lot more complex than characterising low molecular
weight compounds. A variety of conﬁgurational and conformational variables are present in the
chemical structure. Thus, a broad range of analytical techniques is needed to identify, quantify, or
isolate the chemical structure, and to determine the thermal and physical properties of the epoxy network
system. This section will introduce different analytical and evaluative tools used for characterisation of
coatings before and after HPHT exposure. The main motivation of the characterisation is to understand
the chemical and physical changes occurring in the epoxy network system when exposed to HPHT
conditions.
2.8.1
Differential Scanning Calorimetry
Differential scanning calorimetry (DSC) is a technique commonly used for quantitative studies of
thermal transitions in a polymer network. This method uses two individual heaters to maintain identical
temperatures for two small sample holders, one containing the sample and the second containing an
inert reference [58], as illustrated in Figure 24. The thermal transitions are measured by detecting the
energy needed to heat the sample as a function of time compared to the reference. When the molecular
mobility of a solid increases, more energy is needed to increase the temperature of the sample at the
same rate as the reference sample. This is referred to as heat ﬂow, where a change in heat ﬂow is
detected once an endothermic or an exothermic thermal transition occurs.
Figure 24: Schematic representation of a DSC measuring cell.
DSC is widely used in the coating industry, mainly for studies of the curing process or to identify the
Tg of the cured system. Tg marks the transition from an amorphous solid-state, to a state of increasing
kinetic energy. For a highly cross-linked system Tg represents the point of increasing molecular
mobility, but the material will remain relatively stiff due to the great forces between the closely packed
molecules [59]. Figure 25 shows a DSC heating curve of neat epoxy resin and epoxy resins modiﬁed

2.8.2
Fourier Transform Infrared Spectroscopy
Most compounds having covalent bonds absorbs various frequencies of electromagnetic radiation in
the infrared region of the electromagnetic spectrum [61]. The vibrational portion of the infrared region
is used for chemical analysis. A molecule absorbs selected frequencies of infrared radiation, encom-
passing the stretching and bending vibrational frequencies of the covalent bonds. Every type of bond
has a different natural frequency of vibration, which can be used to determine structural information
about the molecule. Fourier transform infrared spectroscopy (FTIR) is a common characterisation
technique in coating technologies. FTIR brings a great versatility for studies of polymer structures as
the analysis can be done in a matter of seconds. It, therefore, facilitates studies of polymer reactions
such as cross-linking and degradation, by continuously analysing changes of functional groups in the
polymer network [62].

2.8.3
Scanning Electron Microscopy
Scanning electron microscopy (SEM) utilises a focused beam of high-energy electrons which is
scanned across a sample surface in synchronisation with a beam in a cathode-ray tube. The scattered
electrons generates a variety of signals, producing an image with a great depth of ﬁeld and an almost
three-dimensional appearance. SEM is highly effective in coating technology, as it provides useful
information on surface topology with a resolution of about 100 Å [62]. A typical application of
SEM in coatings includes studies of pigment dispersion, cracking, blistering and adhesive failure.
It can therefore be used to analyse the surface topology of the epoxy coating system after HPHT
exposure, to detect if any cracking or blistering has occured. Subsequently, SEM can be joined with
an energy-dispersive X-ray spectroscope (EDX), which is an analytical technique used for chemical
characterisation and elemental analysis. This technique uses the principle that every atom has a unique
number of electrons in speciﬁc energy levels. An electron beam is used to move an electron from one
electron shell to another, creating an energy difference which can be released as a characteristic X-ray,
which is then detected. The measurements provides information about the chemical composition of
the sample, including the presence, distribution and concentration of different elements [64]. Figure

2.9
Summary of the Literature Survey
This literature study has focused on formulation of epoxy-silicone hybrid systems using silicone
adducted amines and its role in the degradation pathway at HPHT conditions. The increasing emphasis
towards more extreme environments in the oil and gas industry is creating new challenges for the
high-performance coating systems used for oil extraction processes. Highly cross-linked epoxy
coatings have shown excellent anti-corrosive properties due to its high thermal, solvent, and chemical
resistance and excellent adhesion properties. However, investigations of the degradation pathways and
durability of these high-performance epoxy based coatings when exposed to HPHT conditions are
limited. Previous studies performed in CoaST, DTU, [1] have revealed detrimental effects of solvent
interactions and water permeation occurring in these hostile environments. Partial degradation was
observed in the interface between the hydrocarbon and the seawater phase which lead to underﬁlm
corrosion of the substrate. Subsequently, loss of adhesion was observed in the seawater exposed zone.
The addition of a silane adhesion promoter eliminated the adhesion deﬁciencies [3], but degradation
was still observed in the hydrocarbon and seawater interface. The use of epoxy-silicone hybrid systems
has received increasing interest in the coating industry. Modiﬁcation of the epoxy system by silicone
derivatives can expand the range of both physical and chemical properties in the conventional epoxy
system. The addition of silicone derivatives provides a variety of excellent properties to the system
including increased thermal stability, lower water permeability, enhanced ﬂexibility, higher chemical
and corrosion resistance, and reduced solvent interactions. Pre-reacting the silicone with the curing
agent will increase compatibility towards the epoxy resin, leading to a better homogeneity in the system.
For this reason, modiﬁcation of the epoxy system by silicone adducted amines is a promising measure
to improve the performance of the coating system at HPHT conditions due to its ability to enhance
water and solvent barrier properties, increase thermal stability and improve mechanical properties.

2.10
Hypotheses
Hypothesis 1: Silicone derivatives adducted with the amine curing agent enhances the compati-
bility between the silicone and the resin system.
Chemical modiﬁcation of the epoxy system by pre-reacting silicone derivatives with the curing agent
makes the added silicone more compatible with the epoxy resin due to the addition of highly reactive
amine groups. The increased compatibility leads to a more homogeneous dispersion of the silicone
derivatives in the network system compared to when the silicone derivative is dispersed physically.
Hypothesis 2: Silicone adducted amines results in improved corrosion protection for HPHT
compared to the previously used epoxy adducted amines.
Replacing a certain percentage of epoxy adducted amine with silicone adducted amine will increase
hydrophobicity and heat resistance of the epoxy coating system, resulting in higher corrosion resistance
in HPHT conditions. The hydrophobicity comes from the hydrophobic nature of the dimethyl groups
in the silicone backbone and the enhanced heat resistance is connected to the thermal stability of the
high bond energy of the Si-O-Si linkage.
Hypothesis 3: Introduction of silicone adducted amines will enhance the water barrier proper-
ties of the epoxy novolac system
The silicone derivatives used for amine adduction contain hydrophobic dimethyl groups in the silicone
backbone. This gives the curing agent a non-polar nature, making the coating system more resistant
towards the highly polar water molecules.
Hypothesis 4: The use of silicone adducted amines as curing agent will reduce resin-hydrocarbon
interactions, and thereby diminish the softening effect of the hydrocarbon.
Reducing the amount of aromatic groups in the system by replacing it with silicone derivatives can
result in a lower afﬁnity between the aromatic hydrocarbon (p-xylene) phase and the resin. This
restrains the solvent from penetrating into the coating system and increase the free volume of the
network. Diminishing the resin-hydrocarbon interactions will avoid softening of the epoxy-silicone
hybrid system and thereby lower the permeation of corrosive species.

Materials & Methods
Previous research performed by Rajagopalan et al. [1] investigated the performance and the corrosion
degradation mechanism of a system of neat epoxy novolac cured with DGEBA adducted MXDA and
DEAPA under HPHT conditions. The coated panels were exposed to the same HPHT environment
which is used for this study, containing a gas phase of carbondioxide (CO2) and nitrogen (N2), a
hydrocarbon phase of p-xylene and a seawater phase. The results from the study showed a red-coloured
deposition in the interface between the hydrocarbon phase and the seawater phase, as shown in Figure
28. The red-coloured line was conﬁrmed to be deposit of oxides of iron formed on the underlying metal
substrate which had precipitated to the coating surface during the rapid gas decompression (RGD).
This deposit was expected to have been formed due to supercritical CO2 and seawater penetrating
through the coating, reaching the metal-coating interface. The great afﬁnity between the aromatic
content in the coating system and the p-xylene (aromatic hydrocarbon) resulted in softening of the
epoxy novolac network and thereby allowing the penetration of CO2 and water into the network. The
difference in density between the two medium (hydrocarbon and seawater) might have resulted in a
concentration gradient of supercritical CO2 in the interfacial hydrocarbon-seawater subzone where the
CO2 would diffuse from a less dense phase to a more dense phase [66]. Thus, promoting the formation
of underﬁlm corrosion in this particular zone.

3.1
Raw materials
The raw materials for the present study were chosen on basis of a combination of the literature study
and previous research conducted by CoaST, DTU. A coating system is a complex mixture of chemical
substances providing different functionalities to the system. In order to understand the degradation
process of the system in an HPHT environment, the present research focuses solely on the resin system.
The resin system will consist of an epoxy novolac base and a mixture of two amine curing agents.
In order to pinpoint the degradation pathway of the resin system at HPHT conditions, the current
formulation is without pigments, extenders and ﬁllers. The curing agent will be chemically modiﬁed
with the purpose of enhancing the performance of the resin system alone. Each raw material used for
the experiments will be presented and explained in this section.
3.1.1
Epoxy resin system
Epoxy novolac resin, shown in Figure 29, was chosen as the binder for the system. As previously stated,
novolac has a signiﬁcantly higher functionality compared to many other commercial epoxy resins.
This high functionality leads to an increase in cross-link density and thereby a more closely packed
network system. A previous study performed by Rajagopalan at CoaST, DTU, [1] investigated the
difference in performance for a DGEBF and a novolac system when exposed to an HPHT environment.
The study showed enhanced performance for the novolac-system, particularly in the seawater phase.
Penetration of seawater and loss of adhesion was observed for the DGEBF system in the seawater
phase. Novolac performed superior providing an intact seawater exposed zone after HPHT exposure.
The epoxy novolac (DEN 438, Olin, USA) used for the current experiments has a functionality of 3.6.

Silicone derivatives
The scope of this study was to research the effect of using silicone adducted amines in the coating
system at HPHT conditions. Furthermore, a comparative study between DGEBA adducted amines
and silicone adducted amines would provide a better insight towards the development of a high
performance coating formulation for HPHT applications. A study performed at CoaST, DTU, [67]
tested several different silicone derivatives added directly to the epoxy resin system as an additive in
different quantities to evaluate the corrosion resistance of the coating. The best result was observed
with the addition of DMS-EC31 (Epoxycyclohexylethyl terminated polydimethylsiloxane, Gelest,
USA). The chemical structure of DMS-EC31 is shown in Figure 34. The cycloaliphatic terminal
group on DMS-EC31 can provide characteristic properties such as superior thermal stability and
denser cross-linking due to the bulky cycloaliphatic groups, as well as reducing its afﬁnity towards the
aromatic hydrocarbon phase. Additionally, the strong Si-O-Si linkage in the backbone of DMS-EC31
has the potential to provide enhanced ﬂexibility and thermal stability to the system.

Although the long-chain silicone polymers can provide many great characteristics, it may also impart
certain defects to the system. One of the reasons for this is that the non-polar nature of the two
methyl functional groups in the silicone chain and a low level of intermolecular attractions can lead to
mechanical and thermodynamical incompatibility between the silicone resin and epoxy resin. This
incompatibility can be reﬂected in the low solubility parameter of PDMS (d = 14.9 J1/2cm−3/2)
compared to that of other polymers (d = 17.6 −28.6 J1/2cm−3/2) [68]. This creates a possibility
of obtaining a two-phase morphology in the epoxy/PDMS copolymer. Additionally, the non-polar
nature of the long-chain may affect interactions between the hydrocarbon phase and the coating. It was
therefore decided to analyse the difference in performance between the addition of a long-chain silicone
polymer and a short-chain silane. The silanes does not contain the characteristic methyl groups of
DMS-EC31 and are more easily miscible in the resin system. Two epoxy functional silanes was selected
for the analysis, the epoxy functional silane (3-Glycidyloxypropyl)trimethoxysilane (GPTMS) and the
cycloaliphatic epoxy functionalised silane Trimethoxy[2-(7-oxabicyclo[4.1.0]hept-3-yl)ethyl]silane

Results & Disussion
This section will present the results from the experimental work and discuss different factors involved in
the experiments. These factors includes miscibility between the curing agent and the silicone derivative,
the effect of using an adhesion promoter, the effect of adduction and the effect of the different silicone
derivatives. Additionally, the inﬂuence of these parameters on the degradation pathway of the coating
when exposed to HPHT conditions will be discussed.
4.1
Preliminary Experiments for Coatings Containing Silicone Adducts
Utilisation of silicone adducted curing agents for HPHT applications is still a relatively unestablished
ﬁeld. Thus, a few elements of the experiments need to be carefully considered to obtain a comprehensive
understanding of the observed occurrences when silicone adducts are used. These elements will be
discussed in the upcoming sections.
4.1.1
Miscibility between the curing agent and silicone derivatives
The miscibility between the silicone derivatives and the curing agent is an important factor to consider
when modifying the coating formulation. If the two compounds are immiscible it might result in
a less homogeneous system. Subsequently, having two separate phases in the system can lead to a
non-uniform coating layer. Mixing a high molecular weight siloxane such as DMS-EC31 with a low
molecular weight curing agent can create challenges such as visual surface defects and microphase
separations due to incompatibilities in the system. This may affect the performance of the coating
in an HPHT environment. It is therefore important to consider the chemical structure of the two
components. DMS-EC31 consists of a long-chain polar Si-O-Si linkage, non-polar methyl groups and
a moderately polar terminal cycloaliphatic group. Adduction was performed both on a cycloaliphatic
amine (AMICURE PACM), an aliphatic amine (DEAPA) and an aryl diamine (MXDA). Pictures
of the three adducts are shown in Figure 38. The siloxane adducted MXDA clearly showed some
immiscibility after mixing (Fig. 38,a). However, when AMICURE PACM was mixed with the siloxane
it seemed miscible at ﬁrst, but after storing for a few days the mixture started separating (Fig. 38,c).
No phase seperation was observed for the DEAPA adducts (Fig. 38,b) or for any of the short-chain
silane adducts for any of the three curing agents (Fig. 38,d).

The Tg of the standard epoxy novolac system cured with MXDA and DEAPA (STD 1) before HPHT
exposure was measured to be 155±3 °C. The addition of DMS-EC31 to the network system resulted
in an increase in Tg to the value of 164±3 °C for DMS 2 and 165±3 °C for DMS 6. This increase
can be attributed to the high bond energy and thermal stability of the Si-O-Si linkage. The Si-O
bond has a strong average s bond energy of 368 kJ mol−1 [70]. Additionally, the d-orbitals in the
electronegative silicone which interacts with the free electron pair in oxygens p-orbital creates a strong
d-p p bonding [71]. The low rotation barrier of the siloxane chain can also provide ﬂexibility to the
network, which could lead to more molecular mobility and thereby a lower Tg. In spite of this, the
analysis demonstrated that although the siloxane chain provides ﬂexibility, the Tg still increased in the
presence of the Si-O-Si linkage. Comparing the standard system (STD 1) with the systems containing
silane adducts (GPTMS 1 and CEFS 1), the difference is much less than for the siloxane adducted
systems with a Tg at 158±3 °C for GPTMS 1 and 160±3 °C for CEFS 1. This aligned with the
presumed expectations, as the adducted silanes were generally not expected to affect the cross-link
density or add additional bulkiness to the system. The only exception of this was the cycloaliphatic
functional group in CEFS which might have a certain effect on the bulkiness and steric hindrance, seen
by the slightly higher Tg for this sample.
The use of a cycloaliphatic curing agent was expected to provide an enhancement in thermal properties
of the network due to the addition of more bulky groups. Incorporating bulky groups into the cross-
linked structure generally leads to a more densely packed system and will thereby increase the Tg of
the network. However, bulky groups owing to its steric hindrance could affect the spatial arrangement

Previously it has been shown that no changes could be observed in the GP exposed zone for any of the

the Tg of the GP zone did not show any signiﬁcant difference from the unexposed sample, indicating
that the high temperature together with the high pressure N2 and supercritical CO2 did not have any
effect on the coating network individually. Although it has been reported that supercritical CO2 has the
potential to swell even glassy polymers [72], Duthie et al. [73] proved that a higher cross-link density
suppressed the CO2 induced plasticisation. Thus, due to the highly cross-linked network used for these
experiments, the supercritical CO2 alone was not expected to have a detrimental effect on the coating
network.
Previous results from HPHT exposure tests of the DGEBA adducted MXDA/DEAPA cured epoxy
novolac system, showed a decrease in Tg of 20 °C in the HC exposed zone [1]. This decrease was
attributed to an increase in chain mobility (free volume) due to p-xylene diffusion, which led to a
softening of the network system. The diffusion of p-xylene was found to originate from intermolecular
attractions, resulting in non-polar interactions between p-xylene and the epoxy network system. This
reduction of Tg in the HC exposed zone was not found in this present study, demonstrating the effect
adducting a silicone derivative into the amine curing agent. The curing agents in the previous study
were adducted by DGEBA which has a high afﬁnity towards p-xylene due to its aromaticity. In
this study, DGEBA was completely replaced by a silicone derivative. Hansen solubility parameters

(HSP) are often used for predictions of compatibility between two substances. HSP is based on
the cohesive energy of a liquid which arises from the contribution of three different interactions:
nonpolar dispersion interactions, dipolar interactions, and hydrogen bonding interactions [74]. HSP
of p-xylene has been found to be around d = 17.7 J1/2cm−3/2 [75] and benzene has a HSP around
d = 18.4 J1/2cm−3/2 [76]. These values are very close to each other which means that the afﬁnity
between the to substances are high. The HSP of PDMS, however, is around d = 14.9 J1/2cm−3/2.
Thus, by replacing the aromatic DGEBA with the DMS-EC31 structure, the afﬁnity between the
coating network and the aromatic hydrocarbon phase has been reduced. Silanes generally have a
similar HSP to p-xylene at around 15-19 J1/2cm−3/2 [77], but the silanes tend to hydrolyse into silanol
which can further react and create a siloxane chain, as was presented in Figure 10. This reaction would
change the HSP of the substance. Faasen et al. [78] found the solubility parameter of hydroxylated
silicone dioxide to be as high as d = 37.5 J1/2cm−3/2 which might explain the great effect of the
silanes on the suppression of hydrocarbon diffusion. Besides, reducing the similarities in cohesive
energy between the coating network and the p-xylene phase, the aforementioned strong bond energy of
the Si-O-Si linkage can provide enhanced solvent resistance. Thus, combining these factors resulted in
an almost complete mitigation of the hydrocarbon-resin interactions and thereby avoiding the softening
effect of the coating network.
Seawater penetration is one of the most detrimental factors in the HPHT environment due to the highly
corrosive properties of salt towards metals. Considering the results from the DSC analysis of the SW
exposed zone, a Tg depression of 8-9 °C was observed for the siloxane adducted samples and 2-5 °C
for the silane adducted samples. This decrease was expected to be a result of water penetration into
the coating network accelerated by the presence of high pressure [21]. The afﬁnity of water towards
the hydrophilic epoxy amine network is well known. Moreover, the high pressure environment made
these sites susceptible for seawater ingress and accumulation over time causing a strained cross-linked
structure. Then, the entrapped seawater and the rapid release of pressure during RGD led to the
development of blisters and cracks. The increased ﬂexibility from the siloxane chain resulted in more
blister formation, as illustrated in Figure 61. As previously stated the Tg measurements clearly showed
an enhancement in thermal properties from the addition of a siloxane chain. However, as mentioned
in Section 2.5.5, long-chain siloxane network with high number of repeating units can result in more
intra-molecular and inter-molecular entanglements. These entanglements creates voids acting as a free
space for water to penetrate and accumulate over time. Although it was expected that the non-polar
methyl groups on the siloxane backbone would result in a reduced afﬁnity towards the polar seawater,
the DSC analysis showed that the effect of the entanglements was more dominant than the effect of the

4.2.6
DGEBA Adduct vs. Silicone Adduct
Adduction of the curing agent can play a paramount role in enhancing curing conditions, processing
and end-use properties of the epoxy resin system. When considering the curing conditions and
the processing, the previously used DGEBA adduction technique provides several advantages. The
chemical structure of DGEBA is similar to the chemical structure of epoxy novolac, resulting in a
highly enhanced afﬁnity between the adducted curing agent and the epoxy resin system. Additionally,
the reaction between DGEBA and the curing agent distinctly decrease the volatility of the curing
agent due to the high molecular weight of the DGEBA resin. Replacing the DGEBA with a silicone
derivative imparts desirable characteristics to the system, but can also have certain drawbacks. Firstly,
the silicone derivative reduces afﬁnity between the curing agent and the resin due to the variance
in polarity and chemical structures of the two compounds. Although, the long-chain siloxanes are
expected to decrease the volatility of the curing agent owing to its high molecular weight of around
900-1100 g/mol, the same can not be stated for the silanes. The silanes are low molecular weight
compounds which would not effect the volatility to a great extent. This might create a hazard risk
when working with for example the highly volatile DEAPA curing agent adducted by a low molecular

weight silane. The silane adduction can, however, help prevent the silanes from evaporating during the
heat-curing process. All the adducts were expected to lower the curing temperature, as the pre-reaction
between the the primary amine and the epoxy group occurs for all systems. This effect was, however,
not analysed in the present study.
The aim of this study was to enhance the performance of the DGEBA adducted MXDA/DEAPA cured
epoxy novolac system at HPHT conditions previously reported by Rajagopalan et al. [1], shown in
Figure 62a. To examine the end-use properties of the different adducts, the results from the HPHT

amount of iron oxide deposit could be found in HC-SW interface for the DGEBA adducted sample. As
mentioned, a previous study showed a decrease in Tg of 20 °C in the HC exposed zone after exposure
to the HPHT environment, caused by softening of the network in this area [1]. Although, some micro
pinholes were found by SEM in this area for the silicone adducted samples, no Tg depression was seen,
suggesting that replacing the DGEBA with a silicone derivative had mitigated the hydrocarbon-resin

4.3
Silicone as an adduct vs. silicone as an additive
The present study focuses on adding the silicone derivative by chemically modifying the curing agent
through adduction, and thereby replacing the DGEBA which was previously used for adducting the
curing agent. This leads to many desired properties as the DGEBA has a great afﬁnity towards the
HC phase which is proven to have a softening effect on the coating system resulting in a weakening
of the highly functional cross-linked network [1]. Additionally, the adduction was expected to create
a more even distribution of silicone in the coating network by increasing the compatibility towards
the epoxy resin. This could help prevent the silicones from clustering in one area. A parallel ongoing
research at CoaST, DTU, analysed the use of DMS-EC31 and GPTMS as an additive instead of as an
adduct in an epoxy novolac system cured with DGEBA adducted MXDA and DEAPA [67]. Moreover,
additional samples with CEFS as an additive were prepared in this present study, as well as samples
using DMS-EC31 as an additive and DGEBA adducted AMICURE PACM and DEAPA as curing
agents. The adducted and non-adducted panels after exposure to the HPHT environment are presented
in Figure 63.

Conclusions
All silicone derivatives were successfully incorporated into the epoxy novolac system by adduction of
different curing agents. IR spectra of the different modiﬁed coating systems veriﬁed the changes in
chemical structure resulting from the incorporation of the silicone adducted curing agents. However,
some microphase separations were observed by SEM for all samples. The addition of an adhesion
promoter (AP) proved valuable both as an adhesion promoter and to enhance the ﬁlm formation
properties and thereby mitigating the microphase separation observed for the samples without an AP.
Although, some microphase separations were observed both from the DM and the SEM micrographs,
studying the Tg of the siloxane adducted sample and the sample where siloxane was added as an
additive conﬁrmed enhanced compatibility between the silicone and the resin system as a result of
adduction. The Tg increased by 18 °C when the DMS-EC31 was used as an adduct instead of as
an additive. This increase in Tg was evidence of a more homogeneous distribution of the silicone
derivative in the network system.
The HPHT system is highly complex and the effect of all the different phases and physical factors on
the silicone-epoxy hybrid system needed to be analysed to understand the degradation mechanism
of the modiﬁed network. The GP exposed zone remained the same after exposure, meaning that the
high-pressure N2 and the supercritical CO2 had no considerable effect on the coating. Reducing the
aromaticity of the novolac system by adducting a thermally stable siloxane chain instead of DGEBA
was expected to lower the afﬁnity between the p-xylene and the coating system. The SEM micrographs
showed some micro pinholes in the HC exposed zone, suggesting that supercritical CO2 interactions
had occurred due to softening of the network resulting from resin-hydrocarbon interactions [66].
However, the DSC measurements showed that there was no signiﬁcant difference in Tg between the
HC exposed zone and the unexposed sample. This proved that although some interactions might have
occurred on the coating surface, the cross-linking remained intact. Thus, the softening effect of the
p-xylene phase, previously seen for the standard epoxy novolac formulation [1], has been diminished
by the addition of a silicone adduct. A slight Tg depression was observed in the SW exposed zone,
which was assumed to be a result of seawater penetration into the coating network accelerated by
the presence of high pressure. This seawater penetration caused strain on the cross-linked structure
leading to a rise in internal energy which resulted in a decrease in Tg. The IR analysis did not present
any changes in chemical structure and water ingress was presumed to be mainly caused by physical
factors. Regardless of this Tg depression, the highly cross-linked network was able to prevent the
seawater from completely penetrating the system as no underﬁlm corrosion was observed in this area.
The expected effect of the non-polar methyl groups from the siloxane chain was not veriﬁed, as the
78
==PAGE:90==
The Hempel Foundation Coatings Science and Technology Centre (CoaST)
January 31, 2021
silane samples showed a lower Tg depression than the siloxane samples in the SW exposed zone. The
addition of the siloxane adduct resulted in enhanced thermal stability and less afﬁnity towards the
hydrocarbon phase. By visual inspection, no under ﬁlm corrosion could be observed in the HC-SW
interphase. However, trace amounts of Fe was found from the DM micrographs and the EDX analysis
indicating that formation of oxides of iron on the substrate during exposure has not been completely
eliminated. Although this issue was still present, the peculiar underﬁlm corrosion has been highly
reduced by the modiﬁcations performed in the present study.
It was difﬁcult to distinguish the performance of the long-chain siloxane adducted system with the
short-chain silane adducted system, as both systems provided different desirable characteristics, as
well as a few deﬁciencies. However, it can be concluded that the addition of a silicone adduct has
improved the corrosion protection properties of the conventional DGEBA adducted coating networks
at HPHT conditions.

Future perspective
The results from this study presents possibilities for further research on the topic of using silicone
adducts. Some valuable possibilities for future studies are presented below.
• The high thermal stability and enhanced ﬂexibility of the siloxane adducted system could provide
highly desirable properties for many applications. A more extensive study into the versatility
of the modiﬁed system and the possibilities of expanding the applications of the coating would
therefore be valuable.
• The present study has proven that adding silicone by adducting the curing agent resulted in a
better dispersion of the silicones in the system. This discovery could be used to further research
the possibilities of using this modiﬁcation technique for incorporation of other functional groups
into a coating network.
• Investigating the utilisation of lower molecular weight siloxanes (oligomers) for adduction, to
mitigate the microphase separation observed in the present study, could provide an enhanced
understanding of the siloxane adducts.
• The chemically modiﬁed coating system showed enhanced performance in the HPHT environ-
ment. However, the present study focused solely on modiﬁcation of the resin system. Thus, the
full formulation including the conventional coating constituents such as pigments, extenders
and ﬁllers is yet to be explored. Further research is therefore necessary in order to develope a
complete coating formulation.
