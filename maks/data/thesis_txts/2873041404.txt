Abstract
The healthcare sector is currently experiencing a significant shortage of nursing staff, a situation
exacerbated by the COVID-19 pandemic. This crisis has increased stress levels among healthcare
workers, reduced job satisfaction, and created a detrimental cycle that ultimately affects patient
care quality and safety. As studies have shown a direct link between staffing ratios and workload-
induced stress, accurately estimating nursing workload becomes vital for both staff well-being
and clinical outcomes. Traditional metrics, such as the Nursing Activities Score (NAS), are often
unavailable in historical electronic health records (EHRs), especially outside critical care units.
Consequently, there is a growing need for innovative, data-driven methods that leverage routine
EHR data to support workload estimation.
This study investigates the use of machine learning techniques to classify daily nurse workload
levels using structured EHR data collected from the University of Augsburg hospital between 2021
and 2023. The dataset spans both intensive care units (ICUs) and general wards, capturing day-
level counts of vital sign monitoring, medication administration, access procedures, and nursing
care activities. Workload was operationalised as the aggregated frequency of these events per 24-
hour shift and categorised into three levels: low, medium, and high. An XGBoost classifier was
employed for this multi-class prediction task due to its suitability for tabular healthcare data and its
ability to capture non-linear interactions. Within the three-tier classification framework, one class,
typically the medium or low workload tier, consistently posed greater predictive challenges. This
was due to class imbalance and overlapping feature patterns, making it harder to distinguish from
neighbouring categories.
The model achieved strong overall classification performance, particularly in distinguishing low
and high workload days. Feature importance analysis revealed that vital signs, access procedures,
and general care events were the most influential predictors of workload. These findings are consis-
tent with prior literature and reinforce the relevance of EHR-derived care intensity proxies. While
automation filtering and event capping were applied to improve data quality, the core methodology
remained interpretable and scalable.
By demonstrating that nurse workload can be inferred from routinely collected EHR data, this
study offers a practical framework for real-time workload assessment and staffing optimisation.
The approach contributes to the broader vision of data-driven workforce planning in healthcare,
particularly as institutions navigate rising patient acuity and chronic staffing shortages.

Introduction
There is no universally accepted definition of nursing workload, though it is commonly associated
with the volume, complexity, and intensity of care-related tasks that nurses perform within a given
time frame. In their review of psychiatric inpatient settings, Sousa and Seabra emphasise that cur-
rent workload assessment tools vary widely, and they highlight the need to clarify the concept of
nursing workload, recommending that it encompass both direct and indirect patient care activi-
ties and be meaningfully linked to patient outcomes [1]. Earlier, Morris et al. underscored that
concepts such as patient dependency and nursing intensity are often inconsistently defined, yet all
serve as proxies for workload, reinforcing the need for operational clarity when measuring work-
load quantitatively [2]. Overloaded nurses are associated with higher rates of adverse events and
burnout, whereas a balanced nurse-to-patient workload helps ensure that essential care is delivered
safely. Accurately measuring and predicting nurse workload is therefore a key concern for hospi-
tal management and healthcare researchers. Given this context, the present thesis defines nursing
workload as the cumulative, patient-driven demand on nursing staff, captured through electronic
health record (EHR) indicators such as vital sign monitoring frequency, medication administration,
procedures, and care activities. This definition focuses on measurable, patient-specific factors that
vary across shifts and drive differential workload. Non-patient-related activities (e.g., planning,
documentation, handovers, breaks) are not explicitly modelled, as they are often constant or shared
across staff and not well captured in structured EHR data. While these contribute meaningfully to
total workload, this thesis emphasises task variation attributable to patient care.
Traditionally, nurse workload is measured using metrics such as patient-to-nurse ratios, hours of
care per patient day, or standardised scoring systems that rate patient acuity and care complexity
[1, 3]. For example, classification tools like the Nursing Activities Score or the Perroca Patient
Classification System categorise patients into levels (e.g., minimal, intermediate, intensive care
needs) based on the number and complexity of nursing interventions, which estimate the nursing
effort required. Nursing interventions refer to any direct or indirect actions performed by nurses
to meet patient care needs, ranging from routine monitoring and hygiene support to administering
medication or managing invasive procedures. While such tools provide structured assessments,
they often rely on manual data entry by nurses, which increases documentation burden and risks
variability and delays in data availability. Recent work emphasises the importance of leveraging
routinely collected EHR data to automate and standardise workload estimation, thus improving
both accuracy and timeliness [4]. Continuous manual assessment is time-consuming and not al-
ways feasible amidst busy clinical duties. This limitation has motivated the exploration of EHR
data as a source for automated workload estimation. Modern EHR systems record a wealth of
routine care activities such as vital sign monitoring, medication administrations, and nursing in-
terventions on a continuous basis. However, not all nursing activities are captured in structured
EHR data. Many routine or informal care tasks, such as comfort measures, emotional support, or
verbal re-orientation may go undocumented, particularly for stable patients. Conversely, formal
interventions are more likely to be documented in the EHR, especially for high-acuity or deterio-
rating patients. This may introduce a bias toward over-representing the workload of more severe
cases. These digital records offer an opportunity to quantify workload objectively by analysing
the frequency and types of care activities documented, without adding work for staff [5]. In other
words, care activity frequency in EHRs can serve as a proxy measure for nurse workload intensity.
If this EHR-derived workload indicator can be accurately predicted or categorised, it could support
real-time staffing and resource decisions.
Recent advances in data science and machine learning (ML) have further enabled EHR-based

workload analysis. Artificial intelligence techniques, particularly ML algorithms, excel at detect-
ing complex patterns within large datasets and have been employed in various nursing applications.
As demonstrated by Ahmed (2024), AI can streamline clinical workflows, enhance monitoring,
and assist decision-making, thereby offering promising avenues for automating nurse workload
estimation directly from EHR data . However, the same review also highlights potential pitfalls:
concerns include the erosion of human-centred care, algorithmic bias, data privacy threats, and the
risk of over-reliance on AI systems without sufficient oversight [6]. Consequently, while AI-driven
methods hold strong potential for improving workload measurement accuracy and efficiency, their
design and implementation must be carefully managed to support nurses rather than add cognitive
or ethical burdens. In the context of workload prediction, prior studies have demonstrated the
potential of ML models to learn from historical care data and anticipate workload needs. For in-
stance, da Rosa et al. (2024) developed a predictive model for classifying nursing workload using
secondary EHR data. The algorithm correctly assigned approximately 72% of cases to the appro-
priate workload category and demonstrated good discriminative performance, with an area under
the receiver operating characteristic (ROC) curve of 0.82 indicating a strong ability to distinguish
between different workload levels[7]. This result indicates that, by leveraging routine electronic
records, an automated classifier can approximate nurses’ workload levels with fairly high accu-
racy, reducing the need for manual scoring. Similarly, Song et al. (2024) demonstrated that ma-
chine learning methods can significantly enhance the prediction of daily nursing care time: in their
prospective cohort study involving longitudinal EHR records, a random forest model achieved a
coefficient of determination (R²) of 0.74 in predicting the number of minutes of direct nursing care
per patient per day of hospitalization (i.e., per patient-day) [8]. These findings suggest that data-
driven models can effectively capture a substantial portion of the variability in nursing workload
tied to patient characteristics and care activities.
In fact, certain patient factors have been identified as especially influential; for example, one study
found that a single functional status index (the Self-Care Index) could explain up to 40–66% of the
variance in recorded nursing workload minutes for hospital patients, and adding clinical factors
like fatigue or pain levels further improved the prediction [9]. Building on these prior works, the
study aims to harness structured EHR data to predict daily nursing workload in a hospital setting,
thereby contributing to the growing body of research on EHR-based workload estimation and its
applications in nurse staffing management.
Project Overview: This project focuses on predicting nursing workload at the day level using ret-
rospective EHR data from the University of Augsburg hospital. The dataset comprises structured,
shift-level records of nursing-related care activities spanning the years 2021 to 2023, covering both
intensive care unit (ICU) and general ward settings. Each data instance corresponds to a single hos-
pital shift (for a given unit or patient), with features derived from that day’s EHR logs, for example,
counts of various nursing tasks performed, patient census information, and other relevant clinical
or administrative indicators captured electronically. “Nurse workload” is operationalised in this
context as the frequency of documented nursing care activities per day, under the
assumption that a higher number of tasks and interventions recorded in the EHR reflects a higher
workload demand on the nursing staff. Based on this workload indicator, the outcomes are cate-
gorised into three levels: low, medium, and high workload days. These categories were defined
using threshold criteria on the activity counts (details of which are provided in the Methodology
chapter), in order to translate the continuous workload measures into actionable classes. Such cate-
gorisation is useful for management interpretations, for instance, identifying “high workload” days
that may warrant increased staffing, versus “low workload” days where resources may be reallo-
cated. It should be noted that the three-tier workload classification framework introduced addi-
tional complexity due to class imbalance. As is common in hospital operations, most patient-days
clustered toward clearly low- or high-workload extremes, while intermediate (moderate) workload

cases were less frequent. This skewed distribution can bias the learning process, often resulting
in poorer model performance on the least represented class, typically the low or medium tier, de-
pending on the setting. These classification challenges underscore the structural ambiguity of in-
termediate workload levels and highlight the need for mitigation strategies such as class weighting
or resampling, which are discussed later in the thesis.
To tackle the prediction task, a state-of-the-art machine learning model for tabular data: XG-
Boost (Extreme Gradient Boosting) is employed. XGBoost is an ensemble tree-based algorithm
that has gained widespread recognition for its high performance on structured datasets [10]. It
builds many decision trees in sequence, iteratively improving the model by correcting the errors
of previous trees in a boosting framework. XGBoost is chosen for this project for several reasons.
First, nursing EHR data in tabular form (consisting of counts, categorical labels such as ward type,
and possibly summary statistics) is well-suited to tree-based methods, which can natively handle
mixed data types and capture non-linear relationships without requiring extensive feature transfor-
mations. Unlike deep learning models originally designed for sequential or image data, a gradient
boosting tree model can effectively model feature interactions (e.g. a combination of patient factors
that jointly increase workload) and handle missing values or outliers in the data. In fact, XGBoost
and similar gradient boosting machines have been used to achieve leading results in many ma-
chine learning challenges involving structured features [10] , often outperforming neural network
approaches on tabular problems. Second, XGBoost includes built-in regularization and efficient
handling of large datasets, which is advantageous given the dataset’s size (daily records over mul-
tiple years) and the potential noise in raw EHR logs. The model’s fast training speed and ability
to scale to big data [10] allowed us to iterate on feature engineering and model tuning relatively
quickly. Overall, using XGBoost aligns well with the project’s aim to deliver a robust predictive
model for nurse workload classification and to do so with a tool that is practical for deployment
in hospital IT environments (where interpretability of feature importance and reasonable training
times are valued).
As the study utilises real-world hospital data, significant attention was given to data preprocess-
ing and quality control (without delving into advanced time-series modelling, which was beyond
scope). Raw EHR event logs are aggregated into meaningful daily features and applied several
cleaning steps to ensure the reliability of the input data. In particular, an automation filtering
procedure was used to remove or correct anomalous records (such as duplicate entries or system-
generated artifacts) that could skew the workload counts. An event capping strategy was also im-
plemented, wherein extremely high values for certain activity counts were capped at a reasonable
threshold to mitigate the influence of outliers (for example, a situation where a technical glitch
might record an implausibly large number of a certain event in one day). These preprocessing
steps were automated and consistently applied, resulting in a curated dataset that more accurately
reflects true nursing activity levels. These steps are acknowledged here to highlight that raw EHR
data often requires refinement, but defer the technical details to the Methodology chapter. By
preparing the data in this structured and cleaned form, a solid foundation is set for the modelling
phase. The objectives of this thesis are aligned with addressing the practical challenge of nurse
workload prediction and are framed as a set of research questions.
To guide this thesis, the following primary research question has been established:
How effectively can machine learning classification models predict
nurse workload using structured, day-level electronic health record (EHR)
data in hospital wards?
Secondary research questions further explore vital aspects of this investigation, including:
• Which key features derived from day-level EHR data (e.g., vital signs, medication adminis-

Key Contributions:
• Demonstrating EHR-driven workload prediction: This work shows the feasibility of using
day-level EHR data to automatically estimate nursing workload without requiring dedicated
staff scoring. Unlike prior studies focusing on regression or single units, it applies classi-
fication to a multi-ward hospital dataset. The XGBoost model’s performance indicates that
coarse daily EHR summaries contain sufficient information to distinguish high versus low
workload.
• Adapting a tree-based ML model (XGBoost) for workforce analytics: A modern tree-ensemble
method is applied and tuned for nurse staffing, highlighting its effectiveness on tabular
healthcare data. This choice demonstrates that advanced deep learning models are not the
only solution for temporal healthcare data. Feature importance analysis identifies key EHR
variables (e.g., admissions, documentation events, ICU vs ward) influencing workload clas-
sification, aiding interpretability and stakeholder trust.
• Insights on workload patterns and class imbalance: Analysis over multiple years reveals the
frequency of peak workload days and discusses the impact of class imbalance on predictive
accuracy. While the current study does not explicitly examine seasonal effects or psycho-
logical aspects of workload, future research could explore whether peaks align with periods
of increased morbidity, such as flu season, or whether patient severity impacts the mental
load on nursing staff. Techniques to improve medium-class predictions, such as merging
adjacent classes and using specialised metrics, are explored, offering guidance for future
studies addressing similar skewed data challenges.
In summary, this thesis is motivated by the practical need to support nursing workforce decisions
with data-driven tools. It aligns with the broader goal of leveraging health informatics to improve
care quality and operational efficiency. By predicting when high nursing workload will occur (and
identifying what drives it), hospital managers and unit leaders could proactively adjust staffing lev-
els or redistribute tasks, ultimately ensuring that patient care demand is met without overburdening
nurses. The study, using the University of Augsburg’s 2021–2023 ICU and ward data, serves as
a proof-of-concept for such predictive analytics in nursing management. The following chapters
will detail the methodological approach, data preparation (including the mentioned filtering and
capping procedures), model development with XGBoost, and an evaluation of the results in light
of the research questions. Findings to existing literature on nursing workload are also related and
discussed how this work can be expanded or implemented in real hospital settings. Ultimately,
this research aims to contribute to safer staffing practices and to inspire further innovations in
EHR-based workload estimation for healthcare [8].

2.1
Conceptualizing Nursing Workload
Nursing workload is a multidimensional concept that encompasses not only the volume of tasks
but also their complexity, required competencies, and emotional or cognitive demands. Griffiths et
al. [11] highlight how inconsistent terminology has led to fragmented understanding. Alghamdi et
al. [12] propose a more inclusive definition: “the amount of time and care that a nurse can devote
(directly and indirectly) towards patients, the workplace, and professional development.”
One framework for conceptualising workload is discussed in Kragler (2023) (see Section 2.3.1),
which differentiates workload into physical, psychological, and organisational components. These
distinctions help illustrate how documentation, patient mobility, and cognitive decision-making
tasks each contribute to the overall burden. Understanding workload requires separating and quan-
tifying its components: direct patient care, indirect care (e.g., documentation), coordination, and
administrative burden.
Workload perception is also shaped by shift dynamics, patient turnover, unit type, and staffing
levels. Several studies underscore that subjective workload perception does not always align with
measured workload, particularly when support structures (e.g., team culture or automation tools)
are present. This discrepancy justifies the growing interest in objective, data-derived workload
indicators.
A further challenge in conceptualising workload is recognising the temporal aspect. The demands
faced by nurses can fluctuate considerably across different hours of a shift, based on patient deteri-
oration, admissions/discharges, or time-sensitive procedures. This temporal volatility complicates
workload estimation from daily aggregates and highlights the trade-off between data granularity
and model feasibility. Importantly, patient-induced variability in nursing workload is strongly tied
to the clinical condition, acuity, and dependency of patients. As such, it is crucial to ensure that
training and test datasets reflect comparable patient compositions. Failing to do so may introduce
sampling bias and reduce the generalizability of predictive models. This consideration informs
both data splitting strategies and the interpretation of model performance across care settings.
2.2
Traditional Workload Assessment Methods
Numerous tools have been developed to quantify nursing workload, especially in ICU settings.
These methods fall into three broad categories: scoring systems, subjective assessments, and phys-

2.2.1
Scoring Systems
The most widely adopted objective scoring systems include:
• TISS and TISS-28 – The Therapeutic Intervention Scoring System (TISS) was introduced
by Cullen et al. and later refined into the more practical TISS-28 by Reis Miranda et al. [13,
14]. It quantifies workload based on the number and intensity of therapeutic interventions
provided to ICU patients.
• NAS (Nursing Activities Score) – Developed to address the limitations of TISS, the NAS
provides a more comprehensive representation of nursing workload by estimating the pro-
portion of time a nurse spends on direct patient care during a shift [15]. It includes both
technical and non-technical activities.
• NEMS (Nine Equivalents of Nursing Manpower Use Score) – A simplified workload
estimation tool derived from TISS, NEMS focuses on nine critical interventions and is widely
used in some European ICU settings due to its ease of implementation [16].
• RAFAELA System (based on Oulu patient classification) – Originating from Finland,
RAFAELA system links patient classification to actual nursing workload using the Oulu Pa-
tient Classification instrument. It includes the PAONCIL method, which compares available
nursing resources with the estimated workload, helping managers assess optimal staffing
levels [17].
These scores are useful for retrospective analysis but are rarely embedded in EHRs, making real-
time application difficult. They are most applicable to ICU settings, where interventions are more
frequent and standardised. In general wards, their use is minimal due to sparse documentation and
the lower acuity of patients.
Recent meta-analyses have shown that while NAS and TISS provide reliable indicators of care
demand, they often fail to capture indirect care burden such as documentation, coordination, and
psychosocial support. In particular, these tools were developed before the widespread digitisation
of hospitals, and thus are not easily adapted to real-time dashboards or predictive pipelines.
2.2.2
Subjective Measures
Subjective workload assessment tools such as the NASA Task Load Index (NASA-TLX) [18]
and the Subjective Workload Assessment Technique (SWAT) [19] quantify perceived workload
across multiple dimensions, including mental demand, effort, and temporal pressure. While these
instruments have provided valuable insights in controlled or experimental settings, their use in
routine clinical environments is limited. Self-assessments can be heavily influenced by individual
fatigue levels, team dynamics, shift-specific culture, and interpersonal stressors; factors that in-
troduce variability and reduce reproducibility. As such, subjective tools are difficult to scale and
standardise across hospital units, limiting their utility in real-time staffing decisions or predictive
modelling frameworks.
2.2.3
Physiological Indicators
Physiological stress indicators such as heart rate variability (HRV), cortisol levels, and galvanic
skin response have been explored as real-time proxies for nursing workload [20, 21]. These
biosensor-based methods offer objective insights into nurse stress responses during shifts, but they
require wearable technologies, are intrusive, and typically impractical for large-scale or routine
deployment in hospitals. Moreover, biosignals primarily reflect overall physiological stress rather
than task-specific workload, and can be influenced by non-work factors such as sleep, hydration,
or emotional state.

2.2.4
Patient Acuity Scores as Indirect Proxies
Although not designed to measure nursing workload directly, physiological severity scores such
as the Acute Physiology and Chronic Health Evaluation II (APACHE II), Simplified Acute
Physiology Score II (SAPS II), and Mortality Prediction Model (MPM) are widely used in ICU
settings to assess patient condition and predict mortality risk. Although originally developed for
clinical risk stratification, several studies have found moderate correlations between these scores
and nursing workload, as sicker patients generally require more intensive care [22, 23, 24]. For
example, higher APACHE II or SAPS II scores have been associated with increased Nursing Ac-
tivities Score (NAS).
2.3
Machine Learning-Based Approaches
Machine learning (ML) models offer a scalable alternative to manual workload assessment by
identifying patterns in routinely collected EHR data. These methods can infer latent workload
levels from care activity frequencies, patient complexity, and treatment intensity.
This chapter also draw comparative insights from the master’s thesis by Julian Kragler (2023) [25],
which focused on binary workload classification using EHR data from the University Hospital of
Augsburg. While this project expands to a multi-class setting, Kragler’s approach offers useful
benchmarks for data preprocessing, feature selection, and modelling design.
2.3.1
EHR-Derived Predictors
EHR systems log structured data on vitals, medications, lab orders, care procedures, and admin-
istrative events. Studies such as Song et al. [8] and Hunstein and Fiebig [9] demonstrated that
these logs can be transformed into workload features, such as daily event counts or time-weighted
intervention scores.
This chapter also draw comparative insights from the master’s thesis by Julian Kragler (2023)
[25] (see Section 2.3.1), which focused on binary workload classification using EHR data from
the University Hospital of Augsburg. While this project expands to a multi-class setting, Kragler’s
approach offers useful benchmarks for data preprocessing, feature selection, and modelling design.
Case Study: Song et al. (2024)
Song et al. conducted a prospective cohort study between Mar-2019 and Aug-2021 in three gas-
troenterology wards across two tertiary hospitals in China [8]. Their primary objective was to
predict daily nursing workload using machine learning models based on patient characteristics.
The study included 133 patients over 1,339 days, with an average length of stay of 8.56 days.
Nursing workload was operationalised as direct nursing care time—the time spent on patient-facing
activities such as hygiene, nutrition, medication, condition monitoring, and documentation. This
was measured daily using a time-and-motion approach with the Nursing Time Measurement Ques-
tionnaire, which lists 116 nursing tasks across six categories. Non-patient activities like meetings
and administrative work were excluded from the workload definition.
The researchers applied three supervised machine learning algorithms: Decision Trees, Random
Forest, and XGBoost to predict the number of minutes of nursing care required per patient per
day. The study utilised R language to randomly assign 80% of patients to the training set, with
the remaining 20% used for testing. Predictive performance was evaluated using Mean Squared
Error (MSE), Root Mean Squared Error (RMSE), and R2. Among the models, Random Forest
achieved the best performance, with an R2 of 0.74 and an RMSE of 1148.38 minutes. XGBoost
showed comparable performance, highlighting the potential of ensemble learning for real-time
workload estimation. Key predictive variables included Activities of Daily Living (ADL) score,
complications, comorbidities, BMI, past illness history, and economic income. The ADL score

was the strongest contributor to workload, followed by the presence of complications. Contrary to
previous studies, factors such as documentation time or medication count were not reported as top

This study demonstrates the feasibility of using structured patient characteristics many of which
are routinely captured in EHR systems, to predict daily nursing workload with high accuracy.
However, the model’s applicability outside gastroenterology wards remains untested, and broader
factors such as ward dynamics or organisational variables were not considered.
The relative importance of workload predictors in this study may be influenced by the charac-
teristics of patients typically treated in gastroenterology wards. These units often involve stable,
longer-stay patients with chronic conditions and recurring support needs such as nutrition, hygiene,
and routine monitoring. This context may elevate the importance of ADL scores and reduce the
relevance of acute interventions or high-frequency documentation. As such, the model’s feature
importance profile may not directly transfer to other clinical contexts, such as ICUs or surgical
units. Further validation across diverse ward types is necessary to assess generalisability.
Benchmark Study: Kragler (2023)
One relevant benchmark is the master’s thesis by Julian Kragler (2023), which explored EHR-
based workload classification using data from the University Hospital of Augsburg. His study
focused on binary classification of nursing workload (high vs. low) using features extracted from
structured EHR data, such as vital sign frequencies, medication administrations, access procedures,
and patient mobility events.
Kragler employed tree-based models, including Random Forest and AdaBoost (but not XGBoost),
achieving an AUC of approximately 0.74 based on ROC curve evaluation. Feature importance
analysis revealed that medication and care activity frequencies were the strongest predictors. How-
ever, his thesis did not address class imbalance in multi-class settings, nor did it stratify analyses
by ward type (e.g., ICU vs. general ward). His data preprocessing pipeline also differed, partly
due to the use of a different workload scoring approach. Furthermore, the thesis document and
codebase are not publicly available, limiting reproducibility and direct comparison.
This project extends Kragler’s methodology by expanding to multi-class workload prediction (low,
medium, high), incorporating class-weighted XGBoost, and applying it across both ICU and gen-
eral ward settings.
2.3.2
Predictive Models
Several machine learning models approaches have been used to to estimate nursing workload, each
with different trade-offs between interpretability, data requirements, and predictive performance.
Most approaches fall into three broad categories: tree-based ensemble models, deep learning meth-
ods for sequential EHR data, and simulation-based strategies for staffing optimisation.
Tree-based models, such as Random Forest, AdaBoost, and XGBoost, are among the most
commonly used techniques for EHR-based workload prediction. Their popularity stems from their
ability to handle high-dimensional, tabular data with minimal preprocessing, as well as their in-
terpretability via feature importance metrics. In Kragler’s thesis, Random Forest and AdaBoost
models were used to classify high vs. low workload shifts, achieving AUC values around 0.74.
In this study, XGBoost was selected for its strong performance and model transparency, which is
essential for potential deployment in clinical settings.
Deep learning models, including Transformer-based architectures like Med-BERT and Hi-BEHRT,
have also been applied to workload-related prediction tasks, particularly for estimating length of
stay or resource utilisation. These models leverage temporal sequences of EHR events to learn

contextualised patient representations and have demonstrated improvements of 2–7% in predictive
accuracy over traditional methods [26, 27]. However, their practical application to nursing work-
load prediction is limited by the need for fine-grained, time-stamped event data often unavailable
in shift-level documentation. Furthermore, deep learning models are typically more opaque and
require careful validation before being used in high-stakes environments.
Agent-based and scheduling models provide a different paradigm by simulating the interactions
between nurses, patients, and hospital systems. These approaches, while not directly trained on
EHR data, offer useful insights into optimal staffing strategies and workflow dynamics [28]. They
are particularly valuable for exploring ”what-if” policy scenarios or the impact of staffing changes
on system-wide outcomes.
While deep learning models hold theoretical advantages in capturing temporal and nonlinear in-
teractions, current evidence especially from hospital deployments favours simpler, interpretable
models. Tree ensembles such as XGBoost and Random Forest continue to dominate in practice
due to their ease of integration, explainability, and compatibility with structured EHR data.
However, it is important to note that many of these studies do not predict nursing workload directly.
Instead, they rely on proxy outcomes such as length of stay, resource intensity, or direct care time
as surrogate indicators of workload. While these proxies provide measurable and often clinically
relevant targets, they may not fully capture the multidimensional and contextual nature of actual
nursing burden.
2.3.3
Handling Class Imbalance
A common challenge in nursing workload classification is the imbalance between workload cat-
egories. Medium workload days often fall near class boundaries and are particularly prone to
misclassification.
See Section 2.3.1 for model details. Some studies have addressed imbalance through techniques
such as SMOTE or class weighting to improve model sensitivity for minority classes. This project
adopts class-weighted XGBoost with a focus on improving precision and recall for the medium
workload class, which tends to be underrepresented and conceptually ambiguous.
2.3.4
Model Interpretability and Deployment
Tree based models like XGBoost offer interpretability via feature importance metrics, facilitating
transparent communication with clinical teams (see Section 2.3.1). Interpretable models are also
better suited for deployment in dashboards, audits, and shift planning tools. For instance, a Nor-
wegian hospital’s field trial of an NLP based decision support system found that embedding clear
visual explanations into existing workflows improved clinician trust and adoption—highlighting
the importance of explainability and user-centered design [29].
2.4
Summary of Literature Review
This chapter reviewed the evolution of nursing workload assessment, from manual scoring sys-
tems to modern EHR-based machine learning approaches. While NAS and TISS provide valuable
frameworks in ICUs, their limitations outside critical care highlight the need for automated, gen-
eralizable tools. Machine learning models, particularly interpretable ones like XGBoost, offer a
practical path forward. Taking inspiration from the modelling techniques used in Kragler’s master’s
thesis, this project applies and adapts structured EHR features to predict nurse workload in both
ICU and ward settings, with special attention to class imbalance, interpretability, and deployment
feasibility.

Methodology
This chapter details the methodology to answer the research question: How effectively can machine
learning classification models predict nurse workload using structured, day-level electronic health
record(EHR) data in hospital wards? A four-stage pipeline is presented:
• Data Preparation: Temporal segmentation of 201,044 shift-level records into daily aggre-
gates, addressing documentation gaps (Section 3.1).
• Target Definition: A weighted composite score (Section 3.2) derived from vital signs, care
activities, procedures, and medications, calibrated to clinical effort.
• Feature Engineering: Clinically interpretable predictors (e.g., abnormal vitals, comorbidity
bins) from 120 structured attributes (Section 3.3).
• Modelling: XGBoost classification with ward-specific adaptations, evaluated for opera-
tional relevance (Section 3.4).
The dataset, anonymised via Fall_ID (treated throughout this project as individual patients), as-
sumes independence between admissions (see Section 3.1.5). Key challenges in this dataset include
the sparsity of shift-level documentation and the absence of a direct workload label. To address
this, a proxy workload measure is defined, based on the frequency of manually documented care
activities, such as vital sign entries, medication administrations, and procedural logs while filtering
out automated observations to better reflect actual nurse effort (see Section 3.2.3).
3.1
Data Source and Cohort Description
This study uses structured electronic health record (EHR) data from the University Hospital of
Augsburg, covering the period from January 2021 to April 2023. The dataset was selected for its
granular documentation of nursing activities across four units: two intensive care units (IZ21 –
Station 2.1, IZ22 – Station 2.2) and two general wards (085 – Station 8.5, 088 – Station 8.8).
To simplify analysis and align with the study’s research objectives, these four units were grouped
into two categories: ICU (IZ21, IZ22) and general ward (085, 088). The full dataset comprises
201,044 shift-level records corresponding to 10,999 unique hospital admissions.
Each record captures the following information:
• Clinical Actions: Frequency and presence of vital sign measurements, medication admin-
istrations, procedures, and direct care activities (see Table 3.2).
• Operational Metadata: Shift type and timing, ward classification (ICU vs. ward), and
patient demographic attributes.
Data were extracted from a single Excel workbook, where each row represents a discrete nursing
shift of 12, 8, or 4 hours. This high temporal resolution enables reconstruction of day-to-day
workload patterns and reduces potential for recall bias commonly associated with aggregated or
manually reported measures.

recurring hospital shifts (Table 3.1). This segmentation appears to reflect the hospital’s internal
scheduling system, although official confirmation of shift policy was not available.

3.1.5
Gaps, Assumptions, and Limitations
Several assumptions were made to enable analysis:
• Re-admissions: Treating each Fall_ID as an independent case may lead to underestima-
tion of workload for patients who are admitted multiple times, potentially biasing workload
estimates at the per-patient level.
• Proxy Validity: Unrecorded tasks (e.g., family counselling) are excluded, potentially under-
estimating true workload. Conversely, some recorded tasks (e.g., administering oral medi-
cation) may occasionally be performed by family members, leading to overestimation.
• Automation Threshold: The 0.5/hour vital sign threshold, while clinically justified, may
misclassify manual measurements during acute deterioration (explained in Section A.3.
• Patient Case-Mix Drift: Patient condition is a key determinant of workload. While ICD10
codes and secondary diagnoses (Nebendiagn) were available, stratifying or balancing train-

3.2
Target Variable: Workload Definition
The primary modelling target is the Total Workload, defined as a composite measure of nursing
effort aggregated at the patient-day level, that is, per patient per day of hospitalisation. This proxy
captures the manual burden on nurses by summing weighted contributions from four structured
EHR domains: vital sign monitoring, direct care activities, procedural access, and medication
administration.
Weights for each domain were derived from clinical workload frameworks, including the Nursing
Activities Score (NAS) [30] and time-motion studies such as Ball et al. [31].
• Vital Signs Frequency: Includes manually recorded parameters (e.g., heart rate, blood pres-
sure, respiratory rate). Each manual set contributes 1 unit ( 5 minutes). Automated measure-
ments are down-weighted (see Section 3.2.3).
• Care Activities: Encompasses labor-intensive tasks like hygiene, wound care, and suction-
ing. Each care type is flagged once per day and contributes 4 units ( 20 minutes).
• Access Procedures: Covers interventions like catheter insertions, tracheostomies, and teleme-
try setup. Contributions range from 1 to 4 units, depending on complexity.
• Medication Administration: Includes oral, intravenous, subcutaneous, and other routes.
Each route used contributes 1 unit ( 5 minutes).
3.2.1
Calculation of Total Workload
The total workload for each patient-day is computed as:
Total Workload = w1·Vital Load+w2·Care Load+w3·Access Load+w4·Medication Load (3.1)
Where the weights w1, w2, w3, w4 reflect the estimated time and clinical intensity of each domain,
calibrated using real-world data and validated workload scoring systems.
In summary, the Total Workload serves as an interpretable, task-weighted proxy for nursing effort
that accounts for both the volume and complexity of patient care. It enables practical downstream
modelling while aligning closely with how workload is perceived and documented in clinical set-
tings.
3.2.2
Rationale for Daily Aggregation
The goal of this study is to estimate nursing workload per patient per day. Although the raw data
are structured by nursing shift (e.g., Tag, Nacht1, Nacht2), this level of granularity posed several
challenges for workload estimation.
First, documentation practices may vary across shifts, and it is not always clear whether certain
care activities are consistently recorded at the time of occurrence or in aggregate form. This un-
certainty introduces potential inconsistencies in shift-level data. Second, shift durations are not
uniform, ranging from 4 to 12 hours making raw count comparisons unreliable without further
normalization.

3.2.3
Vital Sign Automation Adjustment
To more accurately estimate the manual nursing workload associated with vital sign monitoring,
a rule-based correction was applied to distinguish between manually and automatically recorded
measurements. Automated monitoring especially common in ICU settings can otherwise inflate
workload estimates if not properly adjusted for.
Although the primary modelling objective is to estimate daily workload, shift-level granularity
was retained (Tag, Nacht1, Nacht2) during preprocessing in order to detect intra-day variation in
automation status. Since automation flags may differ between shifts, this step allowed a more
accurate classification prior to aggregation. Each shift was assigned its documented duration (Tag
= 12h, Nacht1 = 8h, Nacht2 = 4h), enabling normalisation of event frequencies on a per-hour basis.
A threshold of 0.5 measurements per hour was applied (equivalent to 6 readings in a 12-hour shift)
to distinguish manual from automated recordings:
• Vital signs recorded more frequently than 0.5 times per hour were flagged as automated
(automation flag = 1). Their workload contribution was weighted as 0.2, reflecting minimal
manual effort.
Note: The workload unit weights are defined in Section 3.2, where 1 unit corresponds to approx-
imately 5 minutes of manual effort. Automated measurements were assigned a reduced weight
of 0.2 (i.e., 1 minute), based on a time-motion study by Skyttberg et al. [32], which found that
automated documentation of vital signs took on average 0.6 minutes per set, compared to sev-
eral minutes for manual entry. This rounding to 1 minute ensures interpretability while remaining
conservative.
Threshold Justification: The 0.5/hour threshold is grounded in typical nurse-to-patient staffing
ratios and practical expectations of manual monitoring frequency:
• In ICU settings, nurse-to-patient ratios typically range from 1:1 to 1:3 [33]. Although fre-
quent checks are feasible, sustained manual measurements more often than once every 2
hours (approx 6 times per 12-hour shift) are unlikely due to competing clinical demands.
• In general wards, where ratios often range from 1:5 to 1:10 [33], such frequent manual mon-
itoring is operationally implausible. Therefore, high-frequency entries in these contexts are
likely generated by automated systems.
This rule-based adjustment ensures that workload estimates more accurately reflect realistic man-
ual effort, while accounting for staffing limitations and care setting differences.
A figure illustrating per-hour vital sign measurement frequencies and the automation threshold is
in Appendix A.3.

3.2.4.7

While the 33rd and 66th percentile cutoffs served as the primary stratification method for tri-class
workload classification, additional thresholding strategies were explored to assess the robustness
of model performance under different label definitions. These alternatives were motivated by dif-
ferences in workload distribution shape across settings, particularly the bimodal pattern observed
in ICU cases and the near-normal distribution in general ward cases.
The following strategies were evaluated:
• Median cutoff (binary classification): Total workload values were split into low and high
classes based on the median value within each ward type. This simple, balanced approach
was tested to reduce ambiguity around “medium” workload cases.
• Quartile-based cutoffs (three classes): Workload was divided into low (≤25th percentile),
medium (25th–75th), and high (> 75th percentile). This narrower definition of high workload
aimed to isolate extreme cases more clearly.
• High-load flag (binary classification using 75th percentile): A binary flag was created to
identify only the top 25% of patient-days as high workload, with the rest considered not high.
This formulation aligns with real-world scenarios where hospitals may prioritise detecting
only the most resource-intensive shifts.

3.3
Feature Selection and Feature Engineering
3.3.1
Rationale for Manual Feature Selection vs. PCA
Although principal component analysis (PCA) is commonly used to reduce dimensionality and
highlight feature importance, it was deliberately avoided in this study. PCA transforms features
into orthogonal components, which, while mathematically useful, are less interpretable in a clini-
cal context. Instead, domain-informed feature selection was prioritised and used direct empirical
correlations with the total workload target to retain clinically meaningful variables. This manual
process ensures that individual features maintain their interpretability, supports transparency in
decision-making, and aligns more naturally with hospital deployment scenarios where actionable
insights are critical.
3.3.2
Selection Criteria
Feature selection was guided by clinical logic, relevance to nursing workload, and representation
in structured EHR data. Variables were chosen if they met the following criteria:
• Routinely and reliably documented across patient episodes
• Clinically linked to nursing workload based on task-level effort, acuity, or complexity
• Available early in the patient timeline to enable real-time workload estimation
Feature categories were primarily inspired by the Nursing Activities Score (NAS) framework [15],
and recent literature emphasizing the predictive value of structured EHR data for nurse workload
modeling [9, 8]. These studies support the inclusion of vitals, lab values, medication routes, care
procedures, and access interventions as relevant proxies for nursing effort.

This aggregation yielded one row per patient per day, capturing the cumulative burden and clinical

3.3.3.4
Comorbidity Stratification (Nebendiag)
To incorporate patient complexity into workload prediction, a comorbidity score variable named
Nebendiag was extracted, which represents the count of secondary diagnoses (Neben-Diagnosen)
recorded during each hospital stay. This metric is a commonly used proxy in EHR systems to
reflect the overall burden of illness beyond the primary diagnosis.
Since Nebendiag is right-skewed and differs in distribution between ICU and general ward pa-
tients, quantile-based binning was applied to transform it into a categorical feature suitable for
modeling:
• The Nebendiag value at the patient-day level was retained using a mode-based imputation
strategy, selecting the most frequently occurring value within each admission to ensure con-
sistency and minimize the influence of documentation artifacts.
• Next, tertile-based quantile binning (q = 3) separately for each ward type was performed to
account for distributional differences between ICU and general ward cohorts.
• This resulted in three comorbidity groups per ward type, labeled as Low, Medium, and High.
• A combined categorical feature named WardType_Quantile_Bin was then defined as:
WardType_Quantile_Bin = Ward_Type + _bin_ + Quantile_Index

To account for the impact of ICU stays on overall care complexity, two temporal features were

3.3.3.7
Clinical Flags: Abnormal Vitals and Laboratory Results
To augment frequency-based workload features, binary clinical flags were introduced based on
abnormal vital signs, critical laboratory results, and ICU transfer status. These flags serve as in-
dicators of acute patient complexity that may not be captured through documentation frequency
alone. By incorporating both the intensity (event frequency) and severity (clinical abnormality) of
care needs, these features aim to enhance the model’s ability to identify high-burden shifts.
Thresholds for clinical abnormalities were hardcoded based on widely accepted cut-offs used in
adult inpatient care, rather than derived from statistical distributions within the dataset. This ap-
proach supports interpretability, generalizability across hospitals, and alignment with real-world
clinical decision-making. Although many clinical reference values (e.g., for creatinine or
haemoglobin) are gender-specific, gender-neutral thresholds were applied here for simplicity and
modelling consistency, acknowledging a trade-off between precision and feasibility.
Justification for Hardcoded Thresholds: These thresholds reflect established diagnostic norms
and typical intervention triggers used in hospital workflows. By focusing on clinically meaning-
ful deviations rather than statistical outliers, the resulting flags increase clinical trust and provide
interpretable signals of workload pressure. While prior studies have not explicitly modeled EHR-
derived alerts or deterioration indicators for nursing workload estimation, this approach concep-
tually aligns with documented associations between patient acuity and nurse effort in critical care
settings.

egorical variables such as gender and ward type were encoded using one-hot or label encoding,

group-wise imputation) is described earlier in the data preprocessing section and was not repeated
here.

3.4.1
Model Selection and Justification
The task as a supervised classification problem was formulated, categorizing patient-day records
into low, medium, and high workload classes. These were derived from ward-specific 33rd and
66th percentiles of the continuous Total Workload target to ensure balanced class distributions (see
Section 3.2.4.6).
Extreme Gradient Boosting (XGBoost) was selected as the primary algorithm, due to its proven
performance and robustness in healthcare contexts. Key advantages include:
• Native support for missing data, which reduces preprocessing overhead.
• Efficiency and scalability, suitable for large EHR datasets.
• Interpretability via feature importance scores, aiding clinical trust.
To account for setting-specific patterns, separate models were trained for:
• ICU patients only,
• General ward patients only,
• A combined dataset including both ward types (with Ward_Type as a feature).
3.4.2
Training Procedure and Evaluation Design
To reflect deployment in real-world settings, a time-aware holdout strategy was implemented:
the most recent 20% of patient-day records (by admission date) were used as a test set, while the
earlier 80% served as training data.
Within the training set, 5-fold stratified cross-validation was performed to evaluate generaliza-
tion and tune model hyperparameters. Stratification preserved class balance across folds, ensuring
fair representation of low, medium, and high workload cases.
Model performance was assessed using:
• Macro-averaged F1-score, to account for imbalanced class distributions
• Class-wise precision and recall, to evaluate model utility across workload levels
• Overall accuracy on the held-out test set
• Confusion matrices for interpretability and error analysis
Details of the final hyperparameter configuration are provided in Appendix A.5.

period. This could explain elevated baseline acuity, particularly in hypoxia and CRP-related fea-
tures, which are known to be common in both heart failure and COVID-related complications. The
thresholds used to define clinical flags, such as hypoxia (e.g., SpO2 < 92%) and elevated CRP, were
based on conventional clinical cutoffs commonly used in ICU and ward triage protocols. While
these serve as reasonable baselines, they may not fully account for patient-specific or diagnosis-
specific variability. For instance, a CRP level considered elevated in one context may be less
meaningful in another, depending on comorbidity or treatment stage. Future models could benefit
from dynamically adjusting these thresholds based on diagnosis groups, patient age, or baseline
vitals. Doing so may improve the sensitivity of clinical flags as early warning signals and enhance
their predictive value in workload modelling

Summary of Findings
This study developed and evaluated machine learning models for nurse workload prediction using
structured, shift-level EHR data from ICU and general ward settings. The key findings include
both methodological insights and empirical results from comparative classification experiments:
• Classification outperformed regression: Converting continuous workload into discrete
tiers led to improved robustness, especially in handling outliers and aligning with practi-
cal decision-making. Regression models struggled to capture the high end of the workload
spectrum and explained only 30% of variance in total workload.
• Binary classification with median cutoff was most effective: Among all stratification
strategies, the median-based binary classification yielded the highest and most consistent
macro F1-scores (up to 0.80 for ICU). This framing offered a balanced and interpretable
structure with strong separation between low and high workload days.

• 66th percentile high-load flag focused model attention but reduced recall: Framing only

5
Discussion
5.1
Key Findings
This study shows that structured EHR data can be used to effectively estimate nursing workload
using machine learning, with classification models, particularly gradient boosted trees outperform-
ing regression. Binary classification achieved macro F1-scores up to 0.8, while regression models
explained only a modest share of variance (R2 between 0.28 and 0.33) and systematically under-
estimated high workload cases.
Binary clinical abnormality flags (e.g., hypoxia, fever) offered minimal performance gains, likely
due to redundancy with underlying vitals and labs. Across all tri-class experiments, one class, typ-
ically low or medium, consistently underperformed, reflecting clinical ambiguity and overlapping
feature patterns rather than model limitations.
Interpretability analyses confirmed clinical validity: top predictors such as respiratory rate, CRP,
comorbidity scores, and ICU history aligned with known drivers of nursing effort. Feature im-
portance varied by setting, with ICU models emphasising acute physiological trends and general
ward models focusing on chronicity and length of stay, highlighting the value of ward-specific
modelling.
5.2
Interpretation of Results
5.2.1
Workload is Better Framed as a Classification Task
The results confirm that nurse workload, at least as approximated from structured EHR data, is
better modelled as a categorical outcome than a continuous one. Regression models explained only
a modest share of variance, while classification models reliably identified high and low workload
days. This reflects the real-world nature of nursing effort: it tends to be experienced in discrete
tiers (e.g., calm vs. overwhelmed), rather than as a fine-grained numeric scale. The success of
classification also supports its practical utility in hospital operations, where decisions like staffing
escalation are triggered by threshold-based categorisations.
5.2.2
Limitations of Tri-Class Workload Classification
Although the models distinguished high and low workload tiers with reasonable accuracy, tri-class
classification consistently proved more challenging. In both ICU and general ward settings, one
class—typically the low or medium tier—exhibited substantially lower F1-scores. This likely re-
flects the clinical heterogeneity of moderate or low-intensity workload days, which often combine
routine care with sporadic interventions and lack consistent documentation patterns.
These challenges are further compounded by the long-tailed distribution of workload values and
the sparsity of structured EHR data in lower-intensity cases. Patient-days with minimal recorded
activity are common, making it difficult to capture nuanced differences between low and moderate
workload levels using structured features alone.
SHAP analyses showed that top predictors such as CRP levels, pulse rate, and comorbidity scores
were often associated with the medium class, but did not provide sufficient contrast to distinguish
it reliably. This suggests that the medium class may not reflect a well-defined physiological or
operational state, but rather a transitional or mixed category.
Overall, these findings highlight a key trade-off: while tri-class classification provides greater
granularity, it introduces label ambiguity that can reduce model robustness. In applied settings,

binary formulations (e.g., high vs. not-high) may offer more stable and interpretable outputs for
workload prediction.
5.2.3
Differing Drivers Across Care Settings
The contrast between ICU and general ward models reveals the importance of context-aware mod-
elling. ICU workload was more responsive to short-term physiological fluctuations, especially
lagged vitals such as pulse rate and temperature, while general ward predictions leaned heavily
on chronic factors like comorbidity burden, age, and inflammatory markers. These differences
align with clinical expectations: ICU care is reactive and vitals-driven, whereas ward-based care
involves more longitudinal management.
Yet, it is important to note that the workload weighting scheme used in this study was adapted from
the Nursing Activities Score (NAS), which was developed specifically for ICU settings. This may
have introduced a structural bias favouring the ICU models. The strong performance of the general
ward models despite this mismatch is encouraging and suggests that our feature set captured key
workload signals even beyond the constraints of ICU-oriented labels.
5.3
Comparison with Prior Work
This study extends the growing body of literature on EHR-based nursing workload prediction,
particularly building on the work of Song et al. [8] and Kragler [25].
Song et al. used structured patient characteristics, such as Activities of Daily Living (ADL) scores
and complication status, to predict direct care time in gastroenterology wards. Their prospec-
tive data collection and homogeneous patient population enabled high predictive performance
(R2 = 0.74). In contrast, our study relied on retrospective EHR event logs and proxy workload
scores derived from weighted task counts. Despite this, we achieved competitive classification per-
formance for high workload classes (F1 up to 0.72), suggesting that routinely collected EHR data
can be repurposed for operational workload forecasting without the need for resource-intensive
annotation.
Kragler’s thesis focused on binary workload classification (high vs. low) in ICU settings, using
Random Forests and AdaBoost. He reported AUC values near 0.74, with medication and care activ-
ities as top predictors. Our work extends prior research by (1) introducing a multi-class framework
to capture intermediate workload states, and (2) validating the relevance of similar features across
both ICU and general ward settings. Notably, while these features were informative overall, they
failed to consistently distinguish one workload tier, typically the low or medium class, highlighting
the difficulty of clearly separating moderate or low-acuity cases in practice.
Together, these comparisons underscore the importance of data context, label design, and model
interpretability when translating workload analytics into clinical decision support tools.
5.4
Strengths and Limitations
5.4.1
Strengths
This study demonstrates the feasibility of predicting daily nursing workload using only structured
EHR data, without relying on direct annotation or NAS score collection. Key strengths include:
• Real-world generalisability: The models were trained on heterogeneous, multi-day pa-
tient data across ICU and general ward settings, improving robustness beyond single-unit or
condition-specific studies.
• Transparent methodology: By combining interpretable machine learning (XGBoost) with

SHAP-based feature analysis, the study provides explainable predictions that align with clin-
ical expectations, facilitating deployment in hospital settings.
• Proxy workload design: A composite workload target was constructed from clinically
grounded event weights, allowing workload estimation even in the absence of NAS-style
manual scoring.
• Ward-specific models: Separate pipelines for ICU and general wards respected contextual
differences in care delivery, documentation, and patient acuity.
5.4.2
Limitations
Despite these strengths, several limitations must be acknowledged:
• Proxy target validity: The total workload label is a heuristic approximation based on event
counts and estimated time weights. Without gold-standard comparisons (e.g., NAS or time-
motion studies), its real-world accuracy remains uncertain.
• Temporal aggregation: All data were aggregated at the patient-day level, masking intra-
shift fluctuations and peak load periods that nurses experience.
• Single-centre dataset: The models were trained and tested using data from one hospital.
Documentation practices, staffing ratios, and care protocols may differ in other settings,
limiting generalisability.
• Challenges inherent to tri-class workload classification: Across tri-class experiments,
one class, typically the low or medium workload tier, consistently underperformed. This re-
flects the difficulty of clearly separating intermediate or low-intensity care demands, which
often lack distinct patterns in structured EHR data and may overlap substantially with neigh-
bouring classes.
5.5
Future Work
Building on this study, future work could focus on the following areas:
5.5.1
Methodological Improvements
• Temporal modelling: Workload dynamics could be better captured over time by incorpo-
rating sequence-based models (e.g., recurrent neural networks or transformers), particularly
to detect workload spikes or transition periods.
• Hybrid classification-regression: Using ensemble outputs (e.g., “high vs. not high” clas-
sification alongside continuous estimation) could improve flexibility in deployment, espe-
cially for prioritisation and triage tools.
• Cross-site validation: Testing the model across other hospitals with different EHR sys-
tems, patient demographics, and staffing practices would help assess generalisability and
adaptation needs.
5.5.2
Operational Integration
• Real-time dashboard use: Showing workload predictions directly in nurse manager dash-
boards could help with planning shifts and spotting overload early.
• Clinician feedback: Getting input from nurses and doctors when designing alerts or inter-
faces can improve trust and make sure the tools fit well into daily routines.
• Comparing with NAS: Future work could compare the model’s results with NAS scores to
check accuracy and fine-tune how sensitive the model is.

Conclusion
This study presents a comprehensive approach to predicting nursing workload using structured
day-level electronic health record (EHR) data and interpretable machine learning techniques. By
applying XGBoost classifiers across ICU and general ward settings, the study demonstrates that
routine EHR data without requiring manual workload annotation can approximate nurse effort with
clinically useful precision, particularly for identifying high-burden patient-days.
Key findings highlight strong classification performance in both ICU and general wards, with high
workload cases predicted most reliably. Vital sign dynamics, care procedures, comorbidity indices,
and lab markers emerged as consistent predictors, aligning well with real-world clinical expecta-
tions. SHAP and feature importance analyses further confirmed the plausibility and transparency
of the model outputs.
Importantly, this work also reveals key blind spots, like, one workload tier, typically the medium
or low class, consistently proved most difficult to classify, likely due to clinical ambiguity and
overlapping feature patterns. Furthermore, reliance on structured EHR data inherently overlooks
undocumented aspects of nursing labor, such as emotional support, family communication, and
rapid prioritisation. This risks under-representing invisible yet effort-intensive tasks that signifi-
cantly contribute to nurses’ workload.
These limitations highlight the need for hybrid human-AI systems that combine algorithmic pre-
diction with the contextual understanding of frontline nurses. While machine learning can identify
and quantify observable tasks, it often fails to capture the nuanced reasons that make some tasks
more demanding than others. Bridging this gap by integrating richer data, temporal patterns, and
clinical context remains a central challenge for advancing the next generation of workload analyt-
ics.
In conclusion, this study contributes meaningfully to the nursing workload literature by showing
that machine learning models trained on structured hospital data can support real-time workforce
planning and early escalation detection. With further validation and integration, such tools may
enhance staffing fairness, improve patient safety, and reduce nurse burnout in complex clinical
environments.
