Abstract
This thesis centers on the design, construction, and characterization of a heating system for liquid-phase
transmission electron microscopy (LPTEM). The first part covers the fundamentals of heat transport and
the derivation of thermal distribution models. The following section details the selection and assembly
of heating system components and how they integrate with an existing TEM holder.
Programming
work in Python to facilitate serial communication among components and to provide a user interface,
including a PID controller for temperature regulation, is also discussed. The system is experimentally
evaluated in both air and vacuum environments to determine its thermal properties. The performance of
the heating system is further tested by studying its thermal drift as well as the precipitation of CaCO3.
The thesis concludes with an evaluation of the systemŌĆÖs strengths and weaknesses, and suggestions for
future improvements.

Therefore, the process of increasing the T - heating - is extremely relevant in science and research,
and we list out a few possibilities of what can be achieved with heating in science:
1. Phase Transition - where physical matter undergoes a change in its physical state between solid,
liquid, and gaseous phases (excluding plasma). By applying heat, researchers can investigate the
underlying mechanisms and properties of these transformations. The study and tabulation of phase
diagram for different materials such as metal alloys, glass, polymer are fundamental in material
science and industry [2]. The phase transitions of nanoparticles [3] as well as solid-state dewetting
of thin-films in TEM by annealing the sample are also [4] interesting research topics and can be
utilized in micro/nanofabrication.
2. Diffusion - the macroscopic net movement of particles driven by the concentration gradient. On
the other hand, Brownian motion is the microscopic random movement of particles, that facilitate
the macroscopic movement. Both motions are effected by heating since it increases the average
kinetic energy of the particles, accelerating their motions thus promoting diffusion. For example,
the diffusion coefficient D for Brownian motion of the nanoparticles in LPTEM is stated by the
Stokes-Einstein relation as D = kBT/6ŽĆ╬Ę╬▒ [5] which scales linearly with temperature.
3. Hydrothermal Synthesis - to crystallize substances from aqueous solutions at high-temperature
and high vapor pressures [6], which allows for high purification and high crystallinity in the produc-
tion of single crystals. However, the synthesis reactions and its involved process steps have rarely
been well studied in in-situ TEM before. Therefore, achieving hydrothermal condition in LPTEM
study can lead to new findings in this field.
4. Catalyst Reaction - Catalysts are substances that facilitate chemical reactions by lowering the
required activation energy. By heating, the reaction rate can be increased following Eq.(2.2) and
the efficiency/selectivity of catalysts can be further improved. For example, in the dry forming of
methane to syngas, heating is used to improve the selectivity of the catalyst [7].
5. Thermal Expansion - the change in volume, area or length of an object when it undergoes a
temperature change without incurring phase transition. The increase in temperature leads to an
increase in the separation between atoms and molecules. This is an extremely prevalent topic in
construction design such as railway track, bridge building. However, it is also crucial to study for
in-situ TEM with heating because of thermal drift, which we will study in details in Ch.6.1.
This is by no mean an exhaustive list and there are plenty other interesting research fields that are
affected by heating. However, for this thesis, we will focus on heating in LPTEM especially and how it
impacts the imaging of the samples and what possibilities of researches that heating can bring.

2.2
Introduction to LPTEM and Heating in LPTEM
Liquid-phase Transmission Electron Microscopy (LPTEM) is a powerful platform for nanoscale imaging
and characterization of physical and chemical processes of materials in liquids [8]. By confining the liquid
sample between two ultrathin electron-transparent windows such as carbon or SiNx, and with different
cameras, sensors and detectors built into the TEM, one can perform in-situ TEM study to characterize
how various materials and solutions behave under different conditions, such as a change in temperature,
external electrical bias, flowing and mixing with other solutions, etc. The study of LPTEM allows us
to study these phenomena at an atomic resolution and provides us with insights into the fundamental
mechanism and principle behind the various transformations, processes and chemical reactions.
However, to perform LPTEM, a liquid cell (we shall also call it chip) is needed to hold the liquid sample
inside the TEM vacuum environment. There are several archetypes of liquid cells designs, summarized
by Utkur M. el at. [8] in Fig.(1), each with their own advantages and limitations.
Figure 1 ŌĆō Illustrations of the different archetypes of liquid cells for LPTEM. [8] (a) The first liquid cell made using

two ultrathin (10-20 nm) SiNx membrane windows that are separated by a spacer.
These liquid cells protect the
encapsulated thin solution layer from the vacuum of the TEM. A camera underneath captures the dynamics of the

BN and MoS2, can be used to make a simple static liquid cell). (e) Hybrid organic/inorganic liquid cell nanocapsule
with liposome bilayers coated with a thin layer of gold.
In this thesis, we will be using the microfabricated liquid cell, provided by InsightChips, which has the
advantages of having an ultrathin SiNx membrane allowing for high-contrast imaging and allowing for
fast and steady multiline-flowing with its 4 liquid inlets/outlets better than any existing solution the
market. Furthermore, heating in LPTEM is also an interesting research topic and it can be done in two
different ways - bulk heating or local heating, which are reviewed by Fabrizio G. el at. [9].
The core idea is that, bulk heating heats up the entire the holder tip (and the rod if the tip is not
thermally insulated from the tip perfectly) while local heating concentrates its heating onto the chips.
As we will discuss in Ch.6.1, local heating is preferred because of the significant thermal drift that the
bulk heating causes.
However, the difference in practical engineering aspect makes bulk heating much more feasible and
realistic in this project for several reasons. First, we should only characterize the heater once and the
same heating performance can be expected for repetitive usages in the future regardless of the mounted
chip. Second, unlike local heating, the bulk heating does not require dedicated electrodes for the chip,

Theory
As we have discussed in Ch.2.1, temperature is a measure of the average kinetic energy inside a system
and is vital to many physical transitions and chemical reactions.
When two bodies of different temperature are present whether with or without physical contact, there
will be a transfer of energy from the body of higher temperature to the one with lower temperature. The
energy being transferred here is known as heat.
There are three important mechanisms of heat transfer - heat conduction, heat convection and radi-
ation [10]. In the following, we will describe how each of this mechanism works and whether they should
be taken into consideration when designing the heating system.

3.1.2
Heat Convection
When an object is immersed or in contact with a bulk of fluid, and suppose that the object is hotter
than the fluid, the temperature difference between the object and the bulk fluid will cause heat to be
conducted from the object to the fluid initially, which heats up the fluid around the object and reduces
their density.
The drop in density causes the hot fluid to flow towards the cooler region, while the
cool fluid refill the space left behind. It is the movement of the bulk fluid that differentiates this heat
transport mechanism from the heat conduction discussed previously, and it is illustrated in Fig.(5).

4
Design and Assembly of the Heating System
To design the heating system, we need not only the physical components to put up a mechanically stable
system, but we also need to find the appropriate electronics components to perform the heating and
control the heating. In addition, programming is also needed to effectively communicate and control the
electronic components. A stable system cannot be achieved if any of this aspect is failed. Since electrical
components are physical in nature, let us discuss them first and see how they can be incorporated into
the design.
4.1
Electrical Setup of the Heating System
To heat up the tip, we need something that can measure the temperature, and something that can heat
up the tip, while being to electrically communicate with them so that we can fetch the data digitally. In
the following sections, we will discuss how we choose each electrical component.
4.1.1
How to measure temperature?
There are many ways to measure temperature, such as sensing the temperature physically with a ther-
mometer or other sensing elements, which requires physical contact between the sensing element and
the region of interest; or sensing the temperature optically by the infrared radiation as we discussed in
Ch.3.1.3. Here we list out several options that are commercially available
1. Thermocouple
To understand how a thermocouple functions, it is important to understand thermoelectric voltage and
Seebeck effect. Suppose that we have a single copper wire, as illustrated in Fig.(11) where one junction
(the end of the wire) is heated up to a temperature T2, called the hot junction, and the other junction
is at a lower temperature T1, call the cold junction.

temperature based on the thermoelectric voltage generated in one single metal wire unreliable as it is
very susceptible to electrical noises.

2. Thermistor and Resistance Temperature Detector (RTD)
Another type of popular temperature sensors are thermistors and RTDs, while the two differ in materials
where the thermistors are generally made of ceramic or polymer, and RTDs are made of pure metal.
Nonetheless, both of them exploit the property that their electrical resistance change linearly with
temperature in a given temperature range.
Therefore, to measure the temperature at a location of interest, one can mount them (RTDs with
size on mm scale are also commercially available) onto the location of interest and run a power source
through them with another resistor (to limit the current) and measure the change in current/voltage in
the circuit as the temperature changes.
Furthermore, they are also able to measure temperature to a higher accuracy compared to the ther-
mocouple. For example, PT-1000, a common RTD, has an accuracy of ┬▒0.4 ŌŚ”C at 50 ŌŚ”C for a temperature
range from -200 ŌŚ”C to 850 ŌŚ”C [13], which out performs all thermocouples at this sensing range.
However, there are two problems using a thermistor or RTD. First, they both require an external
and steady power supply and a multi-meter that can be used for real time data logging, both are rather
pricey and bulky in practice, making them difficult a difficult choice in our first prototype. Second,
resistor naturally generate Joule heat, meaning as we are using them to sense temperature, we are
also pumping extra heat into the system. This presents as an extra heat source in the heat equation
derived as Eq.(3.21) and (3.27). If this extra heat source is also temperature dependent, it will makes
differential equations much more complex and it is practically challenging to determine and verify the
power-temperature dependence.
Therefore, even though theoretically thermistors and RTD perform
better than thermocouple at the temperature range that we are interested in, these two issues make us
eventually giving up on using them in this thesis.

pyrometer and infrared imaging (used interchangeably with thermal imaging). However, to use them,
it is important that the emissivity ŽĄ of the surface of interest must be close to 1 and homogeneous,

For our heating system, we decided to use the K-type thermocouple as the temperature measurement
device, for it is small in physical dimension and has a fast response time with an acceptable accuracy
and temperature range, and we already have some available in the lab. Of course, T-type thermocouple
would provide more accurate reading but but K-type thermocouple shall suffice for now.
However, a huge issue with using the thermocouple is that it is nearly impossible to read out the
thermoelectric voltage accurately that is on the sub-mV scale. We tried to measure the thermoelectric
voltage using an oscilloscope via BNC cables, but the thermocouple itself and its wires cannot be perfectly
shielded and the thermoelectric voltage is just drown out in the 50 Hz noise. This situation forced us to
come up with a way to amplify the signal and operational amplifier (OpAmp) came into the picture.
We will not dive into the details of how an OpAmp is built, but just how it is used - an OpAmp
amplifies the input voltage Vin by some gain G such that the output voltage becomes Vout = VinG.
One can adjust the setup of the circuit and change the resistances of the resistors to manipulate G.
Nonetheless, with an OpAmp, we can amplify the weak thermoelectric voltage signal and read out how
it changes with temperature.
However, just as the voltage signal is amplified, so is the noise. The good thing is that the thermo-
electric voltage is a DC signal while the noise is AC in nature (they are from the 230 V power supply
surrounding us). Therefore, we can filter out the AC signal with an inductor (acts like a wire for DC
signal and an open circuit for AC signal). This sounds simple in theory but is actually difficult to accom-
plish in practice, due to the fact that we now must use electric wires that are of the same materials as
the ones of the thermocouple to establish electrical connection in the circuit. Otherwise, any other metal
wire we use for the circuit will introduce an extra thermoelectric contribution in the final measurement.
Not to mention another fundamental problem with accurately reading the thermoelectric voltage is
that we must perform cold junction compensation, meaning we must know the temperature at the cold
junction at all time whenever we wish to measure the temperature, this means we would have to use
another temperature sensing element.
Fortunately, there is a commercially available chip that solves all these problems - MAX31856PMB1
from Maxim Integrated. It is a chip that is able to amplify the thermometric voltage signal, filter the
AC noise, performs the cold junction compensation and converts the voltage signal into temperature
reading based on the built-in data base. We simply need to feed the thermoelectric voltage it into its
thermocouple plug, and establish communication using some microcontroller and a computer, then we
will be able to read out the temperature. This will be explained in more details later on.

How to heat up the tip?
The simplest and controllable way to heat up the holder tip is by Joule heating of an electrical resistor,
where the heating power P is expressed as
P = I2R = IV = V 2/R
(4.4)
where I, V and R are the electrical current, voltage and resistance of the resistor in use. The equation
itself has no restriction on how high or low any of these values can be, but in practice, the actual power
that we can apply is limited by two factors.
First, there is a maximum amount of current that can be drawn out from any power supply. For
example, an USB 2.0 type-A port (standard port on our laptop or desktop PC) or an USB type-B port
(standard port on microcontrollers) can supply up to 5 V voltage and 0.5 A current, meaning that the
maximum power that can be drawn out from the port is 2.5 W. Moreover, to draw such power, the load
must be matched, meaning the resistance of the resistor used for heating must be 5 V/0.5 A = 10 Ōä”. If
any other resistor is used, the power that can be drawn out will be lower.
Second, resistor has a power rating value that indicates what is the maximum allowed power that
can be applied onto the resistor. This value can usually be looked up in their techinical data sheet. For
example, the resistor we choose to use as the heater for our heating system is PCAN1206E75R0BST3
from Vishay, which is a 75 Ōä”┬▒0.1% thin-film resistor with a power rating of 2 W [14]. However, if we were

4.3.1
Communication between Electrical Components and Computer
Communicating with electrical components is a tricky business. There are countless available options of
microcontrollers on the market and not all of them can be programmed directly using the same program-
ming language. This makes the communication between multiple microcontrollers rather challenging.
For example, LabJack U3-HV was originally intended to be operated by the LabView software, which
is a graphical programming language, while Arduino UNO in contrast is programmed by Arduino, a
variation of C++. This means, it is impossible to control both microcontrollers using either LabView or
Arduino alone.
However, we managed to find an alternative that can control both microcontrollers - Python. The
company behind LabJack and LabView, National Instrument, has released some basic Python packages
for their microntrollers, including the u3.py package for LabJack U3-HV, allowing Python to open up
a serial communication with LabJack via a USB wire. Furthermore, this allows us to write the DAC
voltage directly via the Python script or terminal.
On the other hand, controlling Arduino via Python is more convoluted. First, an Arduino script
should be written and uploaded onto Arduino UNO board, which uses the <AdafruitMAX31856.h>
package and sets up the SPI protocol between the Arduino UNO and the MAX31856PMB1 chip. Then
we must fetch and decode the feedback message from MAX31856 chip which contains the time and
temperature data, which we shall elaborate more in the next section.

4.3.5
How to execute multiple loops concurrently?
As mentioned in Ch.4.3.4, both GUI and the temperature sampling/heating control are a loop on their
own. At an introductory programming level, one would know that running a for/while loop will occupy
the terminal/console of your computer, meaning you cannot execute any other commands nor other loop
until the current loop has finished running. This means that, if we were to execute the GUI loop, then
the control loop could not be initiated until the GUI loop has finished. However, without the control
loop feeding the data, nothing would be plotted out in the GUI loop. To tackle this hurdle, a Python
package called threading is used. that solves exactly this problem.
Threading, borrowed from the concept in sewing where several threads are twisted together to make
a stronger thread, is a programming concept where multiple programs can be executed concurrently
(albeit pseudo in Python) as threads. For our program, threading allows us to run multiple loops at the
same time without the terminal being occupied.
To achieve this, we assign the control loop and the GUI loop each to a thread, which both can be
initialized simultaneously at the start of the execution of the main script. However, this is only the ideal
case. The first problem that we encountered is that, given the difference contents contained within each
loop, the time taken to finish one iteration in each loop is also different.
Hence, if though we set a time interval of 0.5 s for each loop, the actual time interval including
executing each iteration is different. For example, as we shall see in Ch.4.4.2, the iteration in the control

loop always takes longer than the one in the GUI loop, which often leads GUI fetching empty data from
the temperature sampling program, and crashes the GUI in the process.
This is in fact a common problem (called ŌĆØracingŌĆØ between threads) encountered when one uses multi-
threading and one way to handle the problem is by using a Python package called queue.py. Instead of
storing the data as numpy arrays, we can store them as queue arrays, where each time the GUI demands
new data to be plotted, it will wait in ŌĆØqueueŌĆØ until a pair of time and temperature have been provided,
which effectively solves the asynchronization between the two loops.
4.4
Practical Challenges of the Heating System
4.4.1
Vacuum Problem
During the assembly of the holder, it is very important to avoid any virtual leak possible, with three
things that we have encountered and learnt:
1. Void/Cavity - void nor cavity can be allowed to be present in the electrical wires or the compo-
nents of the TEM holder that goes into the TEM chamber. Any void/cavity can store air and will
take hours for the TEM chamber to pump down to a safe vacuum level. Therefore, it is always
important to use the vacuum-compatible wires, epoxy and metal pieces (we ran into a problem
where the brass X-ray blocking piece is porous and leaky) and out gas the TEM holder for at least
12 hours after the assembly.
2. O-rings - the O-rings that are above the inlets/outlets and on the rod must not be damaged nor
dirty, otherwise the TEM chamber would not be able to be pumped down.
3. Gaps - especially the gap on the X-ray blocking piece in the backside casing where the wires and
liquid channels come out. If the gap is not sealed properly, air will leak through the gap and go
through the hollow rod and enter the vacuum chamber, making a poor vacuum.

6.3
Precipitation of Calcium Carbonate
The experiment is prepared by the following:
1. Sample preparation: To prepare a saturated solution of CaC03, CaC03 powder was added to
25 ┬ĄL of milliQ water until a white precipitation of CaC03 was visible at the bottom of the vial and
could not be dissolved further. In order to prevent the microchannels from being blocked during
the filling of the nanochannel chip, the saturated CaC03 solution was passed through a syringe filter
with a pore size of 0.25 ┬Ąm. Following filtration, a clear saturated CaC03 solution was obtained
and stored in 2.5 ┬ĄL microtubes.
2. Nanochannel chip preparation: In this experiment, a nanochannel chip with deep nanochannels
was used in order to increase the liquid volume within the field of view (FOV). The nanochannels
had a height of approximately 450 ┬▒ 20 nm, while the microchannel depth was 3 ┬Ąm. Each SiNx
membrane had a thickness of 39 nm, resulting in a total thickness of 78 nm. Prior to liquid filling,
the SiNx membranes on all four inlet/outlets were punctured and subsequently verified using an
optical microscope.
3. TEM sample preparation: A droplet of 0.5 ┬ĄL of the saturated solution was carefully deposited
onto the two specified inlets, as shown in Figure 1. The capillary force then facilitated the filling
of the liquid into both the micro and nanochannels, displacing any air present in the channels.
Once it was confirmed by OM that the channels were adequately filled with the liquid, the chip
was assembled into the nanochannel heating holder. Following the assembly, the holder underwent
testing in a pumping cube to ensure a successful seal, preventing any vacuum-related issues in a
TEM.
4. TEM imaging: The Tecnai T20 G2 microscope from ThermoFisher Scientific and the XF416
camera from Tietz Video and Image Processing Systems GmbH (TVIPS) were utilized for imaging
the reaction.
The primary imaging technique employed was bright field TEM to observe the
precipitation of CaCO3 at elevated temperatures. To mitigate potential beam effects, adjustments
were made to the C2 aperture and spot size to decrease the electron flux. Throughout the entire
chemistry experiment, low electron flux was implemented during imaging. The electron flux value
was measured at 0.02 e/╦ÜA
2s.
First, we study the beam effect at room temperature, which is shown in Fig.(34), where we can see
that small amount of precipitations can be observed after being exposed by a beam dose of dose of
2.8 e/╦ÜA
2s for 130 s, indicating that the beam could induce precipitation at high electron dose. Therefore,
a lower magnification and a spread beam is used throughout the rest of the experiment to avoid the
beam-induced precipitation.
Next, we heat up the nanochannel to 100 ŌŚ”C, and precipitation of CaCO3 can be observed in Fig.(35)
indicating that heating does reduce the solubility of CaCO3 in water.
Lastly, we also measure the thermal drift at 100 ŌŚ”C, which seems negligible as can be seen from
Fig.(36).
