Abstract
Unmanned aerial vehicle (UAV) geomagnetic surveying oﬀers a great alter-
native to the existing survey methods that are maimed by their logistical
complexity, time consumption and expensive nature, while still oﬀering high
quality results. With low noise levels, viability in complex terrain and pre-
cise positioning it fulﬁlls the most critical speciﬁcations for a good magnetic
survey. Here proof of concept that UAV geomagnetic surveying is a viable
option for heavy mineral exploration is provided, by conducting a magnetic
survey on the beach of Høfde Q in Denmark, where research on depositional
activity in the area suggests that placer deposits of heavy minerals exists. It
was found, through comprehensive data processing and qualitative investi-
gations, that heavy minerals was indeed present on the beach and that there
was a good correlation between the areas with magnetic sand, and the areas
with substantial magnetic ﬁeld anomalies measured by a UAV drone.

1
Introduction
As the demand for minerals increases globally[1] it is clear that new methods
are needed to accelerate the process of prospecting of minerals. A solution
to this may be a newly developed type of drone, which seemingly oﬀers
spatial ﬂexibility, reduced time expenditure, cost eﬃciency and high quality
magnetic surveying compared to traditional heliborne methods.
Areas in Denmark with sand enriched in heavy minerals may contain an eco-
nomically viable source of valuable elements such as titanium. These heavy
minerals could thus be of interest to mining companies looking to supply the
demand for heavy minerals. To ﬁnd placer deposits with magnetic survey-
ing often requires a deep understanding of the magnetic ﬁeld components
that cloud anomalies originating from heavy minerals, knowledge on where
relevant depositional activity could have taken place, and a comprehensive
toolkit of data processing techniques, to perform meaningful interpretations
of observed data. The most important tools and knowledge needed for geo-
physical detection of placer deposits is thus provided in this project.
It is especially the minerals ilmenite and rutile which is of interest in this
project, as they contain titanium, which can be separated from the mineral by
high-temperature vacuum distillation[2] to make lightweight alloys, that can
then be used to make anything from rocketships to prostheses[3]. In addition
ilmenite and rutile are typically found together with other proﬁtable heavy
minerals like magnetite, zircon or almandine. Magnetite is the main ore in
of iron prospecting and used to make steel[4], whilst almandine and zircon
can be sold as jewelry.[5]
It is the goal of this thesis to use state of the art drone-borne geomagnetic
methods, and research on depositional environments in order to locate ar-
eas with high concentrations of heavy minerals.
Furthermore, to analyse
drone-observed geomagnetic data and compare it to samples of sand that are
collected in the survey area. The sand samples are qualitatively analysed
using a stereolope microscope to check for a connection between the areas
where heavy minerals was found and the areas, which recorded an anomalous
magnetic ﬁeld on a magnetometer, in order to provide proof of concept for
drone-based heavy mineral exploration.

2
Theory
2.1
Earth’s magnetic ﬁeld
The magnetic ﬁeld of earth is the sum of several magnetic components orig-
inating from within and outside the Earth. Namely the internal ﬁeld, the
external ﬁeld and the relatively static ﬁelds originating from subsurface mag-
netizations. The ﬁelds derived from subsurface magnetization are particu-
larly of interest in determining the composition and structural properties
beneath the surface, and thus needs to be separated from the internal and
external ﬁeld of the Earth to perform a meaningful interpretation of the sub-
surface. This calls for a mathematical model like the IGRF[6] or CHAOS-7[7]
which makes use of an extensive set of magnetic data collected by the Swarm,
Oersted and CHAMP satellites to estimate the internal magnetic ﬁeld at any
area on Earth, so that it can be separated from the ﬁelds derived from sub-
surface magnetizations. At the surface of Earth the magnetic ﬁeld from the
internal and external ﬁeld has a strength of about 50.000 nT depending on
where on Earth you measure it[8], whilst the magnetic ﬁeld strength from
subsurface magnetic material, measured by a drone, is expected to be in
order of a few nano tesla.
2.1.1
Internal magnetic ﬁeld of Earth
The Internal ﬁeld encompasses the magnetic ﬁeld contributions from the
Earth’s core and lithosphere.
While the magnetic ﬁeld originating from
Earth’s core is generated by electromagnetic currents in the outer core of
the Earth, the ﬁeld originating from the lithosphere is generated by inter-
actions between external ﬁelds and the core.[9] Both contributions to the
internal ﬁeld can be empirically evaluated by the CHAOS-7 model at any lo-
cation on Earth, in order to ultimately isolate the subsurface magnetization
contrasts that are interesting in geoexploration studies.
The CHAOS-7 model works under the assumption that all measurements
are conducted in a region where there is no electric current, so that Amperes
equation ∇B = µ0Jinduced[10] relating the vector magnetic ﬁeld B to the
induced magnetization (Jinduced) and magnetic permeability (µ0), reduces to
∇B = 0 so that the internal magnetic ﬁeld Bint may be described by a scalar
potential as: Bint = −∇Vint. The CHAOS model exploits this and ﬁnds the
vector magnetic ﬁeld by evaluating the scalar potential of the internal ﬁeld as

2.1.2
External magnetic ﬁeld of Earth
The dynamic External ﬁeld generated by the sun introduces spatial and
temporal diurnal variations in the magnetic ﬁeld measured by a magnetome-
ter. To correct for these variations and remove most of the external ﬁeld
contributions from survey data, a Base station can be used as an alternative
to modelling. Rather than using complicated models and satellite data, a
base station can accurately monitor the magnetic ﬁeld at a static reference
position close to an area, which is being surveyed. The ﬁeld monitored by the
base station can then easily be subtracted from the surveyed data in order
to correct for most diurnal variations.

2.2.3
Magnetic anomalies from magnetic materials
Like Earth, all magnetic materials are dipoles i.e. they have two poles a
negative(-) and a positive(+). Moreover, the magnetic materials that ex-
hibit high magnetic susceptibility will, as mentioned, in the presence of the
Earth’s geomagnetic ﬁeld become magnetized and polarized. Buried magne-
tized material will thus exhibit positive and negative ﬁelds associated with
the material, as an anomaly on a magnetometer when observing the magnetic
ﬁeld[9]. Figure 2. illustrates how such an anomaly from highly susceptible
material combines the the ﬁelds of both of its poles (although dominated by
the pole closest to the observer) to alter the magnetic ﬁeld recorded by an

2.3.1
Ilmenite
Ilmenite (FeTiO3) is the primary ore for titanium prospecting and is a weakly
magnetic mineral by itself. It is however, typically intergrown with mag-
netite, which is strongly magnetic, and the magnetic anomaly from mag-
netites magnetic ﬁeld observed by a surveyor can therefore be used as an in-
dicator for possible ilmenite deposits. It is formed from the cooling of magma,
and will typically be concentrated as black sand in placer deposits.[19]

2.3.2
Magnetite
Magnetite (Fe3O4) is one of the main ores for iron prospecting and is natu-
rally strongly magnetic as shown in Table 2. It is typically found in beach
sand due to erosion of igneous and sedimentary rocks, which have been car-
ried to the sea by rivers and then carried back onto a beach by wave action.
2.3.3
Almandine
Almandine (Fe2+3Al2Si3O12) is silicate mineral and a sub-type of garnet
gemstone, that is used abundantly in jewelry. It is generally found all over the
world and its chemical composition and atomic strength gives it a hardness of
7.5 on mohs scale as shown in Table 3, making it the hardest of the Garnets.
Its high magnetic susceptibility (see Figure 4.), sometimes allows for the
detection of almandine using magnetic surveying.
2.3.4
Rutile
Rutile (TiO2) is also an important ore of titanium prospecting and is formed
under high pressure and temperature in igneous and metamorphic rocks. Its
high index of refraction (See Table 4.) also makes it very useful in optical
apperatures. Rutile is diamagnetic, which means that it is repulsive but also
that the magnitude of its ﬁeld is negligible in comparison to the ﬁelds derived
from other rock magnetizations, as it will typically have a susceptibility in
the order of 10−5 SIu as shown in Figure 4.
2.3.5
Zircon
Zircon (ZrSiO4) is a silicate mineral, and is often a co-product of ilmenite
and rutile mining operations for titanium. It can be mildly radioactive when
containting tracesof Uranium or Thorium.
2.3.6
Chemical stability
Unlike other minerals in igneous rocks, Ilmenite, Magnetite, Garnet, Rutile
and Zircon are very durable. This means that their concentrations can be
expected to increase with the downstream distance that weathered igneous
rock debris is carried by a river, as less durable minerals of the igneous rocks
steadily breaks down.
The two processes that are mainly responsible for the chemical weathering
and break-down of the less durable igneous rocks are hydrolysis (produces
clay), and oxidation (produces iron oxides e.g. hermatite and geothite). [20]

3
Method
3.1
Overview
Geomagnetic surverying using UAV oﬀers the possibility of thoroughly char-
acterizing the subsurface over large areas of land in a short span of time
compared to traditional methods. A good survey desires a relatively ﬂat and
accessible surface, good weather conditions to avoid short-term variations
due to variable-velocity winds and minimal vehicular traﬃc.
The reality
however is often far from a utopia with no disturbances, and variations from
both wind, man-made objects, traﬃc and noise are crucial to correct for, in
order to perform a meaningful interpretation of a survey.
The purpose of this section is to provide meaningful techniques for processing
magnetic ﬁeld data using MATLAB®. This includes trimming & despiking,
correcting for wind, correcting for the internal ﬁeld from the crust and core of
the Earth, correcting for the external ﬁeld of the Earth using a base station,
correcting for ringing eﬀects i.e. the Gibbs errors close the edge of the grid,
levelling, micro-levelling, and upward-continuation of data.
3.2
Processing
3.2.1
Trimming & Despiking
When the data observed by a magnetometer bird has been loaded into matlab
using the script LoadData.m, data showing non-lock on the magnetometer
is simply removed using CleavingData.m.
The data is then manually
trimmed using TransitLineRemoval.m which removes any transit lines
leading towards and away from the survey area, so that the data set only
contains observations within the desired survey area.
Despiking is done by inspecting each survey line for spikes and then manually
removing them. The primitive nature of this method is essential because no
ﬁlter will be capable of completely removing spikes passing through it, as the
spikes are impulses that contains all frequencies, and so some component of
a spike would always be present after ﬁltering.
With despiking there is always the risk of removing data. Some general rules
to follow in order to avoid this are:
1. If it looks like a dipole, then it’s probably data
2. Is it of signiﬁcant amplitude and only visible on one ﬂight line and not
the neighbouring ﬂight lines, then it’s probably noise
13
==PAGE:20==
3. Look at all the lines as one body to get a feel of the general noise level,
before despiking.
The despiking is handled by the MATLAB scripts LineAnalysis.m, which
extracts and saves each individual survey line from the survey dataset, and
LineAnalysisSpikeRemoval.m which removes any spikes from the lines,
and saves them as a new line. Likewise the survey lines, the ﬁeld observed
by the basestations is inspected and despiked using BaseStationCorrec-
tion.m.
3.2.2
Correcting for wind
The new and unique system used in this survey[22] consists of a UAV drone

The CHAOS-7 model CHAOS.m is then used to correct the base station
and survey data for the internal ﬁeld coming from the core and crust of the
Earth. The script takes GPS coordinates and the time span of the survey
as a Modiﬁed Julian Day (MJD2000) as input and returns the three vector
components of the internal ﬁeld. Br,Bθ,Bφ It then calculates magnitude of
the total internal ﬁeld FInt from them as:

A base station i.e. a system that monitors the magnetic ﬁeld FBase at a static
reference location close to the survey area, is then used to correct the survey
data for the external ﬁeld of Earth and diurnal variations. The correction
is accomplished by synchronizing the base stations measurements with the
UAVs in time, and then extracting the accurately measured external ﬁeld
FExternal from the base station using the CHAOS.m script to model the
internal ﬁeld of the base station FInt.Base and subtract it from the the total
base station ﬁeld FBase as:
FExternal = FBase −FInt.Base

as:
FAnomaly = FSurvey −FExternal −FInt.Survey
(10)
3.2.4
Levelling
Traditionally the next step in processing aeromagnetic data is levelling. In
this step ﬂight-lines are levelled to orthogonal ﬂight lines called tie-lines using
their intersection points to correct for errors[24]. The technique relies on the
fact that the ﬁeld aeromagnetic data is directionally invariant i.e. the ﬂight
and tie-lines should register equal values in their points of intersection, so that
any discrepancy at a crossover point will represent an error. The traditional
method, which involves ﬂying along and across the survey area is visualized
in Figure 7.
15
==PAGE:22==
Figure 7: Scenario when ﬂying both ﬂight-lines and tie-lines
In this thesis, however, a new methodology to level aeromagnetic data with-
out the need tie-lines is applied to the survey data using the script Lev-
elling.m. The new approach used relies on generating synthetic ’virtual’
tie-lines, from the ﬂight-line data to derive a smooth regional magnetic ﬁeld
grid.[24] Initially a one-dimensional piecewise cubic spline interpolation is
used to interpolate the ﬂight-line data values to the desired grid spacing of
1/5th of the survey-line spacing of 2m as shown in Figure 8. These data
value points are then interpolated in the perpendicular tie-line direction to
generate values for the grid.
16
==PAGE:23==
Figure 8: Scenario when ﬂying ﬂight-lines and generating ’virtual’ tie-lines
A low-pass ﬁlter is then applied in each direction of the grid with a cut-oﬀfre-
quency of 10 times the line spacing together with a nine-point Hanning ﬁlter
to remove any high-frequency noise from the derived regional magnetic ﬁeld.
The error ∆d between the unlevelled magnetic ﬁeld md and the regional
ﬁeld mr at each point is then found as:
∆d = md −mr
(11)
Finally values that fall between the 20th and 80th percentile of the normally
distributed values of ∆d is computed for each line and used to level their
location equivalent unlevelled magnetic ﬁeld line.
3.2.5
Natural neighbour interpolation
A natural neighbour interpolation that ﬁts a smooth surface to the scattered
survey data is then applied using the script NaturalNeighbourInterpola-
tion.m. This method of spatial interpolation provides a smoother approx-
imation than many other interpolation methods by conducting a weighted
average of points neighbouring a sample to determine the samples value.

A microlevelling ﬁlter to smooth directional noise is then applied using the

components which may distort anomalies of interest by accurately projecting
any data to a higher altitude.

4
Incentive to explore the possibility of vi-
able placer deposits in Denmark
The intial suspicion of feasible heavy sand deposits Denmark, speciﬁcally
in western Jutland, was spawned by events that took place in the Miocene
epoche (see appendix for geological time table). Evidence presented by se-
nior researcher Erik Skovbjerg Rasmussen suggests the occurrence of heavy
tectonic activity during this early Miocene period, which could have elevated
Denmark and Norway[25]. Related to the tectonic movement that caused this
elevation of Denmark and Norway is the erosion of the Norwegian mountains
that followed. The Norwegian mountains allegedly eroded, and as Norway
was now tipping, any delta complexes progradating south could start de-
positing material upon what eventually became Denmark.[26] The deposi-
tion during this early Miocene epoch is characterized by high sorting and
high concentration of heavy minerals.
4.1
Exposure of the Miocene layer
Supposedly this tectonic movement in the early miocene epoche resulted
in Denmark tilting towards SSW, and because of this, the relatively young
Miocene layer is not expected to be observed as as a ﬂat plane on the sur-
face all over Denmark, but rather a tilted plane[27]. The eﬀect of this is
illustrated in Figure 9. and Figure 10. Where it should be noticed that the
Miocene layers is particularly exposed in the western part of Denmark, which
makes an excavation for heavy minerals deposited during the Miocene epoch
signiﬁcantly easier in this area.

4.2
Palaeogeography, depositions and statrigraphic units
A palaeogeographic reconstruction of the Late Oligocene-Miocene epoch can
further facilitate the eﬀort to understand how and where depositional envi-
ronments in Jutland came to be. The palaeogeographic maps of Jutland on

maps depicts several reconstructions of Jutlands shoreline throughout the

in heavy minerals[29], has occured.
The initial inﬂux of heavy minerals to Denmark in the early Miocene[28] is
marked by a change of deposition from clayey and silty sediments, as those
seen in the Vejle Fjord Formation, to sand-rich deposits[29].This change is
ﬁrst seen in the Billund formation during the Aquitanian (Early Miocene)
age, which corresponds to the time of the palaegeographic reconstruction on

of shifting global climate. During this tug of war between the land and ocean,

7
Discussion & Future work
The results indicate that sand enriched in heavy minerals is indeed present on
the beach of Høfde Q, and that there is a correlation between areas with high
heavy mineral content and the areas with a signiﬁcant anomalous magnetic
ﬁeld, measured by the magnetometer bird, thus providing proof of concept
for drone-based heavy mineral exploration.
In line with the hypothesis on deposition of high sorting and concentration,
several layers of heavy minerals was found in the scale of 1 to 10 centimeters.
It is however hard to say if magnetic anomalies measured by the magnetome-
ter bird stem from these layers, or layers residing deeper than the 1 meter
prospected. A further analysis is therefore needed to bind the correlation be-
tween the samples collected in the surface and the anomalous ﬁeld detected,
with cause, as the true nature of the source anomaly is indeterminable with-
out more information about the geometry, source depth and contrast with
the neighbouring minerals.
This thesis furthermore provides a new insight into the viability of using
drone-borne surveying in complex terrains with interference from man-made
objects and strong winds, rather than the traditional use of helicopters or
vehicles, which is logistically challenging and expensive. The drone-borne
method of surveying proved to be both easy, cheaper than a helicopter and
a very precise method for aeromagnetic surveying, making it an extremely
viable method.
The areas with high probability of containing the most heavy minerals are
those enclosed by yellow to orange zones on Figure 38. as they demonstrate
an anomalous increase of a few nano tesla relative to their surroundings,
which seems a reasonable footprint for a shallow layer of heavy minerals in
the surface or a signiﬁcant layer residing deep underground. The two zone
with immense magnetic anomalies i.e. the 140 nT+ zones are almost without
doubt a man-made structure, and probably two WWII bunkers, that have
been buried by sand.
The reliability of the data around the two presumed bunkers is expected to
be greatly impacted by the ﬁlter applied, as the bunkers seem to exhibit both
high and low frequent signals naturally, and so the ﬁlters applied might have
ended up removing some of signal from the bunkers in an attempt to remove
noise.
46
==PAGE:53==
The new methodology of levelling data without the need for tie-lines proved
to be an extremely successful levelling method, as it reduced the time con-
sumed surveying, and could be applied on an area where the terrain/available
space didn’t allow for tie-lines to be ﬂown. The levelling however had its lim-
itations, and the survey could not be completely smoothened around some
of the lines (i.e. the lines that stand out as small hills running through the
survey on Figure 38.) which might be because the ﬂight lines were too close
to each other in relation to the precision of the drones position.
As the survey could not be completely smoothened by the levelling and micro-
levelling techniques, the upward continuation method was developed as a
tool to remove any remaining high frequent noise, by projecting the survey
1 meter upwards. The method proved to be a very useful tool in enhancing
low frequent signals in general i.e. those from deep or large structures; but
didn’t prove suﬃcient to completely smooth the survey.
Whether or not the heavy sand contains an econoimically viable source of
valuable elements such as titanium remains inconclusive. Whilst some of
the black minerals with non to weak-magnetic signatures could be ilmenite,
which is the primary source mineral of titanium, it is hard to tell without
conducting a statistical analysis of the contents. This could be done in future
studies by X-ray diﬀraction analysis, in which the chemical composition of a
mineral can be determined from its diﬀraction pattern.

8
Conclusion
This research aimed to locate areas with high concentrations of heavy min-
erals using state of the art drone-borne geomagnetic methods.
Based on
comprehensive data processing of aeromagnetic data from Høfde Q and a
qualitative analysis of heavy minerals found there, it can be concluded that
the area does indeed contain high concentrations of heavy minerals. The
results indicate the presence of several thin layers of heavy minerals in the
surface of Høfde Q, and a good connection between the locations at which
the sand with heavy minerals was found, and the locations with signiﬁcant
magnetic ﬁeld anomalies, measured by the magnetometer bird, thus provid-
ing proof of concept for drone-based heavy mineral exploration. While this
research clearly illustrates that heavy minerals of substantial concentration
was deposited on Høfde Q, it raises the question of how much of it is an
economically viable source of valuable elements such as titanium. Further
research is thus needed determine if the heavy mineral layers are of an eco-
nomically viable size, and whether they contain signiﬁcant concentrations of
titanium.
