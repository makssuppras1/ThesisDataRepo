Summary 
 
Filamentous fungi and other microorganisms are a rich source of potential bioactive secondary metabolites. Many 
of these compounds stay understudied, while others, such as mycotoxins, antifungal, and anticancer agents, have 
been reported in the literature to have biological activity. New compounds have been discovered, and many of 
them remain understudied. The Aspergillus genus consists of over 300 species, including many that are harmful 
to humans, plants, and animals. Many of them produce mycotoxins, which are poisonous, such as ochratoxins 
and aflatoxins. However, many of the Aspergilli produce diverse types of compounds with industrial applications, 
including food production and various enzymes.  
The main goal of this project was the discovery of novel fungal NPs produced by Aspergilli campestris. More 
specifically, we focused on the discovery of picrasidine L. (an alkaloid compound produced by Picrasma plants) 
and we aimed to design a possible biosynthetic pathway. We based our screening on using a high-throughput 
analytical method called LC-DAD-HRMS-based dereplication for the exploration of the metabolic profile and 
the comparison of the growth of two A. campestris strains (28561 and 17867) on different media. 
First, the optimal conditions for fungal metabolite (SMs) production were identified by cultivating them on 
various solid-agar media, including PDA (Potato Dextrose Agar), OAT (Oatmeal Agar), YES (Yeast Extract 
Sucrose Agar), CYA (Czapek Yeast Autolysate Agar), MEA, and MEA-ox. Additionally, small-scale 
fermentation experiments were conducted using four diverse types of rice—basmati, grod, whole grain, and 
jasmine—for 14 days under static, dark conditions at 25 °C. The fungal extracts were then analysed using an 
untargeted high-resolution method (HPLC-MS/MS), supported by data from the GNPS molecular network. After 
this process, we found that Picrasidine L. was produced in higher concentrations in the IBT28561 strain in media 
PDA, YES, and CYA, with a retention time of 3.3 minutes. Comparing the metabolic profiles from the rice 
experiments and other media, it was seen that more SMs are produced in YES rather than in Jasmine rice in the 
IBT28561 strain. However, Jasmine rice proved to be the most effective for Picrasidine L. production in the 
IBT28561 strain compared to the other strains. 
The results from this work show that A. campestris produces many SMs that were also found in other Aspergilli 
morphologically close to A. campestris (in the Candidi section). I was able to find SMs that are being produced 
by A. campestris: 3,3''-Dihydroxyterphenyllin, 3-hydroxyterpenyllin, chlorflavonin, Terphenyllin, Candidusin 
(A, B, C), 6'-Desmethylcandidusin B, Terprenin, and Aspergillazine A and picrasidine L. In addition, these 
compounds were also found through manual dereplication on rice media with the basmati rice in IBT17867 strain 
to show that it is producing more picrasidine L. and other secondary metabolites. 
At the same time, four derivatives of picrasidine L. were annotated in the chromatogram, including methylated, 
de-methylated and ethyl- one, while using the GNPS molecular network, picrasidine L and its adduct were 
annotated. In contrast, L-tryptophan was identified as a separate GNPS subnetwork, suggesting its role as a 
biosynthetic precursor. By comparing the secondary metabolites produced when feeding with rice with the solid 
agar, we have seen that jasmine rice produces more picrasidine L.

The biosynthetic pathway of picrasidine L. is still undiscovered, and the project aimed to unravel and shed light 
on these unclear steps of the pathway, and to confirm any relation of previously discovered compounds with the 
pathway. For achieving the goal of this project, I have hypothesized that this indole-like molecule originates from 
the amino acid L-tryptophan. L-tryptophan could be the source of the C14 core scaffold involved in the pathway 
of picrasidine L and its analogues, which undergo decarboxylation to tryptamine. This is followed by imine 
formation with an aldehyde and a Pictet–Spengler-type condensation, producing a tetrahydro-β-carboline 
intermediate. Later oxidation, cyclization, and SAM-dependent methylation steps complete the biosynthesis. 
Supporting this, L-tryptophan feeding experiments were performed in YES media of the IBT17867 strain to 
confirm through chemical analysis how it affects picrasidine L. production and other metabolites.  
In conclusion, this Master's project provides insight into the chemistry of A. campestris in different media and 
the best conditions for finding the highest number of indole-like molecules. The results provided here have been 
obtained by using the soft ionization method coupled with a DAD detector. In contrast, the use of GNPS as a 
bioinformatic tool has provided us with information about the compounds that are structurally related to 
picrasidine L. However, a considerable number of bioactive NPs remain undiscovered and are associated with 
A. campestris. Future studies involve large-scale extraction using size exclusion and flash column 
chromatography (changing from normal phase to reverse phase) to separate these compounds. Through NMR 
characterization, we can find the structure.

Chapter I: Introduction 
Thesis Outline 
In this Master's project entitled ‘Characterization of fungal biosynthesis in Aspergillus campestris’, the main aim 
was to investigate which secondary metabolites   (SMs) are being produced by the two understudied strains of A. 
campestris (IBT28561 and IBT17867). We have mainly focused on identifying picrasidine L. and designing a 
possible biosynthetic pathway for its production.  
The overall objectives from the first part of the project were: (1) to examine the occurrence and levels of 
picrasidine L. in different media, such as four different types of rice (basmati, grod, jasmine, whole grain) and 
six agar media (YES, CYA, MEA, MEAox, V8, OAT).  Additionally, the second part of the project (2) focuses 
on the identification of other SMs which might be derivatives of picrasidine L. or are being produced from other 
Aspergilli species (within Candidi section) morphologically close to A. campestris. Other objectives were to (3) 
hypothesize possible biosynthetic pathways for picrasidine L. production and (4) to test with a feeding 
experiment whether the amino acid tryptophan-L is related to the biosynthetic pathway of picrasidine L. As a last 
objective, (5) to use the GNPS molecular network for compound annotation. 
In summary, this study contributes to the first characterization of A. campestris secondary metabolism, providing 
insights into its biosynthetic pathway, and sets the foundation for the identification of novel compounds, such as 
complex alkaloid-like metabolites and their biosynthesis. 
Overall Workflow of the Project 
Before starting work on this project, the following workflow has been outlined with all the necessary steps to 
understand how to conduct the experimental process and to achieve the goals. As it is illustrated also in Figure 
1, there are five major steps:  
1. Cultivation of the two isolated A. campestris strains (IBT28561 and IBT17486) on different media and 
conditions.  
2. Sample preparation for chemical analysis. 
3. UHPLC-DAD-QTOF-MS/MS data: the aim is to perform comparative MS-based analysis to determine 
the optimal growth conditions.  
4. Untargeted manual dereplication for the identification of secondary metabolites.  
5. Data analysis using the GNPS molecular network for annotation of compounds.

Chapter 2: Background 
 
2.1 Filamentous fungi 
Fungi, unlike plants or animals, are classified under the Kingdom of Fungi as one diverse group of unicellular, 
simple eukaryotic microorganisms, which survive in diverse habitats (Figure 2). As microorganisms, they can 
have both their benefits and adverse effects. For instance, they might cause harm in human life, being poisonous 
or causing food spoilage due to the production of mycotoxins such as ochratoxins and aflatoxins. (Chambergo & 
Valencia, 2016; SOUZA, Luana Virgínia da SILVA, 2025). 
Fungi are considered heterotrophic organisms and are considered nature’s primary decomposers of organic 
matter. They can absorb nutrients from the earth, such as carbon or nitrogen, and decompose them by secreting 
enzymes, transforming them into energy sources. In addition, fungi produce a wide range of compounds, which 
protect them from UV radiation, help in communication, and defend them against toxic compounds. Some 
filamentous fungi are also responsible for nutrient absorption in plants as they cause plant material degradation 
due to the lack of chlorophyll; they are also called ‘chlorophyll-lacking eukaryotes' (Margulis & Chapman, 2009).  
Fungal taxonomy has undergone many changes in its classification over time. Based on their differences in 
morphology and reproductive traits, four major phyla have been identified: Ascomycota, Basidiomycota, 
Zygomycota, and Chytridiomycota. Among them, Ascomycota and Basidiomycota fall under the sub-kingdom 
of Dikarya, which are characterized by the formation of complex structures. Ascomycota is the most expansive 
phylum of the fungal kingdom, and it includes the Aspergilli and Penicillium. (Bindschedler et al., 2016). In this 
study, the focus is on the candidi section and specifically on Aspergillus campestris.  
 
 
Figure 2: Simplified version of phylogenetic tree of the fungal kingdom showing eukarya, bacteria, and archaea. Fungi 
belong to eukaryotes. (Bindschedler et al., 2016). 
2.2 Aspergillus species 
The Aspergillus genus was first described by Pier Antonio Micheli in 1729, who named it based on the 
morphology and conidial color of the spore-producing structures (conidiophores) with spherical shape. It is a 
large genus comprising more than 340 species, and these species can produce many different secondary 
metabolites (especially species like Flavi, Niger, and Nidulantes) (Varga et al., 2007). These species can have 
both beneficial and toxic effects for humans, plants, and animals. Aspergilli species thrive in diverse ecosystems 
such as tropical and subtropical regions, soil, and indoor environments, while they are also encountered in desert, 
marine habitats, and other environments. Members of Aspergilli are also considered among the most significant 
genera with many applications in the industrial world through their production of many bioactive metabolites 
such as antibiotics, antifungal, and lipid compounds. Since several genome sequences have been published, it is

easy to study the biosynthetic pathway of the secondary metabolites produced (Glässnerová et al., 2022; 
Mohamed et al., 2021). One of the first recognized Aspergilli is the Candidi section, which initially contained 
only A. candidus species. However, later morphological, biochemical, and physiological evidence data revealed 
that there are five more species belonging to this section with white to yellow coloration of conidia spores, such 
as A. campestris, A. dobrogensis, A. taichungensis, and A. candidus (Frisvad, 2014) (Figure 3). 
 
Figure 3: Aspergilli belonging to the candidi section, which are morphologically close to A. campestris (Frisvad, 2014). 
The colony morphology of these species differs. A. dobrogensis, for example, is morphologically close to A. 
candidus. However, they might have some distinctive characteristics, such as the diameter and spore coloration 
in their colonies. They are usually xerophilic, and they might not grow rapidly in specific media such as MEA 
and CYA (Glässnerová et al., 2022). 
2.3 Aspergillus Campestris 
Initial interest in A. campestris stemmed from its high number of secondary metabolites. It was the first species 
from the Candidi section described by Christensen (1982). Its strains were isolated from soil in a prairie in North 
Dakota, USA; from rabbit dung in California, USA; from a mouse in Edmonton, Alberta, Canada; and from soil 
in a chipmunk mound in Snowy Range, Wyoming, USA. This project focuses on the least studied strain, 
IBT28561, isolated from prairie soil in North Dakota, and IBT17867, collected from forest soil in Wyoming, as 
shown in Figure 4. 
A. campestris species is characterized by its slow growth on various media, such as MEA and CYA at 25 °C, and 
it cannot grow at temperatures above 37 °C. Due to its velvet-like and sulphur-yellow colour of spores, it was 
initially classified into the section Circumdati. Because this classification was not based on phylogenetic analysis, 
researchers found that A. campestris produces compounds also found in other candidi section species. Hence, it 
belongs to the Candidi section rather than the circumdati. This project aims to investigate the secondary 
metabolite production of two A. campestris strains under different media, focusing on identifying unique or 
possible bioactive secondary metabolites (Varga, Janos & Frisvad et.al., 2007).

2.4 Morphology 
There are slight differences between each strain, which are particularly important for their classification. The 
morphological characteristics of the section candidi: all are slow-growing colonies with round conidial heads 
with white to yellow conidia, while conidiophores are smooth. Macroscopic observation of A. campestris reveals 
that its colour is composed of long, thread-like structures called hyphae, which form a vast network known as 
mycelium. This special structure helps them grow and reproduce. They grow in long cells called hyphae. These 
hyphae are divided into compartments by small, thin walls called septa. In some fungi, such as Zygomycetes, 
septa are absent, and the interior of the hyphae is continuous. (Varga et al., 2007). 
Under favourable conditions, some hyphae grow into stalk-like structures called conidiophores, which rise above 
the surface. Conidiophores are specialized structures of Aspergilli, which produce spores called conidia (Figure

phialides. In some cases, there’s an extra layer in between, called a sterigma. (Ogunleye, n.d.). Phialides make 
spores, called conidia, with asexual reproduction through a process called mitosis. These spores are spread 
through the air, allowing them to survive in new environments and grow into new fungi without sexual 
reproduction. This allows the cell to divide through the reduction of the chromosome number by half after the 
chromosomes duplicate and exchange information (Mahata et al. 2022; Indratiningsih et al. 2013).

Chapter III: Biosynthesis of Natural Products 
3.1 Natural Products 
Natural products are low-molecular-weight molecules produced by various living organisms, such as bacteria 
and plants, serving as means of fungal protection and reproduction. They often have complex structures and may 
exhibit bioactive properties with many applications in industry, food, biology, and biotechnology. (Skellam et 
al., 2024). Many of these secondary metabolites (SMs) remain undiscovered, and their biosynthetic pathways are 
unknown. Genes related to these compounds are organized into biosynthetic gene clusters (BGCs), which are 
most often found in Ascomycota and Basidiomycota, especially in filamentous fungi. The main biosynthetic 
classes include: 1. Terpenes, 2. Polyketides (PK) 3. Shikimate pathway 4. Non-ribosomal peptides (NRPs) and 
5. Hybrid molecules like polyketide–terpene hybrids (terpenoids), peptide-terpene hybrids (meroterpenoids), and 
non-ribosomal or ribosomally synthesized peptides (NRPS/RKS). Classifying secondary metabolites by their 
biosynthetic origin is essential because it helps us understand their chemical structures, genomes, and 
biosynthetic pathways. (Frisvad & Larsen, 2015; Kjærbølling et al., 2018). 
As previously mentioned, A. campestris belongs to the candidi section and produces white to yellow spores while 
being responsible for a variety of secondary metabolites. Jens Frisvard et al. discovered that A. campestris 
produces numerous secondary metabolites, some of which are common to other Aspergillus species, while others 
are unique to A. campestris. Figure 6 illustrates all the metabolites produced by A. campestris and their respective 
classes. (Rahbñk et al., n.d.).

3.2 Polyketides  
Polyketides are large, multifunctional enzymes produced by fungi responsible for synthesizing many complex 
organic molecules, mainly polyketides. They are one of the most important natural products, and they are 
synthesized through a condensation reaction between a starter unit, such as an acetyl-CoA thioester derived from 
malonyl-CoA, and a CoA-linked starter molecule. They can be partially or highly reduced, depending on the 
number of ketoreduction–dehydratase and enoyl reductase domains present and active in the enzyme. These 
enzymes are classified based on the number of subunits (single or multiple), the mode of synthesis (iterative or 
non-iterative), and the extent of reduction during biosynthesis. According to the reduction level of b-keto carbon 
elongation chain, the iterative PKSs can be divided into the following sub-classes: (Hwang et al., 2020) 
1. Highly reducing polyketides include KR (Ketoreductase), which converts keto groups to alcohols; dehydratase, 
which removes water; and enoyl reductase, which reduces double bonds. The polyketide chain is fully reduced, 
resulting in fewer ketones and double bonds. 
2. Partially reducing polyketides (PR-PKSs) contain a subset of reducing domains, producing compounds with 
partially reduced functional groups, such as azaphilones. 
3. Non-reducing (NR-PKSs): Compared to highly reducing and partially reducing polyketides, this class has a 
SAT domain and a product template domain. The other two classes lack the SAT domain at the N-terminus and 
have different domains responsible for reduction. Non-reducing polyketides synthesize chains without extensive 
reduction, resulting in higher levels of ketones and double bonds (Lim et al., 2016). 
PKSs are classified into different types based on their structure and how they elongate the polyketide chain. They 
can be either iterative or non-iterative enzymes. Modular (or non-iterative) PKSs have many modules, each 
responsible for one chain extension, while iterative PKSs use the same cycle repeatedly until the desired chain 
length is reached. Fungal PKSs, which belong to the iterative type I (iPKS), are structurally similar to fatty acid 
synthases. They can consist of multiple modules responsible for chain extension or a single module that extends 
the chain iteratively. Type II PKSs involve multiple smaller enzymes with distinct functions working together to 
form complex molecules. Type III PKSs are multienzymatic complexes, typically composed of ketosynthase 
domains and lacking ACP. They are built from acetyl-CoA and malonyl-CoA units through condensation. 
(Risdian et al., 2019). 
A set of catalytic domains is repeatedly used to extend the polyketide chains. During the conversion of acetyl-
CoA into malonyl-CoA, these include: 1. Starter unit-ACP SAT domain at the N-terminal: acyltransferase. 2. 
Beta-ketoreductase (KR) domain: reduces beta-keto groups to hydroxyl groups. 3. Acyl Carrier Protein (ACP): 
attaches to the growing chain, aiding in various chemical reactions. 4. Dehydratase (DH): catalyzes dehydration 
reactions. 5. Enoyl reductase (ER): catalyzes hydrogenation 6. Methyltransferase (MT): methylates using S-
adenosyl methionine (SAM) as the donor, favoring beta-keto groups. 7. Ketosynthase (KS): responsible for chain 
extension. These domains are further classified into non-reducing, partially reducing, or highly reducing based 
on the reduction level of the beta-keto intermediates. Both building blocks are transferred from CoA esters to 
cysteine thiol groups, and finally to the ACP thiol group, where Claisen condensation occurs.   
3.3 Shikimic acid derivatives 
The shikimic acid pathway is not so common in fungi, as there are not many natural products produced by A. 
campestris derived from this pathway. It produces precursor molecules that serve as building blocks for alkaloid-
derived secondary metabolites (SMs). Depending on the type of alkaloid-derived compounds, we observe 
tyrosine-like alkaloids that generate indole compounds and tryptophan-like alkaloids that form isoquinoline 
alkaloids.

3.4 Terpenes 
Fungal-derived terpenoids, or isoprenoids, are a class of natural products synthesized from a molecule called mevalonic 
acid, which shares structural similarities with isoprene units (C5). Terpenoids are among the primary metabolites produced 
by Aspergilli, including triterpenoids, diterpenoids, and more complex molecules like meroterpenoids, all with various 
potential biological properties. A. campestris has not yet been reported to produce terpenoid-like SMs. However, it is 
predicted that it has many terpenes biosynthetic gene clusters (Bromann et al. 2012).  
Terpenes are synthesized through a head-to-tail condensation reaction between dimethylallyl diphosphate (DMAPP) and 
isopentenyl diphosphate (IPP), a reaction catalyzed by terpene synthases. Terpene synthases are enzymes that can be 
categorized into groups such as terpene cyclases, prenyltransferases, and other enzymes. Depending on the number of IPP 
units, they will have a different pathway (Drummond et al., 2022). Terpene cyclases are enzymes responsible for forming 
terpenoid structures by linking specific numbers of isoprene units. These compounds undergo structural modifications, 
including cyclization and oxidation reactions. In Figure 8, the biosynthetic pathway of terpinene, which is a terpenoid-
derived compound produced by A.campestris (Darragh et al., 2021).

Biosynthesis begins through the mevalonate pathway (MVA) from acetyl-CoA, producing IPP and DMAPP, or 
via a secondary pathway called the 2-C-methylerythritol phosphate (MEP) pathway, also known as the non-

3.5 Compounds with mixed biosynthetic origins: Hybrid Molecules 
In addition to the PKSs and NRPS mentioned above, there are SMs known as hybrid molecules, which are 
produced by combining one or more of these major groups. Hybrid compounds are structures formed by merging 
two or more molecules from different biosynthetic pathways into a new, potentially bioactive compound (Chiang 
et al. 2009). Most hybrid secondary metabolites can be classified into two main groups: polyketide synthase 
(PKS) hybrids, non-ribosomal peptide synthase (NRPS) hybrids, and meroterpenoids (partially derived from 
terpenoids). For example, chlorflavonin, which was the first chlorinated flavone found in fungi, is a secondary 
metabolite produced by A. campestris. It is a hybrid molecule, and its structure includes a PKS/ hybrid backbone, 
three monooxygenases, three methyltransferases, and a chlorinating enzyme (Lo et al. 2012a).

 Chapter IV: Analytical Chemistry 
Fungi, as mentioned earlier, are prolific producers of natural products that display a high level of structural 
diversity. Analytical chemistry is the scientific field that provides essential tools for studying these structures, 
utilizing advanced instrumentation and methods to analyze their chemical compositions. By examining complex 
fungal extracts, it is possible to discover novel metabolites, purify them through separation techniques for further 
studies, and connect their structure to the biosynthetic pathway by using mass spectrometry combined with 
bioinformatics tools for data analysis. (Arruda et al., 2023; Uy et al., 2025). 
In this project, we have employed a method called UHPLC-DAD-QTOF-MS/MS to analyze complex fungal 
extracts produced by A. campestris. It consists of four main parts, each with a different functionality. As shown 
in Figure 9. 1.UHPLC is used for the separation of the analytes. 2. Ion source: ESI soft ionization method 3. Mass 
analyzer (QTOF) 4. Detectors (for example, DAD) are the main parts of the system. In this chapter, we will 
explore how this system works and how we can identify SMs in fungal extracts (Zhou et al., 2012). 
Initially, the complex fungal sample can be separated with high resolution based on its different chemical 
properties, such as polarity and size, using analytical instrumentation (UHPLC) with a reversed-phase column 
(RP). Then, the analytes are ionized using a soft ionization method  (ESI) to convert the liquid sample into the 
gas phase before they enter the mass analyzer. Then, the QTOF-MS high-resolution instrument measures accurate 
mass-to-charge (m/z) values, enabling precise identification of the compounds (protonation or deprotonation). 
The precursor ions are then fragmented into product ions within the MS/MS system. (Li et al., 2017; Klitgaard 
et al., 2014). The detector identifies compounds based on their UV absorbance, with each compound having a 
unique spectrum at various wavelengths depending on its chemical structure.

4.2 Chromatography 
Chromatography is an essential analytical method used for the separation, identification, and purification of 
compounds when working with complex samples such as those from fungi. There are different methods in 
chromatography, and the basis of all the methods is that there is a mobile phase and a stationary phase. The mobile 
phase runs through the stationary phase and interacts with the analytes (compounds) that are being tested. However, 
there are differences in the types of chromatography depending on how the separation occurs, the types of analytes, 
and the difference in the stationary and mobile phases (Bachhav et. al. 2023; Hashim, Hayder 2018). For instance, 
depending on the physical state of the stationary phase and mobile phase, there is Liquid Chromatography (LC) or 
Gas Chromatography (GC). If we have volatile compounds, the method used is GC, but if we are working with 
non-volatile compounds, it is liquid chromatography. Their main difference is in the mobile phase. In LC, the 
mobile phase is fluid, while in GC, it is a gas phase. This project focuses on the HPLC method, since the compounds 
we aim to analyse are mostly polar, large-sized, unstable molecules at high temperatures, as they can degrade. 
(Hashim, Hayder 2018; Sticher, 2008). 
In addition, there are other separation methods based on the chemical interactions of the stationary phase with the 
mobile phase: 1. Ion-Exchange Chromatography (separates analytes based on the interactions with the oppositely 
charged stationary phase) 2. Size Exclusion Chromatography (separation due to molecular size: for example, large 
molecules will elute first through the column) 3. Reverse Phase Column (RPC) chromatography (separation 
happens due to the difference in hydrophobicity) 4. Normal Phase column Chromatography (separation occurs due 
to he difference in polarity of the stationary phase and the mobile phase) (Hostettmann, K; Marston, A; 
Hostettmann, 1998). Chromatographic techniques can also be preparative or analytical, and their main difference 
is in the sample scale that is being analyzed and the aim of the analysis. More specifically, preparative 
chromatography is used when we aim to purify a specific compound on a large scale for downstream use. In 
contrast, analytical chromatography is used for a small-scale sample to detect the compounds of interest in the 
sample. (Kostantyan AA et al., 2020; Ma et al., 2021). 
4.3.1 Reverse Phase Column Chromatography (RPC)   
Reverse phase chromatography (RPC) is a separation method based on hydrophobicity, achieved by adjusting 
the mobile phase conditions. Hydrophobic compounds bind to the hydrophobic part of the column (stationary 
phase), which is equilibrated with a polar eluent system. After the sample has been loaded onto the column and 
bound, a gradient will run through the column (typically organic solvent such as methanol or acetonitrile). By 
changing the polarity of the mobile phase that runs through the column, we can achieve the best separation of the 
compounds. The molecule will start eluting when its affinity to the mobile phase is larger than that of the 
stationary phase (Hartmut Schlüter 2000). 
By adding a small amount of acid (counter ion) to the mobile phase, we can lower the pH of the solution, which 
can help achieve an effective separation. This happens when we aim to separate compounds that can ionize. For 
instance, fungal metabolites can lose one proton; thus, by adding an acid, these compounds increase their 
hydrophobicity so they will be able to interact with a reverse-phase column (Eriksson, 2018). Hydrophobic parts 
of the secondary metabolites interact with the non-polar parts of the column to form van der Waals interactions. 
Selecting a suitable stationary phase is essential because it allows molecules to be separated most effectively 
(Schieppati et al., 2021). 
4.3.2 High-Performance Liquid Chromatography (HPLC)

HPLC is an advanced analytical method characterized by short separation time, high accuracy, precision, and 
sensitivity. It is based on the following principles: adsorption, partition, ion exchange, and gel permeation. The 
system required for carrying out separation through HPLC consists mainly of the following components: HPLC 
solvent, HPLC pump, injector, column (commercially prepared), detector (which provides the retention time of 
the analyte), and a recorder of the data (Coscun O.2016; Czaplicki, 2013). 
In this study, the reversed-phase HPLC separation method is used with a column packed with C18. The main 
principle behind this method is the hydrophobic interactions between the analytes dissolved in the mobile phase 
and the stationary phase. Each component in a sample moves over the stationary phase of the column, and they 
are separated from each other in different retention times. Compounds with more polar functional groups are 
diluted in the mobile phase, and they have fewer interactions with the non-polar column. On the other hand, the 
more hydrophobic compounds interact more with the column and less with the mobile phase (Bachhav et al. 
2023). 
The separation process begins when the sample is introduced near the column inlet using a sample injector. 
Firstly, the sample is diluted with the mobile phase (usually an organic solvent) and then it is injected into the 
HPLC column. The components (analytes) pass between the mobile phase and the stationary phase (Bachhav et 
al, 2023; Bhatti, Manisha, 2019). The column is made of C18 silica-based material, and the adsorption occurs 
through various interactions. Compounds move in the column when they are in the mobile phase. When 
compounds are distributed in the mobile phase, they migrate fast, while when they are distributed in the stationary 
phase, they migrate more slowly. Each compound, when it is eluted through, is detected by the detector (Bhatti, 
Manisha; 2019; Siddiqui et al. 2017; Pesce et al. 2010).

injected into the column inlet, and the components pass between the mobile phase and the stationary phase. 
Each compound, when it is eluted, goes to the detector. (Czaplicki, 2013).   
4.3.3 Stationary Phase (Column Material)  
The stationary phase used in the system is the core part of column chromatography, responsible for the separation. 
In reverse-phase chromatography, a non-polar material with hydrophobic alkyl chains (C18 or C8) is packed inside 
the column bound to silica-based particles (SiO2). Different types are illustrated in Figure11. This non-polar 
material interacts with the hydrophobic part of the analyte sample and the polar mobile phase used for the 
separation (Harris, 2016). For RP chromatography, the most common column material used is C18, as it provides 
the best separation, especially for compounds with aromatic rings, polymers, or a high number of hydrocarbon 
chains. One way for effective seperation is to change the eluent system during the seperation or by switching the 
stationary phases while the mobile phase remains the same(J.J. Kirkland 2004; Suárez Suárez et al. 2011).

4.3.2.2 Mobile Phase (solvent system) 
The mobile phase composition changes during a gradient elution; it usually starts from aqueous and becomes 
more organic. This happens for effective separation, as it gradually weakens the hydrophobic binding and allows 
more hydrophobic molecules to elute in the end, while the more polar ones are initially retained. (Liquid 
Chromatography-Mass Spectrometry Third Edition, n.d.). 
In the HPLC system, the mobile phase is usually a mixture of solvents such as organic solvents (acetonitrile or 
methanol) and water with a small quantity of acid. Common acids used are Formic Acid (FA), TFA, or acetic 
acid, as they improve separation by providing protons into the RP-LC/MS. Using acid lowers the pH of the 
mobile phase that enters the column, resulting in sharper peaks at low concentrations compared to acetic acid, 
and thus in the UV signal, lower suppression. It helps to balance the pH in the column. So, compounds can get 
deprotonated and protonated; therefore, separation also becomes successful. Performing LC at low pH is 
preferred as most SMs have functional groups that can get protonated. In channel A, aqueous solvent enters, 
typically water with 0.1% acid (helps protonation and ionization in ESI). In contrast, in channel B of the system, 
an organic solvent, such as acetonitrile with 0.1% FA, is used (so hydrophobic compounds will be eluted). To 
elute more polar compounds, we are starting with a predominantly aqueous gradient, and over time, we increase 
to 95% of gradient B (more organic). In this way, more hydrophobic compounds can be eluted later. By changing 
the polarity of the solvents, the separation of the compounds becomes more effective (Brookhart et al., 2023).

4.4 Mass Spectrometry 
Mass spectrometry is an analytical method used to identify and quantify substances in the gas phase by their 
mass-to-charge (m/z) ratio and their isotopes in a sample by accelerating the ions into the electric field. Mass 
spectrometry is usually coupled with an HPLC system, as compounds need to be separated first using reversed-
phase chromatography before entering the MS system. The combination of HPLC-MS/MS makes it possible to 
analyse more complex mixtures obtained from natural products. Some standard MS systems used are: Triple 
Quadrupole, electric or magnetic sector, and TOF (Talha, n.d.). 
The HPLC system is used to separate the mixture into individual components before they enter the mass 
spectrometer based on the different chemical properties. However, the liquid sample before entering the MS 
system must be vaporized and the compounds ionized, as the substances in the sample need to be separated before 
they are inserted into the mass spectrometer. This happens through different ionization methods, such as ESI 
(soft ionization method), hard ionization, or atmospheric pressure ionization. For this project, as we are working 
with fungal-derived compounds, we are using the ESI-ionization method (Malik et al., 2015; Wooding & Auchus, 
2013). After the molecules have been converted into ions, they enter the MS1, which is the first mass analyzer 
that measures the mass-to-charge ratio of the ions when coming from the ion source. Then they pass through the 
collision cell, which fragments the precursor into smaller ions (product ions) by using collision dissociation 
energy (CID). In the MS2 mass analyzer, the pattern of fragments can be used to determine the structure of the 
molecules, and the analyzer separates the ions from each other based on the mass-to-charge ratio. In the last part, 
there is a detector that records the abundance of the ions in the chromatograms. This process will be explained 
further in the following step, but it is illustrated in Figure 12 (Kaklamanos et al., 2015; Li et al., 2017). 
 
 
Figure 12: Schematic representation of MS instrumentation with two Mass Analyzers. 
4.4.1 Electrospray Ionization method (ESI) 
ESI is a soft ionization method used to transfer molecules from liquid samples into gas-phase ions with minimal 
fragmentation. Thus, compounds get protonated in the positive mode of ESI while they get deprotonated in the 
negative ESI mode. An HPLC system is connected to the MS, and samples are injected into the mobile phase 
from the HPLC. During ESI, the solvent is evaporated, which helps protect the equipment from overload, and 
evaporation is an essential part of the process. (Tang et al., n.d.). 
The ESI setup includes the following components (see Figure 13): 1. When the mobile phase enters the ionization 
chamber, it enters a narrow metal capillary emitter (spraying nozzle) through which the solvent flows. 2. A funnel 
at the end of the capillary. When the analyte solution is pumped through the capillary emitter, a high voltage 
(typically between 2 and 4 kV) is applied at the capillary tip. This high voltage causes the liquid to form a cone-
like shape called a Taylor cone. Then, charged droplets are generated before they spray out as a mist (gas ions) 
3. The charged droplets are going into a superheated gas, which is used to dry the solvent, causing droplets to 
decrease in size as evaporation occurs (Arruda et al., 2023; Tang et al., n.d.). The drying gas used is usually 
nitrogen, which directs the spray from the capillary tip to the mass spectrometer. The size of droplets decreases 
while the charge density increases, thus becoming more unstable until they reach the Rayleigh limit. Beyond this 
==PAGE:30==
 
DTU Biotechnology & Biomedicine  ן 
30 
 
limit, the charge density has increased. In this state, the electrostatic repulsive forces exceed the surface tension 
of the droplets. As a result, the droplets become smaller (a process called coulomb fission) (Draper et al., 2013). 
4. The droplets eventually transition into a gas phase within the evaporation chamber, where all these transitions 
happen. 5. The outgoing droplets have the same charge, as the capillary gives two forms of ESI. The solvent 
evaporation process continues until the analyte is solvent-free or until it reaches the mass analyzer (Li et al., 
2017; Uy et al., 2025). 
 
Figure 13: Illustration of the ESI ionization process: Production of charged droplets. How are these droplets being 
formed, and how do their sizes change with the coulombic interactions. 
Within the ionization chamber, ions are produced and moved through the mass analyzer region via several ion 
optics, which separate the ions based on their mass-to-charge ratio. Electrons flow to or from the capillary 
depending on the polarity, causing an electrochemical reaction of the solvent. More specifically, as the charged 
droplets pass through a capillary tube, they become separated. A material, C18, is a silica-based material, and the 
adsorption happens based on different interactions, charge density, creating an electric field within each droplet. 
These separated ions then enter the detector, where the concentration of the compounds we want to analyse is 
measured, and the results are displayed on a chart called a mass spectrum. This entire process occurs in a high-
vacuum system, as the gas ions formed are very reactive.  
4.4.2 Quadrupole Time of Flight Mass Spectrometry  
4.4.2.1 Hybrid Mass Analyzer (QQQ: triple quadrupole mass analyzer) 
The Q-TOF mass analyzer is a hybrid mass analyzer that combines four quadrupoles with a Time-of-Flight (TOF) 
mass analyzer (Figure 14). It is used to determine the mass-to-charge ratio. There is also a Q0, which acts as an 
ion guide to transfer the selected ions into the Q1 mass filter. The quadrupole consists of four parallel metal rods 
(quadrupoles) (Quad 0, Quad 1, sample through the column and the different components interact with the 
stationary phase. The quadrupole (Quad 0) is used to introduce the ionized samples into the instrument. The 
second quadrupole (Q1) works as a mass filter, where the ions are selected and filtered based on their mass-to-
charge ratio. By only applying RF voltage, the quadrupole works as a transmission device and not as a filtering 
device. The selected ions enter after the Hexapole (H1) or another quadrupole (Q2), which is known as a collision 
cell. Collision energy is used to fragment the selected ion into small pieces by a process known as collision-
induced dissociation (CID) using nitrogen gas or argon. More specifically, the ions collide with the reactive 
gases, such as nitrogen. The generated collision ions are sent to a flight tube (Quad 3) after traveling a specific 
distance. Then the direction changes into orthogonal compared to their original path. A mass spectrum of the 
product is being obtained by Q3, which helps obtain structural information of an ion (adduct patterns of secondary 
metabolites).

4.4.2.2 Quadrupole -MS 
A quadrupole is a device with four rods parallel to each other and cylindrical. There are two different types of 
quadrupoles. The one illustrated in Figure 15 has 4 parallel rods connected to their paired rod with Radio 
Frequency (RF), and a voltage is applied to the analyzer. (Nielsen et. al.,2011) One pair of rods is positively 
charged (+) and the other is negatively charged (-). The applied voltage RF creates an electric field, so the ions 
that can travel through the quadrupole oscillate. Only ions with specific m/z can pass through the quadrupole to 
reach the ion detector. Quadrupole, in this way, tends to act as a mass-to-charge filter (see Figure 15) (Reiner et 
al., 2014). By changing the DC voltage, it is possible to change the mass-to-charge ratio of the ions.  
 
Figure 15: Quadrupole: Illustration to show how the quadrupole works. There are two oppositely charged pairs of rods, 
and an electric field is created. Only ions with a specific mass-to-charge ratio coming from the ion source in different 
directions can pass through the quadrupole to reach the ion detector. 
4.4.2.3 Collision cell energy 
Collision-induced dissociation energy induces fragmentation of selected ions in the gas phase. Fragmentation of 
molecules provides valuable structural information that helps us identify unknown compounds. The selected ion 
passes through an octapole ion guide, where positively charged ions collide with gas molecules (nitrogen or 
argon) inside the octapole. The Octapole ion guide helps to direct the ions properly, and when leaving, it knocks 
off parts of the molecules. In the collision cell, a gas is introduced (usually nitrogen or argon) and the kinetic 
energy is converted into internal energy, thus chemical bonds of the compounds are fragmented and form product 
ions (fragments). CID is happening by using different energy levels, such as 10, 20, and 40 eV and generated 
different fragmentation patterns. The higher the energy, the more fragments are produced. (Mörlein et al., 2020).

4.4.2.5 High Resolution MS (HRMS) 
High-resolution mass spectrometry (HRMS) is a powerful analytical method used to detect accurate mass 
differences between two peaks in a mass spectrum. High resolution MS can detect compounds with 10-4 amu 
differences in mass between two compounds, which allows the determination of elemental composition 
boundaries of compounds under 600 Da in a sample. Sometimes, varied factors affect this type of determination, 
such as isotopes of the elements with the same mass or doubly charged ions. In case of elements that have more 
than one isotope, HRMS can provide more information about the specific atoms and their quantities in the 
molecule. In natural products, the most used elements are halogens like Cl and Br, and their mass difference is 
two Da between these most abundant atoms. These atoms exist in two different isotopes, and the chlorinated, 
brominated compounds are very distinct – they can be used to determine the number of atoms. Sometimes there 
are. High MS is an essential method for differentiating ions with closely matching masses; thus, it is to be used 
for natural product identification, as they undergo many metabolic changes in the biological systems. High-
resolution MS needs to be used to find and quantify these analytes precisely.

Tandem Mass Spectrometry (MS/MS), also known as a multiple-step process of mass spectrometry with 
fragmentation occurring between stages, is a powerful analytical technique used to gain more insight into the 
structure of complex metabolite mixtures, in this case, in A. campestris fungal extracts, and characterize them 
based on the mass to charge ratio. It is a two-step process as two or more analyzers are coupled. More specifically, 
after the sample has been introduced into the MS system and the eluting metabolites have been ionized by ESI 
or another method, they are separated based on mass-to-charge (m/z) ratio during the MS1 fragmentation process. 
The most abundant ion (parent ion) is selected by the first analyzer (Q1) and fragmented further (in the Q2 
analyzer). So, the parent ion is fragmented into the product ions using collision-induced dissociation energy 
(CID). To achieve high-quality MS/MS spectra, different fragmentation energies ranging from 10 to 100 eV are 
used. Then, in the quadrupole mass analyzer (Q3), the product ions are separated using adduct patterns and 
detected in the detector to confirm the structure using databases. As some groups are strongly associated with the 
molecule, more energy needs to be applied to get it fragmented (higher CID). The most common degree of 
fragmentation is 10, 20, 40 eV. The data from the MS/MS helps resolve unknown molecules.  
4.5 Detectors 
4.5.1 UV-Vis spectroscopy 
UV-VIS spectroscopy measures the interaction of the analytes in the sample with the ultraviolet or visible light. 
Deuterium lamps, which are typically used, emit UV light in the range of 190-400 nm, while tungsten lamps 
cover visible light from 200-640 nm. Compounds with double bonds or conjugated systems may absorb light at 
specific wavelengths in the UV-Vis spectrum, or they may emit light. If they absorb light, then the molecule will 
go from a lower excitation state (low-energy excitation state) into a higher excitation state (excited state). When 
molecular excitation happens, electrons move from the LUMO to the HOMO state, so from the unoccupied to 
the occupied state. (Klitgaard, 2025a; Shakir et al., n.d.). 
For example, isoquinoline alkaloid derivatives, which are a class of nitrogen-based secondary metabolites with 
a fused ring system, show characteristic peaks in UV spectra. These usually include a strong absorption around 
205 nm and a weaker one between 255-280 nm. The intensity of UV absorption may shift depending on the 
substituents attached to the structure. Moreover, a yellow pigment will absorb light around 400 nm. Monitoring 
this wavelength can give us information about the compound we're trying to identify or its derivatives, as they 
also absorb UV light at the same wavelength due to the presence of a chromophore in their structure. 
Chromophores are functional groups with conjugated systems, and the absorption varies depending on the 
derivative. (Hendry, n.d.). 
Analysis of the UV/VIS can also be applied in studies of biosynthetic pathways. For example, suppose 
compounds have a similar structure but different functional groups. In that case, they will have similar UV 
absorption, we can hypothesize that they are biosynthetically related (derivatives), such as the derivatives of 
picrasidine L. compound. (Rizwan & Gwenin, 2021).

4.5.2 Diode-Array-Detector (DAD)  
Dad detector is an advanced detector that is used in UV spectroscopy. UHPLC-MS is often combined with a 
DAD detector because a UV detector records an absorption spectrum and provides information about the 
chromophores of compounds, which can be used for database searching (Eshaghi, 2011). 
DAD operates by passing light coming from a deuterium or tungsten lamp through a sample in a flow cell. This 
light source can be either a deuterium or a tungsten lamp, and it separates into different wavelengths detected by 
a photodiode array. The light after it passes through the flow cell is divided into different individual wavelengths 
by a fixed grating, and the DAD detects these different wavelengths. Some of the light is absorbed by the 
substances in the mobile phase, while other parts are diffracted in various directions. The DAD captures the entire 
UV-Vis spectrum of a sample as it flows through the cell. The diffraction works by directing light into an array 
of photodiodes. The DAD can scan the full range of UV spectra, which is especially useful for complex samples 
like fungi, since it allows multiple peaks to be distinguished and more structural information to be obtained across 
a wide wavelength range simultaneously (Medhe, 2018).

4.6 Data Analysis  
4.6.1 Dereplication 
One of the most crucial steps in NP chemistry is the rapid identification of previously reported Secondary 
metabolites produced by A. campestris. The method used to distinguish between known and unknown 
compounds in the MS data is called dereplication. In this way, we don’t have to spend time analysing already 
known compounds, but search for unknown compounds. In this project, dereplication is used as part of the results 
section to find known SMs produced by A. campestris. It consists of generating a mass spectrum of a peak in 
ESI+ mode and using a database search to confirm the reported m/z,  retention time, and UV absorption in the 
compound (Gaudêncio et al., 2023; W.F. Reynolds, 2017).  
Dereplication starts by selecting one peak from a known compound in the Base Peak Chromatogram (BPC). It 
will show the mass-to-charge ratio, and then we will calculate the difference with the other ion. The related 
adduct ions were found by calculating the distance between the different ions (Nielsen 2011). If dereplication is 
not correct, we cannot proceed with the large-scale extraction or with further experiments. We need to be sure 
that we have the right compound before we move to the next steps (Klitgaard, 2025b; Gaudêncio et al., 2023) .

All MS/MS data were converted from Agilent MassHunter ( .d files) to the. mzML format using MS- Convert 
and uploaded to the GNPS Web platform (http://gnps. Ucsd.edu) for molecular networking analysis in Classic

creating five groups; additionally, eight standard samples formed a sixth group. 
Parameter Optimization: 
Various parameters, such as cosine similarity and the number of minor matched peaks, were evaluated to find 
the best networking conditions. A cosine score of 1 shows identical spectra, while 0 shows no similarity. 
Data Filtering and Preprocessing: 
Fragment ions within ± 17 Da of the precursor m/z were removed from all MS/MS spectra. Spectra were window-
filtered to include only the top 6 fragment ions within a ± 50 Da window. The precursor ion mass tolerance was 
set at 0. 0.1 Da, and the MS/MS fragment ion tolerance at 0.02 Da.

Optimal fragmentation was achieved using 40 eV CID, as it reveals more fragments of the compound in the 
sample. Low-mass fragments, such as 115.0539 Da, probably result from the fragmentation of the aromatic ring, 
especially at higher collision energies, as they are very stable. The easiest bond to break is a weak bond, N-CH3, 
while higher collision energy is needed to break the ring system and the intermediate ring (stronger bonds). When 
the first methyl group is lost, it creates a fragment with a mass difference of 15 Da, 235.05 m/z. This is likely the 
methyl group attached to the nitrogen. Another fragment is 223 m/z with a 28 Da difference from the 
demethylated product ion, suggesting the cleavage of the lactam ring in the amide bond. An intense fragment is 
a compound (3) with m/z 195.0916, representing the loss of a larger substituent. This cleavage occurs on the 
lactam ring. The most stable fragment is the [M+H] + ion, which stays consistent across all energy levels. The 
variation in peak abundancies also reflects differences in the stability and formation efficiency of the fragmented 
ions. Figure 32 illustrates possible fragments resulting from applying 40 eV to the compound.

6.2.7 Proposed biosynthetic Pathway for the hybrid molecule: picrasidine L. (shikimate-
derived product)  
Based on the chemical analysis of the two fungal strains from A. campestris and the GNPS molecular network, 
a detailed biosynthetic pathway for picrasidne L. was proposed, as illustrated in Figure 37. The hypothesis is that 
the path for the production of picrasidine L. starts using as a precursor molecule the aromatic amino acid 
tryptophan L., which is derived from the shikimate pathway. Tryptophan is decarboxylated to tryptamine, which 
then undergoes a cyclization reaction, Pictet-Spengler intramolecular cyclization reaction (condensation)  with 
an aldehyde to form a new aza-heterocylic compound, creating a tetrahydro-beta carboline structure. More 
specifically, the indole nitrogen acts as a nucleophile during this cyclization step, leading to the formation of an 
iminium intermediate with an intramolecular 6-endo cyclization. An aldehyde or ketone then attacks this 
intermediate under acidic conditions (Maskos et al., 1992; Jamil et al., 2020). The intermediate then undergoes an 
oxidation reaction to regain aromaticity, which is followed by cyclization for lactam ring formation, and then it is oxidized. 
The last step of the reaction is a S-adenosyl methionine (SAM)-dependent methylation reaction to the -NH of the ring, 
yielding the final structure of picrasidine L.  
The data support the proposed pathway found through HPLC-MS chemical analysis for picrasidine L: 1. Picrasidine L 
elutes at a retention time of 3.3 minutes with a mass to charge ratio of 251, and two adducts have been found, M-H and M-
Na 2. By applying 40 eV to the molecule, MS2 is generated. The precursor molecule is fragmented into product ions 
(fragments). The compound was mostly abundant in media YES from IBT28561 and basmati rice from IBT17867, which 
shows that its production is strain and media-dependent. Using a feeding experiment to strain IBT17486  with tryptophan 
L., we were not able to prove that it is the precursor molecule due to the lack of metal traces in the YES media.

6.4 Discussion 
Based on manual dereplication in different media, A. campestris was shown to produce a wide variety of 
secondary metabolites with diverse structures, including alkaloids, flavonoids, and polyenes. The alkaloid group 
comprised several canthin-6-one derivatives, such as 5-methoxycanthin-6-one, 9-hydroxycanthin-6-one, and 
luotonin, all of which share a beta-carboline ring system similar to picrasidine L. Picrasidine L and picrasidine I 
displayed more complex architectures as hybrid molecules. In addition, flavonoid-like compounds such as 
chlorflavonin and terphenyllin were detected, alongside polyene macrolides from the candicidin family. This 
structural diversity confirms that A. campestris expresses multiple biosynthetic gene clusters responsible for 
producing chemically distinct metabolite families. 
Among the rice-based media, basmati rice supported the highest chemical diversity, with multiple metabolites 
detected. In contrast, YES, CYA, and PDA media in A. campestris IBT28561 were found to be the most favorable 
for picrasidine L production. Manual dereplication confirmed these findings by annotating adduct ions, UV 
absorption spectra, and comparing experimental and theoretical masses. Furthermore, three picrasidine L 
derivatives (lacking a methyl group and displaying increased hydrophobicity) were detected in jasmine rice 
cultures in both strains. 
A possible biosynthetic pathway for picrasidine L was proposed, involving tryptophan L as a precursor. However, 
since the experiment was performed in YES medium without trace metals, it was not possible to confirm the 
involvement of the shikimate pathway, and further experiments are required. Cultivation of A. campestris 
IBT28561 on rice and of other strains on different media confirmed the absence of sclerotia formation, in 
agreement with previous literature. 
MS/MS data were further analyzed using GNPS molecular networking, which revealed picrasidine L as a distinct 
node within the network. Several additional compounds were annotated from the GNPS analysis, complementing 
the manual dereplication results. HPLC proved to be a sensitive method for compound identification in A. 
campestris, and the use of a DAD detector allowed the detection of the UV absorption spectrum of picrasidine 
L, which showed a characteristic maximum around 400 nm, possibly explaining the yellow coloration observed 
in the cultures

6.5 Conclusion and Future Perspectives 
The master‘s thesis aimed to conduct chemical analysis of two strains (IBT28561 and IBT17867) from 
Aspergillus campestris and to study their metabolic profile. Above all, we aimed to investigate a novel plant-
derived compound called picrasidine L. and to propose a possible biosynthetic pathway. Chemical analysis was 
conducted using a UHPLC-DAD-QTOFMS method with manual dereplication and library search methodologies. 
YES from IBT17867 starin was used for the tryptophan L. feeding experiment due to its low concentration in 
picrasin L.  
In this study, other common secondary metabolites were detected, confirming that these strains can produce 
compounds found in other Aspergilli within the candidi section morphologically close to A. campestris. 
Furthermore, these compounds were confirmed based on adducts, UV, dereplication, and isotope patterns.  
Through this project, we have concluded that there is still much work left for the future. The experiment with the 
precursor molecule tryptophan L. would be great to repeat it using the correct media in the same strain (YES 
with trace metals). So we could compare the two metabolic profiles after the cultivation (with or without 
tryptophan L.). It would also be interesting to perform extensive scale extraction for purification of the picrasidine 
L compound and then send it to NMR. There are, of course, more novel compounds to be discovered. Further 
manual dereplication on the annotated compounds from the GNPS could be performed to find more metabolites 
produced by A. campestris.
