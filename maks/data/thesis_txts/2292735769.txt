Summary (max. 2000 characters):
In this thesis a bedframe model is designed for the DTU 10MW ref-
erence wind turbine. A new model is created in Hawc2, where the
position of the simple beam elements is redeﬁned. by simple beam
elements with approximate stiﬀness and mass, an actual design is
made. From a CAD drawing for a simpliﬁed real bedframe structure,
an ANSYS solid element model of the designed bedframe is made.
Then, an improved model of the bedframe is obtained for HAWC2
by dividing it up into one or more super-elements and a new load
basis is computed. Stress analysis for speciﬁc points of the detailed
FEM model using the time series of reaction is made and stresses
are compared with those obtained by the superelement deformations
calculated in HAWC2.

Abstract
The work in this thesis deals with the development and description of a methodology
in order to link the aeroelastic tool Hawc2 with a more detailed FEmodel. It deals
with the bedplate component which lies at the top of the tower of the reference 10MW
wind turbine. The bedplate was chosen due to its central function and importance in
the nacelle.
The model is primarily intended for use as a general and rough model of a real
bedframe.
Two new structural model as input for the load cases simulation are
created. One with a modiﬁed conﬁguration, where beam elements are rearranged in
a more representative topology of the shape of a bedframe. At the second case, a
superelement is coupled with the entire structure instead. It is generated straight
from the ﬁnite element model which was roughly designed. The goal of this project is
to develop a procedure of obtaining stress history at speciﬁc points of the FEmodel,
from the reactions and displacements of the superelement’s nodes during a 10-minute
simulation of the entire wind turbine structure.
Keywords:wind turbine, super-element, bedframe, Craig-Bampton

Introduction
Nowadays more and more people are addressed to ”cleaner” ways of producing energy.
For this reason, a continuously growing and developing industry is noticed; a man
tries to be beneﬁted from the wind and the energy that it can be generated.
Wind energy is deﬁned as the kinetic energy which is included in a mass of ﬂowing
air that is created because of temperature diﬀerence on earth. Wind power is an
alternative to fossil fuels, abundant, inexhaustible and, most importantly, ”for free”.
However,in real life, the conversion to electrical energy is still quite expensive. Wind
energy is world wide a sharply growing segment within the energy sector. Wind power
technologies are considered to be one of the most environmentally friendly methods
of generating energy.
The idea seems simple; The rotor blades capture the kinetic energy of the wind. This
is converted into rotational energy that makes the shaft to rotate. Then, through a
gearbox, if present, it is transmitted to the generator which converts the mechanical
energy to electrical. Finally, this is provided to a utility grid and it can be used to
cover daily demand. Eventually, more and more wind farms are built around the
world and the size of the wind turbines is growing trying to capture more wind.
At the same time, an intense research is taking place in order both to improve the
technologies but also to reduce the cost of the energy produced. Turbine designs
are pushed to the limits in the name of proﬁtability. For that reason, every single
part of a wind turbine or technologies regarding the grid connection are continuously
under research. For instance, one way is to reduce the operational costs. That can be
achieved by designing a more reliable turbine, whose parts are less prone to failures
but have suﬃcient capacity to withstand the increased loading. Therefore, a really
important aspect in design of the components and eventually a wind turbine is deep
understanding of its structural dynamic behaviour. This is done by experiments and
continuous simulations in order to realise and estimate loads, stresses, deformations
that occur to every single component.
All in all, the demand on characteristics of technological products, such as wind
turbines, and their simulation is increasing, and at the same time, requirements for

1.1
Super element concept
A super element is a reduced order model of a structure. It is developed aiming to
reduce the number of degrees of freedom which describe a large structure; however,
the main and important dynamic characteristics of the real, complex structure are
maintained. It is an analysis process in which collaborative analysis is done and is
very useful for complex models that are developed for various purposes. It can be
used in diﬀerent manners and cases:
A big structure can be divided in smaller individual parts that are analyzed and
then assembled together, by combining the reduced matrices for further and more
complete analysis. On the other hand, as in our case, a super element is attached
to the model. The big amount of DOFs of the real body is replaced by a limited
set of DOFs. It is introduced into the entire wind turbine model. A component is
described and processed independently in an external dll system which contains the
information about the condensed component. This results in a set of reduced matrices
which describe the behaviour of the superelement as seen by the rest of the structure.
1.2
Related work
It is common tactic for structural analysis, bodies to be modelled with superelements
to reduce the required computation time. Superelement or substructuring approaches
using Guyan or Craig-Bampton methods are widely used. It represents the stiﬀness
and mass of the reference structure.
Similar work can be found across the literature. Vorpahl et.al implement a super-
element technique, which allows for a detailed modelling of local joint and its ﬂexibility
in the oﬀshore wind turbine (OWT) design tool ADCoS-Oﬀshore [19]. There, the
Guyan method is used. The superelement model is based on a shell model where all
the joints -the shell substructures- are replaced by superelements using the Guyan
reduction procedure.
In that way, joint stiﬀness with the same level of accuracy
as a detailed ﬁnite element model is taken into account in the computations.

More speciﬁcally, the transformation matrix was formed based on the Guyan static
reduction technique and then on Craig-Bampton approach. According to Guyan re-
duction a set of nodes are retained and the remaining set of nodes are eliminated
by condensation[8]. Only stiﬀness properties are considered during the condensation.
Moreover, inertia coupling between the two sets of nodes is ignored. Contrary to
Guyan method, the Craig-Bampton approach accounts for both mass and stiﬀness.
Guyan’s principle is that unit loads are applied at the interface node of the sub struc-
ture and the tower and the corresponding deﬂection shapes are contained as columns
in the transformation matrix T. However, with the second method the transforma-
tion matrix is generated based on a static and dynamic technique. In contrast to the
previous attempt, the dynamic modes are now produced with ﬁxed constraints at the
interface node. The static modes are still obtained using ﬁxed constraints at mud-level
and no constraints at the interface node where the unit loads are applied.[13]
Furthermore, reduced super element modelling was also used by B.P. Nortier et.al
to model and analyze complex foundations structures of oﬀshore turbine installation
[15].
Another application of superelement introduction has to do with a wing structure
[11]. In that study by Kuntjoro et.al, the wing is divided to four substructures, which
are the superelements created in MSC Nastran[14].

1.3.1
Bedplate
A variety of the components that are lying inside the nacelle should, in general, be
supported. Therefore a structure should be there in order to support the shaft, the
generator and the gearbox depending on the drive train topology. The bedplate is a
central part of the nacelle of a wind turbine and is exposed to continuous variation in
loading. It is mostly made by cast iron exists to endure and transmit all the thrust
forces and moments created by the wind, from the hub to the tower.
However, in the 10MW reference wind turbine the nacelle is represented by mass and
inertia moments attached on the beam elements of the tower top. That means that
the bedplate is excluded as a part and applied as a mass point generally in the nacelle
representation.
In this project, modiﬁcation of the structure deﬁned in Hawc2 and especially the
components between the tower and the shaft at the main bearing, before the hub are
implemented. It has to do with the intermediate part inside the nacelle, which trans-
fers loads or vibrations between the rotor arrangement and the tower, the bedplate
or bedframe or main frame or king’s pin, as it is known(Figure1.3).

2.1
Existing model and Hawc2 Software used
The numerical simulations at this thesis have been carried out using a software called
Hawc2 which was developed at Risø National Laboratory in Denmark. Hawc2 stands
for Horizontal Axis Wind turbine Code 2nd generation and it is a tool used to study
the static and dynamic behaviour and response in time domain of a wind turbine,
which is exposed to diﬀerent kind of loads; various forces of excitation by diﬀerent
kind of wind conditions.
A multibody formulation of coupled independent bodies deﬁnes the conﬁguration of
the wind turbine model. More speciﬁcally, the method implemented is ”ﬂoating frame
of reference”[12]. In short, the real Hawc2 model consists of bodies representing the
diﬀerent structural elements. Each one interacts with the neighbouring with joints as
seen in Figure 2.1.
Figure 2.1: Hawc2 multibody simulation tool [12]
The constraints are imposed at their interface and are implemented as algebraic equa-
tions. In that way, non-linearity caused for example due to large deﬂection or rotation

among two bodies can be modelled. They ensure that all translations and rotations
relative to each other are correctly accounted for. What is more, Hawc2 is based on
beam theory to describe the mechanical behaviour of various components as blades
or tower. Hence, each body is divided in a set of one dimensional Timoshenko beam
elements with 6 degrees of freedom, where the deformations are assumed to be small.
The Timoshenko beam elements add the eﬀect of ﬂexibility to each body.
The model consists of the tower, tower top and then the shaft body leads to the hub,
and ﬁnally the hub is connected to the blades. The nineteen structural properties of
each ﬂexible body, like mass, stiﬀness and inertia distribution are deﬁned in separate
”st-ﬁles”. There, the cross section parameters are deﬁned. All beam elements are
isotropic which implies that their characteristics are constant through each of them.
The structural and material damping for these beam elements are also deﬁned in
the ”htc ﬁle”. Each !!!main body’s!!! properties are deﬁned on its own coordinate
system which has an orientation with respect to the global system. The orientation
of each body is deﬁned inside the ”htc ﬁle” in the orientation block. Following, the
constraints of motion between the bodies interface are deﬁned too.

This section addresses the theory needed to apply superelement modelling to the
simulation of the wind turbine including the bedplate super element.

basic component model reduction theory is described on the basis of the popular

2.2.1
Craig-Bampton reduction method
Use of a real Finite element model in order to do an analysis of a wind turbine is
the ideal. However, this would result the whole structure to be consisted of a rather
big amount of DOFs. Hence, a practical issue of the size of matrices involved raises.
Additionally, many diﬀerent load cases should be evaluated, in order to account for
the various loads to which a wind turbine is subjected during its lifetime. Therefore,
simulations would be time consuming. In order to make the ﬁnite element models
more compact, but at the same time ensure that they maintain their characteristics,
reduction methods are used.

5
Conclusions and
Recommendations
5.1
Conclusions
The objective of the thesis was to link the aeroelastic tool Hawc2 with the FE model
of the bedplate component for the 10MW Reference wind turbine.
It can be concluded that results match but with some variations. It is seen that
depending on the location of the nodes/elements there is better or worse accordance
of the stresses obtained. The quality of mesh might also inﬂuence the results.
Moreover, it results that the eﬀect of the gravity can be captured to the static response
of the super element. When it is not taken into account, stresses match better. for
the static simulation.
Diﬀerences in stiﬀness of the super element model were observed depending on the
way that the sections are constrained. It is important that the constraints used to the
boundaries of the solid model are in accordance with the behaviour imposed when
the super element is created. Otherwise, diﬀerences in stiﬀness between the models
occurs, and the stress results are incomparable. Therefore, inclusion of more sections
to the structure could handle that problem, and the real behaviour could be captured
in a better way.
5.2
Recommendations for further study
Although main aspects of the method and its application in the wind turbine model
have been covered in this thesis, there is always room for further research. In this
section some recommendations are given, to the focus of future eﬀorts in that area.
42
==PAGE:55==
5
CONCLUSIONS AND RECOMMENDATIONS
First of all, it is suggested that an investigation, whether using more sub sets of
DOFs is improving the results. More sections should be selected in order to deﬁne
more than two nodes representing the bedplate component and compare the results.
It was noticed that the way the two sections at the FE model are constrained aﬀects
the total behaviour of the nodes. Even the nodes which are located at a distance
are quite aﬀected by the boundary conditions. Hence, it is interesting to investigate
whether the use of more subset of DOFs changes the response. A section including
the open hole might give better results.
What is more a better quality of mesh, more dense mesh of the FE model, can aﬀect
the results as less variation would be between the neighbouring elements, and less
sharp variances of stresses would appear.
Furthermore, a more detailed solid model could be created. A ﬂange at the base
where holes for bolts are taken into consideration and the loads are transferred from
these points, instead of the bottom ﬁxed rough model used.
Fatigue analysis can be implemented in order to deﬁne the real hot spots where stress
concentrations are observed. In this thesis, three!! points after applying unit loads
are used in order to collect and compare the stresses. Hence, two points around the
opening and one at the ”neck” of the bedplate were chosen. After implementing a
fatigue analysis, points of stress concentration can be deﬁned. These places are of
more interest,due to the likelihood of failure occurrence and should be taken into con-
sideration for the design of such a component. Therefore, the methodology described
in this thesis, could be used for extraction of stresses. Afterwards, these results can
be used in post-process, for damage computation for the component.
