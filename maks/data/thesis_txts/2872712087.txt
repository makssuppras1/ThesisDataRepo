Abstract
This thesis explores the longitudinal interactions between flux-tunable transmon qubits inductively coupled
to a shared linear resonator. The work here progressively moves towards implementing multi-qubit gates in
the architecture at hand, with potential applications in quantum computing and quantum error correction
in mind. We begin by establishing a fundamental understanding of superconducting circuits and the
single-qubit realization of the inductive coupling mechanism. By an analogy to cavity optomechanics, we
motivate intuitive ways of understanding implications of the longitudinal coupling, as well as a drive-induced
Jaynes-Cummings interaction. We show by a change of reference frame that a sideband drive can induce
an on-demand transverse qubit-LR coupling, and then provide comprehensive numerical simulations to
validate this theory. Simulations of Flux arches, avoided level crossings and Rabi oscillations reproduce
published experimental data, confirming the single-qubit theory of the coupling. We then extend our scope
to a two-qubit system. Remaining in the sideband driven frame, a Schrieffer-Wolff transformation reveals a
mediated qubit-qubit coupling through the linear resonator. This is used to demonstrate an 𝑖SWAP gate in
simulations. We then return to the undriven system, where the cavity optomechanics perspective on the
device clearly motivates an all-to-all ZZ-coupling, again mediated by the linear resonator. A Lang-Firsov
transformation isolates this interaction and allows us to formulate the theory behind a multi-qubit parity gate,
native to the longitudinal architecture. This controlled duality between transverse and longitudinal coupling
mechanisms brings a powerful versatility to the system that could lead to important future applications.

Introduction 1
The year 2025 marks the 100th anniversary since the onset of the first
quantum revolution. A period where pioneers like Bohr, Schrödinger,
Heisenberg, Born, Dirac and many others laid the foundations of quantum
mechanics, fundamentally altering our understanding of the world
around us [1]. Since then, great minds have continued to develop on the
theory and push experimental verifications to unprecedented accuracies
[2].
A century later, we now find ourselves in the midst of what is often
referred to as the second quantum revolution [3]. Enabled by technological
advancements in high-speed electronics, precision sensing, sophisticated
micro-fabrication and ultra-low-temperature cryogenic environments,
we are now capable of controlling and manipulating quantum systems in
ways previously thought impossible. These advancements have led to an
emergence of new technologies in areas as quantum sensing, quantum
cryptography and quantum computing [4][5].
This work focuses on quantum computing, zooming in on the most
fundamental building blocks: the quantum bits (qubits) and interactions
between them.
A classical bit encodes one of two values, 0 or 1. A qubit sets out to do the
same, but this time encoding the binary values in the energy eigenstates
of a quantum mechanical two-level system, which we can refer to as |0⟩
and |1⟩. Since qubits behave according to the laws of quantum mechanics,
we can make use of the non-classical properties of superposition and
entanglement, forming the basis for quantum computing [6].
There is a variety of physical platforms which can realize such two-level
systems, each with their own advantages and challenges. Examples of
potential candidates include photons, semiconductor quantum dots,
trapped ions and superconducting circuits [7].
Here we focus our attention on a qubit architecture composed from
superconducting circuits.
1.1 Scope and Structure of Thesis
While the work presented here pushes towards quantum computing in
the bigger picture, it will be beyond the scope of the thesis to give a
thorough description of the advantages of quantum computing and the
necessary algorithms and error correction implications that come with it.
For more insight on this, consider [8][6].
The thesis starts off by giving a brief background on superconductivity
throughout the remainder of this chapter.
Then in Chapter 2 we formulate the Hamiltonian of the superconducting
qubit as a foundation for working with the specific device introduced
in Chapter 3. This device features an inductive coupling between a
superconducting qubit and a linear resonator, giving rise to the interesting
interaction mechanisms which form the basis of the remaining work.
In Chapter 4, the single-qubit theory studied in the preceding chapter

1.2 Introduction to Superconducting Circuits
To introduce superconducting circuits, we need to define a few key
concepts of superconductivity. The description here will remain mostly
qualitative, as the focus of this work does not lie in the nature of supercon-
ductivity itself, but rather in its applications. For a more comprehensive
treatment, see for example [10] and [11].
Now, the superconducting devices considered here will be cooled to very
low temperatures. This is not only to avoid thermal decoherence, but also
necessary to reach below the critical temperature where the materials we
work with become superconducting.
An interesting consequence of superconductivity is that as the critical
temperature is reached, electrons in the material pair up in so-called
Cooper pairs. As we know, each electron is a spin-1/2 Fermions, meaning
that the Cooper pairs effectively become integer-spin Bosons. The Cooper
pairs will then behave accordingly, meaning that the electron pairs can
now occupy the same states, which would otherwise be prohibited by
the Pauli exclusion principle. As such, a new ground state forms and
the Cooper pairs condense into the so-called BCS state (named after
researchers Bardeen, Cooper, and Schrieffer) [11].
The consequence of this is that each electron in the circuit no longer
has to be described by a distinct wavefunction, since all Cooper pairs
condense into the same BCS state.
Quite spectacularly, we now have a single macroscopic quantum state
describing the electron state throughout the entire superconductor. If we
can then carefully shield this state from the environment and engineer
its dynamics in clever ways, this can become the basis for our qubit.
This is exactly the field of circuit quantum electrodynamics (cQED), where
the macroscopical nature of the quantum state is leveraged to engineer
and fabricate tailored devices that interact with the BCS state, allowing
us to form qubit subspaces. In Chapter 2 we see how the Hamiltonian
of the system can be engineered by designing electrical circuits in the
superconducting material, i.e. circuits of capacitors, linear inductors and
a nonlinear inductor known as a Josephson Junction, which is introduced
below.

This chapter aims at deriving the Hamiltonian for a superconducting
qubit. We do this by starting from the well-known LC-circuit, seeing
how it behaves as a harmonic oscillator. Then to mitigate the issue of
equidistant energy-levels, we introduce a nonlinear inductor (Josephson
junction) to the system. This finally allows us to treat the circuit as a
two-level system - and thus a qubit.
The chapter is concluded in Section 2.3 by introducing a flux-tunable
variation of the Transmon qubit.

3.2 Drive-Induced Jaynes-Cummings
Interaction
One immediately interesting property of the longitudinal interaction de-
rived above is that the qubit and LR never exchange excitations, although
they are coupled to one another. This is in contrast to more conventional
superconducting qubit coupling mechanisms, where interactions are
mediated by capacitive couplings, giving rise to intrinsically transverse
interactions described by the Jaynes-Cummings (JC) model:
ˆHint/ℏ= 𝑔(ˆ𝑎ˆ𝑏† + ˆ𝑎† ˆ𝑏)
(3.15)
For more details on Jaynes-Cummings interactions, see App. B.2 and
textbooks, e.g. [17].
In this section, we show how a particular qubit drive, known as a
sideband drive, turns the intrinsic longitudinal interaction into an effective
transverse Jaynes-Cummings (JC) interaction. Thus, by applying this
specific drive tone, we can get an on-demand qubit-LR exchange of
excitations. Since the physical device design aims for a purely longitudinal
coupling, the parasitic capacitive qubit-LR coupling is negligible in the
absence of sideband drive (and it is far off-resonant as well). This, in turn,
suggests a controllable qubit-LR energy exchange with a very promising
ON/OFF ratio.

The single-qubit device offers plenty of rich physics to study, which we

In this chapter, we turn our attention to scaling the device to multiple
qubits. This is absolutely essential with a quantum computing perspective
in mind - requiring us to not only perform single-qubit gates, but also
performing gates across multiple coupled qubits. These multi-qubit gates
are used to establish entanglement, which can be leveraged through
carefully designed quantum algorithms to unlock the possibility of
solving certain problems that are otherwise infeasible to solve in a realistic
time. It will be beyond the scope of this thesis to give a full review of
quantum computing, but we establish here that multi-qubit gates are

5.3 Rabi Experiments
In the previous experiments, we first understood from the flux-arch that
the qubit is flux-sensitive. Then, an investigation of the avoided level
crossings verified that this gives rise to a transverse (Jaynes-Cummings)
qubit-LR coupling when driving the red sideband. Both of these ex-
periments relied on measuring the energy levels of the system, rather
than the actual state of the system at any particular time. Here we turn
to time-resolved experiments, stochastically probing the state through
measurable expectation values.
The following experiments study the drive-induced Rabi oscillations,
seeing coherent exchange of excitations between the qubit and the LR.
We first study the Rabi oscillations with a single excitation in the system,
and then proceed to run the experiment with two excitations. Finally,
we investigate how the full labframe Hamiltonian of the system aligns
with both the linearized model and experimental data, to emphasize the
validity of approximations made throughout Chapter 3. With this, we
can confidently proceed using the far more efficient linearized model
when studying multi-qubit systems in Section 5.5.

One approach would be, letting the system relax to its ground state,

is fair to assume a perfect initial state in the single-excitation manifold.

5.4 Labframe Investigations
So far, all simulations have been carried out using the linearized qubit-LR
Hamiltonian in the 2 × 2 qubit subspace. Despite the approximations
made in Chapter 3 to arrive at the linearized Hamiltonian, we saw
above that it captures all relevant dynamics of the experimental results
remarkably well. It also estimates the shifted qubit frequency thanks to
the AC-Stark shift approximation from [27].
In that sense the validity of the linearized model has already been verified,
but it will still prove quite instructive to step out of the displaced-, rotating
frame and take a look at the dynamics that have been discarded along
the way.

5.4.2 Leaving the Rotating Frame
We have now demonstrated that explicit sideband driving of the longitu-
dinal coupling yields the expected steady-state displacement of qubit-
and LR modes, giving rise to the sought after JC interaction. And by
driving adiabatically, we successfully avoid a non-steady-state response
of the circuit, which would otherwise cause rapid oscillations, obscuring
the underlying dynamics.
With this, we can step out of the rotating frame, entering the lab frame

Conclusion and Outlook 6
As the thesis comes to an end, we dedicate these sections to reflect on the
results obtained through our theoretical and numerical investigations,
and discuss their implications as well as potential directions for future
research.
Conclusion
We began by establishing a solid theoretical foundation by deriving the
Transmon Hamiltonian from circuit elements.
We then took to analyzing the longitudinal qubit-LR coupling, introduc-
ing an analogous cavity optomechanics system to build intuition on the
interaction. This analogy allowed us to understand the implications of
driving the red sideband and how it leads to an on-demand qubit-LR
Jaynes-Cummings interaction. Under the assumption of steady-state
driving, we entered a rotating displaced frame to obtain a linearized
model of the system, governing these induced transverse interactions.
This model became the subject of thorough simulations in the proceeding
work.
The single-qubit device was comprehensively simulated to verify the
sideband-induced interaction predicted by the linearized model. By
repeating a series of experiments from the paper, which proposed the
device [9], the linearized model was found to behave according to data.
In these experiments we simulated the flux-arch (revealing flux-sensitivity),
avoided level crossings (revealing qubit-LR coupling) and Rabi exper-
iments demonstrating sideband induced excitation swapping in the 1-
and 2-excitation manifold of JC ladder.
To further verify the linearized model, we compared it to the full labframe
Hamiltonian. This led to some interesting observations.
First, since the linearized model implicitly assumes the drive is in steady
state, discontinuous drives can be applied without concern. However,
when explicitly simulating the driven system, we found that smoother
pulses are necessary to avoid rapid oscillations that obscure the underly-
ing dynamics.
Next, an analysis of the RWA on drive- and interaction terms revealed
an unexpected increase in Rabi frequency of the driven qubit-LR inter-
action. We present no clear explanation to why this happens, but note
that it could both arise in equations if one would carry through the
counter-rotating terms, and that it could also stem from poor handling
of rapidly oscillating terms, as warned against in the numerical solver
documentation [41].
Lastly, making estimates of circuit parameters through a fit of the flux-
arch (simulated in charge/flux basis), we were able to compose a version
of the non-RWA labframe Hamiltonian including 6th-order corrections
to the Kerr term. This showed similar behavior to the linearized model,
despite the unresolved discrepancy in Rabi frequency.
==PAGE:71==
6 Conclusion and Outlook
67
The system was also scaled to multiple qubits inductively coupled to the
same LR.
Subject to dispersive sideband driving, we showed that the LR can
be approximately decoupled from the qubits using a Schrieffer-Wolff
transformation (SWT). The resulting qubit-qubit coupling then predicts
the ability to drive an 𝑖SWAP gate between two qubits. By carefully
matching drive parameters to realistic qubit parameters, we simulated
the 𝑖SWAP gate for different dispersive criteria, seeing the SWT breaking
down, effectively re-introducing the LR in the dynamics. The gate fidelity
remains unchecked, but we expect decent fidelities at reasonable gate
times, considering the expected parameters of future devices.
That concludes the findings of the simulations. As a final note on these,
especially in the labframe: They took a long time to run, despite using
multiprocessing. This posed a significant challenge in building an in-
tuition of the labframe dynamics discussed earlier. We strongly advice
future simulations to be run on a remote PC, or HPC cluster. The .ipynb
notebooks are submitted as an appendix to the thesis, see also App. D.
Lastly, we also applied the cavity optomechanics analogy to the undriven
multi-qubit system, finding that the longitudinal, radiation-pressure-
like, qubit-LR coupling causes the qubits to shift the frequencies of one
another. This intuition was followed by a Lang-Firsov transformation
(LFT), showing that the LR can be decoupled from the remaining system,
leaving an all-to-all ZZ interaction between the qubits. We then drew on
inspiration from literature to show how the state-dependent frequency
shift can be utilized to drive a multi-qubit parity gate. This also implies
the availability of a CNOT gate, and opens the door to considering
quantum error correction schemes in this longitudinal architecture.
We note, that with current device parameters, the state-dependent qubit
frequency shift, 𝜒, falls below the qubit linewidth, demanding improve-
ments in 𝑔0 and/or the qubit linewidth. There are good prospects of
overcoming this, however, with expected qubit parameters in future iter-
ations of the device reducing the linewidth and increasing the coupling
strength. We also propose investigating whether lowering the resonator
frequency, to increase 𝜒would be feasible, keeping thermal effects in
mind.
In the end, this work has demonstrated remarkable capabilities of the
longitudinal architecture provided by the inductive qubit-LR coupling.
We showed the versatility of the platform in theory and simulations,
enabling tunable couplings between multiple qubits, leading to native
transverse and longitudinal gates controlled by flux biases and applied
sideband drives.

A.2 Rotating-Wave Approximation
The rotating-wave approximation (RWA) is a very commonly used tool
in quantum mechanics, when dealing with interactions either between
multiple systems, or with drives, or as shown above it also applies
to systems interacting with themselves. It often becomes possible to
transform into a frame where a portion of these terms pick up a rapid
time-dependence. The approximation then most often comes down to an
argument claiming that contributions to the dynamics coming from the
rapidly oscillating terms will average out. Unfortunately the scope of the
work here will not permit us to go far into the details of this either. But
we note that the averaging out originates from integrating the Schrödinger
equation to find the time-evolution operator, giving the time-ordered
integral we also encounter in Eq. (B.4). And concluding this, we mention
that when applying the RWA, one has to consider what time-scales are
of interest. If you are only interested in evolving the system for a very
short time, then the fluctuations of terms that you might commonly drop
under the RWA suddenly play a non-negligible role.

the phenomenon known as hybridization.

system, since the energy splitting follows Eq. (B.13). For a finite coupling
strength 𝜆, the JC model tells us that the energy levels will never truly
cross at Δ = 0, but rather have a finite splitting of 2ℏ𝜆√𝑛. This is exactly
what is observed in the experiments of Section 5.1 and 5.2, where we are
effectively sweeping Δ through the external magnetic flux and sideband
drive, respectively.

B.3 Semi-classical Nature of the Drive
The approach to modeling the drive in Section 3.2.2 and elaborated on in
App. B.1 above, couples a classical variable, 𝑉𝑑(𝑡), to our quantum system
described by the (ˆ𝑎, ˆ𝑎†)-mode. Hence, this is referred to as a semi-classical
drive. The true nature of the system is of course fully quantum, and the
semi-classical treatment is just an approximation. The approximation
turns out to be very convenient and accurate when driving with states of
light that behave in a way we may deem classical. To see this, consider the
fully quantum description as a Jaynes-Cummings interaction between
the qubit mode (ˆ𝑎, ˆ𝑎†), and the field driven into the drive-line described
by (ˆ𝑏, ˆ𝑏†):
ˆHint ∝(ˆ𝑎ˆ𝑏† + ˆ𝑎† ˆ𝑏)
Now, since the drive field is prepared as a classical state of light, it is
very well-approximated by a large coherent state |𝛽⟩. In doing so, we can
displace the state by its coherent amplitude, or conversely displace the
operators as follows:
ˆHint ∝(ˆ𝑎(ˆ𝑏† + 𝛽★) + ˆ𝑎†(ˆ𝑏+ 𝛽)) = (ˆ𝑎ˆ𝑏† + ˆ𝑎† ˆ𝑏) + (ˆ𝑎𝛽★+ ˆ𝑎†𝛽)
This displacement follows the same intuition, discussed in Section 3.2.3.
The semi-classical description is then achieved by dropping the first
term here, thus ignoring the quantum nature of the drive field. This is
reasonable since the amplitude of the coherent state is large relative to
the quantum fluctuations described by (ˆ𝑏, ˆ𝑏†). This is intuitive from the
phase-space representation of the coherent state seen in Figure B.1. Since
the coherent state fluctuations are independent of the amplitude, driving
a large |𝛽|, makes the uncertainties appear much smaller, relative to the
amplitude. This leaves:
ˆHint ∝(ˆ𝑎𝛽★+ ˆ𝑎†𝛽) = |𝛽|(ˆ𝑎𝑒𝑖𝜃+ ˆ𝑎†𝑒−𝑖𝜃),
where the polar form of 𝛽∈ℂmakes it clear that the drive-strength
is proportional to the coherent state amplitude on the drive side, map-
ping |𝛽| →𝜖𝑑(𝑡). Meanwhile, the drive frequency enters through time-
dependency of the phase, mapping 𝜃→𝜔𝑑𝑡+ 𝜃0. As such, the form
of the semi-classical drive has been recovered, starting from the fully
quantum description under the assumption that the drive is in a classical
state (i.e. a large coherent state).

Introduction to the Lindblad
Master Equation C
To simulate the Rabi experiments in Section 5.3, it is no longer sufficient
to simply construct the Hamiltonian numerically and solve for the eigen
energies. Due to the time-resolved nature of the experiment, we have to
compute the time-evolution of an initial state |𝜓(𝑡= 0)⟩, as it evolves into
|𝜓(𝑡= 𝜏)⟩, subjected to ˆH(𝑡). This involves solving the Time-Dependent
Schrödinger Equation (TDSE) for the initial value problem starting at
|𝜓(𝑡= 0)⟩. The TDSE is essentially a set of coupled ordinary differential
equations (ODEs), and can be solved using any numerical ODE solver,
e.g. scipy.integrate.solve_ivp.
The above is sufficient when studying closed quantum systems, i.e. the
ideal case where our qubit-LR system would not spuriously interact with
the environment. To reproduce the experimental results from [9], we
need to consider the inevitable losses to the environment causing the
Rabi oscillations to die out as excitations leave the system.
To model the full system including the system-environment interaction,
you can think of expanding the Hamiltonian as follows:
𝐻tot = 𝐻sys + 𝐻env + 𝐻int,
where the three terms govern the system, environment, and system-
environment interaction, respectively. From here you want to solve for
the time-dynamics again, but this time generalizing the problem to
density operators rather than state vectors, to include the option of
describing mixed states. If you let 𝜌tot = 𝜌⊗𝜌env, the TDSE equivalent
for the density operators is the Von Neumann equation [41]:

into a mixed state, due to the decoherence mechanisms of the interaction.

C Introduction to the Lindblad Master Equation
80
The Lindblad master equation will not be derived here, but there are
a few key assumptions from the derivation that one should keep in
mind [41]:
▶Separability: The initial state is not entangled with the environment,
i.e. 𝜌tot(0) = 𝜌(0) ⊗𝜌env(0) is a product state.
• In our case, we have no need to consider the system initially
entangled with the environment.
▶Born approximation: Assumes that the change in the environment
due to interaction with the system is negligible, i.e. making the
state of the environment time-independent. This is equivalent to
treating the environment as a large bath, weakly coupled to the
system.
• The environment here being the surrounding chip and cryostat
can safely be treated as a large bath. And since the device
is intentionally designed to have as large coherence times
as possible, the coupling coupling to the environment is
minimized.
▶Markov approximation: Assumes a memory-less bath. You can
think of an excitation being lost to the environment. For a short
time it will still be coupling back to the system, but here we assume
that it couples further out into the environment at a much faster
time-scale than any interactions within the system. In that way,
seen from the system perspective, the bath appears invariant to
any previous interactions.
• Considering the environment a large, weakly coupled bath,
it is extremely unlikely that a lost excitation couples back
into the qubit-LR system. Hence the bath can be treated as
memory-less.
▶Secular approximation: Assumes that rapidly oscillating terms
of the Hamiltonian can be neglected, as well as neglecting terms
leading to small energy-level corrections.
• This assumption is applicable when using the linearized
model here, since counter-rotating and non-linear terms have
already been dropped when deriving the drive-induced JC
interaction.
• When studying the lab frame Hamiltonian in Section 5.4 on
the other hand, the secular approximation will have to be
re-considered.
There are multiple ways to derive the Lindblad master equation, see for
example [17].
