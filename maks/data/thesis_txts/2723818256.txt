Abstract
The enzyme L-asparaginase converts the amino acid L-asparagine into L-aspartate and
ammonia. This ability is used to starve leukemic cells during chemotherapy and to mitigate
the formation of acrylamide, a probable human carcinogen, in food during cooking. L-
asparaginase is produced with filamentous fungi, though for medical purposes, it is primarily
obtained from the bacteria Escherichia coli and Dickeya dadantii. This is despite the higher
efficiency of fungal secretory systems for enzyme production and secretion. It is, therefore,
desirable to attain an effective fungal host to produce recombinant L-asparaginase for medical
use. For this purpose, the L-asparaginase overproducing Aspergillus oryzae strain ORY26
has been constructed, which has a recombinant homologous L-asparaginase (AOahtA) in its
genome.
To improve the protein secretion of ORY26 the morphology related genes for the putative
chitin synthases Chs7, the cell wall remodeling Gul-I, and the polarisome component SpaA,
were knocked out. Single-, double-, and triple knock out mutants were obtained through Cas9
induced homologous recombination repair using oligomer donor fragments. The resulting
morphological mutants were investigated for phenotypical changes in both macro- and
microscopic scale. As it has been observed in other species, gul-I was believed to give a
hyperbranched expression, while spaA seemingly resulted in thicker hyphal elements. Little
to no effect on morphology was found for chs7 disruption.
During 1 liter bioreactor fermentation experiments, morphological mutants of ∆gul-I, ∆gul-
I ∆spaA, and ∆chs7 ∆gul-I ∆spaA genotypes, were able to obtain significantly higher
concentrations of extracellular protein (up to 180.5% increase) and L-asparaginase activity
(up to 124.9% higher) compared to their parent strain. This was observed without a clear
decrease in their ability to proliferate. Deletion of these morphological genes is concluded to
result in prominent candidates for improved production of L-asparaginase.

Introduction
Filamentous fungi produce a plethora of enzymes that are used industrially in the production
of food and feed, pulp and paper, textiles, detergents, beverages, and biofuels [1, 2]. They
also possess the ability to secrete a variety of secondary metabolites that are either potentially
harmful mycotoxins, such as ochratoxin and aflatoxin [3, 4], or of desirable character for
biotechnology-, food-, or pharmaceutical purposes, which yearly results in a billion dollar
market [5]. Due to their posttranslational capabilities, as well their natural ability to secrete
proteins, filamentous fungi are sometimes to be preferred for enzymatic production compared
to the more well known bacteria, Escherichia coli, and the yeast Saccharomyces cerevisiae
[6, 7]. It is estimated that 2.2 to 3.8 million fungal species exist of which only 120,000 novel
species are currently accepted [8]. With so many species yet to be described, a great potential
of both enzymatic and metabolic character is yet to be utilized, and one of the ways to
explore this potential is through genetic engineering [9].
1.1
Aspergillus oryzae
One of the fungi that play an important industrial role is Aspergillus oryzae. It has traditionally
been used for production of fermented foods, including sake, miso, and shoyu. Due to A.
oryzae’s enzyme secretion abilities and its Generally Regarded As Safe (GRAS) status it is
also used to produce a variety of enzymes such as proteases, α-amylase, lipase, and, as the
focus of this report, asparaginases [10, 11].
A. oryzae belongs to the subsection of aspergillic species known as Flavi. These include
species such as the aflatoxin producing A. flavus and A. parasiticus, as well as the industrially
relevant A. sojae [12, 13]. A. oryzae has only been isolated from industrial factories and is
considered to be a domesticated form of A. flavus, though despite this relation, A. oryzae is
not considered to produce aflatoxins [12, 13, 14]. A. oryzae produces conidiophores which,
depending on the isolate, are either biseriate with both a metulae- and a phialide layer, as
seen in Figure 1, or uniseriate and only covered with phialides [12, 14]. The hyphal cells
are multinucleate, and, unlike some other aspergillic species, the spores of A. oryzae tend
to be as well. These spores have the possibility to contain four or more nuclei in a single
conidium with two nuclei being the most common [15, 16]. Spores that are multinucleate
germinate faster than those that are of uninucleate origin [15]. Though this may result in
shortened lag phases for fermentation purposes, it may complicate the exact classification of
the micromorphological growth of the morphological mutants that this report will focus on.

1.2
Asparaginase
An enzyme of industrial interest is L-asparaginase (EC 3.5.1.1) which catalyzes the hydrolysis
of the amino acid L-asparagine to L-aspartate and ammonia. Asparaginase has long been
known to be widely spread within all matters of life, consequently several potential industrial
candidates exist [17]. Enzymes of this type are widely used in both the food industry
(products include PreventASeTM from DSM and Acrylaway® from Novozymes A/S)[18]
and in medicine for chemotherapy treatment of leukemia (products include Medac® and
Crasnitin®) [19].
1.2.1
Asparaginase to Mitigate Acrylamide
In the food industry, asparaginase is used to reduce the levels of acrylamide in food products.
Acrylamide is formed predominantly from free asparagine reacting with reducing sugars
through Maillard reactions when certain foods, such as potato chips and bread are fried,
baked, roasted, or toasted at temperatures above 120°C [20, 21]. Acrylamide is classified as a
neurotoxin, as well as a probable human carcinogen and for this reason the European Food
Safety Authority and the World Health Organization have issued regulation of the compound
in certain foods [21, 22]. Asparaginase is used to reduce the levels of free asparagine prior
to cooking, thereby reducing the formation of acrylamide in the finished food products [18].
This reaction can be seen in Figure 2. Pretreating potatoes with commercially available
asparaginase before frying has been found to reduce the concentration of acrylamide with up
to 92% [23]. Many of the asparaginases used for acrylamide mitigation are produced using
aspergillic species including A. oryzae [18, 24]. This further emphasizes A. oryzae’s abilities

1.2.2
Asparaginase in Leukemia Treatment
Asparaginase is also used in the treatment of Acute Lymphoblastic Leukemia (ALL), which
is a type of cancer that affects the bone marrow involved with lymphocyte production.
Leukemia is one of the most common types of cancer to affect children. In the United States
(US) leukemia corresponds to 13-28% of all malignant neoplasm in children between 0 and 14
years, of which 75% are ALL [25]. In patients with ALL the cancerous cells are incapable
of producing asparagine from aspartate. As the amino acid is necessary for their survival
and growth they rely on external supplies from the blood stream. By using asparaginase to
break down asparagine in the blood stream, the cancerous cells are thereby deprived of this
essential nutrient stopping their protein synthesis as seen in Figure 3. This eventually leads
to cell death and the reduction of tumor growth. This conversion does not affect healthy cells
that are capable of endogenous asparagine synthesize using asparagine synthetase [19, 26].
Figure 3: The effect on leukemic cells as a result of asparaginase driven asparagine starvation.
Healthy cells absorbe the aspartate and convert it back to asparagine, using the enzyme asparagine
synthetase, that can be used for protein synthesis. ALL cells lacking this enzyme, enter starvation and
eventually cell death. Adapted from Van Den Berg, 2011 [19] made with illustrating tool Biorender.

Since its medical approval by the US Food and Drug Administration (FDA) in 1978 and
the introduction of asparaginase treatment of ALL patients, the complete remission rate has
increased from 20% to 80-90% [26, 27].
Although asparaginase is found in all matters of life, the variants used for ALL treatment are
derived from the bacteria Escherichia coli or Dickeya dadantii (formerly known as Erwinia
chrysanthemi) [19]. Depending on the species, these can obtain a yield of asparaginase between
10 and 38 g/L. The enzyme is found within the cytoplasmic or periplasmic environment
meaning a subsequent lysis is required for purification [28, 29].
Side effects of asparaginase treatment may occur due to development of hypersensitivity
resulting in anaphylaxis, edema, serum sickness, bronchospasm, urticaria and rash, itching
and swelling of extremities, and erythema [26], but events such as pancreatitis, hyperglycemia,
and coagulation disorder may also occur [30]. To circumvent this, a PEGylated version of
the enzyme has been approved by the FDA, which both extents its half-life in humans and
lowers the side effects in terms of immunological responses [27]. Should the patient still
be required to stop therapy because of serious side effects, an increased chance of tumor
relapse is observed and for this reason the search for novel, effective, and safe asparaginase
enzymes is essential [27]. Due to its efficient secretory system, and GRAS status, A. oryzae is
considered an obvious candidate for production of recombinant asparaginase with decreased
requirements for downstreamprocessing.
1.3
Fungal Secretory Pathway
Filamentous fungi possess a great potential for industrial processes due to their natural ability
to secrete chemicals and enzymes to their surroundings. If this secretory pathway is exploited
it makes them prominent host for recombinant protein production [31, 6, 7].
The secretion of protein primarily follows the Classical Protein Secretion (CPS) pathway
as seen in Figure 4. It starts when the mRNA is bound to the ribosome and translation
is initiated. If the emerging peptide sequence contains an Endoplasmic Reticulum (ER)
signal peptide, which the Signal Recognition Particle (SRP) recognizes, it will bind to the
nascent protein and lead it to the ER. The translation is then finalized into the ER lumen
and the protein is folded and may be modified by glycosylation [32]. The maturing proteins
are then transported in vesicles from the ER to the Golgi apparatus. In the Golgi they
are matured before being transported further to the hyphal tip known as the Spitzenkörper
[33, 34]. This region is densely populated with vesicles containing enzymes for secretion, but
also components and materials for building new cell wall to extent the hyphae. Along this
path vesicles are transported on the microtubule cytoskeleton by the motor-protein kinesin
from the ER to the Spitzenkörper. At the Spitzenkörper the vesicles are then transported all
the way to the membrane to fuse with the membrane releasing its contents. This transport is
facilitated by the motor-protein myosin, on the dynamic structure of actin filaments [7, 33].

If the cell starts accumulating high levels of unfolded protein it may activate the Unfolded
Protein Response (UPR) or ER-Associated protein Degradation (ERAD). Unfolded protein
may accumulate as a result of physiological conditions, but also in the case of overexpression,
as protein folding enzymes cannot keep up. UPR functions by activating the transcription
factor Hac1 mRNA as a response to the high concentration of unfolded protein within the
ER lumen. This allows for translation of Hac1, which then leads to transcription of genes
encoding protein-folding catalysts, that folds the proteins. ERAD on the other hand functions
by marking the unfolded proteins, that are then dislocated by the ERAD complex to the
cytosol for degradation [7, 32].
1.4
Fungal Strain Engineering
As may be apparent, the secretory pathway of filamentous fungi is a complex system with
multiple steps from nucleus to hyphal tip, which may limit the protein secretion. Traditionally,
strategies of improving the secretion include fermentation process optimization and random
mutagenesis. In addition to these it may be preferential to obtain rationally genetically
engineered strains, as exploitation of the cells own systems may result in a highly overproducing
strain [7].
Multiple strategies and targets exist to promote secretion of proteins. These may include a
change of promoter to a more efficient one to increase transcription, replacement of signal
peptide and codon optimization for increased translation, inducing protease deficiency to

transfer. This less dispersed growth is also less susceptible to shear stress [31].
1.4.1
HR and NHEJ
Homologous Recombination (HR) and Non-Homologous End Joining (NHEJ) are the two
main strategies used by cells to repair Double Strand Break (DSB) of DNA and can be seen
in Figure 5.
HR is a precise repair mechanism that is used when a donor DNA with similar sequences is
available to act as a template for DSB repair. HR is a complex process that requires several
enzymes and proteins to work together. HR involves the exchange of DNA sequences found
between two homolog regions. The damaged DNA strand is cut, and a homologous sequence
on the undamaged donor DNA is used as a template to repair the break. In nature this results
in the restoration of the damaged strand to the sequence found in the donor DNA, giving
two exact copies of the region. In molecular biology this trait is exploited by introducing

the sequence of the donor DNA to mend the DSB, resulting in an exact copy of the donor strand.

On the other hand, NHEJ is a less precise and more error-prone repair mechanism that
is used when there is no homolog that can be used as repair template. It functions by
initial formation of a complex between the Ku70/Ku80 heterodimer and the DNA-dependent

together. This repair mechanism may result in deletions or insertion at the site of repair,
which can lead to mutations, though it does not necessarily render the gene unusable [9, 38].
To more efficiently utilize the HR for gene knock out or insertion it is advantageous to
produce a NHEJ deficient strain. The host is then required to use the provided DNA repair
template for HR, as it will otherwise perish. For filamentous fungi a NHEJ deficient strains
is often obtained by deletion of ku70 gene rendering the host unable to form the Ku70/Ku80
heterodimer [37, 39]
1.4.2
CRISPR Cas9
To induce the site directed DSB in a filamentous host, it is common in academia to use
the CRISPR-Cas9 [39, 9]. Cas9 is a powerful genome editing tool that utilizes a bacterial
adaptive immune system for RNA-guided endonuclease activity. It consists of the Cas9
nuclease and a Single-Guide RNA (sgRNA). The sgRNA is composed of a CRISPR RNA
(crRNA), which contains a short target sequence typically composed of 20 nucleotides, and the
TRans-Activating crRNA (tracrRNA) that activates crRNA-guided DNA cleavage. When the
sgRNA is formed, it can lead the Cas9 nuclease to the complementary sequence of the crRNA
to induce a DSB of the DNA. Besides the target sequence, the Cas9 requires a protospacer
adjacent motif (PAM) right downstream of the target sequence on the unbound strand. The
PAM sequence need to be 5’-NGG-3’ to efficiently cleave the target DNA. The cleavage will
be induced 3 Base Pairs (bp) upstream of the PAM and will leave a blunt end DSB [40].
CRISPR-Cas9 is originally used by bacteria to defend against invading viruses by cutting
their DNA, but has been adapted for use in genome editing by repurposing the crRNA target
sequence to target specific DNA sequences of interest. In this way a specific locus within the
genome can be selected for DSB, allowing for easy utilization of HR for genetic recombination
[9].

1.4.3
USER Cloning and Multiplexing
To effieciently utilize the Cas9 induced HR DSB repair approach, for insertion and removal
of genetic material, it is convenient to use a vector based system for multiplexing [41, 42].
Multiplexing is a method where the Cas9 nuclease is given multiple sgRNA that can target
several genes within the host and/or multiple sites within the same gene. This increases
the chance of recombination at the locus of interest, as correct DSB is not reliant on the
specificity and affinity of a single sgRNA [42].
A vector system of this character can be assembled through USER cloning [43]. This technique
utilizes a plasmid containing a USER cassette consisting of a PacI restriction site which is
flanked by a CG and a AT base pair and two BbvCI restriction sites. By digesting the PacI
site and nicking the the backbone at the BbvCI sites, the cassette is opened up linearizing the
plasmid and creating two distinct 8 nucleotide overhangs. PCR fragments, which have been
amplified using uracil containing primers, can then be digested with an uracil excising enzyme
to create complimentary overhangs permitting insertion into the cassette [43]. Depending on
the number of target sites, several PCR fragments, with each their complimentary overhang
can be assembled and inserted to the vector as seen in Figure 6 [42].
Figure 6: USER cloning of PCR fragments containing promoter, tRNA, sgRNA, and terminator
into an open vector. Overhangs are marked with different colored tails. This gives a vector with the
cas9 gene, AMA1 for amplification within fungal host, selection marker, and gene encoding tRNA
and sgRNA. Adapted from Nødvig et al., 2018 [42] and Nour-Eldin et al., 2010 [43].

1.5
Pellet formation of Filamentous Fungi During Fermentation
An often-used mode of cultivation when employing filamentous fungi for production is
submerged fermentation (SmF). During SmF the fungal host is innoculated into a liquid
media which is colonized and the enzymes are secreted into the surroundings. When cultivated
this way, the filamentous growth will proliferate either as dispersed mycelium, clumps, or
pellets [31]. These structures may occur as a consequence of the inoculum, as some spores
have a tendency to form coagulative clusters which can give rise to agglomerate hyphal growth,
while others will not cluster the same way and lead to dispersed growth [35]. Clumps and
pellets of growth may arise throughout the entirety of the fermentation process due to different
parameters related to the process and not only as a consequence of spore accumulation [35, 31].
While accumulated pellet growth results in increased oxygen transfer and allows for better
mixing, it is considered to result in lowered productivity of enzymes compared to dispersed
growth. This is a result of the fact that the main part of enzymes are secreted at the hyphal
tips meaning that it is favourable to have the maximal number of hyphal tips compared to
the biomass [35]. During pelleted growth the pellets are only able to grow to a certain size
before the center is limited by the nutrients dispersal as depicted in Figure 8. If the pellet
is to exceed this diameter the activity in the center will cease and cells will start to lyse,
while only its growing surface will be active [44, 45]. These zones will vary depending on

the diffusion and concentration of individual nutrients, density of the pellet, and the specific
uptake rate of the substrate [45]. For these reasons, it is often attempted to avoid pelleted

1.5.1
Target Genes
The genes that will be targeted for deletion in this project are the chitin synthase ChsC
[46], the cell wall remodeling protein Gul-I [47], and the polarisome component SpaA [48].
When deleting chsC in A. niger, a decrease in culture viscosity was observed, as well as a
42.6% increase in citric acid production [46], while disruption of the ortholog in A. nidulans
has been observed to enlarge the hyphal tips and lead to a degree of branching without
showing signs of lysis [49]. gul-I encodes a mRNA-binding translational regulator involved
with transcripts of cell wall remodeling proteins [50]. In Neurospora crassa it has been shown
to increase viscosity when overexpressed and its disruption was observed to decrease viscosity
while also increasing protein secretion by 25% and giving a hyperbranched structure [47].
Similarly increased lateral branching was observed when disrupting gul-I in Trichoderma
reesei as well as an increase of 22% in cellulase production [51]. SpaA is localized at the
hyphal tip and its deletion results in lowered growth rate, increased hyphal diameter, polarity
defects, and a hyper branched phenotype in A. niger [48].
Deletion of the ortholog variants in A. oryzae is believed to have the potential of giving the
same hyperbranched phenotypical structures, lowered culture broth viscosity, and increased
protein secretion. Obtaining strains with combined deletions of chsC, gul-I, and spaA may
lead to further increased branching and secretory abilities. Such morphological mutants

1.6
ORY7 and ORY26
This report will focus on the two genetically engineered A. oryzae RIB40 strains ORY7 [37]
and ORY26 [52]. Their description can be seen in Table 24 Appendix A.6). ORY7 is a ku70
and pyrG deficient strain with a COmmon Synthetic gene Integration (COSI) site for simple
insertion of a GOI using two 500 bp flanking regions for homologous recombination. The
COSI contains the E. coli β-glucuronidase uidA as a reporter gene and is controlled by the
A. nidulans gpdA promoter and trpC terminator. When grown on X-Gluc containing media
ORY7 will cleave the marker, making it blue, while strains with successful insertion of GOI
into the COSI will not give a respons [37].
ORY26 is an ORY7-derived strain in which the gene identified by FungiDB ascension number
AO090102000492 (AOapnA), which has a predicted role in cellular amino acid metabolic
process, has been deleted. This was observed to make the host unable to metabolize asparagine.
Furthermore, the putative L-asparaginase AO090005000816 (AOahtA) has been integrated
into the COSI site under the control of A. nidulans gpdA promoter and trpC terminator for
overexpression [52].
The aim of this project will be to create single, double, and triple knock out mutant strains of
the homolog A. oryzae genes gul-I and spaA, as well as the putative chitin synthase chs7 to
obtain increased secretion. This is do be done for both the COSI containing ORY7 and the
asperaginase overproducing ORY26. These strains are to be investigated for the gene deletion
effect on morphology on both the macro- and microscopic level. Furthermore, they are going
to be tested for their protein and enzyme secretory abilities in small scale experiments using
a static plate cultivation technique and 1 L bioreactors.

Discussion
4.1
Obtaining morphological mutants
First thing in terms of discussion is the confusion of the chsC from CAoGD and the chsC
of FungiDB. This problem arises as these databases likely do not exchange information and
the same gene may share name with genes of separate origin or be named differently. Since
the FungiDB accessed chsC of A. niger, which is the ortholog of chs7, encodes a putative
chitin synthase it was believed to be the one described by Sun et al. [46]. Even though chs7
in A. oryzae is a putative chitin synthase, its deletion could not be observed to give similar
morphological effect to that described in A. niger by Sun et al. [46] or in A. nidulans by
Borgia et al. [49]. The exact function of this putative chitin synthase is likely different but
may become apparent given the right circumstances.
The chs7 and spaA targeting plasmid pAC2066 was observed to have a mismatch in the
expected sequence for the chs7 target sequence, which is likely why ORY47 and ORY56 could
not be obtained using this vector. It is also thought to be the reason for the unexpected BspEI
restriction pattern, as one of the restriction sites was located there. Since the transformations
without a donor fragment did not result in any growth, it suggests that the spaA targeting
sgRNAs were functional and the observed colonies were likely spaA deficient, though this was
not tested. When constructing the double mutants ORY47 and ORY56, and the triple mutant
ORY58, by pAC2062 induced chs7 deletion, a transformation without a pyrG encoding vector
should have been performed as negative control. If plates without donor fragments did show
growth, a negative plate would help explain whether this was a result of inefficient target
sequences.
The chs7 targeting pAC2062 vector is believed to be functioning as expected, despite
unexpected growth when transformed into ORY7, as all other transformations using this
plasmid required the donor fragment. The observed growth is believed to be a result of an
inaccurate addition of the donor fragment, allowing it to repair the DSB and proliferate.
In addition to this, it may be beneficial to use a fixed concentration of the bioblocks for
the volume for the USER cloning, as this ensures equal ratio and more accurate assembly.
Different tRNA coding sgRNA interspacers may also be useful to avoid removal of ectopic
recombination in the bacterial host. For transformation, it would be useful to standardize
the concentration of protoplast, to more easily assess the efficiency of the applied target
sequences. As the host strains were known to be able to utilize the vector system and the
primary goal was to obtain the strains of interest, this was omitted.
It was only possible to obtain the triple mutant ORY51 using the single target triple deletion
vectors pAC2068 and pAC2069. This is believed to be as the individual crRNAs have a more
limited chance of success to induce DSB in all three genes. As the TAPE plates contained no
growth, the plasmids has likely been successful in inducing at least one DSB. This makes the
practicality of efficient multiplexing apparent [41].

4.2
Change in Morphology
The macromorphology was clearly observed to be affected by the deletion of gul-I and spaA,
while little to no effect could be found for chs7 deletion. Deletion of this gene may not
have effect under the given conditions, but could still influence growth in other unknown
circumstances. Especially gul-I disruption was found to decrease sporulation, which may be
as the colony is not spreading as wide as it would otherwise. All strains were found to reach
approximately the same colony size on all MM with different carbon sources. Of the MM,
pectin was found to give the most sporulated colonies while standard MM gave the least. On
MM containing starch, all strains were observed to produce a clearing zone indicating that
they secreted hydrolytic enzymes for starch degradation. Despite of the decreased colony size
for gul-I and spaA mutants a broad clear zone was also observed for these.
It cannot be determined from the macromorphology whether the deletions lead to hyper-
branching. It is, however, speculated that the decrease in radial growth of gul-I mutants on
YES is a direct consequence of hyperbranching. This is thought to be the case, as the relative
colony size difference was removed when disrupting it. A hyperbranched phenotype could
give similar results, as more hyphal tips are produced rather than extending the existing.
Sclerotia were observed for gul-I and spaA mutants when cultivated on CYA. This was
thought to be as their deletion induces stress, but these genes may have an unknown function
in repression of sclerotia formation. These structures have been observed for A. oryzae, but
as of yet it has not been found to transition into the teleomorph reproductive ascomata [14].
For the micromorphology the pictures suggest that gul-I deletion leads to increased hyphal
branching, while spaA deletion gives enlarged hyphal branches as observed in other species
[47, 48]. To properly determine this it may be necessary to make sure only a single spore is
inoculated into each well to avoid the problem of overlapping hyphal elements. This would
require a change in method as the spore suspensions with estimated concentrations cannot
confidently supply individual spores. As A. oryzae also produces multinucleate spores that
germinate faster than the mononucleate counterparts [15], a larger sample may be needed
to correctly encompass the full scope of growth within the 12 to 16 hour period. It is also
not known whether deletion of these genes changes the frequency of obtaining multinucleate
spores. None of the mutant strains were seemingly found to be limited when cultivated in the
microtiter plate. This suggests that deletion of the genes is not compromising their growth.
For the micromorphology it will also be necessary to settle on what can be considered different
morphologies. The mutants would have to be differentiated in parameters such as branching
frequency, branching angles, hyphal thickness, and total length of hyphal elements. As the
method leads to distinctly stained hyphae on a dark background, this may be obtainable
using an image analysis program.

4.3
Static plate cultivation
The static plate cultivation is an efficient way to gain discover the possible effects of gene
deletions [54, 55]. The resolution is considerably lower than the bioreactor experiments,
since these were only sampled daily and could not be sampled for CO2 and biomass during
the cultivation. Other mutants than the selected ORY53, ORY57, and ORY58 may show
reasonable results between sampling, but these enzymes may be denatured at time of sampling,
as was observed to be the case for late samples of the bioreactor fermentations. It is likely
better to do sampling in the period between 48 to 72 hours, if the experiment was to be
repeated, to check for increased secretion of asparaginase in other mutants. This is as both
protein concentration and activity are generally observed to increase at this point, but the
peak may lie between these time points.
Even though the ORY26 was observed to have a higher activity per protein mass at 72 hours
than all mutants, it should be noted that this value did not exceed any of the mutants at
48 hours. This further suggests that the enzyme had been denatured. The steep protein
concentration differences, between the asparaginase overproducing chs7 gul-I deficient ORY55,
chs7 spaA deficient ORY56, and chs7 gul-I spaA deficient ORY58 in comparison to their non-
overproducing equivalents ORY48, ORY49, and ORY51, is curious, as these were expected to
have equal secretory abilities. It could be because more energy is invested into asparaginase
production, as most asparaginase overproducing strains were observed to have secreted less
than their none overproducing variants at both 48 and 72 hours. It is also noticed that the
relationship between these strains is the shared chs7 deficiency in combination with both
or either gul-I and/or spaA deficiency. This may highlight an effect of chs7 deletion in
combination with other morphology related genes.
4.4
Bioreactor experiments
When the asparaginase overproducing strains ORY26, gul-I deficient ORY53, gul-I and spaA
deficient ORY57, and chs7, gul-I and spaA deficient ORY58 were cultivated in the bioreactor,
all four strains were observed to have similar, specific CO2 growth rate on starch, maltose
and, during the secondary exponential phase, presumably on glucose. The length of the
exponential phases and the biomass growth rate are also perceived to be similar, with the
exception of ORY53. This all suggests that despite deletion of these morphology related genes,
their ability to proliferate under the given process conditions is not limited, contrasting what
was observed when grown on solid media. This could indicate a hyperbranched phenotype, as
these would be limited in colony size on solid media, but not when supplied with nutrients in
a well mixed liquid fermentation. The simplest way to investigate this would be to withdraw
biomass for microscopic analysis.

4.5
Further Work
As the strains ORY53, ORY57, and ORY58 have been observed to result in a higher
concentration of protein without limiting its growth, more energy can be directed towards
increasing the ratio of asparaginase. Numerous strategies to obtain increased overexpression
exist, including codon optimization of the transcript, multiple copies within the genome,
possible exchange of the constitutive gdpA promotor with a stronger alternative, and as
mentioned the folding capacity may be increased to avoid ERAD induced degradation [7]. It
may also be possible to obtain even higher secretion through additional deletion of morphology
genes, such as the intended chitin synthase, or arfA and racA [57, 58].
As the asparaginase activity in the extracellular environment during the bioreactor experiment
was observed to decrease shortly after the exponential phase, it is likely denaturing. It is
not known what leads to this denaturation, but it may be countered by enhancing the
enzyme’s stability, deletion of extracellular proteases, or a possible combination of the two.
If the enzyme is to be used for treatment of ALL, it is likely required to undergo structural
changes to improve half-life within the human bloodstream [59]. One of the asparaginase
overproducing morphological mutants may prove an efficient cell factory to produce cancer
reducing enzymes if the yield can be improved through engineering of protein structure
improved overexpression.
Since gul-I and spaA deletion leads to a decreased colony diameter their fruiting zones are not
formed as quickly. Plates need to be incubated for longer to sporulate, and more plates are
needed to obtain a sufficient number of spores for bioreactor inoculation. This may become
problematic when preparing spore suspensions in larger scale production. By placing the
genes under the control of an inducible promotor, they could be switched on when needed
to cover large areas for sporulation and switched off when required to form hyperbranched
growth for increased protein secretion.
The ORY7 derived morphological mutants may also be utilized for production and improve-
ment of other enzymes as they have a site prepared for insertion. If this goal is pursued it may
be interesting to view the results relative to those found for the asparaginase overproducing
strains to explore possible similarities.

5
Conclusion
It was possible to obtain morphological mutants with singular and combined deletion of the
putative chitin synthase chs7, as well as the ortholog gul-I and spaA genes in the fungal host
A. oryzae. These mutants were all found to proliferate on solid media, and the deletion of
gul-I and spaA was found to reduce the colony diameter. The deletion of chs7 was found to
have little to no morphological effect under the given parameters, even when combined with
deletion of gul-I and spaA.
While it is not possible to conclude whether deletion of these genes resulted in a different
branching frequency, it was found that the combined staining and microscopy method, with
the right optimizations and parameter settings, would be useful to determine a difference in
branching in early growth.
During one liter bioreactor experiments, the gul-I deficient ORY53, gul-I spaA deficient
ORY57, and chs7 gul-I spaA deficient ORY58 were all found to secrete significantly higher
concentrations of protein than their parent strain ORY26. ORY53 and ORY58 were also
observed to achieve more than double the observed asparaginase activity of ORY26. Further-
more, ORY57 and ORY58 were found to have a higher activity per secreted protein of 180.5
and 143.6 percent respectively. It is apparent that the deletion of gul-I in A. oryzae leads
to increased secretion. These morphologically different strains were also marked to develop
similarly to ORY26 under submerged growth conditions. Further work is, however, required
in order to obtain adequate information about their yields.
ORY53, ORY57, and ORY58 are considered to be prominent strains for improved production of
extracellular recombinant asparaginase with the possibility of eventual use for ALL treatment.
It is also expected that the other obtained morphological mutant has a clear potential for
improved secretion. Disruption of morphology associated genes, as a whole, is concluded to
be a viable method for increased protein secretion.
