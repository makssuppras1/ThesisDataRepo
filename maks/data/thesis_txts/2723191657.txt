Abstract
Roll-on/Roll-off (RoRo) ships are a crucial part of maritime transport to transfer cargoes,
especially for short sea shipping. Recently, people aim to develop green shipping. This
paper focuses on simulating the container loading and discharging operations with dy-
namic container arrival times and dealing with uncertainty in RORO ship planning. The
goal of this method is to minimize the total operational time of discharging and loading
a vessel, reduce bottlenect and avoid unnecessary recourse waste. A simulation model
is applied to simulate terminal operations. By comparing this result with the traditional
method and static planning. The paper verifies that dynamic schedule planning can pro-
vide a positive contribution to improving the efficiency of container terminal loading and
discharging and reducing resource wastage.

Introduction
Roll-on/Roll-off(called RORO) describes a vessel transporting wheeled cargo, like cars,
trucks, etc. It has either built-in or shore-based ramps so that vehicles can be driven
straight on and straight off the vessel(Figure 1.1), which makes it easier to load and unload
cargo compared to doing them with a crane.
Figure 1.1: The structure of RORO vessels
Short-sea shipping is an important transportation mode for European countries with long
coastlines or a large number of inhabited islands. It made up 60.4% of the total sea
transport of goods in 2020(EUROSTAT, 2020). The Netherlands, Spain, Italy, and Turkey
were the European countries with the largest amount of maritime freight handled in their
main ports, each handling more than 100 million tonnes of goods, which all with short sea
shipping shares of 70% or more in their main ports(EUROSTAT, 2020). RORO shipping,
because of its flexibility, operational speed advantage and features of multimodal transport
compared to traditional shipping mode, is getting more and more popular in the Atlantic
Ocean, which accounted for 22%(EUROSTAT, 2020).
An early study by Kris and Eef(2010) shows that short-sea shipping(SSS) performs better
than the road in reducing CO2 emissions, and the road sector has not been successful
in further reducing CO2. Besides the impact of slow steaming(Nastia et al.,2021), fuel
choices for SSS(Selma et al.,2014), etc., on reducing CO2 emissions. RORO ships are
competitive in short-sea shipping because of their features of flexible, operational speed
advantage, and multimodal transport. Hence, we are interested in bringing Europe a
step closer to sustainable and green RORO shipping and increasing the efficiency of port
operations.
Our study works for discharging and loading operation planning for RORO ships. A RORO
2

vessel consists of a number of decks, each of which is divided into different channels by
lines parallel to the hull. Each channel is in turn made up of a number of slots, so the con-
tainer with a trailer can be loaded to or discharged from each available slot. Discharging
and loading assignments are performed by trailer heads driven by terminal staff. They
can go onto the vessel to grab containers out or grab containers from the loading yard
and deliver them onto the vessel.
How many drivers work for each deck and which assignments should be assigned to them
at what time are important for a terminal operation because it determines how long the
RORO ships stay in the terminal. In many terminals, loading assignment starts after a
deck is fully discharged. It is time-consuming and also results in a waste of resources,
because almost half the time, drivers are traveling without a trailer. Discharging and load-
ing at the same time(dual cycling) can apparently increase efficiency. Beizhen et al. first
formulated a model for dual cycling in RORO shipping in 2020. Their results denote dual
cycling significantly reduces the move number of the drivers, thereby shorting the total
operational time of discharging and loading a vessel.
On the basis of dual cycling ideas, the novel part of our paper is to consider the situation
of uncertain container arrival. We aim to find a better way to do planning for discharging
and loading operations to handle uncertain container arrival situations.
It is common that not all containers that need to be loaded are waiting in the loading
yard at the beginning, some of them are probably on their way. We generally have an ex-
pected timetable to show when they will arrive, but we could not expect them all to arrive
on time. Some of them might delay or ahead of arrival, and we do not know how long they
will be delayed or ahead, we call this situation an uncertain container arrival situation. In
this paper, we talk about two ways to make schedule planning for both traditional and
dual cycling terminal operations and discuss their applicability to different expectations of
delay or ahead-of-time arrival and the different number of drivers.
1. Make static schedule planning based on expected container arrival time
2. Make dynamic schedule planning based on constantly updated arrival time
To ensure the smooth flow of terminal operations, the operational planning of processes
within the ro-ro terminal must be perfect. Congestion and bottlenecks have to be iden-
tified in advance. For example, congestion due to too many drivers, and the waste of
resources. Simulation is a useful tool for decision-making and operational planning in the
planning process of port operations, it can avoid the difficulties and high costs of practi-
cal implementation(Yavuz Keceli et al., 2013). Also, help terminal managers identify and
avoid bottlenecks to improve efficiency. This paper uses simulation-based model to sim-
ulate the operation of our two decision options in RORO terminal discharging and loading
operations.
The remainder of this paper is organized as follows. In section 2, we have a review of the
relevant literature. Section 3 formulates the problem, and provides a conceptual model
and assumption. Then We define the simulation model in section 4. In section 5, we
use an example to show how the simulation model work in dynamic and static schedule
planning for traditional and dual cycling operations. Then, we introduce the Monte Carlo
simulation in Section 6. The results are presented in Section 7. We conclude this paper
with a discussion and outlook in Section 8.

Literature review
Studies over the past two decades have provided significant information on liner ship-
ping research. Recently, due to the rise of short-sea shipping, there is a growing body
of literature that investigates RORO shipping. A series of recent studies have indicated
RORO ship stowage planning to sequence of cargo loading to reduce the time spent han-
dling at each port, such as using a novel MIP model(Seyed et al.,2022), using a shortest
path based heuristic(Jone R et al.,2017), using a semi-automated method(Xiaofeng et
al.,2022) and so on. Much of their research has focused on how to optimize stowage
planning to achieve cost savings while maintaining hull stability and improving naviga-
tional safety. There are also some research on how to integrate RoRo Shipping into
sustainable intermodal transport chains(Anastasia et al.,2019) and analysis of the impact
of sulphur emission limits on RORO operations(Psaraftis,2017).
A series of studies about dual cycling is focused on maximizing the number of dual-cycle
operations of quay cranes(Haipeng et al., 2009), because quay crane schedule planning
can significantly impact the vessel turnaround times and QC is the most expensive han-
dling equipment in the container terminal(Crainic and Kim, 2007), that is the reason why
people spent a lot of working on it, which includes Anne described the double-cycling prob-
lem using greedy algorithm in 2006 to improve the efficiency of quay cranes and container
ports and Dandan proposed composite algorithm to improve efficiency of operations and
utilization of quay cranes.
To date, dual cycling in RORO vessels has received little attention in the research litera-
ture. For discharging and loading operations in RORO vessels, Beizhen et al(2019) tested
module-based framework using statistical analysis to estimate discharge times and first
formulated a model for dual cycling in RORO shipping in 2022.
For the study of dynamic schedule planning for terminal operaions, Daofang(2017) worked
on container handling with dynamic ship arrival times, she proposed a multi-vessel shore
bridge scheduling method for container terminals based on a dynamic rolling level strategy
to minimize the operation time of all ships. So far, however, there has been very little
discussion about how to use schedule planning for RORO ship with dynamic container
arrival.
Monte Carlo simulation is better than many conventional methods in the estimation of
uncertain events(Christos E.,2001). A growing body of literature has investigated to sim-
ulation methods to simulate and build the model of RORO terminal operations. Yavuz
Keceli(2013) developed a simulation model for Ro-Ro terminal operations decision sup-
port in Turkey, and his model has proved to be quite effective by terminal model.

Assumption

To consider saving money and increasing time efficiency, we assume our terminal is us-

(a) The driver part
(b) The unaccompanied trailer with the driver
Figure 3.3: Unaccompanied trailer
Stowage planning is also one of the most important operational tasks in RORO shipping
to keep safe, we assume that our stowage plans are rated as safe. We do not consider the
weight of containers that need to be loaded. The RORO vessels will not lose their flotation,
stability and strength under every discharging and loading plan and action (Xiaofeng Sun

We assume that the slots on the vessel are standard that fit all trailers, and all containers
requiring loading and discharging are already with trailers.
RORO ships have multiple decks, the main deck has an external ramp that connects to
the shore and through which containers located on the main deck can be discharged off
the vessel or loaded onto the vessel directly. For containers on other decks, they can go
to the main deck from an internal ramp. In practice, drivers are assigned to specific decks
and once one deck is cleared, they can be assigned to another. In this paper, we have
modeled the decks independently of each other, so we have assumed that there are a
reasonable number of drivers for each deck.

We cannot randomly select containers for discharging and loading operations, due to the
features of RORO ships we have to follow some precedence rules and constraints, this
section mainly defines them for our problem.
4.1.1
Precedence rules
Containers can only be discharged if all containers before them have been discharged.
Containers can only be transported in a straight line. For example, container E can not
be discharged, containers D and G can be discharged(Figure 4.1).
Figure 4.1: Discharging precedence rules
Containers can only be loaded if all the containers behind them have been loaded and
the slots they are going in are empty. Containers can only be transported in a straight
line. For example, containers E and F can not be loaded, containers G and L can be
loaded(Figure 4.2).

5.1.1
For dual cycling operations
During the decision-making process, if we only have feasible discharging containers, we
make a decision to discharge them and give priority to discharging them from the leftmost
of the vessel, because for a full ship, discharging from the left of the vessel could quickly
empty one line to start dual cycling. If we only have feasible loading containers, normally
it would happen after all containers have been discharged, we will make the decision to
load them. But if we both have feasible discharging and loading containers, we make a
rule below to achieve dual cycling as much as possible:
• Discharging is preferred if the last task of the driver is loading.
• Loading is preferred if the last task of the driver is discharging.

5.2
Static schedule planning
In this section, we mainly discuss the planning of discharging and loading activities for
both traditional operations and dual cycling operations based on the expected container
arrival time, and how to use iterated local search to find the optimal solution to minimize
makespan for the dual cycling problem based on the expected container arrival time.
5.2.1
For dual cycling operations
In the previous section, we talked about how real-time decisions can be made to deal with
uncertain container arrivals. However, we cannot ensure that these decisions are optimal.
As delayed or early arrivals are random events in the context of a known expected arrival
schedule, hence, real container arrival, to a certain extent, is related to the expected
arrival time. We could make static schedule planning based on the expected timetable,
and use an optimal algorithm to optimize operational sequence and reduce makespan. In
this subsection, we want to text how static schedule planning performs if some containers
are delayed or ahead. Here, we use the iterated local search algorithm to help us find the
optimal static schedule planning.
Local search is a heuristic algorithm for solving optimization problems. For many complex
problems, the time required to solve the optimal solution can be extremely long. There-
fore, various heuristic algorithms have been created to retreat to the next best solution,
and local search is one of them. A local search algorithm is a simple greedy search algo-
rithm that selects one optimal solution at a time from the space of solutions close to the
current solution as the current solution until a local optimal solution is reached. The local
search starts from an initial solution and then searches the neighbourhood of the solution,
moving to that solution and continuing the search if there is a better solution, otherwise re-
turning to the current solution. The local search will start with an initial solution and move
through the neighbourhood. Neighbouring solutions to the initial solution are generated,
and then neighbouring solutions are selected according to some strategy. The process is
repeated until the termination condition is reached.
Iterative local search(ILS) is one of the explorative local search methods. It adds a per-
turbation to the locally optimal solution obtained from the local search and then re-runs
the local search.

Monte Carlo Simulation
In the last section, we obtain a conclusion that using real-decision-making method for the
example Figure 5.1 under uncertain container arrival situations is better than using the
previous optimal solution made by the expected container arrival time. However￿we
cannot get a generic result from one example, there would be a big bias. And, the sched-
ule we expect is obtained from a random Poisson distribution, and the delayed or early
arrival times are also obtained from a random exponential distribution. So this makes our
example highly random and we cannot get a conclusion with a high degree of accuracy
from a single experiment.
Monte Carlo simulation is a mathematical technique for predicting the likely outcome of
uncertain events. Monte Carlo simulation is a probabilistic model that can include an el-
ement of uncertainty or randomness in its predictions. It can show a much clear picture
than deterministic prediction. When using a probabilistic model to simulate results, we
will get different results each time. For example, there is a six-sided die, every time you
have a one-sixth chance to land on a specific number(1 to 6). You might not get the dice
to land on a total of six different numbers when you roll the die six times. However, as you
continue to roll an infinite number of times, each number of the die will have a theoretical
probability of one-sixth. The more times you roll the die, the more accurate result you will
get. In other words, rolling a die 10000 times will give you a closer one-sixth probability for
each number than only rolling it 100 times. In our RORO dual cycling problem, because
the expected container arrival timetable and delay are obtained from stochastic Poisson
and exponential distribution, just as we do not know the outcome of every the roll of the
dice. So we use the Monte Carlo simulation to solve these problems, as it provides a wide
range of possible outcomes

Discussion
In this paper, we discuss dual cycling operations for RORO vessels. Dual cycling oper-
ations can truly reduce the movements of drivers when they are doing discharging and
loading assignments. On the one hand, this significantly reduces the total operating time.
on the other hand, it improves resource utilization, the driver does not have to work with
an empty vehicle, and it reduces energy consumption to reduce the emission of short-sea
shipping.
For achieving dual cycling operations, we address two different schedule planning meth-
ods, static and dynamic. Static is to make schedule planning at the beginning, so the
drivers know what containers they need to discharge or load for the vessel and the order
to do them. This is a good method if all the containers can arrive at the loading yard on
time. Also, we could do optimize the operations to minimize the total operational time
using the expected arrival time. However, in real terminals, all containers arriving on time
are not realistic. Unpredictable events happen frequently, such as weather, operational
errors, delay from the last delivery and so on. If we continue at this point with the static
schedule planning, it might be unreasonable. For example, according to the expected
container arrival time, a driver is assigned to load a container that is actually delayed, this
is when the waiting time is generated. But not every delay of a container can affect the
operations if it can arrive before its expected operational time, the total operational time
would not be changed even if it is delayed.
Therefore, to figure out which method is better to apply to actual terminal operations. We
build up a simulation model to simulate real terminal operations. We implement dynamic
schedule planning and also accept static schedule planning results after updating them
by real arrival time.
We use Monte Carlo methods to repeat our simulation model of 6 instances with 15 dif-
ferent scenarios, like different expected delays and driver numbers. Then we compare
the results for both methods and traditional operations.
From the experimental results, we find that dual cycling operations have significant ad-
vantages over traditional operations when dealing with uncertain container arrival. Static
and dynamic schedule planning perform similarly when the expected delay time of con-
tainers is low. As the delay time increases, the advantages of dynamic schedule planning
become apparent because it can stay in a relatively stable state, while the performance of
static schedule planning has an obvious drop. Hence, we think dynamic schedule plan-
ning is more applicable to real terminal operations.
In addition, we simulate terminal operations with different numbers of drivers, we find that
it is not that the more drivers, the better the whole loading and unloading processes. It is
true that when the driver number increases from 1 to 3, the total operational time has a
significant decrease. However, when its number keeps rising, the makespan down slowly,
and the advantages of dual cycling are less, especially when the delay is long. This shows
that when the number of drivers is not reasonable, there is a bottleneck and congestion
in terminal operations. Hence, it is an unreasonable investment to assign more than 3
drivers in our instances.
