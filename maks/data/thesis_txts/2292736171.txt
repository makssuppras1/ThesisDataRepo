Abstract
This project addresses the Vehicle Routing Problem with Cross-Docking (VRPCD), where
a fleet of vehicles is responsible for the transportation of goods from suppliers to cus-
tomers via a cross-dock. There is a consolidation process done at the cross-dock, where
the goods are exchanged from vehicles in order to optimize the delivery routes. The ob-
jective of the VRPCD is to minimize the total distance travelled, the number of vehicles
used, the route durations and the number of goods consolidated at the cross-dock, while
respecting the capacity of the vehicles and the time window constraints at the nodes.
First, an integer programming formulation for the VRPCD is proposed, but the main
focus of this thesis relies on the use of heuristic methods to obtain a solution. First, the
construction heuristic is implemented, to obtain a feasible solution, then the Iterated
Local Search is applied to solve the problem. The proposed algorithm is implemented
and tested with data sets that have around 200 requests, provided by Transvision, a
Danish consultancy. Computational results show that the solution found uses an aver-
age of 3.4% more vehicles than the lower bound. Furthermore, there are two analyses
discussed; the first one in which different lengths of the time windows are studied and
the second one where the cross-dock does not act as a consolidation center, but as an
intermediate node to pass by. The latter one evinces the positive effect that the addition
of the cross-dock has in a problem where commodities have to be delivered from and to
a specific location.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Cross-docking (CD) is a logistics practice introduced by many companies, which aims
to reduce inventory and distribution costs while maintaining the level of customer ser-
vice. A cross-dock can be seen as a distribution center or warehouse where goods are
consolidated according to their shipment destination, and therefore greater savings can
be achieved. Cross-docking is defined as the continuous process of delivering goods to
the final destination through the cross-dock, without storing products and materials in
a distribution center [AV10].
According to [BF10] the decision problems to be solved during the life cycle of a
cross-dock terminal, ordered from strategic to operational, are as follows:
(i) Location of cross-docking terminal(s).
(ii) Layout of the terminal.
(iii) Assignment of destinations to dock doors.
(iv) Vehicle routing.
(v) Truck Scheduling.
(vi) Resource scheduling inside the terminal.
(vii) (Un-)Packing loads into (or from) trucks.
In this project, the problems (iv) “Vehicle Routing” and (v) “Truck Scheduling” are
going to be tackled simultaneously. Furthermore, the objective will consider different
aspects such as the total travelled distance, the number of vehicles used, the route du-
rations and the number of goods consolidated.
Instead of treating distribution problems as VRP’s, the idea is to study how fruitful
it can be to divide the distribution of goods into two “legs”, one for picking up the goods
and transport them to a cross-dock facility and one for delivering the goods to the cus-
tomers. The main problem lies not only in finding the shortest routes for both processes,

but also to coordinate the exchange of goods at the cross-dock, always considering the
time window limitations and the capacity restrictions of the vehicles.
A special motivation of this project relays on the fact that we are dealing with a
real-life problem. It is also of special interest that the goods transported are not specific.
Even though it is recommended for products which demand is stable and where the unit
stock-out cost is low, this logistics practice can be applied to almost all types of goods.
The remainder of this project is structured as follows. A detailed description of the
VRPCD and the most cited literature in the subject can be found in Chapter 1. Chapter
2 develops a mathematical formulation for the VRPCD based on simpler models like the
CVRP and the VRPTW. Later on, the data provided by Transvision and the instances
created for the computational tests are examined in Chapter 4.
The description of
the construction heuristic, the Iterated Local Search and algorithms used to solve the
problem are presented in Chapter 5. Chapter 6 contains the results and discussion of
the computational tests examined. Finally, the conclusions are provided in Chapter 7.

In this chapter, an overview of the problem studied in this project is introduced. For this,
a preliminary presentation of the Vehicle Routing Problem with Cross-Docking (VRPCD)
is given. Subsequently, the most cited literature in the subject is summarized.
2.1
Problem Overview
The problem considered in this project is the Vehicle Routing Problem with Cross-
Docking (VRPCD), a variant of the classical Capacitated Vehicle Routing Problem (CVRP),
which introduces the concept of having a cross-dock facility. When a single cross-dock
is introduced, products from different suppliers are collected by a fleet of vehicles and
transported to the cross-dock, where they are consolidated. Afterwards, the vehicles
deliver them to their final destinations, with little or no storage in between.
The problem has two separate stages and could be divided into two different VRPTW.
The first stage is called the pickup process and consists of the physical flow from suppliers
to the cross-dock. The second stage is called the delivery process, which is the flow from
the cross-dock to the customers. Finally, the consolidation process consists of unloading,
classifying and loading the products into the corresponding vehicles according to their
destination.
==PAGE:18==
4
2 The Vehicle Routing Problem with Cross-Docking
The task is to assign routes to a set of vehicles in order to satisfy some transportation
requests. A request consists of a supplier-customer pair and has an associated number
of goods that needs to be picked up from the supplier and delivered to the customer at
a specific day of the week. The same vehicles that pickup the goods are the ones that
drive to the delivery destinations, but it is possible to use more vehicles if necessary. In
that case, the vehicles that pick up the goods do not need to unload all their pallets,
only the ones that are being transferred to another vehicle for their delivery. In this
project we will refer to nodes or customers and commodity or good indistinctly.
Figure 2.1 shows a small example of the problem tackled in this project. It contains
five different requests and two vehicles. The pickup nodes are represented with a circle
and the delivery nodes with a square. During the pickup process, Vehicle 1 picks up the
commodities from the orange, red and green suppliers and goes back to the cross-dock;
Vehicle 2 only picks up commodities from the purple and blue suppliers, and returns
to the cross-dock. The consolidation process for Vehicle 1 consists of unloading the or-
ange commodity, as it will be delivered by the other vehicle, and reloading the purple
commodity. Vehicle 2 unloads the purple commodity and reloads the orange one. Af-
terwards, the vehicles start the delivery process and come back to the depot at the end
of their routes.
Figure 2.1: Example of a VRPCD with five requests and two vehicles.

The main characteristics of this problem are to consider that the vehicles have a
limited capacity, that they always start and finish their routes at the cross-dock and
that the goods have to be picked up and delivered within the time windows defined
for each supplier or customer. The total route duration is the total time that the ve-
hicles spend away from the cross-dock and is limited by the time horizon of the problem.
Driving times between the different destinations. as well as service times to load or
unload a truck at the cross-dock, are known, although service times at the pickup or
delivery locations are assumed to be negligible for this project. The idea is to find the
set of routes with the minimum cost, which satisfy all requests without violating any of
the above mentioned restrictions. The problem is NP-hard, since it is an extension of
the Capacitated Vehicle Routing Problem (CVRP).
2.2
Literature Review
In this section, we present some of the most cited literature on the VRPCD. We have
divided the literature into two main components: the original problem of VRPCD and
other ways of looking at and solving the problem.
2.2.1
The Vehicle Routing Problem with Cross-Docking
One of the first ones to propose a mathematical model for the VRPCD was Lee et
al. [LJL06]. Here, the goal is to determine the number of vehicles and the best route,
schedule, and arrival time of each vehicle at the cross-dock. The objective minimizes
the transportation costs while considering a planning horizon for the whole operation.
Each time goods are moved from or to a vehicle at the cross-dock, additional time is
needed to perform the loading and unloading operations. One major characteristic of
this model is the fact that, after picking up the goods, the vehicles have to perform si-
multaneous arrivals at the cross-dock in order to perform the corresponding operations.
Moreover, they applied a tabu search heuristic algorithm to solve the different instances.
They claim that this meta-heuristic algorithm is the most suitable for this problem as:
1) it allows to escape from a local solution by the creation of various neighbourhoods,
==PAGE:20==
6
2 The Vehicle Routing Problem with Cross-Docking
2) prevents from returning to the local solution, and 3) has a memory of the search
processes used. They randomly generated instances with 10, 30 and 50 vehicles. In or-
der to test the results, they produced the enumeration method which finds the optimal
solution. They obtained at most a 4% average error of total cost between their proposed
algorithm and enumeration.
Wen et al. [Wen+08] proposed a mixed integer programming formulation. A tabu
search heuristic is embedded within an adaptive memory procedure to solve the prob-
lem. The main difference between this study and the one by Lee et al. [LJL06], is the
absence of the constraint on simultaneous arrivals for all the vehicles. Instead, the de-
pendency among the vehicles is determined when the goods change vehicle at the cross
dock. Moreover, each customer and supplier has a specific time window to be visited
and there is a fixed time for preparation for the unloading, but also a certain amount
of time to unload each pallet. Wen et al. also remarks that without the consolidation
at the cross-dock, the problem becomes two independent Vehicle Routing Problem with
Time Windows (VRPTW), which provide a lower bound for the VRPCD. Therefore, the
search for an optimal solution is not only to find shortest routes for both operations, but
also to coordinate the exchange of products at the cross-dock while all time windows and
the time horizon are respected. They conclude that, for small instances (20 customers),
they can produce near optimal solutions (less than 1% away from the optimum) within
less than 5 seconds of computational time. For the large instances (from 30 to 200 cus-
tomers), the gap between the average solution value and the lower bound (2 separated
VRPTW’s) is consistently below 5%.
Liao et al. [LLS10] considered the same problem as Lee et al. [LJL06], but do not de-
scribe the mathematical model explicitly. They propose an initial solution scheme that
is composed by two steps: to arrange the suppliers to vehicles and to assign retailers to
available vehicles. In the creation of the initial solution, the number of vehicles is deter-
mined. Their tabu search algorithm shows two major differences compared to the one
by Lee et al. [LJL06]. The first one is the definition of the neighbourhood, they assign a
single node to another vehicle one at a time, whereas Lee et al. [LJL06] try to exchange
nodes between two vehicles. The second difference relays on the fact that they allow to
remove an empty vehicle. This algorithm shows a superior solution and improvements
in the computational time. The results have an average improvement of 16.7% , 31.4%
==PAGE:21==
2.2 Literature Review
7
and 14.7% for those instances with 10, 30 and 50 vehicles available respectively. The
authors relate this improvement to the fact that the number of vehicles is determined
once the initial solution is created while the algorithm in Lee et al. [LJL06] continuously
tries to reduce the number of required vehicles.
2.2.2
Different perspectives, same problem
Another way to analyze the same problem, is to see it as a Pickup and Delivery Problem
(PDP) with a transfer point. Here, the transfer point is used as the cross-dock where
vehicles interchange goods or passengers. The main difference is that there might be
several transfer points instead of only one cross-dock. Cortés et al. [CMC10] propose a
problem that considers a transportation request, which consists of picking up a certain
number of passengers from a predetermined pickup location and dropping them off at
a predetermined delivery location. They also include transfer nodes where vehicles may
interact by interchanging passengers. Furthermore, they suggest different objective func-
tion expressions to ensure that in practice the policies and objectives are well covered.
They solve the problem by Branch-and-Cut, based on Benders’ Decomposition. At the
end, they mention a set partitioning formulation, which they are currently working on,
which splits the routes into three different types: from a pickup point to a transfer point,
from a transfer point to a delivery location, and from a pickup to a delivery without any
transfer.
The problem studied in Petersen and Røpke [PR11] is similar to the one by Cortés
et al. [CMC10] but they formalize it into the Pickup and Delivery Problem with Cross-
Docking opportunity (PDPCD), which is applied to a major Danish transporter of flowers.
No formulation is presented, but the objective and constraints are described in the text.
Requests can either be served by one vehicle, where the vehicle does not stop at the
cross-dock, or by different vehicles, which implies a consolidation of goods at the cross-
dock. This decision is the basis for the construction heuristic, where routes that stop
at the cross-dock are separated during the route construction stage. The objective is to
minimize transportation costs, which are divided into five different elements: 1) a daily
cost for using each vehicle, 2) a cost per minute for using each vehicle, 3) a cost per km
driven, 4) a cost per container handled at the cross-dock, and 5) a road toll for crossing
==PAGE:22==
8
2 The Vehicle Routing Problem with Cross-Docking
the bridge between Funen and Zealand. Instances with 500 to 1000 requests are solved
using an Adaptive Large Scale Neighbourhood Search (ALNS) metaheuristic, which also
introduces two features (pre-sort at insertion and return to best solution) that prove to
be a successful measure. The required number of iterations used in the ALNS is con-
siderably different for the different instances. Another successful intensifying measure
is the use of parallellization, where the current and best solutions are shared among all
processing units, while working on a local version of the temporary solution.
Just like Petersen and Røpke [PR11], Santos et al. [SMd13] also propose the PDPCD,
where two different types of routes are possible. Nevertheless, they present an integer
programming formulation and solve it with a Branch-and-Price algorithm. The formu-
lation is based on routes and not on nodes, and the costs are computed as the sum of
all arcs traversed. No constraints on time windows are imposed. The instances tested
are generated from the instances used in Wen et al. [Wen+08], which included 200 re-
quests. The instances created just contain a small set with a maximum of 30 requests,
and four different values of loading and unloading costs are considered. The results are
compared with previous work done by the same authors, where all routes had to stop at
the cross-dock before being delivered. They show that transportation costs are reduced
when the problem is proposed as a PDPCD.
A more recent variant of this problem is to allow splitting loads in the delivery pro-
cess, which means that the demand of a customer can be picked up or delivered by more
than one vehicle. Moghadam et al. [MGK14] propose a model where the pickup process
is done as usual, fulfilling the time windows and capacity constraints, then there is a
set of consolidation processes according to the delivery routes, and finally, the loads
in the delivery routes can be split. This problem formulation also minimizes the time
at the cross-dock to load and unload products. To solve this problem, Mghadam et al.
[MGK14] propose three heuristic algorithms: Simulated Annealing (SA), Ant Colony
System (ACS) and a combination between these two called the hybrid ACS-SA algo-
rithm. In the results they only report the ACS-SA and SA algorithms because the ASC
alone has a very long running times. For small instances, they found the exact solution
with the mathematical programming software GAMS. This software was only able to
solve data sets with five pairs of nodes. Therefore they decided to create a lower bound
with 2-VRPTW in GAMS. For the instances that had less than 30 pairs of nodes, they
==PAGE:23==

compare their algorithms with this lower bound. The gap for both algorithms is always
less than 5%. For the larger instances, they cannot use GAMS to find a lower bound,
so they compared the results only between the two chosen methods. They verify that
the ACS-SA algorithm shows a better performance compared to the SA algorithm with
a one way analysis of variance with 95% confidence level.

CHAPTER 3
Mathematical formulation
In order to fully comprehend the mathematical model for the VRPCD, it is important
to start by defining the base problem, the VRP, and its extensions with capacity and
time windows constraints. In this section we develop a mathematical formulation for
the VRPCD based on simpler models such as the CVRP and the VRPTW and models
for this specific problem found on the literature review. Moreover, we define the specific
problem treated in this project.
3.1
The Capacitated Vehicle Routing
Problem
The Vehicle Routing Problem (VRP) involves the design of a set of optimal delivery
or collection routes for a fleet of vehicles, which service a set of customers with known
demands. By optimal, in this case, we mean the routes that have minimum cost. Fur-
thermore, all the routes originate and terminate at one or several depots. A solution
consists of ensuring that each customer is serviced exactly once and that there is a
route designed for each vehicle. In the generalization of the problem, more than one
depot might be available, but in this case we only considered one. The problem was

In this case, the objective is to minimize the cost of the routes used in the solution.

3.3.1
Additional considerations
This problem can be adjusted in order to include singularities for different problem
definitions. Some of them can be included in the costs of the objective function, like:
• a specific pull-out cost for every vehicle used;
• cost per driven minute;
• cost for the time duration of the route;
• cost for the number of pallets moved at the CD;
• penalty of exceeding a certain number of working hours.
The constraints can also be extended in order for the problem to resemble real prob-
lems better. Some examples are:
• consider the service times at each node, or at the depot when loading and unloading
the pallets;
• to limit the duration that a vehicle can operate;
• to include breaks for the drivers or change of shifts;
• to account for the durability of some goods such as perishable foods;
• to fix the number of vehicles used in the solution;
• to consider when vehicles need to refuel, etc.
Other ideas have been developed around this problem, one being a division of the
pickups and deliveries in case the demand of goods is too big for only one vehicle.
Another idea is that some routes do not necessarily stop at the cross-dock; with this
one could have two types of routes: the ones that have to consolidate or go through the
cross-dock and the ones that go directly from a pickup location to the delivery location.

Heuristic Algorithms
Due to the importance in practice of problems such as the VRPCD, different types of al-
gorithms that try to solve them have been developed. These algorithms can be classified
as either exact or approximate. Yet, solving to optimality problems that are NP-hard
may lead to exponential computational times. Therefore, the use of approximate meth-
ods is necessary and is the focus of this project. In approximate methods we sacrifice
the guarantee of finding optimal solutions for the sake of getting good solutions in a
significantly reduced amount of time [BR03].
In this chapter we initially detail the construction heuristic implemented, then we
describe the local search with the different move mechanisms and strategies, and finally
we present the Iterated Local Search (ILS) metaheuristic.
5.1
Construction heuristic
Route construction methods were among the first heuristics for the CVRP and still
form the base of many implementations for various routing applications like VRPCD.
Heuristics typically use a greedy approach to obtain a good initial solution in an effi-
cient manner and then incrementally improve the solution by neighbourhood exchanges
or local searches [JC06]. Here, the construction heuristic attempts to build a solution
from scratch, but does not improve it.
Due to the complexity of the problem that we are dealing with, the construction
heuristic is divided into different steps that lead to a feasible initial solution. This solu-
tion might be costly because it is not yet optimized, but it uses very low computational
time and ensures the feasibility of all constraints. The different steps followed to con-
struct the initial solution are detailed in Algorithm 1 and will be further developed in

5.1.2
Feasibility checks
The routes created in steps 1 and 4, for the pickup and delivery nodes are feasible if
they are seen as independent problems, but not when they are considered as part of the
same one, as the goods or the vehicles might not be available. Therefore we need to use
two different feasibility checks that ensure the feasibility of the entire problem. The first
check ensures that the pickups are able to reach their corresponding delivery node on
time. The second check makes sure that none of the delivery routes leave before all its
corresponding goods have arrived at the cross-dock.
5.1.2.1
Checking that pickups can reach deliveries’ time windows
After the creation of the pickup routes, we verify that all nodes are able to meet their
corresponding delivery time window. It could happen that a node that was scheduled at
the beginning of the route arrives at the cross-dock later than the maximum arrival time
at the delivery node. For example, a node is picked up at 7 am but the route, in which it
is being transported, does not arrive to the cross-dock until 3 pm. If the corresponding
delivery node has a time window from 12 pm to 2 pm, then the goods will not be able
to arrive on time. When this is the case, we remove the node from its current route
and place it in a new one. This new route is exclusive for the given pickup node, hence
arriving to the cross-dock earlier and being able to reach the delivery time window.

5.1.2.2
Modification of the schedule for the delivery routes
The delivery routes are created with the same procedure as the pickup routes. At the
end of this creation a specific schedule is provided for each route. Nevertheless, some
modifications have to be made in order to ensure their feasibility. Just as we ensured
that all nodes for the pickup routes are able to reach their corresponding delivery, now
it is necessary to check that all the delivery routes depart after all their goods have
arrived at the cross-dock. This procedure looks at each delivery route separately and
then searches for the goods that arrived the latest. If this time is earlier than the time
at which the route has its departure from the cross-dock, then it is kept as it was. If, on
the contrary, the goods from a specific request arrived after the desired departure time,
the schedule is modified accordingly. This modification might lead to some nodes not
being able to reach their time windows. If this is the case, the nodes are removed from
the routes and after all the routes have been modified, new routes are created for them.

5.2
Local Search
Local search methods form a general class of approximate heuristics based on the concept
of iteratively moving from one solution to a neighbouring one. The new solution is then
evaluated. If it is accepted, then we move to that solution and consider its neighbours.
The search is repeated until no more neighbours are found. When this happens the
process stops. This means that the solution found is a local minimum (i.e. best in
its neighbourhood). In short, the basic local search components needed to design an
algorithm are:
• Initial solution
• Move-generation mechanism
• Acceptance criteria
• Termination criteria
Every local search starts from a candidate solution. As it is often done, the initial
solution used in this project is the one obtained from the construction heuristic. After
this initial feasible solution, it is reasonable to seek for improvement possibilities. For
this, we created several move-generation mechanisms that are going to be described in
the following subsections. These mechanisms create the feasible neighbouring solutions
by changing one attribute, or a combination of attributes, of the given solution. In this
case, if a better solution (i.e. a solution with a lower cost in case we have a minimization
problem) is identified, then we move to this new solution and replace the current solution.
The local optimum produced by any local search procedure can be very far from the
optimal solution. Local search methods mainly accept solutions that produce reductions
in the objective function value when dealing with minimization problems. Thus, the out-
come depends heavily on initial solutions and the neighbourhood generation mechanism
used [BG05].
5.2.1

For this project, a variety of move-generation mechanisms are introduced. They can be
split into intra-route neighbourhoods, if they operate on a single route at the time, and

a move is performed, we always ensure that all constraints such as time, capacity and

in a higher level framework and guide the search process. The goal is to efficiently explore
the search space in order to find near-optimal solutions. In this project, it was chosen
to implement an Iterated Local Search (ILS).
The essence of the ILS metaheuristic can be given in a nut-shell: one iteratively
builds a sequence of solutions generated by the embedded heuristic, leading to better
solutions than if one were to use repeated trials of that heuristic. The main idea is to
improve the solution by the use of iteration [Pap+07].

As mentioned before, applying local search allows us to get to local optima, but they

solution that cannot be improved by applying the local search algorithms. One way the
ILS can “escape” from these local minima, is by applying a perturbation to the current
solution or the current local minimum. Choosing one or the other might have different
consequences, this idea will be discussed further on in the report. Two different ways of

Cutting strategy
This type of perturbation splits a pickup or delivery route, with at least two cus-

is applied to a random node, excluding the last customer or supplier in the route and
all the nodes that were scheduled after this random node are inserted in a new route,
in the same order. Since we are splitting a route that is feasible, there is no need to
check the feasibility of the new solution again.
This is because the distances follow
the triangle inequality. The new vehicle can freely depart at any time from the CD and
it can reach the first node in the route, the latest, at the same time it was reached before.

The second type of perturbation is a swap 1-1 between two routes. This neighbour-
hood has already been used in the local search (Figure 5.6 to the right). The two nodes
to be swapped are selected randomly. To do that, we create a list with all nodes in the
solution in a random order. The first node in the list is selected and a new list with all
the feasible swaps for this node is generated. If the second list is not empty, a random
swap from the list is selected and performed. Otherwise, we select the next node in the
first list and follow the same procedure until a swap is found.
The selection of the perturbation strategy is given by a probability. In Chapter 6
we define the parameters for this distribution and how they have been found. Another
parameter from the perturbation phase that needs to be adjusted is the number of times
each perturbation is performed. One iteration of the cutting strategy perturbation is
definitely stronger than one swapping move perturbation. By stronger we mean that
the solution is more affected than if we only apply a swapping move. This is clearly
reflected in the objective value because of the cost of adding a new vehicle to the solution.
Therefore, each perturbation will have its own parameter tuning. In the case of the
cutting strategy, we will set an upper bound in the number of cuts, and for the swapping
strategy we will define the minimum and maximum values for a uniform distribution,
which gives the total number of swapping moves made.

5.3.2
Acceptance Criterion
The ILS does a randomized walk in S∗, the space of the local minima. The perturbation
mechanisms, together with the local search, define the possible transitions between a
current solution s∗in S∗to a neighbouring solution s∗′ in S∗[LMS02]. The acceptance
criterion determines whether s∗is accepted as the new incumbent solution (i.e. the best
current solution found in the searching procedure) and controls the balance between the
different search strategies.
• Intensification: this strategy can be achieved by always applying the perturba-
tion to the best known solution.
• Diversification: this strategy applies the perturbation to the last solution ob-
tained from the local search. In this way, we avoid the stagnation of the process.
Intermediate choices between these two strategies are also possible.
In this project
we have implemented a strategy called mixed 5×5, where every 5 iteration of the ILS
switches the acceptance criteria from intensification to diversification.

6.2.1
Costs for computational tests
The costs were created with the intention to simulate real costs in Danish Krones (DKK),
nevertheless they might be a bit higher than reality. Typical trucks on the Danish roads
are for instance Scania and Volvo trucks, which normally have between 400 and 800
horse power. This means, especially when pulling many tons, that the fuel economy is
not too high. The price of fuel varies a lot, but it is justifiable to claim that the diesel
price in Denmark is around 10 kr/liter in average. A truck can normally drive around 2
to 3,5 km/liter, which means that the truck in worst situations consumes 5 kr/km. To
be completely covered though, taking the extra pulling of many tons, we have decided
to set this number at 8 kr, taking into consideration the extra usage of diesel and oil.
Having spoken to an employee at the international shipping company Kuehne+Nagel,
we have realized that most companies that rent vehicles for this type of use, pay per
kilometre driven. The price for renting a vehicle can vary depending on the task, length,
time, etc. An average price would be around 1,3 e/km, which is equivalent to approx-
imately 10kr. The average number of kilometres driven by a vehicle for an instance of
our problem is a bit less than 500 km. All our pickup customers and the CD are lo-
cated in Zealand, while the delivery customers in Jutland. Therefore, we considered the
price for crossing the Great Belt Bridge in Denmark, which for a 10-20 meter long truck
costs 1125 kr every time it crosses and 1685 kr for longer trucks. All these costs rounded
up equal 9,000 kr per vehicle which is not far from the 10,000 kr that was decided to use.
According to the largest trade union in Denmark by the name of the “United Fed-
eration of Danish Workers (3F)”, the mandatory minimum salary for a qualified driver,
under the agreement with the ATL – Association of employers in the Transport and

184,36 and 235,62 kr/hour, which is why we wanted all costs to be accounted for a total

CHAPTER 7
Conclusion
In this project we have considered the Vehicle Routing Problem with Cross-Docking in
which goods have to be transported from suppliers to customers located in Denmark.
All vehicles have to stop at the cross-dock before starting with the deliveries in order
to consolidate the goods according to the delivery routes. This implies that the same
vehicles used for the pickups, are also used in the deliveries, making the creation of
routes for pickups and deliveries not independent from each other, and thus adding a
degree of complexity when modifying a route.
A construction heuristic and an iterated local search have been proposed for solv-
ing the problem. The latter one includes a perturbation strategy and it also combines
intensification and diversification for the acceptance criteria. The proposed algorithm
has been tested on real data sets involving up to 250 requests.
The algorithm pro-
vides good solutions, where the average number of vehicles used is 3.4% away from the
lower bound. However, the computational times are considerably high. For this cate-
gory of problems, in which the planing of routes and schedules for vehicles is done on
a daily basis, the algorithms for optimizing the routes should be able to run much faster.
To analyze the development of the algorithm, two different types of tests have been
carried out. The first considers a variation of the length of the time windows, and the
second examines the situation where the cross-dock does not act as a consolidation cen-
ter, but as a mere intermediate node where vehicles go through.
From the variation of the time windows, it has been observed that reducing its
length leads to an increment of the cost of the objective function, mainly associated to
an increase in the number of vehicles. On the other hand, an enlargement of the time
windows’ length does not produce a reduction in the objective as it would have been
expected. This may have been caused by the fact that the time windows are already
==PAGE:102==
88
7 Conclusion
wide enough.
The analysis neglecting the cross-dock functionality, indicates that the local search
implemented is not completely capable of avoiding the consolidation at the cross-dock.
Despite this fact, the solution found has a considerable more expensive objective func-
tion regarding distance and hourly costs. These results show the positive effect that the
addition of a cross-dock has in operations of this kind, if the costs at the cross-dock are
disregarded or considered to be low.
For further work with the VRPCD, other metaheuristics are suggested to obtain bet-
ter solutions. Some examples are, as mentioned in the literature review, the tabu search
algorithm and the simulated annealing. These metaheuristics could include interesting
features, such as the possibility of moving to infeasible or worse solutions with a certain
probability or exploring neighbourhoods of different sizes.
The problem examined in this project is a small piece of the life cycle of a cross-dock
terminal. Once the location and the layout of the terminals are setup, the greatest chal-
lenge comes with the design an optimisation of the routes. With the scientific method-
ology proposed, the routes created have fuller loads, therefore saving in transportation
costs while also being more environmentally friendly, because of the reduction in the
number of vehicles as well as the distance traveled.
