Master’s Thesis
Abstract
Safe navigation of autonomous surface vessels relies on the availability of reli-
able collision avoidance systems. Despite the research done within the area, limited
studies are available on the impact of route exchange on collision avoidance perfor-
mance. The objective of this thesis is to explore the effects route exchange between
vessels has on collision avoidance, by comparing the safety and performance of a
decision making system based on varying levels of knowledge regarding the target
ship’s trajectory. The decision maker is designed as a non-cooperative game, first
utilizing straight-line prediction methods based on AIS data, and then extended to
include full trajectory information obtained via the VDES.
A range of simulations covering the most common encounter scenarios were used
to evaluate the performance of the decision maker with respect to safety and com-
pliance with the International Regulations for Preventing Collisions at Sea (COL-
REGs). The game-theoretic system avoided collisions successfully with both levels
of information. Yet, utilizing route exchange information improved collision avoid-
ance performance considerably in situations where the target vessel did not follow
a straight trajectory.

Introduction
1.1
Background
Various industries are breaking ground on technological advancements towards
autonomous systems following the lead of the automotive industry. The develop-
ment of autonomous shipping has gained increasing interest within the maritime
industry and research, as autonomous vessels have the potential for providing nu-
merous benefits over manually operated ships. Human error is one of the leading
causes of accidents at sea, with estimates rising up to over 81% [1]. By diminishing
human error, ship autonomy could significantly improve safety at sea. Autonomous
vessels can also provide reduction of financial costs. According to studies, replac-
ing a manned bulk carrier by an autonomous substitute can save from four [2] up
to seven [3] million US dollars in crew costs and fuel consumption over a 25-year
period.
Safe autonomous navigation at sea relies on the availability of key technologies
that can provide information about vessel surroundings, evaluate potential threats,
and plan and execute path alterations to avoid groundings and collisions with both
static and dynamic obstacles. A collision avoidance system is thus an integral part
of all unmanned and autonomous vessels. All seafarers must also adhere to the
International Regulations for Preventing Collisions at Sea (COLREGs) [4], a set of
rules published by the International Maritime Organization (IMO) that describe the
actions expected to be taken by all vessels in order to prevent collisions. COLREGs
must be taken into account in the design of collision avoidance systems for au-
tonomous ships for them to safely navigate areas together with human operated
vessels.
Having an eﬀicient and reliable collision avoidance system is essential for en-
suring safe travel of an autonomous vessel. The decision maker of such system must
be able to select an optimal path to avoid collisions while fulfilling other desired
performance metrics, such as financial and environmental objectives. The optimal
decision depends heavily on the anticipated trajectory of the target vessel. Current
collision avoidance systems highly rely on data from the Automatic Identification
System (AIS) which shares the current position, speed and heading of the target
vessel [5]. The future trajectory is then predicted through various schemes, most
often using straight-line prediction. VHF Data Exchange System (VDES) [6] is an
extension to AIS, which allows vessels to not only exchange current information but
also their intended future routes, and it could replace the current information system
on vessels in the future. It is relevant to explore the effects and potential advantages
that the knowledge of the intentions of a target vessel has on the collision avoidance
system of an autonomous ship.
1.2
Scope and Objective
The main objective of the project is to evaluate the performance and safety
of a game-theoretic collision avoidance system decision maker and to assess how
route-exchange between vessels affects the performance and safety of the system.

1.3
Adopted Methods
Safety of the system implies safe navigation with respect to avoiding collisions with
target vessels, maintaining safe distance during encounters and complying to relevant
COLREGs rules. Performance relates to the degree of the system satisfying desired
objectives as well as overall robustness of the system, namely how sensitive it is to
fluctuation of parameters.
In order to realise the main objective, the project is broken down into interme-
diate goals. Foremost, the collision avoidance problem is formulated as a game and
solved in order to find the optimal avoidance maneuver. Accordingly, a path planner
is created to generate suitable action sets for all players. The paths are evaluated
through cost functions that account for the risk of collision, COLREGs compliance,
and other factors to be optimized. The system is first designed with straight-line
trajectory prediction and then modified to include route-exchange between vessels
to see how the knowledge of others’ intentions affects the optimal maneuver. The
systems are evaluated and compared through simulations of common encounter sce-
narios. The testing is focused mostly on single vessel encounters. The scope of the
project limits on the decision maker, thus any necessary control systems to realise
the maneuvers are out of scope.
The scope of the project concerns open waters neglecting the possibility of
grounding and static obstacles. The design is based around the nominal path of
a vessel, which is predefined to be optimal with respect to land, water depth and
known, static obstacles. Furthermore, it is assumed that all vessels are equipped
with basic navigational equipment, including AIS and/or VDES. To that end, it is
also assumed that positional data provided is accurate and failure of the equipment
for any reason is out of scope. Knowledge exchange is limited to the AIS/VDES
data, and further cooperation and negotiation is out of scope. Moreover, it is also
assumed that all vessels act according to COLREGs rules and follow their nominal
paths accurately. The simulations are based on certain ship size and speed and
generalization of the results on other vehicles is out of scope. Further assumptions
regarding the design are stated in Section 3.1.
1.3
Adopted Methods
The decision maker is based on game theory. The framework is chosen, because
the introduction to game theoretic collision avoidance systems show potential to safe,
collision free navigation, since the approach takes into consideration the dynamic
actions of target vessels [7], [8].
Game-theoretic collision avoidance systems are
popular and exhibit advantages also in other industries, such as with autonomous
cars [9], [10] and unmanned aerial vehicles [11], [12]. More precisely, non-cooperative
game theory is implemented as vessels are presumed to act independently towards
their own interest.
Path planning is used to generate the action sets for players. More specifically,
a state lattice planner is chosen for several reasons. The method is rather simple
and a number of paths can be generated that fit the constraints of the given players.
It is also believed that the resulting decision maker is easy to combine with other
planning approaches, when the initial design is done using a simple planner.

1.4
Novelty and Contribution
The results of the game are simulated in a Python environment and evaluated
through part of the Imazu problem. Imazu problem is a series of ship encounter
scenarios, which are considered challenging for collision avoidance and thus suitable
to be used for assessing the collision avoidance ability of autonomous vessels [13].
Similar simulations are provided widely in maritime collision avoidance research.
1.4
Novelty and Contribution
Collision avoidance is a highly researched area in the field of autonomous sur-
face vessels. However, limited research is done on the effects of route exchange on
collision avoidance. Most studies implementing a level of knowledge of target vessels’
future intentions are based on negotiation. Therefore, the significance of the future
level of information sharing regarding route exchange between vessels is yet to be
fully researched. Furthermore, implementation of game-theoretic approaches exhibit
advantages to collision avoidance in various fields, but studies in the maritime field
could still be expanded.
This project proposes a game-theoretic system design which combines frame-
works from various studies, considers COLREGs navigational rules and produces
optimal collision free trajectories. Furthermore, the game-theoretic system is ad-
justed to utilize route-exchange information which to the best knowledge of the
author has not been done to date. The evaluation and comparison of the systems
is believed to contribute positively to research in the field of maritime collision
avoidance.
1.5
Sustainability
This project along with the development of autonomous vessels in general con-
tributes to the Sustainable Development Goals set out by the United Nations to
be reached by 2030. Foremost, the computation of optimal trajectories enhances
safety. Moreover, carbon impact and pollution is decreased, as fuel consumption
is optimised. The advancement of ship autonomy has huge impact on the industry
as well as prompts economic profit, as the use of autonomous vessels lowers cost of
shipping compared to traditional means.
1.6
Thesis Outline
The thesis is organized as follows. Chapter 2 provides relevant background the-
ory to familiarize the reader with concepts related to marine navigation and game
theory, along with an overview of the research done within the field of collision avoid-
ance. Chapter 3 details the approach taken to develop the initial game-theoretic
decision maker based on partial knowledge and later the modifications made in or-
der to implement full knowledge of target vessels’ intentions on the system. The
simulation results are given in Chapter 4 and performance of the two algorithms
are compared and discussed. Chapter 6 draws a conclusion that summarizes the
findings of the thesis and gives suggestions for future work.

2.1
AIS and VDES
Vessels are required to carry communications infrastructure listed under the
Global Maritime Distress and Safety System (GMDSS) as specified in the International
Convention for the Safety of Life at Sea 1974 (SOLAS) by IMO [14].
The Automatic Identification System (AIS) is an integrated element of the
GMDSS mandatory to be used by vessels over a certain size to aid in collision
avoidance. It allows vessels to share current information containing id, position,
course and speed with other AIS-equipped vessels and base stations on shore via
Very High Frequency radio waves (VHF). The information provided by the AIS is
more detailed and accurate than that of the radar and Automatic Radar Plotting
Aid (ARPA) alone, thus enabling more effective radio communication between ships.
AIS uses the VHF band, which unlike the radar is not limited to line-of-sight opera-
tion, therefore the AIS can detect vessels out of view and provide better navigational
overview. Especially, when linked with the onboard Electronic Chart Display and
Information System (ECDIS), AIS is an effective tool for situational awareness and
collision avoidance. Finally, AIS supports Application-Specific Messaging (ASM)
which allows authorities to push forward small text messages such as weather up-
dates. Along its high popularity, ASM causes sever congestion of the AIS system.
In areas with high traﬀic and crowded waters, the AIS system is overloaded, which
represents danger to its core safety objective. [15], [16]
Developments towards autonomous shipping and Arctic traﬀic have raised de-
mand for new systems, which have been recognized in the IMO e-Navigation strategy
[18], one of the goals of which is to enhance safety at sea. The VHF Data Exchange
System (VDES) is the new communication solution to implement the e-Navigation
strategy. VDES is an extension to AIS, linking AIS, ASM, and VHF Data Ex-
change (VDE), to preserve AIS core functions while delivering higher capacity and
global two-way data exchange over VHF. Figure 1 illustrates the VDES informa-
tion flow. The system comprises of two subsystems: a traditional terrestrial com-
ponent (VDE-TER) for ship-to-ship, ship-to-shore and shore-to-ship communica-
tion, and a satellite component (VDE-SAT) for ship-to-satellite and satellite-to-ship
communication. Since VDES is VHF-based, the coverage range of the VDE-TER
system is quite limited. By using robust satellite uplink and downlink channels, the
VDE-SAT component increases the coverage range and enables global communica-
tion and connectivity, which conforms with the requirements of many e-Navigation

2.2
COLREGs
2.2
COLREGs
The International Regulations for Preventing Collisions at Sea 1972 (COLREGs)
is a set of rules published by IMO to standardize safe navigational practice at sea
[4]. COLREGs must be followed by ships and other vessels at sea to prevent colli-
sions during both single and multi vessel encounters. Adhering to the rules is thus a
fundamental prerequisite also for any autonomous surface vessel or decision support
system.
As of 2022, COLREGs include 41 rules and four annexes covering all aspects of
navigation [4], however rules 8 and 13-17, which describe the most common collision
risk scenarios between two power-driven vessels, are the most covered in research.
The rules are described in Table 1.
Rules 13-15 depict the three most common
encounter scenarios - crossing, head-on and overtaking - and are considered the
base requirement for COLREGs compliance. They are illustrated in Figure 3. Fur-
thermore, rules 8 and 16-17 define how a vessel is obliged to behave depending on
whether it is considered a give-way or stand-on vessel. In a crossing scenario, the
vessel that has the target vessel approaching on its starboard (right) side is the
give-way vessel. In an overtaking scenario, the vessel that wishes to overtake is the
give-way vessel. Lastly, in a head-on scenario, both vessels are to give way. [21]

ther side of the stand-on vessel.
Rule 14
Head-on scenario: Both vessels must perform a manoeuvre such that
they pass one another on their respective port sides.
Rule 15
Crossing scenario: The vessel having another vessel approaching on its
starboard side is required to give way by preferably passing behind it.
Rule 16
Give-way vessel behaviour: The give-way vessel should take early action
and remain a safe distance from the stand-on vessel.
Rule 17
Stand-on vessel behaviour: The stand-on vessel should maintain con-
stant speed and course, unless it is apparent that the give-way vessel is
not taking action to avoid collision.
2.3
Game Theory
Game theory is the mathematical study of decision-making of independent ac-
tors in which one’s choices affect and are affected by the interests of other inde-
pendent actors. The origin of modern game theory travels back to the 1930s and
founder John von Neumann. In 1970s it grew significance in engineering, and to-
day has applications in all fields [23]. The structure and mathematical foundations
of game theory also make it a prime tool for modelling and designing automated
decision-making processes in interactive environments as stated in [24].
This section will introduce the game theoretic concepts necessary for the un-
derstanding of the project. [23]–[25] Foremost, to establish a game, the following
fundamental elements must be clearly defined:
• The set of players making the decisions.
• The strategies associated with each player. That is, all the possible actions
that a player can choose from.
• The utilities associated with each action. That is, the payoff a player will re-
ceive or lose if choosing a certain strategy given the strategies of other players.
Most often in game theory it is implicitly assumed that all players are rational,
meaning that one makes decisions with the purpose of maximising or minimising
one’s own objective function. The solution to a game is then a set of combined
strategies that together satisfy a defined optimality condition.
A game can be either cooperative or non-cooperative. In a non-cooperative
game, players act independently towards their own interest, in contrast to cooper-
ative games in which cooperation among players is explicitly enforced. However,
Page 8 of 48
==PAGE:21==
Master’s Thesis
2.3
Game Theory
cooperation can also happen in non-cooperative games, when it happens in players’
own best interest. Furthermore, a games can be divided into zero-sum and nonzero-
sum games. In a zero-sum game, players have opposite objectives and the total
benefit of the game always equals zero. In a nonzero-sum game, the total benefit is
not bounded and the gain of one player does not necessarily correspond to a loss of
another. This project will focus on non-cooperative, nonzero-sum games.
A strategic game is one in which each player decides on an action without
having knowledge of the actions of other players. Inherently this coincides with
simultaneous play in which players choose their strategies simultaneously. A simul-
taneous game is often represented in normal form, i.e. payoff matrices. An example
of a game in normal form is shown in Figure 4. A repeated game is one in which
a strategic game is played repeatedly while assuming that the current decision of a
player is affected by all players’ decisions made in previous rounds.
Figure 4: Simple two-player game in normal form.
Solving a game regards finding the combined set of each player’s chosen strategy
which together satisfy some defined optimality condition. A dominant strategy is
the optimal move and always yields a better payoff (lowest cost) for an individual
player regardless of how the other players act. A security strategy, on the other
hand, is the strategy that guarantees the least possible cost assuming the worst
possible choice of the other player.
Nash equilibrium is one of the most central concepts in game theory and the
most common way of defining a solution to a non-cooperative game. It holds the
notion of no regret in the sense that no player can improve their payoff by deviating
from their chosen strategy while all other players’ strategies remain unchanged.
The mathematical definition of Nash equilibrium varies between pure and mixed
strategies. If a player plays an action with 100% certainty, it is called a pure strategy.
On the other hand, when playing a mixed strategy, a player randomizes between
the available actions according to a predetermined probability distribution. This
project applies pure strategies only (see equations 36-37).
Every finite strategy game has a Nash equilibrium in mixed strategies. However,
in pure strategies one does not necessarily exist, or there can be several. Pareto
optimality refers to an allocation from which it is impossible to deviate from while
mutually improving payoffs of all players, that is it is impossible to reduce the cost
of one player without increasing cost of another. A Nash equilibrium is said to be
Pareto optimal, or admissible, if there is no other Nash equilibrium which improves
the payoff for both players.

Collision Avoidance at Sea
Collision avoidance is a highly researched area in the scope of autonomous
surface vehicles. Comprehensive state-of-the-art reviews analyzing and comparing
different approaches to collision avoidance are available in literature [26]–[28]. The
challenge of developing autonomous collision avoidance systems for the marine en-
vironment was brought up in [29], who argued that full COLREGs compliance is
diﬀicult to establish due to the qualitative nature of the rules, which proposes no-

The work in [30] extended the maritime collision avoidance research of the
time with a COLREGs compliant method of assessing collision risk for close-range
encounters, also for cases in which own vessel is in the stand-on role.
The risk
assessment is based on the dimensions of the safety area, which are conditional on the
relative speeds of the vessels, and the type of encounter, for which a categorization
based on the target vessel’s relative position and heading with respect to the own
ship is provided. The outcome of the study interprets COLREGs more precisely
compared to previous methods, as it eliminates unnecessary maneuvers of the stand-
on vessel without restricting it. The geometrical COLREGs scenario interpretation
scheme was later adopted by [31] and [32].
The algorithm presented in [33] was one of the first implementations of Model
Predictive Control (MPC) used for collision avoidance with COLREGs compliance.
The scenario-based approach computes the optimal trajectory by penalizing risk
and maneuvers against Rules 8 and 13-16. An MPC-based mid-level collision avoid-
ance algorithm using nonlinear programming and a gradient-based approach was
presented in [34]. The study addresses both static and dynamic obstacles, however,
focuses only on the compliance of COLREGs rule 8 of observable maneuvers. A
branching-course MPC method is proposed in [22], which presents increased robust-
ness with respect to noise on obstacle estimates in comparison with previous studies,
and is fully compliant with COLREGs Rules 8, 13, and 17 while favoring maneuvers
following rules 14 and 15.
The study in [31] combined and built on the authors’ previous research to
account for full COLREGs compliance by adhering to Rules 8 and 13-17. They
present a three-layered hybrid collision avoidance system for autonomous surface
vessels, which consists of a high-level planner producing an energy-optimized trajec-
tory [35], the MPC-based mid-level collision avoidance algorithm, and the short-term
branching-course MPC algorithm for emergency situations. The mid-level algorithm
is promoted to include a state machine based on the geometrical COLREGs scenario
interpretation scheme proposed in [30]. The objective function of the hybrid system
consists of four components, of which the COLREGs-specific component utilizes
potential functions that map costs based on the currently applicable rule.
The
presented results indicate that the system leads to collision-free trajectories in all
situations with multiple active COLREGs rules in which target vessels may obey or
violate COLREGs. A similar study is conducted in [32]. The potential functions
for rewarding COLREGs compliant maneuvers are modified to utilize a nonlinear

2.4.2
Collision Avoidance with Route-Exchange
Collision avoidance algorithms in the maritime domain have less emphasis on
collaboration and route exchange compared to algorithms developed for ground and
aerial vehicles, where vehicle-to-vehicle communication is identified even crucial for
collision avoidance [39]. Often proposed methods are dependent on active nego-

trajectories through negotiation between active vessels, while intentions of uncoop-
erative and passive vessels are predicted using a probabilistic model based on AIS
data. The method presents a problem with vessels not carrying AIS transceivers.
However, interest towards the impact of route exchange and the potential ad-
vantages that knowledge of target vessels’ intentions can have on collision avoidance
has recently increased. A review of the maritime collaborative collision avoidance
studies is given in [41], which emphasizes that majority of current research relies on
iterative data exchange and ignores non-cooperative vessels. The lack of interaction
between vessels in existing collision avoidance algorithms was mentioned in [42],
who proposed a decentralized two-ship negotiation protocol and later extended the
system by integrating planned route exchange [43].
The work in [44] presented an Informed Scenario-Based MPC algorithm that
builds on the existing system proposed in [33] by including route exchange between
ships to make trajectory predictions of target vessels. The proposed solution com-
plies also with COLREGs Rule 18 which is not considered in many other papers.
The algorithm displays advantages compared to the classical algorithm, especially in
cases where target ship’s path swifts before encounter. The algorithm works short-
range, thus the authors suggest implementation of interactive negotiation, such that
surrounding vessels acknowledge COLREGs non-compliant intentions. A fallback of
the solution if the tuning process of parameters. A similar study has been conducted
in [45], which concluded that knowledge of other vessels’ intentions improved per-
formance especially in cases where passive obstacle ships did not follow a straight
path and where own ship had stand-on responsibility, both in safety and COLREGs
compliance.

Collision Avoidance Using Game Theory
Safe navigation in the marine domain has also been approached through game-
theoretic frameworks, as the theory suits the dynamic nature of the collision avoid-

3
Methodology and Design
This section details the approach taken to develop the collision avoidance de-
cision maker. The underlying assumptions followed by the path planning approach
are detailed first. Thereafter, the game-theoretic system is designed based on par-
tial information, after which adjustments are made to implement full knowledge of
target vessels’ intentions on the system.
The collision avoidance system and optimal decision maker is designed based
on game theory. The set of actions for each player is created by the planner, which
generates a number of path alternatives to deviate from a potential collision scenario.
The game is designed to be a static, non-cooperative, finite, nonzero-sum matrix
game, in which the players act rationally, namely they act in a way to minimize their
own expected costs. The term non-cooperative does not eliminate the existence of
mutual interdependence. In fact, the collision avoidance and COLREGs compliance
objectives depend on the decisions of all players. Hence, each cost includes both an
independent and an interactive component. Finally, the game is played repeatedly:
After a solution is found for the matrix game, the players move along the chosen
path for a single time step, after which the current state becomes the new starting
state for each player. The planner then generates a set of new paths to the goal
state, and the game is repeated until the player has reached its goal. Although
played repeatedly, it is highlighted that the design does not follow the definition
of a repeated game (see Section 2.3), since the decision made by the players have
no direct influence on the following rounds, in addition to the whole action spaces
being regenerated.
3.1
Assumptions
The design of the system is based on some underlying assumptions. The scope
of the project assumes a predefined nominal path for each player, which is optimized
in terms of safety, environmental and financial objectives, including, for example,
fuel consumption, travel time and water depth. The nominal path is defined as a
set of waypoints, where each waypoint is a state including the position and heading
in the North-East-Down (NED) coordinate frame. It is assumed that the vessel is
equipped with a situational awareness system, which alarms of potential collision
threats occurring on the nominal path at any point of the travel. The designed
collision avoidance system is then triggered, which returns a path deviation that
safely maneuvers around the collision risk. The system design assumes that a path
deviation returns either on the same leg of the nominal path or the one directly
after, i.e.
the path deviation skips at most one waypoint of the nominal path.
Moreover, the scope of the projects limits to the decision maker, thus it is assumed
that necessary control systems are in place to act on the maneuvers.
The design is based on the following additional assumptions:
• Vessels are considered as single points at their center of mass, which is to be
taken into account regarding computed distances.

3.2
Planner
The decision maker is designed to find the optimal path deviation from a num-
ber of path alternatives.
The aim of the planner is to find a selection of path
alternatives from a given starting point to a given end goal. These points are some-
where along the nominal path and defined such that the point of assumed collision
risk is located between them as described in the previous section. For the purpose
of the project, the planner is wanted to be kept fairly simple such that it could be
replaced by another planning algorithm without affecting the decision maker as a
whole. Therefore, an adaptation of the lattice planner is opted for. The state lattice
planner is a graph-based approach that ultimately reduces a path planning problem
into a graph-search problem by discretizing the search space into a repeated and
regular pattern of nodes corresponding to states and edges that are feasible regard-
ing the robot’s kinematic constraints, which can be solved with any appropriate
graph-search algorithm [57]. An example of a state lattice is shown in Figure 5.

3.3
Interactive Costs
The total cost function of each player in the game is divided into an independent
component and an interactive component. The independent costs rely only upon the
actions of the player itself, whereas the interactive cost component is also dependent
on the actions of all other players. The interactive costs comprise of a term for
collision avoidance and a term for COLREGs compliance.

3.6
Independent Costs
The independent costs rely only upon the actions of the player itself. The inde-
pendent cost function can differ between players, as it is outlined by what objectives
a specific player lays the most importance on. The performance metrics can balance
between for example safety, environmental and financial objectives. Furthermore,
each element is multiplied by a weight, which can be used to add more impact on
certain elements.
In this project, the independent component comprises of two elements, path
elongation and deviation from the nominal path. It is important to think about the
actual ramifications of the elements either alone or together. Simply minimising path
length alone could lead to risky situations, for example by leading a vessel to take
short cuts across shallow waters. On the other hand, constraining the deviation to
follow the nominal path too closely would often become infeasible for larger marine
crafts. Therefore, the two elements are considered simultaneously to balance each
other out.

Solving the Game
The game is formed once a set of actions, i.e. a set of path deviations, has been
defined for each player, and each action has been allocated with a cost. The game

is formed as a static, two-player, non-cooperative, finite, pure, nonzero-sum matrix
game that is played again after every time step.
The objective of the game is to minimize the cost of each player. The optimal
solution would, therefore, be to find the Nash equilibrium as described in Section
2.3. In an ideal situation, the game has only one Nash equilibrium, in which case
the optimal solution is found. However, the algorithm must find a solution also to
a game in which either zero or multiple Nash equilibria exist. In the case of no
Nash equilibria, each player moves according to their security policy. In the case of
multiple Nash equilibria, the equilibria pairs are checked for Pareto optimality in
hopes of narrowing them down to one. If multiple Pareto optimal Nash equilibria
remain, the solution is chosen at random between the remaining pairs as is done in
[55].

3.7.2
Nash Equilibrium
Nash equilibrium is a game theoretic concept in which the optimal outcome is
defined such that no player has an incentive to deviate from their chosen strategy.
That is to say, no player can improve their payoff, i.e. decrease their expected cost,
by changing strategy while other players keep theirs unchanged. A game may have
zero, single, or multiple Nash equilibria. [61]
The Nash equilibrium is found by first identifying the best strategy of P1 in
response to each strategy of P2, and vice versa. If the same pair of actions is chosen
both ways, that strategy pair is a Nash equilibrium (γ∗, σ∗). Figure 10 illustrates
the approach in a simple game.

In an ideal situation, the game has only one Nash equilibrium, in which case
the optimal solution is found. However, if the game has multiple Nash equilibria,
the algorithm checks for ones which are admissible or Pareto optimal, and discards
the inadmissible allocations. Pareto optimality means that it is impossible to cut
the cost of one player without raising the cost of at least one other player [25].
The algorithm compares the cost for each player of two different Nash equilibrium
allocations, and if the costs of both player are higher in one allocation, the pair is
no longer in consideration.
Ideally, the game is left with one admissible Nash equilibrium, which would
then act as the solution to the game. In case a game has multiple admissible Nash
equilibria, research says that players must communicate or collaborate in some way
to eliminate the possibility of regret otherwise it is unavoidable [61]. In this project,
if the game is left with multiple Pareto optimal Nash equilibria, the solution is chosen
at random among them as is done in [55]. Due to the random nature, the game may
lead to varying outcomes despite identical starting points and other parameters.
Finally, the chosen equilibrium trajectories of the current round are added to
the players’ action sets for the next iteration considering the movement progressed
within the elapsed time.
3.8
Route Exchange
Up to this point, the system has been designed solely based on the starting
view of each iteration.
The design has utilized straight-line prediction methods
and assumed no changes in time per iteration. For example, CPA is a straight-line

scenario at each iteration is found based on the starting position and applied on the
entire path without considering changes along the way.

Results and Discussion
The following section details the simulation results obtained from applying the
proposed framework on several different scenarios. A comparison of results between
the partial knowledge (straight-line trajectory prediction based on AIS data) and full
knowledge (implementing route exchange between vessels through VDES) systems
is provided and discussed. The design is written and tested through simulations
in a Python environment. The performance of the system is tested on all of the
three most common encounter scenarios - crossing, head-on and overtaking. The
simulations focus on single vessel encounters, however the system was also briefly
tested on an encounter of three vessels. Additionally, the simulations vary in the
degree of complexity of the nominal route.
The implementation of both own and target vessels loosely utilize the ship
parameters and operational profiles of a medium-to-large merchant vessel, Luna
Maersk [62], which has a length of 230 meters and nominal cruising speed of 25
knots (12.5 m/s). A time step of tstep = 30 seconds is used for all simulations, in
which a vessel at nominal speed advances 375 meters.

It is derived from the values in Table 6 that the full knowledge system results in a
safer distance between vessels, whereas the partial knowledge system displays better
performance regarding path elongation and following the nominal path.
The larger evasive maneuver of the full knowledge system is due to the tuning
of the safety distance and other parameters of the collision risk in Equation 40.
Paths with larger curve are outside of the risk range and thus gain zero interac-
tive costs but higher independent costs, whereas straighter paths gain both risk of
collision and COLREGs cost, which also have more weight than the independent
costs. Furthermore, the collision costs in the two systems are calculated differently;
the partial knowledge system has Boolean cost, which means that a path gains cost
only if a collision was to happen. In retrospect, cost for risk of collision could be
implemented in both systems.

4.3
Discussion
Overall, the design implementing full information shows improved performance
in regards to safety in comparison to collision avoidance with partial information.
The results show that with using route exchange information, own ship is able to
maneuver in the correct direction considering the future states of the target vessel’s
nominal path and maintain a safe distance from any risk of collision, whereas a false
prediction of the target vessel’s future trajectory may mislead the own ship into
riskier situations. However, it would not be correct to blindly assume that the target
ship follows strictly its nominal path. Therefore, a supervisory control mechanism
could be implemented to monitor whether the target ship follows its planned path
as suggested in [44]. If the route exchange information was to contain a value for
cross-track error, it could be compared to some threshold to decide whether to trust
the nominal route waypoints or to convert back to straight-line prediction based on
the current positional data.
Furthermore, it could be argued that the modified system does not adhere to
COLREGs, as maneuvers are taken against Rules 13-15. This could be the case
if avoidance action is taken too close to the encounter. However, route exchange
facilitates preemptive maneuvering and thus a situation requiring collision avoidance
and COLREGs compliance can be eliminated. Nonetheless, it is important that the
vessels comply to Rule 8 of easily observable maneuvers, so that the intention of the
give-way vessel’s non-compliance can be acknowledged by other ships, in case they
decide to sway from their nominal path and start COLREGs compliant maneuvers
that could result in close encounters.
Tuning of the parameters was a laborious process, as both systems were sensi-
tive to fluctuations. The potential functions for the different COLREGs scenarios
returned values in different ranges, therefore the weights were iteratively tuned to
give comparable values. This was even more challenging for the modified system,
as the scenario could change at every time step along a path, and it had to be
guaranteed that no scenario would return too high or too low cost in relation to
other scenarios. Otherwise, the game would always favor path combinations with

Due to the use of game theory, the decision maker expresses a level of unpre-
dictability caused by the random choice of a solution in cases of multiple admissible
Nash equilibria, which is visible during simulation mainly for the partial knowledge
system. Figure 20 demonstrates the impact multiple admissible Nash equilibria can
have on the chosen path. A difference can be seen on the first half of the own ship’s

very apparent when the total path consists of fewer iterations, but as the number
of iterations increases the effect usually balances out. Overtaking scenarios make
an exception. As the target vessel can be passed on either side, the game may have
symmetrical set of solutions with respect to own ship’s initial heading and the di-
rection of the path deviation may be fully determined at random by the game. This
was observed in Figure 19. As mentioned, the partial knowledge system displays a
clear limitation in an overtaking encounter compared to the system implementing
route exchange. If the design were to be used on a manned or partly autonomous
vessel, the final decision could be confirmed over radio to guarantee safety.

5
Conclusion
The thesis aimed to explore the effects of route exchange between vessels on
collision avoidance, by comparing the performance of a decision making system
based on varying levels of knowledge regarding the target ship’s trajectory. The
decision maker was designed as a non-cooperative game that is played repeatedly,
first utilizing straight-line prediction methods based on AIS data, and then extended
to include full trajectory information obtained via the VDES.
The design process comprised of creating a lattice planner for computing pos-
sible path deviations for all vessels, which were then assessed through various cost
functions, and finally determining the optimal path to avoid collision while adhering
to COLREGs and minimizing path elongation, by finding the admissible Nash equi-
libria. The collision assessment of each path regarding the partial knowledge system
was based on CPA, whereas a function for computing the risk of collision was im-
plemented for the full knowledge system, as it supports dynamic trajectories better.
COLREGs compliance was imposed through the use of potential fields that favor
compliant maneuvers in regards to the common encounter scenarios. The encounter
type was determined based on the relative bearing and heading of the target vessel
with respect to the own ship.
The system was tested through simulations of crossing, head-on and overtaking
scenarios and evaluated with respect to safety and performance. The system avoided
collisions successfully with both levels of information. Yet, utilizing route exchange
information improved collision avoidance performance in situations where target
vessel did not follow a straight trajectory. Major improvement regarding safety was
evident especially during the overtaking encounter. However, the evasive maneuvers
of the full knowledge system can be regarded as unnecessarily large, while the partial
knowledge system leads to shorter paths with relatively smaller distance between
vessels. Optimality regarding path length and deviation from nominal path could
be improved through finer tuning of parameters.
5.1
Future Research
The proposed design showed promising results indicating that route exchange
between vessels can improve collision avoidance performance. However, much can
be done to improve the system.
Foremost, finer tuning of the system parameters can generate better results.
The project also focused on simulations with single vessel encounter, and expanding
to broader multi vessel encounter testing is essential and could rise new points of
interest. Furthermore, the design was based on several simplifying assumptions.
Thus, the system could be made more applicable to realistic scenarios by consid-
ering change in speed, turning radii of vessels, static obstacles and grounding, or
implementing additional performance objectives to optimize over.
The current design with a full information space considers only the nominal
path for the target vessel’s action space. The advantages of utilizing a game-theoretic
framework could be emphasized by broadening the action space in a similar manner
