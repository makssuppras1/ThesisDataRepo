Abstract
This thesis has the goal of investigating parameters for enzyme immobilization on a poly-
meric ultrafiltration membrane to further the research of enzymatic membrane systems
(EMS). Two enzymes, alcohol dehydrogenase (ADH) and laccase, were immobilized on
a commercial polysulfone membrane (GR51PP). The enzymes were immobilized via two
methods, incubation and filtration.
Furthermore, membranes were modified with MoS2
nanoparticles to investigate the impact on the enzyme immobilization procedure and the
enzyme activity. The study utilizes a range of membrane characterization techniques, in-
cluding FTIR spectroscopy, water contact angle and zeta potential measurements.
The
produced EMS were evaluated on performance characteristics such as immobilization yield,
enzyme activity and water permeability. The results showed that the MoS2 modification
had positive impact on enzyme loading and activity, particularly for ADH immobilized via
incubation. Filtration based methods generally showed a higher biocatalytic activity of the
membrane due to the high immobilization yield, but at the cost of membrane permeability.
The findings in this report demonstrate the potential of combining and exploring immobi-
lization methods with membrane modification to improve EMS for sustainable biochemical
processes.

Objectives
The goal of this thesis is to document the efficacy of enzyme immobilization on a poly-
meric ultrafiltration membrane. The enzymes that will be experimented with are alcohol
dehydrogenase (ADH) and laccase. The immobilization will be performed in two ways, a
common method of adsorption of the enzymes onto the selective layer of the membrane, and
a more novel method, of entrapment by filtration into the support structure of the mem-
brane. Furthermore, the membrane will either be pristine or modified with molybdenum
disulfide (MoS2), an inorganic compound that historically has been used as a lubricant, in
catalysis or microelectronics, but which has recently show potential as a novel membrane
modification substance. By investigating and mapping the behavior of this mixture of con-
ventional and novel techniques and materials, it is desired to further develop the knowledge
of enzymatic membranes for use in green chemical processes.
The goals of this thesis can be summarized as follows:
To investigate and evaluate enzyme immobilization on membranes to increase performance
for use in green bio refinery processes by:
• Investigating and comparing two immobilization methodologies (adsorption and en-
trapment) on two different enzymes (ADH and laccase)
• Characterizing the impact of membrane modification by MoS2 on the membrane sur-
face properties and the reuslting effect on the enzyme immobilization
• Evaluating the produced enzymatic membrane reactors on performance parameters
such as enzyme loading, specific enzyme activity and water permeability

Literature review
At this point in time, enzymes are a staple of many industries due to their advantageous
catalytic properties. Enzymes are often used in their free form, but are also sometimes
immobilized onto or into a surface, to promote thermal and pH stability or for ease of sep-
aration. A relatively recent innovation in the world of enzymes is the enzymatic membrane
reactor (EMR) where enzymes and membranes are used in conjunction to take advantage
of the many possibilities that arise with simultaneous catalytic reaction and separation[1].
One subset of these EMRs is the biocatalytic membrane, where the enzymes are immobi-
lized specifically on or in the membrane. To summarize, these enzyme-membrane systems
(EMS) are able to facilitate continuous operation, increase enzyme stability, re-usability
and sometimes activity, increase reaction rate by performing in-situ product removal, thus
controlling equilibrium or removing products before they are reacted into by-products, mit-
igate the effects of pollutants or inhibitors and much more. Currently, the technology is
immature, and production is costly and complex, but this degree of complexity is necessary
to achieve the ambitions of green bio-manufacturing and bio-refinery. Apart from having a
direct role in these chemical processes, EMS also have potential to be utilized in biosensors,
bioremediation, carbon dioxide capture and more [2].
Apart from the general arguments, this work is further motivated by work done previously
by the group. From initial development of a simple but novel, fouling based immobilization
method [3], to refinement of the same model [4], the exploration of adsorption-based immo-
bilization, membrane modification and development of the methodologies associated with
this [5], [6].
1.1
Enzymes
Enzymes are biocatalysts, a group comprised almost entirely of complex protein molecules,
that exists at the foundation of all living things.
They are capable of accelerating the
speed of a chemical reaction, promoting the conversion of a specific substrate into a specific
product. All living cells utilize enzymes as the basis for their metabolism. Without enzymes,
most of the reactions necessary to sustain life, would not occur at the required, or even at
a quantifiable rate. The key characteristics of enzymes are indeed their selectivity, their
efficiency and their required reaction conditions. Enzymes are highly specialized catalyst

promoting only a single reaction or few, closely related reactions. The selectivity is a result
of the complex, but consistent 3-dimensional structure of any given type of enzymes. By
promoting a specific reaction mechanism for a given reaction they facilitate a high efficiency
both in terms of reaction speed and limiting of side rations. It is normal to observe increased
reaction speeds of 106 −107 times faster than the uncatalyzed reactions and the formation
of wasteful by-products are typically limited. Finally, enzymes, being biological molecules,
operate optimally at very mild conditions (low temperature and pH) which makes them
especially interesting for any process that is economically or sustainably conscientious[7].
Almost all known enzymes are proteins and, as such, consist of polypeptide chains of amino
acids. Amino acids are a group of organic compounds containing an amino- and a car-
boxylic acid group. They are bound together into linear chains via peptide bonds, resulting
in polypeptides, or proteins. A large number of amino acids exists, however, only around 20
are commonly seen in enzymes and proteins. The ordering and number of these 20 different
amino acids is called the primary structure of the protein, and is what ultimately decides
the functionality and properties of the greater molecule. What separates each amino acid, in
the context of proteins, is the side group. Different side groups impart different properties to
the protein molecule. This may be hydrophilic or hydrophobic properties, or the ability to
partake in hydrogen bonding or salt bridge formation. In particular, the hydrophobicity of
certain amino acid side chains is thought to be one of the strongest thermodynamic driving
forces behind the protein folding into its secondary and tertiary structures, as the pro-
tein folds to isolate these groups from the aqueous environment. The secondary structures
are localized patterns of amino acid folding which appear very frequently. The two most
common are alpha-helices (where amino acids form a helix with residues pointing outward,
stabilized by hydrogen bonds) and beta-sheets (where linear sections of the polypeptides are
linked, also by hydrogen bonding), although several other less frequent structures are also
observed. The tertiary structure relates to the arrangement of the primary and secondary
structures in 3D-space. The folding of the protein brings together different parts of the
amino acid chain. This can both lead to stabilizing interactions between side chains, but
also the formation of folds and pockets, which can be chemically reactive leading to what
is known as the active site of the enzyme. Here, a specific substrate may bind initiating a
chemical reaction. Finally, sometimes two or more distinct polypeptide chains are required
for bio-catalytic activity. When this is the case, each chain is identified as a subunit in

the quaternary structure of the protein. If several copies of the same subunit are required
in the quaternary structure it is denoted a homomultimer, while different subunits result
in a heteroomultimer. It is important to note that even though the 3D structure is highly
complex, the native conformational structure of a protein is believed to be completely deter-
mined by the amino acid sequence of the polypeptide chain. This has been demonstrated by
reversibly denaturing proteins in vitro, removing the denaturing agent, and then observing
the proteins reestablishing their native conformations[8].
Many chemical reactions cannot be completed by amino acids alone. As such, some enzymes
incorporate non-enzyme molecules into their conformations. These molecules are known as
co-factors. Common co-factors are metal ions, vitamins and other small organic compounds.
If an enzyme requires a co-factor for catalytic activity the enzyme without the co-factor
is known as the apoenzyme while the complex of enzyme and co-factor is known as the
holoenzyme.[8]
The mechanism of action of enzymes can be described as the substrate binding to the
active site of the enzyme forming an enzyme-substrate complex. The binding of the sub-
strate induces a conformational change of the enzyme, a transitional state, which initiates
the reaction. At this time the complex is denoted as the transition-state complex. Upon
completed reaction, the products dissociate from the complex and the enzyme returns to
its initial state. This interaction was originally described by Emil Fisher using the Fisher
model (or the Lock and Key model). He used the analogy of a key (the substrate) fitting
into a lock (the enzyme) to articulate the specificity of the reaction.
However scientist
took issue with the models implied rigidity of the enzymes. As a response to this, many
years later, Koshland introduced the induced fit model which alleviates this problem, and
also includes the concept of inhibition.[7] Today it is understood that the catalytic activity
of enzymes is highly impacted by its degree of flexibility and the possibility to transition
between many different micro-states. These micro-states may be heavily influence by the
conditions surrounding the enzymes. This includes pH and ionic strength of the solution
as well as the temperature and presence of inhibitory species. As such, all enzymes have
specific conditions that must be met to optimize their catalytic activities. Furthermore, any
modification to the native conformation of the enzyme via chemical reactions or interactions
may result in partial or complete inhibition of the catalytic activity[9].

1.1.1
Alcohol dehydrogenase
Alcohol dehydrogenases (ADHs) are a large group of enzymes that catalyze the reversible
oxidation of alcohols to aldehydes or ketones utilizing the cofactors NAD+ or NADP+.
These types of enzymes ar known as oxidoreductases. They have been observed in many
different organisms and domains, namely vertebrates, plants, eukaryotic microorganisms
and prokaryotic bacteria[10]. Depending on the species they can have many different amino
acid sequences, conformations and emply different cofactors. The ADH used in this project
is derived from Saccharomyces cerevisiae (Brewer’s yeast) specifically yeast alcohol dehy-
drogenase 1 (YADH1). Based on sequencing of the genome of S. cerevisiae, genes encoding
seven differen YADHs has been identified, however only the first three (YADH1, YADH2 and
YADH3) have widespread application. They are all zinc containing homotetramers, each
monomer containing a structural zinc ion and a zinc ion in the active center. Of the three,
YADH1, is present in the highest concentration. It is a constitutive enzyme that is respon-
sible for the main conversion of glucose to ethanol under anaerobic fermentation. YADH2
is glucose suppressed, but expressed when ethanol is used as a carbon source and mainly
converts stored ethanol to acetaldehyde. YADH3 is separate in that it is mitochondrial
located and present in relatively small quantities[11].
Alcohol dehydrogenase have many industrial applications and are especially coveted for their
broad substrate range, good selectivity and sustainable reaction requirements. Especially
their enantioselectivity is important as the production of chiral alcohols is necessary to be
further synthesized into chiral drugs [12]. The production of these chiral pharmaceutical
intermediates have seen an increase in interest over the last two decades. Chiral pharma-
ceuticals may be items such as anti-depressants, anti-anxiety drugs, anti-cance drugs and
anti-hypertension drugs[13]. ADH is also vital in biorefineries for the production of increas-
ingly attractive bulk chemicals such as γ-valerolactone (synthesis of fuel and polymers),
2,3-butanediol (platform chemical for industrial solvents, synthetic rubber and potentially
aviation fuel) and phenylethanols (optically active variants are important in production of
fine chemicals)[13–15].

1.1.2
Laccase
Laccase is a large group of copper-containing oxidoreductases. They catalyse the oxidation
of several phenolic and non-phenolic compounds including many environmental pollutants,
with the concomitant reduction of molecular oxygen to water. [19]. Although laccase was
first observed in the roots of a Japanese lacquer tree the most common and most widely
studied types are found in fungi. Wood degrading fungi, such as white-rot, use laccase to
break down cellulose and lignin for nutrients[20]. The laccase used in this project is derived
from Trametes Versicolor a common and widespread type of white-rot fungus. Laccase is
not currently widely used in industry, but has the potential to be applied to many indus-
tries including food, cosmetics, paper and pulp, textiles and as a tool for bioremediation.
As an example, the removal of polyphenols and reduction of molecular oxygen has potential
benefits for the quality and stability of wines, beers and fruit juices. In the textile industry,
effluents containing waste dyes can be treated with laccase systems, but laccase can also
be used in the manufacture of dyes and for cloth production and washing detergents. For
treatment of wastewater containing dyes, an immobilized laccase systems has great poten-
tial. Furthermore, in the context of bioremediation, laccase-based systems can be used to
eradicate some types of pesticides and herbicides[21]. The use-cases for laccase only increase
when mediators with high redox potential such as 2,2’-azino-bis(3-ethylbenzothiazoline-6-
sulfonic acid) (ABTS) is also utilized. ABTS is a possible substrate of laccase and is used
in the experimental section of this project as an indicator of laccase activity due to its in-
tensively colored blue-green radical form. Mediators are low-molecular weight compounds
with high redox potential that can be oxidized by the enzyme, after which they diffuse from
the enzyme and (non-enzymatically) oxidases the substrate. This allows for the oxidation
of molecules which are incompatible with the active site of the enzyme (such as polymer

1.2
Enzyme immobilization
The term ”immobilized enzymes” have been cited as having many different definitions,
however to paraphrase the most common they are ”Enzymes which are physically confined
or localized in a certain defined region of space with retention of their catalytic activities, and
which can be used repeatedly and continuously”[23–25]. Although this definition includes
some relevant points and generally accepted requirements, it also falls somewhat short of
communicating the complete idea.
Immobilization of enzymes is a group of techniques
attempting to mitigate some of the inherent, undesirable traits in native enzymes whilst also
combating some of the issues that arise from using them outside of their natural context. It
involves fixating enzymes on or in a support material utilizing a method of immobilization
(of which many exist). Enzymes, having evolved in the context of cell metabolism, work
on natural substrates, operate primarily at physiological conditions and may be inhibited
by high substrate or product concentrations[26]. These traits are constraints on the design
of an optimal process. They can probably most completely be mitigated using disciplines
such as protein engineering but, as will be demonstrated, can also be alleviated by chemical
engineering. When using enzymes (genetically engineered or natural) in industrial processes,
the main issues are the enzyme activity, stability and irrecoverably. Using free enzymes in
a reactor is the most simple optimization of the enzyme activity, as this allows for complete
conformational freedom of the enzyme and high substrate exposure. However the isolated,
soluble enzymes are very unstable and readily denatures in response to temperature, pH or
shear stress. Upon completed reaction it is often desirable to recover unspent enzymes due to
the high material cost, however this is also normally a costly process in of itself. As such, the
goal of immobilizing enzymes should be threefold. First, the stability of the enzymes should
be greatly increased. Second, this should be achieved without too significant a detriment to
the activity of the enzyme. Third, the immobilization should allow for easy isolation and
recovery of the enzymes and supports[24]. An additional point of interest would be that the
design of the enzyme/support-system can be done intelligently to promote other desirable
traits not included in the above points. An example of this would be enzymes immobilized in
a micro-porous particle protecting it from hydrophobic interfaces in an aggressively stirred

The first attempt at immobilizing enzymes to improve its properties has been identified
as Grubhofer and Schleith in 1953. They immobilized several different enzymes, including
carboxypeptidase and ribonuclease, using a polyaminepolystyrene resin. The following two
decades include several studies into different immobilization techniques and the properties of
the immobilized enzymes. In 1969 a standardization of a continuous industrial process uti-
lizing immobilized aminoacylase was completed. This achievement most likely contributed
to the fact that two years after (in 1971) the first Enzyme Engineering Conference was held.
Here the term immobilized enzyme was recommended[21, 25]. Since then, a great deal of
research has been carried out regarding immobilized enzymes. Efforts have been made to
map the behavior and requirements of a multitude of enzymes, the benefits and drawbacks
of different support materials and immobilization techniques, and the development of new
ones. More complex systems involving several enzymes have also been documented, for ex-
ample, used in the regeneration of spent co-factors[25]. Despite several decades of research,
Bolivar et al. concludes that our understanding is nowhere near exhaustive. They argue that
many of the currently existing protocols are poorly optimized and often based on a weak
or arbitrary foundation. The design of an optimal immobilization protocol requires careful
selection and experimentation of different support materials and immobilization techniques
for any given enzyme in any given process. Furthermore, innovations in material sciences,
reactor engineering, protein chemistry, organic chemistry and bio-process engineering will
continue to provide new avenues that may be exploited for more economical and sustainable
bio-processes. As such, much further research should be conducted on the topic[27].
Many methods of immobilization have been developed over the decades and the methods
have been grouped in different manners depending on the perspective of the author. For
the purpose of this chapter, the grouping of methods outlined by Nguyen et al. will be
referenced[24]. They divide the known methods into four categories, namely immobilization
by adsorption (utilizing weaker bonds), covalent bonding (between the enzyme and the
support material), entrapment (physically restricting the movement of the enzyme), and
cross-linking (aggregating multiple enzymes together). Most methods consist of three parts,
the enzyme, a supporting material onto or into which the enzyme is immobilized and linkers
or modifiers which facilitates or supports the reaction that results in the immobilization.

1.2.1
Adsorption
Adsorption includes methods based on weaker bonds such as Van der Waal’s forces, hydrogen
bonds, ionic bonds, hydrophobic interactions, and more. The type of interaction applied
depends heavily on the selection of enzyme and support material. This type of immobi-
lization is generally considered reversible. Adsorption based on physical properties of the
enzyme and support, utilizing Van der Waal’s force or hydrogen bonds, is one of the simplest
and weakest methods of enzyme immobilization. It typically involves soaking the support
material in an enzyme-containing solution for a relatively long period of time, allowing for
the adsorption of enzyme molecules onto the surface of the support. The bond formed is
very weak and results in little to no conformational impact on the enzyme. However, the
weak bonds may also lead to enzyme leakage during operation. Adsorption is not driven by
formation of chemical bonds but instead entropically by the hydrophobicity of the enzyme
and support. The dispersion of water molecules near the surface of the to species result in an
entropic gain leading to attraction. The strength of the bond is highly dependent on on the
hydrophobicity of each species. Finally, ionic bonds utilizes negative and positive charges
on the surface of the support and enzyme to facilitate bonding. The surface of the enzyme
may be charged either way depending on the relative pH of the solution to its isoelectric
point. The support surface may either be intrinsically charged or modified using linkers.
An example of such linkers could be poly-electrolytes, which will be explored further later

in the report. All of the above mentioned immobilization methods are relatively weak and
may be highly impacted by the pH, temperature, and salt concentration of the operating
solution. However, the reversibility of the reaction may lead to a simpler recovery of either
the support material or the enzymes.[24, 25, 28]
1.2.2
Covalent bonding
Covalent bonding is one of the most used methods of immobilizing enzymes. The stable
covalent bonds result in low levels of enzymes being release during operation, but also
promotes increased thermal-, chemical-, and storage stability of the enzymes. The process
of creating covalent bonds between support and enzyme often involves activation of the
surface or enzyme as well as the use of linker molecules. One conventional way of achieving
this is by bonding a carboxyl group at the support surface with an amine group in the
enzyme using a carbodimide group. An amine group present in a functionalized support
and a carboxyl group on the enzyme could also be used. Alternatively, two amine groups,
one present on the support and on on the enzyme could also be bonded using a linker such as
glutaraldehyde. Other functional groups could also be available for bonding and apart from
amino-, and carboxylic groups are thiol groups also commonly used. It is of great importance
that the groups participating in the bonding are not too close to or part of the active cite
of the enzyme as this will hinder the activity. One way of mitigating this is to perform the
bonding in the presence of the substrate or an substrate analogue to promote bonding at
other areas of the enzyme. The covalent bonding is considered an irreversible process. The
consequence of this is, that when the enzymes inevitably denatures, the support material as
well as the enzymes must be discarded. On unique exception to this rule is if the bonding
is achieved through disulfide bonds. This method allows for the bonds to be broken again
under mild conditions utilizing a suitable agent.[24, 25, 28]
1.2.3
Entrapment
Entrapment functions by locking the enzymes within the support matrix, thus physically
restricting movement of the enzymes. This is typically done by utilizing a polymeric support
material to which the enzymes are added before the polymerization reaction. A solution is
created of the monomeric units that are precursors to the polymer matrix and the enzymes.
The polymerization reaction is then initiated and the monomers cross-links trapping the
enzymes within. Not all types of polymers are applicable for this type of use, as the initia-

triggered by thermal decomposition or ionizing radiation are examples of non-compatible
processes. On the other hand, both electrochemical polymerization and photopolymerization
have been successful used to entrap enzymes. A sub-category of this type of immobilization
is micro-encapsulation. Here, enzymes are enclosed in spherical semipermeable membranes.
The enzymes may move freely within the membrane but cannot cross the boundary. All
types of entrapment are reliant on the fact that both the substrate and the product are able
to move through the support/membrane. As such, it is not feasible to use this methods when
substrate or product sizes are to large or when other physical properties hinder mobility.
Another drawback is that neither enzyme nor supports can readily be recovered from each
other.[24, 25, 28]
1.2.4
Cross-linkning
Cross-linking is an immobilization technique that generally does not require a support ma-
terial.
The cross-linking is between individual enzymes resulting in cross-linked enzyme
aggregates (CLEAs). A common way of achieving this is with glutaraldehyde which, as pre-
viously described, is able to covalently bond to two amino groups. One principle feature of
this method is the low amount of non-catalytic mass introduced in comparison to the other
immobilization techniques. CLEAs are the current iteration of a classical immobilization
technique called cross-linked enzyme crystals (CLECs), which removes the need for high
purity enzymes and a complex crystallization process. The size and physical properties of
the CLEAs depend on the type of enzyme which is immobilized, however, they do gener-
ally show a certain elasticity allowing them to momentarily deform in multiple directions.
Despite this, they are readily isolated from dispersion by centrifugation or filtration.[24, 25,
29]
1.3
Membranes
Generally speaking, a membrane is a barrier through which different chemical species pass
at different rates[30]. This semipermeability allow them to be used in the separation of
undesired materials from a feed solution using either size of the species affinity towards the
membrane as the exclusion principle. The concept of membranes was first described in 1748
by the scientist Abb´e Nolle when he demonstrated the concept of osmosis using a pigs blad-
11 of 64
==PAGE:20==
DTU
Chemical and Biochemical Technology
June 27, 2025
der, water and an alcohol containing solution. It was only by the end of the 19th century
that artificially created membranes became more widespread in the scientific community,
with Bechhold devising a method of reproducibly creating micro-porous nitrocellulose mem-
branes in 1907. Many different types of membranes made from different materials were
discovered and developed during the 20th century. However, it is only during the last 50
years that membrane technology has become mature enough to be widely applicable and
found use cases in both chemical, biochemical and pharmaceutical industries[31]. Today,
membranes are used in everything from medical devices and transdermal patches, to water
purification and sewage treatment, and a large variety of downstream processes[30].
Membranes are of particular interest as support materials for enzymes for use in enzymatic
membrane reactors (EMRs). EMRs combine enzymatic catalysis and membrane separation
in a single reactor unit. The concept of EMRs will be explored in greater detail in chapter
1.4. In addition to the generic beneficial attributes that can be achieved by immobilizing
enzymes, as described previously, by using membranes, one can ensure complete rejection of
all enzymes in the permeate stream. Fine-tuning of the process is also possible with respect
to the reaction time and the presence of other chemical species in the permeate. Finally,
the simultaneous enzymatic reaction and membrane separation is a great example of process
intensification. By reducing the number of unit operations and the footprint of the overall
process the financial viability and the sustainability increases[32].
Membranes can be made from both organic and inorganic materials. Organic polymeric
membranes, such as polysulfone, polyethersulfone, polyacrylonitride or polyamide-imide are
most commonly used, both in laboratory and industrial settings.
Inorganic membranes
can be made from metals, ceramics or zeolite materials.
They generally show superior
properties having a higher mechanical strength and being more chemically and thermally
resistant. They are, however, also significantly more expensive to manufacture than most
organic membranes[33].
Most membranes function either by size exclusion or chemical affinity between the permeants
and the membrane. Size exclusion is the most easily visualized. Here the micro-porous
structure of the membrane simply holds back any particle larger than the pore size. For
this type of membranes, the pore-flow model has been used for over a century to describe
the mechanism of permeation. It is worth noting that the relationship between permeability
and pore size is not always obvious, as the internal structure of the membrane may not

==PAGE:21==
DTU
Chemical and Biochemical Technology
June 27, 2025
allow for direct passage through it. Winding paths or dead ends are to be found in many
membranes. As such, particles smaller than the observed pore size of the membrane may
still be retained.
An effective pore size is instead used denoting the smallest particles
that are completely retained by the membrane. Differing from size exclusion membranes,
membranes that function by utilizing affinity, are described by the solution-diffusion model.
These membranes are dense, without a porous structure. Instead the permeant solubilize
into the membrane and diffuses across it. As such, the modeling of the permeability of this
type of membrane must include both the solubilization and the diffusion. Finally, there are
also membranes that exist on the border between the two models, showing characteristics
of both[30].
Membranes may be further classified as either isotropic (in this context meaning symmet-
rical) or anisotropic (here meaning asymmetrical). Isotropic membranes have a uniform
structure throughout, but may either be non-porpous or micro-porous. This type of mem-
branes is generally only used in research, mainly for the investigation of the membrane
properties. The reason for this is that any membrane needs a certain thickness to have any
mechanical strength, a thickness that typically greatly surpasses the actual required thick-
ness to carry out the separation. As such, the flow rate through the membrane becomes
unreasonably, and unnecessarily, low. Anisotropic membranes will generally have a thin
layer of micro-porous or dense membrane material, situated on top of a more open structure
that is primarily there for support. This allows for a separation with sufficient selectivity
but much higher permeability and mechanical strength than the isotropic counterpart[30].
1.3.1
Common membrane processes
This section will briefly introduce the most commonly used types of membranes specifically
the microfiltration (MF) membrane, ultrafiltration (UF) membrane, nanofiltration (NF)
membrane and reverse osmosis (RO). One commonality of all these membrane types is that
the driving force behind the separation is a pressure difference.
MF is the term used to describe filters capable of separating particles with a diameter
between 0.1 and 10 µm from a solution. Thus, MF membranes are capable of removing
many particles, viruses and bacteria. The separation is driven by a pressure gradient, and
due to the relatively open sutrcture of the membrane, relatively low pressure is required
(<2 bar). The main principle behind the separation is size exclusion also called sieving,

be classified as having a 0.2 µm effective pore diameter the actual pore diameter may be
several times larger. This is due to the fact that the particles may be caught in the internal
structure of the membrane or adsorb to the surfaces of the internal geometries. This type of
MF membrane is called a depth filter, as the effective pore diameter decreases with increased
depth (or height) of the membrane. In contrast to this, screen filters also exist, where the
entire separation is completed on the surface of the membrane. This is achieved by having
pore sizes equivalent to or smaller than the effective pore diameter. The benefit of using a
depth filter is that the fouling, the clogging of the membrane by retained particles, is spread
out across the depth of the membrane, making it less impactful compared to a screen filter.

of any other kind. MF membranes are typically characterized either by testing their in situ
capabilities to retain either microorganisms or microscopic latex spheres, or by the bubble

correlated to the pore size[31, 34].

the dominating principle is size exclusion. This allows for the separation of all proteins

more costly to operate than micro- or ultrafiltration membranes[31, 33].

across a semipermeable membrane from a low salt concentration solution into a higher
salt concentration solution.

1.3.2
Properties and characterization
This section will touch of some of the main properties, methods of characterization and
potential challenges connected to the operation of membranes. Although much of the theory
is applicable to a broad range of membrane types, the section primarily concerns itself with
UF membranes, as they are the ones used in the experimental section of the project.
The properties of a membrane is generally divided into two categories, one related to perfor-
mance and one to morphology. The morphological characteristics and properties of different
types of membranes has already been discussed in section 1.3 and 1.3.1. However, in sum-
mary, these parameters are parameters such as pore size, pore size distribution, membrane
thickness, pore shape, and several physicochemical properties such as adsorptive properties
and charge density. Performance related properties are ones that directly relate to the mem-
branes property of separation within a reasonable time frame. As such, these are properties
such as permeability, rejection and rejection specificity, as well as solution and diffusion
coefficients. Currently, much work is done to optimize these performance related properties
as they directly influence the viability of any process they are incorporated into[37].
Due to the importance of the membrane flux the most extensively measured parameter of
membranes is the pure water flux. Pure water flux through the membrane is highly depen-
dent on many membrane characteristics such as the pore size and pore size distribution, the
tortuosity and thickness of the membrane as well as the hydrophilicity, hydrophobicity and
16 of 64
==PAGE:25==
DTU
Chemical and Biochemical Technology
June 27, 2025
other physicochemical properties of the membrane. As such, it is not necessarily indicative
of membrane performance during actual operation as the solution and operating conditions
may impact one or more of these. However, it is widely used as a base line and and as a
parameter for communicating the performance of individual membranes, most often pristine
membranes.
Zeta potential is another important parameter and is an expression of the solid surface
charge. It is a valuable parameter for comparing similar material surfaces, or more specifi-
cally, to compare surfaces before and after modification. It can identify complete or partial
coverage of a surface with a different species, as well as provide information about their
different behaviors at the surface/water interface. Specifically, regarding membranes, sur-
face charge (in combination with surface roughness, hydrophilicity, etc.) is the driving force
behind membrane fouling[38].
A final parameter, water contact angel (WCA) also provides insight into the membrane
surface. Depending on the hydrophilicity or hydrophobicity of the surface, the WCA will
be lower or higher.
The membranes hydrophilicity or lack thereof can be impactful on
performance parameters such as permeate flux, rejection efficiency and fouling. Generally,
a more hydrophilic surface is correlated with better antifouling properties. The change in
WCA as a result of membrane modification is also an indication into the degree and potential
effect of the modification[39].
1.3.3
Membrane fouling
The most prominent challenge associated with membrane operation is the concept of fouling.
Fouling refers to the reduction in permeability of the membrane as a result of continuous
use. If the membrane is operated at a constant flux, this is seen as an increased in the
pressure required to sustain the flux. If the membrane is operated at constant pressure, it
is seen as a reduction in the flux over the membrane[35]. Naturally this is an issue, as the
membrane flux is one of the key characteristics impacting the effectiveness and viability of
the membrane operation. Membrane fouling can be reversible, meaning that it can rela-
tively easily be undone by techniques such as back-washing, or irreversible, meaning that
only harsh cleaning, if any, can restore the membrane. Six principal fouling mechanism has
been identified. Which is the dominant mechanism is dependent on foulant, membrane type
and operating conditions. The six mechanisms, as identified by Guo et al. are pore block-
17 of 64
==PAGE:26==
DTU
Chemical and Biochemical Technology
June 27, 2025
ing(i), cake formation(ii), concentration polarization(iii), organic adsorption(iv), inorganic
precipitation(v) and biological fouling(vi)[40].
Pore blocking can occur when foulants are approximately the same size as the pores of the
membranes. The foulants enter the membrane space but are stuck on the inside effectively
sealing of the pore. This type of fouling is one of the most severe and is often irreversible.
If pore blocking has already occurred, or if the foulant is of much larger size than the
pores, cake formation can happen.
Here, foulants are not able to enter the membrane,
but deposit in layers on the surface of the membrane. The permeability of such a cake
formation will typically be much lower than that of the membrane, but is considered a form
of reversible fouling and can easily be mitigated by back-washing. The third mechanism,
concentration polarization is considered the primary cause of membrane fouling in many
systems and is somewhat similar. Due to the removal of solvent molecules at the membrane,
the inability for substrate or foulant to enter the membrane and the low rate of diffusion
back into the solution high above the membrane, the concentration of substrate/foulant at
the membrane surface greatly increase. The concentration is typically 30-50 times higher
than in the bulk solution. This phenomenon is described by the so-called gel layer model
and results in a limitng flux thorugh the layer that cannot be overcome.
This type of
fouling is also combated by back washing, but the fast re-establishment of the gel layer
makes it difficult to combat fully. Organic adsorption involves the attachment of dissolved
organic compounds like humic substances or proteins to the membrane surface, altering
its properties and potentially leading to irreversible fouling. Inorganic precipitation occurs
when salts or metals such as calcium, silica, or iron form solid deposits on the membrane
due to changes in pH or concentration, often resulting in hard-to-remove scale. Biological
fouling refers to fouling by micro-biological organisms that either through methods similar
to those described above or more complex methods adhere to the membrane. They provide
additional challenges in that they in some cases can successfully survive and multiply on or
within the membrane resulting in biofilms.

Enzymatic Membrane Reactors (EMR)
Enzymatic membrane reactors are a type of chemical reactor that specifically employs both
enzymes and membranes. Many conventional types of reactors such as stirred tank reac-
tors(STR), continuous stirred tank reactors(CSTR), fluidized bed reactor (FBR) or packed
bed reactor (PBR) may also be operated using enzymes, however the inclusion of membranes
qualifies the EMR as a unique reactor type. The combination of enzymes and membranes
offer simultaneous bioconversion and separation resulting in many potential operational ad-
vantages and some new challenges[1, 45]. EMRs can be classified based on the form of the
enzyme, on the location and function of the membrane and on the reactor type and mode
of operation. The enzymes may either be freely dispersed in the substrate solution or im-
mobilized onto particles, by encapsulation in spherical membranes or onto a flat membrane
surface. The membrane can be present as a separate module recirculating reorientate into

reactor type and mode of operation. While all of the conventional reactor types potentially
could be used for EMRs, the most commonly seen is the STR or CSTR[46]. Indeed, the

The integration of membrane separation into bio-catalysis reactors present several advan-
tages over conventional bio-catalysis reactors.
The retention of the enzymes within the

removed with the product stream, requiring either inactivation or isolation and recycling.
Both are expensive, either in operational or material costs. If the enzymes are immobilized

1.2) are also present, i.e. increased re-usability, stability etc. A more uniquely advanta-

substrate concentration and low product concentration, maintaining a higher average reac-

and separation reduces the required amount of downstream purification steps. This process

The generalized challenges affecting most types of EMRs are challenges like membrane foul-

ductivity. It is further accentuated when the membrane is used as a support for immobilized
enzyme. It can be mitigated by operational modes such as cross-flow or backwash however
it remains a challenge. Mass transfer limitations include both diffusion through nonporous
membranes and multiple types of heterogeneity near the membrane surface where enzymes,
substrate, product or co-factors may be present in higher concentration than in the rest of
the reaction volume. If immobilized enzymes are employed the untimely and uncontrolled
leakage of enzyme into the reactor or product stream is a potential issue as well, being more
common in certain types of immobilization such as adsorption, than if covalent bonding is

be mentioned that not all enzymes are compatible with this type of process intensification,
as the pressure and other conditions required for membrane operation are too harsh for the
enzymes (for example if NF or RO membranes are to be utilized). Finally, developmental
costs of such a complex solution may not be reasonable for some processes[32, 45, 46].
Despite the challenges that are posed, EMRs appear to be powerful tools in the work towards
greener chemistry and process intensification. They being widely research and have already
found implementation in industrial sectors such as the agro-food sector, the pharmaceutical

2.3
Enzyme immobilization methodologies
This section describes the methods with which the enzymes were immobilized onto the
membrane.
Two methods of immobilization was used, incubation and filtration.
Both
methods were used to complete immobilization of enzymes on the pristine membrane. For
immobilization of enzymes on the modified membrane, the same procedure was used, after
the modification procedure described in section 2.4.
2.3.1
Membrane pretreatment
Before each experiment that uses membranes, the membranes were pretreated in the same
way. Initially, larger pieces of membrane were submerged in 0.01 M NaOH solution and agi-
tated on a platform rocker for 30 minutes according to the manufacturer’s recommendation.
The membranes were then gently washed three times by submersion in pure Milli-Q water
for a few minutes on the platform rocker. The membranes were then stored for a minimum
of 2 hours in pure water according to the manufacturers recommendation; however, in all
cases, the membranes were stored overnight.
Before being used in further experimentation, membranes were cut to fit the Amicon stirred
filtration cell (25 mm) and mounted therein. The membrane pieces were then compacted for
30 minutes at 2 bar using pure Milli-Q water, manually refilling the filtration cell whenever
it was completely emptied. At this point the membrane was ready for further treatment or
for experimentation.

Immobilization via incubation

3.1.1
ADH buffer and pH selection
The specific activity of ADH for the reaction of ethanol was measured by UV-Vis for different
buffers and at different pH. The measured specific activity of the enzyme can be seen in

The highest specific activity can be observed for Tris buffer at pH 8.5.
This

of a systematic error, as all variations of the experiment was performed on the same day,
using the same stock enzyme and stock substrate solutions. This was deemed acceptable due

an even higher specific activity may have been achieved at even higher pH (e.g. pH 9), this
was not done for this same reason. This is less of a problem for the enzymes immobilized by
filtration than for those immobilized by incubation, as the time required for immobilization
is significantly less.

formed. Triplicate, independent experiments were performed. The resulting specific activity

expected. The reason for this is undetermined, however, the relatively low buffer concentra-
tions used may be a factor, as well as the condition of the ADH used, as the ADH used for
all experiments in this thesis was some of the last in the container. Errors in the method

3.2.2
FTIR
The FT-IR spectrum of the pristine and modified membrane can be seen in figure 7 and
figure 8 for the active/incubated and support/filtrated sides of the membrane respectively.
The spectra have been measured from 4000 cm−1 to 400 cm−1 and have been reversed as
per convention.
The selective layer spectrum closely represents polysulfone (PSf) membrane spectra found
in literature. The two smaller peaks found at 1290 cm−1 and 1325 cm−1 are recognizably
symmetric stretching of the O=S=O bonds of the polysulfone backbone. The absorption
peak at 1585 cm−1 can be attributed to the stretching of C=C bonds in the benzene rings
of the backbone, while out of plane bending shows up as peaks between 650 cm−1 and 1000
cm−1. A final characteristic peak is the small peak at around 3000 cm−1 that represents
37 of 64
==PAGE:46==
DTU
Chemical and Biochemical Technology
June 27, 2025
asymmetric CH3 stretching vibrations [49]. Little difference is immediately observed between
the pristine and modified membrane, which may primarily be due to the small amount of
modification material. However, across multiple measurements, there are minor variations
that are only present in the modified membranes, confirming that some modification has
been achieved. Specifically, the augmentation of the peaks 2800 cm−1, the two unique peaks
around 1650 cm−1 and the broad band between 1100 cm−1 and 900 cm−1. Of these, the
peak at 1650 cm−1 has been identified as potentially being Mo-O bonds on the edges of the
nanosheet structures, where MoS2 is available for reaction with water or water and oxygen
[50]. The broad peak between 3100 and 3400 cm−1 is likely highly adsorbed water that was
not immediately removable by the relatively mild drying the samples underwent. One final
takeaway from this spectra is that the modification did not significantly alter the chemical
structure or functionality of the bulk polymer.
The support side spectrum also appears as polypropylene (PP) spectra found in literature.
The two large peaks at 2918 cm−1 and 2848 cm−1 are representative of CH2 asymmetrical
and symmetrical stretching vibrations respectively. The peaks at 1450 cm−1 and 1380 cm−1
are representative of CH2 scissor vibrations [51]. As opposed to the incubated side, we here
see a rather large variation between the pristine and modified membrane samples. Before
any attempted analysis of these ”new” peaks, their remarkable similarity to the locations of
the peaks found in the polysulfone phase is noted. It is highly possible that these new peaks
are simply a smaller fraction of the polysulfone being reached by the instrument through
the polypropylene layer.
The argument for why the same peaks are not present in the
pristine membrane spectrum (and why the PP peaks did not show up in the PSf spectra)
would then have to be found in the modification procedure. It is hypothesized that the
compression of the membrane prior to and during the modification by filtration may have
brought the two phases of the membrane close enough together for the polysulfone groups to
be measured. This could quite easily be tested by compressing and measuring the support
side of a pristine membrane, however this was not feasible within the time-frame of this

Water contact angle measurements were completed on four different types of samples. Both
the selective and support side of the pristine membrane and the selective side of membranes
modified by incubation and support side of membranes modified by filtration. The results

The modification appears to have increased the WCA slightly both in the case of incubation
and filtration. The increase in WCA is related to a more hydrophobic surface. These results
clash with the general understanding that exfoliated MoS2 nano-sheets exhibit hydrophilic
properties as described by Ma et al.. This is due to the edge-cases of the nano-structure
being readily available to react with water or both water and oxygen[52]. However, also as
demonstrated by Ma et al., the bulk MoS2 has hydrophobic properties. This is also confirmed
from simulation by Luan et al. who found that a singular, complete 2D nanosheets of MoS2
(comparable to bulk phase MoS2 due to the lack of edge cases) possessed hydrophobic
properties.
This is an indication that the exfoliation procedure (generation of flakes of
MoS2 nano-sheets) may have been incomplete, as the modification posess the characteristics
of the bulk MoS2 phase.
The only other variation on the surface of the samples is the potential presence of residual
SDS. However, since SDS is amphiphilic, the impact on the hydrophobicity of the surface
from this source is unclear.
The large difference in WCA seen between samples measured on the selective side and on
the support side stems from the fact that the support side is made from polypropylene and
completely nonpolar and thus hydrophobic. The polysulfone, which constitutes the selective
layer, is only partially hydrophobic due to the oxygen and sulfur dioxide in the backbone.

3.2.4
Zeta potential
Zeta potential measurements were carried out on both the selective and support side of
the pristine membrane as well as on the selective layer of the incubated membrane and the
support side of the filtrated membrane. The results of the zeta potential measurements can
be seen in figure 10 and 11.
The pristine membranes selective layer is observed to have a no charge at pH 4 dropping to
app. -15 mV between pH 4 and 6 and the remaining stable at the rest of the measured pH
range. This trend is consistent with the findings in literature. Here, Nystr¨om et al. found
a similar membrane (albeit with a lower MW cut-off) to have neutral charge at pH 4 and
a moderate but consistently negative charge up til pH 8 [53]. The modification appears
to have added a small but significant negative charge to the membrane. The curve of the
modified sample generally follows the same trend as the pristine sample, stabilizing a bit
later at around pH 7. This is a clear indication that the bulk material is stil dominant in
the determination of the zeta potential. Finally, an additional drop in negativity is observed
beyond pH 10 for the modified sample.
The pristine membranes support side is similarly observed to essentially have no charge at
pH 4, and generally has a shape similar to that of the selective layer. At pH >4 however
the charge becomes significantly more negative than that of the selective layer. The shape
of the curve and size of the charge is consistent with literature findings on the behavior of
polypropylene materials. In a study investigating zeta potential of polypropylene films, the
pure material film was found to be neutral at pH 4 and slope down before stabilizing between
-30 and -35 mV around pH 9[54].
The measurements of the pristine membrane posses
relatively large standard deviations on both axes. This specific sample showed the largest
degree of swelling (going from between 100-110 µm to between 75-90 µm), thus reducing
the gap height during the experiment, which may be a cause of uncertainty. The coupling
of the parameters is also likely to further accentuate any inaccuracy.
The modification
appears to have increased the negative charge at lower pH and decreased them at higher
pH. However, the actual variance between the two samples is rather small, especially when

Conclusion
The aim of this thesis was to investigate the performance of enzymatic membrane systems
(EMS) by way of different enzymes, enzyme immobilization methods and membrane surface
modification. Two enzymes, alcohol dehydrogenase (ADH) and laccase were immobilized
on/in polysulfone ultrafiltration membranes (GR51PP, 50 kDa MW cut-off, polypropylene
support layer) , both pristine and modified with exfoliated MoS2 nano-sheets. The exper-
imental methodology consisted of incubation and filtration based techniques used both for
enzyme immobilization and MoS2-modification. Membrane characterization techniques were
employed, namely water contact angle (WCA), zeta potential and FTIR spectroscopy. The
EMS were tested in small scale stirred cell batch operation for biocatalytic activity (BCA),
specific enzyme activity and water permeability. Free enzyme systems were also analyzed.
The following are the key findings from the experimental work.
Membrane modification and characterization:
• MoS2-modification resulted in a slight increase in hydrophobicity as seen by the in-
crease in WCA. This is in opposition to the expected behavior of exfoliated MoS2
nano-sheets.
• Zeta potential measurements indicated a slight increase in negative charge on the
active membrane surface across the entire observed pH range. For the support side,
change in charge varied with pH but was overall minimal (< ±8).
Enzyme immobilization:
• ADH and laccase were successfully immobilized on both pristine and modified mem-
brane.
• The observed enzyme loading was significantly higher than values found in related
literature literature (standout result is ADH via incubation with 4.43 µg/cm2 in the
present study compared to 0.72 µg/cm2).
• The enzyme loading via filtration was similar to those observed in literature (>95
µg/cm2 for ADH and >245 µg/cm2 for laccase compared to 88-28 µg/cm2 for ADH in
literature)
• The enzyme amount determination method suffered (based on Bradford assay) from
54 of 64
==PAGE:63==
DTU
Chemical and Biochemical Technology
June 27, 2025
very high standard deviations. The method should be reconsidered and revised before
future work.
• Immobilization yield of ADH appeared to benefit from MoS2-modification, going from
14.75 ± 6.59% to 31.89 ± 0.25%
EMS performance:
• Laccase, immobilized on pristine membrane by either incubation or filtration, showed
similar or increased specific activity when compared to the free enzyme
• ADH showed significantly reduced specific activity for both incubation and filtration
• Modification with MoS2 showed increase in enzyme loading and specific enzyme ac-
tivity of incubated ADH. The modification had little to no impact on laccase enzyme
loading and a negative impact on the incubated laccase specific activity
• Incubated MoS2 had a positive effect on membrane permeability for both ADH and
laccase immobilization. Filtrated MoS2 significantly reduced membrane permeability
Further work involves optimizing the immobilization and modification procedures. Namely a
range of concentrations of immobilization and modification solutions should be investigated.
The stability and behavior of the membrane performance characteristics could further be
investigated, observing longer reaction times (larger batches), and multiple conversion cycles
over a longer period of time. The detection methodology of lower concentrations of enzyme
and for solutions with contaminants should be expanded, especially for laccase, to provide
accurate future results. The impact of the modification on the membrane surface chemistry,
and the resulting interactions with the enzymes should be further expanded upon with
other methods such as microscopy or Raman spectroscopy. Finally, due to the difference in
behavior of the two enzymes tested, the list of enzymes could be expanded to better be able
to generalize the observations.
These findings suggest that the combination of specialized immobilization methods with
membrane modification techniques provides a promising avenue for developing and improv-
ing EMS.
