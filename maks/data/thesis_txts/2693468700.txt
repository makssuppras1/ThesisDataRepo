Abstract
The energy transition promotes the integration of renewable energies as distributed en­
ergy resources (DERs). Because of them, distribution networks are expected to operate
closer to their capacity limits. Moreover, lines congestion may occur if wholesale mar­
kets overlook distribution network constraints when clearing. Instead of investing in grid
reinforcement, these situations can be handled by harnessing system’s flexibility. Local
flexibility markets (LFMs) are a tool to trade flexibility in limited areas such as small towns.
This thesis analyzes the implications of continuous and auction­based clearing in LFMs.
We design an energy multi­period LFM that matches flexibility requests from the DSO
with flexibility offers from prosumers or aggregators. It considers network constraints by
performing a DC power flow to ensure the technical feasibility of the trades. Our LFM
also includes block bids trading under an all­or­nothing condition to model the rebound
effect of flexibility providers such as thermal­electrical loads. Within this framework, two
models are created. The continuous model is a novelty since there are not references of
a continuous LFM explicitly including network constraints and block bids. The algorithm
clears the market every time two bids match, with the first­come first­served principle. In
contrast, the auction­based model clears once for the whole market time span. It is an
optimization problem formulated as a MILP with binary variables to account for block bids.
We compare both outcomes with the same study case. The LFM operates in a 33 buses
radial distribution grid with consumption acting as flexible loads and small­scale renewable
generation representing DERs. For the continuous clearing the social welfare is 2% lower
and the energy volume traded is 3% higher than for the auction­based. In both cases, the
acceptance ratio of block bids is around 60% and the needs for curtailment and load
shedding are reduced around 25%­30%. Whereas auction­based clearing is not affected
by the arrival time of the bids, continuous clearing highly depends on it. To further develop
our analysis, the best and worst scenarios for continuous clearing should be considered.
We also conduct a qualitative analysis on the payment methods of both mechanisms. The
payment rule used in continuous markets is quite standardised and applies to our LFM.
However, due to the binary variables in auction­based clearing, the common methods that
use dual variables of balance constraints cannot be directly applied. Thus, we present five
different payment methods for the LFM that approach this challenge. The choice between
them depends on the market clearing properties that the LFM wants to address.
We conclude that the use of one or the other clearing mechanism in a LFM depends
on the target of the market operator. Using auction­based clearing the maximum social
welfare is obtained, whereas continuous clearing may end up with higher volume traded
depending on the bids’ arrival order. Moreover, while implementing block bids consider­
ably complicates the continuous model formulation, in the auction­based model implies
that the usual payment rule does not apply. In any case, the flexibility trade alleviates
network congestion and decrease the need of curtailment and load shedding.

CHAPTER1
Introduction
This chapter introduces the framework of the thesis to understand its motivation. Further­
more, the objectives of the project and its limitations are stated. Then the contributions
are presented and the chapter concludes with an outline of the thesis.
1.1
Background
Nowadays, electricity and heat production accounts for more than 40 % of global CO2
emissions, as shown in Fig. 1.1. To face global warming by reducing CO2 emissions,
society is moving towards an energy transition. Most of those emissions are produced
when fossil fuels are used as energy sources. As presented in Fig. 1.2, a large share
of the electricity generated globally comes from fossil fuels, so the change involves the
increasing penetration of renewable energies in electricity production. Nevertheless, the
integration of these energy sources presents challenges for power systems due to their
stochastic nature.
Figure 1.1: Global CO2 emissions by sector (IEA 2018) [1].

Figure 1.2: Global electricity production by energy source (IEA 2018) [1].
The development of renewables as distributed energy resources (DER), particularly chal­
lenges the operation of distribution grids. If the current network architecture is not suffi­
cient to cope with such stochastic generation, the lines likely operate close to their capacity
limits. Infeasibilities at distribution level may occur if wholesale markets do not consider
distribution network constraints when clearing, which is generally the case. These situ­
ations are usually handled by investing in grid reinforcement, but an alternative solution
could be to harness and manage the flexibility available in the system. This resource is
increasing at a local level since it can be easily provided by domestic consumers.
In this context, flexibility is defined as a controlled power variation that can be performed
at a specific time, with a given duration and at a localised point in the network [2]. The
proper use of this resource not only facilitates the integration of renewable energies, but
also allows to operate the grid more cost­effectively and securely.
Apart from congestion management, flexibility also contributes to voltage control at a dis­
tribution level and power balancing and frequency regulation at a transmission level. Flex­
ibility markets are the tool introduced to trade this resource. They create a framework in
which flexibilty demanding entities and entities offering it get in contact with each other.
This product can be traded as energy, balancing capacity or reserve capacity [3].
To manage these flexibility markets exists the figure of the flexibility market operator
(FMO). It is in charge of collecting all the submitted bids, matching them using a market
clearing mechanism and making the corresponding payments to participants. In some
cases, the FMO is an entity external to the market participants, in others, the distribution
system operator (DSO) plays this role, as flexibility exchanges are often conducted locally
and the DSO is the main stakeholder.
Besides the DSO, other players can participate in flexibility markets, such as aggregators
or balance responsible parties (BRP). These players would take part in the market with
different perspectives. The aggregator is an entity in charge of gathering and managing

==PAGE:14==
groups of consumers and prosumers1 to participate as a whole in energy and flexibility
markets [4]. In this way, small­scale DERs and flexible loads, which cannot participate in­
dividually, would exploit their flexibility potential. Examples of flexibility providers from the
demand side are the thermal­electrical loads. They offer flexibility because they can vary
their consumption without comprising their purpose. However, by offering this service,
thermal­electrical loads suffer a rebound effect. It consist in a need for energy recovery
after an energy variation to return to the initial steady state. On the other hand, BRPs
are traders responsible for adjusting energy supply and consumption according to their
clients’ portfolio. Therefore they would procure flexibility to optimize the portfolios and
realize their energy obligations [2].
Although flexibility markets are not yet implemented in the electricity systems, they are
relied on to manage grid issues. They are intended to operate at a local level due to the
emerging controllable and flexible consumers (e.g. prosumers).
1.2
Motivation
This thesis aims to contribute to the development of flexible markets in distribution grids.
By harnessing the flexibility available in the system they can alleviate lines congestion and
solve voltage issues, and therefore delay grid investments in the long term. However, to
fulfil this purpose they must be properly designed. This is important to ensure that the
grid situation is improved and not aggravated, to motivate participants to enter the market
and thus promote its implementation.
A suitable environment for trading flexibility is not only profitable for the DSO, but also for
other potential traders. As aforementioned, market players do have different motivations
and interests when participating in the market. Therefore, a flexibility market model must
reflect as far as possible their requirements and preferences to provide the service. By
doing so, trading flexibility becomes more appealing for the players to take part in the
market.
Furthermore, when developing these markets it is essential to examine which market
clearing mechanism to use, as it determines the market’s results. It is important to be
aware of the implications of using them in flexibility markets. Depending on the intended
outcomes, there will be a more appropriate clearing mechanism for the market.
1.3
Project Objectives
The purpose of this thesis is to assess the performance of different market clearing mech­
anisms in a local flexibility market (LFM). We propose the layout of a multi­period energy
LFM. The market design includes network constraints and block bids. The checking of

network constraints when clearing the market ensures that the system situation is not
aggravated because of the trade. The possibility of trading with block bids is needed to
model the rebound effect suffered by some flexibility providers. Within this framework,
two different setups for market clearing are developed: continuous clearing and auction­
based clearing. In the end, these two configurations will be discussed and evaluated
based on a case study.
This project focuses on the following specific objectives, that are developed along the
report:
• Explain the concept of local flexibility market.
• Design block bids for a LFM, taking into account the rebound effect, and include

• Model an energy continuous flexibility market that considers network constraints and
block bids.
• Model an energy auction­based flexibility market that considers network constraints
and block bids.
• Conduct a numerical study case for the LFM and analyze the results obtained.
1.4
Limitations
To accomplish this project, different limitations are considered as follows:
• Only active power is considered.
• The LFM is focused on alleviating system congestion, disregarding voltage issues.
• The DSO is the single buyer, BRPs are excluded from the market.
• Market payments are only compared qualitatively.
• Block bids apply for flexibility offers, but not for flexibility requests.
• Block bids are fully accepted or fully rejected.
• Auction­based clearing is compared to continuous clearing just for one scenario.
1.5
Contributions
This thesis proposes a simulation­based comparison of continuous and auction­based
clearing mechanisms for a multi­period energy LFM, that explicitly considers both network
constraints and block bids. This comparison has been carried out previously for energy
markets as shown in Ref. [5], but we are extending it for flexibility markets.

Within the framework of the LFM designed we develop two models to operate the market
using continuous and auction­based clearing. The one including auction­based clearing
is an optimization problem, defined as a mixed integer linear program (MILP). The model
with continuous clearing is an extension of the algorithm proposed in [3], adapted to suit
the market framework. The formulation of the first one is commonly used for auction­
based energy markets, whereas the development of the latter is one of our main contri­
butions since no references have been found in the literature for a continuous LFM with
these features.
We also design a realistic study case to perform both models and compare the outcomes
of the two clearing mechanisms. It includes the creation of an environment in which the
market could be implemented and the generation of the bids that could be submitted to
the market.

2.1
Local flexibility markets
The state­of­the­art on LFMs, their different formulations and potential participants is pre­
sented in this section.
Ref. [2] analyzes the concepts, models and clearing methods for LFMs. According to it, the
main drivers for introducing flexibility in electric systems are the uncertain and intermittent
generation which hinder the grid operation, the bi­directionality and decentralisation of the
system caused by the DERs, the controllable and flexible consumers and the multi­energy
systems. In order to integrate this resource into the power system, LFMs are proposed.
Authors of [2] define LFMs as platforms to trade electricity flexibility in limited geographical
areas such as communities or small towns, which is desirable given the presence of DERs.
Furthermore, the different formulations and designs of LFMs proposed so far are compiled
and classified as illustrated in Fig. 2.1. Depending on the framework in which they are
embedded and the market clearing mechanism they include, LFMs can present different
architectures.

In Ref. [8] the Danish project EcoGrid 2.0 is described. It consists in the design, imple­
mentation and testing of a LFM. In this case, flexibility is provided by households with
flexible loads. The aggregator controls those loads remotely and has access to smart
meter data to offer the service. The DSO also uses these data to calculate the flexibility
needed and starts an auction by sending the FMO its requests. Then aggregators make
their bids according to DSO’s needs and the market clears choosing the most economi­
cally beneficial offer. Contracts between the parties are created one to twelve months in
advance.
The design of a market platform for Peer­to­Peer (P2P) energy trading to facilitate the
DSO’s use of DERs is detailed in Ref. [9]. The P2P approach provides localized energy
trading, which makes the operation of the system easier. Prosumers and consumers
participate as buyers or sellers in this hourly auction­based market. The paper also iden­
tifies market clearing performance indicators to compare different types of auction mech­
anisms.
A continuous LFM that includes network constraints is designed in Ref. [3]. This market
trades flexibility as reserve capacity, follows the first­come first­served principle to match
flexibility requests and offers and uses the pay­as­bid pricing rule. The paper emphasizes

the need of including a network check when clearing the market to ensure that the trades
do not aggravate the grid situation.
Several studies point to flexible loads as potential local flexibility providers. For example,
Ref. [10] describes the flexibility services that could be offered by electric vehicles (EVs) at
distribution level. Once EVs users provide the aggregator with their future transportation
needs and the location where the car is connected to the grid, congestion and overloading
could be mitigated. Moreover, the authors make some recommendations to consider
when designing a market to trade flexibility supplied by flexible loads. One of them is to
keep the minimum bid capacity as minimum as possible to allow demand­response (DR)
participation into the market and another is to promote the use of smart meters to enable
a proper use of flexibility.
Ref. [11] aims to exploit the flexibility potential of DR units. It proposes a mechanism to
manage congestion in distribution grids accounting for the rebound effect of DR units.
This phenomenon is experienced by the units that offer the service starting from a steady
state to which they need to return afterwards. Thus, a decrease in consumption must
be followed by an increase or vice versa, due to technical requirements. If this effect
is overlooked, new congestion could appear in the rebound period. The authors model
the rebound effect using asymmetric block bids, which consists of two bids of different
dimensions for consecutive time periods. The mechanism includes block bids through a
set of binary variables and uses optimal power flow (OPF) models to manage congestion.
Another way to exploit the flexibility of DR with asymmetric block offers is described in
Ref. [12]. It proposes a DSO­level fixed­term contract market for DR. The tool used for
the DSO to clear the market is an optimization problem formulated as MILP. Integer and
binary variables are used to include asymmetric block bids in the market. Moreover, this
tool determines a fair payment for the aggregators that provide demand response, based
on a pay­as­bid auction.
As stated in Ref. [11, 12], there is a need for using asymmetric block bids so that certain
flexible loads can offer their flexibility to the system. Papers [13, 14, 15] present different
approaches to provide flexibility from the demand side through this type of bids.
In Ref. [13] thermal electric loads such as building heating and cooling, water heating and
refrigeration are pointed as ideal candidates for load shifting. They can vary their power
consumption while maintaining an acceptable temperature range to continue fulfilling their

in several ways, which means that LFMs can show different architectures. It is important,

must consider thermal­electrical loads as potential providers and promote their participa­

variables.

2.3
Clearing mechanisms
As seen before, flexibility markets can present different clearing mechanisms. The most
commonly used according to the literature review are continuous and auction­based clear­
ing.
Several publications argue about continuous and auction­based clearing for intraday mar­
kets. For this thesis, such discussion can be extended to flexibility markets, since they
also take place after the wholesale markets.
A publication of the European Commission about the design of a future electricity intraday
market [18], states that in the current transmission level framework, the two main mech­
anisms are the ones above­mentioned. On one hand, in continuous markets bids are
matched on an online basis following the first­come first­served principle and the pay­as­
bid rule, i.e. the participants get what they bid. On the other hand, as in auction­based
markets the bids match at a fixed point in time, they can allow both pay­as­bid or pay­as­
cleared, i.e. the bidder gets its marginal bid.
The report [18] also compares both clearing mechanisms using Fig. 2.2. It shows the

Another comparison between continuous and auction­based market models is carried out
in Ref. [5]. These models address future electricity markets promoting the participation
of small players such as prosumers. Three different algorithms accounting for the market
participant’s preferences are formulated. The benchmark is a discrete double auction
in which the incoming orders are collected and matched at once for a given time period.
Then, two continuous clearing algorithms are presented. In one of them, an incoming offer
matches at the best available price, whereas in the other the incoming offer matches with
a random bid among the standing ones. The authors conclude that the maximum social
welfare is achieved in the auction­based model, but the continuous clearing algorithms
result in a higher volume trade. In addition, this paper provides an upper bound on the
sub­optimality of continuous clearing compared to auction clearing.

kets. Whether one mechanism is more advantageous than the other for a LFM depends
on the characteristics and preferences of the environment in which it operates. If the mar­
ket has low liquidity it may be more desirable for the FMO to use continuous trading to try
procure more flexibility. However, if the FMO has the intention of getting the maximum
social welfare, it will use auction­based trading.
Although literature compares both clearing mechanisms, it is the first time for LFMs that
explicitly includes network constraints and block bids. These features affect not only the
formulation of the clearing algorithms but also their outcome. The check of network con­
straints when clearing limits the energy volume that can be exchanged through each line.
It ensures that energy trades are technically feasible and affect positively network con­
gestion. On the other hand, including the trade of block bids means having different time
periods of time linked. For this reason, the auction market must be cleared once for all
the time periods, so the maximum social welfare is obtained overall, but not for every time
period. In addition, matches that involve block bids are dependent on the acceptance of
the whole block bid. Even if trading block bids adds more constraints to the LFM, they are
needed to model the rebound effect experienced by potential flexibility providers.

3.1
Market Framework
We have designed a LFM as a tool for the DSO to make a more efficient use of the distribu­
tion network in which it operates. Flexibility is traded in form of active energy for different
time periods. Participants place bids specifying the power variation they offer or require
during the span of one time period. The market takes place after the wholesale market,
i.e. in an intraday time frame, and can account for any number of periods of any duration.
In this platform, flexibility requests and offers are matched under certain conditions en­
suring that the exchange does not negatively impact the system’s congestion. Moreover,
the preferences of some systems when providing flexibility are included by allowing block
bids in the market.
3.1.1
Participants
LFM connect flexibility buyers and sellers. In this case, the only buyer is the DSO, as the
tool is designed having it as the main stakeholder. This entity uses the service to manage
congestion, control voltage or reduce network losses.
On the other hand, the sellers are actors with the ability to provide flexibility to the system,
i.e. prosumers and aggregators. They can offer this service by modifying their electricity
use. Potential flexibility suppliers are certain thermal­electric loads that can vary their op­
erating temperature, and therefore their power consumption, without compromising their
purpose.

power system and market participants.

Flexibility Requests
FlexReqs indicate the need for a certain energy variation at an specific time and location
and how much one is willing to pay to cover that need. If more energy is required, the
direction of the bid is up, whereas if there is an energy surplus it will be down. Regarding
the price of FlexReqs, it is assumed that the buyer sets a profitable price for it. The DSO
will purchase flexibility for a lower price than curtailing generation or shedding loads. In
addition, FlexReqs are only considered as single bids, as no technical constraint is found
for the DSO to use block bids, as it happens with some FlexOffs. Being all single bids,
the quantity specified in the bid corresponds to the highest requirement.
Flexibility Offers
FlexOffs show the energy variation available at a specific time and location and for how
much it is sold. For sellers, the bid’s up direction involves reducing consumption or in­
creasing generation, while a down bid implies just the opposite, either an increase in
consumption or a decrease in generation. Flexibility sellers also make their bids in a way
that is profitable for them but without compromising their other activities, since providing
flexibility is considered a sideline business for them. For example, prosumers who have
batteries in their systems can offer to charge and/or discharge them, and the same applies
to electric vehicles fleet. When batteries are storing energy which is not planned to be
used an up FlexOff could be made, whereas if they have room for storing energy a down
FlexOff could be submitted. Aggregators could also offer variations of their customers’
consumption as long as their needs are not compromised. In the case of the thermal­
electric loads mentioned, they could only make a FlexOff as an asymmetric block bid,
since their flexibility is linked to a rebound effect. These sellers will make a block FlexOff
consisting of an up FlexOff for a time period linked to a down FlexOff for the following
time period or vice versa.

3.2
Continuous Market Model
Continuous markets clear as soon as there is a match between bids, but matching con­
ditions are defined when designing the market, so they can vary from one to another.
This continuous flexibility market is based on the model proposed in Ref. [3]. That model
trades flexibility reserve capacity following the first­come first­served principle to clear the
market, which means that the older bid sets the clearing price and has priority for the same
price. Requests and offers match partially or completely if they have the same direction
and if the request’s price is higher than the offer’s price. Furthermore, when creating a
match between bids, a network check algorithm calculates the maximum capacity that
can be exchanged, considering previous matches. This base model has been adapted to
be multi­period for including block bids and to trade flexibility as active energy.
3.2.1
Matching
The model takes as inputs the new bid entering the market, the grid setpoint and three
different orderbooks. The new bid has the format shown in Tab. 3.1, the Setpoint gives
information about the state of each node in every time period and the orderbooks store
unmatched requests (orderbook_request), unmatched offers (orderbook_offer) and tem­
porary matches (orderbook_temporary). Temporary matches are those in which a part of
a block bid is involved, but the whole block bid is not matched. They are collected in an
orderbook waiting for the remaining part of the block bid to be matched.
As outputs, the model returns the Setpoint, the orderbooks and the matches. The Set­
point is modified every time there is a non­temporary match so that previous trades are
considered when evaluating a possible new match. The orderbooks are updated every
time a new bid is submitted and also when a match occurs. Matches gathers the matches
that occur with the new bid.

it as different single bids, which means that the FlexOffs implied are handled separately.
Regardless of whether the new bid is a FlexReq or a FlexOff, once the conditions for a
match are met, we examine if the FlexOff involved is part of a block bid. Then we verify if
not only this FlexOff but the whole block bid is matched. As in this LFM it is established
that block bids follow an AoN condition, the creation of a match is only considered if
the entire block of FlexOffs is matched, i.e. if all the FlexOffs that constitute it are fully
matched. Otherwise, all the matches in which a block bid is involved are considered
temporary matches and registered in the orderbook_temporary.
Whenever a FlexOff belonging to a block bid is partially or completely matched, it is
checked whether the rest has been previously matched as well. To do that we examine
the orderbook_offer and the orderbook_temporary, to evaluate the state of the FlexOffs
that are linked to each other in that block.

3.2.2
Payment
As mentioned before, the continuous LFM market follows the first­come first­served prin­
ciple. Within the orderbook_offer and the orderbook_request, bids are sorted by price so
that the ones with the best prices are prioritised. If there is more than one bid with the
same price, they are sorted by order of arrival. This ensures that the new bid matches
the oldest bid at the best price. Therefore, we create the match with the highest social
welfare within the previous bids available. The bid submitted later acts as a price taker,
whereas the earlier one is the price maker, so the pay­as­bid rule applies for it. A price is
determined for each match, which means that a bid that matches with more than one bid
could trade its flexibility for several prices.
Returning to the properties of the clearing mechanisms shown in Tab. 3.2, we analyze
which correspond to the continuous market model designed. Market efficiency is not
achieved since as bids are matched as they come to the market, social welfare is not
maximized. Moreover, nothing ensures incentive compatibility as the sellers could not bid
at their marginal cost. However, this market model recovers costs and adequately adjusts
for FMO revenues. In every match there is a specified energy volume traded for a certain
price. The matching price is ensured to be better or equal than to the bidding price, so
market players never make negative profits. As the buyer’s payment is given directly to
the seller, the FMO makes no deficit or profit from the trade.
3.3
Auction­based Market Model
Auction­based markets clear once for each time period, considering all the bids submitted
for it. As they are planned to obtain the highest social welfare, they are approached as
an optimization problem. Our model is proposed as a double­auction in which FlexReqs
and FlexOffs can be placed for different time periods. The market dispatch is calculated
to achieve the maximum social welfare for the whole time horizon of the market.
Many electricity markets introduce integer constraints in their formulation to enable par­
ticipants to better describe their preferences when bidding. Therefore, the problem is
a mixed integer linear program (MILP), since it accounts for block bids by using binary
variables.

Block Bids Matching
As block bids link different time periods, all the time periods have to clear at once to
establish whether the block bids are accepted or rejected. Such bids can only cover the
time span of the market. In other words, if the market clears for example for 8 time periods,
there cannot be a block bid linking the last of these time periods with the following group
of 8 time periods.
After solving the optimization problem, the value of the binary variable AR is obtained
for every block bid. A value of 1 means that the FlexOffs that constitute a block are
accepted when clearing the market, and they are therefore taken into account both in the
balancing equations and when minimizing the total market costs. A value of 0 implies just
the opposite, meaning that the block bid is rejected.
3.3.2
Payments
When formulating the optimization problem as a LP, the clearing price is usually derived
from the dual variables of the balance equations. However, as it is a MILP containing
binary variables, the pricing is not so straightforward. We analyse five different pricing
methods that can be implemented in this LFM: Side­payment, Paradoxically Rejected
Bids, Convex Hull Pricing, Vickrey–Clarke–Groves and Pay­as­bid. The first three use
the dual variables of the optimization problem to derive uniform prices, whereas the last
two propose other solutions for the pricing.
The dual variables of a MILP are obtained by solving it in two steps [19]:
1. Solve the optimization problem and obtain the optimal value for all the binary vari­
ables.
2. Solve again the same optimization problem but fixing the binary variables with their
optimal values obtained in the previous step. This turns into a linear problem in
which dual variables are easily derived.
The disadvantage of fixing binary variables is that when the problem is transformed into
an LP some terms become parameters. In both the objective function and the constraints,
all the terms associated with block bids will be parameters instead of variables. Therefore,
the cost of the block bids will no longer be included in the price formation. This implies
that some market participants may end up with a negative profit, so some adjustments
are needed to compensate for it.
Side­payment
In the US, market operators compensate the negative profit of the players using uplift
mechanisms. They consist of side­payments outside the market clearing. To recover
these payments, the operator usually distributes the additional costs in a fair way among
the buyers, but the way of proceeding depends on the market.
Market Clearing Mechanisms for Flexibility Markets in Distribution Grids
29
==PAGE:41==
The uplift payments guarantee cost recovery for the market players and even revenue
adequacy for the market operator. In Ref. [12], the authors develop a model of an auction­
based market which incorporates side­payments to ensure the above­mentioned proper­
ties. However, this is achieved at the expense of market efficiency, since this mechanism
implies deviations from the market outcome.
Convex Hull Pricing (ConvHP)
One way of calculating side­payments is through the ConvHP. This method identifies uni­
form market prices that minimize the total side­payments needed, through the aggregate
cost function. Several market operators in the US use it, but through approximations to
address the computational challenge in an accessible way, as proposed in Ref. [23].
In Ref. [24] the formulation, analysis and implementation challenges for ConvHP in elec­
tricity markets are discussed. Its authors conclude that this method is not preferred over
the common ones, since simple pricing schemes achieve similar benefits, being more
practical and transparent.
Paradoxically Rejected Bids (PRB)
Alternatively, in Europe, instead of using uplift payments, there are PRB. These are non­
convex bids, e.g. block bids, that fits the market according to the uniform prices computed,
but they are rejected.
In a situation where a bid incurs negative profits, but its price is in line with the clearing
price, it is paradoxically rejected. Then the algorithm is run again without that bid until
all the participants’ costs are recovered. This leads to a sub­optimal market result since
social welfare is not maximised. However, the mechanism complies with the revenue
adequacy for the market operator.
Vickrey–Clarke–Groves (VCG)
Leaving aside the use of dual variables, the VCG auction is another mechanism proposed
to price the market. In Ref. [25] the authors state that it is the only mechanism being
efficient and incentive compatible. It maximizes social welfare while making it optimal for
each participant to bid its true value.
First, the market clears to maximize social welfare and then the price of the transactions
is determined. This is not the initial bidding price, but the marginal harm caused to the
other participants, which is better than or equal to the original price [26]. Thus, it is more
profitable for the players to bid truthfully regardless of other bidders. The marginal harm
is calculated considering the best combination of bids excluding the participant under
consideration since the mechanism is defined assuming that there is a feasible solution
when a player is removed [27].

==PAGE:42==
Pay­as­bid
Finally, there is a pricing rule that can be applied to all the markets, which is pay­as­bid.
It is the simplest mechanism since each participant gets or pays according to their initial
bid. In this way cost recovery and revenue adequacy are ensured. In fact, the market
operator ends up with a surplus. In addition, market players can increase their profit by
over bidding, so this mechanism does not promote incentive compatibility.
In short, this chapter explains the design of the LFM and the development of the mar­
ket clearing models. First, we create the framework of an energy multi­period LFM in
which the DSO is the single buyer and aggregators and prosumers are the sellers. We
also define the format of single and block bids and analyse possible FlexReqs and Flex­
Offs. Then the four desirable market clearing properties are presented to qualitatively
assess the clearing mechanisms. We explain the implementation of network constraints
in the market clearing and the clearing conditions of the block bids. Continuous and
auction­based models are described in terms of matching and payment. For the contin­
uous model, we explain step by step how does the algorithm work, highlighting how it
integrates network constraints and matches block bids. The payment method commonly
used in continuous markets is analyzed. We present the auction­based model as a MILP
and explain the formulation of the optimization problem, also emphasising network con­

the payment method is more difficult to be determined. Because of the binary variables,
the common methods that use dual variables of balance constraints cannot be directly
applied. Thus, different possible payment methods for this LFM have been presented
and qualitatively assessed according to the clearing properties stated before.

The origin of prices for FlexReqs and FlexOffs is not entirely clear. We assume that the
DSO set them considering whether the acceptance of the FlexReq would avoid capping
generation or shedding loads. In the first case, the price must be higher than the price of
selling the energy produced and in the second case it must cover the damage caused by
interrupting the energy supply. As the DSO wants to avoid load shedding at any cost, the
prices of upwards FlexReqs are quite high compared to downwards FlexReqs to guar­
antee their acceptance. Regarding FlexOffs, their price is even more difficult to quantify
since in many cases to sell flexibility is a sideline business for market participants. As
a starting point we determine that the price of FlexOffs can be closed to the wholesale
market price. Block bids’ price is assumed to be a bit lower to promote their acceptance,
since it is more difficult for them due to their AoN condition.

The auction­based model (Fig. 4.7a) ensures that FlexReqs are matched in descending
order of price with FlexOffs in ascending order of price. In this way the difference between
the willingness to pay and to sell is as high as possible, i.e. the social welfare is maximized.
In contrast, the continuous model (Fig. 4.7b) matches bids on a first­come first­served
basis. Our continuous model ensures that the entering bid is matched with the older bid
at the best available price. If a bid entering the market has no other bids no match with it is
stored in an orderbook and sorted first by ascending price if it is a FlexOff or by descending
price if it is a FlexReq and then by arrival time. When a new bid comes, that orderbook
is checked looking for possible bids to create a match. This is how we guarantee that
for each match, social welfare is maximized within our possibilities and that for the same
price the older bid is prioritized.
Even if in auction­based clearing the social welfare is the maximum for the whole time span
of the LFM, it is not for every single time period, as illustrated in Fig. 4.8. In the auction­
based model, the social welfare per time period is calculated as the result of the objective
function (3.12a) for this period multiplied by ­1 to make it positive. In the continuous model,
the social welfare is calculated as the sum of the social welfare obtained in every match,
i.e. the difference between the bids’ price multiplied by the quantity matched. As the price
of the FlexReqs upwards is much higher than the FlexOffs’ upwards price, the trade of
this product significantly increases the social welfare.

Regarding social welfare, continuous clearing is sub­optimal compared to auction­based
clearing. While the latter knows all the bids placed in the LFM, the other clears having
limited knowledge of the submitted bids. Nevertheless, having the highest social welfare
does not necessarily imply the greatest amount of energy exchanged. In our study case,
social welfare in the continuous clearing is just 2% lower than the optimal, but the total
volume traded is 3% higher than in the auction­based model. The continuous model’s
outcome is highly dependent on the arrival order of the bids, so the results we obtain rep­
resent just one of all the possible arrival combinations. To further evaluate these methods,
it would be interesting to know what is the best and worst performance that continuous
clearing can have compared to auction clearing. From the results of the auction­based
model, we know which bids should be accepted to obtain the highest social welfare, which
could allow us to define the best arrival sequence. The authors of Ref. [5] explain that to
achieve the maximum social welfare in the continuous market, first FlexReqs must arrive
in descending price order and then FlexOffs in ascending price order. They also state
that to obtain the minimum social welfare it should be the other way around.
To build the best arrival sequence we have to sort the bids accepted in the auction­based
model by time target, type (first FlexReqs and then FlexOffs) and then FlexReqs by de­
scending price and FlexOffs by ascending price. The FlexOffs contained in a block bid
are submitted together. The one with the earlier time target sets the arrival time of the
other one, otherwise, it would not be realistic. After all the accepted bids, the rejected
bids are added to the sequence following the same criteria. In this way, we are simulating
the order of arrival that maximizes social welfare.
For the worst case, the rejected bids from the auction­based model have to be sorted by
time target, type (first FlexReqs and then FlexOffs) and then FlexReqs by ascending price
and FlexOffs by descending price. The block bids are also placed together. After all the
rejected bids, the accepted bids are added to the sequence following the same criteria.
We recall that the formulation of our models includes network constraints and block bids,
so not necessarily the most expensive FlexReqs and the cheapest FlexOffs are the ones
accepted. It depends on whether the energy traded can be transferred through the lines
and on the acceptance of the whole block bid if it is the case. Due to the complexity
of creating the arrival sequences, the analysis of the best and worst continuous clearing
scenarios are not included in this thesis. However, we are currently working on their
development for a future publication.

CHAPTER5
Conclusions
This thesis has been developed with the aim of assessing the implications of continuous
and auction­based clearing in LFMs. First, we have designed a multi­period LFM that
trades energy considering distribution network constraints and allowing the submission
of block bids. The platform matches flexibility requests (FlexReqs) from the DSO with
flexibility offers (FlexOffs) from prosumers or aggregators. Within this framework, we have
developed two models implementing auction­based and continuous clearing respectively.
The latter is one of our main contributions since no references have been found in the
literature for a continuous LFM with these features. To evaluate the performance of the
two clearing mechanisms we have created a study case. It reflects the operation of the
LFM in an environment with distributed energy resources and flexible loads.
First of all both clearing mechanisms differ in their formulation. The continuous model has
been defined as an algorithm that clears the market as soon as there is a match between
bids, following the first­come first­served principle. The auction­based model has been
formulated as an optimization problem that minimizes system costs and clears once for
all the time periods.
In both models, the implementation of block bids has been challenging due to their all­or­
nothing acceptance condition. Block bids are included in the LFM to model the rebound
effect suffered by thermal­electric loads when providing flexibility. Thus, as block bids are
needed to handle technical requirements, they can only be fully accepted or fully rejected.
The continuous clearing algorithm has been developed to store possible matches that in­
volve a block bid, but not to make them effective until all the parts of the block bids are
fully matched. The auction­based model has been formulated as a MILP to include binary
variables that account for block bids. Whereas in the auction­based clearing bids are di­
rectly accepted or rejected, in continuous clearing this process is much more tedious as
the status of the different parts of the block bids has to be checked continuously. Further­
more, the fact that these types of bids link several time periods forces the auction­based
model to clear the market for all the time periods at the same time. Therefore, the con­
tinuous clearing can be performed on an online basis, whereas auction­based clearing
implies the definition of a market horizon.

47
==PAGE:59==
We have operated the LFM in a 33 buses radial distribution grid with residential and com­
mercial consumption and small­scale renewable generation. The FlexReqs submitted
represent the DSO’s need for curtailment and load shedding to reach a feasible setpoint,
in case it has not access to system’s flexibility. FlexOffs have been created randomly
but within reasonable ranges of price and quantity offered. How to determine the price of
flexibility bids is not evident. The DSO can make up a price for the FlexReqs according
to the damages caused when curtailing and shedding loads. However, for FlexOffs the
price is difficult to quantify since in many cases to sell flexibility is a sideline business for
market participants, so we cannot ensure truthfully bidding.
When comparing the outcomes of both models, we have focused on the social welfare and
the volume traded. The auction­based model aims to maximize social welfare, whereas
the continuous model matches the entering bid and the oldest one with the best available
price. As the auction­based model clears the market once, only the total social welfare is
maximized, but not the one of each time period. On the other hand, the volume traded
is limited by network constraints. In the auction­based clearing, the volume traded has
a unique value considering the set of bids submitted, but in the continuous clearing, it
can vary depending on the bids previously matched. The auction­based clearing selects
the combination of submitted bids that yields the maximum social welfare while meeting
network constraints and block bids acceptance condition. In contrast, continuous clearing
does not has the condition of maximizing social welfare. Thus, it is likely that more bids
can be accepted in the continuous model, and therefore more volume can be exchanged.
In our study case in particular, the optimal social welfare is just 2% lower than the one
obtained with the continuous model. In terms of energy traded, the continuous model ends
up in a total volume traded 3% higher than auction­based clearing. Regarding block bids,
they present in both models similar acceptance ratios, being accepted around 60% of the
block bids submitted. However, a higher percentage of the single FlexOffs submitted have
been matched. We set a price range for block FlexOffs lower than the one for single offers
to encourage their matching, but block bids are still more difficult to match because they
link several time periods requiring full acceptance or rejection. Finally, model’s outcomes
show that the DSO’s need for curtailment and load shedding are reduced 25% and 30%
using auction­based clearing and 27% and 29% with continuous clearing.
We are aware that, while the outcome of the auction­based model is the same given a
set of submitted bids, the one of the continuous model is highly dependent on the arrival
order of the bids. To make a further comparison we propose the simulation of best and
worst case scenarios for continuous clearing. Then we could know how close and how far
the results of both clearing mechanisms are from each other. We are currently developing
this analysis for a future publication.
Apart from matching bids, the market operation also involves payments to the participants.
We have assessed the payment methods to include in flexibility markets based on four
desirable properties to have: market efficiency, incentive compatibility, cost recovery and
Market Clearing Mechanisms for Flexibility Markets in Distribution Grids
48
==PAGE:60==
revenue adequacy. The payment rule used in continuous markets is quite standardised
and can be easily applied to our LFM. It states that the older bid set the price of the match.
Social welfare is not maximized and neither the incentive compatibility is ensured. How­
ever, costs are recovered since negative profits cannot occur and the revenue adequacy
is guaranteed because the market operator never has a financial deficit. Determining
the payment for the auction­based clearing is more challenging. Because of the binary
variables introduced to model block bids, the common methods that use dual variables of
balance constraints cannot be directly applied. Thus, different possible payment methods
for this LFM have been presented in Section 3.3. The choice between them depends on
the market clearing properties that the LFM wants to address. Ideally, a market clearing
mechanism should have the four properties listed, but since they are contradictory, we
cannot ensure all of them at the same time.
In short, we conclude that the use of one or the other clearing mechanism in a LFM
depends on the target of the FMO. If it aims to have an efficient market achieving the
maximum social welfare, auction­based clearing must be chosen, complemented by a
payment rule that also supports its purpose. If the market has low liquidity the FMO
might prefer the possibility of having more volume traded at expenses of losing efficiency,
choosing the continuous clearing. This situation can occur in the early stages of LFMs’
implementation. Disregarding social welfare it is more likely a match to happen since it is
subject to fewer conditions, so it may lead to more trades.
Regardless of the clearing mechanism used, we have verified that having a LFM is ben­
eficial for the grid operation. Flexibility exchanges positively affect lines’ congestion by
spreading the load more evenly across the network and by avoiding lines operating close
to their capacity limits. In the short term, it avoids load shedding and generation curtail­
ment. In the long term, it delays grid investments.

optimal power flow might not be precise enough to perform network check.
• The price of the bids created could be further elaborated to make it more accurate.
• A mathematical proof for the suboptimality of the continuous clearing over the auction­
based clearing could be provided. We are also working on this for a future publica­
tion.
