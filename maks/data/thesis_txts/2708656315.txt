i 
Preface 
 
This master thesis of 35 ECTS credit points is the result of the experimental work carried out by Christoffer 
Torvin Andersen. The experimental work took place in the autumn of 2021 at the Department of Chemistry, 
Technical University of Denmark. The project is a part of the research in the Luca Laraia Lab focusing on 
compounds inhibiting the Aster family of cholesterol transport proteins. The project includes a literature 
study of the concept of Proteolysis Targeting Chimeras (PROTACs) and cholesterol transport proteins, 
synthesis of various PROTACs inspired by work done in the Laraia Lab and finally a cell study to test the 
synthesized compounds. This thesis is written by Christoffer Torvin Andersen (s164093)

iii 
Abstract 
 
Cholesterol transport proteins regulate many cellular processes, including lipid metabolism, vesicular and non-
vesicular trafficking, organelle contact sites, and autophagy. Despite this, few inhibitors and degraders for 
cholesterol transport proteins are known. Identifying inhibitors for this class of proteins has been challenging 
due to the structural similarities in the cholesterol-binding site. From prior work in the Laraia lab, a selective 
inhibitor of the cholesterol transport protein Aster-C, (-)-Astercin, was identified. To study the Aster family 
and potentially treat any diseases related to the protein family, it is also of interest to attempt degradation of 
the protein. Targeted protein degradation is an emerging therapeutic modality that can tackle disease-causing 
proteins that have historically been highly challenging to target with conventional small molecules. What kick-
started this research field was the discovery of proteolysis-targeting chimeras (PROTACs). PROTACs are 
hetero-bifunctional molecules that recruit a ubiquitin ligase (E3) to a given substrate protein resulting in 
targeted protein degradation. The project aims to synthesize a range of PROTACs targeting Aster-C based on 
recent work in the Laraia lab. A collection of six PROTACs (Figure 1) were synthesized starting from the 
enantioenriched starting material 9 and 25. The PROTACs were tested in U2OS cells to investigate if they 
would elicit degradation of Aster-C. Preliminary data did however not show any degradation of Aster-C.

iv 
Resumé 
 
Kolesterol transport proteiner regulerer mange cellulære processer, herunder lipiders metabolisme, vesikulær 
og non-vesikulær transport, organelle kontaktsteder og autofagi. På trods af dette findes der kun få inhibitorer 
og nedbrydere af kolesterol transport proteiner. At identificere inhibitorer for denne gruppe af proteiner har 
været udfordrende på grund af strukturelle ligheder i kolesterol bindingsstedet. Baseret på tidligere arbejde i 
Laraia laboratoriet, blev en selektiv inhibitor af kolesterol transport proteinet Aster-C, (-)-Astercin, 
identificeret. Til at studere Aster familien og potentielt behandle sygdomme relateret til denne proteinfamilie, 
har det også interesse at prøve og degradere proteinet. Målrettet protein degradering er en terapeutisk tilgang 
under udvikling, som har potentialet til at ramme sygdomsfremkaldende proteiner, som historisk set har været 
meget udfordrende at ramme med konventionelle små molekyler. Det som kickstartede dette 
forskningsområde, var opdagelsen af proteolyse-rettede kimærer (PROTACs). PROTACs er hetero-
bifunktionelle molekyler som rekrutterer en E3 ubiqutinin ligase til et givent substrat protein, hvilket resulterer 
i målrettet protein degradering. Dette projekt har som formål at syntetisere en række PROTACs rettet mod 
Aster-C med afsæt i tidligere arbejde i Laraia laboratoriet. En samling af seks PROTACs (Figur 2) blev 
syntetiseret, med udgangspunkt i de enantioberigede start materialer 9 og 25. Disse PROTACs blev testet i 
U2OS celler for at undersøge om de ville føre til degradering af Aster-C. Indledende data indikerer dog ingen 
degradering af Aster-C

1. Introduction 
 
The field of drug discovery has undergone significant changes over the last decades. The focus has shifted 
from targeting “traditional” drug targets, such as G-protein coupled receptors and various kinases, to being 
more focused on targets deemed “undruggable”1. Historically successful discovery of in vivo bioactive 
molecules has focused on identification and optimization of molecules with good activity against a biological 
target or pathway. Additionally, finding a safe and tolerated dose with a high enough concentration to yield a 
pharmacological effect has also been the focus. These principles of target occupancy and maintaining exposure 
at the site of the disease have guided the drug discovery and optimization process for years2. The majority of 
today’s drugs are therefore small-molecule based and primarily operate via occupancy-driven pharmacology 
as the mode of action (MOA)3. Despite being successful, this MOA cannot be applied to all biological targets, 
especially those lacking enzymatic activity, such as scaffolding proteins or proteins which function using 
protein-protein interactions (PPI)4. Following these principles have yielded highly effective treatments, but the 
approach is not without limitations. Identifying an agent with high potency and selectivity for a given target is 
not easy. Furthermore, it is challenging to de novo predict the pharmacodynamic behavior of molecules in vivo 
and in clinical trials, which subsequently leads to higher costs and delays. Development of resistance to 
inhibition or occupancy-driven drugs occurs in many disease indications, including cancer and bacterial 
infections5,6. As a result, efforts have been made to develop new therapeutics using different MOAs to 
modulate these “undruggable” targets.  
 
Targeted protein degradation using small molecules have successfully been proposed and developed as an 
alternative approach to target protein-causing diseases2. The strategy utilizes cellular degradation systems to 
control protein homeostasis. Degraders can chemically knockdown the protein of interest (POI) at a post-
translational level through modulation of the ubiquitin-proteasome system (UPS). A type of degrader that has 
attracted the attention of both academia and industry to solve the aforementioned problems is PROteolysis 
TArgeting Chimeras (PROTACs). PROTACs regulate protein function by degrading target proteins instead of 
inhibiting them. PROTACs make use of an event-driven MOA, where rather than compound activity relating 
to affinity, activity is instead defined as the ability of a PROTAC to elicit a desired biological event, namely 
ubiquitination3. PROTACs could be applied to study and possibly treat diseases related to cholesterol transport 
proteins (CTPs). CTPs have been implicated and proposed as targets for the treatment of viral infections, 
numerous types of cancer, and neurodegenerative disorders7. Very few selective inhibitors are known, despite 
CTPs having clear biological importance. This is partly due to the difficulties targeting the structurally related 
cholesterol-binding domains7. Identifying new selective modulators of CTPs would enable further study and 
potentially elicit therapeutic targets. One family of CTPs is the GRAMD1/Aster family, a recently discovered 
class of proteins8. The Aster proteins are responsible for the transport of cholesterol between plasma 
==PAGE:16==

membranes (PM) and the endoplasmatic reticulum (ER). Aster proteins have various roles for protein 
homeostasis, but their relevance as drug targets is yet to be elucidated7. In this introduction, I will provide a 
background to the development of PROTACs and the importance of CTPs in health and diseases. 
 
1.1 PROTACs 
 
1.1.1 
The Ubiquitin-Proteasome System 
 
Degradation of intracellular proteins is vital for maintaining normal upkeep of a given cellular function, 
including cell proliferation, differentiation, and cell death9. The degradation is mainly carried out via the UPS 
or via autophagy/lysosomal routes. Ciechanover, Hershko, and Rose made groundbreaking studies on the 
discovery and elucidation of the mechanisms of ubiquitin-dependent protein degradation, which were awarded 
the 2004 Nobel Prize in Chemistry, underlining the importance of this discovery10. In 1971 early studies were 
published by Hershko et al. which found protein degradation to be ATP-dependent11. Subsequently, the group 
discovered an ATP-dependent proteolysis factor (APF-1) that was vital for the degradation12. This factor was 
later elucidated to be ubiquitin, a highly conserved 76 amino acid protein found throughout nature13. The role 
of the UPS is to label target proteins with polyubiquitin chains, which are then delivered to the proteasome for 
destruction by the 26S proteasome, a large protein complex that consists of six proteolytic sites3. 
 
Ubiquitination of proteins is carried out by a range of ubiquitinating enzymes, named E1, E2, and E33. Initially, 
ubiquitin is activated via an ATP-dependent formation of a reactive thioester bond through adenylation 
between cysteine in the active site of the ubiquitin-activating enzyme (E1) and the carboxyl terminus of 
ubiquitin. Ubiquitin is transferred to ubiquitin conjugase (E2) to form a new thioester bond via a trans-
thiolation reaction and ultimately is conjugated to either a lysine residue within the given substrate or the N-
terminal amino group through a ubiquitin ligase (E3) that identifies specific motifs in the target protein14. E3 
is responsible for recruiting the target protein and is vital for the specificity of the UPS15. To be degraded by 
the 26S proteasome, a protein must contain at least four ubiquitin molecules, therefore the process of 
ubiquitination is repeated until a polyubiquitin chain has been formed. Ubiquitin contains seven lysine 
residues, which facilitates reiterative rounds of ubiquitination by the E3 ligase. The 26S proteasome removes 
and recycles the ubiquitin units, and ultimately it proteolytically cleaves and degrades the target protein2,15 
(Figure 3).

For the UPS pathway, E3 ligase-mediated ubiquitin transfer to the target protein is catalyzed by the proximity 
of the ligase complex alongside specific recognition elements and the availability of residues onto which 
ubiquitin can be delivered2. This proximity-induced enzymatic ubiquitin transfer presents an opportunity as a 
possible therapeutic target. The UPS system is highly specific and efficient at subsequent degradation of 
proteins, but if this machinery is to be exploited from a therapeutic point of view, a series of cellular events 
must work correctly for the degradation to take place. This includes proper access to the correct intracellular 
compartment, successful ternary complex formation, and correct ubiquitinylation2.

PROTACs present a relatively new area of medicinal chemistry and are designed to recruit the UPS for the 
degradation of a POI. PROTACs are heterobifunctional molecules that bind simultaneously to a E3 ubiquitin 
ligase and a POI (Figure 4). The E3 ubiquitin ligase and the ligand for the POI are joined by a chemical linker. 
The linker can have a significant impact on the PROTAC activity depending on its composition and its length, 
which will be explained in more detail in section 1.1.4. The concept of PROTACs was first coined by 
Sakamoto et al. in 2001, in studies of cellular degradation of the methionine aminopeptidase-2 (MetAP2), 
using the compound PROTAC-A, a MetAP2 inhibitor ovalicin linked to an IκBα phosphopeptide epitope 
which was known to bind to the E3 ligase SCFβTrCP (Figure 5)17. PROTAC-A recruited MetAP-2 to SCFβTrCP 
for polyubiquitination and subsequent proteasomal degradation. In a later study, the general applications of 
the strategy were demonstrated through the development of PROTAC derivatives of oestradiol and 
dihydroxytestosterone to degrade the estrogen and androgen (AR) receptors18. In 2004 a short hydroxyproline-
containing peptide sequence derived from the Von Hippel-Lindau tumor suppressor (VHL) natural substrate, 
the transcription factor hypoxia-inducible factor 1α (HIF1α), was exploited to design the first PROTAC which 
recruited the VHL E3 ligase19. When exposed to physiological oxygen levels, HIF1α is hydroxylated by 
enzymes and is recognized by VHL for degradation, thereby maintaining homeostasis by preventing 
expression of hypoxic-response genes19,20.

due to its highly polar peptide sequence, limiting the therapeutic scope. The issue of cell permeability was 
solved with microinjection into the cells to obtain activity. The early PROTAC compounds faced several 
challenges to become relevant therapeutic candidates, including the polar nature, high molecular weight, and 
poor cell permeability. The next step towards a functioning PROTAC in drug discovery was made when 
nonpeptidic E3 ligase binding moieties showed evidence of 
degradation in 200821. The issues highlighted previously, were

At high concentrations, unwanted and unproductive binary complexes may be observed. This is referred to as 
the hook effect36. Furthermore, favorable or repulsive interactions between the POI and E3 can affect ternary 
complex conformation. To describe these interactions, the term cooperativity (𝛼)  is used. When the 
cooperativity is positive (𝛼> 1), then stabilizing PPIs between the POI and the E3 promote the formation of 
the complex. If the cooperativity is negative (𝛼< 1), then the interactions disrupt ternary complex formation36. 
If positive cooperation occurs, this will minimize the hook effect, yielding a more productive ternary complex 
formation37. A crystal structure of a ternary complex was first reported with the PROTAC MZ1 bound to the 
bromodomain of BRD4BD2 and VHL by the Ciulli lab in 201738. This finding highlighted that positive 
cooperativity results in a higher ternary complex stabilization and thereby also a more productive formation 
of the complex.  
 
Positive cooperation has been shown to minimize the hook effect in a recent study where a PROTAC targeting 
Bruton´s tyrosine kinase (BTK) showed significant positive cooperativity in the ternary complex, and no 
observable hook effect below 2.5 μM was seen39. A 
suggestion to improve the cooperative binding is by 
using macrocyclization. From previous work, Testa et 
al. assumed that macrocyclization of a PROTAC would 
increase the energetic bias towards a productive ternary 
complex formation rather than towards the binary 
complexes, as it is favorable thermodynamically to 
adopt a bound state because of reduced conformational 
flexibility38. The macrocyclic PROTAC (Figure 9) 
showed enhanced degradation potency and selectivity 
towards homologous targets compared to a linear 
degrader, MZ140. This indicates that understanding the

dissociates, this is favorable as it permits the PROTAC to form additional complexes with remaining POI 
molecules34. Recently it was elucidated that PROTACs with weak interactions with a POI can still be potent 
degraders if a sufficiently stable ternary complex is formed. Crews et al. showed with a PROTAC, based on 
the pharmaceutical Foretnib, an affinity of ~ 10 micromolar (µM) for a p38 kinase, and the PROTAC could

1.1.3 
Inhibition vs. degradation 
PROTACs can be advantageous compared to small-molecule inhibitors (SMIs). Due to a PROTACs 
mechanism of action, a PROTAC has the potential to catalyze the degradation of multiple POI molecules. 
Hence, PROTACs can often be used at lower concentrations than SMIs to obtain a pharmacological effect, 
limiting off-target effects and toxicity. PROTACs offer a method of modulating the functional selectivity of a 
ligand, yielding highly selective degraders with limited side effects. There is also the possibility of targeting 
previously “undruggable” target proteins that traditionally were targeted via the conventional occupancy-based 
strategy21. Inhibitors need to bind in well-defined pockets to affect protein function, which might not be 
sufficient to block protein activity. PROTACs only need to bind to recruit the UPS and form the ternary 
complex for degradation to occur. This mimics genetic knockdown/knockout strategies, yielding a more 
sustained cellular effect and extended duration of action, improving the overall pharmacology10. Finally, 
PROTACs can be broadly applicable due to the conservation and essentiality of the UPS and that E3 ubiquitin 
ligases such as CRBN and VHL are widely expressed across different cells42.  
 
Some concerns remain before arguing for a PROTAC to proceed to clinical trials. Being bifunctional 
molecules, PROTACs have a high molecular weight, which may reduce cell permeability and tissue 
penetration and the possibility for oral administration. In general, the toxicities associated with PROTACs can 
be divided into on-target and off-target toxicity. On-target toxicity is mainly due to PROTACs removing the 
enzymatic functions of a protein and the scaffold functions of a protein, as the protein is degraded21. SMIs 
rarely inhibit their target completely, while a potent PROTAC can fully deplete the target. If the POI possesses 
vital functions, then depletion of the POI could result in detrimental effects. Another on-target toxicity of 
PROTACs comes from the accumulation of the natural substrates of an E3 ligase when the tissue E3 ligase 
expression is low21. As mentioned earlier, PROTACs do not need a strong binding affinity to the POI compared 
to the parent SMIs to induce target degradation. Therefore, off-target toxicity can occur from the degradation 
of proteins not directly bound by a PROTAC, which are part of the same protein complex or is in close vicinity. 
Off-target toxicity also arises from the hook effect, where the binary PROTAC-E3 ligase complexes degrade 
non-targeted proteins via binding to targets with a lower affinity than the intended POI43. Overall the on- and 
off-target toxicity can be avoided by designing ligands for tissue-specific E3 ligases21. 
 
1.1.4 
PROTAC design  
PROTAC design relies on the combination of three different chemical moieties, all with different properties: 
The E3 ligase binder, the POI ligand, and a chemical linker to connect the two moieties. The best combination 
of these elements requires careful consideration and a study of the structural characteristics of the E3 ligase 
and the POI. The spatial orientation and how the PROTAC aligns to form the ternary complex is crucial for 
an effective protein knockdown by the UPS44.  
==PAGE:23==
 
 
 
 
9 
To date, most PROTACs have been developed from a well-characterized ligand, often an inhibitor, as the POI 
ligand45. Often the synthesis of the POI ligand has been guided by structure-activity relationship (SAR) 
information or from a crystal structure with the POI ligand bound to the target. This information has been used 
to guide PROTAC design, for example suggesting a suitable attachment point for the linker on the POI ligand. 
However, if a well-characterized ligand is lacking, this limits early-stage drug discovery projects, especially 
on biological targets with a poorly understood pharmacology46. If a non-solvent-exposed binding pocket fully 
embeds its POI ligand, this represents a significant challenge to discover the optimal attachment point for the 
linker. To combat this, orthogonal approaches have been applied for target validation and research purposes. 
Here one can mention the techniques HaloPROTAC and dTAG to elucidate early target validation47–49. To 
address the need for structural information for facilitating proper POI development, X-Ray crystallography 
and cryo-electron microscopy have become the favorite methods to avoid working in the dark50. Another 
exciting approach is the AI technology called AlphaFold developed by Google, which can accurately predict 
a protein’s 3D shape from the amino acid sequence51. This discovery has the potential to accelerate the drug 
discovery process, also within the field of PROTACs, if an experimental structure of a protein is not available. 
 
Choosing the correct E3 ligase determines how successful the protein knockdown is. For a given target, the 
degradation profile is different depending on the recruited E3 ligase, making it more challenging to choose the 
correct E3 ligase52. Some evidence suggests that E3 ligases with a tissue-selective expression profile are 
expected to present unique opportunities, even if the mechanism has not been elucidated53. For now, only a 
few E3 ligases, including CRBN, VHL, MDM2, and cIAP have been targeted by PROTACs, but more than 
600 E3 ligases are predicted to be encoded by the human genome53. Traditionally recruiters bind reversibly to 
the E3 ligase, but it has been demonstrated that targeting E3 ligases with covalent reactive small molecules is 
also a viable approach to finding E3 ligases. To explore this approach, chemical proteomics have become a 
powerful tool for large-scale profiling of small molecule interactions across the proteasome54. Another method 
utilizing electrophilic covalent fragments targeting “undruggable” proteins was built into a compound library 
to discover electrophilic E3 ligands in cell-lysates55,56. This resulted in a chloroacetamide-bearing PROTAC, 
which alkylated previously uncharacterized E3 ligases, the E3 ligase DCAF16. The approach yielded more E3 
ligases and Tong et al. recently developed a PROTAC with a bardoxolone recruiting KEAP1 ligase57.  
 
Since ligands for the POI and the E3 ligase are often optimized fully for the binary interactions, the linker 
moiety connecting the two parts is the primary opportunity for optimizing protein degradation using 
PROTACs. The linker must reach a delicate balance between forming a stable ternary complex and sufficient 
POI ubiquitination, while ensuring the PROTAC is still soluble and cell-permeable. This field is generally 
coined linkerology. The linker should orient the E3 ligase and the POI such that the two proteins are in 
proximity and allow for favorable PPIs to occur34, leading to degradation by the UPS. The linker length needs

1.1.5 
Challenges for PROTAC development 
The field of targeted protein degradation has been expanding exponentially over the last 20 years; however, 
many questions remain to be addressed for ensuring development into therapeutic candidates. There are many 
factors to consider when designing PROTACs, all of which significantly impact the outcome. Key choices to 
make for the design are the E3 ligase, the POI ligand, and the properties of the linker. Parameters such as 
chemical stability, permeability, and solubility are also factors that need to be considered. Therefore, the 
identification of promising PROTACs have been and continues to be based on an empirical approach of library 
synthesis with compounds differing in linker length, composition, and attachment point to the E3 and POI 
ligand. The library is then screened in cells for degradative activity, and the process is iterated with minor 
changes based on the results. This is a time-consuming and costly process, which could be reduced if a targeted 
approach for developing heterobifunctional degraders was available. Just as structure-guided design 
accelerated small molecule discovery, a similar approach in the targeted protein degradation field could do the 
same3. Further development must also be made to explore the E3 ligase space. Well over 600 E3 proteins exist, 
but only a few quality ligands have been discovered and used for PROTACs, mainly VHL and CRBN3. 
 
Efficacy at low nM and pM concentrations have been achieved, however cell delivery and bioavailability 
remain an issue due to the high molecular weight of the PROTACs59. Most PROTACs fall outside what is 
generally considered the drug-like space regarding their physicochemical properties. The MOA for PROTACs 
can compensate for the challenges revolving around cell permeability. Some PROTACs are orally bioavailable 
despite breaking the conventional medicinal chemistry rules such as Lipinski´s Rule of Five65,66. If a PROTAC 
is administered orally, additional complications can arise as the hook effect affects the binding at high

1.2 Cholesterol and cholesterol transport proteins 
 
Sterols, like cholesterol in mammalian cells and ergosterol in fungi, are crucial molecules for a correctly 
functioning PM. Cholesterol has attracted attention due to its role in regulating cellular functions, cell 
membrane integrity, and its complex metabolism67. In recent decades there has been an increase in the 
understanding of the underlying mechanism of cholesterol homeostasis and cholesterol transport between 
tissues however much is still unknown68. Cholesterol is highly heterogeneously distributed throughout 
intracellular membranes, with most of the total cholesterol levels found in the PM. Intermediate cholesterol 
concentrations are found in early endosomes and the trans-Golgi network, while only lower amounts of 
cholesterol are found in the mitochondria and the ER68.  
 
To ensure proper cellular function, the cholesterol levels within each organelle must be maintained. Cholesterol 
homeostasis is a complex and highly regulated system that ensures the correct distribution of cholesterol is 
achieved and maintained69. The source of cholesterol comes from the de novo biosynthesis in the ER, and the 
cellular uptake of low-density lipoprotein (LDL) derived cholesterol. The low levels of cholesterol found in 
the ER allow cholesterol-sensing mechanisms to detect subtle changes in cholesterol levels, allowing them to 
adjust accordingly to maintain cholesterol homeostasis. This is seen with high cholesterol levels in the ER, 
which accelerates the degradation of 3-hydroxy-3-methylglutaryl-coenzyme A reductase (HMG-CoA 
reductase), a coenzyme that has an important role in cholesterol biosynthesis. When low levels of cholesterol 
are found in the ER, sterol regulatory element-binding proteins (SREBP) is translocated from the ER to the 
Golgi, and mature SREBP is transported into the nucleus, where transcription of genes associated with 
cholesterol uptake and biosynthesis are upregulated. When increased intracellular cholesterol levels are 
observed, SREBP is trapped in the ER membrane via a sterol-mediated PPI with SREBP-cleavage-activating 
protein (SCAP). SCAP activates proteolytic cleavage and translocation of a SREBP fragment into the nucleus, 
regulating the genes involved in cholesterol synthesis70. The de novo synthesized cholesterol is transferred to 
the PM by non-vesicular trafficking mechanisms. The cellular uptake of LDL-derived cholesterol arises from 
receptor-mediated endocytosis of LDL, which delivers lipoproteins to the late endosomes/lysosomes where 
free cholesterol is produced from the hydrolysis of cholesterol esters. The cholesterol is recycled back to the 
PM or transported to the ER. In the PM, cholesterol can be found with sphingolipids combined into detergent-
==PAGE:27==
 
 
 
 
13 
resistant protein-lipid domains, often referred to as lipid rafts. These rafts have been proposed to provide a 
membrane region for signaling and other functions71,72.  
 
Dietary cholesterol is also a source of cholesterol, where it is absorbed from the gastrointestinal tract. This can 
lead to an excess of cholesterol, resulting in a decrease in de novo biosynthesis of cholesterol as there is a 
decrease in SCAP mediated SREBP cleavage activation. In addition to this, a hepatic response occurs, which 
activates the nuclear hormone receptor, liver X receptor (LXR), leading to bile acid synthesis and secretion. 
Cholesterol can also undergo esterification by the acyl-CoA:cholesterol acyltransferase (ACATs) as another 
way of decreasing excess levels of cholesterol73.  
 
1.2.1 
Cholesterol in health and disease 
 
Cholesterol homeostasis is tightly regulated to maintain proper cellular functions, as excess cholesterol can be 
harmful. Cholesterol serves as a precursor for the synthesis of vital steroid hormones and bile acids. 
Furthermore, cholesterol is important for maintaining and modulation of transmembrane signaling pathways. 
The upkeep of the homeostasis is performed by the liver via metabolic processes, including the de novo 
biosynthesis, esterification of excess free cholesterol, intake, and exportation of cholesterol74. Dysfunctional 
homeostasis is connected to a variety of diseases, among others diabetes, neurodegeneration, and chronic 
inflammation75. A known disease associated with dysfunctional cholesterol homeostasis is the lysosomal 
storage disorder Niemann-Pick type C (NPC). NPC is characterized by accumulation of cholesterol and 
sphingolipids in the lysosomes, due to mutations in the lysosomal proteins, NPC1 and NPC2, which both play 
a role in cholesterol transport8.  
 
1.2.2 
Sterol transport proteins 
 
As mentioned, cholesterol is highly heterogeneously distributed throughout intracellular membranes, but 
cholesterol can move dynamically between the different organelles. In general, cholesterol can be trafficked 
between organelles in two different ways, either by vesicular or non-vesicular. In vesicular transport, an intact 
cytoskeleton is required, as well as ATP to provide energy for the movement76. Non-vesicular transport is 
mediated efficiently by membrane contact sites (MCSs) and sterol transport proteins (STPs). MCSs provide 
spatial proximity between neighboring organelles and enables direct transport of cholesterol over a small 
distance. STPs bind and transport cholesterol in their sterol-binding domains and often have a module for 
localization of a certain membrane alongside a transmembrane module that connects the STPs to a 
membrane77. The majority of STPs can be grouped into three families; oxysterol-binding protein (OSBP)-
related protein (ORP) family, steroidogenic acute regulatory protein (StAR)-related lipid transfer (START)-
related domain (STARD) protein family, and lipid transfer proteins anchored at a membrane contact site (Lam) 
family77.  
==PAGE:28==
 
 
 
 
14 
The ORP family consists of 12 members in mammals and seven in yeast. Except for ORP3(2), all contain a 
highly conserved C-terminal OSBP domain which selectively binds sterols and/or phospholipids. ORPs can 
target different membranes due to the pleckstrin homology domain and the diphenylalanine in an acidic tract 
motif close to the N-terminus77. The STARD family is a family made up of a START domain that diversly 
binds sterols, phospholipids, and sphingolipids. The mammalian STARD family compromises 15 members, 
which subsequently are grouped into six subfamilies. Of these, the STARD 1/3 and STARD 4/5/6 subfamilies 
are involved with sterol binding and transfer77. The Lam family consists of three proteins (GRAMD1A-C) in 
mammals and six (Lam1-6p) in yeast. GramD1A, -B, and -C are also named Aster-A, -B and -C respectively. 
The proteins contain a GRAM domain, a START-like domain, and a transmembrane segment for ER 
localization. Both in mammals and yeast, the proteins are highly conserved, indicating an vital physiological 
function8.  
 
1.2.3 
The Aster family of CTPs 
The Aster family resides in the ER and contains unique domains for binding cholesterol to facilitate the transfer 
of cholesterol from the PM to the ER, thereby serving as a molecular bridge 75. The functions of each of the 
three domains in the Aster family can be seen in Figure 12. 
 
Figure 12: Sandhu paper. Adapted from Sandhu et. al8 
The proteins contain a START-like domain, named the ASTER domain, which binds sterols via a hydrophobic 
pocket. This is necessary for the transport of cholesterol. The N-terminal GRAM domain regulates cholesterol-
dependent Aster transport to the PM-ER contact sites by binding to phosphatidylserine, found on the PM. If 
cholesterol levels exceed homeostatic levels, the Aster proteins will form bridges from the PM to the ER, and 
the sterol-binding ASTER domains facilitate extraction of cholesterol from the PM and down the concentration

gradient to the ER. The proteins are anchored to the ER membrane by a single transmembrane helix originating 
from the C-terminal to bring the membranes into proximity8.  
 
The three Aster proteins are expressed in varying levels in the tissues with active lipid metabolism, (Figure 
13). This indicates that the Asters potentially have an essential role in sterol transport in other tissues. Aster-
B is expressed in low levels in the liver, when other Aster proteins are present. Aster-A is highly present within 
the cortex, indicating that this could be of interest for exploring sterol trafficking in neurons. This different 
expression levels in tissue emphasizes the importance of understanding the mechanisms of cholesterol 
transport for each member of the Aster family8.  
 
Figure 13: Aster family relative expression of Aster-A,-B, and -C in different tissues. Adapted from Sandhu et al.8 
Aster-A was recently discovered to modulate the regulation of autophagy and autophagosome biogenesis78. 
During autophagy, double-membrane structures known as phagophores are needed to engulf cytoplasmic 
components and form autophagosomes, which fuse with lysosomes for degradation79. Aster-B expression is 
required to deliver high-density lipoprotein (HDL) cholesterol from the scavenger receptor class B member I 
(SR-BI) to the ER in adrenals. Aster-B is highly expressed in steroidogenic organs and is needed for the 
steroidogenesis in the adrenal cortex. This was shown by Sandhu et al. by using ster-B knockout mice. These 
mice were shown to be deficient in adrenal cholesterol storage and steroidogenesis because of an inability to 
transport cholesterol from SR-Bi to the ER8. Aster-C acts as a negative regulator of COPI vesicles in lysosomal 
trafficking and activates mammalian target of rapamycin complex 1 (mTORC1) in mammalian cells, which 
subsequently regulates eukaryotic cell growth and metabolism. The mTORC1 complex is required to regulate 
autophagy in response to changes in the nutritional status. Aster-C deficiency significantly inhibits autophagic 
degradation of lipids in the lysosomes, which results in accumulation of lipid droplets in the lysosome80.  
 
1.2.4 
Known small molecule inhibitors of the Asters 
The selective expression of Asters in metabolic tissues indicates that the proteins have a varied role in lipid 
metabolism and trafficking. Therefore, there is a need for tools that selectively target Aster-dependent non-
vesicular cholesterol transport. Figure 14 shows the chemical structures of known inhibitors of the Asters, 
where the inhibitors AI-1l and U18666A are based on a cholesterol scaffold81. Autogramin-2 was recently 
==PAGE:30==

discovered as a result of a high-content, image-based phenotypic screen78. Autogramin-2 is a selective inhibitor 
for Aster-A with good potency and inhibits autophagosome biogenesis, as it is directly engaged in the initiation 
of the autophagosome biogenesis through regulation of cholesterol homeostasis at phagophores and 
autophagosomes. This results in increased cholesterol content in the autophagosome. Upon autophagy 
induction, Aster-A accumulates at the autophagosome initiation sites in the ER, possibly due to the increased 
levels of the Phosphatidylinositol 3-phosphate (PIP3) phospholipid which recruits proteins, where some are 
involved in protein trafficking to the membranes78. U18666A is a non-selective inhibitor for NPC proteins and 
CTPs, but has a higher potency for Aster-B over either Aster-A or -C. It is shown to inhibit both vesicular and 
non-vesicular cholesterol transport. AI-1l is more selective for Aster-C over Aster-A and -B, but also inhibits 
the SREBP mechanism, making it non-selective over other STP families81.

2. Aims and Objectives 
 
This project aims to synthesize PROTACs based on the work previously carried out in the Laraia lab, where a 
sterol-inspired compound collection composed of different sterol AB-ring heterocyclic fusions was 
synthesized with the aim of testing them against various CTPs. This led to the discovery of the potent molecule 
(-)-Astercin, which inhibits the protein Aster-C7.  
 
Figure 15: Structures of (-)-Astercin and the E3 ligase ligands pomalidomide and VH-032 used in this project. 
 
PROTACs are heterobifunctional molecules that simultaneously bind to an E3 ubiquitin ligase and a POI. (-)-
Astercin and (+)-Astercin will provide the ligand for our POI, Aster-C. Pomalidomide and VH-032 will be the 
ligands binding to cereblon and VHL, two E3 ligases (Figure 15). These two parts will be joined by a linker, 
which will vary in chemical composition and length. Using different linker lengths and composition is 
important, as the ternary complex formation is not a straightforward process and reduced or even lack of 
activity can be observed for PROTACs with minor changes in the linker. Two examples of PROTACs are 
given in Figure 16. The objective is to synthesize eight to ten PROTACs with a CRBN E3 ligase ligand and a 
VHL E3 ligase ligand. The PROTACs will differ in linker length and chemical composition to investigate the 
optimal parameters for the PROTACs to obtain formation of a ternary complex to induce degradation of Aster-
C. The PRTOACs will be tested in U2OS cells to investigate if they will induce degradation of Aster-C.

3. Results and Discussion 
 
With the synthesis of (-)-Astercin, a potent inhibitor for the Aster-C protein is available. It was discovered that 
(-)-Astercin was more selective towards Aster-C protein than compared to (±)-Astercin7. If compared to 
cholesterol then (-)-Astercin has an unnatural stereochemistry, which could explain the selectivity. With proof 
that the protein Aster-C can be selectively inhibited with  (-)-Astercin, forming an altered POI ligand from (-
)-Astercin as a part of a PROTAC, is the aim. If racemic Astercin is analyzed, there are several positions 
suitable for functionalization (Figure 17). The alcohol in the parent AB ring system could be functionalized or 
oxidized to the ketone. Another approach would be to oxidize at the benzylic position to form a ketone. Based 
on work in the Laraia lab, both of these positions were successfully functionalized, but their IC50’s compared 
to (-)-Astercin were worse, suggesting their functionalization with a linker would be too detrimental for 
binding.  Xiao et al. obtained the crystal structure of AI-1l bound to Aster-C, which showed that the OH of the 
A-ring usually pointed into the binding pocket81. This rules out functionalization from the A-ring as a potential 
handle for linker attachment. From a synthetic standpoint, the heterobenzylic oxidation would require more 
work to make viable. The A ring would need to be protected, the carbonyl in the B ring would be reduced, 
leading to formation of diastereoisomers, which would both have to be functionalized as the diastereoisomers 
would make the handle point in different directions. This leads to the final position for functionalization, the 
pyrazole. Upon formation, the pyrazole could be synthesized with an ester handle, allowing for hydrolysis of 
the ester to the corresponding acid. The same reactions could also be applied to (+)-Astercin, which has the 
natural stereochemistry, which was the case during this project.  
 
Figure 17: Possible functionalization positions in (-)-Astercin. 
Alternatively, the pyrazole ring could be halogenated, allowing for cross-coupling reactions. This was 
attempted by Ph.D. student Thomas Whitmarsh-Everiss; however it was unsuccessful. Keeping the typical 
PROTAC chemistry in mind, then it is also easier to attach a linker using an ester handle. The synthesis of the 
(-)-Astercin ligand starts with the formation of the (R)-Wieland-Miescher Ketone (WMK), which lays the 
foundation for the continued synthesis having the correct stereochemistry.

3.1.1 – Synthesis of the triketone 3 by Michael addition 
 
  
Scheme 1: The reaction scheme for the Michael addition to form the triketone 3. 
Starting from commercially available diketone 1 and methyl vinyl ketone 2, a standard Michael addition can

3.2.1 – Acetal protection of ketone 9 
 
 
 
Scheme 4: The reaction scheme for the acetal protection of 9. 
To obtain the desired structure of the AB-ring system, the conjugated double bond in the A ring must be 
reduced, which will be achieved via a dissolving metal reduction. To ensure reduction at the A ring, the ketone 
in the B ring must be protected, as ketones will react with strong nucleophilic bases otherwise yielding the 
alcohol. The ketone is protected before reduction of the enone, as it is possible to selectively protect the 
carbonyl which is needed for further synthesis. This is made possible by the difference in reactivity between 
an enone and a ketone. The ketone was protected as an acetal, as this carbonyl derivative is stable and will not 
react in strongly basic environments. The acetal deprotection happens under mild acidic conditions, making it 
easy to remove again. Formation of acetals is reversible and therefore the reaction is run using anhydrous 
ethylene glycol. Molecular sieves were also added, which will remove water as it is formed from the 
hemiacetal, driving the reaction to completion. The reaction proceeds quickly, and the reaction time was kept 
to 30-45 minutes, with the main impurity observed in LC-MS being the unreacted starting material 9. 
Formation of the acetal at the saturated ketone versus the α, β-unsaturated ketone, can be explained with the 
saturated ketone being more electrophilic if compared to the α, β-unsaturated ketone due to resonance 
stabilization making it less electrophilic, and therefore also less reactive to nucleophiles.

result of free solvated electrons. This solution generates the electrons needed to perform a one electron 
reduction. The proton source is an alcohol, in this case t-BuOH (Figure 19).

The transfer of an electron forms a radical anion, which in turn is protonated by the alcohol, forming a radical.

will adopt a trans-conformation, as this is the more stable intermediate having all the carbons in an equatorial 
conformation. The enolate will not be further reduced and during acidic workup the ketone is formed. In

reaction by TLC and LC-MS, which required the addition of lithium (0.5 equiv.) and t-BuOH (0.2 equiv.) 
added to the reaction to try to increase conversion of the product. The reaction was quenched using isoprene

3.2.3 – Ketone reduction using sodium borohydride 
 
 
Scheme 6: The reduction of 11 to form 12a and 12b using NaBH4.

reduction. Ketone 11 was dissolved in a 4:1 mix of THF/MeOH and cooled to 0°C, where NaBH4 was added. 
A mix of THF/MeOH was shown to give an improved diastereomeric ratio (d.r.) compared to using either pure 
THF or MeOH87. A hydride transfer happens between NaBH4 and the ketone forming the alkoxide. This 
alkoxide was then protonated by the protic solvent MeOH yielding the secondary alcohol 12a. After the 
reaction was deemed complete, water was added to decompose any residual NaBH4. Sodium borohydride is 
considered a non-bulky reagent, meaning it will predominantly approach the ketone axially putting the formed 
secondary alcohol in an equatorial position (Figure 20). NaBH4 preferentially attacks axially to avoid any 
eclipsing interactions from the formed alcohol and hydride. For small reagents the 1,3-diaxial interaction is 
minimized making the axial approach the preferred.

3.5. Fluorescence Polarization studies 
 
The synthesized analogs of (-)-Astercin, were tested in fluorescence polarization (FP) assays by a Ph.D. student 
in the Laraia lab, to determine how well they would bind to purified ASTER domains, and how they would 
compare against (-)-Astercin. For the FP assays performed, the fluorophore was nitrobenzoxadiazole bound to 
cholesterol (22-NBD-Cholesterol), which binds to the Aster proteins. FP is often used to monitor molecular 
interactions in solution. FP is based on the principle that the degree of polarization is inversely proportional to 
the molecular rotation. The fluorophore is excited with polarized light, and if the fluorophore is bound to a 
larger target (i.e. a protein), it will rotate slower due to being a larger complex and emitted light will remain 
largely polarized, whereas if it is unbound, the fluorophore rotates rapidly and the emitted light becomes more 
depolarized, the ratio between the two giving the net polarization of the system. The FP assays were performed 
as a competitive assay, giving information about whether the compounds bind to and outcompete 22-NBD-
Cholesterol from the ASTER binding site. By testing compounds at different concentrations, the IC50 values 
could be determined from polarization values of the system. FP is used as it is rapidly performed and the results 
can be analyzed quickly.  
 
The FP assays were performed for the compounds 18, 19, and 21 (Figure 30). We expected that we would see 
similar activity as for (-)-Astercin, as the modification was expected to point out of the binding site in Aster-
C and therefore not interfere. It would be especially interesting for compound 21 to retain activity as this would 
identify if the attached linker was well tolerated. Opposite to what was expected, the FP assays indicated that 
none of the compounds had similar potency for Aster-C compared to (-)-Astercin. For ester 18 no inhibition 
was observed, and for acid 19 (IC50 ≈ 20 µM) a reduced binding capability if compared to (-)-Astercin (IC50 = 
1.51 µM). These findings indicate that the (-)-Astercin analogs synthesized will not efficiently bind to Aster-
C and will not function as appropriate POI ligands for Aster-C degrading PROTACs. For compound 21 an 
IC50 greater than 40 µM was observed, and it was hypothesized that this weakened binding could be due to the 
available proton in the amide 21, which could introduce an unfavorable hydrogen bond donor resulting in less 
efficient binding in the ASTER binding site. This hypothesis was investigated in parallel by another Ph.D. 
student in the Laraia lab, where smaller amine linkers and a N-methylated secondary amine were attached to 
the ester handle. These analogs were also tested in FP, where they showed similar activity to each other, but 
lower activity compared to the ester, indicating the available proton was not the reason for the reduced binding.

3.8 Cell studies of synthesized PROTACs 
 
To test the synthesized PROTACs, a cell study was carried out by a Ph.D. student from the group to see 
whether the PROTACs could induce degradation of Aster-C in cells. For the degradation experiment U2OS 
cells were used, as they are a common cell line that expresses Aster-C in a greater amount compared to other 
commonly used cell lines. If successful, degradation and therefore an absence of a gel band would be seen 
using a Western blot with an Aster-C antibody. PROTACs 40-45 were incubated with the U2OS cells for 18 
hours at a concentration of 3.0 and 0.3 µM alongside DMSO treated cells serving as a negative control. Cell 
lysates were prepared, and Aster-C levels probed via western blot. Disappointingly, no degradation of Aster-
C had occurred when compared to the DMSO controls (Figure 37) This is preliminary data, and the experiment 
would have to be repeated to be certain of the results. The cell study also had some minor technical issues, 
why a second run to confirm the results would be valuable, however it will not prevent any degradation from 
taking place.

One possible explanation of why the PROTAC did not successfully degrade Aster-C is the linker length. As 
described the linker length and composition can influence the ternary complex formation and thereby also 
potency. Seeing as all the synthesized PROTACs have a similar length, a longer linker might induce the 
formation of the ternary complex and elicit degradation. This is supported by the recent paper by Xiao et al. 
where a crystal structure of Aster-C in complex with the inhibitor AI-1l, showed that the ligand was binding 
deep within the cholesterol binding pocket81. Another explanation could be that the choice of attachment point 
for the ester handle was not tolerated by Aster-C and leads to insufficient binding. This can however not be 
concluded, as the data to support it, is lacking.

4. Conclusion and Future Work  
 
In conclusion, six PROTACs have been synthesized. A viable route for the functionalization of (-)-Astercin 
and subsequently synthesis of (-)-Astercin based PROTACs have been reported. All the synthesized 
PROTACs were derived from one of the two enantiomers of the Wieland Miescher Ketone 9 and 25, starting 
from the commercially available materials 1 and 2. (R)-9 and (S)-25 WMK were obtained through a Robinson 
annulation using an organocatalyst to ensure enantioenriched 9 compounds. Through four reactions the known 
ketones 14 and 30 were synthesized. From a Claisen condensation, using LDA as the non-nucleophilic base, 
the diketoesters 16 and 31 were synthesized with yields ranging from 41-90%. Following a Knorr pyrazole 
synthesis, the compounds 18 and 33 were afforded with yields between 4-50%. A simple basic hydrolysis 
yields the final functionalized acid analogs 19 and 34. Initial attempts of performing an amide coupling 
between acid 19 and the linker 20 was successful using HATU and DIPEA in DMF, but issues with low yields, 
purification and difficulties removing residual DMF, led to investigating using DIPEA, EDC and HOBt 
instead, which proved more successful.  
 
The compounds 18, 19 and 21 were tested in fluorescence polarization studies to see how well they would 
inhibit the Aster-C protein. Surprisingly, the compounds did not inhibit Aster-C. In parallel racemic versions 
of ester 18 and acid 19 were synthesized and tested, and here similar inhibition was seen if compared to the 
parent compound (-)-Astercin. This indicates that the enantiomer responsible for binding is the (+)-enantiomer. 
Therefore, the project shifted focus to use (S)-WMK 25 to synthesize acid 34. The two enantiopure Astercin 
analogs 19 and 34 were coupled to a linker (35-39) with an E3 ligase ligand attached, yielding the PROTACs 
40-45. These reactions differed in yield and purity, but a viable route has been investigated and reported. When 
tested in U2OS cells PROTACs 40-45 did not elicit any degradation according to the preliminary data.  
 
There are multiple possible explanations for the lack of degradation, but one suggestion is due to the linker 
either being too short or not having the right chemical composition. The PROTACs primarily had a CRBN 
based E3 ligase ligand, but many more E3 ligases are known. Changing the E3 ligase ligand could also lead to 
degradation, as a more favorable ternary complex could be formed. Future work would therefore include 
synthesizing more VHL based PROTACs to see if this will lead to degradation of Aster-C. PROTACs with a 
longer linker length, either with a PEG based or an alkyl linker, would also be of interest to investigate. If 
increasing the linker length of a PROTAC leads to successful degradation, this could then be optimized with 
attempts at rigidifying the linker, by either incorporating a heterocyclic scaffold or an alkyne. Possible new 
structures of PROTACs which could be of interest can be seen in Figure 38.

Linkers 35-39 (1 equiv.) was dissolved in DCM (3 mL) and cooled to 0°C. TFA (3 mL) was added dropwise 
over 5 min. The mix was stirred for 2 h. Upon completion the solvent was evaporated, and the residual solid 
was basified with aq. NaHCO3. The mix was extracted with DCM and the organic phases were dried with 
MgSO4, filtered, and concentrated in vacuo. Acids 19 or 34 was added to a round bottomed flask and dissolved 
in DCM (3 mL). DIPEA (2.5 equiv.), EDC (1.20 equiv.) and HOBt (1.20 equiv) was added. The mixture was 
stirred for 15 minutes, after which the deprotected linker dissolved in DCM (5 mL) was added via syringe. 
The reaction was then stirred overnight. The reaction was diluted in DCM and washed with H2O. The organic 
phase was dried with MgSO4, filtered, and concentrated in vacuo. Purified by flash column chromatography 
with 0-10% MeOH, ending in 1:9 MeOH/DCM with 0.1% TEA.
