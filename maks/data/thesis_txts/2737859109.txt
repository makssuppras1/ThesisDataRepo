Abstract
This thesis aims to investigate the extent to which Grey-box modelling coupled
with smart building data can enhance buildings’ heat signature. The Grey-box mod-
elling is implemented in 3 sequential stages. Firstly, the smart building data is filtered
in terms of heating season, operation type, and occupancy hours to form a selected
data domain for the modelling. The second stage is to model the selected data domain
with a multivariable linear regression model using heat balance equations. A heat sig-
nature model that characterizes the heating demand of the building as a function of
the most statistically significant exogenous variables is obtained. Exogenous variables
are inside and outside temperatures, global horizontal irradiance, and wind speed.
The accuracy of the model is assessed based on the root mean squared errors, RMSE,
which measures the error the model makes on average. The model is validated using
10-fold cross-validation. Finally, the optimal case scenario of the heat signature of
the building is optimized based on the RMSE score, the statistical significance of the
exogenous variables, and the practicality of the estimated parameters. The study case
focuses primarily on the Technical University of Denmark, Lyngby campus buildings,
namely buildings 118 and 450. The buildings are mainly offices, team areas, laps, tech-
nical, and storage rooms. The Lyngby campus has its own local district heating plant.
The two buildings are connected directly to the plant. The results conclude that both
buildings’ statistically most significant variables are inside and outside temperatures
and global horizontal irradiance. The electricity is included in the model as a time
series constant. The heat loss coefficients of buildings 118 and 450 are estimated to
be 2.72 kW
K
and 1.99 kW
K , respectively. The effective solar heat gain area estimate is
approximately 4.27m2 for the north wing of building B118 and 4.33m2 for building
B450. In contrast, the south wing of building B118 had an estimated effective area of
almost double 7.59m2 due to the building being more exposed to direct global hori-
zontal irradiance. The optimal heat signatures of the two buildings are, on average,
offset by the RMSE test within a range between 4.58kW and 2.71kW. Moreover, the
biggest heat loss the building experiences in the heating season is due to envelope
heat loss caused by temperature differences between inside and outside. In contrast,
the largest internal heat gain comes from the electricity load heat gains. For outside
temperatures greater than 10◦C, the share of the heat demand provided by internal
heat gain is more than 50%.

1
Introduction
To become CO2-neutral, the heating sector in Denmark must transform. This transfor-
mation has many faces, such as phasing out fossil fuels, integrations of renewables, and,
most importantly, taking significant energy conservation measures. These measures are
important in reducing greenhouse emissions and increasing efficiency gains. Moreover, con-
servation measures can reduce capacity, making it possible to integrate renewable energy
sources like geothermal or solar thermal. Taking significant energy conservation measures
is one of the key messages from the EU’s briefing on climate mitigation and security of
supply - combining energy efficiency and conservation measures [1].
1.1
Background
Heating for space and domestic hot water constitutes about 45% of worldwide energy
demand, 80% of direct CO2 emissions [2]. The energy consumption of the buildings sector
has steadily grown on average by 1% per annum over the last decade [2]. The share of
electricity has increased to more than one-third of energy demand in the buildings due to
the electrification of heating and cooking [2].
In the EU, in 2020, the building sector consumed 42% of all final energy. Moreover, 78%
of the house hold energy use was used for space and water heating [1]. In the EU, the
target to reduce greenhouse emissions by 20% by 2020 relative to 1990 has been met [1];
however, fulfilling the 2030 and beyond targets demands drastic transformation. Hence,
the call for taking significant energy conservation measures[1].
Buildings in Denmark have come a long way in terms of being a major source of emis-
sions thanks to an increasing use of district heating, electricity and bioenergy. Since 2005,
emissions from energy consumption in buildings have fallen by more than 39%, accounting
for 10% of total energy. Thanks to the reduction in carbon intensity, residential build-
ings’ emissions have decreased strongly. In Denmark, the Danish Climate Act mandates
significant greenhouse gas emission reductions [3]:
• 50 −54% by 2025 relative to 1990 levels.
• 70% reduction targeted by 2030.
This is done through new policy measures such as CSO23’s modelling 2022 to 2035. This
includes those aligned with the green tax reform for industry and the climate agreement
on renewable energy and heating. To address Europe’s energy crisis, Denmark enacted a
set of energy-saving measures[5]. One of the measures is lowering the maximum heating
temperature in public buildings and schools.
• Moving temperature set-points from 22◦C to 19◦C.
• Shortening the heating season and reducing operating time for heat.
Buildings must now maintain a cooler setting of 19◦C, down from the previous standard
of around 22◦C. District heating share is increasing and becoming the dominant heating

1.2
Scope
The energy balance of a building is affected by several factors, such as its thermal prop-
erties, season, and occupancy. These factors can be categorised into 3 categories.
• Thermal properties
• Weather variables
• Operational settings
• Dwelling behaviour
The extent to which the category contributes to the energy balance is unique for each
building. In that regard, the topic of interest in this study is to model the head signature
of buildings with these factors in mind. The goal is to produce a multi-variable energy
signature of smart buildings that optimises the energy profile of the building while main-
taining thermal comfort as regulated in the building codes. This is done through the use
of Grey-box modelling and smart data from buildings.

Literature review
2.1
Decarbonising heating
The significant increase of renewable alternatives such as heat pumps and solar collectors
[1], creates a substantial potential for further deployment increases. Copenhagen’s four
district heating companies – HOFOR, CTR, Vestforbrænding, and VEKS – united to
complete the fourth "Heat Plan Capital" project phase. The "Future District Heating
Supply in the Metropolitan Area 2050" phase shows a heightened dedication to a shared
vision for the heating future [8].
The consideration of future technologies highlighted the implementation of large heat
pumps and surplus heat from industry for low-temperature district heating [8]. While the
increased interest in renewable energy sourcess is primarily based on reducing emissions,
it is worth noting that an essential factor is the cost factor. The cost of energy generation
from solar and wind power has decreased in the past 15 years. These sources have become
cost-competitive with fossil fuels, eliminating the need for subsidies [9].
Two important factors must be considered to utilize the full potential of the technology
above for district heating. The first factor is shifting demand capacity. The second factor is
optimizing the energy demand so that even low-quality surplus heat from industry can be
utilized. This makes having optimal heat signatures for the buildings essential. Moreover,
optimizing the heating operation is vital to spread the peak demand using as low energy
as possible while not compromising comfort.
2.2
Low temperature heating
Current research in academia and industry points towards the possibility and potential
of implementing low-temperature heating. An analysis of Denmark’s leading four district
heading providers, HOFOR, CTR, Vestforbrænding, and VEKS, concluded that switch-
ing to a low-temperature level on both transmission and distribution levels is technically
possible. The transmission supply temperatures are as high as 115◦C, and distribution
supply temperatures are as high as 90◦C. The potential is to lower these to as low as
90◦C and 70◦C, respectively. It is estimated that this will lead to significant savings in
production costs of 8%. Moreover, it will reduce the transport capacity of the district
heating network, 25%, which is essential for future decentralized heat production [8].
A review article titled Low-temperature operation of heating systems to enable 4th gen-
eration district heating [10]: A review thoroughly reviews the heating systems, typical
malfunctions, and potential obtainable supply and return temperatures. The review high-
lights the possible technical improvements to the heating operation. According to the
review, district heating systems are running at a high supply temperature. The review
concludes that it is essential to improve the operations of heating systems inside buildings
to secure comfort and hot water hygiene with lower temperatures [10].
A study titled Lowering District Heating Temperatures—Impact on System Performance
in Current and Future Danish Energy Scenarios concludes that primary fuel consumption
is reduced by approximately up to 7% at design temperatures of 60◦C to 70◦C [11]. There
are two low-hanging opportunities for achieving this. The first one is the potential inte-
gration of renewable sources. The second one is the possibility of meeting heat demand
at low temperatures. The two opportunities can simultaneously be realized by having an
optimized heat signature on the consumption side, for instance, in buildings.
2.3
Heat signature modelling
Various methods have been developed to model the thermal dynamics of buildings. Each
has critical advantages for given scenarios. Depending on a given corner case, a method-

ology can be more suitable than others. This creates ample opportunity or a challenge
to assess the potentiality of the methods and choose the most optimal for the problem
scenario at hand. Here are some of the most well-known modelling methods.
2.3.1
White box models
Physics-based or white-box modelling is a methodology for simulating building thermal
energy performance. It primarily uses mathematical equations to govern the fundamental
physical laws of heat transfer in buildings. This modelling method involves conduction,
convection, radiation and internal heat gains. It models the building energy as closely
as possible to the physical reality and has high accuracy. The model allows for targeted
improvements, and the underlying equations can be adjusted to fit a given scenario. It
provides the most accurate picture of the thermal dynamics of buildings. However, the
model complexity and the data requirements make it less suitable for projects that are
either time or resource-constrained.
A paper by DTU researchers, titled: Development and validation of energy signature
method – A case study on a multi-family building in Sweden before and after deep ren-
ovation [12]. This paper builds on and further validates a previously introduced energy
signature method. It focuses on its core parameters. The methodology is applied to a
family building in Sweden that underwent deep renovations. Thermal energy data from
the district heating company, available for both pre- and post-renovation periods, was used
in the analysis. The researchers created a building energy simulation (BES) model of the
structure to validate the method’s accuracy using IDA ICE simulation software [13]. This
paper is an excellent example of white-box modelling. It includes detailed thermal gov-
erning equations of the building and further validates it with building energy optimization
simulation software.
2.3.2
Black box models
The black-box modelling method, or the data-driven method, mainly relies on historical
data from intelligent building systems on various aspects of the buildings. The histori-
cal data includes energy, heating, and occupancy time series data. Moreover, the model
utilizes time series data of weather conditions such as outside ambient temperature and
wind speed.
This type of modelling uses statistical learning methods to pinpoint any
existing correlations between target variables and predict future energy or dependent vari-
ables. Black-box models are a good option when there is a large amount of smart building
data, and the aim of the modelling leans toward performance rather than explaining why
correlations occur.
Black-box models may not provide insights into the underlying physical processes within
the building that explain these correlations. Moreover, one must be careful about the
context and the solution span.
A paper by researchers titled: Endowing data-driven
models with rejection ability- Out-of-distribution detection and confidence estimation for
black-box models of building energy systems, a study case for a new concept named
rejection ability. Its function is to improve the performance of data-driven models. This
approach identifies and rejects solutions that are out of the span or unreliable predictions
based on a predefined threshold [14]. This highlights the importance of not blindly using
the results from this modelling type.
2.3.3
Grey box models
Grey-box modelling is a hybrid of the two other extreme models, the black-white-box
models. It combines elements of the two methods to capitalise on their advantages. Grey-
box models describe the system using simplified thermal principles and correlations to
simplify the calculation process. They balance accuracy and complexity, giving Grey-box
models the edge over the other two model types. It does this by describing the physical

reality of the system enough to get insights into the underlying physical processes within
the building that explain these correlations. Moreover, it takes full advantage of data-
driven methodology to arrive at results quickly and accurately.
A paper by DTU researchers titled Identifying Suitable Models for the Heat Dynamics
of Buildings [11] - the Study Case proposes a procedure for identifying the most suitable
models to represent a building’s heat dynamics.
Accurately selecting these models is
crucial to fully leveraging the potential of data collected from smart meters. The study
implements Grey-box models of a dynamic system, including significant parameters such
as thermal resistance, heat capacity and relevant temperatures. Moreover, it uses RC-

model’s performance by considering the models’ statistical and physical profiles. This gives
the Grey-box modelling methodology the edge over the other two. It allows researchers to
harness the combined benefits of the two and arrive at results that can be explained and
accounted for.
2.4
Gap in literature
This study aims to model the heat signature of buildings as a function of statistically
significant exogenous variables such as outside temperature, wind speed, and global hor-
izontal irradiance. The target result of this model is the heat consumption power as a
function of the statistically significant exogenous variables for a defined heating season
period using smart building data. The studies place substantial emphasis on two aspects.
The first is heat forecasting as a function of time. The second aspect is the heat signature
as a function of ambient outside temperature.
A study done by DTU researchers titled Non-linear grey-box modelling for heat dynamics
of buildings [16] presents a non-linear grey-box model for a school building in Denmark.
Based on stochastic differential equations, the model describes the building’s heat dynam-
ics considering its water-based heating system. It forecasts heat load as a function of
temperatures of the inside, the wall, the heater, and the return water. Another study,
titled: Development and validation of energy signature method, A case study on a multi-
family building in Sweden before and after deep renovation, models the heat consumption
signature of the building as a function of outside ambient temperature.
This thesis aims to produce the heat signature of buildings as a function of not just
outside ambient temperature but also other statistically significant exogenous variables
such as wind speed and solar irradiance. Of course, this depends on the building itself.
For instance, due to the apparent infiltration profile of the building, it might be relevant
to include wind speed and solar irradiance in the heat signature model. The modelling
process will, of course, draw conclusions based on the performance of the models in terms
of how well they fit the data and whether the physical interpretations of the estimated
parameters complement the physical reality of the building.

3
Problem formulation
3.1
Objective
While existing research on modelling the heat signature of buildings provides priceless
insights into the topic, there is room for improvement in directly modelling a building’s
energy signature while considering the contributions of exogenous variables such as outside
and inside temperatures, wind speed, solar gains, and electricity consumption.
In that regard, this thesis focuses on developing a data-driven method to identify building
heat signatures. This signature is a unique fingerprint of the building’s heat consumption
pattern, reflecting how the building uses heat under different environmental conditions,
i.e., weather and operational scenarios such as occupancy and equipment use. The choice
of exogenous variables depends entirely on the statistical optimization of the marginal
improvement of the heat signature model and its contextual application.
3.2
Rsearch question
To what extent can Grey-box modelling with smart building data improve the energy sig-
nature of buildings?
The research question examines energy balance and statistical modelling perspectives. The
study is based on particular buildings in the Lyngby Campus of the Technical University
of Denmark. This presents local, political, and technical requirements applicable only to
these buildings. However, many results can still be extended to other buildings as the
models can be generalized and the case scenarios extended.
3.2.1
Sub-research questions
While the study aims to answer the main research question, several sub-research questions
are also answered along the way. Answering these sub-research questions is either a corner
case and basis for answering the main research question or is a by-product of answering
the main research question. These questions are stated as follows:
• What are the most significant exogenous variables in the energy signature model?
• To what extent is smart building data significant in the energy signature model?
3.3
Layout
This thesis adopts the IMRAD structure to answer the above-stated main research and
sub-research questions. This structure organizes the thesis logically and sequentially, start-
ing with the introduction, followed by the literature and methodology, gradually building
upon that foundation with a case study, and ending with results and conclusions. This lay-
out allows for a clear and concise presentation of the research methodology and showcases
a detailed analysis of the findings.

10 fold cross validation
Cross-validation is a resampling technique used to assess machine learning models that
are trained and tested on limited data samples [6]. The general steps of the validation can
be stated as follows:
• Step 1: Shuffle the data
• Step 2: Divide the data set into k-sets.
• Step 3: In each fold, 1 set is set aside for testing and the rest for training.
• Step 4: Keep the model performance score.
• Step 5: Assess the model performance and the accuracy score of the k folds.

The effect of the data resolution on the model’s accuracy is tested before anything else.

whether the data resolution affects the model’s accuracy by resampling the data to the

in term filter out short-term fluctuation and making long-term trends clearer.

5.4
Data selection limits
Outliers can significantly affect the learning process of any machine learning model. The
BSM data has the potential to contain such outliers. This can happen in two ways. The
first is data error due to sensor glitch. The second is the human factor, for instance, the
scenario a window was left open and because of that high heat consumption is recorded.
This does not reflect the ideal conditions.
For those reasons, it becomes essential to
implement a method to weed out potential outliers to get a cleaner training data set.
This could potentially improve model accuracy. In this light, it has been decided to select
the data within 2STD, which includes 95% of the data, for modelling. These limits are
highlighted in figures 13 and 14 for the two buildings and their respective substations.
(a) North wing
(b) South wing

The linear trend is not apparent in building B450, in figure 14.

6.1.2
Data resolution test
The original data’s resolution is a 15 minute resolution, and the comparison is with hourly
mean resolution. Taking the hour mean can help smooth out any short-term fluctuations
in the data. However, it reduces the sample size available for model training and testing.
The cross-validation test of the model performance is done in 10 folds; a smaller sample
means fewer data in the folds. The decision is ultimately based on the trade-off between
the improvement and the reduction in sample size. There are 2 heating seasons and 4
cases, hence the 8 test results.

7
Discussion
The thesis aims to answer the research question:
To what extent can Grey-box modelling with smart building data improve
the energy signature of buildings?
The assessment criteria for answering the research question involved implementing Grey-
box modelling on heating data from smart buildings. The results of these models have
been presented and examined thoroughly in the results section 6. This section reflects on
the outcomes of the results in light of the following factors:
• Heating operation constraint
• Model assumptions and limitations
• Building selection and data limitations
7.1
Heating operation constraint
The heating operation of building B118 does not have a night setback. Moreover, the
weather compensation curves of the building have changed many times, specifically in
heating season 2. The weather compensation curves changed 5 and 6 times for the North
and the South wings, respectively. The data for the heating season 2 was left out of the
modelling due to these changes in the weather compensation curves. This eliminated one-
third of the data, which leaves less data for the models to train on. Regarding building
B450, there was no access to weather compensation data. Thus, it was assumed that simi-
lar changes had occurred in the weather compensation curve of the building. Consequently,
the data from heating season 2 was left out of the modelling.
7.2
Model assumptions
Some assumptions had to be made in model formation, including the contributions of
electricity load, global horizontal irradiance, and heat loss through the envelope. These
assumptions allow the model to overlook the detailed thermal dynamics of the buildings.
The first assumption was that the inside temperature time series data of the building
was assumed to be from a critical room that is a good representative of the rest of the
building.
This assumption is not entirely true.
In the case of building B118, it was
discovered that the sensors were located in rooms where, especially during the heating
season 3, the occupants were female colleagues. It is well documented that gender can
impact preferred room temperatures [24].
Secondly, the electricity load contribution was assumed to be 100% heat gain to the
building’s heat demand.
However, the electric heat dissipation in the building is not
necessarily at where the actual heat demand is. The largest share of the electricity could
be from a server or technical room, which is far from the critical areas of the building
that are in heat demand. Finally, the same assumption has been made regarding the solar
gains. It is not certain that gains are attributed to where the heat demand is. However,
the effect of the solar heat gains is limited in the wintertime, when the global horizontal
irradiance is generally low.
The heating data of the two building substations, B450, were also merged. That was
mainly because one of the substations provided more than 94% of the heating demand.
To simplify the study, the small substation was merged with the larger one and with that
came the assumption that the small substation had approximately the same behaviour as
the bigger one in terms of supply temperatures found inside temperatures and that the
differences were negligible since the larger substation was far more dominant in terms of
48
Grey-box modeling for identification of thermal
dynamics of smart buildings
==PAGE:59==
heat share. However, this limits the model as it does not reflect the real picture of the
data.
7.3
Model limitations
The solar heat gain contribution is added to the model as a function of the global horizontal
irradiance. The estimated parameter ζ of the contribution is the effective area. However,
it could have been more optimal to include the glazing area of the buildings in model. The
estimate would have estimated the efficiency of the solar heat gain. This would have better
reflected the profile of the building envelope. Similarly, the surface area of the envelope
could also be included in modelling temperature difference heat loss.
Additionally, the heat contribution from infiltration, defined in equation 3, has a pressure
difference component, which is not constant. The wind pressure coefficient is a function
of pressure. The limitation is that in the model, it is modelled as a constant. If this had
been modelled properly, the results could have been different. However, this could defeat
the purpose of the grey-box modelling, as so much detail about the building dynamics has
to be included.
7.4
Data limitations
The limitation on data quality is mainly due to the time series data on buildings’ electricity
loads. In the final stages of the project, additional information from the building manage-
ment system came to light. The BMS engineer pointed out that the building’s electricity
load time series data is stored in the building management system under the code name
B118TotLoad. The time series data used in the model came from the matter under the
code name B118EFEM, which is stored at the place as the building heat consumption.
This is what created the confusion. It has to be noted that the electricity load data used
in the model also measures the electricity load of the building, but it also measures some
extra loads.
The heating data of the two building substations B450 were merged because one substation
provided more than 94% of the heating demand. As justifiable as that might be, it created
data limitations on the model. The merge meant that the model modelled the data as one
substation, leaving out the differences in heat dynamics in the substations. Moreover, the
assumption that the weather compensation curves changed as much as those in building
B118 led to dropping season 2 data from the model. This, however, was just an assumption
since the weather compensation curves of building B450 were available. This also creates
a data limitation challenge. Additionally, the optimal case of building B450 was selected
based on season 3 data. Season 3 had only 2 months’ worth of heating data. It might be
that season 3 outperformed season 1 due to overfitting because of a smaller data sample.
7.5
Building selection limitations
A better selection process of the buildings could have been set up to capitalize on the
capabilities of grey-box modelling and intelligent building data.
The final selection of
the exogenous variables in the optimal cases of the heat signatures did not include the
contribution from infiltration. It was not statistically significant enough to include it in
the optimal model. The reason could be that the selected buildings were not suitable
for modelling the effect of infiltration on the heat balance due to not facing the apparent
wind directly and/or being surrounded by other buildings. The apparent wind speed in
the area was between directions South West and West as shown in the Wind rose plot
in figure 63 in Appendix C. Regarding building B118, the apparent wind is blocked by
B119. Thus, perhaps B119 could be a better option when considering model infiltration.
Similarly, B450 is also partially blocked from the wind by B427. In conclusion, the two
buildings are not a choice for modelling infiltration.

7.6
Suggestions for future studies
The study has yielded good results. It has highlighted the potential of harnessing the
technical abilities of Grey-box modelling coupled with intelligent building data. It has
also shown that heat signature models can be developed and optimized while capitalizing
on smart building data. However, this is no way near perfect, and there is always room
for improvement. Several potential improvements for future studies are highlighted and
refracted upon next.
• The building selection process of such studies should be done with the utmost atten-
tion paid to the exogenous variables that the study will consider. This helps prevent
selecting buildings that might not yield significant results for that given exogenous
variable, such as wind speed, in this study.
• Rigorous data quality and relevance checks should be implemented for such a study
to avoid data mix-up, especially when sourcing data in the building management
system where the data is poorly organized, for instance, for electricity load mix-up
in this study.
• The location of the temperature sensors should be considered to ensure that the time
series data is a good approximation of the state of the building.
• The critical room of heat contributors such as electricity loads and solar heat should
examined to ensure that they contribute to the heat balance of the building.

Conclusion
It is critical to have optimal building heat signatures to transition to low-temperature
heating effectively. In light of that, the primary objective of this study was to examine
the extent to which Grey-box modelling coupled with smart building data improves the
modelling of buildings’ heat signatures. The results of the study underline that Grey-box
modelling techniques combined with smart building data significantly improve the heat
signature of buildings to a great extent.
The first step towards the Grey-box modelling was to form the data domain. This was
implemented in a process of filtration that targets the data that is suitable and significant
for the heat signature modelling process. The data was filtered first by heating season
since the seasons had different weather compensation curves. Next, the data was filtered
by operation hours of which the buildings were in normal function, for instance, selecting
weekday hours between 08 : 00 and 17 : 00, which showcase the typical occupancy hours
of the educational and office buildings. Finally, the data was filtered to select the data
within 2standard deviation, which contains approximately 95% of the data to weed out
any potential outliers. The data that went through this filtering process formed the basis
for the Grey-box modelling stage.
The grey-box modelling used multiple-variable linear regression with 10 fold cross-validation
to model the heat signature using each building’s selected data domain. A forward selec-
tion method was used to investigate the effect of the exogenous variables, such as Outside
and inside temperatures, global horizontal irradiance, and wind speed. The forward selec-
tion process was done using test scenarios and cases. The marginal improvement of the
test scenarios and case was measured using the test data’s root mean square error, RMSE
test, in the cross-validation process. A further assessment regarding the performance of
the models was done using the practicality and P values of the estimated parameters.
The results conclude that the optimal exogenous variables to include in the Grey-box
models are outside and inside temperature and the global horizontal irradiance. Moreover,
electricity loads are included as well. The heat loss coefficient and the effective area of
solar heat gain of the buildings were estimated in the process. The estimated heat loss
coefficients of buildings B118 and B450 are, on average, 2.72 kW
K and 1.99kW
K . The effective
glazing area is estimated for the solar gain contribution to the heat balance due to global
horizontal irradiance. The effective solar heat gain area estimate is approximately 4.27m2
for the north wing of building B118 and 4.33m2 for building B450.
In contrast, the south wing of building B118 had an estimated effective area of almost
double 7.59m2. Note that the south wing is exposed more to direct sunlight than the
north wing. The optimal heat signatures of the two buildings are an average offset by
the RMSE test within a range between 4.58kW and 2.71kW. Moreover, the biggest heat
loss the building experiences in the heating season is due to envelope heat loss caused by
temperature differences between inside and outside. In contrast, the largest internal heat
gain comes from the electricity load heat gains. For outside temperatures greater than
10◦C, the share of the heat demand provided by internal heat gain is more than 50%.
The results, methodology, and findings of this study are not limited to the two buildings
that were the subject of the study.
With access to smart building data, they can be
adapted and applied to other buildings. The energy balance equation can be used in any
building, and Grey-modelling techniques are applicable and have the potential to yield
great results.
