Abstract 
This study investigates the interaction between two marine bacteria Phaeobacter sp. and Pseudoalteromonas 
piscicida. Both are isolated from similar marine environments and produce potent antimicrobial secondary 
metabolites. This prompted the questions; how do these two bacteria behave when they co-exist, and what 
role do their secondary metabolites play in the interaction between Phaeobacter sp. and Pseudoalteromonas 
piscicida. 
This study examined Phaeobacter sp. A36-5a and thirteen strains identified as P. piscicida. Spot assays 
revealed that pigmented colonies inhibited Phaeobacter sp. and non-pigmented P. piscicida strains were 
inhibited by Phaeobacter sp. Genome mining discovered that all the pigmented strains contained a 
bromoalterochromide biosynthetic gene cluster, whereas the non-pigmented strains did not. Furthermore, 
phylogenetic analysis revealed that four of the P. piscicida strains were more closely related to 
Pseudoalteromonas byunsanensis.  P. piscicida B39bio was identified as a potent inhibitor of Phaeobacter sp. 
and selected for expanded analysis. We succeeded in establishing a liquid co-culture between Phaeobacter 
sp. and P. piscicida B39bio, if Phaeobacter sp. was given a cell density advantage. Metabolomic analysis 
showed that P. piscicida B39bio was responsible for most of the metabolites produced in co-culture and that 
multiple metabolites from Phaeobacter sp. became undetectable in the co-culture. Targeted metabolomic 
analysis of P. piscicida B39bio detected production of bromoalterochromides in co-culture. Coupled together 
with the observation of pigmented strains inhibiting Phaeobacter sp., we argue that bromoalterochromides 
are an important secondary metabolite that may be responsible for the observed inhibition of Phaeobacter 
sp. in the co-culture system. In conclusion, Phaeobacter sp. and P. piscicida B39bio can co-exist but the 
interaction between Phaeobacter sp. and P. piscicida B39bio in liquid co-culture is antagonistic with P. 
piscicida B39bio inhibiting Phaeobacter sp.

1. Introduction  
Microbial secondary metabolites play a significant role in shaping microbial communities, and confers 
important physiological and ecological functions for the producing organism[1]. The most renowned 
secondary metabolites are famous for their antimicrobial activities and a common assumption is that they 
are produced to gain a competitive advantage over other species [1]. Hence, it is often assumed that bacteria 
that produce antibiotics will do this to kill competitors, thus the strongest will survive [2].  
An example from the marine environment of these secondary metabolite producers are bacteria from the 
Roseobacter group [3], [4]. Genera within this group, such as Phaeobacter spp., are able to produce a range 
of secondary metabolites such as the tropone derivative tropodithietic acid (TDA) [3]. TDA is known to have 
a broad range antimicrobial inhibitory effect [3], [5], [6]. 
TDA and TDA-producing bacteria have been shown to alter the relative abundance of bacteria belonging to 
the Alteromonadales order, including the genus Pseudoalteromonas [7], [8]. Interestingly, bacteria from the 
Pseudoalteromonas genus have been shown to encode potent secondary metabolites such as the 
bromoalterochromide in Pseudoalteromonas piscicida [9].Therefore, one could assume that Phaeobacter 
and Pseudoalteromonas species would be antagonistic towards each other. An example of such antagonistic 
behaviour from soil is antimicrobial production by Streptomyces diastatochromogenes that can inhibit 
Streptomyces scabies on potatoes [10]. Nevertheless, bacteria from the genera Pseudoalteromonas and TDA-
producing Phaeobacter species can be isolated from similar marine environments [11]. Considering the 
assumption, that antimicrobial secondary metabolites are inhibitory for competitors, it begs the questions; 
How are these two antimicrobial producing species existing in similar ecological niches? Do their 
antimicrobial secondary metabolites affect each other? Do they even interact through their secondary 
metabolite production?   
The aim of this project is to study how Phaeobacter sp. A36a-5a interact with 13 Pseudoalteromonas piscicida 
isolates when co-cultured. Furthermore, this study will try to examine the degree and importance of any 
secondary metabolite mediated interactions between the two species.    
Answers to these questions will cover knowledge gaps within microbial interactions, however since both 
bacteria are being considered for probiotic use in aquacultures [12]–[15], knowledge about their interactions 
will also aid in the application of the species as probiotics.

1.1. Bacterial secondary metabolites and their biosynthesis   
Opposed to primary metabolites, bacterial secondary metabolites are biosynthesized and not considered 
essential for cell survival. Even though not strictly essential, they still have important functions and help the 
producing organism succeed in the natural environment [16], [17]. For this reason, researchers have tried to 
introduce the terms “natural products” and “specialized metabolites” to avoid associations with the word 
“secondary”  [2], [18]. Given the title of this thesis secondary metabolites will be used to describe this group 
of compounds. Biosynthesis of secondary metabolites is often carried out by large enzyme complexes that 
are encoded in biosynthetic genes. In bacterial genomes biosynthetic genes are often clustered together and 
referred to as biosynthetic gene clusters (BGCs) [18], [19]. The exact process of biosynthesis occurs in 
numerous ways depending on the type of natural product being synthesised. The three main categories are, 
but not exclusively to: polyketides (PKs), non-ribosomal peptides (NRPs), and ribosomally synthesized post-
translationally modified peptides (RIPPS). 
The first category of compounds – the polyketides – are synthesized by polyketide synthases (PKS). A PKS is 
made up of enzymatic modules that can be encoded on multiple genes [20]. The logic of a PKS is that of an 
assembly-line in which building blocks are assembled by multiple modules into the final polyketide (Figure 
1). Certain iterative PKSs also exist where one module can repeat its action multiple times, but will for the 
sake of simplicity not be covered here. The most common building blocks of PKs are acetyl-CoA and malonyl-
CoA that can be coupled together through condensation reactions to form long carbon chains. The three 
basic features of a module in a PKS are: an acyl transferase domain responsible for substrate selection, an 
acyl carrier protein domain that tethers the growing carbon chain to the PKS, and a ketosynthase that 
catalyses carbon-carbon bond formation between the units of the growing chain. Beyond this functionality 
each module can also contain other modification domains such as a ketoreductase, a dehydratase, and an 
enoyl reductase domain that can reduce the ketone group on building blocks. Finally, tailoring enzymes can 
modify the polyketide even further for instance through methylation and other modifications. When multiple 
modules are couple together to form a PKS a huge number of possible polyketides can be assembled.

The second category of natural products are NRPs. These peptides are, in comparison to peptides that are 
translated by ribosomes, synthesized by non-ribosomal peptide synthetases (NRPS) [20]. NRPSs function in 
the same assembly line format as PKSs, thus NRPSs also have a modular structure. The main building blocks 
used to construct a NRP is amino acids including nonproteinogenic amino acids. The basic features of a NRPSs 
module are an adenylation domain (responsible for selection of the right amino acid), a peptidyl carrier 
protein domain (responsible for tethering the growing peptide chain and the incoming building block), and a 
condensation domain (responsible for forming new peptide bonds between the peptide chain and new 
building blocks). As for PKSs, NRPSs modules can also include optional domains that can confer extended 
functionality, such as epimerization, oxidation, and methylation domains. 
Besides creating NRPs or PKs both NRPSs and PKSs enzymes can function together and produce hybrid 
compounds[20]. The enzymes that synthesise these hybrid compounds often retain the modular and 
assembly line structure of the regular NRPSs and PKSs. Though multiple proteins can be involved in forming 
the catalytic domains responsible for the biosynthesis of these hybrid compounds [21]. 
The third main category of secondary metabolites are RIPPs. These peptides are, as the name suggest, 
synthesized by the cells ribosomal machinery and afterwards modified. In short, the ribosomal machinery is 
responsible for synthesizing a precursor peptide, that consist of core peptide and a leader peptide. The leader

is recognized by enzymes that can make a specific post-translational modification and cleave of the leader to 
form a functional product. The building blocks of these RiPPS are therefore the regular proteinogenic amino 
acids but all the possible post-translational modifications allow for extended variation in the final secondary 
metabolite [22]. 
1.2. Secondary metabolites and their role in microbial interactions 
Microbial secondary metabolites may play important ecological roles in microbial communities, facilitating 
chemical communication [23], nutrient acquisition [24], and antagonism [25] that all play a role in bacterial 
interactions. Production of quorum sensing (QS) molecules, such N-acylhomoserine lactones (AHLs), is a 
prime example of communication facilitated by secondary metabolites [23]. QS production occurs in bacteria 
belonging to the Roseobacter group [26], and in fact the antimicrobial secondary metabolite, TDA, has been 
shown to be regulated by QS in Phaeobacter inhibens [27]. Secondary metabolites also play a key role in 
nutrient acquisition. Here, siderophores are responsible for chelating iron [28], a mechanism that are well-
studied [29], [30].  In addition, nutrient acquisition can be an antagonistic behaviour towards competing 
microorganism by limiting the availability of nutrients [25]. Finally, antagonistic interactions are often 
facilitated by production of antibiotic compounds that can kill competitors. For example the production of 
andrimid by Vibrio sp. SWAT3  can completely inhibit the colonisation of Vibrio cholera on marine particles 
[25]. In spite of this, many presumed antibiotics might not actually be antagonistic in nature because the 
concentration of these compounds are often too low compared to what is required for them to have 
inhibitory effects [16]. Furthermore sub-inhibitory concentrations of antibiotics have been shown to increase 
secondary metabolite production [31]. Genome sequencing together with genome mining approaches [32] 
have also uncovered that many bacterial genomes contain BGCs that encode for unknown secondary 
metabolites [33], also known as orphan BGCs [34]. Orphan BGCs are normally not expressed under laboratory 
conditions, hence they remain silent [35]. However, stimulation by sub-inhibitory concentrations of 
antibiotics can lead to expression of silent clusters [36].  An example of this is the discovery of antibiotic 
production in Streptomycetes spp. when exposed to the antibiotic goadsporin at sub-inhibitory 
concentrations [37].  Additionally, co-cultivation of two antibiotic producing bacteria, Vibrio coralliilyticus 
and Photobacterium galatheae, lead to increase in their antibiotic production  [38].  
Up- or down regulation of the production of antimicrobial secondary metabolite as a response to the 
presence of other foreign antimicrobials can be explained by the “competition sensing” hypothesis [39]. The 
general understanding of competition between bacteria can be dived into two categories: exploitative 
competition and interference competition [40]. Exploitative competition encompasses the process by which 
one microorganism might consume a nutrient making it inaccessible to another. When this type of 
==PAGE:11==
Introduction 
11  
 
competition occurs in a group of cells with aligned evolutionary goals (such as cells of the same genotype), 
they might start producing group-beneficial phenotypes to alleviate the competition [41]. In contrast, 
interference competition occurs when individuals inflict direct harm to each other. This is, as mentioned 
previously, commonly seen in the microbial world in the form of secretions of antimicrobial secondary 
metabolites. This type of competition will often dictate which genotype that will prevail in a mixed 
culture[39]. Furthermore - as with conventional war - the development of new weaponry, e.g. antimicrobial 
secondary metabolites, will drive the development of defensive countermeasures, e.g. antimicrobial 
resistance. To survive in these two types of competitive environments, bacteria have evolved ways to directly 
detect harmful effects on the cell, hence allow cells to respond. This phenomenon is what we term 
competition sensing. The term encompasses physiological responses a cell might express when it detects 
harm caused by other cells and that have evolved to some extend for that purpose [39]. Therefore, 
competition sensing could be the evolutionary explanation to why the presence of foreign antimicrobial 
compounds can lead to expression of secondary metabolites.  
1.1.1. The ecology and secondary metabolites of marine Phaeobacter  
The Phaeobacter genus belongs to paraphyletic Roseobacter group, who is part of the Alphaproteobacteria 
class [42], [43]. The group represents one of the most abundant and ubiquitous groups of marine bacteria 
[44]. Species in the group are known to be secondary metabolite producers and have been shown to produce 
the potent antibiotic tropone derivative TDA [3]. Species producing TDA within the group include several 
species from the Phaeobacter genus such as Phaeobacter gallaeciensis , Phaeobacter piscinae and P. inhibens,  
[42], [45], [46]. However, there are also species within the Tritonibacter genus that produce TDA[47]. TDA 
have been shown to have antibiotic activity towards a broad range of bacterial species, including both Gram-
negative and Gram-positive bacteria [3], [5], [6]. Furthermore, resistance development towards TDA have 
been demonstrated to be rare and a fleeting phenotypic trait [6], [12].  
TDA exists in two tautomeric forms – TDA and thiotropocin [48] (Figure 2). The basic structure of TDA consists 
of a tropolone substituted with a carboxyl group and two sulphur atoms that form a disulfide bridge. The 
broad range antibiotic effect of TDA is attributed to its mode of action. TDA has been proposed to function 
as an electroneutral proton antiporter that targets the proton motive force [49]. The carboxyl group of TDA 
can be protonated in the low PH environments, such as around the outer cell membrane. The protonated 
state is neutral and can diffuse across the cell membrane. Inside the cell the change in pH results in TDA 
releasing the proton hereby disturbing the proton motive force. After release, the ketone on the tropone and 
the carboxyl group can chelate a metal ion and diffuses out of the cell [49].

TDA-producing Phaeobacter species are often found associated with eukaryotes [51]. They are great surface 
colonizers and will often colonize living surfaces of algae, bryozoan, and crustaceans and abiotic surfaces in 
harbors and aquaculture systems [42], [52], [53]. In fact, Phaeobacter species can invade natural biofilms 
when present in high cell density, supporting that Phaeobacter species generally are involved in surface 
colonization [54]. Antagonistic interactions are believed to be more frequent in particle- and between 
surface-associated bacteria due to the high cell densities in structured microenvironments[55], [56]. Thus, 
the production of TDA could aid the Phaeobacter during surface colonization, thus providing a competitive 
advantage for the TDA-producing species. Furthermore, it has been proposed that TDA-producing 
Phaeobacter species provide eukaryotic hosts with an antifouling defense against other fouling organisms - 
as in the case for the marine alga Ulva australis when it is colonized by P. inhibens [57]. Phaeobacter spp. 
have been shown to be antagonistic in co-culture systems. For example, P. inhibens were able to eradicate 
Vibiro vulnificus in a co-culture model system when inoculated at respectively 106 cfu/ml and 105 cfu/ml 
[58]. P. inhibens was also shown in biofilm co-cultured with Pseudoalteromonas tunicate to produce dispersal 
cells that had reduced phenotypic variation and where better competitors than cells grown without the

1.1.2. The ecology and secondary metabolites of Pseudoalteromonas piscicida 
Pseudoalteromonas is an exclusively marine genus, that can constitute on average up to 3% of the bacterial 
abundance in the upper ocean surface [44]. Members of the Pseudoalteromonas genus, are great biofilm 
formers and are often found associated with marine eukaryotes, such as algae [60]. Pseudoalteromonas 
species can be divided into two clusters based on their ability to produce pigments or not [14]. Most 
pigmented strains can produce a broad range of secondary metabolites, such as prodigiosin produced by 
Pseudoalteromonas rubra [61] and bromoalterochromides produced by P. piscicda [62].  
P. piscicida is a gram-negative, rod-shaped, motile Gammaproteobacterium [13]. In the marine environment 
P. piscicida has been isolated in association with macroorganisms such as Amphiprion clarkii (yellowtail 
clownfish), Hymeniacidon Perleve (a marine sponge), and crustaceans [14]. It is therefore speculated that it 
helps these macroorganisms by forming a protective layer against pathogens [14].  
P. piscicida has been shown to possess both direct and indirect methods for killing other marine bacteria such 
as bacteria from the Vibrio genus [13]. The direct method is production of surface vesicles that can be transfer 
to specific bacteria. These vesicles have lytic activity that kill bacteria that interact with the vesicles [13]. The 
indirect methods for killing is through secreted antimicrobial compounds released into the environment [13]. 
P. piscicida is able to produce a number of secondary metabolites that have been shown to have antimicrobial 
activity (Table 1). This includes three alkaloids, and one group of NRPs known as bromoalterochrmides [14].  
Additionally, some P. piscicida strains have been shown to produce and secrete siderophores. Siderophores 
are iron chelating molecules that can bind Fe3 and be transported back in to the cell via specific receptor 
proteins. Within the cell the Fe3 can be converted to soluble Fe2 making it available to the cell [63]. Examples 
of such siderophores are Pseudochelin A a NRPS derived metabolite produced by P. piscicida S2040 [63] and 
hybrid NRPS/PKS derived compound Pseudoalteropeptide A produced by Pseudoalteromonas piscicida 
SWA4_PA4 [64]. Generally the marine environment is iron scarce [63] and production of siderophores might 
sequester iron making it unavailable to other microorganism. Interestingly P. piscicida interactions with 
bacteria from the same environmental niche have not received much investigation.

1.3. Approaches to profiling bacterial secondary metabolites 
To study microbial interactions mediated by secondary metabolites, it is important to utilize the right tools. 
Genome sequencing and mining play an important role and can be used to identify the biosynthetic potential 
of a specific bacteria. Furthermore, omics methods such as metabolomics allow for the study of the 
metabolome present in a sample. This can be used as untargeted metabolomics to describe overall changes 
in the metabolome during interactions and targeted where you can identify specific metabolites. In the 
following section some background to the different methods used in this project will be presented.  
1.3.1. Phylogenetic analysis and genome mining  
When performing genome mining multiple tools exist, with the gold standard and most widely used tool 
being antiSMASH [65]. AntiSMASH is used to predict BGCs in genomic sequences and can be used to search 
fragments and whole genomes. Different categories of secondary metabolites are encoded in BGCs that 
follow highly conserved biosynthetic logic. AntiSMASH uses this conservation and a rule-based strategy 
(based on the different types of secondary metabolites) to search for homology in a given genomic sequence 
for regions that encode elements that are similar to previously described BGCs [32]. This allows for sensitive 
and precise identification of currently 71 different classes of biosynthesis gene clusters [65]. The downside 
to this approach is that only BGCs with implemented rules can be identified meaning that BGCs that use 
unknown enzymes or enzymes that are not encoded in a BGC will not be predicted [32]. Furthermore, the 
sequence quality will also affect prediction, e.g. short-read sequenced genomes will results in less reliable 
prediction compared to long-read sequenced genomes due to more predictions being on contig edges  [32], 
[66]. This can lead to an overestimation of the number of BGCs in a genome.  
The development of cheap sequencing technologies has led 16S sequence analysis to become common place 
for determining phylogenetic relationships [67]. Though other methods exist such as calculating average 
nucleotide identity for assembled genomes. Average nucleotide identity scores is a in silico approach that 
tries to achieve similar results to DNA-DNA hybridization performed in the lab by comparing assembled 
genomes instead of only one gene sequence [68]. Furthermore, tools that expand the approach of 16S 
sequence analysis have also been developed such as autoMLST. AutoMLST is a web-based tool that uses 
multi-locus-sequence analysis and to generate a phylogenetic three. It is based on multiple conserved loci in 
the genomes that are automatically selected along with an out group ensuring a stronger phylogenetic signal 
compared to only using 16S data [67].   
1.3.2. Metabolomic analysis 
Metabolomic analysis allows you to study the entire metabolome in a sample. This analysis therefore results 
in large amounts of data that often require statistical analysis or target knowledge-based approaches to 
==PAGE:16==
Introduction 
16  
 
extract information. Multiple instruments have been developed to generated metabolomic data but all have 
in common that the end result is a set of metabolomic features that all have a mass-to-charge ratio (m/z) 
assigned. Depending on the instrument and method used additionally information such as fragmentation 
data, mass accuracy, retention times etc. may also be included [69]. After obtaining the data multivariate 
statistical analyses can be used to describe trends between samples or even find features of interest within 
samples [69]. Additionally, analysis of specific metabolites can be done if the monoisotopic mass of the 
compound is known and an adduct pattern can be identified in the metabolomics data.  
 
1.4. Research ambitions 
The purpose of this project is to determine how Phaeobacter sp. and P. piscicida co-exist and potentially 
interact through secondary metabolite production. We will be studying Phaeobacter sp. A36a-5a that at the 
time of writing belongs to an unclassified species[70]. The strain will therefore be referred to as Phaeobacter 
sp. in this thesis. Phaeobacter sp. was originally isolated in Jyllinge harbour in Denmark alongside five P. 
piscicida strains [11] which is why it was chosen to this project. A total of thirteen P. piscicida strains are 
included in this project. They are all isolated from Danish coastal waters which are similar environments, but 
with some slight differences in salinity and temperature [11].  Within this group a total of five strains (A22bio, 
A34a-4a, A36a-5b A38q-4a, A40q-4a) are isolated from the same harbour (Jyllinge Harbour) as the 
Phaeobacter sp. [11].  
1.4.1. Research objectives  
The project comprises of four experimental objectives: The first is to decipher the biosynthetic gene clusters 
and potential secondary metabolites produced by Phaeobacter sp. and the 13 Pseudoalteromonas piscicida 
strains by using short and long-read sequencing technology and bioinformatics. This was conducted under 
the assumption that any secondary metabolites mediating interactions would be encoded in BGCs. The 
second objective was to determine the sensitivity/tolerance of the two target species to each other’s 
secondary metabolites from supernatant. We believe that this could help elucidate if any secreted secondary 
metabolites can inhibit the other species. Thirdly, an experimental setup was developed to facilitate co-
cultivation between Pseudoalteromonas piscicida and Phaeobacter sp., to allow investigation of co-culture 
dynamics and metabolic interaction. Finally, using the developed co-culture setup, chemical extractions were 
performed to profile the metabolome and search for secondary metabolites of both species in the co-culture 
using mass spectrometry.

1.4.2. Hypothesis   
We hypothesize based on the competition sensing hypothesis that if interactions are occurring between 
Phaeobacter sp. and P. piscicida in co-culture then we will be able observe them as changes in metabolite 
production.

2.1. Bacterial Strains and Culture Conditions 
Phaeobacter sp. A36a-5a and all Pseudoalteromonas piscicida strains (Table 2) were cultured in Marine Broth 
(MB) (Difco 2216 Marine Broth 37.4 g/L) or on Marine Agar (MA) (Difco 2216 55.1 g/L). All strains were grown 
at 25 °C and stirred at 200 rpm when grown in liquid cultures. For extended storage a 20% glycerol stock was 
prepared from each strain and stored at 80°C. 
2.2. Spot assay 
Overnight (O/N) cultures were prepared as described in section 2.1. All O/N cultures were diluted 10-fold in 
MB. 6 spots (10µL each) were spotted on a vertical line for each Pseudoalteromonas strain and left to dry.  6 
spots (10µL each) of Phaeobacter sp. A36a-5a were then spotted next to the first spots with increasing 
distance, with the closest spot being right on top of the first vertical line spot. Finally plates where incubated 
at 25 °C degrees for 3 days.

2.3. Genome Sequencing and Mining 
Genomic DNA was purified from both Phaeobacter sp. A36a-5a and Pseudoalteromonas piscicida B39bio 
using the Wizard® Genomic DNA Purification Kit (Promega), and the purified DNA was resuspended in 10 mM 
tris-hcl ph 8.0 buffer. Quality control was ensured using a nanodrop (DeNovix ds-11) to check for 
contaminates, and a Qubit (Thermo Fisher) to ensure sufficient DNA concentration. 
Before sequencing samples were labelled using Rapid Barcoding Kit SQK-RBK004 (Oxford nanopore 
technologies). Sequencing was done using the MinION (Oxford nanopore technologies) flow cell with data 
being analyzed using MinKNOW (Oxford nanopore technologies) and base calling performed using Guppy v. 
4.2.2. All nanopore reads were trimmed using porechop v. 0.2.4 [71] to remove adapters. A hybrid assembly 
from the nanopore reads together with Ilumina reads (courtesy of Mikael Lenz Strube) for each strain was 
constructed using the Unicycler assembly pipeline (Unicycler v0.4.9) [72]. Before assembly all Ilumina reads 
were pre-processed using fastp v. 0.23.0. Finally Bandage v. 0.8.1 was used to assess assembly quality. 
Genomic DNA from the remaining strains was purified courtesy of Trine Rahbek Pérez using a 
NucleoSpin®Tissue (Macherey-Nagel, 740952.5) and resuspended in 10 mM tris-hcl buffer. Quality control 
was ensured using a nanodrop (DeNovix ds-11) to check for contaminates, and a Qubit (Thermo Fisher) to 
ensure sufficient DNA concentration. The strains were sent for external Ilumina sequencing (Novogene). 
Ilumina reads were pre-processed using fastp v. 0.23.0 and Ilumina-only assembled using the Unicycler 
assembly pipeline (Unicycler v0.4.9) [72]. Average Nucleotide Identity calculations were performed using

pyani v. 0.2.11 [73]. Multilocus sequence analysis was performed using the autoMLST [67]. Genome mining

2.4. Liquid Co-culture Experiments 
Phaeobacter sp. A36a-5a and Pseudoalteromonas piscicida B39bio were inoculated in different starting cell 
ratios from overnight cultures that had reached an approximate CFU of 109 cells in 100 mL Erlenmeyer flask 
containing 20 mL MB media. Co-cultures were grown for 42H at 25°C with and without stirring at 135 rpm. 
CFU was measured after 24H and 42H.  
2.5. Minimum Inhibitory Concentration Assay 
Two minimum inhibitory concentration (MIC) assays were setup to test tolerance of Phaeobacter sp. A36a-
5a, Pseudoalteromonas piscicida B39bio and A38q-4a against supernatant from the other bacterial species. 
A culture of each was prepared (see section 2.1) and grown for 48H.  2 mL of bacterial suspension was 
centrifuged at 8000 x g for 5 min. The supernatant was collected and sterile filtered. MB was sterile filtered 
and used to dilute the supernatant to desired concentrations in a well plate. Approximately 103 bacterial cells 
(of the reciprocal strain) were added to each well with a total volume of 100 µL in each. The samples where 
incubated in a SpectraMax i3 (Molecular Devices) for 45,5 hours at app. 25°C, with shaking every 15 min for 
a duration of 15 min and OD600 was read every 30 min.   
2.6. Metabolomic analysis of co-cultures 
O/N cultures were prepared as described in section 2.1. O/N culture of Pseudoalteromonas piscicida B39bio 
was 10-fold diluted 5 times and Phaeobacter sp. A36a-5a was 10-fold diluted twice. 10µl of P. piscicida was 
mixed with 100 µl of Phaeobacter sp. resulting in an approximate ratio of 1:1000 cells of each strain 
respectively. Then 110 µL of co-culture mix and diluted monocultures were inoculated into separate 100 mL 
Erlenmeyer flasks containing 20 ml MB and incubated at 25°C with stirring (135 rpm). Samples were taken 
after 24H and centrifuged for 2 min at 8,500 x G.  The supernatant was collected and diluted in 1:1 ratio with 
isopropanol: ethyl acetate 1:3 v/v with 0.1% formic acid. Pellets were resuspended in equal amount 
isopropanol: ethyl acetate 1:3 v/v with 0.1% formic acid as used for the supernatant. The resuspended pellets 
were then sonicated for 15 min at 50/60 HZ (Delta 220T Aerosec Industrie). Supernatant samples were 
vortexed for 30 sec and the surface phase was collected. The sonicated samples were centrifuged for 1 min 
at 8500 x G and supernatant collected. All samples were evaporated under N2 flow. All samples were 
resuspended in 500 µL methanol.

that was prepared as described in section 2.1. Approximately 104 cells were inoculated into a 100 mL 
Erlenmeyer flask containing 20 mL MB media and grown at 25°C and with shaking of 135 rpm. Over a 20-
hour period CFU was measured and supernatant samples were extracted as described in section 2.6 for 
metabolomic analysis. The metabolomic analysis was conducted as described in section 2.6.

3. Results  
3.1. Interaction between colonies of Phaeobacter sp. and Pseudoalteromonas piscicida  
A so-called spot assay where dual colonies of the interacting bacteria were spotted on agar at different 
distances was conducted to examine the interaction between Phaeobacter sp. A36a-5a and the 13 different 
Pseudoalteromonas piscicida isolates. All P. piscicida strains could grow next to Phaeobacter sp. A36a-5a 
(Figure 4), except from P. piscicida A34a-4a (data not shown).  
We had expected all P. piscicida to be bright yellow but noticed that the colony colour of the P. piscicida 
isolates varied ranging from bright yellow to a pale shade of beige/brown. The yellow isolates were B39bio, 
C31bio, I38bio, E39bio, J37bio, J38bio, and K31. The beige/brown isolates were A36a-5b, H21a-1bio, A22bio, 
A40q-4a, and A38q-4a.  The response of Phaeobacter sp. to the presences of different P. piscicida isolates 
diverged in association with the colony colour of P. piscicida. The yellow-coloured P. piscicida B39bio, C31bio, 
I38bio, E39bio, J37bio and J38bio strains inhibited Phaeobacter sp. with increased inhibition the closer the 
Phaeobacter sp. colony was to the P. piscicida colony. Furthermore, the distinct brown colony colour of 
Phaeobacter sp. was reduced in an oval shape in the centre of the colony, with most reduction nearest the 
P. piscicida. Direct contact in mixed spots between all the yellow-coloured P. piscicida isolates and 
Phaeobacter sp. suggested to favour the P. piscicida isolates and resulted in yellow colonies.  
The brown/beige P. piscicida isolates did not inhibit Phaeobacter sp., and the brown colony colour of 
Phaeobacter sp. was only reduced as described for the yellow isolates when spotted next to P. piscicida H21a-
1bio. This disturbance was only in the form of a slightly less brown colony. Direct contact in mixed spots 
between the brown P. piscicida isolates and Phaeobacter sp. favoured Phaeobacter sp. or allowed co-
existence. 
Overall, yellow P. piscicida isolates produced stronger inhibition in Phaeobacter sp. colonies compared to the 
brown isolates. Based on these observations, P. piscicida B39bio was selected for the following co-culture 
experiments given the strong inhibition of the Phaeobacter sp. colonies and P. piscicida A38q4a given the 
lack of inhibition in Phaeobacter sp. and for being isolated from the same harbour as Phaeobacter sp.

3.2. Genome sequencing and mining of Phaeobacter sp. and Pseudoalteromonas piscicida 
To uncover the secondary metabolite production potential, all isolates were genome sequenced, assembled 
and subsequently mined using antiSMASH.  
The genomes of Phaeobacter sp. and all 13 Pseudoalteromonas isolates were short-read (illumina) 
sequenced. Furthermore Phaeobacter sp. and P. piscicida B39bio were also long-read (nanopore) sequenced. 
The two best assemblies were obtained of Phaeobacter sp. and P. piscicida B39bio, due to hybrid assembly 
(Table 3), with N50 values of 2,274,524 and 2,281,485, respectively. The assemblies of Phaeobacter sp. and 
B39bio (Figure 5) revealed that Phaeobacter sp. carries two small plasmids and B39bio carries one small 
plasmid.  
The remaining Pseudoalteromonas genomes, based on short-reads only, expectedly contained more 
segments, ranging between 147 and 595, and had lower N50 scores, ranging between 65,079 and 301,652 
(Table 3). Furthermore, the quality of the short-read assemblies varied with the best assembly being that of 
E39bio and the worst being A22bio based on N50 scores. Despite the incompleteness of the genome 
assemblies, we concluded that the assemblies were acceptable for prediction of biosynthetic potential, and 
allow for acceptable analysis of relatedness.

3.2.1. Pseudoalteromonas relatedness 
Given the difference in colony responses observed for the P. piscicida isolates when spotted against 
Phaeobacter sp. A36a-5a (Figure 1), we wanted to know how related the isolates were and if they indeed all 
were P. piscicida, as predicted by previous 16S rRNA gene sequencing [11]. Therefore, we conducted two 
==PAGE:26==
Results 
26  
 
analyses using the assembled genomes of all the Pseudoalteromonas strains (Table 3) and included P. 
piscicida WCPW15003 a type strain obtained from NCBI. The type strain was included as a reference point 
for the analysis. First, the average nucleotide identities (ANI) and coverage were calculated for each isolate 
and visualized with a heat map (Figure 6 and Figure 7).  
Two distinct groups of the Pseudoalteromonas isolates were identified. The largest groups (i) contained nine 
of the Pseudoalteromonas isolates (C31bio, I38bio, B39bio, J37bio, J38bio, K31bio, E39Bio, H21a_1bio and 
A22bio) and the type strain for P. piscicida (Figure 6 and Figure 7), suggesting that these isolates are closely 
related to P. piscicida. Isolates in this group all displayed yellow colony morphology and inhibited 
Phaeobacter sp. in the spot colony assays (Figure 4), with the only exception being isolates H21a_1bio and 
A22bio that produced brown/beige colonies in the spot assay.  
The second group (ii) is containing the isolates A34a_4a, A36a_5b, A38q_4a and A40q_4a (Figure 6 and Figure 
7). All these isolates did not display yellow colony morphology and furthermore did not have an inhibitory 
effect on Phaeobacter sp. (Figure 4). Finally, they all have a low ANI score (<0.85) and low coverage (<0.05) 
compared to the type strain, indicating that they are not P. piscicida but might be another species.  
To decipher the phylogenetic relationship between all isolates, the Pseudoalteromonas genomes were run 
through autoMLST [67], to generate a phylogenetic tree. The tree is based on multi-locus sequence analysis 
(MLSA) of automatically selected genes.  The small cluster of isolates from the ANI analysis, displaying a 
brown/beige colony morphology, also clustered together in the autoMLST tree (Figure 8). The isolates were 
less related to the yellow-coloured isolates, and instead, the closest relative was the type strain of 
Pseudoalteromonas byunsanensis JCM 12483, rather than P. piscicida (Figure 8). All other isolates, including 
all yellow-coloured isolates, where closely related to P. piscicida strains, with the exception of strain E39bio, 
for whom Pseudoalteromonas elykovii was its closest relative. 
Overall, the analysis suggests the yellow-coloured isolates are related to P. piscicida whereas the 
brown/beige isolates are more closely related to P. byunsanensis.

3.3. Minimum Inhibitory Concentration Assay of Phaeobacter and Pseudoalteromonas 
To enable the setup of live co-culture systems in which we could study the overall effect of secreted 
metabolites from Pseudoalteromonas isolates B39bio and A38q-4a on Phaeobacter sp. and vice versa, their 
inter-inhibitory potential was determined in a minimum inhibitory concentration (MIC) assay. The MIC was 
determined using serially diluted sterile filtered supernatant of the respective cultures. MIC was set as the 
lowest concentration of supernatant that inhibited growth (measured as OD) after 48 hours. 
The MIC of supernatant from P. piscicida B39bio against Phaeobacter sp. in MB was determined to be the 
addition of 25% supernatant to the media. The MIC of supernatant from Phaeobacter sp. towards P. piscicida 
B39bio was 100% supernatant (Figure 9). The assay therefore showed that supernatant from P. piscicida 
B39bio have a stronger inhibitory effect on Phaeobacter sp. as compared to the inhibitory effect of 
supernatant from Phaeobacter sp. against P. piscicida B39bio when the supernatant is collected from fully 
grown cultures.  
==PAGE:33==
Results 
33  
 
In comparison, the MIC of supernatant from A38q-4a against Phaeobacter sp. was 100% (Figure 10). 
Supernatant from Phaeobacter sp. was more potent against A38q-4a with 50 % supernatant diluted in MB 
being required to inhibit growth. The assay therefore showed that supernatant from Phaeobacter sp. have a 
stronger inhibitory effect on P. piscicida A38q-4a as compared to the inhibitory effect of supernatant from P. 
piscicida A38q-4a against Phaeobacter sp. when the supernatant is collected from fully grown cultures.  
In conclusion the MIC assay shows that supernatant from B39bio is a more potent at inhibiting Phaeobacter 
sp. compared to supernatant from A38q-4a and that Phaeobacter sp. supernatant  is a more potent at 
inhibiting A38q-4a compared to B39bio.

3.4. Liquid co-cultures of Phaeobacter and Pseudoalteromonas 
To examine the interaction between Phaeobacter sp. and Pseudoalteromonas piscicida B39bio, a liquid co-
culture model system was established.

4. Discussion 
The predominant assumption is that microbial secondary metabolites, with antimicrobial activity, serve as 
microbial defense molecules in natural microbial communities. However, at sub-inhibitory concentrations, 
antimicrobial secondary metabolites may also act as signals altering gene expression, metabolite production 
and induce a range of phenotypic changes in ecological neighbors [38], [77]. In this project, we investigate the 
interactions between Phaeobacter sp. and P. piscicida, two marine bacteria that produce potent 
antimicrobial secondary metabolites, through a combined bioinformatics and experimental approach. 
We found that Phaeobacter sp. and the Pseudoalteromonas strains displayed different phenotypic responses 
when cultured next to each other (Section 3.1).  The response of Phaeobacter sp. seemed to be associated 
with the colony colour of the adjacent Pseudoalteromonas strain: Yellow pigmented strains inhibited 
Phaeobacter sp. growth and production of brown pigment, whereas the non-pigmented (brown/beige 
coloured) Pseudoalteromonas strains appeared to be inhibited by Phaeobacter sp. This observation can be 
explained by  the fact that  pigmented Pseudoalteromonas strains are known to produce bioactive 
compounds with antibacterial properties, in contrast to non-pigmented species where production of 
antimicrobial compounds have been less evident [60], [78]. One group of compounds associated with the 
yellow colony morphology in Pseudoalteromonas are the bromoalterochromides [79]. We therefore 
suspected that it is bromoalterochromides that give rise to the observed colony colour of our yellow P. 
piscicida. One might therefore hypothesis that the non-pigmented brown/beige Pseudoalteromonas strains 
lack the genetic potential to produce bromoalterochromides resulting in the non-pigmented brown/beige 
colonies.   
In conjunction with this hypothesis genome mining with antiSMASH (section 3.2.2) revealed indeed that all 
the yellow colonies contained a BGC encoding a bromoalterochromide cluster whereas the non-pigmented 
brown/beige colonies did not. Therefore, one could assume bromoalterochromides are responsible for the 
different inhibition observed between Phaeobacter sp. and the yellow or non-pigmented brown/beige 
Pseudoalteromonas strains. It has previously been reported that P. piscicida strain S2724 and S2040 
contained respectively 10 and 14 BGCs [80], which is similar to the amount of BGCs found in most of the 
Pseudoalteromonas strains in this project. However, some Pseudoalteromonas strains contained 17-19 
predicted BGCs. This means that the number of secondary metabolites that can be produced by 
Pseudoalteromonas vary substantially between strains. Nevertheless, to get a better prediction of BGCs it 
would be important to expand the analysis to multiple closed genomes in the future.

Discussion 
49  
 
At the onset of this study all the Pseudoalteromonas isolates were believed to be P. piscicida isolates, based 
on identification using 16S rRNA gene sequencing [11]. Meanwhile, the ANI and autoMLST analysis suggested 
that four of the thirteen isolates in fact belong to another species (Section 3.2.1). This is crucial information 
to be aware of because different species can impact the interaction[81]. This begs the question which method 
is more reliable 16S identification or the ANI and autoMLST approach? Identification using the 16SrRNA gene 
has long been the workhorse for phylogenetic analysis and is generally great at identification on genus level 
[67]. One pitfall is that multiple species within a genus can have identical 16S rRNA sequences  which makes 
species differentiation difficult, exemplified by Bacillus anthracis, Bacillus cereus, and Bacillus thuringiensis 
that cannot be distinguished using the 16S rRNA gene [82], [83]. Recently it was demonstrated that the genus 
Pseudoalteromonas contains species with this problem, thus they share the same 16S allele and therefore 
cannot be differentiated based on 16S gene sequences alone [84]. Our analysis using autoMLST is based on 
MLSA. MLSA integrate multiple genomic loci to calculate phylogenetic relationships and therefore mitigates 
the problem of identical 16S alleles [67]. Based on our analysis we therefore propose that four of the thirteen 
isolates are not P. piscicida but more likely to be P. byunsanensis. This is of course of interest given that these 
four isolates all were isolated from Jyllinge Harbour, the same place as the Phaeobacter sp. is isolated, and 
might therefore not be indicative of interactions between Phaeobacter sp. and P. piscicida. Nonetheless one 
identified strain of P. piscicida (A22bio) was isolated from Jyllinge harbour and could suggest co-existence of 
P. piscicida and Phaeobacter sp. in the marine environment. A22bio did produce non-pigmented 
brown/beige colonies and might indicate that P. piscicida and Phaeobacter sp. coexist when no 
bromoalterchromides are being produced.  
In this study, we focused on the interaction between specifically Phaeobacter sp. and P. piscicida B39bio, 
using liquid co-culture setups and metabolomic analysis. The co-culture setups revealed that Phaeobacter 
sp. required a large cell density advantage (>103 cells) to be able to co-exist in co-culture with B39bio for 24 
hours (section 3.4). This indicates that P. piscicida is dominating the setup and is better at competing in liquid 
co-culture. Other co-culture studies of Phaeobacter inhibens and Pseudoalteromonas tunicata in mixed 
biofilms resulted in Phaeobacter inhibens dominating the biofilm [85]. It might therefore be speculated that 
Pseudoalteromonas are more dominant in a planktonic system whereas if we had studied the interaction in 
a biofilm system the roles might have been reversed. Thou these experiments were conducted on different 
spices and that could also be why the interaction is different. One explanation for the dominance of B39bio 
could be growth rates. Based on the MIC assay it was approximated that B39bio had a generation time that 
was 3.7 times as fast as Phaeobacter sp. (data not shown) which can affect the abundance in the co-culture 
system. Another explanation could be the inhibitory effects of secreted metabolites. The MIC assays (Section

3.3) discovered supernatant from B39bio to be more inhibitory towards Phaeobacter sp. than vice versa. This 
further adds to the conclusion that B39bio is inhibitory towards Phaeobacter sp when they interact. 
The global metabolome of mono- and co-cultures of Phaeobacter sp. and P. piscicida revealed large changes 
in detectable metabolites especially comparing Phaeobacter sp. monocultures and co-culture indicating 
Phaeobacter sp. is influenced by P. piscicida. Interestingly the most intense metabolites produced by 
Phaeobacter sp. in monoculture where undetectable in co-culture. In a biological context this can be caused 
by Phaeobacter sp. decreasing production. Decreased production could be caused by antimicrobial 
compounds from P. piscicida inhibiting specific proteins or even protein synthesis in general [86]. On the 
other hand P. piscicida could be catabolising the compounds from Phaeobacter sp. as a result of cross-feeding 
interactions [87].  Moreover, these two options are not mutually exclusive.  One might argue that the 
compounds could also be unstable and degrade independently, but this would also be occurring in the mono-
culture and would therefore not account for the large difference in detectability. These results coupled 
together with the lack of brown pigmentation the closer the Phaeobacter sp. colonies where to B39bio 
colonies in the spot assay leads us to speculate that it is a decrease in production that is causing the lack of 
detectable metabolites in co-culture. It would therefore be interesting in the future to identify these 
metabolites and uncover if their expression is reduced (potentially trough transcriptomics) or if they are 
being catabolised by P. piscicida in co-culture. 
Additionally, the metabolomic analysis discovered a group of metabolites that where undetectable in both 
mono-cultures but became detectable in co-culture. This group indicates that either P. piscicida or 
Phaeobacter sp. is responding to the other when present in co-culture and producing these compounds. 
Other researchers have shown that co-cultivation of 12 different marine Micromonosporaceae strains with 
either Mycobacterium sp. (strain WMMA-183) or Rodococcos sp. (strain WMMA-185) lead to production of 
unique secondary metabolites in co-cultivation setups. which could also be occurring in our system [88] 
Therefore, compounds that are hiding in this group could potentially be secondary metabolites that effect 
the interaction e.g., antimicrobial compounds. 
One secondary metabolite that at the onset of this study was suspected to play a role in the interaction was 
TDA. The initial monoculture of Phaeobacter sp. showed that TDA was primarily found in the supernatant but 
when the CFU/mL was adjusted to lower cell numbers it could not be detected in either monoculture or the 
co-culture system. This could be due to production by Phaeobacter sp. being lower than the detection limit 
in the model system or due to a lack of expression. A future experiment using qPCR could determine if the 
lack of production it is due to reduced gene expression, if it is due to actions by P. piscicida or simply due 
chemical detection limitations. Studies have shown TDA production is coupled to quorum sensing [23], [27]

5. Conclusions 
This project discovered that Phaeobacter sp. and P. piscicida interactions were strain depended. With yellow-
pigmented P. piscicida inhibiting Phaeobacter sp. and Phaeobacter sp. inhibiting brown/beige P. piscicida 
strains.  
Furthermore, the biosynthetic potential of P. piscicida was also strain dependent. We found that supernatant 
from P. piscicida B39bio was more inhibitory towards Phaeobacter sp. compared to supernatant from 
Phaeobacter sp. towards P. piscicida B39bio. 
We succeeded in establishing a liquid co-culture system between Phaeobacter sp. and P. piscicida B39bio, 
when Phaeobacter sp. was given a 1000 -fold cell density advantage at the time of inoculation. TDA 
production by Phaeobacter sp. could not be detected in monoculture with less than 1.02 ∙107 CFU/mL. Also, 
TDA was undetectable in co-cultures with Phaeobacter sp. and P. piscicida B39bio. P. piscicida B39bio 
produced bromoalterochromides in both monoculture and co-culture with Phaeobacter sp. There was no 
significant difference in production between mono- and co-culture detected.  Since the yellow-pigmented 
strains were the only ones inhibiting Phaeobacter and bromoalterochromides being associated with yellow 
pigments and the compounds were produced in the co-culture system, we argue that bromoalterochromides 
are an important secondary metabolite that can be responsible for the observed inhibition of Phaeobacter 
in the co-culture system. 
We find that metabolomic changes are detectable between mono and co-culture of Phaeobacter sp. and P. 
piscicida B39bio and is a result of interactions between the two. Metabolite production in Phaeobacter sp. is 
reduced when co-cultured with P. piscicida. It was also discovered that production of 14 unknown 
metabolites was increased in co-culture and are suspected to originate from P. piscicida B39bio.  
Additionally, it was discovered that four strains previously characterised as P. piscicida A34a_4a, A36a_5b, 
A38q_4a and A40q_4a, were more closely related to P. byunsanensis and should therefore be reclassified to 
another species.  
In conclusion, interactions between Phaeobacter sp. and P. piscicida B39bio were confirmed in a co-culture 
system. P. piscicida B39bio was antagonistic towards Phaeobacter sp. and secondary metabolites production 
was determined to play be an important role in the interaction between Phaeobacter sp. and P. piscicida. 
Finally, it would be essential in the future to expand the co-culture study to P. piscicida strains that do not 
produce bromoalterochromides in order to confirm that they are actually as important as our results suggest.
