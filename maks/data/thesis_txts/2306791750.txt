Summary: 
 
The aim of the MSc Project is to look into the application of flaps or morphing 
trailing edges on wind turbines in order to reduce the fluctuating loads and 
thus lower cost of energy. The DTU 10 MW reference turbine is used as basis 
and the rotor will be enlarged as a first step. Then, the different design load 
cases will be examined according to IEC standard and flaps with different 
control approaches will be investigated in order to alleviate loads to the level 
of the original rotor. Moreover, the possible contribution of flap technology in 
increasing Annual Energy Production is investigated. The scope of this project 
is to verify a business case in which a larger rotor could be implemented to 
an already designed platform as well as researching different control 
strategies and implementations to utilize the Adaptive Trailing Edge Flaps 
potential in specific operational conditions.

ABSTRACT 
 
 
Wind energy today is the fastest growing renewable energy source and the second highest in 
installed power capacity globally. The trend in the wind energy industry is to lower the cost of 
energy based on economy of scale principles. This leads to larger and larger rotors aiming for 
higher rated power as well maximizing energy yield in low wind speeds. In order to enable this 
upscaling smart rotor concepts are proposed and becoming more relevant as the dimensions 
grow. Adaptive Trailing Edge Flaps (ATEFlaps) with active control for distributed load control 
on a blade is one of the most promising with numerical and experimental results showing the 
potential and proof-of-concept. The technology is considered mature and is close to an 
industrial implementation. 
The scope of the project is to utilize the considerable work (numerical modelling and 
experiments) that has been carried at DTU Wind Energy in order to numerically identify the 
potential of ATEFlaps in Annual Energy Production (AEP) increase and load alleviation 
through different control implementations in an applied manner close to the industrial one. For 
this purpose a business case frame is defined where the DTU 10 MW reference wind turbine 
blades are upscaled while the rest of the components are kept identical. The aim is to investigate 
whether the ATEFlap implementations can reduce the Ultimate and Fatigue design loads to the 
level of the baseline suggesting a technology enabler for rotor upscaling. Hence, the scope of 
the present work is dual; on one hand to research and identify novel and already applied control 
implementations for different objectives and on the other hand evaluate these concepts in the 
context of the aforementioned business case. 
Current scientific investigations focus mainly on reducing fatigue loads on wind turbine 
components for specific operational states. The present study investigates dedicated control 
approaches for the different objectives and their possible integration utilizing robust and 
applicable control methods and sensors already accepted by the industry. Moreover, these 
investigations take into account the total life of the wind turbine as it is represented by the 
complete set of design load situations considered for load validation and certification processes 
by the relevant international standards. According to the previous, the present report can be 
divided in five main parts.  
The first copes with the upscaling procedure, explaining the considerations and the reasoning 
for the decisions taken regarding structural and operational characteristics. The second is 
dedicated to investigations of different methods to enhance power production using the 
ATEFlaps. Next part focuses on investigations for ultimate design load alleviation. The fourth 
part includes investigations on fatigue design load reduction. These last three parts, include the 
conceptual formulation and practical algorithmic implementation of the controllers, the 
identification in terms of parametrical and sensitivity analyses and the evaluation of the results 
for all design load cases. The final part deals with the integration of the different dedicated 
controllers to one unified with the different possible implementations tested. The results of the 
different implementations for the upscaled turbines are used to evaluate the controller’s 
efficiency as well as the applicability of the business case considered.

1 INTRODUCTION 
The present part of the thesis defines the research objectives and scope of the project. The current 
status in wind turbine active load control research and applications is briefly presented with the focus 
on the findings and methodologies suggested for ATEFlap implementations. The motivation of the 
current work is explained in this context as well as the business case hypothesis. Finally, an 
overview of the report’s structure and contents is presented.   
1.1 State of the art and motivation 
 
Scaling up in order to reduce Cost of Energy (COE) leads to increased blade radii which means 
larger and stiffer components in order to obtain the increased strength required. Reduction in design 
loads will reduce cost of energy due to reduced manufacturing cost. Moreover load alleviation 
methods can reduce R&D costs by removing the need to redesign components, useful for upscaling 
of already existing concepts.  
In order to control the aerodynamic loads and power performance of a wind turbine, the aerodynamic 
loads lift and drag have to be controlled (assuming no external inflow control like a diffuser). This 
can be done, from an aerodynamic point of view, actively - by changing either the angle of attack 
(AoA) or the lift and drag coefficients (     ) for the corresponding AoA or passively – by defining 
the corresponding aerodynamic and structural properties in the design. The most common methods 
being used up to now for this purpose are individual or collective blade pitching (active method) and 
stall regulation (passive method). Both have advantages and disadvantages, with active control 
giving more flexibility and controllability but also adding complexity (sensors, actuators, control) to 
the system. 
This flexibility allowed by active control has shifted research mainly towards these systems, 
although passive load control possibilities like twist-bend [1] or twist-sweep [2] are still under 
research. Furthermore, mainly for power enhancement and noise reduction, passive control methods 
are used in modern blade design optimization like vortex generators, stall strips, serrated trailing 
edges, flat-back airfoils and riblets.  
Active control aims to control the aerodynamic characteristics of the blades along the span by using 
a control algorithm based on sensoring devices’ signals and mechanical actuators. These kind of 
implementations, targeting mainly load alleviation, are called ‘smart rotors’ and many of such

concepts investigated for wind turbines are based on aerospace research (mainly helicopter related). 
The main active control concepts being investigated (besides traditional pitching) are: trailing and 
leading edge flaps (adaptive or rigid), active twist, micro-tabs and plasma actuators. The report by 
Sandia [3] and paper by Barlas et al. [4] classify and evaluate analytically the different methods so 
far with respect to their efficiency and maturity level. The PhD dissertation by Pechlivanoglou [5] 
includes numerical and experimental comparison of the different methods. The extensive literature, 
like the aforementioned sources, suggests that one of the most promising in terms of efficiency and 
applicability (backed up also by the industrial focus) proposal is the use of Adaptive Trailing Edge 
Flaps (ATEFlaps). 
The ATEFlap approach has its origin to rotorcraft industry and is the evolution of the rigid trailing 
edge flaps (or ailerons) which were considered as means of aerodynamic braking and load control 
but where not applied widely due to is issues with size, weight, complex linkage and slow actuation. 
ATEFlaps use smart adaptive materials (usually rubber based), occupy less chord (5%-20%) require 
no hinges, are lightweight and the actuation system (pneumatic or piezoelectric) is fast and able to 
cope with the time scale of inflow variations. Their application can be divided to smaller individual 
flap sections along the span in order to optimize the local effect according to requirements while the 
effect on lift and lift-to-drag ratio is found to be quite linear with flap deflection, enhancing 
controllability. Hence, ATEFlap is a method allowing to directly control local lift and drag and 
indirectly, through fluid–structure interaction the angle of attack (AoA). Compared to individual 
pitching (which controls AoA collectively for each blade and indirectly the lift), the main advantages 
are the significantly reduced actuator damage (pitch bearing is a much larger and expensive 
component) and the possibility to distribute locally the effects.  
The past decade the proof-of-concept is done through dedicated research mainly in DTU and 
TUDelft. The more relevant ones, building the foundation for ATEFlaps is briefly presented here. In 
the research by Barlas [6] 2D aerodynamics modeling and experimental validation in static airfoils as 
well as scaled rotors is done. Wingerden [7] on parallel contributed with experiments for the 
verification of aerodynamic response and actuator dynamics [8] as well as investigating theoretically 
the potential in the context of LPV control applications. Baek [9] investigated with CFD calculations 
and wind tunnel experiments the potential in real blade designs by LM, contributing to unsteady 
aerodynamic modelling and from there on investigating the fatigue load reduction potential with 
different control methods in comparison with individual pitch techniques. The present thesis is 
partially inspired by this work which suggested the validation of ATEFlaps load reduction potential 
by upscaling a rotor and targeting baseline load envelope. Andersen [10] investigated the 
improvement of the aerodynamic stall model for ATEFlaps and contributed to its validation 
experiments with as well as the possible control strategies for fatigue load alleviation using different 
sensors. Bergami [11] further improved and validated the aerodynamic model as well as 
implementing it in the aeroelastic code HAWC2 and the linear model of HawcStab2 (both used in 
the present work). Furthermore, these investigations included LQR, MPC and PID controllers for 
fatigue reduction and identification of key parameters of the ATEFlap model. 
Building upon the previous numerical framework a lot of studies have been published the past years 
focusing on different control methods including Proportional Integral Derivative (PID) control, 
Model Predictive Control (MPC), Linear Parameter Varying (LPV) control,    control and Linear 
Quadratic Regulators (LQR) mainly for alleviation of fatigue loads. The Ultimate load alleviation 
has not been investigated as single control objective but rather as a ‘byproduct’ of fatigue load 
alleviation. Moreover, a few recent studies have investigated the AEP increase potential using 
ATEFlap with theoretical steady state calculations or low turbulence regime in specific speeds. The 
relevant specific literature findings are presented/compared to the relevant sections of the thesis.

Experiments in test field have also been performed, demonstrating the mature level of the technology 
where practical aspects of implementation have been investigated and the concept validated. Sandia 
has conducted a field test [12] using a Micon 65/13 turbine (115kW) with the 9m blades covering the 
20% of the span near the tip. The time scales associated with active control were identified in 
different operational conditions contributing to system identification of model based control for 
flaps. Furthermore, results validate the influence in measured strains along the blade. Moreover, 
DTU and Vestas tested at Riso facilities a V27 (225kW) turbine with one of the 13m blades 
equipped with a 70cm ATEFlap [13]. An MPC controller was implemented showing reduction in

Furthermore, based on experimental system identification the same controller numerically in order to 
evaluate and calibrate the computational models. Furthermore, the long term research on ATEFlap 
systems at DTU Riso [14] included a field experiment where a rotating test rig equipped with a 10m 
long boom having a blade section of 2m span and 1m chord equipped with an ATEFlap system. This 
test section fully equipped with sensors was used to check steady, flap step and azimuth control flap 
cases in order to identify the system and check the potential in load alleviation as well as validate the 
computational model. The results showed reduction in normal force, root moment and sectional 
forces[15],[16].  
In general the research done so far is focused in a single control objective (mainly fatigue reduction) 
and results for the methods proposed are limited in steady state calculations or few specific design 
load cases (mainly normal operation with normal or low turbulence). Moreover, some of the control 
approaches considered, while indicating to perform well, are highly complex and not proven robust 
enough for industrial implementation while others utilize input signals like local deflection or local 
inflow signals (AoA, wind speed) that makes them inadequate for application in commercial wind 
turbines since sensoring systems like pitot tubes or pressure taps are not considered mature enough 
yet. 
1.2 Research Objectives – Business Case  
Taking into account the previous discussion, the present study aims to investigate the three control 
objectives (fatigue and ultimate load reduction, AEP increase) individually for the total life of the 
wind turbine as it is represented by the complete set of design load cases (IEC standard 61400-1, 3rd 
edition [17]) that is used for load certification purposes by the industry. Furthermore, these dedicated 
controllers is decided to be based on simple control algorithms focusing on applicability and 
robustness. Different control approaches and parameter derivation will be tested for the controller. 
Additionally, parametrical/sensitivity analyses for tuning, identifying key mechanisms and 
limitations will be performed. 
The control inputs will be limited to blade root flapwise bending moment and hub height wind speed 
measurements which are based on sensors (strain gauges and nacelle mounted cup 
anemometer/LiDar) already implemented and accepted by turbine manufacturers. Furthermore, the 
possible integration of three individual controllers in one, achieving the same load reduction level 
and AEP increase will be investigated. 
Moreover, a business case is formulated in order to evaluate the potential of ATEFlap system in a 
practical implementation. The DTU 10MW RWT will be used as baseline and the blades will be 
upscaled so that the rotor is enlarged in order to increase energy yield. The rest of the components 
will remain the same so that no re-design is needed (reducing R&D costs). Then, on these upscaled

2.1 Aeroelastic tools 
 
HAWC2 
The software HAWC2 (Horizontal Axis Wind turbine Code) is developed in order to calculate wind 
turbine response in the time domain. It includes an aerodynamic part consisting of an unsteady 
BEM-based code extended to handle dynamic inflow, dynamic stall, skew inflow, shear effects on 
the induction and effects from large deflections along the blade. This is coupled with a structural 
Finite Element (FE) model using Timoshenko beam elements to represent the different parts of the 
turbine in order to form the equation of motion based on a multi-body dynamics solved on time using 
a Newmark scheme to solve numerically the non-linear differential equation in time. Further 
information on the formulation of the unsteady BEM and its extensions can be found in the book by 
M.O. Hansen [18], while for the structural model in the book by A.A Shabana [19] Information 
about the models used in HAWC2 and can be found in the manual [20] and in the comprehensive e-
learning course provided by Risoe DTU [21]. The version used for all the simulations is 12.2.

The core code of HAWC2 is developed in FORTRAN© and distributed as a compiled executable for

The interaction with the main code (e.g. for control, servos external forcing) is done through

HawcStab2 
The second DTU Wind Energy in-house aeroelastic tool used is HawcStab2. HawcStab2 is a 
frequency based aeroservoelastic code for steady state calculations and stability analysis of wind 
turbines. The model is based on an analytical linearization of a nonlinear finite beam element model 
and an unsteady BEM aerodynamic model including shed vorticity, dynamic stall, and dynamic 
inflow. Further information about the stability analysis methods can be found in [23] and for the 
aerodynamic model used in [24]. 
HawcStab2 was used to calculate preliminarily the operational characteristics of the upscaled 
turbines as well as to perform modal analysis in order to identify the frequencies of the dominant 
modes and create the Campbell diagrams. Moreover, the software enables the user to get a linear 
model of the turbine (both closed and open loop) in a state-space representation format that can be 
used for controller analysis. In the latest version the ATEFlap model is also introduced and can be 
included in the state-space representations, a feature used to tune the flap Proportional Differential 
(PD) controller for fatigue presented in chapter 6.  Finally, a feature is included allowing to tune the 
gains for the classical PI main controller (pitch, rotational speed and generator torque regulation both 
in partial and full load) of the DTU 10MW RWT. This feature was used in order to retune the 
controller for the modified upscaled turbines. Further information about the features and use of 
HawcStab2 can be found in the user’s manual [25] and for the gain scheduling techniques used for 
the tuning tool in [26].  
2.2 ATEFlap model and implementation in HAWC2 
 
Since the thesis is focused on the application of ATEFlap model a brief presentation of the model is 
relevant in order to have an overview of the theoretical as well as the practical aspects.  
Unsteady aerodynamics background  
Traditional 2D aerodynamic models calculate forces and moments for a rigid non deformable airfoil 
based on the instantaneous effective angle of attack (AoA). Where if AoA is steady the coefficients 
are derived as function of AoA from a steady curve (obtained either experimentally, analytically or 
numerically). Otherwise if AoA changes dynamically a dynamic stall model has to be implemented 
to capture the effect of the time-delayed aerodynamic load response which is proportional to the ratio 
of chord by inflow velocity. This effect is attributed to the boundary layer’s state (attached, partially 
or fully separated) which depending on the magnitude of change needs time to adjust and stabilize.  
Theodorsen’s (1935) theory of aerodynamic instability [27] based on potential flow assumption was 
the basis for studying the unsteady loading in the attached flow regime below stall. Advances in

aircraft wing design and helicopter rotors pushed the research to investigate the mechanism of 
dynamic stall in all regions and create more efficient models. Leishmann and Beddoes (1989) 
introduced a complete model a complete model for calculating unsteady aerodynamic loads 
including effects of trailing and leading edge separation, unsteady 2D inviscid wake and 
compressibility effects [28]. The model was formulated both in state-space (suitable for eigenvalue-
based stability analysis) and indicial formulations (suitable for numerical solving of aeroelastic 
equations like in HAWC2 solver). Details on the formulation of the models can be found in [29].  
Based on the aforementioned models Hansen et al. [30] proposed a modified model more focused on 
wind turbine aeroelasticity where the main phenomenon is trailing edge initiated separation and thus 
neglecting compressibility effects (relatively low speeds - tip speed less than 100 m/s) and leading-
edge initiated flow separation (common use of relatively thick airfoils >15% relative thickness). This 
model is the one also implemented in HAWC2 for blades with non-deformable airfoils.  
Unsteady aerodynamics including trailing edge deformation 
The ATEFlap is an ‘engineering’ aerodynamic model that calculates the integral aerodynamic lift 
and drag forces as well as the pitching moments of on an arbitrarily moving 2D airfoil with a trailing 
edge deflection. It is based on the previously mentioned model but taking into account the 
contribution of trailing edge deformation and analytical presentation of the model is given in the 
report by Bergami and Gaunaa in [31]. The effects reproduced by the model include added mass 
effects-forces induced by the acceleration of the flow due to the airfoil shape and motion (no 
memory effects, non-circulatory based on potential flow assumption, only instantaneous AoA), wake 
dynamics effects-(memory effects of the vorticity shed into the wake due to changes in AoA or flap 
deflection, circulatory based on potential flow assumption) and dynamic stall effects (dynamic load 
response in flow separation regime).

in the inflow conditions are approximates as changes in AoA and treated as a superposition of

aero-servo-elastic calculations), instantaneous   (prescribed by a flap controller) and inflow velocity 
it returns the lift, drag and pitching moment. Hence, the ATEFlap is modelled as changes in the 
aerodynamic characteristics and changing beta angle and can be seen as changing airfoils in the 
specific position. The effect of the moving part itself in the structural formulation of the equation of 
motion (e.g. inertial or gyroscopic effects from the flap) is not considered at all which can be 
considered close to reality depending on the chord percentage occupied by the flap, the chords 
length, the airfoil’s thickness and flap’s frequency and angle.  
Implementation in Hawc2 
The ATEFlap model was formulated, implemented and validated in the aeroelastic code HAWC2 by 
Leonardo Bergami as part of his PhD [11]. Thorough details about the implementation along with 
instructions and examples can be found in [20], [31].  
The first part of the implementation is an external application Preproc_ATEFlap.exe which takes as 
inputs the steady state airfoil data and created the rest based on the algorithm described previously 
while it allows for visual inspection and manual corrections by the user. The output .ds file contains 
all the data needed for the ATEFlap model appropriately formatted and is passed as input data file 
along with the others in HAWC2. 
The flaps (single or multiple per blade) need to be defined as starting and ending points along the 
blade spanwise length along with their corresponding input files. Also, the stall model described 
previously needs to be selected in the aerodynamics definition part. This model works also for the 
case of rigid airfoils without any bias in the results. In order to be consistent for all simulations this 
model was used always regardless of the existence of flaps in the model.   
Moreover, the flap angles need to be assigned by a controller. This is done through DLL interfaces 
where the controller can have any of the possible outputs of HAWC2 as inputs and define at each 
simulation time step an angle.  
Another aspect of the implementation of the ATEFlap model is the servo model used in order to take 
into account the mechanical response of the system. The servo is also passed as a DLL to HAWC2 
main code and uses as inputs the indicated instantaneous flap angles by the flap controller. Then 
these values undergo a first order low pass filtering as well as a saturation check to simulate real 
==PAGE:32==

actuator dynamics and limits. This means that flap has a maximum flap speed (deg/s), a response 
delay as well as upper/lower limits for the flap angle. The outputs are the actual flap angles that will 
be fed into the ATEFlap model for the instantaneous aerodynamic calculations. In our case, actuator 
time response (modelled as filter’s time constant) was a variable while the angle limits where 
restricted by the available CFD data which restricted the angles to [-15:15].

2.5 IEC standard and DLB – Loads and AEP calculations 
 
In order to evaluate and certify a wind turbine design and define the design loads international 
standards are used. Most wind turbine manufacturers and certification authorities use the one 
provided by International Electrotechnical Community (IEC). In the latest published version of IEC 
61400-1 ed.3:2005 ([17]) standard the minimum design requirements are defined. This standard 
includes different combinations of wind conditions and turbine states that should be evaluated in 
order to identify Extreme or Ultimate design loads (U) as well as the Fatigue (F) design loads for 20 
year life time. Based on this verified from simulations loads the manufacturers are then responsible 
to make sure that all the components are able to handle them. 
The wind climate is defined through classes (shown in table 2.1) which indicate the reference wind 
speed which is considered as five times the mean speed of the site which is used to determine the 
Rayleigh wind distribution for fatigue and AEP calculations. Moreover, the reference turbulence 
intensity is defined indicating the mean value of turbulence intensity at 15 m/s based on which the 
standard deviation can be calculated keeping it constant the turbulence intensity for each mean speed 
can be defined. For the DTU 10 MW RWT the design class is IA and this is the class based on which 
all the fatigue and AEP calculations are executed. More details about the wind conditions can be 
found in [17]. 
 
Table 2-1 Wind turbine classes as defined in IEC 61400-1 ed.3:2005 standard ([17]) 
Estimation of loads is based on different operating cases under different wind conditions. For this 
purpose different Design Load Cases (DLCs) are defined covering all cases of operation (normal 
operation, extreme conditions, fault, and maintenance) with different wind conditions. The set 
including all DLCs describing the life of a wind turbine and the most significant conditions that may 
be experienced is referred to as Design Load Basis (DLB). DLCs are evaluated for U, F or both kind 
of loads and their influence is added in a weighted manner to evaluate the total loads. The wind 
conditions and the minimum set of different turbulence seeds to model the stochastic nature of wind 
is also defined.  
The DLB described in IEC standard is the one followed in order to simulate the turbine response in 
the present thesis. Ultimate loads in IEC standard are derived through statistical extrapolation to 50

year extreme wind. This approach is considered quite conservative and many manufacturers and 
certification authorities use other methods. In the present work ultimate loads are calculated as the 
maximum loads reported in the simulated time-series using different partial safety factors (PSF) for 
the design load cases. This method is considered better for the scope of our analysis since the relative 
comparison of different flap set ups need to be evaluated and this is a more direct method in order to 
identify the effects of the setups in loads. The full DLB set including all the PSFs considered here is 
presented in the report from Risoe [42] and a brief table summarizing all DLCs is given in the 
appendix (Table 11-1).  
Fatigue loads are calculated as fatigue damage equivalent loads (DEL). These loads are calculated 
using a rain-flow cycle counting algorithm along with Palmgren-Miner rule regarding linear fatigue 
damage accumulation. For the blades which are made of composites the Wohler exponent is 
considered 10 and for the rest of the structure material is assumed steel and Wohler exponent equal 
to 4. The weighting distribution for the life time contribution of each speed is given by the wind 
distribution defined by the turbine class. The theoretical formulation of the method can be found in 
[43] [17], while the exact methodology and formulas used in the present work can be found in [37].  
Regarding the determination of power curves and subsequently AEP from simulations there is no 
standardized procedure, with the IEC referring only to requirements for defining power curves from 
prototype measurements. It was decided to calculate the power curves by simulating the different 
setups for 700 seconds (first 100 considered transient and disregarded) with a step of 1 m/s (cut-in to 
cut-off wind speeds), with a steady turbulence intensity of  10% for all speeds, six turbulence seeds 
per speed, no yaw misalignment and a wind shear exponent of 0.2.  Annual hour-distribution of 
speeds was defined by the wind Rayleigh distribution and constant operation for 365x24 hours per 
year was considered for calculation of AEP. The method followed for AEP after the derivation of 
power curves is the same described in IEC 61400-12-1 2006 [44].

This chapter presents the upscaling procedure of the original rotor by 5% and 10%. Firstly, the 
assumptions and decisions needed to be taken are discussed. Then, the new blade designs are

3.1 Theoretical and practical considerations 
 
The hypothesis formulated for the project was that the increased loads generated by a rotor upscaling 
(on the same platform) could be compensated by the flaps’ contribution. In order to investigate 
clearly this effect but also make a realistic case some assumptions had to be done and decisions to be 
taken. Since the objective is not to redesign the blades but to keep the original design and linearly 
‘stretch’ the blades there are restrictions based on limitations of geometric and aerodynamic 
similarity. 
Aerodynamic and structural 
Most of the studies done on upscaling laws for wind turbine components refer to an upscale where 
all the components scale up (hence total weight is increased) with the rated power increasing 
proportionally to the cube of the geometrical scaling factor (GSF) and thrust with the square. 
Aerodynamic loads and weight are the main drivers for the components’ upscaling  [45]. In our case 
all the components except the blades are the same leading to a smaller increase in weight (depending 
on the decision of the blades’ structural properties). In order to be consistent with keeping the same 
nacelle components (generator, gearbox etc.) and decrease maximum aerodynamic loads the turbine 
was decided to be restricted to 10 MW like the original one. This trade-off decision leads to a de-
rated low induction machine where the gain in energy production will come from increased power

3.2 Tuning the controller of the upscaled turbines 
 
The main controller [41] (section 2.4) tuning is another part of the upscaling. Although aerodynamic 
properties and weight is not significantly changed the gains of the controller needed to be fined 
tuned. This was done with HawcStab2’s module featuring an automated procedure of partial 
(generator speed and torque control) and full (pitching) load PI gain tuning based on the linearized 
closed-loop state space representation of the turbine [25].  
There are several methods proposed to achieve this [51]. For our case, since the original gains were 
already close to optimal, the tuning is done with a systematic check of values around the original 
ones. In this procedure, values of natural frequency and damping ratio (regulating mode reaching 
instability) were given as inputs for each region and the output was the proportional and integral 
gains for the two regions (considering constant torque above rated). These values were then imported 
in HAWC2 were simulations with step and ramp wind were executed in order to check the response 
of the controller. The criteria for this check were to reduce overshooting, fluctuations and settling 
time in the rotating speed of the rotor and the electrical power produced. Finally, the selected gains 
were tested with a simulation with 10% turbulence around rated speed in order to ensure smooth 
transition between regions. In the appendix (figures Figure 11-3Figure 11-4) two examples plots are 
shown with different gains and step wind input.

3.4 Load and AEP validation of upscaled turbines 
 
The baseline turbines were simulated for the full DLB (section 2.5) in order to derive and compare 
the Ultimate (U) and Fatigue (F) design loads as well as to compare power production and AEP. The 
scope of this section is to present an overview of the response of the baseline turbines while more 
detailed analysis about specific DLCs and load generation mechanisms will be carried out at the 
relevant chapters. 
Loads 
The response of a turbine through simulations is investigated through time-series response of 
different channels. The total amount of channels is large and in order to simplify and make a more 
focused analysis it was decided limit the analysis in the following characteristic channels: 
 
Tower Bottom: Bending moments at the first node of the tower where it is fixed to the 
ground. The fore-aft bending moment channel will be addressed as MxTB while the side to 
side MyTB. 
 
Tower Top: Tower top refers to the node representing the nacelle attached to the last node of 
the tower. MxTT represents the tilt moment, MyTT the roll moment and MzTT the yaw 
moment of the nacelle. 
 
Main Bearing: It is the bearing supporting the main shaft, usually one of the highly stressed 
components needed to be designed cautiously. MxMB is the tilt moment. MyMB is the yaw 
moment and MzMB is the torsion. 
 
Blades: For the analysis of the blade loads in different positions spanwise are relevant 
especially when blades become so large like in our case. Complex cross-sections with 
varying strength and loading need to be inspected for phenomena like local buckling. Again 
the decision was to simplify the analysis and check only the blade root (annular cross-
section) channels. Conventions: MxBR will indicate the flapwise blade root bending 
moment, MyBR the edgewise and MzBR the torsional moment. 
 
Tower Tip clearance: For our applications where blade became longer and less stiff the 
blade tip-tower clearance is an important value. HAWC2 is not producing this output so an 
extra dll was created taking into account instantaneous blade azimuth, tip global position and 
tower wall global position (assuming a more or less steady height on the tower). The output 
value was recorder only when any of the blades where at      azimuth from the tower 
center. The channel will be symbolized as TTdist. 
All the values (including TTdist) absolute or relative relevant to loads presented throughout the 
thesis include partial safety factors as they are defined in [42]. Only the time series where 
presented will be in raw format not including PSFs. 
It is standard procedure in the industry to present ultimate loads in terms of minimum and 
maximum loads and the same will be followed here. Moreover, an ultimate design load table is 
created giving the maximum absolute value for each channel which is considered simpler and 
faster way to have an overview of the load comparison especially when the effect of the flaps to 
load reduction is investigated. Finally, for annular cross-sections like the tower and main bearing 
channels the ultimate design load can be considered as the absolute maximum of the two 
directions. On the blade channels though, both directions (flap and edge) are interesting since the 
blades in most of their length are not axisymmetric with different stiffness properties and loading 
in the two directions.

3.6 Summary of upscaling and business case definition 
 
The key points concerning the upscaling can be summarized as: 
 
All components kept exactly the same with original turbine except blades.  
 
Rated power kept to 10MW and early pitching was introduced in order to constrain load 
increase. 
 
Blades where stretched without structural upscaling, aerodynamic and geometrical similarity 
kept. 
 
Damping was added to the tower in order to remove resonance effects and have a clear 
picture for the flap investigation. 
 
Operational characteristics defined based on the constraints of keeping gearbox ratio and tip 
speed same to the original. 
 
DTU wind energy main PI controller (pitch and torque regulation) retuned based on 
HawcStab2 for the upscaled turbines. 
Load and AEP validation of the baseline turbines is the basis for setting up the business case 
proposed. The expected increase due to the upscaling is estimated as 3.4% for the 5% and 6.3% for 
the 10% baseline turbine. Then the ATEFlap implementation will aim to decrease the fatigue and 
extreme loads to the level of the original baseline as well as increase the AEP even more. The 
investigation will be limited to the channels presented in section 3.4.  
Since the fatigue and ultimate load margin is not known for the original components it is assumed 
that the original design has 0 margin for both types of loads. This is of course a hypothesis leading to 
a ‘worst case scenario’, probably in a real case there would be known margins that would decrease 
the load alleviation requirements.  
Moreover, the decision of keeping the rated power same, results to a machine targeting low wind 
speeds. This is contradicting to the turbine’s IEC class (IA). This class has the highest average wind 
speed of the 3 standard classes formulating again a ‘worst case scenario’ in terms of AEP. In order to 
investigate fully the potential in other classes too would require a different load analysis too since the 
fatigue loads should be recalculated which is considered out of the scope of the thesis. Thus, in 
section 4.4 the expected AEP increase for different climates is shown in order to have an overview of 
the full potential.  
Finally, the DTU 10MW RWT is a highly optimized concept which gives less room for 
improvement than a ‘real’ design. This can be verified also from [39] where the methods used for the 
rotor design DTU 10MW RWT and comparison with the NREL 5MW RWT which was used as 
initial point is done. Furthermore, Figure 11-7 shows the steady state axial induction for different 
speeds with the plots verifying the high level of aerodynamic optimization.

4 AEP INCREASE POTENTIAL 
INVESTIGATIONS 
The present part of the thesis includes the investigations of AEP increase potential using ATEFlaps. 
The main concept is to use ATEFlap in speeds below rated in order to increase power production 
while over rated the system is deactivated and flap angle is constantly 0. As explained in section 1.1 
several studies show that there is a potential in AEP increase using the flaps. The target of the 
present study is to quantify this increase with full unsteady inflow simulations, while the control 
applied to the system should be based on simple and robust model using already implemented 
sensors accepted by the industry. There are two main concepts investigated; first control with wind-
speed based gain scheduling of static flap angles (section 4.2) and second azimuth-wind speed 
(section 4.3) based control following the 1P aerodynamic load fluctuations. The structure of the 
chapter includes an overview where the methodology of different investigations is summarized 
followed by case-by-case results discussion. Section 4.4 contains an overview and comparative 
discussion of the different control approaches. 
4.1 Overview and concepts of AEP Investigations 
The ATEFlap control approach for the AEP increase is to use low-pass filtered wind speed (time 
constant 2s) and azimuth position as only inputs. It was decided to use the filtered and not the 
measured speed in order to avoid high frequency turbulence fluctuations that would add noise to the 
output signal without contributing to the goal of increasing power production. 
The controller (figure 4-1) checks input velocity and if speed is between the defined limits then a 
flap angle is calculated. In the case of static angle tracking the flap angle is defined by a polynomial 
expression (        ) with the same being valid for multiple flaps per blade where different 
coefficients are calculated for each section. In the case of azimuth related control       is used to 
calculate (through polynomials again) values of amplitude, phase and offset for a sinusoidal function 
of azimuth angle that calculates flap angle. For speeds outside the predefined values, controller 
applies steady flap angles in order to ensure smooth transitioning between partial and full load and 
enhance power production when the instantaneous speeds reach values lower than cut-in.

The initial point for the calculations of the parameters needed was the parametrical sweep (   step)

These sweeps were used as basis for the calculations of the controller’s input parameters. Initially, 
the unsteady results (statistics) were used to identify the flap angles that produce the maximum 
electrical power for each velocity bin. Parametrical analysis for these gains was performed in order 
to fine tune but also sensitivity analyses to check the influence of the key parameters of flap section’s 
size and servo’s time constant. 
The next step was to use the steady sweep time-series results in order to localize optimal results in 
terms of spanwise position (for multiple flaps per blade) and azimuth position (cyclic approach). 
This was done using different criteria and the different suggested solutions were evaluated in fully 
unsteady conditions by testing the turbines response in DLC1.2 simulations. This transfer of results 
from steady to unsteady conditions required significant manual fine tuning and was a significant 
bottleneck as described in the following sections. The unsteady aerodynamics coupled with the 
flexible structure’s response increase highly the complexity of the system the steady state’s open 
loop control results cannot be applied as derived.  
Increase of AEP is the only aim of the present investigation hence the increase in fatigue 
contribution of below-rated speeds expected is not considered as a parameter. Although, in order to 
have an estimation of this impact, short term equivalent loads were calculated for each control 
approach tested and presented in the relevant sections. This is relevant for the integrated control 
where the fatigue-aiming control part is activated only above rated (where most of the fatigue is 
contributed as seen in section 3.4) hence no compensation is possible for this increase. 
The whole analysis and calculation of parameters was done with the 5% baseline turbine and the 
optimal parameters were after tested in the 10% case. This assumption can be considered as accurate 
due to the similarities of the two turbines.

INVESTIGATIONS 
The present chapter presents the investigations done for ultimate load reduction potential using flaps. 
Structure includes a first part where the main concept and the control approach implemented is 
explained. Then, the parametrical investigations for the different controller variables are presented 
for fault and parked DLC cases. In section 5.4 the results in the full DLB are presented, compared 
with the baseline. Finally, the last part summarizes the methods and the derived results as well as the 
general conclusion derived by the analysis. 
5.1 Overview and concepts of ultimate load reduction investigations 
Studies until now have mainly focused in fatigue load reductions using flaps with the influence on 
ultimate loads being a byproduct of these methods. Studies like work done by Baek [9] and Andersen 
[10] consider only normal operation conditions with turbulence lower than and shear exponents 
lower than the values defined by IEC standard. A more recent study by Bernhammer et al. [55] 
investigates the extreme load alleviation in normal and extreme turbulence as well as the start-up 
procedures according to IEC standard (DLCs 1.2, 1.3, 1.4 and 3.x. There, an individual flap PI 
control targeting 1P fluctuations using the Coleman transformed blade root flapwise moment is used 
mainly for fatigue but also the extreme loads are checked. A similar control approach is used in the 
report by Innwind project [56] where the extreme reduction side effect of the fatigue controller is 
checked for DLCs 1.2 and 1.3 as well as for the parked cases (DLCs 6.x).  
As seen from the load analysis in section 3.4 most of the channels present their extreme in fault 
DLCs (2.x) where the loading level is significantly increased compared to the other DLCs (especially 
in MxBR channel). This difference in loading level becomes more significant with the upscaling, 
showing that as rotors get larger and in order to calculate accurately design loads in an applied 
manner, these cases should be taken into account. Hence, in the present work the aim is to 
investigate the potential reduction in the full DLB. 
Moreover, this difference of loading levels observed was the basis for the idea developed to separate 
the different ‘modes’ of the flap controller based on the level of MxBR. Previously works usually 
correlate the reduction in loads (fatigue and extreme) with a reduction in AEP. The aim is to avoid 
this (with a cost in fatigue below rated) by activating the extreme load control over an MxBR

threshold and the fatigue over a speed threshold. Further discussion about this is done in chapter 7 
referring to the integrated control. 
5.1.1 Cut-off control approach 
As seen in the next chapter, the investigation with the individual PD fatigue controller showed some 
reduction in ultimate loads which was not considered enough. Based on the idea given by the 
supervisor T. Barlas a control approach specific to extreme loads was investigated. The hypothesis is 
that based on the known extreme load envelope a threshold can be defined such that when it is 
reached the flap will move to a (negative) angle in order to give instantly a decrease in lift force. 
This reduction should be able to reduce the peak loads in blades (and hypothetically this reduction to 
be propagated to the other components too) without affecting the operation of the turbine if the 
parameters are tuned correctly and the load envelopes of the DLCs allow for it. In the turbine 
investigated, which can be considered as representative for multi-MW, large rotor modern (and near 
future) turbines this is valid. 
The controller’s configuration is shown as a block diagram in figure 5-1. The inputs from the sensors 
and main controller include LP filtered wind speed, MxBR measurements for each blade and a grid 
status signal from the main controller which is 1 when the turbine is connected and 0 when 
disconnected. 
The procedure is as follows: First, wind speed and grid status is checked and if the speed is over a 
limit or disconnected a static flap values is assigned. Else, the instantaneous MxBR values are 
checked and if the defined threshold is exceeded the controller imposes a value to the flaps. When 
MxBR do not exceed the threshold then the controller is not active (flap to 0 or any other ‘mode’ like 
AEP tracking).

The proposed method using an ATEFlap system with a cut-off switch control approach was found to

6 FATIGUE LOADS’ REDUCTION 
INVESTIGATIONS 
Fatigue load alleviation using ATEFlaps has been a subject of several studies, since this has been the 
main objective of such a system up to now. Different control approaches have been investigated as 
well as field tests [13], [12] verifying the potential the potential of such a system. The numerical 
investigations include traditional PID control approaches mainly as well as more complex control 
approaches like Model Predictive Control (MPC) [57],    control [58] and Linear Quadratic 
Regulators (LQR) [59], incorporating input signals mainly for bending moment (on blade root or 
local sections) and sometimes (coupled or exclusively) with local inflow measurements. Interesting 
overview comparisons of results in MxBR fatigue reduction obtained from different methods is 
shown in [60],[55]. These comparisons are done for the NREL 5MW RWT and present a high 
divergence of the suggested results varying from 6% to 47% while with PID methods are found to be 
between 6 and 30%.  
6.1 Overview and concepts of fatigue load reduction investigations 
Most studies utilizing PID control methods, apply the control either individually to each blade based 
on each of the three signals (can be seen as three completely decoupled controllers) or collectively to 
the whole rotor based on Coleman transformations from rotating coordinates to fixed and back. In 
our investigations, based on the objectives set (robustness and applicability), it was decided to use a 
formulation of Individual Flap (IF) single-input-single-output PD control based on MxBR input 
signals only, without intermediate transformations.  
Similar investigations using the same tuning methods, for an IF PI control applied on Coleman-
transformed yaw and tilt rotor moments can be found in [56]. Moreover, Baek in his PhD [9]   
proposes a PD IF control scheme along with a semi-automated algorithm for tuning. Another 
interesting comparison of control methods incorporating PID strategies can be seen in [61]. These 
are just some examples of literature relative to our implementation showing. In general, the topic of 
fatigue load alleviation with ATEFlaps using PID methods is extensively covered in literature and 
our implementation is a rather simplistic method following previous ideas with small alterations. 
As for the Ultimate loads investigations, our main assumption is that by targeting MxBR (reducing 
standard deviation and amplitude) the effect will be propagated to other components too. As seen in

6.3 Controller’s tuning 
Control methods as the one proposed here are as good as their gain tuning. In literature different 
methods are proposed without one being globally accepted as the most effective. Tuning methods 
proposed include systematic parametrical tuning, semi-empirical methods based on stability (like the 
Ziegler-Nichols used here), step- or ramp-response based. The method used in the present work is 
inspired by Leonardo Bergami’s work [62] and consists of two parts. Firstly, a rough tuning is done 
using Ziegler-Nichols method [63] based on a high fidelity state space representation of the turbine 
(in open and closed loop) per speed obtained from HawcStab2. Then, these values were tuned 
approximately for saturation limits based on a Matlab script including no-feedback and finally 
representative example cases where checked (visual inspection) with results from applications on the 
controller in HAWC2 simulations. Finally, having the values from the previous step as a starting 
point parametrical studies were executed by running DLC 1.2 simulations in order to find the 
optimal gains for fatigue reduction. 
For the first part of identifying gains using the Ziegler Nichols method the critical gains and 
frequencies for instability per speed were needed. HawcStab2 returns a linearized high order state-
space description of the turbine in an open-loop configuration for user-specified operating points, as 
well as state-space matrices for the linearized collective power control. For a simplified tuning 
procedure, the three individual flap controllers are considered as a single PD controller of the 
symmetric collective flap actions, based on a feedback of the variations in the collective MxBR. A 
closed loop description including both power and flap control returns then an indication of the 
stability for the complete aero-servo-elastic linear system. Analytical presentation and validation of 
the model including flaps in HawcStab2 can be found in [64]. According to Ziegler Nichols method, 
for every speed, Kd was set to 0 and Kp was increased progressively until a pole of the obtained 
closed loop system became unstable (damping ratio becomes 0). The gain at which instability is 
reached is the critical gain    and the corresponding frequency is the critical frequency   . An 
example of this procedure is shown in Figure 6-2. For all the speeds checked (12:2:24 m/s) the 
frequency was around 1.52 Hz corresponding to the second asymmetric flap tilt mode for our turbine 
(see section 3.3).

7 INTEGRATED CONTROL 
In the previous chapters different control approaches and derivation methodologies were investigated 
for different control objectives; AEP increase, Ultimate design load reduction and lifetime damage 
equivalent load reduction. The final part of the present work is the investigation of the possibility to 
combine the different control, occasionally contradicting, objectives to one unified controller. For 
these investigations no further optimization on results is considered with the objective set being to 
derive an integrated controller delivering the same effects as the previous ones individually. 
Moreover, different possible applications of the cases investigated earlier are explored by checking 
the potential coupling of AEP and U as well as U and F controllers in pairs.  
The first section explains the reasoning behind the hypothesis of decoupling the different control 
objectives based on wind speed and MxBR thresholds. In the second, the methods used and DLB 
results for a coupled AEP-U controller are discussed. Third part includes the total integration of the 
controllers with the different approaches tested. Next, based on the results of the integrated control, 
the AEP part of controller is deactivated in order to check the maximum efficiency of an integrated 
controller aiming exclusively to load reduction. Finally, the last part includes a comparison of the 
best results achieved using the integrated controller with the original and 5% baseline turbines.  
7.1 Overview of integrated control implementations 
The load envelope of MxBR channel in DLC 1.2 (power production under normal conditions) is 
observed to be in a different level than in DLCs 1.3 and 3.x (power production in extreme and fault 
conditions) as seen in Figure 11-19. As seen in the baselines load comparison this decoupling 
becomes clearer as the turbine scales up. Based on this observation it was decided to use different 
thresholds in order to decouple the different modes of the integrated controller. The fatigue controller 
should be active only in power production under normal conditions which is expected to be the 
conditions that the turbine will operate under, for the majority of its lifetime. Hence, the idea is that 
there will be a global MxBR thresholds that will activate the cut off controller on top of the other 
modes, then if speed is below a level and main controller signal indicates partial load the AEP 
targeting mode will be active. When the turbines reaches rated power or the speed threshold is 
surpassed the flap controller should switch to the fatigue reduction objective. As seen in the 
following analysis this caused the issue that the cut-off U controller became less efficient due to the 
fact that the fatigue part was active when the threshold for cut-off was reached reducing the dynamic 
effect of cut-off switching. This was handled by adding an extra upper threshold for fatigue

Vasilis Pettas - June 2016

controller (defined by DLC 1.2 MxBR extreme loads) after which the angle is set to 0, assuming that 
these extreme or fault conditions occur rarely and the interest there is to reduce extreme loads rather 
than the contribution in lifetime fatigue loads. Finally, the last issue that was handled was the 
efficiency reduction of fatigue controller due to the switching between AEP and fatigue modes. In 
order to make this effect less severe and more favorable for fatigue reduction it was tested to use a

to verify that they are completely decoupled (as expected by their formulation) and also quantify the 
increase in fatigue expected which cannot be compensated by the controller whose fatigue mode is 
active only in full load. 
As a next step, the integration of all three modes where tested with three different implementations. 
Firstly, the case with only an MxBR threshold for the cut-off part was tested so the interference of 
fatigue and ultimate modes could be checked. Secondly, the aforementioned second MxBR threshold 
for fatigue mode deactivation implementation was investigated so that agreement with the individual 
implementation could be quantified. Finally, the more aggressive switching between AEP and 
fatigue modes was implemented for investigating their trade off. 
The last part of the investigation included the check for this last aggressive-switching 
implementation with the AEP part deactivated. The maximum efficiency in load of the controller 
was checked this way, while the AEP increase was a result of the upscaling only.

7.3 Fully integrated control applications 
Having verified that AEP and U combination, the next part is to integrate the IF PD fatigue 
controller presented in chapter 6. The different approaches and reasoning are explained in the 
overview (section 7.1). In the following sections the three different implementations are explained 
and the issues addressing each one is covered. The results for all cases are presented comparatively 
in section 7.3.3 so that the differences are visualized clearly.  
7.3.1 One threshold 
For this implementation the AEP-U controller presented in the previous section is used with the 
fatigue controller added on top. The first check in the controller is with the cut-off MxBR threshold 
checking for peak loads. Then, the AEP part is active when speed is lower than 13 m/s or rated 
switch signal lower than 1. In all other cases the fatigue controller is active.  
Results showed that in general the integration is possible with some issues. For ultimate load 
reduction part, MxBR (the target channel) presented 3% less decrease compare to the individual case 
while tip clearance not only did it not reach the level achieved in the individual implementation but it 
was increased (meaning tip-tower clearance got smaller) compared to the baseline no-flap case. The 
rest of the channels shoed marginal worse results than the individual case with the exception of 
MxTB in which reduction was further decreased. The reasoning was identified by looking the time 
series response of the channels. As explained in the extreme load reduction part, the dominant effect 
in the reduction is the dynamics of the abrupt movement of the flap from 0 to -15 degrees. When the 
PD controllers is active its continuous operation makes the flap change angle following the MxBR 
signal. When the cut-off threshold is reached the flap is already moving towards negative angles with 
a certain rate (since threshold is always reached form above) according to the loading history. Thus, 
the cut-off cannot deploy the full dynamic potential while when settled down to -15  it becomes less 
effective while the efficiency of the application depends on the random combination of instantaneous 
and short-term loading history. 
Regarding fatigue loads, the majority of channels present smaller decrease than the individual 
implementation at a level of 1-6% with the highest difference observed in MxTB (6.1%). This is 
attributed firstly to the increase of the fatigue from the AEP part of the controller which, if 
considered as a simple superposition, as seen in the previous section for all channels except MxTB is 
in the order of 1% or less. Secondly, to the fact that in speeds around rated the cut off is occasionally 
activated which does not impact AEP but increased the standard deviation of MxBR response. 
Finally, the switching is focused on AEP only with the criteria not allowing fatigue to be activated in 
partial region. Hence regarding power production and AEP, as expected, no difference was observed 
since there is no change in the partial load operational region. 
These issues were treated with the methods proposed in the following sections. In order to be easier 
for the user to understand the state of the controller at each instance, an extra output was defined 
giving the instantaneous state of the controller as a value between 1 and 5 (1. AEP, 2. U, 3. F, 4. 
Fixed -15  (over a speed limit or in parked if chosen) 5. No action 0 ).

7.3.4 Aggressive switching

7.5 Conclusions of integrated control application 
The previous investigations showed that the integration of different control objectives in one flap 
controller is possible with some tradeoffs. The main conclusions are: 
 
The cut-off control approach can be implemented on top of the other controllers without 
affecting each other separated based on MxBR load thresholds. The only limitation in this 
application is the difference in load envelopes between normal operation (DLC 1.2) and the 
fault and extreme cases. As our results have shown the scaling favors this separation of the 
load levels, but still this is a very case specific result and other turbines should be examined 
in order to generalize.

 
AEP-targeting controller increases MxTB fatigue in a level of 6% while the others channels 
are marginally increased, although these contributions cannot be alleviated in an 
implementation like the one proposed here. Its efficiency is coupled with the fatigue part of 
the controller. The margin in switching between the two modes is allowing to choose the 
focus of the controller according to the desired operation.  
 
Fatigue reduction oriented module seems to be smoothly coupled with extreme reduction by 
using MxBR thresholds. Defining an upper limit which on one hand covers the normal 
operation envelope and on the other hand is not activated in extreme conditions allowing for 
the cut-off part to act seems to be effective and the results are same with the individual 
application of the modules.  
 
The different combinations of the control modes demonstrates the versatility of such a 
system which can be integrated in a design points and be formulated in order to achieve 
design objectives. 
 
The blade root torsion channel MzBR is influenced by increased pitching moments and, as 
seen in all calculations, affected negatively by the flap control with increase in both ultimate 
and fatigue loads. The different control methods or coupling of the different modules do not 
change this effect.

8.2 Issues and Limitations 
As seen from the previous analysis, assuming no fatigue or strength margin for the components, 
although significant reduction was observed in some cases the load alleviation level achieved for the 
5% upscaling case is not adequate for all channels even with the AEP control mode deactivated. 
More specifically, tower channels, main bearing and blade root torsion are exceeding the load 
requirements. This shows that with our assumptions a smaller upscaling should be considered in 
order to make valid our initial hypothesis along with modifications in tower. The 10% upscaling 
considered initially seems to be far out from the system’s capabilities. Knowing the actual fatigue 
and ultimate strength margins of the components along with the cost functions including flap system 
implementation cost, R&D and manufacturing cost for increasing some of the components’ strength 
and expected gains in AEP, an estimation of the feasibility of such a project could be done in order 
to properly evaluate the business case. 
Furthermore, the fact that the blades are being elongated without being redesigned structurally, 
makes them more prone to phenomena like buckling or local failure. More blade sections should be 
investigated in order to derive local loads and investigate ATEFlaps efficiency in their alleviation.  
8.3 Applicability and Robustness 
Assuming that such a business case is feasible some further considerations should be taken into 
account as the present investigations have shown.  
Firstly, some channels have shown a level of 15% reduction in ultimate loads compared to the no-
flap case (5% baseline). Then the structure will be designed to be able to handle this load level, but 
then what will happen e.g. in case extreme events like the blade stuck occur and the flap system is 
not working at all, or properly? If the structure is designed such that it will be able to handle the 
baseline loads there is no gain from the flaps implementation. 
Different solutions could be proposed, like a structural design that would impose a favorable for load 
alleviation angle (probably the negative limit) in case of flap activator’s failure but the point is that 
like all the other sensors and actuators already implemented; fault cases and corresponding 
certification DLCs should be standardized for such a system.  
Moreover, the efficiency of the system relies heavily on MxBR sensors for feedback control. 
Although more complex sensoring systems like local inflow measurement were not considered, these 
kind of sensors have not been used yet for control purposes. The inspection or replacement of

compared to the baseline DTU 10MW RWT. This increase is higher for the other IEC 
classes since the turbine’s design is targeting low wind speeds. The upscaling itself 
contributed 3.4% and ATEFlap control targeting AEP (by collectively tracking optimal flap 
angle based on wind speed input) another 0.3%.  
 
 
Significant reduction was achieved to ultimate design loads examined in the full IEC DLB 
taking into account the turbine’s complete operational life. The collective, cut-off switch 
control based on MxBR sensors applied showed that the channels decreased the most where 
MzTT (15%), MxTT, MxMB, MxBR (all in the level of 12%) and tip-tower clearance 
increasing by 15% compared to 5% upscaled baseline. Tower channels were practically not 
affected and channels MyMB and MzBR where slightly increased by 2%. 
 
 
Fatigue loads with the use of individual flap PD (based on HP filtered MxBR) control were 
decreased mainly in MxMB and MyMB (8%) and MxBR by 12%. The rest of the channels 
were marginally decreased in a level less than 2%. MyBR was marginally increased while 
the blade root’s torsion is highly significantly by 8%.

 
The integration of the controllers and decoupling of control objectives based on wind speeds, 
MxBR thresholds and main controllers full load indicator was shown to be applicable. The 
extreme load alleviation part is completely decoupled from the AEP part while the fatigue 
part is coupled with both the other parts. The inclusion of AEP mode increases slightly 
fatigue (only MxTB increases significantly by 6%) in the partial region which cannot be 
compensated by the fatigue module operating in full load. Moreover, there is a tradeoff 
between AEP and fatigue reduction based on the controller switching around rated. Fatigue 
and extreme reduction were successfully decoupled and results same as with the individual 
applications were achieved by introducing a second threshold after which the fatigue part is 
deactivated. In general, the present study showed that a flap controller can include different 
modes, becoming more versatile and effective according to the application’s objectives. 
 
 
For the AEP part the most efficient method was found to be the optimal flap angle tracking 
based on LP filtered wind speed (slightly more efficient than fixed flap angle). The 
implementation with 1 flap section was more efficient and the size of the flap was found 
proportional to the effectiveness of the system. Servo’s time constant (representing flap 
actuator’s dynamics) sensitivity analysis indicated no effect in the results. The investigations 
of multiple flap sections and azimuth-based control were influenced by the high level of 
aerodynamic optimization of the blade section considered, issues with methods identifying 
local optima (span- and azimuth- wise) and transferring steady state results to turbulent 
regime. 
 
 
The extreme load alleviation investigation showed that for the cut-off switch approach 
(intermittent operation based on MxBR threshold) the limits of the application are imposed 
mainly by the dynamics of the flap motion and secondarily from the size or     of the flap. 
Moreover, the collective instead of individual blade approach seemed to be more effective 
especially for the tip deflection. The actuator’s dynamics are coupled with the threshold 
(which is the dominant parameter) and cannot be investigated separately. 
 
 
Investigations for fatigue load reduction based on individual flap PD control on HP filtered 
MxBR, with wind speed based gain scheduling showed that the application is compromised 
by the phase delay of the system compared to the tracked channel. Moreover, a tradeoff 
between channels was observed where with the gains changed some channels presented 
further reduction while others increased. Additionally, the tuning method based on linearized 
state-space (open and close loop) matrices for stability calculations with Ziegler Nichols 
method and manual tuning seem to have room of improvement. Flap actuator’s dynamics 
should be as fast as possible since the higher time constant introduces further delay to the 
system while size, as seen from previous studies, is proportional to the reduction effect. 
 
 
A bottleneck identified in the investigations is the transfer of the steady or quasi-steady state 
results (obtained using either a high fidelity linear model like HawcStab2 or a non-linear one 
like HAWC2) to the unsteady conditions. The unsteady aerodynamics coupled with the 
flexible structure’s response increase highly the complexity of the system and the steady 
state results cannot be applied as derived. 
 
 
The different criteria checked for identifying local values (derived from steady simulations) 
in order to calculate local optimal flap deflections per operational state presented divergence 
==PAGE:110==
Investigation of ATEFlap potential in load alleviation and maximizing energy yield for large wind turbine rotors 
92

to their results. The methods suggested in the present study can be a starting point in order to 
formulate and validate a more efficient approach. 
 
 
Following the two previous points, comes the issue of increased computational cost. The 
approaches giving the best results in the methods checked are based on unsteady simulations 
(either full DLB or specific DLCs). Simulating full parametrical sweeps and post processing 
time-series or statistics results from these is very expensive computationally and time 
consuming.  Different methods like surrogate models, characteristic sub-sets of the DLCs or 
more accurate algorithms based on steady state / linear models can improve this and also 
give more flexibility to the research. 
 
 
A basic assumption of the control methods proposed related to load alleviation is that by 
controlling (limiting) the response of the input channel (flapwise blade root bending 
moment) the effect will be propagated to the other channels/components. This, as seen from 
the results, is not accurate for all cases as some components like the tower or loads like blade 
root torsion are not affected at all or negatively.  
 
 
All the results derived in the present work are very case-specific depending on the 
components’ properties, aerodynamic design of the rotor and climate considered (class IA). 
Some examples include: the effect in AEP relies heavily to the aerodynamic optimization 
(which in our case is very high) and the class’s climate, the distribution of fatigue 
contribution of each speed to DEL (and subsequently the fatigue reduction potential of the 
flap) relies on the climate, the effect of parameters like servo’s time constant depends on the 
aeroelastic behavior of the blades, the decoupling of control objectives is based on the 
discretization of load envelopes between normal operation with normal turbulence and 
fault/extreme conditions. Thus, the results indicate the potential but should be verified by 
checking other turbines and different climates. Moreover, since all the derivations are based 
on models and numerical simulations, the uncertainty in a real implementation should be 
taken into account. 
 
 
Related to the previous conclusion is also the dependence (or not) on turbulence. Many of 
the optimal values derived (especially for the AEP and extreme load alleviation parts) were 
based on best results and fine-tuning on the response of the turbine in particular wind inflow. 
For instance the crucial factor of dynamic behavior of the cut-off controller is identified 
based on the peak loads produced by specific wind time series over which the optimal 
parameters were derived. It has to be verified that the optimization is not done ‘on the 
turbulence’ but is more generalized.  
 
 
Some channels present ultimate values in the fault cases of extreme yaw misalignment. It 
was observed that these values are outliers coming from the stall model’s inability to handle 
these extreme angles of attack. Anyway the results cannot be realistic since even the 
aerodynamic data cannot be verified in these regions. Heuristic methods, like changing the 
particular turbulence seeds or changing rotor’s initial position were checked but were proven 
not enough. In the results’ presentation and discussion the cases where this was observed are 
mentioned and an effort has been done to exclude these values when they proved to be 
clearly outliers. This was not possible for all cases since the pattern was not clear and an 
evaluation of the influence of these results should be done.

9.2 Recommendations for further research 
As for the previous section, these recommendations are a summary of the further research proposed 
in the individual sections where the identified issues not solved are followed by suggestions for 
further investigations or different methods to be followed.  
 
Regarding the business case considered different implementations of the upscaling can be 
investigated. Initially, lower levels of upscaling and/or further reduction in thrust through 
pitch scheduling or rated power can be checked in order to see if and where is the upscale 
limit for which the ATEFlap system can compensate for while the gain in AEP remains 
significant. Moreover, a different concept of upscaling can be considered where the blade 
does not undergo any redesign or stretching and is extended using add-on parts (similar to 
the concepts investigated by LM, GE and other manufacturers [65], [66]). 
 
 
In order to investigate the potential of extending flap system’s load reduction effectiveness 
to more channels/components it would be useful to check firstly each channel’s sensitivity to 
the flap’s actions for all operational conditions and speeds (similar to the investigations done 
here for MxBR) and then try to formulate a transfer function between the response of the 
channels of interest and the controller’s input channel(s). 
 
 
As seen in section 8.3 regarding the applicability and robustness of a system like the one 
proposed here, it is important to explore the behavior of the system in cases where there is 
fault to the flap actuator system or control inputs. A framework can be set where the critical 
parameters of the system including fixed flap angle, erroneous MxBR or wind speed inputs 
etc. can be checked in the worst case extreme scenario similarly to the already standardized 
DLCs. 
 
 
The effect of the flap’s motion to the structural part including gyroscopic and inertial effects, 
flap system’s weight and stiffness should be included to the HAWC2 model implementation 
which in the case of multi-MW turbines with high flap span-coverage can be influential.  
 
 
Different tuning methods should be tested for the fatigue PD controller since the method 
proposed here is relatively rough. The non-linear model of HAWC2 could be used through 
step or ramp flap angle definition for each speed in order to identify     and tune the gains. 
  
 
Another proposal addressing the previous issue and in general the steady to unsteady transfer 
and subsequently the localization issues is to use full unsteady simulations (with multiple 
seeds in order to achieve stochastic independency) and post process these data. The main 
bottleneck for this approach and the reason that it was not performed here is the huge 
amount of simulations and large data post processing required.  
 
 
Higher fidelity aerodynamic models like near wake or vortex methods (currently being 
implemented in HAWC2 and already implemented in other aeroelastic tools) could be used 
to check and compare the flap potential taking into account 3D effects like shred vorticity at 
the flaps spanwise edges. 
 
 
The versatility and tradeoffs of the controller can be used in order to change the system’s 
focus according to the needs dynamically. For example, when high speeds or extreme wind 
conditions are detected the cut-off threshold can be reduced in order to further reduce peak 
==PAGE:112==
Investigation of ATEFlap potential in load alleviation and maximizing energy yield for large wind turbine rotors 
94  
Vasilis Pettas - June 2016 
loads, or based on short term evaluation of the climate (daily, weekly etc.) when low 
turbulence conditions with appropriate speeds are detected the controller can favor AEP 
increase over fatigue and vice versa. These applications of more advanced and complex 
control of course would reduce robustness.  
 
 
Other objectives can be included in the flap controller’s operational envelope like pitch and 
flap actuator damage and usage which was not covered thoroughly in the present analysis. 
 
 
Finally, as stated in most of the chapters the assessment of the proposed implementations 
should be done knowing the exact fatigue and strength margins of each component and 
evaluated through a financial feasibility study taking into account all relevant inputs.

! ----------------------------- Initialization SubRoutine --------------------------------- 
subroutine init_aep_u_f_flap_ctrl(array1,array2) 
implicit none 
!DEC$ ATTRIBUTES DLLEXPORT, C, ALIAS:'init_aep_u_f_flap_ctrl' :: init_aep_u_f_flap_ctrl 
real*8 array1(22) ! Input array, from hawc2 to dll 
real*8 array2(1)  ! Array dll -> hawc2 In this case dummy 0 
! 
!  Input parameters 
!   . Scheduling Parameters: 
!   1: constant 1   ; 1st order term for linear interp of proportional term gain scheduling based on V_filt 
!   2: constant 2   ; offset for linear interp of proportional term gain scheduling based on V_filt 
 
!   3: constant 3   ; 1st order term for linear interp of differential term gain scheduling based on V_filt 
!   4: constant 4   ; offset for linear interp of differential term gain scheduling based on V_filt 
 
!   5: constant 5   ; Min. wind speed, below min gain is kept [m/s] 
!   6: constant 6   ; Max.  wind speed, above max gain is kept [m/s] 
!   7: constant 7   ; Threshold of "rated power indicator" above which flaps are fully on [-] should be between 0 and 1 
!   8: constant 8   ; corner frequency for blade flapwise moments high-pass filter [rad/s] 
!   9: constant 9   ; simulation time step [s] 
!  10: constant 10  ; Fixed beta angle when the turbine is idling or parked with rotor locked or off-grid or.... not in normal op 
!  11: constant 11  ; Value of the status signal that indicates normal operation with grid connection 
!  12: constant 12  ; wind speed limit at which the flaps should be set to beta fixed n [m/s]  
!  13: constant 13   ; gain 1st order for opti beta tracking below rated [-] 
!  14: constant 14   ; gain 1st order for opti beta tracking below rated [-] 
!  15: constant 15   ; gain 3rd order for opti beta tracking below rated [-] 
!  16: constant 16   ; gain 4th order for opti beta tracking below rated [-] 
!  17: constant 17   ; offset beta tracking below rated   [-] 
!  18: constant 18   ; V_filt upper limit until which opti static beta tracking is active [m/s] 
!  19: constant 19   ; MxBR threshold for cut-off controller to be activated [kNm] 
!  20: constant 20   ; Blade root bending moment threshold after which the fatigue load reduction mode will be deactivated [kNm] 
!  21: constant 21   ; Speed Limit to switch to second threshold in order to decouple extreme and fatigue [m/s] 
!  22: constant 22   ; Blade root bending moment second (for V higher than the 2nd lim threshold) after which the fatigue load reduction 
mode will be deactivated [kNm] 
! 
    ! - Read in scheduling parameters for PD fatigue part -  
gain1_p      = array1(1) 
offset_p     = array1(2) 
gain1_d      = array1(3) 
offset_d     = array1(4) 
gs_v_min     = array1(5) 
gs_v_max     = array1(6) 
thr_ratflaps = array1(7)   ! Read in at which value of "rated conditions indicator" have flap fully on  
   ! - Read in filter setup and PID gains -  
fc_mxfilt    = array1(8) 
dtsim        = array1(9) 
   ! - Read in flap angle at no connection - ! 
flap_target  = array1(10) 
status_lim   = array1(11) 
   ! - Read in global V limit at which all flaps will be turned to target value - ! 
V_limit_global  = array1(12)  ! [m/s]  V upper limit to apply fixed angle  
   ! - Read in scheduling parameters for opti static beta tracking below rated -  
gain_1_stat  = array1(13)   ! [-] gain 1st order 
gain_2_stat  = array1(14)   ! [-] gain 2nd order 
gain_3_stat  = array1(15)   ! [-] gain 3nd order 
gain_4_stat  = array1(16)   ! [-] gain 4nd order 
offset_stat  = array1(17)   ! [-] offset 
V_limit_aep  = array1(18)   ! [m/s] V_filt upper limit until which opti static beta tracking is active 
   !- Threshold for extreme load reduction switch to be activated 
load_thres_extr = array1(19)!  
   !- Threshold after which the fatigue load reduction mode will be deactivated 
fatigue_load_thres = array1(20)!    
V_limit_fatigue_thres_2 = array1(21)!              
fatigue_load_thres_2    = array1(22)!                
! 
 
! - Initialize blade root bennding moment values to 0.0 - ! 
mx_old = (/ 0.d0, 0.d0, 0.d0 /) 
mx_filt_old = mx_old 
 
! dummy output to HAWC2 
array2(1) = 1.d0; 
end subroutine init_aep_u_f_flap_ctrl 
! ------------------------------------------------------------------------------ ! 
! ----------------------------- Update SubRoutine -------------------------------! 
! ------------------------------------------------------------------------------ ! 
subroutine update_aep_u_f_flap_ctrl(array1,array2) 
implicit none 
!DEC$ ATTRIBUTES DLLEXPORT, C, ALIAS:'update_aep_u_f_flap_ctrl' :: update_aep_u_f_flap_ctrl 
real(8) array1(6) 
! Input array, from hawc2 to dll 
real(8) array2(4) 
! Array dll -> hawc2 
==PAGE:137==
 
 
! Input array1 must contain 
!    
!    1: Blade 1 flapwise root bending moment Mx [kNm] 
!    2: Blade 2 flapwise root bending moment Mx [kNm] 
!    3: Blade 3 flapwise root bending moment Mx [kNm] 
!    4: LP Filtered wind speed [m/s]   (output 6 of main ctrl) 
!    5: Status signal given by the main controller for normal operation (0 normal op, 1 overspeding, 2 not connected to grid) usually 
output 22 from main ctrl 
!    6: Power rating status: 0. below rated power, 1. above rated power, 0<r_switch_filt<1. smooth transition ( usually output 14 from main 
ctrl 
! 
! Output array2 contains 
! 
!    1: Flap angle reference for Flap 1       [deg]     
!    2: Flap angle reference for Flap 2       [deg]     
!    3: Flap angle reference for Flap 3       [deg] 
!    4: output of flap controller's operating mode 1:AEP increase, 2:Extreme load reduction,  3:Fatigue Loads reduction, 4: fixed to target 
turbine not in production mode or over the global V_lim, 5: none of the cntrl modes switched to 0 beta 
! 
! --------------------------------------------------------------- ! 
! Main Body 
! --------------------------------------------------------------- ! 
!--- Read Inputs ---- ! 
! . Bending moments 
Mx_1 = array1(1) 
Mx_2 = array1(2) 
Mx_3 = array1(3) 
! . Signals from main ctrl  
V_filt           = array1(4)                ! Filtered wind speed from main ctrl 
status_sig       = array1(5)                ! Main Ctrl status flag 
full_load_ind    = array1(6)                ! indicator from main ctrl of full load(1) or partial load (0) 
 
IF (full_load_ind/thr_ratflaps>0.8) THEN 
    r_switch_filt        = MAX(MIN(full_load_ind/thr_ratflaps,1.d0),0.d0)     !  Rated power indicator (0.0 to 1.0). Smooth transition from 
below, above rated based on power rating status 
ELSE 
    r_switch_filt        = 0.d0 
ENDIF 
 
! --------- First calculate and store all the values and inputs independently of the operating conditions   --------------- ! 
    ! -- 1. High pass filter on blade root flapwise moments -- ! 
Alpha1=((1/fc_mxfilt)/((1/fc_mxfilt)+dtsim)); 
flap_moment_1_filter_new=(Alpha1*mx_filt_old(1)) + (Alpha1*(Mx_1-mx_old(1))) 
flap_moment_2_filter_new=(Alpha1*mx_filt_old(2)) + (Alpha1*(Mx_2-mx_old(2))) 
flap_moment_3_filter_new=(Alpha1*mx_filt_old(3)) + (Alpha1*(Mx_3-mx_old(3))) 
    ! . Store for next step: 
mx_filt_old(1) = flap_moment_1_filter_new 
mx_filt_old(2) = flap_moment_2_filter_new 
mx_filt_old(3) = flap_moment_3_filter_new 
mx_old(1) = Mx_1 
mx_old(2) = Mx_2 
mx_old(3) = Mx_3 
 
    ! -- 2. Derivate filtered root flapw. moment --  
    ! derivated flapwise blade root moments 
flap_moment_1_D = ((flap_moment_1_filter_new-mx_filt_old(1))/dtsim) 
flap_moment_2_D = ((flap_moment_2_filter_new-mx_filt_old(2))/dtsim) 
flap_moment_3_D = ((flap_moment_3_filter_new-mx_filt_old(3))/dtsim) 
    ! -- 3. Compute Actual PID gains -- ! 
gs_PD_V_filt= MAX(MIN(V_filt,gs_v_max),gs_v_min) ! set V filtered among limits 
gfl_p = offset_p+ gain1_p *gs_PD_V_filt! can also be made to polynomial regresion instead of this linear tapproximation 
gfl_d = offset_d+ gain1_d *gs_PD_V_filt 
 
    ! --Take action according to the different operating conditions -- ! 
    IF (status_sig /= status_lim) THEN         ! signal status showing that the turbine is not producing (shut down, parking grid loss etc) 
does not indicate errors like DLC2.2b 
 
 
beta_ref_1    = flap_target 
 
 
beta_ref_2    = flap_target 
 
 
beta_ref_3    = flap_target 
 
 
ctrl_mode_out = 4.d0 
    ELSE IF (V_filt> V_limit_global) THEN      ! if wind speed is over a threshold  then switch flaps collectively to beta target 
 
 
beta_ref_1    = flap_target 
 
 
beta_ref_2    = flap_target 
 
 
beta_ref_3    = flap_target 
 
 
ctrl_mode_out = 4.d0 
    ELSE IF (ABS(Mx_1) >= load_thres_extr .OR. ABS(Mx_2) >= load_thres_extr .OR. ABS(Mx_3) >= load_thres_extr ) THEN ! MxBR threshold 
reached switch flap collectively to target beta 
        beta_ref_1    = flap_target 
 
 
beta_ref_2    = flap_target 
 
 
beta_ref_3    = flap_target 
 
 
ctrl_mode_out = 2.d0 
   ELSE IF (V_filt <= V_limit_aep) 
THEN       ! if filtered wind speed below the threshold and normal status signal is 0 
(production) then track optimal static beta for power increase 
 
 
IF (V_filt <= 4.d0) THEN               ! when speed below cut in keep the fit to cut in 
 
 
beta_ref_1 = offset_stat+(gain_1_stat*4.d0)+(gain_2_stat*(4.d0)**2)+(gain_3_stat*(4.d0)**3)+(gain_4_stat*(4.d0)**4) 
 
 
beta_ref_2 = beta_ref_1 
 
 
beta_ref_3 = beta_ref_1 
 
 
ELSE               ! normal operation in partial load controller tracking optimal collective beta for AEP  
 
 
beta_ref_1 = offset_stat+(gain_1_stat*V_filt)+(gain_2_stat*V_filt**2)+(gain_3_stat*V_filt**3)+(gain_4_stat*V_filt**4) 
 
 
beta_ref_2 = beta_ref_1 
 
 
beta_ref_3 = beta_ref_1 
 
          ENDIF 
 
    ctrl_mode_out = 1.d0 
 
   ELSE IF (ABS(Mx_1) <= fatigue_load_thres .OR. ABS(Mx_2) <= fatigue_load_thres .OR. ABS(Mx_3) <= fatigue_load_thres )  THEN    ! Turbine 
is in normal operation and MxBR is within the normal production operational envelope: PD on blade root bending moment 
        IF      (ABS(Mx_1) <= fatigue_load_thres_2 .AND. V_filt >= V_limit_fatigue_thres_2 )  THEN   ! check blade 1 
 
 
 
beta_ref_1 = r_switch_filt *(gfl_p *flap_moment_1_filter_new + gfl_d *flap_moment_1_D) 
        ELSE IF (ABS(Mx_1) <= fatigue_load_thres .AND. V_filt <= V_limit_fatigue_thres_2 )    THEN     
            beta_ref_1 = r_switch_filt *(gfl_p *flap_moment_1_filter_new + gfl_d *flap_moment_1_D) 
        ELSE  
 
 
 
beta_ref_1 = 0.d0 
        ENDIF 
 
==PAGE:138==
Investigation of ATEFlap potential in load alleviation and maximizing energy yield for large wind turbine rotors 
120  
Vasilis Pettas - June 2016 
         
 
 
IF     (ABS(Mx_2) <= fatigue_load_thres_2 .AND. V_filt >= V_limit_fatigue_thres_2 )   THEN   ! check blade 2 
 
 
 
beta_ref_2 = r_switch_filt *(gfl_p *flap_moment_2_filter_new + gfl_d *flap_moment_2_D) 
                      ELSE IF (ABS(Mx_2) <= fatigue_load_thres .AND. V_filt <= V_limit_fatigue_thres_2 )    THEN   
                                 beta_ref_2 = r_switch_filt *(gfl_p *flap_moment_2_filter_new + gfl_d *flap_moment_2_D) 
 
 
ELSE  
 
 
 
beta_ref_2 = 0.d0 
 
 
ENDIF 
 
 
 
IF     (ABS(Mx_3) <= fatigue_load_thres_2 .AND. V_filt >= V_limit_fatigue_thres_2 )   THEN   ! check blade 3 
 
 
 
beta_ref_3 = r_switch_filt *(gfl_p *flap_moment_3_filter_new + gfl_d *flap_moment_3_D) 
                      ELSE IF (ABS(Mx_3) <= fatigue_load_thres .AND. V_filt <= V_limit_fatigue_thres_2 )    THEN   
                                 beta_ref_3 = r_switch_filt *(gfl_p *flap_moment_3_filter_new + gfl_d *flap_moment_3_D) 
                      ELSE 
 
 
 
beta_ref_3 = 0.d0 
 
 
ENDIF 
 
 
ctrl_mode_out = 3.d0 
    ELSE   ! Any other case, set the flaps to 0.0  
        beta_ref_1    = 0.d0 
        beta_ref_2    = 0.d0 
        beta_ref_3    = 0.d0 
 
 
ctrl_mode_out = 5.d0 
    ENDIF  
! --------------------------------------------------------------- ! 
! Output to servo 
! --------------------------------------------------------------- ! 
 ! flap control actions 
 array2(1)  = beta_ref_1 
 array2(2)  = beta_ref_2 
 array2(3)  = beta_ref_3 
 array2(4)  = ctrl_mode_out 
 
end subroutine update_aep_u_f_flap_ctrl 
!************************************************************************************************** 
end module aep_u_f_flap_ctrl_mod
