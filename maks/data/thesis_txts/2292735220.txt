Abstract
In this study, the long term shoreline evolution of the Golok Beach is investigated using
MIKE21 Shoreline Model. The long term morphological model is formulated in order
to represent hydrodynamics, sediment transport and long term shoreline evolution corre-
sponding to actual condition at the study site.
The model calibration result shows that tide-driven current has important inﬂuence in the
study site. Besides, the waves are important to be included in the morphological model
as the study site is exposed to the tropical monsoon climate. Hence, both tide and wave
information are included in the model.
However, long term morphological simulation
under tidal action is prohibitively expensive due to time consuming computation. Hence,
the morphological acceleration techniques are used in order to reduce computation cost.
The morphological tide and schematic wave are in the long term morphological model.
The long term shoreline morphology is simulated for the existing condition, the no-groyne
case and the beach retreat case. The simulation result of the existing condition is used
as the baseline information. The eﬀect of the groyne ﬁeld is investigated by removing
19 groynes constructed between the Golok breakwater and the Tak Bai breakwater from
the existing condition. The eﬀect of beach retreat is investigated by removing deposited
sediment near the Malaysian breakwater. The results and ﬁndings of the study are impor-
tant to understand the feasibility of option for coastal zone management along the Golok
Beach.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Overview
Coastal morphology is the study of the shoreline change due to natural processes and the
impact due to development of human infrastructure within the coastal zone. It is well
known that the hydrodynamics of waves and currents, and the mechanics of sediment
transport under waves and currents have direct relation to the shoreline evolution. As
wave approaching the shore, the wave shoaling and eventually wave breaking generates
strong unidirectional currents within the surf zone. The hydrodynamic actions may trans-
port signiﬁcant amount of sediments along a sandy coast in which the sediment transport
includes cross-shore and longshore sediment transport. The cross-shore sediment trans-
port is responsible for forming coastal proﬁle in short term or during a single storm event;
where the long term shoreline evolution is governed by the longshore sediment transport.
In Peninsular Malaysia, the socio-economic growth and increased utilisation of coastal dis-
tricts has consequently increased the need for coastal development and shoreline protec-
tion. In this thesis, morphological modelling is simulated for the Golok Beach, Malaysia-
Thailand to understand the hydrodynamics, sediment transport processes and shoreline
evolution. Besides, the coastal response and the eﬀectiveness of the coastal management
option are investigated. The study will be focused on the long term eﬀect of the existing
condition and the coastal management options, therefore longshore sediment transport is
calculated and considered. The results and ﬁndings of the study will suggest the feasibility
of option(s) for coastal zone management along the Golok Beach.

Chapter 1: Introduction

1.2
Objective
The overall objective of this study is to develop a coastal morphological model which is able
to predict and analyse sediment transport processes and long term coastal morphology.
The speciﬁc objectives are as the following:
(a) To develop a long term morphological model which can represent the sediment
transport processes and shoreline changes.
(b) To apply the long term morphological model of the Golok Beach, Malaysia-Thailand
in the coastal environment described by morphological tide and schematic wave
forcing.
(c) To investigate the eﬀect of the groyne ﬁeld to the coastal morphology along the
Golok Beach in long term period.
(d) To investigate the eﬀect of beach retreat to shoreline changes along the Golok Beach
in long term period.
(e) To investigate the sensitivity of the morphological model close to the river mouth
area.
1.3
Structure of thesis
The objectives outlined above are reﬂected in the structure of this thesis.
Chapter 2
describes the Golok Beach site and available data that is used for model calibration.
Chapter 3 describes the concepts and governing equations used in the morphological
modelling. The morphological acceleration techniques referred and applied in this study
are presented. The model setup is described in Chapter 4, where the model calibration
and the selection of morphological tide and schematic wave for long term morphological
modelling are presented. The model results are discussed in Chapter 5. The eﬀect of
the groyne ﬁeld and beach retreat to sediment transport and shoreline changes along the
Golok Beach is discussed. Chapter 6 summarises the results and ﬁndings obtained in this
study and recommendations for future work.

Chapter 2
Study site: Golok Beach,
Malaysia-Thailand
2.1
Site description
The Golok River is part of the land boundary between Malaysia and Thailand. The river
empties into the South China Sea at the southern extremity of the Gulf of Thailand.
In this study, the site is extended approximately 5km along the Malaysian coast and
10km along the Thailand coast from the Golok River mouth as shown in Figure 2.1. The
Golok River mouth improvement works including the breakwater construction, the groyne
ﬁeld (30 groynes) construction and dredging of the navigation channel were completed in
1997.

Chapter 3
Morphological modelling
3.1
Introduction
The MIKE21 Shoreline Model refers to the coupled model when four modules are included:
the Hydrodynamic Module (HD), the Spectral Wave Module (SW), the Sand Transport
Module (SW) and the Shoreline Morphology Module. The MIKE21 Shoreline Model is
used to formulate the long term morphological model in order to replicate the hydrody-
namics, sediment transport and shoreline morphology corresponding to actual conditions.
The waves are deﬁned at the oﬀshore boundary. The current is driven by wave radiation
stresses where the input comes from the SW simulation. Sediment transport signal is
inﬂuenced by the wave ﬁeld from the SW simulation where the wave ﬁeld changes are
based on water level variation from the HD simulation. The simulation results from each
module are coupled with the Shoreline Morphology Module to update morphological bed.
The hydrodynamic ﬂow ﬁeld and wave ﬁeld is continuously updated according to the
change in bathymetry until the end of simulation.
3.2
Hydrodynamics
The resulting ﬂow and water level variation subjected to a variety of forcing and bound-
ary conditions are simulated using the Hydrodynamic Module in the MIKE21 Shoreline
Model.
The Hydrodynamic Module is able to include eﬀects of ﬂooding and drying,
momentum dispersion, bottom shear stress, Coriolis force, wind shear stress, Barometric
pressure gradients, ice coverage, tidal potential, precipitation/evaporation, wave radiation
stresses, sources and sinks. The solution of the model is obtained by solving the local
continuity equation and the horizontal momentum equations.

4.1
Introduction
The long term morphological model shall be formulated in order to represent hydrody-
namics, sediment transport and long term shoreline evolution corresponding to actual
conditions. Model calibration is performed using measured data to have a better under-
standing of the hydrodynamics at the study site. Simple model setup is used to calibrate
model parameters prior to long term morphological model simulation in order to limit the
margin of uncertainties. The model calibration result indicates that tide-driven current
has important inﬂuence, hence tidal information should be included in the model. As the
long term morphological simulation under tidal action is prohibitively expensive due to
time consuming computation, it is essential to reduce computation cost. The selection of
morphological tide and schematic wave for the long term morphological model is discussed
in the following sections. The long term morphological model setup applied in this study
is described in Chapter 4.7.

Bed resistance
Bed resistance is one of the primary calibration parameter in the MIKE21 FM Shoreline
Model. It is essential to calibrate the model in order to replicate sediment transport and
wave attenuation process in the project area.
Bed resistance in Littoral Processes FM
It is observed from the historical Google earth images that accretion occurred on both
Thai coast and Malaysian coast. The deposition volume is calculated and is compared
with the annual drift deposition as discussed in Chapter 2.7. The annual drift along the
shoreline is simulated using Littoral Drift Model in the Littoral Processes FM. The nom-
inal grain size, d50 of 0.15mm is used in the simulation for both coast. It is noted that
bed roughness of 0.01m is used for the Thai coast and 0.006m for the Malaysian coast in
order to obtain comparable accreted volume.
Bed resistance in Hydrodynamic module
In the Hydrodynamic Module, bed resistance is deﬁned in terms of Manning number. The
eﬀect of bed resistance in terms of Manning number is investigated and the results are
illustrated in Figure 4.5. The results are compared with current measurement at location
CM2 (near the Malaysian coast) and at location CM3 (near the Thai coast). Observations
from both analysis show that it is infeasible to represent bed resistance using a single value
for Manning number in the whole study site. Hence, a Manning map is used in the mor-
phological model setup (see Figure 4.6). The Manning number 35 and 40 corresponds to
bed roughness of 0.006m and 0.01m respectively. The Manning number 15 is introduced
at the boundary of the domain for high resistance in order to avoid stability problems
(blow-ups) at the boundary as suggested in DHI (2015b).
Bed resistance in Spectral wave module
In the Spectral Wave Module, bed resistance is deﬁned in terms of sand grain size (d50).
A constant nominal grain size, d50 of 0.15mm is used in the coastal area, which is in
agreement with the grain size used for the Littoral Processes FM. In order to increase
stability of the model, a constant nominal grain size, d50 of 0.23mm is used in the river
area. The sand grain size input is presented in the sand grain size map (see Figure 4.7)
and it is used in the morphological model setup.

4.5
Morphological tide
Long term morphological simulation under tidal action is found to be prohibitively expen-
sive due to time consuming computation. Hence, it is necessary to reduce the simulation
of actual tide conditions by selecting a representative morphological tide. In this study,
the technique for long term morphological simulation under tidal action suggested by Lat-
teux, 1995 is adopted. Referring to Latteux (1995), the representative tide that provides
the most accurate transport rates normally ranges between 7% to 20% higher than the
mean tide range.
Due to lack of data for long term tidal variation, the tide level extracted from the pre-
diction based on global tide model data is used. A simple model using schematic wave
and the predicted tide as boundary conditions is simulated for 7 days where the measured
data is available. The model results simulated under Tide condition are compared to the
water level and current measurements as illustrated in Figure 4.8. This is to check if the
predicted tide level can be used in the study site. It is observed that the model results
are comparable to the measurements. Therefore, it is reasonable to refer Tide condition
to select a morphological tide.
Next, the choice of morphological tide for the study site is tested using tide which is
20% lower than (MorptideV1), 15% higher than (MorptideV2) and equal to the mean
tide range (MorptideV3). The choices were selected from a single point (Point 11) at
the oﬀshore boundary (boundary Code 5, see Figure 4.1). The duration to complete one
morphological cycle for MorptideV1, MorptideV2 and MorptideV3 is 23, 18 and 21 hours
respectively as shown in Figure 4.9. The morphological tides of the corresponding time
are extracted along the boundaries and are applied as boundary conditions (varying in
time and along boundary) in the Littoral Processes FM.
The corresponding sediment discharge and comparisons are as tabulated in Table 4.2. It
is noted that the sediment transport signal is best described by MorptideV3 when the
tide is equal to the mean tide range. Based on these comparisons, MorptideV3 is selected
as morphological tide of the study site.

4.6
Wave analysis
Littoral drift on the Thai coast and the Malaysian coast are simulated using the wave
data P4, P5 and P6 as illustrated in Figure 4.10. When wave data P4 and P5 is used in
the simulation, comparable drift is obtained in both the Thai coast and the Malaysian
coast; where the simulation using wave data P6 underestimate the littoral drift. Thus,
wave data P5 is selected in this study.
In order to reduce the simulation of actual wave climate, a statistic analysis has been
undertaken. The wave data is classiﬁed by signiﬁcant wave height, mean wave direction
and peak wave period. The schematic wave climate is deﬁned by combining the duration
of each wave class with its associated mean wave height, mean peak wave period and the
energy weighted mean wave direction. The wave rose for wave P5 and schematic wave is
shown in Figure 4.11. The eﬀect of schematic wave is tested in Litdrift and it is observed
that both littoral drift is comparable as shown in Figure 4.12. Therefore, it is feasible to
use schematic wave to obtain comparable littoral drift in the study site.
The eﬀect of number of sea-states is further tested for the ﬁrst 20, 30, 35 and 40 sea-states
sorted according to sediment transport contribution. The results are shown in Figure 4.13.
It is noticed that the 30, 35 and 40 sea-states simulation has achieved at least 90% gross
littoral drift and almost identical net littoral drift compared with schematic wave including
all sea-states.
In addition, the saving in simulation time steps for using the schematic wave climate
is considered. It is noted that a real time wave climate requires 20 years × 365 days
× 24 hours = 175,200 time steps to represent 20 years simulation. For the schematic
wave climates, the required time steps is calculated by: number of sea-states per year ×
number of hours per cycle of morphological tide × 20 years in order to represent 20 years
simulation. Hence, schematic wave climate with all sea-states, 40 sea-states, 35 sea-states
and 30 sea-states required 148,260 time steps, 16,800 time steps, 14,700 time steps and
12,600 time steps respectively to represent 20 years simulation. By selecting 30 sea-states,
there is a speed up gained approximately 93% in terms of time steps saving compared to
the real time wave climate. This will signiﬁcantly reduce the computation cost. Thus, it
is sensible to use 30 sea-states of the schematic wave in the morphological model.

Long term morphological model setup
The long term morphological model is formulated in order to represent hydrodynamics,
sediment transport and long term shoreline evolution corresponding to actual conditions.
The MIKE21 FM Shoreline Model is used for long term morphological modelling in this
study.
In the Shoreline Model, the hydrodynamics including current ﬁelds and wave
ﬁelds are described and calculated in the Hydrodynamic Module and the Spectral Waves
Module; where the sediment transport ﬁelds are determined based on the local hydro-
dynamic quantities in the Sand Transport Module. The sediment transport information
is converted into shoreline response using one-line equation and the shoreline movement
is updated in the Shoreline Morphology Module. The concept of Shoreline Model is as
discussed by Kristensen et al. (2010) and Kaergaard et al. (2014).
In this study, the morphological tide is selected to represent the actual tidal action and
annual wave climate is represented by 30 sea-states as discussed in previous sections. Each
sea-state is applied along the oﬀshore boundary for the duration of a single cycle of the
morphological tide (21 hours). The frequency of occurrence of the sea-state is represented
by use of a morphological speed up factor deﬁned by:
Speed up factor ≈4 ×
Duration of occurrence of a sea-state

where the multiplier 4 is included in the speed up factor to obtain comparable littoral
drift to the sediment budget as discussed in Chapter 2.7. In this study, the speed up
factor vary between 1.03 to 161.58 for the 30 sea-states climate.

has reﬂected by signiﬁcant erosion between Groyne No. 2 and the Golok breakwater.
The eﬀect of the groyne ﬁeld is observed through the increase of net littoral drift with
the absence of groynes. The results show that the coastline becomes dynamic near the
Thai breakwater and have a tendency for continuous erosion without the groynes. This
is due to removal of littoral drift blocking eﬀect resulting from the removal of groynes,
which will eventually increase littoral drift to the downdrift of the coast. For the existing
condition, erosion can be controlled and improved by minor modiﬁcations to increase the
stability of the coastline.

Chapter 6
Conclusion
In this study, long term morphological model is developed and simulated to understand
the hydrodynamics, sediment transport processes and shoreline evolution for the Golok
Beach. From the model calibration, it is noted that tide-driven current has important
inﬂuence in the study site. Besides, the waves are important to be included in the morpho-
logical model as the study site is exposed to the tropical monsoon climate. Hence, both
tide and wave information are included in the model. However, long term morphological
simulation under tidal action is prohibitively expensive due to time consuming computa-
tion. To reduce computation cost, morphological tide and schematic wave for the long
term morphological model are selected. The contribution of wave occurrence and mor-
phological tide is reﬂected by using the speed up map in the morphological model, where
the sediment transport is magniﬁed to the ratio of wave occurrence and morphological
tide. The morphological bed level is updated by the edge map.
The long term shoreline morphology is simulated for the existing condition, the no-groyne
case and the beach retreat case. The results are compared with the existing condition
to identify the eﬀect of the groyne ﬁeld on the Thai coast and the beach eﬀect on the
Malaysian coast. It is understood that the shoreline movement is directly related to the
gradient of net littoral drift where positive and negative gradient indicate erosion and
accretion respectively.
The existing condition
For the existing condition, negative gradient of the net littoral drift is observed for the
Malaysian coast which indicates accretion on the coast. The average littoral drift over
20 years simulation is approximately 70,000m3 into the domain and 15,000m3 out of the
domain. Hence, the sediment deposited on the Malaysian coast is approximately 90,000m3
67
==PAGE:82==
Chapter 6: Conclusion
68
(corrected for porosity) which in agreement with the annual deposition calculated from
the historical Google earth images. For the Thai coast, the net littoral drift into the
domain is found to be equivalent to the net littoral drift out of the domain. Hence, the
Thai coast is stable.
From the simulation result, the average sediment discharge from the river mouth is found
to be approximately 12,000m3/year. It is noted that this sediment supply from the river
is insuﬃcient to maintain the equilibrium of sediment budget in the study site. Hence, 2D
morphological model is proposed to be included within the river mouth area to improve
the solution of the simulation. The proposal includes 1D shoreline model that integrates
the inputs from the 2D morphological model to simulate hydrodynamics signal, longshore
sediment transport and update shoreline movements. The hydrodynamics and sediment
transport signal are observed to have signiﬁcant diﬀerence when diﬀerent discharge con-
dition and bathymetry level is applied. The accumulated sediment discharge at the river
mouth is observed to be inﬂuenced by the river discharge and the bathymetry. Hence, it is
understood that the solution can be improved by simulating 1D shoreline model with 2D
morphological model in the river mouth area, provided a better description of the river
discharge and the bathymetry of the river.
The eﬀect of groyne ﬁeld
The eﬀect of groyne ﬁeld is investigated by removing 19 groynes constructed between
the Golok breakwater and the Tak Bai breakwater from the existing condition. For 20
years simulation, the net littoral drift out of the domain is found to be higher than the
net littoral drift into the domain. This indicates that the overall coast is unstable and
has a tendency for erosion in particular in the eastern end of the groyne ﬁeld near the
Thai breakwater. Local erosion and accretion can be observed along the Thai coast after
removal of the groynes. This is due to the littoral drift blocking eﬀect is removed after
removal of the groynes. Thus, the increased or decreased of net littoral drift at any section
along the Thai coast is likely due to shifting of sediments from the updrift direction. The
simulation results show that the coastline become unstable with the absence of groynes.
Hence, the groyne ﬁeld is important to stabilise the coast.
The eﬀect of beach retreat
The eﬀect of beach retreat is investigated by removing the deposited sediment near the
Malaysian breakwater. The intention of removing the deposited sediment is to modify
the littoral drift along the coast by straightening the coastline. The beach retreat is fully
recovered after 12 years where the coastline is moved back to the initial coastline position.
==PAGE:83==
Chapter 6: Conclusion
69
It is observed that the beach retreat after recovery still beneﬁts in terms of slowing down
the accretion on the Malaysian coast compared to the existing condition. For the existing
condition and the beach retreat case, it is expected that the Malaysian coast will have con-
tinuous accretion due to the equilibrium in littoral drift along the coast is not obtained.
However, the beach retreat is observed to delay the accretion on the Malaysian coast
compared to the exiting condition. Besides, it is noted that the beach retreat decreased
sediment transport bypassing the breakwater head and hence slow down accretion in the
navigation channel. This beneﬁts in terms of saving for navigation channel dredging works.
Recommendations for future work
The long term morphological modelling using the MIKE21 Shoreline Model is capable to
simulate the hydrodynamics, sediment transport and shoreline evolution corresponding
to actual conditions. The simulation solution can be improved by simulating 1D shoreline
model with 2D morphological model in the river mouth area. Better and more accurate
solution can be obtained by providing more detailed inputs including bathymetry in the
river and in the domain area, cross-shore proﬁle, river discharge variation and etc.

D.2.1
Certain changes in Shoreline morphology module
Certain changes are made in the Shoreline morphology module to obtain better and more
accurate solution:
Type of boundary condition
Convergence problem is noted at boundary of the morphological model. Hence, the type
of boundary condition for the one-line model is increased from default value (1) to 5 in
order to avoid misleading solution at the boundary.
Option for oﬀshore transport
There is option to include oﬀshore transport in the Shoreline morphology model. When
oﬀshore transport is included, the sediment transport out of the shoreline morphology
domain is allowed to pass through and reinsert into the domain when the edge map is
available. A simpliﬁed model is tested for the eﬀect of negative elements in the edge map
as tabulated in Table D.1. The edge map for Test 3A and 3B includes negative elements
at the boundary, where the edge map for Test 3C and 3D excludes negative elements as
shown in Figure D.1. Speed up map as shown in Figure D.2 is applied for all test cases.
Constant wave condition (Hs = 2m; Tp = 10s; Mean wave direction = 30o; Directional
spreading index, n = 23.93) is applied for all test cases. The option to include oﬀshore
transport is activated for Test 3A and 3C.
