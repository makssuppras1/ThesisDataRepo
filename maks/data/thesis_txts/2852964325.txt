Abstract
This thesis presents a post-occupancy evaluation (POE) of academic and research
laboratory facilities at the Technical University of Denmark (DTU), with a focus on the
histology lab in Building 202. Using a post-occupancy evaluation (POE) approach, the study
examines feedback from lab users, project managers, and architects to assess both the
successes and limitations of the lab’s design. Data were collected through walkthroughs,
interviews, and thematic analysis, highlighting areas where the design meets functional
needs as well as areas needing improvement, particularly in furniture adequacy and lighting
design. The findings emphasize POE’s role as a valuable tool for enhancing user
satisfaction, yet it remains underutilized in laboratory settings. As a feedback mechanism,
POE can significantly inform future design practices and update user involvement criteria by
ensuring that spaces are better aligned with the real-world needs of their occupants. This
research demonstrates how POE contributes to creating academic spaces that are
sustainable, adaptable, and more effective for those who use them daily.

Introduction
Academic and research institutions aim to create dynamic and sustainable environments
that facilitate knowledge acquisition through learning, teaching, and research. High-quality
facilities are essential to achieving these goals, as comfortable and well-performing
environments directly contribute to teaching effectiveness and research productivity
(Sanni-Anibire and Hassanain, 2016). A critical tool for maintaining and enhancing facility
quality is Post-Occupancy Evaluation (POE), a structured approach for assessing building
performance and verifying that a space effectively meets user needs after occupation
(Preiser et al., 2015). POE provides feedback that is valuable for continuous improvement,
helping to optimize building functionality and user satisfaction across various facility types,
including academic and research labs.
Despite the importance of POE, its application in laboratory environments has lagged
compared to other educational spaces, even though laboratories are integral to hands-on
scientific research and education (Ma and Nickerson, 2006). Laboratories, especially those
in academic institutions, must support the complex technical requirements of students,
faculty, and researchers while promoting user well-being, safety, and efficiency. Studies
indicate that factors such as spatial configuration, lighting, and thermal comfort significantly
impact laboratory functionality and user satisfaction (Schneider, 2002; Ruohomäki et al.,
2014). However, specific POE studies on laboratory settings remain limited, leaving gaps in
understanding how these spaces can best serve their users.
This thesis conducts a POE of the histological laboratory at the Technical University of
Denmark (DTU), located in Building 202, to evaluate how well the design meets users'
needs. The POE focuses on aspects such as ergonomics, environmental conditions, and
equipment layout, seeking insights from lab technicians, researchers, and facility managers.
By examining user experiences, this research aims to identify design improvements that can
enhance future projects, supporting DTU's goal of providing adaptable, efficient, and
sustainable academic environments.

1.1
Problem framing
In recent years, the importance of user-centered design in academic and research
environments has gained significant attention, particularly in the context of highly specialized
facilities such as laboratories. A well-designed lab should meet the functional, ergonomic,
and environmental needs of its users while supporting the technical requirements of its
intended operations. Despite the inclusion of user involvement processes during the design
phase of Building 202 at the Technical University of Denmark (DTU), where the histology lab
is housed, it remains unclear whether the current design fully supports the needs and
expectations of the end-users.
This study employs a Post-Occupancy Evaluation (POE) to systematically assess the
degree of user involvement both before and after occupancy and to determine how
effectively the current design of the histology lab meets the demands of lab technicians,
research assistants, and postdoctoral researchers. Initial observations suggest that lab users
were not directly consulted during the early design stages, raising potential concerns about
mismatches between design goals and real-world functionality.
This research seeks to identify and analyze these potential gaps by conducting a POE of the
histology lab, with the goal of enhancing the overall building design process for future
projects. Through a combination of walk-through observations, interviews with key
stakeholders (including project managers, one of the building’s architect and end-users), and
thematic analysis, the study aims to provide actionable insights into how well the lab meets
the needs of its users and what improvements could be made to better align with user needs
and operational effectiveness.

Background
2.1
Post-Occupancy Evaluation Analysis
Exploring Post-Occupancy Evaluation (POE) is essential to understand how well buildings
meet the needs of their users, especially in academic and research settings like DTU. While
POE has gained popularity the recent years, there’s relatively little research focused on its
application within DTU's own labs and learning spaces. This section dives into what POE
means, its history, and the key benefits it offers. By evaluating how spaces perform for those
who use them daily, POE provides valuable insights that can guide future designs, making
facilities more efficient, comfortable, and supportive of DTU’s academic mission.
2.1.1
Definition of Post-Occupancy Evaluation
Admittedly, one of the key features of Post-Occupancy Evaluation (POE) is mutability.
Therefore, it makes sense that several definitions of the term would occur. One of the most
well-known examples is the US Federal Facilities Council’s definition: “Post-Occupancy
Evaluation ... is a process of systematically evaluating the performance of buildings after
they have been built and occupied for some time” (Preiser, 2002, p. 42). Preiser emphasizes
the importance of establishing a more specific definition of the concept, as he perceives it as
a “process of systematic data collection, analysis, and comparison with explicitly stated
performance criteria about occupied built environments” (Preiser et al., 1988).
Furthermore, Friedmann introduces an anthropological aspect when defining the term, as it
states that POE is “an appraisal of the degree to which a designed setting satisfies and
supports explicit and implicitly human needs and values of those for whom a building is
designed” (Friedman et al., 1978, p. 20). The anthropological dimension of Friedman’s
definition has its roots in the works of Zimring and Reizenstein, who emphasized the
“examinations of the effectiveness for human users of occupied design environments”
(Zimring and Reizenstein, 1980).
Regarding architecture, it is useful to delve deeper into the Royal Institute of British
Architects’ definition: “Post-Occupancy Evaluation is the systematic study of buildings in use
to provide architects with information about the performance of their designs and building
owners and users with guidelines to achieve the best out of what they already have” (RIBA,
1991, p. 191). One would argue that the central aspects of this definition are on the same
page with Preiser’s approach as both definitions perceive POE as a “diagnostic” tool in
building performance evaluation.
While the early endeavors regarding POE were initiated in the 1970s and the 1980s, it
becomes clear that terms like Building Performance Evaluation (BPE) and Universal Design
Evaluation (UDE) contributed to the evolution of POE, demonstrating that this procedure
should be characterized by a “more holistic and process-oriented evaluation’’ (Preiser, 2002,
11
==PAGE:13==
p. 9). This turn placed more emphasis on the non-technical aspects that should be taken into
account when it comes to designing and building new facilities.
From a critical perspective, it seems that these definitions of POE do not set a clear and
widely accepted framework regarding the understanding of the term. Similarly, these
approaches do not clearly define the standards that the industry needs to follow.
Nevertheless, the aforementioned definitions contribute to the establishment of the
fundamental understanding of POE as a holistic procedure of assessing a building based on
a comparative analysis of technological and anthropological insights. In addition, it should be
considered a systematic process that is guided by research outcomes and is focused on
user needs, optimizing building performance and facility management.
Undoubtedly, it would be easier for someone to clarify the mutability aspect of the POE’s
definition if they paid attention to the dynamic and complex relationship that humans have
with their built environment. As far as research is concerned, apart from architecture,
insights from several disciplines like (behavioral) psychology and sociology should be
leveraged. All research initiatives must recognize this need for adopting multidisciplinary
insights that range from technological and mechanical approaches and psychological and
sociological concerns. In conclusion, this would be a scientifically correct approach to define
a term that blends multiple elements.
2.1.2
History of Post-Occupancy Evaluation
As mentioned above, Post-Occupancy Evaluation (POE) is defined as a systematic process
for evaluating buildings after a certain amount of time during their occupancy. POE is a
framework that showcases the degree to which a building satisfies user needs and how the
design of the building can be reconceptualized based on future needs. Although many
greatly support the positive impacts POE can have today, historically, the term has
experienced ups and downs.
After World War II, the need for rapid construction led to the emergence of an early version
of POE. According to Eke, Clinton, and Wellington (2013), user needs were seriously taken
into account during the construction of new buildings. Significant POE research took place in
the US during the 1960s, establishing the foundations of the term. The basic goal was to
identify user needs in hospitals, college dormitories, and public administration buildings.
However, the next decade played a crucial role in developing new methodologies in POE.
Evaluations focused on user satisfaction through interviews, surveys, and other methods,
leading to design guides for various building types like schools and offices. By the end of the
1970s, the first book dedicated to Post-Occupancy Evaluation was published (Akman, 2002).
Early POE approaches emphasized user needs and satisfaction. A few years later, a more
holistic framework was established, examining how organizational and physical aspects of
the work environment impact occupant behavior. The term "well-being" was introduced and
became part of POE research, leading to a more comprehensive definition of POE.
12
==PAGE:14==
During the 1990s, POE further developed. Several well-known US companies started
implementing POE practices in their design and building processes, with diversity emerging
as a key aspect. POE frameworks were enriched with considerations of health effects,
financial costs, and aesthetics, apart from user satisfaction (Akman, 2022).
In contrast, POE faced several obstacles in the UK. According to Ian Cooper, there was
limited interest in including these principles in architectural education. David Canter claimed
that the influence of environmental psychologists' research had a negative impact on POE
progress (Canter, 1984). In the early 1990s, POE was removed from the curricula of several
architectural schools, as it was seen as mostly connected with other disciplines (Cooper,
2001).
Nonetheless, the Egan Report of 1998 highlighted the need for improved construction quality
and efficiency, sparking renewed interest in POE in the UK (Macmillan, 2003; Cooper, 2001).
The report demonstrated that the construction industry did not emphasize user needs or
develop a sufficient and systematic procedure for evaluating client satisfaction. Discussions
about user needs led to initiatives like the Movement for Innovation, which developed a more
modern and comprehensive framework for POE (Cooper, 2001).
In recent years, there has been a systematic attempt to combine energy efficiency, user
comfort, and engineering. Tools like PROBE (Post-occupancy Review of Buildings and their
Engineering) have played a crucial role in establishing a new framework for POE, aiming for
a comparative analysis of user satisfaction, environmental conditions, and energy
consumption (Akman, 2002). Analyzing these data paved the way for formulating new
principles regarding facility management and improving building performance.
Ultimately, the history of POE is characterized by various ups and downs. It is clear that POE
had to overcome initial obstacles and challenges for experts to recognize its positive impact.
However, POE is constantly evolving, and its growing recognition worldwide signifies a
promising future in architecture and engineering.

2.1.3
Purpose & Benefits of Post-Occupancy Evaluation
As mentioned before, it has been observed that, in recent years, POE has garnered
attention among academic circles. However, the discussions about POE are not
accompanied by a systematic evaluation process that could be established as a fundamental
aspect of the reconceptualization of the design and construction industries. Therefore, it is
quite important for current studies to establish a systematic framework that clarifies the goals
and the values of POE.
The fact that several alternative interpretations of POE have been developed over the years
sheds light on the necessity of conducting a POE. Nonetheless, studies like those of
Zimmerman and Martin (2001) propose that, although several approaches have been
developed in terms of defining the goals and the fundamental reasons behind conducting a
POE, there is a common outcome: POE provides invaluable information to support the
continuous improvement of the design processes of future buildings (Zimmerman and
Martin, 2001, p. 169).
In addition, Whyte and Gann (2001) define some of the most common benefits of POE that
are reflected in most interpretations:
●
applying design skills more effectively
●
improving commissioning processes
●
reconceptualizing user requirements
●
enhancing and optimizing management processes
●
providing knowledge for design guides and regulatory processes
●
targeting of refurbishment (Whyte and Gann, 2001)
In a general context, it turns out that POE is an effective method for gathering and
disseminating insights that could be leveraged from various stakeholders that are involved in
the life cycle of a building. Moreover, certain insights are also useful to certain stakeholders,
in different ways. For example, as Preiser proposes, some aspects of POE are especially
useful for facilities managers (Preiser, 1995, 2002).
In a primary context, many researchers agree there should be a fundamental shift in POE
studies in order to appreciate and validate their benefits (Green and Moss, 1998,
Zimmerman and Martin, 2001, Leaman and Bordass, 2005, Roberts, 2001). More
specifically, there should be a shift in the perception of building procurement and usage.
Zeisel suggested that the design phase should be cyclical, and not linear, meaning that it is
not a one time task that is done at the beginning and the end of a project, which is a
procedure that is very common among design and construction industries in the UK (1981).
This shift in perception requires an accrued database of pre-existing knowledge, which is
continually fed by information garnered from specific successes, and failures of particular
building types and configurations, using the POE process.
Zimmerman and Martin (2001) propose that POE should be considered a “rational final step”
in the cyclical process, which provides insights that act as lessons that derive from the
feedback of previous users in completed projects. These findings could be leveraged not
14
==PAGE:16==
only to improve the spaces in current buildings, but also to define a framework for future
projects (Zimmerman and Martin, 2001, p. 169).
Another similar practice regarding the creation of beneficial circles of improvement was
supported by Leaman and Bordass (2001). In this case, POE is implemented as a strategy
throughout the design cycle, aiming to create a dynamic knowledge, which constantly
changes with new contributions. This change leads to continuous improvement while a
project is still in progress, instead of simply documenting a final feedback in the occupancy
phase (Leaman and Bordass, 2001, p. 151).
Furthermore, the contribution of Green and Moss (1998) provides more nuances to this
cyclical theme. Green and Moss propose that organizations should be responsible for
establishing learning cycles, especially in the context of facilities management. In this case,
“the design of the workplace must be viewed not as a finite process which ends on
completion of a new building, but as an ongoing process which extends to the upgrading and
refurbishment of buildings in occupation” (Green and Moss, 1998, p. 36). Another notable
research is that of Zimring (2001), which suggests that POE and organizational learning
should be similarly interconnected. In this context, the main goal of POE is to continuously
update the information regarding the requirements of the users and performance within the
building, which could play an important role in changing or reconceptualizing business
practices (Green and Moss, 1998, p. 37).
Taking the importance of the users into account is helpful in terms of improving building
procurement through conducting well-targeted POS processes. This is heavily based on the
perspective that a building is essentially a means to an end (Leaman and Bordass, 2001).
This perspective is based on observing the fundamental aspect of designing a building,
which could be misunderstood either by clients, or by designers as well (Macmillan, 2003).
Leaman and Bordass (2001) propose a reconceptualization of the traditional understanding
of the essence of a building. The procurement processes are based on the building as a
physical object, which is in contrast with the actual purpose of the building.
Leaman and Bordass’ contribution showcases the social, cultural, economic and
environmental benefits that could improve health, safety and comfort (Leaman and Bordass,
2001, p. 151). The behavioral aspect should be considered a fundamental criterion
throughout the stages of a buildings’ life cycle. In that sense, buildings could be established
as entities that encourage a wide range of human interactions. Therefore, “proposals for new
buildings and refurbishments need to stand up to scrutiny as realistic and practical means
and not as ends in themselves” (Leaman and Bordass, 2001, p. 151). In that sense, POE is
perceived as a tool that examines the human interaction in a certain building.
Hence, POE provides the organizations a framework that helps them focus on ensuring that
they provide the means (buildings) that encourages their most valuable asset, their
employees, to work more effectively. Despite the fact that the design and construction
industry is not quite familiar with the benefits that POE processes provide, some high profile
organizations started showing interest in leveraging POE insights either in the design phase,
or in a post-occupancy context (Rouse 2003, Macmillan 2003).
15
==PAGE:17==
Regarding the analysis of the benefits of POE, it is crucial to identify these potential benefits
by their relation to time. First of all, examining the short-term benefits of POE is a useful
starting point. In comparison to other theories, POE provides a better framework for the
identification of problems in facilities, as it solely focuses on user feedback. Additionally,
another short form benefit is that POE could encourage a more proactive approach in facility
management, which could be focused on being responsive to user values.
Gaining feedback on building performance is also an effective method of instantly improving
space utilization. At the same time, reversing the logic of POE is helpful in the process of
identifying that the behavior of building occupants is improved through active involvement in
the evaluation process. From a behavioral perspective, POE can make the users perceive
the building as an intimate setting and that their presence there matters, as they are the
ones that make the building useful for the society. Another short benefit of conducting POEs
is identified in cases where the designers are able to identify the consequences of their
design.
In addition, it is crucial to examine the medium-term benefits of POE. On a fundamental
level, POE offers the opportunity to conceptualize the “recycling”of facilities into new ones.
Consequently, facilities management can easily adapt organizational change and growth
over time. As a second medium-term benefit, it is useful to highlight the cost-saving aspect
of conducting POEs. Reducing the cost could be beneficial either in the building process or
throughout the life cycle of the building. POE could also reinforce accountability, which is a
crucial element for design professionals, owners, and other stakeholders. Nonetheless, it is
also beneficial to place emphasis on the long-term benefits.
Conducting POE on a regular basis could offer long-term improvements in building
performance. POE offers the opportunity of creating and maintaining design databases,
establishing effective standards, and benefiting from updated literature and cutting-edge
technologies. In conclusion, apart from the qualitative insights that POE can provide, it
becomes clear that current and future projects could benefit from the quantification of the
accumulated insights, and this could reconceptualize the design and building processes.
Admittedly, the benefits of POE encompass several aspects of a building’s life cycle.
However, it is useful to highlight how POE can effectively transform the industry itself.
Although most designers are not paid to review the outcome of a finished building, a great
percentage of architects and designers recognize how and why conducting POE could be
beneficial for their future projects. As mentioned earlier, POE is responsible for assessing
the functionality of a certain design based on its initial purposes.
POE gives the architects the chance to provide solutions for problems that arise, after they
thoroughly examined and identified areas that need improvement. These problematic
aspects of a particular design can be studied and turned into advantages in the design
phases of future projects. POE can reduce costs for the clients as it is easier to detect where
the invested money did not produce a return (Zimmerman & Martin 2001). Riley et al. (2010)
showcased similar benefits to POE, placing emphasis on how POE could enhance and
reconceptualize new ideas for future designs. Therefore, apart from the benefits for the
assessment of current buildings and the design of future projects, it becomes clear that POE
16
==PAGE:18==
can be beneficial both for clients and designers, which can change the dynamics of the
industry (Zimmerman & Martin 2001).
In conclusion, examining numerous sources provides useful insights regarding the numerous
goals and benefits of conducting POE. Most of these research findings meet up to a certain
point: the primary benefit of POE is the utilization of all gathered information in the process
of improving the procurement of buildings to the benefit of all the involved stakeholders.
2.1.4
Post-Occupancy Evaluation Methods and Techniques
As it has been highlighted in the previous sections of this thesis, POE is a structured
approach for evaluating building performance from the perspective of its users after
occupation has taken place. POE assesses buildings based on the needs of the occupants,
and the main focus is the examination of factors like health, safety, functionality, comfort, and
satisfaction (Zimring & Reizenstein, 1980). The main goal is to verify if the design objectives
are being met and to gather data for future improvements (Preiser, 1995). On a fundamental
level, POE is specifically focused on examining user satisfaction and building functionality to
refine design and operation approaches over time (Cohen et al., 2001).
The POE framework provides the opportunity to mainly address three main lines of
investigation. Focusing on the technical performance of the building includes assessing
environmental conditions such as thermal comfort, acoustics, air quality, and fire safety,
which are crucial when it comes to user well-being and effective building operation (McGrath
& Horton, 2011).
Furthermore, another line that is crucial in all POE approaches is functionality. Evaluating
the functionality of the building based on meeting its initial efficiency goals can provide
valuable insights that could be leveraged in the improvement of the current building or in
future projects. Focusing on aspects like space management and facilities accessibility is
helpful in the process of determining whether the building meets its intended goals (Zhang &
Barrett, 2010).
A third line that provides a more fulfilled framework regarding the efficiency of a certain
building is behavioral performance. This line includes the evaluation of the interaction
between end-users and their environment, placing emphasis on social, cultural, and
aesthetic factors that influence user satisfaction and comfort (Preiser et al., 1988).
Admittedly, these three lines of investigation, particularly the technical and functional
aspects, are essential in determining a building’s overall effectiveness. Nonetheless, it is
also useful to focus on how different approaches on POE could help in highlighting the
importance and the numerous potential benefits of POE. In this endeavor, the secondary
goal is to showcase how many different aspects could be investigated through the lens of
POE. Preiser (1995) identifies three levels of POE, which could be leveraged in different
contexts based on the assessment needs:
17
==PAGE:19==
-
Indicative POE includes brief analyses that are characterized by “quick walkthrough
evaluations involving structured interviews with key personnel, group meetings with
end users as well as inspections” (Preiser, 1995, p. 21).
-
Investigative POE leverages in-depth analyses that include interviews and
questionnaires that assess a number of buildings of the same or similar type.
-
Diagnostic POE is widely considered a more sophisticated approach that employs a
broad range of technological and anthropological insights. Preiser proposes that the
in-depth nature of Diagnostic POE produces “high validity and generalisability of data
collected... (that has) the potential of being transformed into guidelines” for use in the
public realm (Preiser, 1995, p. 53).
Additionally, it is helpful to delve deeper into feedback techniques. Bordass and Leaman
(2005) categorize POE feedback techniques into five distinct approaches. Firstly, “audit”
involves quantitative evaluations, such as the CIBSE TM22 energy assessment, which
gathers measurable data regarding building performance. The second approach,
“discussion”, leverages qualitative methods like workshops and interviews that provide
foresight, insight, and hindsight perspectives on the usage of the building.
Furthermore, “questionnaire”, includes tools like the BUS occupant survey and CIC design
quality indicators to gather user feedback on satisfaction and comfort (Whyte & Gann, 2003;
Leaman, 2004). The fourth approach, “process”, incorporates feedback into building
management frameworks, exemplified by “Soft Landings,” which supports continuous
monitoring and adjustment (Bordass & Leaman, 2005). The fifth approach, “packages”,
consists of hybrid methods that combine multiple feedback mechanisms, such as the Probe
method, incorporating both qualitative and quantitative data collection (Bordass & Leaman,
2005, p. 350). These techniques allow POE practitioners to choose the most suitable
methods for gathering actionable data.
A range of specific POE tools has been developed to assess various building performance
aspects:
-
Design Quality Indicators (DQI) is a method that evaluates a building’s functionality,
quality, and impact, improving building quality through a 100-question structured
survey (Preiser et al., 1988).
-
The Overall Liking Score (OLS), developed by UMIST, gauges user satisfaction with
key environmental factors like lighting and ventilation in workplace settings (Leaman,
2004).
-
Building Use Studies (BUS) Occupant Survey is a rapid assessment tool for
understanding occupant satisfaction and benchmarking various building types

(Cohen et al., 2001).

2.1.5
Barriers to Post-Occupancy Evaluation
Although the purpose and the benefits of POE were thoroughly discussed in the previous
sections, it is also useful to analyze the barriers that make the ubiquitous adoption of POE
difficult. After all, if there are so many potential benefits why do companies and organizations
avoid the implementation of POE processes? Several literary sources confirm that there are
certain reasons why POEs are not carried out on a regular basis. Vischer (2001, p. 23)
perceives cost, time and lack of skills as the main barriers to the widespread adoption of
POE.
At the same time, Zimmerman and Martin (2001, p. 168) attempt to delve deeper into the
possible barriers: “standard practice in the facility delivery process does not recognise the
concept of continual improvement or, indeed, any ongoing involvement on the part of the
designers” (Zimmerman and Martin, 2001, p. 171).
As mentioned in a previous section, the fact that the designers are not getting paid for going
back to the building and investigating potential functionality issues through gathering
feedback from end-users does not allow them to have a clear picture of their design
decisions (Zimmerman and Martin, 2001, p. 171). Also, data collection is an expensive
process and in most cases POE is limited by resources available (Pati & Pati 2013).
Another important barrier to POE is its absence from the education curriculum of engineering
and design schools. Cooper (2001, p. 160) thoroughly analyzes how POE has been
excluded from most curriculums in architecture schools in the UK by the early 90’s. Another
barrier showcased by Cooper is that “client organizations are unlikely to pay for POE unless
the benefits of such evaluations are both evident and substantial in value” (Cooper, 2001, p.
159).
Therefore, it becomes clear that organizations are not convinced regarding the quantifiable
value of POE, which is one of the major challenges that POE proponents should overcome.
Furthermore, Bordass et al. (2001, p. 145) indicates another important barrier. From a
financial perspective, it is not explicit who pays for POE, as various different stakeholders
are involved not only in the surveys and the gathering of feedback, but also in the
implementation of these findings.
It becomes clear that POE requires the coordination of all specialists, which means that the
implementation is a difficult process and that’s why it is not considered a top priority. As Pati
& Pati (2013) point out, most of the stakeholders are mainly interested in the outcome rather
than functionality. Similarly, facilities managers emphasize the technical performance of the
building, which means that priorities change because of the standpoint of the stakeholder in
question (Pati & Pati 2013).
Furthermore, most commercial building owners avoid conducting POEs, because they are
not willing to risk tenants moving out in case the inconsistencies would be identified as
problems that affect all users (Zimmerman & Martin 2001). As Riley et al. (2010) aptly
showcase the commercial building owners avoid POE because they perceive it as a process
that could potentially reduce the value of their assets (Riley et al. 2010).
20
==PAGE:22==
Ultimately, from an architectural standpoint, there is one barrier that could be considered the
most crucial. Most researchers tend to agree to the perspective that the professional liability
of the designers discourages the implementation of POE processes. Constructive feedback
is perceived negatively in most cases and the design community tends to adopt a defensive
and cautious stance.Therefore, most architects are not willing to sacrifice their professional
reputation (Bordass et al., 2001, p. 145) if it’s going to be associated with negative criticism
regarding a finished project, as this could potentially affect their future professional
agreements with other clients and organizations.
In conclusion, adopting a critical standpoint would be helpful in terms of leveraging these
insights. The study of Pati & Pati (2013) sheds light upon the fact that, in several cases,
even when POEs are conducted, nothing guarantees its effectiveness, if the data would not
be gathered and analyzed in a meaningful way (Pati & Pati 2013). These data should include
several insights that the designers could reflect upon. However, as Cohen et al. (2001)
suggest, in several cases, many surveys suffer “data bloat”, because there is too much data
and limited time to process this information for an insightful analysis (Cohen et al., 2001, p.
89). Ultimately, it becomes clear that a well-designed and properly implemented POE can
provide a plethora of benefits to all the involved stakeholders, but a poorly designed POE
could be pointless to a certain extent.

2.2
Post Occupancy Evaluation Implementation
In 2015, Goçer and Hua proposed a system diagram that further expands the understanding
of the briefing process within the building design cycle involving the concept of POE. The
diagram presents a comprehensive architectural and facility management cycle, including
the stages of inception, design, construction, maintenance and feedback. Post-Occupancy
Evaluation (POE) is strategically positioned within the feedback loop after the building is
occupied. This critical step allows for the evaluation of a building's performance, user
satisfaction, and overall functionality. The iterative process, incorporating POE and feedback
loops, ensures continuous improvement and adaptation to evolving needs. This holistic
approach, centered around POE, aims to create more effective, efficient, and sustainable
built environments.
22
==PAGE:24==
POE encompasses an expansive range of processes and activities that systematically
evaluate a building’s performance subsequent to its handover (Ilesanmi, 2010; Tookaloo and
Smith, 2015). The majority of the discussion on POE planning and implementation, however,
is produced by higher education institutions' real estate departments and is not frequently
used across the larger AECO (Architecture, Engineering, Construction and Operations)
sector, even after fifty years of development (Leaman, 2004; Hadjri and Crozier, 2008;
Zhang and Barrett, 2010).
When applied for a newly designed facility, POE may provide several benefits, including
increased space utilisation, lower operational expenses, and lower maintenance costs (cf.
Shohet et al., 2003; RIBA, 2016; RIBA, 2017a; RIBA, 2017b). Undertaking a POE provides
an excellent chance to obtain informative input on design, construction, and management
decisions made throughout the building's life cycle (O'Neill and Duvall, 2005; Skills Funding
Agency, 2014). POE reports can offer useful information on: i) users feedback on facility
performance; ii) the team's feedback on both the design and construction phases; iii)
technical details from the facility's building management system; and iv) strategic data from a
company's estates management viewpoint (HEFCE, 2006; RIBA, 2016; RIBA, 2017a; RIBA,
2017b).
Garbowski and Mathiassen (2013) argue that effective real estate decision-making is critical
to an organization's financial and strategic growth. According to García-Peñalvo and Conde
(2013), having meaningful knowledge leads to more efficient and informed decision-making.
However, despite the abundance of 'big data' accessible, large gaps exist between a
building's expected and actual performance.
From an implementation perspective, POEs can be divided into three time periods: 3 to 6
months after occupation for operational review, 12 to 18 months for performance review, and
3 to 5 years for strategic review. An operational review is conducted shortly after the
completion of a project to analyze the project delivery process when events are still fresh in
people's memories (Barlex, 2006). However, it may be necessary to wait some time before
proceeding to allow the 'dust' to settle, especially if the project's connections have been
damaged.

2.2.1
Post-Occupancy Evaluation in User Involvement Processes
in Architectural Engineering and Construction AEC Projects
Undoubtedly, incorporating users, their preferences, and their needs into architectural,
engineering, and construction projects is becoming increasingly important. The need for user
involvement has increased as AEC projects often reveal gaps between what users want and
what architects deliver (Margolin 1997; Sanoff 2000). Consequently, several methods have
been leveraged to incorporate user feedback in AEC projects, including architectural
programming, post-occupancy evaluation (POE), and ergonomic design practices.
To shed light upon the often ambiguous concept of user involvement, Tae Wan Kim,
Youngchul Kim, and Seung Hyun Cha developed a framework that attempts to assist
architects in choosing the right user involvement methods based on the context of the
project, featuring two primary dimensions: the degree of user involvement (lateral dimension)
and the time span of involvement (longitudinal dimension). As it becomes obvious
throughout this thesis, the term "users" refers specifically to the end users of the facilities.
Production for Users: In this indirect involvement type, users do not participate in the
decision-making processes. The architects are the ones that gather data about user needs
and preferences, and then integrate this into the project (Kujala 2003). This method is
characterized by minimal user engagement like questionnaires (Wulz 1986) and it is widely
considered one of the most inexpensive approaches since users only provide primary
information, and no further input is sought until the building is complete.
-
Architectural Programming: Architectural programming allows architects to analyze
users and define design prerequisites (Cherry 1999; Erhan 2003; Pena and Parshall
2001). Techniques and qualitative approaches like observation, questionnaires, and
interviews help the architects establish user profiles, targeting function, budget, and
time requirements. However, user feedback is not part of the finalization of user
profiles and the requirements in architectural programming and it aligns with the
"production for users" category.
Production with Users: This approach is more straightforward as the users provide
feedback on different versions of the design. Accordingly, this process can be especially
valuable when it comes to refining a design (Kujala 2003). This user involvement method
leverages feedback loops (Wandersman 1979) and dialogues (Wulz 1986) that allow users
to review design choices. By incorporating the perspectives of the users, architects can
improve their designs as they can leverage these multifaceted insights.
-
Ergonomic design: The engagement of users during the early stages is crucial when
it comes to ensuring a more ergonomic design of the building layout that, at the same
time, meets user needs through feedback on functional diagrams, layouts, and
mock-ups (Remijn 2006; Villeneuve 2000). This approach improves the quality of the
design by leveraging human insights, user standards, and measurements, aligning
with the "production with real users" category as user input directly shapes functional
outcomes (Attaianese and Duca 2012).
-
Post-Occupancy Evaluation: As mentioned earlier, POE gathers data from the users
in a post-occupancy context to improve the building or create a strong foundation for
24
==PAGE:26==
future projects, fitting both "production with users" (for immediate improvements) and
"production for users" (for future applications) (Frontczak et al. 2012; Riley et al.
2010). By basing design decisions on user data rather than assumptions, POE
increases architects' understanding of building use (Choi et al. 2012; Zimmerman
and Martin 2001). Ultimately, POE is a more holistic approach that could enhance
several processes throughout each phase of the life cycle of a building.
Production by Users: This is the most participative approach that allows users to make
decisions among various design alternatives and, sometimes, the users are even allowed to
create their own solutions (Kujala 2003). This user-driven method is based on forms like
co-design and self-design (Wulz 1986), maximizing the benefits of user involvement by
enabling users to shape the design themselves and be part of major decision-making
processes. Nonetheless, it becomes clear that this approach requires significant
coordination and efforts from architects, who must manage user motivation and time
constraints to keep the process effective (Sanoff 2000).
The framework’s second dimension, the time span of user involvement, is divided into five
stages: (1) programming – developing user profiles, (2) programming – developing design
requirements, (3) design – developing a design solution, (4) construction – implementing
building production, and (5) occupancy – gathering real usage data.
Programming
User Profiles: In this initial stage, architects gather data about users to create profiles
reflecting functional needs, psychological insights, and user priorities in order to create a
strong foundation for building production (Cherry 1999).
Design Requirements: This stage leverages the insights from the user profiles and translates
them into design requirements, defining specifications for the layout, the environmental and
lighting conditions, as well as other factors. These requirements provide essential guidelines
for the design phase.
Design
Design Solution: User involvement in this stage contributes to the development of design
solutions that satisfy the project’s requirements while establishing the role of the users as the
main focus of the process. Users often participate in formal meetings, offering feedback on
design alternatives.
Construction
Implementing Plan: During the phase of construction, user involvement methods assist with
implementation plans, such as work schedules and site layouts, with particular relevance for
renovation projects where user interactions need consideration (Yee 2009).
Occupancy
Real Usage Data: After completion, user involvement is oriented towards gathering real
usage data and providing insights into how the users interact with the facilities. This data
becomes extremely valuable for architects in future projects, especially since pre-occupancy
25
==PAGE:27==
involvement can be challenging (Jensen, Alexander, and Fronczek-Munter 2011; Oijevaar,
Jovanovic, and Otter 2009).

2.2.3
POE and Sustainability
On a fundamental level, POE is by definition able to promote and achieve sustainable
development within the built environment, directly aligning with several Sustainable
Development Goals (SDGs), which are thoroughly showcased in the 2030 Agenda. In
general, buildings contribute greatly to climate change, as they are responsible for about
40% of the global carbon emissions (The Circularity Gap Report, Denmark, 2023). Meeting
high energy efficiency standards by establishing a balance between design expectations and
actual performance will play a crucial role in achieving net-zero emissions. POE bridges this
divide by verifying operational energy use against design goals, spotting inconsistencies,
and enabling targeted improvements that maximize energy efficiency and reduce carbon
emissions.
The 2030 Agenda for Sustainable Development, adopted by the United Nations in 2015,
places emphasis on the significance of follow-up and review procedures that could
contribute to achieving the 17 SDGs, informed by high-quality, accessible, and timely data
from country-led assessments (Rob D. van den Berg, Indran Naidoo, Susan D. Tamondong,
2017). POE plays a crucial role in this context and its contribution in promoting and
achieving the SDGs is remarkable.
More specifically, POE contributes to SDG 7 (Affordable and Clean Energy) by reducing
energy consumption in buildings and also promotes SDG 11 (Sustainable Cities and
Communities) through the development of energy-efficient urban environments.
Furthermore, POE is responsible for improving thermal comfort, which promotes SDG 3
(Good Health and Well-being) through the mitigation of health risks that are related to
extreme temperatures, which is widely considered a major issue in several countries.
Moreover, by ensuring that buildings perform as designed, POE promotes SDG 12
(Responsible Consumption and Production) through responsible and value sensitive
resource management. In addition, it supports SDG 13 (Climate Action) by optimizing
building operations and extending the lifespan of the buildings. This restricts the need for
new construction and minimizes greenhouse gas emissions. This is particularly relevant in
the architecture, engineering, construction, and operations (AECO) sector, which accounts
for 40% of global energy use and carbon dioxide emissions, 25% of total timber harvested,
and 16% of freshwater consumed globally (Milutienė et al., 2012).
Additionally, POE aligns with the "Leave No One Behind" principle of Agenda 2030 by
ensuring that buildings meet the needs of all occupants, particularly marginalized groups.
The assessment of crucial factors like accessibility, usability, functionality and user
satisfaction, makes POE a tool that strives for inclusivity within the built environment.
In conclusion, POE is a critical process that helps in achieving SDG targets by verifying and
enhancing building energy performance and contributing to a sustainable and equitable built
environment. Through structured evaluation, POE aligns building performance with the
SDGs, addressing the environmental impact of the built environment and promoting the
values of sustainability.

2.3
Post-Occupancy Evaluation in Academic Buildings
In general, a great percentage of experts tend to share the opinion that a rewarding learning
experience relies on the performance of a learning environment (Amin et al., 2015). Abend
et al. (2006) proposed that “all individuals have a right to a quality educational facility, a
physical space that facilitates the learning process and demonstrates cost-effectiveness over
time; one that respects and is in harmony with the environment; and one that encourages
social participation, providing a healthy, comfortable, safe, secure and inspirational setting
for its occupants” (Abend et al., 2006).
As mentioned in previous sections of this thesis, POE studies include performance
measurement of building facilities. Traditionally, POE studies were a result of problems
associated with housing needs of disadvantaged groups within the care industry such as
institutional care facilities, mental hospitals, nursing homes and correctional facilities.
However, several researchers showcase a fundamental shift in POE over the years (Preiser,
1995; Council, 2001; Riley et al., 2010). The stakeholders that were involved in the
management of facilities like military housing, prisons, hospitals, and academic buildings
started conducting POEs, aiming to improve the accessibility and the performance of these
buildings. Therefore, in the context of this thesis, it would be useful to delve deeper into the
fruitful relationship between POE and learning environments.
2.3.1
Post Occupancy Evaluation and Learning Environment
After some thorough research, it becomes clear that post occupancy evaluation of school
buildings and educational environments has a nearly fifty-year history. The Building
Performance Research Unit (BPRU) at the University of Strathclyde evaluated over fifty
schools in Scotland in the late 1960s. This study provided one of the most influential
paradigms of the post occupancy evaluation of school buildings. The methods that were
followed correlated several important factors like space and its organization, people's
responses to the building, space use, costs, and services.
All these areas together showcase how POE can broaden the minds of the designers, even
in terms of reconceptualizing their field of responsibility. Whether being qualitative or
quantitative, these methods provide a compact framework that could help several involved
stakeholders. In a historical context, for the first time in 1986, there was an effort to regulate
the assessment of educational facilities by the Council of Educational Facility Planners
International (CEFPI).
Twenty years after this first attempt of POE, these regulations were introduced in response
to major problems experienced in building performance with particular emphasis on the
building’s user perspective. Preiser identified over 125 items that affect the functionality of
school buildings in the following areas: school site structural and mechanical features, plant
29
==PAGE:31==
maintainability, school building safety and security, and educational adequacy and
environment for learning. (Preiser, 2002).
Within the environment of school facilities, post occupancy evaluation is mostly related to the
degree in which the building supports the goals of the educational processes by evaluating
the physical environment's educational capability. Educational adequacy is the degree to
which school facilities effectively encourage educational goals and activities. Admittedly, this
form of evaluation is very close to traditional post occupancy evaluation methodology, where
the emphasis on user needs and experience is a central concept.
After some thorough research, it becomes obvious that many examples of post-occupancy
evaluations are available for university researchers in schools of architecture and
engineering. Despite the fact that various academic centers around the U.S. and the world
are actively interested in leveraging POEs, it is generally assumed that the POE is "widely
acknowledged but rarely practiced". During the process of reviewing documents and data
from the above case studies and other previous work in this area, it becomes clear that they
all have placed their emphasis on specific projects like: “post occupancy evaluation on
Oregon Health & Science University”. Unfortunately there is no intention regarding
conducting comparative analyses or spotting the connections between them. While there are
design standard handbooks for each educational center or university, and there are climate
guidelines and many other manuals in the construction industry, there is no general design
standard for higher education as a whole.
2.3.2 Performance evaluation of laboratory facilities
An extensive search of previous POE studies indicates that little effort has been made in the
POE of laboratory facilities. Few studies have been conducted, which have sought to use a
research tool presented by (Fraser et al., 1995) called Science Laboratory Environment
Inventory (SLEI). SLEI is similar to POE in functionality but is more focused on methods of
learning. However, delving deeper into the benefits of using it showcases that filling this gap
in the literature regarding POE studies is necessary.
An example of a SLEI study was conducted by Aladejana and Aderibigbe (2007) in an
elementary school in Nigeria. The goal of the study was to assess different parameters in
the science lab environment based on the students’ perspective and evaluate how and if the
environment affects the learning outcomes. They found that from five different factors that
the students could evaluate, cohesiveness had the highest assessment, material
environment was the least with open-endedness, while integration and rule clarity were in
between. They suggested that the laboratory environment should be enhanced to produce
better outcomes from students.
Nonetheless, it becomes clear that SLEI perceives learning as a process that is affected by
certain factors, while recent studies suggest that learning is a multifaceted concept with
several different layers that are not easily examined. Methods like POE are able to unfold
these layers and shed light on several different factors that affect learning.
30
==PAGE:32==
Studies that are closer to the core of a POE were conducted by Badolato (1995) and Yau
et al. (2012). Badolato (1995) carried out a study on the physical environment of computer
labs using a questionnaire survey designed with a five-point Likert scale. The study
concluded that laboratories designed in accordance to design guidelines are rated higher by
users than the other laboratories.
On the other hand, Yau et al. (2012) conducted a study to evaluate the comfort conditions in
pharmaceutical laboratories in Malaysia. Questionnaire surveys as well measurement tools
were deployed in four laboratories. Among other inadequacies, the study showcased the
dissatisfaction of the users regarding the thermal environment.
As for studies directly related to POEs, Morhaim and Meir (2008) conducted a study in an
office laboratory building, where they leverage questionnaire surveys and interviews with the
users of the building. The POE results confirmed that the building was not designed properly
and laboratories were lacking basic features. They concluded that POE should be conducted
in all buildings after the commission process.
Finally, Hua et al. (2011) carried out a POE to evaluate the efficiency of daylight design and
user visual satisfaction in a LEED gold laboratory building on a university campus. They
enabled a plethora of different methods of data collection including user surveys and
interviews as well as different instruments to capture data. Their study revealed four key
findings. Firstly, they found out that daylight in laboratories is mostly appreciated by users.
Furthermore, the horizontal shading devices that have been used in the building satisfied
users' visual and lighting concerns. Also, leveraging the presence of natural lighting is helpful
regarding the improvement of the light levels in the laboratory building. Ultimately, controlling
the amount of light by occupants is key in improving the satisfaction of the users in the
laboratory environment.
In conclusion, when it comes to laboratories, some of the previous studies were conducted
in school environments using the SLEI method, which is tailored towards the learning
environment, and not necessarily the physical environment. Other studies are not essentially
influenced by the philosophy of POE, and at times focused on certain systems of the
building. Therefore, the concept of POE has not been fully explored in the specific domain of
laboratory facilities, and filling this research gap might be beneficial for future research
endeavors.

User Involvement Processes in CAS projects
CAS has implemented several user involvement processes to ensure that projects align
closely with user needs across the project lifecycle, from initial design phases to
post-occupancy follow-up. These processes provide CAS with valuable user input, allowing
them to create functional facilities that meet the evolving needs of DTU’s academic and
research community.
One main user involvement method CAS involves is a series of regular meetings with user
groups during project and design requirements. These meetings involve stakeholders such
as department heads and end-users, offering them the opportunity to discuss their needs,
raise concerns, and review drawings. Through these discussions, CAS gains insight into
both general and specific requirements for each project, ensuring that user priorities are
incorporated early in the design process. Additionally, CAS conducts surveys to collect
structured feedback, assessing user satisfaction and identifying areas for potential
improvement.
To enhance user engagement further, CAS has adopted digital tools such as the Dalux
platform. Dalux enables users to provide real-time feedback on design elements, including
reviewing and commenting on 2D drawings within Dalux Box. Dalux Box (Appendix, Figure
1) is a comprehensive Common Data Environment (CDE) designed to simplify project
collaboration and connect users with BIM and essential project information. This tool
centralizes document and model management such as design validation and user feedback,
making information and design management more accessible for all stakeholders (CAS, its
partners and users) involved in the design process.
For post-occupancy support, CAS invites users to participate in an inspection 11 months
after building occupancy, where they can report issues or suggest adjustments. Beyond that,
the Dalux HelpDesk (Appendix, Figure 2) allows all campus users to report ongoing
maintenance needs and track progress, helping CAS to respond quickly to operational
needs. By involving users throughout the project and facility management phases, CAS aims
to continuously refine their approach to deliver user-centered, sustainable, and adaptable
facilities.
While CAS has several user involvement processes, Post-Occupancy Evaluations (POEs)
are rarely, if ever, conducted and they are not considered as a standard part of the project
lifecycle. Based on the discussions, the architect noted only one POE conducted for Building
324, while a CAS manager mentioned gathering feedback pre- and post-completion for
Building 208. Expanding POEs could enhance CAS’s ability to improve long-term building
performance and user satisfaction.
During the last meeting with the CAS Manager, a discussion emerged about the concept of
generic buildings and the idea of "Designing for No One." According to the project manager,
CAS attempts to work as generically as possible during the building design phase. In recent
years, there has been a shift towards "Design for One," emphasizing adaptability. The
underlying principle is that if a space is designed generically, it can be utilized by anyone and
adjusted with minimal alterations to cater to different groups' needs. This approach not only

In addition to the Biosphere, the building accommodates specialized clinic labs designed to
provide a more focused research environment. These labs are characterized by their natural

The primary goal of Building 202 is to create a dynamic academic environment that

3.4
Histology laboratory facilities DTU
The histology lab is a specialized facility dedicated to studying the microscopic structure of
tissues. Technicians and scientists in this lab prepare and examine tissue samples using
various techniques such as embedding, sectioning, and staining to observe cellular details
for research and medical diagnoses. This lab is part of the biological facilities in the
Department of Health Technology and consists of around 20 rooms, including offices.
Located on the 2nd floor of the 202 building, it serves not only researchers from its
department but also those from other departments.
The lab’s personnel are part of the Research Group for Molecular and Reproductive
Toxicology, which studies how environmental chemicals, especially endocrine disruptors,
affect development and health. Their research focuses on how these chemicals disrupt sex
hormone signaling pathways, leading to reproductive and cognitive disorders, and how
thyroid hormone disruptions impact brain development. Using a range of methods, including
in vitro assays, organ cultures, and rodent studies, the group works to identify the health
risks posed by these chemicals and improve safety assessments.
In addition to conducting foundational research, the group contributes to chemical hazard
identification and regulatory toxicology, providing expert guidance to Danish authorities,
including the Danish Environmental Protection Agency and the Danish Veterinary and Food
Administration. The ultimate goal is to protect human health by enhancing chemical safety
testing and risk assessments
3.4.1 Overview of Procedures in a Histological Laboratory
The histological lab routine is characterized by emphasis on tissue analysis, mainly
conducted in Room 5208 (Figure 3.4.1, where samples are embedded after preservation in
10% formaldehyde. The processes of tissue collection and initial processing occur in Room
5206, usually taking one day, under the supervision of the Lab Technician.
Before embedding, cassettes labeled for identification are prepared in Room 5203. After
embedding, tissues are stored in Room 5217 to be used in the future. The Sectioning
procedure is conducted with specialized machines in specialized rooms, and slides may be
processed in Room 5208 to remove bubbles before being placed in the histology scanner
room, which is in Room 5214.
A plethora of staining methods like immunohistochemistry and immunofluorescence, are
performed in Room 5208 as well, generally requiring two days of commitment. Microscopy
for result analysis takes place in Rooms 5207, 5208, and 5215, with tissue scanning
preferred for comprehensive evaluation. Additional revisal is taken in a dark room near
Room 5202 for immunofluorescence. Overall, Rooms 5203, 5206, 5214, and 5217 are used
for the main experimental procedures, while Room 5208 is the focal point for histological
procedures.

Methodology
This research employs a comprehensive Post Occupancy Evaluation (POE) framework to
assess the histological laboratory located in Building 202 of the Technical University of
Denmark (DTU). The methodology is structured to systematically gather qualitative and
quantitative data through literature review, walk-through assessments, structured interviews,
and thematic analysis.
1. Literature Review of POE
The methodology commenced with a thorough review of existing literature on Post
Occupancy Evaluation (POE) to establish a solid theoretical foundation for the research.
This review highlighted key concepts and frameworks, particularly focusing on Preiser's
theories of POE (Preiser, 1995), which advocate for an indicative approach. Indicative POE
methods emphasize identifying user needs and potential issues in a space to inform design
improvements. This approach is particularly relevant in the context of academic laboratory
environments, where user satisfaction and functionality are paramount.
2. Walk-Through Assessment
The first phase of the data collection involved a walk-through of the histological lab to
observe and identify any issues affecting user experience. During this walk-through,
potential problems related to ergonomics, safety, environmental conditions, and functionality
were noted. This initial assessment served to establish a baseline for the research, guiding
the selection of specific POE methods. The indicative approach facilitated a focused inquiry
into users' lived experiences within the space, setting the stage for subsequent data
collection.
3. Interviews with Stakeholders
Interviews were conducted with various stakeholders involved in the design and operational
phases of Building 202. This included discussions with the CAS manager and the architect,
who played critical roles in the building process. The structured interview format utilized a
questionnaire designed to gather comprehensive feedback from end-users, including two lab
technicians, three research assistants, and one postdoctoral researcher. The aim was to
explore critical areas such as ergonomics, environmental conditions, equipment functionality,
facility management, and user satisfaction.
The structured questionnaire was crafted to ensure that all pertinent aspects of user
experience were addressed. Questions were developed to probe into users' perceptions of
the lab environment, focusing on issues related to lighting, equipment usability, and
collaboration spaces. The goal was to collect qualitative data that reflects users' satisfaction
and identifies specific challenges they encounter while utilizing the lab facilities.
4. Follow-Up Interview with CAS Manager
To enrich the findings from the end-user interviews, a follow-up discussion was held with the
CAS manager. This session aimed to analyze the results obtained from the end-users and to
explore any barriers or challenges faced by the management during the design and
43
==PAGE:45==
operational phases of the laboratory. This iterative dialogue is crucial for understanding the
discrepancies between user experiences and management perspectives, thereby allowing
for a more nuanced analysis of the overall design and functionality of the lab.
5. Data Analysis
After the data collection phase, thematic analysis was employed to identify patterns and
recurring themes within the interview responses. This involved meticulously reviewing the
qualitative data to categorize and group feedback into key themes, such as lighting
conditions, ergonomic issues, and collaborative spaces. Comparative analyses were
conducted between the perspectives of the end-users and the insights provided by the CAS
manager and architect. This approach allowed for the identification of specific problems
faced by users and the exploration of potential barriers to optimal lab performance from a
managerial standpoint.
In summary, the methodology adopted for this POE focused on a systematic, multi-faceted
approach to data collection and analysis. By engaging a diverse range of stakeholders and
employing a combination of qualitative methods, the research aims to provide a
comprehensive understanding of how the histological lab at DTU supports its intended
functions and meets user needs. The findings will ultimately contribute to enhancing the
building design process, ensuring that future laboratory environments are better aligned with
the expectations and requirements of their users.

5.3
Data Analysis methodologies
While delving deeper into this thesis, it quickly became clear that the qualitative data could
be thoroughly explored under various lenses. However, each lens could result in different
interpretations. Therefore, the choice of the primary method for data interpretation had to be
deeply connected with the main goal of the thesis. Thematic was selected due to its flexibility
and robustness in identifying patterns within qualitative data (Maguire and Delahunt, 2017),
which is very useful when it comes to investigating complex and multifaceted environments
like a histological laboratory.
Given that this research project aims to understand the experiences of end-users and the
operational challenges that emerge after the occupancy of a space, thematic analysis
provides a systematic way to examine their feedback. After all, the main goal of the research
is to identify aspects that several people recognize as problematic, and that’s why thematic
analysis could greatly contribute to this effort as it provides the opportunity to safely extract
robust conclusions. This method allows for a thorough examination of issues that may not be
immediately visible from a quantitative perspective, such as ergonomic problems or
functional limitations of the space.
In addition, thematic analysis is highly relevant to POE, as it points out the gaps and the
contradictions between the design intentions and the actual experiences of users. In this
context, identifying recurring themes from interviews with the involved stakeholders, such as
the CAS manager, architect, and lab users, offers the opportunity of discovering specific
areas where the design of Building 202 may not fulfill certain user needs. This realization
makes thematic analysis an ideal method for unfolding latent issues that might not be
thoroughly examined in more structured evaluations, which means that POE is a more
dynamic and all-around user involvement process.
Undoubtedly, the process of determining the themes that could provide better outcomes for
this thesis was a challenging process. However, several studies like the ones of Al
Shimemeri et. al (2011), Dall (2013), and Preiser et. al (1998) contributed to the idea of
establishing three main categories, in which the themes that derive from thematic analysis
are incorporated: technical performance elements, functional performance elements,
and behavioral performance elements. These three categories encompass all the
essential aspects of user involvement and serve a strong foundation for the process of
defining more specific features that need to be assessed in a user involvement process.

Designers and researchers can get a more thorough overview by adding more context and
nuance in these categories through spotting different themes that cover these areas.

examined:

5.4
Users’ Interviews analysis
The interviews were conducted between March and July, 2024. The six users that
participated are lab technicians, research assistants and postdoctoral researchers. They
responded to 23 questions and each interview lasted approximately 30 minutes.
5.4.1
Functional performance elements
On a fundamental level, this analysis identifies the key ergonomic, technical and functional
challenges faced by users in the lab, placing emphasis on issues related to work routines,
layout difficulties, collaboration, storage space, and workstation comfort. By incorporating
user feedback, the primary goal is to highlight potential areas that need improvement and
emphasize the importance of incorporating post-occupancy evaluations in the design
process.
Adequacy of furniture and fixtures
Users reported varied work routines that revealed certain inefficiencies in the layout design,
especially regarding how tasks were organized within the space. User A, for instance, works
within a single room but faces some difficulties as they have to place their computer on
different pieces of furniture due to the lack of a dedicated, ergonomically designed space for
it. They also propose that the scan machine is placed too high, which makes the workstation
uncomfortable. While they do not face major difficulties, their comment emphasizes how
minor changes in equipment placement could significantly improve user experience. At the
initial part of the interviews, while the users admitted that they are satisfied with the lab in
general, they still highlighted some issues related to functionality.
More specifically, workstation comfort was identified as a major concern for several users.
User A referred to their inability to adjust the height of their workstation, which affects their
49
==PAGE:51==
productivity. They also highlighted discomfort caused by the scan machine's placement,
suggesting that it seems that ergonomics was not a priority in the initial design phase. In
contrast, User B expressed satisfaction with the availability of adjustable chairs and tables,
which greatly improved their comfort. Both User D and User E also mentioned problems with
adjusting workstation heights, particularly the cupboards, which are too high and force users
to use high chairs, creating ergonomic strain. In contrast, User F reported no issues with
their workstation, benefiting from both lab and natural lighting.
Nonetheless, it becomes clear that the functionality issues that were highlighted could be
easily fixed in the current lab, and could be also used as a reference for future lab projects.
The users showcased how these inefficiencies affect their work, and it turns out that a
designer could not be aware of this kind of insights in the first place, as most of them are
architects, and not biologists or lab technicians.
Sufficiency of spatial configuration
Regarding spatial configuration, the users provided some valuable feedback. In particular,
User B follows a repetitive staining process that takes up most of the day but admits that
their tasks do not require multiple rooms, which simplifies their workflow. However, their work
is highly protocol-driven, suggesting that efficient organization within their immediate work
area is quite important. Furthermore, other users have to be more flexible and access more
rooms within their day. User D moves between rooms to access necessary equipment, which
can lead to inefficiencies in their workflow. These issues illustrate that while some users can
perform all tasks within a single room, others are affected by the need to move between
spaces, which may not be optimized for easy access to the equipment.
For other users, such as User C, the ability to move between the lab and office with ease
significantly enhances their workflow. This suggests that spatial proximity between different
workspaces is a crucial consideration that has been successfully implemented in some
cases but neglected in others, where users are forced to navigate between multiple rooms.
Therefore, it seems that spatial configuration is highly important as the dynamics are
multifaceted.
Several users mentioned certain challenges related to the physical layout of the lab.
Although User B and User C did not report significant layout challenges, they did mention
logistical issues. User B admitted that handling toxic waste (H-Waste) is made difficult due to
the inconvenient design of the waste disposal system. Undoubtedly, this should be
considered a major safety risk and it also affects how User B performs critical tasks.
Similarly, although User C was generally satisfied with the layout, they proposed that more
space would improve collaboration and make working alongside others more comfortable.
Other users like User D and User F did not express major concerns regarding layout
difficulties, but this could reflect differences in their work roles rather than an absence of
problems. The fact the user experiences have not many similarities suggests that while the
layout may accommodate some workflows well, it is not universally suited to the range of
tasks carried out in the lab.

In general, most users felt that the layout supports collaboration and communication

Adequacy of storage space
A common pattern throughout the interviews is the inadequacy of storage space. While User

Furthermore, User F highlighted a specific issue with fridge storage, where users face
difficulties when they have to to store new specimens. This indicates that while some
storage areas, like general shelves or cupboards might meet the users' needs, specialized
storage solutions like fridges are lacking. Again, the varied feedback suggests that storage
provisions were not properly planned to accommodate the long-term growth of the lab or the
acquisition of additional equipment, which should be a critical consideration in future lab
designs.
As it will become clear through the analysis of the interviews with the CAS representatives,
this fundamental aspect is not addressed in the design phase, as the designers cannot be

proactive design approach.

Technical performance elements
The thematic analysis of the technical performance of the lab reveals key insights into the
experiences of the lab users regarding lighting, temperature, ventilation, and noise
distractions. The responses provide a nuanced understanding of how these factors affect
their work routine and overall comfort in the lab environment, highlighting both positive
aspects and areas for improvement.
Artificial light adequacy
Lighting emerges as a critical issue for several users, impacting the quality of their work.
While some users have no problems with the current lighting setup, others place emphasis
on the need for more adjustable lighting options. For example, some users mentioned that
certain areas of the lab, particularly workstations, are too dim for detailed tasks. Although the
lab is equipped with different light sources, the uneven distribution of light across the
workspace raises major concerns in the context of a lab, especially in cases where precision
is a fundamental requirement. A common workaround mentioned was the use of multiple
light sources, but this highlights a gap in the initial design of the lab’s lighting. The user
admitted that they often use external lighting that was not part of the initial design to
efficiently perform their tasks. In addition, personal lighting preferences and sensitivities, like
those related to aging or light sensitivity, indicate that the users should be able to easily
adjust the lighting of the space, as the needs of each user are different.
More specifically, user A mentioned that lighting is inadequate while user B pointed out that
some of their colleagues struggle with light sensitivity, and the current artificial lighting
system is not flexible enough to accommodate their needs. This showcases an important
issue with the automated lighting system, which is not as adjustable as it should be. User C
and User D expressed similar concerns about lighting. User D noted that additional light
sources are often necessary because the main lights are positioned inefficiently, leaving
parts of the workspace in shadow.
Temperature and ventilation adequacy
Although some users expressed certain concerns regarding the temperature and the
ventilation of the lab, the users are generally satisfied. The primary issue lies with the

ventilation in larger spaces, where airflow may be less effective. Additionally, the
temperature control is seen as adequate, although one user expressed concerns about
working in warm weather with lab coats. While there is no general discomfort among the

AC indicates a potential source of frustration, as it limits natural ventilation and might not be

functional, but slight improvements could enhance the working environment.
Noise levels
Noise distractions do not appear to be a major issue for the majority of the users. Most
describe the lab as a quiet environment, with little to no disruptive noise. Nonetheless, some
users note that certain equipment, like machines, can be noisy and may require additional
noise mitigation strategies, such as using earplugs or listening to music with headphones.
This highlights that while noise is not a pervasive issue, there are moments when equipment
sounds could potentially distract the users, especially those that are sensitive to background
noise. Given that distractions may depend on personal preferences or task difficulty, having
noise management strategies—whether through equipment improvements or providing
personal tools like noise-canceling headphones—could improve user satisfaction.
Overall, the environmental conditions in the lab are largely adequate but could benefit from
targeted enhancements in lighting, ventilation, and noise management. While most users are
satisfied with the current setup, a more flexible and adaptable design that accounts for
individual needs and sensitivities, particularly with respect to lighting and noise, would further
optimize the lab environment for all users. Ultimately, the experiences of the lab users
suggest that, although functional, the current design does not fully address the diverse
working conditions required for high-quality laboratory work.

do not face any issues using the lab equipment, which reinforces the sentiment of adequacy
among the users. Similarly, User D and User E ensured that the existing equipment meets
the needs of a histology lab, indicating that it is sufficient for their operational requirements.
Despite the overall satisfaction, the lack of depth in responses suggests a need for further
exploration regarding additional equipment that might enhance laboratory performance.
Users C and F, while they are satisfied with the available equipment, also hinted at the
potential for improvements. From another angle, User C was hesitant to provide a definitive
answer about the need for additional equipment since they are new in the lab environment.

satisfaction with the lab equipment, the limited depth of feedback highlights an area for
further inquiry, especially regarding potential enhancements or additional tools that could
better support users' work and improve the functionality of the lab.
5.4.3
Facility Management
The theme of Facility Management reveals varied experiences and sentiments regarding
the interaction between lab users and CAS, particularly focusing on responsiveness and the
proposed need for a dedicated facilities manager.
User A expresses a mixed experience regarding CAS's responsiveness. While
acknowledging that they are quick to reply to emails, they note that their physical presence
for implementing changes or fixing other issues can be inconsistent. This inconsistency can
potentially disrupt the lab's efficiency, as timely interventions are crucial for maintaining
workflow. User D also expressed the same concern, stating that although CAS is generally
fast in their responses, their capacity to facilitate substantial changes is limited. They point
out that certain improvements, particularly those requiring physical alterations to the lab
layout, are often met with inflexibility. This highlights a significant challenge in addressing the
evolving needs of the lab environment, as radical changes are constrained by existing
limitations and protocols.
Interestingly, User B and User C did not have enough experience with CAS to provide
comprehensive feedback, with User C being new to the lab. This absence of insights
suggests a gap in knowledge and familiarity with the processes of reaching out to CAS,
which could impact their ability to effectively utilize available resources for addressing lab
needs or their ability to quickly handle a crisis situation that requires the intervention from
CAS. Furthermore, User F, who has not contacted CAS yet, indicates that there is a lack of
proactive engagement with the facility management team, which can hinder timely
responses to issues as they arise.
It becomes clear that User A and User E think that the lab would greatly benefit from a better
relationship with the facility management support. User A believes that having a dedicated
facilities manager could simplify the process of addressing the needs of the lab, making it
easier for both lab technicians and the researchers to communicate their concerns. User E
supports this idea, noting that direct contact with a responsible individual would be more
effective than relying on an impersonal reporting platform, where several things could go
wrong. This indicates a shared idea among users that enhancing the support structure could
lead to better management of lab facilities.
54
==PAGE:56==
Overall, while users recognize the responsiveness of CAS in some areas, there is a clear
call for more consistent and flexible support, which could be addressed through the
implementation of a dedicated facilities manager. Such a role could serve as a link between
lab users and facility management, ensuring that user needs are met promptly and
effectively, which could have significant benefits for the overall functionality and efficiency of
the lab environment.
5.4.4
Overall User Satisfaction
Ultimately, the overall user satisfaction theme provides insights into both the strengths and
challenges experienced by users in the histology lab at DTU. The users considered the
organization and spatial design of the lab as its main strengths. For example, several users
applauded the rational arrangement of equipment and materials, which facilitates their work
routine and enhances their efficiency in performing certain tasks. User B placed emphasis
on the effective categorization of chemicals for better traceability, indicating a systematic
approach that contributes to safety and ease of access.
User C also highlighted the spaciousness of the lab, noting that it allows several individuals
to work simultaneously without feeling cramped. This spatial design is supported by features
such as glass doors, which improve visibility and, therefore, it is easier for the users to
communicate. User D’s emphasis on the strong organizational culture within the team further
reinforces the notion that collaboration is fostered through shared responsibilities and
cleanliness, creating a positive working atmosphere.
However, despite these strengths, users identified several challenges and areas for
improvement. User A specifically pointed out the need for better optimization of the
workspace to support various tasks. They noted that while the design does not significantly
hinder their work, there are certain aspects that could be enhanced for better usability. User
B mentioned redundancy in equipment, where duplicate parts complicate workflows in
certain cases, particularly when quick access is needed.
This issue of inefficiency is also compounded by the aging microwave, which sometimes
heats samples too rapidly, indicating that outdated technology might be an issue in certain
cases. Furthermore, User D expressed frustration over the lack of flexibility in rearranging
the lab layout, especially when accommodating new equipment, which can cause difficulties
in the operational efficiency of the lab.
Lighting emerged as a recurring concern, with users acknowledging both its strengths and
weaknesses. While User E recognized that the lighting contributes positively to the work
environment, they also indicated that adjustments could further improve the overall
experience. The same concern was expressed by User C, who mentioned that the varying
dynamics of lighting in the space could present challenges, particularly as they navigate their
new role.
The thematic analysis of overall user satisfaction reveals a complex interplay of positive
attributes and critical areas for improvement. While the lab is generally well-organized and
spacious, with effective collaboration among team members, there remain challenges
55
==PAGE:57==
related to equipment redundancy, layout flexibility, and lighting dynamics. Addressing these
issues through targeted enhancements could significantly improve the user experience and
overall functionality of the lab.
Admittedly, conducting thematic analysis is a challenging process. However, as mentioned in
the previous section, POE could greatly benefit current and future designs. Furthermore,
gaining insights regarding the strengths and the inefficiencies of a design is extremely
valuable, as designers would be aware of what went well and what didn’t. In this case, it
becomes clear that these insights could be taken into account in the design phase of a
different lab in the future.
Another useful insight that derived from this analysis is that, in academic or other
professional environments, the users are the ones who should provide feedback for an
occupied building not only because they are the humans that use the space. In these
contexts, the users are the professionals that use the space and their insights are even more
valuable, as the designers are not familiar with how certain specialized tasks are performed.
Additionally, while the users and the managers admitted that it is not possible to rebuild the
place, it becomes clear that most of the concerns the users mentioned require minimal
changes that could be easily implemented. In conclusion, the interview with the users
validated the potential of POE and that these insights could be leveraged from the
histological lab and other labs as well.
5.5
CAS Managers' and Architect’s Interviews analysis
5.5.1
CAS’s Position on Post-Occupancy Evaluation (POE)
In the context of this master thesis, conducting interviews with the users and CAS
representatives was crucial in terms of approaching the topic more holistically. It quickly
became clear that approaching the topic from both angles would provide better insights. The
manager and the architect of CAS were willing to share their insights, and their contribution
added more depth and nuance to this thesis.
In general, both the CAS manager and the architect recognize that POEs could provide
significant value by offering insights into how buildings are actually used and highlighting
areas for improvement. While they recognize the importance of POE, they mention that
currently there is not a formalized process for conducting these evaluations, primarily due to
budget limitations and lack of knowledge on how to implement it. This lack of a structured
approach means that while feedback might be collected from time to time, it’s not done
consistently, which limits CAS’s ability to systematically improve building designs based on
actual use.
It is good to mention that CAS gets external help when it comes to the design of a project.
Although the people that work in CAS are mainly architects and engineers, external
architects take over the design of a project. The CAS project managers and architects are
mainly responsible for ensuring that all processes are properly implemented.
56
==PAGE:58==
A notable exception was Building 324, where the architect’s team conducted an informal
POE because of the building’s popularity among students. Although this highlights an
interest in understanding user behavior, it depends heavily on curiosity rather than a formal
requirement. Additionally, feedback often flows from the CAS maintenance team, who
provide firsthand observations and practical insights once they begin managing a new
building. When maintenance staff first take over a building, they sometimes question design
choices or raise concerns about functionality.
These insights from the maintenance team are invaluable because they are based on real,
everyday operational challenges. The CAS manager and project teams try to take this
feedback into account to improve collaboration with maintenance staff, as they’re
responsible for the building's ongoing functionality. A formalized POE process could help
CAS gather this feedback in a structured way, making sure that maintenance insights are
documented and used to inform future projects.
5.5.2
User Feedback Challenges
Both CAS representatives mentioned that they gather user feedback that they try to take into
account during the design phase of a project. This feedback primarily comes through
institute management rather than directly from end-users, which means that this could lead
to filtered or incomplete information. This lack of direct communication can lead to frustration
and disappointment when users find that the final design doesn’t align with their
expectations. The CAS representatives admitted that the practical problem is that more
broad user feedback is time-consuming and this affects the operational aspect of the design
and construction process of a building.
The CAS manager noted that if users understood why certain features were cut, they might
be more accepting of the final design. However, without transparency there can be a
disconnect between user desires and the final outcome. Moreover, the academic
environment is dynamic, with research needs constantly evolving and staff turnover
happening regularly. This creates additional challenges, as new user groups often have
different requirements from those who initially provided input, leading to further
dissatisfaction. Furthermore, it is good to mention that, in Building 202, CAS hired different
external architects, which means that this could affect user experiences because of possible
inconsistencies in the design.
As mentioned earlier, one of the major challenges CAS faces with user feedback is the high
turnover of academic staff and the constantly evolving nature of research. The individuals
involved in the initial design process are not the same as those who ultimately use the
space. When new research groups or faculty members move in, they may find that the
facilities, tailored to the needs of their predecessors, don’t fully support their specific
requirements or workflows. This mismatch can lead to dissatisfaction, as new users may feel
that their needs weren’t considered, even though CAS had tailored the design based on
detailed input from the previous occupants. This continual shift in user groups makes it
difficult for CAS to design spaces that remain relevant and functional for everyone over time.
57
==PAGE:59==
Therefore, as research priorities shift, the specific demands on a space—like specialized
equipment, lab layouts, or collaborative areas—can change, often in ways that weren't
anticipated during the original design process. For example, a laboratory built to
accommodate one type of research may require significant modification when new users
with different methodologies arrive. This adaptability challenge becomes especially apparent
in highly specialized spaces, such as labs designed for specific experiments or equipment,
where flexibility is harder to achieve without major redesigns. The CAS manager
acknowledged that balancing user-specific needs with the demand for adaptable, generic
spaces is complex, as expectations evolve with each incoming research group.
Nonetheless, as it has been clarified throughout this thesis, the fact that this shift takes place
every four or five years makes the process of conducting regular POEs more necessary. If
new users arrive every time the research cycles change, the data that could be gathered
from POEs would be valuable and sufficient in terms of adjusting the lab to the needs of a
different scientific project. For example, there are projects that require a systematic volume
of experiments and the researchers need to be focused to perform a series of repetitive but
crucial tasks, while other projects require more coordination and communication within the
team. Gathering POE data on a regular basis would be beneficial in the process of
transitioning from one research project to another. Therefore, while the CAS representatives
emphasized the constantly evolving nature of the academic environment, it becomes clear
that this could be considered a chance and not a dead end.
Without a structured, ongoing feedback system to capture these evolving needs, CAS
struggles to keep up with user satisfaction in the long term. Currently, feedback is largely
reactive, gathered only when issues arise, rather than being part of a continuous process
that could help CAS adjust proactively to new demands. It becomes clear that CAS could
benefit from developing a feedback loop that is adaptable and captures input not only at the
planning stage but throughout the lifecycle of the building. Such a process could help CAS
better anticipate and address changing user needs, providing a more dynamic response to
the inevitable shifts in academic research and personnel, ultimately leading to facilities that
are both functional and responsive.
5.5.3
User Involvement in the Design Process
Admittedly, involving users in the design process is essential yet complex. CAS typically
consults a small group of core users during the planning process, but it is not possible that
this group can fully represent the broad spectrum of researchers who will end up using the
space. The project manager of CAS states that these core users help establish the bigger
picture of the project. However, once a building is complete, new users with different needs
may experience frustration if the design doesn’t meet their requirements. Furthermore, the
discussion with the CAS representatives indicates that these core users do not focus on
specific needs and features.
Both the project manager and the architect of CAS noted the difficulty of balancing specific
user requests with CAS’s goal of creating flexible, adaptable spaces. Building 202, for
example, was originally designed to foster collaboration in shared areas, but individual
58
==PAGE:60==
groups ultimately requested private spaces, resulting in a more compartmentalized layout.
This situation underscores how challenging it can be to fulfill individual requests while still
achieving a cohesive design vision.
5.5.4
Facility Management and User Interaction
Facility management is another area where CAS sees room for improvement, especially in
gathering continuous feedback from users. Currently, maintenance teams take over once
construction is completed, but there’s no dedicated facility manager for each building. This
setup means that feedback from day-to-day building users—like researchers or
students—often gets lost, as there’s no formalized method for collecting it. Undoubtedly,
conducting systematic user involvement processes like POE is an efficient way to fill this
gap.
Furthermore, the CAS manager pointed out that the maintenance staff often provide
valuable insights and practical feedback to the project managers once they start managing
the building. These frontline observations are critical, as maintenance teams directly
encounter the strengths and weaknesses of the building’s design in their day-to-day
operations. However, without a formal feedback loop, these insights may only be
communicated informally, limiting their impact on future projects. It becomes clear that
establishing a regular feedback system that includes maintenance staff, CAS could capture
these practical observations and systematically incorporate them into future design
decisions.
5.5.5
Shifting Toward Generic, Adaptable Spaces
CAS is increasingly shifting toward designing more generic, adaptable buildings to
accommodate a wider range of user needs over time. This approach aims to future-proof
spaces, making them easy to modify as needs evolve. For example, Building 313 was
designed with flexibility in mind, allowing changes to furniture, walls, and installations. In
contrast, Building 202 was much more customized for specific departments, which makes
future modifications more challenging. As the architect of CAS points out, some labs in
Building 202 where certain experiments (the ones that involve animal testing) take place are
specialized and tailored to specific needs, while other labs are more standard and generic.
This shift toward generic design offers benefits but also presents challenges. Adaptable
spaces can accommodate a broader range of users, but they may not fully satisfy
departments with highly specific needs. CAS will need to clearly communicate this shift to
current users, so they understand that spaces are designed with flexibility in mind rather
than tailored to each department’s unique specifications. Balancing the needs for both
customization and adaptability will be key as CAS continues to expand its facilities portfolio.
However, the outcome of the interview with the CAS representatives is valuable, as it
becomes clear that they are willing to leverage user involvement processes like POE since
59
==PAGE:61==
they recognize the direct and the long-term effect it may have in addressing a plethora of
issues like these.
5.6
Results
Undoubtedly, the in-depth analysis of the interviews with both the users and the CAS
representatives was an invaluable process for this thesis. Delving deeper into the
perspectives of both the end-users and facility management, made the understanding of the
strengths and the weaknesses of the design easier. It was insightful to gain insights from a
management perspective, understanding the constraints and the challenges that can hinder
the implementation of ideal design solutions. However, placing emphasis into the user
experience provided a critical lens, highlighting the impact of design decisions on user
satisfaction and productivity.
The findings from this analysis underscore the significant benefits of conducting POEs. By
systematically gathering and analyzing user feedback, it is possible to identify and address
design flaws, optimize workflows, and enhance overall user satisfaction. In the context of
academic environments, where research and innovation are paramount, well-designed and
user-friendly labs can significantly contribute to the quality and efficiency of research
activities. By prioritizing user experience and incorporating regular POE into the design
process, it is possible to create spaces that foster creativity, collaboration, scientific
discovery, and well-being.

Conclusions
On a fundamental level, this thesis has demonstrated the critical role of post-occupancy
evaluations in enhancing the design and functionality of specialized academic environments,
particularly within the context of laboratory facilities. Through a detailed evaluation of the
histology lab at DTU, it has become clear that POEs can provide actionable feedback that
aligns building design with real-world user needs, which contributes significantly to the
functionality, adaptability, and sustainability of research facilities.
The findings suggest that while the initial design efforts incorporated user feedback to some
extent, gaps remained in addressing specific ergonomic, environmental, and
equipment-related needs that directly affect user satisfaction and productivity. This study
highlights the importance of including lab users, such as technicians and researchers, in
both early design phases and post-occupancy evaluations to create spaces that not only
meet operational requirements but also foster a positive working environment.
This research also underscores the value of POEs in advancing sustainable design
practices. The DTU histology lab evaluation revealed how POEs can identify areas where
energy efficiency and resource management can be improved, aligning with broader
sustainability goals. Implementing POEs on a regular basis within academic institutions
could support DTU's ongoing commitment to sustainable building management by ensuring
facilities continuously meet evolving research needs while minimizing environmental impact.
Despite the evident benefits, the study identifies several barriers to widespread POE
adoption, including cost, time constraints, and limited user involvement, especially
post-occupancy. Addressing these challenges requires a strategic shift towards integrating
POE as a standard, iterative process within the building lifecycle, from initial design to
continuous operational adjustments. By institutionalizing POEs, organizations like DTU can
enhance the adaptability and longevity of their facilities, fostering spaces that are not only
technically sound but also responsive to the needs of their academic communities.
In conclusion, this study affirms that POE, when implemented effectively, serves as a crucial
feedback mechanism, facilitating user-centered design and optimizing building performance
over time. Expanding the application of POEs in laboratory settings and academic facilities
can lead to more resilient, efficient, and user-friendly environments, ultimately contributing to
improved research outcomes and a supportive academic culture.

listen, design becomes dialogue.
