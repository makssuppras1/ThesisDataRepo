Abstract
Early stage decisions in architectural design have a great influence on the environment and energy
efficiency of a building. Still, only few Architectural studios carry out energy performance analyses
during early design stages. To address that, the purpose of this thesis is to propose and produce a tool
that enables architects to perform building energy simulations for decision support in early design
stage. The tool is created within Grasshopper3D, a parametric modelling plugin for Rhinoceros 3D,
which acts as scripting platform for the design tool Rhinoceros3D. The tool further builds upon
Honeybee which acts as a plugin for the building performance (energy) simulation tool EnergyPlus.
The architectural studio Schmidt Hammer Lassen architects has been used as a test subject to
determine the requirements and use of the tool during the development. A survey was launched across
the studio and the tool was developed in close cooperation with the company focusing on the
company’s design processes. A central requirement was brought forward by the test subject that argued
that deep knowledge in building energy design could not be expected from the users, and thus the tool
must be very simple, with few inputs and the results should be clear. As a request from the studio, the
HVAC systems in the simulations were reduced to a set of default systems settings which comply to the
Danish building codes running on the tool background. As a result of these requirements, the tool was
built with inputs based mainly on building geometry, location, fenestration, and envelope properties to
perform an energy benchmark.
The benchmark is done by comparing the single zone geometry created by the architects with a dome-
shaped building which has the lowest possible envelope area to volume ratio.
The principle is that in theory a dome with the same volume and element properties has a lower total
thermal load (heating and cooling energy demands) than any other building geometry. During its
development, the tool was put through improvements to lower simulation speed, validation of results
against other software such as Be18 and it was tested in different design set-ups. The final tests were
made at the studio and the proposed improvements were implemented in the tool.

Introduction
The building sector is the largest energy consumer and responsible for a significant share of
greenhouse gases production (IPCC, 2014). As reported, “In 2010 buildings accounted for 32% of the
total global final energy use, 19% of energy-related GHG emissions (including electricity-related),
approximately one-third of black carbon emissions, and an eighth to a third of F-gases” (Lucon et al,
2014).
The need is recognized to reduce the building energy consumption and there is an increasing pressure
on the designers to engage in more environmentally responsible practices, mainly through building
codes (Toth, 2017). It is especially important in early design, because the decisions at this stage will be
determinant on 80% of the operational costs and environmental impacts (Bogenstätter, 2000).
According to W. Wang (2005), the energy consumption can be reduced up to 30-40% without additional
costs, just by choosing the convenient shape and orientation of the building.
In an early stage of the design, the shape, orientation and layout of a building is decided upon, and all
further choices develop from there. The early decisions create further constraints that become costly
and hard to change as the project move forward (Pollock, 2009).
The integration of an architecture studio with the engineering teams from an early design stage has
proven to be beneficial to the project design, but it requires in many cases deep changes in the design
process, changes to software that supports integrated design, user training, etc, (Goldstein and Khan
2017). Also, for Architects, building performance simulation tools remain unexploited, as a result of not
considering performance assessment as part of their professional role (Madhavi et al., 2003). Also, in
the current days, there is a shortage of software that can rapidly bridge the gap between architecture
and Services Engineering regarding energy consumption in early design stage (Toth, 2017).
Normally the design is not shared with engineers before the documentation stage, which results in the
existence of many simulation tools that are created to support performance verification tasks and
support systems specifications, rather than early design development (Maassen et al., 2003).
Goal of the research
The goal of this thesis is to propose and create a tool that enables architects to perform building energy
simulations for early design stage decision support.
To achieve this, the following objectives must be met:

Tool Predevelopment
Analysis background
The tool is intended to disregard the variations from different mechanical systems in the energy
assessment as requested by SHL. It means that at this stage, the same standard HVAC system will be
considered for the tool analysis so only the energy features directly influenced by the geometrical
characteristics are influencing the results.
According to the survey, SHL Architects give importance to simulation speed over precision, also
preferring visual results with key numbers over detailed, and more accurate results. Based on those
facts, it was chosen to benchmark the studied building geometries against a specific building and use
relative values to quantify the difference. For that, both buildings should be considered to have the
same attributes such as element composition, fenestration, schedule, etc. As a starting point for the
analysis, the building attributes should comply with the national standards of the studied building’s
location.
Standards
As mentioned before, a significant part of SHL’s business is in Denmark and knowing that the Danish
building codes are among the strictest in the world (IEA, 2019), these will be used as guidance in the
tool. Although the simulation method used in EnergyPlus does not comply with the Danish codes
specifications, all the other requirements such as the materials U-values, minimum ventilation, etc. are
compliant with BR2018. In this way, the tool can be also seen as a mean to reach the building
standards more easily.
Quasi-steady-state calculations may be enough to estimate building energy consumption, but they also
are considered to be too uncertain as a method of thermal indoor environment estimation (Negendahl).
Changes in heating, cooling and ventilation strategies may lead to very different energy consumptions,
which are not included in the tool. The energy use by a building is the focus of the project, whereby
other indoor climate conditions such as CO2 concentration will also not be regarded in the tool.
As a mean to quantify the dome efficiency, a full analysis in Be18, will be done later in this report.
Dome
An important drawback about using relative values is that the simulations cannot be performed for a
building alone, there must be another building or a set of data in order to compare the results. This

Ideally the standard building has specific characteristics, e. g. a building with an average energy
consumption according to the location standards, or an especially low energy building. The building
geometry with average energy consumption was not chosen because its efficiency strongly depends on
the building geographic location, meaning that an average building that verifies one country energy
codes may not verify in others. One of the first demands from SHL was that the tool could be used in
any country to be in accordance with the company’s business needs. This would be solved by making a
standard building geometry for every location or find an especially low energy geometry to set the
benchmark standard instead.

Benchmarking process
The process of comparing a function, a product or a process with the best in the same class, by using
the appropriate method is called Benchmark. (Techtarget, 2019)
“Benchmarking features several benefits, namely increasing productivity and individual design,
enhances learning and is ultimately a vehicle to improve performance” (Elmuti, 1997).
There are several benchmarking models that can be used according to the object of study and in this
case,  it will be used an Academic/research-based model: “In these models, the academic/researcher
tend to look at it from theoretical and conceptual aspect, which may or may not have been
implemented and validated through real life applications” (Anand, 2008).
Table 8 – Chosen benchmarking model (Elmuti, 1997)
Being the most efficient building geometry regarding envelope area to volume ratio, the dome will be
the benchmark for the tool. The above benchmarking model is applied in this project, not exactly in the
same order, but following all the steps.

Tool development
After the conceptual mass model is imported into Rhinoceros as a BRep for energy analysis, it is treated
and analyzed to guide design decisions. It will give insight about alternative building forms, fenestration
and U-values. It is important to note that the resulting performance metrics from the tool’s results can
be used to validate a better design, but it does not guarantee performance or compliance achievement
by itself.

Building Program
The building program/use can also be chosen from the beginning, as school, office, retail, etc. This will
have a direct effect on the building schedules and occupancy loads. The selected program for the case
study was an office, as it is one of the most typical building types designed by SHL.
Window to wall ratio
The window to wall ratio can be specified for each direction – North, South, East, West. The tool
considers the window area evenly distributed over all the surfaces and facing the same directions, but
the window locations and heights can be specified as well.
Convert masses into zones
Every closed BRep included in the input file can be converted into a single zone, for which specific
program properties can be assigned. The characteristics that can be assigned vary from loads for
occupancy and internal electronics to settings for a HVAC system, boundary conditions and adjacencies
i.e. exterior, ground, minimum roof angles. It is set as default that there is a single zone for massing
purposes, but multiple zones can be set with minor changes to the algorithm.
Associated with this, there are side components allowing the user to specify and customize the HVAC
settings, materials and elements, based on the properties and thicknesses.

EnergyPlus is based on the ASHRAE codes, which have an inbuilt database with the typical building
elements and materials that fulfill the energy requirements for each of the US climate zones. These
elements can be automatically assigned according to the different climate zones in the US territory. To
make the tool applicable to any other part of the world, the materials must be assigned by the user and
there is a tool to assign the number and thickness of the different layers based on the database
materials called “honeybee EnergyPlus Construction” (Figure 12). In this case, the materials were
selected according to what is widely used in building construction throughout the north of Europe, such
as precast concrete, mineral wool, and gypsum panels for the interiors.
For simplicity, many of the input tool components are shared by the dome simulation and building
geometry simulation. This is done not only to reduce the number of components in the Grasshopper
canvas, but also to make sure that both simulations have the same input.
HVAC
As required by SHL, the HVAC systems should not be among the main topics of this project, despite the
importance it has for the building energy demands. If the systems characteristics are not set by the
user, EnergyPlus will use default values. To assure that the EnergyPlus simulation has the same input
data as Be18 in simulation comparisons done later in this thesis, some values still have to be set and
will be chosen to comply with the standards.
An important component to assure compliance/proximity with the minimum required in the standards is
the “set zone loads”, which allows to specify important aspects such as ventilation per person/area,
people density, airtightness, equipment density, etc. It was chosen to have an average density
equipment load per area, high airtightness, 20m² per person and a ventilation rate of 7 liter/m²/s. Even
though the compliance with the standards is not the objective of this project, it is important to have in
mind that the building optimization is a mean to achieve it later.

EnergyPlus Simulation
The “Honeybee EnergyPlus” component converts the data into an IDF file and runs it through
EnergyPlus, based on the input conditions. EnergyPlus supports a wide range of analysis types such as
Thermal gains and losses, comfort metrics, HVAC parameters, glazing solar analysis, etc., over the
==PAGE:27==
25
chosen period. For the project benchmark purposes, it was decided to use annual values for heating
and cooling demands as it is common practice as part of the total thermal loads.
Create output data
The amount of data retrieved from the simulation is proportional to the simulation time, so it was
reduced to a few factors: Annual Heating Demands, Annual Cooling Demands and Lighting energy
demands. The heating and cooling demands as part of the total thermal load are directly influenced by
the building geometry, materials and glazing area, which are some of the most important variables in
early stage architectural design.
The reason behind choosing also lighting energy demands for analysis is that it might be interesting to
check if EnergyPlus analyzes if there is enough daylight to not use artificial lighting, but it was later
discovered that the software bases the artificial lighting energy analysis only on the schedule and floor
area. This fact gives a hint to later improve the lighting energy analysis by creating an algorithm that
performs a daylight simulation for a full year to find out at what time and which areas of the building will
have an amount of lux higher than 300. And by crossing information with the building schedules, it is
possible to check how many hours the light does not need to be on. Although it may contribute to the
overall accuracy of the model, with the current advances in low energy lighting solutions such as LED
technology, the difference in the overall energy consumption may not be very significant.

from SHL, the wording was replaced by “XX% more energy than a dome with equal volume”, as it may be

Dome Simulation – Implementation
To maximize the relevancy of the study it is imperative that both the building to benchmark and the
dome share the same characteristics:
Volume: The first part of the sphere simulation receives the volume of the building to benchmark in
cubic meters and converts it to the necessary radius the dome needs to have in order to feature the
same volume. The following figure has an example of how a building’s volume is converted into a dome
with roughly the same volume.
==PAGE:31==
29
Figure 17 – Algorithm to create a dome with the same volume as the building
Elements: Since on the dome there is no difference between roof and walls, the materials and
properties for the roof of the studied building will be the same as the walls. The element characteristics
can be adjusted but it will not matter, as the results are relative, not absolute. The U-value used for the
roof and walls is 0,2 W/m²K which complies to the BR18 standards.
Windows: The windows were double pane and the Window to wall ratio (“glazing ratio” in Honeybee) can
be chosen for the four main cardinal point directions – North, South, East and West. Even though the
dome doesn’t have specific walls facing those directions, EnergyPlus considers a certain amount of wall
and corresponding window area facing those directions.
Although very convenient, EnergyPlus has some limitations. One limitation that has a large influence in
the dome simulations is the inability to compute curved surfaces. To use the dome for this project, the
problem was mitigated by converting the curved surface into triangular surfaces. A set of meshing
components was used to accomplish this.
Figure 18 – Algorithm to divide the dome curved surface into planar surfaces
==PAGE:32==
30
The slider marked in green in the picture above enables the user to control the number of triangles the
dome is divided into. It is important to have the curved surface covered of as much triangles as possible
to have a better approximation, but not too much as this slows down the computing time.
Figure 19 – Dome with planar surfaces
The best compromise between CPU use and simulation accuracy was found when the overall
simulations result did not diverge more than 1% from the maximum detail. Still, the current algorithm
implies running two simulations at the same time, meaning that the overall simulation will take a
considerable amount of time.
Dome Simulation Optimized
Bearing in mind that according to the survey, the simulation speed should be a priority, and the
variability of the building geometries requires new and different simulations, the tool element that has
the least variability and therefore more potential to be optimized is the dome. If the full simulation of
the dome is replaced by a set of mathematical functions, the computing time can be greatly reduced - if
the result deviation is acceptable.
A set of simulations was done for a dome, in which the main input variables are the volume and the
window to wall ratio. As for output it will be the total thermal load (heating + cooling loads) and the
lighting electricity for a full year. There were considered minimal air exchanges with the exterior, and
high airtightness so the results could be related mainly with the geometry, wall and window areas and
their properties.
The simulations were done considering a Window to wall ratio from 0 to 0,5 with intervals of 0,05, and a
dome volume of 10m³, 100m³, 500m³, 1000m³, 5000m³, 10000m³, in a total of 66 simulations.

Simulation time
The simulation time is directly influenced by the complexity of the model. As said before, EnergyPlus
only analyses planar surfaces, which means that by using a dome, the curved surface needs to be
converted in a set of planar surfaces. The number of surfaces will dictate the accuracy of the model.
Unless there are curved surfaces on the models to benchmark or a high model complexity, the number
of surfaces in these models will be smaller than the dome.
For weather files other than Denmark, e.g. Shanghai, the full dome simulation should be done. On an
ordinary computer (Intel Core i5 dual core, 8Gb RAM) this is the average timetable for dome
simulations:

higher volume-to-heated-floor-area ratio. This means that a one zone building with high ceiling is likely

climate inside acceptable parameters.
According to Mortensen (2018), the most complete approach to assess the Energy frame of a building
according to BR18, is the “Combined Approach”, based on a calculation made in the software Be18. In
the software a model of the building is “built”, including descriptive parameters for constructions (U-

climate simulations with, for example BSIM. The internal heat loads from equipment and people is also
quantified with improved data regarding the number of people and the amount of heat emitted. This

Conclusion
The thesis demonstrates how the tool was accepted and developed to enable architects to perform
building energy simulations for decision support in early design stage. By using a software already
known in the studio - Rhino Grasshopper- facilitated the acceptance of the tool and allowed a more
organic adaptation to the current practices at SHL.
Even though a dome is an efficient building regarding envelope area-to-volume ratio, it does not
necessarily mean that it has an advantage over other buildings to comply with the building codes.
However, evidence was found that especially for a larger volume, the dome can have a good
performance, and therefore, be a valid benchmark. The use of a curved surface of the dome was
difficult to implement due to the EnergyPlus constraints and the added simulation time it requires. The
envelope area-to-volume ratio was successfully used as benchmark metrics.
By comparing the results from the Grasshopper tool with Be18 for the different case studies, it was
found that there is a good consistency between the two methods.
SHL accepted well the challenge of thinking beyond the current methods and embraced a tool that
supports sustainable practices.
The author believes that a relevant tool was produced and sees it as a platform for further
developments.
Further Developments
The evolution of this project touched a broader set of study areas related to building energy
optimization that architects can benefit from in many strands. The further development of this tool and
the creation of other snippets to perform further energy optimization may be an asset for the studio.
Multiple zone analysis
As an architectural project is developed and gains complexity, the toolset should be able to keep up
with it. Building a snippet that enables the architects to subdivide the building into multiple zones and
floors is a need that has been expressed by the studio. This can help to create building masterplans, by
taking advantage of thermal load discrepancies in different rooms/areas and improve fenestration
distribution more efficiently. The added complexity may require more advanced knowledge in energy
optimization by the end user.
HVAC systems analysis
Another important step would be to include different HVAC system options in the tool analysis, so it
would be possible to make a more accurate estimation of the building energy consumption. This would
==PAGE:50==
48
allow to optimize the building geometry and other characteristics further, by considering each system
particularities.
Window shading
Window overhangs and side fins are an important object of study in optimizing the energy consumption
in a building. According to Ebrahimpour (2010) “using of the most appropriate overhang or side fin that
has been established for the single clear pane glazing is more useful for any direction of window than
the advanced glazing windows (double clear pane glazing, low-E glazing)”. Building a snippet that
enables the user to test different window shading solutions to increase thermal gains in heating season
and reduce cooling demands is another area of improvement.
User Comfort
Another concern that was shown by SHL as having potential for development is glare analysis. By taking
advantage of Ladybug Grasshopper capabilities, a glare analysis can be performed throughout the year,
giving a solid basis for fenestration optimization.
