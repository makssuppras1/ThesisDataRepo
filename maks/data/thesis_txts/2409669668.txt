Abstract
Technical University of Denmark
Department of Mechanial Engineering
Section of Construction and Product Development
Master of Science, Engineering Design and Applied Mechanics
Robust design using kinematic constraint analyses and application of
paired constraints conversion
By Rasmus Lindegaard Tulinius
This thesis covers early stage robust design principles.
The main focus of the thesis, is kinematic
constraint analysis. The existing robust design tools: Kinematic analysis and System clarity analysis,
are described and applied. The two robust design tools are extended by the invention of Paired constraints
conversion. Paired constraints conversion is an interpretation method, that clariﬁes how a body can be
rotationally constrained by two translational constraints. The extended robust design tools are used to
analyse a backwater valve designed by the start-up company, Urban Water. From the analyses, over-
constraints are identiﬁed, and how to remove these. Thus providing potential improvements reducing
unnecessary and damaging friction in the backwater valve mechanisms. The robust design tools are also
used to identify critical functional ﬂaws in the backwater valve. Once found, potential solutions are
suggested, only involving replacement of simple parts. Selected suggested improvements are physically
build and tested, proving the theory to be true.

Resumé
Danmarks Tekniske Universitet
Institut for Mekanisk Teknologi
Sektion for Konstruktion og Produktudvikling
Kandidat, Konstruktion og Mekanik
Robust design ved brug af kinematiske styrings analyser samt anvendelse af
’paired constraints conversion’
Af Rasmus Lindegaard Tulinius
Denne afhandling omhandler tidlig stadie robuste design metoder. Hoved fokus af afhandlingen er kine-
matisk styrings analyser. De eksisterende robuste design værktøjer: "Kinematic analysis" og "System
clarity analysis" er beskrevet og anvendt. De to robuste design værktøjer er udvidet med opﬁndelsen af
"Paired constraints conversion". Paired constraints conversion er en fortolkningsmetode, der tydeliggør
hvordan et legeme kan være rotations styret af to translationsstyringer. De udvidede robuste design
værktøjer er brugt til at analysere en højvandslukke designet af startup ﬁrmaet, Urban Water.
Fra
analyserne er overstyringer identiﬁceret, samt hvordan disse kan fjernes. Dette muliggjorde skabelsen
af potentielle forbedringer, der kan fjerne unødvendig og skadende friktion i højvandslukkens mekanis-
mer. De robuste design værktøjer er også brugt til at identiﬁcere kritiske funktionsfejl i højvandslukken.
Da funktionsfejlene var fundet, kunne et løsnings forslag skabes, der kun involverer udskiftning af sim-
ple dele. Udvalgte løsningsforslag blev bygget fysisk og testet i en prototype, der viste sig at virke i
overensstemmelse med teorien.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Before the industrial revolution, that took place in the 18th century, most products were hand made, and
by that, crafted to ﬁt itself. Each part of a product would be made to ﬁt the existing parts of the product,
by continuously applying small adjustments until the parts match. With the industrial revolution came
machine processing, and with that, mass production of single parts. At later stages these parts were to
be assembled with other mass produced parts. This procedure drastically increased the material wealth,
but at the same time brought new challenges.
The main challenge, from a mechanical perspective,
was to make the parts able to ﬁt one another without any post process adjustments. This developed
two branches of solutions: First, the ability to produce parts with high precision. Second, the ability
to design products cleverly so that production variations have little impact on the functionality of the
products. The second solution, is today called principles of robust design. Robust design is a relatively
new knowledge that today is still undergoing development. Robust design principles covers many areas
within product design. This thesis, however is dedicated to the development of a few selected robust
design tools. The primary focus of this thesis, is constraint analyses and robust design tools that aid
this.
1.1
Objective
The main objective of this thesis is to expand the level of detailed information gained from a constraint
analysis of any given multi body system. This is to be achieved by extending the two existing robust
design tools: Kinematic analysis and System clarity analysis. This will be done by thoroughly deﬁning
what a rotational constraint is, in relation to a translational constraint, and integrating this into the
existing tools.
The use of the extended tools will be explained step by step in order to enable others to apply the
extended models to any other case by themselves.
To showcase the value of the extended tools, these will be used to optimize two diﬀerent mechanisms in
a physical product made by the company, Urban Water.
To validate the eﬀect of the extended tools, physical implementations of the resulting optimizations will
be crafted and tested in a prototype of the Urban Water product.
The secondary objective of this thesis is to help Urban Water to overcome some real, and pressing,
mechanical challenges they experience with their product. This will primarily be achieved, by lowering
the required force for each mechanism to work, by applying robust design principles, that ultimately will
lower internal counter productive forces. In other words; the mechanisms will potentially be redesigned
to not contain any over-constraints by using the extended kinematic and system clarity analysis.

CHAPTER 2
Theoretical models
This chapter will contain a mix between deriving theoretical models and which terminologies are used
throughout the thesis. The goal of this is to allow for full focus upon the optimization process in the
later chapters, rather than how each eﬀect is interpreted. This is achieved by deﬁning a range of eﬀects,
and how these are interpreted. All the deﬁned eﬀects, and interpretations, will be used in the application
of the robust design principles, in later chapters.
2.1
Moment of interest
Throughout this thesis, all models and illustrations, only consider the depicted moment of interest. To
provide a clear example of moment of interest, a pendulum in motion is used. The pendulum in Figure 2.1,
is presented in two diﬀerent moments of interest. Figure 2.1A depict a moment of interest, where the
pendulum is at rest, and thereby has an instantaneous velocity of zero. Figure 2.1B depict a moment of
interest, where the pendulum is in motion, having a horizontal instantaneous velocity direction. In any
other moment of interest, between these two, the pendulum will have a diﬀerent velocity and velocity
direction.

2.2
Degrees of freedom
The term Degrees Of Freedom (DOF), account for the number of independent motions a body or structure
can have in either two dimensions (2D) or three dimensions (3D). In 2D, a single body has three DOF,
two translational, and one rotational. In 3D, a single body has six DOF, three translational and three
rotational. All motions will be described in relation to the Cartesian coordinate system with the three
axes named X, Y and Z as seen in Figure 2.2. This is the same notation as in [1], where supplementary
explanation can be found in section 1.1 and 1.2.
Figure 2.2: Cartesian coordinate system that also describe the six DOF in 3D
2
==PAGE:16==
The DOF of a body, or a set of bodies, is always in relation to a locally stationary point or object. In
all cases, in this thesis, the reference object is called "the frame". As the frame is the locally stationary
reference, it is always considered fully constrained. As an example: The frame could be a wall, or a table,
that a mechanism is connected to, via a joint, or a set of joints. Anything that is rigidly connected to the
frame, like via a welding, is considered part of the frame. As done in [2] section 13.3, when calculating
the total DOF in a system, only non frame bodies are counted. This provide an easy calculation of the
total number of DOF, as shown in Equation 2.1 and Equation 2.2 for 2D and 3D respectively. Where:
DOFT is the total number of DOF without considering constraints, and Nb is the total number of bodies
in the system.
2D :
DOFT = Nb · 3
(2.1)
3D :
DOFT = Nb · 6
(2.2)
It is important to note, that the frame can be a part in a mechanism. If this is the case, then the
movement of the body, or bodies, in the mechanism, is seen in relation to the one selected part. This is
clariﬁed by using a known mechanism as example: The four bar mechanism. The four bar mechanism
is, as the name suggests, built of four bars and four revolute joints. Two models of a four bar mechanism
is depicted in Figure 2.3.
Figure 2.3: Two models of the same four bar mechanism in 2D
The four bars are easily recognisable in Figure 2.3A. However, the properties of the mechanism is exactly
the same for both presentations, despite the fact that the model in Figure 2.3B, is seemingly missing a
fourth bar. In this thesis, the black coloured parts in models, are always considered the frame of the
model. When calculating the total number of DOF (DOFT ), both models in Figure 2.3, are considered a
three body systems (Nb=3). The calculation for the DOFT , can be seen in Equation 2.3 and Equation 2.4
for 2D and 3D respectively. Supplementary explanation can be found in [2] section 13.3
2D :
DOFT = Nb · 3
→
DOFT = 3 · 3 = 9
(2.3)
3D :
DOFT = Nb · 6
→
DOFT = 3 · 6 = 18
(2.4)
2.3
Constraints
When a body is hindered from moving in a particular motion, the body is considered constrained in
that particular motion. When a body is hindered from moving by a single constraint the resulting DOF
(DOF) of the body is reduced by one. To clarify this, an example calculation of a single body in 2D
having a single motion constrained, is shown in Equation 2.5. Note that a single body in 2D, has a total
of three DOF (DOFT = 3).
DOF = DOFT −"single motion constraint"
→
DOF = 3 −1 = 2
(2.5)
As there are two types of motions, there are also two types of constraints. Translational constraints and
rotational constraints. Despite this seeming trivial, this will be explained in the following sections, for
reasons that will be shown throughout the thesis.

2.3.1
Translational constraint
In order to eliminate any potential misunderstandings, the deﬁnition of translation is stated: A body
is translating when all points of the body is moving the same distance in the same direction. This is
illustrated in a 2D example in Figure 2.4, where a bar purely translates in the X-direction. Note that
multiple motions can happen at once, and that the individual motions can be identiﬁed, by the use of
superposition.
Figure 2.4: Example of pure translation in the X-direction
A translational constraint is, as the name suggest, when a body is hindered from translating in any
one direction. Physically, this happens when a force is applied to a body in the particular direction.
The constraining force is typically the reaction force from an interaction to another body or frame. An
example of a single translational constraint, is shown in Figure 2.5. The same ﬁgure is drawn in a simpler
2D view with added DOF and constraint indications, in Figure 2.6.
Figure 2.5: A pin attached to a frame acting as an X-constraint to a ﬂat body lying upon the frame
Figure 2.6: 2D view of Figure 2.5. Purple arrows depict free DOF, while red arrows depict constraints
In Figure 2.5 and Figure 2.6 the body is hindered from translating in the X-direction by the reaction
force from the interaction between the body and the pin frame. The body is still able to translate in
the Y-direction, and to rotate around the Z-axis. This translation and rotation, is called a Y-translation
and an Rz-rotation respectively. This is the same terminology of a translational constraints, as in [1],
where supplementary explanation can be found in section 1.3 and 1.4.
Note that the body is able to rotate 90°, making the pin connection a Y-constraint, rather than an X-
constraint. However, recall that models are depicted in moments of interest, as explained in section 2.1.

2.3.2
Over-constraint
When the position of a body is deﬁned by more than a single interaction, in any one direction in the
same line, the body is considered over-constrained. To illustrate this, a body constrained by two frame
pins in the X-direction, is shown in Figure 2.7. Assuming that the body is incompressible, the body and
the frame can only be assembled if the dimensions match exactly.
Figure 2.7: Example of a body over-constrained in the X-direction
In reality, product size variations are unavoidable, however bodies are neither incompressible. If a body
like this, was produced and assembled to a double pin frame in reality, the body would either only have
contact with one of the pins. Or, the body would experience stress and deﬂection in order to ﬁt to the
pin positions. It is possible to create a near perfect ﬁt, by using narrow tolerances or special "production
methods". An example of a production method that creates two near perfect hole and pin connections, is
when a stapler pushes the two pins of a clips through paper. Supplementary explanations can be found
in [1] section 1.5.
When taking a closer look at a single pin connection, it is arguably already an over-constraint. The
same as explained above applies to each side of the pin. Either, only one of the sides of the pin, is in
contact with the body, or both is, while the body experiences stress and deﬂection. This is illustrated
in Figure 2.8.
Figure 2.8: Single pin connection with exaggerated loose ﬁt (A) and tight ﬁt (B)
The internal pin and hole over-constraint, is a local over-constraint. A local over-constraint, do not have
an eﬀect on other constraints in a system. For this reason, the internal over-constraint, can in many
cases be ignored. However, if rotation around the pin is needed, then the pin and hole connection is
required to be a loose ﬁt. Supplementary explanation can be found in [1] section 1.13
In this thesis the double sided constraint of a pin and hole connection is not interpreted as an over-
constraint. The reason for this is that the focus of the thesis is on macroscopic eﬀects of mechanisms
that are insigniﬁcantly aﬀected by local over-constraints.
An important note: In order to reduce the amount of information in each model; when having a double
sided constraint, like the pin and hole, only a single red arrow is depicting the constraint. The reason
for doing so, is to make it easier to count the numbers of constraints in a model.

Nesting force
A constraint can also be single sided as the example in Figure 2.9, show. To maintain the constraint,

2.3.4
Rotational constraint
A rotational constraint is, as the name suggest, when a body is hindered from rotating.
Similar to
a translational constraint, in order for a rotational constraint to exist, a moment to counter potential
angular acceleration is required. For this reason, the deﬁnition of moment is described. An illustration
of a bolt with two forces applied, is shown in Figure 2.10.
Figure 2.10: Illustration of a bolt with applied forces generating a moment
The bolt in Figure 2.10 is applied a pair of parallel forces, that act in opposite direction and has a
perpendicular distance d1 between them. The pair of parallel forces generate a moment in the bolt, that
is ultimately used to twist the bolt in place. The moment generated by the pair of forces is calculated
in Equation 2.6, where M is the moment, F is the force and d1 is the perpendicular distance.
M = F · d1
(2.6)
In order for the bolt to stop rotating, despite being applied this moment, a rotational constraint is
required.
A moment can only be countered by another moment, and since a moment can only be
generated by a pair of forces, a rotational constraint will always include a pair of translational constraints.
When a bolt is tightening, it is because the thread of the bolt is pressed against the thread in the hole,
creating friction. This friction is what is hindering the bolt from rotating further, and can be interpreted
as two translational constraints. In Figure 2.11, the described friction is depicted as two Y-constraints
upon the thread of the bolt.
6
==PAGE:20==
Figure 2.11: Bolt constrained by friction interpreted as two Y-constraints
To describe a more intentionally created constraint situation, the pin and hole example is used again. Two
diﬀerent bodies that are rotationally constrained by pin and hole connections, are shown in Figure 2.12.
Figure 2.12: Two bodies rotationally constrained by the pair of X-constraints
With the deﬁnition of what a rotational constraint really is, the following can be stated: Rotational
constraints diﬀers from translational constraints by the fact that, from one body to another, a pure
rotational constraint cannot exist. This is also stated in [1] section 1.20. A body can be rotationally
constrained, but not by a rotational constraint. It is rather a pair of translational constraints generating
a moment that is rotationally constraining the body. Within constraint analysis, the term rotational
constraint is used, as it is a simple and easy communication. However, it is a matter of interpretation. The
user of a constraint analysis simply chose to interpret a pair of constraints as a translational constraint
and a rotational constraint. With this, the term "Paired constraints conversion", is introduced.
2.4
Paired constraints conversion
Paired constraints conversion is a systematic procedure to identify pairs of translational constraints, that
are rotationally constraining a body. Once a pair is identiﬁed, one of the translational constraints is then
converted into being interpreted as a "rotational constraint". The beneﬁt of doing so, is to more clearly
depict which of the body’s DOF have been constrained. To illustrate the paired constraints conversion
process, the models in Figure 2.12 are used.
In Figure 2.12, the two X-constraints are positioned a perpendicular distance d apart from each other.
It is assumed that the depicted distance is suﬃciently large to be able to counter a moment, without
generating a critically large force.
In the theoretical world, where bodies are incompressible and unbreakable, any perpendicular distance
larger than zero, would be suﬃcient.
However, in the real world, bodies can deﬂect and break.
A
perpendicular distance that is approaching the value zero, will require a force approaching inﬁnity, to
counter any size of moment. A critical amount of force, will deﬂect or break a body in the real world.
How big the perpendicular distance has to be, will depend on the body material, the body geometry and
the size of the applied moment. For this reason, the theoretical demand, is just a "suﬃcient" distance.
The two X-constraints are hereby identiﬁed as a pair of translational constraints that is rotationally
constraining the body. To depict this in the model, any one of the translational constraints is converted
to be interpreted as a rotational constraint. This is depicted by a yellow line, marking the pair, and
7
==PAGE:21==
crossing out the converted translational constraint and ﬁnally writing the interpretation as a yellow
constraint. This conversion can be seen applied to three 2D examples, in Figure 2.13.
Figure 2.13: Two bodies rotationally constrained by the pair of X-constraints
It is possible to have a rotationally constrained body, without having any parallel constraints.
An

2.4.1
Paired constraints conversion in three dimensions
In the 3D domain, there are three translations and three rotations. This provides more options for the
paired constraints conversion, thus making it more complex. The argument is exactly the same in 3D:
In order to constrain a rotational motion, a moment around the same axis of rotation is required. A
moment is generated by a force and a perpendicular distance to the point of rotation. This distance is in
Figure 2.13 called d. In 3D, this distance becomes more tricky to identify. To explain how to identify this
distance, the force from each constraint is ignored, and only the point where the constraint is applied, is
considered.
Two points that do not coincide in 3D, have seven relative position possibilities. To ease the explanation,
the three possible relative positions in 2D, are explained at ﬁrst. Figure 2.15 is used as illustration to
the following explanation, where the position of point B, C and D, with respect to point A, is discussed.
8
==PAGE:22==
Figure 2.15: The three relative position possibilities for two points in 2D
1. The ﬁrst position, is when the two points share the same X-axis, just as the two ends of a horizontal
bar. This is also called an X-axis coinciding position. In Figure 2.15, this correspond the the region
that point B is in. Points in this region, can only have a distance to point A, in the X-direction.
This X-distance, together with a pair of Y-forces, can generate a moment around the Z-axis
2. The second position, is when the two points share the same Y-axis, just as the two ends of a
vertical bar. This is also called an Y-axis coinciding position. In Figure 2.15, this correspond to
the blue region that point C is in. Points in this region, can only have a distance to point A, in
the Y-direction. This Y-distance together with a pair of X-forces, can generate a moment around
the Z-axis
3. The third position, is when the two points do not share either X- or Y-axis, just as the two ends
of an inclined bar. In Figure 2.15, this correspond to the green region that point D is in. Points in
this region, have a distance to point A, in both the X- and Y-direction. Thus, either a pair of X-
or Y-forces, can generate a moment around the Z-axis in this position.
In 3D, it is necessary to expand the deﬁnition of what a moment is. When a pair of forces have a
perpendicular distance between each other, they generate a moment, with an axis of rotation, that is
perpendicular to both the distance and the force direction. 2D cases are easier to grasp than 3D cases. A
reason for this, is that in 2D, the only possible axis of rotation, the Z-axis, is perpendicular to both the
X- and Y-axis. For this reason, the demand for double perpendicularity, between the axis of rotation, the
forces applied and the distance between them, is always fulﬁlled for as long as a perpendicular distance
between the forces exist.
In 3D, the double perpendicularity need to be actively considered. Luckily, the Cartesian coordinate
system provide three axes, that fulﬁl this double perpendicularity. By this, a simple rule can be written:
To generate a moment around any axis, a pair of forces acting in another axis is needed, with a distance
between them in the direction along the last axis. To clarify this, all possibilities are listed:
• An Rx-moment can be generated by a pair of Y-forces with a Z-distance between them
• An Rx-moment can be generated by a pair of Z-forces with a Y-distance between them
• An Ry-moment can be generated by a pair of X-forces with a Z-distance between them
• An Ry-moment can be generated by a pair of Z-forces with an X-distance between them
• An Rz-moment can be generated by a pair of X-forces with a Y-distance between them
• An Rz-moment can be generated by a pair of Y-forces with an X-distance between them
9
==PAGE:23==
In 3D, it is diﬃcult to illustrate entire regions. For this reason, only a single point in each region is
illustrated. A cube has eight corners, that correspond exactly to the number of relative positions two
points in 3D can have. The seven relative positions, are illustrated in Figure 2.16 and listed below.
Figure 2.16: The seven relative position possibilities for two points in 3D
The ﬁrst three relative positions, are the same as in 2D. As these are explained below Figure 2.15, only
the remaining four relative positions are explained:
4. The fourth position, is when the two points share the same Z-axis. This is also called a Z-axis
coinciding position. Two points in this position can only have a distance between them along the
Z-axis. This distance can be used together with a pair of X-forces, to generate a moment around
the Y-axis. The distance can also be used together with a pair of Y-forces, to generate a moment
around the X-axis.
5. The ﬁfth position, is when the two points share the same X-Z plane, but have no axis coinciding.
Two points in this position have a distance between them along both the X-axis and the Z-axis.
The Z-distance can be used to generate a moment, as explained in the fourth position. The X-
distance can be used together with a pair of Y-forces, to generate a moment around the Z-axis.
The X-distance can also be used together with a pair of Z-forces, to generate a moment around
the Y-axis.
6. The sixth position, is when the two points share the same Y-Z plane, but have no axis coinciding.
Two points in this position have a distance between them along both the Y-axis and the Z-axis.
The Z-distance can be used to generate a moment, as explained in the fourth position. The Y-
distance can be used together with a pair of X-forces, to generate a moment around the Z-axis.
The Y-distance can also be used together with a pair of Z-forces, to generate a moment around
the X-axis.
7. The seventh and last position, is when the two points share no directional position. Two points in
this position have a distance between them along all axial directions. For this reason, any pair of
forces can be used to generate a moment around any, to the forces, perpendicular axis.

2.4.2
Paired constraints conversion summary
Whenever two constraints provide forces that are able to generate a moment, the two constraints are
able to constrain a body from rotating in the speciﬁc rotation. A paired constraint conversion, is a
communication tool, that interprets one of two constraints in a pair, as a rotational constraint. A paired
constraints conversion, is an optional application in order to make a kinematic analysis more clear. As
some pairs of constraints have two paired constraints conversion options, the conversion is chosen in
relation to other existing constraints in the system.
2.5
Part to part interaction
In reality, whenever two parts interact, one or both parts will deﬂect to some degree. Take a steel ball
upon a plastic table for example. The weight of the steel ball, regardless of size, will be enough to deﬂect
the table slightly. The interaction area of the table will be shaped to match the curvature of the steel
ball. An exaggerated illustration of this deﬂection, is presented in 2D, in Figure 2.21.
Figure 2.21: Interaction and exaggerated deﬂection between steel ball and plastic table
The interface between the ball and the table is a surface. This surface is what is used to transfer forces
from the one part to the other. When the force is transferred from one part to the other, both parts are
12
==PAGE:26==
stressed an amount that is equal to the force divided by the interaction surface area. When the surface
area is low, the stress in both parts will increase. This is called the Hertz contact stress, and more about
it, can be found in [3] section 10.3. Within a Hertz contact stress zone like this, there will always be a
single point that is more stressed than any other point in the zone. This single point is the subject of
the next section.
2.5.1
Point contact
In a kinematic perspective, the interaction seen in Figure 2.21, is called a point contact.
A point
contact is the interpretation that two parts interact only in a single point, despite that this would cause
the interaction area to be approximately zero, thus cause an inﬁnite amount of stress in both parts.
However, in kinematics, stresses are not considered, which is why the point contact is acceptable in a
kinematic perspective. The beneﬁt of the point contact interpretation, is that there can only be a single
force acting in the normal direction within this interaction point. In Figure 2.21, the single force would
act in the Y-direction. This also means that only a single translational constraint can exist in a point
contact. In this case it would act as a Y-constraint.
2.5.2
Line contact
The same illustration can be made for a line contact, however the steel ball is replaced with a steel
cylinder, as shown in Figure 2.22. The same way as the ball contact was reduced to a single point,
so can the cylinder contact be reduced to a line contact. The kinematic properties of a line contact is
that it constrains translation normal to the surfaces, while also constraining rotation around the double
perpendicular direction. In this case, the line contact acts as a Y-constraint and an Rx-constraint.
Figure 2.22: Interaction and exaggerated deﬂection between steel cylinder and plastic table
In a kinematic perspective, it is possible to reduce the line contact into two point contacts. The two
point contacts will have the exact same kinematic properties. This can be shown by using the paired
constraints conversion. The line contact, and the point contact notion, is shown in Figure 2.23, along
with the paired constraints conversion. Notice that the pair of constraints, are Y-constraints with a
Z-distance between them, enabling a conversion to an Rx-constraint.

2.5.3
Surface contact
A surface contact is when two ﬂat surfaces interact, like a box upon a table. The Kinematic properties of
a surface contact, is that it constraints the translation normal to the surface, while also constraining two
rotations around any two axes that are perpendicular to the normal vector of the surface. For simplicity,
this thesis only consider rotations around the, for the model chosen, coordinate axes. As an example:
If the normal vector to a surface contact is in the Y-direction, then, the surface constrains rotations
around the X- and Z-axis. This is not an entirely correct consideration, however it is more simple and is
therefore chosen as a project conﬁnement. To ﬁnd more about this, see [1] section 1.8.
Again, in a kinematic perspective, a surface contact can be reduced to three point contacts. These three
point contacts will have the same kinematic properties as the surface contact, and can again be shown
by paired constraints conversion, as done in Figure 2.24.

2.8
Kinematic analysis
The Kinematic analysis, is the ﬁrst of the two Robust design tools, that is to be extended with the paired
constraints conversion. The original Kinematic analysis will be explained at ﬁrst, and then the extension
will be added.
The kinematic analysis, is a non-visual tool to describe the constraints in a system, by listing all con-
straints as numbers in a table. A table from a kinematic analysis, is called a kinematic table. The
advantage of a kinematic table is, that it is small and dense in informations, making it easy to read. In a
kinematic table, "1" means a constraint is present, while "0" means no constraint is present. This is the
positive constraint terminology and can be found in [4] and Appendix C. There are other methods, where
one would use "0" for a constrained motion, "1" for a free motion and "-1" for an over-constraint. This
is the negative constraint terminology and can be found in [5] section 4. However, it is more intuitive
to operate with only positive numbers. The extended kinematic analysis uses multiple operations to the
numbers of constraints, which is why the positive constraint terminology is used.
A 2D model, of three bars connected to each other by revolute joints, is used as an example to describe
the anatomy of a kinematic analysis. In Figure 2.30, two models are presented to also recall previously
described terminology. The models are depicting the exact same system, using the terminology from [2]
section 13.3. Only the non frame parts are counted as bodies with DOF. As model A is simpler to draw,
this drawing method is primarily used throughout the thesis.

2.9
System clarity analysis
The system clarity analysis, is the second robust design tool where the paired constraints conversion is
integrated. The system clarity analysis is a useful tool to visually locate over- and under-constraints in
a system. The tool highlights the connection between all bodies, in three or six motions whether in 2D,
or 3D. Figure 2.32, shows a simple example of a body (B), that is constrained with respect to the frame
(F), as following: Fully constrained in the X-direction, partially in the Y-direction and not at all in the
Rz-rotation. From the diagram alone, the following can be concluded: B cannot move in the X-direction.
B can not move towards F, but can move away from F, in the Y-direction. B is completely free to rotate
in the Rz-rotation. This is the same notation as in [6], that can also be found in Appendix D.
As explained in subsection 2.3.2, a double X-constraint is not considered an over-constraint. B is slightly
smaller that the gap in F, and is therefore only in contact with one side at a time.
The body can
only move an insigniﬁcant distance, before colliding with the other side. For this reason, the body is
considered as constrained in both X-directions at the same time.
Figure 2.32: Simple, 2D, system clarity analysis example
However, as described in subsection 2.3.3, all single sided connections are assumed to be maintained by a
suﬃciently large nesting force. For this reason, single and double sided connections, are not distinguished.
All constraints are interpreted as double sided. This will also be indicated in the following system clarity
analyses, by a double line.
The kinematic analysis excels in keeping an overview of the current and desired number of constraints
in a system. The system clarity analysis excels in showing which actions must be taken, in order to
reach that goal. In a system clarity analysis, over-constraints can be identiﬁed by loops of constraints.
In Figure 2.33, three bodies and a frame is modelled. In this example only the X-direction is considered.
In the diagram, a loop between B1, B2 and F can be seen. The loop indicates the presence of an over-
constraint. The X-position of B2 is fully deﬁned by both F and B1. It is only possible to assemble this
assumed rigid system, if the alignment is perfect.
The system clarity analysis indicates that either the F-B1, B1-B2 or B2-F X-connection, should be
removed in order to improve the system, as doing so would eliminate the loop. More about the system
clarity analysis tool, can be found in [6] and Appendix D.

table and the pencil, is added. For simplicity, the obstacle is considered a part of the table. In 2D, the
pencil has two contact points, providing two Y-constraints. Friction, between the table and the pencil,
provides an X-constraint. The described is shown in Figure 2.36, with paired constraints conversion

2.11
Free body diagram
In mechanics, a Free Body Diagram (FBD) is commonly used. In an FBD, any number of bodies are
modelled isolated from their full system. Surroundings aﬀecting the isolated body, or bodies, are depicted
in an FBD as forces, or in a kinematic perspective, constraints. When performing a constraint analysis,
one have to be careful with an FBD. Conventionally, an FBD is used to analyse forces and moments.
Forces and moments between bodies are always double sided. Double sided means, that if one body is
applied a force from another, the other body is applied the same force back. However, in a constraint
analysis, constraints are not double sided. It can still be advantageous to use an FBD if, for an instance,
the full system is to big and advanced in one global model. For this reason, a simple example showing
how constraints should be applied to bodies in an FBD, is provided. In the example, a bar and a box is
the full system analysed. The same joint can apply constraints diﬀerently to bodies, depending on the
situation. For this reason, the full system is presented in two diﬀerent situations, as shown in Figure 2.53.
Recall moment of interest, as explained in section 2.1.
Figure 2.53: Illustration of a bar and a box forming an arbitrary system in two situations
First, a physical description of the example is provided:
The full system in Figure 2.53, consist of a frame (F), a bar, a box and two revolute joints connecting
the three. In the ﬁrst situation, the box do not have contact with the frame, thus being free to rotate.
In this situation, the bar is also, and independently, free to rotate. In the second situation the box has
contact with the frame, and this is maintained by a nesting force. By this contact, the box is no longer
free to rotate, and neither is the bar.
Conventionally when performing a constraint analysis, each joint, and the constraints they provide, is
independently counted and added up. The relative position of each joint is then located in a global
model of the full system. To state a point, the full system, being the bar, the box and the frame, is
now assumed to be too big and advanced to grasp in a global model. While performing the conventional
constraint analysis, one would be missing the relative position of each joint, as shown in Figure 2.54.
Figure 2.54: Conventional constraint analysis, counting joints and their constraints

2.12
The eﬀect of play
An experienced robust designer know that play in joints has a large, and positive, impact towards the
robustness of a design. How play aﬀect the robustness of a design, will be discussed in this section.
Additionally, to speed up explanations in following chapter, the eﬀect of play will be communicated as
"weak constraints". How weak constraints are to be interpreted, will also be deﬁned in this section.
As the only play in joints, that will be encountered in this thesis, is in revolute joints, such a joint is
used as an example. In Figure 2.57, a frame (F) and a body (B) is connected via a revolute joint.
Figure 2.57: A body connected to a frame via a single revolute joint
A revolute joint, oriented as in Figure 2.57, is in 3D able to constrain all three translations along with
the Rx- and Ry-rotation. However, for a revolute joint to be able to rotate, play is necessary. Play
means that the hole diameter is slightly larger than the pin, while the pin is slightly longer than the
depth of the hole. As a result of play, the part is able to tilt slightly in both the Rx- and Ry-rotation.
Additionally the part can translate slightly in all three directions.
In an extreme case, the play allow two bodies to not have any contact at all. As a result, all constraints
the joint can provide, are not engaged. In another extreme case, all constraints can be engaged. To
visualize engaged and non-engaged constraints, two physical examples are shown in Figure 2.58.
Figure 2.58: Physical example visualizing the eﬀect of play in revolute joints

==PAGE:43==
In Figure 2.58A, ﬁve bodies hang by the force of gravity. The slight tilt of each body, caused by play, is
made visible by multiplying the eﬀect with multiple joints. In Figure 2.58B, The bodies lie ﬂat on the
table. To explain in detail what is happening, a model of a single body, from each situation, is sketched.
The model for situation A can be seen in Figure 2.59.
Figure 2.59: Simpliﬁed model of the physical system in Figure 2.58A
In situation A, the joints have engaged the X-constraint, and the Z-constraint twice, one converted to
an Ry-constraint. This is shown in Figure 2.59. Friction between F and B will provide a ﬂexible Y-
constraint. Thus, the potential Y-constraint of the joint is not engaged. At both Z-constraints, the
contact is the edge of a circle, not providing any perpendicular distance, nor enough contact points, to
constrain the Rx-rotation. Thus, the potential Rx-constraint of the joint is not engaged.
Figure 2.60: Simpliﬁed model of the physical system in Figure 2.58B
In situation B, as seen in Figure 2.60, only the joint between B1 and B2 is discussed.
The model
visualizes that only a single Z-constraint is engaged between B1 and B2. All other potential constraints
are overruled by the contact to the table, and by friction.
To communicate the potential for constraints being, or not being, engaged, the term weak constraint is
used, and implemented in both the kinematic analysis and system clarity analysis.
2.12.1
Weak constraints
A weak constraint, is a constraint that potentially can be, or not be, engaged. A weak constraint is only
engaged if nothing else is constraining the body in the speciﬁc motion. This is under the assumption
that the play is suﬃciently large enough, to make room for the other constraining element. Notet that
an other constraining element can very well be a paired constraints conversion. Additionally, engaged
weak constraints can be used for a paired constraints conversion.
Reusing Figure 2.57, as example: Revolute joints are most often used to transfer force in the X- and
Y-direction. Thus, the X- and Y-constraint in a revolute joint is not considered weak, as they will be
maintained by the force they transfer.
In a kinematic table, a weak constraint is displayed by "(1)", rather than "1", as shown in Table 2.4.
However, once the engaged and non-engaged weak constraints have been determined, the kinematic table
is simpliﬁed. An engaged weak constraint is displayed as a regular constraint, while a non-engaged weak
constraint is displayed as no constraint. Using the model from Figure 2.59, to show engaged and non

4.3
Suggested improvements
To reach a solution that provides less over-constraints, the following options are available: Removal of
one or more joints, modiﬁcation of one or more joints, and/or adding one or more new bodies to the
mechanism, providing additional DOF. Removal of existing joints is the simplest and cheapest option.
Modifying joints are almost just as cheap, however redesigning a joint provides several possibilities. Thus,
the designer is required to know exactly what is needed from the new joint. Adding bodies provides any
desirable solution as a redesign of the mechanism can be made. Hence, this is also the most complex
and expensive option, and is therefore preferred avoided if possible.
When creating a solution to the current problem, the simplest option, removing joints, is tested ﬁrst.
The only removable joint types in the expander mechanism are: A revolute joint; providing two to three
constraints, and a translational joint; providing four constraints. When looking at Figure 4.18, and
all the loops between F and P2, an obvious improvement is to remove one of the translational joints.

Main function 2: Shutter mechanism one
As brieﬂy explained in chapter 3, the shutter mechanism is a purely mechanical solution that utilizes
rising water to shut a valve. The mechanism is driven by a ﬂoater unit, that is driven upwards via
buoyancy from back ﬂowing water. The limitations for this mechanism is, that there are only room for
a ﬂoater unit on top and to the side of the pipe as shown in Figure 5.1. Besides the ﬂoater unit, the
mechanism contains a valve lid which is closed by a force referred to as the shutter force. The shutter
force must be roughly centred on the valve lid, in order to apply an evenly distributed shutter pressure.
For this reason, it is convenient to have the mechanism placed in the middle of the product in the Z-
direction. In the current design, the shutter mechanism would collide with the expander mechanism,
were it not for a hole in the expander arms, A1 and A2 discussed in chapter 4.

5.2
Four bar mechanism
A model of the four bar mechanism is sketched and can be seen in Figure 5.4. Each part in the four bar
mechanism is named in accordance with the notation in [8], which is also shown in Appendix E.
Figure 5.4: Model of shutter mechanism one, the four bar mechanism
The shutter mechanism, and the four bar mechanism in it, need to fulﬁl six criteria in order to function
as intended. The criteria are deﬁned in the following:
1. When the input bar (a) rotates counter clockwise, the output bar (b) has to rotate clockwise. This
demand is needed due to the location of the shutter mechanism and the available space for the
ﬂoater unit.
2. The force transfer need to be at maximum eﬃciency when the mechanism is at closed position.
The reason for this is, when the mechanism is at closed position the rubber seal need to be pressed
against the pipe to seal tight.
3. The relative angle between each of the moveable bars, can at no point be critically blunt or
sharp. This will hinder the four bar mechanism to transfer force into movement. As the four bar
mechanism is able to move at low forces, the critically blunt and sharp angles are estimated, by
physical experiment, to be above 165° and below 15°.
4. The minimal required water level, to fully shut the lid, need to be as low as possible. The minimal
water level determines how shallow a well the product can be installed in, and still protect the
household from back ﬂowing water.
5. The force required to move the mechanism from open to closed position, need to be suﬃcient
enough to counter the gravitational pull in the ﬂoater unit. However, the system is designed to
be very close to gravitational equilibrium, like a seesaw, and is therefore considered to be easily
overcome. For this reason it is assumed always achieved and is not discussed any further.
6. The gravitational pull in the ﬂoater unit is required to be greater than the gravitational pull in the
shutter lid. This enables the shutter lid to open when no water is present. The current mechanism
fulﬁl this demand. The changes made to the mechanism is assumed to have an insigniﬁcant impact
upon the gravitational equilibrium. It is therefore not considered due to project conﬁnement.
The ﬁrst criterion is fulﬁlled for as long as the bars are assembled in the right relative position. This is
assumed not to be a great challenge and is therefore not discussed any further.
In order to fulﬁl the second criterion, the demand of highest force transfer in the closed position, two
analyses are made. A constraint analysis to locate potential over-constraints, that could provide unnec-
essary friction causing energy loss. And a force transfer analysis that will also discuss both the third
and fourth criterion in detail.

The input force is transferred to become the output force, through the four bar mechanism from one
part to the other. The ﬂow of forces is described in the following bullet points:
• The input force (Fi) acts upon the input arm (i)
• The input moment (Mi) acts upon the input bar (a)
• The input bar force (Fa) acts upon the connector bar (c)
• The connector bar force (Fc) acts upon the output bar (b)
• The output bar moment (Mb) acts upon the output arm (o)
• The output force (Fo) is the resulting shutter force.
The three angles between the bars in the mechanism is shown in Figure 5.8.
These angles play an
important role when calculating the total transferred force through the system. These, and their eﬀect
are explained in the following bullet points:
• θi is the angle between the input arm (i) and vertical. As the ﬂoater unit will be forced vertically
upwards, this angle is used to calculate the eﬃciency of the force transfer from the ﬂoater unit to
the four bar mechanism.
• θac is the angle between the input bar (a) and the connector bar (c). This is also used to calculate
the eﬃciency of the force transfer between the two bars.
• θbc is the angle between the connector bar (c) and the output bar (b). This is also used to calculate
the eﬃciency of the force transfer between the two bars.
The force transfer eﬃciency, being the size of the output force with respect to the input force, is deﬁned
by two separate eﬀects. The angular eﬃciency and the gearing ratio. Angular eﬃciency, is how much of
the total force is transferred to the next part in the mechanism, and how much is lost within the joints to

the frame. The gearing ratio is basically the relationship between the length of the arms. In this section
the two eﬀects will be described mathematically, and based upon that, improvements will be suggested.

calculation is the force transfer eﬃciency, rather than the resulting force. The calculation will follow the

5.3.2
Shutter lid to pipe alignment
The Shutter lid to pipe alignment is more diﬃcult to control. However, the demand for precision in this
case is even higher, as a false alignment will prevent the product from performing as required. For this
reason the following analysis hold great value for Urban Water.
For the mechanism to function as intended, the lid surface has to have a completely unbroken contact
with the end of the pipe. In order to do so, the lid surface and the end of the pipe have to fulﬁl the
following conditions:
• The lid surface has to be ﬂat.
• The end of the pipe has to be equally ﬂat.
• The lid size has to be the same as the end of the pipe.
• Relative to one and other, the lid surface and the cut pipe surface needs to be aligned with precision
in all six DOF.
The ﬁrst condition, the lid surface being ﬂat, is not fulﬁlled with the current product. The output arm
has been welded to the steel shutter lid, and as a result, the steel lid has been thermally deformed causing
the surface to be warped. The consequence of the warped surface can be seen in Figure 5.16.
61
==PAGE:75==
Figure 5.16: Thermally deformed steel lid resulting in miss alignment to pipe
As seen from Figure 5.16, the warped surface makes it impossible, within the current ﬂexibility of the
soft rubber seal, to ensure a tight seal between the lid and the pipe. To overcome the demand for ﬂatness
one or more of the listed things can be done:
1. Increase the range of ﬂexibility by adding a bigger and/or softer rubber seal.
2. Add a ﬂatness tolerance requiring an additional production step.
3. Replace the welding connection with another fastening connection that do not cause deformation.
The third option is the cheapest an easiest to achieve. For this reason, it is the recommended solution.
Shortly after the analysis was performed, Urban Water decided to change the welding connection with
a screw connection, which proved to solve the ﬂatness issue in the shutter steel lid.
The second condition, the end of the pipe needing to be equally ﬂat, is also currently not fulﬁlled.
The issue is revealed in Figure 5.17, where a black steel ruler is pressed against the end of the pipe,
revealing a non-ﬂat surface. The cause of the non-ﬂat surface, is estimated to be due to the supplier
being conservative with material. The angle of the cut surface reveals that the pipe ends before the cut
ends, as illustrated in Figure 5.18(a). The solution to this is to specify that the cut has to end at least
one centimetre from the pipe end, as shown in Figure 5.18(b).
There is an additional risk of a non-ﬂat surface cut in the production process. When the pipe is cut, it
is clamped within the cutting tool. The clamping may elastically deform the pipe into an oval shape,
during the cutting process. When the pipe is unclamped, it will spring back to the original circular
shape. As a result, the cut of the oval shaped pipe will not produce a ﬂat cut in the circular shaped
pipe. This production process needs to be attended to, one way or the other, as the ﬂatness of the cut
pipe is a crucial factor of the shutter mechanism to work.

Before getting into the solution, the phrase "driven by demand of alignment itself", is explained using a
simple example of a box on a table. Between the box and the table, an alignment is demanded. The
demand is that the box has to lie ﬂat upon the table. For the box to lie ﬂat on the table, three things
must be achieved. The box must be placed directly above the table, while being oriented rotationally
in agreement with the table in two out of three rotations. It is possible to create a contraption, that
"holds" the box in the correct position and orientation. However, it is also possible to let the position
and orientation of the box be driven by the demand of alignment it self. This is achieved by not having
a contraption constraining the box, but rather a ﬂexible force that maintains the connection between
the box and the table. Doing so, the table can be tilted, thus altering the demanded orientation of the
box, while the box will follow the new demand automatically. In comparison, the contraption holding
the box, would need to be altered to ﬁt the new demand caused by the tilted table.
The goal is to have the system not critically over-constrained and driven by the demand of alignment
itself. The demand is, that the ﬂat surface of the lid has an unbroken connection to the cut end of the
65
==PAGE:79==
pipe. This can be translated in to the following alignment demand: The lid has to lie ﬂat on the pipe
end surface, while the X- and Z-position, along with the Ry-rotation, of the lid, has to correspond with
those of the pipe end. In the current design, the shutter lid is in fact also the output bar (b) of the four
bar mechanism. For this reason, the new design is also required to maintain the functionality of the
four bar mechanism. To reach a solution, each demand is broken down and described in detail in the
following ﬁve points:
1. For the four bar mechanism to function, a hinge point to the pipe delivering an X- and Y-constraint
is necessary.
2. To have the lid lie ﬂat on the pipe surface, driven by the demand, the three Y-constraints between
the pipe and the lid is necessary. For these constraints to be driving, there should be no other
Y-constraints action upon the lid.
3. Assuming that the lid is the correct size.
To achieve the Z-alignment, driven by the demand,
the potential Z-constraint, between the inspection chamber and the lid, should be the driving
constraint. Doing so, will in reality let the lid be ﬂexible in the Z-direction, ensuring that the lid
is never pressed against the inspection chamber wall.
4. The demand for the Ry alignment, is far less critical, and can be controlled without the use of a
demand driven eﬀect. This could be achieved by the relative Z-position of the hinge point to the
pipe, in relation to the walls of the inspection chamber.
5. The demand for the X-position of the lid is critical, but can unfortunately not be driven by the
demand itself. This is due to the geometry of the mechanism and is explained using the sketch
in Figure 5.22. The connection between the lid and the ispection chamber bottom, is in reality
both an X- and Y-constraint with the chosen coordinate system orientation. The Y-component,
along with friction, will hinder the lid to slide into the desired position. The connection, between
the lid and bottom of the inspection chamber, is only named an "X-constraint" for the following
reason. The connection can be avoided if the X-position of the lid is controlled by a non demand
driven eﬀect. In the current mechanism this is achieved by the following: The X-position of the
hinge point to the pipe, the X-length of the output arm and the X-length of the lid. Using the
name X-constraint, will in the constraint analysis return that the system is over-constrained in the
X-direction. This is intended as it highlights the demand for tight tolerances in this direction.
Figure 5.22: Sketch of the problem using the demand driven X-constraint
Summing up the demands: Two X-constraints cannot be removed, whereas one is unwanted. Four Y-
constraints cannot be removed, while it is also demanded that a maximum of three Y-constraints controls
the shutter lid. Finally, one Z-constraint cannot be removed.

CHAPTER 6
Discussion and Conclusion
6.1
Discussion
During the project, new robust design theory has been expanded into a set of existing robust design
tools, which aids the implementation of robust design into physical products. The value of the extended
tools are shown, by using them to analyse a mechanical backwater valve designed by the company, Urban
Water. The analyses locates potential functional pitfalls in the current design, and how to avoid these,
using the simplest and or cheapest changes to the design. Each ﬁnding is discussed in the following:
6.1.1
Theoretical ﬁndings
In chapter 2, the method of applying paired constraints conversion is invented and implemented in the
two existing robust design tools: Kinematic analysis and System clarity analysis.
Paired constraints conversion
In subsection 2.3.4 and section 2.4, by applying basic kinetic knowledge of forces and moments, a kine-
matic terminology, paired constraints conversion, has been invented. Paired constraints conversion is
a method to validate and communicate the rotational constraints that can occur when bodies interact
with each other. The method translates translational constraints, into contact points, into forces, into
moments, into rotational constraints. In short, the method converts one of the two in a pair of parallel
translational constraints into a rotational constraint.
In order for this method to be applicable in any situation, all constraints must be aligned with either the
X-, Y- or Z-axis of the Cartesian coordinate system chosen for the model. In the encountered problems
in this thesis, the limited constraint orientation has not revealed any issues. However, the subject is not
fully investigated and could use a validation, by application in a variety of problems.
Additionally all constraints in the thesis are interpreted as double sided, either by being so, or by the
assumption of a suﬃciently large nesting force maintaining the contact to the constraint. This was chosen
as a simpliﬁcation for project conﬁnement. Paired constraints conversion require some communication
adjustments in order to be applicable in single sided constraints.
Kinematic analysis
In section 2.8, the kinematic analysis is extended by the implementation of paired constraints conversion.
The extension enhances the detail level of informations provided by the tool. The original kinematic
analysis helps the user locate the number of over- or under-constraints in a system. With the extension,
it is now possible to locate the exact motion in which the over- or under-constraint is located. This makes
it easier for the user to locate potential solutions to a given problem. The extended kinematic analysis
has, throughout the thesis, proved to be a tremendous help in the process of improving the mechanisms
in the Urban Water backwater valve.
The drawback of the extended kinematic analysis, is that it cannot be used alone. To apply paired
constraints conversion, a model providing informations of the relative positions of the constraints in a
system is required. However, this is not seen as a crucial drawback, As a model of the system most often
already is made prior the a kinematic analysis.
System clarity analysis
In section 2.9, the system clarity analysis is extended to mitigate its weakness: False indication of over-
and under-constraints in systems where the position of a constraint is aﬀected by rotation af a body.
The tool is extended by implementing paired constraints conversion. The use of the extended tool is
somewhat complex, but has been thoroughly described with three examples, in order to enable others
to apply the method in any given problem. The tool was also extended using the term weak constraints
70
==PAGE:84==
from the eﬀect of play, as deﬁned in section 2.12. This proved to be a surprisingly eﬃcient method to
enhance the communication of constraints acting in a system. Throughout the thesis the extended tool
has proven a great help, especially when it comes to selecting which actions are necessary to provide a
valuable solution.
When analysing a system consisting of less than three bodies, the use of system clarity analysis has been
deemed excessive. However, in a multi body system it has proven to be a valuable tool.
6.1.2
Practical application of the theoretical models
To test the theoretical models, a unique opportunity of working with a late stage prototype product,
was oﬀered by the start-up company, Urban Water. As the product is a mechanically driven backwater
valve, it contains several mechanisms that are ideal for a robust design analysis. The ﬁndings of the
analyses in chapter 4 and chapter 5, are discussed in details in the following sections:
Expander mechanism
The backwater valve contains an expander mechanism, that by two inclined bars, pushes each side of the
product away from each other. The physical mechanism is able to perform the required task, however
several issues can be recognised at a glance. To validate and identify the assumed issues, a constraint
analysis is performed, using the extended robust design tools. From the analysis found in section 4.2, the
expander mechanism is concluded to be over-constrained ﬁve times. Two risks are involved with having
an over-constrained mechanism. First, product variation can result in the mechanism not being able to
be assembled, as the mismatch between parts easily becomes larger than what play in the joints can
account for. Second, an assembled mechanism has a high risk of containing stresses that causes friction,
thus hindering the intended motions. However, by identifying the exact cause of the issues, via the
analysis, it is possible to generate a cheap and eﬃcient solution. In section 4.3, it is suggested to remove
one revolute joint and modify a translational joint to a version that only applies a single constraint. The
suggested solution has not yet been physically tested. However, it is estimated that implementing this
solution, will result in not only increased production reliability, but also greatly reduced the required
input force to activate the mechanism. This will enhance the user experience when installing the product.
Shutter mechanism
The backwater valve contain two shutter mechanisms, that by two ﬂoater units mechanically seal of
back ﬂowing water from entering households. To both mechanisms improvements were suggested and
implemented physically, however only one of them is included in this thesis due to project conﬁnement.
Both shutter mechanisms contains a four bar mechanism, that transfers the input force from the ﬂoater
units to the shutter lid. For this reason the detailed analysis is divided into two sections, one covering the
four bar mechanism, and the other covering the alignment of the shutter lid. Each of these are discussed
in the following two sections:
Four bar mechanism
In subsection 5.2.1, a constraint analysis of the four bar mechanism is performed, proving the conclusion
that it is properly constrained. As this mechanism is widely used in multiple designs, this conclusion
is not a great surprise. On the contrary, in subsection 5.2.2, the four bar mechanism is revealed to
be surprisingly complex in terms of force and motion transfer eﬃciency. Using the 2D drawing tool in
the CAD program inventor, a suggested improvement to the four bar mechanism is generated, by using
appropriate relations. The suggested solution is calculated to improve the mechanism by approximately
30% in both force and movement transfer eﬃciency. The suggested solution can be implemented by
replacing two small parts that are cheap to produce. Which makes the value gained from the improvement
worth implementing.
The complexity of the four bar mechanism is caused by the eﬀect of multiple circular motions interacting
with each other. In contrary to the gearing eﬀects, that is a trade oﬀbetween force and movement,
utilizing the multiple circular eﬀect, it is possible to improve both of these. A mathematically optimum
study of this is possible, but was considered out of scope, and is therefore worth some more attention in
another study.
71
==PAGE:85==
Shutter lid to pipe end alignment
In addition to constraint analysis, in section 5.3, other robust design aspects is touched during analysis of
the alignment. These include the analysis of production methods, and the variation eﬀects of these. The
shutter lid had a welding connection, that caused thermal deformation, resulting is substantial warpages
of a surface that is required to be ﬂat. A change of production method was suggested, and implemented
instantly by Urban Water. The end of the pipe is also required to be ﬂat. Analysing the surface of the
cut pipe end, reveal that the production of this also contain issues. These are guessed to be caused by
too conservative use of material and elastic deformation by clamping the pipe during cutting. However,
these are not certainty identiﬁed, therefore a single deﬁnitive solution is not suggested.
By analysing the shutter lid to pipe alignment, two critical functional ﬂaws in the mechanism is identiﬁed.
For the mechanism to function as intended, the alignment of shutter lid has to correspond with the surface
of the pipe end. The lid is identiﬁed to be rotationally over-constrained in two rotations, and as a result,
the slightest production variation will cause the two surfaces to misalign critically. Less critical, yet
counter productive over-constraints, were also identiﬁed. The solution to remove the critical and less
critical over-constraints, required adding an additional body to the system. This is the most expensive
design change, however, taking the criticality of the functional ﬂaw, the solution is deemed a worthy
implementation.
With the current fundamental function of the shutter lid, multiple robustness issues are unable to be
corrected. The ﬁst issue is, that the dimensions of an oval shaped part is required to ﬁt another with tiny
variation allowed. The issue is critical due to two reasons: If the requirement is not met, the mechanism
will not even slightly perform the desired function. And that the material that needs to ﬁt the tight
requirement, is a soft rubber, that is both ﬂexible and can thermally shrink during use. The second
robustness issue, is that the alignment of the same oval shaped rubber part cannot robustly be aligned in
a single direction. In the suggested solution, the alignment of this part, in this direction, is controlled by
three individual steel parts, connected by two joints. It is possible to apply tolerances, to this chain, that
will suﬃciently align the part. However being expensive, this is considered less critical than the previous
robustness issue. Having seen these robustness issues, it is recommended to redesign the fundamental
function of the shutter mechanism, knowing that it will be a long and expensive process.

6.2
Conclusion
Paired constraints conversion is a simple method that utilizes the deﬁnition of how a body can be
rotationally constrained by a pair of forces generating a moment. The method is used to provide quick
communication in a constraint analysis. The implementation of paired constraints conversion into the
existing robust design tools, kinematic and system clarity analysis, provides a more detailed description of
how parts are constrained in relation to each other. The extended robust design tools are now applicable
to dynamic systems, and systems where rotating bodies aﬀect the position of one or more joints. As
a result the extended robust design tool were able to be used for the optimisation of the Urban Water
product. The Urban Water product is by this process estimated to be able to perform better, if the
suggested improvements are implemented. Some of the suggested changes have been implemented into
a prototype of the product, and have in test proven to work as intended, and by that improved the
function and value of the product.
6.3
Future work
During the project, a number of encountered subject were considered out of scope, and could be in-
teresting to investigate more thoroughly in another study. These subjects are brieﬂy described in the
following:
It could be interesting to investigated more thoroughly which types of environments one can encounter
when performing constraint analyses. If relevant, the method of paired constraint conversion should be
tested in, and expanded to ﬁt in, an environment where single directional constraints are necessary. This
could include the extended kinematic analysis, as seen in [9] section 3.2 where the direction of constraints
is added to the table.
The eﬀect of inclined constraints, and the forces it applies to a system, could be investigated in relation
to paired constraints conversion. The investigation could be to see how paired constraints conversion can
be applicable in any rotationally constraining situation. Or just to be able to argue why, if it already is.
The analysis of the four bar mechanism revealed an optimum study using the multiple circular interaction
eﬀect. This could be an interesting theory to model mathematically. By a mathematical model it will
be possible to identify an optimal design of a four bar mechanism with a given set of demands.
Potentially all other key characteristics listed in Figure 3.5 can be applied a robust design analysis,
which in turn would reveal potential improvements to the current Urban Water product. Additionally,
a follow up of the suggested improvements could be interesting. The follow up could contain producing
the improved expander mechanism, and see if the theory holds true, or in general, whenever a change is
made to a system, it may aﬀect other functions in an unforeseen manner.
