Abstract 
The oral route is the preferred drug administration route. However, this administration route is 
associated with degradation of the drug as is subjected to the harsh acidic environment in the 
stomach and first-pass metabolism in the liver. As diseases like diabetes affects more and more 
people around the world, a new drug delivery route is needed to meet the increasing demands for 
drugs like insulin. A route that is easy to access, shows systemic absorption, and avoids 
macromolecule degradation. The buccal route avoids these disadvantages and holds great potential 
as a new drug delivery site. 
The main objective of this master thesis was to fabricate hydrogel films and test their release of the 
model compound Dextran. The influence of the concentration, type, and molecular weight (MW) of 
the applied polymer in the hydrogel in consideration to drug release was also to be evaluated. 
Compounds with different charges alongside model compounds with different MW were also tested 
to evaluate their effect on drug release. Subsequently, this project focused on fabricating hydrogel 
forming microneedles (HFMN) using 3D-printet master template, polydimethylsiloxane (PDMS) 
soft lithography, and UV-assisted micromoulding for buccal delivery of macromolecules. 
The release of Dextran from a hydrogel patch was successfully measured by means of fluorescence. 
A clear tendency between low MW of poly (ethylene glycol) diacrylate (PEGDA) and higher 
release was also demonstrated. Furthermore, a polymer concentration above 20% affected the 
release of Dextran negatively. Different compositions of the polymer PEGDA and poly (ethylene 
glycol) methacrylate (PEGMA) was investigated to determine if the different compositions had an 
influence of release of Dextran. A relation of 5:90 (PEDGA: PEGMA) showed the highest release 
of Dextran. With regards to the effect of adding a charge to the system, it was found that a positive 
charge had the highest release. Model compounds of Dextran with MW of 4 kDa, 10 kDa, and 20 
kDa was investigated with regards to release. It was concluded that the 20 kDa had the highest 
release followed by 4 the kDa. The hydrogel with lowest release contained Dextran with a MW of 
10 kDa. The results indicated that 20 kDa Dextran was not incorporated into the hydrogel matrix, 
and therefore was released faster. This indication was confirmed by fluorescence images. 
Finally, the mechanical strengths of the MN were investigated and evaluated for their ability to 
penetrate an agarose gel. Microneedles were fabricated and Insertion Force and Young´s Modulus 
was calculated. The microneedles with PEGDA, photoinitiator (PI) and dextran showed the highest 
insertion force.

1 Introduction 
The preferred drug administration route is orally [1]. It is safe, easy to use, inexpensive, and 
painless - all of this will increase patient compliance. But there are numerous disadvantages 
associated with an oral tablet, capsule, or liquid formulation. The formulation must be swallowed 
through the mouth and pass down the esophagus to the stomach. Here, the tablet will continue down 
to the small intestines where the drug is mainly absorbed. The drug is first exposed to a harsh acidic 
environment and grinding in the stomach. Subsequently, it will be degraded due to the first pass 
metabolism in the liver [1]–[5]. Oral delivery is made difficult for more drugs due to difficulties in 
solubility and permeability across the intestinal membrane which will limit their therapeutical 
efficiency [6]. This problem exists for small as well as larger molecules. Proteins and peptides, like 
insulin, is difficult to administer orally and new delivery systems are needed. As the buccal route 
avoids these disadvantages, it holds great potential as a new drug delivery site.  
By using buccal delivery, it is possible to achieve systemic bioavailability [3], [7]–[9]. The first 
studies of this were reported back in 1935, even though oral cavity absorption was noted as early as 
1847 [8], [10]. But even earlier, it has been known that compounds could be absorbed by chewing 
tobacco leaves, herbs, and other plant material [11]. In 1947, the first mucoadhesive formulation 
was described and in the 1980s the first oral drug for transmucosal delivery was presented [12], 
[13]. Buccal delivery has shown promise as a safe, simple, fast, familiar, and flexible way of 
delivering drugs to controlled systemic release distribution [11], [14]. Through buccal mucosal 
delivery, it is possible to reduce dose and toxicity, because of the reduced degradation of drug in the 
gastrointestinal tract, by enzymes in the small intestines, and by the first-pass metabolism in the 
liver [12], [15], [16].  
Buccal delivery by patches could benefit people with difficulties to swallow, small children, and 
people with anxiety towards needles [4], [7], [17]–[19]. In the future, vaccination to battle local 
and/or systemic infections through the buccal mucosa can be achieved, since the buccal mucosa is 
rich in antigen presenting cells that can mediate both an innate and an adaptive immune response 
[9].

1.1 Buccal drug delivery 
This introduction section is divided into multiple parts. An overview of the oral cavity and the 
buccal mucosa, drug release and drug delivery, delivery systems such as patches and microneedles, 
and finally the polymer system will be presented.     
In this project, dextran with a molecular weight of 6000 Da is used as a model compound. This 
molecular weight is chosen as it is similar in size to human insulin (5800 Da), thereby giving an 
indication of possible drug release from a hydrogel matrix [20]. For type 1 diabetes mellitus, insulin 
is administered by injections. For type 2 diabetes mellitus, insulin is initially administered orally but 
as disease progresses insulin injections are required [21]. A new delivery system is preferred, to 
eliminate the discomfort of needles, reduce drug degradation, and increase patient compliance. 
 
1.1.1 Oral cavity and buccal mucosa  
The oral cavity is the first line of defence towards outside foreign matters and consists of the 
gingival areas, the tongue, the cheeks, the inner lips, and the floor of the mouth [2], [8]. The oral 
immunity consists of saliva, gingival crevicular fluid, and the physical barrier [22]. In saliva, there 
is immunoglobulin A (IgA), mucins, and enzymes that all protect from bacterial attack and 
colonization. The gingival crevicular fluid contains IgG, IgM, and IgA together with leucocytes that 
all enhance oral immunity. The physical barrier consists of the epithelium and the lamina propria 
and mediates either an innate or an adaptive immune response [9], [22]. 
The oral cavity has three different types of mucosae: The masticatory, the specialized, and the 
lining mucosa as shown in Figure 1 [3], [22]. The masticatory mucosa covers about 25% of the oral 
cavity and is the keratinized epithelium, located on the hard palate and tooth gingiva. The 
specialized mucosa covers about 15% and consist of the semi-keratinized and non-keratinized 
epithelium located on the tongue [14]. The lining mucosa covers around 60% of the oral mucosa 
and is the non-keratinized epithelium which covers all other surfaces in the oral cavity like lips, 
cheeks, and floor of the mouth [3], [22]. The area of the non-keratinized and the epithelium is 
approximately 50 cm2 out of the total surface area of the mouth of 170 cm2 [7]–[9].

Drug administration through the oral cavity can be done by three different methods, the sublingual 
delivery, the buccal delivery, and the local delivery [1], [2], [10]. Sublingual drug delivery is used 
for systemic delivery and is primarily used when rapid onset is required. The sublingual mucosa is 
more permeable, thinner, and has richer blood supply which makes it preferable for drugs with a 
short delivery range [8]. Local delivery is for treatment of disease in the mouth such as gingivitis, 
dental carries, and cancers [8]. In this project, the focus is on buccal delivery. The buccal mucosa is 
more permeable than the skin and has the advantages of a rich blood supply, accessibility, by-pass 
of first pass effect, and a good permeability [1], [3], [8], [19]. The buccal mucosa consists of 
phospholipids, cholesterol esters, and glycosylceramides which gives a more polar intracellular 
profile and thereby increasing permeation compared to the skin [3], [14]. The buccal mucosa has 
approximately 40-50 cell layers, which correspond to an epithelium thickness of approximately 
500-700 µm [1], [9], [12], [14]. The overall thickness of the oral epithelium is between 500 µm and 
800 µm [1], [3], [10]. The thickness of epithelium on other parts of the body for comparison is 
between 500 µm on the eyelid, and 4000 µm on the heels.  
The great blood supply to the oral mucosa is essential for drug absorption and systemic distribution. 
The blood flow to the buccal mucosa is approximately 2.4 mL min-1 cm-2 [12]. The blood flows 
through the carotid-, the maxillary-, the lingual-, and the facial-arteries before ending up in the 
internal jugular vein [3], [14]. The direct way to the blood supple through the jugular vein enables 
fast onset. Bypassing the first-pass effect and further drug degradation and metabolism will enhance 
bioavailability [3], [7], [14], [15]. Besides the rich blood supply, the absence of tight junctions is 
enhancing the ease of drug delivery [7], [10]–[12]. The pH of the mouth is approximately 7.4 so 
drug degradation is minimal. The enzymatic activity in the buccal mucosa is low compared to other 
mucosal routes [8]. The oral mucosa is estimated to be 4-4000 times more permeable than the skin 
[10].

However, saliva present in the oral cavity contains fluids (~99% water) and other enzymes and 
proteins (~1%) which can possibly degrade and “wash out” the API [1], [15]. Therefore, it is 
advantageous to release to the mucosa to avoid these extensive amounts of saliva. The buccal 
mucosa is covered with mucins, which is a high molecular weight glycoprotein that is present in 
saliva. This will form a viscoelastic gel layer increasing adhesion of hydrogel to the buccal mucosa 
[7], [13].  
 
1.1.2 Drug release and buccal delivery 
There are 3 key parameters related to enhancing drug delivery. The first parameter to improve is the 
availability of the drug by improving it´s half-life. Second, maximize drug concentration in the 
tissue and thirdly, maximize duration of the drug therapeutic efficacy. When developing a new drug 
delivery system, it is important to shield the API from degradation and enhance the drug efficacy 
while minimizing the possible side effects of administration [23]. By enhancing the drug delivery 
system, one can minimize dosing and/or frequency of drug administration, while keeping the 
concentration of the drug within the therapeutical window and minimize side effects [4], [16], [24]. 
This will increase patient compliance [23]. 
The buccal mucosa is suitable for a patch system because of its relative smooth surface [10]. A 
disadvantage to the buccal delivery is the mechanical influence of the tongue and the saliva in the 
mouth. Both can decrease dosage from patch or film to the buccal epithelium [9]. Another 
disadvantage is the low flux, which result in low drug bioavailability [12]. Adhesion is a key 
parameter in the buccal drug delivery [8]. The mucin layer in the mouth has anionic groups attached 
and will react with hydrophilic polymers to increase mucoadhesion [9]. Furthermore, features to 
increase mucoadhesion in general are high molecular weight, chain flexibility, hydrogen bonding 
groups, and surface energy properties [25]. 
Permeability and stability of the drug is increased by using buccal delivery compared to oral 
formulations. Furthermore, absorption is improved due to the softer mucosa, the low enzymatic 
content, and long cellular turnover which could facilitate a long delivery [1], [17]. Buccal mucosa 
turnover time is estimated to be 25 days, and between 41-75 days in the gingiva. For specific buccal 
epithelia cells, the turnover time is 5-7 days [1], [10], [12]. For comparison, the skin is renewed 
every 52-75 days. In the gut, epithelia cells are renewed every 4-14 days [22].

By achieving buccal drug delivery, the drug concentration in the body will increase since drug 
degradation is lowered. The lower degradation of drug allows for lower dosing and results in less 
side effects. At present time, 43% of insulin is being degraded due to first-pass metabolism when 
administered through portal infusion [3]. When taking tablets through the oral route, between 9% to 
83% of the API is being degraded due to first-pass metabolism [3]. 
 
1.2 Delivery systems 
For drug delivery through the buccal mucosa, there are different opportunities like patches, gels, 
tablets, and films [13]. Each of the fabrications have their own strengths and weaknesses.  
 
1.2.1 Patches and films 
Polymeric buccal films were first tried in the late 1980s or early 1990s [8]. Buccal films are more 
flexible and more comfortable than adhesive tablets as they can protect the wound surfaces, reduce 
pain, and they have longer residence times compared to oral gels [8], [26]. An ideal buccal film 
should be flexible, elastic, and soft. It must however be strong enough to withstand breakage from 
mouth activity. Furthermore, it must have good bioadhesive strength and demonstrate moderate 
swelling [8], [9], [12], [27].  
Buccal patches have a bioadhesive drug reservoir polymeric layer and an impermeable backing 
layer to provide unidirectional flow of drug across the buccal mucosa. Their sizes are usually in 
range of 1-3 cm2. A size in this range is more convenient and comfortable for the patient. They must 
also be flexible and may be ellipsoid in shape to fit comfortably onto the center of the buccal 
mucosa [8]. 
Patches are easy to administer to the smooth buccal surface, easy to handle, and to remove again 
[25]. Patches or films can be used for buccal delivery since they are highly versatile. There is also 
the possibility of enhancing drug delivery using permeation enhancers and mucoadhesive polymers 
[3]. Patches or films are designed to be polymeric networks placed in the buccal mucosa, where 
saliva will hydrate the polymeric network making it swell and thereby releasing its drug load. It is 
possible to design the network with multiple layers to control drug release and increase 
bioavailability. Disadvantage to the system is small maximal dosage, variation in taste, and texture 
of the hydrogel patch or film. Patches are not as invasive as microneedles, but they do have lower 
bioavailability in comparison.

1.2.2 Microneedles 
Microneedles (MN) are a new way to deliver drugs through the buccal route. The first time MN 
were mentioned as a drug delivery device was in the 1970s, but it was not until 1990 that proof-of-
concept was established [4], [28]. The first sweellable MN was first introduced in 2012 [6]. MNs 
has shown to be effective, safe, painless, and easy to administer – all aspects that can expect to 
increase patient compliance and convenience [2], [29], [30]. Furthermore, no educated staff is 
required to administer the drug. A key parameter of the MN to penetrate the oral mucosa is the 
geometry of the tip. In Figure 2, different kinds of microneedles are portraited. They can be either 
solid, hollow, coated, dissolvable, or hydrogel-forming [2], [4].

In this project, the focus will be on the HFMN for buccal administration since they show promise as 
drug delivery systems. The controlled delivery via this HFMN system will make it possible to give 
more efficient and higher concentration of drug [6]. A hydrogel patch will be fabricated and test of 
release from this will be performed. The hydrogel patch will be fabricated using the solving casting 
process. Hydrogels are well suited for oral drug delivery of small, hydrophilic molecules, and 
macromolecules drugs ranging from 400 Da to 30 kDa [23]. They can protect the model compound 
from metabolism, and compared to oral drug administration, hydrogels for buccal delivery protect 
the model compound from drug degradation. They require no educated person to administer and

elicit better compliance. The administration way holds great promise for children or elderly who 
have difficulties in swallowing, and for people with needle anxiety. The hydrogel system can be 
moderated to withhold the desired drug, independently of drug molecular weight. Permeation is 
independent of molecular weight and Log P values [30].   
 
HFMN are formulated as a polymeric system. This system has shown promise in the transdermal 
delivery of high molecular weight compounds like insulin [28]. Crosslinked hydrogels can protect 
the API from acidic environments. An API can be incorporated and diffuse from the network, 
dependant of the mesh size of the crosslinking agent [12], [23]. The HFMN can be fabricated in 
different geometries to fit target site. They can be removed after drug release and can be easily 
inserted [28]. The principal for hydrogels is that they can encapsulate proteins and shield them in 
the hydrogel matrix. In proper aqueous environment, the hydrogel will swell and release they load 
[4], [6], [31], [32]. The HFMN will be placed in the buccal mucosa where it will absorb excess 
water from the environment, that will make it swell and subsequently release the drug load. The 
sweeling will increase the mesh size of the matrix and thereby making enough space for the drug to 
leave the polymeric system.   
 
1.3 Polymer system 
Monomers are the basic organic molecule, that is linked together as long chains called polymers. 
Polymers consist of many monomer units in a long chain, held together through crosslinking. The 
reaction where monomers form chains of polymers are called polymerization.   
In general polymers with high hydrogen bonding capacity will form secondary chemical bonds and 
enhance mucoadhesive attachment [8]. Polymers MW has an influence on the bioadhesive strength, 
the higher a MW of the polymer a stronger adhesion is observed [8]. In general, the more flexibility 
of the polymer the greater diffusion to the mucus network, and by adding polyethylene glycol 
increased the flexibility. The lower glass transition temperature the more flexible [8].  
 
1.3.1 PEG based hydrogels 
Hydrogels are polymers with bioadhesive properties that are important in buccal delivery systems 
[5], [8]. Initial work with hydrogels were performed in the mid-1930s, but the general 
understanding of hydrogels cross-linked structure was first described in the 1940s by Paul Flory 
==PAGE:17==
Master Thesis project 2022 
 
Sidsel Anker Alexius (s154034) 
Page 17 of 76 
 
[33]. Hydrogels are a 3-dimensional polymeric network, that is fabricated by cross-linked 
hydrophilic macromolecules [33]–[37]. The hydrophilic matrix will swell when in contact with 
aqueous solutions [12], [38], [39]. Hydrogels are ideal matrix systems for controlled release of 
biomacromolecules [35]. The network can withhold between 60-99% of water, and this makes them 
advantageous in biomedical applications compared to ceramic and metal materials [40].  The 
relative high-water content in hydrogels furthermore results in rapid release within a few hours [36], 
[41].  
PEGylation is performed by conjugation on the C-terminal or N-terminal of the PEG-chain and was 
first described in 1970 by Davis and Abuchowski [42]. PEG is used as a crosslinker and provides 
high solubility in both hydrophobic and hydrophilic solvents. PEG is approved by the Food and 
Drug Administration (FDA), it is hydrophilic, water soluble, biocompatible, non-toxic at a 
molecular weight above 400 Da, has low immunogenicity, and is excreted from the human body 
[42]–[44]. PEG has shown great potential as a swellable release system due to its release behaviour 
[34]. PEG has good optical properties and is easily conjugated with various compounds [44]. 
PEGylation is widely used for enhancing pharmacokinetic properties of therapeutically relevant 
proteins [45]. Three parameters are essential to the polymer system, average pore size, molecular 
weight, and density of cross-linking [8]. The mechanical properties of PEG can be modified by 
changing the molecular weight and/or the crosslinking ratio [44].  
The most important thing to ensure is that the API can be absorbed in the network and be released 
at appropriate time and place in a controlled manner. An obstacle is achieving high API loading to 
the small surface area bioadhesive system [9]. The API can be either dissolved, entrapped, 
encapsulated, or adsorbed in the network. Defining the polymer network, the average pore size, 
MW of cross-linked polymers and cross-linking density are important for its properties [4].  
Macromolecules are large complex molecules consisting of many smaller units held together by 
intermolecular forces. Macromolecules can be proteins, nucleic acids. Lipids, and carbohydrates. 
Macromolecules are biological polymers.  
 
In a previous master thesis project at DTU, a PEGDA polymer was employed successfully for 
fabrication of microneedles [46]. The properties and advantages in strength made this polymer a 
good hydrogel. Thus, the polymer had disadvantages in drug delivery as the compound drug was 
captured inside the polymer matrix and could not be released. Therefore, this project will optimize

The research hypothesis was to make hydrogel-based microneedles for buccal delivery of

composition and/or concentration.  
Microneedles will work in two ways; it will penetrate the buccal mucosa as well as work as delivery

2 Theory 
2.1 Cross-linking 
Cross-linking is defined as the linking of polymer chains by ionic and/or chemical ways to obtain 
hydrophilic or hydrophobic materials [24]. Cross-linking can be either covalent or non-covalent 
depending on the form of bond [47]. Covalent bonds are formed when electrons are shared between 
atoms and non-covalent bonds are formed by complete exchange or no exchange of electrons. Non-
covalent bonds can be hydrogen bonds, dipole interactions, hydrophobic interactions, host-guest 
interactions, π-π stacking, and metal coordination [47].  
Cross linking can be performed using light, chemicals, or temperature [33], [43]. Visible light (400-
800 nm) or UV-light (200-400 nm) in low doses will create the desired hydrogels without degrading 
the API. It is easy to control the light beam, directing it at the right place, control the light dosage, 
and easily turning the beam on and of [43]. When using chemicals and/or temperature to form cross 
link, there is a risk of API denaturation.  
To perform UV-crosslinking, a photoinitiator must be added to initiate the photopolymerization 
reaction. When UV-light is present, the photoinitiator will absorb the photons and is cleaved 
forming a free-radical molecule, see Figure 3. This molecule will react with the vinyl bonds in the 
pre-polymer and form the chemical crosslink between polymer chains [43].

Swelling of the hydrogel is related to the structure of the crosslinked polymer and the density of the 
hydrogel [8], [31], [43]. In addition to swelling, elasticity, and degradation is relevant in biomedical 
applications [43]. Solvent casting is used to fabricate the patches, since this fabrication method is 
suitable for heat-sensitive drugs. An alternative to solvent casting is hot melt extrusion, but this 
method is not preferable with heat sensitive compound [3], [48]. 
 
2.2 Mesh size 
The crosslinked polymer system will create a mesh, which is characterized as the open spaces 
between the polymer chains [49]. Mesh size is defined as the end-to-end distance of the polymer 
chain between junction points [23]. The size of the mesh is crucial for compounds to attach and for 
small molecules to diffuse. A typical mesh size for hydrogel-based network systems is in the range 
of 5-100 nm [23], [49]. Mesh size is determined either by theoretically calculation or by 
experimental swelling studies [23], [50]. One could also characterize mesh size by rheological 
methods and/or NMR measurements.  
The mesh size should be large enough to contain the macromolecules and keep them inside until 
swelling and subsequent release. The hydrodynamic radius of insulin is approximately 20 Angstrom 
(Å) [50].  
When increasing the mesh size, the flexibility of the system becomes higher and greater diffusion is 
possible into the network [8]. Incorporating PEGs into the system makes the system more structural 
flexible and this can lead to higher interpenetration within the system [8]. Varying the crosslinking 
density could change the hydrogel mesh size, which could influence the macromolecule diffusion 
[51]. 
 
2.3 Swelling properties 
When the hydrogel-based microneedle is placed in a hydrophilic environment, water will diffuse 
into the network making the system swell [52]. This will allow for the release of API. The stronger 
the density of the cross-linking, the slower diffusion of water into the network and this leads to 
decrease swelling and less release of API [8]. Proper swelling is important and swelling of the 
network should be large enough to increase mesh size so that release of API is possible, but the 
network should not swell to extensive so that it would cause pain to the patient [27]. Polyethylene 
glycols are characterized as a highly water-swollen hydrogel [53].

enhance water solubility, biocompatibility, biodegradability, increase stability in drug delivery, and

3.6 2-hydroxy-2-methylpropiophenone 
2-hydroxy-2-methylpropiophenone (PI) is an initiator for the photopolymerization. PI is necessary 
for the formation of covalent bonds between the hydrogel monomers in the precursor solution. UV-
curing has shown great potential because of its benefits such as instant drying, high productivity,

reacts with UV-light. Under UV light, the free radicals in the PI will initiate a reaction between the

the hydrogel [74], [75]. Photoinitiator concentration can be up to 10% (w/w) of the total 
formulation. Excess volume of PI will migrate to surroundings due to its low molecular weight, and

In this project a PI concentration of 5% (w/v) is used.

4.2 Preparation of hydrogel

4.2.1 Preparation of hydrogel patches 
Hydrogel patches with polymer content of 10%, 20%, and 30% (w/v) of the total solution were 
fabricated using the solvent casting method. A calculated amount of PEGDA (w/w) or (w/v) was 
dissolved in demineralized water. To this solution a 5% (w/v) of the polymer concentration, amount

to shield it from light and to protect it from initial crosslinking.  
The solution was vortexed for 2 min at 3-4 rpm. Afterwards, the solution was placed in a vacuum 
desiccator for approximately 30 minutes to reduce bubbles. After vacuum, the solution was 
transferred with a pipette to a 3D printed mould and cured under UV-vis light at 365 nm. UV-
chamber and UV-light source was from Opsytec Dr. Gröbel, Germany. After curing, the hydrogel 
was ready for further examination.

4.3 Release studies 
Release of the model compound from the hydrogel matrix to the surrounding liquid was 
investigated using a Varioskan Lux plate reader from ThermoScientific. A connected computer with 
SkanIt RE 6.1 software was used to interpret the results and the result was collected in an excel 
worksheet. Every experiment was conducted in PBS with a pH of approximately 7.2 and a 
temperature of 37 C° to imitate conditions in the human buccal cavity. The pH of the PBS was

conditions, without damaging the hydrogel. At predetermined timepoints, 200 µL were collected

solution to avoid sink conditions. After the last collected sample, the plate was placed in the plate 
reader and results were collected. Release study experiments are in general time consuming, and 
reliability is poor due to standard curves [41]. See further in Appendix 1.

camera will create an image from the repelled electrons. The information obtained from SEM is

4.4.2 Fluorescence images 
Fluorescence imaging is used to visualize cell and tissues marked with a fluorophore [81]. Low 
concentration fluorophores can be detected with high selectivity and specificity. Normally, 
unexcited fluorophore molecules are in their stable ground state, with low rotational and vibrational 
energy. Then, the fluorophore is exposed to light (excited), a proton is being absorb and will move 
from the stable ground state to a higher-energy state with higher rotational and vibrational energy. 
Due to internal forces the fluorophore molecule is pushed back into its ground state and in that 
process emission of fluorescence light at a characteristic wavelength appear. Fluorescence emission 
occur when fluorophore goes back to its ground state from the lowest energy state. Energy of 
excitation is greater than energy from emission. When performing fluorescence imaging 
photosensitive detectors measure the intensity of emitted light and create a digital image of the 
sample [81].    
The fluorescence images are obtained using the Leica optical microscope in fluorescence mode.  
Images are processed using LAS X software.

6 Conclusion 
The purpose of this master thesis project was to make hydrogel-based microneedles for buccal 
delivery of macromolecules. I successfully fabricated microneedles within the hydrogel that 
demonstrated the highest release of the model compound dextran. 
The release of a macromolecule from a hydrogel patch was successfully measured. I fabricated 
different hydrogel patches with different MW of PEGDA and evaluated them according to their 
release. A clear tendency between low MW of PEGDA and higher release was also demonstrated. I 
looked at different polymer concentrations, 10%, 20%, and 30%. With polymer content 10% and 
20%, the difference in release was not different, but with 30% polymer content the release of model 
compound was decreased. I investigated UV-curing influence on the release profile of model 
compound dextran. Curing in the range 10-60 seconds do not differentiate as this curing interval 
was too narrow. The UV-curing interval from 30 second to 600 seconds showed that with more 
UV-curing, less model compound was being released from the hydrogels.  
Different compositions of PEGDA and PEGMA was investigated to determine if the different 
compositions had an influence of release of the model compound from the hydrogel. PEGDA: 
PEGMA with the relation 5:90 was the hydrogel that showed the highest release of model 
compound.  
CEA and AEM was added to the hydrogel solution to evaluate their influence of release of model 
compound. CEA had the most influence as it had higher release of model compound from the 
hydrogel. 
During the 24-hour release study, the PEGDA with MW 700 Da had the highest release of model 
compound. The maximal release was measured after 210 minutes, and afterwards the release 
decrease.  
The second 24-hour release study investigated the largest MW of model compound possible to be 
released from the hydrogel. MW of 4 kDa, 10 kDa, and 20 kDa was investigated together with a 
control and FITC. FITC did not show decrease intensity during the 24 hours. The control only had 
small intensity, which was concluded to come from the plate or PBS. The same tendency of 
decreased release after 210 minutes was observed as the previously 24-study. It was concluded that 
the 20 kDa FITC-dextran had the highest release, then FITC-dextran 4 kDa, and the hydrogel with 
lowest release was Rhodamine-dextran 10 kDa. The results indicated that 20 kDa dextran was not

confirmed by fluorescence images. 
The mechanical strengths of the MN were investigated and evaluated for their ability to penetrate an

calculated. The microneedles with PEGDA, PI and FITC-dextran showed the highest insertion

7 Future perspectives  
Many questions have been answered with this master thesis. However, questions have also emerged 
during this project. Many of these questions could not be investigated within the projects time 
frame. 
As systemic effect can be obtained with buccal drug delivery, are there other fields where buccal 
microneedle patches could be relevant? Patient groups, like elderly people, people with difficulties 
to swallow, small children, where such delivery system could have advantage instead of tablets or 
injections. 
I would have like to investigate the release profiles using HPLC. As demonstrated in the Appendix, 
the current quantification by means of fluorescence is linked with a high degree of variation. It 
would also have been interesting to investigate the influence of the swelling properties and the mesh 
size on the hydrogel further. This could be done by rheology studies, NMR, and mass spec. 
After the microneedles penetrates the buccal mucosa and the subsequent removal of the patch, there 
will be small holes in the mucosa. It would be interesting to investigate the saliva wash-out effect. 
Do I lose some of my model compound and in that case, how much?  
Finally, it would have been interesting to investigate the actual “cut off” of the MW that the 
hydrogels can incorporate into is matrix before release. Testing should be made with the same 
fluorophore and with a MW range from 10 kDa to 20 kDa.

8.1.3 Effect of difference from plate to plate 
The difference between plates was investigated on the same standard solution and the result is 
presented in Figure 41. 
 
Figure 41: Difference between plates on the same standard solution. Wavelength 519 nm. 
In Figure 41 it is displayed that there is a difference between plates as the same standard solution

solution. This gives a good indication of the error that is possible on the release measurements.

corresponds to a difference of 29% and demonstrates why a standard curve for a given set of sample

To sum up, always make new standard solutions and analyse them on the same plate as the 
experiment and on the same day. Be cautious about dynamic range and aim at having minimum 8 
measurements within the relevant range.
