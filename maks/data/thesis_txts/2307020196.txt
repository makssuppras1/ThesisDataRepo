Abstract 
It is a more and more accepted view that the safety of traffic is the shared 
responsibility of the designers, operators and users of roads. In the past years, 
numerous advancements have been made in the field of traffic safety, but many areas 
are still unexplored. 
This study – conducted in the scope of a master thesis – looks at the effect of socio-
economic differences, driving style, and the need for closure; together with the provision 
of roadside information (in the form of traffic signs and road surface markings). Effects 
on road users’ understanding of traffic locations, their perceived level of traffic safety, 
and their perception of the improvement in traffic safety and level of comfort are 
checked. 
An online questionnaire was used to gather 753 responses from two Hungarian target 
groups: professionals and non-professionals in transportation. Respondents were 
presented with images of 12 traffic locations (5 from Copenhagen, Denmark, 7 from 
Budapest, Hungary). The locations were sorted into two sections, depending on the 
relationship between the roadside information and the road infrastructure. If traffic 
signs and surface markings were used to compensate ambiguity in the road geometry, 
the location was placed in the “ambiguity” section, whereas if the traffic signs contained 
information that was also implied by the road environment, the location was placed in 
the “redundancy” section. All locations were presented first in a low info condition, and 
afterwards with additional information (in the form of road signs or surface markings), 
creating the high info condition. Respondents afterwards filled out personality tests 
belonging to two personality scales: the Need For Closure Scale and the 
Multidimensional Driver Style Inventory. Socio-economic parameters and professional 
experience was also observed. 
Respondents answered questions regarding legal maneuvers in the locations, and 
provided an estimation of the frequency of traffic conflicts in the low info condition, 
while responding with their estimation of the improvement in the number of conflicts 
and their level of comfort in the high info condition, and in the “redundancy” section, 
with the helpfulness of the additional traffic signs. 
Collected data was analyzed using three analytical methods: firstly, factor analysis 
was performed to extract behavioral factors by which respondents can be categorized. A 
total of 8 factors were identified. Afterwards, standard factor scores were computed, and 
correlation with standardized dependent variables was checked. Significant correlations 
were found. 
Next, Latent Class Analysis was performed in two separate models: one with only the 
socio-economic variables, and the other additionally including the items belonging to the 
two personality scales. 4 road user classes were identified for both models, but results 
==PAGE:4==
4 
 
did not yield significant differences in answering. Frequency distributions however led 
to the suspicion that factors serve as mediator variables between the socio-economic 
parameters and the dependent variables. 
Finally, a Structural Equation Model was designed to uncover any additional 
correlations between the explanatory variables, the factors and the dependent variables. 
Three sets of equations were used to estimate model parameters: (i) measurement 
equations linking the personality scale items to the factors, (ii) structural equations 
linking the socio-economic parameters to the factors, and (iii) structural equations 
linking the factors to the dependent variables. SEM revealed significant correlations in 
all 3 sets of equations.

1 Introduction 
A study commissioned by the USDoT (Treat et al., 1979) concluded that an 
overwhelming 90% of all traffic accidents to occur in the United States could be traced 
back to behavioral causes. Back then, when traffic safety was not yet a standalone 
science, the prevailing view was that if a traffic incident occurs, the driver alone is to 
blame. This is a philosophy that has changed greatly, as today, when more and more 
emphasis is on the safety of the roads, it is believed that traffic safety is a responsibility 
shared between three stakeholders: the designers, the operators and the users of the 
road. 
Perhaps the most important way of interaction between these three stakeholders 
happens by road signs and road surface markings.  Therefor it is no surprise that in 
every country, strict regulations are in place in order to ensure the consistency and 
efficiency of the road sign system. Furthermore, as the most significant international 
agreement in place today, the Convention on Road Traffic (UNECE, 1968) ensures that – 
at least between the contracting countries – a foreign road user will be mostly familiar 
with symbols, signs and general practices regardless of the country they are driving in. 
This however does not mean that the road sign system is the same in every country. 
On the contrary: road manuals for different countries have quite different attitudes 
particularly towards the provision of information. Not only are there differences in what 
information the road operator needs to convey, but also there are multiple ways the 
conveying of this message can be done. For example if in an intersection a certain 
amount of movements are allowed, the road operator can decide to signal the permitted 
maneuvers, or to forbid the illegal ones. Both strategies are supposed to produce the 
same result, but as it was shown in a study by Roca, Castro, Bueno, & Moreno-ríos, 
(2012), the different ways of conveying the same message can be perceived differently 
depending on the type of goal the road user has (e.g. where to go, or where not to go). 
It is also important to note that - perhaps trivially – every person is different. And 
since road traffic is the mode of transportation that is most open to the public (with 
some road users having minimal training, and others having none at all), it is essential to 
understand just how much these individual differences factor into the safety of such a 
heterogeneous system. 
1.1 The deselection process 
The effect of individual differences in a road safety context can be investigated in a 
multitude of ways, so it was necessary to narrow down the focus of present study. 
During the creative thinking process preceding the start of work, several ideas came up 
that were worth exploring.

One idea for example was based on research conducted by current author’s bachelor 
thesis, in which highway directional signs were re-designed using a new layout 
standard, and then comprehension of these signs was measured using eye tracking 
equipment. In this previous study, due to technical limitations, only a small sample was 
included, and no consideration was taken to personality differences.

via a questionnaire, while reserving the use of the simulator for later work.

1.2 Motivation 
After consulting a number of road manuals, it soon became evident that – aside from 
the general principles being largely agreed upon – the details that determine the 
mentality of the signage system in each country can vary quite a bit. For example, in New 
Zealand, no more than one traffic sign can be mounted on the same pole, wall or fence 
(New Zealand Transport Agency, 2010), while this number is two in the United 
Kingdom, the United States and most other places, or it could be three, as is the case in 
Hungary for example. This means that road users could be faced with a different amount 
of information, depending on which country they are driving in. So it is justified to 
question if this difference in the level of information in the road environment accounts 
for differences in road user performance. 
1.3 Hypothesis 
The hypothesis of this thesis is threefold. 
H-1 
Firstly, that road users are heterogeneous in the way they perceive and 
process the information in the road environment; 
H-2 
secondly, that for every road user, the amount of information provided in the 
road environment is positively correlated to their perception of traffic safety 
and comfort, and 
H-3 
thirdly, that for every road user, a certain amount of information is enough to 
not see a traffic location as ambiguous any more, but from thereon consider it 
redundant, and also, that this point comes at a different level of information 
for each road user. 
In particular, hypothesis H-3 can be visualized with the graph in Fig. 3. 
The theory is that when the minimum possible level of information is provided by the 
road environment, every road user perceives the location as ambiguous, and thus (in 
most cases) experiences discomfort, stress or anxiety. But as the level of information in 
the road environment increases, there are less and less aspects of the traffic location 
road users are uncertain about, until finally assuming a certain level of information, 
every blank is filled in, and the road user is sure how to behave in the given situation. 
This point can be titled the “point of clarity”, as this is the tipping point between a traffic 
location being perceived by a road user as ambiguous and redundant. 
But this is not a single point that holds true for all road users. The second part of H-3 
is that for each type of road user, the point of clarity comes at a different information 
level, thus making the point of clarity more of a distribution over road users, better 
visualized by the orange bell curve on Fig. 3. 
==PAGE:10==
10 
 
 
Fig. 3: The theorized connection between the level of information in the road environment and road users’ perceived level 
of safety 
This creates the range of information provision within which a compromise needs to 
be made when determining the strategy behind the signage system in order to fit the 
needs of most road users.  
After the point of clarity, additional pieces of information do not help road users any 
further, and thus their level of perceived safety does not change significantly. It could 
also be argued that heading towards the maximal level of information, road users start 
experiencing discomfort again. This could be for multiple reasons: for example, road 
users might think that the location is so heavily supplied with traffic signs because it is a 
particularly dangerous location, or they could get the false sense that they are not 
processing every bit of information they should be, even though the excess information 
is just redundancy. But this aspect of the graph is outside the scope of this thesis.

2 Literature review 
In order to uncover the state of the art in this particular context, a multi-disciplinary 
literature review needed to be conducted. The literature review was focused on three 
disciplines: the science of cognition, traffic signs, and personality scales, since the topic 
sits in the section of these three disciplines. 
2.1 Cognitive science 
First of all, it was important to find out the way individuals can be different when it 
comes to perceiving and processing information. There are some constructs that are 
believed to be universal among individuals. Such is the theory of the working memory 
by Braddeley & Hitch (1974), which talks about a central executive in the human mind 
that is in charge of distributing and allocating attention as a limited and type-specific 
resource. This means that for example two different types of tasks can be performed at 
once with relative ease, whereas two tasks of the same type take far more concentration. 
But the relevance of this to the topic is that everybody’s attention is limited, especially in 
a situation where only a short amount of time is available to interpret the information, 
such as a driving scenario. This supports the relevance of looking into the level of 
information in the road environment. 
The theory of the working memory having a limited capacity is further strengthened 
by Deese & Kaufman, (1957), who explored a phenomenon later named the Serial 
Position Effect, which says that individuals are the most likely to recall the first and the 
last item in any given sequence of information they are presented with, and later 
questioned about. The first item due to the primacy effect, and the last one due to the 
recency effect. An explanation for the primacy effect is that at the time of perception, the 
working memory of an individual is presumably empty, and thus all of the attention can 
be allocated to that one item. But later on, as further elements arrive, attention needs to 
be split between them. This, once again suggests that attention is a limited resource. 
Working memory capacity can also be measured in time. For example, the New 
Zealand road manual – among many other cognitive science considerations – limits the 
distance between a warning sign and the hazard it warns about to 15 seconds of driving, 
since this is the so called refractory period of the working memory (also called the short 
term memory). 
Previous research also suggests that significant differences exist between individuals 
in their need for cognitive closure, measured by the Need For Closure Scale (NFCS from 
hereon), first theorized by Webster & Kruglanski, (1994). It was later shown that this 
indicator can determine the way people process information (Kossowska & Bar-tal, 
2013) recall it correctly (Dijksterhus, Van Knippenberg, Kruglanski, & Shaper, 1996), or 
cope with uncertainty (Kossowska, Czernatowicz-Kukuczka, & Jas, 2014). Relevance of 
the NFCS will be further detailed in sub-chapter 3.1.1. 
==PAGE:12==
12 
 
Another study that uncovered differences between individuals in their paying 
attention was one conducted by Costa et al., (2014): while analyzing looking behavior of 
participants driving in actual traffic, they discovered that their subjects were 
significantly different with regards to paying attention. An additional discovery of the 
study was that the participants only looked at an average of 25% of all posted traffic 
signs (note: the maximum allowed number of traffic signs in Italy is 2 per pole). This 
seems to further strengthen the motivation of present study, regarding the amount of 
information provided in the road environment. 
2.2 Traffic signs 
Research dealing with traffic signs is plentiful. In most studies, in connection with 
drivers recognizing signs, such factors are considered as gender, age, driving experience, 
income, and other socio-economic parameters. Sometimes also nationality, or foreign 
driving experience enter as explanatory variables. 
For example, Al-Madani, (2000) and Al-Madani & Al-Jahani, (2002) have investigated 
the role socio-economic characteristics play in drivers comprehending traffic signs. The 
studies targeted large samples (4774 responses used as basis of analysis) across the 
Arabian Gulf countries. In their studies, they computed explanatory variables using 
driver characteristics such as accident involvement and years of driving experience. 
Afterwards, using ANOVA, they analyzed correlations for their sample between the 
explanatory variables and comprehension of the traffic signs (measured as answering 
the correct meaning of the sign out of four answer options). In both studies, the 28 
analyzed traffic signs were presented as single images. 
Shinar et al., (2003) asked close to 1000 respondents from 4 different countries to 
identify the meaning of 31 signs. After answers have been collected, 3 different aspects 
were investigated: road users’ performance as a function of their belonging to one of 5 
user groups (novice drivers, tourists, older drivers, problem drivers and university 
students); causes of opposite comprehension of meaning and finally the comprehension 
of local and non-local traffic signs. Signs were presented one by one, and responses were 
collected verbally, in an open format. Correlations were analyzed using ANOVA. When 
checking which of the signs were best comprehended, they discovered that the presence 
of good ergonomic design principles (spatial compatibility, conceptual compatibility, 
physical representation, familiarity, and standardization) were positively correlated to 
correct comprehension. 
Ng & Chan (2008) in a much smaller scale study targeted 109 Hong Kong drivers, and 
measured their comprehension scores computed in a way that guessing the correct 
meaning of a sign among multiple guesses also produced fractional points. In this case, 
sign design features (such as familiarity, correctness, simplicity and meaningfulness) 
were also entered as explanatory variables. Traffic signs, similarly to the previous study 
were also presented without context, and were assigned 4 answer choices. They too 
==PAGE:13==
13 
 
used ANOVA to uncover correlations between the explanatory variables and the 
comprehension score. 
As shown by the previous two studies, design principles can also enter as explanatory 
variables. Apart from basic ergonomic design principles, such as familiarity, simplicity, 
semantic closeness (Ou & Liu, 2012), explanatory variables can also be created for 
attributes such as colors used (B. Liu, Wang, Song, & Wu, 2010). In this study, although 
not the primary research question, the researchers concluded that changing the 
background color of traffic signs did not significantly affect familiarity by test subjects. 
Moving onto studies that consider the context of traffic signs as well, the location of 
traffic signs is also a key issue. For example Borowsky, Shinar & Parmet (2008) have 
investigated that schemata drivers employ while driving can also be harmful to 
perceiving information, as more experienced drivers are less likely to notice traffic signs 
posted in unexpected locations. Novice drivers did not show the same preconception, 
and females were also less likely to have impaired perception due to unusual placement 
of traffic signs. Participants’ eye movements were recorded as they were presented with 
still images of different traffic locations, some with the signs in the expected place, and 
some with signs somewhere else. 
X. Liu & Lu, (2013) determined that not only position, but frequency, information 
content and lighting are also important factors in road users processing the necessary 
information. In their study, they assessed the comprehension of way finding signs as a 
function of environmental factors. Here as well eye tracker equipment was used, but in 
this case, participants were driving in actual traffic. 
Environmental factors were also considered in the study conducted by Ho, Scialfa, 
Caird, & Graw, (2001). Luminance and clutter were varied, and together with age, the 
effect of these three parameters were checked on visual search strategy (measured by 
eye tracking, on still images or driving scenes). The most important novelty of this study 
is that it considers traffic signs in relation to either other traffic signs, or surrounding 
objects. The study concluded that the elderly tend to make more mistakes, but more 
importantly also, that clutter had an adverse effect on search efficiency. 
Young et al., (2009) have proven that road side advertisement has a clear adverse 
effect on the lateral control of drivers, and their attention (due to their inscreased 
mental workload). In a simulator study, they have concluded that a higher visual 
demand increases lateral variation in driving. Bendak & Al-saleh, (2010) conducted a 
similar study, where they were observing 5 performance indicators. Two of which were 
significantly worse (drifting from lane and recklessly crossing intersections) in the 
condition with road side advertisements. Edquist, Horberry, Hosking, & Johnston, 
(2011) additionally concluded that roadside advertisements delay responses to traffic 
signs by 0,5 to 1 second, and that individual (socio-demographic) differences influence 
the magnitude of the distraction. Metz & Krüger, (2014) took another approach when 
==PAGE:14==
14 
 
commercial information signs were placed at highway exists in Germany, informing 
drivers about their dining and shopping options at the upcoming exit. In this situation, it 
could be argued that the distracting (not traffic-relevant) content is within the traffic 
sign. Contradicting the previous studies, they found no adverse effects of the 
supplementary signs containing commercial information, and attributed this to the fact 
that drivers use top-down processing1 when seeking for specific information on 
directional signs.  
The type of information (text, symbol or both) is yet another way to look at the issue.  
Koyuncu & Amado, (2008) discovered – as one part of their three part research question 
– that traffic signs are best comprehended when they contain both text and symbol. 
Shinar & Vogelzang, (2013) found something else to be true: their study showed that 
purely text signs were comprehended the fastest, even in the case of unfamiliar signs. 
They argued however that the different methods of presentation could account for the 
different findings: while they presented the signs as still images, Koyuncu and Amado 
used a driving simulator. They explained the relevance of this with symbols being 
comprehensible from a greater distance, and thus interpretation being possible from 
earlier on.  
Finally, studies in this category, that have the closest relation to present study are 
those concerned with the amount of information within signs. For example Y. Liu, 
(2005) assessed a total of 187 regulatory and 1272 directional signs with regards to 
their information content. Participants were then presented with the signs and answer 
correctness, time and visual search strategy were recorded. Liu concluded that the 
greater the amount of information, the less likely full processing is to occur. This, once 
again can be linked to the theory of the working memory, and attention being a limited 
resource. It was also discovered that pictorial content in signs enhances participants’ 
capacity in remembering the content. The study also proposes that separating 
information by type, and displaying each type on a different sign can have positive effect 
on efficiency of comprehension, and can reduce search time by indirectly promoting the 
favorable search strategy of the two possibilities. 
2.3 Road user heterogeneity 
One of the first studies to look into road users as different personalities was one by 
Gwyther & Holland, (2014), who conducted interviews with groups of drivers in order to 
find out what scenarios make them feel stressed, vulnerable, or generally at risk while 
driving. Nine focus groups were compiled, after which the interview sessions were 
recorded and transcribed. Themes were afterwards identified using thematic analysis. In 
the end, they made a connection between the feeling of anxiety, and three coping 
                                                          
1 Top-down processing refers to individuals using their pre-existing knowledge in order to make sense of 
information, as opposed to bottom-up processing, which is a strategy used when individuals are faced with 
information they have no existing knowledge of (Reisberg, 2015). 
==PAGE:15==
15 
 
mechanisms identified by Lajunen & Summala, (1995) and Matthews, Desmond, Joyner, 
Carcary, & Kirby, (1997): alertness, aggression, and dislike of driving. Only one of these 
three is desirable, so it seems important to avoid individuals feeling discomfort or stress 
while being road users.  
A similar study was conducted in Mexico, to uncover what scenarios cause stress in 
road users over there (Dorantes, Tortosa, & Ferrero, 2016). The most significant finding 
of this study was that socio-demographic characteristics (specifically gender and age) 
had no effect on drivers experiencing different levels of stress. In other words, socio-
demographic parameters cannot account for all behavioral differences. 
Even though most studies consider road users to be different only in their socio-
demographic or –economic parameters, a tool developed by Taubman-Ben-Ari, 
Mikulincer, & Gillath, (2004), the Multidimensional Driving Style Inventory (MDSI from 
hereon) allows for differentiation between road user types in a number of ways. After 
combining items from existing measures, such as the Driving Behavior Inventory 
(Gulian, Matthews, Glendon, Davies, & Debney, 1989), the Driver Behaviour 
Questionnaire (Reason, Manstead, Stradling, Baxter, & Campbell, 1990) and the Driving 
Style Questionnaire (French, West, Elander, & Wilding, 1993), 8 driver factors were 
revealed, namely dissociative, anxious, risky, angry, high-velocity, distress reduction, 
patient and careful.  Factor scores were then obtained by averaging participants’ ratings 
over the items belonging to each factor. 
Later the inventory had also been adapted and validated for an Argentinian (Poó, 
Taubman-ben-ari, Ledesma, & Díaz-lázaro, 2013), and also a Romanian (Holman & 
Havârneanu, 2015) sample, where one additional factor, violation of rules contextually 
perceived as irrational emerged, due to specifics of the driving scene in Romania. 
Loo, (1978) in his study, additionally to some socio-demographic aspects, considered 
drivers’ personality differences regarding field dependence and temperament. First 
examined by Witkin, Dyk, Fattuson, Goodenough, & Karp, (1962), field dependence 
describes the cognitive style of perceiving information in connection to its context. Field 
dependent individuals would have a stronger tendency to experience items as part of 
the field they perceive it in, whereas field-independent individuals would see the item 
more separate. 
Loo checked temperament using the Eysenck Personality Inventory, developed in 
1975 by Eysenck & Eysenck. This inventory differentiates individuals in three 
dimensions: 
extraversion/introversion, 
neuroticism/stability 
and 
psychoticism/ 
socialization. Later, a fourth dimension, lying was added to find out to what extent 
would participants try to control their scores. 
In the experiment, participants were presented with traffic signs in embedded and 
disembedded contexts, and reaction times were measured by verbal signals. Loo 
concluded that field-dependent subjects and extroverts took longer to comprehend 
==PAGE:16==
16 
 
embedded traffic signs (compared to field-independent and introvert subjects, 
respectively). Possibly this study bears the closest resemblance to present study, but 
even though traffic signs are considered with their environment, they are still viewed as 
single items, and not instruments of controlling the level of information in the road 
environment. 
If anything is to be taken away from the literature review, it is that all aspects of 
present study have already been explored on some level, but most of them separately. 
Present study can thus offer a novice way of combining previously existing areas of 
behavioral and traffic research.

3 Methods 
3.1 Adapting the behavioral frameworks 
Since the hypothesis of this thesis relies on differences between road users, 
behavioral frameworks needed to be selected in order to observe and enter these 
differences as explanatory variables. When selecting the frameworks, two different 
purposes needed to be fulfilled: one scale was needed to capture differences between 
individuals in their way of information processing, and one that allowed the observation 
of different driving styles, or how individuals behave as a road user. This is why the two 
frameworks that were selected are the NFCS, and the MDSI. 
3.1.1 NFCS

individuals differ in their need for cognitive closure.

They defined the NFCS as a latent measure comprised of five factors, which are desire 
for predictability, preference for order and structure, discomfort with ambiguity, 
decisiveness, and close-mindedness. The idea that each person has a different rating on 
these factors is highly relevant to a transportation context. For example an individual 
who has a higher discomfort with ambiguity might end up feeling stressed or vulnerable 
in the same traffic location, where another road user with a lower level of discomfort 
with ambiguity will have no problems. It is therefore important to understand the 
composition of road users regarding their behavioral characteristics in order to create 
traffic solutions which are suitable for the widest range of road users possible. 
Webster and Kruglanski used a 6 point Likert scale to measure the level of 
respondents’ agreement or disagreement with a total of 42 items. These were 
statements regarding the respondent’s behavior or habits, e.g. „I do not usually consult 
many different options before forming my own view”. 
However, due to its length (the original NFCS contains 42 items) researchers started 
shortening the measure themselves, adapting it to their research needs, so recognizing 
the need for a shortened and validated version, Roets & Hiel, (2011) created a 15 item 
reduced version, including only 3 items per factor. 
Since present study needed a questionnaire that contained not only the NFCS, but also 
the MDSI, the number of items needed to be reconsidered. So as to ensure a high number 
of full responses, the decision was made to limit the NFCS items by using the reduced 
scale, and further excluding items (a total of 3) by discarding ones not so relevant to 
transportation (such as „I feel irritated when one person disagrees with what everyone 
else in a group believes”).  
The remainder of the items needed to be adapted to the topic of the questionnaire, in 
order to avoid respondents becoming confused about the relevance of the questions, or 
losing respect for the study altogether. For this reason, each of the items was re-worded 
in a way that they became related to traffic, but still captured the original intention 
behind the item. A list of the original items, and their re-worded pairs can be found in 
Table 1.

Respondents – similarly to the NFCS study – rated each item on a 6 point Likert scale,

3.2 Survey design 
In order to collect responses from Hungarian road users, a Hungarian language online 
survey was designed (Attachment 2, for the English version, please see Attachment 1). 
The aim was to gather information about respondents’ previous interest in the topic of 
traffic signs (and road surface markings), their socio-economic characteristics, and their 
ratings on the NFCS and MDSI items as explanatory variables. Afterwards, their 
performance on a number of traffic locations was assessed as a function of these 
explanatory variables. 
The survey was created using the online service SurveyMonkey, and consisted of 7 
main sections: 
1. General information regarding the goal of the study, and detailed information 
of the non-monetary incentives (to ensure a high number of responses) 
2. The respondents’ previous interest in the topic of the study 
3. The first set of locations, titled the “Ambiguity section” 
4. The second set of locations, titled the “Redundancy section” (both sections 
described in detail in sub-chapter 3.2.1) 
5. The NFCS items 
6. The MDSI items 
7. Socio-economic parameters, professional experience 
When deciding the order of the sections, special care was taken so as to place the 
respondents in the correct mindset (hence the placement of section 1), and afterwards, 
to introduce sections and questions in ascending order, regarding their level of being 
intrusive. This way, when filling out answers in the traffic locations sections, 
respondents had anonymity, which they only needed to break, if they wished to 
participate in the raffle. 
Aesthetic and linguistic considerations were also made to ensure that the language of 
the questionnaire is not demeaning, but still understandable for non-professionals at the 
same time, furthermore questions were shortened and organized in a way so that each 
page showed a clear, uncongested layout. At the bottom of each page, a progress bar 
showed the rate of completion to respondents. And in order not to create the feeling of a 
long survey, questions were not numbered when the questionnaire was live, but are 
displayed now in the attachments for easier identification by code.

3.2.1 Traffic locations 
At the core of the analysis are the images that were presented to participants. When 
selecting and preparing the images, a number of considerations needed to be made in 
order to provide the most realistic materials for respondents to assess. 
The choice of cities to take locations from was determined so as to fit the focus of the 
study: if the level of information is to be varied, locations needed to be selected from 
countries which have distinctively different traffic signage policies regarding the level of 
provided information. Opportunely, Budapest (current author’s original place of 
residence) has a road design policy which allows for slightly more traffic signs than the 
average (a maximum 3 per pole), while Copenhagen generally employs less traffic signs, 
and maintains a clean streetscape. 
After this, the first task was to decide which specific traffic locations to feature. An 
obvious choice for selection criterion would have been to investigate accident hotspots. 
In the case of Copenhagen, GIS accident data was obtained and consulted, and produced 
a number of intersections, but upon reviewing the locations with regards to their 
geometry, complexity and level of information, no obvious pattern emerged as to what 
might cause a higher number of accidents that is related to information perception and 
processing. Also, regarding the accidents, a problematic aspect was that the 
administration of the cause of the accidents can never be truly accurate, and so locations 
might have falsely entered the selection. So finally, the choice of locations was made 
using a heuristic approach: bigger intersections were surveyed in the Copenhagen road 
network using Google Street View.

turns are permitted in this intersection. 
Regarding Budapest locations, the same method (Google Street View) was used, but 
in this case, locations were sought where information was present in the road 
environment in a way that at least two pieces of information regarded the same aspect 
of the location. 
For example, in Fig. 5, the “go straight” sign on the right is meant to tell road users 
that turning into the street on the left is not permitted. However, the same restriction is 
also implied by the physical barrier of concrete studs lining the side of the tram tracks.

Of course the paradoxical nature of selecting the locations must be noted. Since the 
starting hypothesis of the study is that individuals perceive traffic locations differently, 
regarding their feelings of uncertainty, it is impossible for any individual to objectively 
determine if a location is ambiguous or redundant. Therefore, when sorting the locations 
regarding their level or ambiguity or redundancy, discussions with the supervisors were 
the deciding factor. In this way, a total of 12 locations were selected, and sorted into an 
ambiguity and a redundancy section. In the former, 5 locations were selected from 
Copenhagen, and the remaining one from Budapest, while in the latter, all 6 locations 
were selected from Budapest.

The next task was to prepare the images for presentation. First of all, any street labels 
and image errors needed to be cleaned up so as to not distract participants from the goal 
of the study. Then, in every picture depicting an intersection, the decision was made to 
set all traffic lights to yellow. This way respondents did not apply the schemata of having 
the right of way at green, or an inevitable stop at red. Further considerations aimed at 
avoiding the triggering of undesired schemata were keeping the weather similar, and 
the traffic volume low enough to allow for good visibility. Google Street View’s history 
option proved to be useful in offering multiple recordings of the same locations. 
Finally, the level of information needed to be modified in order to create two 
configurations of each traffic locations. Commonly across both sections, the first (low 
info) configuration of each location always contained less information than the second. 
However, in case of locations from the first, “ambiguity” section, information either 
needed to be added or removed to create the second (high info) configuration. For 
example if an intersection had an unusual layout, but contained road surface markings 
for all available lanes, markings were removed in the lane from which the direction of 
travel seemed not to be straightforward. The type of information removed or added was 
also varied between traffic signs or road surface markings. 
In the second, “redundancy” section, in all cases, information needed to be removed to 
create the first, low info configuration, and this was always done by the removal of 
traffic signs.

Two important behavioral phenomena needed to be accounted for that posed the risk 
of skewing the results. The familiarity heuristic is a phenomenon in cognitive psychology 
that is based on the availability heuristic, first studied by Tversky & Kahneman, (1973) 
and it simply states that individuals, when faced with multiple scenarios, will favor the 
more familiar one. This can be applied to a number of situations. 
For example if respondents are explicitly told that one of the two presented 
configurations are modified, and the other one is the original setup, they might end up 
systematically associating the status quo with a higher level of safety. The need to 
consider such phenomena is further strengthened by the fact that the higher the level of 
uncertainty, the more likely individuals are to employ heuristics as a rule of thumb, to 
make judgments easier (Tversky & Kahneman, 1974). For this reason, it was never 
explicitly stated which one of the two configurations is the original setup, and which one 
was digitally modified to add or remove information. 
And in order to not give away which configuration is the original one, special care 
needed to be taken when manipulating the images. Digital correction was sometimes 
performed using images taken from multiple angles of the same location, in order to 
replace traffic signs with what could be seen in their absence. Once again, Google Street 
view proved to be advantageous in this regard as well. 
The second phenomenon to consider is a much more straightforward concept: since 
the same location needed to be presented twice in a row, participants could not have 
been tested on their knowledge in both cases, since they would have likely maintained 
the answer they provided in the first configuration. 
In case of marking the legal maneuvers/actions in the second section, initially only 
maneuvers relating to the removed signs were questioned, but since this would have 
created a scenario where the majority of checkboxes would have needed to be left 
unchecked, participants would have grown “immune” to those instances where a 
maneuver/action can be marked legal. To avoid this, legal maneuvers/actions were 
added to most of the locations, in order to maintain a seemingly balanced amount of 
checked and unchecked boxes.

4 Analysis and results 
Obtaining such a large sample with so many explanatory, observed dependent and 
latent variables offers a multitude of analytical approaches. Adhering to the main goal of 
the study, associations and correlations needed to be uncovered between road users’ 
personality traits and their performance and feelings. For this purpose, three analytical 
methods were selected: 
 Factor analysis, in order to create the framework for categorizing respondents 
based on their individual differences, 
 Latent Class Analysis, in order to uncover any additional road user clusters 
that the factors or socio-demographic characteristics could not account for, 
and 
 Structural Equation Modeling, to get a better idea of the topology of the model 
that best describes the relationship between the explanatory, and observed 
dependent variables. 
4.1 Sample characteristics 
The questionnaire was live between March 7th and March 27th of 2016. During this 
period, a total of 978 responses were collected. Out of this, 197 records were omitted, 
due to being incomplete, and another 28 records were discarded due to an error in the 
settings of two questions (regarding engineering and field experience): here, for 3 days 
during the live period, respondents were allowed to leave certain sub-questions 
unanswered. After the error was corrected, data collection continued as initially 
intended. After the closure of the survey, a total of 753 full responses (76% of all 
responses) were ready to be used as the basis of data analysis.

The majority (65%) of the sample was male, and aged between 15 and 30 years old 
(59%). 90% of respondents live in Hungary, 7% in Denmark, and 3% in other countries. 
Most of the respondents identified as personal car drivers (76%), while the second most 
populated road user group was vulnerable road users (cyclists and pedestrians, 11%), 
followed by 9% car passengers, 3% professional drivers (e.g. taxi, truck or bus drivers) 
and 1% motorcyclists. For an overview of the entire sample, please refer to Table 4.

multivariate statistics, for the purpose of developing scales to measure various 
constructs (Sharma, 1995). With the help of this technique, an equation can be created

terms, how strongly it loads on the factor. Equation (4. 1) describes the relationship 
between the items and the factor loadings.

4.3.1 Exploratory factor analysis 
In exploratory factor analysis, the structure of the latent factors representing the data 
is unknown, and therefor needs to be explored. 
Exploratory factor analysis was performed on the items from both the NFCS and the 
MDSI, using IBM’s SPSS Statistics 22. Even though the latent factors have been 
determined from both measures in previous literature, the adaption of certain items 
made it necessary to re-perform the factor analysis, and combining items from both 
measures allowed for latent constructs to emerge from both scales combined. 
The reliability of the survey was established as very good, at the overall level (with 
Cronbach’s Alpha = 0,75). Next, the suitability of the survey for factor analysis was 
verified by obtaining very good values of sampling adequacy according to the Kaiser-
Meyer-Olkin measure at an overall level (KMO = 0,846). On a per-item level (according 
to Sharma, 1995),  obtained from the anti-image correlation matrix, 6 of the items are 
middling (KMO between 0,7 and 0,8), 25 of the items are meritorious (KMO between 0,8 
and 0,9), and 3 of the items are marvelous (KMO above 0,9, see Table 6). 
Bartlett’s test of sphericity was also passed (chi-square = 6494,6; df = 561; p = 0,000, 
rejecting the null hypothesis of the correlation matrix being an identity matrix). Finally, 
the Spearman correlation matrix contained elements with absolute values between 0,3 
and 0,7, and the determinant was over 10-5, meaning that the existence of correlations 
without multi-collinearity is verified (Sharma, 1995).

Factor 3 (explaining 7,3% of the variance) contained 6 items (from the NFCS), 3 of 
which had to do with a tendency to make choices out of habit, and 3 items suggesting 
that an individual would avoid situations where an on the spot decision needs to be 
made. Both phenomena can be traced back to a tendency to avoid dynamic 
troubleshooting, and instead to develop a routine, so the factor was labeled “Habit”. 
Factor 4 (explaining 4,4% of the variance) contained 3 items (from the MDSI), all of 
which portray behavior that overrules traffic regulations in order to increase travel 
speed. All of these items were inspired by the Romanian version of the MDSI, but were 
novice additions. On this basis, the factor was named “Tendency to speed”. 
Factor 5 (explaining 3,8% of the variance) contained 5 items (from the MDSI), but the 
cohesive attribute of the factor was not so clear right away. 3 of the items clearly were 
related to lapses of road users, particularly car drivers. One item surprisingly was 
connected to increasing travel speed, and one was a deliberate violation of traffic 
regulations. However, with the exception of item Q50f, all of the items can be explained 
by general disorganization in one’s routine, and thus the item was named 
“Distractedness”. 
Factor 6 (explaining 3,7% of the variance) contained 4 items (1 from the NFCS, 3 
from the MDSI) that had something to do with patience, or lack thereof. Two items 
explicitly contained the word impatient, and the rest described actions one would 
perform when their travel is being delayed by other participants of traffic. For this 
reason, the factor was labeled “Impatience”. 
Factor 7  (explaining 3,4% of the variance) consisted of 4 items (1 from the NFCS, 3 
from the MDSI) which were all the types of statements a road user would say who is 
aware of proper conduct in traffic, and holds themselves closely to it. Cautious behavior 
and concentration were dominant elements among the items, but at the same time, the 
importance of traffic regulations so as to facilitate safe travel was also included, so the 
factor was named “Tendency to follow rules”. 
Factor 8 (explaining 3,2% of the variance) contained only 2 items (both from the 
MDSI) that hold close resemblance to each other, with the difference of a triggered 
emotion by the same phenomenon. While in item Q50d, it is simply stated that the 
individual considers some road rules to be unnecessary, Q49e additionally says that this 
makes the road user feel frustrated. Analyzing the factor loadings, it would seem that the 
common element between the two items is the opinion that some road rules should not 
be in place, so the factor was named “Tendency to consider some rules unnecessary”.

4.4 Latent Class Analysis 
Similarly to factor analysis, latent class analysis (or LCA for short) looks for patterns 
in data, in most cases, categorical data. Analyzing covariance between variables allows 
for the identification of underlying, latent constructs (in this case, classes instead of 
factors), and assigning class membership to each record. According to the goal of the 
==PAGE:40==
40 
 
analysis, considering the class membership, no additional covariance should exist 
between the variables (within each class; Chandola, 2011). 
For the purpose of identifying additional road user groups that can be described by 
more than just their socio-economic parameters and their behavioral traits, LCA was 
employed on the dataset. In order to test the effect of the behavioral factors, two 
separate models were iterated: one where only the socio-economic parameters entered 
as explanatory variables (model 1), and another where the personality scale items also 
provided basis for analysis (model 2). 
SAS 9.4 was used for the purpose of the analysis, and the PROC LCA module within 
that, developed by The Methodology Center at Penn State University (Lanza, Collins, 
Lemmon, & Schafer, 2007). 
4.4.1 Model 1 
As the first task, the number of classes needed to be determined. According to Nylund, 
Asparouhov, & Muthén, (2007), in their study investigating the reliability of different 
information criteria (including the AIC – Akaike Information Criterion, BIC – Bayesian 
Information Criterion, CAIC – Consistent AIC, and the adjusted BIC), they concluded that 
the AIC is rarely a good indicator for class number selection, especially for larger 
samples, for its tendency to overestimate the number of classes, among other reasons. 
They also found that in the case of a categorical dataset, the CIAC performed 
considerably bad for an n=500 sample. When looking at the BIC and aBIC, they found 
that the former performed very well when the sample size was nearing 1000 (for 
categorical datasets), whereas the latter showed weaknesses when the sample size was 
around 200, with a tendency to overestimate the number of classes. In conclusion, they 
recommend that the BIC be used in any case. 
Also, in order to reduce computational time and model complexity, the categories 
within the variables needed to be reduced. After running a preliminary iteration with 
minimal category reduction, it was determined where the main division was within each 
variable, and categories were reduced further. Table 8 shows the variable categories 
before and after reduction. 
The model was estimated by maximum likelihood (expectation-maximization 
algorythm), but due to the risk of the algorithm being stuck in a local maximum 
(likelihood function not necessarily concave), 50 sets of random starting values were 
used to increase the probability of converging to the global maximum. 
The model was run for class numbers between 2 and 20, in order to compute the BIC 
and entropy measures. Fig. 12 shows the BIC plot for the first model; based on this, 4 
classes were selected, with an entropy of 0,832. Although acceptable entropy levels are a 
subject of discussion, according to Prof. Bengt. O Muthén (one of the developers of 
Mplus), on the official support forum for Mplus, the use of most likely class membership

own are not sufficient to uncover behavioral differences between road users, in the 
second model, all of the items belonging to the two personality scales were also entered 
as explanatory variables. Same as in model 1, model 2 was run with the number of 
classes between 2 and 20. Fig. 20 shows the BIC plot, based on which – same as before – 
4 classes were defined, with an entropy of 0,892 (an improvement on the first model’s 
0,832).

movement between the two classes is to be expected. Aged below 30 years is no longer a 
relevant attribute for Class 2, likely due to cross-movement. Regardless, Class 1 and 
Class 4 were extracted to largely have the same attributes, so this can serve as validation 
for the existing latent patterns among respondent attributes.

In model 2, Class 1 can be defined as car drivers who drive regularly, and have high 
control and awareness while behind the wheel. Respondents belonging to class 1 had a 
higher tendency to express agreement with terms such as “I feel comfortable while 
driving”, and “I think the road signs generally provide necessary information”, while 
disagreeing with terms like “I sometimes misjudge the speed of an oncoming vehicle” and 
“I feel nervous while driving”. 
Class 2 has a lot in common with Class 1 in the sense that both classes contain 
respondents who drive cars regularly, and feel comfortable doing so, but in Class 2, 
many of the respondents showed a tendency to exhibit behavior suggestive of “being 
their own boss”. For example members of Class 2 had higher agreement with statements 
like “I do not fully stop the car at a STOP sign” or “I feel that some of the road rules are 
completely unnecessary”, while having a higher tendency to disagree with statements like 
“I pay close attention to road signs” and “I tend to drive cautiously”. 
Class 3 is largely defined by their professional interest in the topic of the study, and 
their high level of field experience. Respondents from Class 3 tend to be male, and below 
30. 
Class 4 – much as Class 1 – can be viewed as an expansion of the same class in model 
1: members tend to be females below 30, who identify as non-car drivers, and drive 
rarely. Agreement and disagreement with statements revealed that Class 4 respondents 
have a tendency to be frustrated and nervous by driving. They also follow the 
philosophy “better safe than sorry”, and are generally ready to react to unexpected 
manoeuvers by others on the road. Furthermore they had a higher tendency to claim not 
liking intersections that offer multiple lanes for the same direction, and to give the first 
route that comes to mind when prompted for directions. 
4.4.3 Comparison of models  
Upon comparing frequency distributions belonging to model 1 and 2, it can be noted 
that Class 2 and Class 3 sometimes „switch place”. In essence: the magnitude and 
direction of difference between the class and the other classes for Class 2 and 3 would 
be the opposite in model 2, than it was in model 1. This however is not a concern, since – 
as previously noted – these two classes are somewhat similar. 
The other difference (that holds true in case of some of the dependent variables) is 
that including the personality scale items caused differences in distributions between 
classes to be a bit more pronounced. This could suggest that the scale items (and thus 
the factors) are mediator variables: they do not directly explain individual differences, 
but they help in doing so (compare Class 1 and Class 4 on Fig. 22 and Fig. 23).

4.5.3 Factors and dependent variables 
In the final set of equations, the effect of the factors on the observed dependent 
variables was checked. As previously mentioned, based on the LCA, the suspicion was 
that factors serve as mediators between the socio-economic parameters and the 
dependent variables. The SEM results would support this theory. A visualization of the 
SEM can be found on Fig. 24. 
In the first set of traffic locations (the “ambiguity” section), the number of correct 
answers to the knowledge questions in the low info condition (KN1) was found to be 
negatively connected only to the anxiety factor. I.e. the higher a respondent scored on 
the anxiety factor, the lower the number of correct answers they provided. Since the 
correlation is weak, not much significance should be attributed, but a possible 
explanation for this could be that anxiety is not a cause, but an effect: since these 
individuals tend to perform worse in understanding traffic situations, they often get into 
undesired situations, and thus have become anxious drivers. 
The number of conflicts that respondents estimated to have happened in the past 5 
years (“ambiguity” section, low info condition, NC1) has a strong positive correlation to 
the factor titled “Tendency to follow rules”. This is to be expected: if an individual 
believes in paying close attention to road signs, it is logical that in a situation where 
regulatory information is scarce, they would think that conflicts would be more frequent 
as a result of this. And it could also be assumed that respondents belonging to this 
category would think other road users would share their uncertainty. As described by 
Dawes, (1990), the so-called false consensus effect is the egotistic cognitive bias of 
==PAGE:65==

believing that others (in a group of which one is also a member) will react the same way 
as oneself.

On the other hand, NC1 is negatively correlated with the factor titled “Considers some 
rules unnecessary”, so i.e. the more somebody thinks “some road rules are completely 
unnecessary”, the less traffic conflicts they think occur in a low info condition. This 
makes good sense: it is a direct manifestation of their belief in self-regulation in driving 
as opposed to (certain) movements and actions being regulated by traffic signs. 
The improvement respondents felt in the number of conflicts in the first section, high 
info condition (SI1) is positively correlated with the tendency to make habitual choices, 
the tendency to speed, and the tendency to follow rules closely, while it is negatively 
correlated with distractedness. The fact that those who use habit more often while 
driving feel that the location is safer when it has traffic signs and / or surface markings 
is not surprising. Since the sample population was largely composed of individuals living 
in Hungary, for whom it is safe to say that they are not used to encountering 
intersections with a lack of traffic signs, it makes sense that they would prefer the 
configuration they are more familiar with (the high info condition), and would thus feel 
that the improvement compared to the low info condition is greater. The positive 
correlation to the tendency to speed is a bit more unexpected. A possible explanation 
could be that since those who have a higher tendency to speed usually also have less 
time to comprehend traffic information, it is more helpful, if the location offers 
information readily available, and it in turn does not need to be extracted via elaborate 
comprehension of such infrastructure attributes as the geometry of the intersection. The 
positive correlation with a tendency to follow rules can be viewed as a mirroring of the 
correlation between the same factor and NC1: individuals who estimate the number of 
conflicts to be higher will also feel that the improvement is greater, once the situation 
becomes more familiar to them. 
The explanation for the negative correlation to distractedness does not seem to 
present itself right away, especially since this is the factor that has the strongest 
correlation to SI1. The expected direction of correlation would be positive: i.e. the more 
distracted a road user is, the more they appreciate every piece of additional information 
in the road environment. But the answer might lie in the type of items that belong to 
factor 5. Each of the 5 items belonging to the factor are statements that do not reflect 
socially desirable behavior. Leaving the car parked in an illegal position, moving over 
quickly to a neighboring lane to gain advantage, forgetting headlights are on high beam 
and thus inconveniencing oncoming vehicles are all actions that are frowned upon. 
Paulhus, (1984) describes socially desirable responding as any systematic tendency to 
answer questionnaire items on some basis that interferes with accurate self-reports. In 
light of this theory, individuals scoring high on factor 5 labeled “Distractedness” could 
==PAGE:68==

possibly also be categorized as those who do not exhibit socially desirable responding, 
and then, in practice, are more “honest”. If this is the case, this could be an indication of a 
flaw in the experimental design for observing respondents’ estimates of improvement in 
safety and comfort. This aspect will further be discussed in sub-chapter 5.2.2.

traffic signs in the expected places. Respondents with a higher tendency to speed 
provided more correct answers in the low info condition of the 2nd section. A common 
attribute of these locations is that the road geometry is more suggestive of priority and 
travel direction than in the 1st section. So this could suggest that high velocity drivers 
rely more on road geometry than on traffic signs, since this is the main difference 
between the low info conditions of the 1st and the 2nd section.

taking up their resources while answering the questionnaire (as further confirmed by a

5 Discussion 
5.1 Implications 
5.1.1 National personality profiles 
Since it has been uncovered by this study that differences in personality and cognitive 
styles lead to differences in road user performance, feelings of safety and comfort, it 
would be important for road manuals to consider these differences world-wide. A good 
example of leading edge initiative in human friendly road design is that of New Zealand, 
where the road manual contains a paragraph urging road operators to map the areas 
where there is a larger population of older road users. In these areas, they argue, it is 
necessary to place traffic signs that are more conspicuous (with larger symbols and 
increased letter height). It is true, that in this case, it is a difference in physiological 
parameters they are catering to, but the next logical step could might as well be 
considering how individuals differ in their ability to perceive and process information. 
Differences in driving culture are widely known to exist on an international scale. For 
example, many would agree that the traffic scene in India is quite different from the one 
in Norway, and different still compared to the one in Hungary. Different countries drive 
differently, so it is logical to assume that the underlying reason for these differences are 
largely behavioral. With this established, it is easy to admit that in order to develop a 
road design manual that is truly human-centric, a personality profile needs to be 
developed for the entire nation. For example, as some accounts confirm about Italy, an 
expected finding would be that Italian road users would place higher on factor 8 
(tendency to consider some unnecessary), while in Denmark, due to the high number of 
bicycles in everyday traffic, road users might place higher on factor 7 (tendency to 
follow rules, because of its implications about cautious driving). 
Since driving culture does not change in the short-term, such profiles would need to 
be conducted no more frequent than once every 5-10 years, or before the next revision 
of the road manuals. 
5.1.2 Self-explanatory roads 
The relationship between the road environment and traffic signs can be understood 
as a two level communication system. The first level is the infrastructure (lane width, 
curb height, intersection angle, type of surrounding area, etc.) and the environment, and 
in most cases, the second level (additional signage) is there to compensate for the 
shortcomings of the first. In the New Zealand road manual, this relationship between the 
two levels is acknowledged in the following way: 
“In some instances, an excessive number of signs may have been installed in an attempt 
to address deficiencies in road geometry, environmental conditions or road safety 
==PAGE:74==
74 
 
concerns. Although they may be used to address such issues, traffic signs are unlikely to be 
an effective long-term solution for problems caused by road geometry inadequacies. 
It is important that signs can be easily understood and are sufficiently spaced to allow 
enough time for the message to be interpreted and acted on. Where there are too many 
signs, the most important message may be lost among the clutter. In instances where an 
excessive number of signs are installed, their use should be reviewed and rationalised. 
However, the preferable alternative is for geometric improvements to be undertaken.” 
The concept of self-explanatory roads is one of the leading areas of research in traffic 
safety. The expression first coined by Theeuwes & Godthelp, (1995) means that the 
image of the road environment is so clear and unambiguous, that there is no doubt in 
road users as to how to behave on that specific stretch of the road, or in that 
intersection.  
Research has since shown that road users indeed are affected by the environment and 
layout of the road, let that be the presence or absence of a divider line (Garrick, 2011), 
the number and width of lanes (Kosztolanyi-Ivan, Koren, & Borsos, 2015), or the type of 
area the road leads across (Iván, 2012). These studies have shown that drivers are 
affected the strongest in their choice of speed, but research into the effect on other 
factors remains scarce, even though schemata activation by road environment has 
shown to be very effective in drivers, since they were able to differentiate between 4-5 
road categories just by its layout and environment (Kosztolányi-Iván, Koren, & Borsos, 
2015). But how should the threshold be identified? If individuals indeed differ in what is 
unambiguous and what is not, to whom should the level of self-explanation be set? 
Since the image of the road environment is influenced by the relationship between 
the infrastructure and the road signs, one of the uses of self-explanatory roads is the 
complete elimination of traffic signs, and thus can be considered a highly desirable 
concept (along the lines of a Dutch experiment lead by Hans Monderman that involved 
removing all road signs from the city of Drachten, (DW.com, 2006)). But can it be 
assumed that every country is capable of adopting the idea? 
Earlier in this study, it was addressed that since the individuals selecting the traffic 
locations from Copenhagen and Budapest also have differences in personality, it is 
virtually impossible to objectively state if a location contains ambiguity or not. So does 
this also hold true for self-explanatory roads? Also on a national level: it might be for 
example, that in a country, many road users place high on the “aversion to ambiguity” 
scale, and therefor self-explanatory roads would have an adverse effect, while in another 
country, where most road users have a higher score on “tendency to consider some rules 
unnecessary”, the concept would be a welcome idea. 
==PAGE:75==
75 
 
5.2 Limitations, considerations 
As every piece of scientific work, this study is also not without limitations, the 
existence of which is important to address. 
5.2.1 Presentation of images 
Firstly, due to time and sample size considerations, the decision was made to create 
an online questionnaire, which made it quite difficult to feature video segments. The 
idea of using the DTU driving simulator was discarded due to similar considerations 
regarding sample size, so the only option left was to feature still images. As suggested by 
one of the participants, GIF images could also have been made, but to film footage for the 
segments of film from Budapest was also not possible. Therefor it can be argued that 
presenting still images as opposed to a video, or a real-time simulation is inferior, and 
previous cases have shown that some differences can arise between the two types of 
studies (Shinar & Vogelzang, 2013), but numerous studies have used still image 
presentation in the past, and have produced accepted results. Therefor this aspect of 
present study has not been considered to be a limitation. 
5.2.2 Reliability of self-report 
Lajunen and Özkan are the authors of the chapter on self-report instruments used in 
traffic psychology studies in the Handbook of Traffic Psychology (Porter, 2011). In their 
chapter, they say that self-report has gained popularity as a data collection method over 
the past years, and is reasonably reliable, but is not capable of measuring every kind of 
explanatory variable. They divide the driving task into two components: driver behavior 
and driver performance. While the former consists of driving style and habits, and is not 
considered to change considerably over the years, the latter includes those information 
processing and motor skill capabilities that enable the driver to perform the driving 
task. 
They argue that in case of driving style, there exists a legal and a social baseline to 
which drivers can compare their behavior, and thus are more aware of their 
performance, and are better suited to rate themselves compared to the norm. But in the 
case of driver skills, no such norm exists, so drivers are likely unaware of their place on 
the scale. Furthermore, some motor functions are performed without the driver even 
being aware of them, making it further troublesome for a driver to rate themselves in 
terms of skill. Consequently Lajunen and Özkan caution against using questionnaires for 
data collection regarding driving skill, but pose no protest against self-report being used 
in behavioral studies, even stating that in some cases (e.g. measuring attitudes, beliefs, 
opinions, such as the majority of present study), it is the only option. 
==PAGE:76==
76 
 
5.2.3 Limited experiment 
Due to considerations regarding questionnaire completion time, not all aspects of 
hypothesis H-2 (sub-chapter 1.3) could be investigated. Namely, that checking the effect 
of information overload was not included, because that would have required the 
introduction of a third section of images, resulting in either the reduction of the number 
of cases per section, or the increase of completion time. For this reason, the nature of the 
correlation between information level and feeling of safety and comfort has not been 
checked, but is hypothesized to go either of two ways shown on Fig. 3. 
Connecting to the topic of checking adverse effects: the dependent variables SI and CL 
were observed on a 7 point Likert scale, ranging from “no improvement” to “complete 
improvement”. This scale however did not include possible answers for retrogression. It 
is possible that this served as a suggestion towards the fact that the high info 
configuration should be considered better than the low, and caused respondents to skew 
their answers in a positive direction. As mentioned before, socially desirable responding 
is a phenomenon that needs to be considered. But since factor correlations were 
observed mostly in the expected directions, it can be hypothesized that respondents still 
rated SI and CL according to their preferences, only instead of marking retrogression, 
they marked less improvement, or no improvement. 
5.2.4 Confirmation bias 
As every instance of scientific research, this study is also susceptible to the risk of 
confirmation bias, defined by Reisberg, (2015) as: 
“A family of effects in which people seem more sensitive to evidence that confirms their 
beliefs than they are to evidence that challenges their beliefs. Thus, if people are given a 
choice about what sort of information they would like in order to evaluate their beliefs, 
they request information that is likely to confirm their beliefs. Likewise, if they are 
presented with both confirming and disconfirming evidence, they are more likely to pay 
attention to, to be influenced by, and to remember the confirming evidence, rather than the 
disconfirming.” 
A good way of decreasing the risk of confirmation bias is to submit one’s work to peer 
review. Professionals with an unbiased, and more objective viewpoint (regarding the 
research topic) are more likely to spot mistakes, or suggest alternative methods. Present 
study (in its early form, up to the results of the exploratory factor analysis) has been 
peer-reviewed, presented at an international transportation conference, and is pending 
publication (Bortei-Doku, Kaplan, Prato, & Nielsen, 2016). As such, it is reasonable to 
assume that there are no gross mistakes in the methodology or the analysis. 
==PAGE:77==
77 
 
5.3 Further research 
Since present study offers a novice direction in road safety research, the possibilities 
of continuation are abundant. Any number of actions, feelings or phenomena could be 
observed as dependent variables, and checked against differences in personality and 
cognitive ability. 
Future studies could include for example an experiment where respondents are 
profiled using personality scales, and afterwards equipped with eye tracking gear while 
driving a specific route in actual traffic. Visual search strategies would then be analyzed 
in order to uncover any patterns in strategies correlated to personality type.  
5.3.1 Sign re-design 
Another angle is to analyze the efficiency of different sign designs, as a function of 
personality type. The study previously mentioned in sub-chapter 1.1 could be modified 
in a way that different sign designs are devised according to cognitive styles analyzed by 
e.g. Kirton, (1976); Alessandra, (1995); Epstein, Pacini, Denes-Raj, & Heier, (1996); 
Moore, O’Maidin, & McElligott, (2003); de Vries, Bakker-Pieper, Konings, & Schouten, 
(2011); Volkova & Rusalov, (2016), and afterwards presented to the subjects in some 
way that simulates the real life movements of the signs. Participants would have a 
specific question to answer by searching the content of the signs, and would have to 
verbally confirm once the target information is located. Afterwards, if each participant 
group performs the best with the sign type designed to suit them, the point could be 
made that along with physiological and socio-demographic aspects, cognitive styles 
should also be considered in the process of sign design. 
5.3.2 Simulator study 
In another experiment, participants would similarly be profiled on a number of scales 
(personality, cognitive style, etc.). They would then be divided into two groups, with 
special attention on each group containing an equal amount of each participant type. 
Wearing eye tracking equipment, subjects would be asked to drive in a simulator. In 
order to avoid the learning effect (resulting from the same participants driving the same 
course in two configurations), a between-participants design would be needed: one 
group would drive a course with one level of a certain attribute (e.g. low road 
environment info level, fewer traffic signs, low visual clutter), and the other group would 
drive a course with the same attribute at the opposite level (e.g. high road environment 
info level, more traffic signs, high visual clutter). Differences in visual search strategies, 
driving parameters (speed, lateral variation) and even physiological signs of anxiety 
could be observed.

6 Conclusion 
This study has been designed in the scope of a master thesis, in order to investigate 
the effect that the level of information in the road environment has, together with 
personality differences among drivers, on road user cognition and certain feelings, 
perceptions. Information was collected from 753 Hungarian respondents with the help 
of an online questionnaire. 
 Present study has discovered that members of the target population (road users) are 
generally interested in the topic, and are willing to be involved in making road traffic 
safer, and more anthropocentric. 
In the questionnaire, respondents were presented with images of 12 traffic locations 
(5 from Copenhagen, Denmark, 7 from Budapest, Hungary). Locations were divided into 
2 sections, the first one containing locations where traffic signage was used (or should 
have been used) to compensate for the ambiguity in the road geometry, while on the 
second section, all locations contained traffic signage conveying information about 
aspects of the location that were also implicit in the infrastructure. 
Each location was presented in 2 configurations, the first one always being the low 
info condition, and the second one the high info condition. In the low info condition, 
respondents had to answer a question relating to legal or illegal maneuvers in the 
locations, and were asked to provide an estimate of the frequency of traffic conflicts that 
had occurred in the location in the past 5 years. In the high info condition, respondents 
were asked to estimate the improvement in the frequency of conflicts and their level of 
comfort, and also in the second section, the helpfulness of the additional traffic signs. 
Collected data was analyzed using a total of 3 methods. Firstly, factor analysis on the 
personality scale items was completed with good internal consistency and reliability 
measures, and 8 behavioral factors were extracted: anxiety, aversion to ambiguity, habit, 
tendency to speed, distractedness, impatience, tendency to follow rules, and tendency to 
consider some rules unnecessary. Significant correlations were found between anxiety 
and tendency to speed in terms of respondents answering the knowledge questions 
correctly. The estimation of traffic conflicts was linked to all behavioral factors except 
for impatience, and the same can be said for perceived safety after information was 
added to the locations, and improvement in the level of comfort. Helpfulness of 
additional traffic signs was linked to all factors with the exception of tendency to speed, 
impatience and tendency to follow rules (see Table 6, Table 7) 
Latent Class Analysis revealed no significant differences in the dependent variables 
across the classes. Two models were iterated, both for 4 classes, the first containing only 
socio-demographic parameters, while in the second model, the personality scale items 
also entered as explanatory variables. Frequency distributions of the dependent 
variables revealed that – while within the two models, differences in answers were not 
==PAGE:79==
79 
 
significant, – the differences were slightly more noticeable in model 2 than model 1, thus 
leading to the assumption that the behavioral factors could perform as mediator 
variables between the explanatory variables and the dependent variables (see Table 9, 
Table 10). 
On these terms, Structural Equation Modelling was performed in order to investigate 
the mediator quality of the factors. Three matrix equations were drafted: one to serve as 
the measurement equations for the factors, one to estimate the parameters for the effect 
of the explanatory variables on the factors, and one to describe the effect of the factors 
on the dependent variables. 
An additional, ninth factor was formulated using items from the first question, 
regarding respondents’ previous opinion about the signage system in Hungary, and was 
titled “Satisfaction with the system”.  Factors that were found to correlate with 
dependent variables from the 1st set of locations were anxiety, habit, tendency to speed, 
distractedness, tendency to follow rules and satisfaction with the system. In the 2nd set 
of locations, all factors were found to correlate with the dependent variables (see Table 
11, Table 12, Table 13, Fig. 24). 
In conclusion, it can be said that the direction of this research is worth pursuing, and 
anthropocentric road- and traffic sign design could represent the next step in achieving 
sustainable traffic safety throughout the world.
