Abstract
The objective of this thesis is to investigate the feasibility of different solar heating systems,
in combination with utilizing low-cost electricity through the integration of an electric heating
element.
The first part of this thesis focuses on the study of electricity prices over the past nine years.
The outcomes of this study indicate that spot prices have experienced a significant increase
since 2018, with the exception of 2020, particularly in the past two years. Consequently, this
rise implies a corresponding increase in annual electricity costs for consumers. While predicting
future spot price trends remains challenging, pursuing greater independence from the electric
grid fluctuations can prove beneficial for the end-users. The present study aims to implement
heating systems that enhance the self-sufficiency of the residential sector in Denmark. This
objective is accomplished through the utilization of solar energy to meet the space heating and
domestic hot water demand, along with the controlled integration of the electric grid during
periods of low electricity prices. The main focus of this approach is to secure the supply of the
demand while simultaneously aligning with the country’s environmental objectives. Additionally,
it aims to explore the potential of reducing the overall annual expenses for end users. To achieve
this, three solar heating systems have been designed, aiming to maximize the performance, by
means of comprehensive calculations and analysis, to determine the optimal design, component
combination and sizing of these systems.

1.1
Background and motivation
The 2030 Climate target plan presented by the European Commission outlines a proposal to
reduce greenhouse gas emissions by a minimum of 55% by 2030. This proposal aligns with the
objective of reaching climate neutrality by 2050, demonstrating the Commission’s commitment
to ambitious climate objectives [1].
In accordance with the ”Renewables 2022” report [2], almost half of all final energy consumption
worldwide is used for heating, which makes it the largest energy end-use. The final energy uti-
lized for heating is generated through industrial activities, which account for 53% of it, while the
remaining 44% is mainly utilized in buildings for domestic water and space heating. The rest of
the amount is mainly utilized for greenhouse heating in agriculture. In 2021, as the world econ-
omy began to grow again, heat consumption rose by 4% annually, surpassing pre-pandemic
levels and breaking records with 219 EJ. With the traditional usage of biomass excluded, mod-
ern renewables accounted for only 13% of this rise. This means that fossil fuels still dominate
the majority of the global heating sector. Annual CO2 emissions from heat increased to 14.1 Gt
CO2, accounting for 39% of all CO2 emissions related to energy worldwide.
In order to fulfil the European Commission environmental objectives, the importance of renew-
able heat technologies is gaining more attention from policymakers worldwide. This attention is
driven not only by environmental considerations but also by the need to address energy security
concerns, particularly in light of the ongoing global energy crisis.
1.1.1
State of art of solar heat worldwide
According to the data collected in the ”Solar Heat Worlwide” report [3], at the end of 2021, the
global operational capacity of solar thermal reached 522 GWth, equivalent to 746 million square
meters of collector area. After seven consecutive years of decline, the solar thermal market
experienced a 3% growth in 2021 compared to the previous year. This corresponds to a net rise
of 21 GWth of installed capacity in 2021. Furthermore, solar thermal yearly energy production
reached 425 TWh, resulting in savings of 45.7 million tonnes of oil and 147.5 million tonnes
of carbon dioxide. These numbers demonstrate the significant role played by solar thermal
in reducing global greenhouse gas emissions. In terms of renewable technologies installed
capacities, solar thermal stands behind photovoltaics, with 942 GWel, and wind power with 837
GWel, while concentrated solar power (CSP) and geothermal energy lag further behind.
China substantially leads the global solar thermal market. Out of the 21 GWth of installed
capacity worldwide in 2021, China accounted for a remarkable 83%, or 18 GWth, highlighting
the dominance of the Chinese solar thermal sector. Promising market trends were observed in
Italy with a significant growth rate of 83%, as well as Greece (18%), Poland (17%), and the UK
(18%).
Focusing on Denmark, the country had previously held a dominant position in the market for
large-scale solar thermal systems, particularly in the field of solar district heating. However, the
Danish solar district heating industry collapsed in 2020 due to significant changes in funding
requirements and energy technology legislation. This huge decline in the solar district heating
market resulted in a 45% decrease in new solar thermal capacity. Consequently, Denmark
dropped from first to third place in terms of newly installed large-scale plants in 2021.

1.2
Buiding renovation. Passive and low-energy houses
Based on the available data, it is evident that the building sector has a substantial influence on air
pollution and the release of greenhouse gas emissions. To achieve the objectives outlined in the
2030 Climate Target Plan mentioned earlier, the European Union (EU) must reduce greenhouse
gas emissions from buildings by 60%, decrease final energy consumption by 14%, and lower
energy consumption for heating and cooling purposes by 18%.
Europe’s building stock is ageing and developing very slowly, 85% of the EU’s building stock,
was built before 2001. The majority of the existing buildings are not energy-efficient. Many rely
on fossil fuels for heating and cooling and use outdated technologies and inefficient equipment
[4]. As a consequence, the residential sector emerges as a key area where intensified efforts
are necessary. It is crucial for the European Union (EU) to prioritize efforts aimed at enhancing
the sustainability, energy efficiency, and overall carbon footprint of buildings. A global focus
on net-zero energy buildings or nearly zero energy buildings (NZEBs), is growing currently,
as a solution to mitigate emissions [5]. NZEBs are defined as buildings with very high energy
performance, that produce the minimum amount of energy to meet their annual consumption
[6]. Achieving residential NZEB status can primarily be achieved through building renovation,
reducing building energy demand and improving the occupant’s behaviours. Building renovation
efforts should be concentrated on increasing the integration of renewable energy generation,
with a particular emphasis on utilizing local sources. Smart buildings have the potential to
facilitate the efficient generation and utilization of renewable energy.
Currently, the focus is on three distinct categories of buildings: the standard building, the low-
energy building, and the passive house [7].
• The conventional building or standard building refers to a building constructed in accor-
dance with the prevailing practices of a specific country during a particular period, meeting
the minimum energy standards mandated by law.
• Low-energy building describes a building that is constructed based on specialized design
criteria aimed at minimizing the building’s energy consumption during operation.
• Passive house is a subtype of low-energy building design that prioritizes the utilization
of passive technologies such as optimal insulation, high-performance windows, and con-
trolled ventilation. A passive house can operate during the heating season with more than
ten times less heat energy compared to conventional houses. Potentially, passive houses
incorporate certain active solar technologies, as there is no need for temperatures above
50ºC, renewable energy sources are ideal for the generation of the building hot water and
space heating demand [8]. In addition, the passive house standard is a proven and ac-
cessible approach for achieving NZEB. It has a strong track record of over 25 years and
can be applied in any climate. With reliable performance and no cost barriers, it has the
potential to become a widely accepted pathway to NZEB throughout the European Union
[9].
Regarding the residential sector, the TABULA project [10] established a Danish typology for
residential buildings, categorizing them into three main types: single-family houses, terraced
houses, and apartment blocks.
• Single-family houses refer to all residential buildings in Denmark that consist of a single
dwelling. This category also includes old farmhouses located on large farms.

1.3
The aim of the project
The aim of this master’s thesis is to determine the optimal solution for a solar heating system
that can efficiently meet the heating demand, including domestic hot water and space heating,
for a residential building. In this study, POLYSUN calculations are conducted for a low-energy
and passive single-family house located in Denmark. The solar heating systems are equipped
with a hot water storage system, which can be charged using solar collectors. Additionally, the
solar energy systems are intelligently operated by integrating the electrical grid, through a smart
electric heater. The energy storage can be charged using electricity from the grid during periods
when electricity costs are low. The primary objective of these calculations is to optimize the
system’s performance and examine the maximum potential for utilizing solar energy resources.
For this purpose, three solar heating systems have been designed.
System 1 has been designed to investigate the feasibility of covering the entire heating demand
of specific residential buildings using solar energy. If a complete supply of the energy demand
is not achievable, the objective is to establish the limits of solar resource utilization, determining
the maximum portion of the energy demand that can be met only by solar energy.
System 2, on the other hand, aims to compensate for the energy deficit resulting from the insuf-
ficiency of solar energy in covering the demand. This system is designed to be able to supply
the energy demand at all moments allowing the size reduction of the system components. This
is achieved by incorporating an auxiliary electric device, powered by the electric grid, within an
auxiliary tank. This electric device adequate the flow going to the end units to meet specific
requirements. The primary objective of System 2 is to investigate the impact of introducing an
electrical device on the performance of the solar heating system and to determine the optimal
configuration among all system components.
In System 3, a smart integration of the electric grid is realized during periods of low electricity
prices by introducing a controlled electric heater. The aim of this solar heating system is to
examine the effects of this integration measure on overall system performance and to explore
the potential cost savings it can offer to end users on an annual basis.
These three systems have been developed and studied to explore various aspects related to the
integration of solar energy, electrical devices, and the electric grid in residential heating systems.
This investigation aims to determine the optimal design and sizing of the solar heating systems
to ensure that the overall energy demand of the house can be met effectively.
This thesis encompasses multiple objectives and aims to provide practical solar energy supply
solutions in line with the IEA SHC Task 66.

The impact of renewable energy penetration on electricity prices has been extensively examined
in various studies. The European Commission, as part of the ”Delivering on the European Green
Deal” package [20], aims to accelerate the adoption of renewable energy within the EU. In line
with Directive 2018/2001/EU, the existing EU-level target has been raised to at least 40% of
renewable energy sources in the overall energy mix by 2030, which represents a doubling of the
current renewable energy contribution of 19.7% within a decade. Furthermore, it is anticipated
that the energy system will experience substantial electrification, with electricity accounting for
over 50% of total demand, and an increased utilization of renewable energy sources (RES)
generating more than 80% of the electricity produced [21].
Denmark, in particular, stands out as a pioneering nation in the energy transition, driven by its
commitment to net-zero emissions by 2050 and its goal of reducing greenhouse gas emissions
by 70% compared to 1990 levels by 2030, in line with the Paris Agreement. A political agreement
within the country has set a target of achieving 100% renewable energy for electricity generation
and 55% for overall energy consumption by 2030 [22]. In 2021, Denmark witnessed an 11%
increase in renewable capacity, reaching 10,340 MWp of installed capacity [23].
However, it is crucial to examine how the penetration of these renewable energy technologies
impacts electricity spot prices. The electricity supply curve illustrates the relationship between
the quantity of electricity that producers are willing and able to supply to the market at different
prices. It is constructed based on the production costs of electricity and the available production
capacity at any given time. Renewable energy sources (RES) typically possess relatively lower
marginal costs compared to conventional technologies. Consequently, the inclusion of RES
in electricity generation causes the electricity supply curve to shift to the right [24]. Assuming
constant demand, the intersection of the demand and supply curves occurs at lower prices, re-
sulting in a decrease in spot prices. In conclusion, in theory, there exists an inverse relationship
between renewable capacity and electricity prices.
According to this theory, it would be expected that electricity prices would exhibit a downward
trend from 2014 to 2019, considering the increase in renewable energy capacity during this
period. However, the impact of renewable energy development on the decrease in electricity
prices is not clearly evident, as shown in the figure. This could be attributed to the fact that
the advancement of these technologies is primarily driven by public support schemes, such as
feed-in tariffs, premiums, green-certificate trading, quota obligations, and so on. Since most
of the renewable energy support systems are subsidized by the electricity market, consumers
may ultimately bear a higher price burden [25].
Furthermore, the utilization of renewable energy technologies, particularly wind and solar en-
ergy, introduces a direct dependence on climatic conditions for electricity generation. When
the wind blows or the sun shines, a larger proportion of electricity is generated from renewable
sources, causing the supply curve to shift to the right. Consequently, the price of electricity may
exhibit year-to-year fluctuations based on weather conditions. It is plausible that climate change
could increase the sensitivity of electricity demand and supply, although it remains uncertain if
this would directly translate to changes in electricity prices. The extent to which electricity prices
are affected will depend on the specific shape of the supply and demand curves [24].
On the other hand, the impact of electricity price liberalization has also been explored as a fac-
tor influencing spot prices. The objective of liberalizing the energy sector was to introduce free
competition into the electricity market, eliminating market monopolies and allowing for more
participants. This process often involved unbundling, which separated the generation, trans-
mission, and distribution/retail functions within the energy sector [26]. Competitive markets are
expected to enhance economic efficiency and result in lower electricity costs. However, the
actual benefits of increased competition are subject to debate, as the opening of the market

For climatic conditions such as the ones found in Denmark, it is necessary for solar energy
systems to be resistant to temperatures below zero. If the solar collector fluid inside the collector
freezes, it expands and can cause the destruction of the collector. In order for the heat transfer
fluid to be able to withstand these conditions, water is mixed with a certain quantity of propylene
glycol. The freezing point will drop when increasing the percentage of glycol. The propylene
glycol concentration for Denmark is around 40-50%, 40% will be used in our systems.
3.0.5
Storage tank
To ensure that the solar heating system can fulfil the heating and DHW demand while maximiz-
ing efficiency, it is essential to size the storage tank properly. The needed tank size is dependent
on various factors such as the SH and DHW demand of the building, the solar collector area
and the dimension of the auxiliary energy supply system. For instance, a small tank will need
a larger electric heating energy than a big tank to meet the consumption at all times. To find
the optimal storage tank dimensions, a sensitivity analysis will be carried out in the subsequent
sections. The following description applies to the preheating tank present in all three systems.
The heat distribution along the tank is an important aspect to consider when doing the sizing of
the system. Normally, the water is stratified based on temperature. The warmest layer of water,
which is less dense than other layers, is at the top of the tank, while the coldest water, which
is denser than in other layers, is at the bottom. As this stratification is desired, it is attempted
to transfer and remove heat from the tank with as little disturbance as possible [29]. In order
to fulfil this attempt, the solar collector loop is connected to the storage tank by means of a
stratified lance. A stratified lance consists of a vertical tube, inside the tank, with about a dozen
apertures covered externally by movable caps, similar to a flute. The water rises inside the tube
until its density is similar in respect to that present in the corresponding point in the tank, and
then flows out. The fluid is therefore deposited exactly at the point at which it has the same
temperature as the tank’s liquid. The stratified lance has approximately the same length as the
tank height, going all the way through the tank until the top layer, this way optimum stratification
is guaranteed in the tank.
An external counterflow plate heat exchanger is used to connect the solar collector loop and the
tank loop. There the fluids of the collector and that of the tank exchange heat by means of thin
plates, which separate the two liquids. A pump is located on both sides of the heat exchanger
and a controller will be set to control the flow.
Regarding the withdrawal of heat from the tank, the hot water is taken directly from the top layer
of the tank. After this, the water flow is mixed with cold water to the desired temperature by
means of a three-way valve, in case the temperature of the top layer in the tank is higher than
the hot water setting. In addition, there exists another plate heat exchanger before the hot water
tap. The space heating output is also from the top of the tank.
In relation to the return flows to the tank, both are deposited via a direct connection at the bottom
layer of the tank. Domestic hot water return is placed at the bottom of the tank, positioned at
5% from the bottom of the tank, while the space heating return is placed at 10% from the bottom
of the tank. In terms of system performance, the closer to the tank’s bottom these connections
are located, the better the solution.
With respect to the insulation of the tank, in order to minimize the heat losses, a thermal insu-
lation of rigid PU foam with a thickness of 15 cm and a thermal conductivity of λ = 0.03 W/m/K
has been used. Generally speaking, the top cover should be thicker because hotter water is
found there, while the bottom cover, on the contrary, could be much thinner, this way the envi-
ronmental heat helps to warm the cold water. However, in our case study, the same thickness

and type of insulation are used on all the surfaces of the tank.
Another important aspect that should be considered when designing the storage tank is connec-
tion losses. Connection losses are referred to as heat losses produced in the pipe connections
to the tank. The solution for these losses is mainly S-bending the pipes and insulating the con-
nections. S-bending is a downward-curving piece at the output of the tank. The water will cool
at this point but the S-bend will prevent hot water from the tank from penetrating into the pipe
by natural convection and thereby reducing the connection heat losses. The connection losses
also depend on the temperature and thus the position of the direct connections. Temperatures
at the top of the tank are higher so connections at this point will have higher losses. For this rea-
son, it is preferable to place the connections at the lower parts of the tanks where temperatures
are lower, decreasing potentially the connection losses.
3.0.6
System pipes and pumps
The pipes used in all of the systems are equal. The pipes are considered to be made of copper
with an insulation of 30 mm of loose glass fibres and mineral wool. Two different pipe cross-
sections are used in the system; for the space heating loop pipes with an inner diameter of
20 mm and external diameter of 22 mm are used, on the rest of the loops pipes with an inner
diameter of 10 mm and an external of 12 mm are implemented.
3.0.7
System controllers
Space heating controller
The heating controller regulates the status and flow rate of a pump based on building simulation
settings. In this case, the controller can also control a mixing valve.
• The controller has 7 inputs: average outdoor temperature, building temperature setting,
actual room temperature, the total design flow rate for the heating/cooling element, the
inlet temperature to the heating/cooling element, and the temperature of the pipes 12 and
5. These three last parameters are used in case a mixing valve control is required.
• The controller 3 outputs: the pump heating loop status and flow rate and the three-way
valve position. Heating loop cut-in and cut-off are based on thermostatic room temperature
control. The inlet temperature is calculated based on the outdoor temperature which,
in turn, is used as the temperature setting for the mixing valve. The outdoor set point
temperature has been selected to be 16ºC.
The functioning of this controller is as follows:
• The pump heating will be off if the actual temperature is higher that the room temperature
setting plus 1 K
• The pump heating will be on if the actual temperature is lower than the setpoint room
temperature.
DHW mixing valve controller.
The mixing valve controller regulates the mixing ratio of two different inflows so that the desired
outflow temperature can be reached. Such outflow temperature may be specified through a
constant or variable value.
• The controller has 3 inputs: temperatures in pipes 17 and 21 and the hot water tempera-
ture setting.
• The controller has 1 output: the three-way valve position.
With the variable temperature setting, the mixing will be performed based on the hot water
demand: temperature setting plus 10 K. The hot water temperature setting will be defined in

temperature drop between the mixing valve outflow and the hot water tap.
Variable speed DHW pump controller
The variable-speed pump controller is a two-channel controller regulating the status and flow
rate of the pump of the fresh-water station. The controller has 5 inputs: the temperature at
pipe 18(right before the heat exchanger), the cold-water inlet temperature, the hot water design
flow rate, the hot water demand temperature, and the heat exchanger capacity. The controller
has 2 outputs: pump potable water status [%]: On/Off and pump potable water flow rate. With
this setting the pump of the fresh-water station cuts in as soon as hot water is tapped. The
pump speed is adjusted so that the output temperature on the secondary side is as high as
the hot water demand temperature setting plus 5 K. The temperature shift value enables to
adjustment of temperature drops in the plate heat exchanger and pipe cooling after the plate
heat exchanger. If the value is positive the outflow temperature will be increased by this value.
If the value is negative the outflow temperature will be decreased by this value.
Pump controller solar collector loop
The solar loop controller is a two-channel controller that regulates the pump in the solar loop.
The controller regulates the status and flow rate of up to two pumps based on the temperature
difference of two distinct measured values. The controller has 4 inputs: outflow temperature,
storage tank bottom layer temperature, collector aperture area, and top layer tank temperature.
The controller has 4 outputs: the on/off state of the solar collector loop pump, the transfer circuit
pump on/off state, the solar collector loop pump flow rate, and the transfer circuit pump flow rate.
With this setting the solar pump cuts in as soon as the collector outflow temperature is 6 degrees
higher than the temperature in the bottom layer of the tank. The flow rate is 15 l/h/m2 of the
collector field. The solar collector loop pump will cut off if: the collector temperature is higher
than 155 ºC, the tank temperature is higher than 95ºC, or the collector outflow temperature is
lower than the tank temperature, at the bottom of the tank, plus the 2 K.
3.1
System 1: Pure solar heating system
This first system is composed of just one tank, the preheating tank, connected to the solar
collector loop. In this system, there will be no auxiliary heating device, the aim for this is to
determine how much of the total heating demand of the dwelling can be supplied just with
solar energy. Apart from the previously mentioned controllers, this system will also include two
additional programmable controllers. A space heating programmable controller controls that
energy is only transferred to the heating element if the temperature in the tank is higher than
the temperature in the building. Lacking this controller, energy would be transferred whenever
there was a demand without considering the temperature in the tank. Consequently, the energy
flow could heat the tank. This programmable heating controller works as follows:
• Operation status 1: the space heating pump is turned on if the following three conditions
are met: the mean outdoor ambient temperature is lower than 16 ºC; the building temper-
ature is lower than 20 ºC and the tank temperature is higher than the building temperature
plus 5 K.
• Operation status 2: the space heating pump is turned off if the following two conditions
are met: the pump is on in the previous time step and the building temperature is higher
than 21 ºC. Or if the tank temperature at layer 10 is lower than the building temperature
plus 2 K or the ambient temperature is higher than 16ºC.
Another programmable controller for the DHW is needed. It controls that only energy is trans-
ferred from the tank to the hot water tap if the temperature at the top of the tank is higher than the
cold-water temperature plus 5 K. Without this controller, energy would be transferred every time

heating controller works as follows:
• Operation status 1: the potable water pump is turned on if the following two conditions
are met: the temperature at the top of the tank is higher than the cold-water temperature
plus 5 K and the flow rate at pipe 23, which is the pipe connected to the hot water tap, is

3.2
System 2: Solar preheating system
This system consists of 2 water storage tanks. The preheating tank connected to the solar
collector loop, same as in the tank used in System 1 and an auxiliary storage tank. Both tanks
are connected through a direct connection from the top right of the preheating tank to the bottom
part of the small tank.
This second tank denominated ”auxiliary tank” has a smaller volume, sufficient to supply the
domestic hot water demand. It is composed of steel material with a wall thickness of 2 mm. It is
insulated with a layer of 150 mm thickness, covering the entire surface of the tank. The tank’s
design maintains a height/diameter ratio of 2. Located at the top right of the tank, there are
two direct connections, that are connected to the domestic hot water (DHW) and space heating
loop, respectively.
This small tank includes an internal electrical heater at its bottom. The internal heater is con-
trolled by an auxiliary heating controller. This controller has 2 inputs and one output. For this
specific case, the controller measures the temperature at the bottom and top of the tank. If the
temperature at the bottom layer is lower than 53ºC the controller is activated the internal electric
heater, and it remains activated until the temperature at the top of the tank is equal to 55ºC,
where the controller will cut off the auxiliary heater.
The top temperature of the preheating tank immediately impacts the temperature at the bottom
of this tank. There exists a temperature difference between these two points, resulting from heat
loss in the same connection pipe and at the tank’s pipe connections. Both layers’ temperatures
fluctuate in proportion as a result of their close relationship. The electric heater is turned on
when the temperature top layer of the preheating tank is insufficient to raise the smaller tank’s
temperature to at least 53ºC.
3.3
System 3: Smart solar preheating system
Similar to System 2, System 3 is composed of two tanks. The main difference with System 2
is the integration of the electric grid through the installation of a second electric heater inside
the preheating tank. In this way, both the solar collectors and the electric heater contribute to
heating the preheating tank.
This second electric heater is denominated as a ”smart heater”, as it is designed in a smart
manner, utilizing the electricity grid selectively during periods of low spot prices. To achieve
this, a controller is installed to regulate the operation of the electric heater. It is desirable that
this electric heater operates to the maximum extent, in order to benefit as much as possible
from these periods of low electricity prices. Additionally, it is convenient to regulate the volume
that the electric heater will heat. For this reason, the sensor of the controller is placed at the
same height as the heater position within the tank. The electric device is activated every time

Parameter analysis
This investigation aims to conduct a comprehensive parameter analysis of the different compo-

of varying parameters on the performance of these systems.
The analysis of each parameter is performed in a separate manner, without considering the

each variable is determined for that particular study, independently of the other parameters.
Certain parameters of the system are held constant in systems across all the parameter investi-
gations. The influence of pipes and pumps is not included in this particular parameter analysis;
hence, they are maintained as constant variables in all scenarios. Furthermore, a specific type
of evacuated tube collector is utilized in all analyses, except for the collector-type analysis. Ad-

except when investigating the tilt and azimuth angle influence. Finally, in System 2 and System

4.1
Storage tank
This investigation carries out a parameter analysis of the storage tank volume for the three
solar heating systems, with the aim is to determine the impact of varying tank volumes on the
performance of these systems.
The analysis of the preheating tank involves a range of volumes, starting from 2500 litres and
increasing by 2500 litres up to 20000 litres. For System 2 and System 3, the auxiliary tank
volume varies from 100 litres to 300 litres, with a step size of 100 litres. Each different size of
the auxiliary tank is combined with each of the preheating tanks.
Specific parameters are held constant across all systems to ensure a fair comparison. A stan-
dardized collector area of 100 m2 is considered for all the systems. In this analysis for both the
passive and low-energy house, it is assumed that the smart heater is positioned at 10% from
the bottom of the preheating tank.
The tank’s capacity must be sufficient for meeting both SH and DHW requirements when solar
energy is unavailable. The heat capacity of the storage tank is influenced by several factors,
including the tank volume, the temperature of the tank, and the specific heat and density of
the water contained within it. Larger tanks have higher heat capacity, however, they require
larger amounts of energy for full charging. Also, the storage capacity increases with higher
tank temperatures. Meanwhile, the heat losses experienced by the tank are determined by the
temperature differential between the tank and the surrounding environment, as well as the heat
loss coefficient of the tank. Higher temperatures in the tank leads to increased tank losses.
Additionally, the insulation and thickness of the tank material may affect heat losses, but these
parameters remain constant across all scenarios in this analysis.

The incidence angle modifier (IAM) is mathematically defined as the ratio of the peak efficiency
at a specific incident angle, to the peak efficiency at normal incidence (θ = 0°)[32].
Flat plate collectors can be described using a single incidence angle modifier, which is deter-
mined solely by the angle between the incident solar radiation and the normal vector to the
collector’s aperture. These collectors possess optical characteristics that remain consistent
across all directions within the aperture plane, resulting in an incidence angle modifier that is
unaffected by changes in the solar azimuth angle. In contrast, evacuated tube collectors, exhibit
optical properties that vary depending on the direction within the aperture plane. Consequently,
the incidence angle modifier for such collectors depends on both the solar azimuth and inci-
dence angles. The incident beam of solar radiation needs to be divided into transverse and
longitudinal projections. Consequently, ETC uses two separate incidence angle modifiers to
accurately describe their optical behaviour. The longitudinal incidence angle modifier Kθl and
the transversal incidence angle modifier Kθt. The incidence angle modifier may be approxi-
mated by the product of these two separate parameters [33].

Results and discussion
The POLYSUN software, used for these calculations, utilizes two terms: the energy consump-
tion and demand. The energy consumption represents the actual amount of energy consumed
by the end user. On the other hand, the energy demand represents the amount of energy
required by consumer units to meet comfort conditions [37].
It is important to note that energy consumption by the end user can be slightly higher than the
energy demand, due to higher flow temperatures than required. This may be due to the cal-
culation method used for POLYSUN to determine energy consumption. However if the energy
demand exceeds the energy consumption, it signifies an energy deficit.
For System 1 and System 2, the solar fraction is defined as the proportion of energy consumption
supplied exclusively by solar energy. However as in System 1 there is no auxiliary energy
source, all of the energy is provided by the solar energy. In this case the solar fraction is
defined as the portion of energy demand that can be covered with the solar energy, so the
energy consumption divided by the energy demand.
The main outcome obtained from the sensitivity analysis is the net utilized solar energy for each
scenario. Net utilized solar energy refers to the final energy consumed by the end user, minus
the energy supplied by the auxiliary electric heating elements. In the absence of an auxiliary
heating element as in system 1, the net utilized solar energy corresponds only to the final energy
that is actually consumed by the end user, which is supplied entirely by solar energy. The net
utilized solar energy is an indicator of the performance of the solar heating system.

Consequently, during this time, the system becomes more susceptible to stagnation issues.
Stagnation in solar thermal collectors occurs when the flow within the collector loop is dis-
rupted, even though sufficient solar irradiation is present for the loop to function [38]. If the
solar pump is stopped and solar irradiation continues to be available in the collector, the solar
energy absorbed by the collector can not be fully utilized or adequately removed from the sys-
tem, causing a substantial rise in the collector’s working fluid temperature, especially at the top
of the collector, until the working fluid reaches its maximum temperature and it starts to evap-
orate. Under these conditions, there is the potential risk of seriously damaging solar collectors
or system components. In extreme situations, if the temperature rises above the collector’s
maximum operating temperature, it may fail or malfunction [39]. Current solar heating systems
implement many measures to avoid this risk. Each closed circuit containing heat transfer fluid
is equipped with expansion vessels capable of accommodating the expansion of liquids due to
temperature elevation. The formed steam will push the working fluid to the expansion vessel,
when the temperature decreases the liquid will be pushed back into the collector. Addition-
ally, a pressure-controlled safety valve is compulsory, which activates when a predetermined
maximum pressure is attained, releasing gaseous heat transfer fluid [38].

7
Conclusions
In conclusion, this investigation has provided valuable insights into the performance of solar
heating systems in residential buildings and the potential integration of the electric grid in a
smart manner. The following section will summarize the key findings and provide potential
future investigations after this study.
The first part of this thesis focuses on examining the electricity prices in Denmark over the past
nine years for both price zones. The findings of this investigation reveal that electricity prices do
not exhibit a consistent trend across the different years. Spot prices are influenced by numer-
ous variable factors, making it challenging to predict future trends accurately. Additionally, the
outcomes of this study indicate that there is no uniform pattern regarding the duration and timing
of periods with low electricity prices. This suggests that it is reasonable to presume that periods
characterized by low-cost electricity will persist at a comparable level in the future, as evidenced
by their past occurrence. Nevertheless, it is observable that electricity prices have experienced
a noticeable increase over the past years in Denmark. This escalation has a direct impact on
the annual electricity bills incurred by end-users of the residential buildings. For this reason, it
is crucial to develop strategies that simultaneously reduce these associated expenses, secure
the energy demand, and contribute to the country’s environmental goals.
System 3 smartly integrates the electric grid, during periods characterized by comparatively
lower electricity prices. As indicated by the outcomes of this investigation, this solution leads to
a significant decrease in the annual electricity bill incurred by end users, compared to Systems
1 and 2, especially for the low-energy house typology. Based on the results, for a reference
system with a collector area of 150 m2, a 4000 litres preheating tank, and a 200 litres auxiliary
tank, the electricity cost levels that are most favorable for implementing smart control strategies
are those below 0.2 DKK/kWh. These price levels lead to a remarkable reduction of almost 50%
in annual electricity costs compared to the other two systems in the low-energy house. In the
passive house, although the reduction is less pronounced, it remains highly significant. On the
other hand, the net utilized solar energy is notably reduced in System 3, as the system becomes
more dependent on electric energy, decreasing the solar resource utilization. Nevertheless it is
essential to acknowledge that System 3 cannot rely entirely on the smart electric heater to cover
its complete heating energy demand, as its operation is limited to specific hours during restricted
periods. Consequently, a potential misalignment exists between these periods, of sufficiently
low electricity prices, and the energy demand. In cases where energy deficits occur when the
smart heater is off operation and there is not enough solar energy available, an auxiliary heater
is needed to operate to compensate for this energy deficit and cover the demand, maintaining
a reliance on the electric grid.
Regarding the possibility of covering the entire heating demand, of the residential houses, solely
through solar energy, the results obtained from System 1 indicate that it is potentially feasible to
fulfil the demand 100% with solar energy for both house typologies. For the studies component
dimensions, in the context of a low-energy, the heating demand is entirely supplied by solar
energy when the collector area is between 550 and 650 m2 and the preheating tank volume is
20000 litres. However, despite its capability, this system is not optimized or efficient. A system
of such dimensions would result in significant heat losses in the tank and pipes of the system.
Furthermore, the dimensions of this system are excessively large for the typical requirements
of an average single-family residential building. Consequently, the associated system costs for
the end user would be exceptionally high, leading to an economically unfeasible solution for
a single-family dwelling. Finally, due to the possible constraints of limited space, associated
Solar Energy Buildings with a high degree of self-sufficiency

==PAGE:71==
with this type of building setting, implementing a system of such dimensions is not technically
feasible.
In the case of the passive house, the heating energy demand is completely covered at different
collector areas and preheating tank volumes. In the case of this specific type of dwelling, it
may be economically feasible to satisfy the entire heating demand purely through solar energy.
Further investigation is necessary to assess the investment costs associated with a 100% solar
energy system compared to a conventional system.
Nonetheless, the current situation in the residential sector indicates that while efforts are be-
ing placed to renovate and improve the building envelopes in order to reduce overall energy
consumption in residential houses, not all buildings in the country have undergone such im-
provements. Many single-family houses, in particular, have not been renovated and continue
to rely on outdated technologies to meet their heating demands. Therefore, it is crucial to de-
velop viable solutions for these houses with higher heating demands. Feasible alternatives
involve downsizing the systems, by introducing an auxiliary energy source, to suit the scope of
the residential sector.
System 2 is a viable solution, which integrates electric energy from the grid and solar collectors
into a single system. This approach ensures the supply of energy demand at any moment while
allowing for the reduction of system size to meet spatial and cost requirements. However, it
should be noted that the performance of the solar heating system will be negatively affected,
decreasing the utilization of the available solar energy. In addition, this system remains heavily
dependent on high electricity prices and grid fluctuations.
Another notable finding from 6 is that the energy deficit from System 1 is lower that the energy
supplied by the auxiliary electric heater in System 2. This suggest that placing the auxiliary
heater separately from the solar tanks, as assumed in System 1, offers the advantage of utilizing
the maximum solar energy available in the tank, decreasing the need for electric energy. On the
other hand, placing the electric heaters inside the preheating or auxiliary tanks may potentially
increase the tank’s temperature, leading to a reduction in net utilized solar energy. This provides
a foundation for further research on the optimal positioning of the electric heater within the
system.
7.1
Further investigations
The scope of this thesis is constrained by time limitations, which restrict the number of system
combinations that could be explored to identify an optimal solution. However, it should be ac-
knowledged the potential to design and evaluate numerous additional combinations that could
lead to more optimized system solutions in term of performance and economic feasibility. The
current research serves as an initial reference point for further investigations focused on new
system configurations. Recommendations for future research may include:
• A more detailed parameter analysis for each of the studied system component could be
conducted based on the findings of this investigation. The objective would be to identify
the advantageous combination for integrating the electric grid through the smart heater
while determining the optimal dimensions of the system components.
• Assessing the advantages of supplying cheap electricity periods at different levels of heat
storage during different periods of the year.
• An accurate economic feasibility analysis should be undertaken to compare the viability of
these systems with the conventional heating systems currently employed in the residential
sector. This analysis could include an economic optimization of the various systems to
identify the most attractive solutions for houses with different heat demands. This would
Solar Energy Buildings with a high degree of self-sufficiency
60
==PAGE:72==
involve considering initial investment costs and operational costs, as well as energy sav-
ings. This evaluation will contribute to the understanding of the potential advantages and
drawbacks of these innovative systems in the residential heating sector.
• In order to further advance in the IEA SHC task 66, it would be beneficial to investigate
the most attractive energy systems in combinations with utilising the low electricity prices.
This could include studying the performance and cost-effectiveness of solar/heat pump
systems, PV/heat pump systems, PVT systems, and other relevant technologies. One
promising direction could involve incorporating photovoltaic (PV) panels to supply the
electricity demand of the dwellings. Additionally, the design of a smart controller could
be developed to effectively utilize periods of low electricity prices by either storing excess
electricity in batteries or directly using it to meet the electric demand. Investigating combi-
nations of solar thermal collectors and PV panels, supported by energy storage in the form
of water storage or batteries presents an interesting preliminary area for further study.
• Investigating the future advantages gained by systems utilizing low-cost electricity periods,
not only for the end user but also for the grid and society as a whole. This includes
evaluating the potential impact on energy demand, grid stability, CO2 emissions savings,
and the overall sustainability of the energy system.
Future research can contribute to the development of more innovative systems that optimize
the use of solar energy and low electricity prices, while increasing the level of self-sufficiency in
the residential sector.
