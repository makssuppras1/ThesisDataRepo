Abstract
Deployment of ﬂoor cooling systems is considered as a prominent instrument for attaining the transition
from an outmoded system, such as an air-conditioner, to a more integrated one. The current project
delved into the investigation of a radiant ﬂoor cooling system which can be implemented to a low-energy
residential building in order to avoid overheating hours, during periods with high solar gains. Since
most of the houses in the countryside operate with a ﬂoor heating system, a reversible heat pump
would combine the two systems and provide the essential heating and cooling for the whole year. The
test house that was selected is a 4-persons family building covering 141.1 m2 facing south and at the
current state has only passive cooling, meaning no mechanical cooling, only opening of the windows.
The plant conﬁguration, includes the heat pump, an ambient heat exchanger, the top heating, a brine
tank, a hot and cold tank as well as heat recovery to enhance energy savings as much as possible. The
heating part would be covered by the heat pump and the top heating, which essentially is an auxiliary
heating element on top of the tank, to keep the temperature stable at 55 oC for the domestic hot water
use.
In terms of the cooling part, diﬀerent scenarios were tested throughout the duration of the project,
in order to make the system as eﬃcient energy-wise as well as comfort-wise. A free cooling system
was implemented, which uses the ambient heat exchanger as a dry cooler to cool the tank, bypassing
the use of the heat pump. Moreover, to this extent a combination with passive cooling was added to
eliminate any overheating hours that occurred. In addition, exterior shutters that provide shading
were included to the simulations, giving a variety of diﬀerent choices and combinations.
Since there is a large variety of parameters that could be taken into account, a choice was made
for speciﬁc ones to cover both thermal comfort and energy consumption. The parameters that were
investigated were operative temperatures, ﬂoor surface temperatures, predicted mean vote (PMV),
relative humidity, coeﬃcient of performance (COP) and energy eﬃciency ratio (EER) of the heat pump
as well as energy consumption for both mechanical cooling and free cooling.
The outcome of the tested simulations, showed a combined system capable of providing the necessary
needs for cooling and heating. The operative and ﬂoor surface temperatures in every occupied area of
the house were in accordance with the restrictions from Danish Building Regulations and the Danish
standards. The free cooling scenario came out as a prominent system, that with a combination of
passive cooling in some hours that outdoors’ temperature is rising, can compete with mechanical cooling.
This would result in an increase in energy savings, as the dry cooler itself consumes signiﬁcantly less
energy than the heat pump.
The average operative and ﬂoor surface temperatures were found to range between 22-24, which range
is found to be the most optimal towards occupants satisfaction by several articles. What is more, the
building is well insulated, which leads to less heat loss to the outside. In order to prevent solar heat
gains from overheating the house, a windows glazing with a low G-value (solar gain). This indicates
that the window lets through a low percentage of solar heat radiation. However, it was kept at a level
where daylight would not be a issue for the residents. This counteracts the high insulation, while not
preventing solar gains during the winter.
In addition, it is worth mentioning that the energy consumption calculations for both cases were found
above the recommended ones from the regulations. This is highly inﬂuenced by the fact that not only
cooling consumption is still not considered in the regulations but the primary energy factor for electricity
is more than twice than district heating as well. The need for cooling in the years to come and the im-
plementation of such systems may lead to several changes in the future towards the prementioned factors.

Introduction
The construction sector is perhaps the most expensive sector in the EU, mainly to meeting heating
needs. A small share of consumption is attributed to cooling, especially during the summer. At
the same time, the rising global temperature, as of climate change, combined with the technological
development and know-how in the ﬁeld of renewable sources and energy savings, makes the need for
eﬃcient residential cooling systems more urgent. The overheating hours in a house are rising and
causing problems to the occupants throughout the day. [2] This can decrease the productivity, eﬃciency
and upsurge the dissatisfaction of the occupants, making the less productive to their jobs and overall life.
Due to high urbanisation advances, not only in Denmark, but in the whole world as well, it is projected
that power consumption for cooling will surge. Particularly in Denmark, it is becoming regressively
essential the need for cooling in residential houses, even though until now was not considered as a
major issue. People are becoming more and more optimistic about implementing a cooling system in
their house, thus ensuring indoor comfort conditions.
Throughout the years, diﬀerent types of cooling systems, such as Room Air-Conditioners, Central Air
Conditioners and Heat Pumps, Evaporative Coolers etc. have been developed and produced, all of
them with the same aspect : to satisfy the cooling needs and provide the best indoor environment.
In the current report, the use of a ﬂoor cooling system is investigated and whether the use of it would
meet the requirements of a sustainable future energy-wise and high thermal comfort levels. As many
domestic buildings in Denmark have ﬂoor heating systems, the use of these panels for cooling in the
summer make it cost-eﬃcient for the occupants. Floor cooling is a radiant cooling system and operates
via cooling coils implemented in the ﬂoor, which provides radiant heat transfer. In order to reduce the
energy consumption and increase energy savings, the use of an outside dry cooler, while bypassing the
mechanical cooling with a heat pump, is also considered in the report as a backdoor solution, making
it more climate-friendly. A comparison of the systems is made both energy-wise and for indoor air
quality (IAQ) conditions.

3.1
Literature review
Floor cooling systems use radiant heat transfer to cool the room/oﬃce and provide acceptable operative
and surface temperatures. A brief depiction of it can be shown in ﬁgure 1 below. [3]
Figure 1: Heating and Cooling coils
More speciﬁcally, heat transfer from a body with a high temperature to a body with a lower temperature,
when bodies are not in direct physical contact with each other or when they are separated in space, is
deﬁned as heat radiation. The diﬀerentiation between conduction and convection is that heat transfer
by thermal radiation does not necessarily need a material medium for the energy transfer. In the
case of thermal radiation from a solid surface, the medium through which the radiation passes could
be vacuum, gas, or liquid. Molecules and atoms of the medium can absorb, reﬂect, or transmit the
radiation energy. [4]
The major beneﬁt of making use of this cooling system is the even distribution of heating or cooling in
the house. In particular, when cooling with radiant ﬂoor heating, no hot or cold spots or draft or no
wind noise occurs.
In comparison to a more traditional air-conditioner is that an all-air system controls only the air
temperature while a radiant system combined with a ventilation system controls both the air and
radiant temperature. The level of thermal comfort experienced by the building’s occupants is dictated
by both of these factors. [5]
Furthermore, a common problem faced in warm and humid climates is perspiration. People enter a
building on a warm day and naturally perspire as their bodies attempt to cool down. In a building
cooled with a traditional HVAC system there is a lot of air moving around. Although the temperature
is desired, occupants are easily overcooled. [5]
However, a radiant system removes excess airﬂow and helps occupants cool down by absorbing their
excess heat, hence making it more eﬃcient and comfortable. [5]
As far as the radiant cooling systems are concerned, they typically use chilled water running in pipes in
thermal contact with the surface. The circulating water only needs to be 2-4C below the desired indoor
air temperature. The way they work is that heat is removed by the water ﬂowing in the hydronic
circuit once the heat from diﬀerent sources in the space is absorbed by the actively cooled surface ﬂoor.
Radiant cooling system is quiet, dust-free, and eﬃcient and has been used in Europe for decades.

In order to avoid condensation and a wet ﬂoor, the implementation of a dew point switch with a
combination of a controller, you can avoid the chance of moisture on the ﬂoors ensuring cooling. A
dew point sensor is utilized to monitor the condensation on the lines going to the slab. If these lines
condensate, the sensor will instruct the controller and the controller will adjust the input temperature
that goes into the slab to ensure there won’t any ﬂoor condensation. [6]
Typical ﬂoor cooling systems operate via a heat pump. In order to combine the use of heating and
cooling for the house the whole year, a solution of a reversible heat pump is essential. A schematic of
it shown in ﬁgure 2. [7]
Figure 2: Schematic of a reversible heat pump
Reversible heat pumps work in either direction to provide heating or cooling to the internal space. They
utilize a reversing valve to reverse the ﬂow of refrigerant from the compressor through the condenser
and evaporation coils. In heating mode, heat pumps are three to four times more eﬀective at heating,
for example they can have COP (coeﬃcient of performance) = 4) than simple electrical resistance
heaters using the same amount of electricity. In terms of investments, installed cost for a heat pump is
about 20 times greater than for resistance heaters.
In heating mode, the outdoor coil is an evaporator, while the indoor is a condenser. Whereas, in
cooling mode, the ﬂow is reversed and the outdoor coil is a condenser, while the indoor is an evapora-
tor. The COP for cooling mode is less than for heating mode, because the work done by compressor
is utilized only during the heating mode. The operation of this type of heat pump is shown in ﬁgure 3. [8]

In terms of the most preferable conditions and achieving thermal comfort for the occupants, several
reports and articles have been written. According to the REHVA [1] a ﬂoor temperature range of 19
oC to 29 oC is recommended in the occupied zone for rooms with sedentary and/or standing occupants
wearing normal shoes. This is a limiting factor for the capacity of ﬂoor systems. More speciﬁcally,
for heating, the maximum temperature is 29 oC, and for cooling, the minimum temperature is 19 oC.
These values are based on an average between seated and standing occupants. Seated persons would
prefer 1 K higher ﬂoor temperatures and standing persons 1K lower surface temperatures.
Figure 4 illustrates the most optimal ﬂoor temperatures. A range between 20-25 oC is recommended
as it provides the lowest dissatisfaction for the occupants.

According to an article from Mitja Koˇsir et al. [9] low-temperature radiative heating and cooling
systems represent an eﬃcient solution for creating good thermal environment. The article mentions
the importance of low temperature systems and how it enables the transport of heat through radiation,
eliminating the problems of user discomfort due to annoying air movement [10]. In addition to better
thermal comfort of users, such systems also exhibit improved energy eﬃciency due to utilization of
lower temperatures of heating and/or cooling medium, thus direct energy savings due to better boiler
eﬃciency and lower thermal losses of the entire system [11].
Furthermore, Chang-Ho Jeong et. al, published in 2018 [12], tested the feasibility of a radiant ﬂoor
cooling system in residential buildings with massive concrete slabs, in Korea, and the conclusions were
encouraging. As it is mentioned, the room temperature remained at the indoor set-point temperature
during most of the measurement period, and the ﬂoor surface temperature was higher than the indoor
dew point temperature [12]. This outcome leads to the proposition that an RFCS combined with
supplementary equipment for dehumidiﬁcation and cooling can satisfy the requirements for cooling
and condensation prevention in a residential house with multi-zones. In addition, they mention that
even when the system was shut down, the cooling capacity of it could last for a long time, due to the
heat storage and time lag by the thermal mass such as a massive concrete slab[12]. All in all, the
feasibility of such a system is considered valuable, and future developments would integrate it to reach
the utmost level of residents’ satisfaction.

Regulations
In terms of the regulations, the Danish Building Regulations (DBR) have continuously tightened the
requirement of the energy consumption of buildings. It is established that for newly built residential
buildings the total energy consumption for heating, cooling, ventilation and domestic hot water must
not exceed 30.0 kWh/m2 + 1000 kWh per m2 heated ﬂoor area per year. In addition, for the even
stricter building class 2020 (low energy building) the total energy consumption must not exceed 20.0

kWh/m2 per year.
According to BR18 [13], the thermal indoor climate of building must be suﬃciently healthy and
comfortable in consideration of their use. The planning, construction, operation and maintenance of
heating and cooling systems must be carried out in a way which ensures that:
• there is no risk of ﬁre or explosion
• there are no health risks for people or comfort-related nuisance
• there is no risk of injury to people or damage to building parts or installations
• no unnecessary use of energy will occur
Furthermore, Heating and cooling installations must be planned and established to ensure that:
• the location and installation does not result in vibration nuisance or damage to building parts or
installations
• they are protected against frost
• unintended leakage is avoided
• they can resist normal static, dynamic, chemical and thermal impact
• there is no risk of rupture or harmful pressure or water hammering
• corrosion or deposits which may reduce the capacity does not occur
• they have a useful life which is suitable for their location and replacement options
• noise nuisance, moisture or odour is not carried via pipelines
• surface temperatures do not occur which may result in injuries to persons with due consideration
of their use
• they can be cleaned, operated and maintained to a necessary degree. Components which require
operation, inspection or maintenance must be easily accessible in order to ensure that these
operations can be carried out in an expedient and responsible way
At the same time, DBR dictates the maximum amount of overheating hours for residential buildings of

To conclude, documentation of the thermal indoor climate must be obtained via calculations based on
the conditions in the critical room and based on Design Reference Year, DRY 2013 for the calendar
year 2010. [13]
3.3
Objectives
The aim of this master’s thesis was to demonstrate detailed modelling of mechanical cooling and free
cooling systems in Danish residences, with the use the simulation tool IDA ICE. The base case scenario
was a house with an installed ﬂoor heating system and without any cooling system, and how the
implementation of one would integrate the occupants satisfaction through diﬀerent scenarios. These
scenarios make use of passive cooling such as opening the windows or using an exterior shutter. All
simulations were based for a typical family house of four people, and the obtained results were analysed
and discussed.

4.5
Conﬁguration of the ﬂoor cooling system
The installation of the ﬂoor cooling system was established after calculating heating and cooling loads of
the house. It was set to perform with a PI controller with a sensor of the operative temperature. A PI
(proportional–integral) controller is a feedback control loop that calculates an error signal by taking the
diﬀerence between the output of a system and the setpoing. The massﬂow in the coils is controlled by
a two-way valve. This was set-up with regard to an already existing ﬂoor heating system as described
earlier, which operates via a two-way valve most commonly [15]. The location of the coils in the slab
was set to a depth of 0.02 m under the surface. The temperature diﬀerence (dT) at the design was set
to 5 o C for both heating and cooling. A brief illustration of the panels as seen in IDA ICE is shown below.

6
Discussion
All in all, the results taken show a stable RFCS and capable to provide the necessities of heating and
cooling a residential building. The RFCS came out as prominent system of cooling, and with the
combination of a reversible heat pump, can provide the essential energy savings for the residents, while
covering thermal comfort needs. The use of the free cooling system enhances energy savings, while
maintaining the same comfort levels.
For the simulations a window glazing with a low G-value (solar heat gains) was used in order to reduce
as much as possible solar heat gains, but keeping it at a reasonable level for the heating season in
winter. A glazing with higher solar gains would lead to an increase of cooling loads. However, heating
loads would decrease, hence the heat pump would operate in the same manner.
Another important aspect of the results that is worth mentioning, is that the house is insulated in
such a way that prevents high amount of heat loss. This acts as an advantage during the winter, where
the heating is operating and heat is kept within the occupied areas with minimal losses. On the other
hand, in the cooling season, heat due to high internal and solar gains is trapped inside the house, and
the heat pump must operate with high integrity to satisfy the demands. In the case of free cooling,

In the kitchen it was observed a lot overheating hours even with free and passive cooling combined. This

fridge, as well as the small area that covers. In addition, the presence of occupants during some periods

seen in table 12 these hours are eliminated, with only 10.8 being above 27 oC. Since the solar heat gains
are not the major issue for overheating, in this case, a better construction of this area, where the kitchen
is connected directly to the living room and is regarded as a unique area, is recommended. Small
areas tend to trap the heat inside the walls and make the occupants feel discomfort while operating there.
For the total utilization of the reversible heat pump a rearrangement of the primary energy factors
should be considered, as electricity has a multiplier factor of 1.9 instead of 0.85 district heating. This
means, that for the heating purposes of the house, and in order to satisfy the need for domestic hot
water, a boiler connected to district heating would be more optimal.
As software IDA ICE does not have in its components a reversible heat pump, all simulations had to be
made by implementing two separate heat pumps (one for heating purposes and one for cooling ones).
Though, the results should be almost the same, there is a slight possibility that a unique reversible
heat pump would adjust better to the system and make the transition from heating to cooling and vise
versa more smoothly.
7
Conclusion
The goal and scope of this project was to design, implement, conﬁgurate and ﬁnally test through
various simulations a ﬂoor cooling system with the aim to eliminate overheating hours, during high
solar gains periods, in a low-energy bulding. Additionally, two diﬀerent systems were put to the test,
in order to make the system comply with the energy reduction systems for a more sustainable future.
The ﬁrst scenario, which was a cooling system operated via a reversible heat pump proved to meet
the requirements from the Danish Building Regulations and provide the necessary thermal comfort
to the occupants for the entire season, mostly during summer. The main reason for implementing a
reversible heat pump, was due to the fact that several houses in Denmark use ﬂoor heating systems,
thus a combination of these two systems operating via one heat pump would cover the occupants’
demands for heating and cooling.
The second scenario was a free cooling system, where an ambient dry cooler would provide the necessary
cooling, totally neglecting the use of a heat pump. This system showed prominence, as it reduces
energy consumption of the house, while maintaining the same indoor conditions, with the contribution
of other factors. These factors were the opening of the windows during periods with high indoor
temperature or the use of exterior shutters for shading.
The project used as a simulation tool software IDA ICE, and all the corresponding data analysis were
illustrated through ﬁgures and tables. Each and every parameter was investigated against several
factors, to stimulate the ﬁnal outcome. Towards the heating part of the project, it was optimized
to operate via the reversible heat pump as well as using an electric heating element to maintain all
times the 55 oC temperature for the domestic hot water use. The temperature to the zones was found
adequate to cover the demand and the results comfort-wise were satisfactory.
From the cooling point of view, the mechanical cooling with the reversible heat pump was found to

eliminate overheating hours and keep a stable operative temperature as well as ﬂoor surface temperature.
In terms of energy consumption it was found to be way ahead of the recommended ones from Danish
Building Regulations. This outcome comes to question whether these primary energy factors that are
used today, and the disregard of the cooling should be re-evaluated. As Denmark moves towards a
sustainable future, the integration of the heat pump with the renewable energy would make this system
more friendly energy-wise to the occupants.
On the other hand, the free cooling system demonstrated that can provide a reduction in energy
consumption and with the addition of windows opening can provide the necessary thermal comfort.
The scenario with the exterior shutters was also found to be promising, however it is objective to
the occupants on whether limited daylight or the concealment of the view would aﬀect their overall
behaviour.
As this system is typically new to the market, since there are limited reports of implementing such a
system to residential buildings, there is still a lot of room for improvement. As mentioned in a study
from Rhee, Kyu-Nam et al., [17] for improving thermal comfort and energy performances, control
strategies need to be used for the radiant ﬂoor cooling systems, such as average water temperature
control depending on outdoor temperature, predictive control models, hydronic balancing strategies,
etc. Although in Denmark the climate is not so hot during the summer, which boosts the system to
make good use of the outdoor air, in hot and humid climates the incorporation of a ventilation system
with the RHC system makes it possible to cope with latent load and to prevent condensation risks [17].
This proves that the current investigated system can be implemented in every area of the world and
provide the occupants using it the best thermal conditions, integrating the cooling systems as whole
for a more sustainable future.
