Abstract
This thesis explores and documents the development process of creating a 3d mod-
elling system in Augmented Reality through the usage of Microsoft HoloLens. The
interactions with the HoloLens is studied and how to enhance the interactions. To
perform this the Leap Motion controller is used allowing the hands to be shown inside
the augmented reality of the HoloLens. The leap motion controller is to be aligned
with the HoloLens providing an immersive experience.
The interactions with the
holograms is studied to achieve clear affordances with the holograms. The modelling
is performed using cubes which can be connected in a similar manner as the blocks
in the game Minecraft.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Every day people become more reliant on modern technology that improves our lives
and make it possible to do things never imagined. The primary way in which we
interact with modern technology is using smartphones and computers giving us a
window into the digital world. They allow us to model buildings, cars and everyday
items giving us the opportunity to create a digital model that can be easily shared
and iterated up on before making it into the physical world. The cost of making in
the physical world can be expensive thus there is a huge reliance on building proto-
types that puts the digital model into the perspective of the physical world to create
a better visualization.
In recent years Augmented Reality have started breaking the barrier gaining more
traction from companies offering software with off the shelf augmented reality that
utilize the existing hardware. Others have created devices which allows for more com-
plex augmented reality which can map the physical world thereby making it possible
to project the digital world on top of the physical world.
Figure 1.1: Example of holograms shown in the pop culture movie Iron Man

Figure 1.2: Example of holograms shown through the HoloLens
One company which have set themselves apart from the others is Microsoft. By
creating the HoloLens, they have started to break the window. The HoloLens has
brought the world of augmented reality closer to what can be seen in the pop culture
movie Iron Man, where holograms are placed in the world and “Tony Stark” can
manipulate the holograms as if they were physical objects. By using an optical see
through display for each eye, the HoloLens can show 3D objects in the same way and
the mapping of the world allows the wearer to walk around the holograms seamlessly,
while the holograms will keep their position and rotation intact.
Devices such as the HoloLens allows for new ways to explore the world around us,
which will change the way in which we interact with digital media. The mix between
the physical and the digital world can enhance 3d modelling, as the physical world
allows for another perspective where the wearer can see the 3d model in relation to
physical objects. This makes it easier to visualize how the model is going to hold up
and then being able to take the model and scale it to the actual size to get a full and
immerse view of the model.
When we interact with the physical world we’re using our eyes and fingers, as
reaching out for a book uses our spatial perception to locate the book and use our
fine motor skill to grab the book. By grabbing the book our fingers provide a sense
of touch, providing haptic feedback that tells how much the book weigh and the feel
of the books cover. With the HoloLens, we get the spatial perception but we can’t
grab nor touch the hologram in front of us even though it feels like it is there.

The motivation of the project is to bring the physical world closer the digital world
using an augmented head mounted display, which in this case is the HoloLens. It
should be possible to interact with the digital world as if it was the physical world but
also providing an enhanced experience. The digital world might not have the feel of
the physical world, instead it provides the possibility to manipulate objects in manner
that is not possible in the physical world. An example of such manipulation could be
a cube. In the physical world it is solid and we can use tools to make it smaller or
other cubes can be glued together to create a bigger cube. But in the digital world,
the same cube can be resized and formed as pleased thus making such a simple thing
as a cube into something much more. This combination also allows the possibility to
put the digital models into context, allowing one to create models around the context
of where it is supposed to be used, thus providing a lot more value than modeling
the same thing in front of a computer screen.
The goal of this project is to create a system for 3d modelling, where models
can be visualized in relation to physical objects. The modelling will use a bottom
up approach, much like the style of “Lego blocks”. To create the best UX design,
the limited interactions from the HoloLens will be supplemented with another way
of interacting with the objects. It should be possible to use both hands as a way of
interacting with the holograms, thereby enhancing the system.

2.1
Defining Augmented Reality
The term Augmented Reality covers a broad amount of applications where it can be
hard to separate the different methods from one another. To separate the different
approaches Paul Milgram et al [1] came up with a representation of the difference
between the real environment and the virtual environment in the early 1990’s.
Figure 2.1: Figure of the Reality Virutality as shown in [1]
The suggested representation is called the “Reality-Virtuality Continuum“ a sim-
ple representation of it can be seen on figure 2.1. They used four prominent versions
to highlight their simplified version of Reality-Virtuality.
• Real Environment: Only physical objects is present
• Augmented Reality: The real environment with digital objects layered on top
of the real.
• Augmented Virtuality: The virtual environment with physical objects intro-
duced into the virtual environment.
• Virtual Environment: Only digital objects no physical objects present.
The term mixed reality is then used as a mean to describe the combination when
the difference between AR and AV is narrow making it hard to distinguish.
==PAGE:14==

2 Related Work
Microsoft refers to the HoloLens as a mixed reality device this fit the RV continuum
but due to the growth of virtual reality headset in the last couple of years, a clearer
distinction is needed. The HoloLens still needs a big leap, to get into the Augmented
Virtuality as only a rough version of the real world is scanned and moving objects
and object recognition is limited. In this project, the HoloLens will be referred to as
Augmented Reality.
2.2
Modelling in context
In recent years the game Minecraft[2] have grown to become a bigger part in the
development of kid’s creativity[3]. The virtual world of Minecraft is built exclusively
of 1 meter blocks with few exceptions. This is both the weakness and the strength of
Minecraft, as it provides an easy to learn method of creating models but at the cost
of the more advanced models. In the other end of the spectrum there is the physical
equivalent Legos, which allows people to create sculptures using small blocks that
can be stuck together.
(a) Minecraft
(b) Lego
Figure 2.2: Illustrations of castles in Minecraft and Lego
CubeBuilder[4] is an interactive and tangible 3D Modelling system which com-
bines both the physical and the digital process of creating 3D models.
The user
places tangible blocks in the physical world which then through image recognition
is transformed into a 3D model. The system supports the functionality of applying
different themes to the blocks model. They created a castle theme, which they put
on the blocks making it possible to create different castles with the blocks.
The
model can then be viewed on top of the physical blocks creating and augmented view.
Their approach provides a tangible interface which make it possible for people to
pick up and build castles without any prior knowledge of 3D modelling. The usage
of physical blocks does limit the buildings due to the constraints of the physical world.
==PAGE:15==
2.3 Human-Computer Interaction
7
The more advanced way of 3D modeling is done through 3D CAD programs.
These applications lets the modeler create the models that they need. However, most
of the CAD programs suffer from a steep learning curve requiring a novice to take a
deeper dive into getting familiar with the concept of 3d modelling. The programs let
the modelers start out within an empty space with no reference to what they want
to model. Most of the programs do contain a feature to add a reference image in 2
dimensions so that the modeler has some context to work from, but it requires the
modeler to take time to find and setup suitable reference images.
Other projects have tried to create applications that provide better context for the
modeler, Lau et al [5] created a system for doing 3d modelling in augmented reality.
By using a head mounted display “Canon vh-2007” they could track markers and
place them in the real world. They used physical objects with markers to represent
the shapes and the tools for their system. Their work let them to define a new term
for this sort of modelling, which they called “situated modelling”, the ability to use
the real world as a guidance for creating models. An example of theirs is modelling
a dish holder, where the modeler can take a real plate and hold it up against the
current 3d model, to compare its dimensions and placement. Thus, allowing for a
clearer view of how the model should end up while also allowing for a more iterative
approach to modelling.
Creating a modelling system within augmented reality questions the way we choose
to interact with the world. Tang et al[6] created an application of augmented reality
for smartphones using markers to place the augmented object. The unique feature
they gained from using a smartphone was the combination of being able to use the
touch screen for interactions in combination with markers used for placing 3d objects.
Compared to head mounted displays such as the HoloLens this allows for more com-
plicated interactions but can also be a disadvantage, since it takes up screen estate.
2.3
Human-Computer Interaction
There exist a lot of research on designing the right buttons and layouts for apps,
websites and other software running on computer or on a tablet/smartphone. It is
however a bit different in AR where objects from the virtual world are mixed with
the real world.
From the book “The Design of Everyday Things” [7] Don Norman describe the
terms for creating useful design that feels both natural and intuitive. The two main
parts of useful design according to Norman, is “Affordance” and “Signifier”. Affor-
dance is used to describe the possible relations between an object and an actor. In
terms of the HoloLens, the wearer is the actor and the HoloLens is the object. An
affordance could be the following “The wearer can wear the HoloLens on the head”

the head, requiring a “sign” to show that it is worn on the head. For being a useful

in hand to create a design, where the person doesn’t have to think twice doing the
affordance and is not aware of the design behind it is just how it works.

The gestures are kept simple and their affordance is clear and is learnt quickly

be achieved within the mixed reality, making simple actions become clumsier than

CHAPTER 3
Technology Overview
3.1
HoloLens
Microsoft HoloLens is a head mounted display which is the first device running the
Windows Mixed Reality platform. It allows for users to interact with digital objects in
the real world. By using the combination of see-through holographic lenses, gestures,
voice, and sensors, the HoloLens creates an immersive experience where the digital
world is brought into the real world. The following image show how a guy wearing
the HoloLens fights of robots in the game “RoboRaid”.
Figure 3.1: Example of holograms shown through the HoloLens [8]
To place the holes and the objects on the wall the HoloLens scans the room using
infrared sensors and creates a digital representation of the room, which is used in
combination with its gyroscope to track the position and rotation of the wearer in
relation to the room. The sensors used are based on the foundation made by the
Microsoft Kinect[9][10] which was first introduced as an accessory for the Xbox.
3.1.1
The power of the HoloLens
Microsoft have created a special processor for the HoloLens referred to as “HPU”
(Holographic Processing Unit) running 24 cores which is responsible for taking the
data from the different sensors to build the 3d mesh of the environment and tracking
the hand gestures and ensures that the 3d objects retain their position in the real
==PAGE:18==
10

world. The data is then send to an Intel atom CPU with 32-bit architecture[11].
Microsoft have mentioned on twitter[12] that the poly count of the HoloLens
should be kept below 100000 polygons, but the exact number depends on the opti-
mization of used shaders. This should be enough for most applications since there is
no need to render a background, so it is only used for the objects.
3.1.2
Supported developing platforms
There are several ways to develop applications for the HoloLens [13].
• Visual Studio + Windows 10 SDK
• Unity
• JS + WebGL[14]
The above methods are all compiled into a Universal Windows Platform appli-
cation such that it can run on the HoloLens. There is however another method of
making applications that run on a computer and is streamed to the HoloLens called
“Holographic Remoting Player”. Holographic remoting[15][16] is an application devel-
oped by Microsoft. It allows apps to run on a different machine where the HoloLens
sends back input data to a device, and then the device renders a video which is then
send back to the HoloLens. The advantages of doing this is that an external device
such as a pc provides more CPU and GPU processing power, thereby allowing for
more advanced applications to run on the HoloLens.
3.2
Leap Motion Controller
The leap motion controller[17] is a device used to capture the hands placed in front
of the device. The controller uses two monochromatic IR cameras and the infrared
LEDs to detect hands in front of the controller. The detection area can be viewed as
an inverted pyramid it can detect hands up to 80 cm from it. The device supports
multiple usages it can be placed on a desk to detect hands above it, or it can be used
in a virtual reality mode where it is placed on the VR headset and is detecting hands
horizontally. The device can only be connected to other devices through a USB port.
The controller supports a wide variety of development tools and languages. The
controller has SDK’s for the following platforms[18].

Design
There were a lot of choices to be made when designing the setup of the project, so
that the optimal platform could be created. This section goes into depth with these
decisions, explaining why the hardware and software was chosen as well as some of
the choices made for the interaction with the platform.

4.1
Choosing the hardware and development
environment
4.1.1
HoloLens
From the start of the project, HoloLens was targeted as the development platform. It
provides an experience unmatched by any other devices currently obtainable on the
market. There are other platforms obtainable, such as the Google Tango[19], which
creates a digital representation of the world in a similar manner. However, when
creating a 3D modelling system, the HoloLens does provide a better experience as it
allows for a more immerse experience using the stereoscopic view. The stereoscopic

view allows for the user to experience the depth of the holograms, and combined with
the ability to anchor objects at fixed positions in the physical world, it opens up for
a lot more opportunities within the augmented world.
4.1.2
Leap Motion
The HoloLens have simple interactions as explained in section 2.3, as these gestures a
limited and require a lot of head movement from the user. This breaks the illusion of
the HoloLens being a productivity device and makes it more of a media consumption
device. To overcome this, another way of interacting was deemed necessary for the
project.
Some of the choices considered where the use of physical blocks in a similar matter
as in CubeBuilder[4]. This would allow for a more tangible interface that would give
the user a better sense of the combination of both the physical and the digital world.
The use of markers as was used in [6] and [5] was also taken into consideration as it
would open up for the possibility to have different tools within the environment.

Another option is the use of physical controllers much in the same way as with
the controllers used in virtual reality such as the HTC Vive[20] and Oculus Rift[21],
which can be seen on figure 4.2. They add a lot of value, because they provide precise
positioning within the virtual reality, allowing the user better interactions with the
digital objects, thus making it a much more immersive experience. Currently there is
no such devices for the HoloLens, and Microsoft’s upcoming controllers for the mixed
reality platform, is not going to support the HoloLens[22]. The drawback of using
controllers is the need to always have them in the hand when interacting with the
holograms, which makes it difficult to pick up physical objects.

4.2 Combining the Hardware
15
The Leap Motion controller was chosen for this project, as it provides another
approach where the physical hands are captured and put into the digital world. This
allows the user to use the hands within the augmented reality, in the same manner
as in the physical world. This also allows for a more immersive experience, where the
ability to go beyond physical constraints of the physical world is made possible. The
advantages and disadvantages of using the Leap Motion controller in comparison to
the interactions within the HoloLens ,will be further explored in section 4.3.
4.1.3
Unity
The platform of choice was Unity using C#, as it provides a crisp and clear envi-
ronment to work with, as both the HoloLens and the Leap Motion device as both
work natively with Unity and C#. Unity is built for rapid prototyping, which ensures
fast creation of dynamics which allows one to make fast iterations, thereby making it
possible to test out different approaches to a problem without having to spend a lot
of time rewriting code.
4.2
Combining the Hardware
This section explores the possibilities of combining the HoloLens and the Leap Motion
controller. The Leap Motion controller needs to be paired with the computer over
an USB connection. However, the HoloLens does not support external USB devices,
thus requiring a workaround to use the Leap Motion Controller. Two methods of com-
bining the HoloLens and the Leap Motion was considered. One using a WebSocket
and the other one using Holographic Remoting.
4.2.1
WebSocket
An application written to the HoloLens needs to be targeted at the Universal Win-
dows Platform “UWP” and supports only 32bit applications. This limits the amount
software development kits which can be used, thus requiring the developer to do a
workaround.

4.2.1.1
Computer
The Leap Motion Controller is hooked up to the computer through USB. It tracks
the hands and sends it off to the Web socket Server.
The Web socket server reads the data and compile it into a JSON frame. This
data is transmitted through WIFI over to the client running on the HoloLens.
4.2.1.2
Hololens
On the HoloLens, the Web socket client runs it retrieves the packages and decom-
pile the JSON frame so that the application can use it.
Then the Unity application runs on the HoloLens, which uses the retrieved data
and translate it into a leap motion frame, which then is being updated on the HoloLens
allowing the user to perform gestures and interact with the application in real time.
4.2.2
Holographic Remoting
Holographic remoting[15][16] is an application developed by Microsoft. It allows apps
to run on a different machine, where the HoloLens sends back input data to a device,
and then the device renders a video which is then sent back to the HoloLens. The
advantages of doing this is that an external device such as a pc provides more CPU
and GPU processing power, thereby allowing for more advanced applications to run
on the HoloLens.

Computer
The Application is run on the computer, where it can utilize the processing

Choosing the Combination
The implemented method uses the holographic remoting. The reason for choosing this
solution is a combination of several factors. By rendering everything on the computer
and sending the information to the HoloLens, the Leap Motion Controller can be used
with its Unity SDK[23]. This also means that other devices could be connected to the
computer and if they’re compatible with Unity they can be used with the HoloLens.

4.3.1
Bringing affordance through Leap Motion Controller
The Leap Motion controller allows for the hands to be digitally presented inside the
augmented experience in the HoloLens. Figure 4.8 shows the hands as seen in the
augmented reality.
This allows for creating better affordances, since some of the
properties in the physical world can be brought into the augmented reality.
There is a clear affordance when moving a cube and through the leap motion this
property can be used to create a hologram that uses the affordances of a physical
cube. An example of moving a cube using the leap motion controller can be seen on
figure 4.8.
To bring a better connection between the hologram and the interactions, some
of the affordance provided by the physical cube can be utilized by using the Leap
Motion Controller. The hands lay on top of the real hands thus allowing the user
to grab the cube holograms. This creates an experience where the interactions don’t

It is noticeable that the thumb is represented as having 3 bones com-

each finger as seen on the figure. A hierarchy is used to store the fingers in the unity
scene, and the bones 1 to 3 are children of the finger they are attached to, and then
the fingers is children of the hand and the palm is a child of the hand as well.

colliding with it.

Keeping track of which fingertips that are currently touching any given cube, is
done through the usage of a binary representation of the hand. The fingertips are
each represented as 1 bit, as it allows for defining different gestures based on how the
hand is interacting with the object. This means that the total representation of both

hands, and it is worth noticing that the bits have been translated to their decimal

By using this method, it is easy and fast to define new gestures on objects as the

thumb and left index finger touching the cube.

When the user grabs a cube the middle point between the tip of the thumb and the

A check is performed to see if the model is moving below the platform to ensure that
the blocks aren’t going to be hiding beneath the table. In the current state, it only
checks for the y axis but it is possible to add a check for the other axes. If the object
is going to be beneath the platform the position is calculated to place it right on top
of the platform and updates the suggested position.
5.4.1.4
Snapping
It is possible for the cubes to snap together with other cubes making it easier to build
with. The snapping is done using Ray Casting. It calculates the direction the cube
==PAGE:40==
32
5 Implementation
is moving by using the position the cube is currently in and the suggested position
based on the gesture used. Then a ray is cast in that direction with a length equal to
the length of the cube starting from the center of the cube meaning it checks half the
length of the cube in front of it. If it hits another cube it will calculate the position
that is right beside the cube hit and with the normal vector this position can be
found. If the ray isn’t hitting another cube the suggested position will be used as the
position for the block.
Once the cube snaps to another cube a timer is added to let the user remove the
fingers without moving the cube away from the snapped point. Through testing the
optimal time seems to be around 0.75 seconds.

CHAPTER 7
Discussion & Future
works
Throughout the project the work done have been in the spirit of creating a great
system that utilize the full potential of the augmented reality. The primary objective
has been on finding the best interactions and affordances with the holograms and to
figure out how to use these findings to create the most immersive modelling experi-
ence.
Through testing of the system some of the values of using the hands to interact
with the augmented reality environment became clear. The following is the most
important values found, alignment with the physical world, interactions, context.
Alignment with the physical world
The alignment of the physical- and the digital world proved to be of great importance.
In the beginning the digital hands were placed at a distance from the leap motion
controller. It was unclear how the physical hands should be placed above the Leap
Motion Controller. Once the alignment with the HoloLens and the leap motion con-
troller the issue where gone. It was intuitive how to use the hands with the system.
Some of the confusion came from the limited field of view provided by the HoloLens
meaning the users weren’t looking at the correct place. It was however fixed when
the users got to look at their hands when the system was aligned with the physical
world.
Interactions
Interacting with the holograms proved to be easy to understand when the holograms
were shaped as a cube thus allowing the users to grab and interact with the cubes
without any explanation. The digital cubes share the same affordances as the physi-
cal cubes making the affordance clear for the user compared to the limited affordance
provided by the HoloLens interactions system.
Context
The context is a combination of the ability to place cubes on the surface of a table
and the ability to see shadows reflected on the surface. The findings showed that

7.1
Further development
In the current state of the system the modelling could have been achieved using the
interaction system provided by the HoloLens. The holograms example from section
4.3 shows it is possible to perform the necessary task to create a modelling system by
using the HoloLens interaction system.
Gestures and Interactions
The interactions and gestures provided by the leap motion controller provides a bet-
ter and more immersive experience compared to that of the HoloLens. By allowing
the user to use the hands in the same manner as they would interact with the objects
in the real world the affordance between the hands and the holograms is clear.
The method implemented for tracking the hands as seen in section 5.1.2 make it
possible to define new gestures that could further improve the interaction. An inter-
action worth considering would be to utilize both hands to manipulate a cube. The
interaction could be defined as gesture for pinching a cube with both hands allowing
the user to stretch a cube into the desired size thus providing an experience not pos-
sible in the physical world.
Occlusion
It would be interesting to include occlusion into the system as this could drastically
increase the immersion and context with the physical objects. Currently the holo-
grams will be shown in front of physical objects even though they are placed behind.
This would allow the system to provide a better sense of contexts allowing the user
to model around the physical objects. The spatial mapping would not allow for such
precise mapping on smaller objects so another approach is to be used. It should how-
ever be enough if the physical objects modelled around is close to the size of a chair
and consist of larger surfaces.
Markers
The use of Vuforia would have allowed for using markers in the physical world which
then could have been placed on the leap motion controller mount. By placing it on
the mount it would be possible to automate the process of aligning the physical and
the digital world. Unfortunately, it was not possible to use Vuforia through the holo-
graphic remoting. This could however by implemented if the HoloLens was receiving
the leap motion data from a WebSocket as described in section 4.2.1.

CHAPTER 8
Conclusion
The aim of the project was to create a more natural way of interacting with holo-
grams through the HoloLens and creating a system which allowed for modelling in
context to the physical world. The enhanced interactions were achieved through the
usage of the Leap Motion Controller. The ability to use the hands as natural input
improved the affordance of the system. It provided a strong relationship between the
holograms and their actions.
The interactions for this project was based around moving holograms in the phys-
ical world with the shape of a cube. The affordance provided by the digital cube
was comparable to the affordance of a physical cube. This allowed the system to
be intuitive for new users removing the instructions which were needed for users to
understand the interactions provided by the HoloLens.
The modelling system allows the user to place digital cubes in the physical world.
The cubes can be snapped together to create simple models. The modelling system
could have been implemented using only the interactions provided by the HoloLens.
However, the interactions provided by the HoloLens do not bring a satisfying affor-
dance with the holograms. Thus, the modelling system benefited heavily from the
interactions presented in this project.
Another aim for the project was to create context for the modelling system. This
was achieved to a smaller degree by utilizing the spatial mapping from the HoloLens
the cubes could be placed on the surface of a table next to a physical object placed on
the table. To get full immersion occlusion should have been present so that holograms
could be placed behind the physical model.
