Summary
Large differences in calculated energy consumption and actual energy consumption in
buildings are experienced in the construction industry. Multiple studies have shown
that the amount of energy used in buildings are significantly larger than the amount
calculated in building performance software, such as Be18.
In this project a review of the potential of using the software BeREAL as a predictive
tool and as an analytic tool for analyzing the operation of installations in a building is
carried out. The case building is Gladsaxe Company House (GCH) in Søborg - the head-
quarters of the construction company NCC. The building is awarded with a platinum
DGNB certification and the energy consumption in the building is calculated in Be10
as 24.8 kWh/m2/year and the building is thereby complying with the demands for low
energy class 2020. Gladsaxe company house have an Internet of Things (IoT) set up in
the building to monitor energy consumptions and indoor climate parameters.
Two forecast analysis were performed in BeREAL - one with standard settings and
one with adjusted settings to reflect the actual conditions and control settings at GCH.
The results of the two forecasts were an energy consumption of 32.7 kWh/m2/year and
38.3 kWh/m2/year respectively.
Furthermore, BeREAL provided a 84, 99 and 99.5
% confidence interval for the energy consumption. The 99 % confidence interval was
predicted to be between 16.8 kWh/m2/year and 56.7 kWh/m2/year for the standard
calculation and between 16.9 kWh/m2/year - 52.3 kWh/m2/year for the optimized cal-
culation.
The actual energy consumption for 2017 is extracted from the IoT system and the
building management system (BMS) and shows a yearly consumption of 56.3 kWh/m2/year.
A major part of this energy consumption is from the ventilation system which is using
more than twice the calculated value in Be10 and the heating system that uses three
times more than calculated in Be10.
Furthermore, BeREAL was used for analyzing the operation of the installations at
GCH. This analysis showed a large consumption for ventilation and heating compared
with expected values. From extracted data from the IoT system and BMS an analysis
of why this large energy consumption was seen was made. This analysis showed that
supply air is cooled down after being heated up by the heat recovery unit and subse-
quently heated up again by the heating coils. This causes a large heating consumption

during the summer. This control setting is concluded to be the main reason for the high
heating consumption compared to the calculated values.
Setpoints for room temperature in the building were also seen to be much higher
than the assumed values in the Be10 calculation. The average heating setpoint in the
building is found to be 24.3 ◦C while it is only 20 ◦C in the Be10 calculation.
For the ventilation it is discovered that the AHUs were operating on full capacity
most of the occupied time in the office. This increases the specific fan power (SFP) for
the system and causes a high ventilation consumption.
Alternative control settings for heating and ventilation was suggested and validated
by simulation in IDA ICE. It was found that lower supply temperatures during summer
combined with a CO2 controlled supply flow and a cooling set point of 23 ◦C instead of
24 ◦C in meeting rooms could reduce the total energy consumption by 2.5 kWh/m2/year
equivalent to a reduction in operation cost of 100.000 DKK/year for the entire building.
The main reason for the differences in calculated and actual energy consumption is
incorrect assumptions for user behavior and preferences made in the energy calculations.
However, additional contribution can be found in all phases of the construction project
- errors in the construction method and insufficient knowledge about the operation of
the systems are also contributing to the large energy consumption in the actual building.
IoT and data gathering is shown to be crucial for improving energy calculations in
the future due to the possibility of establishing a foundation for better comparison of
actual energy consumption for different building types.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Since the oil crisis in the 1970’s and until now, focus on energy consumption in buildings
have gradually increased. Climate goals have been set by the EU commission to reduce
green house gasses by 80-95 % compared to the level in 1990 before 2050.[1] In Den-
mark these goals have caused a tightening of the requirements for energy consumption
in buildings stated in the building regulation. The limits for the energy frame in offices
have been reduced by more than 50 % from 2006 to 2015 and even higher demands are
set for low-energy class buildings. The high demands are difficult to achieve in reality
and can have a negative effect for the indoor environment in a building.[2]
Building owners in Denmark can only receive a building permit for renovation or
construction of a new building if documentation of the building’s future energy con-
sumption is presented. This documentation is in Denmark created in Be18 - a software
developed by the Danish Building Research Institute. The software is developed as a
tool for benchmarking and comparison of the energy performance of buildings to ensure
an energy efficient building and compliance with the building regulations.
However,
Be18 is often seen used as a design tool for energy optimization or as a calculation tool
for the actual energy consumption. The actual energy consumption is, in practice, seen
to be much higher than the calculated value. This is due to a use of generic inputs
and standard weather data in Be18. In 2013 ELFORSK initiated a project to develop a
software that was able to predict the actual energy consumption in buildings by using
realistic inputs suited for the current project and weather data from the exact location of
the building instead of the currently used weather data, which is averaged data from all
of Denmark. The outcome of the ELFORSK project was the software BeREAL which
by adding uncertainties to each parameter, is able to predict an interval of the final
actual energy consumption in the building. As an additional tool, an analysis module in
the software was added. This analysis module gives the user the opportunity to analyze
the energy consumption for operation of installations in the building.
To analyze the operation of installations in a building, data of energy consumption
for the heating system, HVAC system, hot water, lighting, pumps, cooling etc. is needed.
Furthermore, data of the indoor environment such as CO2 levels, humidity and temper-
atures can be necessary in order to perform a sufficient analysis of the building. In
Denmark monitors for heating, electricity and water consumption are regularly used.
Unfortunately, the data from these monitors are in most cases not sufficient to perform
a thorough analysis of each system. To do this, separate monitoring is needed for each
system. Building Management Systems (BMS) are used to control and gather informa-

tion about the condition in each system and for controlling setpoints. However, most
BMS are not set to log and store the collected data for later analysis due to the small
amount of storage space in the BMS. For this, an Internet of Things (IoT) system, is
applicant. IoT systems can collect data from installed monitors and store it online. It
can communicate with other connected devices and combined with the BMS, create
an intelligent control system based on the conditions in the office and user preferences.
Buildings equipped with these types of setups are referred to as ”smart” buildings.
In this report, the potential reasons for high energy consumption in buildings com-
pared to the energy forecasts from Be18 are assessed and evaluated. First by literature
study and then by investigating and analyzing the energy consumption in Gladsaxe
Company House - an office building built as a low-energy class (Building Class 2020)
building in 2015 and rewarded with a Platinum DGNB certificate. For this investiga-

2.2
Energy frames in Denmark
In Denmark, not all energy consumption is included when a building is evaluated. The
included part in the evaluation of a new or renovated building is called the energy frame.
The energy frame includes the energy consumption for heating, cooling, ventilation and
hot water consumption. The building regulations takes the building type into account
and differs between residential buildings and non-residential buildings. Eg. in office
buildings, energy consumption for lighting is included in the energy frame but not in
dwellings. The consumption from electrical equipment is never included but knowledge
about this consumption is still needed to be able to estimate heat generation from these
which can have a great impact on the resulting heating or cooling demand.
The energy consumption in a building is affected by many different parameters and
all of them are to be included in a calculation of the energy frame. A set of important
parameters to assess when calculating the energy consumption in a building, is listed
below.
• The building envelope: U-values, window types etc. have a big effect on the
heat loss/gains in the building. A low U-value can cause high energy consumption
for heating while a high U-value can increase cooling. The type of windows can
affect solar gains and daylight in the building.
• Location and orientation of the building including daylight and outdoor
climate: The weather in Denmark can vary from one part of the country to
another and the distance to shore has a large impact on temperature and wind
conditions. The orientation of glass facades or windows influences the solar gains
==PAGE:26==
6
2 Background
which, if not considered, can cause overheating and increased need for mechanical
cooling.
• Heat source: The chosen heat source in a building can have major impact on
the calculation of the energy demand. The energy used by district heating only
counts as a third of the energy consumption generated by electricity due to primary
energy factors (when using energy factors for BC2020).
• Hot water production: The amount of consumed hot water in a building as well
as the used energy source for heating the water affects the calculation immensely
(This is due to the different primary energy factors for electricity, district heating
and other heat sources).
• Heat capacity of the building materials: A building with a high heat capac-
ity will have a more stable indoor temperature when the exterior conditions are
shifting. This will give the building the ability to stay cooled in summer and warm
in winter and save energy compared to buildings with a low heat capacity.
• Ventilation system: The demand for mechanical ventilation has increased as
requirements for the tightness of the building has increased as well. To obtain a
healthy indoor environment, mechanical ventilation can be necessary. Mechanical
ventilation can have a large energy consumption if not optimized and operated
efficiently.
• Solar gains and shading: The amount of sunlight in a building is important
to obtain a sufficient daylight level, but is a major heat generator as well. An
optimized balance between these two parameters can be crucial for a good indoor
environment and a low energy consumption.
• Natural ventilation: Natural ventilation is a ”free” way of ventilating a building
and in warmer periods, where overheating is experienced, an excellent way of
cooling the building. Natural ventilation can also cause an increase in heating
consumption due to the lack of heat recovery compared to mechanical ventilation.
• Internal gains such as occupants and electrical equipment: Due to the
heat, CO2 and moisture generation, occupants and electrical equipment can have
a great impact on the consumption for both heating, cooling and ventilation.
• Desired indoor climate: The owner of the building can have different prefer-
ences to the level of the achieved indoor environment and a higher indoor climate
class can cause an increased consumption for heating, cooling, ventilation and
lighting.
• Tightness of the building envelope: The demands for tightness in a building
have increased along with requirements for energy consumption. The tightness of
a building has an impact on the heat loss through the building envelope caused
by pressure differences inside and outside (infiltration). The demand for increased

Building Certification
Besides the permitted energy labeling of buildings, an increased interest in getting a
sustainability certification for new buildings has observed lately. Certification has been

Some of the benefits of having an energy certificate for a building is that it makes the

2.5
Energy Simulations
To ensure that a new or renovated building is complying with the requirements of the
energy frame, the Danish Building Research Institute (SBi) developed a software called
Be06 in 2006. The purpose of this software was to make a calculation tool that could
demonstrate that the building could fulfill the requirements for energy consumption un-
der the right conditions and to be able to compare the different energy performances
of buildings. The outcome of this calculation is also used for energy label of buildings.
The software is updated with every new building regulation and the newest version is
called Be18 (2018).[12]
The calculation in Be18 is based on monthly averages of all used input parameters.
It is not dynamic and it is not possible to create time schedules for either occupants,
ventilation, lighting or similar. The software uses multiple predefined settings such as a
heating setpoint in the building at a constant value of 20 ◦C, assumed airing when room
temperatures exceed 23◦C, night cooling at 24 ◦C and mechanical cooling when room
temperatures exceeds 25 ◦C. The weather data used for the analysis is the current DRY
(Danish Design Reference Year) weather file supplied by SBi as well. The newest DRY
weather file is based on the average weather conditions in Denmark from 2001-2010[13].
The outcome of the calculation gives a comparable indication of how the building
is performing, but because it is based on predefined parameters that are not specific
for the current building, the result is not meant as a prediction of the actual energy
consumption in the building. Unfortunately, it is often seen used as a predictive tool or
a tool used to design certain energy optimization. This gives false conclusions and is not
a reflection of the actual energy consumption in the buildings. Be18 was never intended
to be a tool for predicting the actual energy consumption, but merely a calculation tool
that made it possible to compare different buildings’ energy performance and from this,
show that under predefined conditions, the building would comply with the requirements

2.6
The Performance Gap
The difference between the calculated energy consumption and the actual consumption
is called ”the performance gap” in the construction industry. The performance gap has
been studied for more than two decades in an attempt to find reasons for it’s existences
and methods to reduce it. In 1989, a major investigation of 27 buildings’ energy con-
sumption was performed by Princeton University. The buildings were equipped with
sensors submonitoring all energy consumption in the building for two years. The ex-
tracted data was afterwards compared with the predicted values in the design phase.
The outcome of the investigation was an average of 10 % higher energy consumption
than predicted for the first year and a 20 % higher energy consumption the second year.
[16]. In Denmark, similar studies have been performed. In 2010 ELFORSK initiated a
project called ”Energy sinners in the construction phases” where the energy consump-
tion of 7 office buildings were investigated.[17]

frame as BR06 of 95 + 1000

BeREAL
BeREAL is a software for energy forecast and analysis of building operation. The initial
development started in 2013 as a part of the ELFORSK project called ”Energy sinners
in low energy buildings” (Org: Energisyndere i lavenergi-byggeri) mainly developed by
Aarhus Univeristy, Dansk Energi Management & Esbensen and Living Strategy. The
software is still under development and is at the current time, an online web-tool with
free for all access. BeREAL uses the inputs from an existing Be18 calculation and recal-
culates the values to more realistic inputs by including uncertainties for each parameter
and using weather data from the exact location of the building. The output from the
software is an interval of the energy consumption that has been shown to be closer to
the actual energy use in the building than other predictive energy calculation tools. [19].

CHAPTER 4
BMS and IoT
In most office buildings, installations such as ventilation, lighting, heating, cooling -
even solar shading and venting through windows are automatically controlled. This is
executed by the use of a BMS - Building Management System (In danish: CTS - Central
Tilstandskontrol og Styring). The BMS monitors the indoor conditions in the building
and through predefined setpoints, it controls the installations to establish the desired
indoor environment in each room. BMS improves energy efficiency, indoor climate and
gives the user the possibility to monitor the state of the building operation and locate
errors in the system.[21]
The BMS monitors the indoor conditions in different zones of a building and the
state of the installations, but does not necessarily log the monitored information for a
longer time. To give the best foundation for optimization of both indoor climate, energy
efficiency and building operation in general, knowledge about temperature, humidity,
CO2, occupancy, separate energy consumptions of each installation etc. is needed. To
gather this information, a network of sensors in the building collecting data and logging
it into a cloud service could be a solution. Such a system is called an IoT (Internet of
Things) system. IoT systems are not only used in the building industry, but are also
used in many other fields of work such as the car-, farming-, production- and medical
industry.
4.1
Building Management System
A traditional BMS consists of three parts:
• A main station: At the main station all data is accessible through a computer.
Here, it is possible to see the current state of all systems or go through logged data
for earlier periods on a monitor located in the building. In some buildings, this
information is available online as well.
• Substations: Each installation has it’s own substation. This is the place where
the control of the installations is executed. Measurements of the current state in
the related zone are received from the sensor and compared with the predefined
setpoint. If changes are needed, the substation sends a message to the actuator to
react in relation to the desired changes.
==PAGE:44==
24
4 BMS and IoT
• Sensors: Sensors are placed in all zones to measure the needed information in
order to ensure that defined setpoints are achieved.
Most often, the information between the sensors and the substations is processed in
a closed loop. The principle of a closed loop is that a controller compares the setpoint
for a certain parameter with information about the actual condition of the specific
parameter received from the sensor. As long as a difference is present, the controller will
communicate to the actuator to change settings. When the desired setpoint is achieved,
the sensor keeps feeding the controller with information, but the controller will keep the
current settings. The principle of a closed loop is visualized in Figure 4.1.
Figure 4.1: Principle of a closed loop used in the BMS [22].
A closed loop is not the only way of setpoint control. Due to the many possibilities
of different types of installations linked to the BMS, many different control loops are
used. Eg. open loops, where there are no feedback from the sensor to the actuator, are
often used where the controlling element is not dependent on feedback eg. time.
The installations linked to the BMS could be:
• Illumination (lighting) control
• Plug load control
• Heating, ventilation and air conditioning
• Security and observation
• Fire alarm systems
• Lifts, elevators etc.
• Plumbing
• Alarm monitoring
==PAGE:45==
4.2 Internet of Things
25
The closed loops are primarily used to control HVAC systems and lighting which are
also the main focus in this report.
In most buildings, systems linked to the BMS represent 40 % of the building’s total
energy consumption. In case lighting is included in the BMS, this number can increase
to 70 %. If a BMS system is not configured or managed in an optimized way this can
cause a building to use tremendous amount of energy on operation. Optimization of the
operation in the building can be done using the BMS to ensuring that the control of
building services are executed as intended, the right information is logged and stored,
and by updating outdated software or equipment.
4.2
Internet of Things
The amount of devices capable of connecting to the internet worldwide increased 31 %
from 2016 to 2017 and now equals approximately 8.4 billion devices. In 2020 Internet of
things, as this collection of devices are referred to as, have been estimated to represent
more than 30 billion devices.
The definition of an Internet of Things (IoT) system is basically a network of all
electronic devices capable of collecting and sharing information online. It is a system
that makes is possible for the connected devices to communicate with each other and log
information online. Depending on what system and which devices are included in the
IoT network, the collected data can be used to improve systems, products or costumer
experience. The use of IoT systems was first seen in 1982 where the Carnegie Mellon
University implemented it into a Coke machine. With the machine connected to the
internet, it was able to report the current inventory and if newly loaded drinks were
cold or not.[23] Other examples of existing IoT networks are described below:
Building and home automation: IoT in buildings are used to monitor and con-
trol mechanical and electrical systems in private homes and public buildings. It can
be used for multiple kinds of monitoring such as monitoring for security, fire safety,
lighting, constant monitoring of all electrical equipment, for HVAC systems and energy
management. With an integration of IoT in the BMS, it is possible to include occupant
behavior in the control system and create a ”smart-building” and obtain a better en-
ergy efficiency. Furthermore, an IoT system used in buildings gives the opportunity to
gather information about indoor climate, energy consumptions etc. and store it into an
online cloud. This stored information is very useful when optimizing the operation of
the building and evaluating energy consumption. A combined IoT system and BMS is
believed to be the future monitoring system for buildings due to it’s ability to monitor
and control the different system based on predefined control schedules, its inclusion of
user behavior and preferences, and its directly logging of data into a cloud service.[24] [25]
==PAGE:46==
26
4 BMS and IoT
Agriculture: IoT is also used for optimizing in the farming industry. Here, it is
used to collect data concerning environmental conditions such as temperature, rainfall,
humidity, wind speed, pest infestation, soil humus etc. The information is collected
through wireless sensors and sent to a cloud service. This gives beneficial conditions for
improving and automating farming techniques. [26]
Medical and health care: In health care IoT is used for remote health monitoring
and emergency notification systems. Monitors for blood pressure, heart rate, pace mak-
ers or for specialized implants can be linked in an IoT network and make the patient feel
safer even when they are at home. Furthermore, ”smart beds” that are able to detect
if the bed is occupied or a patient is getting up are implemented into some hospitals. [27]
These examples are only a few of which IoT have been implemented in a successful
way. The focus in this project will be IoT used for monitoring of operation of installations
and for monitoring of energy consumption in buildings.

Neither lighting, fire protection or security is connected to the BMS but is operated

As described previously, the building is divided into zones. Each zone has at least
one sensor connected to the BMS. These sensors monitor the following parameters:
• Relative humidity
• CO2-concentration
• Temperature
The monitored information is sent to the substation of the BMS where it is compared
to the setpoint temperature of the zone, the occupants’ adjustment of the setpoint, the
damper-position and the supply air temperature. By comparing this information, the
BMS can obtain the desired conditions in the room by adjusting on damper-position,
valve-opening or supply air temperature.
Setpoints for indoor temperature and the air temperature supplied by the ventilation
system can be controlled by the BMS according to control schemes. These schemes are
used to optimize the energy consumption by taking advantage of the change in outdoor
conditions during summer and winter and day and night.
At GCH the following control schemes are used by the BMS:
• Time schedules: Different control settings are applied between 7 AM to 6 PM on
workdays compared to night time and weekends.
• Heating and cooling curves: The relation between supply temperatures and out-
door temperature is not the same for heating and cooling mode. The mode depends
on the indoor temperature.
• Night cooling: Cold outside air is used in summer to cool the building down at
night.
• Fan control: The used fan power is regulated based on the conditions in the sup-
plied offices.
Previously, morning boosts were performed as well, but this proved to be ineffective
by the operation staff and was removed from the control schemes.

CHAPTER 9
Discussion
During this study a major gap between the calculated energy consumption and the ac-
tual consumption at GCH was found. By using BeREAL as an analytic tool an excessive
energy consumption for ventilation and heating was discovered. By assessment of the
installations in the building several inexpedient control settings showed to cause an in-
creased energy consumption compared to the energy calculations.
This chapter provides a thorough discussion of the results obtained in the study and
a validation of their accuracy. Furthermore, the potential of BeREAL as a more precise
energy calculation software compared to other similar software is assessed. Pros and
cons of using IoT for collecting data for analysis of energy consumption and operation of
installations in buildings is also discussed. At last, previous studies about other reasons
for the performance gap than the reasons found in the design and operational phase are
assessed and discussed.
9.1
The actual consumption in GCH
compared with the BeREAL forecasts
By using BeREAL as a forecast tool a total energy consumption of 32.7 kWh/m2/year
was calculated with standard inputs and a total energy consumption of 38.3 kWh/m2/year
was calculated with optimized inputs for GCH. Even though a substantial amount of
time was used to optimize the input values for the corrected forecast, the results were
not far apart from each other. This indicates that the standard inputs in the forecast
calculation is well estimated by the developers and can be used if a building is assumed
to operate under ”normal” conditions.
The actual consumption in GCH was 56.3 kWh/m2/year and this equals a difference
between the calculations and the actual consumption of 32 % for the corrected calcula-
tion and 42 % for the non-corrected calculation. The BeREAL forecast was only slightly
closer to the actual consumption compared to the Be10 calculation that represented 46
% of the actual consumption.
The corrected forecast analysis including standard deviation predicted that the ac-
==PAGE:102==

tual consumption would be in the interval from 16.9 kWh/m2/year to 52.3 kWh/m2/year
for the 97 % confidence interval. The actual consumption was outside this interval even
though the confidence interval covered a span of 35.4 kWh/m2/year which in relation to
the size of the energy consumption is a huge interval. The size of the interval reduces
the usability of the output from BeREAL in this case, but by reducing the interval the
output would serve as a more useful prediction.
Due to the large variation in energy consumption caused by weather conditions, it is
inevitable not to get an interval for the energy consumption. The energy consumption
is not going to be the same each year. This is defiantly an advantage of using BeREAL
because it provides the user with information about the sensibility of the building in
terms of energy consumption. By adding the energy consumption of a building as an
interval instead of a single value, it will give the user of the building an idea of when the
building is exceeding expected energy consumption and when high energy consumption
is due to weather conditions.
Due to the large intervals for energy consumption obtained in this study, a similar
output as from the BeREAL forecast might be obtained by adding standard uncertain-
ties to the Be18 output. If the energy consumption from Be18 was presented with a
standard deviation corresponding to eg. ± 30 % this would cover the actual energy
consumption in most cases without using an additional tool such as BeReal. To use this
approach a general reevaluation of the inputs used in Be18 would be necessary due to
the unrealistic values used.
At GCH, an average setpoint of 24 ◦C was found. However, a setpoint for room
temperature of 20 ◦C was used in the Be18 calculation. A room temperature of 20 ◦C
was never used at GCH and is not a realistic indoor temperature for any office buildings
with sedentary work. By a reevaluation of the input parameters and adding a standard
deviation to the Be18 calculation, the output from Be18 could serve as a forecast of the
actual energy consumption. More realistic input for Be18 is difficult to obtain at the
current time, but by increasing the use of IoT networks in buildings, data can be gath-
ered over time and create the foundation for better estimation of the actual conditions.
The forecasts in BeREAL are based on assumptions made by the user of the software.
This demands a detailed knowledge about user behavior and the actual conditions in
which the building is going to operate. Information about the conditions in similar build-
ings is difficult to obtain at the time because only a few building owners are monitoring
their consumptions separately for each type of installation and even fewer are sharing
the knowledge with others. The forecasts made in this project were given quite high
ranges between lower and higher estimates, which resulted in large confidence intervals
and a rather imprecise prediction. More shared information about consumptions in sim-
ilar buildings could present the user with knowledge to make a more accurate prediction
and by this, a smaller interval for the forecast of the final consumption.

will experience higher solar gains compared to a north facing wing and this condition
will affect the need for ventilation, the room temperature and the heating- and cooling
demand. The energy consumption fir ventilation, heating and cooling in the building
were analyzed for the entire building and were therefore not generalized based on the
limited data. However, the analysis of room temperatures was based on information
from the 5th floor and might not be representative for the entire building.

Occupancy is a crucial factor when performing energy calculations. Due to humans

consumption and by this, optimize the operation of the building. The difference be-

easily access, log and store data, not only in relation to energy, but also in relation to
security, fire alarm systems and control of installations or equipment in the building for
optimization of indoor environment.

energy consumption and actual consumption. This comparison is not possible with the

In this project, the analysis module in BeREAL showed a significantly higher con-

For heating, setpoints were slightly higher than assumed in the BeREAL analysis.

temperature was the same in the weekends as in the work days and this causes the
heating consumption to increase in the weekends due to the reduction in internal heat
gains caused by occupants, electrical equipment and lighting. An investigation of the
heating consumption during a week was not performed but a such investigation could
show the amount of energy used for heating an empty building. A lower setpoint during
the weekend might lower the consumption for heating without reducing the quality of
the indoor environment in the occupied hours.
The correlation between the indoor-, supply and the outdoor temperature at GCH
in winter showed that the AHU was shifting between heating and cooling mode and
only supplying either 28 ◦C or 20 ◦C air. There was no correlation between the outdoor
temperature and the supply temperature and the supply temperature did not vary in
either heating or cooling mode. It seems like the AHU operates at full heating capacity
as soon as the room temperature is below 22 ◦C. A higher (than 20 ◦C), varying supply
temperature while the indoor temperature is between 22 ◦C and 24 ◦C at a slightly
higher ventilation rate might reduce the need for the system to operate at the minimum
and the maximum supply temperatures of the heating- and cooling curve. The indoor
temperature was kept between 21 ◦C and 24 ◦C and was only slightly affected by the
outdoor temperature. This indicates that the heating power of the system was sufficient
during the investigated period.

and only vary the supply flow to satisfy the current cooling demand in the zone. The
system is able to keep the supply temperature above 18 ◦C by heat recovery as long as
the outdoor temperature is above -12 ◦C so no heating should be needed during summer.
For ventilation, it was seen that the AHU were operating at full capacity for the
majority of the occupied time. The supply temperature was between 16 ◦C and 21 ◦C
and the CO2 concentration in the investigated zones was found to be below 650 ppm
at all times. The zone temperature was close to the setpoint temperature (± 1 ◦C)
during occupied hours. This indicates that the building might be over-ventilating which
is supported by the high ventilation consumption and the high utilization of the AHUs
capacities.
To reduce the utilized capacity of the AHUs, the max. supply temperature could be
lowered. This would cause a larger cooling capacity at a lower supply flow rate. The
building only has a cooling demand and no heating demand during summer and too
large intervals in supply temperature will cause the reheating of the supply air as de-
scribed above. A constant supply temperature would prevent this reheating because all
zones is supplied with the same air temperature. A low constant supply temperature
could furthermore cause the supply flow rate to be reduced due to the increased cooling
capacity of the supplied air. This control solution could be beneficial by removing the
heating demand during summer and decrease the SFP in the AHU due to the lower
capacity utilization.
To verify if the suggested control settings could be an energy saving solution, two
energy simulations are performed in the software IDA ICE - one for the current settings
and one with the supply temperature curve shown in Figure 9.1 and with a varying
supply flow inside the existing limits for the flow and no active heating coil.

Previous studies of reasons for the

To obtain a wider overview of the reasons for differences between calculated energy con-
sumption and actual energy consumption, a review of previous work in the subject is
performed. In this project, the design phase and the operation phase have been investi-
gated, but several studies have shown that the reasons for the performance gap can be
found in most phases of a construction project and in all types of buildings.
In 2017 LØB (National Association of Organic Construction) investigated the energy
consumption and indoor conditions in four family dwellings build to comply with BR10.
The investigation consisted of registration of the energy consumption for heating, elec-
tricity and hot water, registration of indoor temperatures, humidity, CO2-level, noise and
pressure and performing of a thermografic test to identify leaks and cold bridges. The
results for the energy consumption from the investigation is shown in Figure 9.2. The
yellow bar in the diagram is a correction made for two of the buildings that were heated
with geothermal energy instead of district heating to be able to make the comparison.

losses through foundations and cables breaching moisture barriers, that all contributed
to the higher energy consumption.
Based on their findings they came up with the
following suggestions:[42]
• The measured energy consumption should be the basis for regulatory approval and
not the calculated (this is done in Sweden). [43]
• Development of methods to estimate energy consumption independent on user
interference.
• Energy consumption should be evaluated based on the CO2 emission (As in Eng-
land) [44] and the CO2 emission from the production and transportation of building
materials should be included in the energy frame - these represent approx. 50 %
the total emission throughout the lifetime of the building.
• The demand for energy consumption is related to the size of the building and not
just energy/m2 (A total CO2 emission pr. resident)
• Tightness demand is related to size, so large constructions is not favorited.

So besides an improved construction phase, the suggestions from this investigation

In June 2012 ELFORSK hosted a workshop for people involved in all parts of the
construction industry and the purpose was to identify possibilities of why the new build-
ings’ energy consumption were seen to be so far from the calculated values. All phases
of a construction project were assessed.
The conclusion of the workshop was the following:
• Programming and Planning Phase: Building owners often lack knowledge and
competences. This leads to ill-defined specifications, ’sloppiness’ and deficiencies
in the following processes.
• Design and Engineering Phase: Design and simulation tools are used in the
wrong way. This leads to design and dimensioning errors which can be difficult to
fix later on in the construction process.
• Construction Phase: Shoddy workmanship e.g. poor insulation, grouting, cov-
ering and corrosion. Components are used wrong and configured wrong. Plans and
drawings are not followed due to poor coordination between craftsmen. Quality
control is sporadic and inadequate and errors are often difficult to correct when
the building is finished.
• Handing over the building: Commissioning is sporadic and inadequate.
• Operations Continuous commissioning is rare. Competences among the blue col-
lar operational staff are often insufficient. Often the buildings are used differently
than intended but operations are not adjusted accordingly.
Based on their conclusion, not only an optimization of the energy calculation, the
requirements and the construction phase is needed, but an optimization in all phases of
the construction project is necessary to be able to make a more realistic prediction of
the energy consumption. [17].

Using IoT technology is not per se giving the user a more energy efficient building but

online from all locations. The BMS and the IoT system at GCH are performing similar
tasks, but the IoT system makes the managing and the analyzing of the extracted data
more simple.
Long term, collection of data in buildings would give a basis for evaluating energy
uses in buildings and compare actual consumptions. This information could be used for
creating more realistic values to use in energy forecasts for future buildings.

CHAPTER 10
Conclusion
The amount of energy used in buildings for heating, cooling, ventilation, lighting, pumps
and hot water is much larger than the calculated values in the design phase. The tools
for predicting the energy consumption - such as Be18, use standard inputs that are
not representative for the actual conditions in a building. Furthermore, weather files
applied in these calculations are made from national averages of weather conditions and
not on the basis of the actual location of the building. The energy consumption in a
building vary from year to year due to differences in weather and user behavior in the
building. Therefore, predictive energy calculation will always be subject to an amount
of uncertainty. BeREAL is used as an add-on to Be18 and uses three-point-estimation
to predict an interval for all input parameters in Be18 and thus, gives a more accurate
energy forecast for any building.
An additional tool in the BeREAL software is an
analysis module that compares estimated and actual energy consumption in a building,
when used to discover faulty operation of the installations.
The Be10 calculation for GCH showed an energy consumption of 24.8 kWh/m2/year.
The actual consumption in the building was measured with an IoT setup and through
data extraction from the BMS system. All data was extracted for 2017 except for light-
ing with were not available as a separate measure through either of the systems and had
to be estimated. The actual consumption was found to be 56.3 kWh/m2/year - 127 %
more than the predicted consumption from Be10.
By using BeREAL as a forecast tool, an energy consumption of 32.7 kWh/m2/year
was obtained without optimizing the input values. By optimizing the 10 most sensitive
parameters in relation to the heating and electricity consumption, an energy consump-
tion of 38.3 kWh/m2/year was calculated. The two values represents 58 % and 68 % of
the actual consumption respectively.
The actual energy consumption for each type of installation at GCH was compared
with the optimized forecast analysis from BeREAL which showed an excessive energy
consumption for ventilation and heating in the building. By extracting information of
indoor climate conditions, temperature in the heating and ventilation system, flow rates
and energy consumption from the IoT system and the BMS and by performing a thor-
ough analysis of the systems, several issues with operation was discovered. High heating
setpoints were recorded throughout the year including during the weekends. The aver-
age heating setpoint measured at GCH was 24.2 ◦C. The heating setpoint used in Be18
==PAGE:114==
94
10 Conclusion
is 20 ◦C. A large consumption for heating was measured during summer. In low-energy
buildings, heating through heat recovery should be sufficient to heat the building most
of the year.
By analyzing the air temperature through the ventilation system from the heat re-
covery unit to the supplied zone, it was found that a large amount of energy was used
to reheat the air after being cooled down by the cooling coil. This reheating of air is
included in the control settings for the supply temperature at GCH, but is not possible
to include in the energy calculations. These control settings for the supply temperature
causes the primary part of the gap between calculated energy and actual energy used for
heating. Correlation between outdoor temperature and supplied temperature showed
that both heating and cooling curve was utilized but only by supplying the maximum
supply temperatures for both curves. For ventilation, the AHU was seen to operate
at maximum capacity most of the occupied time which had an effect on the SFP and
therefore reducing the energy efficiency of the system.
A suggestion to change the control settings for the ventilation to a lower supply
temperature in summer was made as an attempt to lower the utilization of the AHU’s
capacity and the heating consumption. The suggestion was validated in IDA ICE which
showed potential for a reduced energy consumption of 2.5 kWh/m2/year equivalent to
a cost of approximately 100.000 DKK/year.
The occupancy factor used in all calculation was 0.55 but according to previous stud-
ies executed by Clausen T. in 2017 the average occupancy factor at GCH is far below
this estimated value. Clausen T. measured the actual average occupancy factor to 0.18.
The lack of heat gain from the reduced amount of occupants compared to the estimated
in the energy calculations was furthermore concluded as a potential reason for the higher
energy consumption for heating.
The performance gap at GCH was partly caused by inexpedient operation of the
installations and poorly estimated values in the energy calculation.
Other potential
reasons investigated in previous studies are errors in the construction phase, inaccurate
configuration of installations, lack of quality control, inadequate commissioning, and an
absent follow up on the performance of the systems in the building.
In this study, the IoT setup at GCH was crucial in order to enable an analysis of the
condition of the building and locate the excessive energy consumption. By increasing the
use of IoT systems in buildings, an increased knowledge of user behavior and preferences
will be obtained and establish a better foundation in the future for performance of energy
calculations. BeREAL gave a better estimate of the actual consumption than Be18 but
the result was still far from the actual consumption. The greatest challenge of creating a
precise forecast of energy consumption is that the operation of the systems in the building
is not always executed as intended in the design phase. In order to further investigate and
discover this unintended behavior, substantial monitoring and data analysis is needed.
