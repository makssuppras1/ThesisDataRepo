Abstract
This study investigates the application of the Extended Kalman Filter (EKF) for time-varying param-
eter estimation in the context of a nonlinear loudspeaker model. The objective is to improve the
accuracy of the model by predicting and incorporating the variation of the DC resistance and sus-
pension stiffness when driving the loudspeaker with high voltage over time. Both simulation and
real measurement cases are analyzed to evaluate the effectiveness of the EKF for this purpose.
The report presents the theoretical background, experimental setup, and results in terms of error
measures and prediction figures.
The research reveals that the EKF demonstrates promising performance in predicting time-varying
parameters, with improved accuracy compared to the time-invariant model. It is suggested that the
proportionality between the uncertainties of measurements and the model is crucial for the perfor-
mance of the EKF and their careful tuning is necessary. Furthermore, the local SNR of the system
states was found to be a determining factor in the prediction quality. The analysis also identified a
high-frequency (>1000Hz) problem associated with linearization and discretization issues, leading
to prediction errors.

Introduction
1.1. Motivation
Parameter estimation is an essential part of accurate modeling across many different disciplines.
Indeed, this is a topic of research in such fields as system control, machine learning, statics, and
applied mathematics. Similarly, acoustic engineering also has an interest in these tools, for example
in precise modeling of a complex acoustical system such as a loudspeaker.
Time-invariant estimations of parameters are already commonplace in current acoustical practices.
Essentially this case reduces to an optimization problem that tries to minimise a loss function by
fitting an optimal set of parameters that best tune the model to some reference target data [3].
These kinds of algorithms can find the optimal solution to the given problem with a set of time-
invariant parameters.
However, in time-varying dynamical systems parameters can change over time and thus it is useful
to estimate their variation. This can improve the accuracy of the model and offer useful insights into
complex systems.
In the field of acoustics and audio DSP, a few instances where it could be useful are:
• A loudspeaker subject to changes in the voice coil temperature or the suspension compliance
• Numerical parameter prediction where analytical solutions are difficult to develop or imple-
ment
• Avoid additional or difficult measurements in loudspeaker analysis (EG: voice coil temperature
measurements)
• Audio devices subject to temperature changes
• Audio devices with control knobs such as guitar pedals and amplifiers
1.2. Methodology
The family of algorithms chosen for time-varying parameter estimation is the Kalman Filter. This
is an estimation algorithm commonly used in Control System practices to balance measurements
and model predictions to estimate the optimal states of a system. In particular, the main subject of
the study will be the Extended Kalman Filter, a derivation of the Kalman Filter for non-linear systems.
1.3. Scope and Research Goals
The scope of the project is to test the Extended Kalman Filter as a tool for predicting time-varying pa-
rameters for the full non-linear loudspeaker model. More specifically, the test case is a loudspeaker

that is subject to potential changes in the voice coil temperature (and thus DC resistance) or in the
suspension compliance/stiffness because driven with high-level voltage over time.
Thus, the goal is to predict their variation and check if the model becomes more accurate when fed
with an array of changing parameter values rather than a single constant one. Both simulations and
real measurement cases will be analyzed.
1.4. Target Reader
This paper is aimed at the engineer with a fair understanding of loudspeaker electroacoustics but lit-
tle experience with state estimators such as the Kalman Filter. Thus, the theoretical sections will not
describe the loudspeaker model extensively, but rather focus on the estimation methods. However,

2.2. Time-Variant Parameters in Loudspeakers
Since the ultimate scope of the study is to predict time-varying parameters for a loudspeaker model,
it is important to establish what these could be in a real physical scenario.
First of all, a time-varying parameter can be defined as one whose variation is relatively slow com-
pared with the lowest frequency component used in loudspeakers [8]. In the paper, when referring
to ”time-variances” they will be meant slow, according to this definition. Parameters such as Bl(x),
K(x) and L(x) vary with frequencies in the loudspeaker range and oscillate depending on the dis-
placement position. Thus, they are considered part of the nonlinear system but not time-varying.
In very general terms, it can be assumed that a loudspeaker (running at normal operating level and
in average listening conditions) will not display highly dominant time-variances in its parameters
within the time span of short measurements. If this weren’t the case, low errors displayed in [3] with
time-invariant parameters couldn’t be justified.
2.2.1. DC Resistance, Re
Over longer time frames, one can consider the time variation relative to the temperature increase
in the voice coil due to usage, especially at high levels. Indeed, given the high inefficiency of the
loudspeaker, most of the electrical power input gets converted into heat in the voice coil rather than
in acoustical power. In turn, the voice coil temperature directly affects the coil’s DC resistance, Re
[4].
Hsu stated that the expected behaviour of the voice coil is to rise in temperature relatively quickly
over a short period of time (minutes), but then will only have a gradual increase in temperature as
it continues to operate [5]. However, it is important to notice that the variation of temperature is
generally slow (in the time-varying sense outlined above), as stated by Klippel [8]. This makes Re a
perfect candidate for parameter prediction.
Research by Hill shows the relation between Re and the voice coil temperature [4]. The behaviour
shown in Figure 2.1 is the one outlined by Hsu [5], with a fast (approximately logarithmic) increase of
temperature and Re over the first 100 minutes, which then stabilizes to an equilibrium of heat pro-
duction and dissipation. These measurements were conducted on a copper coil driver. Please note
in the picture, the voice coil resistance is calculated directly from the DC resistance value, which ex-
plains the perfect resemblances of the two. The back plate temperature, closely related to the voice
coil temperature, is instead a direct measurement which indicates a similar rise in temperature.
The relationship between Re and voice coil temperature can be stated as
Re,t = Re,a[1 + α(Tt −Ta)],
(2.1)
where α represents the temperature resistance coefficient, T the temperature in Celsius degrees,
the subscript a indicates ambient room temperature and t the heated coil temperature. In the case
of copper, α = 4.33 · 10−3 [5] [4].
So, for example, the increase in temperature of Figure 2.1, ∆T = 55°C (from 20°C to 75°C), would
imply in a copper coil an increase in Re by ∼24% over ∼100 minutes.

2.2.2. Suspensions Stiffness, K
Another parameter that can be time-varying in loudspeakers is the stiffness K0 (inverse of compli-
ance), associated with the behaviour of the loudspeaker suspension.
According to [2], the compliance/stiffness of the loudspeaker suspension depends on the recent
excitation level history, influenced by the electrical power, displacement, velocity, and suspension
stretch relative to the maximum displacement. Also, the suspension temperature (which can de-
pend on excitation history) is another factor influencing the compliance.
Thus, predicting K0 is definitely not as straightforward as it was for Re. It can be said that in gen-
eral, the compliance can rise with temperature or by a ”softening” of the suspension after a stretch.
However, according to the situation, K0 could change over the length of measurements anywhere
from −(< 1)% (very slight suspension softening) to −50% (doubling of the compliance).
Given the complexity of an analytical solution, the EKF (if validated properly) could be a useful pre-
diction tool suggesting changes in the suspension.

2.4. Kalman Filter

2.4. Kalman Filter
The Kalman Filter (KF) is an algorithm used to estimate the optimal state of a system by carefully
balancing data from noisy measurements and a system model [7].
The state of the system refers to the collection of dynamic variables which fully describe the system
[11]. In the case of the loudspeaker system outlined in 2.1, the four variables are i current, i2 eddy
current, x displacement, v velocity, which can be referred to as the states.
When talking about noise, an uncertainty about a state is expressed. The uncertainty relative to
the measurements is labelled as measurement noise and the one relative to the model as process
noise.
It can now be understood that the term ”filter” refers to the possibility of filtering out noise from
measurements to estimate the statistically-optimal state of the system. In LTI systems, the KF is
indeed the optimal state estimator if given the correct information about the uncertainties and if its
assumptions are respected [11] [12].
The KF operates recursively, updating its estimate of the system state based on measurements.
It follows a two step process: the prediction (mainly based on the system model) and the update
(mainly based on the measurements data, balanced with the information from the prediction step).
2.4.1. Continuous State Space Representation
The KF follows the state-space model representation, a convenient way to express the system dy-
namics through first order ODEs of the states over time.

2.6. KF Algorithm
2.6.1. Notation
Before proceeding, it is important to clarify a notational aspect of the upcoming section.
First, the hat notation (ˆ.) indicates an estimate of some kind of quantity.
Second, as specified in the introduction to this chapter, the KF follows a two-step algorithm, the first
of which is relying on the model and the second one incorporating the measurement data.
In this context, the term ”a priori”, commonly found in the literature, simply refers to a prediction
done before the most recent measurement data is considered (thus relying on the model). One
would talk about an ”a priori” estimate in this case, found in the prediction step.
Instead, by an ”a posteriori” estimate, it is meant a prediction that considers the most recent mea-
surement data, found in the update step.
An ”a priori” estimate is here indicated by a ”−” in superscript (−).
Instead, an ”a posteriori” one is indicated by a ”+” in superscript (+).

2.7. Extended Kalman Filter
The KF operates within the assumption of a linear system. However, in more complex nonlinear sys-
tems (such as the one discussed in Section 2.1) the regular KF would yield significant inaccuracies.
In those cases, the state space model needs to be reformulated and a KF variant shall be used for
better performance. In this paper, the Extended Kalman Filter (EKF) is the KF nonlinear alternative
considered.
Eqs. 2.9 and 2.9 need to be reformulated as defining a model through matrix multiplication and
addition relies on a linearity assumption [12].

Here f represent the model function which returns the states at time step k based on the states,

even measurements that have a nonlinear relationship with the states can be incorporated.

its first derivative at each of its point. In this way, a nonlinear function can be locally (point-by-point)
approximated linear and the states can be estimated as in the linear case. It is important to note that
the linearization in an approximation process which breaks the estimation optimality of the regular
KF. Furthermore, given incorrect information, the EKF may diverge. Thus, in general, the EKF can be
more inconsistent and prone to errors and divergences than its linear counterpart.

3.1. Loudspeaker Measurements
The first step in this process is to perform measurements of the states (current, displacement, veloc-

The goal of these measurements is to stimulate a progressive change of Re and K over time, so

The time-invariant parameters can be estimated over the first five seconds of the measurements.
Then, the goal of the EKF will be to estimate the variation of Re and K after the first five seconds.
The chosen driver is the 4.5” mid-range/woofer of copper-coil loudspeaker DALI Spektor 1, seen in

to the model, without the addition of any noise. Here the parameter variation for Re and/or K0 is
specified. These will serve as a basis for creating the simulated measurements and to evaluate the
performance of the predictions.

3.4. EKF Setup

uncertainty is so small and to reduce the computational load, it was not considered necessary to
run multiple iterations and take the mean or median.
Uncertainty matrices R and Q can be defined by setting their diagonal value to σ2, as
R[i, i] = σ2[i], for 0 < i < nMeas, i ∈N
Q[i, i] = σ2[i], for 0 < i < nStates, i ∈N
By adhering to this methodology, where noises and variances are calculated relative to the ”real”
states of the system and an error percentage (ξ%), one can be sure that the additional error is pro-
portional to the amplitude of the states. Thus, the prediction errors later calculated are equally fair
across the states by maintaining the error relative to a unified percentage.
X0 will be most often initialized with zeros given that the simulations (as well as the real measure-
ments) start from a position of rest independent of history. Consequently, P0 will thus also be initial-
ized with all zeros, as the uncertainty about the states at the beginning is null.
For some cases, the KF/EKF setup could end here. However, there are instances where the ”correct”
values of R and Q defined as above will give sub-optimal or divergent results and it is necessary to
tweak them.
It is either possible to modify the whole uncertainty matrices
ˆR = r · R
ˆQ = q · Q,
or selecting specific entries relative to a specific state
ˆR[i, i] = ri · R[i, i]
ˆQ[i, i] = qi · Q[i, i],
where ˆR, ˆQ represent the tweaked versions of R, Q and r,q (or ri, qi) are constant coefficients.
Unfortunately, this method relies on trial and error and manual tuning to find the best values for r and
q. Ideally, an optimization algorithm could individuate the optimal values to minimize the prediction
error. It was not implemented to reduce the computational load.
It is important to point that it will be very often essential to reduce the entries of Q relative to the
parameter states, given the normalization scheme by the RMS value. In colloquial terms, this allows
the EKF to be informed of how fast the parameter should change. In other words, if the entry Qi,i
is too large the prediction will be too noisy, thus there will be a lot of uncertainty (noisy oscillations)
around a presumably correct trajectory. If Qi,i is too small, the prediction may diverge or monotoni-
cally converge to the real solution too ”slowly”.
The entries of Q relative to the time-varying parameter will be often noted at Q(Π), where Π is the
time-varying parameter.
3.4.2. Real Measurements
Most of the considerations stated for the simulations are similarly valid for the real measurements.
However, in this instance, the input and the measurement data are ready to be used.
The correct solution is here represented by the measurements, over which the error is calculated.
Although the measurement contain some inevitable noise, they still represent the best reference
data for parameter estimation.
The procedure for RMS normalization and definition of the uncertainties matrices defined in the
simulation cases will be similarly kept here. The states will be however computed with the time-
invariant model as the parameter prediction is not known beforehand. ξ% is set at an even 20%,
to encompass the largest error for the states, represented by the ∼18.5% error of i from model to
measurements.

3.5. Experiments
The ultimate goal is the experiments is to show the efficacy and limits of the EKF in predicting param-
eters to reduce the model error. Thus, for all experimental instances the following errors, relative to
the target solution, will be analyzed.
• EKF prediction (Pred): states computed by the EKF while predicting the parameter(s). This
allows to verify that the EKF does not display internal inaccuracies which may in turn reduce
the parameter prediction accuracy
• Time-invariant model (TInv): feeding the model the set of constant parameters θ
• Time-varying model (TVar): feeding the model the array of time-varying parameters values
predicted by the EKF, alongside the other constant parameters θ
If the time-varying model error is lower across all states than the time-invariant model it means that
the EKF is effective and improves the model accuracy.

3.5.2. Realistic Simulations
The simulated experiments of highest interest are the cases where the parameters Re and K0 vary by
a realistic amount (to mimic the one-minute measurements described in 3.1) and when they vary by
the maximal amount possible (for a general realistic loudspeaker). In this way, the EKF possibilities
are explored at the two ends of the possible realistic parameter variation, assuming the behaviour
in the middle of this range will be congruent.
Let us evaluate the range of Re possible variations.
For the realistic case, a copper coil loudspeaker run at high voltage for one-minute could expect
approximately a +5 °C temperature increase in the voice coil from 20 °C room temperature, as seen
from Figure 2.1. Using Eq. 2.1, with an initial Re,initial ∼4.52 Ω(Appendix A), it can be expected to
have a final Re,final = 4.62 Ω. This is a pretty small increase (+2%) compared to the possible Re
change discussed in Chapter 2.2 (+24%). Thus, this well represents the lower end of the parameter
variation.
To guess the maximal possible Re variation, the maximum manageable temperature of a copper
voice coil is considered. As specified in Chapter 2.2, a loudspeaker normally finds an equilibrium
temperature between voice coil heating and dissipation. In Figure 2.1, the equilibrium is around 75 °C
and one could probably guess this would be the case for most loudspeaker. However, research
by Klippel indicates that loudspeakers could momentarily also reach voice coil temperatures up
to 130 °C [8]. Thus, in the interest of representing the largest Re variation, a temperature change
from 20 °C to 130 °C will be considered. With the procedure used above, an increase of +48% to
Re,final = 6.68 Ωis expected.
Instead, considering the variation of K0, the range −[< 1, 50]% shall be investigated as outlined in
Chapter 2.2, starting from K0,initial ∼1775.13 N/m (Appendix A). For the realistic case, a change of
−0.1% is proposed, leading to K0,final = 1773.36 N/m. For the maximum case, by a change of −50%
the stiffness becomes K0,final = 887.57 N/m.
Six simulations will be run in total:
1. Realistic Re (+2%)
2. Realistic K0 (−0.1%)
3. Maximum Re (+24%)
4. Maximum K0 (−50%)
5. Realistic Re + K0 (+2%, −0.1%)
6. Maximum Re + K0 (+24%, −50%)
These simulations will have a total duration of 96’000 iterative steps, which corresponds to one
second at the set sample rate. This keeps the computation time manageable. The validity of the
experiments is not effected as the parameter variation does not depend on an actual physical mea-
surement, but it is defined in the script. The parameter variation in these simulations will happen
over the duration of a second.

3.6. Limitations

3.6. Limitations
The study encompasses several limitations, many of which have already been disclosed and will be
summarized in this section.
Regarding the prediction of Re for the real measurements, one-minute is barely enough to warm up
to voice coil and see any difference in Re. Longer measurements could not be arranged. Further-
more, the Python script is pretty slow, so long simulations or multiple iterations become computa-
tionally intense (time-wise) for a normal computer.
Following this computation problem, there is no optimization algorithm for the uncertainties. The
manual tuning can be still effective but does not guarantee the optimal solution and requires more
effort on the user side.
Instead, considering K0, there is no guarantee for a stretch in the surroundings with the current
measuring setup. Also, only the linear component is predicted time-varyingly while the polynomial
coefficients are held constant, which may effect the results.
Another problematic aspect of real measurements is that, given that the model is not perfect, the
EKF may try to compensate the model error though the degrees of freedom given by varying Re and
K0.
Last, Forward Euler is a basic discretization scheme and a more advanced one may have lead a
lower error. Same goes for a high sample rate.

Single tone sinusoids
The correct and predicted states of the loudspeaker system when driven with a 10Hz sine wave are
shown in Figure 4.5. It can be seen in Figure 4.5b that the state predictions display a way more
nonlinear behavior than that correct solution of Figure 4.5a, as suggested by the highly distorted
sinusoidal estimates. Simultaneously, the prediction of Re shows a very specific noisy estimate
which invalidates the predictions and noticeably escalates the error. Indeed, by observing Figure
4.7a demonstrates a clear pattern of periodic spikes in the estimate.
By studying Figure 4.5b, it can be noted that those spikes match the zero crossings of i, x and i2.
Those three are all in phase below the resonance frequency. v, being the first time derivative of x will
always be ∼90° out of phase, given that the time derivative of a sine implies a multiplication by jw
in the complex domain, i.e. a 90° phase shift. Furthermore, in the figure it could be noticed how the
points corresponding to the zero-crossings of i, x and i2 match the greatest signal distortions. v is
also especially distorted in those points despite the phase shifts, with a compensation that reduces
the peaks and valley down to near zero.
This error-prone behaviour is presented in the whole frequency range below fres, progressively re-
ducing until converging to fres.
The behaviour at resonance is shown in the baseline case in 4.1.1. In that instance, i and x are 90°
out of phase, which in turn makes v in phase with i. i2 is in phase with x. It is interesting to notice
that also in the case Re spikes for the zero-crossing of i (Figure 4.1b).
Above resonance, frequencies 150, 300, 1k, 3k, 5k and 10k Hz were tested. For brevity, only the
essential data will be reported in this section and the rest of the error result can be found in C. 150
Hz, with an Re prediction error of 4.52%, 300 Hz, with 4.56% and 500 Hz, with 5.12%, performed
better than the baseline case (with 5.99% error).
However, at 1000Hz the error in the prediction of Re rises again to 7.05%. By rising the frequency
to 3k, 5k and 10 kHz the algorithm starts to break down and completely fail the predictions. The
problem is well shown in Figure 4.7b. The prediction of Re follows the correct trajectory but it is com-
pletely shifted downwards, even to the negative region. The same happens for 4.7c. Upsampling
reduces this problem, as indicated in Figure 4.7d for 3kHz at fs = 3·96kHz. At 10kHz the script runs
into overflow given the problematic nature of a negative Re.
This high frequency issue will be highlighted all throughout the paper and discussed in detail in
Section 5.

Noise and Bandpassing Issues
The noise inputs display the same high frequency issues highlighted above. Indeed, the downward
shift of Re implies illogical solutions (often with negative values of resistance) that invalidate the
prediction and increase the error immensely.
The full spectrum (non bandpassed) noise even runs into overflow issues given the estimation of
negative Re. Similarly, the [10,10k] Hz bandpassed noise has 54.31% prediction error for Re and
likewise estimation problems. Instead, the [10,1k] Hz has an acceptable 7.4% Re prediction error,
without significant issues.
This confirms once again that the current EKF setup with this model is very susceptible to high
frequency content.
The errors for the noise signals are reported in full in Appendix C.
Regarding bandpassing, the reader is informed that the author found it to be problematic, at least
with the implementation provided by Scipy.Signal (in the form of a cascaded lowpass + highpass,
4th order).
Indeed, as seen in Figure 4.8, when the noise input is bandpassed [10,10k] Hz, the start and end re-
gion of the states present an anomaly. While the issues in the start region could be easily explained
by a transient, the end region has no apparent reason to behave as in the figure. This problematic
behaviour appears only when bandpassing the signal and often results in the prediction behaving
erratically towards the end of the simulation. According to the setup, those level ”jumps” may im-
prove the estimate of the parameter with increase in SNR. Other times the prediction is uncontrolled
and unpredictable towards the end region.
The reasons behind this are numerous and were not deeply investigated. With more time, propri-
etary filters could have been developed and tested. Readers are thus left with a suggestion for
future research.
It is to be mentioned that lowpassing alone does not imply this erratic behaviour. However, total
errors are not substantially changed and thus they are not included in these preliminary tests for
brevity.
In any case, the author decided to minimize filtering in all other instances of the study. Given the
unstable nature of the process in the current setup, bandpassing was completely avoided: On the
other hand, lowpassing was occasionally used if necessary.

5
Discussion
5.1. Uncertainties
Although the math behind the KF/EKF does not show any immediate correlation between R and Q,
it would seem that at least numerically and empirically there is a sort of parallelism to them, at least
in terms of the predictive outcome.
The author found that in many cases (like in Section 4.1.2) it would seem like the most important in-
formation that the KF/EKF needs is the proportionality between Q and R and instead their absolute
values do not matter as much. In other words, it is of little relevance how big or small these uncer-
tainties are, but only their relationship. If R > Q there will be more weight to the measurements for
the predictions and inversely if Q > R there will be more to the model. The noise of the predictions
will follow.
A plausible counterexample to this principle may be the data shown for the measurement error re-
sults in Section 4.1.5. When ξ% = 50%, despite the proportionality of the uncertainties of measure-
ments and model is 1:1, their values matter in the sense of simply declaring too much uncertainty to
the EKF. Indeed, now the states will have heavy oscillations around a presumably correct trajectory
(sometimes much larger than the magnitude of the state itself, especially for Re), resulting in noisy
predictions. Thus, this does not necessary contradict this proportionality idea because there may
be a threshold where the uncertainty is just way to high and thus results in more noise.
When referring to the entries of Q relative to the time-varying parameters, it would seem like those
values just adjust for the noise that the prediction will result in, pretty much informing the EKF of how
fast the parameter can change, as demonstrated by the analysis in Section 4.1.2.
The data for the measurement error of ξ% = 0.1 and ξ% = 1e−10 (Section 4.1.5) further demonstrates
this point and highlights again how the proportionality of Q and R is dominant over their absolute
values.
It shall be made very clear that these are by no means a definitive statements, but a mere observa-
tion and a suggestion for further study. It may as well be a byproduct of the simulation setup.
5.2. Frequency Analysis
For the 10 Hz sinewave, it can then be assumed that the in-phase zero-crossings that were de-
scribed in Section 4.1.3 are the reason for the failure of the prediction. The explanation behind this
is likely to be a much lower local SNR at the zero-crossings of i.
The EKF doesn’t have enough information to justify the simultaneous drop to zero of i, x and i2.
Essentially the EKF tries to balance process noise with measurement noise, while no real signal
41
==PAGE:53==
5.2. Frequency Analysis
42
information is provided (locally). This can logically results in erratic behaviour. The EKF may com-
pensate the model (especially i ∼0) by raising Re drastically. Indeed, as previously outlined in 4.1.1,
increasing Re reduces the level of the states.
For the resonance frequency case, it was pointed out how Re spikes for the zero-crossing of i (Figure
4.1b). This indicates that the EKF is mainly relying on i to estimate Re, which makes sense given
that in the system equations it only appears in di
dt (Section 2.1). Thus, when approaching the zero-
crossings of i, Re is increased to compensate. This matches and further justified the observations
made for the 10 Hz case.
The improvement of error for frequencies 150, 300, 500 Hz may be due to a higher system states
SNR compared to the baseline case. By referring to the current TF in Figure B.5, the level of i usually
follows a curve that lowers at the resonance and for high frequencies. Frequencies such as 150,
300, 500 Hz thus should be in the sweet spot of highest level for i, justifying the higher SNR.
Regarding higher frequencies, it was clearly demonstrated numerous times throughout the paper
how the EKF presents a considerable weakness to them. This may be due to a number of factors,
which are here proposed as possible explanations.
The most likely reasons justifying this problem are to be found in linearization and discretization,
known for being sources to high frequency errors
Indeed, linearization error gets higher with frequency. In the case of sinewave, being the oscilla-
tions more dense (per sample points), the linear approximation with the Jacobian are going to be
progressively more inaccurate at the current sample rate. On a similar note, the Forward Euler dis-
cretization method is known for having phase issue at higher frequencies. Both of this points are
confirmed by the fact that upsampling reduces this problem, as it was indicated in Figure 4.7d for
3kHz at fs = 3 · 96 kHz.
Other motivations can be found in the nature of the loudspeaker system. The loudspeaker current is
very dependant on Re at low frequencies and thus it is easy to estimate it. Instead, at high frequen-
cies the inductance is predominant. This explains the TF in Figure B.5. Also, a lower SNR across all
states (including i) at high frequency (as shown by the TFs in Figure 2.2) could imply more error.
To explain the downward and negative shift of Re, considering all the possible sources of error out-
lined above in the first place, two somewhat reasonable explanations are provided.
First, given that the TF of i has more high frequency content than the ones for x and u, the EKF can
compensate by reducing Re to increase i.
Second, at high frequency the displacement is extremely small and it fluctuates minimally around
a (very small) negative DC offset, thus its value is close to zero but always negative for very high
frequencies. Although it is unlikely, given the dependency on displacement for Bl, K, L and all the
other sources of error listed, it may be that the EKF predicts a negative Re locally as a byproduct of
this.
It is to be mentioned that pointing out to one precise and specific reasons for this type of issues is
very complicated given the complexity of the loudspeaker and EKF systems. All of the above expla-
nations are thus to be considered suggestions for further investigations and definitely not absolute
findings.
Regarding the multitone and noise signals, similar conclusions can be suggested.
In the case of the 3-tone multitone, the additional information provided by the multiple sines (also
considering a mixed phase response) allows the EKF to have a slightly better SNR and reduce the
error. In the case of a 4-tone multitone, the 10Hz prediction is successful again suggesting a better
SNR locally. The higher frequencies of the 7 and 8-tone multitone follow in the same bank of issue
highlighted for high frequnecies above.
The noise behaves similarly to the multitone considering frequency content. Regarding the band-
passing issues, no clear explanation is found to explain the problem. Possibly the cascading pro-
cess (lowpass + highpass) corrupts the data in a way that is harmful to the EKF predictions. In any
case, this is a secondary issue to the problem and it is just noted in case the reader encurs in a
similar problem.

Considering all the frequency information gathered, with the current model, EKF setup and Forward
Euler disctiztation, an upper limit of 1000Hz is proposed to guarantee successful parameter predic-
tions (with parameter prediction errors at maximum ∼7%).
5.3. Realistic Simulations
The main conclusion to be derived from the realistic simulation cases is that predictions of small
changes in the parameters will require more precise tuning and will be more prone to error and noise,
if compared to bigger changes. Unfortunately, for most real loudspeaker scenarios, the changes are
not going to be as great as the maximum cas and thus a better tuning method seems necessary.
Still, with that said, the EKF has potential to correctly identify those parameter changes.
Also, it is great to see that the simultaneous predictions of multiple parameters does not seem to
impact the results in these cases.
5.4. Measurements and Measurement-like Simulations
The real measurements cases were more complex to be dealt with (as one may expect), but they
showed the real potential of the method.
It is to be noted that the since no ground truth for the parameter variation is available for the mea-
surements, there is no guarantee that the EKF isn’t using the degrees of freedom given by the free
Re and K0 to compensate for errors in the model. However, considering the successful prediction
from the measurements, the great results of the lowpassed simulation and how close the parame-
ter estimates were to theoretical expectations, it can be concluded that the EKF can be indeed a
powerful tool that can be used for time-varying parameter prediction in the nonlinear loudspeaker
model.
It is interesting to reflect on why the increase of R lead to the optimal solution by using the framework
of understanding of the EKF built during this paper. Increasing the measurement uncertainty informs
that the EKF that the measurements are to be considered less trustworthy that the model and less
weight has to be given to them in the predictions. This puts more stress on the model to estimate
the parameters correctly. At first it seems pretty unintuitive: since the goal is to tune the model by
the variation in the measurements, why should the measurements be trusted less? Why instead the
model, which has no information on these changes, is relied more upon?
These are not such easy questions and no certain solution was found. It can be guessed that this is
the an effective strategy considering that that the parameters are not measured directly but can only
be estimated indirectly though the model. By having more emphasis on the model, the EKF may be
lead to tweak the model significantly to match the measurements. Even if the measurement data is
used less, it is not ignored and the model needs to be appropriately changed (with the time-varying
parameters) to match that.
Lastly, the lowpassed measurement prediction failed for the simple reason that it is incorrect to
(linearly) filter all measurements in a nonlinear system such as the loudspeaker. The nonlinearities of
the system produce additional harmonics above the content of the input signal which are apparently
necessary for correct estimations. Cutting this data restrains the EKF from proper state information,
thus resulting in incorrect estimations.

6
Conclusion
The study investigated the use of the Extended Kalman Filter (EKF) for time-varying parameter esti-
mation in the context of a nonlinear loudspeaker model. The goal was to demonstrate how predict-
ing time-varying parameters through the EKF can improve the accuracy of the model compared to
using only time-invariant parameters.
A theoretical framework for the loudspeaker model was laid out suggesting possible parameter vari-
ations in DC resistance and suspension stiffness. The Kalman Filter and Extended Kalman Filter
were explained including a mathematical and an intuitive understanding of its workings, especially
considering the uncertainty matrices. The method for predicting time-varying parameters through
the EKF was also illustrated.
Real measurements on a loudspeaker were used as the basis for the research. Time-invariant pa-
rameters were indeed fitted to this data. The software implementation, EKF setup, and list of exper-
iments were exposed, as well as detailing the limitations of the study.
Several different simulations were initially performed to demonstrate the effect of the EKF setup
on the predictive results. Uncertainties, input type, input level and simulated measurement error
were carefully analyzed against a baseline case. Afterward, simulations of realistic and maximum
parameter variations for Re and K0 were conducted. The real measurements were also included in
the EKF prediction analysis, with emulative simulations confirming the findings of such experiments.
The results of these experiments were shown through NRSME(%) error tables and figures highlight-
ing the parameter prediction outcomes.
The main focus of the discussion revolved around uncertainties, frequency content of the input sig-
nal, parameter variation level and measurements strategies for prediction. The main findings of the
study are summarized as follows.
The proportionality between the uncertainties of measurements (R) and the model (Q) is crucial for
the performance of the EKF. The absolute values of these uncertainties often matter less compared
to their relationship. If R > Q, the measurements have more weight in predictions, and if Q > R, the
model has more influence. However, excessively high uncertainties can result in noisy predictions.
Indeed, is often necessary to further tune the entries of Q relative to the time-varying parameters to
improve the quality of the predictions.
The analysis of different frequency cases of the input signal provided useful insights into the behav-
ior of the EKF.
The failure of predictions for the 10 Hz sinewave is attributed to the lower local signal-to-noise ratio
at the in-phase zero-crossings of the current. The EKF struggles to balance process noise and mea-
surement noise in the absence of significant signal information. Furthermore, the fast and periodic
oscillations in Re make the system more nonlinear, which in turn distorts the results.

6.1. Future Work
45
Also, as demonstrated by the simulations at and slightly above the resonant frequency, a better local
SNR justifies the improvement of the predictions
Higher frequencies pose important challenges for the EKF due to linearization and discretization
errors, as well as the nature of the loudspeaker system. It was found that in the current setup, 1000
Hz is roughly the upper limit to maintain coherent predictions. If the input signal contains frequen-
cies after this limit, Re may be estimated with a downward shift, even towards the illogical negative
region.
Predictions of small changes in parameters require precise tuning of the uncertainties and are more
prone to error and noise compared to larger changes. The EKF shows potential for correctly iden-
tifying parameter changes, but further tuning methods may be necessary for real loudspeaker sce-
narios. Simultaneous predictions of multiple parameters do not significantly impact the results.
Real measurements demonstrated the real potential of the EKF for time-varying parameter predic-
tion in nonlinear loudspeaker models. Although no ground truth for parameter variation is available
in the measurements, the EKF produces successful predictions, closely matching theoretical ex-
pectations. The increase of measurement uncertainty (R) leads to optimal solutions by emphasizing
the reliance on the model to estimate the parameters correctly. The reasons behind this strategy are
not definitively determined but could be attributed to the indirect estimation of parameters through
the model and the need for the model to be adjusted to match measurements.
In conclusion, this paper provided valuable insights into the application of the EKF for time-varying
parameter prediction in nonlinear loudspeaker models.
The observations and conclusions drawn from the research findings suggest areas for further inves-
tigation (discussed in the next chapter) and highlight both the potential and limitations of the EKF in
this context.
Those who would like to proceed in the research can be aware that uncertainties tuning, signal SNRs,
and high frequency content (due to linearization and discretization erros) are the main considera-
tions and sources of issues for the EKF setup for loudspeakers.
6.1. Future Work
It is the author’s opinion that the most essential next step to this research is to develop an optimized
version of the EKF script in a fast language like C/C++. This allows to have a much faster running
code that allows for uncertainties matrix optimization, fast testing, and statistical analysis.
It is possible to have the EKF running in almost real-time, as shown for example in a STM32 embed-
ded system in [9]. The challenges of this task are dealing with matrices in a non-scientific-oriented
languages like C/C++ and fast matrix inversion. While the author would have liked to embrace such
a task, it was impossible due to the insufficient time within the thesis duration.
Following that, a better discretization scheme (such as Runge-Kutta) should be tested to improve
upon the high frequency performance. Also, the Unscented Kalman filter, which in theory can allow
for a higher degree of nonlinearities, may improve upon some of the linearization issues.
Other models can also be considered besides the loudspeaker one, perhaps even when more than
two parameters are simultaneously time-variant. For example, this method can be used to track
changes in model parameters when rotating knobs on analog audio devices such as guitar pedals
or amplifiers.
