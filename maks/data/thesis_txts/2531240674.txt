Abstract
The wind turbine simulation tool QBlade has recently been expanded by an in-
house coupling of the lifting line free vortex wake method to a structural solver.
This method, its rigid aerodynamic version as well as the rigid basic BEM im-
plementation was validated through simulations of the IEA-10 MW rotor. The
gathered results were compared to diﬀerent ﬁdelity simulations of the aeroelastic
tool HAWC2, including the CFD code EllipSys3D. Moreover, since this work was
done within the framework of the SmartTip project, the inﬂuence of several tip
extensions on the loads were simulated and compared to the benchmark results.
The rigid lifting line code was proven to produce accurate results that can be
situated between the NW and MIRAS codes from an accuracy point of view, with
room for improvement in the induction calculation at high tip speed ratio conditions.
The inﬂuence of the tip extensions on the loads were modeled accurately. The BEM
code underpredicted loads compared to higher ﬁdelity codes. Furthermore, the inﬂu-
ence of the tip extensions was found to be one magnitude below higher ﬁdelity codes.
The new aeroelastic method yielded accurate results for loads that were projected
into in plane direction and therefore power production. In out of plane direction,
stronger dissimilarities to the benchmark were noted. Additionally, the downwind
bend tip extension (Dihedral Tip 05) was found to have a stronger inﬂuence in
QBlade compared to the HAWC2 methods. Nonetheless, the aeroelastic code
generally delivered reliable results for the investigated baseline rotor and the rotors
with tip extensions.

Zusammenfassung
Das Windkraftanlagen-Design-und-Simulationstool QBlade wurde kürzlich durch
eine Kopplung des aerodynamischen lifting-line basierten Lösers mit einem Struk-
turlöser erweitert. Der Rotor der IEA-10 MW Referenzturbine wurde mit diesem
Code sowie dessen rein aerodynamischer Version und der BEM Implementierung
in QBlade simuliert. Die Ergebnisse wurde mit denen von verschiedenen ﬁdelity
HAWC2-Simulationen verglichen und validiert. Da diese Arbeit im Rahmen des
Projektes SmartTip statt fand, wurde außerdem der Einﬂuss unterschiedlicher
Tip-Erweiterungen auf die Lasten simuliert und mit den Benchmark-Simulationen
verglichen.
Der rein aerodynamische lifting-line Löser lieferte Ergebnisse, die in der Genauigkeit
ziwschen den NW und MIRAS codes angesidelt werden können. Die Berechnung
der Induktion bei Situationen mit hohen Schnelllaufzahlen lässt dennoch Raum für
Verbesserungen. Der Einﬂuss der Tip-Erweiterungen auf die Lasten in Tangential-
und Normalrichtung wurden gut abgebildet. Die BEM Methode resultierte in nied-
rigeren Lasten als die aufwendigeren Simulationsmethoden. Außerdem schätzte sie
den Einﬂuss der Tip-Erweiterungen um eine Größenordnung geringer ein als die
übrigen Methoden.
Der neue aeroelastische Löser lieferte präzise Ergebnisse für die zu der Leistung
beitragenden Tangentialkräfte. Die in Normalrichtung wirkenden Kräfte hingegen
wiesen größere Unterschiede zu den Benchmarksimulationen auf. Darüber hinaus
konnten für Tip 05, eine leeseitig gekrümmte Tip-Erweiterung, größere Einﬂüsse auf
die Lasten als bei den HAWC2 Methoden festgestellt werden. Nichtsdestotrotz konn-
te festgestellt werden, dass der neue aeroelastische Code grundsätzlich zuverlässige
Ergebnisse für den untersuchten Rotor mit den verschiedenen Tip-Erweiterungen
lieferte.

1. Introduction
Thematic Overview
Denmark set itself an ambitious target by transforming its energy sector with the
goal to be independent of fossil fuels by 2050. An intermediate target has been
set to a total market share of 55% of renewables in 2030 [1]. With the further
commitment of France and Germany to increase their renewable electricity target to
40% and 65% by 2030 respectively, a signiﬁcant expansion of the existing resources
in wind and solar energy may be expected in Europe [2].
In the case of Germany however, subventions for a crucial amount of wind turbines
will run out in 2021 [3], weakening their position in a competitive energy market.
Consequently, further investment in Research and Development (R&D) will be
necessary to lower the cost per unit energy, so wind energy can stabilize and
strengthen its role in the energy market in the future.
According to the International Energy Agency (IEA), larger rotor diameters and
higher hub heights are necessary in order to increase power production and decrease
the costs per unit energy [4]. While upscaling brings the upside that power scales
to the power of two to the rotor diameter, mass does so as well, however to the
power of three [5]. Therefore, high loads caused by gravity push the materials that
are used in wind turbines to its limits.
The more accurate a wind turbine can be modeled, the more potential to reduce
investment costs in expensive materials can be exploited. Moreover, the number
of iterations in the long-lasting design process of wind turbines could be reduced.
Therefore, the ability to predict the acting forces and loads on a wind turbine over
its lifetime as accurate as possible will become even more important. This data
could be gathered in open ﬁeld measurements. Yet, the manufacturing of prototypes
is expensive and time consuming. Furthermore, open ﬁeld measurements depend on
environmental conditions that are hard to reproduce. Wind tunnel measurements
oﬀer the ability to measure in reproducible conditions. Nonetheless, wind turbines
have to be scaled down considerably in order to ﬁt into a test section. This results
in problems to achieve realistic Reynolds numbers. Additionally, realistic wind
conditions like turbulence and the shear layer are hard to recreate in a wind tunnel
set up [6]. Therefore, simulation tools that make use of diﬀerent ﬁdelity models to

This work has the aim to benchmark simulations of a newly developed aeroelastic

QBlade
The wind turbine simulation tool QBlade was developed as an open source frame-
work at TU Berlin. It can be used for the simulation and design of Horizontal
Axis Wind Turbines (HAWT) as well as Vertical Axis Wind Turbines (VAWT).
Simulations can be performed either by using a BEM algorithm or a lifting line
based free wake vortex algorithm. While the BEM tool relies on the coupling to the
open source structural wind turbine simulation code FAST [7] to run aeroelastic
simulations, the lifting line algorithm is coupled directly to an in-house structural
algorithm, which is based on the CHRONO libraries[8][9][10]. The latter coupling
has been improved during this work and will be published in the near future.
HAWC2
The simulation tool HAWC2 was developed by the Aeroelastic Design Research
Program at DTU Wind Energy, DTU Risø Campus in order to calculate wind
turbine response in time domain. The tool has been used in various projects, both
for research and industrial purposes. Due to its continuing development, wind
turbines can be simulated with diﬀerent level of ﬁdelity aerodynamics. A detailed
BEM model that includes additional corrections provides a basic, although due to
the corrections, quite accurate and cost eﬀective simulation model. Additionally, the
higher ﬁdelity aeroelastic algorithm MIRAS, which is based on a lifting line vortex
method, has been added to HAWC2. The highest ﬁdelity algorithm represents
a structure-ﬂuid interaction coupling to the CFD code EllipSys3D. Besides the
several aerodynamic models, a multi-body formulation that can handle multiple
degrees of freedom has been integrated. In combination with the aerodynamic
codes, a wind turbine can be analyzed from an aeroelastic point of view. Along with
HAWT and VAWT, multiple rotor wind turbines can be analyzed. Furthermore,
the tool is able to simulate oﬀshore and ﬂoating wind turbines [11].

SmartTip
The project SmartTip focuses on the design of innovative blade tips for wind
turbines that can be retroﬁtted to an existing rotor. The designs are innovative in
the sense, that their goal is to increase the annual energy production (AEP) up
to 8%, without the need to reinforce the existing blade structurally. Four wing
extensions with diﬀerent geometric properties are simulated in this work. The full
description of the project is provided in [12].
Tip 01 is a blade extension that basically continues the blade’s curvature. Tip 05 is
a dihedral tip extension that is bent in downwind direction. Tip 21 is a backwards
swept tip extension and Tip 25 is a combination of the previous two. The added
tips increase the size of the rotor by about 5%. The geometries are shown in

2. Theory
2.1. Aerodynamic Basics
2.1.1. 2-D Aerodynamics
A fundamental element that inﬂuences the aerodynamic behavior of a wind turbine
blade, is the airfoil. According to [13], airfoils can be described as ’more or less
ﬂat surfaces producing or [...] experiencing lift in a direction essentially normal to
their plane’. As modern wind turbine blades are long and slender structures, the
ﬂow around them may be assumed to be two-dimensional. This is due to its much
higher chordwise velocity component compared to the spanwise component. When
two-dimensional ﬂow is assumed, the ﬂow around an airfoil can be described as
shown in ﬁgure 2.1 [14].
Figure 2.1.: Forces on an airfoil section of an inﬁnite wing [14]
The drag force D is deﬁned as the force acting in the same direction as the incoming
wind speed. The lift force L acts normal to the drag. By applying simple vector
addition, the resulting force F can be determined. Lastly, the moment M, that
usually is computed around the quarter chord point has to be mentioned [14]. Lift,
drag and moment can be expressed in a non dimensional manner by the lift, drag
and moment coeﬃcients Cl, Cd, Cm that are deﬁned as:

2.1.2. 3-D Aerodynamics
Two-dimensional aerodynamics provide a good understanding about the ﬂow around
a singular airfoil section, it however is only applicable to inﬁnite wings or certain
setups in wind tunnel testing. In nature, wings usually consist of a ﬁnite span,
making the assumption of two-dimensional ﬂow invalid. Lift is created due to the
pressure diﬀerence between the lower and upper side of a wind turbine blade. The
diﬀerence in pressure leads to a ﬂow around the tip edge that brings air from the
pressure to the suction side, creating a strong tip vortex [13]. As a result, the
streamlines that pass over the wing will be pulled inwards, while the streamlines
under the wing will be pushed outwards. Consequently, the streamlines spanwise
velocity points in opposing directions when they eventually meet at the trailing
edge. Subsequently, a continuous sheet of streamwise vorticity, known as trailing
vortices, is created behind the wing [14].
A commonly known theory to describe the ongoing phenomenons on a wing, when
exposed to a ﬂow (at small angles of attack is the lifting line theory. The ﬂow
past an airfoil can - according to the theory - be modeled by a vortex ﬁlament of
strength Γ. The airfoils lift is then described by the Kutta-Joukowski equation [14].
L = ρvtot × Γ

According to the Helmholtz theorem a vortex can not discontinue in a ﬂuid.
Subsequently, the bound vortex must end in two trailing vortices building the
Helmholtz’s horseshoe vortex. Since the vorticity Γ has to be constant along a
vortex line, a wing is modelled accurately when a series of multiple horseshoe
vortices is applied. This is due to the lift distribution of high lift in the inner
regions and lower lift at the outer regions [15].

2.2. Winglets on Wind Turbines
2.2.1. Working Principle
Diﬀerent approaches to reduce the tip vortex can be taken in order to lower the
induced drag that comes with it. The conventional way is to increase the wings
aspect ratio by distributing the produced lift on a larger surface[15, 16]. This
method is limited by structural properties as the root bending moment (RBM)
increases by the length of the wing. Another possibility is to manipulate the tip
vortex with devices acting at the tip of the wing, such as wing tip sails and winglets.
A summary about the several methodologies to minimize the induced drag can
be found in [16]. Furthermore, an interesting solution is provided in nature. The
feathers at the wing tip of some birds are spread out vertically and horizontally to
slotted wing tips that act like winglets [17].
The aviation industry adapted winglets in diﬀerent conﬁgurations as a consequence
of the oil crisis. An optimized glide ratio3 led to savings in fuel cost and CO2
emissions [18].
In wind energy the advantages of a winglet at the end of a wind turbine blade have
been investigated in several studies [19, 20, 21, 22, 23]. Optimizing the tip region
of a wind turbine blade holds huge potential, since it produces the most energy
and noise [12]. Yet, the beneﬁts of an increased power coeﬃcient come to cost of
an also increased thrust coeﬃcient [24], causing challenges in maximum loads and
tower clearance.
Gaunaa describes the basic working principle of a winglet in [22]. According to
him, the idea behind winglets is to reduce the spanwise ﬂow along the blade
and therefore lower the induced drag. On non-rotating systems this basically is
achieved through diﬀusing and moving the tip vortex away from the main wing
plane. On a rotating system, the load can be extended onto the winglet, enabling
higher bound circulation on the outer main wing and thus increasing the power
production. Additionally, the already mentioned beneﬁt of moving the tip vortex
away of the main wing comes into play. Yet, one has to diﬀerentiate between
upwind and downwind winglet conﬁgurations. The downwind winglet shifts the tip
vortex downstream from the rotor plane, reducing downwash and hence also the

induced drag. The upwind winglet brings the vorticity upstream from the rotor,
which results in reduced axial velocities for the main wing and thereby less power.
When the power production of a winglet itself is considered, the downwind winglet
contributes negatively to the turbines power. For the upwind case this phenomenon
is reversed. Therefore, both conﬁgurations have contradicting inﬂuences on power
that cancel each other out if a simpliﬁed model with an inﬁnite number of blades
(no tip eﬀects) is applied. Theoretically, it therefore does not matter if an up- or
downstream winglet is added. As a conclusion, the winglets positive impact comes
from a stronger reduction of tip-eﬀects compared to the increase in viscous drag
from the winglet [22].
2.2.2. Retroﬁt Winglets
Winglets hold an important advantage against other, more complicated methods
that reduce the induced drag, since they oﬀer the possibility to be added to an
already existing wind turbine. Theoretically, a turbines AEP can be increased by a
substantial amount if a winglet is retroﬁtted. However,the addition of large, heavy
winglets leads to signiﬁcant blade root bending moments, making an expensive
structural reinforcement indispensable [20].
Therefore, the challenge in retroﬁtting a winglet lies in balancing an improved
rotor performance with an increased root bending moment that does not require
structural changes to the main blade. A design philosophy for retroﬁt winglets that
balances the centrifugal and aerodynamic normal force that is generated by the
winglet, while still improving CP by an acceptable amount of 2.45% is introduced
in [20].
2.3. Aeroelasticity
Aeroelasticity describes the interaction between aerodynamic, elastic and inertia
forces that act on an elastic body when it is exposed to a ﬂow ﬁeld. In the case
of a wind turbine, the control forces may be added to that group, since pitch and
generator control inﬂuence its stability considerably. When the aerodynamic forces
act on the structure, they cause a deformation. That will result in a change in the
aerodynamic characteristics of e.g. the wind turbine blade, what again will have
an inﬂuence to its deformation [25].
The ongoing trend to larger rotor diameters [28] increases the importance to predict
a wind turbine’s aeroelastic behavior accurately, since the blade’s edge- and ﬂapwise
instabilities become more inherent as the radius increases [25].

Blade Element Momentum Theory
The Blade Element Momentum (BEM) method is still used in most aerodynamic
simulation tools and is therefore the basis for the development of current wind
turbines. Its ability to calculate aerodynamic loads in an incomparably inexpensive
manner is the main reason for that [9]. While the method may have low requirements
regarding computational resources, its physical representability is below higher

tip and root corrections are necessary in order to provide reliable results. In the
following, the BEM algorithm is brieﬂy described. For a more detailed description
of the algorithm, the reader is referred to [14].

MIRAS
The Method for Interactive Rotor Aerodynamic Simulations (MIRAS) is a three-
dimensional viscous-inviscid solver for HAWT computations. The code couples an
inviscid part to a viscous boundary layer solver. The former consists of a panel
method, that uses a surface distribution of quadrilateral sources and doublets. The
latter is a s solver, that accounts for the viscous eﬀects at the vicinity of the blade
surface, such as Coriolis and centrifugal forces [38, 39]. Instead of the panel method
the MIRAS results that are presented in this study were computed using the lifting
line module, also used in [40], that relies on polar data for drag and lift.
Further development led to the usage of a hybrid ﬁlament-mesh vortex method, the
hybrid particle mesh. It is a computationally more eﬃcient method that resolves
the wake of a wind turbine with fewer data points [39]. Since the data used in the
present paper were calculated with the hybrid particle mesh version of MIRAS, a
brief description of the methodology is presented in the following.
The hybrid particle mesh divides the wake into a near wake and a far-wake region.
In the near wake, the vorticity is shed at the trailing edge of the blade in form of
quadrilateral doublet panels in order to satisfy the Kutta condition. Behind this
row, the quadrilateral doublets are transformed into straight-line vortex ﬁlaments
that are grouped into vortex ring elements, formed by interconnected ﬁlaments.
Lagrangian ﬁlament markers xi that are placed at their end points describe the
ﬁlaments motion. By the calculation of the total velocity at the ﬁlament markers
u(xi) (see equation 2.25) the trajectory of the ﬁlaments can be observed.

Near Wake
The near wake model (NW) is positioned between the BEM and lifting line based
codes from a computational cost point of view. It can be seen as a hybrid code
that couples the lifting line approach in the near wake region with a BEM model
in the far wake [41].
The costly part of wake calculations is the computation of the induced velocity
in the rotor plane. As described previously, this is achieved by solving the Biot-
Savart law. In order to improve performance, the NW method avoids this step
by assuming that the trailing vortices follow a circular arc in the rotor plane, in
which the computation is limited to a quarter rotation. The decreasing induced
velocity vΓ a vortex element causes on the blade, the further it moves downstream,
is approximated by exponential functions for that quarter rotation. Beddoes work
in [42] is the base to the later optimized approximation proposed by Pirrung in [43].
Furthermore, the far-wake region has to be accounted for as well when the induction
is computed. Therefore, a modiﬁed BEM model is connected to the near wake,
so the total induced velocity at the blade is computed as the sum of the induced
velocity contributed by each region (see equation 2.26).
vΓ = vFW + vNW
(2.26)
where vFW is the induced velocity component contributed by the far-wake and
vNW the one from the near wake, which is calculated by the approximation of the
Biot-Savart law [41]. For the former component a BEM model with reduced thrust
is used (in the data shown below the BEM model that is implemented in HAWC2
was used). The BEM model is modiﬁed in two aspects. As the near wake method
takes care of the tip eﬀects, they don’t need to be computed by the BEM code. In
addition, the thrust coeﬃcient is reduced by a coupling factor kFW as the induction
caused by the near wake has to be taken into account. The BEM model therefore
computes the induction using the corrected thrust coeﬃcient [41, 43].

2.3.2. Structural Models
Structural models represent the second part of aeroelastic codes and are equally
as important. In this section the structural models that are implemented in the
aeroelastic simulation codes are described brieﬂy.
QBlade Structural Dynamics Model
The open-source multi-physics library Project Chrono4 [8] serves as the basis to
QBlade’s FEA solver. The turbine model consists of Euler Bernoulli beams, that
are related in a co-rotational formulation [48] to each other. A beam consists of
several rigid beam elements, that inhere a ﬂoating coordinate system. Subsequently,
the total deformation of a beam can be calculated by the addition of the beam
element movement [9].
The turbine’s structural model consists of multiple body-objects, that are connected
via constraints to one another. Usually, a turbine is assembled by four bodies, one
for each blade and one for the tower, where the blades are attached via a revolute
constraint to the nacelle node and the tower via a ﬁxed joint to the ground [9].
The structural properties of every body is deﬁned via a text input ﬁle, where the
fractional mass, the stiﬀness and the centers of elasticity, of mass and of shear as
well as further information is included in a tabulated form. Generally the structure
of the body input ﬁles in the utilized QBlade version is inspired by the HAWC2
format, described in [49].
HAWC2 Structural Model
HAWC2 uses a structural model that is similar to the one in QBlade. It is also based
on a multibody formulation that uses the ﬂoating frame of reference approach [50].
The wind turbines structure is again divided into several bodies, that consist of
a number of linear Timoshenko beam elements. The wind turbine consists of
assembled bodies that are interconnected via constraints [51].

2.3.3. Coupling Methods
In order to combine the aerodynamic and structural simulation, which is necessary
for aeroelastic simulations, a coupling method has to be implemented. Coupling
methods may be categorized into two main categories, the strong coupling method
and the weak coupling method. The former solves coupled equations directly, which
leaves all variables updated at all times, while the latter solves each physical model
separately and transfers the data between the solvers5 [52].
QBlade Coupling Method
Aeroelastic simulations in QBlade are performed with a loose coupling method
between the aerodynamic and structural model [9]. Hence, both solvers run through
their algorithm successively and transfer the information as an input to the other.
In the beginning of a time step t = t0, the aerodynamic model iterates, until
a converged bound circulation distribution prevails and the aerodynamic forces
can be computed. Subsequently, the forces are interpolated onto the mesh of the
structural turbine model. Furthermore, if preprocessed, the controller signals are
applied to the controllers and the structural model is advanced by a (structural)
time step6, t = t0 + ∆ts until the simulation time reaches t = t0 + ∆t. In this case
the geometry of the turbine is updated and advanced by ∆t. In the ﬁnal step,
the wake is convected and circulation is assigned to the added wake elements. A
complete description of the coupling method is provided by Marten in [9].
HAWC2 Coupling Method
To the knowledge of the author, there has not been a paper where the coupling
method in HAWC2 is described in detail. However, the coupling scheme that is
implemented in the HAWC2 code is supposed to be a strong coupling method.

demonstrate the nonability in the BEM code to model the tangential loads accu-

behind the the non swept regions. This induces a local up-wash, which increases
the local angle of attack and subsequently the loads. Due to the two-dimensional
assumption in the BEM code this phenomenon is not resolved.
Generally, the three lifting line based methods model the ﬂow around complex tip
geometries accurately. The largest diﬀerences to the CFD results were present at
high wind speeds, where the blade was pitched by approximately 18.5◦.
Especially with regard to the swept blades the alignment of the aerodynamic
coordinate system was found to have a strong inﬂuence on the normal loads. In
simulations that were performed in the process of this work, they were ﬁrst aligned
so they pointed into normal direction to the pitching axis. Those results showed
strong discrepancies between QLLT and both HAWC2 lifting line based codes
MIRAS and NW for Tip 21 and Tip 25. After a re-alignment of the coordinate
system in normal direction to the lifting line, a signiﬁcant improvement of the
results could be achieved. Figure 4.14 demonstrates the result for the normal loads

5. Conclusion and Summary
During the course of this work the IEA-10 MW reference turbine with the addition
of four wing extensions was simulated in the aeroelastic simulation tool QBlade.
The results were compared to several ﬁdelity simulations, including the CFD code
EllipSys3D of the HAWC2 tool. Compared were rigid aerodynamic results of the
rather basic BEM code and a higher ﬁdelity lifting line free wake vortex method
that are implemented in QBlade. Moreover, results of a newly coupled in-house
aeroelastic simulation code, are compared to diﬀerent HAWC2 codes.
The rigid aerodynamic simulations imply the largest diﬀerences at high tip speed
ratio conditions. The axial induction and its calculation pose a strongly impact
on the angle of attack and therefore the loads on the blade. The QBlade lifting
line replicated the loads of the CFD benchmark simulation for medium and low
tip speed ratios and overestimated them for high tip speed ratios in the mid span
region. A detailed comparison to the NW code that is accurate in the mid span
region for high tip speed ratios illustrated an underestimation of the axial induction
by the QBlade lifting line. The MIRAS lifting line code is more accurate in high tip
speed ratio conditions and therefore demonstrates further room for improvement
in QBlade’s lifting line. The QBlade BEM is the only code to underestimate power
compared to the CFD benchmark below rated wind speed. An overestimation of
the induced velocities in the inner third of the span is the cause for that. Ad-
ditionally, strong diﬀerences between QBlade and HAWC2 were revealed in the
drag coeﬃcient. QBlade utilizes the exact drag coeﬃcient listed in the tabulated
polar data. HAWC2 reduces the tabulated drag coeﬃcient through the UAM model.
A detailed analysis of the loads at the vicinity of the tip showed promising results
for the QBlade lifting line. The inﬂuence of a swept, a dihedral and a combined
tip extension in reference to a basic extension was presented. All major trends
in the tangential loads were modeled as accurate by QBlade’s lifting line as by
MIRAS or NW. In the out of plane loads, larger diﬀerences for the tip extensions
with a swept component can be pointed out between QBlade, MIRAS and NW.
Nevertheless, the principle inﬂuence of each tip extension is modeled accurately.
The alignment of the local coordinate system could be singled out to have a major
inﬂuence on the normal force in QBlade. A positioning normal to the pitching
axis lead to erroneous results. An alignment normal to the lifting line brought

strong improvement. The poly-grid BEM displayed ﬂaws in the ability to model
the tangential force correctly for high tip speed ratios for both tip extensions with
a swept component.
A controller was implemented for the aeroelastic simulation. Since the assigned
pitch angle diﬀers depending on the method above rated wind speed, a fair compari-
son is limited to slower wind speeds. The generator power indicates good accordance
in the in plane loads between MIRAS, CFD and the lifting line. Notwithstanding,
the thrust force reveals an overprediction by QBlade’s lifting line compared to
the benchmark. The corresponding parameters reﬂect this trend: the in plane
tip deﬂection and root bending moment reach close values for all compared codes.
The out of plane tip deﬂection and root bending moment are overpredicted as a
consequence of the higher thrust force. The torsional root bending moment implies
a diﬀerent deﬁnition of the reference point for the moment coeﬃcient between
QBlade and HAWC2.
The aeroelastic simulations of the tip extension rotors conﬁrm the previous obser-
vations, as they displayed the strongest discord between QBlade’s lifting line and
the HAWC2 methods at high tip speed ratios in the out of plane root bending
moment. Yet, the in plane root bending moment and generator power demonstrate
comparable results. MIRAS and QLLT show similar trends close to rated wind
speed to the benchmark CFD result. Tip 05, the dihedral tip, is an exception as
QBlade displays an underprediction compared to MIRAS (and CFD).
The main observations that were found in this work are that the BEM implementa-
tion in QBlade underpredicts power and thrust at high tip speed ratio conditions.
The strongest deﬁciencies to the benchmark simulation were present in the inner
third of the span. The inﬂuence of complex tip extension geometries was predicted
by one magnitude below the other methods.
QBlade’s lifting line code has been analyzed comprehensively with regard to rigid
and aeroelastic simulations of the IEA-10 MW reference turbine. Diﬀerent ﬁdelity
simulations of the aeroelastic simulation tool HAWC2 served as a benchmark.
Despite the distinctive nature of the set up structure among the tools, accurate
results were obtained. Generally, the results were situated in between the ones of
the NW and MIRAS code. The largest ﬂaws were found in the induction calculation
for high tip speed ratios, the thrust force of aeroelastic simulations and the results
of the dihedral Tip 05 in the aeroelastic case.
In following investigations, a comparison of the NW, MIRAS and QBlade lifting
line codes in unsteady conditions should provide further interesting information
about the newly coupled aeroelastic simulation code in QBlade.
