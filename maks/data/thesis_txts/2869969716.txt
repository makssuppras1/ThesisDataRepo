Abstract 
The decarbonization of industrial processes is a critical step toward achieving global climate 
goals. The dairy industry, characterized by its substantial thermal energy demands for heating and 
cooling, presents a significant opportunity for electrification. This thesis investigates the integra-
tion of flexible thermal pathways in fluid milk processing, focusing on the role of heat pumps and 
thermal energy storage in optimizing energy efficiency and reducing operating costs. 
The study explores multiple system configurations, comparing air-source heat pumps (ASHP) 
and internal heat pumps (INTHP) under different operational scenarios. A computational model 
is developed to evaluate the annual operating costs of each configuration, incorporating variations 
in ambient temperature, electricity prices, and system performance. Pinch analysis is employed 
to identify heat recovery potential, while thermodynamic modelling and optimization techniques 
assess the benefits of integrating thermal energy storage. 
Results indicate that configurations combining ASHPs and INTHPs can significantly reduce re-
liance on external heating and cooling utilities, improving overall system efficiency. Including 
thermal energy storage further enhances cost-effectiveness by enabling load shifting, minimizing 
peak electricity consumption, and reducing the number of system start-ups. The findings demon-
strate that optimized thermal management strategies can lead to lower operating costs while main-
taining process reliability. 
The research contributes to the ongoing transition toward sustainable dairy processing by using 
heat pump technology and energy storage solutions. The outcomes offer valuable insights for 
industrial stakeholders seeking to implement low-carbon strategies while ensuring economic vi-
ability.

1. Introduction 
1.1 
Motivation 
In 2023, global energy-related CO₂ emissions increased by 1.1%, reaching a record high of 37.4 gigatons.1 This 
rise highlights the challenge posed by the global reliance on fossil fuels for energy production. Despite ongoing 
efforts to transition toward more sustainable systems, electricity and heat generation remain heavily dependent 
on carbon-intensive sources, and current projections suggest emissions may peak by the middle of this decade.2 
In this context, electrification emerges as a strategy to reduce fossil fuel dependence, enhance energy efficiency, 
and align with international climate commitments. 
According to the International Energy Agency, the industrial sector accounts for 37% of total global energy 
consumption,3 making it a critical area for intervention. Given that a significant share of this energy is lost as 
waste heat, accelerating the adoption of Power-to-Heat technologies and advancing electrification are essential 
steps toward achieving zero emissions by 2035. The urgency of this shift is underscored by projections that 
industrial heat consumption will increase by 17% (+20 EJ) by 2030.4 Despite this growth, renewable heat is 
expected to cover less than half of the increase, with its share in industrial heat supply slowly rising to only 
16%. This gap highlights the need to accelerate the deployment of electrification, energy-efficient solutions, 
and storage technologies to meet rising industrial energy demands. 
Heat pump technology offers a promising solution for addressing this challenge. By capturing and upgrading 
low-grade waste heat, heat pumps reduce primary energy consumption while supporting the broader transition 
toward electrification. In parallel, energy storage solutions are becoming increasingly vital as the integration of 
renewable energy sources expands. Managing the intermittency of solar and wind power is essential to  
maintaining grid stability and ensuring a reliable energy supply. Thermal energy storage (TES) plays a  
particularly significant role in this transition by allowing surplus heat from renewable sources or recovered 
waste heat to be stored and utilized when needed, thereby enhancing the efficiency and flexibility of industrial 
energy systems. 
Within this broader industrial landscape, the food sector presents a particularly relevant opportunity, consuming 
approximately 30% of global energy, over 38% of which is used for food manufacturing and distribution.5 The 
dairy industry stands out within this sector due to its high energy demand for heating and cooling processes. 
The reliance on various energy sources, such as steam, hot water, compressed air, chilled water, and electricity, 
makes it an ideal candidate for the integration of heat pump technology, and thermal energy storage solutions.

1.2 
Objectives 
This project aims to identify flexible electrification solutions for utility systems in fluid milk production within 
the dairy sector. By enhancing load-shifting capabilities, the project aims to contribute to a more secure and 
cost-efficient energy system. To achieve this main goal, the following objectives have been defined: 
- 
Analysis and characterization of the fluid milk production process: conduct a detailed analysis of the 
production process, examining and evaluating each unit operation to understand its specific functions 
and technical requirements. 
- 
Thermal demand assessment: quantify the specific heating and cooling demands across the production 
process and select the unit operation with the highest demand. 
- 
Utility system analysis: develop case scenarios exploring how different utility configurations can satisfy 
thermal needs and assess the potential integration of storage solutions. 
- 
Model development and scenario analysis: build a model to calculate the annual operating costs of the 
defined scenarios and incorporate flexibility measures, including storage options. The model will  
optimize the utility system to enhance flexibility and minimize costs. 
 
1.3 
Method of approach 
The project will examine thermal utility options to satisfy the thermal demand for fluid milk pasteurization. 
Figure 1 provides an overview of the two main scenarios defined. In case 1, the heating demand is met by an 
air-source heat pump, while a separate cooling system covers the cooling demand. Case 2 addresses the heating 
demand using both an air-source heat pump and an internal heat pump, with the internal heat pump also  
supplying the cooling demand. 
 
Figure 1. Overview for case 1 (top) and case 2 (bottom) 
To provide operational flexibility and reduce operating costs, storage is incorporated into the system across 
three scenarios based on cases 1 and 2. In case 1A, the air-source heat pump and the refrigeration system from 
case 1 are maintained, and storage is added to both units. In case 2A, the air-source heat pump is equipped with 
storage and in case 2B storage is integrated with the internal heat pump.

2. Theoretical background 
2.1 
Dairy sector 
The dairy industry refers to the processing and manufacturing of raw milk into various products such as fluid 
milk, yogurt, ice cream, butter, cheese, and desserts.  
From a dietary perspective, dairy products are invaluable due to their nutrient density, offering energy, high-
quality protein, and essential micronutrients such as calcium, magnesium, selenium, riboflavin, and vitamins 
B5 and B12. These nutrients play an essential role in reducing hunger and combating malnutrition, particularly 
among vulnerable groups such as pregnant women and children. Moreover, milk consumption has been linked 
to a lower risk of non-communicable diseases, including osteoporosis, potentially colorectal cancer, and type 2 
diabetes.6,7 At a global level, milk contributes on average 134 kcal of energy per capita per day, 8.3 g of protein 
per capita per day, and 7.6 g of fat per capita, or 5%, 10%, and 9% to the global supply of energy, protein, and 
fat respectively. As the fifth largest provider of energy and the third largest provider of protein and fat in human 
diets, milk's nutritional benefits make it a product of high demand worldwide.6 
In addition to their dietary importance, the energy-intensive nature of dairy production highlights the industry's 
significant resource requirements. The food industry is responsible for approximately 30% of global energy 
consumption, with over 38% of this attributed to food manufacturing and distribution.5 Within the food sector, 
the dairy industry stands out as one of the most significant and sensitive sectors.8 One of the reasons is that this 
industry is characterized by substantial energy demands, particularly for heating and cooling processes integral 
to production, including drying, evaporation, fermentation, pasteurization, and cleaning operations.9 Table 1 
shows the energy consumption percentage for the different generic dairy products.  
As milk processing industries expand their production capacities, energy consumption in both existing and 
emerging facilities is expected to increase. In dairy plants, energy use primarily involves utilities such as steam, 
refrigeration, electricity, compressed air, and water. Water and steam serve as essential heat transfer mediums 
in dairy operations, with water consumption being notably high across most processes.10 These utilities must be 
heated to thermal energy levels ranging from 60°C to 200°C to meet the requirements of various heat treatment 
processes, such as pasteurization, sterilization, and spray drying.11

Fat-free or non-fat milk, also called skim milk, has had nearly all its fat content removed, leaving less than 
0.1%. Despite the fat reduction, it is still required to contain 8.25% of milk solids not fat (MSNF), ensuring 
consistency with other milk types. Fortification with vitamin A is mandatory for fat-free milk, whereas vitamin 
D fortification remains optional.13 Reduced-fat milk, in contrast, has undergone a process to remove part of its 
fat content, typically containing around 1.5% fat. Like whole milk, both varieties must include at least 8.25% 
MSNF, contributing to their texture, taste, and nutrient profile. While fortification with vitamin D is optional 
for these types, the addition of vitamin A is required by standard regulations. To be classified as such, whole 
milk must contain a minimum of 3.5% milk fat and at least 8.25% of MSNF. Although fortifying whole milk is 
optional, many varieties on the market are enriched with vitamins A and D for added nutritional benefits. 
On the other hand, cream has a fat content of 10% (ranging between 10% and 18%). To produce it, cream is 
often removed and separated from the non-fat portion also known as skim milk and then various amounts of 
skim milk are added back to the cream to reach the desired fat percentage.

Danish dairy market 
The manufacturing industry in Denmark is notably driven by food production, with key sectors including dairy, 
beverages, building materials, and pharmaceuticals.14 Among these, the dairy industry holds a particularly  
significant position, contributing 17.8% of Denmark’s total food export value in 2023.15 This highlights not 
only the economic importance of dairy products but also the sector’s integral role in shaping Denmark’s 
international trade profile. 
Denmark’s dairy industry is characterized by a highly efficient and technologically advanced agricultural  
system. With an average of 237 dairy cows per farm, one of the highest averages in the European Union, the 
country produces 5.8 billion kg of milk annually.16 This high level of productivity reflects Denmark's  
commitment to modern farming techniques, and resource optimization. Within this production, liquid milk 
products account for approximately 700 million kilograms each year. Of this, drinking milk products represent 
60%, indicating the significant domestic and export demand for fresh dairy consumption. Cheese production 
accounts for 497 million kilograms. The majority of this production, 455 million kilograms, is exported. Milk 
powder accounts for 234 million kilograms produced annually. Of this, 154 million kilograms are exported.17 
The core of Denmark’s dairy industry is Arla Foods, the world's eighth-largest dairy company. Arla anchors the 
sector with its expansive operations and state-of-the-art facilities across Denmark, producing a wide range of 
products, including drinking milk, cheese, milk powder, butter, and blended products. Alongside Arla, around 
30 smaller dairy companies contribute to the industry’s diversity, ranging from medium-sized cooperatives and 
privately-owned businesses to niche farm dairies specializing in small-scale, artisanal, or unique production.  
 
2.2 
Fluid milk production process 
The production of fluid milk combines the unit operations of clarification, separation, pasteurization, and  
homogenization. While the fat content of most raw milk is 4% or higher, the fat content in most beverage milk 
has been reduced to 3.5%. Lower fat alternatives, such as 1.5% fat, or skim milk (<0.1% fat) are also available 
in most markets. These products are either produced by partially skimming the whole milk, or by completely 
skimming it and then adding an appropriate amount of cream back to achieve the desired final fat content. 
Vitamins may be added to both full-fat and reduced-fat milk. Vitamins A and D (the fat-soluble ones) are often 
supplemented in the form of a water-soluble emulsion to offset that quantity lost in the fat separation process. 
Regarding the specific production process, milk reception and storage is the first step, which involves  
transporting raw milk from farms to processing plants, where it is kept in insulated stainless steel silos  
maintained at 4°C.18 This low-temperature environment is necessary to ensure the milk's freshness and quality 
before it undergoes further processing. The silos are equipped with agitators to prevent the natural separation 
of cream, and the milk undergoes rigorous quality checks, including assessments for bacterial contamination 
and antibiotic residues. After storage, milk undergoes standardization and pasteurization to ensure it meets 
safety and quality standards. During standardization, the milk is first separated into skim milk and cream, which 
are then blended in precise proportions to achieve the desired fat content.  
 
==PAGE:15==

Once standardized, the milk is pasteurized to eliminate pathogenic bacteria and extend the shelf life of the 
product without affecting quality. In simple terms, pasteurization is the process of heating milk to a specific 
temperature for a set period to kill harmful bacteria while keeping its taste and nutritional value intact. Minimum 
temperature and time requirements for milk pasteurization are based on thermal death time studies for the most 
heat-resistant pathogen found in milk, Coxiella burnettii.19  
 
Figure 2. Fluid milk production unit operations 
Often following pasteurization, a vacuum deaeration step is included to remove dissolved air, which can affect 
the milk’s flavour and aroma.20 Homogenization is the subsequent step, wherein the milk fat globules are  
reduced in size to create a uniform texture and prevent cream separation during storage.21 This process uses 
high-pressure homogenizers, typically operating at an outlet pressure of 20 MPa. An alternative approach is 
partial homogenization, in which only the cream undergoes this process, reducing energy usage while achieving 
similar results.22 
Cream processing follows a similar protocol to milk pasteurization but at a higher temperature. Excess cream 
is directed from the standardization process into separate processing streams, producing items such as heavy 
cream, light cream, and buttermilk. The next phase is filling and packaging. In this operation, milk and cream 
are packaged into containers suitable for retail. Milk is generally filled into high-density polyethylene (HDPE) 
gallon jugs produced on-site through blow molding, while cream is packaged in paper pint containers. Filling 
machines operate continuously to meet production demands, preparing the product for the distribution phase. 
Cold storage is crucial for maintaining product quality before distribution. The storage facilities, typically main-
tained at 1.8 – 3°C, house the packaged products until they are ready for transport. 
Pasteurization 
Figure 2 presents an overview of all unit operations in the process. Among these, pasteurization, deodorization, 
and storage have significant thermal demands, with pasteurization being the highest.23  This is reflected in Table

mild heat treatment. To enhance its effectiveness, pasteurization is often combined with other preservation 
methods, such as concentration, acidification, or chemical inhibition.  
Given its broad applicability across different food industries, pasteurization is particularly well-suited for

Energy integration and thermal analysis 
Pinch Analysis 
Pinch Analysis (PA) is a powerful thermodynamic tool used to analyze heat flows and evaluate the potential for 
heat exchange within processes. Initially developed as a conceptual optimization method for energy conserva-
tion in process industries, PA has since been extended to address a variety of production planning challenges. 
By employing graphical and numerical techniques, Pinch analysis evaluates individual process units or entire 
plants to identify opportunities for resource recovery.23,32 Within a system that requires energy input (Qi),  
including both heating (Qh) and cooling (Qc), heat can be effectively transferred from higher-temperature 
streams to lower-temperature streams through internal heat recovery, optimizing energy efficiency and  
minimizing external utility demands. 
Composite curves are Q-T diagrams, constructed by plotting the cumulative enthalpy for the hot and cold 
streams. These representations are used to visualize the heat transfer potential between the hot and cold 
streams,33 presenting both the heat source and heat sink profiles. The superposition of the source (hot CC) and 
sink (cold CC) curves shows the Minimum Energy Requirement (MER) for both hot and cold streams. As 
Figure 3 shows, the hot streams add together the enthalpy contributions and the cold streams the requirements. 
 
Figure 3. Hot and cold composite curves 
PA utilizes the minimum temperature approach (ΔTmin) as a key decision variable. The minimum temperature 
difference is a critical parameter in Pinch Analysis that defines the smallest allowable temperature gap between 
hot and cold streams in a heat exchanger. Under ideal conditions, ΔTmin would be 0ºC, however, this is only 
theoretical because it would require a heat exchanger of an infinite transfer surface.34 This parameter directly 
influences the design of the Heat Exchanger Network (HEN) by balancing the trade-off between investment 
cost (heat exchangers) and operating cost (energy).35 A lower ΔTmin allows for more heat recovery but requires 
larger heat exchangers, increasing capital costs.36

Furthermore, the minimum temperature difference between the hot composite curve and the cold composite 
curve defines the pinch point. A pinch occurs when the vertical distance between the hot and cold composite 
curves equals the ΔTmin. As the figure shows, the pinch point divides the system into two thermodynamic  
regions, above the pinch and below the pinch. The first mentioned will require external heat and the second one 
will require external cooling. The pinch temperature is central to efficient heat integration, including the  
appropriate incorporation of heat pumps. Heat pumps can recover low-grade heat below the pinch and provide 
heat above it, with the shape of the Grand Composite Curve (GCC) and the pinch position guiding their inte-
gration and design.37 The maximum heat recovery (Qi), and the corresponding minimum external heating (Qh)

The cascade coupling of the heat flows is shown in the GCC which represents the excess heat available to a 
process within each temperature interval. GCC is a very important tool for studying the interface between the

2.5 
Principles of a heat pump system  
A heat pump is a device that transfers heat from one or more low-temperature sources to one or more high-
temperature sinks simultaneously, utilizing an external energy source. It is designed to move thermal energy 
against the natural direction of heat flow by extracting heat from a cooler space and releasing it into a warmer 
space. Currently, heat pumps can be classified according to various criteria, including 1) according to heat pump 
functions (heating, cooling, heating, and cooling, heating water), 2) the type of heat source (ground, water, air, 
solar power), 3) the kind of energy supplied (thermally driven HPs, mechanical-compressor HPs,  
electric-thermoelectric HPs), and 4) according to their purposes (residential/commercial buildings, district  
heating, industry).41 Regarding the type of heat source, air-source heat pumps (ASHP) are the most widely used 
since ambient air is free and widely available.42 The amount of electricity required by ASHPs depends on the 
air temperature, as this affects its efficiency and capacity to deliver the necessary heating output.  
 
Figure 5. Cycle configuration of a one-stage heat pump process (left) and the principle of a T-Q diagram (right) 43 
As Figure 5 shows, the heat pump process comprises an evaporator (state 4-1) in which the heat from the heat 
source is added, a compressor (state 2-1) in which the refrigerant is compressed from the evaporation to the 
condensing pressure, a condenser (state 2-3) where the heat is rejected to the heat sink and an expansion (state 
3-4) where the pressure is lowered from the condensing to the evaporation pressure.  
The figure shows a heat load-temperature diagram (Q-T) on the right side. This illustrates the temperature  
differences between the external sink and source streams, and the internal condensation and evaporation 
streams. As seen, a minimum temperature difference is applied between both the condensation and the heat sink 
and between the evaporation and heat source. This minimum temperature difference will be referred to as the 
pinch point temperature difference, ΔTmin, and is applied to ensure a finite heat transfer area. Consequently, a 
low value of ΔTmin indicates a good heat exchanger performance and vice versa.

There are three primary strategies to enhance the performance of a heat pump under specific external conditions. 
The first involves improving the efficiency of individual components, such as optimizing heat transfer in the 
heat exchangers or increasing the compressor's efficiency.44 The second approach focuses on adapting the heat

rejection pressure in a transcritical cycle based on external conditions. Despite these improvements, there is an 
upper limit to the heat pump's performance. External conditions dictate this maximum efficiency, typically  
defined by the ideal refrigerant cycles of Carnot or Lorenz, depending on the application.45

The operation of large-scale heat pumps involves a start-up phase in which the heat pump operates in a transient 
state before reaching steady-state performance. This process typically takes several minutes, allowing the heat 
pump to safely adjust its load. A controlled start-up helps prevent condensation in the suction line during ramp-
down, which could otherwise lead to mechanical damage in the compressors.50 By doing so, the refrigerant 
remains in the vapor phase, ensuring stable performance. Additionally, pressure differences and temperature 
gradients during shutoffs can cause refrigerant migration, leading to efficiency losses.51,52

temperatures improve start-up performance, the system remains less efficient during this transient phase, with

Principles of a refrigeration plant 
When the cooling output is the desired effect, a refrigerator is the device that is used. This device will deliver 
the extracted heat at a low temperature.  
 
Figure 9. Configuration of a heat engine cycle (A) and a refrigeration cycle (B)55 
Figure 9A illustrates the basic configuration of a heat engine operating between two reservoirs to produce useful 
work. The circle shown between the reservoirs represents the heat engine, functioning in a cycle that exchanges 
heat with both reservoirs, while the specific internal processes remain undefined. Refrigeration, on the other 
hand, operates in the reverse manner compared to the heat engine. In this process, heat is extracted from the 
cold reservoir (or cold space) and transferred into the refrigeration cycle, which then expels it into the high-
temperature reservoir, typically the surrounding environment. Refrigeration is therefore defined as removing 
heat from an enclosed space or material to reduce its temperature. This requires an input of work to move the 
heat against its natural flow – from a colder reservoir to a warmer one.55 
The fundamental characteristics of a refrigeration cycle, or system, depend on the specific type of refrigeration 
system. Different systems vary based on how the cold surface is created and the type of fluid used to facilitate 
the cycle's processes. For instance, an air cycle refrigeration system employs air as the working fluid, utilizing 
the temperature changes that occur because of pressure variations within the cycle.

power supplied to the cycle. According to the second law of thermodynamics, the maximum achievable COP, 
often referred to as the Carnot COP, represents the theoretical efficiency of a thermodynamically ideal cycle 
without irreversibilities. However, in practice, achieving the Carnot COP is unrealistic due to unavoidable  
irreversibilities, such as those caused by finite-rate heat transfer. These factors limit the Carnot COP's  
practicality as a design target.56 For this study, similar to the heat pump thermodynamic principles discussed

2.7 
Principles of free coolers/dry coolers 
Free coolers are a type of cooling system that uses outside air to cool the process fluid in a heat exchanger. The 
hot process fluid, typically water or a water-glycol mixture, is circulated through a heat exchanger, commonly 
referred to as a coil, which is positioned outdoors. As the fluid flows through the coil, a fan directs ambient air 
across it, facilitating heat transfer from the process fluid to the air. This heat exchange is generally effective 
with a minimum temperature difference of approximately 5°C. Once cooled, the process fluid is pumped back 
into the facility to cool the intended equipment or process. The working fluid is separated from ambient air and 
the process is approximately similar to tube-type heat exchangers, and convective heat transfer has a major role 
in this method.57 
Dry coolers can deliver free cooling when the ambient temperature is low enough, relying solely on the  
electricity consumed by their fans, as they do not require a compressor to dissipate heat. This consumption is 
directly influenced by the required airflow speed over the cooling surface to ensure adequate heat transfer. Dry 
cooling towers require more space and are less efficient compared to other cooling systems. Additionally, they 
typically consume auxiliary power, which can have a negative impact on the overall efficiency of the plant.58 
 
2.8 
Principles of storage systems 
Energy storage systems can be categorized into three major groups depending on the nature of the energy 
stored,59 including mechanical (pumped hydro, compressed air, and flywheels), electrochemical (lithium-ion 
battery, vanadium flow battery, lead-acid battery, supercapacitors, hydrogen storage with fuel cells), and  
thermal energy storage (sensible heat, latent heat, and thermo-chemical) technologies. 
Thermal Energy Storage (TES) is a technology that stores thermal energy by heating or cooling a storage  
medium, allowing the stored energy to be utilized later for electricity generation through a heat engine cycle.60 
These systems are used for demand-side management, as they can help balance the mismatch between energy 
supply and demand by shifting electrical loads in both domestic and industrial sectors.61 The working principle 
is consistent across all applications: power is supplied to the TES during the charging phase, stored for a period, 
and later extracted during the discharging phase for use. The energy storage capacity depends on the type of 
storage material used, which varies based on the specific process requirements. Figure 10 illustrates the different 
types of TES.

Water tanks are one of the most popular technologies for STES systems. The charging and discharging process 
takes place by pumping hot/cold water directly into or out of the tank. Tanks used for thermal energy storage 
are typically constructed from stainless steel, concrete, or fiberglass and are insulated with materials such as

heat loss but also allows for a smaller tank volume.

The pressure in TES systems is typically at ambient levels, which limits the maximum water temperature to 
95ºC, resulting in low energy density. Short-term TES systems are designed to overcome this limitation at  
elevated pressures. This allows water to be stored at higher temperatures without reaching its boiling point.  
Pressurized water tanks, which serve as storage vessels in these systems, are specifically built to withstand 
increased internal pressures. By maintaining a higher pressure within the tank, the boiling point of water is 
raised, enabling it to store and retain thermal energy at temperatures beyond 100°C without phase change. 
Water in the tanks can be heated or cooled either directly or indirectly through a heat exchanger.68 While directly 
heated or cooled tanks are highly efficient for heat exchange, they often experience weak thermal stratification 
due to significant mixing. To address this, the internal structure of such tanks is often improved with features 
like baffle plates, diffusers, or membranes, which help maintain stratification and optimize performance.  
Thermal stratification occurs in a storage tank when hotter, less dense water rises to the top while cooler, denser 
water settles at the bottom due to density differences.  
Stable thermal stratification must be established and maintained within the water tank, meaning that mixing 
should be minimized to maximize energy retention. This ensures the formation of a distinct temperature  
gradient, or thermocline, between the hot water at the top and the cold water at the bottom. Thermal stratification 
within the tank can be achieved through several methods: (1) heating of vertical walls, which creates hot thermal 
boundary layers that draw hot fluid into the upper part of the tank; (2) heat exchange between the fluid in the 
tank and that circulating in a heat exchanger placed either inside or outside the tank; and (3) direct inlet into the 
tank of hot fluid at appropriate heights to maintain the layered temperature distribution.69 
The heat transfer principle inside the tank is provided by the methods described in Figure 11. Tanks with  
immersed coils typically show a nearly uniform, unstratified temperature distribution, which increases their 
cost. Despite this, they are commonly used in residential solar water heaters and space heating systems due to 
their separate water loops for consumption and storage. In contrast, external heat exchangers offer greater  
flexibility for maintaining thermal stratification. Among these, mantle heat exchangers are some of the most 
cost-effective options for improving thermal efficiency and enhancing stratification.

2.9 
Mathematical optimization  
Mathematical optimization is the process of identifying the best solution to a given problem by maximizing or 
minimizing an objective function while considering a set of constraints. It is widely used in decision-making 
across various fields, including engineering, economics, and energy systems, where resources must be allocated 
efficiently. An optimization problem consists of several key components: 
- 
Parameters: fixed numerical values that define the problem's structure, such as energy prices, system 
capacities, or efficiency factors. 
- 
Sets: collections of elements that define indices for decision variables, parameters, and constraints (e.g., 
time steps in a scheduling problem). 
- 
Decision variables: quantities that the optimization model seeks to determine, such as energy production 
levels or operational schedules. 
- 
Objective function: the mathematical expression that represents the goal of the optimization, whether it 
is to minimize costs, maximize efficiency, or optimize resource utilization. 
- 
Constraints: conditions that restrict the solution space, ensuring feasibility by incorporating physical, 
technical, or regulatory limitations. 
Optimization problems can be categorized as static or dynamic: 
- 
Static optimization: the problem is solved at a single point in time without considering future  
adjustments. 
- 
Dynamic optimization: the problem evolves over time, requiring continual adjustments based on  
changing conditions. 
The process of solving an optimization problem involves formulating the model, selecting an appropriate  
solution method (such as linear programming, nonlinear programming, or dynamic programming), and using 
computational algorithms to find the optimal solution. The complexity of the solution process depends on the 
nature of the objective function, constraints, and the structure of decision variables.

The following figure shows the monthly cost reductions for the ASHP under variable demand. Compared to 
Figure 42, which presented the same heat pump with fixed demand, the comparison focuses on the percentage 
decrease rather than the absolute values, as the demand load differs between scenarios. While the overall trend 
is similar, variable demand leads to greater percentage reductions in winter and smaller reductions in summer. 
One hypothesis behind this is that in winter, ambient temperatures are lower, reducing the COP and making 
heat production more expensive, regardless of the demand pattern. However, with variable demand, the system 
gains more flexibility by charging the storage during night hours, when demand is only 25% of the load,  
allowing it to store heat at lower costs and use it later during high-cost periods. In contrast, during summer, 
COP is already high, reducing the benefit of shifting operation through storage. Additionally, in summer, low 
nighttime demand limits the system’s ability to fully charge storage using cheap electricity, as the stored energy 
must align with the lower demand during nighttime discharge. As a result, the fixed demand scenario achieves 
greater cost reductions, as it provides more flexibility in timing storage charging and discharging to fully opti-
mize electricity price variations.

5. Future work 
The results of this study provide a strong foundation for understanding the economic and operational benefits 
of integrating heat pumps and thermal energy storage systems in dairy processing. However, several areas  
warrant further investigation to enhance the economic feasibility and technical optimization of these systems. 
One path for future research is conducting a more in-depth analysis of how to identify the switching temperature 
in Case 2. The current methodology successfully determined a suitable switching point between the air-source 
heat pump and the internal heat pump, but a more refined approach could implement a similar Exergy Pinch 
Analysis approach as proposed by Walmsley et al.79. Applying pocket-cutting strategies could help determine 
when switching between the ASHP and INTHP maximizes efficiency.  
Another key area is the study of investment costs associated with implementing storage systems. While this 
thesis primarily focused on minimizing operational costs, including capital expenditures in future analyses 
would provide a more comprehensive understanding of overall system viability. Research should assess whether 
systems that minimize operational costs still offer financial benefits when accounting for investment costs.  
Additionally, a closer look at how storage tank volume affects investment costs would be valuable. Larger 
storage volumes may lead to higher initial costs but diminishing returns in operational savings, and under-
standing this trade-off is essential for determining the most economically viable storage size. Optimizing the 
design of storage systems beyond capacity and volume should also be considered. Investigating thermal strati-
fication, heat loss minimization, and materials could enhance storage efficiency. 
Exploring thermal demand management in other dairy processes, such as milk powder production, also offers 
potential for future research. These processes are energy-intensive and could benefit from similar optimization 
strategies. Evaluating whether thermal storage solutions can minimize costs in these applications would help 
determine the broader applicability of the system improvements identified in this study. 
Further research should also focus on understanding the relationship between electricity prices and variable 
versus fixed demand patterns. This study showed that variable demand scenarios led to greater cost reductions 
during winter, likely due to the interaction between seasonal price fluctuations and storage utilization patterns. 
A deeper analysis could clarify why seasonal variations affect cost savings and help refine operational strategies.

6. Conclusions 
This study has demonstrated that electrifying the thermal processes in the dairy sector using air-source heat 
pumps, internal heat pumps, cooling system and thermal energy storage can significantly reduce operational 
costs and enhance system flexibility. 
The following points summarise the key findings of this thesis:  
- 
Pasteurization is the unit operation for the production of fluid milk with the highest thermal demand, 
which can be effectively addressed by two utility system configurations examined in this study: a com-
bination of an air-source heat pump and a cooling system, and a setup combining an air-source heat 
pump with an internal heat pump. 
 
- 
For the air-source heat pump, the study has explored how variations in ambient temperature affect the 
COP. The results confirm that the difference between the source and sink temperatures significantly 
influences heat pump efficiency. As the ambient temperature rises, the system experiences a smaller 
temperature lift, leading to improved COP and consequently reduced energy consumption during 
warmer periods. 
 
- 
The study defines a cooling system comprising a refrigeration plant and a free cooler, with ambient 
temperature as the key variable determining their operation. This dynamic strategy ensures thermody-
namic feasibility by switching between components based on external conditions. 
 
- 
The switching temperature governing the sink outlet for the ASHP and inlet for the INTHP has been 
identified. The findings indicate that refining the method to find the switching temperature could further 
improve system performance. 
 
- 
The configuration combining ASHP and INTHP resulted in lower operating costs compared to the 
ASHP and cooling system arrangement. This outcome highlights the cost-saving potential of utilizing 
waste heat through the internal heat pump. 
 
- 
The implementation of TES effectively enabled load shifting, allowing thermal energy storage for use 
during high electricity prices or periods of reduced COP due to lower ambient temperatures. This  
strategy reduces operational costs during peak pricing while minimizing system start-ups and improving 
operational stability. 
 
- 
The analysis of tank volumes indicates that larger volumes lead to lower operating costs, as increased 
storage capacity allows for smoother heat pump operation. The cost reductions from increasing volume 
from 6h to 12h were greater than those from 12h to 24h, likely because the optimization benefits were 
largely realized in the initial volume increase. 
 
- 
TES continues to reduce costs when the process operates under variable demand conditions. The  
optimization analysis shows that increased storage capacity allows the system to adapt efficiently by 
shifting energy consumption to off-peak periods.
