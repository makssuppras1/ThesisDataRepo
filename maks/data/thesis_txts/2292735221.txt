Summary
The present thesis consider the development of sandy spit systems. By numerical
studies the physical processes involved in the development is investigated and
the development of the well known Skagen Spit system is tried modelled in Mike
21 FM by DHI.
First the historical processes of the development of Skagen Spit is claried. The
development consist of 7 principle morphological units where both sea-level fall
and aeolian sediment transport is included. Only the later morphological unit
consisting of primary longshore sediment transport is considered.
An numerically academic study of spit developments from a hard coastal points
is carried out in a simplied case. By knowing the relation between the longshore
sediment transport and the incoming wave angle the progression angle of the
spit can be forecast as the transport rate at the initial coast and along the
spit will be equal. An analysis of the eect of the directional spreading of the
waves is carried out and a it is seen that the progression direction of the spit is
inuenced by the directional spreading. The eect is more a numerical problem
than an actual physical dependency as the numerical software is limiting some
wave directions.
The numerical software is including a lter algorithm that
soften out peaks in the sediment transport rates along the coastline in order to
avoid large spacial oscillations in the coastline. A study of the eect of changing
the lter option is carried out as well and also here a huge dependency is seen.
Last several models is made for the Skagen Spit development. Wave climate
from the past 35 years is used to create representative wave elds. The eects
on various wave directions and wave height is analysis together with the eect
of the sediment size and the slope of the coastal prole.

Chapter 1
Historical development of
the Skagen Sand Spit

1.1
Early development
Skagen Spit which form the northern most part of Jylland, Denmark, is a
complex coastal structure developed from seven principal morphological units,
namely The Skagen and Troldkær Spit systems, Damkær and Jerup strand
plains, Tversted lagoon, Tversted barrier and Gårdbo lagoon/lake (Nielsen and
Johannesen, 2009), see Figure 1.1. Over the past 12,000 years these units has
joined together and created the Skagen Spit as it is today.
As the ice coverage retreated at the latest ice age, the water level started raising
but due to glacial rebound the land surface was raised at a faster rate leading to a
relative sea-level fall. As a consequence submerged islands of glacial sedimentary
deposits started to emerge in front of the coastline, formed by the Zirfaea cli
(Figure 1.1), and formed the Råbjerg island and Tversted barrier. Doing shorter
periods where the land surface raise was balanced by the sea-level raise the
landscape was exposed to signicant wave action which enclosed the area and
formed Tversted lagoon. Sediment supply was now available at the seawards
side of Råbjerg island and Troldkær spit system started to evolve at a eastward

direction (Figure 1.2). This development together with additional land surface
raise created Gårdbo lagoon which later was enclosed and became Gårdbo lake
which is still exiting today. At 5,500 years BP Skagen Spit system started to
emerge at a northward direction from the western point of Troldkær Spit which
than came to a standstill due to lag of sediment supply. Over the past 5,500
years Skagen Spit system has progressed into Skagerak in a north/north-east
direction and formed the land as it is today.
1.2
Progression of Skagen Spit
From the anchor point at Troldkær spit system Skagen Spit has propagated
21 km into the ocean. In the period of the progression the rate of relative sea
level fall has been nearly constant which means the bed level of which the spit
has propagated upon has been more or less constant at 20 to 25 m at the front
of the spit, approx 5 m at the east side and approx 20 m at the west side with
depths up to 32 m in between (see cross-sections of the spit in Figure 1.3 and
a SW-NE length prole of the spit in Figure 1.4). With a progression starting
5,500 years BP an average growth of 3.8 m/yr is obtained and with a simple
assumptions approx 1 000 000 m3/yr of sediment is deposited in front of the spit
and 300 000 m3/yr is deposited on deeper water further out in the ocean. This is
in good agreement of the monitored longshore sediment transport rate at 900 000
to 1 550 000 m3/yr along the wast coast of Skagen Spit, (Nielsen and Johannesen,
2009).
The formation of the Skagen Spit is mainly governed by severer storms from west
and south-west generating longshore sediment transport in north-east direction
at the west side of the spit. The supply of sediment comes from submerged
sediment islands and erosion of the upstream coastlines along the west coast of
the spit and is transported partly in suspension and as travelling longshore 2D
and 3D sand dunes.
1.3
Development simplications
As described above the development of Skagen sand spit is complex and consist
of several physical processes. Due to limitations in the numerical software being
used and the capability of describe the development mathematically only the
development processes occurring due to the longshore sediment transport at
the west coast of the spit will be taken under consideration. When needed a
qualitative engineering judgement will be used to describe deviation in computed
results compared to the true development.

2.1.3
Surface stress
Surface stresses is generated by wind in area not covered by ice. Wind generated
stresses will not be used throughout this thesis so surface stresses will be zero
and will not be treated further.
2.1.4
Coriolis forcing
If chosen, Coriolis force can be neglected in the calculation which means f = 0.
Constant forcing is another choice and a reference latitude has to be dened so
f = 2Ωsin φref. The third option is to let the Coriolis force vary in the domain
and latitude in each element is than used.
2.1.5
Radiation stress
In the coupled model which is used here the radiations stresses is computed in
Spectral wave module and than used in the calculations in the hydrodynamic
module.
The computation of the radiation stresses as well as the iteration
process will be discussed later.
2.1.6
Numerical solution
As stated MIKE21 is a nite volume solver which means the domain is divided
into a mesh of a nite number of cells and the governing equations is then
solved in every cells. In the exible mesh model the domain can be divided into
arbitrarily polygons, but typically triangles is used as these gives and optimal
space representation, and the computation technique is here given for arbitrary
sized polygons.

2.1.7
Flood and drying
Following (Sleigh et al., 1998) the solution to the Riemann problem produce
unrealistic high velocities when the water depth is small. To accommodate this
problem a ooding/drying algorithm is implemented in order to remove dry cells
from the calculations and use reformulations for partial wet cells. The cell faces
is monitored to identify ooded boundaries as follow
A element face is ooded if:
 The water depth at one side of the face a is less than hdry and the water
depth at the other side is larger than hflood.
 The sum of the still water depth at the side where the depth is smaller
than hdry and the surface elevation at the other side must be larger than
zero.
A cell is considered dry, partial dry or wet as follow:
 A cell is dry if the water depth is smaller than hdry and no of the element
faces is ooded.
 A cell is partial dry if the water depth is larger than hdry and smaller than
hwet.
 A cell is partial dry if the water depth is less than hdry and at least one
of the faces is ooded.
 A cell is wet if the water depth is greater than hwet.
If a cell is dry it is left out of the calculation, if the cell is partial dry the
momentum uxes is set to zero and only the mass uxes are calculated. For wet
cells all equations is included in the calculations.

of the total erosion along the coastline along the western part of spit.

Chapter 6
Conclusion
The relation between the longshore transport and the incoming wave angle was
analysed for two wave heights with the numerical model and a good agreement
with the analytic solution was seen. By investigating the progression direction
of the computed spits a good agreement with the theoretical formulation was
seen.
The academic spit study included a study of the dependency of the directional
spreading of the wave eld. A clear dependency was seen as the progression
direction tend to increasingly deviate from the predicted as the directional wave
spectrum becomes wider. It turned out that the limiting wave directions im-
posed on the domain boundary had a major eect on the progression direction
but the directional spreading has physical eects on the width of the spit.
The eects on the spit progression by various morphological lter settings was
investigated and a huge eect was seen. No spit development was seen if the lter
was turned of and abnormal evolutions was seen for lter settings of N > 2.
For lter settings with N = 1 and N = 2 good results was seen. For lter
settings with N > 2 deposition of sediment was seen upstream of the coastal
corner leading to increasingly deposition and a coastline evolution in a coast
normal direction. It is believed that the limits for the lter settings of highly
inuenced by the hydrodynamic conditions, the mesh resolution and model time
step, among others, so no exact limits for the lter settings can be given, other
than the lter should be used with caution.

Conclusion
The Skagen Spit model with waves from western directions modelled a spit
progression in a too eastward direction making it clear that waves from eastern
directions could not be ignored. By including waves from eastern directions the
spit progression direction was more north but still too eastward. The eect on
waves coming from north was seen in some cases to increase the width of the
spit by making a south going sediment transport at the east side of the spit.
In other cases the tip of the spit was just turned in a east or south-eastern
direction.
The eect of the sediment grain size was investigated. Obviously the transport
rate decreased as the grain size increased leading to a slower progression rate of
the spit and the coarse sediment turned out to give better results compared to
the estimated transport rates occurring today.
The eect of the slope of the coastal prole was also investigated to see the eect
of using the more at prole from the west side of the spit where the primary
longshore sediment transport occurring. The eect was a signicant dierence
in spit progression primary for wave event W35 where nearly no progression was
seen for the at prole.

Appendix H
Morphological results for
the Skagen Spit model
In this appendix various results from the Skagen Spit model is presented. All
model is simulated in real time with the 1. of January 2000 as starting date.
For all models plot of the coastline evolution is presented for the dierent wave
climates. The plots consist of the evolution of the coastline during a single wave
event, where the gray line is the coastline position at the start of the given wave
event and the black line is the coastline position at the end of the wave event.
The plots is presented in chronological order. Thereafter plots of the longshore
transport rates is presented.
The longshore sediment transport is computed from the deposited sediment
volume as Eq. (3.6) with C = 0 is presented in order to include the eect of
the morphological lter. As C = 0 leads to a non consistent unit between the
plots only the shape of the graphs should be considered. The plots is presented
in chronological order and is extracted 25 days before the plots of the coastline
evolution presented above.
