Abstract
Mineral precipitation is detrimental to process plant operations in terms of reduction in operational
eﬃciency, performance of equipment, cleaning cost and replacement costs. In the dairy industry calcium
phosphates are one of the primary minerals responsible for the scaling of equipment. In membrane
based unit operations, calcium phosphates are responsible for blocking membrane pores and reducing
ﬂuxes.
Arla Foods Ingredients (AFI), Videbaek, reported the decrease in operating time, decrease in membrane
ﬂux as well as an increase in the amount of scaling on the membranes in their reverse osmosis (RO)
plant due to calcium phosphate deposits. Tis project evaluates the methods of removal of calcium
phosphates through precipitation from ultraﬁltration (UF) permeate prior to reverse osmosis at AFI.
Tree primary methods are investigated: ultrasonic irradiation, addition of sodium hydroxide and carbon
dioxide sparging.
Results show that from all the methods tested only the addition of NaOH was eﬀective in precipitating
calcium from the UF permeate. Te removal of calcium through precipitation by adding NaOH removed
upto 93.5% of the calcium from the solution. A membrane study was conducted to investigate the
changes in ﬂux of the RO membrane afer calcium was removed from the UF permeate. However, it
was found that the removal of calcium from the solution had no improvement on the ﬂux of the RO
membrane. Tis revealed that calcium has no impact as a foulant in clogging membrane pores. Lactose
which is a recoverable product from the RO plant was found to not degrade through either the ultrasonic
irradiation, addition of NaOH or the pressurized ﬁltration through the RO membrane. A hypothesis
of protein as a foulant in membrane clogging was tested by removing 50% of the proteins from the
ultraﬁltration permeates which had also no eﬀect on the ﬂux of the RO membrane. From a cleaning
perspective, the operating time on the RO can be extended by removing calcium and hence the costs to
remove calcium at AFI were calculated. Te approximate annual cost of the removal of calcium through
addition of NaOH prior to RO, was found to be 5.55 million DKK.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Background
Mineral precipitation can cause inorganic scale formation which negatively aﬀects the eﬃciency and
lifetime of industrial unit operations, for e.g. by blocking the pores of membranes during ﬁltration,
decreasing the purity of the product, lowering the heat transfer eﬃciency of heat exchangers or interfer-
ing with the performance of wastewater treatment agents. In turn, this leads to an increase of water
consumption on diﬀerent levels, for e.g. increased cleaning water requirements, increased quantities of
steam or cooling water for heat exchangers, reduced potential for cleaning water reuse etc. Te issue of
mineral precipitation poses a particular problem in the dairy industry where there is an abundance of
minerals present in the streams primarily calcium, magnesium, phosphorus and potassium [13],[12].
Trace quantities of selenium, and zinc are also presented. In particular the presence of calcium ions
causes problems due to ﬁrm solid scaling and deposition[12].
Some of the speciﬁc ways in which the foulants disrupt normal operation is described as follows. For
e.g. in the case of pipes and tubes, the ﬂuid ﬂow is blocked by the scales which can cause a signiﬁcant
decrease in the ﬂow rate of process streams, for a given pumping velocity[14]. In the case of constant ﬂow
rates, the reduction of the cross sectional area, results in a higher velocity, which creates an increased
pressure drop across the tube. In the case of heat exchangers, the mineral scales increase the resistance
to heat transfer, thereby causing not only an ineﬀective energy transfer but also an increase in the
energy demand[14]. In the case of membranes this can lead to blockage of membrane pores, lower ﬂux,
reduced operation time, decreased separation eﬃciency and increased use of chemicals for cleaning.
Milk as well as its other derivatives such as curd, whey, cheese etc, are obtained through natural sources.
Tey contain a variety of biomolecules such as proteins, faty acids and other sugars as well as inor-
ganic molecules such as minerals and salts. Scaling in dairy processing can therefore occur due to two
primary reasons[4]. Te ﬁrst reason is the deposition of minerals salts comprising sodium, calcium,
phosphate, magnesium, potassium and citrate ions and the second being the deposition of protein[4][15].

Case study description
Arla Foods Ingredients (AFI), Videbaek, Denmark, is one of the leading whey ingredient suppliers for

AFI is interested in investigating diﬀerent methods for eﬃcient and cost-eﬀective mitigation of mineral
precipitation in their process plant. Calcium phosphates were reported to be the primary foulants on the
RO membrane at AFI. In this study, the methods to remove Calcium salts from ultraﬁltration permeates

the subsequent clogging of the PLRec unit (series of reverse osmosis units for recovery of water and
lactose), will be prevented. Te samples provided are ultraﬁltration permeates wherein most of the
protein content has been removed, before being sent for Reverse Osmosis(RO). Terefore the main
fouling components in these streams are mineral elements, particularly calcium, thereby expounding
the need for the study.
Several techniques have been reported in literature for antiscaling and to prevent or control the scale
formation and deposition such as electronic anti-fouling[16], ultrasonic irradiation [17] and scale removal
using chemical solutions[18]. AFI is interested in methods of precipitation with a minimum amount of
chemical usage. Terefore, in this study there will be an emphasis on non-invasive methods for e.g. by
using an external equipment in which precipitation rate of minerals is accelerated and the resulting
precipitant can be easily removed. Some of the strategies may include:
• Heating or cooling
• Air sparging
• Ultrasonic irradiation using ultrasound horn
• Addition of a base
Te rate of precipitation is a crucial factor while precipitating the salts as the real-time process is
continuous and not batch. Terefore, the rate of precipitation should ideally be enhanced through the

not being present in small quantities, due to the large size of protein molecules.

2.1
Precipitation of Calcium Phosphates
In a chemical reaction, chemical equilibrium is the state in which both reactants and products are present
in concentrations which have no further tendency to change with time, so that there is no observable
change in the properties of the system. Te hydrolysis reaction of calcium phosphate is an equilibrium
reaction. Te Le Chˆatelier’s principle is the governing principle on how equilibrium reactions behave
with changes in reaction parameters such as temperature, pressure, concentration, eﬀect of catalyst
etc. In the following sections, the Le Chˆatelier’s principle and the eﬀects of the important reaction
parameters on the solubility of calcium phosphate are explained.

Eﬀects of pH
Te correlation between the solubility of calcium phosphate with pH can be explained by Le Chˆatelier’s
principle using the above equilibrium. According to the principle, if an acid were to be added to the
above system, the H+ ion from the acid would react with the OH– to produce water. To counter the
production of H2O, the equilibrium shifs to the right resulting in the ionic calcium ion. Adding a base
however creates the opposite eﬀect through generating excess of OH– ions which causes the equilibrium
to shif to the lef causing formation of calcium phosphate which precipitates.
2.1.3
Eﬀects of temperature
Tis diﬀerence in solubility behaviors with temperature can also be explained by this principle. When it
comes to the solubility there are two types of reactions namely endothermic reactions where enthalpy
of solvation is positive thereby absorbing energy and exothermic where the enthalpy of solvation is
negative thereby liberating energy. How the temperature aﬀects the solubility depends on the nature of
solvation. In the case of an exothermic reaction, the products are formed by liberating energy. Tere-
fore if the temperature is increased, the temperature gradient is reduced within the solution and this
facilitates the formation of the products. Te reverse happens in the case of an endothermic reaction
where increasing the temperature results in more formation of the reactant. Te hydrolysis of calcium
phosphate, is an endothermic reaction. Terefore, the solubility decreases with increase in temperature.
2.1.4
Eﬀects of Concentration
Changing the concentration of either the reactants or the products will shif the equilibrium to the side
that would reduce that change in concentration. If more water is added to the equilibrium system in
section 2.1.1, more of ionic calcium would be formed to negate the eﬀects of the added water. Terefore
diluting the solution increases the solubility.

Mineral composition of milk
In the dairy industry fouling by mineral precipitation on equipment surfaces is a major cause of concern.

and phosphates from casein micelles occurs only through dialysis of milk using an appropriate buﬀer,
suggesting a strong bond between proteins and calcium ions. Once the dialysed milk is centrifuged, the
free calcium and phosphate concentrations increase afer separating from the serum[20]. Te bonding of
calcium with citrate ions, is another reason for calcium phosphate to remain solubilized in milk despite
being above saturation [21]. Experiments by Whitier, show that the precipitation of calcium phosphate
did not occur in the presence of citrate[22].

3.4
Membrane fouling by proteins
According to Jeurnink et al. [4], the two main causes of fouling in dairy processing are:
• Mineral deposition
• Protein deposition
In the case of dairy processing, the most important category of proteins is whey protein. Whey proteins
consist of casein, β-lactoglobulin, α-lactalbumin as the primary proteins and bovine serum albumin
and immunoglobulins in smaller fractions [13]. Caseins are of the order of 25-35 kDa in size while
β-lactoglobulin is 18 kDa and α-lactalbumin is 14 kDa. In the case of RO membranes the pore size is
around 0.1-5000 nm[32] which is signiﬁcantly smaller than the size of the protein molecules. Tis causes
the proteins molecules to be retained on the membrane surface, which implies that most proteins are
likely to adhere to the surface of the membrane and cause fouling.
Unlike calcium phosphates that precipitate under alkaline conditions, proteins are solubilized under
these conditions and precipitate under acidic conditions. Te solubility values of whey proteins, which
are the primary class of proteins in milk, were studied by Pelegrine et al.[33]. Te solubility values
were found to be minimum at the pH value of 4.5, which is the isoelectric point [33]. Te range of
pH investigated in this study is between 3.5-7.8 [33]. Te solubility of whey proteins in the milk also
decreased as the temperature increased, which indicated that the protein denaturation occurred as
protein denaturation causes aggregation and eventual precipitation [33].

3.6
Conclusions from literature study
Based on the literature, precipitation of calcium salts through the use of ultrasonic irradiation, addition
of base and gas sparging have been studied, however the majority of these eﬀorts focus on precipitation
of other salts of calcium such calcium carbonate and calcium bicarbonate. Within the framework of
using ultrasonic irradiation and gas sparging for calcium precipitation, the sonication and sparging are
almost always performed on an alkaline supersaturated solution. Tis is important for precipitation
studies as alkaline supersaturated solutions can precipitate even in the absence of ultrasound irradiation
or gas sparging. Also in the studies pertaining to addition of alkalis to precipitate calcium phosphates,
most endeavours have been in the realm of laboratory synthesized solutions or simulated solutions.
Terefore this opens an opportunity to study the true behaviour of ultrasonic irradiation in inducing the
precipitation of calcium phosphate in industrial dairy streams without the inﬂuence of a supersaturated
alkaline medium. Tis is also an opportunity to investigate the inﬂuence of ultrasonic irradiation, gas
sparging and alkaline addition on natural milk derivatives. Tese objectives will be explored through
the course of the experimental studies.

Chapter 6
Conclusion
Te main goal of this project was to ﬁnd an eﬃcient method to remove calcium through precipitation
from the ultraﬁltrate permeate (UF2) of the PLRec plant at AFI, Videbaek in order to prevent fouling
on the surface of the RO membrane. Te assessment was done by considering three diﬀerent methods
namely, ultrasonic irradiation, sodium hydroxide addition and carbon dioxide sparging. A systematic
methodology was followed, starting with the application of the method on a synthetic solution of
calcium hydrogen phosphate, followed by the application of the method on the UF2 solution, followed
by veriﬁcation of ﬂux enhancement through calcium removal. Finally, an economic evaluation was done
to assess the feasibility of implementing the method.
Of the three methods tested to precipitate calcium from UF permeates, the ultrasonic irradiation and the
addition of NaOH successfully precipitated the synthetic calcium hydrogen phosphate solution whereas
only the addition of NaOH produced a precipitate in the UF2 solution. A possible hypothesis to explain
the precipitation using the ultrasonic irradiation in the synthetic calcium hydrogen phosphate solution
and not in the UF2 is that the ultrasonic irradiation could not induce precipitation but simply accelerate
the rate of precipitation. In order to verify this, a combination of ultrasonic irradiation and the addition
of NaOH was tested on the UF2 solution where it was revealed that ultrasonic irradiation had no eﬀect
on the rate of precipitation. Tis could be due to two possible reasons: (1) Ultrasonic irradiation had no
eﬀect on complex multi-ion solutions such as UF2, (2) Ultrasonic irradiation in general is not an eﬀective
tool to induce precipitation since calcium hydrogen phosphate was found to precipitate without an
external force. Carbon dioxide sparging was found to be ineﬀective on both the synthetic solution as
well as the UF2 in generating precipitation.
Te removal of calcium through precipitation by adding NaOH was eﬀective in removing upto 93.5% of
the calcium from the UF2 solution, which produced a ﬁltrate that is almost completely free of calcium.
Since the objective of the study was to remove calcium to improve membrane performance in the PLRec
plant, the ﬁltrate afer calcium removal was passed through an RO membrane at 30 bar for 10 hours and
the ﬂux is measured. Te removal of calcium from the UF2 (used as control) had no improvement on
the ﬂux. Tis revealed that calcium had no impact as a foulant in clogging membrane pores. However,
a decrease in the ﬂux over the 10 hour ﬁltration presented with another hypothesis that the fouling
50

of the membrane surface could be caused by proteins. In order to verify this, a UF membrane was
used to remove the proteins from the solution as it was expected that proteins were retained by UF
membranes. An analysis on the proteins showed that only about 50% of the proteins were retained
possibly because smaller peptide chains passed through the UF membrane. Te protein-reduced ﬁltrate
with lower concentration of protein molecules was passed through the RO membrane system under
the same conditions as the calcium-free solution and UF2 control solutions. Te ﬂux measurement
reported that a 50% reduction in protein concentration had no improvement on the ﬂux. Possibly, the
50% proteins remaining in UF ﬁltrate were large enough to foul the surface of the RO membrane.
An important conclusion was that the ultrasonic irradiation, addition of NaOH as well the pressurized

from the PLRec. Te concentration of lactose remained constant at a value of roughly 0.29 g/L under all

Chapter 7
Future work
Troughout this study, experimental protocols have been developed to study the rate of calcium pre-
cipitation using diﬀerent methods. During the course of the experimental work and based on the
nature of the results, a variety of questions were raised. In this section some suggestions for future
work are given in order to improve the overall performance and cost eﬀectiveness of RO operation at AFI.
One of the primary results obtained through the experimental work was that the removal of calcium
from the UF permeates provided by AFI, had no eﬀect of the RO membrane ﬂux. However, AFI reports
that calcium is indeed one of the primary foulants in their plant operations. On closer inspection many
diﬀerences were found between the operating procedure in the experiments and the operating procedure
at AFI. Te ﬁrst diﬀerence is the mode of ﬁltration of the membrane. Te laboratory experiments
were based on a batch operation using dead-end ﬁltration, while the operations at AFI are continuous
and use cross-ﬂow ﬁltration. Based on the experimental conditions, the membrane ﬂux observed was
signiﬁcantly lower afer 10 hours of operation, however AFI report 40 hours of operation per production
cycle. A study using continuous membrane operations in cross-ﬂow ﬁltration mode can help in coming
closer to the industrial scenario and providing more accurate results regarding the nature and cause of
membrane fouling.
Based on the information provided by AFI, 1.2 g/L or 0.12% of protein is present in the UF permeates.
Although this is a very small percentage in comparison to what is removed through UF, what remains
could be a possible cause for membrane fouling given the large size of the protein molecules. An-
other possible area of investigation could therefore be the elimination of proteins from UF permeates to
check for improvements in RO membrane ﬂux and to check if proteins are the cause of membrane fouling.
Currently AFI uses addition of HCl to the UF permeate streams to lower the pH and prevent calcium
precipitation during RO operation. Tis however shifs the problem of calcium precipitation from the
RO system to the eﬄuent treatment stage. Removal of calcium from the UF permeates using NaOH
addition, could remove the problem of calcium precipitation on the membrane but this is expensive.
Te advantages on the long run nonetheless, could be a reduction in the cleaning time and the cleaning
solutions required for the RO membrane, thereby giving a longer operating time for the RO. Potential

visible precipitation is observed however it is possible that the precipitation commenced earlier. In order
