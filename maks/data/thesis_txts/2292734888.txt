Abstract (English)
In this thesis analysis of a data set of 10579 student comments and rat-
ings of 2122 professors at American universities is performed. The thesis
has three goals: 1) Detect patterns within the comments. 2) Analyse
the relation between the comments and ratings. 3) Analyse if it possib-
le to predict ratings based on comments. The comments are modelled
using a Vector Space Model with binary weighting. The ratings are inte-
gers from 1 to 5, but are being treated as continuous. Latent Semantic
Analysis, Non-negative Matrix Factorization and the proposed Logistic
Non-negative Matrix Factorization is used for the unsupervised model-
ling, while Partial Least Squares and Random Forest are used for the
supervised modelling. It is found that the Latent Semantic Analysis and
Non-negative Matrix Factorization identiﬁes patterns addressing gene-
ral parts of the teaching, characterized by words expected in this kind
of data. The Logistic Non-negative Matrix Factorization is able to ﬁnd
patterns describing similar topics, but identiﬁes other and more speciﬁc
words. By the use of correlation it is found that none of the unsupervised
methods are able to describe the variation in the ratings. The Partial
Least Squares and Random Forest are both able to predict the ratings
from an independent test set with Mean Squared Errors of 1.27 and 1.20
respectively. Analysis of the supervised models shows that they are si-
milar. The models identiﬁes several adjectives to be the most important
for predicting the ratings. A comparison of the patterns found by the
unsupervised and the supervised methods shows that the two sets of pat-
terns have little relation to each other. The project contribute to the
analysis of open ended evaluations and may be used for the development
of an automated text based rating system.

Abstract (Danish)
I denne afhandling analyseres et datasæt bestående af 10579 studerendes
kommentarer og vurderinger af 2122 professorer på amerikanske universi-
teter. Afhandlingen har tre mål: 1) Detektere mønstre i kommentarerne.
2) Undersøge relationen mellem kommentarer og vurderinger. 3) Under-
søge om det er muligt at forudsige vurderinger baseret på kommentarer.
Kommentarerne modelleres ved hjælp af en "Vector Space Model"med bi-
nær vægtning. Vurderingerne er heltal mellem 1 og 5, men bliver behand-
let som kontinuerte. "Latent Semantic Analysis", "Non-negative Matrix
Factorization"og den foreslåede "Logistic Non-negative Matrix Factoriza-
tion"benyttes til den ikke-superviserede modellering, mens "Partial Least
Squares"og "Random Forest"benyttes til den superviserede modellering.
Metoderne "Latent Semantic Analysis"og "Non-negative Matrix Facto-
rization"identiﬁserede mønstre relateret til generelle dele af undervisnin-
gen, karakteriseret ved ord forventet i denne type data. Metoden "Logistic
Non-negative Matrix Factorization"er i stand til at ﬁnde mønstre, der be-
skriver lignende emner, men identiﬁcerer andre og mere speciﬁkke ord.
Ved brug af korrelation ses det, at ingen af de ikke-superviserede me-
toder er i stand til at beskrive variationen i vurderingerne. Metoderne
"Partial Least Squared"og "Random Forest"er begge i stand til at forud-
sige vurderingerne i et uafhængigt testsæt med "Mean Squares Errors"på

værende de vigtigste ved forudsigelse af vurderinger. En sammenligning
af mønstrene fundet af de ikke-superviserede og superviserede metoder
viser, at de to sæt af mønstre har ringe sammenhæng med hinanden.
Projektet bidrager til undersøgelse af åbne evalueringer og kan benyttes
i udviklingen af et automatiseret tekst baseret karaktergivningssystem.

Chapter 1
Introduction
In this chapter I will ﬁrst give a brief motivation for the project. After-
wards the problem formulation that forms the foundation of the project
is presented. Next the project plan, both initial and revised, is shown.
Finally a brief self evaluation of my execution and planning of project is
given.
1.1
Motivation
Mining of natural language is utilized when analysing large amounts of
text data, e.g. on social networks or the like. It can be used to spot
new political topics, or spotting trends and opinions in large popula-
tions. These types of analysis are usually performed using unsupervised
methods to model the data, as the data typically doesn’t contain any re-
sponse. Many of these methods are described in [Pau12], and are closely
related to methods from chemometrics. A diﬀerent class of methods are
the semi-supervised methods. These methods model text data relative to
how text data is usually distributed. One of these methods is the Likey
[PNPH08, PH10].
Another application of text mining is mentioned in the email from Jacob
Kogan found in Appendix B. He describes that, the result from an analy-
sis of comments of teachers/professors could be used as an objective tool
==PAGE:16==
2
Introduction
for deciding whether or not a teacher/professor should be promoted or
get a raise in salary. A tool like this could also be used as an agent for
resolving disputes between the university (or other educational institu-
tions) and a candidate for promotion. This would be an advantage for
both the university and the candidate: The candidate would save time
and money, since the dispute wouldn’t have to go through a court of law.
The university would save money for lawyers. Furthermore the university
would avoid the risk of loosing in the court of law, and thereby get their
reputation damaged. This could possibly damage the university in two
ways: A damaged reputation could result in fewer applications from stu-
dents, and it could become harder to attract highly regarded researchers.
Sliusarenko performed an analysis of student evaluations of the courses
at DTU [Sli13]. Her data were labelled with both course and the speciﬁc
question the students were answering, thus enabling her to analyse the
individual question separately.
1.2
Problem formulation
In this project a dataset consisting of student comments and ratings of
professors will be analysed.
The purpose of the project is to answer
the following questions: Is it possible to detect patterns in the data,
that increases the understanding of the data? Is it possible to extract
keywords and keyphrases from the data that explains the variation in the
ratings? Is it possible to predict a rating based on a comment?
1.3
Project plan
An initial project plan was produced within the ﬁrst month of the project.
This plan was the basis of the work conducted the ﬁrst period, while
getting familiar with the data. While executing the project, the plan was
revised, as the idea for a new formulation of the Non-negative Matrix
Factorization came up. The idea was published at KDIR 2015 in the
paper found in Appendix C. The executed plan therefore contains fewer
methods. Both versions of the project plan are found below.

1.4
Self evaluation
Before I began analysing the data, I was aware that I had included too
many methods in my planning. This mean that I was aware that I had
to skip some of the methods as I got familiar with the data. The initial
planning was therefore slightly optimistic, but it was on purpose.
During the project, I included much text on data handling, model and
methods in the weekly reports. Much of this is used in the ﬁnal thesis.
However, I do regret that I didn’t deﬁne my notation from the beginning,
as it changed slightly as time passed.
If I could change a decision, it would be that I based my choice of weight-
ing scheme solely on the correlation. This decision meant that I favoured
the supervised methods above the unsupervised methods.
I do think
that I got some interesting results from the unsupervised methods, but
a diﬀerent weighting scheme could perhaps improved them.

2.1
Data description
The data considered in this project consists of students comments and
ratings of 2122 professors. The data is collected at www.ourumd.com, a
home page where students at higher American education institutions can
rate their professors. In total 10584 comments where collected, however
5 of them have not ﬁlled in any comments and are therefore disregarded,
i.e. a total of 10579 comments are considered. They are distributed such
that each professor on average have received 4.99 comments, with the
maximum being 91 comments for Prof. B. Dixon.
The original data contained 42108 1-grams - see Section 3.1 for details
on 1-grams and n-grams. After pre-processing, removal of stop words
and stemming the data contain 9400 unique 1-grams - see details on pre-
processing in Section 2.2. Besides 1-grams, 2-grams are also considered
in this report, the data contain 177312 unique 2-grams. The resulting
Term-Document Matrices (TDM) are therefore of sizes 10579×9400 and

2.2
Data pre-processing
In order to do any analysis of the data, the data has to be cleaned. This
is done so that unnecessary noise doesn’t inﬂuence the analysis. Handling
of diﬀerent challenges in the pre-processing is described below.
Interpreter mistakes
When the data was downloaded, mistakes oc-
curred in the interpreter, leading to some mistakes in the comments.
The following errors are considered &amp; &acirc;ÿÿ &atilde; &copy;
&frac14; &gt; &lt; &nbsp; &quot; &shy; &sup3;. These are corrected
using the list of special HTML characters [Aus22]. Note that &acirc;ÿÿ
is considered the same as &acirc;, a conclusion made after looking up
several examples in the data.
Word contractions
Since the data is natural language, several con-
tractions occur. E.g. the following contractions n’t, ’re, ’ll, ’s, ’m, ’d,
have been replaced by not, are, will, is, am, would.
Misspellings
The data is collected at a public available website. Since
it is not everyone who excels at spelling, the data contain misspellings.
An issue when correcting a spelling mistake is that, while lots of lan-
guages contain word contractions, generally English does not. Examples
of this are "all right" and "school book" that are written as "allright" and
"schoolbook". Another issue when correcting a spelling mistake is that,
several words, when spelled wrong, can turn into the same word. An
example of this is "they’re" and "their" that can turn into "there". In
order to to avoid this, a list of unique terms was, after the preliminary
pre-processing, inspected manually using the LATEXdictionary to identify
spelling mistakes. The identiﬁed spelling mistakes were then found in
the raw data, and the most common "correct correction" were then used.
This procedure resulted in a list of 1180 words and their corrections. An
alternative to this procedure would be to use an automated correction
procedure. A downside of this could be that the words would not be
corrected correctly.
Spelling variants
Another issue concerning the spelling of words, is
the fact that some words can be spelled diﬀerently, e.g.
in American
English and British English.
Examples of this are colour and ﬂavour
(British) that also can be written as color and ﬂavor (American). In this

Professor names
The names of the professors occur in many com-
ments, typically only the last name is used, but in some cases the ﬁrst
name is also used. The last names are accessible in the separate ﬁle pro-
vided by Jacob Kogan. When a last name of a professor occurred in a

Course names
The names of the courses are often used in the com-
ments. In order to avoid a direct link between a speciﬁc course and a
subset of comments, the course names, e.g. math101, were replaced by
the tag course.
Numbers
All numbers were replaced by the tag num. Ordinals like
1st, 2nd, 3rd etc. were also replaced by the num tag. Time stamps like
1hr30min were replaced by num hour num minutes. The same procedure
was used for distance measures like 8ft, which is replaced by num feet.
Signs

are replaced by a white-space. Special considerations are made about the
exclamation mark, which is used to emphasize a comment, and therefore
relevant when analysing this dataset. For this reason, the exclamation
mark is considered its own word. Similar actions could have been done
for the question mark, but are not done in this project.
Stop words
A list of common stop words was acquired from the DTU
course 02450 - Introduction To Machine Learning and Data Mining by
Morten Mørup. This list has been examined, and the following words
have been removed from the list best, good, great, name, serious, thor-
ough, thoroughly, since they were considered relevant when analysing the
data.
Stemming
Or lemmatization is the process of reducing diﬀerent in-
ﬂicted forms of a word to their common root form. E.g. "walk", "walk-
ing" and "walked" are all reduced to "walk". Besides reducing the words
to their root form, they are also reduced to the form in which they are

pronounced. An example of this is "lazy" that is reduced to "lazi", which
ensures that it has the same root as "laziness". Lovins created the ﬁrst
stemmer [BL68].
Later Porter developed a popular stemmer [Por80],
which was used in this project.
The above described "contaminations" (except stemming and stop word

• Implement solution
• Clean data
Since this process could be repeated for a large number of times, the
process was stopped when no serious problems were present and the data
was considered ready for analysis.

Chapter 3
Methods
In order to analyse the comments and ratings, a proper model has to
be established. In this project the comments are modelled using a vec-
tor space representation of each comment. Other ways of modelling the
comments could be to use a Suﬃx Tree [Nem14]. After establishing a
model for the data, the data is analysed using two types of methods:

3.2.1
Cross Validation
Many methods has been developed to determine the optimal model com-
plexity, one of the most popular is Cross Validation (CV). By ’optimal
model complexity’ is meant the model that not only describes the known
data, but will also generalize to an unknown future dataset. Complexity
typically refers to the number of parameters in the model - an example
where this is not directly the case is Ridge Regression, where the com-
plexity refers to the eﬀective degrees of freedom df(λ) [HTF09], while all
parameters are in the model. CV does this by estimating the average
generalization error by calculating the error given in Equation (3.17).
Cross Validation is carried out by randomly dividing the training set into
K equal sized folds, typical values for K is 5, 10 or M. A new training
set is created by leaving out one of the folds. The model is trained on
the training set and validated on the remaining data. This process is
repeated K times for each model complexity and the generalization error
is estimated by Equation (3.17), with ˆf −k(j)(xj,α) being the function
ﬁtted on the training set with the tuning parameter α, where the fold
containing the j’th data point is removed.
The special case of using
K = M, meaning that k(j) = j, is known by the name leave-one-out
(LOO) Cross Validation. After repeating Cross Validation for all values
of α, the value of α that minimizes the generalization error is chosen.

3.3
Choosing the class of method
The development of social media, "Internet of Things" and cheap sensors
mean that data are available in larger amounts than earlier, and origins
from a large variety of diﬀerent sources. Common for all kinds of data
is that it can be divided into two diﬀerent classes: data without a re-
sponse, consisting of the set {xi}, and data with a response (or multiple
responses, however, it does not matter in this context), consisting of the
set {xi,yi}. Figure 3.2 shows the typically way to handle data with and
without a response. A model of data without a response is called an
unsupervised model, and tries to model the probability of the data itself
p(X). A supervised model aims at modelling the response y as a func-
tion of the inputs X as y ≈f(X). Said in a diﬀerent way, the supervised
model models the probability of y given X p(y|X). When dealing with
both inputs and responses, the generalization of the data is often more
important than just good predictions. In order to do this, the inputs are
then ﬁrst modelled using an unsupervised method, say g(X). Then the
response is modelled using a supervised method with g(X) as inputs, i.e.
y ≈f(g(X)). Examples of this is Deep Belief Networks and Principal
Component Regression.
In this project only pure supervised and unsupervised methods is used.
The only method that can be interpreted as a combination of the two
is the Partial Least Squares, as it can be interpreted in terms of the
Singular Value Decomposition [Han10, §6.3.2] (note that Per Chrisitan
Hansen consider the Conjugate Gradient for Least Squares, but as this is
the same as Partial Least Squares [PdH02], the SVD expansion applies
to PLS as well).

Unsupervised methods
In this section 5 diﬀerent unsupervised methods are presented: The La-

3.4.1
Latent Semantic Analysis
Latent Semantic Analysis (LSA) is motivated by the Vector Space rep-
resentations inability to copy with two classic problems in Natural Lan-
guage Processing: Synonymy and Polysemy [DMRS09].
Synonymy is
the situation where two words can have the same meaning, this could
be lecturer and teacher. Since each of these words are captured in sep-
arate dimensions, the Vector Space Model will not capture this relation-
ship. Polysemy is the case where a word can have multiple meanings,
such as the word ’right’. The Vector Space representation will not be
able to catch this either, since it assumes that each word only have one
meaning. By assuming that the true structure of the data lies in a k-
dimensional subspace, LSA model such latent semantic associations by
using co-occurrences to describe the context of each word, and thereby
alleviate the issue of Synonymy and Polysemy. Another way of saying
this is that LSA assumes that there is a true latent structure of the data,
which is obscured by the generation process [Ros]. Another motivation
for LSA is the fact that term-document matrices tend to be very large
and sparse matrices. This means that the classical PCA is infeasible since
a centring of the matrix is infeasible due to memory limitations.
Mathematically LSA is formulated as ﬁnding the best rank k approx-
imation of the TDM X in terms of the spectral 2-norm - see Equa-
tion (3.22). The approximation is computed by using a truncated SVD

Bias and variance issues
One problem regarding trees is that a small
change in the data, can lead to a diﬀerent sequence of splits. This mean
that trees suﬀer from a high variance.
On the other hand, they are
having low bias. One method of dealing with this is Bagging, this method
creates B tree and then averages the prediction of all the trees. Each
tree is trained on a bootstrapped training set of the same size as the
original. Bagging has the advantage that a collection of trees have the
same bias as the individual trees. Furthermore, it lowers the variance.

Variable Importance
One way of interpreting a Random Forest is by
addressing the importance of each input variable. There are several dif-

recorded for each tree. Afterwards, the MSEP is recorded again, but with
the diﬀerence that each parameter is permuted individually. The diﬀer-
ence between the two are then average over for all trees, and normalized
by the standard deviation of the diﬀerence. The most important variable
is the variable that results in the largest change in MSEP.

Supervised methods
The comments and ratings are modelled using two diﬀerent supervised
methods: Partial Least Squares and Random Forest. The ratings are
being treated as continuous rather than ordinal. This is done for several
diﬀerent reasons, two of them are: By treating the ratings as continuous
it is modelled that e.g. 5 is larger than the rest of the ratings, rather
than just being another class.
Furthermore, it is not certain that all
students would give the same experience the rating. Hence, by treating
the ratings as ordinal, the labels would have a low signal to noise ratio.

Chapter 5
Discussion
In the following the results presented above are discussed. First the choice
of using a binary weighting scheme is discussed. Next the questions asked
in problem formulation are discussed individually. The discussion will be
based on the subjective titles given to the estimated components.
Choice of using a binary weighting scheme
The binary weighting
scheme of the Term-Document Matrix were chosen based on the correla-
tions between the 1-grams and the ratings. The performance of the unsu-
pervised methods were not taken into account when making this choice.
This was seen as a general trend in the unsupervised methods were that
they extracted components related to the frequency of occurrence of the
individual 1-grams. By including a global weight, this dominant pattern
would be eliminated and other interesting patterns may be found instead
for the unsupervised methods. The use of a global weight would not have
motivated the development of the Logistic NMF.
Is it possible to detect patterns in the data, that increases the
understanding of the data?
The feature described by the ﬁrst LSA
component was the most frequent occurring 1-grams, the component de-
scribed 16.40% of the variation in the data.
This was also the most
protruding pattern of the KL NMF component (see Appendix A.1) and
==PAGE:94==
80
Discussion
the 2nd LS NMF component. Even though they covered topics within
the data, the 3rd PLS component and ﬁrst LS NMF component had a
high correlation with the frequency of the 1-grams. Both the 3rd PLS
component and the 1st LS NMF component were related to the exam.
The 5th LSA component was concerned with the professor, but also cov-
ered topics like test and exam. Compared to the other methods, the Max
thresh NMF was able to identify other important 1-grams in the data,
these were speciﬁc about the exam form.
Common for LSA and LS NMF was that they described the data in
general terms and were not able to identify speciﬁc problems/proposals
within the comments. This may have to do with the fact that the data
covers more than 2000 professors. A general trend for one professor will
therefore be drowned in the general trends among all the professors. An-
other reason for this is, that both methods assume that the residuals are
Gaussian distributed, which is not a reasonable assumption for binary
data. The distributional assumptions underlying methods like the Lo-
gistic NMF or the methods proposed by Tomé et al. matches the data
better, as they are designed for binary data.
Is it possible to extract keywords and keyphrases from the data
that explains the variation in the ratings?
The baseline set by
the correlation showed that the largest absolute correlation between any
individual 1-gram and the ratings were -0.321. This benchmark was not
exceeded by any of the unsupervised methods. The closest was the 8th
component of the Latent Semantic Analysis with a correlation of 0.277.
This component was describing the grading, whereas other components
extracted by the unsupervised methods described more general trends in
the data.
The ﬁrst PLS component had a correlation of 0.638 with the ratings,
exceeding the baseline by some margin. This component described sev-
eral diﬀerent parts of the teaching including the grading. Furthermore,
emotional 1-grams like "easi", "interest" and "worst" were included in this
component. The regression coeﬃcients in Table 4.12, are a weighting of
the loadings. They reveal that a general pattern of the PLS components
is that 1-grams expressing emotions are present among the most impor-
tant when predicting a rating. This is reasonable, as a comment about a
professor probably consist of some general teaching speciﬁc terms, mixed
with adjectives. The choice of adjectives depend on the impression of the
person that is being described. Therefore, if the person has made a good
impression, and thereby deserve a high rating, then generally positive
adjectives are probably used to describe him/her, and vice versa. This
also mean that many examples of Synonymy is expected, as PLS chooses
the combination of inputs that explains the response, and does not take
==PAGE:95==
81
any correlation structure between the inputs into account, when choos-
ing which inputs to include. This pattern is close to what is actually
observed. There are not observed any examples of correlated inputs that
has been included with (large) coeﬃcients with opposite sign. This is
expected as the PLS is related to shrinkage methods.
By comparing the estimated components from the diﬀerent models, it
was seen that the ﬁrst PLS component had little correlation to any of
the other components. This explains why neither of the LSA or NMF
components were correlated to the ratings, as they represented something
diﬀerent in the comments.
The most important 1-grams in the Random Forest were, except for few
perturbations, very similar to the 1-grams with largest regression coeﬃ-
cients for the PLS model.
Is it possible to predict a rating based on a comment?
The com-
ponents estimated using the unsupervised methods all performed worse

Chapter 6
Conclusion
The comments were modelled using a Vector Space Model and repre-
sented in a Term-Document Matrix using a binary weighting scheme.
The binary weighting scheme was chosen as the correlation between 1-
grams and the ratings did not diﬀer much when changing into another
weighting scheme. The comments were analysed using unsupervised mod-
els. It was seen that by using 2-grams, the most dominant features were
contaminated by artefacts from the pre-processing. The analysis of the 1-
grams showed that the models were able to avoid Synonymy between the
dominant 1-grams. Subjective topics were proposed to the components
where it seemed ﬁt.
These topics included lecture, structure, assign-
ments, exam and professor. The Logistic NMF was proposed. It was
seen that the model extracted components describing the same topics as
the other methods, but identiﬁed other 1-grams as being important for
the topics. Neither of the components estimated using the unsupervised
methods described variation in the ratings.
The relation between the ratings and the comments were modelled using
Partial Least Squares and Random Forest. The ratings were considered
continuous, as it was unknown if the students had the same interpretation
of the ratings. A 4 component PLS model was estimated to be optimal.
The two methods determined the same 1-grams, with few perturbations,
to be important when predicting a rating. The most important 1-grams
were in most cases adjectives describing emotions towards the profes-
==PAGE:98==
84
Conclusion
sor or the course. Examples are "worst", "avoid", "great" and "terribl".
Both models performed equally well and acceptable, when predicting the
rating of previously unseen comments. A comparison of the predictions
illustrated the need for a balanced training set as the Random Forest
had a biased prediction towards high ratings. As Partial Least Squares
were trained using a balanced training set, it did not have the same bias.
Instead PLS made predictions smaller than 1 and larger than 5.
Future work
The methods covered in this project revealed many structures in the data.
In order to improve the results from the unsupervised methods, a global
weighting scheme should be applied in order to compensate for the diﬀer-
ent frequencies of the 1-grams. This could be the widely used log-idf, or
a more sophisticated weight. Besides using a global weight, the method
proposed by Tomé et al. should be applied, since they have shown its
usefulness within other ﬁelds with binary data. The performance of the
method should then be compared to the proposed Logistic NMF. In order
to get components that are easier to interpret, methods such as Sparse
LSA and Sparse Coding should be applied.
In order to improve the understanding of the relation between the com-
ments and the ratings, sparse regression methods should be applied. This
could either be the Lasso or the Elastic net. Non-linear methods like
neural networks could also be considered, but as the prediction error is
already relatively small, this is unlikely to be improved much.

Non-negative Matrix Factorization for Binary Data
Jacob Søgaard Larsen, Line Katrine Harder Clemmensen
DTU Compute, Technical University of Denmark, Richard Petersens Plads, 2800, Lyngby, Denmark
s093068@student.dtu.dk, lkhc@dtu.dk
Keywords:
Non-negative Matrix Factorization, Binary Data, Binary Matrix Factorization, Text Modelling
Abstract:
We propose the Logistic Non-negative Matrix Factorization for decomposition of binary data. Binary data
are frequently generated in e.g. text analysis, sensory data, market basket data etc. A common method for
analysing non-negative data is the Non-negative Matrix Factorization, though this is in theory not appropriate
for binary data, and thus we propose a novel Non-negative Matrix Factorization based on the logistic link
function. Furthermore we generalize the method to handle missing data. The formulation of the method
is compared to a previously proposed logistic matrix factorization without non-negativity constraint on the
features. We compare the performance of the Logistic Non-negative Matrix Factorization to Least Squares
Non-negative Matrix Factorization and Kullback-Leibler (KL) Non-negative Matrix Factorization on sets of
binary data: a synthetic dataset, a set of student comments on their professors collected in a binary term-
document matrix and a sensory dataset. We ﬁnd that choosing the number of components is an essential part
in the modelling and interpretation, that is still unresolved.
1
INTRODUCTION
Non-negative matrices are found in many different
forms, from a general matrix with non-negative en-
tries to the case with only binary entries. The lat-
ter is an interesting case used in many ﬁelds e.g.
text data, sensory data etc. A common tool for pre-
processing data by unsupervised decompsition is the
Non-negative Matrix Factorization (NMF) proposed
by Lee and Seung (Lee and Seung, 1999; Lee and Se-
ung, 2001). One issue with the general NMF is that
the resulting approximation is not bounded above, and
hence not suitable for the binary case. Zhang et al.
proposed the Binary Matrix Factorization that factor-
izes the binary data matrix X into two binary matri-
ces W and H (Zhang et al., 2010). The interpretation
of such a decomposition may be difﬁcult, since the
method does not estimate how important an entry in
the components are, and therefore we will not con-
sider this method for our purpose. Gillis proposed that
when NMF is used on text data, the components are
interpreted as topics (Gillis, 2014). The model also
describes how important a topic is for each document
and how important a term is for a topic. We adapt
this approach and propose a logistic non.negative ma-
trix factorization. Recently, Tom´e et al. proposed a
logistic but only partially non-negative matrix factor-
ization, where the model allows for negative feature
components (Tom´e et al., 2015), whereas our method
is strictly non-negative and explicit modelling of the
threshold in the logistic sigmoidal. Tom´e et al. fur-
ther extended the model with a Lagrangian penalty on
the two norm of the columns of W and H. Both our
method and the methods by Tom´e et al. uses a gra-
dient based update scheme. Tom´e et al. uses a con-
stant step length, where we use an adaptive scheme
to ensure non-negativity. Tom´e et al. ensures non-
negativity by projection. In order to evaluate how well
the model generalizes the data, Tom´e et al. uses a set-
up with a test- and training-set, while we have gen-
eralized our method to handle missing data, thus en-
abling the use of cross-validation. The methods pro-
posed by Tom´e et al. is tested on synthetic data with
binary basis vectors and the USPS digits. The em-
phasis is put on how well the model reconstructs data,
while we focus on estimating the correct number of
feature vectors and the interpretation of the model.
Furthermore we test our model on sensory data and
text data.
The training process and selecting the model com-
plexity is another issue regarding NMF. Nielsen and
Mørup proposed to marginalize missing data in order
to perform cross-validation (CV) to choose the num-
ber of components in the model (Nielsen and Mørup,
2014), and we will use this approach in the paper.

5.1
Simulated data
On the simulated problems we saw that the assump-
tions regarding the distribution of the noise, and
thereby the choice of method, inﬂuences the how

many components is estimated as being optimal. We
observed that all four methods have a tendency to es-
timate too many components as being optimal even
though the ”one standard-error” rule was used to
choose the number of components in the models.
When changing the number of components, the inter-
pretation of a model may change, as the distribution of
signal among the components is changed. Thus, the
use of extra components compared to the true number
of components (4) may hinder the interpretation of the
estimated model. A low generalization error is as im-
portant as estimating the true number of component
when building a model. Furthermore, it is observed
that the size of the error in estimating the number of
components, is problem dependent.
The error levels were compared for the Max thresh
NMF and Global thresh NMF. It was seen that when
the optimal number of components were used, the two
methods performed with the same error level. When
the true number of components were used, the Global
thresh NMF performed signiﬁcantly better than Max
thresh NMF.
5.2
Text data
The components or topics extracted from the collec-
tion of student comments are clearly related to teach-
ing and exams. The logistic NMF’s are able to ex-
tract at least as many components from the data as LS

the other methods.

method. LS NMF, KL NMF and Max thresh NMF

have large standard errors.
The 6 component model determined with Global

CONCLUSION
We presented the two well known variants of Non-
negative Matrix Factorization and proposed the logis-
tic Non-negative Matrix Factorization for binary data.
The proposed method was presented in two variants;
one with a global threshold for the logistic sigmoid
function and one with a rank one approximation (max
threshold).
The four NMF methods were applied to a col-
lection of student comments regarding professors at
various universities.
It was seen that all methods
were able to extract components that were describ-
ing teaching and exam. Global thresh NMF and Max
thresh NMF were able to validate more components
than the two usual methods: LS NMF and KL NMF.
The ”Bitterness of Wine” dataset (Randall, 1989)
was analysed using all four NMF methods. It was
seen that methods which had a large standard error
when doing Cross Validation, either estimated few
components (LS, KL and Max) or many components
(Global).
The four NMF methods were also compared on
simulated data with four underlying components. The
methods had good error convergence. However, the
methods had a tendency to choose too many compo-
nents. Furthermore, it was seen that the bias in esti-
mating the number of components was problem de-
pendent.
The interpretation may change with a varying
number of components and as this is where we ob-
served the most issues for all four studies, we recom-
mend that future work should investigate methods to
estimate the number of components.
