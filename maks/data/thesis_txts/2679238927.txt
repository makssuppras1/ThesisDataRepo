Abstract 
The current thesis presents an analysis of climate change's consequences on indoor 
environmental quality, specially mentioned to thermal comfort and energy performance of an 
existing non-mechanical cooled office building constructed in 1975 in Copenhagen. 
Additionally, passive and active retrofit adaptation practices were examined to which extent 
could reduce the overheating risk and further investigate how they affect indoor environmental 
quality under given climate change conditions. The IDA ICE dynamic simulation software was 
used for the building's performances for the reference and projected future scenarios, with 
climate data based on SRES A2 scenario for two future periods by the end of 2040 and 2070 
with reference to the year 2013. 
The results indicated that thermal comfort degraded over the years. The annual overheating 
risk increased by 51% in 2040 and 86% by 2070, with a remarkable rise recorded not only in 
summer but also during the winter. Since the need for heating was declined due to climate 
change, the annual energy needs were reduced. That led to lower thermal energy demand in 
the heating season and growth in the electricity demand for cooling in summer. 
When passive retrofit measures introduced, the examination of thermal comfort concluded 
that neither shading devices nor improved glazing units along with natural ventilation would 
deliver the required mitigation and adaption strategy, but glazing units were more efficient. 
Although both passive measures upgraded the thermal comfort conditions; still, the 
overheating risk during the summer increased.  
Solar controlled external shading devices significantly reduce the overheating risk during the 
shoulder and winter season and less in summer but resulting in a higher need for space 
heating. On the other hand, improved windows minimized the overheating in all seasons while 
noted a significant potential of reducing the overheating risk during the summer. 
Simultaneously, the upgraded thermal properties of improved windows provided higher 
insulation. Indoor thermal conditions became more comfortable and less affected by the 
exterior climate, leading to a reduction in heating demand.  
Active cooling system with a cooling setpoint at 26 oC diminished the overheating hours during 
occupancy by 73% and 66% in 2040 and 2070, respectively. However, it could not achieve 
thermal comfort conditions at the desired level during occupancy, when the total energy 
demand rose by more than 40% and 50% in the same order. 
The annual number of hours with CO2 concentration above the desired limit increased at about 
5% by 2040 and 9% when ventilation flow rate remained unchanged and ambient CO2 
concentration rose by 18% and 44%. When passive retrofit measures were applied, followed 
by natural ventilation to prevent overheating, hours with CO2 concentration significantly 
increased, indicating that the ventilation system needs to be adapted to climate change 
conditions.

1. Introduction  
Scientists widely acknowledge climate change as one of humanity's greatest challenges 
as it is expected to affect all future generations by a negative impact on ecological, 
infrastructure, and economic systems. The growing pace of anthropogenic CO2 
concentration that has reached 413.95 ppm in December 2020 [1] projected to reach 
870 ppm by 2100 contributes to an ever rise in Global surface temperature increased 
by 2-5 oC by 2100. Both raise of atmospheric CO2 concentration and global warming 
led to the destabilization of the global climate system followed by extreme weather 
phenomena such as heatwaves and sea level rises, floods etc. [2].  
Due to the emerging climate change, the building sector is of great concern and 
discussion. People spend almost 90% of their time indoors [3], and therefore climate 
change expected to have potential implications in indoor environmental quality 
affecting the health, physical and mental wellbeing of occupants. Higher outdoor 
temperatures and more frequent heatwaves could cause higher indoor temperatures 
associated with heat stress. At the same time, higher indoor CO2 concentrations levels 
driven by outdoor air pollution could cause respiratory problems, while indoor 
dampness and mold problems could be increased due to more frequent and severe 
storms [4].  
On the other hand, the prevailing outdoor conditions set the indoor environment's 
limits. Thus, the ever-increasing trend of surface temperature and the higher CO2 
levels are expected to modify the need for heating and cooling loads of buildings, 
requiring significant additional energy for cooling and better ventilation. According to 
the last assessment report (AR) of the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change 
(IPCC) reported that for 2010 the 32% of global primary energy corresponded in the 
building sector that emitted 19% of global CO2 emission [2]. The increase in electricity 
demand, since it is not produced by zero CO2 sources, will result in an additional 
positive feedback mechanism driving climate change. 
Continue efforts are discussed at the regional, national, and global levels to implement 
mitigation and adaption strategies, following international commitments as the Kyoto 
Protocol [5], to minimize the negative climate change impact on the indoor 
environment and primary energy demand.  
Designing and retrofitting buildings is a challenging task involving various 
uncertainties. Thus, it requires a holistic approach, considering that building 
==PAGE:13==
2 
 
constructions generally have a life span of 40-100 years [5]. The weather (climate) 
data currently used in planning concern typical weather files that do not practically 
match building stock's actual situation in the following years under the climate change 
estimations. This could reduce the building's life span, lead to high energy 
consumption, and increased emitted CO2 or indoor environmental quality degradation. 
Given that most buildings by 2050 have already been constructed, the need for 
adaptation becomes even more substantial. This report investigates the prototypical 
design and builds the impact of not considering the climate scenarios in energy 
calculations and the potential impact on indoor environmental quality. 
 
1.2 Objectives/ Purpose of research  
This thesis aims to examine the energy behavior of a Danish building in light of the 
emerging climate change. In this context, changes and impacts on IEQ (mainly thermal 
conditions and indoor air quality) and energy performance are investigated, studying 
the changes that occur in an existing building in Copenhagen area under different 
climate change scenarios by the middle of the 21st century. 
Additionally, climate change mitigation strategies on building design are explored for 
the estimation of whether and to which extent they could influence the indoor 
environmental quality and energy demand according to the current Danish standards. 
Drawing conclusions is achieved through the quantification of the examined IEQ 
parameters using the IDA ICE simulation tool.

2.1 Climate change  
“Climate Change” is defined as the variation in the state of the climate that is proved 
by changes in the mean and/or the variability of its properties observed over 
comparable periods, typically decades or longer. It may be caused by natural internal 
processes or external forces like modulations of the solar cycles, volcanic eruptions 
and persistent anthropogenic changes in the composition of the atmosphere or land 
use [5]. This term encompasses various changes mainly led by the ever-increasing 
global temperature, extreme weather events, shifting wildlife populations and habitats, 
rising seas, and a spread of other impacts.  
The “Framework Convention on Climate Change” (UNFCCC) defines climate change 
[6] as:  
“a change of climate which is attributed directly or indirectly to human activity that 
alters the composition of the global atmosphere and which is in addition to natural 
climate variability observed over comparable time periods.”  
According to this view, there is a distinction between climate change caused by human 
activities changing the atmospheric composition and, therefore, climate shifts from 
natural causes.  
The Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC) [6] has produced a series of 
Assessment Reports in order to quantify how sensitive the climate might be under 
increased concentrations of greenhouse gas in the atmosphere. This effort is based on 
creating a range of possible future emission paths so that they can be used to further 
to investigate realistic sets of future climate change scenarios. According to the IPCC, 
human activities are likely to cause the observed global warming.  
The main climate scenarios that have primarily led to discussions by policymakers and, 
therefore, the public include the following: 
− 
1992: 2nd Assessment Report (SAR) with six IPCCs (IS92) scenarios,  
− 
2000 and 2004: 3rd (TAR) and 4th (AR4) Assessment Reports with six Special 
Reports on Emission Scenarios (SRES),  
− 
2014: 5th (AR5) Assessment Report, with four Representative Concentration 
Pathways (RCP) scenarios,  
==PAGE:16==
5 
 
− 
Upcoming in the year 2021: 6th Assessment Report (AR6), with nine forcing 
scenarios based on the Shared Socioeconomic Pathways (SSPs). 
The RCPs scenarios that are the most used nowadays are based on radiation exposure 
changes by the end of the 21st century, mainly forced by changes in greenhouse gas 
concentration in the atmosphere. The scenarios are called RCP2.6, RCP4.5, RCP6.0 
and RCP8.5, where the numbers 2.6, 4.5, 6.0 and 8.5 indicate the radiation exposure 
impact in watts per square meter. They are also referred to as stabilization scenarios 
based exclusively on the increment of greenhouse gas concentrations in the 
atmosphere. [2] 
The very high GHG emissions Scenario RCP 8.5 is also called “business as usual” as 
the most likely to happen. It is the only one that does not project the stabilization of 
CO2 concentration, projecting a temperature rise between 3-7oC, solar radiation 
impact above 8.5 W/m2 and CO2 concentration above 1,300 ppm by the end of the 
century [7]. Figure 1 illustrates the 4 scenarios as a brief reference.

Denmark is a Scandinavian Nordic country in Northern Europe characterized by a 
heterogeneous climate as is affected by air masses from the Atlantic, Arctic and 
Eastern Europe. It is a predominantly humid continental climate (Köppen climate 
classification Dfb), and about one-fifth of the country within Capital Region of 
Copenhagen displays an oceanic climate (Köppen Cfb) [8].   
==PAGE:17==
6 
 
The annual mean temperature is around 8°C; it increased by 1.5°C since the end of 
1870s. The annual mean temperature varied between 6-10oC between 1870-2020, 
showing a significant rise over the years. Since 1872 it increased thirteen times above 
9oC, with eleven of them after 1989 and most recent in 2019 [9]. The annual rainfall 
recorded at 750 mm, increased by 15% compared to the end of 1870 (650mm).   
According to the RCP8,5 scenario, in the future, Denmark will face a warmer climate, 
with annual mean temperature projected to rise by 3.4°C with an uncertainty interval 
of 2.9 to 4.3°C compared to the period 1981-2010. Milder winters are expected, with 
the extension of summer periods. [10] 
Summers are projected to be warmer with more frequent heatwaves and temperature 
remaining at 20°C even during the nighttime. Longer dry spells along with increasingly 
dry days are expected, with precipitation mainly occur as heavy showers. By the end 
of 2100, the mean summer temperature expected to reach 19.3°C (uncertainty range 
18.2-20.3°C).  The intensity of heavy rains during summer is predicted to increase, 
although, in contrast, the number of rainy days in summers decreased. 
Higher temperatures during winter will reduce the frosty days, which will drive to 25% 
more precipitation than the period 1981-2010. The mean temperature in winter 
projected at 5.2°C with an uncertainty of 4.6to 5.8oC.  
Heavy rains will strike harder, resulting in a rise in sea levels - floods are expected on 
an annual basis when nowadays, occur once in twenty years [11]. 
 
2.2 Climate change in indoor environment & health  
The overheating due to higher outdoor temperatures is estimated to shift the global 
energy demand for cooling by 31% compared to the current energy use. At the same 
time, the energy need for heating is expected to be decreased by 22% [12]. Although 
combined the share of energy demand for heating and cooling a decrement of 4 to 
5% is expected, however, due to the high summer temperatures, the peak cooling 
load is expected to increase by up 28.5% than in typical summer conditions [13]. 
Buildings are the dividing line between the indoor environment - which should be 
characterized by appropriate living conditions to ensure occupants’ wellbeing - and the 
outdoor environment, which depends directly on the climatic peculiarities of the and 
therefore from the relevant differences noted in it. The climate change that has been 
observed in recent years, but also those that predicted to occur in the near future,

significantly affect the buildings’ energy performance as well as the indoor 
environment.

show that a warmer planet with increased temperatures and increased outdoor air

2.3 Mitigation and adaption strategies  
The impacts of global warming on buildings’ energy performance have gained much 
attention worldwide. In that context, it has to be considered that even if greenhouses 
==PAGE:19==
8 
 
gases emission stops to emitted today, global warming impacts will continue to affect 
future generations. That way, humanity is already «bounded» by climate change.  
In the last decade, policymakers and the scientific community have focused on 
mitigation and adaption strategies in order to reduce the anthropogenic greenhouses 
gas emissions, by increasing the share of renewables and with efforts based on long 
term - efficient and cost-effective strategies to decrease energy consumption and 
production by zero energy sources. Mitigation strategies are mainly focused on 
developing energy-saving potentials and the use of renewables to reduce greenhouse 
gas emissions. On the other hand, adaption strategies are mainly depended on where 
the buildings are located and focus on ensuring the tolerance and resilience of building 
stock under climate change conditions [14].  
Therefore, in order to achieve the most efficient use of energy in the context of climate 
change, it is essential to realize the potential impacts of climate change in building 
thermal and energy performance and consider the most effective adaption measure to 
prevent energy waste but also to achieve the optimal operational management.  
Thermal comfort is considered one of the most crucial parameters of the indoor 
environment, while at the same time, the overheating risk is expected to be dominant 
under future climate change. Mitigation and adaption strategies include active and 
passive measures aiming to reduce the cooling need by managing the overheating 
risk. Some of the most effective solutions in building design for managing the 
overheating risks include [15]: 
− 
Planting, shading and advanced glazing systems to reduce the incoming solar heat 
gain. 
− 
Thermal insulation on buildings envelope 
− 
Cool building material, green roofs and walls, to prevent the heat from entering 
the indoor spaces.   
− 
Increased ventilation and removing excess heat using fresh air. That solution is 
only suggested if outdoor air is cool.  
− 
Mechanical active cooling systems, including conventional air conditioning systems 
and chilled beams. 
− 
Use of thermal storage in order to absorb heat during the summer so that it can 
dispel during winter, mainly through ventilation.

3. Methodology 
The current thesis is studying the impact of climate change on thermal comfort and 
indoor air quality as well as in energy demand. It is done by selecting prototypical 
office building in Copenhagen.  
The first step was the definition of future climate scenarios, and the second was the 
implementation of the building in IDA-ICE dynamic simulation software. Then the 
analysis – modelling is performed, and a sensitivity analysis with retrofitting passive 
and active measures. Figure 2 visually illustrates the procedure.

The building was modelled following current standards (operation inputs) or the once

The input parameters of the current study mainly based on assumptions according to

3.5 Internal gains  
3.5.1 Occupancy  
The total number of employees in the building is around 500, with 120 working in the 
studied area in office rooms of 2,4 and 6 persons.   
The metabolic rate was set at 1.2 met as the typical “sedentary activity” value (office, 
dwelling, school, laboratory) [22] with a comfort range of ±0.5. [23].  
The occupancy schedule was defined for all spaces according to the company’s working 
hours from 7:00 to 17:00 on weekdays with the difference that for the restrooms and 
common areas, the occupant’s activity level was set at 1met of “Seated, relaxed”.  
3.5.2 Equipment  
An average value of 100W/ per employee was assumed as a typical value of 1 desktop 
computer (a central processing unit with a keyboard, mouse and a visual display) [24].   
3.5.3 Lighting  
According to the EPC, the lighting system of the building consists of 2 types of 
luminaries. Compact fluorescent lamps of 55W for offices and luminaires with LED light 
sources in corridors and common areas, whereas there is no control for a motion 
detector or daylight control, and so the lighting system was designed to operate for 
100% concurrency throughout a full workday from 7 to 17. 
In order to determine the exact number of luminaries in each room, the DIALUX EVO 
tool was used following the Danish Standards[25] of reaching 500 lux at 0.8 m for 
offices and 100 lux at 0.1 m in corridors ignoring the daylight effect.

3.6 Heating and Cooling System  
The heating setpoint was set at 19°C, modelled by an ideal heater that is a fictional 
unit used to provide all the power needed to maintain the operative temperature within 
the desired range under all conditions. This assumption is based on the fact that 
energy consumption actually reflects the energy demand of the building. So, the 
selected large capacity of the ideal heater does not affect the energy demand. 
The studied part of the building was without mechanical cooling. Instead, natural 
ventilation was used with the opening of the windows controlled by the occupants. 
That was modelled with a cooling setpoint of 27oC (limit of Cat.III) and occupancy 
schedule. That means that windows opened when the operative temperature reached 
27oC.     
The set-point varied from 19°C to 27°C for heating and cooling, respectively, as the 
limits of Category III according to DS 16798/2019 [26] in terms of a moderate level 
of expectation in older than 10 years buildings.  
 
3.7 Climate data 
The current study was conducted based on Hour-by-hour simulations of the indoor 
climate and energy balance of the building with climate datasets for Denmark 
according to IPCC SRES A2 climate scenario[10]. The weather data files obtained from 
“package "DRY_future.7z",, which is publicly available [27]. According to this the 
future projections were produced using the Climate Change tool World Weather file 
Generator (CCWorldWeatherGen) [28]. This tool uses the so-called “HadCM3 A2 
experiment ensemble” from the IPCC (Intergovernmental Panel of Climate Change) 
[7] SRES A2 emission scenarios. 
The SRES A2 [29] scenario is called the "business as usual" scenario, similar to the 
RCP8.5 scenario. It describes how climate is projected to be changed if the current 
progress of humanity, i.e., fragmentation of economic development by regions, 
differentiation in demographic patterns and non-intensification of reversal measures 
remained constant. Then in 2100, the average temperature of the planet will be 
increased by 2-5oC, with expected CO2 concentrations at 870 ppm and population 
growth over 10 billion by 2050.

3.9 Scenarios overview 
 
The current analysis divided into four parts. In the first part, the impacts of climate 
change on IEQ (thermal comfort and indoor air quality) of an existing office building 
under climate change scenarios investigated.  
Afterwards, a sensitivity analysis was conducted to investigate the impacts on IEQ in 
projected future climate scenarios when implementing two passive and an active 
adaption retrofit measures.  
The following is a brief description of the parts and a summary of input data and 
assumptions used. 
The studied building was built in 1975. The predominant lifespan of a Danish building

2075, two different projected future scenarios used based on the IPCC SRES A2 climate 
scenario as well as the reference year 2013, which was the starting point for the 
projections. More specifically, the simulated periods were:

− 
Annual and seasonal CO2 distribution. 
− 
Distribution of CO2 concentration in three IEQ Categories along with hours

Compliance with the 6% acceptable deviation length of hours exceeding the limit

of overheating increased considerably in simulated “2020” and “2050” future scenarios

and higher temperatures resulted in an increment of indoor CO2 and perhaps 
bioeffluents.

scenarios “2020” and “2050” in order to investigate the extent to which the 
overheating risk could be reduced related to indoor air quality. These are: 
1. External shading devices in windows.  
2. Improved windows (glazing units).  
3. Active mechanical cooling.

5. Discussion  
The purpose of the current thesis was to examine the consequences of climate change 
on indoor environmental quality and energy performance of a prototypical office 
building in Denmark by using projected future climate scenarios. Additionally, passive 
and active retrofit adaptation practices were examined (by modelling) to reduce the 
consequences, mainly the risk of overheating and investigate whether they affect 
indoor environmental quality under given future climate scenarios. 
This section is composed of a discussion of main findings and areas for future studies; 
a brief summary is given as well. The discussion helps to answer the following 
questions:  
1. To which extent climate change may affect indoor environmental quality and 
energy performance of an existing office building,  
2. To which extent, different passive and active retrofitting measures applied in 
an existing office building may influence the indoor environmental quality and 
energy demand. 
The current study's findings indicate potential adverse effects on indoor environmental 
quality because of climate change. This is mainly for thermal comfort as the average 
outdoor temperature is predicted to rise by about 1.2°C for the “2020” climate scenario 
and 2.5°C for the “2050” scenario. As the need for heating is declined due to climate 
change, the annual energy needs are reduced, which has a positive impact as less CO2 
is emitted to the atmosphere. Multiple studies show that high temperatures harm 
cognitive performance, particularly with elevated indoor temperatures in a building 
with no mechanical cooling. This may have consequences for productivity and well-
being of building occupants. The relationship between productivity and indoor 
temperature shows that office work productivity will decrease by 2% per each 1oC 
when the temperature is above 25 oC [35].  
Simultaneously, the annual CO2 characterized by higher concentrations in the 
projected future scenarios, when ventilation remained unchanged and as the outdoor 
CO2 rose from 400 ppm in the reference year to 470 ppm (+18%) in the “2020” 
scenario ended at 575 ppm (+44%) in the “2050” scenario.   The hours of indoor CO2 
concentration above the desired limit of 1,750 ppm increased by 16% in “2020” and 
28% in “2050”. 
==PAGE:70==
59 
 
The use of external shading devices significantly reduced the overheating risk during 
the winter and shoulder season. However, shading devices reduce the incoming solar 
radiation in winter and benefits not only for work and well-being (daylight) but also 
heating (gains) [36], resulting in a higher need for space heating.  
In the “2020” climate scenario - the final energy demand for space heating remained 
almost unchanged, and it reduced by 14% in “2050” compared to the current situation 
called a reference year.  
Yet, the results suggest that the higher the ambient temperature, the less impact of 
external shading devices on thermal environment indoors. A more detailed observation 
showed that in the “2020” climate scenario, when annual mean outdoor temperature 
increased by 1.2oC, the annual overheating risk reduced by 11% compared to the 
reference year. However, with an annual mean outdoor temperature increment of 
2.5oC in the “2050” scenario, the overheating risk increased by 15%. Although shading 
devices reduced the overheating in winter and shoulder season, however during 
summer, there was an increment in overheating at about 9% in the "2020" scenario 
and a 40% in the "2050" scenario when the mean outdoor temperature in summer 
rose from 17.7 oC in the reference year to 18.1 oC in “2020” and 19 oC in “2050”. Solar 
shading devices contribute towards solar heat control, and that way, the operative 
temperature remained at a lower level. However, when higher temperature occurred, 
the need for openings the windows was necessary, leading to more hours of 
overheating in summer.  
The improved window provided a lower heat loss coefficient (U value) and lower solar 
transmittance (g value) resulted in minimizing the annual overheating by 29% and 
16% in the modelled “2020” and “2050” climate scenarios. During the winter and 
shoulder season, the indoor temperature remained within acceptable levels defined by 
the standard DS16798-2:2019. Improved windows were more effective in preventing 
overheating during summer, as in the “2020” scenario, the overheating reduced by 
10% compared to the reference year. In the “2050” scenario, the overheating risk 
increased by 14%, but that was much less than in the “2050” scenario with external 
shading.   
As improved glazing properties provided higher insulation, the indoor thermal 
conditions became more comfortable and less affected by the exterior climate, which 
led to a minor reduction in heating demand in both projected future scenarios.   
==PAGE:71==
60 
 
The installation of active mechanical cooling, as expected diminished the annual 
overheating hours by 66% and 61% in the “2020” and the “2050” climate scenarios 
compared to the reference year but increased energy use by 44% and 53%, 
respectively. The simulated cooling load showed that high operative temperatures 
increased the need for cooling even during the winter when the temperatures reached 
more than 26oC. On the other hand, the improved glazing units reduced the 
overheating risk in winter to a greater extent than mechanical cooling. However, the 
active cooling could not dimmish the overheating during the occupied hours due to an 
inaccurate, responsive cooling system reaction that left the temperature to reach more 
than 27 oC. That could indicate the need for a higher cooling capacity or a cooling set 
point at 25 oC or lower, leading to higher energy demand.  
Regarding thermal comfort conditions, none of the above measures alone achieved 
the desired comfort conditions in terms of hours above the desired limits, both annually 
and during occupancy. According to a reasonable deviation length defined in DS16798-
2: 2019, the number of hours exceeding the desired operative temperature limit could 
be until 25% (180 h) in a month but not more than 6% in a year (526h), or 6% during 
occupancy (157h). Put another way; mechanical cooling achieved to keep the 
overheating hours less than 180h per month but not less than 526 in a year, or less 
than 157h during occupancy. Considering that improved windows were more efficient 
in achieving thermal comfort in winter than mechanical cooling did, implementing them 
simultaneously would be more efficient and less costly. 
In the simulated future climate scenarios, there was an increase in hours exceeding 
the limit of indoor CO2 concentrations. The rise of background CO2 caused higher 
concentrations inside the building to some extent but was not responsible for hours 
exceeding the desired limit. Considering that: 
− 
indoor CO2 levels usually are higher than outside buildings because of additional 
CO2 from occupants’ breath exhalation [3], and  
− 
the building was naturally ventilated, and when the operative temperature reached 
27OC during occupancy, the windows were opened (according to the simulation 
input parameters),  
the increment in hours above the desired limits could be explained with the following: 
− 
Comparing the future climate scenarios before implementing any passive or active 
measure, the CO2 concentration above the desired limit of 1750 ppm was mainly 
noticed during the winter (figure 18). However, the examination of future climate 
==PAGE:72==
61 
 
scenarios when installing external shading devices and the improved windows 
showed a rise in hours with CO2 above the limits during the shoulder season 
(figures 28 and 38). That led to fewer hours of free cooling by windows opening 
and thus in more hours with CO2 concentrations higher than recommended levels. 
In the case of future modelled scenarios with active cooling, the hours of CO2 
exceedance were also noticed in summer (figure 49).  
− 
External shading and improved glazing reduced the overheating risk, mainly in 
winter and shoulder season. During the summer, when the windows remained 
open, due to high temperatures, the CO2 concentration remained below 1750 ppm 
in the future modelled scenarios, same as in the reference year. In the “2020” 
scenario, the improved glazing unit caused a 10% reduction in overheating 
compared to the reference year. That led to a slight increase in hours with CO2 
above the limits during summer (figure 38). 
In the case of improved window glazing, the occupied hours with elevated CO2 levels 
were also increased compared to the reference year but to a lesser degree than if the 
shading devices were used. The better insulation of windows kept the operative 
temperature in acceptable range in winter and shoulder seasons, resulting in better 
overall thermal conditions than external shading devices.  
 
5.1 
Modelling Parameters 
 
As presented in the results part, implementing passive retrofits could efficiently reduce 
the overheating risk under climate change conditions and thus be a part of integrated 
mitigation and adaption strategy, reducing the building's cooling load at a high level. 
Denmark is a country with high insulation standards and requirements about buildings' 
airtightness, as a potential for energy savings to reduce the demand for heating in 
winter [32]. That high insulation standards could lead to increasing overheating risk. 
So, it is a crucial factor that should be taken into account concerning climate change 
mitigation and adaption strategies and as a factor that plays a decisive role in 
determining the need for cooling.  
On the other hand, the increased airtightness strengthens the need for adequate 
ventilation to ensure a healthy indoor environment. CO2 concentration in office 
buildings mainly depended on ventilation rates and occupant density [39]. In the 
studied case, the ventilation rate of 0.4l/s/m2 at a constant air volume seemed not to 
fulfil the requirements of hours within the acceptable CO2 levels for the reference year. 
==PAGE:73==
62 
 
In modelled future scenarios, CO2 concentration showed a broader distribution with 
more hours above the desired limit.  
Additionally, when the passive measures of external shading devices and improved 
windows were implemented, CO2 concentration above the limits increased, as the 
reduction of overheating risk minimized the use of free cooling through windows.  
This suggests the need for increasing ventilation rate to minimize the climate change 
effect, which will increase the energy-related costs.  
What should also be considered is that the ever-growing ambient CO2 concentration 
will result in higher indoor concentrations of CO2, especially during extreme weather 
events such as heatwaves, when natural ventilation will be less effective, and more 
will bring hot air indoors. It has been discussed whether CO2 can aggravate cognitive 
performance. Although the evidence is not systematic, several studies discussed the 
adverse effect of increasing indoor CO2 over decision making and cognitive thinking 
of humans that, in some cases, led to marginal or dysfunctional performance [37], 
[38].    
Additionally, from the examination of indoor air quality, using CO2 as a proxy in terms 
of hours above the suggested limit according to DS16798-2: 2019, a significant issue 
occurs. According to the current Danish Standard, the limit of Cat.III for CO2 
concentration is 1350 ppm above outdoors, with outdoor CO2 concentrations range 
between 350-500ppm. The current background CO2 concentration reached 413.95 
ppm in December 2020 that in the current study was rounded to 400 ppm. Thus, the 
limit of cat. III turned to 1750ppm. Following the IPCC A2 SRES scenario, the ambient 
CO2 concentration is projected to rise at about 870 ppm by the end of the 21st century 
[29]. That will change the existing upper limits of IEQ Categories by setting new 
thresholds. This practically means that for the reference year 2013, the 1750 ppm is 
an acceptable concentration that turns to 1,820 ppm by 2040 (ambient CO2 
concentration at 470 ppm) and even more at 1,925 ppm (ambient CO2 concentration 
at 575 ppm) in 2070. So, it should be further considered to what extent the limits could 
shift in the future in order to maintain a good and healthy indoor air quality.  
Another thing worth mentioning and being considered is that although indoor relative 
humidity remained within the desired length of 20% - 60%, as suggested in DS16798-
2: 2019, a slight increase was recorded in simulated future scenarios in cooling season. 
According to the weather data files used in the current study, based on the SRES A2

scenario [28], future summer in Denmark is predicted to be characterized by high 
outdoor temperatures and heavy rains. The precipitable water will increase (Table A3, 
Appendix A), and at the same time, the temperature will rise (Table 5). As the indoor 
air temperature rises, so does its ability to hold moisture. In case of buildings with no 
adequate ventilation, the high indoor relative humidity could cause fungal growth 
resulting in increased odours. At the same time, high relative humidity indoors is 
associated with mould and bacteria that could cause respiratory issues and conducive 
to the increase in dust mite populations, which may affect people living with asthma 
[39]. 
 
5.2 
Modelling Limitations  
 
− 
The implementation of the building and its parameters was linked with several 
limitations. As there were insufficient data about the building components and the 
operating systems such as the glazing units, the ventilation system and the room 
units, assumptions were made according to the regulations at the time of 
construction and renovation (1975 and 1991). Following the recommendations of 
DS16798-2: 2019, the building was assumed to operate within the limits of Cat. 
III as a building, was constructed more than ten years ago.  
− 
The EPW weather file used in the present thesis for modelling the simulated future 
scenarios contains the weather data, including temperature, solar radiation, 
humidity, wind speed, and precipitation in hourly time step representing the typical 
conditions of Denmark. This means that extreme weather phenomena such as 
heatwaves, storms etc., are not considered (modelled), and only average levels 
are simulated.  
− 
The current thesis was carried out based on an older version of the IPCC climate 
future projections, as they were the only publicly available data for Denmark. More 
specifically, the SRES A2 scenario from the 3rd IPCC Assessment Report (issued in 
2000) was used for the current thesis. In 2014 the IPCC published the newer 
Representative Concentration Pathways (RCP), from the 5th (AR5) Assessment 
Report, which are the widely used scenarios for future estimations nowadays. 
However, the SRES A2 scenario considered as comparable to the RCP8.5, as the 
likely outcome is a temperature rise somewhere between 2.0 - 5.4 °C towards 
2100. The main difference is that in case of SRES A2 is not predicted any special 
measures or politics about CO2 stabilization at any particular level. At the same 
==PAGE:75==
64 
 
time, RCPs scenarios are defined according to mitigation practices that leading to 
different CO2 forcing levels [2]. 
− 
According to the EPC, the studied building has an energy label D. That means that 
total energy demand was expected to vary between 138-178 kWh/m2 (label D: 
175+4200/A kWh/m2 year). The simulated results recorded significantly lower 
values corresponded in class B, and only when simulated with an active cooling 
system, the annual energy demand was close to Class D. The reasons that possibly 
contributed to variation are as follows: 
− 
Possible model failure. The building was modelled following current standards 
(operation inputs) or the once in force at the time of construction (buildings 
components, windows sizes, insulation). Therefore, there is a possibility of 
errors in relation to the actual situation. 
− 
Only a part of the building was modelled. Thus, there would probably be 
additional refrigeration or other electrical units allocated out of the study area. 
− 
The EPC is based on theoretical calculations and models that may differ from 
the actual energy consumption [40]. 
However, these discrepancies do not have effects on final conclusions as the 
relative comparisons were made.  
 
5.3 
Future Studies   
 
− 
It is recommended to investigate the interaction of different building design 
adaption and mitigation strategies to determine the optimal performance with 
respect to cost of energy and IEQ. 
− 
An extension of the present could be to which extent the examined measures 
influence daylight and lighting. In the current study, artificial lighting was 
considered to operate with an occupancy schedule. Therefore, it was not possible 
to record the impacts of increasing global solar radiation in terms of lighting. At 
the same time, plenty of issues arise, such as the changes in energy consumption 
or the impact of solar protection in daylight effect in the future. 
− 
An additional future study could examine the potential of ventilative cooling, which 
is considered an efficient passive retrofit strategy for building adaptation in 
Northern Europe [41]. The main idea involves the cooling of buildings during the 
nighttime, driven by natural forces such as the wind or thermal buoyancy. Night-
==PAGE:76==
65 
 
time ventilation could provide a heat sink that absorbs heat gains during 
occupancy, reducing the overheating risk [42].   
− 
Energy use for cooling in office buildings is usually higher than for domestic use 
due to the continuous compensation of the cooling system in order to reduce the 
effect of the internal heat gains produced by occupants and equipment [24]. At 
the same in light of climate change, the use of energy for cooling is becoming more 
and more imperative. An interesting object could be the investigation of several 
active cooling systems, so the most appropriate cooling technique be 
recommended for reducing energy consumption while improving the indoor 
environmental quality. 
 
 
==PAGE:77==
66 
 
6. Conclusions 
 
The current study investigated the influence of climate change on the indoor 
environmental quality and energy performance of an existing office building of 1975 
in Copenhagen by comparing the results delivered under two different climate future 
periods by the end of 2070. Additionally, passive and active retrofit adaptation 
practices of external shading devices, improved windows and mechanical cooling was 
explored to estimate whether and to which extent they could influence the indoor 
environmental quality and energy demand in the light of the emerging climate change 
- the quantification of the examined IEQ parameters achieved through IDA ICE 
simulation tool, based on SRES A2 scenario. The results emerged from the evaluation 
of hours exceeding the desired limits according to DS16798/2019.   
The current study indicated that buildings’ thermal comfort and energy performance 
were significantly affected under climate change conditions. The overheating risk 
increased even during the winter when the operative temperature reached more than 
26 oC. That led to lower thermal energy demand in winter and a growth in the 
electricity demand for cooling in summer. The examination of passive retrofit measures 
as an adaption strategy showed that neither shading devices nor improved glazing 
units alone could deliver the required mitigation and adaption strategy, but glazing 
units were more efficient. Both passive measures upgraded the thermal comfort 
conditions by reducing the overheating risk at a significant degree in the heating and 
shoulder season. However, thermal comfort in the summertime was unlikely to be 
achieved, and eventually, an active cooling system would become necessary. On the 
other hand, mechanical cooling minimized the overheating during summer but required 
cooling demand also observed in winter when passive adaption strategies been more 
efficient. The indoor CO2 concentrations showed a significant increase in hours above 
the limits in the projected future scenarios, which got more intense when passive 
retrofits applied. 
All things considered; the main conclusions can be outlined into: 
− 
The increasing outdoor temperature due to global warming increased the 
overheating risk which led to lower thermal energy demand in the heating season 
and growth in the electricity demand for cooling in summer.  
− 
The external shading devices could effectively reduce overheating risk during the 
shoulder season. However, their operation in winter should be further considered 
==PAGE:78==
67 
 
as potentially could prevent a part of beneficial incoming solar radiation, thus 
increasing the space heating demand.   
− 
Improved windows with low solar transmissivity could significantly reduce the 
overheating risk during the summer. Simultaneously, the lower heat loss coefficient 
could diminish the need for space heating during the winter while contributing to 
maintaining the operative temperature within desired limits. 
− 
Mechanical active cooling diminished overheating risk in summer when the energy 
use increased by 44% and 53% in the “2020” and the “2050” scenarios. However, 
mechanical cooling could not prevent overheating in winter when passive retrofits 
been more efficient.  
− 
The ventilation system needs to be adapted to climate change conditions by 
increasing the ventilation rate. An inadequate ventilation system would cause 
higher indoor CO2 levels when passive retrofit measures to prevent overheating 
are applied. 
Since studied passive measures operate without the use of running energy (only built-
in energy), further studies should investigate if they could be combined to reduce the 
cooling load as much as possible and then an active cooling system could be introduced 
to the building to meet the remaining cooling requirement.
