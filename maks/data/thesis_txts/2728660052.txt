Abstract
The ongoing evolution of malware poses an escalating threat to digital systems and
cybersecurity. To address this challenge, this research delves into the actions undertaken by
different categories of malware, namely Ransomware, Worms, and Spyware, to discern their
methods of infiltration and manipulation. Leveraging state-of-the-art malware analysis tools
such as Cuckoo and VirusTotal, an extensive range of actions performed by these malware
types was detected and categorized. Countermeasures were proposed to mitigate the impact
of these actions, spanning from evasion and anti-analysis techniques to privilege escalation
and execution strategies. The study also explores the utilization of contemporary AI tools,
exemplified by the inclusion of ChatGPT in the research process. This collaborative ap-
proach substantiates the role of AI in assisting every phase of the study, from formulating
hypotheses to refining findings. Notably, the analysis highlights that while common actions
prevail among different malware categories, tailored countermeasures are imperative to ad-
dress specific threats effectively.
The research sheds light on the multifaceted nature of
malware and establishes a foundation for further exploration and validation of countermea-
sures to safeguard digital systems.

Introduction
With the rapid advancement of technology and the increasing digitalization and inter-
connection of our world, the threat posed by malware is becoming an increasing concern in
today’s cybersecurity landscape. In 2022, 38% more cyberattacks happened compared to
2021, according to the study made by Joy LePree Anderson [1]. This tendency has been
growing every year and it is expected that it will keep growing in the future. An impor-
tant part of these attacks is possible because of malicious software, such as ransomware,
spyware, and worms, which serve the cybercriminals in various ways to reach their goals.
This malware is constantly evolving in sophistication, causing devastating consequences for
individuals, businesses, and even nations. Therefore, understanding the modus operandi of
different categories of malware and understanding effective countermeasures is a necessity
for the current society.
The objective of this research is to dive into the intricacies of malware and explore its
activity, behavior, and potential impact. By executing malware samples within a controlled
sandbox environment and closely monitoring their actions, it is possible to unravel the steps
taken by each of the categories. This empirical approach will provide valuable insights into
their strategies to penetrate a system, discover it, escalate its privileges, and exploit the
vulnerabilities found to reach a mischievous goal.
Moreover, the research seeks to propose and evaluate countermeasures against these
malicious software threats. By comprehensively understanding the behaviors and techniques
employed by different categories of malware, several targeted defensive strategies to mitigate
their impact can be set. These countermeasures will not only focus on detection and preven-
tion but also explore potential mitigation and recovery strategies to minimize the damage
caused by malware attacks.

3.1.1
Overview of modern malware
Malware, short for malicious software, refers to any software specifically designed to
infiltrate, damage, or gain unauthorized access to computer systems, networks, or devices.
Malware comprehends a wide range of malicious typologies, each with unique characteristics
and objectives.
The classification of malware labels them under various categories, such as Rawal,
Bharat S et al. did in Cybersecurity and Identity Access Management [5]. The following
contributions have been extracted from it:
• Classification of 7 categories of malware and their characteristics.
• General hints for protection against malware, that will be useful to propose the coun-
termeasures.
• Malware analysis techniques and tools, that will be considered to perform the later
analysis.
Despite their classification counts of up to seven varieties, in the present paper just the
top three, in a current landscape relevant criteria, will be analyzed, this top is formed by:
• Ransomware: a particularly notable and pervasive form of malware that encrypts files
on infected systems and demands a ransom payment in exchange for their release.
• Spyware: on the other hand, is designed to covertly monitor and collect sensitive
information, often compromising user privacy and security.

• Worms, on the other hand, are self-replicating programs that spread across networks,

Modern malware has evolved, employing tactics such as code obfuscation and encryption
to evade detection. Fileless malware operates in memory, making it difficult to detect and
leaving minimal traces. Exploit kits and zero-day vulnerabilities enable targeted attacks,
automating the exploitation of software weaknesses. Cybercriminals also use anti-analysis
techniques like obfuscation and encryption to obscure their code, hindering analysis efforts.
Awareness of these evolving malware techniques is crucial for developing effective counter-
measures and enhancing cybersecurity defenses.
The proliferation of malware is further fueled by the emergence of Malware-as-a-Service
(MaaS) platforms.
These underground services provide cybercriminals with access to a
range of malware tools, infrastructure, and even technical support, democratizing the ability
to launch sophisticated attacks. Ransomware-as-a-Service (RaaS) is a prominent example,
where cybercriminals offer ransomware variants as a service, enabling less technically skilled
individuals to participate in ransomware campaigns.
Mitigating the threats posed by modern malware requires a multi-faceted approach.
Traditional detection methods, such as signature-based scanning and behavior-based anal-
ysis, continue to play a crucial role in identifying known threats. However, the dynamic
nature of modern malware necessitates the development of advanced techniques, including
machine learning, sandboxing, threat intelligence sharing, and deception-based defenses, to
enhance detection and response capabilities.
Understanding the characteristics, behaviors, and evolution of modern malware is vital
for developing effective countermeasures. By staying informed about the latest trends and
techniques employed by malware authors, security professionals can better defend against
and mitigate the impact of these malicious entities.
3.1.2
Emerging malware threats
The evolving landscape of modern malware is characterized by the emergence of new
and sophisticated threats that pose significant risks to individuals, organizations, and the
overall cybersecurity ecosystem. Understanding these emerging malware threats is crucial
for developing effective countermeasures and mitigating their impact. This section explores
some of the notable emerging malware threats:
Ransomware
Ransomware has gained prominence as one of the most prevalent and damaging forms
of malware in recent years. It encrypts files on infected systems, rendering them inaccessible
to users, and demands a ransom payment, typically in cryptocurrency, in exchange for the

decryption key. Ransomware attacks have targeted individuals, businesses, and even critical
infrastructure, causing substantial financial losses and operational disruptions. Notable ex-
amples include the WannaCry and NotPetya attacks, which affected organizations globally.
Ransomware operators have also adopted new tactics, such as double extortion, where stolen
data is threatened to be released if the ransom is not paid. The evolution of ransomware
techniques, including the use of advanced encryption algorithms and targeted campaigns,
underscores the need for effective prevention, detection, and incident response strategies.
Spyware
Spyware represents a growing concern in the realm of modern malware. It encompasses
malicious software designed to clandestinely monitor and collect sensitive information from
infected systems without the knowledge or consent of the user. Spyware often infiltrates
systems through deceptive means, such as email attachments or malicious downloads. Once
installed, it can log keystrokes, capture screenshots, record audio, and video, and steal per-
sonal or confidential data. Spyware poses significant privacy risks and can lead to identity
theft, financial fraud, or corporate espionage. Its pervasive nature makes it challenging to
detect and remove, requiring advanced anti-spyware tools and user education to mitigate
the threat.
Worms
Worms are self-replicating malware that spread across networks, exploiting vulnerabil-
ities in systems and devices to propagate and cause damage. Unlike viruses, worms do not
require a host file to infect systems, enabling them to rapidly spread and impact a large
number of devices. Worms can consume network resources, disrupt services, and compro-
mise the stability of infrastructure. They often utilize various propagation methods, such
as exploiting unpatched vulnerabilities or leveraging social engineering techniques to entice
users into executing them. Notable examples include the infamous Conficker worm, which
infected millions of devices worldwide. Detecting and mitigating worm attacks necessitate
timely software updates, network segmentation, intrusion detection systems, and user edu-
cation to prevent unintentional worm propagation.
It has to be highlighted that there is not always a clear barrier that separates all cate-
gories and classifies all the pieces into one of them. Most modern malware combines charac-
teristics from different typologies, for example a worm that wants to spread inside a system
but once it has established a foothold in several systems inside a network, it starts capturing
data and sending it to the cybercriminals as conventional spyware would do. A good example
of this is the classification that one of the tools did to one of the samples that was introduced
for its analysis. As the following figure 3.1 shows, a sample is categorized as multiple of the
previously mentioned categories.

3.1.3
Advanced Malware Techniques
Modern malware has evolved to employ sophisticated techniques that enhance its stealth,
evasion, and persistence capabilities. These advanced techniques enable malware to bypass
traditional security measures and pose significant challenges for detection and mitigation.
This section explores some of the notable advanced malware techniques.
Understanding these advanced malware techniques is essential for developing effective
detection and mitigation strategies. By staying informed about the latest advancements in
malware techniques, security professionals can adapt their defenses and implement proactive
measures to detect and respond to these sophisticated threats.
Polymorphism and Metamorphism
Polymorphic and metamorphic malware employ code obfuscation and encryption tech-
niques to alter their appearance and behavior. A further explanation of not just these two
variants but also other sophisticated techniques and ways of detecting them can be found in a
chapter in the book Ciso’s Next Frontier[6]. The following contribution have been extracted
from it:
• What polymorphic and metamorphic malware is and the basics of their behavior (ex-
plained below).
Polymorphic malware generates unique variations of its code, making it difficult to
detect using traditional signature-based antivirus solutions. Metamorphic malware goes a
step further by rewriting its code on the fly, changing its structure, and making it even more
challenging to analyze. These techniques enable malware to evade detection by antivirus
software and ruin reverse engineering efforts.
Fileless Malware
In "Analysis of Fileless Malware and its Evasive Behavior", the intricacies of this kind
of malicious software and the way it performs its actions are described in depth. Fileless

Exploit Kits and Zero-Day Exploits
Malware authors often take advantage of exploit kits and zero-day vulnerabilities to
launch targeted attacks, such toolkits represent a growing threat that is explored in "ZEKI:
Unsupervised zero-day exploit kit intelligence" [8], which also investigates possible counter-
measures. The following contribution have been extracted from it:
• What exploit kits (or toolkits) are, and their characteristics (explained below).
Exploit kits are toolkits that contain pre-built malicious code, allowing attackers to
automate the exploitation of vulnerabilities in commonly used software. By leveraging ex-
ploit kits, cybercriminals can infect systems with malware without requiring deep technical
expertise. Zero-day vulnerabilities refer to previously unknown vulnerabilities for which no
patch or defense exists at the time of their discovery. Attackers exploit these vulnerabilities
before a fix is released, giving them a significant advantage. The use of exploit kits and
zero-day exploits underscores the importance of timely software updates and vulnerability
management.
Anti-Analysis Techniques
In the following paper [9], deep research about anti-analysis techniques is conducted
with real malware samples, and a detection tool for this kind of activities is presented in
order to fight them. The following contributions have been extracted from it:

• A list of anti-analysis techniques, many of which will be also found in the own analysis
of this project.
To hinder analysis and detection efforts, malware authors employ various anti-analysis
techniques. These techniques include using virtual machine detection, sandbox evasion, and
anti-debugging methods. Malware may check for the presence of virtualized environments

Sandbox evasion techniques allow malware to detect if it is running in a

Advanced Command-and-Control (C2) Techniques
Malware utilizes advanced command-and-control (C2) techniques to establish commu-
nication channels with remote servers, enabling cybercriminals to control and coordinate
their malicious activities. These techniques include the use of domain generation algorithms
(DGAs) to dynamically generate domain names for C2 servers, making it challenging to
block or disrupt communication. Malware may also leverage peer-to-peer (P2P) networks or
hidden communication channels, such as DNS or HTTP protocols, to evade detection and
bypass network security measures. Jovanović, Ðorđe D et al. present a practical analysis
of the dynamic behavior and command and control channels of two classes of IoT botnets,

• How such an infrastructure can be detected, which is useful information for the coun-
termeasures section.
3.1.4
Malware-as-a-Service (MaaS)
The emergence of Malware-as-a-Service (MaaS) platforms has significantly altered the
scene of cybercrime. MaaS refers to underground services that offer malware tools, infras-
tructure, and support to individuals with malicious intent. These platforms allow aspiring
cybercriminals to access a wide range of malware variants, exploit kits, and even technical
assistance, regardless of their technical expertise. MaaS has democratized cybercrime by
lowering the barriers to entry and enabling less technically skilled individuals to participate
in malicious activities.
In the specific case of ransomware as a service, Per Håkon Meland et al., conducted
research [11] across dark web forums to find the actual level of threat that Ransomware as a
service (Raas) currently suppose. Despite the conclusion of the study that the current threat
was not as big as rumors may suggest, the most likely scenario is that the general landscape
of all variations of Maas will be growing as the time passes.The following contribution have
been extracted from it:
• Context of the current landscape and utilization of Raas (extrapolated to Maas and
explained in this section).

vide a user-friendly interface through which individuals can create, customize, and distribute

liferation of ransomware attacks, as it allows a wide range of actors to initiate ransomware
campaigns without needing advanced technical skills.
MaaS platforms not only facilitate the distribution of malware but also provide ad-
ditional features such as bulletproof hosting, anonymization services, and command-and-
control infrastructure. These offerings make it challenging for law enforcement agencies and
cybersecurity professionals to track and mitigate the activities of cybercriminals.
The rise of MaaS has contributed to the rapid evolution of malware and the frequent
emergence of new variants.
The availability of pre-built malware tools and services en-
ables cybercriminals to quickly adapt and respond to security measures and exploit newly
discovered vulnerabilities. Furthermore, the commercialization of malware has created a
competitive market, with developers constantly seeking innovative techniques to bypass se-
curity measures and maximize their illicit gains.
Combating the threat posed by MaaS requires a multi-pronged approach. It involves
collaboration between law enforcement agencies, cybersecurity professionals, and the tech-
nology industry to identify and dismantle MaaS platforms. Additionally, efforts should focus
on educating users about the risks associated with participating in MaaS activities and im-
plementing robust security measures to detect and mitigate malware propagated through
these platforms.
Understanding the inner workings of MaaS, its business models, and the techniques
employed by cybercriminals to distribute malware is crucial for developing effective counter-
measures. By staying alert to the evolving MaaS landscape, security professionals can better
anticipate and respond to emerging threats, helping to safeguard individuals and organiza-
tions from the malicious activities facilitated by these underground services.

Usage of packers (7 instances)
The most commonly detected action observed in sandbox analysis of malware is the
utilization of packers. Packers serve as obfuscation methods employed by malware authors
to compress and encrypt executables and other content, rendering them more challenging to
analyze. By employing packers, malware seeks to evade detection and hinder the efforts of
security analysts and researchers, also, they hide the malicious part of the software that would
trigger the antivirus and bypass it appearing to be legitimate software. These obfuscation
techniques modify the structure and composition of the malware, making it harder to identify
and understand its underlying code and functionality. The prevalence of packers in detected
malware samples underscores the significance of obfuscation as a defensive measure employed
by cybercriminals.
Anti debugger measures (5 instances)
The incorporation of anti-debugging techniques in malware has become widespread, as
it aims to hinder the analysis and reverse engineering efforts of security professionals. By
checking for the presence of debuggers or other analysis tools, the malware attempts to
evade scrutiny and alter its behavior when detected. These anti-debugging measures pose
challenges for researchers in understanding the malware’s functionality and intent.
Data encryption (5 instances)
This action is particularly associated with ransomware, as it has the distinct character-
istic of encrypting the victim’s data. The encryption of data in ransomware attacks involves
the use of sophisticated cryptographic algorithms to lock the victim’s files, rendering them
inaccessible without the decryption key held by the attackers. This process occurs at a low
level, targeting specific files based on predefined criteria such as file extensions or paths.
Once the ransomware gains access to the system, it generates a unique encryption key to
encrypt the selected files. The original files are modified, transforming their contents into
an unreadable format.
As the encryption of data is normally the main goal of all ransomware malware pieces,
this is a clear example of what was commented on in the introduction of this section 5, when

6.1. General countermeasures
Endpoint Protection
Such solutions, such as antivirus and anti-malware software, are designed to detect and
block malicious software from compromising endpoints. These solutions employ signature-

antine known and unknown malware. By continuously monitoring and scanning endpoints,

Patch Management
Keeping software applications and operating systems up to date is critical for maintain-
ing a secure environment. Patch management involves regularly applying security patches
and updates released by software vendors. By addressing vulnerabilities and weaknesses,
patch management minimizes the chances of malware exploiting known security flaws in
software or the exploit of recently discovered zero-day vulnerabilities, thus reducing the risk
of successful attacks.
Principle of Least Privilege
The principle of least privilege advocates granting users and processes only the mini-
mum permissions necessary to perform their tasks. By limiting user privileges and access
rights, organizations can reduce the potential impact of malware. Malicious software often
attempts to escalate privileges to gain greater control over systems. By implementing strict
user access controls, organizations can mitigate the risk of malware gaining unauthorized
access and spreading across the network.
Application Whitelisting
Application whitelisting is a security measure that allows only authorized and trusted
applications to run on systems, blocking the execution of any unauthorized or suspicious
software. By creating a list of approved applications, the execution of malicious programs
can be prevented. This approach helps in reducing the attack surface and protecting against
the installation and execution of unknown or malicious software.
Backups
Regularly backing up critical data is an essential countermeasure against malware at-
tacks, particularly those involving data encryption or destruction. Backups provide a means
to restore compromised or lost data in the event of a successful malware attack. By main-
taining up-to-date backups, organizations can mitigate the impact of ransomware and other
data-related malware attacks. It is important to note that backups should be isolated from
the system once created, to avoid the attack to pivot into them.

User Account Control (UAC) is a security feature implemented in modern operating
systems, including Windows, to protect against unauthorized system changes and malicious
activities. UAC helps prevent unauthorized software or processes from making changes that
could potentially harm the system or compromise its security.
When User Account Control is enabled, a user is prompted with a consent or credential
prompt whenever a program attempts to make changes that require administrative privileges.
This prompt serves as a confirmation and allows the user to authorize or deny the requested
action.
By default, standard user accounts operate with limited privileges and elevated
privileges are required for certain system-level actions.
Suspicious buffer activity
When the malware is utilizing some suspicious buffers there are some actions that can
be used against it:
• Data Execution Prevention (DEP): Prevents the execution of code from non-executable
memory regions, safeguarding against buffer overflow and code injection attacks.
• Address Space Layout Randomization (ASLR): Randomizes memory layouts to de-
ter attackers from predicting critical memory addresses, reducing the risk of memory
corruption exploits.
• Stack Protection Mechanisms: Add checks and safeguards to detect and prevent stack-
based buffer overflows, preserving data integrity and preventing arbitrary code execu-
tion.

The findings of this research suggest that the utilization of state-of-the-art malware

techniques presented by the samples, which would be nearly impossible to do with similar
efficacy in the scope of a project as the present one. This finding underscores the importance
of leveraging established analysis tools to enhance the accuracy and efficiency of malware
analysis efforts.

employing multiple of these to attain comprehensive and accurate insights into malware

7.4
Future work: Result validation
To evaluate the effectiveness of the proposed countermeasures against different malware
actions, an experimental approach can be employed. The goal would be to assess the im-
pact of the countermeasures on mitigating the behaviors exhibited by malware samples in
a controlled environment. The evaluation methodology consists of organizing experiments
and validating the results through various steps.
7.4.1
Experiment organization
In the first place, a diverse selection of real-world malware samples representing vari-
ous categories as the ones analyzed in this report (ransomware, worms, spyware) would be
selected. These samples may be used to simulate attacks in a controlled environment.
Secondly, two identical virtualized environments can be set up, mirroring real-world
computing systems. These environments may include different operating systems, applica-
tions, and network configurations. The environments will be isolated from the production
network to prevent any actual impact on the organization’s assets. The goal of the environ-
ments would be to become a malware-testing sandbox.
Afterward, the proposed countermeasures in the previous section 6 would be imple-
mented in one of the experimental environments. This includes configuring security tools,
applying patches, and configuring network segmentation.
Finally, Each malware sample would be executed within the controlled environments to
observe its behavior and actions they take on each of them. Monitoring tools may capture the
actions performed by the malware, including attempts to discover files, establish command
and control, modify system certificates, and the consequences of the execution, among others.
