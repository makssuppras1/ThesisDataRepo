Abstract 
III 
Abstract 
This master thesis describes the development and optimization of a new synthetic strategy for the 
formation of (E)-stilbenes from benzyl alcohols and aryl acetonitriles under basic conditions (Scheme I). 
Stilbenes are an important compound class, and their derivatives find application in small molecule 
drugs and materials. Various synthesis strategies for the formation of stilbenes are known. However, 
many lack stereoselectivity, require toxic reagents or suffer from poor atom efficiency.[1]  
The mechanism of the reaction was explored and found to proceed via an autocatalytic Meerwein-
Ponndorf-Verley reduction/Oppenauer oxidation redox type process followed by an E1cB elimination 
of the cyanide, yielding (E)-stilbene. 
In addition, a simple two-step one-pot synthesis was developed to enable the use of benzyl chlorides as 
starting materials (Scheme II). They are converted to aryl acetonitriles via a substitution reaction with 
potassium cyanide. The subsequent addition of potassium tert-butoxide and benzyl alcohol leads to (E)-
stilbene formation. 
 
Scheme I: Reaction scheme for the formation of (E)-stilbene from benzyl alcohol and phenyl acetonitrile under 
basic conditions. 
 
Scheme II: Reaction scheme for the two-step-one-pot synthesis strategy.

Introduction 
1.1 
Project aim 
The formation of C-C bonds is an essential part of organic chemistry. Over the last decades a 
development towards greener and more sustainable procedures has taken place. Multiple methods for α-
alkylation of different functionalities using alcohols as the alkylating agents have been developed, 
replacing the traditionally used alkyl halides. A paper published by Azizi and Madsen in 2020 reported 
the α-alkylation of acetamides using benzylic alcohols through a radical condensation.[2] This was a 
novel reactivity, and it was shown, that many differently substituted benzyl alcohols and acetamides 
could be used. Following this discovery, it was desired to expand the substrate scope of this radical α-
alkylation to other functionalities like esters, alkenes, and nitriles. During a project in the fall of 2020, 
these possibilities were investigated. It was discovered that when benzyl alcohol was reacted with phenyl 
acetonitrile not α-alkylation of the nitrile could be observed, but formation of (E)-stilbene (Scheme 1). 
So far, this reactivity has not been reported in the literature.  
Stilbenes are an important compound class. Their scaffolds occur in biologically active compounds and 
materials due to their photophysical and photochemical properties. Current methods for the synthesis of 
stilbenes require the use of catalysts, produce toxic byproducts and waste or lack stereoselectivity. With 
this newly developed method a base is the only additive required, and (E)-stilbenes can be synthesized 
stereoselectively through a simple reaction. 
 
Scheme 1: Reaction scheme for the formation of (E)-stilbenes from benzyl alcohol and phenyl acetonitrile. 
The goal of this master thesis was the optimization of the reaction conditions and the mechanistic 
investigation of the newly discovered reactivity between benzylic alcohols and aryl acetonitriles forming 
(E)-stilbenes.

1.2 
Synthesis of Stilbenes 
Stilbenes are important scaffolds in biologically active compounds as well as in materials. Several 
different strategies can be utilized to synthesize stilbenes. All these strategies involve the connection of 
two arenes through an ethylene linker.[1] In the following chapter the most important methods for their 
synthesis are explained. An emphasis is put on the understanding of stereoselectivity of the reactions 
since (E)- and (Z)-stilbenes find different applications. 
1.2.1 The Wittig Reaction 
The reactivity between a carbonyl compound and a phosphonium ylide forming an alkene was first 
reported in 1953 by Wittig and Geissler (Scheme 2).[3] Today, the method is commonly used for the 
synthesis of a wide arrange of alkenes. This method is preferred since the reaction occurs under mild 
conditions and the starting materials are easily synthesized and sometimes commercially available. In 
addition, the reaction is regiospecific and tolerates other functional groups making it useful in the 
synthesis of large and complex molecules.[4] The stereoselectivity of the reaction can be directed through 
the choice of the reactants and in particular the choice of the phosphonium ylide.[4,5]  Phosphonium ylides 
are classified based on the substituent attached to their α-carbon (R4 in Scheme 2). Ylides with alkyl 
(Alk) substituents on the α-carbon are termed non-stabilized ylides and their reactions with aldehydes 
yield predominantly (Z)-alkenes. In contrast to that, ylides in which R4 is a conjugating and electron 
withdrawing group (EWG) like a carbonyl, ester, or nitrile, are called stabilized ylides and favor the 
formation of (E)-alkenes. The third class of phosphonium ylides are semi-stabilized ylides, in which the 
substituent is either an aryl or alkenyl group. For these ylides low selectivity between (E)- and (Z)-
alkenes is observed.[5] The stereoselectivity of semi-stabilized ylides can be influenced by the 
phosphorous-substituents of the ylide. Substituting the usual P-phenyl groups partially with alkyl groups 
or using a methyldibenzophosphole can lead to high E-selectivity.[4,6] 
 
Scheme 2: Wittig reaction of a carbonyl group with a phosphonium ylide yielding (E)- or (Z)-alkenes and 
phosphine oxide. 
Even though the Wittig reaction is widely used, the mechanism has long been debated.[4] Johnson 
described eight proposals in his review of the Wittig reaction in 1993.[7] Originally, Wittig and 
Schöllkopf proposed a mechanism involving the formation of a betaine as an intermediate through 
nucleophilic addition of the ylide to the carbonyl, followed by ring closure leading to an oxaphosphetane 
(OPA), (Scheme 3A).[4,8] This mechanistic proposal was widely accepted for a long time and can still be 
==PAGE:13==
Introduction 
3 
found in many organic chemistry textbooks.[4] A different mechanism was proposed by Vedejs et al.. In 
this mechanism, the alkene is formed through a [2+2] cycloaddition of a carbonyl and an ylide followed 
by a syn-elimination and it is the basis for the modern day understanding of the reaction mechanism 
(Scheme 3B).[4,9] 
 
Scheme 3: Proposed mechanisms for the Wittig reaction. A) Betaine mechanism originally proposed by Wittig 
and Schöllkopf. B) Cycloaddition mechanism favored today, originally proposed by Vedejs. 
The stereoselectivity of the Wittig reaction can be explained by analyzing the transition state (TS) of 
OPA formation (Figure 1). An interplay of steric 1,2 and 1,3 interactions, dipole-dipole interactions, and 
C-H···O hydrogen bonding influence the puckering of the PCCO dihedral angle and the TS geometry.[10]  
Non-stabilized ylides have an early TS, where the P-O bond as well as the C-C bond are elongated, the 
P-phenyl groups are in a pseudo-tetrahedral conformation and the PCCO dihedral angle is puckered 
positively (Figure 1). The steric 1,2 interactions are minimized between the carbonyl substituent (R1) 
and the ylide substituent (Alk) as well as those between R1 and the P-phenyl groups. In addition, the 
puckering enables the P-O bond formation, avoiding the P-phenyl group projected into the direction of 
the carbonyl. This TS will lead to the formation of the cis-OPA followed by syn-elimination giving the 
(Z)-alkene, which is the product of the Wittig reaction of non-stabilized ylides. The corresponding less 
puckered trans-selective TS is less favored since it would lead to steric 1,3 interactions between a P-
phenyl group and R1 in addition to an interaction between a P-phenyl group and the approaching 
carbonyl oxygen. Thus, the Wittig reaction for non-stabilized ylides is Z-selective.[4,10] 
Semi-stabilized ylides are generally not very selective for one isomer. This is due to a reduced puckering 
angle in the TS for formation of the cis-OPA. 1,2 interactions become more important in the TS of semi-
stabilized ylides leading to a balance between 1,2 and 1,3 interactions in the cis- and trans- selective 
TS, respectively. This leads to an overall low selectivity.[10]  
Stabilized ylides go through a later TS during OPA formation. In the trans TS the carbonyl group is 
puckered negatively, leading to an antiparallel orientation of the polarized C-O and C-EWG bonds, 
==PAGE:14==

which is electrostatically favorable. Furthermore, the 1,2 steric interactions are minimized compared to 
the cis TS. Therefore, the favored TS for stabilized ylides is trans-selective, yielding the corresponding 
trans-OPA and (E)-alkene.[4,10] 
 
Figure 1: Cis- and trans selective TSs of OPA formation for non-stabilized, semi-stabilized and stabilized ylides 
during the Wittig reaction. Unfavorable 1,2 and 1,3 interactions and relevant dipoles are indicated.[10] 
For the synthesis of stilbenes through the Wittig reaction, semi-stabilized ylides are formed. Therefore, 
in most cases both stereoisomers are found as products (Scheme 4). They can usually be separated easily 
by crystallization. Modification of the phosphine substituents and the substituents of the arenes can 
influence, which isomer is predominantly formed.[11–13] 
 
Scheme 4: Wittig reaction of benzaldehyde and benzyl triphenylphosphonium chloride forming (E)-stilbene and 
(Z)-stilbene with a ratio of 30/70.[12]

1.2.2 The Horner-Wadsworth-Emmons Reaction 
Another method commonly used to synthesize stilbenes is the Horner-Wadsworth-Emmons (HWE) 
reaction (Scheme 5).[1,14] The method exhibits several advantages compared to the Wittig reaction: the 
phosphonates used are more reactive, making reactions with a wider variety of aldehydes and ketones 
under milder conditions possible. Furthermore, high E-selectivity is observed.[15,16] Lastly, the workup 
of the HWE reaction is easier compared to that of the Wittig reaction, since the obtained phosphate is 
water soluble and can hence be easily separated from the reaction mixture.[17] However, only 
phosphonates with α-substituents stabilizing carbanions such as esters, nitriles, aryl groups and sulfones 
can be used in this reaction. Alkyl substituted phosphonates lead to poor yields.[18] 
The mechanism of the HWE reaction is related to that of the Wittig reaction and has similarly been 
debated.[15] Several experimental and computational studies conclude that an oxyanion intermediate is 
formed, followed by OPA formation in a comparable way to the betaine mechanism of the Wittig 
reaction.[18–20] Recent developments favor a concerted mechanism for OPA formation, as it has been 
proven for the Wittig reaction.[15] However, to this date the true mechanism of the HWE reaction is still 
subject to debate. 
 
Scheme 5: Mechanism of the HWE reaction. 
The high E-selectivity of the reaction is explained by an equilibrium between the cis- and the trans-
OPA, leading to an increased formation of the thermodynamically favored trans-OPA and hence the 
(E)-alkene (Scheme 5).[17,21] The stereoselectivity of the reaction can be adjusted by varying the 
substituents on the phosphonate moiety, lowering the reaction temperature, and utilizing additives. Two 
modifications of the HWE reaction have been developed: The Still-Gennari and the Ando modification. 
These allow for the selective synthesis of (Z)-alkenes, utilizing bis(2,2,2-trifluoroethyl) phosphonates 
and diaryl phosphonates, respectively (Scheme 6).[22,23]  
==PAGE:16==
Introduction 
6 
 
Scheme 6: Reaction scheme for the Still-Gennari and Ando modification of the HWE reaction. 
In the Still-Gennari modification the electron withdrawing property of the 2,2,2-trifluoroethyl groups is 
crucial. If the erythro TS is formed, leading to formation of the cis-OPA, they will occupy the axial 
positions of the pentacoordinate OPA phosphonate and thereby have a stabilizing effect. Kinetic control 
will lead to formation of the (Z)-alkene as the primary product. Additionally, crown ethers can lead to 
higher yields and the selection of an appropriate base is vital for obtaining the (Z)-alkene selectively.[17]  
In the Ando modification the Z-selectivity of the reaction is explained by computational and 
experimental results. Ando found that the TS for formation of the oxyanion intermediate is lower for the 
erythro intermediate, since steric interactions between the aldehyde substituent and the large P-aryl 
groups are minimized. The Z-selectivity obtained will increase with increasing size of the aryl 
groups.[23,24]  
The stilbenes formed via the HWE reaction generally exhibit an E-configuration.[1] The stereoselective 
synthesis of (Z)-stilbenes utilizing modifications of the HWE reaction has not been reported in the 
literature. 
1.2.3 Transition Metal Coupling Reactions 
Over the last decades transition metal coupling reactions have gained great importance for selective C-
C bond formation. This includes applications within the field of stilbene synthesis. Especially the 
Mizoroki-Heck and Suzuki cross coupling reactions are favored due to their versatility and efficiency.[25] 
Additionally, other transition metal coupling reactions, like the Sonogashira reaction, McMurry 
coupling, and alkene metathesis find application in the synthesis of stilbenes.[1,26] 
The Mizoroki-Heck reaction was developed and published by Mizoroki and Heck independently in 1971 
and 1972, respectively. They described the arylation of an olefine through catalytic palladium cross 
coupling with the addition of a base. (E)-Stilbene was among the first compounds synthesized by this 
method.[27] The catalytic cycle for the reaction is presented in Scheme 7. In a first step the catalytically 
active Pd0 complex undergoes oxidative addition with an aryl halide. Afterwards the double bond of the 
alkene coordinates to the palladium resulting in a π-complex. A syn-migratory insertion occurs in the 
next step followed by subsequent β-hydride elimination, leading to a new π-complex with the coupling 
product coordinated to palladium by the double bond. The product then dissociates, and the formed 
==PAGE:17==
Introduction 
7 
hydrido palladium complex undergoes reductive elimination with the added base. This leads to the 
regeneration of the catalytically active Pd0 complex. The Mizoroki-Heck reaction stereoselectively 
forms (E)-stilbenes because the substituents will align in the thermodynamically favorable way before 
β-hydride elimination occurs to reduce torsional strain.[28] 
  
Scheme 7: Mechanism of the Mizoroki-Heck reaction for the formation of stilbenes.  
Another commonly used method is the Suzuki cross-coupling reaction, which employs a Pd0 catalyst 
and a base for the coupling of aryl halides to organoboron species, as shown in Scheme 8. The reaction 
is highly selective, and many functional groups are tolerated.[29] In the first step, oxidative addition of 
the palladium catalyst to the aryl halide occurs. The addition of base to the reaction leads to ligand 
substitution. Then transmetalation with the organoboronate forms an organopalladium species, which 
yields the cross-coupling product after reductive elimination. The active catalytic palladium complex is 
then reformed.[30]

Scheme 8: Mechanism of the Suzuki reaction for the formation of stilbenes. 
Both E- and Z-selective Suzuki coupling reactions for the formation of stilbene derivatives have been 
developed. Cella et al. published a method using organotellurides instead of halides and Ag2O as an 
additive to selectively form (E)- or (Z)-stilbenes (Scheme 9).[25]  
 
Scheme 9: Reaction schemes for selective formation of (E)- and (Z)-stilbenes through Suzuki cross coupling.[25] 
When using the Sonogashira reaction to synthesize stilbenes, a second reductive step is required, to 
reduce the alkyne formed as the cross-coupling product selectively into the (E)- or (Z)-alkene 
(Scheme 10).[1] The Sonogashira cross coupling requires the addition of a copper(I) salt and a base to 
the catalytically active palladium complex. These additives are necessary since the terminal alkyne 
==PAGE:19==
Introduction 
9 
needs to be activated through the formation of a cuprate in the first step. This cuprate then reacts with 
the palladium complex, which has undergone oxidative addition with the aryl halide, through 
transmetalation. Reductive elimination leads to the formation of the alkyne product and the active Pd0 
complex is regenerated.[31] Careful selection of the reduction reaction conditions can lead to exclusive 
formation of one stilbene isomer. Reduction using LiAlH4 and diglyme gives an E/Z mixture. 
Subsequent diphenyl sulfide assisted isomerization of the mixture leads to the (E)-stilbene being formed 
exclusively, since it is thermodynamically more stable. Two methods for the reduction of 1,1’-
ethynedibenzene to (Z)-stilbenes exist: heterogeneous reduction using Lindlar’s catalyst or 
hydrosilylation through the Chalk-Haword mechanism followed by TBAF mediated conversion of the 
vinyl silanes to (Z)-stilbene.[1] 
 
Scheme 10: Reaction scheme for the formation of (E)- and (Z)-stilbenes through Sonogashira coupling and 
subsequent reduction. 
The reductive dimerization of aldehydes and ketones to alkenes is generally known as the McMurry 
coupling reaction. Titanium salts such as TiCl3 and TiCl4 in addition to a reducing agent are used for the 
reaction. However, the reaction can only be applied for the synthesis of symmetrical stilbenes. Cross 
coupling reactions lead to the formation of statistical mixtures of the products. The only exception to 
this is the coupling of diaryl ketones with an aldehyde or ketone. In this case, the product stemming 
from cross-coupling is the major product. The effect of this exception is applied in the synthesis of the 
drug tamoxifen.[29] The McMurry reaction leads to formation of (E)-stilbenes in most case. Z-selectivity 
can be achieved if geometric constraints are put on the coupling partners.[29,32] 
Another possible strategy for the synthesis of stilbenes is by alkene cross metathesis using Grubbs 
catalyst. However, the control over the selectivity is low, leading to homo- and cross-metathesis 
products, and making only the synthesis of symmetrical stilbenes possible.[29]

1.2.4 Other Methods 
Stilbenes can additionally be synthesized through other methods, such as the Perkin reaction and aldol 
type condensations.[26] 
The Perkin reaction describes the formation of α-aryl cinnamic acids from benzaldehydes and phenyl 
acetic acids in the presence of a base and acetic anhydride. Afterwards decarboxylation using quinoline 
and copper chromite yields stilbene. The reaction leads to formation of (Z)-stilbenes in most cases 
(Scheme 11).[33–35] However, the use of toxic quinoline and copper chromite in the decarboxylation step 
makes this synthesis strategy less attractive. Recently, a two-step one-pot microwave activated reaction 
has been developed leading to the formation of (E)-stilbenes via the Perkin reaction. This method does 
not require the use of quinoline and copper chromite. Instead piperidine and methylimidazole are used. 
This method is a greener alternative to the classic Perkin reaction.[34] 
 
Scheme 11: Reaction scheme for formation of (Z)-stilbenes through Perkin reaction and decarboxylation. 
For the formation of stilbenes through aldol type condensations as shown in Scheme 12, a methylarene 
needs to be deprotonated at the benzylic position. This stabilized carbanion attacks the electrophilic 
carbonyl-carbon leading to C-C bond formation. The alcohol formed during this step is eliminated as 
water, leading to formation of stilbene.[26] 
 
Scheme 12: Reaction scheme of aldol type condensation for the formation of stilbenes. 
1.3 
Applications of Stilbenes 
1.3.1 Biologically Active Stilbenes 
Stilbene itself is not a natural product, however, stilbene scaffolds exhibit biological activity and are 
found in biological matter such as plants. They can be antimicrobial, cytotoxic, anti-inflammatory, and 
can inhibit cell proliferation. Both (E)- and (Z)-stilbene scaffolds are utilized in the design of small 
molecule drugs.[25,26,36] Figure 2 shows three stilbene derivatives, that show biological activities. 
==PAGE:21==
Introduction 
11 
 
Figure 2: Structures of biologically active stilbene derivatives: Resveratrol, combretastatin A-4, and tamoxifen. 
Resveratrol is a compound, which is produced by plants as an antimicrobial phytoalexin. It can be found 
in berries, peanuts, grapes, and wine and has gained attention for its cardioprotective, chemoprotective, 
and neuroprotective activities. Studies have shown that resveratrol is able to enhance the lifespan of 
yeast, flies, worms, and fish, making it a potential anti-aging medicine. So far, the remarkable properties 
of resveratrol, and how the molecule induces these effects, are still a matter of discussion. Many studies 
concerning resveratrol are still ongoing.[37–39] The synthesis of resveratrol can be achieved via multiple 
reactions. Among those are the Mizoroki-Heck coupling, Wittig, HWE, and Perkin reaction.[40] 
Combretastatin A-4 is part of the family of combretastatins all containing a trimethoxy substituted “A”-
ring and a “B”-ring often substituted at the 3’ and 4’ position. The rings are connected through an ethene-
linker, which can be in the E- or Z-configuration. Combretastatin A-4 is substituted with a hydroxy 
group in the 3’ position and a methoxy group in the 4’ position. It shows higher activity as an inhibitor 
of tubulin polymerization and mitosis as the Z-isomer compared to the E-isomer. The compound is 
administered as a phosphate prodrug and activated in the body through dephosphorylation at the 
3’ position.[11,26,38] Stereoselective synthesis of combretastatin A-4 is crucial since only the Z-isomer 
shows high potency. The drug has been synthesized using the Wittig reaction, but only low 
stereoselectivity was achieved. Slightly better selectivity was observed when an alkyne precursor was 
reduced using a Lindlar-type reduction. The Perkin reaction is the most successful in the stereoselective 
synthesis of combretastatin A-4 as it shows high Z-selectivity. An E/Z ratio of 95/5 could be achieved.[41] 
Tamoxifen is a selective estrogen receptor modulator and was developed in the early 1970s. Today it is 
the world’s largest selling hormonal drug for the treatment of breast cancer. It is additionally used for 
chemoprevention of breast cancer. Tamoxifen decreases recurrence of cancer and improves the long-
term survival. Two metabolites of tamoxifen lead to its potency: (Z)-4-hydroxytamoxifen and (Z)-4-
hydroxy-N-desmethyltamoxifen (endoxifen), which bind to the estrogen receptor of breast cancer 
cells.[38,42,43] Tamoxifen is commonly synthesized via McMurry coupling, Pd catalyzed cross coupling 
or an addition elimination process using ketones and Grignard reagents.[44]

1.3.2 Stilbene Materials 
Stilbenoid compounds find applications in material science due to their photophysical and 
photochemical properties. Their high quantum yield of fluorescence makes them employable as optical 
brighteners, scintillators, and laser dyes. At higher fluorescence quantum yields, less undesirable side 
reactions occur. The most prominent among these photoreactions is the E to Z isomerization, which is 
undesirable since (Z)-stilbenes are not fluorescent. In addition, stilbenoid compounds can act as 
sensitizers or quenchers for singlet oxygen, which is extremely reactive, leading to further side 
reactions.[26,45]  
Stilbenoids like the 4,4’-bis-(triazinylamino) stilbene-2,2’-disulfonate derivatives (Figure 3) are used as 
optical brighteners. They have strong UV light absorption with a maximum at wavelengths between 350 
and 375 nm and their fluorescence band lies in the range from 415 to 445 nm with fluorescence quantum 
yields close to 1. UV light is converted to visible light with a violet to blue-green color, which 
compensates the yellowing of textiles, papers, and plastics.[45,46] 
 
Figure 3: Generalized structure of 4,4’-bis-(triazinylamino) stilbene-2,2’-disulfonate derivatives used as optical 
brighteners.[45] 
These excellent fluorescence properties make their use interesting for dyes in dye lasers. Compared to 
gas or solid-state lasers, dye lasers can be used in a wider range of wave lengths and are tunable, meaning 
that the operational wavelength can be changed controllably. Additionally, extremely short pulses can 
be generated using dye lasers. The emission of dye lasers using stilbenoid compounds is usually limited 
to the violet to blue region. E to Z isomerization and subsequent photoreactions lead to dye fading over 
time. Changing the dye to a 1,4-distyrylbenzene based compound suppresses the unwanted 
isomerization.[45] 
Poly(arylene vinylene)s are semiconducting polymers containing a stilbenoid backbone (Figure 4). They 
find application in blue light-emitting diodes.[45] Upon electronic excitation, electrons are injected from 
the cathode moving to the lowest unoccupied molecular orbital (LUMO) of the emitting polymer, while 
holes are injected from the anode to the highest occupied molecular orbital (HOMO) of the polymer. 
Electrons and holes react in the semiconducting polymer, leading to light emission.[47] The introduction 
of side chains to the polymer backbone can be used to tune the emission wavelength and improve 
polymer processing.[48]

The photochemical processes of stilbenes find application in optical switches and optical data storage. 
Para-substituted (E)-stilbenes were among the first liquid crystal (LC) systems reported. Only small 
amounts of photochemical reactions are needed to break down nematic or smectic LC phases by 
disordering them and altering their optical properties. E to Z isomerization of stilbenoid compounds is

processes.[45] 
1.4 
α-Alkylation of Nitriles 
Nitriles are an important functional group in organic chemistry and can be found in pharmaceuticals and 
natural products. Their α-alkylation is useful in total synthesis since they can easily be converted into 
other functionalities, such as amines, amides, and carboxylic acids.[49,50] The α-protons of aliphatic 
nitriles have a pKa around 25. This makes the use of a strong base for their deprotonation necessary.[49] 
Nitriles can traditionally be alkylated using a base such as LDA in stochiometric amounts for the 
deprotonation and alkyl halides as the alkylating agents (Scheme 13).[51,52]  
 
Scheme 13: Traditional α-alkylation of nitriles using alkyl halides. 
Nitrile-stabilized anions are particularly good nucleophiles. The negative charge is stabilized through 
the inductive effect of the small, electron withdrawing nitrile functionality. Metalation of nitriles can 
generally take place on the nitrogen or on the formally charged α-carbon. A continuum spanning from 
N-metalated ketimines over N-metalated nitriles and C-metalated nitriles to a solvent-separated ion pair, 
only present in solution, describes the possible metalation sites most accurately (Figure 5). The type of 
metalation observed, depends on the metal, the ligands, and the solvent. Lithium and high-valent 
transition metals prefer N-metalation, whilst metals like magnesium and low-valent transition metals, 
capable of covalent bonding, favor C-metalation.[52]

1.4.1 The Hydrogen Borrowing Methodology 
Drawbacks of the reaction described in Scheme 13 are the use of stochiometric amounts of base, the 
production of waste and the toxicity of alkyl halides.[53] Therefore, efforts have been made to develop 
catalytic α-alkylation reactions using alcohols as the alkylating agents via a hydrogen borrowing 
mechanism (Scheme 14). At first, catalysts based on Ru[54], Rh[54], Os[55], and Ir[56] were developed, but 
due to their high cost and limited availability latest catalytic reactions are utilizing first-row transition 
metals including Mn[53], Fe[57], Co[58], and Ni[59]. These catalytic reactions use a low concentration of 
base, have a high atom economy, and produce water as the only byproduct (Scheme 14).[53]  
In the first step a hydrogen auto transfer from the alcohol to the metal occurs, leading to the formation 
of the corresponding aldehyde and a metal-hydride intermediate. Afterwards a condensation takes place 
with an α-deprotonated nitrile, forming an α,β-unsaturated nitrile. The formed double bond is reduced 
during the last step by hydrogenation in the presence of the metal-hydride.[50]

1.4.2 Meerwein-Ponndorf-Verley Reduction/Oppenauer Oxidation 
Recent developments show that it is possible to perform base-mediated alkylations of amines, alcohols, 
ketones, and nitriles without the use of transition metals. These reactions are proposed to proceed via 
Meerwein-Ponndorf-Verley reduction/Oppenauer oxidation (MPV-O) type redox processes.[60–63] 
The Meerwein-Ponndorf-Verley reduction was first published in 1925 by Meerwein and Schmidt using 
aluminum alkoxides in the presence of isopropanol. They describe a catalytic hydrogen transfer reaction

shown in Scheme 15. The Oppenauer oxidation is the corresponding reverse reaction.[64,65]

The mechanism of the MPV-O reaction proceeds through a six-membered cyclic TS, during which 
hydrogen transfer from the alcohol to the keto group occurs (Scheme 16).[66] The reaction can be carried 
out under mild conditions, making it very useful and widely applied.[67,68] However, stochiometric 
amounts of aluminum alkoxides are typically required. Different transition metal catalysts, mainly based 
on Ru, Rh, and Ir, have been developed to catalyze the hydrogen transfer more efficiently. Only small 
amounts of alkali bases are needed to activate the catalysts. Following these developments, first-row 
transition metals have been employed to catalyze the reaction.[68]

Discoveries by Ouali et al. suggest that MPV-O type reactions are possible using only catalytic amounts 
of cheap and easily available alkali bases without transition metal catalysts. They investigated whether 
metal contaminants in the alkali base were responsible for the observed reactivity, since Fe was the main 
metal impurity found. However, it was not possible to find a correlation between the concentration of 
the contamination of the alkali base and the obtained yield, leading to the conclusion that they were not 
responsible for the observed reactivity.[68]  
Transition metal free MPV-O type redox reactions have also been observed for the alkylation of nitriles, 
utilizing alcohols as alkylating agents (Scheme 17).[60,62] They are generally preferred, since the use of 
expensive metals, the difficult synthesis of complex ligands, and metal contaminations in the product 
can be avoided. In the first step, the alcohol is oxidized to the corresponding aldehyde. This oxidation 
is likely to be generated by trace amounts of oxygen present in the reaction.[60] Afterwards, alkylation 
takes place through a nucleophilic attack by the α-deprotonated nitrile. The formed alcohol is then 
eliminated yielding an α,β-unsaturated nitrile, which is reduced to the alkylated nitrile, while the alcohol

1.5 
Decyanation Reactions 
Nitrile functional groups are essential in organic synthesis. They exhibit an electron-withdrawing 
property, which can coordinate metal complexes and can act as directing groups in C-H bond activation. 
In addition, they stabilize α-anions, which are extremely useful in C-C bond formation as shown in 
Chapter 1.4. However, the presence of a nitrile functionality is often unwanted in the final product, and 
it is only a tool to achieve other modifications in the target molecule. In those cases, decyanation 
reactions are required to remove the functionality after it has fulfilled its purpose.[69,70] 
Reductive decyanation is the most commonly employed decyanation reaction. The C-CN bond is 
replaced by a C-H bond and a number of methods can be used to accomplish this transformation. Alkali-
metal promoted reactions use Li or Na in liquid ammonia (Scheme 18). In the first step an electron is 
transferred to the nitrile leading to the formation of a radical anion intermediate. A cyanide ion is then 
eliminated followed by a reduction of the carbon radical to a carbanion, which is then protonated by 
ammonia, or another proton source added to the reaction mixture. This procedure works well for 
aromatic nitriles, tertiary nitriles, and aryl acetonitriles, but reduction of primary or secondary nitriles 
leads to the formation of the corresponding amines. To overcome this issue K/HMPA/toluene or 
K/dicyclohexano-18-crown-6/toluene can be used.[71] Nevertheless, reductive decyanations using alkali 
metals require harsh conditions, limiting its applications.[72] 
 
Scheme 18: Reaction scheme of alkali metal promoted reductive decyanation of nitriles.[71]

Reductive decyanation using aluminum- and borohydrides are commonly applied on α-aminonitriles, 
but with other substrates only moderate yields have been reported.[71] Reactions using NaH as the 
reducing agent for the decyanation of aryl acetonitriles have also been investigated. The mechanism 
proposed for this transformation, supported by DTF calculations, involves a hydride addition to the C-
N triple bond and subsequent formation of an iminyl anion intermediate (Scheme 19). This intermediate 
undergoes isomerization to gain stabilization through cation-π-interactions. In the last step, C-C bond 
cleavage and proton transfer occur with retention of the configuration.[71,73]

Transition metals can be employed to catalyze reductive decyanation reactions utilizing green reaction 
conditions and exhibiting a large substrate scope. The catalyzed reactions proceed through two different 
reaction pathways. One occurs via oxidative addition of the C-CN bond to a metal center, such as Ni, 
Pt, Pd, Co, or Rh. Afterwards, addition of a hydride source like (Me2SiH)2O leads to the formation of 
the decyanation product (Scheme 20A). In the case of (Me2SiH)2O, the proposed mechanism proceeds 
through transmetalation followed by a reductive elimination. In the second pathway, the silyl substituent 
on the transition metal plays a crucial role. After coordination of the nitrile to the metal complex, silyl 
migration takes place, leading to formation of an η2-iminoacyl complex. C-C bond cleavage occurs in 
the following step and an alkyl- or aryl silyl isocyanide is formed. The decyanated product is released 
through reductive elimination (Scheme 20B). Both aliphatic and aromatic nitriles can undergo these 
catalytic reductive decyanations.[72,74,75]

1.6.1 Radical Scavengers and Radical Clocks 
Radical scavengers and radical clocks are used to determine, whether a mechanism involves radical 
intermediates and how reactive these are. The radical scavengers are able to trap radical intermediates 
of the reaction, thereby inhibiting the reaction. Analyzing the formed adducts can give information about 
the nature of the radical intermediates formed. If the investigated reaction does not proceed through a 
radical mechanism, the radical scavengers will not affect the reactivity.[2,76]  
Examples of radical scavengers are 1,4-cyclohexadiene and 4-methyl-2,4-diphenyl pent-1-ene (α-
methylstyrene dimer, MSD).[2] 1,4-cyclohexadiene forms a cyclohexadienyl radical upon reaction with 
a radical (Scheme 21). This radical can react with another radical either by combination to 5-substituted 
1,3-cyclohexadienes or 3-substituted 1,4-cyclohexadienes, or by disproportionation to benzene.[77] 
Radicals react with MSD by addition to the double bond in the first step, followed by fragmentation, 
where a radical adduct and a cumyl radical are formed (Scheme 22). The cumyl radicals can either react 
with other radicals in the reaction mixture to form additional adducts or they can undergo combination 
or disproportionation with other cumyl radicals.[76,78] 
 
Scheme 21: Reaction pathways and products of radicals reacting with 1,4-cyclohexadiene. 
 
Scheme 22: Reaction pathways and products of radicals reacting with MSD.

Radical clocks can be used to identify whether radical intermediates are formed during the reaction by 
incorporating them into the starting materials. If the reaction rate of the radical clock is faster or similar 
to that of the investigated reaction, the reaction products will resemble those of the radical clock. Due 
to these characteristics the radical clocks can help to determine approximate reaction rates. If no radicals 
are formed during the reaction, the radical clock scaffold will be present in the product of the reaction. 
Incorporation of a radical clock scaffold into the starting material and the subsequent analysis of the 
products is viable in most cases.[79] 
An example of a radial clock is the 5-hexenyl radical scaffold (Scheme 23). Upon radical formation at 
the 1 position, a fast intramolecular 5-exo cyclization takes place with rates between 105 to 108 s-1 at 
room temperature. This scaffold can be used to investigate radical reactions of benzylic alcohols by 
using 2-allylbenzyl alcohol. If a radical is formed at the benzylic position it will react with the double 
bond of the allyl substituent forming 2-methylindene or 2-methylindane. This reaction proceeds slower 
(3 · 102 s-1) because the radical at the benzylic position will be stabilized.[79]

1.6.2 The Kinetic Isotope Effect

utilizes the possible difference in reaction rates of isotopically labelled and unlabeled substrates 
(Equation 1). A change in reaction rate can be observed if a bond involving the labelled atom is altered

different vibrational states are quantized and the formula for their quantized energies (en) is shown in 
Equation 3. At room temperature the vibrational states are dominated by n = 0, meaning that Equation 3 
can be simplified to Equation 4, referred to as the zero-point energy (ZPE). The most pronounced KIE 
is observed when hydrogen is exchanged with deuterium, since the difference in mass between the two 
is relatively large.[80]

to 2 equiv. gave a slight increase in yield of 1 to 64% (entry 3). 9% of compound 3 were formed. The 
addition of 3 equiv. of diglyme did not increase the yield further. A yield of 64% for 1 was detected 
under these reaction conditions (entry 4). The yield of 3 was slightly reduced to 8%. 
When triglyme was used at a concentration of 1 equiv., 1 was formed in a yield of 47%, while 3% and 
9% of 2 and 3 were formed, respectively (entry 5). Adding 2 equiv. of triglyme led to a slight reduction 
in the yield of 1 to 45% (entry 6). 2 and 3 were detected in concentrations close to those observed with 
1 equiv. of triglyme at 2% and 8%, respectively. A further increase of the triglyme concentration yielded 
46% of 1 (entry 7). 2 could not be observed by GC-MS and an 8% yield of 3 was detected. 
Using tetraglyme as the complexing agent increased the yield of 1 slightly compared to the reaction 
without addition of a complexing agent. Using 1 equiv. of tetraglyme resulted in a 47% yield for 1 
(entry 8), while 2 and 3 had yields of 5% and 10% of 2, respectively. When the concentration of 
tetraglyme was increased to 2 equiv., 51% of 1 were formed (entry 9). 2 was unchanged when compared 
to the experiment with 1 equiv. of tetraglyme, while 3 decreased to a 4% yield. Increasing the amount 
of tetraglyme to 3 equiv., resulted in a 52% yield of 1 (entry 10). The yields of 2 and 3 were similar to 
those observed when 2 equiv. were added (5% and 3%, respectively). 
Overall, the concentration of the glyme in the reaction mixture played a minor role in influencing the 
yield of 1. The small differences in yield observed, could be attributed to experimental uncertainties, 
since each experiment was only performed once. The type of glyme used gave a more pronounced effect 
on the yields. Highest yields of 1 were obtained with diglyme. Slightly lower yields were determined, 
when triglyme or tetraglyme were used as the complexing agents, but they still improved the yields 
compared to the experiments when no complexing agent was added. Monoglyme lowered the yield of 1 
compared to the reaction without any additives.

high proportion of the monoglyme will be in the gas phase and not available in solution to complex the

2.2 
Mechanistic Investigations 
2.2.1 Radical Scavenger and Radical Clock Experiments 
Since the reactivity of benzyl alcohol and phenyl acetonitrile using potassium tert-butoxide as a base 
was discovered during the investigation of a radical coupling reaction of benzyl alcohol to N,N-dimethyl 
acetamide, it was proposed that it also follows a radical mechanism. To confirm this, radical scavengers 
and radical clocks were used.  
When 1,4-cyclohexadiene was used as the radical scavenger (Table 6, entry 1) a 43% yield of 1 was 
determined, which correlates well with previously obtained yields for the comparable reaction without 
the use of a radical scavenger (Table 2, entry 3). However, the radical scavenger could not be detected 
in the GC-MS chromatogram. The detection of 1,4-cyclohexadiene by GC-MS using the standard 
method described in section 4.2.1 is difficult owing to the low boiling point of the compound. 
Furthermore, the program is optimized to identify high boiling compounds. No peaks were observed,

which would correspond to compounds obtained through reactions of radical intermediates with the 
radical scavenger as shown in Scheme 21.  
A slightly higher yield of 49% for 1 was obtained when MSD was used as the radical scavenger (Table 6, 
entry 2). In the acquired chromatogram a peak with a mass corresponding to the mass of the unreacted 
radical scavenger ([M]+ = 236) was visible. When a GC-MS chromatogram was recorded for the pure 
radical scavenger, the retention time and obtained mass spectrum were identical, confirming that the 
radical scavenger had not undergone any reactions. No peaks corresponding to adducts of the radical 
scavenger to possible radical intermediates were detected by GC-MS. 
Due to these findings the formation of stilbene from benzyl alcohol and phenyl acetonitrile through a 
radical mechanism as proposed for the coupling of benzyl alcohol to N,N-dimethyl acetamide seems 
unlikely.

Additionally, the radical clock 2-allylbenzyl alcohol was used to probe the reaction mechanism. A 
radical formed at the benzylic position would lead to formation of 2-methylindene, 2-methylindane and 
2-propylbenzaldehyde as shown previously by Azizi and Madsen (Scheme 23).[2] Analysis of the 
reaction mixture by GC-MS revealed that no 2-methylindene or 2-methylindane were formed. Instead, 
peaks with a mass corresponding to 2-allylstilbene and 2-propylstilbene were found (Scheme 25). 2-
Allylstilbene would be the expected product of the reaction if no radical intermediates are present. This 
shows that the intermolecular reaction forming the stilbene occurs either faster than the intramolecular 
radical reaction forming 2-methylindene and 2-methylindane or that no radicals are present as 
intermediates of the reaction. Intramolecular reactions, especially for the formation of 5- or 6-membered 
rings, occur very rapidly and are entropically favored over intermolecular reactions.[82] This supports the 
hypothesis of a non-radical reaction mechanism. 
2-Propylbenzaldehyde was detected in the reaction mixture, which was likewise formed when 2-
allylbenzyl alcohol was used in a reaction with a radical mechanism.[2] However, in all reactions using 
benzyl alcohol approximately 4% of benzaldehyde were detected in the reaction mixtures, indicating 
that some oxidation of the benzylic alcohol occurs under the reaction conditions. The oxidation of 2-
allylbenzyl alcohol to 2-allylbenzaldehyde most likely happens through the same process and not 
through a radical reaction. The reduction of the allyl group to the propyl group occurs in the formed 
stilbene product as well and is not an indicator of a radical reaction.

2.2.2 Kinetic Isotope Effect Study 
Due to the high concentration of base used in the reaction it was investigated whether deprotonation is 
a rate limiting step of the reaction. A KIE study was conducted using α,α-d2-benzyl alcohol and 
comparing the reaction to that of non-deuterated benzyl alcohol. The two reactions were done 
simultaneously, samples were taken every 30 min for 5 h and analyzed by GC-MS. Figure 9 illustrates 
the detected stilbene yields at their respective times. Neither of the curves follow a linear correlation 
throughout the entire period. When non-deuterated benzyl alcohol was used, the reaction rate was slow 
within the first two hours and increased afterwards. In the case of α,α-d2-benzyl alcohol the reaction 
rate only increased after 150 minutes. The observation of non-linear curves for both reactions could be 
explained by an autocatalytic reaction mechanism. With these mechanisms critical amounts of the 
catalytic compound would be needed before the reaction could reach higher reaction rates. This would 
be in coherence with the observed low rate of stilbene formation in the beginning of the reaction and the 
increased reaction rate after 120 min or 150 min for non-deuterated and deuterated benzyl alcohol, 
respectively. When analyzing the reaction rates after 120 minutes and 150 minutes for benzyl alcohol 
and α,α-d2-benzyl alcohol, respectively, a KIE of 4.3 was calculated for the reaction. This shows that 
deprotonation at the benzylic position of benzyl alcohol is the rate determining step of the reaction. To 
confirm the curves obtained in the KIE study, the experiment should be repeated.

2.2.3 Mechanistic Proposal 
Based on the evidence gained during the mechanistic investigations an MPV-O type redox reaction with 
subsequent E1cB elimination is proposed as the mechanism for stilbene formation from benzyl alcohol 
and phenyl acetonitrile (Scheme 26). In an initiation step benzyl alcohol is oxidized to benzaldehyde. 
This occurs most likely through an aerobic oxidative process with residual oxygen present in the reaction 
mixture. Afterwards the formed benzaldehyde reacts with the α-deprotonated phenyl acetonitrile 
forming species 4. This is followed by the elimination of the alcohol to give intermediate 5, which has 
been detected in traces by GC-MS. 5 is then reduced to 2 through an MPV-O type redox reaction while 
benzyl alcohol is oxidized to benzaldehyde. The benzaldehyde will then again react with the α-
deprotonated phenyl acetonitrile, hence looping the autocatalytic cycle. In the last step, elimination of 
the cyanide of species 2 through an E1cB mechanism leads to the formation of 1. The E-configuration 
of 1 is formed since it is thermodynamically favorable compared the Z-configuration. Deprotonation at 
the α-carbon will occur faster since the nitrile as well as the aromatic ring stabilize the corresponding 
carbanion. This reaction is however unproductive and reversible. If deprotonation occurs in the β-
position E1cB elimination can occur irreversibly, forming 1. This can explain the high temperature and 
high concentration of base needed for the reaction to occur.

Low amounts of benzaldehyde (around 4%) could be detected in all reaction mixtures by GC-MS, 
showing that benzaldehyde can be formed under the reaction conditions and supporting this mechanistic 
proposal.

2.3 
Scope and Limitations 
The scope of the reaction was evaluated using different substituted benzyl alcohols and aryl acetonitriles 
(Table 7). Diglyme was used as an additive to improve the yields, since it gave an increase in yield 
during the optimization (Scheme 30). 
1 was isolated in 67% yield (entry 1), which corresponds well with the GC-MS yield measured for the 
reaction. (E)-4-Methylstilbene was obtained in 39% yield (entry 2). A yield of 19% was achieved for 
the synthesis of (E)-4-methoxystilbene (entry 3). All products were characterized using GC-MS as well 
as 1H- and 13C-NMR.  
The coupling of 4-bromobenzyl alcohol and phenyl acetonitrile did not lead to the formation of (E)-4-
bromostilbene. 1 was the only product observed and isolated in a yield of 28% (entry 4). 
Dehalogenations of benzyl alcohols under basic conditions have been reported in the literature.[2] Aryl 
dehalogenations with alkoxides are known to proceed via radical intermediates.[83] However, since the 
presence of radicals in the reaction is unlikely, the dehalogenation reaction could alternatively occur via 
benzyne formation.[84] 
Using 4-fluorobenzyl alcohol to form (E)-4-fluorostilbene gave no yield of the desired product (entry 5). 
Additionally, no dehalogenation product could be detected. (E)-4-trifluoromethylstilbene was not 
formed through coupling of 4-trifluoromethylbenzyl alcohol and phenyl acetonitrile (entry 6) and no 
conversion of phenyl acetonitrile could be observed by GC-MS. When 4-chlorophenyl acetonitrile was 
used as the starting material and coupled with benzyl alcohol, (E)-4-chlorostilbene was afforded as the 
major product. 1 was additionally formed through dehalogenation. After purification, a 61:39 mixture 
of (E)-4-chlorostilbene and 1, determined by 1H-NMR, was obtained in 55% yield (entry 7). (E)-4-
nitrostilbene could not be obtained using the standard reaction procedure with benzyl alcohol and 4-
nitrophenyl acetonitrile as the starting materials (entry 8). 
Stilbenes with electron donating substituents could be synthesized using the general reaction. When 
chloro- or bromo substituted starting materials were used dehalogenation was observed, which yielded 
1 as a side product. Strongly electron withdrawing substituents on the starting materials were not 
tolerated and in those cases no stilbene formation occurred.

2.4 
Two-step One-pot Reaction 
As phenyl acetonitrile derivatives are rarely commercially available, an easy two-step one-pot synthesis 
starting from benzyl halides and potassium cyanide forming aryl acetonitriles in the first step was 
developed. In the second step potassium tert-butoxide and benzyl alcohol are added to the reaction 
mixture, starting the coupling reaction and stilbene formation (Scheme 31). Benzyl halides as well as 
potassium cyanide are commercially available, and the reaction produces potassium chloride as the only 
byproduct, which does not affect the coupling reaction to benzyl alcohol. The addition of 18-crown-6 to 
such substitution reactions has been shown to be favorable and lead to faster conversion to phenyl 
acetonitrile.[85] Since it increased the yield of 1 during the coupling reaction it was added to the reaction 
mixture in the first step. 
Performing the substitution reaction with 3 equiv. of potassium cyanide yielded phenyl acetonitrile from 
benzyl chloride in a yield of 83% after 24 h (Table 8, entry 1). The following coupling step with benzyl 
alcohol led to a 46% yield of 1. Increasing the concentration of potassium cyanide to 4 equiv. gave 100% 
conversion to phenyl acetonitrile and in the second step 1 was formed in 70% yield (entry 2). 
Performing the same reaction with 4-methoxybenzyl chloride led to formation of (E)-4-methoxystilbene 
in a yield of 47% (entry 3). The yield of 4-methoxyphenyl acetonitrile after 24 h could not be determined 
by GC-MS since no calibration curve was available for this compound. 
The substitution reaction with 4-nitrobenzyl chloride did not occur (entry 4). Therefore, the second step 
of the reaction was not performed.  
During the substitution reaction a partial positive charge will be formed at the benzylic position, which 
can be stabilized by electron-donating groups, such as a methoxy group. Electron-withdrawing groups, 
like the nitro group will have a destabilizing effect. This can explain the results seen for the substitution 
reactions of 4-methoxybenzyl chloride and 4-nitrobenzyl chloride. 
Using benzyl bromide as the starting material for the substitution, led to lower yields compared to benzyl 
chloride. Only 8% of phenyl acetonitrile were formed after 24 h (entry 5). Due to this low yield, no 
coupling reaction was conducted. Usually, bromides are expected to be better leaving groups than 
chlorides in substitution reactions.[82] However, it has also been reported in literature by Cook et al. that 
the substitution of benzyl chlorides with potassium cyanide occurs much faster than that of benzyl 
bromide.[85] 
The long reaction time for the substitution reaction can be attributed to the solvent used in the reaction. 
Ideal solvents for substitution reactions are polar solvents since they can stabilize the full and partial 
charges present during the reaction. o-Xylene is an apolar solvent and not able to sufficiently stabilize 
the substitution. A similar substitution reaction has been reported, where acetonitrile was used as the 
solvent. In this case almost full conversion of benzyl chloride to phenyl acetonitrile was achieved after 
0.4 h at room temperature.[85]

catalytic amounts and promoted the reactivity. Poor yields were obtained without the addition of the

Conclusion 
In summary, a new method for the synthesis of stilbenes from benzyl alcohols and aryl acetonitriles has 
been developed, showing stereoselectivity for (E)-stilbene formation. After optimization, the best yields 
were obtained when a two-fold excess of the aryl acetonitrile, 3 equivalents of potassium tert-butoxide, 
and 3 equivalents of 18-crown-6 were refluxed in o-xylene for 20 h. A GC-yield of 86% was achieved 
under these conditions. 
The use of sodium tert-butoxide as the base led to predominant formation of 2,3-diphenylpropionitrile, 
while only trace amounts of (E)-stilbene were obtained. It is necessary to add an excess of the aryl 
acetonitrile to the reaction to achieve decent yields. At a stochiometric ratio only 16% of (E)-stilbene 
were formed. Addition of diglyme, triglyme or tetraglyme as complexing agents improved the yields of 
(E)-stilbene as well. The yields obtained were, however, inferior to those obtained with 18-crown-6. 
It was possible to synthesize several substituted stilbenes. Strongly electron withdrawing substituents 
on the starting materials were not tolerated and partial dehalogenation was observed. 
Mechanistic investigations using radical scavengers and radical clocks showed, that a radical mechanism 
can be excluded. A KIE of 4.3 was determined, revealing that deprotonation at the benzylic position of 
benzyl alcohol is a rate limiting step of the reaction. In addition, the curves obtained during the KIE 
study indicated an autocatalytic mechanism, because the rate was initially low and increased 
significantly after two hours. The reaction is proposed to proceed via an MPV-O type redox reaction 
followed by irreversible E1cB elimination of the cyanide giving (E)-stilbene as the final product.  
Since substituted aryl acetonitriles are expensive to acquire a simple two-step one-pot reaction has been 
developed using benzyl chlorides and potassium cyanide as the starting materials. The substitution 
reaction gave quantitative conversion to aryl acetonitriles after 24 h and excellent yields of (E)-stilbene 
were observed after potassium tert-butoxide, and benzyl alcohol were added to the reaction. The yields 
of (E)-stilbene obtained with the two-step-one-pot reaction are slightly lower but comparable to those 
of the one step coupling reaction, using phenyl acetonitrile as the starting material.

4.1.1 Reagents 
All chemicals were purchased through Sigma Aldrich, Enamine, and VWR Chemicals. All acquired 
chemicals were used as received, besides the glymes, which were further purified. The glymes contained

4.1.2 Solvents 
Dry toluene was obtained through the in-house Innovative Technology PureSolve MD-7 system. o-
Xylene and mesitylene were obtained from Sigma Aldrich and dried using 4 Å molecular sieves. The 
solvents were degassed using the freeze-pump-thaw technique. First the solvent was added to a Schlenk 
flask equipped with 4 Å molecular sieves, attached to the Schlenk line, and sealed with a septum. The 
flask was lowered into liquid nitrogen to freeze the solvent. Once the solvent was frozen vacuum was 
applied for 10 minutes while the flask was still submerged in liquid nitrogen. Afterwards the stopcock 
to the Schlenk line was closed and the solvent thawed in a water bath. This was repeated two times for 
a total of three freeze-pump-thaw cycles. After the last thawing the headspace was filled with nitrogen 
gas. The solvents were stored connected to the Schlenk line under a continuous flow of nitrogen. The 
water content was regularly checked using an 899 Coulometer by Metrohm and never exceeded 20 ppm.

4.3 
Experimental Procedures 
4.3.1 General Reaction Procedure  
All reactions were carried out in oven dried two-necked Schlenk flasks equipped with a cold finger, 
septum and stirring bar (Figure 10). Potassium tert-butoxide (337 mg, 3 mmol) was added to the 
Schlenk flask, which was attached to the Schlenk line, evacuated, flame dried, and filled with nitrogen. 
This was repeated four times for a total of five cycles. o-Xylene (3 ml) was injected followed by heating 
to 144 °C. Complexing agents were added to the reaction mixture depending on their melting point, with 
the base before evacuation or with the solvent. Afterwards, benzyl alcohol (1 mmol), and aryl 
acetonitrile (2 mmol) were added, and the reaction was refluxed for 20 h. The reaction mixture was 
cooled to room temperature and the solvent removed in vacuo. Silica plug filtration (EtOAc) was used 
==PAGE:49==
Experimental 
39 
to remove potassium tert-butoxide from the mixture. The obtained crude was purified by flash column 
chromatography (95:5 n-hexane/EtOAc).  
 
Figure 10: A) Schematic drawing of the two-necked Schlenk flask with cold finger. B) Experimental setup. 
4.3.2 Synthesis of Stilbenes from Benzyl halides 
An oven dried two-necked Schlenk flask was charged with potassium cyanide (4 mmol, 261 mg) and 
18-crown-6 (793 mg, 3 mmol), followed by attachment to the Schlenk line, evacuation, flame drying, 
and filling with nitrogen five times. o-Xylene (3 ml) was injected, and the flask was heated to 144 °C. 
Benzyl chloride (2 mmol) was added, and the reaction mixture was refluxed for 24 h. At that point 
potassium tert-butoxide (337 mg, 3 mmol) and benzyl alcohol (1 mmol) were added, followed by 
refluxing for 20 h. Workup and purification were performed following the same procedure as described 
in the general reaction procedure (Section 4.3.1). 
4.3.3 Reactions with Radical Scavengers 
The reactions were performed using the general reaction procedure described in section 4.3.1 with the 
addition of 1,3,5-trimethoxybenzene (84 mg, 0.5 mmol) as an IS. Radical scavengers (2 mmol) were 
injected before benzyl alcohol and phenyl acetonitrile were added. The reactions were analyzed by GC-
MS after 20 h. 
4.3.4 Reaction with 2-Allylbenzyl alcohol 
The reaction was performed using the general reaction procedure described in section 4.3.1. 2-
Allylbenzyl alcohol (0.14 ml, 1 mmol) was used instead of benzyl alcohol and 1,3,5-trimethoxybenzene 
(84 mg, 0.5 mmol) was added as an IS. The reaction was analyzed by GC-MS after 20 h. 
4.3.5 Kinetic Isotope Effect Study 
Two reactions were set up following the general reaction procedure described in section 4.3.1 using 
benzyl alcohol (0.10 ml, 1 mmol) in one and α,α-d2-benzyl alcohol (0.10 ml, 1 mmol) in the other. 1,3,5-
trimethoxybenzene (84 mg, 0.5 mmol) was added as an IS to both. Samples (0.05 ml) of the reaction

4.3.6 Synthesis of 2,3-Diphenyl propionitrile  
2,3-Diphenyl propionitrile was synthesized following a procedure reported by Roy and co-workers.[62] 
An oven dried Schlenk flask equipped with a stirring bar and a reflux condenser was charged with 
KOtBu (898 mg, 8 mmol), phenyl acetonitrile (1.2 ml, 10 mmol), benzyl alcohol (3.2 ml, 30 mmol) and 
toluene (40 ml). It was sealed with a septum and dipped into a preheated oil bath at 120 °C. The reaction 
was refluxed for 3.5 h after which TLC showed full conversion of the starting materials. The reaction 
was cooled down to room temperature and the solvent removed in vacuo. The base was removed from 
the crude by silica-plug filtration. Purification by flash column chromatography (9:1 n-hexane-EtOAc) 
yielded the product.

GC-MS after 20 h.
