Abstract  
Enhanced use of daylight in indoor environment is dependent on a series dynamic 
physical and psychological parameter. The main challenge is to reach energy efficient 
and human-centric solutions that result in optimal of daylight with energy saving 
benefits. Such solutions should enrich building occupants’ comfort, health and visual 
quality while avoiding glare and overheating. Most studies on daylighting and energy 
savings target commercial buildings with less focus on residential buildings’ needs 
(Mardaljevic et al., 2011). It is, however, commonly known that in residential buildings 
direct sunlight penetration is desired and several countries have regulations to ensure 
a minimum required amount of direct daylight penetration (Engineering, 2016). 
However, there are no conclusive methods to validate design strategies, e.g. window 
size or shading device choices, to ensure the desired amount of daylight and its 
qualitative aspects such as view without compromising visual comfort in residential 
buildings. Thus, the decision-making phase becomes lengthy and prone to usage of 
several tools for simple decision or pertaining to limited assumptions and rules of 
thumb. It was selected to use infographics for data visualization that is gives an easy 
and fast overview in a design making process.  Our preliminary results show that the 
three indicators and their infographic representation can be a useful visual aid to 
assess the state of comfort in the design scenario at the hand.  The new comfort-rating 
concept attempts to use infographics in order to represent the comfort state in 
residential buildings based on three indicators. In order to show its applicability and 
strength in communicating the comfort rating, several simulation scenarios were 
considered and assessed. This new concept and its representational strength can be 
used in the design phase to give a quick assessment of the comfort state of the 
building. Some of the limitations of such a method are longer simulation time due to 
the attempt to have a wide-visual-span analysis of the space for visual comfort 
assessments.

2 Introduction 
Adequate daylight is essential for occupant health and wellbeing and is by far the most 
desirable light source. The right amount of daylight influences our biological processes 
(circadian system) as well as our visual performance. The circadian rhythm is essential 
for our daily cycle of day and night and affects physiological and behavioural processes 
in our body (Mardaljevic et al., 2011). In modern societies spend people most of their 
time indoors and is often not espoused to enough natural light during the day. Lack of 
daylight can lead to excessive exposure of artificial light that have been proposed as a 
potential role in breast cancer (Stevens and Rea, 2001; Stevens et al., 2014) and 
disrupts occupants sleeping rhythm (Cajochen, Kräuchi and Wirz-Justice, 2003; 
Cajochen, 2007; Dijk and Archer, 2009) with consequences in mood and productivity. 
Windows is a crucial issue for enhanced use of daylight and access to view. A poor 
daylighting design can lead to large amounts of lights and cause periods of visual 
discomfort due to glare issues (Vos, 2003; Boyce, 2014) or thermal discomfort due to 
overheating.  To allow for better use of daylighting can several adaptations to 
buildings be considered. Solar-control system has proven to be beneficial for limiting 
overheating through facade openings (Tillson, Oreszczyn and Palmer, 2013). 
Experiences from high-performance buildings have shown that cost-efficient dynamic 
solar shading can lead to energy savings related to cooling of up to 62% (Tzempelikos, 
2008). Wisely-selected solar-control systems are therefore crucial to achieve the CO2 
reduction goals worldwide. Appropriate selection of solar-control devices contributes 
significantly to optimised use of daylight by maximising use of healthy lighting, 
reducing visual discomfort, and reducing energy-use by minimised artificial light 
consumption (Dubois, 2003). However, selection of an appropriate solution of a solar-
control system to provide a balanced daylit, and thermally comfortable indoor 
environment proves to be a great challenge.

2.1 Problem statement 
Although several studies have explored the applicability of different daylight metrics 
to residential buildings, the visual comfort assessment methods in this building 
typology have not been addressed in a similar extend. It is, however, commonly known 
that in residential buildings direct sunlight penetration is desired and several countries 
have regulations to ensure a minimum required amount of direct daylight penetration 
(Engineering, 2016). To date, visual discomfort caused by daylight has been greatly 
investigated in commercial buildings with a general focus on office buildings. These 
studies have proposed several indices to predict discomfort caused by glare or 
unbalanced luminous contrast within a fixed Field of View (FOV) with respect to an 
office visual task-area, e.g. monitor screen. However, task-oriented visual comfort 
evaluation is not an optimal method for visual comfort assessment in residential 
interior spaces where the classification of a task and its position is less defined. Thus, 
a need for a holistic evaluation for the whole space proves to be essential for such 
buildings. In parallel, pertaining to known comfort indicator for a holistic evaluation 
of a design can aid our decision-making processes. View out is a parameter that plays 
a crucial role in definition of daylight and comfort in buildings which is considered in 
this study. View out is a parameter that overrides the negative effects of excess heat 
and brightness. Studies have shown that people tolerate thermal and visual 
discomfort in exchange for view out. Another parameter  
What is lacking is a method that allow for simultaneous evaluation of these three 
daylight comfort indicators. Such method can assist evaluation of the design strategies 
as well as design components such as solar control systems which prove to be a 
challenge.

2.2 Objectives 
In order to address visual comfort in residential buildings in a holistic manner, here 
we propose a new comfort rating method based on the three mentioned aspects of 
visual comfort and visual quality in daylit spaces. Here we are presenting a new 
comfort rating method for residential buildings, which represents an overall rating of 
comfort in residential buildings using infographics. The new concept is built upon 
three main indicators: 
 
- 
Visual discomfort for worst case scenarios   
- 
 View out – visible layers  
- 
Daylight access  
 
The new rating concept and its representation is shown in a comprehensive 
simulation-based study where several internal, e.g. surface materials, and external 
parameters, e.g. adjacencies and surrounding buildings, as well several solar controls 
systems are considered in three residential apartments. Two external and one internal 
solar shading devices were selected and simulated. The apartments have been 
carefully selected in a newly built and modern apartment complex in central 
Copenhagen.

2.3 Methodology  
Photometric behaviour of a selected case study is observed with simulations using the 
lighting rendering tool, Radiance. The chosen residential building for this study 
represents newly built building types in central Copenhagen. These buildings have 
relatively large windows for daylight access. three shading systems were evaluated for 
their daylight performance, visual comfort using Daylight Glare Probability (DGP) 
index for different orientations considered as worst-case scenarios.  
Daylight performance of the simulated shading systems with manual control were 
investigated for two floors of the building and with the consideration of the 
surrounding buildings. Visual comfort simulations were done for several view 
directions in order to assess the photometric behaviour in a larger visual span. For this 
part high dynamic range (HDR) rendering techniques with angular fisheye perspective 
view types rendered in Radiance.  HDR renderings over a range of view directions were 
simulated and evaluated for visual comfort using the DGP model. The occupant 
position in a room (a viewpoint) was assumed to be at 1.20m from the floor in a 
distance 2m from the window. All simulations were done for annual evaluations. Using 
geometrical methods, view out was assessed from the same occupant position. For 
the view out was the simplified verification method no-sky line and no-ground line 
concept used to assess the visible layers from a sight line. The sight line was defined 
as the distance between the window and interior wall.  
 
The three comfort indicators for the mentioned simulation scenarios were then 
assessed separately and compiled in order to present the comfort rating of each 
building based on the three indicators. Data visualization methods where used to 
simply create a visual representation of the three mentioned measures in three 
representative charts.

3 Background 
3.1 Daylight in Residential Buildings 
Daylight has been observed in extend targeting commercial buildings with a focus on 
office buildings. These studies have proposed several metrics to describe discomfort 
caused by excessive or unbalanced luminous contrast within a fixed Field of View (FOV) 
with respect to an office visual task-area (Boyce, 2014) (Kuhn, 2017) ,e.g. monitor 
screen. Task-oriented visual comfort evaluation, however, does not seem to be as 
relevant in residential interior spaces where the classification of a task and its position 
is less defined. Although several studies have explored the applicability of different 
daylight metrics to residential buildings (Mardaljevic et al., 2011) and daylight energy 
saving benefits (Negendahl and Nielsen, 2015), the visual comfort assessment 
methods in this building typology have not been addressed. 
There is an ongoing process in visual discomfort should from excessive brightness be 
considered in the evaluation of daylight sufficiency. It is accepted worldwide that 
dwellings should have occasional direct sun penetration through at least some of the 
windows.  
3.1.1 Daylight Performance 
Daylight performance can be predicted by use of daylight metrics developed in the 
past decades. These metrics can mainly be categorized as either static or dynamic. 
Daylight factor (DF) is a static metric and it is used to measure the amount of diffuse 
daylight delivered to a point in space under overcast daylit conditions. DF is hence 
insensitive to climate, orientation, and surrounding of the building (J Mardaljevic, 
Heschong and Lee, 2009). This metric limits exploiting the daylight potentials in 
buildings while neglecting eventual visual discomfort risks (Reinhart, F, Mardaljevic 
and Rogers, 2013). A number of Climate-Based Daylight Modelling (CBDM) metrics 
have, however, been proposed in order to overcome this shortcoming. In recent years 
the metrics such as Daylight Autonomy (DA) and Useful Daylight Illuminance (UDI) 
have been used for dynamic evaluation of daylight potentials in indoor environments 
==PAGE:15==
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
Page | 13 
(Reinhart, F, Mardaljevic and Rogers, 2013) (See section 2 for detail description of 
these metrics). While these metrics show the level of daylight on horizontal plain, 
visual comfort is assessed at the eye level or by introducing an arbitrary higher bounds 
on horizontal light distributions. (See section 3 for more detail descriptions).    
3.1.2 Requirements and recommendation  
The requirements specified in the Danish building regulation for light and view of the 
surroundings are intended to ensure sufficient access of daylight and view in buildings, 
as well as electrical lighting, should be provided to be adequate in relation to its use. 
According to BR18 (Trafik-og-Byggestyrelsen, 2015) should the following be 
considered for construction and planning of the building design:   
 
• 
Daylight must be utilized as a source of light to the extent possible  
• 
Unnecessary energy consumption should be avoided  
• 
Unnecessary heat transfer should be avoided  
• 
Discomfort from direct sunlight should be avoided  
• 
Discomfort from glare must be avoided 
  
 
Concerning the previous building regulation BR15 is it now possible to use climate-
based metrics for documentation of sufficient daylight levels instead of the widely 
used Daylight Factor. To document that sufficient daylight can be provided should it 
according to BR18 be demonstrated that either a minimum light intensity of at least 
300 lux is achieved for at least half of the relevant floor area for half of the daylight 
hours. Or the glass area without shading is equivalent to a minimum of 10 percent of 
the relevant floor area. The glass area does not include the frames and is defined as a 
total for the room. The relevant floor area for residential buildings is usually defined 
as the surface area enclosed by the walls for kitchens, living rooms and bedrooms 
whereas for working rooms it is defined as at that area where workstations area 
placed. (Trafik-og-Byggestyrelsen, 2015) In residential buildings is lower daylight levels 
in bedroom accepted as fare the glass to floor area is equivalent to a minimum of 10 
percent without any adjustment for conditions that can reduce the daylight levels and 
==PAGE:16==
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
Page | 14 
the daylight levels is achieved for the dwelling in general. This means that the glass 
area with corrections for the overall dwelling should be equal to at least 10 percent. 
In Beside sufficient daylight levels should also the risk of glare and heat radiation be 
considered. If shading devices is implemented to regulate incoming daylight as a 
feature to provide a sufficient indoor environment must the view out also be 
considering. According to BR18 should shading systems be planned and implemented 
so as a view to the surroundings can be maintained for a satisfying part of the usage 
time (Trafik-og-Byggestyrelsen, 2015) 
3.2 Daylight & Comfort  
3.2.1 Annual Sunlight Exposure - Direct sunlight  
The Annual Sunlight Exposure (ASE) is calculated as the percentage of the horizontal 
work plane that exceeds a specified sunlight illuminance level of at least 1000 lux for 
at least 250 hours per year with all operable shading devices retracted. To minimize 
the potential of glare risks is the ASE designed to raise a warning flag if an interior 
space exceeds a specified number of hours of direct sunlight exposure (Dogan and 
Park, 2017).  For residential buildings plays direct daylight access a different role and 
is it accepted that dwellings should have occasional direct sun penetration (Kuhn, 
2017). For the assessment of how well a particular space can access direct light was it 
in a recent study (Dogan and Park, 2017) suggested to reverting the ASE metric to 
make it more suitable for residential buildings. The introduced metric for direct light 
access (DLA) provides an insight of how much of a daylit space that can access direct 
daylight over a year with the consideration of a specified climate file (Dogan and Park, 
2017).

3.2.2 Discomfort glare 
Visual comfort influences the performance and well-being of human beings and is an 
important aspect of lighting quality. The key aspects of visual comfort are often 
related to the physiology of the eye but is also depended on physiological factors such 
as view quality.  
 
Glare caused by daylight can generally be divided into two categories disability glare 
and discomfort glare. Disability glare is caused by an intense light source in the Field 
of View (FOV) and occurs when the sun or sun specular reflections can be seen. 
Discomfort glare is defined as irritating or distracting and are in many cases below the 
level of disability glare. It is not directly measurable and can therefore only be 
evaluated by subjective measures as questionnaires. A number of glare rating 
methods is available to evaluate visual discomfort in a space. However, most of these 
glare rating equations are developed under electric lightning conditions and assuming 
a constant luminance at the building facade. They can only predict the static lighting 
conditions and appear to produce an unreliable judgment of glare (Wienold, 2010). A 
dynamic glare metric applicated for daylight condition was introduced by J.Wienold 
(Wienold and Christoffersen, 2006). The Daylight Glare Probability (DGP) is an 
approach to predict the fraction of persons who will experience glare in the given view. 
The DGP is determined by user assessment for an office setup under real daylight 
condition and is currently the most accepted and validated glare metric (Kuhn, 2017). 
The DGP formula (1) is based on the vertical eye illuminance calculated with DAYSIM 
and individual glare sources detected with the Evalglare tool from an HDR image 
(Wienold, 2010).

An even distribution of luminance and contrast within an interior space is a significant 
determinant of visual comfort. A wide range in light levels over the viewing field can 
lead to visually uncomfortable conditions as the eye is forced to re-adapt for each gaze 
shift. An assumption behind the DGP metric is that the gaze direction is fixed towards 
a task area and occupants are not able to choose a position or viewing direction. In 
residential buildings are the classification of the task and its location less defined. 
However, visual comfort assessment still proves to be beneficial for this building 
typology (Sarey Khanie et al., 2017).  
 
In a recent study (Khanie et al., 2017)  was a newly developed Relative Contrast model 
RCpi presented as a new approach for visual comfort assessment. The study was 
presented for a typical old residential building in Copenhagen with small windows and 
small levels of direct sunlight. The relative contrast model is based on relative contrast 
in the FOV and allows detection of potential visual contrast-induced discomfort in the 
room. The presented study showed that high contrast cannot be detected by the 
commonly used DGP index as the eye illuminance is the dominant part of the overall 
result and are less effected by the illuminance contrast. (Khanie et al., 2017).

3.2.3 View quality  
View to the outside provide information about the natural environment, weather 
conditions and movement and changes in light levels throughout the day. The visual 
contact daylight openings provide with the surroundings is an important aspect of 
visual comfort and contribute to the phycological well-being of occupants. It is found 
(Tuaycharoen and Tregenza, 2007) that occupants are more likely to tolerate 
moderate levels of glare from an exciting view compared to a poor view having the 
same daylight glare index. It is therefore important to consider both daylight and view 
quality when a building is assessed (Hellinga and Hordijk, 2014). View openings should 
provide a sufficient view and comprise a distinct layer of sky, landscape and ground. 
Views with more visible layers provides a dynamic and diverse view and is favoured 
over single layer views. It is known that a view of nature can have a positive influence 
on the wellbeing of occupants and is generally preferred over a view towards built 
environment (Dansk Standard, 2018) 
 
Different methods can be used to verify the view quality. The following mentioned 
methods is based on the procedure specified in the DS/EN 17037:2018 - Daylight in 
buildings (Dansk Standard, 2018) For both methods is the view quality assessed from 
selected reference points in the occupied area with a reference height either from eye 
level of a sitting person 1.2m or a standing person 1.7m.

3.2.4 Illuminance levels on horizontal plane 
One of the most common used metrics for evaluating the daylight levels is the 
Daylight Factor (DF). The DF is defined as the ratio between the internal illuminance 
at a point on a given plane to the external horizontal illuminance under a standard 
overcast sky. Since only the illuminance from the diffused sunlight is considered and 
no account is taken for variable sky and sun conditions will the result vary from the 
actually illuminance level in space (J. Mardaljevic, Heschong and Lee, 2009). The result 
of The DF is affected by the shadow impact from the surroundings, the building 
geometry and surface properties whereas building orientation and location has no 
effect (Reinhart, F, Mardaljevic and Rogers, 2013). Although The Daylight Factor not 
fully describe the realistic daylight conditions is it due to its simplicity one of the most 
used performance measures of daylighting (Nabil and Mardaljevic, 2005) This metric 
is/has been used in many standards including the Danish building regulations and have 
the capacity to influence design choices. Because of its lack of realism is it 
recommended to use climate-based modelling to assess daylight in buildings 
(Mardaljevic and Nabil, 2006). The key advantage of climate-based modelling 
compared to DF is the dynamic consideration of daily and seasonal variations, as it 
offers a more realistic measure of the daylight illuminances for an interior space 
(Reinhart, F, Mardaljevic and Rogers, 2013). A number of climate-Based metrics has in 
recent years been proposed. The most significant of these is the Daylight Autonomy 
(DA) and the Useful Daylight Illuminances (UDI).  
The Daylight Autonomy (DA) was the first of a string of climate-Based metrics. The 
definition of the DA was originally proposed by the Association Suisse Des Electriciens 
in 1989 and later redefined by Reinhart and Walkenhorst. The modified DA represent 
the percentage of occupied hours a required work plane illuminance can be 
maintained by daylight alone. Depended on the analyse is it possible to calculate the 
DA for either a calculation grid or a defined point set by the user. The threshold for 
which the DA is calculated depends on the room type and the required minimum 
illuminance levels (Reinhart, F, Mardaljevic and Rogers, 2013).  
Based on the annual hour-by-hour calculation method has different variations to the 
==PAGE:24==
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
Page | 22 
original DA been developed. The Continuous Daylight Autonomy (DAcon) proposed by 
Rogers (Rogers, 2006) gives in contrast to the original DA partial credit to values below 
the defined threshold as these illuminance levels still can contribute to a daylit space. 
In combination with DAcon is it recommended to use the Maximum Daylight 
Autonomy (DAmax) to indicate when and where potentially glary conditions or high 
illuminance contrast appear in a space. The DAmax represent the percentage of 
occupied hours a maximum illuminance level is met and is typically calculated for a 
threshold ten times the design illuminance of a space (Reinhart, F, Mardaljevic and 
Rogers, 2013). Another approach to the original DA is the Spatial Daylight Autonomy 
(sDA) this method describes how much of the work plane that receives a specified 
illuminance level for at least half of the occupied hours.  
The Useful Daylight Illuminances (UDI) proposed by Nabil and Mardaljevic (Nabil and 
Mardaljevic, 2005) gives the range within the daylight should be considered as useful 
for occupants. UDI is defined as the percentage of a year the daylight illuminance 
across the work plane fall within a range of 100 – 3000lux. The suggested useful range 
between the maximum and minimum illuminance levels is based on surveys of 
occupant preferences and behaviour in an office environment. For illuminances below 
100lux is the daylight level generally considered to be insufficient either for the visual 
environment or the ability to perform a visual task, whereas daylight illuminance 
above 3000lux can cause visual or thermal discomfort. When daylight levels are 
evaluated is it recommended by the author that illuminance levels above 3000lux not 
entirely should be eliminated as the upper limit for daylight exposure is not yet know.

3.3 Shading Systems  
The properties of the window design are essential to the architectural appearance and 
the performance of a building. They allow visual contact with the exterior 
environment and affect the indoor comfort and the energy budget in many diverse 
ways. Too much sunlight penetration can cause thermal or visual discomfort and lead 
to an unpleasant indoor environment. To prevent these issues can a wisely chosen 
shading system contribute positively to the building performance and the well-being 
of the users. A well-designed shading system should reduce unwanted solar radiation 
without increasing the need of electric light or blocking the visual contact with the 
exterior environment (Kuhn, 2017). The best design strategy of shading devices is 
determined by building type and use, aesthetic requirements, sky conditions and the 
availability of natural light depended on building location and orientation (Jacobs, 
2001).  These aspects together with the aesthetic impact needs to be assessed when 
a shading system is chosen for a specific building (Kuhn, 2017).  
 
3.3.1 Typology of shading systems  
Different shading systems designs with various qualities are available on the market. 
In order to characterize theses devises has a simple classification been introduced by 
(Laura Bellia, Concetta Marino, Francesco Minichiello, 2014) presented in figure 2 
where the shading devices are classified by the principle of the operating system and 
their position in relation to the glazing. These different design parameters affect how 
shading systems preform and the ability to meet the requirements for visual and 
thermal comfort.  
==PAGE:26==
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
Page | 24 
 
Figure  2 classification of shading devices (Laura Bellia, Concetta Marino, Francesco Minichiello, 2014) 
Another parameter also to consider is the angular selectivity of a shading system. 
Typical systems depended on the suns angle of incidence is Venetian blinds, overhangs, 
and fins. These systems are characterized by its cut-off angle defined as the highest 
angle at which direct radiation is transmitted into the interior space. Above this angle 
is direct sunlight blocked, while diffuse radiation can be used for a space illumination. 
Not all shading devices present angular selectivity. Systems such as screens, curtains 
and roller blinds are not depended on the suns angle of incidence but will still reduce 
direct and diffuse radiation (Tsangrassoulis, 2015) .   
 
3.3.2 Positioning  
3.3.2.1 Internal 
Internal shading systems are effective at controlling diffuse and reflected light and are 
best used for glare control, backup shading or night time blocking. Internal shading is 
typically adjustable and in the form of curtains, roller or venetian blinds. It is in general 
less expensive and easy to maintain and control. Alone has interior shading only a 
limited ability to control solar heat gain as it does not prevent solar radiation to be 
absorbed before it has passed through the glazing. For this reason, are interior shading 
devices thermally inefficient and should only be used for back up where shading is 
required for preventing of overheating (Stack, Goulding and Owen, no date).  
 
==PAGE:27==
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
Page | 25 
3.3.2.2 External 
External devices are the most effective way to reduce the incoming solar heat gain 
before it enters the interior of the building. External solar shading can take many 
forms, ranging from an integrated part of the building design to a more independent 
facade attachment (Alcock, Fitzgerald and Lewis, no date). From highly visible to more 
anonymous devices can exterior shading have a great impact on the architectural 
expression. The most common types of external shading devices are fixed horizontal 
overhangs, awnings, shutters and blinds but can also be in the form of vegetation or 
urban design. Trees correctly applied can work well as an external shading. In the 
summer time will a leafy tree be able to prevent from direct sun and with its seasonal 
adaptation still allow winter sun to reach the building interior (Stack, Goulding and 
Owen, no date).   
 
3.3.2.3 Intermediate 
Intermediate shading devices are located between the panes of a doubled glazed unit 
or within a curtain wall for commercial buildings. Such devices combine the 
advantages of external and internal shading devices and can be either a fixed or a 
movable system. Typically, are Venetian blinds used, but newer types of shading 
systems such as MicroShade are also an option. MicroShade consists of microscopic 
lamellas fully integrated between glazing panes. Such shading system is almost 
invisible but provides a passive and highly active shading. Intermediate shading 
systems does not provide the same protection against overheating in the interior 
space as external ones. On the other hand, is intermediate shading systems, protected 
against weather conditions, which make this type of systems a good solution for high-
rise buildings, where it is not always possible to install external shading due to high 
wind loads.   
 
 
==PAGE:28==
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
Page | 26 
3.3.3 Fixed shading devices  
Fixed systems are often preferred as it is easier to install and maintain, and less 
expensive compared to other shading systems (Stack, Goulding and Owen, no date) 
The main determinant for the design is the orientation of the facade since theses fixed 
systems cannot be controlled and adjusted in proportion to the movement of the sun 
(Jacobs, 2001). Fixed systems are typically designed as a horizontal overhang, vertical 
fin or a combination of both where the most common type is the horizontal overhang. 
In general, are fixed devices effective at blocking direct sunlight, but are less useful 
against reflected and diffuse sun light. In the northern hemi-sphere are horizontal 
overhangs typically installed on south facing facades as it provides efficient control 
against high-angled light from direct summer sun and allows solar penetration in 
winter. Vertical fixed fins are preferred for east and west orientated directions as it 
provides a better control of the low-angled morning and afternoon sun compared to 
horizontal overhangs (Stack, Goulding and Owen, no date).  
 
3.3.3.1 Light shelf 
Light shelf can protect from high angled solar radiation and is designed to redirect 
daylight into the interior space. The effectiveness of a light shelf is depended on the 
orientation of the facade as is also the case for horizontal overhangs. This type of 
system is most preferred on south facing facades and do not perform as good on west 
and east facing facades. An outer light shelf is best for shading whereas an internal 
one is better for glare protection. Parameters such as room detentions and window 
orientation must be considered to choose the right system. To redirect sunlight 
deeper into the room is highly reflective materials used on the top surface. A lower 
placed light shelf can reflect more daylight light at the ceiling, but it is essential that 
the top part of light shelf cannot be seen by the occupants in the room, as it can be 
unpleasant for the occupants due to glare issues (IEA SHC, 2000). Therefore, is the 
ceiling height essential for the light shelf to work correctly and it is recommended to 
use such a system at a ceiling height above 3m (Tsangrassoulis, 2015) 
==PAGE:29==
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
Page | 27 
3.3.4 Movable shading devices  
Movable shading devices can be controlled both for direct summer sun and low-
angled winter conditions and offer more advantages in comparison to fixed shading 
systems. A movable system can be controlled either manually, automatically or a 
combination of both. How the system is operated can have big impact on how well it 
is performing and the satisfaction among occupants. Manually controlled system 
allows occupant to control after its desired needs concerning privacy and glare issues 
as well as seasonal variations and the movement of the sun. At the same time can 
incorrect user behaviour have a negative impact on its effectiveness (Stack, Goulding 
and Owen, no date).     
 
3.3.4.1 Venetian blinds 
Venetian blinds are used both for shading and redirecting of light for all facade 
orientations. It a classic daylight system installed as an internal, external or 
intermediate device. In terms of optical and thermal properties are venetian blinds a 
complex type of shading system. Venetian blinds consist of equally separated louvres 
operated as a movable or fixed system. Its properties and mechanism to control direct 
daylight is depended on tilt angle, louvre characteristics and sun angle of incidence 
(Tzempelikos, 2008)  The surface properties of venetian blinds should be chosen 
carefully. The use of highly reflective materials can help redirecting of daylight deeper 
into the space, but at the same time cause problems with glare (Tsangrassoulis, 2015) 
In order to increase the performance is it recommended to subdivide the system into 
an upper and lower controlled part. The upper section is important for daylighting 
whereas the lower part should be controlled manually to avoided glare and discomfort 
in the interior space (Kuhn, 2017).  
 
 
==PAGE:30==
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
Page | 28 
3.3.4.2 Roller blinds  
Roller blinds can be installed internal, external or between intermediate panes and 
improve both the thermal and visual environment. Its performance is almost 
independent from the sun angle of incidence and is mainly determined by the optical 
properties of the curtain. The reflectance determines the solar control performance 
and the transmittance the ability to block direct light and the possibility for visual 
contact with the exterior environment. Both aspects cannot be for filled at the same 
time and a compromise between direct light transmittance and exterior visual contact 
is needed for this type of system (Kuhn, 2017). In a simulation study (Dubois, 2003) 
was it indicated that not all types of screens are acceptable.   
 
3.3.4.3 Awning blinds 
Awnings is a classic shading system installed external as a movable or fixed device. 
Generally, when the shading system is in use does it cover the upper part of the 
window alone and allows visual contact with the exterior environment. It protects 
against direct daylight and allows reflected daylight to enter the building. Moreover, 
can awnings be categorized into two subtypes drop-arm Awnings and Sliding-arm 
(makisolette) Awnings depended on how it is operating. Drop-arm Awnings project 
the fabric forward away from the window. In unfolded conditions is the drop-arm 
perpendicular to the facade but can also be operated for angles above 90° depending 
on the sun angle of incidence. Sliding-arm Awnings consist of an upper and lower part. 
The upper part remains vertical on the facade and acts as a screen whereas the lower 
part is functioning as an awning. It provides a more effective shading solution for 
different faced orientations as the lighting effect is reduced from the sides.

3.4 Simulation-based studies for daylighting  
Several tools can be used for simulation-based studies for daylighting performance in 
buildings. One of these tools is DIVA an energy and daylighting plugin for 
grasshopper3D and Rhinoceros (Rhino). DIVA is a validated and tested simulation 
engine based on EnergyPlus, Radiance and DAYSIM that allows users to carry out 
CBDM metrics, annual glare analysis and other environmental performance 
evaluations. Radiance and DAYSIM are both lightning tools that enable building 
designers to analyse and visualise daylight in the interior space. Radiance uses back-
ward raytracing for lightning simulation and rendering which means that light is 
followed from the measuring point and back to the light source. DAYSIM is a radiance-
based software that is used for annual daylight calculations and allows users to 
evaluate the dynamic performance of shading devices with the use of CBDM metrics. 
DIVA exposes the full complement of Radiance raytracing parameters which gives the 
ability to determine the quality and the time of the simulation. Radiance and DAYSIM 
are also provided in other graphical user interfaces.  
3.4.1 Grasshpper3D 
Grasshopper is a visual programming interface that comes as a plugin for Rhino. The 
advantage of Grasshopper is the ability to work with Parametric and algorithmic 
design, which allows easy geometry manipulation and efficient design methods. 
Parametric design can with slight variants of the original parameters generate a huge 
difference in the outcome and help designers to minimise the time needed to create 
and test design variants through its ability, to automated changes and eliminate 
repetitive tasks. Grasshopper work with dragging components onto a canvas where 
each component represents a specific function. The output from these components is 
given as an input of the subsequent component. Furthermore, is it for more advanced 
use possible to build custom components in Grasshopper.

4.3 infographic 
An infographic (short for information graphic) is a visual representation of information 
or data and is used for efficiently communicating complex information in a manner 
that is easy to understand. One of the reasons why infographics is a powerful tool is 
due to how the human brain process images. Human beings are visual by nature and 
we process and respond faster to images than text. Our brains have the ability to make 
instantly sense of visual patterns and process visual data all in once. Data infographics 
can be used to communicate numbers in a way that is easy to understand and give an 
insight that is not clear with numbers alone (Smiciklas, 2012)   
 
Figure  5 Anatomy of an infographic (Smiciklas, 2012) 
In this study was it decided to use a waffle chart for data visualisation. It is a chart that 
is easy to understand and makes it simple to communicate and comparer the final 
results and its contribution to a whole. A waffle chart is similar to pie charts and is 
basically a grid in which each cell represents one percentage summing up to a total of 
one hundred. The waffle charts were generated in excel with conditional formatting 
where each grid cell is highlighted with different colours based on the given 
percentage value. The waffle charts for the selected metrics is a representation of the 
percentage of hours the calculated DGP or illuminance levels was in a specified range. 
To visually organize and create order is borders and lines often used as a basic design 
element. The gridlines in the created waffle charts help the eyes to scan and compare 
the graphic in a way that is easy to understand. Another important aspect of how the 
data was visualized was the selected colours. Our eyes are often drawn to bold and 
contrasting colours and can be used as a visual tool to make important information 
stand out. In section 6.1 will the infographic be further descried.

5.5 Calculation grid  
To examine the daylight levels in an interior space is it necessary to apply a calculation 
grid and analysis nodes to the work plane of interest. The analysis nodes are located 
within each grid cell and used for annual daylight analysis to calculate the hourly 
illuminance levels for the occupied hours. In DIVA the analysis grid is automatically 
generated when one or multiply planer surfaces are chosen as a reference plan. The 
user is free to set the distance between the grid points that determine the size of the 
uniform grid cells the work plane will be divided in. Both the accuracy of the results 
and simulation time is affected of the grid size and should therefore be considered 
carefully. To avoid very low luminance levels near the walls and very high luminance 
levels close to the windows it is recommended to exclude an area of grid pointes 0.5m 
from the walls (Dansk Standard, 2018). Usually is it not a problem to use the floor 
surface a reference plane as the calculation grid automatically is generated with a 
distance of 0.2m from the walls but due to the complexity of the geometry was the 
grid pointes generated to close to the windows and it was decided to use a manually 
created a reference plane with a distance from the walls of 0.5m. The nodes for the 
calculation grid was set to 0.4m with a measuring height above floor height of 0.85m 
as required in (Dansk Standard, 2018). 
 
Due to its different advantaged has both the DIVA plug-in for Rhino and grasshopper 
been used to study the environmental daylight performance for the case study 
buildings. To be able to use an identical simulation grid for the daylight calculations 
was the generated calculation grid (mesh) in Rhino given as an input to the grid 
component in grasshopper. When a mesh is used as an input to the grid component 
in grasshopper is the mesh plane not further subdivided and the grid sizing and 
measuring nodes will stay the same.

5.6 Shading control  
In buildings can shading devices be operated after the desired needs, meaning that 
the shading devices are not all opened or closed at the same time. The use of shading 
devices and how they are operated effects the amount of available daylight and 
should therefore be considered in a daylighting metric (Reinhart, F, Mardaljevic and 
Rogers, 2013)To evaluate the dynamic performance of the shading systems was the 
mechanical dynamic shading mode used to control the dynamic geometric of the 
shading derives. The mechanical shading systems take Rhino layers as their input and 
it was therefore necessary to model each of the considered shading states on separate 
Rhino layers and assign the selected material to the layer. To determine how the 
shading devices was operated was the manual control mode selected. The manual 
control uses a user behaviour model (Lightswitch algorithm) to predict how occupants 
might operate the shading devices. In DIVA is the occupant behaviour set as an active 
user (Reinhart, 2004). This mean the shading device will be activated if the visual 
comfort is increased or if direct sunlight is present on any of the defined work plane 
sensors. When the shading system is lowered will it first be re-opened again the next 
morning depended on occupancy profile.    
 
 
 
 
==PAGE:50==
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
Page | 48 
In this project was it selected that the shading devices was activated differently for 
the living rooms and the bed rooms. For the living rooms was the shading systems 
operated after the annual glare profile, calculated for the different view directions. 
This option is given for any climate base simulation in DIVA and will causes the shading 
devices to be activated as soon as the DGP in the annual glare profile is above 40%. 
For the bedrooms was the shading devices lowered when direct sunlight above 
50w/m2 was present on the defined work plane sensors. To avoid the shading system 
be excessively active was the work plane nodes of 1.5m from the window not 
considered as reference sensors for activating the shading system.  
 
5.7 Simulation timeframe and occupancy schedules 
To evaluate the daylight levels from climate-based analysis is it necessary to use an 
occupancy schedule to indicate when occupants are presented, and illuminance levels 
should be evaluated. This methodology is used for office buildings and can also be 
apply for evaluating the daylight levels in residential buildings. However, the 
multifunctional room types and the diversity of activities and personal factors makes 
it a difficult task predict how much and for how long a interior space is occupied.  
(Dogan and Park, no date). Depended on the required result is it possible to perform 
analysis in a range from every hour of a year to a given date or time. Typical design 
days are, the 21st of June, the 21st of December and either spring or autumn equinox, 
where day and nighttime are equal in duration. Theses design days represent the 
extreme and average conditions within a year. To access the daylight availability or 
quality for an interior space is it ideal to use a simulation time frame of a full year as 
realistic and time varying daylight illuminances is considered.  
 
To evaluate the daylighting performance for the different case study buildings was 
different custom occupancy schedules considered with a timeframe over a year. The 
first schedule (Daylit hours) presented in table 10 was used to assesses the daylight 
potential of the case buildings without taking any occupancy behavioural into account.

6.1 Processing and DGP analysis 
To run the DGP analysis is it necessary to save the different view directions in Rhino 
to generate the HDR image used for the annual glare analysis. For the correct setting 
of the camera, was the comfort mannequin used in grasshopper. This component is a 
part of the honeybee plugins for grasshopper and initially used for analysis of 
occupancy comfort but can also be used to control the camera setting in the Rhino 
viewport. To use this component was a point placed 2m from the window with a 
centre line in the middle of the window. The camera direction is set from the view 
height of the comfort mannequin and can be specified in grasshopper for a position 
of standing, sitting or lying. 
 
To determine the glare protection capability for each of the shading system was the 
annual glare calculated for different critical view directions facing towards the window. 
For each of the considered shading states was a file generated containing the DGP 
values for each hour of the year for a certain view direction. To evaluate the 
performance of the shading devices was an annual DGP profile for the overall 
performance of the shading systems generated. A custom component see figure 17 
was scripted in python and used within grasshopper to interpolate between the 
calculated DGP values for each of the shading states. The DGP files for respectively 
shading state 1 and 2 was given as an input to the comparing component to defined 
which shading state should be used. A new list was generated and a variable of 0.5 
was assigned if the DGP values was equal or below the compared DGP values for 
shading state 2. If the DGP was reduced to a lower level for shading state 2 was a 
variable of 1 assigned. It should be mentioned that equal DGP values not necessarily 
means they have been reduced to the same level as it can also indication off that 
shading not has been required for glare control. The list output was given as an input

8 Conclusion 
In this study was a new comfort rating concept introduced for a holistic evaluation of 
residential buildings. The comfort state in residential buildings was represented with 
three indicators: Visual discomfort for worst-case scenarios, view out considering the 
visible layers and access to daylight. To give a quick and easy overview of how the 
buildings were performing was it considered to use an infographic for a visual 
representation of the selected main indicators. To show its applicability and strength 
in communicating the comfort rating, were several simulation scenarios considered 
and assessed. Three different case building, and two external and one internal solar 
shading devices were selected and simulated. The considered case building was a part 
of a newly built and modern apartment complex in central Copenhagen. With the 
visual representation of the chosen indicators was it easy to get a quick assessment of 
the comfort state of the building. Two different occupancy schedules were considered 
representing respectively people how were working (W) and people who were 
working at home (WAH).  For the occupied hours (W) was it considered that people 
would be present at home in the morning and between 17.00 to sunset, whereas the 
occupied hours for working at home were examined for all hours doing the day and 
was considering a higher amount of daylit hours. With the visible representation for 
the considered occupancy, schedules were it easy to assess and compare the 
performance of the residential buildings. To provide a higher degree of understanding 
will it be beneficial to extend the comfort rating to include more information. For a 
better understanding of how daylight levels were increased and how the illuminance 
levels were distributed above the threshold will the consideration of more ranges be 
beneficial. The same applies to the DGP analysis where it was difficult to differentiate 
the performance of the shading systems from each other. For the consideration of 
view out and the possibility to see a layer of ground, city and sky was it for most of the 
==PAGE:95==
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
Page | 93 
considered view direction possible to see the three layers for the considered distance 
between the window and the wall. To give a more detailed understanding of the view 
out, will it be beneficial if the infographic for the also was considering how the view 
out was composed, as it is also an essential aspect of view quality.     
 
9 Outlook 
The process with creating the waffle charts in excel was a slow and manual process 
and therefore will a more optimised process be required. To do sow can an idea be to 
create a tool in grasshopper to generate the considered infographics. As mention is 
one of the great features about grasshopper the ability to work with parametric design 
and the possibility to create custom components. Further for a more advanced output 
could the outputted ill files from DAYSIM be used. Theses outputted data files contains 
the measured illuminance levels for each calculation nodes for every hour of the year. 
To work with these files will it be required to do more scripting as they contain a lot 
of information. One of the benefits from this considered method is, that is will not 
require more simulations to create a more detailed infographic. For the DGP analysis 
will it only be required to consider more ranges in the create python script.
