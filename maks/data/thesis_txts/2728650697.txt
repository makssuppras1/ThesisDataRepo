Abstract
In this report the QoS(Quality of Service) gain from communicating with multiple
operators is examined based on measurements conducted during train and car
rides. Additionally, it is examined experimentally what the pathloss coefficient is
at various height which can be used for coverage planning in 3 dimensions.
It was determined the alpha and beta coefficients for the log distance model for
3.45MHz in the altitude 22 to 34 meters is in the range 1.69 to 2.09 and 65.9 to
80.3 respectively. The quality of service tests showed that the quality of service
was improved when communicating via multiple operators, however an improved
test setup is required to more certainly determine the degree of improvement. A
proposal for such an improved setup is also included in the report.

Introduction
The development of wireless communication, laid the foundation, for the creation
of the first widespread mobile communication system, known as 2G or GSM
(Global System for Mobile communication). This system allowed users to commu-
nicate with each other while moving about, which was revolutionary at the time.
With further development, the mobile networks began facilitating communication
with the regular internet and so called M2M (Machine to Machine) communica-
tion.
The development of mobile networks, have been largely driven by use cases dic-
tated by the entertainment industry, such as video and audio streaming. There-
fore there has been a significant focus on maximising the throughput from the
core network to the end user.
However the ability to communicate, while retaining the ability to move geograph-
ically, has many potential use cases beyond entertainment.
For instance, AV (Autonomous Vehicles) can benefit from communicating with
other vehicles or fixed infrastructure, such as traffic lights. Since AV can move
along any road, it is intrinsically a necessity that the communication technology
supports mobility.
A more specific example is an ambulance, which may be able to utilize the com-
munication to transmit information about the patient to the hospital. This can be
used to begin diagnosis during transit and/or start treatment earlier, which can
potentially safe lives.
Trains are likewise mobile entities, although more restricted than cars, that can
benefit from the ability to communicate while traveling. The transmitted informa-
tion can both be user data, but also critical control data.
Other potential benefactors include drones, who benefit directly from the mobility
provided by wireless communication.
For instance, drones are excellent can-
didates for search and rescue missions in mountains or other hard to navigate
terrain. However currently regulations limit drones, so that they may only be op-
erated within line of sight. To operate a drone beyond line of sight, it is necessary
to be able to send and receive control inputs within a reasonable time frame and
to keep track of the drone. This can for instance be done via a video feed from
the drone.
For any given use case, it is necessary to determine if a network can satisfy the
required QoS (Quality of Service). The QoS requirements vary from specific use
case to use case and some typical key parameters include throughput, latency,
jitter, packet loss, etc. If the technology does not satisfy the requirements, then
another technology have to be chosen or the network has to be upgraded.
The ambulance, train and drone use case discussed are critical since they in prin-
ciple can jeopardize human life. For this reason, it is important that the networks
they utilize to transmit data have a high degree of availability.

Even though the parameters for the use cases in table 2 are quite different, they
all require mobility and reliable communication.
There already exists some standards and deployed technologies designed for use
cases that centers around high reliability. Two prominent examples are URLLC
(Ultra Reliable and Low Latency Communication), which is standardized by the
3GPP (Third Generation Partnership Project), and TETRA (TErrestrial Trunked
RAdio), standardized by ETSI (European Telecommunications Standards Insti-
tute).
The 3GPP’s requirements for URLLC in regards to reliability is that a packet of
32 bytes should experience at most 1ms latency and a packet drop rate of 10–5
[4]. However since mobile networks are not currently designed for users above
ground level, there is no guarantee that current implementations of URLLC can
be used for drones, since coverage is not currently designed for 3D.
Another reliability oriented technology is TETRA. It is used by emergency respon-
ders, the military and other organization where reliability is important. TETRA
uses a low frequency from 380 to 460MHz. TETRA supports communication up
to a range of 83km with a maximum throughput of only 500kbit/s. [5]. While
TETRA does support data transfers, it currently does not support real time video,
which makes it unsuitable for some of the use cases mentioned in table 2.
Amongst these options, the mobile network seems most promising as it already
can provide the necessary QoS for the use cases described in table 2. However
relying on a single mobile operator to facilitate the communication can be prob-
lematic if the network is not available. Therefore high availability is an important
aspect.
A network can be said to have a high degree of availability, if the network has
a high amount of uptime. Uptime is in this context understood as time in which
the network can be reached and fulfil its intended function. A typical measure
of availability is the 9’s of availability. For instance, a five nines network has an
availability of 99.999%, which corresponds to approximately 5 minutes per year.

It is important to note that systems with high availability can still experiences an
arbitrary number of errors. The availability only implies that the system as a whole
functions, despite the errors. To highlight this a simple example is considered.
Consider the case where a computer receives data and has to compute some
metric within a given time limit. It does this using a number of modules called n,
which each use their own algorithm. The modules each have a certain probability,
ce, of not computing the metric in time. However the data is always fed to all
modules. From this it can be deduced the only time the metric is not computed
in time, is when all modules fail simultaneously. If the errors are independent,
then the probability of successfully calculating the metric, cs, can be calculated
via equation (1):
cs = 1 – cfn
(1)
For the case where cf=0.25 and n=4, the system has a probability of 0.9961 or
99.61% chance of calculating the metric. If instead n=7, then the chance is above
99.99%.
The key take away from this example is that the overall systems reliability can be
improved by using redundant components or subsystems, which eliminates single
point of failures.
This logic can be extended to pathways within a single network. If there are mul-
tiple paths and each message is send along all paths, then the reliability of the
message arriving increases. However, steps such as building additional path-
ways, buying redundant or high quality network nodes is expensive.
An alternative to building and improving existing infrastructure is to take advan-
tage of the fact that multiple operators exist. Instead of considering paths within
a network, each mobile operator’s network is considered as a path. An important
question then is, how independent these networks are in regards to each other.
If the networks are truly independent and each network is used to transmit the
same message, then the probability of transmitting a message from A to B can
be calculated using equation (1).
However since operators may use the same towers, power sources and experi-
ence the same natural disasters, it is clear that some degree of correlation exists.
If the degree of correlation is significant, then the gain from communicating with
multiple mobile operators is negligible. In that case, it therefore would be better
to use an independent technology as a supplement. However if the degree of
correlation is low, then mobile networks may be able to provide a highly reliable
connection. Therefore it is important to determine the degree of correlation be-
tween operators.
Since both coverage and the correlation between mobile operators are important
for the discussed use cases, the scope of the project is split into two distinct parts:
[I] Determine the correlation between operators in regards to QoS.
[II] Determine the pathloss as a function of both distance and height.

Part [I] is important as it is necessary to determine if there is correlation in net-
work performance across vendors within a technology. The degree of correlation
directly impacts the reliability of the communication as a whole and determining
that correlation will help shape future communication designs.
Part [II] is required, as wireless coverage has typically been planned in 2D, but
flying drones maneuver in all 3 dimensions. It is therefore critical to determine the
coverage in 3D, so that networks can be expanded, modifier, and/or drone flight
paths can be restricted accordingly.
In order to determine these unknowns, data are collected via experimental setups
and then subsequently analysed and visualized.
The rest of this report is structured as follows: In the chapter ”Coverage” the
concepts of coverage and the link budget are introduced and their relationship to
the chosen pathloss models explained. In the chapter ”Network Considerations”,
some of the network aspects are discussed, such as the TCP (Transmission Con-
trol Protocol) protocol. In the chapter ”Quality of Service Tests”, the measurement
methodology for the QoS tests and the developed setup is explained. In the rel-
evant subchapters, the different measurement campaigns and their findings are
discussed. In the chapter ”Pathloss Tests”, the drone setup and the pathloss es-
timation procedures are discussed. In the chapter ”Discussion & Future Work”
limitations of the measurements are discussed. Finally the conclusion sums up
the report’s findings.

Coverage
It is absolutely essential, but easy to take for granted, that a device must be able
to reach a network, and vice versa, in order to communicate with it. This is called
coverage and is an essential aspect of all wireless technologies, such as WiFi,
ZigBee and mobile networks etc.
There are three principle ways to determine if a wireless network is reachable
from a given geographic position. One option is to do experimental measurements
with real devices. Some of the shortcomings of experimental setups include:
• Very time consuming.
• Results may only be valid for that exact or very similar device.
• Conditions at measurement time may be statistical outlier.
However, the data gathered by the device takes into account all physical phe-
nomenon, such as fading and interference.
An alternative is to estimate the coverage and generate so called coverage maps.
This is done based on knowledge of the configuration of the network, path loss
models and fading conditions etc. Some shortcomings of estimated coverage
maps are:
• The choose of path loss model may not suit a given area.
• May not properly account for local factors such as fading.
However, the coverage map is faster to generate, even with sophisticated models,
since it does not require any real life measurements.
The third options is a hybrid of the two extremes mentioned earlier. In a hybrid
model, some experimental data is gathered and then this data is used to generate
models. These models can then be used to estimate the actual coverage map. In
order to determine if a device can reach another device, a useful concept called
the link budget can be used. The simplest and most abstract model is described
by equation (2):
Prx = Ptx – L
(2)
Where Prx is received power, Ptx is transmitted power and L represents losses.
Intuitively equation (2) is correct, because all transmitted power that is not re-
ceived must have been lost somewhere from the perspective of the receiver.
In order to determine if the receiver can sense a signal send by the transmitter,
it is necessary to introduce the concept sensitivity. A very sensitive receiver can
sense low amounts of energy and therefore can interpret signals further away
from the transmitter than a device with a less sensitive transmitter. Equation (2)
can then be used as a basis for a useful inequality shown in expression (3) below:
Psen ≤Prx = Ptx – L
(3)
Where Psen is the receiver’s sensitivity. Expression 3, can in principle be used to
determine if a receiver can interpret a transmitted signal. If the received power

is above the receiver’s sensitivity, the incoming signal can be evaluated. It is im-
portant to note, that a link budget has to be calculated for both the uplink and
downlink direction in order to have duplex communication.
In order to determine the amount of received power in expression 3, it is neces-
sary to describe what causes power to not arrive at the receiver. Typically the
most important component is the path loss, denoted PL. Path loss, also known
as path attenuation, is the attenuation that a signal experiences as it traverses
physical space through a given medium. There are many reasons for the path
attenuation, such as free space loss, reflection, refraction, etc. The importance of
each of these phenomenon varies with the environment and the physical medium
the signal propagates through.
In a simple ideal case, where a transceiver uses an isomorphic antenna, the en-
ergy spreads out as a sphere around the antenna. Consequently as the signal
spreads out, the amount of power per area, known as the power density de-
creases. From this the expression (3) can be extended to expression (4) below:
Psen ≤Prx = Ptx – PL(d)
(4)
Where PL(d) is the pathloss at a given distance d. There are other phenomenon
as well, such as rayleigh fading which is caused by the fact that the transmitted
signal may arrive at the destination via different paths. A visual illustration of this
phenomenon is shown below in figure 1:
Figure 1: Multipath scenario between a cell tower and a phone. The original
signal(orange) is reflected(cyan) and arrives at the phone at different times.
Since the signals traverse different distances, the signals may constructively in-
terfere or destructively interfere. The impact of this may vary significantly even
with only minute changes in geographical position. A simple expression for the
link budget can then be defined as:
Psen ≤Prx = Ptx – PL(d) – F(x, y, z)
(5)

Where F(x, y, z) corresponds to the fading experienced at a given fixed position.
The antenna used for transmitting the signal is also important as it dictates how
spread out the radiated energy is. An ideal isomorphic antenna will spread the
energy evenly. A directional antenna directs the energy in a specific direction.
This means if a receiver is within the spatial region, it will receive more power from
a directional antenna than an isomorphic antenna. This is commonly known as
antenna gain and can be implemented into the link budget as shown in expression
(6):
Psen ≤Prx = Ptx – PL(d) – F(x, y, z) – Ag
(6)
Where Ag is the antenna gain. Based on the expression in (6), it is possible to
determine the edges of a cell and the receiver power at various points within a
geographical area. It is possible to add many additional factors to equation (6),
such as the coupling loss between components internally in the base stations.
For the purposes of this report however, expression (6) is sufficient.
Pathloss
In the section Coverage, the concept of a link budget was explained. The expres-
sions (2) through (6) all contain PL(d), which is the pathloss at a given distance
d. In order to get an accurate coverage map it is therefore essential to select an
appropriate pathloss model.
A pathloss model is a mathematical model that describes the pathloss experi-
enced by a signal that traverses a medium, which is typically air. There exists a
number of pathloss models, such as the free space propagation, log-distance ,
oku-hatamura and the ABG(alpha-beta-gamma) model etc. Amongst these, the
oku-hatamura model in particular was widely used by mobile operators, since it
describes the propagation in urban environments from 100-1000 MHz. These fre-
quencies were utilized in GSM. The model was later extended to 1900MHz. [6]
One reason multiple models exist is because the reduction in power over distance
highly depends on the environment. For instance, an open plain only reflects elec-
tromagnetic radiation that hits the ground. There is also LoS(Line of Sight) at all
times. In an urban environment, LoS may not be present and additionally, there
are potentially a multitude of reflections which can interfere constructively or de-
structively with each other.
Traditional mobile networks and coverage maps are designed for users on the
ground, which is adequate for the railway and ambulance use case, but not for
the drone case. This increases the complexity of coverage map generation.
Due to the importance of coverage, and consequently the pathloss models, stud-
ies have been conducted to try and determine an appropriate pathloss model at
various heights. Experiments have also been done to determine RSRP (Refer-
ence Signal Received Power) as a function of height. One of the most widely
examined pathloss omdel is the log distance model which is shown in expression
7 below:

gained. The SIR values tended to decrease as a function of height and deviated
significantly less.

creased until it became stable at 60 meters. The authors also concluded that it
was possible to have a stable uplink connection of 20Mbps connection, as long
as there was sufficient signal strength.

Network Considerations
In the previous sections, the topics of availability and coverage of networks were
briefly discussed. These topics are concerned with the ability of a network to al-
locate resources to a device and for the device to be able to reach the network.
However, two devices communicating from the same place, with the same entity,
may experience different QoS due to non-physical phenomenons. Since one of
the test setups in this project is concerned with QoS, it is worth briefly examining
why this is the case.
In the networks of today, there is a plethora of protocols in use. Each protocol has
a distinct function and structure. Some examples include UDP (User Datagram
Protocol) and TCP for transferring information between clients, ICMP (Internet
Control Message Protocol) for troubleshooting and NTP (Network Time Protocol)
for synchronizing network devices.
All of the use cases described in table 2, involves sending video or sensor data
between two entities in a network. This type of information transfer is typically
done using UDP or TCP, which reside on layer 4 of the OSI (Open Systems In-
terconnection) model.
One of the main differences between these protocols is that TCP is a reliable
protocol, which uses a combination of timers, ACK (ACKnowledgement) and re-
transmissions to ensure that the message arrives at the destination. Ensuring that
a message send from A to B, arrives at the destination is important in many con-
texts and TCP is therefore widely used. An example showcasing the difference
between UDP and TCP is shown in figure 2:
Figure 2: Simplified example of communication using TCP(left) and UDP(right)
with a series of single packets.
Page 11 of 60
==PAGE:20==
Wireless Networks for Reliable Drone Communication
August 24, 2023
When a packet is lost using TCP, an ACK will not be send and after some time,
T0 on figure 2, the packet is re-transmitted. When the packet is lost in the UDP
case, the packet is never received by the receiver.
There are some use cases such as streaming and voice communication that are
very sensitive to latency. If a packet is lost in such a scenario, the re-transmitted
package may not have time to arrive at the receiver before being obsolete. There-
fore such use cases typically rely on the UDP packet instead of TCP.
For the setup developed in this project to measure QoS, it is not necessary to im-
plement or emulate the exact application. However it is necessary to measure the
latency and the throughput of the communication. To avoid bias in the results, it is
necessary to use the same protocol for the latency, uplink and downlink through-
put measurements. It is also necessary to communicate with a fixed entity in the
network, so that the gathered results are comparable with one another.
For these tests, it was decided to use a server at DTU (Technical University of
Denmark), because it was available on short notice, simplified troubleshooting
and due to cost considerations. The server is located in an environment pro-
tected by a firewall within DTU, which blocks UDP messages. Therefore it was
not possible to use UDP for the developed test setup and instead experiments
with ICMP and TCP was carried out. However it was found that one of the mobile
operators, TDC (Tele Danmark Communications), starts dropping packets if too
many ICMP packets are received within a limited time. This is likely a deliber-
ate security measure against DDoS (Distributed Denial of Service) attacks. As a
result of these limitations, it was decided to use TCP for the developed setup.

Additionally each phone running the application could produce a slightly different
number of entries when running the application. In order to link the two distinct
data sets from the application and the script, it was necessary to compare the
timestamps for each entry in the dataset. The procedure to do so can be de-
scribed briefly as follow:
• Determine if the script dataset or the application data set is the shortest.
• For each entry in the shortest dataset, check how distant all entries are in
the other dataset. This distance is measured in time and called the temporal
distance.
• Pick the entry with the shortest temporal distance and check if it’s below the
threshold value. For all measurements, a threshold of 3 seconds were used.
• If the temporal distance is less or equal to the threshold, then merge the
measurements and add them to a new table, where the data is correlated.
• If the temporal distance is above the threshold, then skip the entry and go
to the next one.
Based on this methodology, it was possible to correlate the two disjoint datasets
together.
It is possible that the mobile network is heavily loaded during tests or that there
is poor coverage in the test areas. This can in turn be reflected both in the QoS
indicators, but also in the time it takes to complete the TCP handshake process.
Since this setup is designed to complete a round of measurements in 4-5 sec-
onds, occasionally the handshake process can not be complete before the allo-
cated time for the handshake process is complete. When a QoS test fails to finish
the initial handshake, this is also logged.
The event of not being assigned any resources during a request is an interest-
ing phenomenon and the correlation between each pair of operators is examined.
For the sake of simplicity, this phenomenon is simply labelled error. The number
of errors for each operator during a trip may not be identical. Therefore when
discussing correlation in this context, the following methodology is used instead:
• First determine which of the two chosen operators error lists that is the short-
est and set the accumulated sum to 0.
• For each error experienced in the shortest list, determine if the number is in
the other operators list.
• If it is in the list, then add 1 to the accumulated sum.
• Finally, divide the accumulated sum with the amount of errors in the shortest
list.
Using this methodology, the correlation will always be a number between 0 and
1. However it should be noted, that if the shortest list only has 1 number and
that number occurs in the longer list, then the correlation will be 1 by using this
definition.

Pathloss Tests
The measurements with the drone were done over multiple days and each day
contained several flights. For the sake of clarity, the collection of measurements
carried out on a single day is called a set. When determining the best fit only data
from the PCI (Physical Cell Id) 532 was used. The transmit power was 100 watts,
which is 50dBm. The antenna height of the base station was 20.5 meters. The
frequency was 3.45MHz in band 78. Since the distance traversed is small, the
true distance was approximated using Pythagoras equation, shown in equation
19:
a2 + b2 = c2
(19)
For each sample during a flight, ”a” was determined as the difference between
the known height of the drone and the antenna. The drone’s coordinates and
the antenna’s coordinate was used to determine ”b”, which corresponds to the
distance between the antenna and the drone at ground level. From this it was
possible to approximate ”c”, which is the true distance between the drone and the
antenna as shown on figure 47 below:
Figure 47: Illustration of the relationship between the height and ground distance
differences and the true distance between drone and tower antenna.
The measurement with the drone were done at Odense airport, which is a very
small airport located on the island ”Fyn”.

In order to better show the expected logarithmic dependence as a function of
distance, the actual distance is not plotted on the X-axis. Instead log10 of the dis-
tance is used. If the log distance model is correct, then there should be a linear
relationship between log10 of the distance and the output.
To determine the expected linear relationship, four different models are used.
These are ordinary least squared, lasso, ridge and elastic net regression. These
models are all linear regression models and are included to offer some compari-
son to the results generated by ordinary least squared regression.
It is important to note that the lasso, ridge and elastic net models attach weights to
each observation and rely on a parameter called alpha, which dictates how much
to penalize the weights. This alpha is an arbitrarily chosen value, thus when do-
ing the regression multiple alphas in the range from 0 to 10 are examined and the
best fit chosen.

Discussion & Future Work
There are a number of design issues with the developed setup QoS test setup,
which means the obtained results are not as reliable or as trustworthy as desired.
The low throughput obtained for downlink measurements are likely an issue of im-
plementation, which therefore means the absolute value of those measurements
are not useful. Additionally it was not possible to measure the number of re-
transmissions during the uplink and downlink measurements, but only during the
latency measurements.
A redesign of the experimental setup used for QoS measurement is therefore re-
quired so that well documented and used services such as IPERF3 and/or MTR
can be used for the uplink, downlink and latency test. This would sidestep the
issues encountered during implementation and add validity to the results.
To
overcome the lack of support for interfaces in IPERF3 on the clientside, multiple
raspberries could be used.
This would also make it possible to avoid all concerns regarding resource con-
straints of a single raspberry running multiple phones. Additionally, since most
linux based systems count TCP-retransmissions systems-wide, it may be pos-
sible to determine the packet loss ratio for an entire trip. The use of multiple
raspberries was also done in source, [11] which indicates this is a viable solution.
Such a setup would look approximately as depicted in figure 81:
Figure 81: Proposed future setup for QoS tests.
In addition to redesigning the measurements of QoS, it will be important to do
tests in additional scenarios such as the bus. Despite the lackluster results ob-
tained from the developed setup, it was possible to demonstrate that the overall
methodology used should be able to produce the desired data that can quan-
tify the degree of improvement gained by multi operator connectivity. It was also
noted that even in the current setup there was a gain in QoS by using multiple
operators, which is promising and warrants further investigation.

Conclusion
For the quality of service tests, it was possible to conclude that the average la-
tency and throughput in uplink and downlink directions were improved when the
best results were used in each round of measurement. Additionally it was found
for all operators that packet drops were more likely to come in bursts, especially
of 9 to 10 packets out of the 10 measured. For the car and train scenarios, it was
found that TNR and TDC are the least correlated when it came to tcp handshake
failures.
Due to the poor quality of results obtained for the QoS tests, it was determined
that it is necessary to make a better lab setup, such as the one proposed in the
section ”Discussion” and shown in figure 81.
It was found that the measured signal strengths varied significantly between each
trip on the train for the 4G measurements for the operator TNR. Even when mea-
surements were done right after each other, the median RSRP could vary 15dBm,
which is quite substantial. It was also noted that the RSRP values were higher
for both TDC and TNR during friday and thursday. The measured RSRQ values
were mostly the same for each operators and in the range from -20 to -4 in 5G.
Based on the data gathered with the drone and the subsequent analysis, it ap-
pears that linear least squared regression is adequate for analysing the data, as
those model consistently scored the highest in terms of R2. It was observed that
in set 1 and set 2, that there were a number of odd measurements, which may
have been caused by a nearby greenhouse, although this can not be said for cer-
tain.
The analysis suggests that the pathloss coefficient is in the range 1.69 to 2.09 for
altitudes between 22 and 34 meters. But more tests, possibly in different environ-
ments are needed to improve the certainty. For 50 meters, a much lower value
was obtained which was not expected and nothing was concluded since only a
single test at this height was conducted.
The beta values were estimated by least squared regression to be in the range
45.8 to 85.6. However if set 1’s measurement at 22 meters and the results for 50
meters were ignored, the range was only 65.9 to 80.3. Therefore it seems likely
that the beta value from 22 meters to 34 meters is located in the range 65.9 to
80.3.
