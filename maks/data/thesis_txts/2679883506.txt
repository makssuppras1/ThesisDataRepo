Abstract   
Nanoparticle (NP) systems have emerged as a strategy to overcome some of the pharmacological barriers 
encountered in traditional drug development. For instance, NPs may improve the pharmacokinetic and 
pharmacodynamic profile of a free drug by increasing the solubility, selectivity and/or biological stability. 
Particularly, protein-based NPs are being increasingly recognized due to their high biocompatibility and the low 
toxicity of the metabolites arising from degradation into constituent proteinogenic amino acids. Many different 
proteins have been used for preparation of protein NPs. The scope of this thesis is limited to NPs formulated 
from catalase (CAT) and bovine serum albumin (BSA). BSA is often used as a carrier of different drugs due to its 
many binding sites, low immunogenicity and high circulatory stability, providing the drugs with desirable 
pharmacokinetic profiles. CAT is an endogenous oxidase transforming reactive oxygen species to molecular 
oxygen and water to alleviate oxidative stress. Thus, formulation of CAT NPs may hold promise as a therapeutic 
strategy offering symptomatic relief of inflammatory disease like rheumatoid arthritis.  
 
In this thesis, the optimization of synthetic conditions and characterization of NPs prepared from CAT and BSA 
are reported. Crosslinking prior to desolvation of BSA resulted in polydisperse NP solutions, whereas surface-
crosslinking of BSA NPs significantly increased their stability. Of several crosslinkers tested on CAT, a 16.1 Å 
long, homobifunctional NHS reactive crosslinker (Sulfo-EGS) resulted in monodisperse NP solutions when used 
prior to desolvation. CD spectroscopy demonstrated intact secondary structures of crosslinked BSA as well as 
crosslinked CAT. Measurement of the activity of CAT determined from a H2O2 decomposition assay indicated a 
slight decrease in the activity after crosslinking and NP formulation. Overall, results from these experiments 
holds promise for further evaluation in experimental models of inflammatory diseases to examine the true 
potential of the herein developed CAT NPs.

Dansk resumé 
Nanopartikel (NP) systemer har evnen til at overvinde mange af de farmakologiske barriere, der forekommer i 
traditionel lægemiddeludvikling. For eksempel kan NP’er forbedre den farmakokinetiske og farmakodynamiske 
profil af et frit lægemiddel ved at øge opløseligheden, selektiviteten og/eller den biologiske stabilitet. 
Interessen for proteinbaserede NP’er er stigende på grund af deres høje biologiske kompatibilitet og lave 
toksicitet af de metabolitter, der dannes ved nedbrydningen til proteinets aminosyrer. Mange forskellige 
proteiner er blevet brugt til fremstilling af protein NP’er. Denne kandidatafhandling er afgrænset til NP 
formuleringer af katalase (CAT) og bovint serumalbumin (BSA). BSA anvendes ofte som bærer af forskellige 
lægemiddler grundet dens mange bindingssteder, lave immunogenicitet og høje plasmastabilitet, hvilket giver 
lægemidlerne bedre farmakokinetiske egenskaber. CAT er et oxidase enzym, som katalyserer omdannelsen af 
reaktive iltarter til molekylært ilt og vand for at aflede oxidativ stress. Derfor kan formulering af CAT NP’er 
være en lovende terapeutisk strategi der lindre inflammatorisk sygedomme så som reumatoid artritis. 
  
 
I denne kandidatafhandling blev syntesevejen af CAT og BSA NP’er optimeret og de dannede NP’er blev 
karakteriseret. Krydslinking før desolvation af BSA resulterede i en polydisperse NP opløsning, mens at 
overfalde krydslinking af BSA NP’er øgede stabiliteten. Ud af flere forskellige krydslinkere testet på CAT førte en 
16.1 Å lang, homobifunctional NHS reaktiv krydslinker (Sulfo-EGS) til en monodisperse NP opløsning når 
krydlinking var udført før desolvation. CD spektroskopi indikerede intakt sekundær struktur af krydslinket BSA 
såvel som krydslinket CAT. Bestemmelse af CAT’s aktivitet ved en H2O2 nedbrydning test indikerede et lille fald i 
aktivitet efter krydslinking og NP formulering. Samlet set er resultaterne fra disse eksperimenter lovende for 
yderligere evaluering i modeller af inflammatoriske sygdomme for at undersøge potentialet af de heri 
udviklede CAT NP’er.

1 Background 
 
1.1 Biopharmaceuticals  
Over the past century, biopharmaceuticals have received increasingly recognition as therapeutic modalities. 
Biopharmaceuticals include high molecular weight (MW) peptides and proteins isolated from biological 
sources. They are composed of natural building blocks leading to complex molecular architectures with high 
specificity and varying modes of action. 1 Proteins are implicated in a plethora of important cellular processes 
throughout the body. For instance, complex protein networks ensure a balanced regulation of the cell life cycle 
including proliferation, migration, adhesion and apoptosis.2 Therefore, protein therapeutics can be used to 
treat a variety of diseases by replacement or enhancement of a certain protein.1 Small molecules have been 
the traditional use of medicines for many years but with the introduction of biopharmaceuticals, new 
possibilities and advantages have followed. These include high efficacy, high specificity, and a broad range of 
actions.3 The half-life of biopharmaceuticals can vary from hours (h) to weeks whereas the half-life of small 
molecules is often less than a day.1 In relation to administration, small molecules must be administered on a 
daily basis, whereas some biopharmaceuticals (e.g. long-acting insulins or monoclonal antibodies) reside 
favorable pharmacokinetic profiles allowing for less frequent dosing and thus enhanced patient compliance.1 
The use of biopharmaceuticals is often rather safe because they are built of natural buildings blocks (often 
amino acids). Therefore, the metabolism of biopharmaceuticals leads to the formation of peptide fragments or 
amino acids which evades activation of the immune system.3 However, the use of proteins has certain 
challenges including poor oral bioavailability and often poor in vivo stability.3 This is reflected in 
biopharmaceuticals being withdrawn from clinical trials because they fail to hit the goalpost rather than the 
observation of adverse effects as traditionally for small molecule drugs.4 The aim of this thesis was to 
investigate the potential of catalase (CAT) and bovine serum albumin (BSA) as biopharmaceuticals. 
1.1.1 Catalase 
CAT is an antioxidant enzyme and a crucial component in maintenance of the redox environment in virtually 
every living organism by catalytic removal of reactive oxygen species (ROS). ROS introduce oxidative stress, 
which in elevated amounts has been related to different diseases including rheumatoid arthritis, diabetes, 
neurodegenerative diseases, cancers and cardiovascular diseases.5 CAT catalyzes the decomposition of two 
hydrogen peroxide (H2O2) to one dioxide (O2) and two water (H2O) molecules.6 H2O2 is highly reactive when 
serving as a source for hydroxyl radical. Upon reaction with cellular components such as DNA, the hydroxyl 
radical can introduce mutations, which can eventually lead to cancer.7 Therefore, CAT plays an important role 
in protecting cells from oxidative damage. Studies have shown that CAT has therapeutic effects on various 
conditions and diseases such as tumor progression, atherosclerosis and ischemia-reperfusion syndrome.6,8

CAT has a MW of 240 kilo Dalton (kDa) with four identical 60 kDa sub-units (Figure 1.A). The sub-units do not 
have any peroxide degradation activity but when four sub-units are combined to form a tetrameric complex, 
CAT is biologically active.9 Throughout this thesis, the term monomer refers to tetrameric and biologically 
active CAT. Each sub-unit contains a heme group in its active site which binds nicotinamide-adenine-
dinucleotide phosphate (NADPH) with high affinity, thereby protecting the enzyme from the damaging effects 
of H2O2.10 CAT is a relatively large biopharmaceutical and is expected to have a high enzymatic activity due to 
its four active sites.11 The isoelectric point (pI) which is the pH value at which proteins have no net electrical 
charge is 5.4 for CAT.12

1.1.2 Albumin 
Human serum albumin (HSA) is a multifunctional protein found in plasma. HSA binds different ligands such as 
fatty acids, steroids and drugs. It carries them with the blood for delivery to the desired target or for 
detoxification. Furthermore, it stabilizes extracellular fluid by modulating the osmotic pressure in plasma, and 
it has anti-oxidation and anti-inflammation properties. HSA is synthesized in the liver by hepatocytes and 
released to the blood stream.15,16 Studies have shown that administration of HSA can enhance the prognosis of 
patients with hepatorenal syndrome and spontaneous peritonitis.15 
In this thesis, BSA is used. To compare the sequence of HSA and BSA a blast alignment was conducted. The 
BLAST alignment of HSA and BSA (entry P02768 and P02769, respectively), revealed 465 identical amino acid 
positions corresponding to 76.4 % homogeneity.13 This has also been reported by Karimi et al. who further 
state that the binding sites, shape and biological function of HSA and BSA are very similar.16 BSA is a globular,

1.2 Nanotechnology  
Nanotechnology refers to structures, devices and systems of sizes in the nanometer range.18,19 Nanotechnology 
is used within many fields such as physics, medicine, biology, and chemistry. The use of nanotechnology has 
grown exponentially over the past 20 years, and researchers call it one of the most promising technology of the 
21st century.20 In 1959, the idea of nanotechnology was introduced during a talk called “There’s Plenty of Room 
at the Bottom” by Richard Feynman. In the talk, he described the idea of manipulation of machines to facilitate 
measurement on molecular level.21 However, the term nanotechnology itself was not coined before 1974 by 
Norio Taniguchi describing it to comprise the process of deformation, consolidation and separation of 
components by one molecule or atom.20,22 In 1991, the word nanomedicine was used for the first time and has 
since gained increased interest from scientists as modalities for prevention, monitoring, controlling, diagnosing 
and treatment of diseases.19,20 
Nanoparticles (NPs) play an important role in the area of nanomedicine and drug delivery as it may repurpose 
drugs that display extremely high efficacy but have been discarded as a result of too high toxicity from non-
specific targeting (e.g., auristatins for treatment of cancer).23 NP-based drug delivery has expanded enormously 
during the last decade due to its great therapeutic potential for prevention or curing of various diseases. NP 
systems have the ability to overcome some of the pharmacological challenges often experienced within drug 
delivery. An example is the degradation of the protein before it reaches its target. Formulation of drugs into 
NPs can limit the accessibility of proteolytic enzymes to the drug and thereby prevent the degradation.24 
Studies suggest that a size of approximately 100 nm is optimal for NPs. This size allows for efficient uptake and 
translocation across cells while minimizing undesired toxicological and immunogenic responses.25 An 
immunogenic response might clear the NPs from the body via macrophages that recognize the NPs as foreign 
materials.26 Studies show an increased risk of immune system activation for NPs above 200 nm in size.27 In 
addition to size and shape, surface characteristics are also important factors with respect to in vivo behavior. 
An advantage of NP systems is their high surface-to-volume ratio, which can be modified with, for example, 
targeting ligands or protective compounds.25

NPs can be utilized to load drugs thereby functioning as a nanocarrier.28 They are usually composed of 
materials like lipids, amphiphilic copolymers or natural polymers.29 Some of the advantages of loading drugs in 
NPs include improved pharmacokinetic and improved site-specificity compared to the free drug.18 One of the 
major functions of NPs is the delivery of chemotherapeutic agents to cancer cells. Typical treatment of cancer 
is surgery, chemotherapeutic drugs and radiation. After a surgery, chemotherapeutic drugs or radiation are 
normally used to kill the remaining cancer cells. Chemotherapeutic drugs and radiation are often associated 
with severe side effects due to targeting of healthy cells thus not only cancer cells.30 This can be overcome by 
site-specific delivery of NPs. Site-specific delivery will result in preferential accumulation of the drug in the 
tumor cell and can be achieved by passive and active targeting.31 
1.2.1 Nanoparticle Delivery 
 
1.2.1.1 Passive Targeting 
Passive targeting is the transport of loaded therapeutic drugs without adding anything to the surface. It is 
commonly described in relation to tumor cells where transport of NPs through the vascular system will result in 
preferential accumulation in tumor cells. This relies on the enhanced permeability and retention (EPR) effect in 
tumors.30,31 The EPR effect is based on discrepancies between normal tissue and tumor tissue and can be 
divided into two effects: The first effect is the enhanced vascular permeability of tumor tissue. This effect is 
caused by larger fenestrations between endothelial cells, which allow both small and large molecules to reach 
the site of the tumor. This is in contrast to healthy tissue where tight junctions prevent entering of larger 
molecules. The second effect, the enhanced retention of NPs, is caused by insufficient lymphatic drainage in 
the tumor tissue. This prevents clearance of the NPs and thereby an increased accumulation (Figure 2).30,32 The 
increased vascular permeability caused by large fenestrations is also observed in different inflammatory 
diseases.33 Here, NPs can penetrate into cells through larger pore sizes. Passive targeting also has its 
limitations. These limitations are related to and depend on the level of vascularization. Because of the easy 
diffusion of small molecules, they may diffuse away from the target site after delivery. It can thus be necessary 
to administrate higher doses to reach the minimum effective dose, which can be toxic to healthy cells.34 Since 
CAT prevents oxidative damage and is non-toxic to healthy cells, this problem does not have to be considered 
for this protein.

1.2.1.2 Active Targeting 
Active targeting is achieved by conjugation of ligands to NPs to promote site-specific recognition and binding. 
Typical ligands include, but are not limited to, carbohydrates, antibodies, growth factors and peptides.30,32 The 
ligands are chosen based on the properties of the target. Examples of properties that can be targeted are 
overexpressed receptors, enzymes or antigens.32 For instance cancer cells may be targeted by conjugation of 
folate by capitalizing on the higher metabolic demands for rapidly and uncontrolled dividing of cancer cells.35 
Some common mechanisms of active targeting are drug release close to target cells, sustained release, and 
internalization by endocytosis.30 A disadvantage of attaching a targeting ligand to the surface is its potential 
impact on NP properties such as size, polydispersity, and safety profile. Furthermore, targeting ligands such as 
antibodies are often very expensive.32  
1.3 Types of Nanoparticles  
In the following paragraphs, some of the most popular NP formulations are described. The aim is to provide an 
overview of the field of NP formulations and ease the understanding of the increased interest in protein NPs. 
1.3.1 Liposomes 
Liposomes are already on the market and more are in clinical trials.18,32 Liposomes are spherical nanocarriers 
composed of naturally or synthetic lipids, however, the most common building block is phospholipids.30 
Phospholipids are amphiphilic molecules containing a hydrophilic head with two hydrophobic chains. When 
exposed to an aqueous environment, the hydrophobic chains will pack together via Van der Waals interactions, 
while the hydrophilic phosphates will face the inner and outer aqueous surface to minimize the surface 
tension. This forms a NP structure with a hydrophilic aqueous core surrounded by a hydrophobic 
membrane.30,32 This makes liposomes able to transport both hydrophobic drugs in the lipid membrane and 
hydrophilic drugs in the aqueous core. Furthermore, the surface can be altered to achieve longer circulation 
time, site-specific delivery via active targeting, increased cellular internalization and decreased recognition by 
==PAGE:19==
6 
 
the immune system. The longer circulation time and decreased recognition by the immune system can be 
achieved by surface-coating with poly(ethylene glycol) (PEG).18,32 
1.3.2 Polymer Nanoparticles 
Polymeric nanoparticles (PNPs) are widely used as NPs for transportation of drugs.18 PNPs can be designed 
from natural polymers such as gelatin or collagen and synthetic polymers such as poly(lactic-co-glycolic) (PL-
GA) and poly(lactide) (PLA).28 Studies indicate that purification of natural polymers is more complicated than 
synthetic polymers. Therefore, synthetic polymers are commonly used.36,37 The formulation of PNPs enables 
both hydrophilic and hydrophobic drugs to be loaded, and it shields the drug from the surroundings. This 
enhances the circulation time and enables the EPR effect. The circulation time can be further increased by 
coating the surface with PEG. Additionally, active targeting can be achieved by binding of ligands to the 
surface.38 
1.3.3 Micelles  
Micelles are water-soluble spherical particles composed of amphiphilic molecules like block-copolymers or 
lipids. In contrast to liposomes, micelles are composed of hydrophobic parts surrounded by a hydrophilic shell, 
which makes it an effective carrier for water-insoluble drugs.30,31 Like liposomes and PNPs, micelles can be 
loaded with both hydrophilic and hydrophobic drugs. Micelles are formed via self-assembly of amphiphilic 
molecules in aqueous solutions when the critical micelle concentration (CMC) is exceeded. In the core of the 
micelle, hydrophobic drugs can be loaded either by physical encapsulation via hydrophobic interaction or 
chemical by covalent binding of the drug before the self-assembly into micelles.39 The hydrophilic part of the 
amphiphilic block-copolymers often consists of PEG, which increases the circulation time and prevent 
recognition by the immune system. Further, micelles have been conjugated to ligands for effective drug 
delivery.40 
1.4 Protein Nanoparticles 
Recently, there has been an increased focus on protein-based NP systems due to their biocompatibility and 
biodegradability. Although NP systems with similar characteristics have been designed using liposomes, 
polymers and micelles, the use of synthetic building blocks increases the risk for toxicity and immunogenic

previously (Figure 3). If this trend continues, it is expected that more proteins for NP formulation will be 
approved in the future.42 In addition to their main advantages of being biocompatibility and biodegradability, 
natural proteins are easy and rather cheap to produce, with potential for chemical modification and high 
loading capacity.41,43 A variety of proteins have been used in the preparation of protein NPs and for different 
purposes. Some proteins are therapeutic, while others are used as nanocarriers for improved delivery and 
cellular uptake.41 Protein NPs are often prepared from proteins such as gelatin, lysozymes, albumin, collagen,

1.4.1 Catalase Nanoparticles 
Huang et al., Yoshimoto et al., and Singhal et al. have prepared CAT NPs using desolvation and shear stress, 
encapsulating in liposomes, and encapsulating by polymers, respectively.8,45,46 Yoshimoto et al. showed that

sub-units compared to free CAT. This was accomplished by preventing the dissociation of the CAT monomer 
into the sub-units which promotes loss of activity.45 Singhal et al. reported successful encapsulation of the CAT 
monomer into poly(lactic co-glycoic acid) where 99 % of its enzymatic activity was retained.46 There was a 
sustained release of CAT from the NPs for a month with no toxicity to neurons. Additionally, they showed 
higher recovery of pre-exposed neurons to oxidative stress by treatment of CAT NPs compared to free CAT. 
Huang et al. prepared NPs consisting exclusively of CAT monomers, which has the advantage that only the 
protein of interest is used.8 By optimizing the desolvation conditions, they obtained CAT NPs with a size of 163 
nm and a polydispersity index (PDI) at 0.189. The stability of CAT was improved in the NP formulation 
compared to the free protein. After exposure to alpha-chymotrypsin for 4 h, CAT NPs maintained 80 % of the 
initial activity, where free CAT only maintained 45 %. This was likely caused by a limited accessibility for alpha-
chymotrypsin because of a dense packaging of CAT in the NPs.8 
1.4.2 Albumin Nanoparticles 
Albumin has been used as a nanocarrier for several therapeutics such as peptides, proteins, antibodies and 
vaccines and drugs of which Abraxane is an example of.16 Abraxane is a NP of HSA to which Paclitaxel (PTX) has 
been bound. Abraxane was approved by the food and drug administration (FDA) in 2005 for treatment of 
breast cancer when chemotherapy was insufficient. HSA functions as a carrier for PTX, which has a low water 
solubility.47 Abraxane is a spherical NP with a size of 130 nm where the hydrophobic PTX are located in the core 
of the NP.48 PTX is a chemotherapeutic that exerts its function by destabilization of microtubules leading to cell

Bevacizumab (BVZ) is a monoclonal antibody administrated in the clinic by itself. In a paper by Redin et al. they

1.4.3 Lysozyme Nanoparticles 
Lysozyme (LYS) is an enzyme with anti-bacterial, anti-inflammatory and anti-proliferative properties. It is 
present in human secretion and plays an important role in the innate immune system.53 Cui et al. prepared LYS 
NPs loaded with curcumin (CUR) using desolvation to obtain NPs with a size of 128 nm.54 CUR has anti-oxidant 
and anti-inflammatory properties. LYS functioned as a protein nanocarrier for CUR and improved its poor water 
solubility and stability. Compared to free CUR, the water solubility of the CUR LYS NPs was 660-fold higher and

LYS NPs have furthermore been used for delivery of LYS itself as it has shown to inhibit tumors.55 Using

Further, the LYS NPs had specific cytotoxicity against cancer cells. To increase the specificity toward MCF-7 
cells, the NPs were conjugated with folic acid. This led to an increased cell death of MCF-7 cells.55

2 Methods  
 
2.1 Nanoparticle Synthesis 
 
2.1.1 Desolvation  
Desolvation is one of the most common techniques utilized for synthesis of protein NPs due to its inherent 
simplicity and good reproducibility.24,56 Desolvation is based on proteins having different solubility in different 
solvents. In aqueous solution, proteins position themselves with their hydrophilic part facing the aqueous 
solution, and the hydrophobic parts facing inwards to form a hydrophobic core. Addition of an anti-solvent will 
lead to precipitation of the proteins as less water molecules will be available for the protein.56 Experimental 
factors including pH, solvent polarity, cross-linking, amount of anti-solvent, stirring rate, buffer and ionic 
strength may influence the physiochemical properties of the resulting NPs.24,57 A disadvantage of the 
desolvation method is the requirement of organic solvents. Organic solvents can damage the protein structure, 
which may lead to diminished activity and loss of function.8 Additionally, some organic solvents are toxic to 
human and animals, and must therefore be completely removed before preclinical and clinical studies.8  
2.1.2 Crosslinking 
Crosslinkers are polyfunctional molecules containing at least two reactive heads that facilitate covalent bonds 
between polymeric chains.58 Crosslinkers have been extensively used in material sciences as they allow a 
material’s physiochemical properties to be tailored to a specific indication. This includes drug delivery systems 
where the incorporation of a crosslinker may improve the formulation or therapeutic effect of a 
pharmaceutical.55 Examples of such improvements are increased stability, solubility, permeability or selective 
release mechanisms via cleavable linker technologies. Cleavable linkers can be designed to be responsive to 
certain environmental determinants like pH values, redox, or enzymatic activity.59  
In this thesis, different crosslinking strategies will be explored with the aim of stabilizing the protein or NP 
structure prior to or after desolvation. Increasing the rigidity of the protein via crosslinking may lead to a more 
controlled NP synthesis by limited the freedom of the protein. Furthermore, crosslinkers can stabilize NP 
structures, prevent clustering and protect against enzymatic degradation.24,60 
Today, there exists a plethora of commercially available crosslinkers with different reactivities and chemical 
properties. Generally, two types of crosslinkers exist; homobifunctional and heterobifunctional crosslinkers. 
Homobifunctional crosslinkers contain identical reactive groups, whereas heterobifunctional crosslinkers 
contain reactive groups with different chemistries.59 PEGylated crosslinkers can be applied to increase aqueous 
solubility, protraction and to lessen clustering.61  
GTA has been a popular crosslinker for many years due to its high reactivity with primary amines. However, 
more sophisticated crosslinkers have matured and are gaining increasing recognition.62 Lysine is an attractive

and the high biological stability of amide bonds which it can form.63 Another common target for conjugation is

controlled crosslinking reactions.63,64 One disadvantage of crosslinkers is that they are prone to hydrolysis,

invasive and commonly used in the development of therapeutic nanoformulations.68 DLS estimates particle size

change in scattering intensity over time, the diffusion coefficient (D) can be determined. Based on the Stokes-
Einstein equation, the instrument estimates the particle size (Equation 1).68,69 The absolute temperature and 
the solvent viscosity must be known as it will affect the particle diffusion rate.68,70

2.2.2 Nanoparticle Tracking Analysis 
Nanoparticle tracking analysis (NTA) is another method used to measure the size of particles. It measures single 
particles in a solution by tracking their movement and scattering of light. Tracking of single particles is an

used to determine if two particles at two different frames are the same particle. From the varying diffusion 
movements of small and large particles the particle size is estimated based on the derived Stoke-Einstein

2.2.3 Sodium-Dodecyl-Sulfate-Polyacrylamide Gel Electrophoresis  
Sodium-Dodecyl-Sulfate-polyacrylamide gel electrophoresis (SDS-PAGE) is a method used to estimate sizes of 
proteins based on MW. In addition, it may be applied to determine the purity of a protein solution. The 
method is based on applying an electrical field over a gel loaded with charged proteins. The proteins will then 
migrate against the opposite charge.74 A ladder with proteins of known MWs is added as a reference. Protein 
samples are dissolved in a buffer containing SDS and/or 2-mercaptoethanol. The role of 2-mercaptoethanol is 
to reduce disulfide bonds, whereas SDS is a detergent that disrupts non-covalent bonds and thus destabilizes 
the secondary and tertiary structure causing the protein to denature. By acting as an anionic surfactant, it 
imparts an overall negative charge to the protein, which is utilized to migrate through the gel towards the 
cathode. The Laemmli buffer contains both agents and ensures protein separation based on size where larger 
molecules migrate slower compared to smaller ones.75 Furthermore, the Laemmli buffer contains bromophenol 
blue and glycerol. Bromophenol blue works as a dye for monitoring the electrophoretic protein migration and 
eases sample loading into the wells of the gel. Glycerol increases the sample density and thereby eases protein 
sample loading as well.76 Proteins are visualized by commassie staining and will be performed according to a 
standard microwave protocol.74,77 The optimum protein amount per well depends on the type and thickness of 
the gel where 0.5-4 µg protein per well is a common used amount.75 
2.2.4 Circular Dichroism 
Circular dichroism (CD) is used to study secondary protein structures and is a well-establish qualitative 
technique within chemistry and biology.78 The secondary structure is the local folding within a protein caused 
by molecular interactions of the protein backbone and the amino acid side chains. Secondary protein 
structures include alpha-helix, beta sheet and random coils. The method measures the relative absorption of 
right circular polarized light (R-CPL) and left circular polarized light (L-CPL) by chromophores of the embedded 
proteins structure. The circularly polarised light is created by passing plane polarised light through a modulator 
that enable the light beam to be sinusoidal alternated between R-CPL and L-CPL. The light will interact with the 
inserted sample.79,80 The signal can be either positive or negative. A positive signal will appears when L‐CPL is 
absorbed to a greater extent than R‐ CPL and a negative signal will appears when L-CPL is absorbed to a minor 
extend than R-CPL.79,80 Most proteins contain chromophores which interact differentially with left and right 
circularly polarized light.80 Amide bonds absorb in the far UV-region (180-240 nm) where the near UV-region 
(400-260 nm) gives information of the aromatic amino acid side chains.80,81 The far UV-region gives information 
about alpha helix, beta sheet and random coils, which have their own characteristic shape. Alfa-helixes gives

2.2.5 Bicinchoninic Acid Assay 
The Bicinchoninic acid (BCA) assay is used to determine the protein concentration. It is a copper-based method 
where a Cu2 +–protein complex is formed under basic conditions followed by a reduction of Cu2 + to Cu+. This 
will cause the color of the sample solution to change from green to purple, which can be measured with a 
microplate reader. The amount of reduced Cu+ is proportional to the amount of protein in the sample.82

information about the activity of protein used to break down the reactant. The activity is based on Lambert-
Beer’s law (Equation 4). Lambert-Beer’s law dictates that the absorbance of a solution is directly proportional

2.2.7 Transmission Electron Microscopy 
Transmission electron microscopy (TEM) is a valuable tool for structural characterization of materials at 
nanoscale. It can be used to analyze shape, size, quality and density of NPs. TEM can image samples with high 
spatial resolution enabling the characterization of small objects. The high resolution is due to the short 
wavelength of the electron.85 The method is based on the interaction of electrons with the sample when a 
high-energy beam of electrons is transmitted through a thin sample. The electrons will interact with the atoms 
to form an image, which is then magnified onto an imaging device. The dark areas indicate decreased 
transmission of electrons through the sample, whereas light areas mean increased transmission of electron 
through the sample.86 
2.2.8 Dialysis  
Dialysis is used for purification of protein samples or solvent change. The method is based on diffusion of 
molecules. The solution is filled in a semi-permeable membrane with a molecular weight cut-off suitable for 
the protein. The membrane is placed in a proper aqueous solution often with a bulk volume. The pore size of 
the membrane determines which molecules are allowed to pass. When equilibrium is reached, the buffer must 
be exchanged to re-establish a membrane potential for further purification.87 
2.2.9 Desalting and Freeze-drying 
Desalting and freeze-drying are methods to purify and stabilize proteins. Desalting is done by 
ultracentrifugation where the sample is filtered through a cellulose membrane with a specific pore size. The 
method can be used for purification, solvent exchange, and to increase the sample concentration. Freeze-
drying, also termed lyophilization, removes water from a sample, which can increase its stability. Water 
establishes an aqueous environment where molecular movement and interactions can lead to clustering and 
precipitation of proteins. Therefore, storage under frozen conditions as the powdered protein improves the 
protein shelf-life.88 
2.2.10 Solvent Precipitation 
Solvent precipitation utilizes the different solubility of proteins in different solvents. The method is similar to 
the desolvation method described in paragraph 2.1.1. Briefly, an anti-solvent is added to the protein solution, 
which will influences protein folding resulting in clustering and precipitation of the protein.89 The solution is 
then centrifuged. The resulting pellet contains the protein of interest and can be re-suspended into a desired 
solvent. Studies have shown that the volume of acetone can be up to three or four times the protein volume.90 
2.2.11 Salt Precipitation 
Salt precipitation is based on adding a high concentration of salt to a protein solution. The general principle is 
the same as in paragraph 2.1.1 where less water molecules are available for interaction with the protein 
==PAGE:29==
16 
 
triggering precipitation. Centrifugation separates the solution into a pellet and supernatant. The pellet 
containing the protein is re-suspended in a desired solvent.89 
2.2.12 Size-Exclusion Chromatography 
Size-exclusion chromatography (SEC) is a method used to separate larger molecules such as proteins based on 
their size and MW.91 A sample solution is loaded onto a column packed with beads with a specific pore size 
allowing only molecules of a certain size to enter. This results in trapping of smaller molecules, which will elute 
at later time points, while larger molecules that cannot enter the pores will elute faster.92 A detector is placed 
after the column where it measures the absorbance of the sample. A wavelength of 280 nm is applied when 
analyzing protein samples because of the high absorbance of aromatic rings found in phenylalanine (Phe), 
tyrosine (Tyr), and tryptophan (Trp) at this wavelength.93  
2.2.13 MTS Assay 
Cytotoxicity is tested using the (3-(4,5-dimethylthiazol-2-yl)-5-(3-carboxymethoxyphenyl)-2-(4-sulfophenyl)-2H-
tetrazolium) (MTS) assay. The method is based on measuring the absorbance of a colored product formed by 
living cells.94 The color change is catalyzed by NADPH-dependent dehydrogenase enzymes, which reduce the 
tetrazolium reagent to form a formazan product. The MTS assay has the advantage that the formazan product 
is soluble in the cell culture medium.94 The formazan product has a maximum absorbance at 490 nm, and the 
amount of formazan product is directly proportional to the amount of living cells.94–96 The MCF-7 cell line 
represents a breast cancer cell line which is commonly used as it is easily to culture.97

3.2 Nanoparticle Synthesis 
 
3.2.1 Preparation of Protein Nanoparticles 
Protein NPs were prepared by dropwise addition of an organic solvent (ethanol, acetone, isopropanol, 
acetonitrile or methanol) to a solution of the protein with a concentration of 0.2-100 mM in an aqueous 
solvent (milli-Q water (MQ) water, 5, 10 or 20 mM sodium phosphate buffer (PB) pH 7.5, 10 mM borate buffer 
(BB) pH 8, 8.5, 9 or 9.5, or 10 or 50 mM carbonate buffer (CB) pH 8.5). The anti-solvent was added using a 
pump system (NE-300 Just Infusion™ Syringe Pump, NEW ERA Pump SystemS Inc.) with a rate of 1 mL/minute 
(min) into a small round bottomed flask under magnetic stirring at 500 rpm. The addition was continued until 
==PAGE:31==
18 
 
the solution became turbid or to a selected time point between 0.75-3 min. After the desolvation process, the 
reaction mixture was immediately centrifuged at 5000-15000 xG for 5-15 min at 4°C (SL 16R centrifuge, Thermo 
Fisher Scientific). The supernatant was removed and the pellet was re-suspended in a re-suspension media 
(MQ, PB pH 7.5, BB pH 8, 8.5, 9 or 9.5 or CB pH 8.5). 
3.2.2 Crosslinking 
The protein was crosslinked using a 1-400 molar excess of BS3, DTSSP, Sulfo-LC-SPDP or Sulfo-EGS in an 
aqueous solvent (MQ water, PB 7.5, BB pH 8.5 or BB pH 9.5). The samples were incubated on a thermoshaker 
at 500 rpm for 1-2 h at 2 or 20°C (MS-100 Thermoshaker Incubator, Labgene Scientific). Unreacted crosslinker 
was in some cases removed via centrifugation rounds at 4000 xG for 25 min at 4°C. Purified crosslinked protein 
was re-suspended in aqueous solutions (MQ water, PB pH 7.5 or BB pH 8.5). 
3.3 Nanoparticle Characterization 
 
3.3.1 Determination of Particles Size and Polydispersity Index 
NPs were diluted in an aqueous solvent (MQ water, PB 7.5, BB pH 8.5, BB pH 9.5 or CB pH 8.5). The diluted 
sample was transferred to a disposable cuvette (Anton Paar) and particle size and PDI were measured using 
DLS (Malvern Zetasizer Nano ZS). A back scattering angle of 173° was applied. The system was allowed to 
calibrate and optimizes its measurements settings before the sample was measured in triplicates 
corresponding to three sub-runs. 
3.3.2 Determination of Particles Size and Concentration 
NPs were diluted in an aqueous solvent (MQ water or PB 7.5). The sample was injected into the chamber of the 
system, and the particle size was measured using NTA (ZetaView® BASIC NTA - Nanoparticle Tracking Video 
Microscope PMX-220). The system was initialized and alignment was performed on 100 nm PS beads according 
to the manufactures protocol. Samples were diluted in MQ water or PB pH 7.5 until the number of detected 
particles reached 50-200/frame. The analysis was carried out measuring three cycles in 11 positions with a 
sensitivity of 65, shutter of 100 and framerate of 30. The temperature was measured automatically. 
3.3.3 Characterization of Proteins by SDS-PAGE Gel 
Protein solutions were prepared by diluting the protein samples to a final concentration of 0.5-2 µg/well. 
Protein solutions were mixed with Laemmli buffer (2X) (Sigma-Aldrich) in a volume ratio 1:1 and heated at 80°C 
for 10 min. MOPS running buffer (20X) (Sigma-Aldrich) was diluted 20-fold and added to the cassette (XCell 
SureLock Mini-Cell Electrophoresis System, Thermo Fisher Scientific) with a gel (NuPAGETM 4 to 12%, Bis-Tris, 
1.5 mm, Mini Protein Gel, 10-well, Thermo Fisher Scientific). 20-25 µL protein solution or 5-15 µL ladder was 
loaded into each well and the electrophoresis was run at 150 V for 1-1.5 h.

The gel was covered with deionized water and heated in a microwave until almost boiling. This was repeated 
three times. Coomassie stain (SimplyBlueTM Safe Stain, Thermo Fisher Scientific) was added to completely 
cover the gel. The gel was heated in the microwave until almost boiling. Staining was done by gently shaking 
the commasie-covered gel at 50-80 rpm on a plate-shaker for 5-15 min. The stain was removed from the gel 
and it was rinsed in deionized water. The gel was left in deionized water under gently shacking to destain

3.3.4 Characterization of Proteins by Circular Dichroism 
NPs were diluted in a desired solvent for CD (MQ water, PB pH 7.5 or BB pH 8.5 or pH 9.5). The optimal

protein solution. The ellipticity (θ) was plotted as a function of wavelength. 
3.3.5 Determination of Protein Concentration by BCA 
Protein concentrations were determined by the BCA protein quantification assay (Thermo Fisher Scientific) 
according to the manufacturer’s instructions. Briefly, 25 µL of each sample was added to wells in a 96-well 
plate (Thermo Fisher Scientific) in triplicates. The working reagent was prepared by mixing a ratio 50:1 between 
reagent A and B. 200 µL of the working reagent was added to each well and the plate was incubated for 30 min 
at 37°C. After the incubation, the plate was cooled to room temperature (RT) and the absorbance was 
measured at 562 nm using a plate-reader (Spark multimode microplate reader, Tecan). Data was analyzed by 
subtracting the background from each sample and absorbance was transformed into concentration based on 
an albumin standard curve (0-2000 µg/mL). 
3.3.6 Determination of Catalase Activity by the H2O2 decomposition test 
The concentrations of CAT, crosslinked CAT or crosslinked CAT NPs were determined by BCA and varied from

solution was added to the cuvette followed by addition of 15-100 µL of the CAT solutions. The optimal amount 
of CAT solution depended on the concentration of the CAT solution as higher concentrations will lead to 
increased bubble formation. Too many bubbles will scatter light and the experiment needs to be terminated. 
The absorbance was measured at 240 nm every 10 s for 2 min. All experiments were conducted at RT and

the decrease in absorbance of H2O2 at 240 nm over time. The reaction rates were used to estimate the total

3.3.8 Purification and Solvent Change by Dialysis 
The semi-permeable membrane (cut-off 10-30 kDa) (Thermo Fisher Scientific) was pre-wetted by placing it in a

solution was added to the cassette chamber and the cassette chamber was returned to the desire solvent. The 
container was covered and placed under gentle stirring at 4°C for 1-4 days. After end dialysis, the CAT solution 
was sucked from the cassette chamber. The purity was determined by measuring the absorbance at 280 nm 
corresponding to the protein and at 400 nm corresponding to the heme group. An OD400/280 ratio between 
0.8 to approximately 1 indicate pure protein.100–102 
3.3.9 Purification and Solvent Change by Desalting and Freeze-drying 
Proteins were dissolved in MQ water or buffer. They were added to pre-wetted centrifugal filter units with 
suitable cut-off (10 kDa, 50 kDa or 100 kDa). Samples were centrifuged at 4000 xG for 30 min at 4°C (SL 16R 
centrifuge, Thermo Fisher Scientific) and re-suspended in MQ water or buffer. This was repeated five times. For 
freeze-drying the protein was re-suspended in approximately 3 mL of MQ water and flash-frozen using liquid 
nitrogen. The frozen sample was placed in the freeze dryer overnight (Scanvac coolsafe).  
3.3.10 Solvent Precipitation  
Purification by solvent precipitation was executed by adding a volume of cooled acetone or ethanol to an 
aqueous solution of the protein (MQ water or PB 7.5) making the final volume of organic solvent in the sample 
10-80 %. The mixture was placed at 4°C overnight. The mixture was centrifuged at 12000 xG for 15 min at 4°C 
(SL 16R centrifuge, Thermo Fisher Scientific) and re-suspended in MQ water or PB pH 7.5. The purity was 
determined using UV spectroscopy as described in paragraph 3.3.8. 
3.3.11 Salt Precipitation 
A CAT solution with a concentration of 1 or 3 mg/mL in PB pH 7.5 was precipitated by different salts 
(ammonium sulfate, ammonium phosphate, sodium sulfate or sodium carbonate). The mixture was centrifuged

MCF-7 cells were added to a 96-well microtiter plate (Nunclon Delta-treated Flat-Bottom Microplate, Thermo

the cells to give final concentration of 20 nM – 0.02 M. The plate was incubated at 37°C with 5 % CO2 for 24 h. 
The media were removed, and the cells were washed with PBS. 120 µL/well of a pre-mixed solution of media 
and MTS reagent in a 5:1 volume ratio was added, and the plate was incubated for 0.5-4 h at 37°C with 5 % 
CO2. The absorbance at 490 nm was measured on a plate reader (Spark multimode microplate reader, Tecan). 
Both controls and samples were run in triplicates.

4 Aim of Thesis 
The overall aim of the thesis is to formulate protein NPs to get an understanding of how protein properties 
affect NP synthesis. In the thesis, proteins with potential as nanocarriers and in therapeutic uses are included. 
Based on protein NP characteristics reported in literature, a size below 300 nm and a polydispersity index 
below 0.3 are perceived as an acceptable threshold. Thus, the aim was to synthesize protein NPs fulfilling these 
requirements. In extension, the stability and biological activity of the protein should be maintained, and the 
NPs should show no toxicity to cells in vitro. The thesis focuses on two proteins chosen for reasons explained 
below.  
- 
Part I of the results and discussion covers the purification of CAT to obtain the monomer.  
- 
Part II of the results and discussion covers crosslinking of CAT and CAT NP formulation. CAT is an 
interesting protein from a therapeutic perspective and the literature on CAT NPs is limited. 
- 
Part III of the results and discussion covers BSA NPs. Although BSA is a well-studied protein commonly 
used in NP formulations, it is a suitable protein for learning laboratory methods and for demonstration 
of NP synthesis reproducibility.

5 Results and Discussion 
 
5.1 Work prior to this Master’s Thesis  
A research project entitled “Methodologies to synthesize protein nanoclusters” was conducted prior to this MSc 
project. The focus was on CAT because of its therapeutic potential and the lack of studies on CAT NPs.5 The aim 
of the project was to synthesize NPs with a size below 300 nm and a PDI below 0.3. In the project, desolvation 
was used as NP synthesis method, and DLS was used for size and PDI estimation. A shift from clear to turbid of 
the protein solution was used as an indicator of completed desolvation. The results showed similar sizes 
around 150 nm of CAT monomer prior to desolvation and of CAT NPs after desolvation. This did not correlate 
with the literature reporting the CAT monomer to be 10 nm.103  These observations raised the question if and 
why CAT self-assembled. The observed NPs could be a result of self-assembly independent of the desolvation 
process. It is likely that the protein batch contained impurities causing CAT to cluster. From the project, it could 
not be concluded whether CAT NPs could be synthesized using desolvation.  In further work, the protein batch 
must be purified, and the monomer of CAT must be obtained prior to desolvation to be sure of what the NP 
consist of. Therefore, part I will include the investigation of obtaining CAT on the monomer form which was the 
first work conducted during the master’s thesis. 
5.2 Part I: Purification of Catalase (CAT) 
 
5.2.1 The Dissociation of CAT in Different Buffers 
To obtain the monomer, CAT was dissolved in different buffers with varying pH values and concentrations. All 
particles had a size above 80 nm independently of protein concentration, type of buffer, desalination and 
sterile filtration (App A. Table 16). Desalination and sterile filtration reduced the size of all samples but the 
peaks still did not correspond to the monomer (App A. Table 16). To further investigate if CAT slowly 
dissociates to the monomeric form, a solution of CAT was incubated at 4°C overnight. DLS measurements 
indicated clustering with sizes around 90 nm (App A. Table 17). Simmchen et al. dissolved CAT in 10 mM 
sodium carbonate buffer pH 8.5 (CB pH 8.5) and obtained the monomer. 104 The authors suspected presence of 
impurities in commercially available batches and desalinated the CAT solution using a centrifugal filter unit with 
a cut-off of 50 kDa. Repeating the experiment using 10 mM or 50 mM CB pH 8.5 resulted in particles larger 
than 80 nm (App A. Table 18). The sizes were not in agreement with those of Simmchen et al. reporting a size 
of the monomer to be 7.2 nm.104 The pH of all samples was examined after dissolution of CAT to ensure that 
CAT did not change the pH significantly, for example by moving the pH towards the pI at which CAT is expected 
to have the greatest tendency to cluster. The buffer pH was preserved in all samples after dissolution of CAT. 
The monomer of CAT was not observed in any of the samples according to the DLS data as other studies report 
the monomer of CAT to be approximately 10 nm based on DLS data.104–106

buffer (BB) pH 9.5. This was reflected by a larger size of CAT batch 2 compared to CAT batch 1 (Table 1). This

manufacturing and purification by the supplier.

A CAT solution was prepared to investigate if precipitation of CAT by ammonium sulfate increased the purity 
thus obtaining the monomer. The size of CAT increased after one and two rounds of ammonium sulfate 
precipitations (Table 3). It may be explained by impurities or residual ammonium sulfate triggering cluster 
formation. Others have successfully used ammonium sulfate for purification of CAT. Sumner et al. added 
ammonium sulfate to a CAT solution until saturation and observed the formation of pure crystalline CAT.112

Chen et al. also used ammonium sulfate for precipitation to improve the purity of CAT. 100 The authors of both 
studies do not report the repetitive number of ammonium sulfate precipitation cycles and it possible that one 
or two rounds is not sufficient. The supplier does not provide information of the purity of CAT and it is possible 
that the batch contains impurities and/or additives. While additives may be necessary in the manufacturing 
processes or to improve the shelf-life of the protein, they may promote clustering of the protein. Salt 
precipitation using other salts (ammonium phosphate, sodium sulfate and sodium carbonate) did not prevent 
clustering and no monomer was obtained (App A. Table 20). In conclusion, salt precipitation alone was not 
successful for purification of CAT to obtain the monomer.

5.2.4 Purification of CAT using Selected Purification Steps 
As salt precipitation was not sufficient for obtaining the monomer other methods were investigated. Ultra-
sonication and dialysis reduced particle size to around 80 and 100 nm, respectively, whereas organic solvent 
precipitation did not change the size (App A. Table 21, Table 22, Table 23). No peaks of the CAT monomer were 
present when the DLS size distribution was presented based on intensity, volume or number. Because the 
intensity based size distribution favours larger particles, the number distribution was used for analysis of all 
future CAT solutions. A combination of dialysis, ammonium sulphate precipitation and acetone precipitation 
resulted in smaller peaks likely representing the monomer of CAT, although larger clusters were present (App 
A. Table 24). The most promising combination was three ammonium sulphate precipitation cycles followed by 
one acetone precipitation and incubation at 4°C for four days. Re-suspension of the protein pellet after each 
salt or solvent precipitation showed very large clusters that could not easily be re-dissolved. Therefore, the 
supernatant was analysed by DLS (Table 4). Although a larger peak was still present in the DLS data, a peak with 
a size of around 10 nm was also present (Figure 10). Evaluation of data based on the number distribution 
confirmed that only few larger clusters were still present and that CAT overall seemed pure (Figure 10). This 
result was observed using PB pH 7.5 as solvent (Figure 10) as well as MQ water (data not shown). However, NP 
synthesis using either of the two solvents resulted in highly polydisperse solutions of NPs with sizes above 1 µm 
(Table 4). It was unknown whether this result was caused by a general tendency of the protein to cluster, the 
presence of a few pre-formed clusters or that the solution still contained impurities.

5.3 Part II: Crosslinking and Nanoparticle Formation of Catalase (CAT) 
 
5.3.1 Influence of Anti-solvent on Nanoparticles Size and PDI 
The effect on NP size and PDI was investigated by five anti-solvents. Turbidity was used as indicator for 
completed desolvation. The size and PDI values of the NPs varied depending on the anti-solvent (Table 6). It 
was difficult to visualize the turbid shift as an indicator of completed desolvation. This might be the reason for 
use of different anti-solvent volumes as well as the resulting different NP sizes and PDI values. According to the 
FDA, the use of class 2 solvents should be limited where class 3 solvents do not constitute any human health 
hazard at levels accepted in pharmaceuticals. Ethanol, acetone and isopropanol are class 3 solvents, while 
acetonitrile and methanol are class 2 solvents.113 Methanol and acetonitrile were excluded due to the 
guidelines from the FDA and the large NP sizes. Further, isopropanol was not used due to high size and PDI 
value. To determine if acetone or ethanol could be used as anti-solvent, the activity of the CAT NP solution 
after desolvation with either acetone or ethanol was investigated. As CAT catalyzes the decomposition of H2O2 
to H2O and O2, bubbles are expected to appear if CAT still contains some activity. Bubbles reflecting some 
activity of CAT were only observed when desolvation was conducted with acetone (App B. Figure 30). Organic 
solvents may denature proteins and cause loss of activity.103 This may explain the completely loss of activity of 
CAT NPs synthesized with ethanol as anti-solvent. Huang et al. used acetonitrile as anti-solvent to obtain NPs 
by desolvation.8 The NPs were in a range of 163.3-175.5 nm dependent of the acetonitrile concentration. The 
data obtained in this study using acetonitrile as anti-solvent resulted in a size of 246 nm and did not 
corresponds to those of Huang et al. (Table 6).8 This study and Huang et al. used different CAT concentrations 
and amount of anti-solvent which could be the explanation for varying NP sizes. Based on the findings 
regarding size, PDI and activity of CAT NPs, acetone was used as anti-solvent for future experiments.

5.3.2 Impact of Solvent on Particle Size and PDI 
The effect on size and PDI of changing the solvent from 10 mM PB pH 7.5 to MQ water or 10 mM BB pH 9 was 
tested. The size of CAT monomer was preserved upon buffer change from PB pH 7.5 to BB pH 9 (App B. Table 
27). However, solvent change to MQ water made the protein to cluster into particles with diameter larger than 
1000 nm (App B. Table 27). After desolvation, a NP size of 592 nm was observed when MQ water was used as 
solvent. Surprisingly, the size of the NPs was reduced compared to CAT in MQ water. The reason for this 
observation might be stirring under the NP formation or re-suspension in PB pH 7.5 thus changing the solvent 
after desolvation. The pH of MQ water will likely be closer to the pI of the protein compared to the buffer, 
which might explain the high tendency of clustering. Although an increase in pH was expected to increase the 
solubility of CAT, NPs with a size of 255 nm were obtained using BB pH 9.5 compared to a size of 120 nm using 
PB pH 7.5 (App B. Table 27).114 As the NP size was reduced and it furthermore was the solvent used in SEC, 10 
mM PB pH 7.5 was used as solvent for additional experiments.  
5.3.3 Impact of Buffer Concentration on Particle Size and PDI 
Using PB pH 7.5 at 5 or 20 mM, the impact of buffer concentration was studied on size and PDI of CAT 
monomer and CAT NPs (App B. Table 28). The size of CAT monomer was independent of buffer concentration, 
whereas NP size and PDI was larger when NP synthesis was executed at higher ionic strength. Oncsik et al. 
stated that particle aggregation normally follows the Hofmeister series, which is a ordering of ions based on 
their ability to salt out or salt in proteins.115 Some ions will promote hydrophobic interactions leading to 
aggregation (salting out), whereas other ions will prevent hydrophobic interactions leading to increased 
solubility (salting in).116 According to the Hofmeister series, phosphate will promote salting out. This may 
explain the increase in NP size with increasing buffer concentration. However, when 10 mM PB pH 7.5 was 
used for NP synthesis, the NPs were slightly smaller compared to both 5 mM PB and 20 mM PB at pH 7.5 (App 
B. Table 28). Therefore, the differences may be caused by general data variation or batch-to-batch variation of 
purified CAT. A complete understanding of the impact of buffer concentration on NP size and PDI requires 
==PAGE:45==
32 
 
further experiments. Because a change in buffer concentration did not improve NP size, and because CAT 
purification by SEC was executed using 10 mM PB pH 7.5, this specific buffer was used for further experiments.  
5.3.4 Influence of Crosslinking before Desolvation on Particles Size and PDI 
Four different crosslinkers were used to investigate the influence of crosslinking of CAT before desolvation on 
the NPs size and PDI value. Crosslinking before desolvation might limit the freedom of CAT thus reducing the 
folding opportunities and control the clustering. Prior to CAT crosslinking, a PyMOL analysis was run to 
estimate the number of lysine and cysteine residues on the surface of the protein. By default, the Protein 
Surface Analyzer, calculates the surface accessibility ratio as a percentage to the entire protein surface. 
Residues which are categorized as buried will get a score below 20 % where residues exposed on the surface 
should exceed 50 %.117 The analysis revealed 27 lysine residues of which 19 were present on the surface and 4 
cysteine residues where only one was presented on the surface (Figure 13, App B. Figure 31). Due to the high 
number of surface exposed lysine residues, a low protein-to-crosslinker molar ratio was picked for initial 
crosslinking experiments.12 
 
 
Figure 13: The 3D crystal structure of catalase (CAT). Purple residues represent lysine, red residues represent cysteine, blue residues 
represent nitrogen and yellow residues represent sulfur. A. Monomeric CAT with all four sub-units. B. A single CAT sub-unit.   
 
Crosslinking of CAT using BS3, DTSSP, Sulfo-LC-SPDP and Sulfo-EGS in a protein-to-crosslinker molar ratio of 1:2, 
1:10, 1:50 or 1:400 did not result in a change in size measured by DLS (App B. Table 29). This suggests that no 
or minimal intermolecular crosslinking had occurred. On the other hand, the event of intramolecular 
crosslinking cannot be excluded. SDS-PAGE analysis revealed intense bands at around 60 kDa for all samples 
corresponding to one sub-unit of CAT (Figure 14). Further, crosslinking with BS3 at 1:50 and higher resulted in a 
weak band around 120 kDa. Crosslinking with Sulfo-EGS showed weak bands around 120 kDa, 180 kDa and 240 
kDa when crosslinking at 1:400 protein-to-crosslinker molar ratio. Based on the molecular weight, the weak 
==PAGE:46==
33 
 
bands at 120 kDa, 180 kDa and 240 kDa might correspond to crosslinking of two, three and four sub-units of 
CAT, respectively. Sulfo-EGS represents the longest crosslinker tested and is therefore expected to have the 
highest degree of flexibility. A high degree of flexibility will likely ease the crosslinking reaction, which might 
explain why this crosslinker gives the highest degree of crosslinking. Similar results were observed by Rozbesky 
et al. who crosslinked human carbonic anhydrase I using two crosslinkers with almost identical reactivity but 
different lengths (7.7 Å and 11.4 Å). They hypothesized that only one end of the shorter crosslinker had contact 
to the protein while the other end was hydrolyzed. In contrast, the long crosslinker likely had contact to the 
protein with both ends due to its higher flexibility compared to the shorter crosslinker.118 The same explanation 
may apply to the results obtained here. It is interesting that no crosslinking was observed using Sulfo-LC-SPDP 
as this crosslinker only varies 0.5 Å in length from Sulfo-EGS. However, the SDS-PAGE was run using reducing 
conditions which break up disulfide bonds. As crosslinking of Sulfo-LC-SPDP in one end consist of a disulfide 
bond this was likely the reason for no observed crosslinking in the SDS-PAGE. To determine if that was the 
reason, a SDS-PAGE under non-reducing conditions could have been done. If similar results were observed 
under non-reducing condition an explanation may be the different reactivities of the crosslinkers. Sulfo-EGS 
reacts with primary amines in both ends, which are highly present on the protein surface (Figure 13). Sulfo-LC-
SPDP only reacts with primary amines in one end where the other end is reactive towards thiols of which only a 
single is represented on the surface. If the primary amine crosslinking reaction occurs first, it may not be 
possible for Sulfo-LC-SPDP to reach a thiol group to form an intramolecular bridge. Crosslinking with DTSSP also 
include a disulfide bond and may be reason for the SDS-PAGE not showing any crosslinking as with Sulfo-LC-
SPDP. Crosslinking of two sub-units have been observed by Leitner et al. which crosslinked pyranose oxidase.119 
They could not confirm crosslinking of more than two sub-units by SDS-PAGE, likely because the maximum 
ladder weight was 150 kDa. Instead they confirmed crosslinking of four sub-units of the enzyme using electron 
microscopy.119  
All crosslinked samples were turned into NPs by desolvation. There was no clear correlation between the 
degree of crosslinking using BS3, DTSSP or Sulfo-SPDP-LC and NP size (App B. Table 30). However, NPs 
crosslinked with Sulfo-EGS resulted in a slightly reduced size at 1:400 protein-to-crosslinker molar ratio (App B. 
Table 30). A possible explanation for the reduced NP size by crosslinking with Sulfo-EGS could be related to the 
hydrophobicity. A ChemDraw calculation of the four crosslinkers revealed that Sulfo-EGS has the lowest 
predicted logP value thus being the most hydrophilic crosslinker (data not shown). Therefore, crosslinking at 
higher excess before desolvation may reduce the surface hydrophobicity of CAT. As desolvation is driven by 
hydrophobic interactions, a reduction in surface hydrophobicity may reduce the clustering of CAT thus 
resulting in smaller NPs. The reduced NP size indicates that crosslinking with Sulfo-EGS at high protein-to-
crosslinker molar ratio before desolvation led to more controlled conditions. Therefore, the following work will 
be concentrated mostly on Sulfo-EGS.

5.3.5 Impact of Sulfo-EGS at Different Crosslinking Ratios on Particle size and OD400/280  
To determine the minimum amount of Sulfo-EGS crosslinker needed for appearance on the SDS-PAGE, eight 
different crosslinking ratios between 1:10-1:45 were tested. As expected from previous results, no 
intermolecular crosslinking at the eight ratios were observed based on DLS (App B. Table 31). The SDS-PAGE 
revealed clear bands around 60 kDa for all eight crosslinked samples corresponding to one sub-unit of CAT 
(Figure 15). A weak band around 120 kDa appeared when a 25-molar excess of crosslinker was used and was 
present for all samples at this or higher protein-to-crosslinker molar ratios (Figure 15). The band around 120 
kDa may correspond to intramolecular crosslinking between two sub-units of CAT or natural occurrence of 
dimers. Further, unreacted crosslinker might induce clustering without crosslinking them together. However, 
the band at 120 kDa was not represented for non-crosslinked CAT which eliminates natural occurrence of 
dimers in the SDS-PAGE. The Sulfo-EGS crosslinker is hydrophilic and will unlikely promote hydrophobic 
clustering. Therefore, this is properly not the reason for the weak band at 120 kDa. Instead, it is concluded that 
crosslinking between two sub-units must have occurred. After desolvation, no clear correlation between NP 
size and the degree of crosslinking with the eight different molar ratios was observed (data not shown). This 
indicated that a higher protein-to-crosslinker molar ratio was needed to obtain controlled clustering.

5.3.6 Influence of Crosslinking Time and Temperature on Particle Size and PDI 
There was no difference in NP size and PDI after crosslinking at 1 or 2 h at 20°C with 1:10, 1:50, 1:200 and 
1:400 protein-to-crosslinker molar ratios (data not shown). This was not surprising as 30 min is reported to be 
sufficient for complete crosslinking with Sulfo-EGS at RT.120 Further, there was no difference in activity of the 
CAT solutions  after crosslinking for 1 or 2 h measured by the H2O2 decomposition test elaborate later (data not 
shown). Based on the results and reduced experiment time, 1 h was chosen as the final setting for further 
experiments. Moreover, there was no difference in the size of crosslinked CAT at the four ratios at 2°C or 20°C 
for 1 h (data not shown). This was confirmed by SDS-PAGE that showed no change in the degree of crosslinking 
at the two different temperatures when comparing the same protein-to-crosslinker molar ratio (Figure 16). 
Additionally, the activity was similar for CAT at the same protein-to-crosslinker molar ratio at the two 
temperatures measured by the H2O2 decomposition test elaborate later (data not shown). The size and PDI of 
NPs after crosslinking at 2°C were slightly reduced compared to the NPs prepared from crosslinking at 20°C for 
all crosslinking ratios (App B. Table 33). A polydisperse solution was obtained at 1:10 protein-to-crosslinker 
molar ratio at both 2 and 20 °C, where the other three protein-to-crosslinker molar ratios showed

and a more hydrophilic surface due to crosslinking before desolvation led to more controlled clustering.

5.3.7 Impact of Crosslinking on the Activity 
The activity of CAT after crosslinking with Sulfo-EGS at different ratios was investigated by CD spectroscopy and 
the H2O2 decomposition test. The CD spectra showed identical shape but different intensity of CAT at the four 
different crosslinking ratios (Figure 17). All four crosslinked CAT solutions had identical maxima and minima 
(Figure 17). Two negative bands at 222 nm and 208 nm and a positive band around 193 indicated an alfa-helix 
structure, which correlated with the results by Huang et al..8 Different CD signal intensities were observed in 
the spectra. A reasonable explanation was the use of different CAT concentrations, which was later confirmed 
by the BCA assay (data not shown). Because the spectrum shape, maxima and minima were identical for CAT 
and the crosslinked samples, it is likely that the protein has an intact secondary structure and is biologically 
active. Crosslinking with DTSSP at 1:10, 1:50, 1:200 and 1:400 protein-to-crosslinker molar ratios also showed 
intact structure. Further, a comparison between BS3, DTSSP, Sulfo-LC-SPDP and Sulfo-EGS all crosslinked at 1:10 
protein-to-crosslinker molar ratio resulted in intact structures (App B. Figure 32).

The activity of the CAT solutions was determined by the decrease in absorbance of H2O2 at 240 nm over time 
using the H2O2 decomposition test. Crosslinking at 1:10 and 1:50 protein-to-crosslinker molar ratios resulted in 
similar specific activity as purified CAT (Figure 18, Table 7). A slight decrease in specific activity was observed 
when crosslinking at a 1:200 and 1:400 protein-to-crosslinker molar ratios. Similar results were observed by 
Vrsanska et al. who explained that crosslinking at high degree may reduce the flexibility of the protein and 
thereby also the activity.121 Their explanation correlates with the results observed in this study as the SDS-PAGE 
revealed higher crosslinking degrees using 1:200 and 1:400 protein-to-crosslinker molar ratios (Figure 16). 
While data from CD and the H2O2 decomposition test showed activity, the OD400/280 ratio indicated 
decreasing activity with increasing degree of crosslinking (Figure 17, Table 7, App B. Table 32). Using a 1:400 
protein-to-crosslinker molar ratio, the OD400/280 value reflected a purity of zero related to no activity. A 
reasonable explanation for the drop in OD400/280 was loss of the heme group, which did not correlate with 
the results of the specific activity. Further, crosslinking may stabilize or constrain the protein structure leading 
to a change in the OD400/280 signal. However, that explanation does not correlate with the CD spectra 
showing intact secondary structure. The explanation for the OD400/280 drop may still be the by-product from 
the crosslinking reaction. Altogether, it was evaluated that crosslinking at high molar ratios was possible 
without completely loos of the activity of CAT. Since there were no significant differences between the SDS-
PAGE data, DLS data and specific activity for crosslinking at 1:200 or 1:400 protein-to-crosslinker molar ratios, a 
1:200 molar ratio was used for further experiments.

5.3.8 Influence of Crosslinking with a molar excess at 200 on Particle Size and PDI 
The final settings from the optimization are acetone as anti-solvent, 10 mM PB pH 7.5 and crosslinking with 
Sulfo-EGS for 1 h at 2°C. Using these settings, crosslinked CAT NPs were prepared by stopping the desolvation

the NP synthesis after 1 min resulted in the best NPs with respect to size, PDI and reproducibility (data not

consisted of non-crosslinked CAT to investigate the influence of the crosslinking on NP size and PDI.

solution. Conversely, NPs prepared from a non-crosslinked CAT solution led to a polydisperse NP solution after

NP synthesis. Therefore, crosslinking before desolvation will be included in the final NP formulation.

5.3.10 Specific Activity of CAT Nanoparticles 
The specific activity of crosslinked CAT NPs and CAT NPs was studied by the H2O2 decomposition test. Only a 
minimum difference in the specific activity for the crosslinked NPs and the non-crosslinked NPs was observed 
(Table 10, Figure 22). A reduced specific activity was observed of CAT NPs compared to CAT (Table 10). 
Comparing crosslinked CAT with crosslinked CAT NPs only a slightly decrease in activity was observed after 
desolvation (Table 10). The change in specific activity provides information regarding the construction of the 
NPs. In solid NPs, H2O2 may not be able to diffuse into the particle meaning that only CAT on the surface can 
decompose H2O2. This will lead to a major decrease in activity compared to the free, native protein. The 
opposite is expected in a porous NP structure.122 The results suggest a relatively porous NP structure of both 
CAT NPs and crosslinked CAT NPs as the activities were relatively close to that of the CAT solution before 
desolvation.

5.3.11 Degradation of the Final Crosslinked CAT Nanoparticles 
Sulfo-EGS contains two esters, which can be cleaved by hydroxylamine to yield two terminal amide bonds. 
Therefore, the degradation of crosslinked CAT NPs with hydroxylamine hydrochloride was investigated.123 To 
confirm the degradation of crosslinked CAT NPs, the solution was measured prior to addition of hydroxylamine 
hydrochloride. A reduced particle size was observed 1 h after the addition of hydroxylamine hydrochloride as 
reflected by size distribution based on intensity and number (Figure 23.A+B, App B. Table 34). Leaving the 
reaction for 4 h reduced the mean particle size even more (App B. Table 34). Analysis of the size distribution 
based on number distribution revealed two peaks after 4 h. The larger peak has a bigger size than the peak 
observed after 1 h (Figure 23.B), indicating that it may originate from clustering of the smaller particles seen in 
the 4 h curve. This correlates well with the observed tendency of CAT to spontaneously cluster in previous 
experiments.

5.3.12 Anti-oxidative Effect of the Final Crosslinked CAT Nanoparticles 
The cytotoxic effect of H2O2 on MCF-7 cells was confirmed by a decrease in cell viability compared to the non-
treated control (Figure 24). Mahalingaiah et al. and Bae et al. suggests an increase in MCF-7 cell death with

H2O2 on incubated MCF-7 cells.

5.4.1 Size and Purity of BSA 
Prior to crosslinking and NP synthesis, the size of the BSA monomer was studied. In 10 mM BB pH 9.5, BSA had 
a size on 3.6 nm with a PDI of 0.207 (Table 11). This is in agreement with findings of Jachimska et al. who 
reported the monomer to be 3.3 nm in 0.15 M NaCl.126 The small difference in particle size compared to the 
study by Jachimska et al. may lie in protein purity, protein concentration, the fact that they used a different 
solvent, or general data variation. As reflected by the PDI, a few larger particles were present in addition to the 
monomer. According to the manufacturer, the purity of the BSA batch is at least 98.5 %.127 Although this purity 
is considered a very high standard, it does not guarantee an absence of clusters or impurities. To investigate 
the purity, sterile filtration and desalting followed by freeze-drying was conducted. Sterile filtration reduced 
the particle size by 0.4 nm but did improve the PDI (Table 11). Freeze-drying increased the particle size by more 
than 250 nm suggesting formation of clusters (Table 11). Possible explanations to this include use of MQ water 
for desalting, incomplete protein dissolution after each desalting cycle, or to a high protein concentration 
during freeze-drying.128 MQ water does not contain ions and can have a pH as low as 5, which is close to the pI 
of BSA at 4.7.17,129 While ions may separate and stabilize protein molecules, a protein will have a low net charge 
at its pI thus being more prone to cluster.114 Finally, the combination of freeze-drying followed by sterile 
filtration did not improve the size or the PDI (Table 11). Examination using SDS-PAGE revealed clear bands 
around 60 kDa and weak bands around 120 kDa and 140 kDa for all four samples (Figure 25). The 60 kDa bands 
correspond to the monomer. Based on the MW, the weak bands at 120-140 kDa are likely dimers, which BSA is 
known to form.130 The weak bands at around 120-140 kDa have also been observed by others although these 
studies do not comment any further on this.130–133 Queiroz et al. successfully prepared BSA NPs by radiation 
despite of the weak bands around 120-140 kDa in their SDS-PAGE analysis.133 However, we cannot exclude that 
the presence of few dimers will affect the size and PDI of the NP after desolvation. Altogether, DLS and SDS-
PAGE data confirmed a high purity of the BSA batch. Simple purification steps of desalting and sterile filtration 
did not seem to improve the purity. Because of the risk of losing protein during these steps, the BSA batch will 
be used without further purification for NP synthesis.

5.4.3 Influence of Protein Concentration and Anti-solvent Volume on Nanoparticle Size and PDI 
To study the impact of anti-solvent volume, the concentration of BSA was kept constant at 66 mg/mL in 10 mM 
BB pH 9.5. The desolvation process was stopped after addition of 4.5 mL acetone as the solution became turbid 
at this volume. Langer et al. used a constant ethanol volume of 8 mL.135 They do not clarify the rational for this 
volume, they only state that the desolvation was completed. To investigate if increasing the acetone volume to 
past the point of turbidity had an impact on size and PDI, 6.5 and 8 mL of acetone was used. The size and PDI 
values of the NPs varied with different amounts of acetone without any clear tendency (App C. Table 35). Thus, 
the impact of acetone volume on particle size remains elusive. DLS measurements of the NPs indicated 
presence of smaller clusters in all samples after desolvation (App C. Table 35). There are many possible 
explanations for this observation. One obvious reason was incomplete desolvation despite of the solution 
being turbid. Or it may be interference by the buffer molecules which prevents association of the proteins, 
thereby inhibiting NP formation. This has also been observed by Langer et al. who achieved the best HSA NPs 
using a non-buffered 10 mM sodium chloride (NaCl) solution.135 The manufacturer reports the solubility of BSA 
to be 40 mg/mL meaning that the above experiments were conducted using a supersaturated solution of 
BSA.136 In highly concentrated or supersaturated solutions, the proteins molecules will be closer to each other. 
This may ease their assembly during desolvation but may also prevent pre-formed protein clusters to form NPs 
in the same controlled manner. These clusters may dissociate into smaller particles after re-suspension. 
Therefore, the concentration was changed to 33 mg/mL. Using 33 mg/mL, the turbidity shift was observed 
after addition of 4.75 mL acetone. A monodisperse NP solution with a size of 187 nm and a PDI of 0.177 was 
obtained (Table 13). Addition of acetone beyond the point of turbidity reduced the size but increased the PDI. 
Therefore, a protein concentration at 33 mg/mL and turbidity as an indicator of completed desolvation was 
used for further experiments.

Inspired by the findings of Langer et al. the use of a 10 mM NaCl solution as solvent was investigated.135 Langer 
et al. studied the impact on NP sizes by several buffers with varying concentrations and pH values. The NP sizes 
were not reproducible which they explained by, buffer salts interacting with the desolvation or crosslinking

process. Langer et al. used 10 mM NaCl at pH 9 and succeed making NPs with a size around 180 nm. In an 
attempt to optimize the NPs, 10 mM NaCl pH 9.5 was used as solvent. The NPs resulted in polydisperse 
solution with a mean of 81.2 nm after addition of 2 mL NaCl solution and were not in agreement with those of

5.4.5 Influence of Centrifugation Time and Force on Nanoparticle Size and PDI 
NPs were formulated with different centrifugation times and forces. There were no differences in NP size using 
5-15 min of centrifugation at 5000-7000 xG (data not shown) and 10 min was chosen as the final setting for 
further experiments. In contrast, a decrease in NP size was observed with increasing the centrifugal force 
ranging from 9000-15000 xG (Table 14). A reasonable explanation is denser packing of the NPs. According to 
Stokes’ law, the sedimentation rate depends on particle size. Larger particles will pellet faster.138 As reflected 
by the sizes, increasing centrifugation power causes sedimentation of more smaller particles.139 Based on the 
obtained results and the fact that an increased centrifugation power is expected to give the highest NP yield, 
15000 xG was chosen for further experiments.

to desolvation (App C. Table 37). After desolvation, a monodisperse NP solution was only achieved at pH 9.5.

5.4.7 Influence of BS3 Crosslinking before Desolvation on Particles Size and PDI 
BSA was crosslinked with BS3 in the attempt to optimize the NPs in relation to size and PDI. Crosslinking of BSA 
resulted in an increase from 2.4 nm to 3.3 nm (App C. Table 38). It is not possible to conclude if the protein has 
been crosslinked, or if by-products from the reaction promote slight clustering of BSA. Desolvation of 
crosslinked BSA resulted in NPs with sizes of 20-38 nm (App C. Table 38). A reason why NP synthesis on 
crosslinked BSA gives smaller sizes may be a reduced flexibility of the protein, which prevents it from packing. 
Therefore, BSA will not be crosslinked before desolvation. SDS-PAGE was used to investigate the crosslinking of 
BSA with BS3 at 1:5, 1:10, 1:20 or 1:50 protein-to-crosslinker molar ratios. The results showed a small increase 
in the size of each crosslinked BSA band compared to the non-crosslinked BSA (Figure 26.A). Similar results 
were observed by Shanafelt et al. who likewise used a lysine reactive crosslinker. They reported a blurred band 
with a slower migration and hypothesized that it could be a result of formation of an intramolecular bond.140 
This was likely the explanation for the results obtained. From the height, width and intensity of the bands, the 
wells appear to be overloaded (Figure 26.A). The experiment was repeated using lower protein concentrations 
(Figure 26.B). Again, the gel showed slower migration during the run, and it was easier to see the blurriness of 
the bands as the wells were not overloaded.

5.4.8 Influence of Surface-Crosslinking of BSA Nanoparticles with BS3 after Desolvation 
Two batches of BSA NPs were prepared by desolvation followed by BS3 surface-crosslinking with 1:10 protein-
to-crosslinker molar ratio of one of the batches. Size measurements at varying time points for 38 days showed 
a gradual decrease in mean size after 5 days of the non-surface-crosslinked BSA NPs (App C. Table 39). In 
contrast, the size of surface-crosslinked BSA NPs increased slightly in size over time (App C. Table 39). The small 
increase in NP size suggests that surface-crosslinking had occurred and indicates that surface-crosslinking 
stabilize the protein structure as envisaged by the increased stability. As the non-surface-crosslinked BSA NPs 
were stable for days, surface-crosslinking was not used in the final BSA NPs formulation.

6 Conclusion 
This master’s thesis project aimed at investigating formulation of CAT and BSA via the desolvation technique to 
produce NPs with optimal pharmacodynamic properties (size > 300 nm, PDI > 0.3). In order to achieve this, 
synthetic strategies were developed and optimized for each protein. 
Preliminary experiments of CAT revealed that purification was necessary. Of several tested purification 
methods, SEC was chosen as the preferred method. Among different tested crosslinkers, desolvation of Sulfo-
EGS-crosslinked CAT gave the most promising NPs with respect to size and PDI. Purified CAT was successfully 
crosslinked and formulated into NPs with a mean size of 153 nm and a mean PDI of 0.270. The final settings 
were crosslinking with Sulfo-EGS at a 1:200 protein-to-crosslinker molar ratio, crosslinking at 1 h at 2°C, 
acetone as anti-solvent, 10 mM PB pH 7.5 as solvent and running the desolvation process for 1 min as indicator 
for complete desolvation. NTA and TEM confirmed the size of crosslinked CAT NPs, and TEM images showed a 
spherical shape of the NPs. The optimization process reflected some of the potential challenges in use of 
organic solvents for synthesis of protein NPs as some of the anti-solvent disrupted the protein activity. The 
study furthermore revealed a slightly reduced specific activity of CAT when crosslinked at high protein-to-
crosslinker molar ratios. CD measurements suggested an intact secondary structure of CAT and crosslinked CAT 
independent of the crosslinking ratio. Monodisperse NPs were only achieved by desolvation of crosslinked CAT. 
Crosslinking before desolvation may reduce the folding opportunities of the protein and thus lead to a more 
controlled clustering. Furthermore, hydrophilic crosslinkers might contribute to more controlled clustering as 
the desolvation is driving by hydrophobic interactions. The limited literature on CAT NPs reflects some of the 
difficulties in synthesizing of CAT NPs by desolvation. 
Synthesis of BSA NPs was successfully optimized to give monodisperse NP solutions with a mean size of 178.5 
nm and a mean PDI value of 0.134. The final synthesis settings were 33 mg/mL BSA in 10 mM BB pH 9.5, 
acetone as anti-solvent, centrifugation time of 10 min, centrifugation force at 15000 xG and turbidity as 
indicator for complete desolvation. The size of the BSA NPs was confirmed by NTA and TEM. The TEM images 
showed a spherical morphology with only a slight variation in NP size. Crosslinking prior to desolvation did not 
improve the characteristics of the resulting NPs. However, surface-crosslinking improved the stability in 10 mM 
PB pH 7.5 compared to non-surface-crosslinked BSA NPs. CD spectroscopy demonstrated that surface-
crosslinking and NP formulation did not alter the secondary protein structure of BSA in the NP formulation. The 
protein is therefore expected to have retained its biological activity and function in the final formulation. BSA 
represents a well-studied protein and desolvation has commonly been used to synthesize BSA NPs. However, 
challenges regarding reproducibility were reflected in the work on the BSA NPs, especially with respect to the 
type and pH value of the solvent. 
The main aim of the thesis was to formulate NPs by desolvation with a size below 300 nm and a PDI lower than 
0.3. This was achieved for both CAT and BSA within the defined timeframe. However, further investigations of 
CAT NPs in vitro and in vivo are necessary to demonstrate the full therapeutic potential.

7 Future Perspective  
The work presented in this thesis successfully demonstrates synthetic pathways to produce protein NPs 
composed of CAT and BSA. The thesis shows that crosslinked CAT NPs retain the activity, but biological 
evaluation is needed for the NPs to reveal their true potential as a therapeutic modality. Intuitively, a series of 
more thorough pharmacokinetic and toxicology studies needs to be conducted to advance the thesis. Distinct 
cytotoxicity assays should be conducted to demonstrate that crosslinked CAT NPs do not alter cell proliferation, 
cell viability or induces cytotoxicity. Next, the ability of crosslinked CAT NPs to prevent H2O2 induced toxicity 
should be measured and benchmarked against native CAT using the MTS assay. This will provide valuable 
information covering the ability of crosslinked CAT NPs to reduce H2O2 thus being a potential medicinal 
product. 
It was only possible to obtain a monodisperse NP solution when CAT was crosslinked before desolvation. 
Therefore, it is only crosslinked CAT NPs that will be tested on cells. A comparison to NPs of non-crosslinked 
CAT NPs would be interesting to get an understanding of how crosslinking impacts the activity. However, to be 
able to make this comparison optimization of non-crosslinked CAT NPs to give a monodisperse solution must 
be completed.   
Furthermore, to obtain a deeper understanding of the degradation of the crosslinked CAT NPs with 
hydroxylamine hydrochloride, higher concentrations and/or longer incubation times could be investigated 
parallel to a negative control.  
Even though the produced NPs fulfil the requirements defined in the aims, there is still room for 
improvements. The NPs synthesis could be optimized to increase the reproducibility as the four final 
crosslinked CAT NPs solution varied slightly in size and PDI. It could be interesting to examine if another CAT 
concentration would visualize the nanoprecipitating and it therefore could be used as indicator for complete 
desolvation like it was possible with BSA.  
It remains unsolved why crosslinking with BS3 before desolvation resulted in a polydisperse crosslinked BSA NPs 
solution. Therefore, it would be relevant to study other crosslinkers in the attempt to obtain a monodisperse 
BSA NP solution when crosslinking before desolvation.  
Crosslinking before desolvation is less used compared to crosslinking after desolvation. However, it is still not 
clear why crosslinking before desolvation for CAT led to a monodisperse NP solution and for BSA to a 
polydisperse NP solution. By including additional crosslinkers and other proteins with different structures and 
pI values, the potential for understanding the relationship between protein, crosslinker and the resulting NPs 
would increase. The limited use of different proteins and crosslinkers in this project makes it difficult to 
observe and describe a general tendency. In the future, a screenings study would be beneficial to obtain a 
general tendency of how the structure of a protein and the choice of crosslinker impact the characteristic of 
the NPs after desolvation.

Oxytocin 
Introduction 
Oxytocin (OX) is a neuropeptide hormone that is predominantly produced in the hypothalamus, but also in the 
placenta, ovaries and endometrium. OX is a small cyclic peptide with a MW of 1006 Da and a pI of 7.7.142 It is 
composed of 9 amino acids of which six residues form a ring structure via a disulfide bond.143 It exerts its 
function as a neurotransmitter via the OX receptor to produce a plethora of physiological functions. Notably, 
OX is increasingly recognized as the “love hormone” due to its physiological effects in establishing social and 
emotional bonds in scenarios such as parturition, breast feeding and sexual intercourse.144 These effects has 
made OX an interesting therapeutic for treatment of cognitive and mental health diseases such as Autism 
Spectrum Disorder.143,145 To support this notion, Zaman et al. synthesized NPs via desolvation with a size of 240 
nm where OX was co-formulated with BSA.145 The therapeutic potential of OX combined with the limited 
research on OX NPs prepared from desolvation make it an interesting protein for synthesis of pure OX NPs. 
Results and Discussion 
Several challenges were present in the study of OX NPs. DLS data was of poor quality reflected by a 30 % 
decrease in particle size during the measurement. Combined with a maximum attenuator index of 11, this 
suggest a highly diluted and polydisperse solution of OX. It seems that OX tends to cluster based on large DLS 
measurements (Table 40). However, sterile filtration reduces the average size. An increase from 4 nm to 133 
nm was observed when sterile filtrated OX was crosslinked. It can be difficult to determine if the increase is 
caused by crosslinking or clustering of OX which may be promoted by unreacted crosslinker. To reveal this a 
SDS-PAGE could have been conducted.

Lysozyme  
Introduction 
Lysozyme (LYS) is an antimicrobial enzyme secreted from different parts of the body including milk, tears and 
saliva.146 LYS is a protein composed of a single chain of 129 amino acids. It has four disulfide bridges which gives 
the protein a high thermal stability. LYS has a MW of 14.4 kDa and a pI of 10.7 making it a highly basic 
protein.147 LYS is a 1,4-β-N-acetylmuramidase that cleaves glycosidic bonds of peptidoglycans which is the main 
component of the bacterial cell wall of gram-positive bacteria.148 Thus, the antimicrobial effect originate from 
LYS’s ability to break down the protective cell wall, destroy its structural integrity and consequently resulting in 
increased osmotic pressure that bursts the bacteria.146,147 Additionally, LYS has potential as an anticancer agent 
as it has shown strong anti-proliferative activity in breast cancer cell lines.55 Mahanta et al. observed cell death 
of more than 90 % of cultured MCF-7 cells when fed with 300 nm LYS NPs.55 The LYS NPs made by Mahnata et 
al. where crosslinked with GTA. Therefore, it could be interesting to prepare crosslinked and non-crosslinked 
LYS NPs to investigate the impact of crosslinking on the size of the NPs.   
 
Results and Discussion 
LYS formed large clusters when dissolved in 10 mM BB pH 8.5 as reflected by the large size and high PDI values 
(Table 41). Freeze-drying did not improve these parameters. On the other hand, sterile filtration resulted in 
small particles with a mean size of around 3 nm (Table 41). Examination of the protein purity using SDS-PAGE 
only revealed bands at around 14 kDa corresponding to the protein monomer (Figure 38). Thus, the clustering 
of LYS does not seem to be caused by presence of larger impurities. Since, BB pH 8.5 was the only solvent used, 
it cannot be excluded that this specific buffer is responsible for or contributes to the clustering of the protein. 
Further experiments were conducted using sterile filtered LYS.

Lactalbumin 
Introduction 
Lactalbumin (LAC) has a MW of 14.2 kDa and contains 123 amino acids. It has a pI of 4.6 and four 
intramolecular disulfide bonds that stabilize its globular structure.150 LAC is an essential protein in lactose 
synthesis and milk production in the mammary glands. LAC is essential for infant growth and development as it 
serves as a nutritional source of amino acids. These include lysine, tryptophan, branched-chain amino acid and 
the sulfur-containing amino acids cysteine and methionine.150,151 Consequently, LAC is  used as a nutritional 
supplement in food production due to its high thermal stability which its intramolecular disulfide bonds mainly 
are responsible for.150,151 Studies show that LAC improves sleep and neurological functions, likely because of its 
high tryptophan content. Tryptophan is the precursor for the neurotransmitters serotonin and melatonin, 
which are important for healthy neurological function and sleep, respectively.151 LAC is digested into smaller 
peptide fragments with antibacterial, probiotic, and immunostimulatory properties. Thus, LAC also has 
potential for treatment of infections.150,151 Successful development of LAC NPs is of great relevance and interest 
because of its multiple properties. Arrory et al. have prepared LAC NPs by desolvation with acetone as anti-
solvent and GTA as crosslinker.152 The group obtained NPs in size around 150 nm when crosslinking after the 
desolvation. The purification after desolvation and crosslinking included 5 cycles of centrifugation at (25000 xG, 
30 min, 4°C) and ultrasonication. As mentioned in the method section GTA may introduce toxicity and 
ultrasonication is time consuming. Therefore, it could be interesting to prepare LAC NPs with another 
crosslinker or without any crosslinker. Further, the purification process could be simplified by reducing the 
number of centrifugation cycles or by removing the ultrasonication step.   
 
Results and Discussion 
There is no product information about the protein purity and solubility of LAC from the manufacturer (Sigma-
Aldrich). A particle size of above 85 nm was observed independent of concentration, buffer or sterile filtration. 
This indicated formation of clusters comparing the size to literature values of 2 nm.153 This together with a low 
solubility in aqueous buffers indicated that the protein was highly prone to cluster or that the commercially 
acquired stock contained impurities. To investigate if LAC slowly dissolved into the monomer overtime, a 
solution of the protein at two different concentrations were incubated at 5°C for 4 h. The sizes of all four 
samples were still too high to be the monomer; however the sterile filtration reduced it (Table 44). Further, 
desalination was investigated at the same two concentrations to reduce the size by removing of impurities. The 
desalination resulted in similar sizes compared to the solutions placed in the fridge (Table 44).

Aprotinin 
Introduction 
Aprotinin (Apro) was identified in the 1930s where it was isolated from cattle.154 Apro comprises 58 amino 
acids in a single chain globular structure and it has three intramolecular disulfide bonds that stabilize its tertiary 
structure. Apro has a MW of 6512 Da and a pI of 10.5 which indicates that Apro is a highly basic protein.155 
Apro is a pancreatic trypsin inhibitor with antifibrinolytic properties. Antifibrinolytic molecules prevents 
breakdown of fibrin, which is important in the process of blood clotting. Therefore, Apro has previously been 
used as a therapeutic during complex and long surgeries to reduce the risk of organ failure due to low blood 
pressure and need of transfusion.156 Albeit, the use of Apro for surgical applications have been discontinued 
due to side effects. Apro is currently investigated for treatment of other diseases such as acute pancreatitis, 
hyper fibrinolytic hemorrhage and traumatic-hemorrhagic shock.155,157 Alai et al. explains how Apro has been 
used as an enzyme inhibitor for enzymes that normally degrade insulin (INS). This results in slower degradation 
rate and improve the absorption of INS.158 Due to the limited use in NP formulation and therapeutic potential it 
was chosen as a protein for this project.

Introduction 
Exendin-4 (EX-4) is a linear peptide, comprised of 39 amino, and isolated from saliva of the glia monster.160,161 It 
has a MW of 4184 Da and a pI of 4.96.161,162 It is a glucagon-like peptide 1 (GLP-1) analogue with potent 
physiological effects on both glucose and energy homeostasis. Consequently, big pharma companies have 
invested huge amounts of money into leveraging novel GLP-1R agonists for treatment of type 2 diabetes (T2D), 
obesity and Alzheimer’s disease. Unfortunately, the half-life of EX-4 is short and requires several daily 
injections resulting in poor patient compliance.160,161 Conversely, EX-4 displays improved circulatory half-life 
relative to native GLP-1 due to a single mutation of the second amino acid residue (A2G) making EX-4 resistant 
towards digestion of dipeptidyl-peptidase 4.163 The optimized pharmacokinetics gives the peptide a better 
profile for the treatment of T2D.160,164  To further improve the pharmacokinetics of EX-4, it has been chemically 
derivatized and formulated in different ways. Nguyen et al. observed an improved blood glucose lowering 
effect of EX-4 when loaded into enteric-coated capsules after oral administration.165 To prolong the half-life of 
EX-4, crosslinked EX-4 NPs were prepared through desolvation.   
 
 Results and Discussion 
Crosslinking of EX-4 was investigated to obtain NPs in an actable range in drug delivery.  DTSSP-crosslinking of 
EX-4 followed by desolvation gave particles with a size around 600 nm and a PDI of 0.55 which were too high 
for NPs (Table 48). Filtration of crosslinked EX-4 removed large particles as seen from a decrease average 
particle size and PDI. As expected, a decreasing pore size from 0.45 µm to 0.22 µm decreased the particle size 
even more (Table 48). The size of non-crosslinkined EX-4 should have been measured to delineate whether the 
clustering is a natural tendency of the protein or caused by the crosslinking.
