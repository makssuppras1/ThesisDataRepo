Abstract
Scenario discovery holds many benefits for decision-makers, such as providing clear insights and
actionable outputs regarding a set of possible scenarios.
However, the work required to get these insights can be tedious as model evaluation can be
computationally expensive and time consuming, especially on large-scale models such as the
complex mobility simulator SimMobility. Thus, there is a need for a more intelligent dataset
creation phase, using extensive machine learning techniques as active sampling strategies such
as Bayesian Optimization or Quality-Diversity algorithms.
Hence, this study assesses the strengths and weaknesses of different active learning strategies and
their optimal integration into the scenario-discovery framework, with the objective of enhancing
its results.
In conclusion, MAP-Elites algorithm demonstrated outstanding outcomes in both optimizing the
target value and exploring the search space effectively, and is thus a really promising technique
to improve the quality of scenario-discovery.
Keywords
Transport Models, Sampling, Scenario-discovery, Patient Induction Rule Method, Bayesian Op-
timization, Quality-Diversity, MAP-Elites

Introduction
An improved representation of human activity and travel decision-making is an essential ingredi-
ent in developing models which can deal effectively with issues such as air quality and congestion,
with sensitivity to policy alternatives such as demand management and intermodal resources
allocation [3]. It thus raises the question of what policies or investments could influence network
performance, and what are the costs of the necessary assessment.
For this purpose, scenario discovery uses statistical and data-mining algorithms to find policy-
relevant clusters of cases of interest in large datasets of simulation model evaluations. Conve-
niently interpreted as scenarios, these clusters help illuminate and quantify the tradeoffs among
alternative policies under deep uncertainty.
This report set the stage for an in-depth exploration of methodological tools such as Bayesian
optimization or quality-diversity algorithms that could enhance scenario discovery possibilities.
By leveraging these tools and techniques, both researchers and decision-makers can gain valuable
insights into the dynamics of mobility systems and reach better conclusions.
The first chapter provides a comprehensive literature review of methodological tools such as
Scenario discovery, Bayesian optimization, and Quality-diversity techniques. They share com-
mon ground in their approach to understanding complex systems and have demonstrated their
eﬀicacy in various domains. By examining these three tools together, we can explore the syner-
gies and complementary aspects they offer in improving our understanding and decision-making
processes.
The second chapter presents the complex mobility simulator SimMobility, which one of the
components serves as the backdrop for the research discussed in this paper.
The mobility
simulator is designed to replicate real-world mobility patterns and capture the interactions
between various entities, such as vehicles, pedestrians, and infrastructure.
This simulation
environment allows stakeholders to test different scenarios and assess the impacts of various
interventions and policies.
Moving forward, the third chapter delves into the results obtained through basic scenario discov-
ery using the complex mobility simulator component. This analysis provides valuable insights
into the key drivers and patterns governing mobility behavior. By identifying these scenarios,
different stakeholders can gain a deeper understanding of the complex system and potentially
develop strategies to improve mobility outcomes.
Additionally, the fourth chapter explores alternative algorithms for active sampling.
Active
sampling algorithms play a crucial role in the optimization process, guiding the selection of
new samples or experiments that provide the most informative and relevant information. This
section discusses various algorithms that can be used to refine and enhance the optimization
process within the context of mobility simulation.
Lastly, the fifth chapter presents the results (using the scenario-discovery framework) achieved

Chapter 1
Literature Review
Transport is the mean by which people are able to reach activities such as education, work or
leisure, thus being fundamental to the lives of everybody independently of their income and
lifestyle. Thus, an appropriate supply of transport infrastructures like roads or bridges is nec-
essary. However, providing it is far from being easy due to the costs that can be enormous,
the number of stakeholders involved that lead to conflicts of interest and a lot of travel-related
policy issues arising from externalities like environmental damages (CO2 emissions), accidents,
congestion … That is why transport systems are also often used by politicians to achieve objec-
tives that are not directly related to transportation such as equity of provision of education and
social services or local prestige [22].
Hence, transportation modeling is playing a key role in how planners and politicians are mak-
ing decisions to manage transport networks and cities as there is always a need for long-term
forecasts of the impact of new transport infrastructure. However, they are subject to a complex
duality. On the one hand, models are growing more and more complex by handling an increasing
number of variables and trying to attain always better accuracy. On the other hand, models
are being asked to be as understandable as possible in order to be more insightful during the
decision-making process, as they can provide important inputs for appraisal methods for trans-
port policy and planning. It means that both the model inherent parameters and its outputs
should be meaningful enough to allow decision-makers to rely on them, despite depicting a lot
of uncertainties.

1.1
Transport modeling
1.1.1
A brief history of transport modeling
The role of transport models is, as its name suggests, to model and subsequently predict trans-
port behaviour as a function of various inputs. Additionally, these models are also expected to
appraise different sets of inputs defining alternative scenarios to help policy makers to choose
the best solution among the available options. Then, what is the current state of the art about
transport models ?
The classical 4-stages model
Established by Wilson et al.
in the 1970’s ([37], [28]), the 4-stage model provides a usable
framework organizing model components related to transport:
• Trip generation determines the number of trip both generated and attracted in each zone.
• Trip distribution produce a calibrated origin-destination trip pattern (usually referred as
OD-matrix).
• Mode choice determines the split between transport modes. It heavily relies on the applied
policies.
• Assignment models are allocating transport to the network based on the OD-matrix that
has been split by mode. The outcome is a network flow that allows to identify transport
demand by area.
Without going into too much details, a visual representation of this framework is shown on
Figure 1.1.
Random utility models
The use of Random Utility Maximisation (RUM) grew out of the need for disaggregate modeling
and economic consistency. It stems from the assumption of a rational behavior from consumers,
to the extent that this is reflected in data through revealed preferences.
To illustrate, let’s
consider a simple utility function Ui for an alternative i ∈D for a given individual n ∈[1, ...N]:
Un,i = Vn,i + ϵn,i
where Vn,i is usually a linear form of the model parameters and the individuals inputs.
Although it resembles a regression model, it is very different in the sense that the utility function
is a latent variable which is not observed. What can be observed, though, is the choice of the
individuals. For transport models, a probabilistic model is usually used to derive it, where the
most common one defines the probability Pn(i) of choosing alternative i for individual n such
as:
Pn(i) =
eµUn,i
∑
j eµUn,j
with µ the scale parameter giving the sensitivity of traveler for differences between the utility
of the alternatives [35].

Activity-based models
Finally, another type of model have been developed by approaching the problem from a different
angle. The main idea is that people’s activities are the driver for transport, and by modelling
activities rather than transport itself we often get a more robust model for transport behaviour.
Typically, activity based models are formulated as discrete choice models, which on their part
incorporate a variety of decisions of the members in the households and their possible interactions
in the space-time domain ([28], [3]).
Additionally, these models share similarities with the
traditional 4-stages model: activities are generated, destination are identified, travel modes are
determined and the specific network facilities or routes used for each trips are predicted.

1.2
Scenario discovery
Robust-decision making and scenario discovery are techniques that were introduced by RAND
(an American non profit global policy think tank) in a series of articles written around 2010
([17], [10], [29], [4]). These articles aim at establishing an appropriate analytic foundation for
inclusion in quantitative decision analyses.
Scenario discovery addresses problems encountered when trying to develop model-based scenar-
ios for problems that involve a large number of actors with diverging world views and values,
or that are characterized by a very large number of uncertain factors [15]. Typical for such
problems is that the analysts do not know, or the parties to a decision cannot agree on several
aspect such as:
• the appropriate conceptual models that describe the relationships among the key driving
forces that shape the long-term future
• the probability distributions used to represent uncertainty about key variables and param-
eters in the mathematical representations of these conceptual models
• how to value the desirability of alternative outcomes
Thus, it aims to summarize sets of plausible future states of the world that illuminate key
vulnerabilities in proposed policies and to describe these scenarios in a manner useful for decision
makers and stakeholders.
1.2.1
Scenario Generation
A way to fulfill this objective is to use scenarios. They contain the story of multiple futures,
from the expected to the wildcard, in forms that are analytically coherent and imaginatively
engaging [25]. They divide into 3 main categories, as defined by the European Environment
Agency [7] per below:
• Exploratory scenarios (also known as descriptive scenarios) are those that begin in the
present and explore trends into the future.
• Predictive scenarios (also known as forecasting scenarios) explore alternative developments,
starting from the current situation with or without expected/desired policy efforts.
• Normative scenarios (also known as anticipatory scenarios) start with a prescribed vision
of the future and then works backwards in time to visualise how this future could emerge
A common way to generate scenarios is by using sampling of the model parameters.
1.2.2
Sampling Strategies
Introduction to sampling
In order to plan computer experiments, a general approach that seeks to design points filling
bounded regions as uniformly as possible is necessary.
Such designs are broadly referred to
as space-filling designs [18]. As the true relationship between the inputs and the response is
unknown and often very complicated, its study requires a lot of pre-thinking such as a priori or
background knowledge, that can be facilitated by the use of designs for computer experiments.
Thus, in order to fully explore the input space possibilities, a space-filling design is a really good
choice.
The uncertainty about the values of the inputs is considered by treating them as random vari-
ables.
Then, obtaining information about them requires balancing the representativity and
exhaustivity of the sample. The 3 main methods are presented below [21].
• Random sampling: using brute-force Monte Carlo, define the domain of the possible inputs
and generate data points randomly based on a probability distribution over the domain.

In a space-filling design, the probability would follow a uniform distribution. However, the

• Stratified sampling: using this technique, every single area of the sample space is to be

disjoint strata Si of size pi = P(X ∈Si), and create a sample inside each of the strata.
• Latin Hypercube Sampling: similarly as stratified sampling, with the extra insurance that

each stratum.
Latin Hypercube Sampling
Latin Hypercube Sampling (LHS) is a method for generating a set of random samples of variables
that have a specified probability distribution. The goal of LHS is to produce a set of samples
that are evenly spaced throughout the probability distribution of the variables, and thus more
representative of the overall distribution than a purely random set of samples. The basic idea
behind LHS is to divide the range of each variable into equally sized intervals, and then randomly
sample one point from each interval. This ensures that each sample falls into a different interval
of the variable and that the overall distribution of the samples reflects the distribution of the
variable [20].

Interpretability
This last measure suggests the ease with which the box set can be understood and used to gain

Additionally, two more measures are assessing the statistical significance of the parameter con-
straints proposed by the scenario discovery algorithm. These techniques, commonly employed
in the field of statistical learning to diagnose the quality of models fit to data, prove appropriate
because the PRIM errors result from the finite and stochastic sampling of the LHS experimental

Resampling test
The resampling test run the algorithm on multiple subsamples of the original dataset and mea-
sure the frequency of appearance of parameter constraints. Ideally, the constraints always lie
down on the same parameters.
Quasi p-values
This test estimates the likelihood that the PRIM algorithm constraints a parameter purely by
chance, thus being almost as such as the definition of a statistical p-value.
At the end of the day, a box representing a perfect scenario would be defined by constraints on
only one or two parameters and would lie with 100% coverage and 100% density, thus capturing
all the cases of interest and excluding all the other cases. Since such a box can not be achieved in
practise, users must choose one with the combination of coverage, density, and interpretability
that best supports their decision application.
Discussion
Literature spotted at least 3 shortcomings related to the PRIM algorithm that one has to be
aware of [19].
• The algorithm may be computationally expensive as it requires to run a lot of occurrence
of the model that is being studied.
• If there is a high collinearity between variables in the input space, it might fail to find
suitable boxes.
• It can only detect one bump, so it would fail in case of an experiment leading to multiple
high target value subregions.
In order to overcome these inconvenients, we need to take a step back and rethink the sampling
method. Active sampling is a method build with Bayesian Optimization or Quality-Diversity
used to carefully select the data points, thereby reducing the labeling cost and improving the
eﬀiciency of the learning process.

1.3
Bayesian Optimisation
Bayesian Optimization (BO) is a powerful optimization technique that uses probabilistic models
to eﬀiciently search for the optimal solution in a complex and expensive design space. This
approach has gained increasing attention in recent years due to its ability to handle black-box
functions, where the analytical form of the objective function is not known, and only a limited
number of evaluations are possible due to the high computational cost. Thus, its use makes
sense in the situation as the mobility model can assuredly be defined as an expensive to evaluate
black-box function.
1.3.1
Introduction
In Bayesian optimization, a probabilistic model is built based on the available data to approx-
imate the objective function. This model provides a posterior distribution that describes the
uncertainty in the predicted values and allows for the quantification of the trade-off between
exploration and exploitation. The exploration of the design space is guided by a search strategy
that balances the need to explore unknown regions and the exploitation of promising areas.
More formally, Bayesian Optimization is a class of machine-learning-based optimization methods
focused on solving the problem
max
x∈Af(x)
where the feasible set A and the objective function f have the following properties ([8]):
• Dimension of the input x is not too large, typically x ∈Rd where d ≤20.
• The feasible set A is a simple set such as an hyper-rectangle or a d-dimensional simplex.
• The objective function f is continuous.
• The objective function f is ”expensive to evaluate”, meaning that the number of evalua-
tions that may be performed is limited due to time or monetary constraints for example.
• The objective function f is lacking known special properties such as concavity or linearity
that would make it easy to optimize using techniques that leverage such structure to
improve eﬀiciency. In other words, f is a “black box” function.
• The objective function f is ”derivative-free”, meaning that when f is being evaluated, f(x)
is observed whereas its derivatives are not (thus preventing the application of first- and
second-order methods like gradient descent, Newton’s method ...).
• The objective function f can be obscured by stochastic noise, where noise is assumed
independent across evaluations and Gaussian with constant variance.
Bayesian optimization is a powerful optimization technique that involves two stages: the first
stage is the construction of a probabilistic model, which uses statistical inference to predict the
optimal design parameters, and the second stage is the use of an acquisition function to decide
where to sample the objective function f. The combination of these two stages allows for the
eﬀicient exploration of the design space, while minimizing the number of evaluations required
to find the optimal solution.
Then, the whole theory behind Bayesian Optimization is build using Bayes theorem:

1.3.4
Acquisition functions
The second stage of Bayesian optimization involves the use of an acquisition function to decide
where to sample the objective function. The acquisition function balances the need to explore
new regions of the design space and the exploitation of promising areas. It selects the next point
to evaluate based on a trade-off between the expected improvement in the objective function and
the uncertainty in the predicted values. Some examples of commonly used acquisition functions
are:
• Expected Improvement (EI), that is heavily focused on optimization
• Upper Confidence Bound (UCB), that can be hacked to become exploration-focused
• Posterior variance, that explicitly look at the area with the more uncertainties
Given all these characterisitcs, there are high expectations that could be helpful in the scenario-
discovery sampling phase.

After exploring optimisation algorithms, let’s now bring the focus on search algorithms, which

1.4.1
Evolutionary algorithms
From nature observations to evolutionary computation
The products of nature have long served as inspiration for the investigation and practise of
computer sciences. For examples, many phenomena in nature such as ant colonies behavior or
neurons in the brain can be described as a cellular automata. Indeed, despite not being strictly
considered as evolutionary algorithms, they share a lot of similarities as they exhibit emergent
behavior (fundamental cells follow a simple set of rules, but at the end of the day the group
structure appears highly complex) and evolution-like dynamics (reproduction, selection, muta-
tion mechanisms).
However, the ability of evolutionary algorithms to match the complexity and sophistication of
nature has fallen behind, leading to the development of more and more powerful algorithms.
Then, there is here an even more fundamental question that is often not explicitly addressed:
What practical applications can evolution be effectively applied to, beyond the challenge of ab-
stracting nature’s remarkable achievements into powerful algorithms? [26]
Until recently, the implicit answer has been to use objective optimization, thus bringing evolu-
tionary computation closer to a subfield of machine learning.
Objective optimization: the example of genetic algorithms [39]
Genetic Algorithm (GA) are being used to solve diﬀicult problems with objective function that
do not possess nice properties such as continuity, differentiability ... The idea is to maintain
and manipulate a population of individuals and to implement a Survival of the fittest strategy
in their search for an optimal solution.
For this purpose, the need to explore the subspace is satisfy through the population generation
step. This step rely on three main genetic operators: crossover, mutation, and selection. Their
roles can be very different .
• Crossover: Swap of parts of the solution with another the solution representations. The
main role is to provide mixing of the solutions and convergence in a subspace.
• Mutation: The change of parts of one solution randomly, which increases the diversity of
the population and provides a mechanism for escaping from a local optimum. Then, it
usually leads to a solution outside of the initial subspace.
• Selection of the fittest, or elitism. The use of the solutions with high fitness to pass on to
next generations, which is often carried out in terms of some form of selection of the best
solutions. It affects the search behavior of an algorithm, ensuring that the best solutions
survive in the population.

1.4.2
Quality-Diversity (QD)
Going back to the question, it is diﬀicult to imagine that evolution in nature is structured in the
same way as a conventional optimization algorithm. In nature, there lacks a clear overarching
objective, and organisms often receive rewards not only for being superior but also for being
distinct.
For example, organisms that are suﬀiciently different from their predecessors may
establish a new niche in which they enjoy greatly reduced competition and enhanced chances of
survival. This observation led to a new perspective in evolutionary computation: evolution as
a machine for diversification rather than optimization, with algorithms that which search only
for behavioral diversity without any underlying objective pressure [26].
Then, the objective of this novel search approach, known as Quality-Diversity (QD), is to discover
a diverse set of individuals that encompasses a wide range of potential behaviors. Simultaneously,
each member within this set aims to achieve the highest level of performance possible. In other
words, the goal of QD is to sample all regions of the behavior space (at some granularity),
returning the best possible performance within each region.
Formally, the behavior space is a repertoire m divided into k niches such as:
m = {c1, ..., ck}
Then, the task of QD is to maximize a measure Q within every single niche ci.
Quality-Diversity algorithms perform especially well in spaces with a deceptive fitness landscape.
In such a domain, the search algorithm following the fitness gradient may perform worse than
an algorithm following novelty gradients because novelty cannot be deceived as it ignores fitness
entirely.
Finally, most famous examples of QD algorithms are Novelty search ([16]) and MAP-Elites.

1.4.3
MAP-Elites
In more restricted search spaces, QD algorithms have also been called illumination algorithms
because they effectively reveal the best possible performance achievable in each region of the
phenotype space. They thus illuminate the fitness potential of each region of the feature space.
The Multi-dimensional Archive of Phenotypic Elites, later on called MAP-Elites is one of these
algorithm [23].
The starting point of the MAP-Elites algorithm is to define a performance measure fitness(y)
that evaluate a solution y = f(x). Additionally, each dimension of variation of the genotype
x is discretized down to the desired cell granularity (following the underneath idea of Latin
Hypercube Sampling sampling). The grid shape thus characterized the MAP-Elites algorithm,
as seen in Figure 1.5.
Then, the search is conducted within the search space: initial random genotypes are generated
and evaluated, and then new individuals are being generated using genetic algorithms principles
of selection, mutation and elitism. Finally, the core concept of MAP-Elite is that the solution is
stored in the appropriate cell if and only if it brings some improvement to the fitness function.

SIM Mobility
SimMobility is a simulation platform, developed at the Singapore-MIT Alliance for Research
and Technology (SMART), that integrates various mobility-sensitive behavioral models within
a multi-scale framework that considers land-use (long-term travel behavior), transportation and
communication (mid- and short-term travel behavior) interactions. Thus, it allows to compre-

This chapter explains the model from the top level down to the lowest level of details for the
components that are being used in this thesis.
2.1.1
Framework
SimMobility is made of three primary modules, each of them having its own specific timeframe.
Figure 2.1 shows the high-level architecture and the interactions between various components.
Short-Term:
The short-term component works at an operational level: its task is to simulate the movements
of agents at the lowest granularity (up to 100ms) as an integrated microscopic mobility simu-
lator [2]. Thus, it is responsible for the movements of vehicles, pedestrians and goods on the
transportation network according to their respecting behavioral and decision-making models.
Additionally, control and management modules are influencing an agent’s decision by creat-

scheduling and dispatching of buses).
Mid-Term:
The SimMobility Mid-term (MT) simulator models daily transportation demand and supply for
passengers and goods: it simulates agents’ behavior including their activity and travel patterns,
and the movement of vehicles at a mesoscopic level.
Thus, it represents moving vehicles in
aggregate, and generates routes using a behavior-based demand model [24], [38].
Long-Term:
The Long-term is designed to simulate on a year to year basis how the interrelations between the
transportation and land-use systems manifest themselves in the housing and commercial real
estate markets, household and firm location choices, school and workplace choices, and vehicle
ownership choices. Thus, it captures the on-going economic activity with a special emphasis on
accessibility, and accounts for interactions among individuals and their environment [41].

2.1.2
Mid-Term
SimMobility MT is an open source framework that aims to simulate millions of agent evolving
in a large network [31]. It consists of three main modules (see Figure 2.2):
• the Pre-day module generates travel demand for each agent
• the Within-day module transforms the previous travel plan into action (departure time
choice, route choice ...)
• the Supply module simulates multimodal network performance
Additionally, SimMobility MT utilizes a hybrid simulation paradigm, where demand is event-
driven while supply operates on a time step basis [24].
Demand
Demand is generated within the Pre-day module through the prediction of the activity-travel
schedule for each agent (activity, number of tours, mode and timing). This will be elaborated
further more in section 2.1.3, as the Pre-day is the core-component of the case study.
Supply
With the given activity schedule, the Within-day module simulates route choice, departure time
choice and re-scheduling decisions in response to information or control given by the Supply
module. Basically, this module is a traﬀic simulator that includes bus and rail controllers to
manage public transit operations such as the frequency/headway-based dispatching of vehicles,
monitoring of occupancy, dwelling at stops ...
Interraction demand-supply
The interactions between demand and supply are explicitly represented in the model through
daily and within-day learning mechanisms.
The within-day component includes a feedback loop of time-dependant link travel times and
pubic transit waiting time, leading to an iterative adjustement of route choice decision of the
individuals. The day-to-day component include a feedback loop of aggregate zone to zone travel
time and public transit waiting time, resulting in an iterative correction of activity decisions of
individuals.
2.1.3
Pre day
The Pre-day module is an activity-based model (ABM) system that adopts the Daily Activity
Schedule approach (Ben-Akiva et al., 1996) to predict agents activity-travel schedule (plan)
including: (1) Activity types and number, (2) Activity durations and time-of-day, (3) Activity
locations, and (4) Modes. The Pre-day ABM is a system of hierarchical discrete choice models
(logit and nested-logit) organized into three levels: the day-pattern level, the tour level and the
intermediate stop level, as shown in Figure 2.3.
Due to external factors, SimMobility model could not be run for this work, and the study case
is then moved to only one of its component.

This chapter aims to demonstrate the capabilities of the scenario discovery approach and tools,

Additionally, this chapter allows to introduce the different assumptions and issues that will be

3.1
Day Pattern Model
The Day Pattern (DP) model is an activity-based model that examines the trip generation
among a given population. This case study focuses on the number of leisure trips as they are
the ones where actionable responses are the easiest to implement in practice for decision-makers.
3.1.1
Generate dataset
The Day Pattern (DP) model has 18 uncertain input parameters as shown in Table 2.1 along with
their variance. Then, the allowed range of variation can be computed using the 95% confidence
interval formula:
CI = [µ −1.96σ, µ + 1.96σ]
Indeed, the standard deviation allows to derived low and high estimates for each parameter.
It is assumed that the input parameters vary independently across their ranges. However, some
solutions exist if they were to be correlated, that will not be developed further here.
Latin Hypercube Sampling
A Latin Hypercube sample is used to create an experimental design over the input space defined
by the 18 uncertain parameters. Running this sample through the simulation creates a database
that explores the implications of all combinations of the full range of possible values. 5000 data
points were generated, which required approximately 6 hours of CPU time. Each record in the
database has 25 entries, 18 with a value for each of the uncertain input parameters, one for the
execution time of the process, and 6 with the resulting value of the model output of interest,
that is, the number of trips for each activity. That leaves a dataset density of J = 1.60 so within
the scope of other studies. Figure 3.1 shows the heatmap of sampling density.

The next step is to characterize the output values of the database. First, the focus is on one
single activity, Leisure, and the objective is to find which factors influence this type of trip the
most. Then, the idea is to differentiate between the cases with really low (respectively really
high) number of leisure trips and the other cases.
As this metric lacks either a natural boundary or a stakeholder preference, the thresholds are set
up to be the 3% (respectively 97%) quantiles. Figure 3.2 shows the distribution of the number

3.1.5
Informing decisions with scenario discovery
By exploring the effects of trade-offs, stakeholders gain valuable insights into the dependencies
between their decisions and system performance.
Through the comparison and contrast of
alternative designs, they are able to discover the cost-benefit compromises inherent in various
strategies, helping them avoid short-sighted decisions. Ultimately, the goal is to explore these
solutions in detail, identifying designs of interest, all while keeping in mind the potential impact
of these decisions on the future. As articulated by the RAND team, ”Scenarios do not aim to
predict the future, but rather bound it” [17].

3.2
Discussion
Throughout this chapter, scenario discovery has been highlighted for its numerous strengths
in impacting decisions and aiding stakeholders in gaining a deeper understanding of complex
problems. Some examples of these strengths include:
• providing a comprehensive overview of the problem by considering all relevant uncertainties
• presenting results in an easy-to-understand and intuitive manner
• enabling the quantification of uncertainties and the effects of different policies
These advantages make scenario discovery a powerful tool for decision-making processes, facili-
tating a more informed and effective approach to tackling challenging issues.
As a matter of fact, the study led in this chapter allowed to uncover what are the major drivers
of the Upper Threshold scenario: the most eﬀicient way to increase the number of leisure trips
would most probably be to increase the utility of retired people toward this activity, as they are
concerned by most of the influential model parameters.
However, it also has some issues, mostly related to the implementation part (and thus are not
really affecting the final product used by the stakeholders). The biggest one, that is one of the
main driver of this thesis and the implicit research question behind the next chapters, is about
the creation of the dataset. Indeed, model evaluation can turn to be really expensive (both in
time and costs), and sampling a large number of points might not be possible.
Given the scenario discovery framework, the sampling phase is then the phase that can be act
upon in order to tackle this issue.

Chapter 4
Active Learning for policy search
This chapter presents the methodology to improve scenario discovery results by using active
sampling strategies. The final objective of these strategies is to find similar insights that the
one presented in Chapter 3 while running less model evaluation.
The first part describes each active sampling strategy. Then, the experimental setup, and how
to optimize it, is introduced, as well as the metrics of success.
4.1
Sampling strategies
Three different sampling strategies are being introduced, based on the different techniques pre-
sented in Chapter 1. They are approaching the same problem using different angles, relying on
their own strengths.
4.1.1
PrimBox
Starting from an original LHS sample, the idea behind this strategy is fairly simple: assuming
that the boxes uncovered by the prim algorithm are attempts to define the area of interest, the
goal is then to increase the focus of the sampling toward this area. In practice, the algorithm is
the following:
Algorithm 3 PrimBox sampling
1: Run the Patient Induction Rule Method (PRIM) algorithm on the initial dataset and find
the optimal box B0.
2: for i = 1, 2, . . . , I do
3:
Generate new data points by sampling 2 points inside the box Bi−1 and one in the whole
search space (to keep diversity of sampling)
4:
Evaluate these 3 new points, update the dataset
5:
Run the prim algorithm with the updated dataset to find the new optimal box Bi
6: end for
By iteratively sampling inside the optimal area of interest at step i, the prim box becomes more
and more accurate, whereas the risk of overfitting is handled through the sampling of a random
point in the whole search space.
Despite looking quite simple, the strength of this algorithm lies in the fact that it is using
scenario discovery techniques in order to enhance scenario discovery outputs. Namely, by running
the prim algorithm again and again, the sampling technique aims at optimizing the scenario
discovery results and looking for the optimal box boundaries.
Additionally, there is no need for hyperparameter tuning for this algorithm as the only model
parameters that could be fitted are peeling and covering fractions, which have already been
Exploring Bayesian Optimisation for Scenario Discovery in Complex Mobility Simulation

looked upon in section 3.1.2. As that the dataset remains similar, it is assumed that the optimal
peeling fraction remains the same.
The outcome of the algorithm is the final dataset alongside the final prim box BI.
Finally, given that the PRIM algorithm is in O(1), the required computational time should stay
close to the benchmark execution time.

MAP-Elites
The MAP-Elites strategy follows the steps mentioned in section 2.4.3 to generate an eﬀicient
repertoire both regarding exploration and optimization of the metrics of interest.
The first step is to generate the repertoire.
The choice was set to use a CVT-MAP-Elites
repertoire, easier to implement in the current situation. In practice, it was made using k-mean
clustering (using E centroids) over the initial LHS sampling, thus allowing to create an equally-
distributed map of elites.
Then, each data point from the original LHS sampling is then evaluated and affected to the
correct niche, while each niche is only saving the data point with the highest evaluation results.
Starting from this pre-filled repertoire, the MAP-Elites algorithm proceeds following the genetic
algorithm principles:
• Selection: pick a random individual x among the list of elites
• Mutation: create a new individual x′ by drawing a truncated multi-variate distribution
([34]) centered on x to stay within the search domain

updated at each iteration if it is being picked in the selection phase.
A quick hyperparameter tuning phase is done in the results section, in order to compare the

4.2.2
Experimental setup
The 3 sampling strategies are holding 2 major common points, mostly inherent to sampling:
• Instead of starting to run from scratch, an initial dataset of size n that has already been
evaluated is provided.
• The number of model evaluations being run by the sampling algorithm is assumed to be
already set up and the same for every strategy.
Let’s address these 2 topics, as they are closely related to the objective of reducing the number
of model evaluations.
Initial dataset size
This first question depends on how much resources can be allocated prior to the active sampling
phase. Indeed, it is expected that the larger the initial dataset is, the more eﬀicient the active
sampling will be as it will be based on more data points.
However, this phase can also be extremely expensive in terms of computational time and cost.
For example, the execution time of the supply module (another component of the Mid-Term
SimMobility model) is 4.16min, which would thus require almost 3 days of computational time
to get to an initial dataset of size 1000.
In the context of the experiment, initial dataset sizes are every hundreds from 100 to 1000.
Number of points to sample
The second question shares a lot with the first one, as the constraint of the budget allocated to
model evaluations remains.

4.3
Metrics of success
The objective of active learning is to reconcile the best of both worlds, by achieving high opti-
mization results while exploring as much as possible of a given search space.
4.3.1
Optimization
Let’s remind here that the scenario discovery framework established previously is aiming at
finding areas where the model evaluations give a high number of leisure trips. Then, it makes
sense to use the average number of leisure trips as a measure of the eﬀiciency of the optimization
process, given that it basically corresponds to its objective function.
4.3.2
Exploration
In order to measure the second objective, several metrics can be used. Their general idea is to
evaluate how well the algorithm performs regarding the exploration criteria.
Distance to benchmark
The metric of distance defined previously in section 4.2.1 can be used to measure the exploration
of the algorithm. Indeed, quantifying the distance from the prim box to the benchmark prim
box highlights the capabilities of the algorithms in terms of scenario discovery.
Sampling bias
Using non-random sampling, there can be a tendency of the sampling process to favor certain
regions of the search space over others, resulting in an uneven distribution of the sampled points.
This phenomenon is often referred to as sampling bias or local clustering.
As targeted sampling strategies are being used in the active learning process, the intention is
definitely to skew the distribution of sample points toward the area of interest. However, the
exploration/diversity criteria have to be kept in mind, and a concentration of data points in
a specific area does not deserve the goal. Thus, the metric of sampling bias was introduced,
in order to measure the gap between the sampling under scrutiny and an evenly distributed
sampling.
Formally, with ni the ratio of data points in the box across feature i and wi the ratio of the
width of the box over the search space boundaries, the relative sampling bias for the feature i
is written:
ρi = ni
wi
−1
For example, the expected sampling bias for every feature for an LHS is 0%, as an even distribu-
tion of samples leads to the number of data points in the prim box being in the same proportion
as the number of data points in the whole search space.

are developed below:
• When the initial dataset is small, the model doesn’t rely solely on it for generalization.
Consequently, active learning allows the model to explore and actively query additional
data points, which can lead to improved generalization when compared to models trained
on a larger dataset.
• As active learning aims to select the most informative samples for annotation, with a small
dataset, the model may encounter more diverse and uncertain examples. This diversity
thus enhances the learning process, as the model focuses on regions where it’s unsure,
improving its performance in those areas.

Chapter 6
Conclusion
In conclusion, the potential of active learning can be used to enhance scenario discovery perfor-
mances significantly. By integrating active learning strategies into scenario discovery methodolo-
gies, this study aimed to address existing limitations and provide a more eﬀicient and informative
decision-making tool for stakeholders.
Three distinct active sampling strategies were implemented and evaluated in comparison to a
baseline random sampling approach.
• PrimBox, which follows a PRIM-oriented algorithm, consistently yielded stable and reliable
results. By actively selecting and evaluating scenarios based on PRIM’s insights, PrimBox
explored the problem space, focusing (maybe too much) its search on the area of interest.
• Bayesian Optimization, a pure optimization algorithm, showed more disappointing perfor-
mances due to the inherent nature of BO, which focuses on optimization and may overlook
the exploration of diverse scenarios. As a result, BO struggled to effectively identify and
understand the full range of uncertainties in the problem space, limiting its ability to
provide comprehensive insights.
• Quality-Diversity, and especially the MAP-Elites algorithm, demonstrated outstanding
outcomes in both optimizing the target value and exploring the search space effectively,
resulting in finding the benchmark PRIM box using 75% less simulations.
The key strength of MAP-Elites lies in its ability to maintain a diverse set of high-quality solu-
tions within a single archive, which facilitates a better understanding of the trade-offs between
different objectives. This allows the algorithm to avoid premature convergence to suboptimal
solutions and promotes the discovery of novel and unexplored regions of the search space. As
a consequence, a substantial understanding of the search space is possible with a relatively low
number of model evaluations.
In fine, we strongly believe that there exists significant potential for further improvement and
refinement in this domain. Many pre-processing and model hypertuning steps such as a better
selection of the initial dataset or a sharper definition of the MAP-Elites repertoire for example,
were beyond the scope of this study but should be integrated to enhance the active sampling
approach.
