Abstract
ATHENA (Advanced Telescope for High-ENergy Astrophysics) is a mission chosen by ESA to observe
the hot and energetic universe. The primary science goals of ATHENA is to detect X-rays emitted from
gas in clusters and groups of galaxies, and by observing the extreme energetic processes which occur close
to the event horizon of black holes. DTU Space is currently working on the development of mirrors for
astrophysical purposes, focusing on the ATHENA mission.
This thesis will investigate the accuracy in X-ray reflectometry characterization of thin-film coatings. Sev-
eral reflectance models will be created and analyzed in order to evaluate the effect of the coating properties
on the performance of the X-ray telescopes.
X-ray reflectivity data obtained from three different beamlines of an substrate based on ESA’s Silicon Pore
Optics coated with a single layer of Iridium will be fitted in IMD and afterwards finding the minimum figure-
of-merit from the parameter sampling to investigate the accuracy in X-ray reflectometry characterization of
the Iridium coating. The characterization resulted in a large variation of best-fit values for the parameters,
which lead to an investigation of the uncertainties in the X-ray reflectivity data.
The introduction of active subspaces, a tool that identifies important directions in the parameter space, may
yield in parameter analysis that otherwise was infeasible. By evaluating these uncertainties in the parameters
describing the mirrors, it is possible to optimize the design process and the development of X-ray mirrors
used for such space telescopes.

1
Introduction
In 1948, the first X-rays where observed from the Sun’s outermost atmosphere, the corona, using rock-
ets from the second world war. This sprouted a branch of astronomy known as the X-ray astronomy [1].
X-ray radiation is emitted from astronomical objects, which contains hot gasses at temperatures between
106-100·106 K. Astronomical objects emitting such radiation could be stars, supernova remnant, binary
systems, or black holes. To understand the physics behind X-ray generation processes within these objects,
one needs to utilize both X-ray imaging and spectroscopy.
As the astronomical X-rays have energies between 0.1 keV and 500.0 keV, they are easily absorbed and
cannot penetrate the Earth’s atmosphere. To observe X-rays from astronomical objects it is, therefore, nec-
essary to be above 99% of the atmosphere [2]. After the discovery of the X-ray radiation from the Sun’s
corona in 1948, multiple rocket-borne missions were carried out to investigate if there were other X-ray
sources in the Universe.
The first X-ray telescope was launched into space in 1978 by NASA to observe X-ray sources from astro-
nomical objects. This telescope was the Einstein satellite designed to observe X-rays in the energy range
of 0.1 keV to 4.0 keV. Since the launch of the first X-ray telescope, the knowledge about X-rays and the
technology has improved, making it possible to design X-ray telescopes for different purposes.
As X-rays are reflected from small grazing angles, a special design for the telescope mirrors needs to be cre-
ated, to reflect and focus the incoming X-ray radiation. In 1952 the German physicist Hans Wolter presented
three different designs that would reflect X-ray photons.
• Type I: A paraboloid mirror followed by a hyperboloid mirror
• Type II: A paraboloid mirror followed by a hyperboloid mirror
• Type II: A paraboloid mirror followed by an ellipsoid mirror
The three different Wolter designs can be seen in Figure 1.1.

The Wolter Type I is the most important design used for X-ray telescopes, as it is a simple configuration and
has an advantage compared to the two other designs. The Wolter Type I design allows for nesting of several
telescopes inside one another and makes it possible to increase the effective area. As X-ray sources are in
general faint it is crucial to maximize the mirrors ability to gather light. [3, 4].

2.3
Bragg’s Law
Theoretically, the Iridium thin-film consists of atoms in a crystal lattice structure, as seen in Figure 2.3.
Figure 2.3: Schematic of X-rays scattering from different atoms in a crystal lattice. The X-rays A and
A’ impinge the sample at different, B and B’, where they will get scattered into X-rays C and C’. For the
scattered X-rays to undergo constructive interference, the path difference must be equal to nλ
The atoms in the crystal lattice are separated by the distance d. When X-rays strike the Iridium layer, they
will get scattered from the atoms in the crystal lattice at an angle equal to the incidence grazing angle. The
X-rays A and A′ strikes the crystal with an incidence angle of θ and a path difference of 2d sin(θ), which
allows them to undergo constructive interference. The two X-rays are reflected in B and B′ with the angle
equal to the incident angle and a path difference of 2d sin(θ), creating the scattered X-rays C and C′.
Bragg’s law describes the condition of constructive interference, and is given by
2d sin(θ) = nλ
(2.9)
When the length 2d sin(θ) is equal to an integer, n, multiplied with the wavelength, constructive interference
occurs. When this is fulfilled, Bragg peaks can be observed as inference patterns above the critical angle.

2.5
Imperfections in the Thin-film and Substrate
In an ideal world, the interface between the substrate and the single layer or between the bi-layers would
be sharp and smooth. Unfortunately, this is not how the real world works. The imperfections at the surface
and the interface can cause scattering in non-specular directions, leading to attenuation of the specular re-
flectivity of the incident X-rays.
The imperfections at the interface and the surface can be classified into two different categories based on
the shape and texture: either roughness and diffusion.
Roughness, σr, refers to the type of imperfection where the interface between two different materials con-
tains sharp height variation. However, if the interface does not contain these sharp height variations, but
instead is fused at the interface, meaning there is a gradual transition from one material to the other, the
imperfection is called diffusion, σd.
Figure 2.4: Illustration of (left) an ideal interface, (center) a rough interface, and (right) a diffuse interface.

in the intensity. The reduction of the intensity is due to the density contrast being reduced at the interface,
this is illustrated in the right panel of Figure 2.4 [13].

Simulated Reflectance Models
This chapter will include a description of the parameters, such as thin-film thickness, thin-film roughness,
substrate roughness, and incident angles of the X-rays, which affects the reflectance, and must be taken into
account when designing and fabricating mirrors for X-ray telescopes.
The coating for ATHENA’s mirrors has yet to be determined. However Iridium, Platinum, Ir/B4C, and Ir/SiC
have been proposed as candidates [14, 15, 6, 16]. It is crucial that the telescope can operate in the energy
range of 0.3-12 keV, and at the same time being able to reflect incoming X-rays at a wide grazing angles
range. The reflectance of the mirror coating highly depends on the material, and the thickness, roughness,
and density of the material.
To get a better understanding of which parameters affects the reflectivity, two models have been created in
the IDL software package, IMD; a single layer model and a bi-layer model, illustrated in Figure 3.1. The
IMD software package can be used to calculate specular and non-specular optical functions of an arbitrary
single layer, bi-layer, or multi-layer structure. IMD can be used for modeling, instrument design, and data
analysis [17].
The IMD package in the IDL software can be used for modelling of optical properties, such as reflectance,
transmittance, etc., for single layers, bi-layers, and multi-layers. The optical constants of each material
used to build the reflectivity models are chosen from the IMD optical constant database, containing over
150 different materials. IMD, however, also allows the option of choosing user-defined optical constants.
The optical functions, in this case the specular reflectivity, is calculated in IMD using the modified Fresnel
coefficients described in Section 2.4, which takes the interface widths and interface profile function into
account. In the following section, the optical functions will be calculated against the wavelength or the
incident grazing angle [18].

3.4
Summary
The effect on the reflectance has been demonstrated for two single layer models and two bi-layer models.
For the single layer models, it is clear to see that a lower thin-film thickness shifts the reflectance curve
towards the higher angles while for the energies the reflectance becomes slightly larger at higher energies.
In contrast a higher thin-film thickness shifts the reflectance curve towards the lower angles and energies.
Decreasing the roughness for both the thin-film and the substrate will increase the reflectance at higher an-
gles, while increasing the roughness of the thin-film and the substrate will decrease the reflectance at higher
angles for the XRR measurements taken with respect to the incident angle. For the XXR measurement taken
with the respect to the energy, varying the thin-film and substrate roughness does not affect the reflectance
much, however, the most significant effect is from the thin-film roughness. What will have an impact on the
reflectance is the incident angle of the X-rays. A smaller incident angle will increase the reflectance while
a higher incident angle will decrease the reflectance.
From the bi-layer, it was shown that varying the low Z-material does not change the reflectance much, as it is
invisible for 8.048 keV X-rays. Varying the roughness of the thin-film of high Z-materials and the substrate
will have the same effect on the reflectance as for the single layer. Decreasing or increasing the roughness
for the thin-film of low Z-materials will not affect the reflectance much for the XRR measurement at a fixed
energy.
The reflectance decreases slightly when varying the thin-film of the high Z-material, the thin-film of low
Z-materials, and substrate roughness. The most significant effect on the reflectance can again be seen for
the roughness of the thin-film of high Z-materials. A smaller incident angle will increase the reflectance
while a higher incident angle will decrease the reflectance.

4.1
DC Magnetron Sputter Chamber
As described in Section 1.1, the mirror modules of the ATHNEA mission are based on SPO technology to
focus the incoming X-ray photons. Initially, the SPO consists of a 300 mm Silicon wafer that is oxidized.
The wafer is cut into ∼60×60 mm pieces before they are ribbed on the backside. A thin wedge is deposited
on the wafer to ensure that the reflecting surfaces are arranged in a radial pattern when stacked. This provides
in-plane focus of the X-ray photons [20]. Photoresist is applied to the reflecting surface. Using a mask with
the desired striped pattern, it is possible to cure the photoresist with a UV lamp. The preferred coating is
deposited on the reflecting surface of the Silicon wafer. The cured photoresist with the coating on top is
removed with a chemical lift-off process, creating the striped coating with 0.17 mm ribs on the coating.
With the pattern on the reflecting surface, it is possible to stack the SPOs and form the optical module of
Wolter-I configuration and its approximations [21]. The process for a single SPO is shown in Appendix B.
The SPO sample that will be investigated and characterized in this thesis is coated with a 100 Å layer of Ir.
The SPO sample was coated the 22nd of February 2018 in the coating facility, known as the Multilab, placed
in DTU Space at the Technical University of Denmark. The coating facility, seen in Figure 4.1a, consists of
a bell-shaped vacuum chamber where four DC magnetron sputtering cathodes of the size 500 mm × 38 mm
are placed. Having four cathodes makes it possible to deposit up to four different coatings in one coating
run. The coating is sputtered in an outward direction towards the walls of the chamber, where the substrate
is placed. The substrates are mounted horizontally to plates, called sample holders, which are placed on a
rotating ring. During a coating run, it is possible to deposit up to four different substrates in one coating run.

The sputtering process that is utilized in the Multilab at DTU Space is the DC magnetron sputtering. The
target material used to coat SPO 217-08 is Iridium, which is placed on one of the four cathodes. To achieve
a highly pure coating, the chamber is pumped down to ∼10−6 mTorr to remove unwanted particles that
otherwise would cause imperfections in the coating. After the evacuation of the chamber, it is filled with
inert gas, Argon. Permanent magnets are installed behind the target, and when adding a negative electrical
potential to the cathode, a magnetic field is produced in front of the target. For the DC magnetron sputtering
of the sample SPO 217-08, the power added to the cathode is 300 W. The magnetic field traps the electrons
over the negatively charged target material, forcing them to move back and forth along the magnetic field
lines instead of being attracted toward the substrate.
The neutral Argon gas is drawn towards the magnetrons, where they will collide with the moving negative
charged electron, ionizing the Aron atom. The ionization occurs close to the magnetic field, where the
positively charged Argon ions are accelerated towards the target with kinetic energy. The kinetic energy of
the ions is larger than the binding energy of the target material, leading to a collision between the positively
charged ions and the target. This results in atoms being ejected from the target material and getting sputtered
on the substrate as a thin film. The ionized Argon recombines with a free electron, transforming it back to
a neutral Argon atom. During recombination, more energy is transferred to the ion from the electron than
needed, which results in a plasma glow. Due to the trapped electrons in the magnetic field, a strong and
dense concentration of plasma is generated in front of the target. To sustain the plasma, the desired pressure
in the coating chamber should be approximately 3 mTorr [23, 24].

5.2
XRR Data-Fitting Using the IMD Software
Besides being able to calculate the specular reflectivity curve for theoretical models, described in Section 3,
the IMD software also allows the user to import measured reflectivity data and fit the data with the different
uncertainty models. The IMD software makes it possible for the user to fit a reflectivity curve with up to
eight adjustable parameters simultaneously. Since the XRR data are measurements of the reflectivity as a
function of the incidence grazing angle or the photon energy, which are independent variables, they must
be chosen suitably. IMD fits the reflectance using the calculated uncertainty and the ranges for the Iridium
thickness, the Iridium roughness, and the substrate roughness specified by the user.
The model used for the fitting is a 100 Å thick Iridium layer with a roughness of 4.5 Å on top of a SiO2
substrate with a substrate roughness of 4.5 Å. The fitting of the sample using the different uncertainties was
made in the following ranges:
• zIr: 80-120 Å
• σIr/SiO2: 0-20 Å
• σSiO2: 0-20 Å
The IMD software package includes four different fitting algorithms, that applies each their mathematical
method to determine the best-fit parameters. From the different fitting algorithms the Differential Evolution
(DE) algorithm has been chosen to fit the data, as it has been proved to converge to a global minimum the
fastest and with the best accuracy [30]. The DE algorithm evolves around two populations for each gener-

5.3
IMD Fitting of Sample SPO 217-08
As mentioned before, XRR measurements of thin-film samples can be used to determine the different pa-
rameters of the sample, such as the thin-film thickness, the thin-film roughness, and the substrate roughness.
Comparing the model to the actual data, can tell if there has been an overestimation or underestimation. For
the thickness of the material, an overestimation will occur if the Kiessig fringes are more narrow, and shifted
towards the left, compared to the XRR measurement. An underestimation of the thickness will be seen as
wider fringes and a shift of the fringes towards the right, as illustrated in Figure 3.2a and 3.2c [31].
The amplitude of the fringes contains information about the thin-film roughness. If the amplitude of the
fringes is lower than for the XRR data, an overestimation of the thin-film roughness has been made. Like-
wise, if the amplitude of the fringes is higher than the XRR data, it has been underestimated. The substrate
roughness will affect the oscillation of the Kiessig fringes. For a low substrate roughness the fringes will
be well-defined, while for a high substrate roughness the fringes will disappear, as illustrated in Figure 3.2b
and 3.2d [31].

6
Parameter Estimation Using an Even
Spaced Sampling Method
In the previous chapter, the XRR data from DTU Space and BESSY II was fitted using the IMD software.
The IMD software package does not provide uncertainty on the best-fit parameters, however, it does give
the evolution of each parameter, making it possible to determine if the parameters converge or not.
In this chapter, an even spaced sampling for the parameters will be carried out in order to fit the three
parameters. The best-fit from the parameter sampling will be used to compute reflectance curves, which
will be compared to the XRR measurements. This parameter sampling will later be used to estimate the
uncertainties in the parameters.
6.1
Even Spaced Parameter Sampling
In this case, the model to be fitted is relatively simple, a single layer of Iridium on a SiO2 substrate, with
only three fitting parameters. Therefore, the even spaced sampling algorithm has been chosen to estimate
the values of the parameters of the SPO 217-08 sample. The even spaced sampling algorithm works in such
way, that it evaluates every possible combination of the parameters - even if it is a bad fit.
The parameter sampling for the three different XRR datasets from DTU Space and from BESSY II were
calculated using the even spaced sampling algorithm in the following intervals:
• z: 90 - 110 Å, with a step-size of 0.5 Å
• σIr: 2 - 8 Å, with a step-size of 0.15 Å
• σSiO2: 2 - 8 Å, with a step-size of 0.15 Å
Since the wavelength of the X-ray beam used for the XRR measurements is of a similar scale to the Iridium
thickness, it is possible to get a direct measurement of the Iridium thickness. For this reason, the step-size
of the sampling of the Iridium thickness should not be less than 0.5 Å.
Starting with the initial value for the Iridium thickness, the Iridium roughness, and the substrate, IDL calcu-
lates the reflectivity curve of the single layer model. To calculate the reflectivity curve for the given values
from the even spaced sampling. IDL utilizes the modified Fresnel coefficients from IMD and the given
angle and energy ranges. To evaluate the goodness of the fit, the computed reflectivity curve is compared
with the XRR data, and a FOM is calculated.

7
Uncertainty Estimation of Fit Parameters
using Active Subspaces
As shown in Chapter 5 and Chapter 6, XRR data is difficult to fit. Due to the large variation in the fitting
parameters, it would be beneficial to estimate the uncertainty in the best-fit values. In several articles, the
uncertainty is calculated for simulated XRR data using a MCMC developed by NIST [33, 32].
This chapter will have a brief explanation of the active subspace algorithm, which will be used to find the
important directions in the input space, investigate the correlation between the parameters, and estimate the
uncertainties using the result from the parameter sampling in Chapter 6 as input.
7.1
Active Subspace
The IMD software fits with the DE algorithm which provides the rate of convergence. As the IMD fitting
converges to a global minimum fast, it is not possible to use the evolution of the parameters for the uncer-
tainty analysis. On the other hand, the even spaced algorithm evaluates all possible parameter combinations
and calculates the FOM. This does not show if the parameters converge, but gives an estimate of the best-fit
parameters.
In order to estimate the uncertainties, the active subspace algorithm will be implemented. The active sub-
space is a tool used to identify the linear combinations of the input parameters which affects the output
when insignificant modifications are made. The linear combinations are found from the gradients between
the input parameters and the quantity of interest, expressed by the eigenvectors. The eigenvalues define
the quantity of interest changes in response to small fluctuations in the input space along the corresponding
eigenvector. While finding the important directions in the input space, the active subspace can also be used
to estimate the uncertainties in the parameters.
Methods already known for parameter studies, such as uncertainty quantification, are not effective as higher
dimensions of parameters are limited by the computation. It is possible to reduce the dimension of the
parameters to be investigated by ignoring inputs that have no significance on the result of the parameter
analysis. However, this can only be done if these inputs are known. If they are unknown, ”active subspaces”
is a method that can be used to reduce the dimensions of parameters.
Active subspaces reduce the dimension of the parameters by classifying a set of essential directions in the
input space. Any direction is a set of weights that define a linear combination of the inputs. If the simulation’s
prediction does not change with the inputs in a particular direction, the direction can be ignored in the
parameter analysis.

The sampling density, ρ, is used for for the dimension reduction. The dimension reduction is based on ran-

Thirdly, as the active subspace model is a low-dimensional model, one must consider how many dimensions
one is working with. The largest acceptable dimension is given by k, and is one greater than the number of
input parameters, k = 1 + m. However, to save computational time, k should be chosen to be less than m.
Lastly, an oversampling factor, α, is needed for the random sampling algorithm. To get a better fit of the
model, it is better to work with a model that has between 2m and 10m evaluations. As ρ, k, and α are
defined, it is possible to proceed to the computation.

at any x in the input parameter space, as the active subspace is derived from the gradient [34].

Conclusion
The aim of this project was to investigate the accuracy in X-ray reflectometry characterization of thin-film
coatings. The motivation for this is that DTU Space and ESA are working on finding a coating combination
for the mirrors of the ATHENA mission.
As the reflectance of the mirrors highly depends on the coating combination, the thin-film thickness, the thin-
film roughness, and the substrate roughness, different coating combinations were investigated in Chapter
3. It was proven that the reflectance mostly is affected by the thickness of high Z-material, the thin-film
roughness, and the incident angle of X-rays for XRR measurements taken with respect to the energy. To
increase the reflectance at both low and higher angles or energies, a bi-layer coating can be used for the
mirrors.
One of the proposed coating candidates is a single layer of Iridium. A SPO substrate coated with a single
layer of Iridium was produced at DTU Space to investigate the properties of the sample. This was per-
formed by multiple XRR measurements performed at DTU Space and the BESSY II facility. Four XRR
measurements were performed with respect to the incident angle, and three XRR measurements were per-
formed with respect to the energy. The four XRR measurements with respect to the incident angle should
be identical, yet measurements of the same sample were not identical. The differences between the XRR
measurements performed at DTU Space and BESSY II, with respect to the incident angle mainly originates
from the different X-ray set-ups.
The XRR data from DTU Space includes a few uncertainties which can be corrected. On of the uncertainties
that could be corrected, was the angular off-set of 0.02◦, which is corrected by manually subtracting the off-
set from each element in the angle array. The large fringe amplitude originates from the size of the slit and
can be corrected by changing the slit size. And lastly, the statistical fluctuations due to the low integration
time can also be corrected. The difference between the three XRR measurements with respect to the incident
angle taken at the BESSY II facility could originate from e.g. human errors or limitations of the equipment.
While the difference in the XRR data for the three measurements with respect to the energy, are caused by
misalignment of the sample along with limitations of the detector.
With all the uncertainties taken into account, the XRR data were fitted in the IMD software with different
errors to determine the Iridium thickness, the Iridium roughness, and the substrate roughness. The loga-
rithmic fitting showed clear improvements on the best-fit values compared to the linear fitting for the XRR
data with respect to the incident angle. However, a variation of a few Å was present in the best-fit values
obtained from the linear and logarithmic IMD fitting with different errors included. The most significant
variation was found for the 8.048 keV XRR data from DTU Space, followed by the 8.048 keV XRR data
from BESSY II and the 0.6◦XRR data from BESSY II. From this, it can be concluded that XRR data with
noise is difficult to characterize.
An even spaced parameter sampling was introduced, which would later be used to estimate the uncertainties
in the best-fit values of the Iridium thickness, the Iridium roughness, and the substrate roughness. From
the parameter sampling, it was possible to create a posterior distribution which could be used to find the
best-fit value that minimizes the FOM. For the sampled parameters based on the 8.048 keV XRR data from
DTU Space, it was only possible to constrain the Iridium thickness. Both the Iridium thickness and Iridium
roughness was constrained for the sampled parameters based on the 8.048 keV XRR data from BESSY II.

As the posterior distributions and parameter evaluations also give variations in the best-fit value, the ac-

estimate the uncertainties. It was possible to identify the Iridium thickness as the important parameter, fol-

a uniformly distributed input space due to the time constraint of the thesis, it was not possible to estimate
any uncertainties using the active subspace method.
Thus, a weighted co-variance matrix was calculated for the parameter samplings based on the 8.048 keV
XRR data from DTU Space and the parameter sampling based on the 0.6◦XRR data from BESSY II. The
standard deviation was calculated from the weighted co-variance matrix and gave a much better estimate of
the uncertainties that the active subspace analysis.
This leads to further research with the active subspace analysis in order to fully estimate the uncertainties
of the parameters.

Outlook
The initial goal of this thesis was to determine the accuracy in the fitting of XRR data. It was proven that
for a simple single layer model, the best-fit value of the Iridium thickness, the Iridium roughness, and the
substrate roughness would vary based on which fitting method and error in the data is used.
This thesis investigated the accuracy in fitting a simple model, a single layer model, by IMD and by finding
the minimum FOM from the parameter sampling. An even spaced parameter sampling was performed for
the single layer model, which will evaluate all possible combinations of the parameters in the given range.
This is an acceptable approach dealing with a simple model consisting of only three fitting parameters. Yet,
when working with bi-layers or multi-layers with N layers, this method is not efficient as it will need a lot
of computation time. To solve this problem, a different parameter sampling is required, such as an MCMC
sampling.
As a part of this thesis, the active subspace algorithm was implemented to estimate the uncertainties of the
input parameters. From the active subspace analysis, it was possible to find the directions in the parameter
space, which significantly impacted the reflectance when insignificant changes are made. The active sub-
space analysis allows estimating the standard deviation of the parameters from the best-fit value. However,
due to the rough sampling of the natural input space and the fact that the normalized input space was not
uniformly distributed, it was not possible to obtain any standard deviations which make physical sense. For
further investigation of the active subspace code, it is crucial that the natural input space is uniformly dis-
tributed and that the normalized input space is uniformly distributed around zero. It has not been possible
to compute uniformly distributed data due to time constraints on the thesis. Yet, when this is possible, it
would give a more accurate estimation of the natural standard deviations of the parameters.
It was proven that the best-fit was obtained from the IMD fitting, which uses the
FOM =
∑w[i]∗| log(Yfit[i])−log(Ymodel[i])|2
∑w[i]
. It is not possible to change this FOM in IMD which is used to
evaluate each parameter combination of the parameter sampling with the XRR data. As an ongoing project
at DTU Space, an algorithm in Matlab utilizing the differential evolution is being written. The Matlab code
aims to fit XRR data in the same manner as in the IMD software, where it is possible to change the FOM.
This makes it possible to fit the XRR data with different FOM and evaluate which FOM to use for the active
subspace analysis, to achieve a more accurate estimation of the standard deviation of the parameters.
The active subspace analysis is highly dependent on the input space, the error in the data, and the FOM. Once
it is possible to estimate standard deviations, it would be interesting to investigate if the most significant
direction in the input space changes or if there is variation in the standard deviations by using different
FOMs.
To solve the problem occurring with increasing number of dimensions, the active subspace analysis can
be used to estimate the important directions in bi- and multi-layers. When the important directions in the
input space is determined, it is possible to execute the parameter sampling focused around the important
directions.
