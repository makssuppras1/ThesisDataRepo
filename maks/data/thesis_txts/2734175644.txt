Abstract 
The impact of the indoor environment on the health, productivity, and well-being of 
occupants is well-documented, but primarily based on research involving neurotypical 
individuals. However, with the increasing prevalence of individuals with autism in the 
population, it has become crucial to explore their unique sensitivity to indoor climate 
influences, which may differ from neurotypical individuals. Despite the growing prevalence 
of this demographic limited evidence-based data on this subject exist. 
The SENSEwellbeing project addresses this gap by conducting field studies and living lab 
campaigns to gather evidence-based data on the effects of various indoor climate parameters 
on neurodivergent individuals diagnosed with autism. This report focuses on the Living Lab 
Campaign and investigates the impact of four indoor environmental domains: thermal, visual, 
indoor air quality, and acoustics. 
The study involved a case group of 10 individuals diagnosed with autism and a control group 
of 14 neurotypical individuals. Three identical experimental rooms, located at the Technical 
University of Denmark were designed with experiments carried out from October to December 
2023. The three rooms were designed with a Predicted Mean Vote (PMV) of 1 (26.5°C), 0 
(22.5 °C) and -1 (18.5 °C). The visual environment was manipulated, exposing participants to 
variations in daylight and artificial light with white, blue, and yellow colour sources during 
each experimental session. 
The results show that the case group and the control group shared similar neutral temperature 
preferences, registering 22.5°C. When exposed to thermal conditions deviating from their 
neutral temperature, individuals within the case group exhibited greater dissatisfaction 
compared to the control group. Despite a similar operation of Predicted Mean Vote, the 
Percentage of People Dissatisfied (PPD) was higher among those with autism. Both groups 
showed more satisfaction with the visual environment when natural light was present compared 
to environments illuminated only by artificial light. The case group indicated sensitivity to 
colour changes, while the control group were more influenced by the session type, whether it 
was natural light or artificial light. The multi-domain effects on the case group revealed 
stronger associations in the four domains compared to the control group aligning with former 
research on heightened sensory sensitivities in adults with autism.

1. Introduction 
1.1 Motivation 
Scientific evidence indicates that the indoor environment holds the potential to affect 
occupants’ health, productivity, and psychological well-being (D'Ambrosio, 2020). However, 
most of the comprehension of how people perceive the indoor climate is primarily based on 
research involving healthy and neurotypical individuals (Zaniboni & Toftum, 2023). 
Individuals with autism are becoming an increasingly significant segment of the population, 
and their sensitivity to the influences of the indoor climate can differ from neurotypical people 
(Cecilia Brynskov, 2023). The increase in people with autism is more probable to be caused by 
the rise in the number of diagnoses rather than an actual increase in the prevalence of people 
with autism (Cecilia Brynskov, 2023).  
Due to their varying response to environmental stimuli, such as heightened or reduced 
sensitivity, individuals on the autism spectrum may have unique requirements regarding 
temperature and the influences stemming from atmospheric, acoustic, and visual factors. The 
effects of the indoor climate, including the light intensity and thus the production of melatonin 
in the body, can create disturbances in the circadian rhythm and potentially lead to sleep 
problems and stress, which ultimately affects repetitive behaviour patterns during the day 
(Tønnies, 2018). 
Although neurodivergent individuals generally exhibit a diverse sensitivity to the 5-senses 
stimuli, there is almost no insight into how individuals on the autism spectrum experience and 
respond to the impact of indoor climate influences (Zaniboni & Toftum, 2023). The significant 
increase in individuals on the spectrum has posed a range of new challenges and potential 
demands for a healthy and comfortable indoor climate, which may vary from existing indoor 
environmental guidelines. 
This lack of knowledge has motivated SENSEwellbeing, an EU-funded research project, to 
investigate and analyse the correlations between the indoor climate and the overall well-being 
experienced among individuals with Autism Spectrum Disorder (ASD). SENSEwellbeing aims 
to explore the indoor climate for adults with ASD, with a particular focus on the combined 
effects of thermal and visual perception. The project will involve individuals from 18 years old 
and above with ASD and encompasses the evaluation of indoor environmental conditions in 
laboratories at DTU.

Page 13 of 148
 
The experiments performed in this study will primarily concentrate on autonomous individuals 
with autism, alongside a control group composed of individuals of similar ages and genders, 
who will be subjected to controlled indoor environmental conditions during a Living Lab 
Campaign (LLC) located at DTU Campus. Throughout the LLC, subjective assessments 
pertaining to indoor comfort will be conducted, along with physiological and environmental 
measurements. These will be analysed to determine potential correlations between adults with 
ASD and indoor comfort in comparison to neurotypical individuals. 
2. Background 
2.1 The Autism Spectrum  
ASD is a collective term for developmental disorders used to describe a constellation of early-
onset social communication deficits and repetitive sensory-motor behaviours. It is also referred 
to as a pervasive developmental disorder (Merete Juul Sørensen, 2022). The umbrella 
diagnosis, which includes several previously distinct conditions including autistic disorder, 
Asperger syndrome, childhood disintegrative disorder, and pervasive developmental disorder 
not otherwise specified, was for the first time suggested as a collective term by Sanchak 
(Kristian E. Sanchack, 2016). Individuals with ASD often have difficulty understanding social 
nuances and may perceive things in a very literal and factual manner. ASD is characterized by 
reduced abilities in social communication and interaction, a tendency toward restricted and 
repetitive behaviours, intense and narrowly focused interests, heightened sensitivity to sensory 
stimuli, and in some cases, challenging behaviour. The behaviour of individuals with ASD 
varies significantly, and this spectrum of variation depends on factors such as age, cognitive 
skills, developmental level, and environmental circumstances.  
What is now identified as autism can be traced back to the 1930s by the Austrian child 
psychiatrist Hans Aspergers. However, the diagnosis itself is relatively recent and has been 
evolving over an extended period. (Autismeforeningen, 2017). Autism was subsequently 
described by psychologist Leo Kanner in 1943 as a disorder in children who experienced 
difficulties in social relationships and high sensitivity to changes in their surrounding 
environment. At that time, autism was considered a rare condition, even though its prevalence 
steadily increased (Kristian E. Sanchack, 2016).  
Over the past 60 years, ASD has transitioned from being a narrowly defined disorder in children 
to becoming a widely recognised lifelong condition with emotional connections and more 
widespread screening methods that have been extensively studied (Autismeforeningen, 2017). 
==PAGE:14==
 
 
 
Page 14 of 148
 
The characteristics of ASD, including social communication deficits and repetitive sensory-
motor behaviours, have not significantly changed since their initial description (Catherine 
Lord, 2020). 
Since the early 1990s, there has been a substantial rise in autism cases. While in the 1980s, 
only 6 out of 10,000 individuals, approximately 0.05% of the population, had received an 
autism diagnosis, whereas the percentage of diagnosed, individuals has now increased to 
approximately 1%, equivalent to 100 out of 10,000 people. Research has explored the reasons 
behind this significant increase, whether it results from a real rise in the number of individuals 
with autism or simply improved detection and diagnosis (Cecilia Brynskov, 2023).  
Nowadays, the prospects for individuals on the autism spectrum appear considerably more 
optimistic than they did merely half a century ago. Children and teenagers with autism 
spectrum disorder often express anger and tears as a reaction to frustration. This is because 
they may struggle to comprehend that other people can feel and think differently from 
themselves. A greater number of individuals with this condition can live in ordinary residential 
settings rather than in institutions, and some may even experience relatively few symptoms in 
adulthood. However, many individuals will still face challenges when it comes to living 
independently or working full-time in a well-functioning job (Catherine Lord, 2020). Within 
adults diagnosed with ASD, individuals exhibiting mild symptoms within the spectrum may 
have varying characteristics or even an absence of identifiable traits. This is particularly 
prominent among those categorized at severity level 1 in Table 1. 
2.2 The Autism Spectrum in Adulthood 
Estimates vary, but research indicates that between 10-33% of adults with ASD use no more 
than simple sentences and have verbal and non-verbal IQs in the range of intellectual disability, 
requiring significant support. Most adults with ASD and intellectual disabilities can 
communicate at some level, manage basic needs, and have the capacity to work, but they 
require daily support. Premature mortality is increased, primarily used by individuals with 
lower intellectual abilities and women (mostly due to congenital abnormalities and 
neurological disorders), but also among more capable individuals with comorbid diagnoses 
(Catherine Lord, 2020). 
An American research study that included a community sample – a non-clinical sample - 30% 
of the adult participants who were diagnosed with ASD as children and had average or higher 
intelligence no longer displayed obvious ASD traits. The symptoms were mild, or they

Bryan H. King, 2023). Regarding independence among the study participants, only about 25%

2.3 Severity 
ASD consists of several symptoms that vary in severity from mild to substantial. The 
Diagnostic and Statistical Manual of Mental Disorders sets out three severity levels for Autism 
Spectrum Disorder in Table 1 (American Phychiatric Association, 2022). ASD spans a 
continuum from requiring relatively minimal support to needing extensive support (Tomoya 
Hirota & Bryan H. King, 2023). Level 1 implies minimal support and reduced social interest. 
Interaction in groups is often limited and is often seen as a failure. An individual at this stage 
can communicate in complete sentences and engage in discussions with others. Level 1 
represents the least severe of the three levels. Level 2 involves deficits in communication skills 
and very limited social interactions. A person at this level speaks in simple sentences and the 
person's interests are often directed towards narrow niche areas. A person in level 3 has serious 
deficits in verbal and non-verbal social communication skills and very rarely initiates social 
interaction. People with Level 3 autism need ongoing support in everyday life (Kristian E. 
Sanchack, 2016).

2.4 Exploration of Autism and Indoor Environment 
People with ASD frequently encounter sensory processing differences referred to as hyper- and 
hyposensitivity. Hyposensitivity implies that a person within the spectrum has decreased 
sensitivity to sensory stimuli like sounds, light or touch compared to individuals without 
autism. Hypersensitive signifies an increased sensitivity to sensory stimuli, leading to a 
stronger response to the same stimuli. For instance, a study illustrates how a range of visual 
sensory symptoms is experienced among individuals with hypo- and hypersensitivity. 
Symptoms expressed in hypersensitive individuals include actions such as covering their eyes 
in bright light, looking down most of the time, disliking sharp flashes of light, and disliking too 
dark and bright colours, while hyposensitive individuals express symptoms like fascination 
with reflections and brightly coloured objects, looking intensely at objects or people, and being 
attracted to light. Variations of sensitivity are similar seen within acoustics, air quality, and 
temperatures. Reports and guidelines have been written to elucidate the need for creating a 
suitable indoor environment for people on the autistic spectrum and consider the sensitivity of

2.5 Danish Indoor Climate Design Standards 
In the industrialized world, human beings spend most of their lives indoors, roughly around 
90% (Katsoyiannis & Cincinelli, 2019). Our daily routines revolve within the four walls of 
apartments, offices, and various institutions. The quality of the time we spend in these enclosed 
spaces affects our overall well-being, health, and performance. (Sedlbauer, 2009). In the 
Danish Building Regulations, specific requirements for indoor environments are established 
based on the type of building and its intended use. Different criteria apply to schools, 
institutions, residences, and commercial buildings.   
Several factors impact our indoor experience including air quality, thermal-, acoustic-, and 
visual comfort. People's perception of the indoor environment can be quantified using metrics 
such as the Predicted Mean Vote (PMV) and Predicted Percentage of Dissatisfied (PPD).  
PMV is a numerical scale that predicts the mean value of the thermal sensation votes of a group 
of people who are exposed to a certain environment. The scale typically ranges from –3 to +3, 
where –3 indicates cold sensation, 0 indicates neutral sensation and +3 indicates hot sensation. 
(DS/ISO 7730, 2006). PMV is influenced by various factors, including activity level, clothing 
insulation, air temperature, mean radiant temperature, mean air speed, and relative humidity. 
The PPD is an index that establishes a quantitative prediction of the thermally dissatisfied 
people who feel too warm or cold. Both metrics provide insights of the expected percentage of 
individuals who may feel either too cold or too hot.   
DS/EN ISO 7730:2006 (DS/ISO 7730, 2006), categorises the quality of the indoor thermal 
climate into three distinct groups related to the level of expectations the occupants might have. 
• Category A meets high expectation levels. 
• Category B meets a medium expectation level.  
• Category C meets a moderate expectation level.  
Category B, representing a medium expectation level, corresponds to a standard level, while 
Category A might be selected for occupants with special needs (children, elderly, disabled). 
However, a lower level is not associated with health risks but might reduce comfort (DS/ISO 
7730, 2006).

When illuminating various spaces and buildings, there are regulations that cover both natural 
daylight and artificial lighting. The Danish Building Regulation, BR18, outlines requirements 
for the daylight. Sufficient daylight can be documented by ensuring that the window glass area 
corresponds to at least 10% of the floor area. Alternatively, it can be substantiated by proving 
that a minimum of 300 lux of daylight pervades at least half of the room during half of the 
daylight hours.  
==PAGE:21==

DS/EN 12464-1 (DS/EN, 2021) establishes standards for artificial lighting in different types of 
buildings. This standard defines design criteria for workplace lighting in offices and 
educational facilities like classrooms, which require, for instance, an illuminance level of 500 
lux on desks and whiteboards, while at least 100 lux in the rest of the room.  
The Danish Working Environment Authority does not impose any legal requirements for 
humidity in buildings but recommends between 25-60 % relative humidity distributed over the 
hot and cold months of the year (Rubin, 2022). Likewise, the National Research Centre for the 
Working Environment (NRCWE) recommends that humidity in buildings should be kept 
between 40-60 % to reduce discomfort in the airways and eyes as well as the number of 
airborne particles and infectious influenza virus in indoor air (Wolkoff, 2022). 
Due to heightened sensitivity to environmental stimuli, individuals with autism may have 
different specific needs influenced by temperature, visual conditions, atmospheric elements, 
and acoustic surroundings. However, current standards and guidelines for indoor climate are 
mainly based on average neurotypical individuals and only very limited knowledge exists 
describing the effect of indoor climate on the comfort and well-being of individuals on the 
autism spectrum.  
2.6 Research Questions  
Studies show the need to conduct laboratory studies and perform measurements that consider 
the effect and needs of indoor climate on people with autism.   
This thesis will thus uncover and describe data that can contribute to a deeper understanding 
of how the indoor climate is experienced and affects the well-being and comfort of autistic 
people with an engineering-focused approach. 
By setting up precise research questions concerning four domains, thermal, visual, indoor air 
quality (IAQ) and acoustic, it is possible to investigate the influence of the indoor environment 
on neurodivergent individuals, diagnosed with autism. The research questions below have been 
developed and should contribute to a representative understanding of this. 
Research question 1: 
How do different thermal conditions influence thermal perception among autistic and 
neurotypical individuals, assessed through thermal sensation voting, satisfaction levels, 
acceptability, preference, and identification of unsatisfactory elements? 
==PAGE:22==
 
 
 
Page 22 of 148
 
• Is there a difference in the perception of neutral temperature between the two 
groups?  
• Are there significant differences in thermal sensation voting, satisfaction, 
acceptability, and preferences for thermal conditions? 
Research question 2: 
How does exposure to natural and artificial lighting and different lighting spectrums of light 
sources affect the visual comfort level of both autistic and neurotypical participants?  
• Are there particular visual stimuli that elicit distinct responses within the two groups? 
• Are there significant differences between the two groups in terms of satisfaction, 
acceptability, and preferences for visual conditions? 
 
Research question 3: 
How do the satisfaction and preference about the four different comfort domains (thermal, 
visual, IAQ and acoustic) correlate with each other for each group?   
• 
How are the thermal environment and the visual satisfaction for the white, blue, and 
yellow colours of light, in both artificial and natural light sessions, associated?  
• 
How do the four comfort domains cross influence each other? 
 
The primary purpose of this project is to investigate and analyse the correlation between indoor 
climate and the overall well-being experienced by individuals with autism. The evidence-based 
data generated in this study expands the understating of the indoor environmental impact on 
individuals with autism and might lead to the development of more adapted indoor climate 
conditions that enhance the well-being of neurodivergent persons. Simultaneously, these 
conditions may have a positive impact on neurotypical individuals.

4. Methodology 
The methodology intends to give the reader an overview of the experiments, including the 
instruments and measuring equipment. Furthermore, the section intends to explain the 
recruitment process, ethical approval, experimental design, and data processing, as well as the 
initial activities and considerations made up to and in connection with the initiation of the 
experiment.  
The study was based on a positivist approach to empirical data, emphasizing measurement and 
quantitative methods (Ahrenkiel, 2022). From a positivist standpoint, observations and data 
collection were conducted using subjective questionnaires and precise quantitative 
measurements within the experimental rooms. This quantitative approach involved gathering 
numerical data for statistical analysis, enabling hypothesis testing, and evaluating the reliability 
and validity of observations and experiences. 
The purpose of randomized controlled experiments was to assess causality and investigate 
whether there was a causal relationship between various variables, including changes in indoor 
climate parameters and the observed effects on the subjects compared to a control group and a 
case group of neurotypical people. The experiment manipulated indoor environmental factors, 
including lighting, daylight, view out, and temperature, to study their impact on participants. 
Both groups experienced the same environmental changes, randomized to ensure variability. 
Quantitative data gathered through ongoing questionnaires, captured participants' subjective 
experiences and preferences related to indoor environments and comfort. Comfort 
questionnaires were prepared in advance according to applicable guidelines and international 
standards, including ISO 28802 (ISO 28802, 2012), which provided an environmental survey 
method for assessing and evaluating comfort and well-being. Structured scales prompted 
assessments of satisfaction, comfort, and discomfort. The questionnaire used as a structured 
method provided insights into how indoor climate parameters influenced participants' 
experiences. Sensory measurements supplemented this approach, collecting precise objective 
data alongside subjective perceptions. Strategically placed sensors in all three experimental 
rooms measured thermal, visual, and acoustic comfort, as well as air quality, following 
standards and guidelines. Participants were equipped with instruments to conduct physiological 
measurements of the impact that the indoor climate may have, using skin temperature and heart 
rate, while participants were during sedentary work. These measurements aimed to enhance 
understanding of subjects' well-being, overall welfare, and stress levels in relation to the 
==PAGE:25==

4.2 Ethical Approval  
To address ethical concerns and protect vulnerable participants, it was ensured that the 
participants were aware of the aim of the study and the experimental procedure. If they still 
agreed to participate, they signed a declaration of consent after receiving this information in 
both verbal and written form. 
The study posed minimal risks and participants were presented with simple cognitive tests and 
questionnaires during the experiments. The various indoor climate parameters, to which they 
were exposed, did not deviate from the participant's daily life and were not classified as 
extreme. The participants were also made aware that they were able to interrupt their 
participation at any time without any consequences, both during the experiment and if they 
chose to withdraw. The study was approved by the scientific ethics committees for Region 
Hovedstaden (Region Hovedstaden - Center for Sundhed, 28-03-2023). 
The storing of personal information required from the participants complied with GDPR 
(General Data Protection Regulation, EU-Regulation 2016/679) rules and ethical principles. 
Data was pseudonymised and encrypted and will be deleted no later than two years after the 
end of the project.

4.9 Questionnaires and Performance Tests  
The participant's performance might depend on environmental and comfort conditions. Hence, 
it is essential to employ a combination of objective and subjective methods to assess workplace 
performance effectively.  
In each session, participants were therefore asked to fill in seven questionnaires (Q), to assess 
their subjective experience of comfort in the given indoor environmental setting and perform 
three performance tests (P). Questionnaires and tests were posed after changes and adjustments 
in the room. The questionnaires used in this specific study were developed as a part of the 
SENSEwellbeing project, in accordance with ISO 10551:2019 standard (ISO 10551, 2019).  
==PAGE:40==
 
 
 
Page 40 of 148
 
4.9.1 Questionnaires 
The questionnaires were utilized to gather information about the thermal, visual, indoor air 
quality, and acoustic indoor climate. The questionnaires were intended to be combined with 
objective measurements, contributing to a more comprehensive understanding of the impact of 
indoor environmental parameters. 
Three types of questionnaires were developed and categorized as Questionnaire 1 (Q1), 
Questionnaire 2 (Q2) and Questionnaire 3 (Q3). Q1 questionnaires were asked once at the 
beginning of the session due to the series of specific questions. Q2 and Q3 were asked three 
times each. 
All questionnaires involved questions regarding Thermal Environment, Visual Environment, 
Indoor Air Quality and Acoustic Environment. Within each comfort domain, the participants 
were asked the following:  
• Satisfaction on a scale from 1-5 (Dissatisfied, just dissatisfied, neither satisfied nor 
dissatisfied, just satisfied, satisfied)  
• Acceptability on a scale from 1-5 (Unacceptable, just unacceptable, neither 
acceptable nor unacceptable, just acceptable, acceptable) 
• Preference on a scale from -3 to +3 (Option depending on the comfort domain) 
• Unsatisfactory elements (Different options depending on the comfort domain)  
Refer to Appendix 10 – Comfort questionnaires for additional details. 
Within the Thermal Environment, the participants were also asked to determine their Thermal 
Satisfaction Vote on a scale from -3 to +3 (Cold, cool slightly cool, neutral, slightly warm, 
warm, hot). Furthermore, the three questionnaires varied in their set of response options 
adapted to the specific environmental factor. 
Q1 contained question 0 (“At this moment, what are you wearing? “) and 19 ("How would you 
assess the quality of your sleep last night?") which appeared only in this specific questionnaire.  
Additionally, Question 18 (“At this moment which of these symptoms do you feel”) only 
appeared in Q1 and Q3.  
The remaining questions 1-17 were common to all three questionnaires.

4.9.2 Test of performance 
The participants were given a d2 test to evaluate their cognitive performance. The d2 test 
consisted of 14 different rows, with p’s and d’s in arbitrary order. The participants needed to 
cross out each ‘d’ with either two quotation marks above or below or with one quotation mark 
above and one below. They received 15 seconds to complete as much as possible in each line 
before the researcher asked them to continue to the next row.  
The performance tests will not be analysed in this specific study; however, it will be a part of 
the SENSEwellbeing project and can be found in Appendix 11 – Performance test d2.  
4.10 
Experimental activities 
Two types of experimental scenarios were presented to the participants. One with a view-out 
involving daylight entering the room and the other scenario with no view-out, excluding natural 
daylight. The two scenarios will be referred to as NL and AL scenarios (Natural Light and 
Artificial Light). 
In the NL scenario the ceiling lights which the rooms were already equipped with, were 
deactivated, relying only on natural light, and accompanying desk lamps for illuminance. In 
the AL scenario, the ceiling lamps and the complementary desk lamps were activated, and the 
room was thus illuminated exclusively by artificial light. In both scenarios, the desk lamps' 
intensity was adjusted to achieve the recommended illuminance level for office spaces (500 lx) 
and alternately emitted three different light spectrum colours - white, yellow, and blue. 
All participants performed both scenarios in all three rooms (18.5°C, 22.5 °C, and 26.5°C), 
resulting in six experiments per individual. During each session, the lighting spectrum of the 
desk lamps varied, always starting with white condition (4000 K) to either blue (6500 K) or 
yellow (2200 K) conditions (Phillips Hue, 2023), randomly chosen as second and third light 
colour. Details about the six scenarios are reported in Table 9.

4.12 
Statistical Analysis 
To support the findings of the study, various statistical analyses were employed. The statistical 
program SPSS was used to conduct Mann-Whitney U tests and a Spearman test. Both tests 
were implemented to examine the null hypothesis, assuming that the distribution of responses 
to each category is the same between the two groups. The null hypothesis was retained or 
rejected at a significance level of 5%. 
A Mann- Whitney U test combines the results from both groups into one dataset and ranks all 
the scores. The sum of the ranks is calculated, and the Mann-Whitney test generates a statistic 
test which is based on the ranks. The Mann-Whitney test served to determine if a significant 
difference between the two groups’ perception of satisfaction, preference and acceptability 
within the thermal and visual domains.  
The Spearman rank correlation test is a non-parametric test used to assess the strength and 
direction of monotonic relationships between two variables. The Spearman test was used to 
reveal correlations among the four domains, thermal, visual, IAQ, and acoustic, for each group. 
The following values were used to determine the coefficient correlation in the Spearman test 
(Susan Prion, 2014): 
• “Negligible” coefficient (Corr. Coef) = 0.000-0.199   
• “Weak” coefficient (Corr. Coef) = 0.200-0.399  
• “Moderate” coefficient (Corr. Coef) = 0.400-0.599 
• “Strong” coefficient (Corr. Coef) = 0.600-0.799 
These statistical analyses were performed to gain a comprehensive understanding of the 
relationships and differences within the data, however, as the statistical analyses were made of 
smaller sample sizes it generally leads to reduced statistical power.

5.4 Thermal Environment 
According to the first research question, one of the purposes of the study was to examine the 
thermal environment. This was accomplished by using questionnaire Q2, which was answered 
approximately 30 minutes after the beginning of the experiment and measurements from both 
short- and long-term measuring instruments. Outliers in the dataset were re-examined for 
additional validation to ensure reliable results. Data from Q2 was chosen to allow participants 
sufficient time to adapt to the temperature and indoor conditions while still being instructed to 
retain the clothing corresponding to approximately 1 Clo. Research question 1, denoted as 
RQ1, is as follows: 
How do different thermal conditions influence thermal perception among autistic and 
neurotypical individuals, assessed through thermal sensation voting, satisfaction levels, 
acceptability, preference, and identification of unsatisfactory elements? 
• Is there a difference in the perception of neutral temperature between the two 
groups?  
• Are there significant differences in thermal sensation voting, satisfaction, 
acceptability, and preferences for thermal conditions? 
 
5.4.1 Identification of the neutral temperature  
The data analyses, which explored the impact of different thermal conditions in the case and 
control group, revealed intriguing differences in thermal satisfaction, based on the variability 
in participants' responses.  
Responses to question 1 related to the Thermal Satisfaction Vote (TSV) were collected from 
the first of three Q2 questionnaires administered during each session. This generated a total 
number of cases of n=144, distributed in case group n=60 and control group n=84, as each 
participant answered this questionnaire six times once per session. These responses were 
==PAGE:62==
 
 
 
Page 62 of 148
 
juxtaposed with the average air temperatures recorded during the corresponding sessions. This 
resulted in a linear regression with the intersection on the x-axis indicating the estimated neutral 
temperature, representing the point at which the satisfaction rating was most likely to be 0 for 
neutral comfort (Zaniboni, et al., 2021). The linear regressions are displayed in Figure 30 and 
show a surprisingly consistent coherence in thermal preferences between the two groups. By 
setting the equation equal to 0, the neutral temperatures were calculated to be 22.72°C for the 
case group and 22.45°C for the control group, respectively. The correlation coefficient between 
TSV and air temperature, for both groups, is 0.55.  
 
5.4.2 Satisfaction with the thermal environment 
The results from the satisfaction score, illustrated in the boxplot of Figure 31, reveal interesting 
patterns. The boxplot is based on individuals' responses to thermal satisfaction from the first 
Q2 questionnaire (Case group n=60, control group n=84). It discloses that the case group 
experienced notably increased dissatisfaction when the temperature deviated from their neutral 
temperature, in colder and warmer environments, compared to the control group. Both groups 
showed a trend towards increased satisfaction at 22.5°C, as measured in Room 2. The case 
group was very similarly dissatisfied in Room 1 as well as Room 3, while the control group 
preferred Room 1 concerning Room 3 but had a substantially greater interquartile of their 
thermal satisfaction in Room 1.

5.4.4 Statistical analysis of the thermal environment 
The Mann-Whitney U test was used to determine whether there was a statistically significant 
difference between the case and control group, comparing the categories of thermal sensation 
vote, satisfaction, acceptability, and preference of the thermal environment. The null 
hypothesis assumes that the distribution of each category is the same between the two groups 
and will be retained or rejected at a significance level of 5%.  
All answers, based on the first Q2 questionnaires, were used to compare TSV, satisfaction, 
acceptability, and preference. This generated a total number of cases of n=144, as each 
participant answered this questionnaire six times (one each session).

whereas the null hypothesis is rejected. This elucidated the difference between the case and 
control group with statistical evidence. However, the null hypothesis was retained for the TSV 
and thermal preference, as the p-values were calculated to 0.804 and 0.740 respectively. This

6. Discussion  
 
This section aims to discuss whether a correlation exists between indoor climate and the overall 
well-being of individuals with autism, based on the findings from this study. Existing research 
will be included to contribute to the understanding of the indoor environmental effects, and 
recommendations for improvements will be provided.  
6.1 Effects of Thermal Environment 
This study showed that both autistic and neurotypical groups exhibited a similar neutral 
temperature of 22.5°C. This neutral temperature indicates that the designed PMV of 0 at 22.5°C 
complies with the comfort level of both groups of participants. No statistically significant 
difference in satisfaction levels was found in Room 2 with temperatures at 22.5°C indicating 
that the neutral temperature environment is optimal for both groups, resulting in similar 
satisfaction levels. The neutral temperature calculation was based on 60 responses from the 
case group and 84 responses from the control group, considering Thermal Satisfaction Votes. 
The results appear to align well with the findings of an indoor climate study conducted in 
physiotherapy centres, which evaluated neutral temperatures and interactions with other IEQ 
domains (Visual, air quality, acoustic) with physiotherapists and their patients. In that study, 
the neutral temperature was found to be 21.3°C indicating a comparable neutral temperature 
across groups (Zaniboni, et al., 2021). The consistent neutral temperature across groups 
indicates broader generalizability within the general population, despite the narrower dataset 
in this study. The corresponding neutral temperatures strengthen the validity and can serve as 
a foundation for designing indoor environments for individuals with autism.  
Despite the comparable neutral temperature between the two groups, this study revealed 
interesting observations regarding thermal comfort concerning extremes in temperature. The 
case group showed lower satisfaction levels in Rooms 1 and 3, with temperatures at 26.5 °C 
and 18.5 °C and PMV values at 1 and -1, indicating increased sensitivity to thermal deviations 
from their neutral temperature. In room 1 and 3 above half of the case group expressed 
dissatisfaction, citing "too warm on my hands" and "too warm on my legs" in room 1, and "too 
cold on my hands" and "too cold on my legs" in room 3 when questioned about environmental 
unsatisfactory conditions. This implied a noteworthy discomfort within the case group when 
deviating from the neutral temperature. In comparison, only a minimal amount of the control 
group reported discomfort with these response options. It is known that individuals with autism 
have a lower tolerance threshold and sensory sensitivity, experiencing more intense reactions 
==PAGE:86==
 
 
 
Page 86 of 148
 
to temperature changes. This is evident in deviations from neutral temperature and is supported 
by previous research (Psykiatrifonden, 2023). There are indications that the control group 
adapted better to extreme temperatures, while the case group exhibited a stronger reaction to 
environmental changes. In Room 2, minimal discomfort was observed as most participants 
responded to the questionnaire regarding unsatisfactory elements of the thermal environment 
with “Nothing”. In room 1 and 3, on the other hand, a relatively large responds with “lack of 
control”, which may be due to the fact that the results are based on the initial questionnaire 
(Q2) answered before the participants were allowed to adjust their clothing. The case group's 
dissatisfaction with a lack of control may also be attributed to heightened sensitivity to sensory 
stimuli and the resulting sense of lack of control over their surroundings. Autists also thrive 
best in familiar environments that are structured and predictable, and deviations from this can 
create discomfort and a feeling of a lack of control (Bedre psykiatri, 2024). An interesting 
aspect for future analysis could be investigate whether the response “lack of control” decreases 
in correlation with participants being allowed to adjust their clothing or if it is more attributed 
to increased sensitivity. 
A difference in satisfaction was observed in Room 3, however, it failed to reach the 5% 
significance level. Despite the relatively small sample size, resulting in reduced statistical 
power, the analysis confirms that both groups perceived thermal satisfaction differently when 
deviating from their neutral temperature. The findings in this study highlighted that individuals 
with autism may face sensory challenges when exposed to temperatures differing from their 
optimal thermal comfort. They are particularly sensitive to environmental changes, supported 
by previous studies (Caniato, et al., 2022).  
Variations in clothing insulation were observed among participants, which could have affected 
the participants' Predicted Mean Votes, designed to be -1, 0, and 1. The participants were 
expected to wear clothing corresponding to the winter season, however, the clothing level 
varied among participants which could be a confounder to the results. In Room 1, the highest 
clothing level was registered at 1.02 Clo (case group) and 1.04 (control group), potentially 
contributing to a decreased sense of thermal comfort compared to the lowest clothing value of 
0.4 Clo (case group) and 0.53 (control group). A similar tendency was observed in Room 3 
where the highest clothing level registered at 0.92 Clo (case group) and 1.16 (control group), 
potentially contributing to an increased sense of thermal comfort compared to the lowest 
measured clothing value of 0.49 Clo (case group) and 0.58 Clo (control group). Since this 
difference in clothing values could have influenced the thermal satisfaction of individuals with 
==PAGE:87==
 
 
 
Page 87 of 148
 
lower clothing levels, a new PMV for each room was calculated. This new PMV, considering 
the average operative temperature, metabolic rate, airspeed, relative humidity, and clothing 
level obtained during the experiment, indicated compliance with the participants’ TSV and the 
PMV used for designing the rooms. 
The Mann-Whitney U test revealed no statistical significance for TSV and thermal preference, 
indicating that the PMV model can probably be applied to both groups. However, a statistically 
significant difference at a 5% level was observed in thermal satisfaction and acceptability and 
coupled with the responses from the case group regarding unsatisfactory elements, this implies 
that the Percentage of People Dissatisfied at each temperature is potentially higher among 
individuals with autism than neurotypical individuals. 
The results indicate a lower degree of satisfaction among individuals with autism when exposed 
to temperatures deviating from their neutral temperatures. The control group showed a greater 
tendency to adapt to their surroundings as they experienced fewer unsatisfactory elements at 
extreme temperatures. Considering these results, future guidelines could consider the need to 
minimize significance variation and more effectively tailor guidelines and standards to the 
needs of neuroatypical individuals. The results suggested that the control group adapted to 
variation to a greater extend and does not experience discomfort to the same degree as 
neuroatypical individuals. It can be crucial to consider individual differences when designing 
the indoor environment, as the two groups shared the same thermal sensation but different 
thermal satisfaction.  
6.2 Effects of Visual Environment 
Considering all three rooms at once, the case group reported different satisfaction levels across 
the three colour temperatures and these results indicated that yellow light was more satisfying, 
followed by white light. However, there was a greater spread in responses to white light from 
the case group when compared to the control group. The preference for yellow light in the case 
group may be attributed to sensory preferences, as yellow light may be perceived as more 
pleasant and less overstimulating due to its warm and muted tones. The variability in 
satisfaction with white light could be influenced by colour associations, colour psychology, or 
sensitivity to white light. The control group consistently expressed satisfaction with the visual 
domain when exposed to all three colours, as measured by their median values, averages, and 
quartiles. The stability observed in the control group indicates a consistent and predictable 
response to visual stimuli.  
==PAGE:88==
 
 
 
Page 88 of 148
 
Individuals on the autism spectrum are known for varying sensory sensitivities, why the white 
light may appear intense to the hypersensitive ones, leading to a variation in responses. 
Similarly, the sensory challenges may explain why the case group exhibited noticeably less 
satisfaction when exposed to blue light, as it can be perceived as overstimulating, which is 
different from the responses within the control group. Not all people diagnosed with autism are 
sensitive to visual stimuli and its impact can affect individuals differently depending on 
whether they are hypersensitive or hyposensitive (Bogdashina, 2016). A list of visual sensory 
symptoms states that hypersensitive individuals, among other things, dislike too dark and too 
bright light, sharp flashes of light, and may cover or close their eyes in bright light (Bogdashina, 
2016). Hyposensitive individuals may be attracted to light, fascinated by reflections, and 
brightly coloured objects (Simmons, et al., 2009). In this study, the case group was not 
categorized based on whether they were hypersensitive or hyposensitive, which could have 
influenced the responses to the visual satisfaction scale. Retrospectively, testing participants in 
the case group for this factor before the experiment could have been a measure to minimize 
confounders and strengthen the results. The specific environments that may trigger individuals 
with autism vary widely on an individual basis and may therefore be the reason for the large 
spread in the responses among autistics. (Chung & Son, 2020) 
When dividing the visual satisfaction into NL and AL sessions, the findings showed that both 
groups were more satisfied with all three colours in an environment where natural daylight was 
present. During AL sessions, the overall satisfaction decreased for both groups, but specially 
within the case group. Meanwhile, within the control group, blue light became the preferred 
choice during AL sessions, potentially due to individual responses to changes in lighting 
conditions, increased alertness, and an invigorating effect (Sundhedsstyrrelsen, 2022). Among 
some people, the blue light can be interpreted by the brain as daylight and then seems to agree 
with the previous results which describe that the control group is more satisfied with daylight 
(Sundhedsstyrrelsen, 2022). The results showed that blue light caused more glare compared to 
the preferred yellow light among individuals in the case group. Furthermore, 53.3% of 
individuals in the case group considered blue light as a "too cold colour of light source" 
compared to 26.2% in the control group. On the contrary, the control group more frequently 
described the yellow light as too warm during AL sessions than the case group. This may be a 
reason for the difference in the preferred colour between the case group and the control group. 
Again, the varying degrees of sensory hypersensitivity can affect the experience of cold and 
==PAGE:89==
 
 
 
Page 89 of 148
 
warm colours and be the reason why the case group was particularly affected by the colour 
changes (Videncenter Autisme, 2022).  
The case group exhibited a more notable response to colour changes and to a lesser extent, 
whether they were exposed to an NL- or AL session, while the control group responded less to 
colour changes but was particularly influenced by the differences between NL- and AL 
sessions. In the case group the satisfaction across colours changed, especially during the 
transition from NL to AL sessions, while the overall satisfaction level between NL and AL did 
not exhibit notable changes. Opposite trends were observed in the control group, with a change 
in satisfaction levels for all three colours during the transition from NL sessions to AL sessions. 
This aligns with the results of the statistical analysis, where a difference in visual satisfaction 
between NL and AL, for all three colours, at a 5% level was observed within the control group. 
This indicated that the case group was more sensitive to details as colour changes and less 
influenced by the difference session type, while the control group was affected by the session 
type and to a lesser extent by colour changes. When the autistic brain receives sensory stimuli, 
it begins with the details and lacks the ability to quickly form a coherent and synchronized 
overview of the entire situation, filter details, and coordinate information from multiple senses 
as observed in neurotypical individuals (Sinkbæk, 2022). Among individuals with autism, all 
details are included, leading to a tendency to get lost in specifics (Bundgaard, 2022). 
Consequently, this may explain why the case group emphasized specific stimuli and primarily 
focused on colors, while the control group prioritized broader aspects and paid less attention to 
details such as colors but exclusively concentrated on the overall surroundings during NL and 
AL sessions. 
Investigating the unsatisfactory elements chosen by the two groups in the multiple-choice 
question, it could be noticed that the category “Lack of control” appeared more frequently 
within the case group, indicating that individuals with autism might experience a greater sense 
of lack of control in the visual domain. Although adjustments and changes to the desk lamp 
were allowed throughout the sessions continuous dissatisfaction may be attributed to various 
parameters affecting their visual stimuli. These parameters include sudden colour changes, 
glare, brightness, multi-domain effects, synaesthesia, and influences from other participants in 
the experimental room. Studies even indicate that control over stimuli helps individuals manage 
sensory overload, improve cognitive regulation skills, and provide a greater sense of agency 
(Toftum, 2010). Low satisfaction with lighting contrast was particularly observed during NL 
sessions among the case group, revealing a noticeable difference between the two groups. Other 
==PAGE:90==
 
 
 
Page 90 of 148
 
studies based on contrast sensitivity found no differences in performance between a group of 
individuals with autism and a control group (Behrmann, et al., 2006).  The difference in results 
between the two studies may be attributed to several factors, including the age group, 
variability within the spectrum, lower uniformity of light during NL sessions in this study, and 
methodological differences such as test methods and study design.  
The findings in this study indicate a trend of increased sensitivity to influences and stimuli that 
individuals with autism cannot control. This is observed through higher levels of dissatisfaction 
and higher frequency of stating “lack of control” as an unsatisfactory element compared to the 
control group. Furthermore, the results showed an increased sensitivity to the colours among 
the case group, where the control group was more affected by NL and AL sessions.  This 
implies that the possibility of controlling and adjusting the indoor environment is an essential 
consideration when designing lighting conditions for individuals diagnosed with autism.  
6.3 Multi-domain Effects of Thermal and Visual Environments 
It is crucial to investigate multiple domains to achieve a comprehensive understanding of the 
interaction and holistic perception of indoor environment. Examining multiple domains 
contributes to a broad comprehension of the complexity associated with indoor environmental 
conditions and how the four investigated domains impact and interact across and with each 
other. Synaesthesia may also influence how participants respond to variations in indoor 
environmental conditions across domains.  
The result within the case group showed no notable changes in thermal satisfaction when 
comparing NL and AL sessions in Rooms 1 and 2. However, the average thermal satisfaction 
decreased from 3 in NL sessions to 1.9 in AL sessions on a 1-5 scale in Room 3. The control 
group experienced thermal comfort similarly depending on NL and AL sessions in Rooms 1 
and 3, with no major fluctuations in perception. In Room 2, the thermal satisfaction decreased 
from 4.23 to 3.75 on a scale of 1-5, when changing from NL to AL sessions. When comparing 
these findings to the Mann-Whitney U test, no statistical difference in thermal satisfaction 
between NL and AL sessions in all three rooms for both groups was found.  
No major variations in the perception of the visual environment were found when investigating 
the correlation with the thermal environment among both groups. The unsatisfied elements of 
the visual environment revealed no new information that could be influenced by the 
temperature in the three rooms. Dividing responses from NL and AL sessions by the three 
experimental rooms, no markable deviations in visual satisfaction are observed across the 
==PAGE:91==
 
 
 
Page 91 of 148
 
rooms, which indicates the correlation found in the Spearman test between the 2 domains 
depends on other factors. 
The results of the Spearman test, which was not divided by NL and AL, showed correlations 
between the thermal and visual domains for both groups. The Spearman test revealed stronger 
associations of satisfaction among the four domains in the case group compared to the control 
group. A moderate correlation coefficient indicated a stronger impact of thermal conditions on 
visual perception and IAQ (or vice versa), as well as the mutual influence between visual 
influence and IAQ or acoustic elements. Existing research has shown that the rate of 
synesthesia in adults with autism is almost three times higher than in neurotypical individuals 
(Simon Baron-Cohen, 2013). Common for synesthesia and autism is that they may experience 
heightened sensitivities to sensory stimuli, making indoor environments particularly 
challenging for individuals with this condition. As this study shows, several sensory inputs, 
like visual, thermal, IAQ and acoustic, can significantly impact the comfort of sensitive 
individuals. When considering the potential for sensory challenges, it is noteworthy that 
individuals diagnosed with autism may perceive and respond to stimuli differently, 
contributing to variations in their comfort levels and ability to concentrate.  
Unlike neurotypical individuals, who may cope better with factors like artificial lighting or 
temperature variations, hypo- or hyper-sensitive individuals within the autism spectrum might 
experience meltdowns or encounter severe difficulties in concentration when exposed to 
similar conditions (Rudy, 2023).  
 
6.4 Improvement of the indoor environment for individuals with autism  
Based on this study and the presented results, several key areas have been identified where 
indoor environments can be improved to provide satisfaction and well-being settings for 
individuals with autism. As presented in the results, there exists a broad variation in sensitivity, 
discomfort, and individual needs among the case group, posing a challenge in creating autism-
friendly spaces and environments. 
Previous literature indicates that individuals with autism can experience hyper- or 
hyposensitivity in acoustic, indoor air quality, temperature, and lighting, including various 
colours and tones (Shell, et al., 2019). Designing based on these parameters can help create 
more comfortable and stimulating environments for individuals with autism. The results 
suggest that considering the thermal preferences of individuals with autism is crucial in the 
==PAGE:92==
 
 
 
Page 92 of 148
 
design and adaptation of indoor environments, as individuals with autism particularly 
experience greater discomfort with temperature fluctuations from their neutral comfort 
temperature. Strategies to address this challenge include specific temperature regulation for 
individually tailored zones, well-insulated environments to avoid temperature variations, and 
the option for individual control over these facilities.  
The case group preferred white (NL) and yellow (AL) colours, while the control group 
preferred white (NL) and blue light (AL). Therefore, it could be recommended to design 
environments with the option to adjust the colour temperature and dimmable lighting, as it was 
observed that individuals on the participants on the spectrum particularly wanted to dim the 
light more frequently. These recommendations are based on this study but are supported by 
research indicating that various factors, including colours and tones, can affect mood, 
concentration, and behaviour among autistic users. Research indicates that daylight is crucial 
for well-being and health and improves sleep for individuals with autism (Shell, et al., 2019). 
Both groups show great discomfort when daylight is removed and replaced with artificial 
lighting.  
Individuals with autism expressed substantial dissatisfaction with the lack of control, 
emphasizing the importance of allowing them to adapt indoor environments to their sensory 
preferences. Studies show that flexible spaces that consider these needs and allow control are 
essential for comfortable and inclusive environments as well as reduce stress and 
dissatisfactory (Shell, et al., 2019). Likewise, research indicates that the same design strategies 
enhance the overall experience for neurotypical individuals. Architecture and design that seem 
autism-friendly may seem more enjoyable for neotypical individuals (Shell, et al., 2019). 
Sensory hypersensitivity and sensory disturbances in individuals with autism mean that the 
autonomic nervous system reacts with alertness (fear and fight) which, in addition to resulting 
in sleep problems and poor well-being, can result in overwhelm and stress and in extreme cases 
lead to anxiety and depression. Structure, predictability and the possibility of control can 
therefore reduce the risk of meltdown and overstimulation (Asensu, 2024). This highlights the 
importance of designing spaces that are comfortable, flexible, and created based on the needs 
of different individuals. 
7. Strength and limitations  
The four questions used as exclusion criteria in the preliminary questionaries developed by 
“Center of autism” have been validated and undergone an extensive development to ensure 
==PAGE:93==
 
 
 
Page 93 of 148
 
accuracy. Similarly, the comfort questionnaires are based on guidelines developed by Danish 
standards (ISO 10551:2019, 2019). They are used as standardized methods and guidelines for 
measuring participants' experiences of indoor climate.  The use of validated questionnaires 
strengthens the study. 
Incorporating a control group makes it possible to isolate indoor conditions, identify influences 
on the case group and determine whether observations in the four domains are unique to the 
case group. The control group was unaware that the purpose of the experiment was to study 
differences between neurotypical individuals and people with autism. This group of reference 
individuals strengthens the study’s overall robustness, and generalizability, and reduces 
randomness. All three experimental rooms were standardized to reduce variations between the 
rooms, ensure uniformity, and minimise interference from external factors. 
To provide a nuanced understanding of influences, the data collection is multidimensional 
allowing for subjective measurements including psychological, physiological, and 
environmental measurements as well as behavioural observations. This has added depth to the 
study and provided a nuanced picture of all relevant influences.  
This study consists of a small population of 10 individuals with autism and 14 neurotypical 
individuals, diminishing the study's ability to establish a statistical significance compared to a 
larger dataset. This weakens the study's external validity and creates potential bias. A potential 
limitation to generalizability is the narrow age group among the participants, ranging only from 
22 to 30 years. Results may be influenced by the relatively young age of the participants’ 
groups. The age group does not represent the entire population. It is therefore important to 
emphasize the need for larger and additional research that takes this into account, as this study 
alone cannot be standardized. Elderly individuals may experience visual fatigue and discomfort 
in unpleasant lighting conditions and vision impairment. Health issues among the elderly can 
be triggered by poor air quality (Øjenforeningen, 2018). Therefore, caution should be exercised 
in drawing conclusions based on this study.  
Since the experiments were conducted from October to December the season could act as a 
confounder as there is an increased incidence of illness. Several participants showed up with 
symptoms like cough and stuffy nose, potentially impacting their performance, and 
concentration, and, consequently, influencing the results. This could have distorted the results 
and the assessment of the final perception of the domains' impact. 
==PAGE:94==
 
 
 
Page 94 of 148
 
With a maximum of three participants and one researcher in a relatively small space, 
psychological influences may occur, leading to another confounder. Participants might adjust 
their quantity and clothing choices based on others, feeling pressured to respond more quickly 
and possibly less accurately to questionnaires. Participants may have refrained from expressing 
their preferences or honest opinions about environmental changes when asked in the room to 
avoid social discomfort and conformity. For example, several participants indicated "excessive 
artificial lighting," but did not express a desire for changes or adjustments when questioned 
during sessions. The same has been noted in thermal discomfort, where several individuals 
have said 'too warm' in Room 1 but kept a sweater or jumper on. The presence of up to 4 people 
in the small room, according to CO2 measurements has caused an increase in CO2 
concentration, resulting in affected participants experiencing issues with concentration and 
fatigue. Concentration and fatigue may also be attributed to other factors such as sleep quality 
and time of the day.  
Sessions were conducted from 8 am to 5 pm, and participants may have experienced fatigue or 
discomfort if they arrived directly from work or studies, influencing their perception of the four 
domains. To minimize the influence of this confounder, the sessions were, as much as possible, 
scheduled according to each participant's preferences.  
Errors and bias could potentially have distorted the results, including participant responses. An 
observation, seen in the floor plan and pictures taken during each trial, reveals that the front 
row (positions 1 and 2) receives more natural daylight than the back row (Position 3). To 
maintain an illuminance of 550 lux at the table, Position 3 experienced increased artificial 
lighting in sessions with weak natural daylight, which may have influenced the visual comfort. 
This is evident in the image in Appendix 16 - Artificial light at position 3, where Position 3 is 
more illuminated by the desk lamp. It must be assumed that the light intensity and artificial 
lighting, including white, blue, and yellow light, may have appeared more intense.  
Daylight variations affected some sessions, leading to the dimming of artificial lighting. 
Despite dimming, the light often exceeded 550 lux, potentially influencing participants' 
perceptions. Significant daylight variations within and across sessions were observed, posing 
a potential confounder for participants' desires to adjust artificial lighting, especially noted in 
the case group. Assessing the visual domain is challenging due to daylight and seasonal 
influences. 
Limitations also include noise pollution from the nearby light rail construction. Continuous and 
==PAGE:95==
 
 
 
Page 95 of 148
 
sometimes sudden high noise levels may have potentially affected participants' responses, 
within the acoustic domain. It is known from previous studies that individuals with autism can 
be sensitive to sound and noise (Black, et al., 2022). This was also evident in some participants 
who expressed significant discomfort with persistent noise disturbances. Nevertheless, this 
could not be remediated due to the location of the experimental building adjacent to the 
construction site. 
Some individuals with autism may use medication to manage co-morbidities associated with 
their condition, for instance, anxiety. Anxiety medication can mitigate or mute sensory stimuli, 
contributing to an improved quality of life for individuals navigating the complexities of autism 
(Videncenter for autisme, 2018). It is important to note that the usage of all types of medication 
was not considered during the experiments as we, due to ethical guidelines, did not ask about 
the participants' privacy and can be a potential factor impacting the findings, as variations in 
medication among the participants might influence the observed sensory responses.  
These observations are derived from the dataset of this study and may not be universally 
applicable to a broader population of individuals with autism. Generalizability requires a larger 
dataset encompassing diverse factors like gender, geography, demographics, and age.  
Further research is necessary to comprehend underlying causes, yet this study highlights the 
complexity of visual perception and needs in individuals with autism. The autism spectrum 
encompasses a broad range of diagnoses, and the exploration of hyper- and hyposensitivity in 
this study is limited by extensive variation within the diagnosis, the relatively small dataset, 
and factors like varying noise disruptions and only three temperature variations. More research 
and data are needed to draw final conclusions with well-documented empirical evidence. 
 
8. Conclusion 
This study aimed at increasing the understanding of the subjective indoor environmental 
perception of individuals diagnosed with autism. The insights were derived from data obtained 
from experiments conducted during a Living Lab Campaign including 10 participants 
diagnosed with autism and a control group of 14 neurotypical participants.  
It was observed that the neutral temperature for the individuals diagnosed with autism and the 
neurotypical participants was similar. When exposed to environments with thermal conditions 
differing from their neutral temperature, autistic people tended to be more unsatisfied in 
==PAGE:96==
 
 
 
Page 96 of 148
 
comparison to the control group who more easily adapted to the environment. The findings 
indicated that Predicted Mean Vote (PMV) operated similarly for both groups, although the 
Percentage of People Dissatisfied (PPD) was higher among individuals with autism.  
Both groups were more satisfied with the visual environment, including all three colours, when 
natural light was present when compared to an environment illuminated only by artificial light. 
The three colours during NL sessions among the control group showed no markable difference. 
The preferred colour for the control group during the AL session was blue, while the case group 
was more satisfied with the yellow light. The results within the visual domain indicated that 
the case group was more sensitive to colour changes and less influenced by the session type 
(NL and AL), while the control group was affected by the session type and less by colour 
changes. Variations in the colour of artificial lighting seemed to have a more pronounced 
impact on individuals with autism as they more often express a “lack of control” when 
compared to the neurotypical group.  
Despite the small sample sizes the analysis of the collected data revealed a statistically 
significant difference at a 5% level when comparing thermal satisfaction and acceptability 
between the two groups. The same statistically significant difference across the two groups was 
found in visual satisfaction and acceptability. This statistical validation contributed to the 
credibility of the experiment and its findings, elucidating that people diagnosed with autism 
exhibit the thermal and visual environment differently from neurotypical individuals.  
When examining the multi-domain effect on individuals diagnosed with autism, it became 
evident that the association of satisfaction among the four domains were stronger when 
compared to the neurotypical individuals. This finding aligns with existing research on 
synaesthesia in adults with autism, indicating that they feel heightened sensitivities to several 
sensory stimuli.   
This report only addresses a fraction of the collected data from the Living Lab Campaign. 
Further research should examine how the different lighting or temperature conditions may 
affect the concentration and stress levels in the case group, compared to the control group. 
These studies should include the performance test results and the heart rate data.  
Isolating each of the four domains could give more accurate results for a specific domain, 
enabling investigations of how light impacts participants without the interference of the other 
domains (acoustic, IAQ, thermal). However, this approach does not provide insights into the 
multi-domain effects. The study indicated that there are several indoor climate conditions that 
==PAGE:97==
 
 
 
Page 97 of 148
 
differentiate individuals with autism from neurotypical individuals, which should be taken into 
consideration in future guidelines. However, considering the limitations of the study, such as 
small sample size and many influencing variables, underscore the need for further research. 
Expanding the knowledge based through larger and more comprehensive studies can provide 
better insight into future studies.
