model for wear prediction is put forward. This thesis is submitted for partial fulfilment of the

Abstract 
Wear exists everywhere where two surfaces are in contact. It is generally unwanted, and steps are 
often taken towards preventing or minimizing it. Wear modelling can be used to help with deciding 
what steps to take. In this project, an industrial sheet metal forming system that experienced highly 
localized wear was considered and modelled using LS-DYNA. Two approaches, qualitative and 
quantitative, were taken towards modelling the system. The qualitative approach largely involved 
finding suitable formulation to use in the model. This includes the friction sensitivity of the system, 
mathematical formulation, mesh sizing, and a maximum viable stroke-step size. The quantitative 
approach involved experimentally determining a wear coefficient by using a curve-fitting 
methodology. The wear coefficient curve was then used in an attempt to realize a variable wear 
coefficient behaviour. Tools that have seen wear in practice are measured and compared to the 
modelled results. The qualitative approach resulted in a model that could accurately predict wear 
sensitive areas. The quantitative approach was not as successful due to issues that came up and serves 
as a suggestion for an approach instead.

1. Introduction 
Wear exists everywhere where there are two surfaces, or a surface and a medium, that come into 
contact. The rate of wear, and the mechanism by which it occurs, will largely depend on the tribo-
system that it happens under. A tribo-system describes the contact conditions, the external influences, 
and the material pairing that are in place in a given system. Any consideration on wear must therefore 
take the tribo-system it occurs under into consideration. [1]  
In industry the fact that tools wear means that they must be maintained, or if that is not 
possible, replaced entirely. If tools are not kept in good shape, it means that any products made using 
them will be of a lower quality, or otherwise not nominal and therefore potentially unusable. Not 
keeping the tools in good shape is therefore not an option. Performing maintenance or replacing the 
tools when they break is therefore necessary; but that can be expensive, both in lost production and 
scrapped parts, as well as in pure costs related to purchasing a new set of tools. It can therefore only 
be beneficial to minimize the need to maintain or replace tools, or at the very least to be able to 
perform maintenance or tool-switches at the right time to minimize halts in production. This 
minimization can be realized in a few different ways, and that is where wear modelling comes in. Used 
as far back as in the tool design phase, wear modelling can be an excellent tool for tool designers to 
predict problem-spots in their designs. These problem spots, if found in the final tool, could mean that 
there is excessive localized wear in a few distinct locations which might reduce the overall tool-life 
significantly. This could cause the whole tool to be deemed unusable while the majority of its surface 
is still in good shape. [2,3] 
 
Grundfos, a manufacturer and innovator in the field of water solutions, has recently 
implemented a new sheet metal forming process line in a bid towards increased automation. The 
process line consists of a few different steps, one of which sees highly localized wear that causes 
problems further downstream in the process chain. This project deals with characterizing this 
excessive wear, creating a model that can predict it, and then verifying said model by comparing it 
with tools that have been used and worn in practice.  
1.1. Process line 
The process line starts with a sheet of EN1.4301 or EN1.4401 stainless steel and ends with a fully 
assembled stage that is ready for use. The line is a small part of a larger process chain, which includes 
fabrication of other components and eventually full assembly of a pump with pre-determined nominal 
dimensions.  
The process line is expected to produce a few different sizes of stages, the different sizes 
having an identification such as CR95, which also relates to the size of the assembled pump and the 
material thickness. The thickness of a blank increases as the size of the stage does. The different 
thicknesses of sheet material that is used ranges between 1.0 −2.0 ± 0.05 mm. So far only some of 
the smaller sizes of stages have been produced. The stage size considered in this project is designated 
CR125, having a nominal material thickness of 1.50 mm.  
A stage is an assembly of an impeller and a chamber. A single impeller consists of ten blade 
components, while a chamber consists of ten guide vane components. After production, the 
components are assembled and joined by laser welding to form a chamber, or an impeller. Both vane 
and blade components are produced in this process line, the only difference between the specific 
process lines being the shape of the blank and tools used in a forming step. 
The focus of this project is the production of a chamber, the reason for why being that the 
localized wear is seen to be much greater in the chamber forming tools. A few different steps make 
up the process line, which occurs in one area for the most part. The different states of a vane blank as 
it travels through the process line can be seen in Figure 1, going from raw sheet-stock to a component 
that is ready for laser-welding. 
First is the production of a blank (a-b), which is done by laser-cutting a virgin sheet of stainless 
steel. A single blank consists of ten components that are attached together on the sides. In order to 
increase hydraulic performance, one end of each blank is sharpened to form a taper (b-c). Next, the

The step that is of interest in this project is that which takes the blank from state c) to d). This 
forming step is performed using a 60-ton press, without any lubricant and at a low stroke velocity. The 
step has experienced problems with excessive wear that describes itself as changes occurring on the 
tool surface which lead to scratches and burrs being formed on workpieces. The underlying 
mechanism behind this is believed to be either adhesive deposition of workpiece material on the tool 
surface, or abrasive wear of the tool. This will be discussed further in section 3.2, in which the specific 
wear mechanism is determined. It is due to this excessive wear that this step will be the main focus of 
this project as it represents a wear problem that can be modelled, and in doing so explained and 
prevented in future tool designs.  
To combat the excessive wear, the tool designer has thus far tried using different tool steels, 
as well as different surface treatments. This has resulted in very slight improvements to the tool life. 
The following tool materials have been used, in ascending order of hardness. Typical compositions of 
the tool steels can be seen in Table 1. 
1. Uddeholm Nimax 40HRC is a low-carbon tool steel. It has a relatively good hardness combined 
with a good toughness. [4] 
2. Uddeholm Orvar Supreme 52HRC is an alloyed tool steel that is made to be easily machinable 
while also showing good through-hardenability. [5] 
3. Uddeholm Sleipner 61HRC is a high alloy tool steel that is characterized by wear resistance, 
high hardness after tempering, and good surface treatment properties. [6] 
4. Uddeholm Vanadis 4E 62HRC is a powder metallurgical tool steel with a high concentration 
of alloying components. It is characterized by excellent wear resistance properties and good 
through-hardenability. [7]

A number of different surface treatments have been tested as a solution to the excessive wear 
problem, a general outline of them can be seen below. The different combinations of tool material 
and surface treatment will then be discussed. 
a. No surface treatment was applied at first. The tool designer did not anticipate the excessive 
wear that resulted from the direct stainless steel to tool steel contact. 
b. Case hardening by plasma nitriding involves heating a component up in a nitrogen rich 
atmosphere, allowing nitrogen to diffuse into the surface of the component. It is commonly 
used to improve the wear resistance properties of steel alloy components. [8] 
c. BALINIT® ALCRONA is a surface coating composed of aluminium, chromium and nitrogen. It 
is tailored to be harder than steel, and due to its composition, shows ceramic properties. [9] 
d. Duplex-VARIANTIC® is a multilayer surface coating composed of titanium, aluminium, carbon 
and nitrogen built up in two layers: TiAlN and a TiCN top layer. The coating is applied on top 
of a substrate that has been surface treated by plasma nitriding. It is applied to tools in order 
to enhance wear resistance and friction properties. [10] 
e. Duplex-VARIANTIC®-1000 is a multilayer surface coating that consists of multiple layers of 
TiAlCN. The coating is applied on top of a substrate that has been surface treated by plasma 
nitriding. The coating is especially tailored to have high layer adhesion and to provide 
excellent protection against abrasive wear. [11] 
f. Duplex-VARIANTIC®-1400 is a multilayer surface coating that consists of a grip layer, multiple 
layers of Al(Cr, Ti)N and a top layer deposited onto a substrate that has been surface treated 
by plasma nitriding. It shows excellent layer adhesion and improved workpiece quality in 
suggested applications, such as a forming tool for forming high-strength sheet steel. [12]

or the coating on the tool has been worn through. 
 
At first, the tool designer believed that the tool stresses found in the process would not be 
very large. Therefore, the decision to use a low-cost, but relatively hard and tough material was made. 
1-a was the first combination used but as can be seen in the table below, the tool-life of that 
combination was wholly unacceptable. Plasma nitriding of the surface was therefore performed in 
order to extend the tool-life, but that showed only a marginal improvement. The decision to adopt a 
harder tool steel was then made. As the stresses that the tools would see was not fully understood, 
the decision of using a harder material, that was still not too hard as to be potentially too brittle, was 
made. 2-a showed an improvement to the tool life found for 1-a and 1-b but only a small one. 2-b 
showed a further extension, but this tool set was not allowed to run to the end of tool-life as changes 
in tool geometry warranted a new set of tools be produced.  
 
After simulating the process conditions to understand the tool stresses better, it was realized 
that a much harder tool material could be used without risking fracture. This is where 4-b, which is 
the hardest material that was tested over-all, comes in. It showed higher wear resistance but was very 
expensive. An intermediate solution was found in using material 3 with surface treatments b-f. The 
material shows a similar performance as material 4 but does not show any major improvement 
through the different surface treatment options.

1.2. Problem description 
Production personnel have noticed highly localized wear that occurs in the forming tools used to form 
the guide vanes. This causes the dimensions of formed parts to be out of tolerance for laser-welding, 
and for scratches to form on the surface of formed components. The change in dimension means that 
automatic welding results in weaker welds, and the scratches can lead to unwanted corrosion in the 
assembled stage. The weaker welds can cause issues in the assembled pump, where additional 
stresses might arise that shorten the service life of the pump. The unwanted corrosion would be 
caused by the scratches acting as initiation sites, which also reduces the service life of the pump. 
 
This could lead to an increase in total cost through either additional post-processing, or 
premature failure of the component [2]. It would be of interest to find some way of reducing the wear 
in this step, in order to ensure that these issues are minimized. The first step towards that could be to 
create a general model that can predict the wear that would otherwise be seen in the physical tools. 
This model could then be used to predict wear issues in the sizes of chambers that have not been 
produced as they share a common tribo-system with the chambers that have been, and to help in the 
prevention of the wear issues altogether. 
Tool failure consistently occurs in the same location on one of the tool components. It is in 
this location, or close to it, that the workpiece first meets the tool. Due to the geometry of the tool, 
contact conditions are very time dependent and essentially mean that a flat blank is meeting a concave 
surface and making contact predominantly in two points before the blank starts conforming to the 
concavity of the upper tool in the later parts of a stroke. The contact pressures are likely higher than 
expected in these specific points [13]. A depiction of the contact conditions can be seen in Figure 2. 
The load is then dragged along the surface for a while before the blank starts to conform to the shape 
of the tool. This means that some specific points in the tool surface will be seeing high sliding distances 
combined with high contact pressures. This, according to Groche and Christiany [14], will inherently 
mean that wear is concentrated there. More distributed and less severe wear can be found where the 
flat blank meets the concave surface close to the end of a stroke. Figure 3 shows a photograph of one 
of the tools that has reached near end of tool-life.

1.3. Project scope 
The outcome of this project should be a model that can be used to predict wear under the given 
contact conditions and material pairing outlined above. The size of the geometry being tested should 
not be important, but only the general shape of it. The model could then be used to predict the wear 
evolution in the forming of larger components that have not been tried in practice so far, as well as 
potentially other components that share a similar tribo-system. 
 
The project as a whole consists of two parts. The first part involves creating a model that can 
predict wear sensitive regions in a pre-defined geometry. This qualitative result will be visually 
compared to pre-existing tools that have worn down in order to validate the effectiveness of the 
qualitative model. The qualitative model could then be used as a tool to for rough checking of relative 
wear sensitivities in a proposed tool design. The qualitative part of the project mostly involves 
formulating the model so that it is known to be reasonably accurate. 
 
The second part involves bringing the qualitative model closer to reality, so that the wear 
model can be used in practice to predict the wear evolution in a given tribo-system accurately. An 
essential part in this is to determine the wear coefficient of the given tribo-system [15]. A different 
part of this task is to determine how the wear coefficient changes as a function of number of strokes 
or wear depth, allowing the model to include a variable wear coefficient which has been shown to be 
a better approximation of real wear development [16]. The quantitative results could then be used 
for various things, such as: aides for optimization studies on wear reduction, tools for planning 
maintenance schedules, or tools for predicting possible production output in a given period. 
 
The last part of this project will be to compare simulated results to practical results. The 
simulated results will be extracted from a model that has been set up to reflect what a tool has seen 
in practice, as far as is possible. Practically worn tools will be examined to determine the wear depth 
at specific points. The resulting wear depths will then be compared to the simulated wear depths. If 
the model is accurate enough, it should show a wear development that is close to the real case which 
should then mean that the model can predict wear in a satisfactory manner for a given geometry and 
tribo-system.

2. Wear and wear modelling 
Wear has been defined in many different but similar ways, the main difference between them being 
under which application it occurs. One definition of wear, under the application of sheet metal 
forming, was put forward by Moghadam [1] where he said: “Wear is defined as a progressive 
deterioration of the surface of a solid body due to mechanical or chemical action.”. Some key factors 
to point out in this definition that will be expanded upon are that wear is progressive, it implies the 
deterioration of a surface, and that it can occur by different means.  
Wear can be said to be progressive in the way that it is deleterious, and therefore not easily 
reversible and unwanted, and that it changes as a function of time [17,18]. The progression of wear 
under sliding conditions has been shown to occur in three distinct regions. First is run-in wear, in which 
asperities on contacting surfaces are worn down under a small load; followed by normal wear, which 
occurs when the contacting surfaces become smooth enough to not plastically deform under this 
small load; and lastly accelerated wear, which starts occurring when tolerable wear is exceeded 
[17,19]. Figure 8 shows a typical wear profile with the different regions marked in as a function of 
number of strokes of a process. The trends that it exhibits are that the wear rate in the two outer wear 
regions is higher than in the normal wear region, and that the normal wear region lasts longer than 
the others. 
 
Figure 8: Typical wear profile showing the (I) running-in, (II) normal and (III) accelerated wear regions. 
Taken from [16]. 
In the beginning, the surfaces in contact are relatively rough or have a large amount of 
asperities on them. The peaks of the asperities have a tendency for touching and flattening, or 
otherwise being removed, leading to the surfaces becoming less rough with time. As the wear that 
occurs in this region is caused by these asperity peaks touching and being removed, the wear rate is 
relatively high. The run-in wear region is also known as the transient wear region as the wear is non-
linearly dependent on time. It generally only lasts for a short while and often results in the surfaces in 
contact becoming smoother than initially. As the surfaces become smoother, and there are fewer 
asperities that meet, the wear rate decreases until at some point it becomes constant. This marks 
entry into the normal wear region, which is also known as the steady-state wear region. This region is 
generally dominated by adhesive or tribo-chemical wear and has a relatively low wear rate compared 
to the other regions. The steady-state region lasts the longest out of the different regions. Once the 
surfaces have worn down further and exhibit a change in geometry, or other contact parameters, the 
final wear region is reached. Entry into this final wear region generally marks the onset of tool failure 
as the wear rate picks up again and keeps increasing until actual tool failure has occurred. [20] 
In most cases, wear is something that is unwanted but impossible to fully eliminate. It is 
unwanted because it inherently means that a component that wears is deteriorating and that it will 
need to be repaired, switched out, or otherwise maintained eventually. If wear is allowed to exceed 
tolerable limits, i.e. to enter the accelerated wear region, it implies the loss of function of a 
component. For this reason, steps are generally taken to reduce the wear rate that will be seen in the 
==PAGE:15==
 
11 
tribo-system, as well as to try to prevent severe wear from occurring. These steps often involve 
choosing tool materials, geometry, and process parameters that help reduce wear. An example of the 
latter was seen when Christiany and Groche [15] found that a 10% reduction of tool load translated 
into a 14-fold increase in the tool life under lubricated sliding conditions. They attributed this to the 
lower load not managing to cause the lubricant to break-down, and the entire wear mechanism 
changing to something more favourable as there was less direct metal to metal contact.  
Pressure peaks can be caused by non-uniform contact between tools and workpieces. The 
geometry of the components can mean that, in some cases, something akin to a point force is applied 
to a surface, leading to contact pressure peaks that are much larger than the applied load should imply 
[13]. These locations will then generally be where tool failure occurs [14]. This was also considered by 
Hortig and Schmoeckel [3] who state that ensuring a load is distributed uniformly on a surface would 
mean that the wear at any one point could be minimized. According to Eriksen [21] this could require 
a rather complex tool geometry and therefore might not be attainable in every case although steps 
could be taken towards it if it were possible to determine the wear sensitivity in a given tool geometry. 
Wear occurs by different means, depending on the tribo-system it occurs under. According to 
Hutchings and Shipway [22], the three main factors that should be considered in relation to the 
contact conditions that are expected for a forming process are: 
(a) Stresses and damage: Sliding contact leads to normal stresses and frictional stresses in 
surfaces which can result in mechanical damage by plastic deformation. This can occur at 
the asperity level, where an asperity on one surface deforms an asperity on the other; or 
at a more macroscopic level. 
(b) Thermal effects: Sliding contact means that frictional work is released as heat. This can 
cause an increase in temperature in the macroscopic components and for their 
mechanical properties to be affected, or asperities on the surfaces of the components can 
see a rise in temperature which can lead to a higher driving force for diffusion. 
(c) Chemical reactions and interactions of the surfaces: Surfaces of tool components will 
initially have a layer of some oxide, or other inert material, as an outer layer. This layer 
prevents the surface from reacting further, which would otherwise mean that it forms 
more oxides. Under sliding conditions, fresh surfaces are constantly exposed as the sliding 
action removes this outer layer, meaning that the reaction can continue.  
These three factors combine in various ways to form the different underlying wear 
mechanisms, which are broadly divided into four base categories. They are: (i) abrasive wear, (ii) 
adhesive wear, (iii) tribo-chemical wear, and (iv) surface fatigue [1]. According to Groche et al. [23] 
any wear will usually occur as a combination of these four basic wear mechanisms. Some examples of 
combined wear mechanisms are (v) erosion, (vi) fretting, and (vii) galling [24]. 
(i) 
Abrasive wear occurs between two surfaces that move relative to each other under a load. 
It is described as a hard surface wearing down a softer surface through cutting or ploughing 
action. Abrasive wear is generally classified as either two-body or three-body, depending on 
the interface between the surfaces. If only the two surfaces are involved it would be two-
body wear, but if a particle is trapped between them it becomes three-body wear. A rule-of-
thumb states that if abrasive wear is to occur then the harder surface, or particle, must be 
at least 20-30% harder than the softer, worn surface. [20] 
a. Two-body abrasive wear requires that the harder surface have appreciable roughness. 
Asperities on the harder surface can then penetrate the softer surface on a micro-scale 
and proceed to cut and remove material from the wear track, leaving scratches; to plough 
it, leaving the material to the side of the wear groove; or in the case of a brittle material 
that is being worn to crack it, where the material cracks in the subsurface region around 
the wear groove. This form of wear generally involves a relatively high wear rate, as the 
asperities of the harder surface are fixed and therefore imply a higher amount of plastic 
deformation in the softer surface. [20,25] 
==PAGE:16==
 
12 
b. Three-body abrasive wear requires that there is a particle trapped in the interface 
between the two moving surfaces. The particle can then cut the wearing surfaces, causing 
material to be lost. As the particle in this case is free to rotate, it causes less plastic 
deformation in the surfaces and therefore involves a lower wear rate. The particle that 
gets trapped can originate from any number of places, such as dust from the air, loose 
wear particles from adhesive wear, or from corrosive wear products. In many cases, the 
particle consists of a mechanical alloy made up of the materials that the wearing 
components are made of and outside contaminants. It can therefore be harder than 
either surface, often being able to wear both of them. [20,25] 
(ii) 
Adhesive wear is the gradual transfer of material between two surfaces that are in contact. 
It is the most fundamental type of wear and is always present to some degree. While it can 
be minimized through careful material selection and process parameter choice, it cannot be 
fully eliminated. [25]  
 
Adhesive wear involves asperities on opposing surface meeting and adhering to one 
another, and then splitting in a location different from the adhered interface. Due to 
mechanical alloying between asperities, strain-hardening of them, or excessive contact 
pressures, splitting the actual interface between the asperities becomes energetically 
unfavourable. The split will therefore occur elsewhere. Wiklund et al. [26] described it as an 
accumulation of plastic strain reaching a critical limit, at which point the material separates 
at an interface. The new interface will be within either surface, causing some part of one 
surface to transfer to the other. This leads to build-up of material on one surface, potentially 
leading to scratching of the other surface (galling); or for the transferred material to be 
knocked loose along with a part of the substrate surface, forming a wear particle. [25] 
 
Adhesive wear occurs due to the diffusion of material of one surface into the other. 
As such, there are some ways to minimize the risk of adhesive wear that are based on 
reducing the amount of diffusion that can take place. This can be done by choosing materials 
in a pair so that they do not form a good diffusion couple, making sure that the temperature 
in the interface does not rise excessively, or by ensuring that the surfaces involved have 
some sort of boundary layer separating them [25]. A special case arises if at least one of the 
surfaces is brittle. Due to the asperities touching without being able to plastically deform, 
they will instead be put under tensile strains. This can lead to rapid crack formation and 
propagation which eventually causes flakes of the brittle surface to break off [20]. Burwell 
[25] proposed a a rule-of-thumb for adhesive wear occurring in sliding contact, in that if 
contact pressure was prevented from exceeding a third of the wearing surface hardness, 
material pick-up would be prevented from becoming more severe. [25] 
(iii) 
Tribo-chemical wear is generally thought of as requiring both corrosive conditions and 
rubbing contact. It begins with friction increasing the temperature in an interface between 
two surfaces that meet under sliding contact conditions. Depending on the environment 
around the surfaces, formation of corrosion products can then start in the interface. 
Subsequent rubbing removes the corrosion product, exposing virgin material to the 
corrosive conditions and starting the cycle anew. An alternative way for this surface 
exposure to occur was proposed by Groche et al. [23] who found that when a critical 
thickness of corrosion products is reached it flakes off by itself and can form loose wear 
particles. This means that rubbing contact conditions are not strictly necessary for tribo-
chemical wear to occur, although the corrosive conditions are. [1,20,23,24] 
 
The corrosion products that are formed in metals can be varied, which means that 
different conditions and circumstances can lead to different wear rates. In humid air, the 
corrosion products are generally hydroxides or hydrates which adhere very loosely to the 
surface they form on. In industrial settings, the corrosion products mainly consist of 
chlorides or oxychlorides due to fumes in the air. Corrosive wear in dry air generally involves 
the formation of oxides. Straffellini [20] refers to this specific type of corrosive wear as tribo-
==PAGE:17==
 
13 
oxidative wear. Tribo-oxidative wear is a combination of oxidative and mechanical action at 
asperity interfaces. It can occur under high sliding speeds and will then occur due to direct 
oxidation of asperities causing a build-up of an oxide layer that will later spall off, or under 
low sliding speeds where it is normally preceded by wear particle formation by adhesive 
wear. [20] 
(iv) 
Wear by surface fatigue occurs as cyclic loading of a surface causes cracks to form and 
propagate. It generally involves rolling contact conditions, as opposed to the sliding 
conditions that the other primary wear mechanisms involve. Burwell [25] describes it as 
cracks starting to form a short distance under the surface of a component, and then 
propagating parallel to the surface for a long way until they eventually change direction and 
reach the surface. This causes large flakes of material to be removed which often renders 
the worn component unusable. It can be likened to the cyclic stressing of machine elements 
causing fatigue failure under small loads, as it occurs under relatively low plastic 
deformations [20,25]. One way to help prevent surface fatigue wear is to use lubricants. It 
can help reduce the pressures that surfaces in the process feel, and therefore reduce the 
tendency for wear by surface fatigue. [1,20,23,24,25] 
(v) 
Erosion involves a surface and a medium. It occurs when a solid, or liquid, particle suspended 
in a medium impacts the surface, weakening it or removing material from it. In the case of 
solid particles, this wear mechanism is very similar to three-body abrasive wear in the way 
that a harder material is wearing down a softer material by plastically deforming the surface 
of it. The rate of wear under erosion is highly dependent on the angle of impingement and 
the velocity of the particles. In the case of liquid particles causing erosion, wear occurs 
mainly due to surface fatigue, as the loads being applied are relatively low. This is also known 
as cavitation erosion. [20] 
(vi) 
Fretting involves two surfaces in near static contact that experience some sort of mechanical 
vibration. The vibration causes oscillatory displacement to occur in the contact surfaces, the 
amplitude of which will decide what wear mechanism occurs. If the amplitude is very low, 
any deformation is elastic, and the wear is generally negligible as there is no sliding. If the 
amplitude is a bit higher it can lead to micro-slip, or tiny amounts of sliding, which can in 
turn cause tribo-oxidative wear. This wear is relatively low but can result in crack formation 
and propagation which will lead to larger scale wear with time. If the amplitude is very high 
it can cause the whole interface to undergo sliding, which in turn causes wear along the 
whole contact interface. The wear rate increases as a function of amplitude until reaching 
steady-state. [20] 
(vii) Galling involves two surfaces in sliding contact. One surface will transfer material to the 
other by adhesive wear, known as pick-up, which in some cases will remain adhered to the 
second surface. After some amount of material has built up on the second surface, it can 
start to scratch the first, scoring it. Together, this adhesive and abrasive form of wear are 
known as galling.  
 
There exist many more wear mechanisms than the ones discussed here, although all of them 
are based on the four primary mechanisms of wear. They will not be discussed, as all of them 
combined are generally only thought to account for about 5% of total wear in tribo-systems [25]. 
Fretting, erosion and galling are also minor wear mechanisms, but as they are often quoted as being 
more prevalent, they are discussed here. [20,25] 
2.1. Wear in sheet metal forming 
Sheet metal forming is a field within metal forming that can refer to a number of different processes 
that all involve a workpiece that has a thickness that is much smaller than its other dimensions. In 
general, the relative movement of the tools and workpiece surfaces that are in contact means that 
sliding contact conditions prevail. Because wear is highly dependent on the contact conditions, this 
means that the prevailing wear mechanisms in sheet metal forming will be ones that are dependent 
==PAGE:18==
 
14 
on there being sliding contact between the wearing surfaces. Tools used in sheet metal forming are 
generally designed to fully form a component in one stroke. After each stroke, there will therefore be 
a renewal of components. This is known as an open tribo-system and is characterized by this material 
flow in-and-out of the system. This means that contact is always between a hard and smooth tool, 
and a relatively soft and rough workpiece. On the other hand, a closed tribo-system involves the same 
two surfaces that are in contact, giving them time to wear each other smooth in the run-in wear region 
and then transitioning into the steady-state wear region. The presence of this open tribo-system leads 
to more severe tribological conditions and makes the prediction of the wear evolution considerably 
more difficult than it is for closed tribo-systems, necessitating that finite element analysis be used. 
[27,28] 
 
Over-all, the wear mechanisms seen most often in sheet metal forming are adhesive wear, 
abrasive wear and galling [28]. The following discussion is meant to explain how likely the different 
wear mechanisms discussed above are to cause wear to the tool surface, i.e. for material to be 
removed from the tool surface. 
• 
For erosive wear to occur, there must be some particles suspended in a medium at play. Sheet 
metal forming typically does not include the sort of medium that would be required. 
Therefore, erosion is very unlikely to occur under sheet metal forming conditions.  
• 
In the case of fretting wear, two surfaces that are stationary and in contact are required. 
Furthermore, the surfaces must be affected by vibrations in some way that cause oscillations 
in the contact. Tool surfaces in sheet metal forming are typically not stationary and in contact 
in such a way that they experience vibrations that can cause oscillatory movement. It is 
therefore very unlikely that fretting wear is a prevailing wear mechanism.  
• 
Galling involves sliding contact between surfaces and requires that there is adhesive transfer 
of material between them. As the workpieces in sheet metal forming are renewed, there is a 
potential for small amounts of material transferring between the surfaces, causing build-up 
of material on the tool surface which later leads to scoring of workpiece surfaces. Galling is 
generally accepted as being one of the largest causes of wear related problems in sheet metal 
forming, even leading tool makers to looking into materials that are especially resistant to it 
[21,26,27]. It is taken to be a likely candidate. 
• 
Wear by surface fatigue requires that there be cyclic loading of wearing surfaces, and that 
rolling contact conditions be ruling. In sheet metal forming, contact generally occurs under 
sliding contact conditions, and relatively slowly compared to other applications where wear 
by surface fatigue might be ruling. There is therefore not enough time for cyclic loads to build 
up cracks and propagate them before another wear mechanism has caused tool failure. Wear 
by surface fatigue is therefore not likely to be a primary wear mechanism. 
• 
Tribo-chemical wear requires that there is a potential for corrosion in the contact interface. 
Nowadays, where air-filtration is being used in manufacturing facilities, it is less likely to see 
things such as chlorides in the air. It is therefore unlikely that chlorides or hydroxides form. 
However, the potential for tribo-oxidative wear still exists. An increase in temperature in the 
contact interface could mean that a driving force for the formation of oxides exists and that 
oxides can form. Tribo-oxidative wear is therefore a candidate for being a problem in sheet 
metal forming but might require certain conditions to occur.  
• 
Abrasive wear involves a hard surface causing wear to a softer surface. In sheet metal 
forming, the contact is generally made between a relatively hard and smooth tool and a 
relatively rough and soft workpiece. Therefore, two-body abrasive wear of tools is unlikely to 
be a primary wear mechanism in sheet metal forming, unless there is significant strain-
hardening occurring at the edges of a workpiece during the process. If particles are trapped 
in the contact interface, they can start to wear down the tool surface through three-body 
abrasive wear. As there is discontinuous contact between the tool and workpiece, and the 
workpiece is renewed after every stroke, it is unlikely that three-body abrasive wear is a 
primary wear mechanism; unless wear particles manage to adhere to the tool surface but are 
==PAGE:19==
 
15 
later knocked free as a new workpiece is formed. All in all, abrasive wear may be a problem in 
specific cases.  
• 
Adhesive wear requires that there is material transfer between surfaces. Adhesive wear is 
generally the first step in galling and is a big problem within sheet metal forming. Stainless 
steel is especially prone to transferring material to tool surfaces, so certain precautions often 
need to be taken if stainless steel is to be formed [21,27]. Adhesive wear is very common in 
sheet metal forming, although the tool is normally not worn as it is harder than the workpiece. 
If a layer of material is built up on a tool surface and flakes off, it can take a part of the tool 
surface along with it thereby causing wear of the tool [23]. The possibility of a softer material 
wearing a harder material through adhesive wear has also been shown, so it should not be 
discounted as a primary wear mechanism [29].  
 
What is common with all of the wear mechanisms, whether they occur in sheet metal forming 
or not, is that they impact the tool-life of a tool. Tool-life is a metric used to describe the total amount 
of components that a tool can produce before it needs to be replaced or maintained. A tool that has 
reached the end of its tool-life cannot be used further without risking damage to workpieces it is being 
used on, to the equipment that holds it, or to personnel around it in extreme cases. This is still a rather 
broad definition as the specifics will vary depending on the tool in question and the application it is 
used for. As has been mentioned before, in the case of this project the tool-life in question is defined 
as the number of components that can be produced until wear causes penetration of the surface 
coating that has been applied to the tools, or otherwise defects to occur in the formed components. 
Using the tools further would cause severe wear to take place which would in turn mean that 
produced components are bound to be out of tolerance, or otherwise defective. Tool-life can be 
extended in different ways, such as by using lubricants or by using tools under less demanding 
tribological conditions. This is not always possible though, so other methods must be employed. These 
other methods might include maintenance or enhanced tool design with extended tool-life in mind. 
This is where wear modelling can be a great asset. [30] 
2.2. Modelling wear 
Wear is a complex thing and it is so especially in sheet metal forming [28]. If wear is to be predicted 
accurately, and in reasonable time, an appropriate numerical model must be used [2,31]. Beyond 
being accurate and reasonably quick, any wear model that is to be used in an industrial setting should 
also be easily realizable, simple and reliable in testing. This would ensure that anyone could use them, 
anywhere [15]. At the time of their writing, Hsu et al. [32] found that there was no wear model in 
existence that could predict wear reliably, based solely on input material data. This is likely due to the 
sheer number of different parameters that affect how wear develops and progresses, many of which 
are not strictly material dependent. This large number makes it exceedingly difficult to measure or 
otherwise characterize all of the parameters for a chosen material pair. [15,32] 
 
In the hands of a tool designer, a wear model could be used as far back as during tool design. 
It would enable the tool designer to predict how wear affects a proposed tribo-system and could then 
be used as an optimization tool for wear reduction. In the hands of an operator, a wear model could 
be used to plan for periods of uninterrupted processing and would allow for more accurate predictions 
of the maximum production output in a given period. Furthermore, it would ensure that the operator 
has a better idea of when the tool should be taken out of production to prevent it from either 
degrading produced components or catastrophically failing, and potentially endangering personnel 
and equipment in the vicinity. If there is a separate maintenance team that handles repairs on the 
tool, an accurate wear model could be helpful for forming comprehensive maintenance schedules in 
order to minimize production stops. In all, having an accurate wear model that can reliably predict 
wear, and show how it behaves and develops, can be very useful in different applications. Most, if not 
all, of these applications will be aimed at reducing lost production and minimising tool costs and can 
therefore be beneficial at any scale. [2]

As is the case with most models, this one requires a set of input parameters. From equation

It is clear that wear is dependent on geometry, as geometry defines contact conditions. The 
geometry of tools will change after every stroke due to wear. A realistic approach towards modelling 
wear would then be to simulate each and every stroke, re-defining the contact conditions in between 
strokes to reflect the new geometry. As tools in sheet metal forming are generally made with a tool-
life of tens of thousands of components in mind, this is not feasible to do in practice. Instead of 
simulating each stroke by itself, and assuming that wear can only be cumulative, many strokes can be 
simulated using one run of a model [35]. Hoffmann et al. [36] showed the difference between using a 
more conventional approach, where all of the applied strokes were applied in one model, versus 
arriving at the same total number with intermittent geometry updating. They found that using this 
geometry updating scheme resulted in a much more realistic development of wear. [35,36] 
It is well recognized that the wear coefficient of a system is variable and a function of the 
loading duration. In the case of metal forming, it is often thought of as being dependent on the number 
of strokes that the system has seen. Archard’s wear model does not have any expression that reflects 
this time- or stroke-dependence, which is often pointed out as being one of the main reasons for it 
not being an accurate approximation of wear in a practical scenario [23,31]. The basic model has been 
shown to compare well with practice qualitatively [17,31]. One application of the basic model has 
therefore been in indicating wear sensitive regions in tool-workpiece interfaces. The model has been 
shown to give more quantitatively accurate results if it is extended [23,31]. [16,17,18,23,31,37]

The next problem then becomes to apply the determined wear coefficient to the contact 
interfaces. As wear progresses, its behaviour will change in areas that have been worn. This is intuitive 
as the changing geometry due to increased wear depth will impose different contact conditions and 
therefore a difference in wear behaviour. It is thus important that the wear coefficient that is to be 
applied to the contact interface is implemented in such a way that it also takes into account the 
difference in cumulative wear depth between different areas of the tool surfaces.  
By default, LS-DYNA applies a wear coefficient to a contact interface irrespective of where on 
the surface contact is made. This might mean that an inappropriate wear coefficient is applied to some 
parts of the tool surface. LS-DYNA has a functionality called ‘Scale factor curve ID’ which is attached 
to the wear modelling functionality and allows the user to scale the wear depth in the current 
simulation as a function of total wear depth [35]. 
A lower wear coefficient will result in a decreased wear volume. This can be seen by 
considering equation (1), which shows that there is a linear relationship between the wear coefficient 
and the wear volume. A scaling of the wear depth that is found, assuming that other dimensions of a 
wear scar that is formed are constant, will therefore mean that the wear depth is scaled linearly along 
with the wear coefficient. Future work might be to determine how the other dimensions of a wear 
scar change as a function of its depth, but that is not done here due to time constraints. 
It should therefore be possible to apply a single initial wear coefficient over the entire surface, 
and then scale it down so that it matches the wear coefficient that is found through experimentation 
for a given wear depth. This will be discussed and applied in section 3.4.3. 
2.2.2. Wear coefficient 
In Archard’s wear model there is an expression for a wear coefficient, a dimensionless factor that can 
be thought of as the probability of contact forming a wear particle per sliding length [2,32]. It is highly 
dependent on the material pairing and the surface conditions of the components in question as well 
as a number of other parameters, varying by a factor of more than 105 between tribo-systems [17].  
 
This wear coefficient is used in place of a range of other parameters that might influence the 
wear behaviour to some degree. Some of the parameters that are accounted for by the wear 
coefficient are: the surface roughness of the mating pair, the specific material compositions, and the 
relative speed of contacting surfaces. The wear coefficient is therefore usually determined 
experimentally, and in many cases through significant effort [15]. The wear coefficient of a tribo-
system is generally unique to that tribo-system due to it depending on such a large number of 
parameters. In order to determine the evolution of a wear profile in a certain tribo-system, it is 
therefore necessary to first determine how the wear coefficient behaves in that specific tribo-system. 
 
Practically, a wear coefficient will change as a function of time [17]. In order to arrive at an 
accurate quantitative conclusion, the wear coefficient applied in the model should therefore follow 
the wear behaviour of the tribo-system closely. Ersoy et al. [16] realized this changing behaviour by 
thinking of the wear coefficient as being a function of the number of strokes that a tribo-system had 
seen. That way each number of strokes that has been performed will relate to a specific wear 
coefficient that can then be implemented to ensure that the wear behaviour seen in a model follows

the one seen in practice. Following their methodology, they found that quantitative results were much

2.2.3. Wear testing 
The wear coefficient of a tribo-system must usually be found experimentally [15]. In most cases it is 
determined by using a simplified laboratory test that is tailored towards a specific tribo-system. There 
are many different tests that can be performed in order to find a wear coefficient for sliding contact 
conditions, so care must be taken that the test that is chosen can be brought as close to practical 
conditions as possible. Some factors to consider include the material of the components, the test 
geometry, the applied load and contact pressure, the sliding speed, and the test environment. By 
quantifying the wear volume for different values of sliding length it becomes possible to determine a 
wear coefficient at certain points along a wear profile. It is due to being able to determine total sliding 
lengths despite complex contact conditions that it is at all possible to use wear models to predict tool 
wear [13]. [13,20] 
Straffellini [20] defines four different types of wear tests. There are field tests, in which a tribo-
system is tested on a real process line and would be performed in-situ in a manufacturing setting; 
bench tests, in which a part of a process line is set up separately in a laboratory and experiments are 
performed on that part of the system; tests on single components in which a single component is 
tested separately from any other part of the larger system; and simplified tests, in which an attempt 
is made to simulate a certain wear mechanism with simplified components. It generally goes, from 
field tests to simplified tests, that cost decreases while the control over individual test variables 
increases. An example is that in a manufacturing setting there might be all sorts of outside influences 
that is hard, if at all possible, to account for. This also means that, in the same order, the level of care 
that must be spent on ensuring that the test reflects the actual conditions increases. [20] 
==PAGE:23==
 
19 
 
As the test is simplified, and made cheaper and quicker to perform, care must be taken that 
it stays true to the original system that it is meant to simulate. Hsu et al. [32] proposed that, at the 
very least, simulated wear should have the same form as actual wear found in the system that is being 
simulated. Straffellini [20] stated that the simulated system should exhibit the same wear mechanism 
as the real system. This is made somewhat easier to realize as the relevant parameters are easier to 
control, or otherwise account for, on a smaller scale such as in a simplified laboratory test. What this 
means is that the results of the test will be easier to interpret and have been shown to be reproducible 
in many cases [15]. Even though care is taken to ensure that a laboratory test simulates a tribo-system 
properly, care must also be taken in interpreting the results and transferring them over to the real 
system. [15,20,32] 
 
The world of wear testing offers a lot of different tools for performing wear tests with. Despite 
this, some tests do not have any set standards as to how they should be performed [38]. Some 
examples of simplified laboratory tests include pin-on-disc tests, disc-on-disc tests, and pin-abrasion 
tests. Each of these simulates different contact conditions, and therefore includes different wear 
behaviour. Whichever test is chosen, it is important to verify that the prevailing wear mechanism 
found in the test is the same as the one seen in the process. Without doing this, there can be no 
certainty that the found results are in any way valid for the real tribo-system. [20,32,38] 
 
Pin-on-disc tests involves two components, a pin and a disc. The pin can have a variety of 
different geometries and should be designed to reflect the contact conditions found in practice as 
closely as possible. The disc is a flat disc that has been polished and surface treated so that it resembles 
a component surface in the tribo-system that is being simulated. The pin is pressed into the disc under 
some load as the disc rotates with a specific speed, causing the pin and disc to wear. This test can be 
used to simulate either adhesive or tribo-oxidative wear, or a mixture there-of. [20] 
 
Disc-on-disc tests involve two discs that touch along their circumference, leading to non-
conformal contact conditions. They are most often used to simulate rolling-sliding contact, and by 
varying parameters it is possible to carry the test out at different percentages of sliding. This set-up 
can be used to simulate wear by surface fatigue.  [20] 
 
Pin-abrasion tests involve a similar set-up as pin-on-disc tests. A pin is put into contact with a 
rotating disc, which in this case has an abrasive surface. In order to prevent wear debris from 
influencing the experiment, the pin is moved towards the centre of the disc, forming a spiral wear 
track. This test can be used to measure abrasive wear under high stresses by examining the pin 
component. [20] 
 
After having performed a wear test by some means, it is important to consider the wear track 
that has formed. This enables the determination of which wear mechanism was prevalent during the 
test and, through measurement, the amount of wear that has occurred. As stated before, this should 
be compared to the real tribo-system and care taken to make sure that the observed wear 
mechanisms are the same. Some rules-of-thumb are put forward by Straffellini [20] as to how one can 
discern which wear mechanism prevailed. 
1. If there is evidence of plate-shaped fragments that have transferred between surfaces, it is an 
indicator of adhesive wear being a prevailing wear mechanism. This can be detected either by 
optical microscopy or electron microscopy. 
2. If there are layers of compacted oxides visible on the surface, which could be seen as dark 
scales, it is an indication of tribo-oxidative wear. They can be detected either by optical 
microscopy or visual inspection. 
3. If the worn surface shows grooves, it is an indication of abrasive wear. These grooves can be 
detected by optical microscopy. 
4. If there is evidence of spallation, but little to no plastic deformation in the surface, it is an 
indicator of wear by surface fatigue. This can be detected by scanning electron microscopy. 
 
Using these rules-of-thumb, the information in Table 2 and the discussion in section 2.1, it 
should be possible to determine which wear mechanism is prevailing in the process step in practice 
as well as which wear mechanism is prevailing during wear testing. This decision would then help in 
==PAGE:24==
 
20 
choosing which wear test will be used to simulate the wear development along with which wear model 
can be used. [20,24] 
 
After a wear test is performed, the next step is generally to measure and quantify the wear 
that has occurred. This can be done in a few different ways, depending on the test that was applied. 
There are three wear measurement methods that are conventionally applied to quantify wear. They 
are based on: (a) mass loss, (b) volume loss, or (c) linear dimension loss. [40] 
a) Wear quantification by mass loss is often employed when the surface of a test component is 
irregular. Using a precision balance, and a known density of the component material, it is 
possible to find the wear volume directly from the change in component weight. 
b) Wear quantification by volume loss is used when there is a distinct wear track on the worn 
surface. By measuring the length, width, and depth of the wear track it is possible to find the 
volume loss caused by the wear. It can be done by using a profilometer or certain microscopes. 
c) Wear quantification by linear dimension is applied when the dimensions of a component are 
more important than its surface. It is possible to measure the loss of linear dimensions by 
using a microscope, a profilometer, or a micro-meter. 
 
The pin-on-disc test discussed in this section will be used to determine the wear coefficient of 
the tribo-system. This is decided as the contact conditions are similar, as well as it being a piece of 
equipment that is available to the author. In order to ensure that the same sort of wear behaviour is 
prevailing under the test conditions as is expected to be occurring in practice, visual inspection of the 
wear tracks will be performed.

3. Simulation of tool wear in industrial sheet metal forming 
This section goes into the different specific tasks of the project. Firstly, the tribo-system as a whole is 
defined, after which the ruling wear behaviour in the process step being considered will be 
determined. When that is done, it is possible to select an appropriate wear model and apply it to 
determine wear sensitive areas of the tools. Some time is spent on performing various studies to 
investigate suitable input parameters, such as the mesh size, stroke-step size, and friction coefficient 
in order to ensure that the model is numerically sound. After finding a qualitative result and comparing 
that with worn tools from practice, the model formulation will be validated using a benchmark test, 
in order to ensure that the combination of input parameters that has been determined is viable and 
can be trusted to reflect reality at all. After the model has been determined to be qualitatively 
accurate, the next step is to bring it closer to the practical case.  
 
The qualitative model is brought closer to a quantitative state in two ways, the first of which 
is by determining the tribo-system specific wear coefficient. This would have been done by using a 
methodology proposed by Yang [38], which allows for the determination of a steady-state wear 
coefficient by using transient wear data. Due to issues that came up, a more conventional approach 
for determining the wear coefficient is adopted instead. This conventional approach involves 
performing wear tests with increasing sliding distances while checking if any two points are found to 
have the same wear coefficient. If that is the case, it marks that the steady-state wear region has been 
reached and the steady-state wear coefficient can be found from those two points [39]. As discussed 
by Yang [38] this conventional approach has some drawbacks to it, such as the difficulty in discerning 
if steady-state has actually been reached. The method that is applied will be used to gather data-
points for curve-fitting. The steady-state wear coefficient as such will not be used and is therefore not 
found but will instead be estimated based on the fitted curve. This should allow for using the more 
“conventional” approach, without risking undue inaccuracies while also allowing for characterizing 
the whole wear coefficient profile. The wear coefficient profile can then be used to implement a 
variable wear coefficient behaviour in the model, which then reflects the time-dependence of wear. 
The wear coefficient will be updated at the same time as the geometry is, and the geometry will be 
updated as per section 3.3.5. This is the second way by which the model is brought closer to the 
practical case.  
 
The last part of this section will involve a comparison between the quantitative results, and 
practically worn tools. Tools that have seen a certain number of strokes in practice are examined at 
locations of interest. After applying the same, or a similar, number of strokes to the simulated tools, 
they are compared to the worn tools. This is done by using wear depth ratios, which should at least 
give an idea of whether the wear model shows a similar wear development as is seen in the practical 
case. The sensitivity of the model to the wear coefficient is also investigated.

towards determining how a system experiences wear is therefore to form a definition of the tribo-
system being considered. A tribo-system consists of four main components: the materials that the 
tool (1) and workpiece (2) are made from, the medium (3) that exists at the interface of the tool and 
workpiece, as well as the environment (4) around the over-all tribo-system. [1]

3.2. Prevailing wear mechanism in the process step

wear mechanism which can then be used to determine which wear models are applicable in predicting 
how wear develops in the system.  
 
It should be relatively easy to determine which wear mechanism is ruling [24]. It can be done 
either by performing a parameter variation study, or by looking at the worn surface and using the 
rules-of-thumb outlined in section 2.2.3. As the process being considered occurs in a larger production 
line, it will quickly become very expensive and time-consuming to perform a parameter variation 
study. This is outside of the scope of this project and is therefore not pursued.

3.3. Employed wear model – The qualitative approach 
The model that is most often used to describe wear behaviour is one proposed by Archard [33]. In the 
field of metal forming, Hoffmann et al. [2] even go so far to state that it is the best suited model 
available for wear prediction. The equation that describes the model can be seen in equation (1), while 
the discretized form of it that is used by LS-DYNA can be seen in equation (2). As discussed in section 
3.2, the ruling wear mechanism is believed to be adhesive wear of the workpiece leaving built-up 
material on the tool surface. Archard’s model was the first quantitative description of adhesive wear 
and should therefore be a good model to base the project around [24,34].  
 
Both of the equations require a set of input parameters in order to be used to move the model 
from the analytical and to the numerical. This move is often considered the key to being able to predict 
wear behaviour in a tribo-system, and so these parameters must be determined [31]. Initially, a 
qualitative approach is taken towards wear prediction which allows for the determination of relative 
wear sensitivities in different parts of the tool surfaces. Therefore, input parameters such as the wear 
coefficient are chosen arbitrarily. Other parameters, such as the hardness of components and the 
system geometry, that it is possible to determine without experiments, are used so that the model 
has a basis in reality. 
 
 This section covers the different basic inputs for the qualitative model and how they are 
arrived at, starting with the geometry of the system and the boundary conditions that are used to 
ensure that the model represents reality. The material data used in the different material models will 
then be covered, after which the mathematical formulation used in the model is discussed. Next 
comes a number of studies that are aimed at finding an appropriate mesh size, a maximum viable 
stroke-step size, and the sensitivity of the model to friction. After the model as a whole has been 
defined, a discussion of the qualitative results that have been found along with a comparison between 
practically worn tools and the qualitative results is put forward. A short look will be taken at how wear 
depends on contact pressure and sliding distance, and a general benchmarking test will be performed 
to validate that the formulation of the model is suitable. 
==PAGE:29==
 
25 
 
LS-DYNA, using the pre-defined wear modelling capability, will always imply a wear depth [35]. 
This means that any wear that occurs will be taken as involving removal of material from the worn 
component. This is clearly an issue, as the ruling wear mechanism would imply material being 
transferred onto the tool surface, thereby causing build-up of material. The rate of material removal 
that is seen should be roughly equal to the rate of material build-up, as they are both based on a wear 
coefficient that describes the probability of a wear particle being generated. This still represents a 
problem, as the very basic change in contact conditions as a function of wear is different. Instead of a 
build-up causing material to stand out from the tool surface, a wear groove is formed. 
 
This issue will not be covered in this section. As discussed, the wear depth that forms will be 
thought of as wear build-up instead as the basic law can account for material transfer in either 
direction. This will result in any models that involve more than one simulation run being inaccurate, 
at least as to the contact conditions that are involved. This is further discussed in section 3.4.0. 
3.3.1. Geometry and boundary conditions 
The process considered in this project involves the forming of a guide vane for a chamber. The 
geometry is quite complex, involving many different curved and convex or concave surfaces. There is 
no symmetry to speak of in the tools, so they must be modelled as asymmetric 3-dimensional parts. 
In order to ensure that the geometry remains as close to the real case as possible, and that numerical 
issues that might occur due to the complex geometry have as little effect as possible, the model 
geometry is derived directly from a 3-dimensional model that has been imported into, and meshed 
within, LS-DYNA. The geometry that is used is received directly from Grundfos.  
 
In finite element analysis, a larger and more complex geometry generally means that more 
processing power is required to compute a model that is based on the geometry. Therefore, it is 
beneficial to simplify a geometry where possible. The geometry that was received was simplified to 
consist of only the immediate surfaces of the tools that contact the blank, as well as a single blank 
from a unit of ten that are otherwise attached together. The tool surfaces are made from shell 
elements, while the blank is made out of solid elements. The actual system geometry and the 
simplified geometry that is used can be seen in Figure 14. 
The geometry that was removed while simplifying the model is accounted for by using 
appropriate boundary conditions. The following boundary conditions were implemented in order to 
ensure that the conditions that are modelled reflect those seen in practice.  
1. The tools are each made from a single layer of shell elements. These shell elements are 
constrained to move only in the direction of tool travel, or along the z-axis. This ensures that 
the tool surfaces behave in the same way as they would in practice. The lower tool (blue) is 
fully constrained as it does not move in practice. 
2. The parts of the blank (green) that would otherwise be in contact with columnar guides are 
constrained so that they can only move along where the guides would be. No other part of 
the blank is constrained, meaning that elements within the blank can move and deform freely. 
3. A movement profile is assigned to the upper part of the tooling (red). It brings the tools from 
being roughly 40 mm apart to being 1.5 mm apart, which is the nominal thickness of the 
component being formed. This occurs at a constant velocity over 80 seconds.  
 
There is a 200 second holding period that follows the movement of the upper part. It holds 
the tools together with a high pressure in order to minimize spring-back in the formed part. The 
holding period does not involve any sliding between tool and workpiece and therefore, according to 
equation (1), it does not form any wear volume. Therefore, it is not included in the model at all. 
 
The boundary conditions and prescribed movements should ensure that the components of 
the model behave as they would be expected to in practice. Meshing of the geometry is discussed in 
a later section, but for now the tool mesh is made from 6/8-node shell elements and the blank mesh 
consists of quadrilateral solid elements.

3.3.3. Model formulation 
In order to minimize the time required for running the model, while also ensuring that the results are 
valid, it is important to consider how the model is formulated. This section covers the decision of using 
explicit time-step formulation, single precision floating points, and a specific mass scaling coefficient. 
 
The first thing to be considered is whether to use explicit or implicit time-step formulation 
[47]. Implicit formulation has the benefit that it can handle large time steps without risking numerical 
instability. However, due to the formulation, it can be very CPU intensive. Explicit formulation can in 
turn not handle time-steps of the same size but is less CPU intensive. Explicit formulation is used 
initially, as it is generally faster than implicit formulation and as long as the result is not unstable, the 
==PAGE:32==
 
28 
time-step is small enough. After running a few explicit models, using mass scaling to account for time-
step size, no major numerical instability is found. In order to verify that the results are not incurring 
inaccuracies caused by numerical issues, a model using an implicit formulation is run as well and the 
results compared. Figure 16 shows a close-up of the resulting force profiles from the simulations. 
There is some chatter early on in the explicit result, and massive chatter later on in the implicit result. 
However, the trend is apparent as they seem to be following one another. As the explicit method 
generally takes less time to run, it is chosen as the time-step formulation for the model.

The second consideration goes into whether to use single or double precision floating points. 
Double precision can take up to a 30% longer time to compute but is often necessary when using 
implicit formulation. Provided that there is no significant difference between the two resulting 
solutions, single precision formulation would therefore be preferred. As a starting point, single 
precision formulation is used. In order to verify that rounding errors are not resulting in large 
deviations, a model using double precision is run and the results compared. Figure 17 shows a close-
up of the resulting force profile. As can be seen, the force profiles are very similar. There are small 
differences, but they do not seem to be growing as more calculations are performed which means 
that using single precision is accurate enough. [48] 
 
The third consideration is towards the mass scaling coefficient that is used. The specific time 
step size in LS-DYNA can be modified by making use of parameters such as penalty stiffness and mass 
scaling [49], which allow for larger time-steps to be used without risking instability. A mass scaling 
coefficient of -1e-4 is used initially and results in a stable solution. In order to verify that the mass 
scaling is indeed suitable, simulations using a larger and smaller mass scaling coefficient are run. Table 
6 shows the mass scaling coefficient that is applied in each model and Figure 18 shows the resulting 
vertical force profile. It is clear that the mass scaling used in the M53 model is too large as there are 
clear signs of numerical instability in the force profile. The M50 model, although starting with slight 
instabilities around when the blank starts to deform, quickly evens out and follows the M54 profile 
relatively closely. As the M54 profile is made with the smallest mass scaling, this should therefore 
mean that the M50 model uses a suitable mass scaling coefficient.

3.3.4. Determining an appropriate size of mesh 
Any discretized model deals with the problem that it can only ever be an approximation of reality. As 
the mesh that is made to approximate a geometry is refined it becomes a closer approximation, but 
at the same time the processing power required to compute the model increases. This means that 
there will be a practical trade-off between refining a mesh enough for a model to be accurate and 
having enough time to run it.  
 
A good way of testing if a mesh is fine enough is to perform a mesh convergence study. This 
involves plotting a few different numbers of elements against a parameter of interest and should show 
that as the number of elements increases, the parameter converges toward some value. If the change 
in the parameter of interest between refinement steps is negligible, the mesh can be judged to be fine 
enough [50]. In this case, as the actual force used as a parameter value is not available, an estimated 
reference convergence value will be found using linear fitting and a 5% margin of error applied to that 
to define limits. 
 
Five different basic element sizes are defined based on average edge length and the LS-DYNA 
auto-mesher used to mesh the geometry. The tools are made from 6/8-node shell elements, having 
extra nodes along the element edges, while the blank is made from solid, quadrilateral elements. The 
blank is meshed in the same way for each model, with an average edge length of 0.5 mm. If the 
elements in the blank were refined along with the tools, it would mean that the total number of 
elements and nodes would increase so much that running the model would quickly become 
impractical without it resulting in a suitable tooling mesh. The blank mesh is also not too important 
for the process, as long as the basic geometry of the blank is captured. 
 
The parameter of interest is the vertical force found during the process, or the forming force. 
All models are set to simulate 1,000 strokes, although that does not affect the found forces [35] but 
will give an idea of the wear distribution that occurs during the processing and if the mesh is fine 
enough to capture details. 
The models are run to the same final stroke length and the vertical force profile found in each 
of them is extracted. The vertical force near the end of the process is of interest as the tools move 
along the z-axis in the system and, due to geometry, the force rises to a sharp peak at the very end 
when the blank conforms to the shape of the tooling. At that point in time, the process force is less 
dependent on the interface friction and more on the geometry, as will be discussed further in section 
3.3.6. In order to prevent the geometry from causing excessive differences in the found forces, and 
thereby complicating further processing, the stroke is stopped just before the tools reach the nominal 
blank thickness. The vertical force that is found, along with the total number of elements and nodes, 
and the CPU time required to process a given model, can be seen in Table 7.

3.3.5. Maximum viable stroke-step size 
The tooling that is available for measuring has seen 8,350 strokes. In order to verify the model by 
comparing it to practical values it would therefore be of interest to simulate 8,350 strokes being 
performed in the model. For every one of these strokes, there will be a small change in geometry. This 
would mean that in order to stay as close as possible to the real case, the geometry should be updated 
after every stroke. However, as that would require at least 8,350 separate simulations to eventually 
verify, it is not feasible to update the geometry in such a way. It is therefore of interest to determine 
the highest number of strokes that can be performed between updating the geometry without losing 
accuracy in the results.  
 
The results found in this section are not representative of the practical case, as each stroke 
forms a wear depth instead of build-up, which leads to a change in contact conditions between 
simulations. The basic idea, if material were building up instead, is the same. This section therefore 
serves as more of a proposal for an approach for determining this stroke-step size.  
 
One of the features of LS-Dyna, showcased by Borrvall [35], allows for simulating a number of 
strokes in a single simulation and then applying a cumulative wear depth to change the geometry. This 
will be used to determine the maximum number of strokes that it is viable to perform in a single 
simulation. 
The way in which the maximum viable stroke-step size is determined is by considering the 
geometrical changes incurred. As mentioned above, the changes in geometry will impose different 
contact conditions and therefore some change in wear rate. A stroke-step size that is too large will 
then inherently mean that the model misses some of these changes, the assumption being that a 
smaller stroke-step size will lead to a more realistic model. However, it also leads to needing to run 
the model more often which in turn translates into a larger amount of time required for a result. Using 
arbitrary values, a study is performed which should highlight lost wear behaviour as a function of 
stroke-step size. 
 
Figure 23: Geometry update scheme. N is the total amount of strokes that are to be performed. 
A single model is prepared using the mesh determined in section 3.3.4 and duplicated. The 
duplicates are then assigned a stroke-step size and run until reaching a common total number of 
strokes. A stroke-step size in this context refers to the number of strokes simulated in one step of 
running a model. The geometry is updated in between every simulation, as the update scheme in 
Figure 23 suggests. The evolution of the maximum wear depth is then used as a metric in order to 
determine the difference between using larger and smaller stroke-step sizes. The different stroke-step 
sizes that are used can be seen in Table 9, along with the resulting maximum wear depths found in 
the tooling after a total of 5,000 strokes and the average per-stroke wear depth. 
Table 9 does not show any indication of a pattern occurring in terms of either per-stroke wear 
depth or cumulative wear depth. The highest cumulative wear depth found in the tool surfaces as a 
function of stroke-step size can be seen in Figure 24. 
Figure 24 shows that the largest stroke-step size deviates significantly from the others. It can 
therefore be considered to be too large for use in the model. Assuming that the smallest stroke-step 
size of 250 strokes per step is closest to reality allows for the decision to be made that either a stroke-

surface coating, or otherwise material depth related parameters. However, as the actual wear rate 
has not been quantified at this point, this is also not possible.

contact pressure found there. This is consistent with what Groche and Christiany [14] found when

the intention were to try to reduce wear. This is consistent with what Hortig and Schmoeckel [3] found 
when they considered the influence of load distribution on wear. It could then be said that if an area 
of a surface sees a large contact pressure, it is much more sensitive to wear than otherwise, while an 
area that sees a larger sliding distance will not necessarily be as wear sensitive. 
3.3.9. Benchmarking test for the qualitative model 
In order to verify that the model is capable of achieving accurate results using the input parameters 
and formulation found so far, a simpler process is simulated using the same general input settings. 
The process that is simulated is a laboratory test, known as a Nakajima-test, which is normally applied 
to analyse the deformation behaviour of sheet metals. The test is performed by pressing a 
hemispherical punch into a blank until the blank ruptures, thereby allowing for the determination of 
forming limit curves for the material in question. In this case, the specific process involves balanced 
==PAGE:47==
 
43 
biaxial stretching, but the straining behaviour can be changed by changing the shape of the initial

3.4. Bringing the model closer to reality – The quantitative model 
As discussed in section 3.3.0, there is a very fundamental difference between the wear seen in the 
tribo-system, and the wear that is possible to model using the basic LS-DYNA wear modelling 
capabilities. This difference was less important for the qualitative model, as the exact contact 
conditions are not too important for the qualitative results. If a quantitatively accurate result is sought 
it becomes a bigger problem however, as the difference in contact conditions will inherently mean 
that the wear that develops in a model is different from that which is seen in practice. Any comparison 
between simulation results and practical results will therefore be based on models that have been run 
only once, as that means that the change in contact conditions does not affect the results. 
 
A secondary wear mechanism, causing wear to the tool, is believed to be removal of the build-
up by either flaking or surface fatigue. This removal is thought to be taking parts of the tool coating 
along, meaning that the tool wears down. The basic wear model described by equation (1) cannot 
include this flaking behaviour in any way, which poses another problem. This problem was not 
discovered in time, so no steps could be taken towards accounting for it. This section will instead serve 
as a proposal for an approach that can be taken, provided that the wear that a system sees consists 
of a single wear mechanism. 
 
After an accurate qualitative result has been found and the model verified to be capable of 
representing reality, the next step is to bring the model towards a quantitatively accurate result. This 
is done in two ways, the first of which is to determine the steady-state wear coefficient of the tribo-
system. As discussed, Yang’s [38] methodology for determining a wear coefficient is not applied, but 
instead a more conventional approach that has the addition of being based around curve-fitting is. 
Determining the wear coefficient of a tribo-system is a key factor in bringing a qualitative wear model 
closer to reality as each tribo-system has a wear coefficient that is unique to that system and will 
define how the wear development in the system proceeds [15]. However, modelling wear by using a 
constant wear coefficient has been shown to cause quantitative predictions to deviate from actual 
wear found in modelled tribo-systems [16]. The second way of bringing the model closer to reality is 
therefore to implement a variable wear coefficient by using the experimental data found while 
estimating the steady-state coefficient. The variable coefficient will initially be a function of sliding 
distance but using a functionality within LS-DYNA it is changed to depend on wear depth instead.  
 
This section goes into why an accurate steady-state wear coefficient is important and covers 
two methodologies that can be followed to determine one. Both methodologies make use of a pin-
on-disc test in order to form wear tracks for later quantification. The different aspects of the pin-on-
disc test that is applied will be discussed, such as the testing parameters and the components required 
to run the test. Some time is also spent on discussing what sort of compromises had to be made in 
using the test, and how they affect the results. The experimental work is then covered, including things 
such as the equipment that is used and experimental methods. After the steady-state wear coefficient 
of the system has been determined, it is possible to start looking into a variable wear coefficient.  
 
The importance of using a variable wear coefficient, that varies with wear development, is 
discussed and an experimentally determined one is found based on a curve-fit made against 
experimental data. The variable wear coefficient is then applied to a simulation by using a scaling 
profile to describe how the coefficient changes as a function of wear depth. After the simulation work 
is done, the next step is to look at the practically worn tools that are available.  
 
Some locations of interest were chosen on the tool surfaces and measured using a confocal 
microscope. Discussion on what is found on the surface is put forward. An attempt at comparing the 
experimental data with the worn tools is made by thinking of the wear depths found on each surface 
in terms of ratios. The model sensitivity to changes in the wear coefficient is then considered by 
applying an invented wear coefficient profile and comparing it to the experimentally determined one. 
 
Figure 41 shows a scheme that will be used as a guideline in executing the model. Starting

consisting of a stroke-step is run. The geometry is updated after the simulation is run, with a new wear

by these means will tend to be higher than one found in practice [38].  
Figure 42 shows how the wear coefficient that might be used in a wear model becomes too 
large as the steady-state region is reached. This being an issue is largely attributed to there not being 
a standardized testing methodology for the determination of wear coefficients. This lack of 
standardization can mean that separate researchers find wildly different steady-state wear 
coefficients for the same tribo-system [38]. Finding an accurate steady-state wear coefficient is 
important for increasing the accuracy of wear prediction because a tribo-system spends most of its 
time in the steady-state wear region. If a single, constant wear coefficient is to be used to predict 
wear, it is therefore accepted that the steady-state wear coefficient be used [16,38].

Methods of determining a steady-state wear coefficient conventionally involve performing a 
large number of tests over increasing sliding distances and attempting to determine when the wear 
evolution takes on a linear behaviour. In many cases, testing is stopped before actually reaching the 
steady-state region and so, through excessive costs relating to the large number of tests and time 
spent, an inaccurate wear coefficient is found. Yang [39] proposed an integrated wear model that 
takes the time-dependence of wear into account along with a methodology that allows for the 
determination of a steady-state wear coefficient by using transient wear data, and therefore a lower 
==PAGE:51==
 
47 
number of experiments than would otherwise be required [38,39]. Using this methodology would 
ensure that the found wear coefficient is based on the steady-state wear region, while also leaving a 
description of the wear coefficient profile. 
3.4.1.1. Methodology for determining a steady-state wear coefficient 
The Yang [38] methodology involves three steps which, when applied properly, can save up to 40% of 
testing time as well as a number of test runs compared to the conventional approach. The first step is 
to perform transient wear tests to determine constants. These involve low sliding distances, and 
therefore relatively low testing times. The second step is to use the constants that have been 
determined to predict both a steady-state sliding distance and wear coefficient. The third step involves 
verification of the predicted parameters by performing steady-state wear experiments using the 
predicted sliding distance. In order to make use of the methodology, one must also consider the 
integrated adhesive wear model that Yang [39] proposed.

3.4.1.2. Applied methodology 
The methodology that ended up being applied, due to both time constraints and issues with the 
specimens, is outlined in this section. The methodology involves using an increasing sliding distance 
and fitting the resulting wear volume data with a power-law curve. This allows for finding a steady-
state wear coefficient, provided that at least two points are within the steady-state wear region, while 
also allowing for the characterization of a wear coefficient profile that describes the system. The wear 
coefficient profile will be found by inserting the power-law curve-fit into equation (3). 
 
The sliding distances that are applied are based on the limited time available to the author, as 
well as the results of pre-testing. Initially the plan was to perform wear tests on two sets of specimens 
so the applied sliding distances will also reflect this, even if the second set of specimens is not used in 
the project. The application of this methodology will be discussed further in section 3.4.2.6. 
3.4.2. Pin-on-disc testing 
A pin-on-disc test is a simplified test and is one of many that can be used to characterize friction and 
wear behaviour in a tribo-system [20]. Optimally, the components used to perform a simplified test 
==PAGE:53==
 
49 
should mirror their practical counterparts, thus ensuring that the simulated contact conditions match 
the practical ones. However, due to the geometry required for the pin-on-disc test, i.e. a pin and disc, 
some simplifications must be made. These simplifications mean that the contacting components are 
often smaller than in practice and they may have a different over-all geometry in the interface, 
although this should be kept as close to the real case as possible. The compromises that were made 
in this project will be discussed further in section 3.4.2.3. 
Before using the pin-on-disc test, some input parameters must be determined. These 
parameters should be determined in such a way that the test resembles the specific part of the 
practical counterpart as closely as possible. These parameters are applied when setting up the 
equipment that the tests will be run on and as such must be determined before the test can be 
performed. The input parameters necessary to run the test are the sliding speed and the contact 
pressure, along with the material hardness. Due to the discontinuous contact in the process, the 
sliding speed and contact pressure are determined using finite element simulation. The first step 
towards using the pin-on-disc test will therefore be to use LS-DYNA to simulate the basic forming 
conditions of the process and to extract typical values for the sliding speed and contact pressure. The 
sliding distance in a single stroke is also found, as it will be used to translate a sliding distance into a 
number of strokes.

3.4.2.3. Compromises for the sake of practicality 
There are some limitations that are inherent to the pin-on-disc test. This sub-section covers the ones 
that are critical to account for and discusses how the test can still be used to simulate the wear 
development found in the practical counterpart.  
• 
Difference in geometry: The geometry involved in the practical contact conditions involves a 
sharp corner and a concave surface. The contact geometry used in the pin-on-disc test, 
however, involves a ball and a flat surface. As the contact pressure is not very large, relative to 
the hardness of the tool surface, there is very little penetration into the surface. This should 
mean that the exact geometry of the contacting surfaces is not all that important. No attempt 
is therefore made to take this difference into account. 
• 
Continuous contact: The test implies continuous contact between the workpiece and the 
tooling, which in practice is not the case. This can cause issues related to rising temperatures 
in the interface of the components as there is no time where the “tool” is not touching the 
“workpiece”. Pre-testing will be performed in order to see if there is any difference in the 
underlying wear mechanism. Provided there is not, the results should not be affected [20,32]. 
• 
Increasing the sliding speed: The process is performed at a relatively low stroke speed, one full 
stroke taking place over 80 seconds. The nominal sliding speed found through simulation is 
therefore also low. As the test should be performed under conditions that are as close to the 
real case as possible, it would be optimal to use the same sliding speed as is found in practice. 
Doing so could mean that a single test takes more than twenty-four hours to perform, which 
limits the total number of tests severely. The compromise that must be made here is therefore 
to increase the sliding speed to increase the number of possible tests. Pre-testing will be 
performed in order to see if this compromise affects the underlying wear behaviour. 
• 
Accounting for a difference in hardness: The hardness of the simulative workpiece is higher 
than that of the practical workpiece. A higher hardness of the workpiece will mean that the 
wear coefficient found in the contact interface is smaller which in turn means that the 
workpiece transfers material more slowly. If the wear behaviour remains the same however, it 
should be possible to account for this deviation by simply scaling the wear coefficient that is 
found as the it takes the hardness into account [52]. van der Heide et al. [29] found that wear 
behaviour remains the same as the hardness of the workpiece increases, up until the hardness 
of the workpiece exceeds that of the tool. This should therefore mean that, as long as the 
hardness is considerably lower than the tool surface hardness, the wear behaviour will remain 
largely the same.  
• 
Accounting for strain-hardening effects: In practice, the workpiece is replaced after every 
stroke which means that the workpiece material is given very little chance to work-harden, 
however the pin used in the test is not replaced. This could potentially result in the wear rate 
being affected as the hardness of the pin increases [52]. Yang [52] found that using the initial 
hardness of materials used in a pin-on-disc test gives a good enough approximation of the wear 
coefficient. Due to the relatively small contact loads that are applied, much strain-hardening is 
==PAGE:58==
 
54 
not expected anyway. No precautions will be taken to prevent the pin component from strain-
hardening, and the initial hardness of it will be used as a reference value when determining the 
wear coefficient.  
• 
Contact load: The nominal contact load to apply to the surface in the pin-on-disc test is 0.5 N 
[53], however the smallest load that is available is 1 N. This means that contact pressure applied 
to the surface is around 643 MPa instead of the 515 MPa that was found to be the highest 
contact pressure to occur in a stroke. The contact load being higher than nominally will mean 
that the wear that is found is increased [15]. However, as long as the 1/3 of material hardness 
threshold is not crossed, the severity of the wear should remain similar [25]. It should therefore 
be possible to scale the wear coefficient as long as the ruling wear mechanism does not change. 
• 
Surface roughness: In order to perform a comparison between test results and practically worn 
tools, it is very important that surfaces are the same. On receiving the samples, it is found that 
they are much rougher than the tool surfaces, as well as having surface textures that are not 
found on the tools. This means that any comparison that will be drawn between the worn tools 
and simulated results based on these experimental findings is doubtful at best. This cannot be 
accounted for, even if the underlying wear mechanism is the same, as it changes the wear 
development too much [15]. 
3.4.2.4. Using the pin-on-disc test for wear coefficient determination in sheet metal forming 
Sheet metal forming generally involves using the same tool through many strokes, but a virgin 
workpiece for every new stroke. This means that the wear development is only on one surface, while 
the other is renewed after each stroke. The pin-on-disc test assumes that the surfaces involved wear 
down at the same time, and under continuous contact, as the test uses only one disc and one pin. For 
this reason, the pin-on-disc test might be not the most suitable method for determining the steady-
state wear coefficient of the tribo-system [29,37].  
 
An alternative test for determining a wear coefficient in sheet metal forming was suggested 
by Gåård et al. [37] where they used what they call a slider-on-flat-surface test. This test is based 
around using a small sheet of work-piece metal in such a way that a relatively large sliding distance 
can be realized. Gåård et al. [37] used a one square meter sheet of metal to realize a total sliding 
distance of a kilometre by performing a stroke of 1 meter, and then lifting the tool and bringing it back 
to its starting point before moving it one millimetre away from the already formed wear track. This 
allows for a closer approximation to the conditions that would be seen in the real case of sheet metal 
forming, as the tool always sees fresh material, as well as it being given a chance to cool down between 
“strokes”. According to Gåård et al. [54] the slider-on-flat-surface test shows excellent agreement with 
experimental and industrial results. It may be better suited to simulate an entire wear profile, 
including varying wear mechanisms as a function of sliding distance, but would also be a very good 
tool to have for determining the wear coefficient in a sheet metal forming tribo-system. 
 
The pin-on-disc test is the only one available for this project and is therefore the one that is 
used. It should be possible to arrive at accurate results despite the differences discussed above [38]. 
3.4.2.5. Quantification of wear and equipment 
The wear coefficient of a tribo-system can be determined if the parameters shown in equation (3) are 
known. One of these is the volume of wear, or the amount of material that has been removed from, 
or added to, a surface. As discussed in section 2.2.3, this can be realized in a few different ways. Due 
to the components being used in this test being small, and that the wear track itself should be well 
defined, wear is quantified by volume loss. Going by the assumption that wear will be uniform in the 
whole wear track allows for measuring points along it in order to determine the total wear volume. 
This assumption is made as the contact conditions are the same along the entire track, as well as the 
sliding speed and contact pressure. There are two ways to realize wear quantification by volume loss 
that are accessible to the author of this project, contact profilometry and confocal microscopy [40].  
 
Contact profilometry is often used when surface roughness is to be determined. It involves 
dragging a stylus along a surface to determine surface height along a line. One of the main drawbacks 
==PAGE:59==
 
55 
of contact profilometry is that it is limited to detecting the height along the line that the stylus is 
dragged along, generating only one profile per measurement. Another huge drawback of this method 
is that there is little to no traceability of measured results, and they are very hard to reproduce. There 
is a good availability of profilometry equipment at DTU, as well as the equipment being relatively easy 
in use for small samples which would allow for generating a number of profiles. It is therefore 
considered a viable option for quantifying the wear volume. [55] 
 
Confocal microscopy can be used to create a sharp image of a specimen that includes a 
variation in height. It does this by excluding information that does not come from the focal plane of 
the microscope. Through stitching and stacking images, it is then possible to re-create the surface of 
a specimen down to vertical features of a few nanometre in height. The biggest benefit of using 
confocal microscopy is that an entire wear track can be imaged, and the data saved, allowing for 
accurate and traceable wear quantification. There are two confocal microscopes that are available for 
this project, one that is based on white-light and another that is based on blue laser. The white-light 
confocal microscope is the Alicona InfiniteFocus G4, shown in Figure 56, which has a vertical resolution 
of down to 10 nm [56]. The laser confocal microscope that is available is the Olympus LEXT OLS4000 
[57]. A picture of the LEXT with a specimen being measured in it can be seen in Figure 57. Both of the 
microscopes mentioned here output files that can be opened using MountainsSPIP, a software 
package developed for processing and analysing images at nano- and microscale. [56,57,58]

3.4.2.6. Experimental work 
The pin-on-disc test is performed using a tribometer from CSM Instruments. It includes various 
features that help with conducting friction and wear tests, enabling testing that is compatible with 
DIN 50324. The tribometer that was used to conduct the tests can be seen in Figure 58. It outputs the 
coefficient of friction and the separation between the pin receptacle and the disc as a function of time 
or sliding distance. [59] 
 
The same experimental procedure was followed around all the tests that were performed in 
order to ensure consistency. It consists of a few steps, the first of which is to designate a name for the 
specimen being considered. Each specimen, and set of parameters, has a name assigned to it, such as 
Duplex_Spec1_Top_Outer5. Specific wear tracks can then be considered in the context of their 
parameters. After this, a program was made on the computer that is attached to the tribometer, 
where testing parameters such as the sliding speed and the track radius are input. The sliding speeds 
are either 0.4 mm/s, as that was the lowest possible speed that would work on the equipment, or 2.0 
mm/s, as it is fast enough to allow for more tests than otherwise but was shown to not be too fast 
during pre-testing. The track radii were chosen to be 1.5 mm and 3.5 mm so that it would be possible 
to perform two tests on each surface. Knowing these track radii also makes it easier to determine 
where the wear track should be located when a specimen is measured. After the program has been 
made, each component that will be a part of the test is cleaned in acetone and let dry. This is done to 
ensure that any contaminants that might be on surfaces following production or handling do not affect 
the results of the test. The components are then installed into the equipment, after which, the 
counterweights at the back of the tribometer are set up so that the loading arm itself is not applying 
any load to the disc. This ensures that the applied load is known and consistent in all tests. The 
program can then be started and run until reaching a pre-defined sliding distance, after which the pin 
component is switched out for a new one and a new track is chosen for another test. The wear tracks 
that have been formed can then be examined by using one of the measuring methods outlined above. 
 
As mentioned, the tribometer outputs parameters such as the coefficient of friction and a 
separation. It would be of interest to see if the difference between the variable input parameters 
affects the friction coefficient and build-up of material. 
 
There is always a similar trend, where the separation between the pin receptacle and the disc 
grows as a function of sliding distance. This is especially clear as the sliding distance becomes larger.

function of sliding distance for the same specimen. Judging from the growing separation and increased 
coefficient of friction, it likely that adhesive deposition of pin material to the disc surface is occurring.

After running the tests, the next step is to measure and quantify the wear that has occurred

A measuring strategy is defined in order to ensure that any measurements that are performed 
are comparable. This strategy was largely based around the time constraints that face the author. The 
strategy is to measure a slice of the surface, going from the outer diameter of a specimen, through 
the centre of it, and to the other side. This allows for at least two measurements to be done on each 
wear track, as it can be seen twice in a single slice, as well as ensuring that it is relatively easy to locate 
where the wear tracks are supposed to be based on the radii that are input in the tribometer. It would 
have been more accurate to measure the whole wear track, and then to quantify the actual volume, 
but due to time constraints this strategy is necessary. The cross-sectional area of each wear track is 
found in at least two different points, and then averaged and multiplied by the circumference of the 
wear track to arrive at a wear volume. To find the cross-sectional area of the wear track, the following 
image processing is performed.

following step, resulting in Figure 73. The next step is levelling of the surface, the result of which can 
be seen in Figure 74, so that any misalignment of the specimen surface does not affect the results. 
With a levelled surface, the next step is to extract a profile from it that is taken as representative of 
that surface. This is done by drawing a horizontal line across the surface, and averaging values at a 
constant distance around the immediate line. This averaging is performed to further reduce the effect 
of measurement noise on the results. A typical resulting profile can be seen in Figure 75. The profile 
has a very apparent shape at this point, seeming to cup downwards towards the middle. In order to

3.4.3. Variable wear coefficient 
As discussed before, wear occurs in three distinct regions. The wear coefficient of a tribo-system will 
depend on which of these regions the tribo-system is in. One way to bring a simulation closer to the 
real case would then be to implement a variable wear coefficient as a function of where on the wear 
profile a tribo-system is at a given point. If applied correctly, the wear behaviour of a simulated system 
using a variable wear coefficient would mimic the practical system much more closely. [16] 
 
The fitted curve found for the wear coefficient was a decent match for the experimental data, 
and as such it is taken to be descriptive of the wear coefficient profile. The wear coefficient can then 
be determined for any sliding distance by using equation (6), which should allow for the 
implementation of a variable wear coefficient. The next step is to determine how the variable wear 
coefficient is applied.  
 
It is important to not change the wear coefficient uniformly over a whole surface as that would 
imply changing contact conditions where that might not be the case. A functionality within LS-DYNA 
is used that allows for defining a scaling factor for wear as a function of cumulative wear depth. This 
should result in a profile that describes the wear coefficient applied as a function of wear depth.

As mentioned, the quantitative results that will be used in this comparison are based on a 
model that has only been run for 500 strokes. This is because running the model further would imply 
that the next 500 strokes would happen under contact conditions that were not seen in practice at all 
and would therefore not result in any sort of realistic comparison. 
 
The actual wear depths that were found using the quantitative model are very different from 
those found in the practically worn tools. This can be explained mainly by the applied wear coefficient 
not being accurate and the wear development therefore being different, as well as the quantitative 
model only seeing 500 strokes. The wear coefficient that was applied is likely too large, despite the 
scaling that was done to get more reasonable results. This is seen as the wear depth found in location 
1 and 4 have almost reached the same value as the wear depth in the practically worn tools. The wear 
coefficient as a function of wear depth for the first 500 strokes, as well as the wear depth found in 
each of the locations is shown in Figure 105. 
 
The highest wear depth is found in the same location in both cases despite the number of 
strokes that the tools saw being different. This is likely due to the wear that occurs there being mostly 
dependent on tool geometry, and less so on the contact conditions implied by a wear coefficient. It is 
therefore clear that, even with a low number of strokes, the most wear sensitive area on the tool 
surface is already established. As shown in section 3.4.2.1, while modelling the basic forming 
conditions found in the process, the highest sliding distance is found in location 2, as well as it being 
found to be the most wear sensitive location in section 3.3.7. This is therefore consistent with prior 
discussion as the applied load is heaviest in location 2. 
 
The second highest wear depth is found in location 1 in both cases, with the lowest wear depth 
being found in location 4. The wear depth ratio found in location 1 is larger in the quantitative model 
than it is in the practically worn tool. This is likely due to the difference in the number of strokes that 
==PAGE:78==
 
74 
have been performed. As more strokes are performed, the wear behaviour changes, which in turn 
might result in the wear depth ratios being different. This difference might also be due to the scaling 
profile that is applied. As shown in Figure 105, the scaling that is performed is very different between 
locations. As the scaling profile is a function of the wear coefficient profile, and the wear coefficient 
profile is likely not 100% accurate, it means that the difference between the quantitative model and 
the practical results becomes all that much bigger. 
 
The wear depth found in location 1 is larger than the one found in location 4. The difference 
in the quantitative model is a factor of about 3, while the difference in the practically worn tools is a 
factor of 2. This indicates that location 4 is at least twice as wear sensitive as location 4. The difference 
between the factor is likely due to both the difference in wear coefficient, how the variable wear 
coefficient is applied, as well as the difference in number of strokes. 
 
The highest wear depth that is found in the quantitative model being so high, relative to the 
rest of the surface, might affect the results of using a scaling profile. As the highest wear depth is so 
strongly dependent on the geometry, it likely means that the wear coefficient in the reference point 
is different from the rest of the surface. This approach might therefore not be suitable for 
characterizing the wear development over the whole surface. Due to the complex geometry of the 
tool surfaces, the contact conditions are different between different areas of the surface. It might 
therefore be more accurate to define a few points of interest and to derive a wear coefficient for those 
places specifically. This was not considered but might represent an interesting piece of future work.  
 
The biggest trend that is common between the quantitative model and the practically worn 
tools is that relative wear depths are found in the same locations in the model and the worn tool. This 
is indicative of the relative wear depths that form being very dependent on the process geometry, 
more so than on a wear coefficient. As the trends are common, despite the quantitative model having 
seen a much smaller number of strokes, it might indicate that a single run of the model is enough to 
determine wear sensitive spots in the tool design. This is far from precise but should give a tool 
designer a rough idea of whether the tool design that is proposed has any glaring flaws. Further work 
will need to be done, if there is interest in actually quantifying the wear development in the tools. Due 
to time constraints, this is not possible and as such the quantitative discussion ends on this note.

4. Conclusion 
The outcome of this project should be a model that can be used to predict wear in a given tribo-
system. This is approached in two ways, one being a qualitative approach and the other being a more 
quantitative approach.  
 
Before either of the approaches could be taken, the basic wear law that would be applied had 
to be known. The tribo-system of the process was defined by a few main components, which allowed 
for determining which wear mechanism is most likely to be the ruling one. The result of this 
consideration, and visual inspection of worn tools, was that adhesive wear of the workpiece causing 
build-up on the tool was the ruling wear mechanism. A secondary wear mechanism is believed to be 
responsible for actual wear to the tool, i.e. material removal, but it was not considered as a combined 
wear mechanism adds a level of complexity that would not have been possible to account for in the 
timeline of the project. Further, the adhesive deposition is believed to initiate the secondary wear 
mechanism and as such, if it is reduced, it should result in a reduction in the secondary wear as well. 
Future work might include modelling this combined wear mechanism, but that would require more 
testing to determine a wear coefficient that describes how the surface of the tool deteriorates. 
 
It was found that the model applied through LS-DYNA would always imply material removal, 
instead of the build-up that might be expected. As both factors are based on a wear coefficient that 
describes material transfer, it should mean that the wear depth that forms can be thought of as wear 
height instead. This means that any model that runs for more than a single simulation is based on 
inaccurate contact conditions and should therefore not be taken at face value. Future work in this 
might be to find a way to model the adhesive transfer of material to the tool surface using LS-DYNA 
to have a better representation of the actual contact conditions in following simulations.  
 
An approach for determining a maximum number of strokes to be performed before updating 
tool geometry was put forward. It was based on the assumption that more frequent geometry updates 
would result in a better approximation of practical conditions. The number that was chosen was found 
to be small enough that the simulation followed trends found using a lower number of strokes, but 
big enough to reduce the number of simulations that had to be run. The model used to investigate 
this implied inaccurate contact conditions in simulations following the first run, but the discussion 
should still give an idea of how to determine a suitable number of strokes to simulate in a run. Future 
work might be to apply a model that allowed for simulating adhesive transfer of material, and to check 
if this approach would work similarly there. 
 
The connection from sliding distance and contact pressure to wear was investigated. It was 
found that contact pressure has a larger influence on wear than sliding distance, as an increase in 
contact pressure will mean that the wear rate increases. The simulated wear rate increasing can be 
taken as the wear becoming more severe in practice. A reduction of contact pressure peaks should 
therefore be among the first goals if trying to reduce localized wear in a process. 
 
After determining the basic wear behaviour to be expected of the system it was possible to 
start the qualitative approach by using LS-DYNA in the context of wear depth representing adhesive 
build-up. The qualitative approach largely involved determining appropriate formulation parameters. 
The results from the qualitative model were shown to be a good match to the practically worn tools 
when compared visually. There were minor differences between the two, but due to lack of 
information on the tool surface it was not possible to account for those. The qualitative model was 
validated by using a benchmarking test which showed that the model was reasonable. 
 
Two extensions to Archard’s wear law were employed in order to arrive at a more quantitative 
result. The first of these was to determine the wear coefficient found in the tribo-system. Two 
methodologies that have been known to allow for determining the wear coefficient were outlined. 
Both methodologies make use of a pin-on-disc test to cause wear that is then quantified. Of the two, 
the one that was employed was a more “conventional” approach that was based around applying 
increasing sliding distances until steady-state was thought to have been reached. 
 
The pin-on-disc test was chosen as it involves a good approximation of the contact conditions 
found in practice. In order to find the nominal parameters to be used in the pin-on-disc test, the basic 
==PAGE:81==
 
77 
forming conditions were modelled. The sliding speed and contact pressure found there were used as 
nominal input parameters. Compromises had to be made in order to allow for the pin-on-disc test to 
be used, the biggest of which being using a sliding speed that is higher than the nominal one. The 
higher speed was found to result in a higher rate of material transfer. This was expected to occur due 
to added heat due to friction, but as the underlying wear mechanism was found to be the same 
through pre-testing it was thought to not affect the results. This was determined by combined use of 
visual inspection, LOM, and SEM EDS. Future work might include re-doing tests using the nominal 
input parameters as that should result in a wear behaviour that is closer to practice. 
 
It was found, after running a few tests, that the surface roughness of the specimens was much 
higher than nominal, which affected test results. It was not possible to account for this difference in 
any way, and as such the project was continued with the knowledge that whatever results were found 
in it would not be representative of the real case.  
 
The same experimental procedure was used when performing all pin-on-disc tests in order to 
ensure that outside influence had as little effect as possible. Confocal microscopy, along with a 
measurement strategy, was used to quantify the wear that had occurred on a specimen surface. Due 
to time constraints, the measurement strategy involved a limited amount of actual wear track being 
measured. This adds uncertainty to the results, but they were taken to be reasonable enough.  
 
The result of the experiments and measurements was a profile that describes wear volume as 
a function of sliding distance. A power-law curve fit was applied and taken as representative of the 
wear development. It could then be used in order to find a wear coefficient profile. The wear 
coefficient profile was used to form a scaling profile, that would be used in LS-DYNA to implement a 
variable wear coefficient behaviour. This variable wear coefficient was the second way by which the 
qualitative model was brought closer to a quantitative one. The wear coefficient found here is purely 
based on adhesive transfer, so future work might include deriving a second wear coefficient that 
describes how the surface of the tools deteriorates. 
 
A simulation was run using an initial wear coefficient determined by the fitted curve as the 
wear coefficient in the interface. The results were then scaled, using the scaling profile, to apply the 
wear coefficient as being variable as a function of wear depth. It could then be applied across the 
entire tool surface. The way in which the scaling profile was applied caused the wear depth to 
accumulate unnaturally. It would have been necessary to find a different approach for defining the 
scaling profile but, due to time constraints, this was not possible.  
 
The tools were measured using confocal microscopy. Due to the slow nature of the method 
locations of interest had to be chosen. Two points in the upper tool were chosen, along with one point 
in the lower surface. The relative wear sensitivity of the different parts of the surface compared to the 
most sensitive part could then be determined. This approach was taken due to the large differences 
in the simulated process and the practical process. 
 
A comparison between the quantitative model and the worn tools was attempted. Due to the 
differences in both the number of strokes, the wear coefficient, and the very basic wear development, 
the comparison could not be direct and had to be based on wear depth ratios instead. It was found 
that the major trends in the practically worn tools were largely reflected in the quantitative model. 
Some differences were seen, mainly in that the wear depths found in less sensitive locations were 
much higher relative to the reference point in the quantitative model. This is thought to be due to the 
scaling profile that was applied, and how it was defined. Future work might therefore include a 
different way of deriving the scaling profile that was used. 
 
The model sensitivity to changes in the applied wear coefficient was studied. It was found that 
a change in wear coefficient will not lead to major changes in qualitative results, although the factor 
by which the wear coefficient was changed was reflected in the change of wear depth that was found.
