Acute and sub-lethal effects of leachates from tire wear
particles on the coastal copepod Acartia tonsa
Rebecca Elisa Andreani Mejlholm
Technical University of Denmark
September 1, 2021
KEYWORDS
Copepods
Tire wear particles
Leachates
ABSTRACT
Tire wear particles (TWP) are a major type of microplastics (MP) in
the coastal ocean.
When exposed to water, toxic compounds from
TWP leach into the aquatic environment, potentially threatening ma-
rine organisms. This thesis assessed the impact of TWP leachates on
the neritic copepod Acartia tonsa, speciﬁcally, the lethal concentration
(LC50) for different life stages, and the effects of long-term exposure
to a sub-lethal concentration on bioenergetic rates.
Mortality of
nauplii and adults was 100% when exposed to leachates obtained
from 5 g TWP L−1.
Leachates were found to have highest toxicity
on the feeding stage of nauplii (27.7±3.6%), followed by nauplii I
(35.6±1.8%), adults (49.6±1.5%) and had no effect on eggs.
No
effects on the on bioenergetic rates (hatching success, egg and fecal
pellet production) or population dynamics were found after expo-
sure to an environmentally relevant leachate concentration (0.1%
leachate dilution). Further, inter-generational effect of the leachates
was found to be insigniﬁcant.
Therefore, although mortality was
measured at concentrations >100 times higher than estimated TWP
concentrations in coastal water, no effect was seen when chronically
exposing copepods at an environmentally relevant concentration.
Future studies should investigate the effect of higher concentrations
of leachates and asses potential differences in responses in other
copepod species.
1
Introduction
Plastic pollution in the worlds oceans is a
known environmental issue that has been well doc-
umented to negatively affect marine life.
It has
been estimated that more than 8 million tons of
plastics per year are discharged to the marine en-
vironment and this rate is expected to increase in
the future (Ritchie & Roser 2018). The most abun-
dant form of plastic pollution in the aquatic en-
vironment are microplastics (MP). These particles
are smaller than 5 mm and formed through chem-
ical, physical or biological processes. Their small
size makes them more bioavailable to marine or-
ganisms and the slow degradation rate leads to ac-
cumulation in the marine environment, posing a
potential threat to the entire ecosystem (Auta et al.
2017). The presence of MPs is especially relevant
in coastal areas where they tend to accumulate,
and where intensiﬁed seawater dynamics increase
the abrasion on the sea/coast line (Corcoran et al.

Tire wear particles (TWP), formed through
abrasion of car tires on road surfaces, and released
to the aquatic environment through runoff, wind
and human disposal, have been estimated to vari-
ate from 24 mg/car km (Dannis 1974) to 360 mg/
car km (Cadle & Williams 1980). These estimates,
considering the number of vehicles circulating, rep-
resent a noteworthy quantity of TWP that may end
up in the aquatic environment. Of the produced
TWP, it has been estimated that 10% end up in
coastal waters trough road run-off (Wagner et al.
2018), while 34% may reach the aquatic environ-
ment through atmospheric transport (Evangeliou
et al. 2020).
Recently, increasing focus has been directed to-
wards these fragments and the compounds that
may leach from the rubber (Capolupo et al. 2020,
Halsband et al. 2020, Halle et al. 2021). Speciﬁ-
cally, chemical additives that make up the car tires
and toxic organic pollutants that are absorbed by
the rubber, pose major risks, as they may transfer
to organisms.
These compounds, such as plasti-
cizers, UV-ﬁlters, ﬂame retardants and trace met-
als have been found to induce toxicological effects
on marine organisms.
The effects include lower
survival rate and decreased hatching rate of early
ﬁsh larvae stages (Le Bihanic et al. 2020), while
reduced growth was measured on freshwater crus-
tacean Hyalella azteca (Halle et al. 2021).
An-
other threat from TWPs, is that the plastic debris
can continue leaching chemicals for months after
it enters the aquatic environment due to the slow
degradation time of MP. Further, as fragmentation
increases, the particle surface becomes bigger and
thus leaching of potentially toxic compounds may
accelerate (Kolomijeca et al. 2020).
Copepods contribute up to 80% of the zoo-
plankton biomass, making them the dominant
group in marine zooplankton worldwide (Mauch-
line 1998).
Speciﬁcally, calanoid copepods are
common in coastal areas where they feed on phyto-
plankton and pelagic protozoa, thus creating an es-
sential link between primary producers and higher
trophic levels (Steele 1974).
Indeed, they are
a fundamental component in biogeochemical cy-
cles such as the carbon cycle, where they "ex-
port" organic matter from upper to bottom layer
of the water column by production of fecal pellet
and active vertical migration (Steinberg & Landry
2017). Copepods may assimilate potentially harm-
ful leachates which may adversely affect their vital
rates. This can potentially result in bioaccumula-
tion of harmful chemicals through the food web
and impact nutrient cycles, carbon sequestration
and productivity of the marine food web. Several
studies have documented how ingestion of high
concentrations of MP impacts copepods (Cole et al.
2019, Cormier et al. 2021), but only a few have
focused speciﬁcally on TWPs (Koski et al. 2021).
Further, few studies have investigated acute toxic-
ity of TWP on different aquatic organisms such as
microalgae (Capolupo et al. 2020) and marine and
freshwater crustaceans (Wik & Dave 2006, Halle
et al. 2021). These studies have shown reduced
growth and increased mortality on the different
species, although at concentrations that are still
not environmentally relevant.
Life-cycle toxicity
experiments on copepods have yet to be fully in-
vestigated.
As plastic discharge rates are increasing due to
increased use of plastic, and presence of MP in
the aquatic environment is expected to persist over
geological time scales (Andrady 2015), the issue
of chemicals continuously leaching from the parti-
cles is increasingly relevant. Because of this, even
though current concentrations of MP in coastal wa-
ters may be relatively low, it is crucial to under-
stand what the consequences of these pollutants
are.
In this study, the effects of car tire leachates on
calanoid copepods mortality, egg and fecal pellet
production, hatching success, female size and de-
velopment time will be analyzed. These bioener-
getic parameters are crucial when considering the
efﬁciency and magnitude of nutrient cycles, food
web energy transfer efﬁciency and particle ﬂux in
the marine ecosystem (Steinberg & Landry 2017).
Common coastal copepod Acartia tonsa was se-
lected as model organism due to their importance
in coastal aquatic environments that are expected
to have the highest concentrations of MP particles
(Paluselli et al. 2018). An acute toxicity test will be
conducted in order to determine the median lethal
concentration (LC50) of the TWP leachates on dif-
ferent life stages of the copepods. Long-term expo-

Based on the hypothesis that TWP leach harm-

following research questions will be answered:
How are the bioenergetic rates of coastal copepods

calanus copepods 2) how does chronic exposure to
sub-lethal concentrations of TWP leachates impact
the ecophysiological rates of coastal copepods and
3) to what degree can coastal copepods acclimatize
and adapt to environments with sub-lethal concen-
trations of leachates from car tire particles.
2
Methods
2.1
Studied species
The copepod Acartia tonsa was obtained from
a laboratory stock culture at the National Insti-
tute of Aquatic Resources, Technical University of
Denmark. The copepods were originally collected
from Øresund and have been in the laboratory for
more than 30 years.
The stock copepod culture
was maintained in 70 L tanks ﬁlled with ﬁltered
(0.2 µm) seawater (FSW) and provided with dim
light, following the natural diel cycle. Salinity was
28.6‰ and temperature around 16-18◦C. The cul-
ture was fed with 1 L of microalgae Rhodomonas
salina 2-3 times per week ad libitum.
Rhodomonas salina were used as food for A.
tonsa. The algae was obtained from a batch culture
grown in B1 media at 18◦C with constant illumina-
tion and at 28.7‰ salinity. The culture was diluted
2-3 times per week.
2.2
Leachate extraction
The production of leachates from tire wear par-
ticles (TWP), followed the protocol of Beiras &
Almeda (2021).
First, the outer layer of a new
car tire was fragmented manually using a stain-
less steel pneumatic milling cutter and ground to
smaller particles. Liquid nitrogen was used to ease
the grinding and slow down plastic melting dur-
ing this process. The TWP were then dry-sieved
through a metallic mesh to obtain a size fraction
of TWP<250 µm. Particle size was checked by sus-
pending a subsample of the sieved particles in a
Tween-80 0.01% solution, that ensured that the
TWP did not stick to each other, and measuring
them in a Beckmann Multisizer3 Coulter Counter.
To produce the leachate solution, 5 g of TWP
were placed into a glass bottle containing 1 L of au-
toclaved FSW (28‰). The bottles were placed on a
plankton wheel in darkness at 20◦C (1 rpm) which
allowed the particles to remain suspended during
incubation. After an incubation period of 72 hours,
the solution was ﬁltered through GF/F glass-ﬁber
ﬁlter (mesh size=0.8 µm) using a vacuum pump.
The ﬁltration did not remove nanoplastics (<1000
nm), which were therefore present in the leachate
solution.
2.3
Acute toxicity test
An acute toxicity test was carried out to asses
the median lethal concentration (LC50) of leachates
for different life-stages of A. tonsa. Different con-
centrations of the leachate solution were tested
on eggs, nauplii I, later naupliar stages and adult
copepods over the course of 72 hours, with mor-
tality as endpoint. In total, seven solutions were
tested: control solution (with FSW only), 100%
concentration (pure stock solution) and ﬁve dilu-
tions at 50%, 25%, 10%, 1% and 0.1% concentra-
tions of the pure stock solution. All tested solutions
had a salinity of 29±1‰ and pH 7.8±0.3. All tests
were run at 20◦C with dim light with 3 replicates
and 30 individuals in each replicate, without feed
and without medium renewal.

2.4
Long-term exposure to sub-lethal concentration
of leachates
Based on the calculated LC50 values for the
tested life stages, and considering the environmen-
tally relevant concentrations of TWP in coastal ar-
eas, one concentration of leachate was chosen as
sub-lethal exposure solution. The chosen concen-
tration was used to run the long-term exposure ex-
periments which, in addition to nauplii to copepod
development experiment, included a 2-generation
egg and fecal pellet production experiments (for F0
and F1) and a 3-generation hatching success (for
F0, F1 and F2) (Fig.1). Toxicity of TWP leachates
on R. salina was tested, and not considered rel-
evant in a 24h period (Page 2021), as the cope-
pods would have to feed during the course of the
experiment. The development experiment, hatch-
ing success and egg and fecal pellet production
experiments were all conducted at 20◦C in dim
light. Salinity of the exposure solution was 28‰
and pH 8±0.3.
Each treatment had 3 replicates
with the control solution being FSW without added
leachates.
To start the development experiment, adult
copepods (F0 generation) were collected by ﬁlter-
ing 4L of water from the original culture onto a 250
µm mesh and placed in 1L of FSW. The density of
adults in the sample was determined by counting
the number of adults in 10 mL (approximately 10
adults per 10 mL). Based on the concentration of
the sample, 40±6 adults were added to a 1L glass
bottle with exposure solution and algal concentra-
tion of ca. 50 000 cells mL−1. The bottles were
mounted on a plankton wheel (turning at 1 rpm)
for 48 hours. The spawned eggs were separated
from each experimental bottle by carefully ﬁltering
onto a 40 µm mesh and divided into two samples:
one for testing hatching success and one to start
the ﬁrst generation (F1 generation).
For hatching success, approximately 30 eggs
were placed in a glass Petri dish with the exposure
solution and left for 48 h. Hatching success was es-
timated as percentage of hatched nauplii from the
initial number of eggs after 48 h.
To start the ﬁrst generation (F1), 280±10 eggs
spawned by the F0 generation, were added to each
experimental bottle with exposure solution and fed
with 20 000 Rhodomonas cells mL−1. The bottles
were mounted on a plankton wheel under the same
conditions as the F0 generation. Every other day,
an aliquot of 100 mL with 25-30 individuals, was
taken from each bottle to observe the development
stage (eggs, nauplii or copepodites) and mortal-
ity using a binocular microscope. In addition, al-
gae concentration was measured and adjusted to
make sure that the concentration of feed remained
constant.
Food levels never decreased below 10
000 cells ml−1, and in case that there was a need
to add feed, this was done by adding the needed
Rhodomonas amount and leachate solution, to en-
sure that the leachate concentration remained of
0.1%. Rates of mortality during the development
stage were calculated by counting the number of

2.5
Statistical Analysis
The LC50 with 95% conﬁdence limits for eggs,
nauplii I, later nauplii stages and adult copepods,
was derived using the R "drc" package (Ritz et al.
2015).
To determine differences in effects of the tested
concentrations and compared to the control, as-
sumption of normality and equal variance of
the response variables was ﬁrst tested with a
Kolmogorov-Smirnov test using GraphPad Prism 9
software. In case these conditions were met, an
ANOVA analysis was run, whereas in the oppo-
site case, a Kruskal-Wallis non-parametric test was
used.
The effects of leachates on the response vari-
ables in the sub-lethal exposure experiment (com-
pared to control) and the differences in the re-
sponse variables between generations (F0 vs. F1
for egg and fecal pellet production, F0 vs. F1 vs. F2
for hatching success), were tested using a paired t-
test. A signiﬁcance level of 0.05 was used for all
tests. The statistical tests were all performed on
GraphPad Prism 9 (GraphPad Software, CA, United
States).

Leachates and TWP
98.3% of the TWPs were within a size range of
20 to 250µm, with the remaining 2% having a size
up to 600µm. The TWPs had a mean diameter of
57.25±57.7µm and median diameter of 47.08µm
(Fig. 3).
Initially it was planned that the chemical com-
position of the produced leachate stock solution
would be analysed.
Unfortunately, the analysis
could not be made in time for this thesis. Capolupo
et al. (2020) analysed and quantiﬁed the con-
tents of marine leachates produced from TWP us-
ing gas chromatography mass spectrometry (GC-
MS) and elemental screening for metal content.
Results showed that leachates contained signiﬁcant
amounts of organic compounds such as acetophe-
none, benzothiazole, n-cyclohexylformamide and
phthalide compared to controls.

3.2
Acute toxicity tests
Mortality of eggs and nauplii in controls was
below 10% in all replicates, corresponding to an
average of 2±1 deaths out of 30 individuals (Fig.
4 and 5).
For adults, the mortality in the con-
trols was higher (21.3±3.5%), thus 7±6 individ-
uals (Fig. 4).
Generally, mortality of all life stages, except
for eggs, increased with higher concentration of
leachate solution.
Mortality for nauplii I, later
naupliar stages and adults was <50% up to the
25% concentration of the leachate solution, and
>60% for the two highest concentrations (Fig. 4),
whereas for the eggs, mortality remained <15%
at all concentrations (Fig. 5). For nauplii I and
later nauplii stages, there was a threshold between
the 25 and 50% concentration that resulted in the
death of all the tested individuals compared to
the lower concentrations where mortality was rel-
atively low. For adult copepods, the relation be-
tween mortality and leachate concentration was
more linear. With the exception of the egg stage,
the leachate solution at 100% concentration was
lethal for all tested individuals already after 48h.

Discussion
4.1
Toxicity of leachates and environmental rele-
vance
Leachates produced with different methods may
differ in the compounds that are present in the
tested leachate solution. In Capolupo et al. (2020),
80 g TWP L−1 were used to prepare the leachates
and the particles were incubated for 14 days,
whereas I only used 5 g TWP L−1 and an incu-
bation time of 72 hours.
The lower concentra-
tion of TWP and shorter incubation time, were
chosen in accordance to the protocol from Beiras
& Almeda (2021), where concentrations ranging
from 1 to 10 g TWP L−1 were advised based on
environmental relevance, and an incubation pe-
Page 9 of 16
==PAGE:10==
R. Andreani Mejlholm
Master Thesis, 2021
riod between 1 to 3 days was deemed sufﬁcient to
obtain toxic leachates from TWP<250µm. These
choices may have impacted the composition of
the leachates, with the composition proposed by
Capolupo et al. (2020) having much higher con-
centration of certain compounds.
Further, the
leachates in Capolupo et al. (2020), lowered the
pH of the solutions, an effect that was not seen in
the present thesis. Therefore, in order to properly
determine what compounds are responsible for the
effects seen in the experiments, a screening of the
leachates used in this thesis needs to be performed.
Age of the car tires used when producing leachates
is also an important factor to keep in mind. Studies
of Halsband et al. (2020) and Koski et al. (2021)
have shown that different responses are measured
when comparing the effect of old versus new car
tires. This may be due to organic compounds that
may be absorbed to the polymer matrix or due to
partial weathering, which alters the rubber prop-
erties. For example, Halsband et al. (2020) found
that weathered car tire rubber had higher toxicity
towards copepods, possibly due to the rubber par-
ticles being more brittle and having an increased
surface area for leakage of compounds. In this the-
sis, a new, unused car tire was grinded to form the
TWP. In reality, TWPs found in the environment are
likely to come from car tires that have been used or
are currently being used. Therefore, the chemical
composition of the leachates produced from TWPs
is likely to be different, stressing the importance
of characterising the leachates used in the present
experiments.
Comparison between the chosen concentration
and other environmentally relevant concentrations
of TWPs may not be straightforward. Factors such
as initial concentration, particle size, incubation
time, temperature and salinity all affect the leach-
ing of compounds from TWPs (Kolomijeca et al.
2020). Thus, a 0.1% concentration of a 5 g TWP
L−1 stock solution is not necessarily the same as
a 0.005 g TWP L−1 leachates solution. Therefore,
when comparing concentrations, it should be kept
in mind that there may be variations between the
concentration, composition and the actual toxicity
of the compounds.
Studies have found between <1 to 500 MP par-
ticles per m3 (Enders et al. 2015) in coastal wa-
ters of the North Atlantic. In the same region, Kooi
et al. (2016) found 1.13*10−6 g L−1 of microplas-
tic fragments, while in coastal waters of the North
Sea, Lorenz et al. (2019) found between 0.1–245.4
particles per m3.
Wik & Dave (2006) estimated
substantially higher concentrations of 0.0013 g
TWP L−1 from road surface runoff. Although it is
difﬁcult to compare concentrations given in parti-
cles per volume with grams per volume, it is safe to
assume that the concentration of 5 g TWP L−1 cho-
sen for the production of leachates is well above
the observed concentrations in coastal waters.
4.2
Acute toxicity test
The results showed a signiﬁcant lethal effect
of TWP leachates at concentrations of 5 g L−1
(100% concentration) and at 50% concentration
of the pure stock solution.
Generally, mortality
increased with increasing concentration and indi-
viduals became more sensitive to the toxicity of
leachates with increasing exposure time. In other
words, the longer the individuals were exposed,
the lower was the concentration of leachate solu-
tion needed to induce mortality. Still, the differ-
ent stages tested reacted differently to acute expo-
sure, both in terms of total mortality and over the
course of the experiment. Nauplii were the most
sensitive to the TWP leachates, with the toxicity of
leachate solution increasing substantially over the
ﬁrst 24 hours.
In contrast, the lack of mortality
and successful hatching in the egg stage meant,
indicated that the leachates were unable to pen-
etrate the egg shell and thus affect the hatching
and survival of the eggs.
Similar high tolerance
of eggs has been documented for other changes
in environmental factors, such as changes in salin-
ity and presence of sulphide (Hansen et al. 2012,
Nielsen et al. 2006). The higher sensitivity of nau-
plii I compared to eggs, indicates that the leachates
killed the majority of the newly hatched nauplii I
over the course of the ﬁrst 24 hours after hatch-
ing. For the later nauplii stages, the change from
non-feeding to feeding stage may be the reason
for the increased sensitivity compared to nauplii
I. Higher sensitivity to pollutants of early copepod
life stages has been documented for several pollu-
tants and different copepod species. Pavlaki et al.
(2016) tested the effects of cadmium on adult and
Page 10 of 16
==PAGE:11==

Master Thesis, 2021
early life stages of A. tonsa, and found that nau-
plii were the most sensitive to the pollutant. Sim-
ilarly, Kulkarni et al. (2013) concluded that nau-
plii of Mesocyclops leuckarti were signiﬁcantly more
sensitive to the fungicide triphenyltin compared to
other stages.
The results of this thesis show that toxicity of
TWP leachates to adults, remained relatively sta-
ble between 49-56% of the 5 g TWP L−1 stock
solution. Other studies, found an LC50 value <5
g TWP L−1 for adult individuals, while a mortal-
ity ≤50% was found when testing lower concen-
trations (Halsband et al. 2020). When looking at
other compounds found in TWPs, speciﬁcally at
PAHs, an LC50 value of >129 µg pyrene L−1 to-
wards adult A. tonsa was estimated (Bellas & Thor
2007).
4.3
Long-term exposure
The results of the long-term exposure to TWP
leachates showed no effect on egg and fecal pellet
production rates, hatching success, development
rate, mortality and female size. The concentration
of leachates used in this experiment was 0.1% con-
centration deriving from a 5 g TWP L−1 leachate
stock solution, which is in the same order of mag-
nitude of the worst-case environmental concentra-
tion found in literature, estimated to 0.0013 g TWP
L−1 (Wik & Dave 2006).
The results showed that egg and fecal pellet
productions were unaffected when exposed to en-
vironmentally relevant concentrations of leachates
from TWP. In the experiments, females spawned
between 10 and 15 eggs per day in the leachate
solutions while in control they spawned between
5 and 10 eggs.
These values are relatively low
compared to the expected egg production rates for
A. tonsa (Støttrup et al. 1986), which could indi-
cate cannibalism by adults on eggs. In addition,
the females separated to test egg production in the
F0 generation, may not have been at the peak of
their egg production time.
Females of A. tonsa
reach the highest egg production rate over the 3-
4 weeks after maturation (Parrish & Wilson 1978),
and some of the incubated females may have been
older than that. The females in the F1 generation
on the other hand, had just matured which may ex-
plain the slightly higher egg production compared
to F0. Still, the egg production for the F0 females
was relatively low. It would be expected, that the
leachates had an effect on egg production rates, as
the presence of compounds such as Zn and PAH in
the tire wear particles have been documented to
negatively affect reproductive success of copepod
A.tonsa. Sunda et al. (1987) found that egg pro-
duction decreased at concentrations >0.65 µg Zn
L−1, while Bellas & Thor (2007) found the same
negative effect at concentrations >64 µg of pyrene
L−1 (a PAH species). The same experiments run
with higher concentrations may therefore result in
a negative effect in egg production due to exposure
to the toxic compounds found in leachates.
Fecal pellet production rate was approximately
50 fecal pellets individual−1 day−1, which indi-
cates that the copepods were well fed during the
course of the experiment, as the rate corresponded
to the expected values at similar food concentra-
tion Kiørboe et al. (1985).
Fecal pellet produc-
tion may be used as proxy for feeding of the tested
individuals.
Koski et al. (2021) found that fecal
pellet production of A. tonsa decreased when ex-
posed to leachates from 10 000 TWP L−1. The de-
crease in the fecal rate was associated with a de-
crease in the measured feeding, suggesting a toxic
effect on the copepods and their feeding rates. In
this thesis, feeding rates were not measured, there-
fore we must assume, solely based on the rela-
tively high rate of fecal pellet production, that feed-
ing behaviour was not impacted by the toxins of
the leachates. Arguably, higher concentrations of
leachates would induce a response in the copepods.
The leachates produced from environmentally
relevant concentrations of TWPs did not affect
hatching success of A. tonsa eggs, irrespective of
the generation. Further, all hatching success rates
were relatively high (>80%). This was expected
in the F0 generations, where females had spawned
in FSW and the eggs were subsequently moved
to the leachate solution.
It is known that the
egg shell acts as an efﬁcient shield towards mul-
tiple contaminants such as PAHs (Jensen & Carroll
2010) and other toxic components (Charmantier
& Charmantier-Daures 2001). Thus, no effect on
hatching in this generation would be expected.
Krause et al. (2017) investigated the effect of
pyrene exposure on hatching success of two gen-

erations of A. tonsa, and found that the ﬁrst gen-
eration had a reduced hatching success, whereas
in the second generations hatching success had re-
covered to control levels. Most likely, this was due
to the exposed females rather than the direct ex-
posure to the pollutant, as females may have accu-
mulated toxins in their lipid stores which in turn
affected the maturation of the egg and the devel-
opment of the embryo. In the second generation,
it was suggested that an increased tolerance due to
a maternal effect or developmental plasticity, was
the reason behind the recovered hatching success.
In my results, generation F1 and F2 showed no ef-
fect. This is likely due to the low concentration of
leachates and in turn low presence of toxic com-
pounds.
If exposed to higher concentrations of
leachates, it would be expected to see similar ef-
fects as Krause et al. (2017) where the ﬁrst gener-
ation is impacted due to the toxins assimilated by
the females, but a second generation may be more
tolerant and adapt to the conditions.
The results show that exposure to the 0.1% con-
centration of leachates had no effect on the de-
velopment time of A. tonsa.
All individuals in
the leachate solution and control, had moulted
into copepods by day 12, which is in accordance
with other measured development rates at 20◦C
(Landry 1975). Other studies investigated the ef-
fect of pyrene on development time of arctic cope-
pod Calanus spp.
The results showed prolonged
development times after the ﬁrst feeding stage of
nauplii (NII) at concentrations of 20 µg of pyrene
L−1 (Grenvald et al. 2013), and it was argued that
it was due to prolonged exposure to the toxic com-
pounds. It is possible that higher concentrations
of leachates will have the same effect on A. tonsa.
During the non-feeding stages, the nauplii rely on
the maternally provided energy reserves (Landry
1983) whereas when they start feeding they will
depend on the surrounding environment. There-
fore, ﬁltering toxic compounds when feeding may
slow down the development time.
Surprisingly, the mortality seen in the acute
toxicity test was not observed over the 15 day
development experiment.
In the LC50, copepods
were taken from the FSW culture, in which they
spawned and hatched, and placed directly in the
leachate solution, whereas during the develop-
ment experiment, the copepods had been spawned,
hatched and had been growing in the leachate solu-
tion. The absence of mortality in the development
experiment could suggest that the copepods had
adapted to the toxicity of the leachates while the
sudden presence of leachates in the LC50 lead to a
mortality between 10 and 20% for all the tested life
stages. A scenario such as the one of the LC50 test,
may be a realistic scenario after a strong runoff
event as would happen after intense and prolonged
precipitation. In this case, the copepods would ex-
perience a sudden input of toxic compounds from
the TWPs that are on the road surface.
It should be mentioned that the results are likely
to be species speciﬁc.
The same concentration
that had no effect on the tested response rates in
A. tonsa, may impact other copepods or other or-
ganisms differently. For instance, lipid content of
copepods may inﬂuence the bio-accumulation of
toxic compound or may act as "buffer" to the tox-
ins. Halsband et al. (2020) looked at the mortality
caused by leachates from car tire crumb rubber on
A. longmeris compared to Calanus sp., and found
that the smaller, lipid-poor Acartia was more sen-
sitive than the larger, lipid-rich Calanus. Other pa-
rameters such as feeding mode and size may also
affect the effect of leachates on the tested species.
5
Future studies and conclusion
First, further research is needed in order to as-
sess the impact of TWPs and their leachates on
calanoid copepods and zooplankton in general. Ar-
guably, higher concentrations that will have a more
evident effect on the copepods bioenergetic rates
should be investigated, although these concentra-
tions are not currently environmentally relevant.
Plastic pollution is expected to persist in the near
future and due to its low decaying time, accumula-
tion of plastic particles is a threat (Andrady 2015).
Therefore, it is relevant to test the response of ma-
rine organisms, and their eventual ability to accli-
matise and eventually adapt to higher concentra-
tions. For example, copepods have been found to
adapt to toxic pollutants such as pyrene (Krause
et al. 2017). Thus the effect of leachates on dif-
ferent generations and at higher concentrations,
would be of interest to investigate. For example,
whether vital rates (such as egg production) can re-

cover after inter-generation exposure to high con-
centration of toxic compounds.
Second, the effect of other environmental stres-
sors should be investigated as it may impact the ef-
fect of leachates. The organisms used in this thesis
were well fed and living in warm temperatures. In
real conditions, copepods are often starved (except
for the period around spring bloom) which makes
the organisms more vulnerable. Multiple stressors
can impact both the physiology of the organisms
but also affect the quantity and type of toxins in
the leached from TWP. For example, higher tem-
peratures would increase feeding rates and thus in-
crease the uptake of leachates, while at the same
time decreasing toxicity of the leachates due to in-
creased microbial activity (John & Salim 2020).
Other stressors such as changes in pH may cause
similar stressor interactions.
Finally, the effect of leachates on different
species should be investigated. As mentioned, the
responses may be species-speciﬁc and impact or-
ganisms with different lipid content, feeding be-
haviour and size differently. This may lead to re-
sponses that vary depending on the region investi-
gated, as copepods from coastal, oceanic, Arctic or
southern communities have different physiology.
The present thesis demonstrates the potential
lethal effects of TWP leachates on A. tonsa at con-
centrations that are far above the worst-case en-
vironmental concentrations reported in literature.
Chronic exposure at environmentally relevant con-
centrations, showed no effect of the leachates.
Comparison to other studies showed a lack of stan-
dardisation in production of leachates which ham-
pers easy comparison between studies.
Further,
different responses seen in other studies demon-
strate the importance of knowing the exact compo-
sition of the produced leachates. Since copepods
play a major role in the marine ecosystem, changes
in their bioenergetic parameters may cause a cas-
cading effect in the rest of the ecosystem. There-
fore, although concentrations are not currently af-
fecting the coastal copepod A. tonsa, the concen-
trations should be monitored, especially in coastal
zone where TWP tend to accumulate.
