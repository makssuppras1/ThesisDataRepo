Abstract
Five diffusers have been measured in diffused and non-diffused conditions. The
measurements in diffused conditions implemented in a scaled reverberation chamber
following the ISO 17497-1 method to calculate their scattering coefficients. The ISO
method has been examined thoroughly for weaknesses and few modifications are
proposed which in theory might increase the accuracy of the method. The
measurements in
non-diffused conditions
implemented for different diffuser
orientations. The change in the orientation of the diffuser proved to have a significant
effect on the sound field in certain cases. The measurement results in non-diffused
conditions compared to PARISM simulated results were it was shown that correlate
well when the diffuser is placed inside the room.

Introduction
In the past decades there has been a growing development towards the sound
characterization of surfaces in order to improve the acoustics of interior spaces.
Measuring methods and simulation tools have been invented to derive the sound
absorption properties of surfaces, and thus describe the sound field in interior spaces.
This was a huge step in room acoustics, but it was found later that the sound field in a
room is more complex, and cannot be defined only by the absorption properties of its
elements. As a result, the sound properties of materials were defined more extensively,
to account also for the way they reflect sound.
Nowadays, we use two random-incidence metrics, to describe the reflections of a
material due to an incident wave. The scattering coefficient, which shows the amount
of the incident energy scattered away from the specular path, and the diffusion
coefficient, which shows the uniformity of the reflected sound. Fortunately, this is
similar to the way acoustic models use these coefficients, so the measurement data can
be directly imported to acoustic models.
In the current project the scattering coefficient will be examined thoroughly. At first,
the ISO 17497-1 method will be investigated, to observe if it is capable to derive
accurately the scattering coefficient of a surface. For this purpose, five diffusing
elements which have different characteristics will be measured and compared.
At a second stage the ceiling of the reverberation chamber, will be replaced with an
acoustic absorber. This will be done to create a non-diffuse sound field in the chamber,
similar to the one found in a classroom. Then the reverberation times of the room will
be measured with and without a diffuser, and compared with simulations. The
simulations will be carried out using the new PARISM acoustic model, which currently
is being developed at DTU. The PARISM model is capable of accounting for the
scattering reflections of a surface. For this reason, the scattering coefficients of the
diffusers measured with the ISO 17497-1 method, will be imported to PARISM.
By comparing the measurements and simulations of the room, it will be investigated if
the scattering coefficient, which is measured in a reverberation chamber, under the
approximation of a diffused field, can accurately describe the scattering properties of a
surface, in a non-diffuse field.

Theoretical part
Scattering surfaces have been placed in rooms and halls for the past centuries by design
or even accidentally [7]. Nevertheless, it was not until the last decades that research has
been implemented towards the characterization of the scattering properties of a surface,
which is very important in order to design diffusers or utilize them in a rooms and
concert halls. Furthermore, the sound reflection properties of a surface, and the
magnitude of its specular and diffuse reflections, are very essential in order to improve
the accuracy of room acoustic models. As a matter of fact, it has been shown that the
room acoustic models that take scattering effects into account due to surface roughness
or edge effects, provide more accurate results [7]. In contrast, the room acoustic models
that do not utilize the surfaces scattering effects, tend to predict higher reverberation
times, especially in the case of rooms with uneven absorption [7].
A sound wave incident on a surface, can be reflected, absorbed or transmitted. The part
of the energy which is absorbed by the material transforms into heat. The part of the
energy which is being transmitted, characterizes the insulation properties of the surface.
Lastly, the remaining part of the energy is reflected back to the surrounding space of
the material. The energy of the reflections can be divided to specular and diffused
energy. The specular energy, is the energy due to the specular reflections, which behave
similarly to the reflections from a large plane surface. In specular reflections Snell’s
law is valid, and the angle of the incident sound waves equals the angle of reflections
moving away from the surface. The diffused energy, shows the energy from diffuse
reflections, which have different angle than the angle of incidence.

Equation {1.3} calculates the random-incidence scattering coefficient, which is

The scattering coefficient can take values within 0 and 1, where 1 demonstrates the case
of a fully scattering surface, and 0 the case of a surface that reflects sound only in the
specular path.
The scattering coefficient does not provide information related to the directivity of the
diffused energy, since this is investigated in the diffusion coefficient. Fortunately, this
separation of the two coefficients, is in accordance with the way acoustic models treat
the scattered reflections and their directivity [4].

3
Method
In the current project the ISO 17497-1 method will be used to measure the scattering
coefficient. The method basically separates the specular and the diffuse energy of a
surface reflection as seen in equation{1.3}. Additional methods exist to calculate the
scattering coefficient by the same equation, but their method to separate the diffuse and
specular energy is different [4]. These are the free field scattering coefficient derived
from free field responses, and the free-field correlation scattering coefficient derived
from the free field polar distribution. Nevertheless, in the current project only the ISO
17497-1 method will be used. The method has great similarities with the ISO 354
standard, where reverberation times T1 and T2 are measured in the reverberation
chamber, with and without the sample, to calculate the sample’s random-incidence
absorption coefficient. The main difference, is that the ISO 17497-1 method needs
additional measurements of the reverberation times T3 and T4, while the sample is
rotated in different orientations. The rotation of the test sample is implemented by
placing it on a turntable, which is located inside the reverberation chamber.
In contrast with the ISO 354 standard, which only recommends full scale
measurements, the ISO 17497-1 method is suitable for measurements inside a
reverberation chamber either in scale, for example 1:5, or in full scale. The test sample
can be circular or squared, and has to be in the same dimensions with the turntable. In
case of a squared sample, it is advised that it is embedded in the turntable to avoid edge
effects as we will see later.
Similar to ISO 354, the ISO 17497-1 also assumes a diffuse sound field within the
chamber, which means that a homogeneous and isotropic sound field exists, with equal
probability of flow of energy to all directions.

3.1
Principle
An illustration of the ISO 17497-1 approach, to derive the scattering coefficient of a
surface can be seen in Figure 2. At first the sample is placed on the turntable, and the
rotation of the sample starts. At the same time, impulse responses are measured
repeatedly for different orientations of the sample. These impulse responses contain the
total reflections, meaning the specular and the diffuse reflections, due to the sample’s
surface. As can be seen in equation{1.3}, what is useful for the experimenter, is to
divide the reflections to specular and scattered. As we will see the specular part of the
reflections can be measured more easily than the scattering part [3]. Thus the ISO
method is trying to acquire impulse responses which only contains the specular part of
the sample’s reflections.
Figure 2 : An illustration of the ISO 17497-1 method in order to derive the scattering coefficients of a
surface. The test sample is placed on a turntable, which turns slowly while impulse responses are
measured repeatedly, for different sample orientations.
This is better illustrated in the time domain. In Figure 3, three impulse responses
measured in three different orientations of the test sample can be seen.
==PAGE:10==
9
Figure 3: Impulse responses measured for 3 different orientations of a sample under test, ISO-17497-1
[2].
A highly correlation can be clearly seen in the initial part of the impulse response, which
is actually the specular component of the sample’s reflection. This is true due to the
Fermat’s principle, where the earliest reflections recorded, reach the microphone
position by following the shortest path [5].
Figure 4 : An illustration of the Fermat’s principle, where it can be seen that the specular reflections
arrive earlier in the receiver position than the scattered reflections [5]
So we can conclude that for every sample’s orientation, the initial part is the same, and
includes the coherent specular reflections of same phase. In contrast, it can be seen that
==PAGE:11==
10
the later part of the impulse response varies for each orientation of the sample, these
are the scattering components of the reflection, which are incoherent reflections of
random phase. So in theory, if we measure many impulse responses of many
orientations of the test sample, and then average them, the scattered components of the
reflections will cancel out. As a result, the final averaged impulse response will only
include the component of the specular reflection. The averaging has to be “phased-
lock”, which means that the starting points of the impulse responses should be perfectly
aligned in the time domain, or else the method will not produce valid results.
Another example of the measuring method can be seen in Figure 5. It is clear that the
initial part of the impulse response up to 20 ms does not change with sample’s
orientation. Nevertheless, the later parts of the impulse response after 200 ms change
for different orientations of the sample at 500 Hz and 2 kHz. Another useful observation
is that at 100 Hz the initial and late part of the impulse responses do not change for
different orientations of the sample, which means that the particular surface scatters
sounds at higher frequencies than 100 Hz.
Figure 5 : Impulse responses measured for 2 different orientations of the sample at three different
frequencies [1]. The plots on the left side show the initial part of the impulse response up to 20 ms,
and the right side plots show the later part of the impulse response. It is seen that the initial parts are
coherent and do not change for different sample orientations. Nevertheless, the late parts change for
different sample orientations.
Furthermore, it has to be mentioned that impulse responses recorded for different
sample’s orientations, would be perfectly correlated in the case of a sample which is
perfectly circular, and its surface produces only specular reflections [5].

3.2
Known limitations and problems
Edge effects
Edge effects due to the visible peripheral area of the diffuser influence the
measurement method and can cause scattering coefficient values higher than 1 [2]. The
==PAGE:17==
16
solution that has been proposed is the addition of a hard reflecting strip, in the diffuser’s
height, around the sample, to compress the edge effect [16, 18, 19]. An example of an
edge strip can be found in Figure 6.
Figure 6 : One of the diffusers under test which has been covered peripherally with a hard plastic
material to suppress the edge effect.
The solution of the edge strip is not approved in the ISO standard, where it is mentioned:
“Do not cover the perimeter with a rigid border of fixed height”. Nevertheless this
solution is proposed in the corresponding amendment [6], which is currently under
evaluation.
In the case of square sample measurements, the ISO standard proposes to embed the
sample within the turntable. This approach can be seen in Figure 7, and it has to be
mentioned that it is the only way to measure square samples, to avoid excessive edge
effects [20].
==PAGE:18==
17
Figure 7 : The diffuser under test is inserted into the turntable in order to suppress the edge effect
An illustration of this statement, can be found in Figure 8, which shows that there is a
good agreement between a circular sample placed on the turntable, and a square sample
placed into the turntable. On the other hand, the scattering coefficient of the squared
sample, which is placed on the turntable, seems to be unrealistic.
Area of the test sample
It has been shown [16, 18], that depending on the area of the test sample under test, the
ISO method derives different scattering coefficients. This makes it difficult to compare
the results between laboratories that have measured the same diffuser, but with different
diameter. According to ISO standard, the sample’s area should be as large as possible,
to provide valid results.
Structural depth
The ISO method can only measure samples of small structural depth (small height). In
case the structural depth of the sample is higher than d/16, where d is the sample’s
diameter, it is likely to measure values of scattering coefficient larger than 1 (due to
edge effects).  This is especially true in high frequencies, where excess scattering can
be measured due to large height variations of the sample [7]. It is essential to mention
that the ISO method calculates the scattering coefficient due to a surface’ roughness, so
==PAGE:19==
18
it is important not to measure deep samples, where the edge effects would influence
considerably the measurement.
Figure 8 : The scattering coefficients obtained for a circular and squared sample. The samples have
equal area (m2), and same structure, meaning that they are essentially the same diffuser of different
shape. The dotted line (--) shows the scattering coefficients obtained for the circular diffuser placed on
the turntable. The bold line (___) shows the scattering coefficients obtained for the squared diffuser
embedded in the turntable. The light line (__), shows the scattering coefficient results, of the same
squared diffuser placed on the turntable [7].
Temperature, humidity and instability of the medium
The temperature, humidity, and the instability of the medium, mainly due to air
movement inside the chamber, influences the measurements a lot. This influence is
more obvious in higher frequencies [4]. For this reason the temperature and humidity
should be measured with a professional and accurate meter. In order for the medium
inside the chamber to be stable, the measurements should start at least 15 minutes after
closing the door of the chamber, in full scale measurements [2]. In case of scale
measurements, this time should be divided with the scale number, for example in a scale
chamber 1:5, the waiting time should be 3 minutes. For this reason, measurements in
scale are preferable, because they take less time. Furthermore long measurement times
should be avoided, because the temperature and humidity in the room can change. This
is one of the downsides of the stepwise method, which requires long measuring times,
and thus it’s not recommended [14]. The disadvantage of the scaled measurements, is
the high air absorption in higher frequencies, which results in low reverberation times,
and thus the results are more sensitive to measurement errors [14].
==PAGE:20==
19
Air gap
It has been noted [15, 16, 17, 18], that the scattering coefficient is influenced by the air
gap below the turntable. If for example the turntable is raised for a few centimeters,
then a general deviation (in magnitude) on the scattering coefficient results can be
observed, when compared with the normal position of the turntable. Nevertheless the
scattering coefficient results, follow the same pattern, meaning that the dips and peaks
in the scattering coefficient results are preserved. Due to these problems, it has been
proposed [16] to place the turntable in a very short distance from the chamber’s floor,
maintaining a short air gap.
Absorption of the test sample
Another limitation of the method is the fact that it is not suitable for samples with high
absorption. Thus the standard proposes that the maximum limit of the sample’s
absorption should be 0.5. Nevertheless it has been shown that this might be an
overestimation, and the absorption of the sample should not be higher than 0.3 [9].
Turntable
It is difficult to manufacture a perfectly flat turntable, especially in the case of full scale
measurements where the turntable has minimum diameter of 3 meters. Usually the
positioning of the turntable in the chamber involves many hours of trials, until the
scattering coefficients of the turntable are low and meet the ISO 17497-1 specification.
The problem is indicated in the fact that even a small asymmetry in the turntable, or in
the distance of the turntable from the floor, can cause high scattering coefficients.
Full scale measurements
In full scale measurements there are some additional practical problems. The turntable
as mentioned before should have a diameter of at least 3 meters, which means that it
has to be assembled within the reverberation chamber, since it will not fit through the
chamber’s door. Furthermore the motor to rotate a full scaled turntable should be
powerful, but at the same time quiet. Also as mentioned earlier, full scale measurements
require longer measuring times. For these reasons scale measurements are in general
preferable. Their only disadvantage is the higher air absorption which might be a
problem in higher frequencies. This can be solved if the air of the scale chamber is
replaced with a medium of low sound absorption, such as nitrogen.
Reverberation chamber
It has been shown that the number of the diffusers placed in the reverberation chamber
influences the results of the measurements [15]. At higher frequencies, this influence is
==PAGE:21==
20
significant, which makes it very hard to compare the scattering coefficient results,
between different reverberation chambers. Another problem, is the diffusivity of the
sound field inside the reverberation chamber [18]. This is also a known problem, in the
random-incidence absorption coefficient measurements of ISO 354. Another issue
which has been observed, mainly in absorption coefficient measurements, in
reverberation chambers, is the uniformity of the incidence sound intensity distribution
that deviates depending on different factors [22]. These factors are the shape and
dimensions of the room, the surface absorption, the locations of the source and the
sample under test.

Sound source
In general the measurements have to be done in 1/3 Octave bands, and the frequency
range for full scale measurements should be from 100 Hz to 5 kHz. Nevertheless for
scale measurements, which is the case in the current project, the frequency limits should
be multiplied with the scale number. Since the scale chamber has a physical ratio of
1:5, the frequency ranged from 500 Hz to 25 kHz. In higher frequencies, there is a
chance that the air absorption will be too high, then the frequencies can be omitted from
the measurement [2].
The sound sources were two tweeters from Vifa (XT25TG30-04) placed in fixed
positions in two corners or the reverberation chamber. The specific model is commonly
used in scattering coefficient measurements implemented in (1:5) scale chambers. This
is due to its flat frequency response in the frequency range 500 Hz to 25 kHz. Further
information about the tweeter can be found in the annex section.
Receiver
The microphone used during the measurements was a B&K 4136 1/4". The microphone
positions were set to four during the scattering measurements, which exceed the
minimum of three proposed by the standard. The microphone positions were in
accordance with ISO 354, as far as concerns the distance between measuring positions,
and the distance from walls and sound sources. These distances were divided by 5, to
correspond to the dimensions of the 1:5 scaled reverberation chamber.
Turntable
Two turntables were used depending on the size of the sample under test. The first one
has a diameter of 76 cm, it is very heavy and thick. It consists of metal material and the
general impression is that it is uncomfortable to use. Due to its weight, it was impossible
to maintain the turntable in a perfectly flat position. Moreover as can be seen in Figure
12, it provided higher values of scattering than the recommended ones. To be realistic,
this is not a problem solely due to the turntable, but also to the floor of chamber, which
is not perfectly flat. Nevertheless, the method is very sensitive in general, and thus
higher values of scattering were obtained for this turntable. The second turntable has a
diameter of 80cm, and it consists of a very thin slice of aluminum. Its scattering
coefficient values are within the limits of the standard, except of a small increase in
higher frequencies. Both turntables were set to complete one full rotation in 320s. The
second turntable can be found in Figure 11.

Test signals
Following the standard’s recommendation MLS signals were used for the excitation of
the chamber. The signal had a duration 2.735s and was provided from DIRAC software.
Then it was passed to the speakers from a Quad amplifier. The averaged impulse
response, that contained only the specular part of the reflections, have been obtained
after 117 averages, to meet an exact rotation of the sample. To be more straightforward,
117 signals of 2.735s duration were emitted during one complete rotation of the sample
(320s). This means that every 3.1 degrees of the rotation of the sample, an impulse
response obtained. These impulse responses were (phase-lock) averaged, as explained
earlier. In this way, it was possible to obtain an impulse response, which includes only
the specular reflections. The number of averages used in this project is more than the
72 proposed by the standard, this has been done to increase the accuracy of the
measurements.

Part 1 – Measurement results of five diffusers based on ISO 17497-1
method
In this section the results of the 5 diffusers that have been measured are presented. The
first four diffusers consist of hard wooden elements placed on low/medium density
wooden base. The 5th diffuser has a sinusoidal shape, and consists from a rough
construction material, mainly used for rooftops. The measurements have been
implemented according to the ISO 17497-1 standard.
The scattering coefficient results of every diffuser are illustrated using Sabine’s and
Eyring’s formula. The last is not provided in the ISO 17497-1 standard but it is
investigated additionally to observe the differences of the two approaches.
The
equations to derive the Sabine’s and Eyring’s scattering coefficients are essentially the
same ({1.22} and {1.23}). The difference relies on the way Sabine and Eyring calculate
the random incidence absorption coefficient, and the apparent specular absorption
coefficient.
The apparent specular absorption coefficient, is also demonstrated, and compared with
the scattering coefficient results of every diffuser. The apparent specular absorption
coefficient as mentioned previously shows the energy scattered into non specular
directions [7]. It should not be confused with the energy conversion into heat inside the
surfaces under test.
Lastly, scattering coefficients are presented when an edge strip is attached around the
sample. The results are demonstrated for the 3rd, 4th and 5th diffusers. The edge strip
results, for the 1st and 2nd diffuser, are not presented due to faulty measurements. The
measurements with an edge strip around the sample, have been implemented to observe
the influence of the edge effect due to the peripheral area of the diffusers.
Generally the measurements took a lot of time, this was especially true for the
measurements that included an edge strip around the sample. The edge strip has to be
attached properly around the sample (as seen in Figure 6). This proved to be challenging
with the current edge strip material which is hard to use.
The scattering coefficient results of the first four diffuser will be compared to the ones
of Andersen [28]. Andersen measured the same diffusers 14 years ago (2000) in the
same scaled reverberation chamber. The differences with the current measurements are
mainly on the equipment used and also to some parameters which are crucial to
mention. For example ISO 17497-1 method published on 2004, for this reason
Andersen’s followed a former method for his measurements. It can be seen in Figure
13 that the turntable rotating device is placed inside the reverberation chamber. This
results in measurements with an air gap between the turntable and the floor.
Furthermore an absorber is placed around the turntable to compress the edge effects
instead of a reflecting strip which is used in the current project. Another difference is
that in this project the reverberation chamber maintained relatively empty. More
specifically the turntable, the sample, the diffusers, the 2 tweeters, and one microphone
were kept inside the chamber during the measurements (Figure 9 and Figure 11). In
Andersen’s case the turntable rotating device, microphone amplifiers and microphone
hangers are placed inside the chamber during the measurements. Last difference is the
==PAGE:27==
26
number of phase-lock averages that has been used to obtain impulse responses which
only includes the specular reflections. As mentioned previously, the number of averages
used in the current project was 117 which results in an impulse every 3.1 degrees (360
° / 117). On the other hand in Andresen’s project the number of averages was 73 which
resulted in an impulse every 4.9 degrees.

4.4
Comparison of room measurements and simulations
In this section the measurement results are compared with simulations obtained by the
new room acoustic PARISM model, which is currently being developed at DTU. The
model uses the impedance of the materials, instead of their statistical absorption
coefficient, which is widely used in other models. In this way it is possible to obtain the
phase shift in reflection, and the angle dependence of absorption, increasing the
accuracy of the simulations. Furthermore, the new model accounts for the specular
reflections, using the image source method, and also for the diffuse reflections, by using
the acoustic radiosity method.
==PAGE:66==
65
The measurements presented in this section include the Modus and Gedina suspended
ceilings with and without the sinusoidal diffuser placed on the side wall for two
different orientations, same as before.
The scattering coefficients that have been previously measured, for the sinusoidal
diffuser, are imported to the model. This includes the Sabine’s results without edge
strip, and the Eyring’s results with edge strip. The Sabine’s scattering coefficients
which are higher than 1 are truncated. The scattering coefficient of the remaining
surfaces (side walls and floor) is set to 0.05 for all frequencies.
The absorption coefficients of the ceilings are calculated based on Komatsu’s model
[29]. The floor and the side walls of the room are described by their absorption
coefficients, which have been calculated using the measured reverberation times of the
completely empty room. This means without the suspended ceiling construction and
without the diffusers that were placed during the ISO 17497-1 measurements. The
surfaces inside the room have been divided into 227 elements for the Acoustic radiosity
part of the model.
Every simulation presented in this section had a duration of 18 hours in a new personal
computer. The model runs in Matlab and has high memory consumption (6-8 Gb). For
this reason it was not possible to run simulations to the databar computers of Acoustic

Discussion
5.1
Comparison of the five diffusers under test
Initially some safe conclusions can be derived from the five diffusers that have been
measured. The first four diffusers have higher absorption coefficients than the
sinusoidal diffuser. This is not due the elements of the diffusers, which consist of a hard
wood, but rather due to the base that holds the elements. The base is made from a
low/medium density wood, and it should have been varnished before the measurements,
in order to lower its sound absorption [16]. This would have increase the reflected
energy, and also make the diffusers more appropriate to measure under the ISO
standard, which has difficulties with moderate and high absorption samples.
Since the wooden base for the four first diffusers is the same, what determines the
scattering coefficients of the samples is the structural depth, the shape and formation of
the elements. The first and second diffuser (Figure 14 and Figure 18) have almost the
same structural depth, the same element shape, but different element formation.
Furthermore, the second diffuser has slightly higher number of elements compared to
the first diffuser. This element increase, and different formation, seems to affect the
magnitude of the scattering coefficient, and also the scattering coefficient pattern. For
the third and fourth diffusers (Figure 22 and Figure 26), a pattern of dips and peaks can
be observed for the scattering coefficient results (Figure 23 and Figure 27). This is more
obvious in the case of the 4th diffuser. This pattern probably is related with the increased
structural depth, which is much higher than the first and second diffuser, the element
shape, and formation. The sinusoidal diffuser (Figure 30), which has completely
different construction of the rest diffusers, features the highest scattering coefficients.
This is due to the fact that it has a rough surface instead of smooth elements, and also
the highest structural depth of all diffusers. Furthermore, it features the lowest sound
absorption, which makes it more appropriate for the ISO method.
Moreover, all samples under test, seem to scatter more sound in the medium-high
frequencies than in the low frequencies. This is more obvious in the first and second
diffuser, where the scattering coefficients are close to zero under 2 kHz. This shows
that the combination of the hemisphere elements, and the structural depth, is not enough
to trigger the scattering in the low frequencies.
5.2
The addition of an edge strip
The addition of an edge strip seems to compress the edge effects as can be seen in
Figure 23, Figure 27 and Figure 31. Generally, the scattering coefficient curves with
and without an edge strip, seem to follow the same pattern in most cases. This is less
clear in Figure 23, and Figure 31, but some dips and peaks are maintained. The main
difference is that the scattering coefficient curves when an edge strip is attached have
lower magnitude than the corresponding ones without an edge strip. This effect is
==PAGE:80==
79
clearer in the case of the 3rd diffuser (Figure 27), where the difference between the
normal sample, and the sample with an edge strip attached is significant. This could
also be an error in the measurement, since the results show that around 8 kHz 1/3 octave
band the surface does not scatter sound at all. Nevertheless, the usefulness of the edge
strip is clearer in the sinusoidal diffuser case (Figure 31). More specifically it can be
seen that by introducing an edge strip, the scattering coefficient based on Sabine’s
formula provides more coherent results, meaning values less than 1. So the previously
mentioned problem, of the excessive scattering coefficients based on Sabine’s formula,
might be explained by the edge effects, which influence the measurements.
As mentioned previously, the ISO method is a measure of a surface roughness, so any
edge or other effects should be avoided during the measurement. Having this in mind,
and by having a closer look at the sinusoidal diffuser (Figure 30), it is clear that the
surrounding openings could influence the measurements. This makes the addition of an
edge strip essential, in order to close the openings, and measure only the surface
roughness, without having other effects influencing the measurement. Concluding the
addition of an edge strip seems to suppress the edge effects, and should also be used in
diffusers with surrounding openings, in order to obtain more coherent results.
5.3
Comparison of Sabine and Eyring results
The scattering coefficient based on the Eyring formula, although not used in the ISO
17497-1, is implemented additionally in this project. This was done to observe if the
differences between Sabine and Euring scattering coefficients are significant. It has
been noted [9], that the scattering coefficient based on Sabine’s formula, can be
overestimated for diffusers featuring a higher absorption coefficient than 0.3. This is in
contradiction with ISO 17497-1, which states that the limitation of the method is for
higher values of random-incidence absorption coefficient of 0.5 [2]. The measurements
in this study showed high deviations between Sabine’s and Eyring’s scattering
coefficients.  This is in accordance with a similar study [9].
The difference between the two coefficients is due to the exponential function inherent
in Eyring’s equations. This can be observed in the equations for apparent specular
absorption coefficient{1.21}, and absorption coefficient{1.19}. The Sabine’s and
Eyring’s results for the apparent specular absorption coefficient, and the random
incidence absorption coefficient, are in accordance in lower values, but deviate for
higher values. This can be observed in all figures of section 4.2, and it can be concluded
that the equations based on Sabine ({1.17} and {1.20}), always predict higher values
than the Eyring based equations ({1.19} and {1.21}). As a result the scattering
coefficients derived with Eyring’s formula for diffusers, have always lower values
when compared with the ones derived with Sabine’s formula. This is due to the
exponential function inherent in Eyring’s equations which compresses high differences
between the reverberation times T1-T2 and T3-T4.
Which equation provides more valid results is not an easy question to answer, and it
should be examined further. However, it might depend on the diffuser under test. For
==PAGE:81==
80
example in a similar study [9] a well diffuser measured following the ISO 17497-1
method. The edges of the wells resulted in excessive edge effects. Therefore the
scattering coefficients based on Sabine formula were above 1 for the most part of the
frequency range, in some cases reaching the value of 2. It is clear that such values are
not coherent to use in room acoustic models. On the other hand Eyring’s formula for
the specific case calculated more useable results up to 0.73. In this project only periodic
diffusers have been measured. In few cases the scattering coefficients were above 1 for
measurements without an edge strip. Nevertheless, as mentioned previously when an
edge strip was attached on the sample the scattering coefficients were compressed to
more usable values. For this reason it is concluded that depending on the diffuser under
test one formula might be better than the other.
Another conclusion, is that when the absorption of a sample is very low, then the
apparent specular absorption coefficient, and the scattering coefficient take similar
values, this is clear for the sinusoidal diffuser (Figure 31 and Figure 32). This is due to
the scattering coefficient equation {1.22} because for very low values of as the apparent
specular absorption coefficient and the scattering coefficient take comparable values.
For example if aspec for a specific frequency is 0.9 and as is 0.1 then we obtain a
scattering coefficient of 0.88 which is very close with the aspec value.
5.4
An important paradigm
At this point it is important to make a practical example, in order to understand more
deeply what the scattering coefficient shows for a surface. The paradigm is inspired by
[1]. Assume a person that wishes to use a diffuser to scatter the frequencies of 20 kHz
in a room, and he is interested to either the 1st, or the 3rd diffuser. The 1st diffuser has a
scattering coefficient of 0.6 at 20 kHz (Figure 15), and the 3rd diffuser has a scattering
coefficient of 0.9 (Figure 23). So after seeing these values the person concludes that the
3rd diffuser is a better choice, but is this true? In order to safely answer to this question,
we need to take also into account the absorption coefficient of the two diffusers at 20
kHz. The absorption of the 1st diffuser is 0.22 at 20 kHz (Figure 16), which shows that
the 78% of the remaining energy is reflected. So we can conclude that the part of the
incident energy that is scattered is 47% (0.78*0.6). On the other hand, the 3rd diffuser
sound absorption at 20 kHz is 0.56 (Figure 24), and the remaining 44% of the energy
is reflected. So the part of the incident energy that is scattered is 40% (0.44*0.9). This
means that the person did not take the right choice, the 1st diffuser scatters more energy
than the 3rd diffuser. So we can conclude that the scattering coefficient of a surface,
should be presented with its absorption coefficients, in order to avoid confusion.
Moreover, diffusers that offer moderate or high absorption properties, are not
essentially ‘good’ diffusers, from the perspective that they do not scatter a high portion
of sound energy.

5.5
Proposals to improve the method
In this project the problems that currently are inherent in the method were specified. It
was shown, that the addition of an edge strip around the sample under test, can compress
effects which influence the measurements. Furthermore, as mentioned in the known
problems and limitations section, the air gap between the floor and the turntable also
influences the measurements. Moreover the positioning of the turntable involves many
hours of trials until someone obtains scattering coefficients within the limitations of
ISO standard. This was more obvious in the thick turntable used in this project, which
had higher scattering coefficients than the recommended ones, as can be seen in Figure
12. A turntable which has scattering coefficients within the ISO limits, also influences
the measurements. The ISO standard proposes logical limits that can be achieved in a
good configuration, where the floor of the chamber and the turntable are almost flat.
Nevertheless, even with such good configuration, the slightest difference in the air gap
between turntable and chamber’s floor, increases the scattering of the turntable. To
make it clearer, if the air gap on one side of the turntable is 0.8 cm, and in the opposite
side is 0.9 cm, this will increase the scattering of the turntable. Furthermore in this case,
the sample under test, will be measured under a very short angle, instead of a flat
condition, which is the correct approach. An illustration of this problem can be seen in
Figure 79.
Figure 79 : An illustration of scattering coefficient measurements with a turntable that has slightly different air
gap in its sides. This results in an increase of the scattering coefficients of the empty turntable and also measuring
in a small angle.
Another influence in the measurements can be the positioning of the mechanism that
rotates the turntable inside the reverberation chamber. This rotator mechanism has a
large surface area, and also results in an increase of the air gap.
Having all this in mind, the final question is what can be done to avoid the previously
mentioned problems that influence the measurements. In Figure 80, the current
configuration can be seen which is proposed by the ISO standard. At first, the empty

implemented when the test sample is placed on the turntable (T4 measurement). In the
amendment, the same configuration is proposed, with the addition of an edge strip

Figure 80 : Current configuration of the measurement proposed by the ISO 17497-1
This is a step forward to solve a part of the problem, unfortunately the influence of the
air gap, the rotator mechanism and the scattering of the turntable still persist.
In contrast, the solution that is being proposed in this project, is to embed the sample in
the turntable at a first stage, as can be seen in Figure 81, to limit the sample’s edge
effects. During the measurement of T3, the turntable is measured including its spare
part. Then for the T4 measurement, the spare part is removed and replaced by the test
sample.
Then the turntable, could be embedded in the floor of the reverberation chamber, to
remove the influence of the air gap, the rotator mechanism, and the scattering of the
turntable. The proposed configuration is illustrated in Figure 82.
When the turntable is embedded in the chamber’s floor, it is easier to make a perfectly
flat turntable, which is in perfect symmetry with the chamber’s floor. A turntable
embedded in the chamber’s floor has been implemented in Aachen University. The
construction with an embedded turntable, in the chamber’s floor, results in very low
scattering coefficients of the turntable, which are essentially zero for the whole
frequency range [24]. The main difference of this configuration, with the solution
proposed in this project, is to embed the test sample in the turntable, to limit the edge

5.6
Separation of the sound field in grazing and non-grazing parts
The sound field in a room which has rigid walls and a ceiling of high absorption, can
be divided into grazing and non-grazing parts, as seen in [25]. A room which has an
absorptive ceiling while the remaining walls are hard is a typical case of a non-diffused
sound field. The non-grazing part consists of waves that hit the ceiling with oblique
incidence, while the grazing part consists of waves that travel essentially parallel to the
ceiling. Following this theory we can conclude that in a typical empty classroom
(Figure 60 for Modus/Gedina ceilings), the non-grazing part will decay faster due to
the strong influence of the suspended ceiling. On the other hand, the grazing part will
decay at a slower rate since the waves are travelling almost parallel to the ceiling and
are less influenced by it. Of course the total decay is given by the summation of the
grazing and non-grazing parts.
When the diffuser is placed on the side wall, the grazing part is suppressed because the
waves that would normally travel parallel to the ceiling are now being scattered by the
diffuser surface. So essentially, the diffuser’s effect on the sound field, is to transfer
energy from the grazing part, to the non-grazing part. This can explain the decrease in
RT values in Figure 60, for the whole frequency range, when the diffuser is placed
inside the room. In simple terms when the diffuser is placed on the side wall it reflects
a portion of the incident sound energy directly (or indirectly) to the absorptive ceiling.
This results in a decrease of the reverberation times inside the room.
The separation of the sound field to grazing, and non-grazing parts, might be better
illustrated by examining the decay curves in Figure 78. When the diffuser is not placed
in the room, a break can be observed in the decay curve at -5 dB. In order to explain
this behavior properly we have to consider the following. Firstly, up to this point, the
non-grazing part influences more the decay [26], because it has more energy content
than the grazing part. Secondly, the non-grazing waves decay rapidly because of the
influence of the suspended ceiling. This explains the fast decay up to -5 dB. After this
==PAGE:86==
85
point, the sound field decays slower, which shows that the energy of the grazing part is
now considerable. The grazing part, decays slower because it is less influenced by the
suspended ceiling. When the diffuser is placed inside the room, we obtain a smoother
decay rate for the whole range. In this case, the grazing waves are scattered by the
surface of the diffuser in the surrounding space including the ceiling area. Thus we have
a suppression of the grazing sound field or more precisely an energy transfer from the
grazing field to the non-grazing field.
5.7
Room measurement and simulation results
It has been shown that different reverberation times are obtained inside the room for
the two suspended ceiling configurations (Figure 60). This was only seen in lower
frequencies where the Gedina based ceiling offers better absorption. This happens
although the Gedina absorber is thinner than Modus. Furthermore it can be seen in the
appendix that it offers overall slightly less practical absorption coefficients, in the
frequency range under question. The increased absorption of the Gedina based ceiling
is probably due to its increased air gap by 35 mm.
The installation of the diffusers within the room has an effect in the reverberation times
obtained for the whole frequency range. This can be seen for example for the sinusoidal
diffuser (Figure 60). This decrease in RT, cannot be solely explained by the absorption
of the sinusoidal diffuser, since it is too low after 1250 1/3 octave band as can be seen
in Figure 32. Instead this decrease in RT values can be explained by the scattering
effects of the diffuser in the non-diffused sound field as described in the previous
section.
The investigation for different positions of the sinusoidal diffuser either in the floor or
on the side wall (Figure 61) showed that both configurations have a great impact in the
sound field. Logically, we would expect the contribution of the diffuser to be more
significant when placed on the side wall, due to the scattering of the grazing sound
waves, as explained previously. However, this happens only in the frequency range
from 500 to 1250 Hz. One explanation for this could be the fact that the positioning of
the diffuser on the floor, also affects the grazing sound waves. For example the grazing
sound waves that travel in a short height, close to the floor, would probably hit the
diffuser. As a result the waves would have been scattered to the surrounding space and
the ceiling. Another explanation is the positioning of the sound source which was
essentially placed in front of the diffuser during the measurements (in a corner on the
floor area).
Diffusers that have comparable scattering coefficients have also a comparable effect in
the non-diffused sound field. This was shown in section 4.3 in the comparison of the
first - second diffuser, and also in the comparison of the third - fourth diffuser for similar
diffuser orientations. However, the comparison between the sinusoidal and the third
diffuser showed in many cases comparable results for similar diffuser orientations. It is
reminded that the third diffuser has considerable lower scattering coefficients than the
sinusoidal diffuser. Logically we would expect high deviations between the 2 diffusers
==PAGE:87==
86
in the non-diffused sound field. More specifically it would be expected that the room
configuration with the sinusoidal diffuser would have significantly lower reverberation
times. This would happen due to the increased capability of the sinusoidal diffuser to
scatter the sound energy towards the absorptive ceiling. However, in the current study
this is not the case. In contrast, it can be seen that the room configurations either with
the sinusoidal or the third diffuser in the same orientation have comparable
reverberation times in many cases. One explanation for this could be that the decreased
scattering capabilities of the third diffuser (Figure 23 and Figure 31) are compensated
by its increased absorption (Figure 24 and Figure 32).
The different orientations of the diffusers have a considerable effect in the non-diffused
sound field as shown in section 4.3. This effect seems to increase with increasing
structural depth of the diffusers. For the first two diffusers which have low structural
depth the effect was not significant. Nevertheless, for the third and fourth diffuser the
effect was more apparent. In the case of the sinusoidal diffuser the effect was significant
for all source receiver positions. More specifically, in section 4.3.3 it was shown that
the highest differences in the reverberation times of the room obtained for different
orientations of the same diffuser. The differences between the third and the sinusoidal
diffuser was considerably lower although they have incomparable scattering
coefficients. This shows the importance of the orientation of a diffuser for the
characterization of the sound field. It has to be mentioned that this is not described in
the scattering coefficient which specifies the same value for all orientations of the
diffuser. Furthermore lower reverberation times were obtained when the sinusoidal and
fourth diffuser were placed in their first orientation in the frequency range from 1 kHz
to 2 kHz. This shows that the diffusers in that orientation scatter more efficiently the
corresponding sound waves towards the ceiling.
5.7.1
Comparison of the measurement and simulation results
In section 4.4 it is shown that the measurement and simulation results do not fit well
when the diffuser is not placed inside the room. Nevertheless, there is a good fit between
the measured and simulated results when the diffuser is placed on the side wall. This is
especially true in the case where the diffuser is positioned in the first orientation. The
disagreement between the measured and simulated results without the diffuser, might
be explained by the theory of grazing and non-grazing sound field. More specifically,
when the diffuser is not placed in the room the grazing sound field decays slower. On
the other hand the non-grazing sound field decays rapidly due to the direct influence of
the ceiling. Thus the influence of the grazing sound field to the total sound field would
be an increase of the reverberation time inside the room. This increase might be
underestimated in the current configuration of the model for this project. In the opposite
case, where the diffuser is placed on the side wall, the grazing sound field does not
decay considerably slower than the non-grazing sound field. This is due to the scattering
of the grazing sound waves from the diffuser towards the ceiling. This hypothesis
explains the deviations between simulated and measured results when the diffuser is
==PAGE:88==
87
not placed in the room. It also explains the good fit between simulated and measured
results when the diffuser is placed in the room.
The simulated and measurement results have a good fit when the diffuser is placed on
the side wall except from the frequency range from 1 kHz to 2 kHz, and in one case at
the frequency range from 4 kHz to 8 kHz. Initially it was believed that the model in its
current configuration for the project has a low number of defined image sources, and
that caused the problem in the specific frequencies. For this reason the simulated decay
curves where examined at the 1250 Hz 1/3 Octave band where interesting observations
could be derived. More specifically it was shown that at least for the first 10 dB of
decay, which are examined in the EDT parameter, the model has enough image sources.
As a result the previous mentioned assumption for the deviations at the specific
frequency range due to a low number of image sources was excluded. The problem in
the specific frequency range is clear if we look the simulated decay curves with and
without the diffuser (Figure 74 and Figure 75). The decay curves are almost similar
which shows that the model in the specific frequency range underestimates the
influence of the diffuser. Normally the two decay curves should have a considerable
difference since the scattering coefficient of the diffuser is 0.46 for the 1250 Hz 1/3
Octave band. Due to time limitations the problem was not investigated further.
The simulations based on the Eyring’s scattering coefficients, for the sinusoidal
diffuser, seem to fit better with the measurement results (Figure 65 and Figure 66).
Nevertheless, the difference between the simulations that use Sabine’s and Eyring’s
scattering coefficients is so small that is hard to derive safe conclusions. Probably in a
full scaled room, this difference would be higher, and thus it would be easier to
conclude which of the two scattering coefficients better describes the scattering
properties of a surface, in a non-diffused sound field.
==PAGE:89==
88
6
Conclusions
In this project, the scattering coefficients of five diffusers have been measured,
following the ISO 17497-1 method. The measurements of the diffusers, were carried
out with and without an edge strip around the samples, to observe the influence of the
edge effects in the measurements. It was clear that the addition of an edge strip
suppresses the edge effects of the diffuser’s peripheral area, which is in accordance with
related studies.
The ISO 17497-1 method has known limitations which influence the measurement
results. For this reason, an alternative measurement method has been proposed, which
in theory can suppress the edge effects, and also overcome known problems that
influence the method, such as the air gap, and the scattering of the turntable.
The diffuse sound field of the reverberation chamber, has been transformed to a non-
diffused sound field, by placing a suspended ceiling. Room measurements have been
implemented for different ceiling configurations, and for different positions and
orientations of the sinusoidal diffuser. The installation of the diffuser within the room,
proved to have considerable effect in the sound field, for the whole frequency range. It
was elaborated that this influence is partly due to the absorption properties of the
diffuser, but also due to scattering of the sound waves towards the absorptive ceiling.
The measurement and simulation results when the diffuser was placed inside the room,
were correlated in most frequency bands. The PARISM model used the scattering
coefficients which have been previously measured in the reverberation chamber. For
this reason, it was shown that the scattering coefficients measured under diffused
conditions, might accurately describe at some extent the scattering properties of a
surface in a non-diffuse sound field. However, it was proved that other factors influence
the behavior of a diffuser in a non-diffused sound field. These were the orientation and
the position of the diffuser inside the room. Moreover the measurement results in a non-
diffused sound field showed that diffusers with comparable scattering coefficients, have
comparable effect in the sound field. Nevertheless, it was also shown that diffusers with
incomparable scattering coefficients, have comparable effect in the sound field in
specific frequencies and source receiver positions. This implies that the sound field is
complex and cannot be described perfectly by the scattering coefficient which is a
single number that describes a surface roughness. Furthermore the scattering coefficient
does not account for the positioning and the orientation of the diffuser. For these reasons
the scattering coefficient cannot describe perfectly the behavior of a diffuser in a non-
diffused sound field but only at some extent.
==PAGE:90==
89
7
Future work
Due to time limitations the proposed alternative method of the ISO 17497-1 could not
be tested. It would be interesting in the future to implement a comparison between the
two configurations.
Although PARISM simulations fit well with the measurement results when a diffuser
is placed inside the room, in certain frequencies underestimates the influence of the
diffuser. Due to time limitations this could not be examined further. It is crucial to find
the reason for this problem in order for the model to provide more accurate simulations.
Furthermore, it is very important to export the model to another software platform. In
the current configuration PARISM consumes considerable amount of resources and the
calculations take a lot of time. If the model run faster it would be possible to compare
with more measurements and thus obtain a clearer picture of its capabilities.
