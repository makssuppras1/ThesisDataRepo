Preface 
This report is the final product of a 30 ECTS project, for the final thesis of my International Master of 
Science in Engineering, in Aquatic Food Production - Safety & Quality, a joint Masters programme at 
Norwegian University of Life Science (NMBU), and Technical University of Denmark (DTU). The project was 
conducted in the period January 2nd to June 2nd 2019 at DTU Bioengineering, Department of Biotechnology 
and Biomedicine, at DTU with the research group Bacterial Ecophysiology and Biotechnology. Supervisors 
of the project at DTU were Professor Lone Gram, Ph.D. students Karen Kiesbye Dittmann and Bastian 
Barker Rasmussen, and Associate Professor Odd Ivar Lekang at NMBU.  
First and foremost I would like to thank my supervisors for the opportunity to conduct this project, and all 
of their support throughout the project. Your advice, guidance and constructive criticism have helped me in 
improving my work and taught me a lot. I am thankful for once again getting to be a part of this research 
group. Thanks to the entire group for always being kind, joyful and helpful when need, and in general 
providing a nice working environment. Lastly a special thanks to Emma James, my fellow master student at 
NMBU, for being helpful with proofreading of the report.  
 
The purpose of this study was to investigate how the probiont Phaeobacter piscinae S26 isolated from an 
aquaculture unit influences a mock community of bacteria isolated from the microalgae Tetraselmis 
suecica. This study describes the investigation of how S26 influenced the growth of the community 
microorganisms, if S26 changed the community structure and how this potentially affected the growth of 
the microalgae. In other words how is the interaction between the microalgae T. suecica and its natural 
bacterial community influenced when the probiont P. piscinae S26 is introduced. 
The results of this study will contribute to further research and development of the implementation of 
Phaeobacter spp. as probiont in aquaculture live feed, and thereby a preventive measure towards diseases 
in marine larviculture.

Akvakultur verden over er udfordret af sygdomsudbrud forsaget af patogene bakterier, f.eks. slægten 
Vibrio, som ofte er fundet i levende foder brugt i marin fiskelarve kultur. Det levende foder kan optræde 
som vektorer for disse patogene bakterier. En høj kvantitet af opportunistiske patogene bakterier kan 
medføre store tab, både i mængden af udbytte samt økonomisk. I dag findes der kun få kommercielle 
midler til forebyggelse og behandling af fiskelarver, så som antibiotika.  Størrelsen og udviklingen af 
fiskelarven hindre brugen af vacciner. Anvendelse af probiotisk Phaeobacter er blevet foreslået som en 
løsning på de manglende midler, dog er der begrænset viden om påvirkningen af Phaeobacter på det 
levende foders naturlige bakterielle community. Således er formålet med dette studie at undersøge 
hvordan probiotiske Phaeobacter piscinae S26, isoleret fra en akvakultur enhed, påvirker et imiteret 
community af bakterier isoleret fra mikroalgen Tetraselmis suecica.  
Seksten bakterielle isolater fra T. suecica udgør seks forskellige slægter; Algoriphagus, Mameliella, 
Marinobacter, Muricauda, Oceanicaulis and Roseovarius. I et inhiberingsassay, hvor alle stammer og S26 
blev testet mod hinanden, blev der observeret at alle andre end stammerne af Mameliella blev inhiberet af 
S26. Yderligere forsagede to af Mameliella stammerne induceret vækst hos majoriteten af stammerne. Fem 
stammer, Algoriphagus, Muricauda, Mameliella, Oceanicaulis and Marinobacter, blev udvalgt på baggrund 
af deres alsidighed, til etablering af et imiteret bakterielt community. Dette community, både med og uden 
probiotiske P. piscinae S26, havde en signifikant påvirkning af væksten af T. suecica, ved straks at øge 
væksten efter tilsætning. Dog blev ingen signifikant indflydelse observeret efter otte dage. Ligeledes, blev 
en signifikant påvirkning over de første to dage observeret ved tilstedeværelsen af S26 i det bakterielle 
community.  Muricauda og Oceanicaulis havde øget vækst, mens Algoriphagus blev hæmmet. 
Tilstedeværelsen af alle fem stammer efter otte dage, og observationen af ingen signifikant påvirkning 
tyder på at det er muligt at danne et stabilt community med S26 efter etableringen af det. Således kan det 
fra dette studie konkluderes at implementeringen af P. piscinae S26 som probiont i T. suecica kulturer er 
muligt, uden at forsage længerevarende signifikante ændringer af både det bakterielle community og T. 
suecica. Dog skal den indledende tilpasning af det bakterielle community medtages i planlægningen af hvor 
ofte probionten tilsættes.  
Resultaterne fra dette studie har til hensigt at bidrage til forskning og udvikling af kommercielt brug af 
Phaeobacter slægten som probiotika i levende foder brugt i akvakultur, og dermed et præventivt middel 
mod vibriosis i marin fiskelarve kultur.

Abstract 
The worldwide aquaculture industry is challenged by disease outbreaks caused by bacterial pathogens e.g. 
Vibrio spp., which are often found in the live feed used in marine larviculture. The live feed can as a vector 
for the pathogens. Takeover of opportunistic pathogens can lead to severe yield and economical losses. 
Few preventive measures and treatments are commercially available for use in larviculture, such as 
antibiotics. The development and size of the larvae hinder the use of vaccines. The use of probiotic 
Phaeobacter spp. is suggested as a solution, however little is known about how Phaeobacter spp. impact 
the natural bacterial community of the live feed. Thus the purpose of this study was to investigate how 
probiotic Phaeobacter piscinae S26, isolated from an aquaculture unit, influences a mock community of 
bacteria isolated from the microalgae Tetraselmis suecica.  
Sixteen bacterial isolates from T. suecica belonged to six genera; Algoriphagus, Mameliella, Marinobacter, 
Muricauda, Oceanicaulis and Roseovarius. An inhibition assay testing all strains and S26 towards each other 
showed that all, except those of Mameliella sp., were inhibited by the TDA production of S26. Furthermore, 
the Mameliella sp. two strains induced growth of the majority of the other strains. Five strains, belonging to 
the genera Algoriphagus, Muricauda, Mameliella, Oceanicaulis and Marinobacter, were selected to form a 
mock community based on the versatility of each strain. This community, both with and without probiotic 
P. piscinae S26, had a significant influence on the growth of T. suecica, enhancing it immediately after 
addition, but causing no significant influence after eight days.  Likewise, the presence of P. piscinae S26 in 
the bacterial community had a significant influence during the first two days, enhancing growth of 
Muricauda sp. and Oceanicaulis sp., while suppressing Algoriphagus sp., but with all five strains present 
after eight days no significant influence was observed. This indicates the formation of a stable community 
after establishment. Thus, this study concludes that implementation of P. piscinae as probiotic in T. suecica 
cultures is possible, without causing long lasting significant changes to the growth of the bacterial 
community or T. suecica. However, initial adaption of the community should be kept in mind in terms of 
how frequently the probiotic is added. 
The results of this study aim to contribute to the research and development on commercial use of 
Phaeobacter sp. as probiotics in aquaculture live feed, and thereby become a preventive measure towards 
vibiosis in marine larviculture.

1 Introduction 
The human population has reached 7.5 billion and is expected to cross the 8 billion mark before 2030 
(United Nations Department of Economic and Social Affairs, Population Division. 2017). The increase of the 
population together with the United Nations Sustainable Development Goals (SDG) for the world is 
emphasising the need for action within several sectors. In terms of reaching SDG No. 2, Zero Hunger, our 
food production systems and thereby the agriculture and aquaculture industries are challenged. This goal 
focuses on obtaining a food supply such that no hunger is present in 2030, but also that the foods produced 
is of sufficient nutritious character. With this the demand for high quality proteins also increases (FAO 
2018; United Nations n.d.). 
When looking into the marine production and supply of such high-value proteins, it can be concluded that 
today’s capture industry is no longer sufficient in terms of maintaining and increasing the high yields. 
Instead, aquaculture has become the main supplier in this field. This is supported by the fact that the 
aquaculture industry is rapidly growing and developing to even larger and more intensive production units 
(Bentzon-Tilia, Sonnenschein, and Gram 2016; FAO 2018). The aquaculture production includes different 
fish species, crustaceans, molluscs and aquatic plants, of which the majority is fish (Figure 1). According to 
the latest report from the Food and Agriculture Organization of the United Nations (FAO) the aquaculture 
industry is dominated by fish production covering 369 different finfish species. Finfish production is mainly 
located in inland waters and the total finfish production in 2016 added up to 54.1 million tonnes, out of the 
80 million tonnes food fish produced globally (FAO 2018).  
 
The finfish industry faces major challenges e.g. diseases and infectious pathogens. With few treatment 
methods available for the larvae and juvenile fish, new measures are sought (Engelsen et al. 2008; Hansen 
and Olafsen 1999; Rørbo et al. 2018). This study investigates the potential probiont, Phaeobacter piscinae 
S26 as agent towards the finfish pathogen Vibrio anguillarum, commonly associated with live feed and 
more specifically how the probiont influence the natural bacterial community of the live feed algae 
Tetraselmis suecica.  
 
Though different finfish are being produced in somewhat the same way, there are differences dependent 
on which species are farmed and what the individual farmer prefers to do. One procedure which is 
consistent is how the individual species of fish is introduced to their first meal after hatching.

1.1 Rearing of finfish using live feed 
In aquaculture, the life stages of the fish are taken into account when handling, treating and feeding. In the 
early life stage after hatching the fish is a so called altrical larvae. This is a relatively undeveloped fish which 
does not have a developed gastrointestinal tract and therefore also lacks a stomach, both morphological 
and functional (Govoni, Boehlert, and Watanabe 1986). During this period, the fish feeds on the yolk sac, 
which is the remaining yolk from their egg stage. When the yolk sac is nearly used, the larvae seek other 
food, but are still relatively undeveloped. The combination of how developed the gastrointestinal tract of 
the fish is and the size of their mouth, determines what the fish can feed on and how they get sufficient 
nutrient. Some species, such as salmon, catfish,  and trout, have large mouths relative to their size, more 
developed digestive systems compared to many other reared species, and can be fed artificial pellet feed as 
their first feed (Conceição et al. 2010; Cahu and Zambonino Infante 2001; Akbary 2010). Other species need 
to be fed more digestible feed. This includes species such as Atlantic halibut (Hippoglossus hippoglossus), 
Sea bass (Dicentrarchus labrax), Sea bream (Sparus aurata), Cod (Gadus morhua) and Turbot 
(Scophthalmus maximus) which are fed live feed (Shields 2001; Sandlund and Bergh 2008).  
The natural live feed of such fish is often copepods; however, they have been difficult to produce at 
commercial scale. Whilst copepods and other zooplankton are used, the typical live feeds include Artemia 
==PAGE:11==
Josefine Hansen 
101577/S144493

and rotifers. For the production of Artemia and rotifers, microalgae is used including Tetraselmis, Dunaliella 
and Chrorella (Conceição et al. 2010; Akbary 2010). Beside the matter of size of feed, the ingestion is also 
facilitated with live feed as the motility of it makes it more accessible to the relative immobile larvae (Cahu 
and Zambonino Infante 2001; Conceição et al. 2010).  The microalgae is mainly used for feed in the 
production of other live feeds, but can also be used for direct ingestion of fish larvae (Conceição et al. 
2010).  
The use of live feed is of great importance in terms of rearing certain species of finfish. However, using live 
feed is not without challenges. Live feed has been found to be involved in the introduction of opportunistic 
pathogens into rearing units, which can cause massive outbreaks of disease and a total collapse of the 
larval production (Grisez et al. 1996; Høj, Bourne, and Hall 2009; Rasmussen et al. 2018).   
 
1.1.1 Opportunistic pathogens in larviculture  
Before looking further into the involvement of live feed in bacterial diseases of the fish larvae, the overall 
state of diseases in aquaculture has been investigated. Diseases are a common issue appearing during all 
stages of the fish life cycle, and the dominant disease causing agents are bacteria, which are responsible for 
around 54.9 % of the outbreaks. Other microbial agents, such as virus (22.6 %),  parasites (19.4 %) and fungi 
( 3.1 %) also cause fish diseases (Kibenge et al. 2012). Debris and organic matters, present due to high 
production density, combined with the addition of live feed results in an excellent environment for 
pathogenic bacteria (Skjermo and Vadstein 1999). Thorough procedures of maintaining water quality and 
general hygiene throughout the production must be implemented. Nevertheless, external pathogens still 
find their way into the system, and can lead to great economic losses (Skjermo and Vadstein 1999; Toranzo, 
Magariños, and Romalde 2005).  
During the larvae stage, the fish is building up their intestinal tract by ingestion of bacteria from the 
surrounding waters. The high densities of bacteria also facilitate the development of their immune system, 
strengthening their natural defence mechanism against later infections. This interaction with bacteria and 
potential opportunistic pathogens makes it a delicate balance whether the interaction benefits the larvae 
or results in infection (Hansen and Olafsen 1999; Engelsen et al. 2008).  
Some of the most economically important pathogens of the finfish species fed live feed are the species, 
Vibrio anguillarum, Aliivibrio salmonicida, Tenacibaculum maritimum (Flexibacter maritimus) and 
Aeromonas salmonicida¸ which cause vibriosis, flexibacteriosis and furunculosis, respectively. These 
diseases all have preventive measure in the form of vaccines available for one or several fish species, 
==PAGE:12==
Josefine Hansen 
101577/S144493  
Page 12 of 66 
 
however, these are not functional in larviculture because of the undeveloped immune system (Toranzo, 
Magariños, and Romalde 2005; Rørbo et al. 2018). Taking cod as an example, their immune system is fully 
developed 2-3 months after hatching, and vaccination is therefore not effective at the very early life stages 
(Engelsen et al. 2008).  
The entry point of the opportunistic pathogens into larvicultures has been evaluated several times, with the 
purpose of designing intervention strategies. Most pathogens are common members of the microbiome of 
seawater, and thereby water is believed to be a source. Though UV radiation is used to treat inlet water to 
the rearing systems, the pathogens still enters the larvicultures, and live feed has been determined as a 
critical sources of it (Skjermo and Vadstein 1999; Hansen and Olafsen 1999; Shields 2001). In 1996,  Grisez 
et al. investigated live feed for turbot and how Artemia could be a potential vector for the pathogen V. 
anguillarum. It was discovered that V. anguillarum are ingested during production of live feed as Artemia 
are filter feeders. Thus, during feeding the turbot ingested the pathogen. Whether or not this leads to a 
disease outbreak depends on the dose of V. anguillarum ingested by both the live feed and the fish (Grisez 
et al. 1996; Høj, Bourne, and Hall 2009). Supported by a later study in 2018, Rasmussen et al. show that the 
copepod Acartia tonsa is a potential vector for the pathogen V. anguillarum. 
With the knowledge of how some pathogens, especially V. anguillarum, enter the rearing units, it is of 
interest how to prevent this and potentially treat in case of disease outbreak. 
 
1.1.2 Current prevention and treatment approaches in larviculture 
Although several well-functioning vaccines are available in aquaculture, they are not necessarily applicable 
during larvae stage (Engelsen et al. 2008; Rørbo et al. 2018). In preventive initiatives the already 
implemented hygienic measures could be extended to be even more rigid. Making more strict flow control 
and cleaning protocols. Disinfection of Artemia has been suggested, but is not believed to be feasible if 
rotifers are also included, as they are more sensitive to the method (Shields 2001). 
Instead, bacterial disease outbreaks are managed with antibiotics. Although the use is authorized and 
regulated, the use of antibiotics in animal production is of concern due to the rapid development and 
spread of antibiotic resistant bacteria. The use of antibiotics as prophylactics is today of limited use, and 
looked upon with discredit (Shields 2001; Cabello 2006). The development of resistance towards one or 
several antibiotics, in bacteria, is not controllable, and continuous use can have negative consequences on  
ecology and  general public health (Oliveira et al. 2012; Shields 2001; Skjermo and Vadstein 1999). 
Furthermore, antibiotics are often given orally via feed and thereby rely on the individual ingestion. The 
==PAGE:13==
Josefine Hansen 
101577/S144493  
Page 13 of 66 
 
reduced appetite of sick fish complicates proper dosage of the individual even more (Oliveira et al. 2012) 
and leads to dispatch of antibiotics in the environment.  
Several solutions have been suggested in the search for new methods to prevent and treat disease 
outbreaks in aquaculture. Immunostimulants, polycultures and bacteriocins are among the suggestions, but 
some of today’s big research areas are the implementation of bacteriophages and probiotics as biocontrol 
in aquaculture (Oliveira et al. 2012). Bacteriophages (virus’ which infect bacteria) have been investigated 
widely and have shown potential several times. Several bacteriophages of pathogens in aquaculture have 
been isolated, including vibriophages, aeromonasphages and Flavobacterium phages. Though showing 
great potential, the implementation of bacteriophages is still being research and developed. Furthermore, 
as many bacteriophages are strain specific  the natural diversity of the present pathogens, can be difficult 
to match in a phage-cocktail (Oliveira et al. 2012; Defoirdt, Sorgeloos, and Bossier 2011; Rørbo et al. 2018; 
Castillo and Middelboe 2016).   
The use of probiotic bacteria, i.e. bacteria which when administered in adequate amounts confer a health 
benefit on the host (FAO 2001), is another type of biocontrol. Probiotic bacteria have proven to inhibit 
pathogens in live feed and in fish larval challenge trials Rotifers were grown in the presence of lactic acid 
bacteria, resulting in the turbot larvae gaining a higher resistance towards pathogenic Vibrio. Though these 
results are limited by their duration of the experiment, and thereby how to influence the production in the 
longer run is still unknown (Skjermo and Vadstein 1999; Gatesoupe 1994). Several commercial microbial 
products are available today for various purposes in the aquaculture industry, such as water treatment or 
as probiotics where one product, Bactocell® is marketed. Most products are based on lactic acid bacteria 
and Bacilli mixes, which, when applied in the aquaculture industry, need to establish for function. A 
potential for further development of probiotic for aquaculture is the investigation of the possibility of using 
probiotic of marine origin. One widely researched group within this field is the bioactive members of the 
Roseobacter group. Though, none of these have yet reached the market (Martínez Cruz et al. 2012; 
Dittmann et al. 2017).  
1.2 Bioactive compounds, the bioactive cluster of Roseobacter group 
The Roseobacter group belongs to the family Rhodobacteraceae of the Alphaproteobacteria and is widely 
found in the marine environment from coast to open oceans. It is a major representative of the community 
in the upper mixing layer and coastal areas, and lives as surface colonizers. Several general/species of this 
group produce antibacterial bioactive compounds. The two most studied of these are tropodithietic acid 
(TDA) and Indigoidine (Buchan, González, and Moran 2005; Dittmann et al. 2018; Gram et al. 2015). 
Indigoidine is a blue pigment which has been known for many years with the colouration of the compound 
==PAGE:14==
Josefine Hansen 
101577/S144493  
Page 14 of 66 
 
in focus (Elazari-Volcani 1939; Cude et al. 2012). Though it had previously been observed that an 
antimicrobial activity might be present, it was not until recently that Cude et al. (2012) proved that this 
activity was related to the production of Indigoidine in a Phaeobacter sp. strain. The previously antagonism 
against Vibrio fischery were in the study correlated with the also observed antimicrobial activity (Cude et al. 
2012).  
TDA was initially isolated from a Pseudomonas sp., found in soil, but has since then only been isolated from 
marine bacteria including several members of the Roseobacter group, and another Alphaproteobacteria, 
Pseudovibrio sp. (Kintaka et al. 1984; Harrington et al. 2014; D’Alvise et al. 2015). Derivatives of TDA are 
known in the medicine industry which makes it highly relevant in the search for bioactive compounds, also 
from a clinical perspective.  It consists of a seven-membered tropolone ring with a disulphide bridge, 
indicated in Figure 2 (Rabe et al. 2014). When produced by the member of the Roseobacter group, 
Phaeobacter sp., it is present in the hydroxyl form, but has also been found in its tautomeric form 
thiotropocin when produced by Pseudomonas sp. (Rabe et al. 2014; Haifeng Geng et al. 2008). 
 
Figure 2. Tropodithietic Acid (A) and its tautomeric form thiotropocin (B)  (Rabe et al. 2014) 
 
The production of TDA has been suggested to be of importance for the colonisation of surface areas as its 
production is induced upon attachment (D’Alvise et al. 2014). Along with the observations of Phaeobacter 
inhibens inducing TDA production during high cell densities, it has further been suggested that TDA 
presence and production facilitate a balanced community. Highly colonised surfaces could be regulated 
with TDA to optimize the living conditions of the TDA producer (Berger et al. 2011; Beyersmann et al. 
2017). The regulation of TDA itself has been speculated on, and suggested that TDA could work as an 
autoinducer when present in sub-lethal concentrations. This function of TDA has been found in both 
Silicibacter sp. and Phaeobacter gallaeciensis, suggesting that it applies to the Roseobacter group and the 
potential of TDA being more than an antimicrobial compound (Berger et al. 2011; Beyersmann et al. 2017; 
H. Geng and Belas 2010).

1.2.1 TDA production of Roseobacter group bacteria. 
The production of TDA Phaeobacter inhibens, Phaeobacter gallaeciensis and Phaeobacter piscinae is found 
both on solid media and in liquid broth. However, Ruegeria only produce TDA in liquid broth when grown 
under stagnant conditions, where the Phaeobacter sp. produces TDA under both stagnant and shaken 
conditions. Though, the highest concentration of TDA by Phaeobacter sp. is reached under stagnant 
conditions (Porsby, Nielsen, and Gram 2008; Bruhn, Gram, and Belas 2007; Grotkjær et al. 2016; E. C. 
Sonnenschein et al. 2017).  
Cultivation of Phaeobacter sp. in certain media results in a formation of brown pigment, which is not 
produced in TDA deficient mutants, and thereby believed to be connected to the production of TDA. This 
led to the investigation and discovery of the involvement of iron in the production of TDA. TDA can form a 
complex with iron, which is the cause of the brown pigment formation but not a necessity for the 
bioactivity. However, high concentrations of iron were still needed for production of TDA  (D’Alvise et al. 
2015). The bioactivity of TDA is seen as antimicrobial activity, as it antagonises various marine bacteria as 
well as human pathogens. This includes the fish pathogens Vibrio anguillarum and Vibrio splendidus. 
(Kintaka et al. 1984; Porsby et al. 2011; D’Alvise et al. 2012; Hjelm et al. 2004).  
 
1.2.2 Members of the Roseobacter group as biocontrol agents in larviculture 
TDA is a potent inhibitor of fish pathogenic bacteria (D’Alvise et al. 2012) and in high concentrations it has a 
bactericidal effect (Porsby et al. 2011). This obviously has led to an interest in TDA producing Roseobacter 
strains as probiotics in aquaculture, specific larvae rearing. Initially Hjelm et al. (2004) investigated the use 
of a Roseobacter spp. strain as probiont against pathogens causing crashes in turbot larvae systems fed 
with rotifers followed by Artemia. Though it was not determined to be the cause of infection, V. 
anguillarum and V. splendidus was found in morbid larvae and thereby assumed to be involved. It was 
found that the mortality decreased with the presence of the probiont, and that it did not have a lethal 
effect on the larvae.  
Later studies have supported the finding of members of the Roseobacter group have potential as probiont. 
The use of P. gallaeciensis in cod larvae was successful in lowering of the presence V. anguillarum and 
mortality of the larvae, both by the addition of a wildtype strain and to a lesser extend when a TDA 
negative mutant was used (Figure 3). In addition it was seen that the presence of P. gallaeciensis also 
reduces mortality, regardless of the pathogen not being present.  Further, Phaeobacter piscinae and P. 
inhibens, which also effects V. harveyi, has also been proven efficient towards V. anguillarum (Grotkjær et 
al. 2016; D’Alvise et al. 2012; E. C. Sonnenschein et al. 2017). In connection to the study by D’Alvise et al.

Another concern is what impact the addition of a bacterium that produces an antimicrobial will have on the 
overall microbial community. This commensal community is important, e.g. for health of the alga (Ramanan 
et al. 2016) and it is therefore important to determine how the natural bacterial communities related to the

1.3 The bacterial community of aquaculture 
The natural microbial community of aquaculture consist of several interactions between the organisms 
present. Among many studies the majority of research within the field of bacterial interaction with fish are 
based on adult fish, thus the knowledge of the interactions with fish larvae is limited (Olafsen 2001; 
Vadstein et al. 2018; Høj, Bourne, and Hall 2009; Natrah et al. 2014). 
From the first spawning and throughout the life, the fish is exposed to a huge amount of microbes from 
both internal and external sources. These microbes have a large influence on how the fish microbiota 
develops over time. During investigation of the microbiota of cod larvae, it was discover that the 
composition of the microbes rapidly changes over time (Vadstein et al. 2018). Microbes present in the 
rearing system are substantial for the composition, while external entering microbes are those causing 
most of the changes.  Though the abundance of the microbiota in rearing systems water flow and the given 
live feed has been found to be distinct from the microbiota of the larvae, these strongly affect the larvae’s 
microbiota (Vadstein et al. 2018; Olafsen 2001). The physical interaction of fish larvae and microbes are 
initiated at hatching, where the microbes attach to the skin, mucus and gills of the larvae. During feeding 
the microbes also colonise the intestines of the larvae, which establish the intestinal microflora of the 
larvae (Vadstein et al. 2018). 
The communication between larvae and microbe, and the overall rearing system, has been investigated by 
Vadstein et al. (2018). They suggested that after attachment and colonisation by the microbe, the larvae 
might sense the microbe through microbe-associated molecular pattern. Thereby communication in a form 
of quorum sensing is present both between microbe-microbe and microbe-larvae (Vadstein et al. 2018).  
The interaction of organisms can have a positive effect for some members, but some are also influenced 
negatively. In general, the interactions are structured as mutualistic, commensal or parasitic. Which of 
these types interactions is present, is dependent of the involved organisms and in which environment they 
live in. The mutualistic interaction is beneficial for both organisms, as they benefit from products from one 
another, while only one benefits from a commensal interaction. These cause no harm to each other, as a 
parasitic interaction does. Here one benefits from the other while harming the host (Leung 2008; Shandy. 
==PAGE:18==
Josefine Hansen

A. Amin, Parker, and Armbrust 2012; Ramanan et al. 2016).  When one type of interaction is established, 
this is not necessarily how it will continue.  This is for instance seen in the interaction between Phaeobacter 
gallaeciensis and the microalgae Emiliania Huxleyi. In nature these can be found in both a mutualistic and a 
parasitic relation. In the mutualistic phase E. huxleyi provides a surface for biofilm formation, and sulphur 
source in the form of dimethylsulfoniopropionate (DMSP) for P. gallaeciensis (Seyedsayamdost et al. 2011; 
Gonzalez et al. 2000). Concurrently the Phaeobacter provides growth promoting compounds and antibiotics 
for protection to the microalgae. When E. Huxleyi senesces, the breakdown product induced to produce 
algicidal roseobacticides in P. gallaeciensis, changing the relationship to a parasitic interaction 
(Seyedsayamdost et al. 2011, 2014).  
As mentioned the interactions between organisms are structured in different ways, and how they 
communicate dependent on which organisms is a part of the interaction 
 
1.3.1 Communication in Roseobacter group related bacterial community 
The communication of a bacterial community is structured by a population density dependent chemical 
communication system, also known as quorum sensing. This is controlled by production of signalling 
molecules which then enables the control of various cellular functions, and thereby a community can act as 
one, and not millions of single cells. This mechanism is also widely utilized among the members of the 
Roseobacter group which most common signal molecules are believed to be the N-acyl homoserine 
lactones (AHL). Signal molecules influences functions such as motility, chemotaxis, antimicrobial 
metabolites and colonisation, and with the knowledge of Roseobacter being great surface colonisers, this is 
believed to be an important key factor in the life of the members (Berger et al. 2011). 
As previously mentioned TDA is believed to be an autoinducer for the production of itself. With this TDA 
acts as a signal molecule for itself, and have thereby also been suspected to potentially be involved in other 
gene regulations than the biosynthesis of TDA (Beyersmann et al. 2017). In the investigation of this, 
Beyersmann et al. (2017) found that both TDA and AHL were involved in the QS of P. inhibens and that at 
sub-inhibitory concentration TDA can substitute AHL. This function is believed to beneficial for the 
organisms as the cost of biosynthesis is decreased when a metabolite can be utilized as both protection, 
weapon and signal molecule.  
From this knowledge the question of the function of Phaeobacter as probiotic was raised; is the probiosis 
an attack on the target organism or is it a result of signal molecules, or a combination of both? This is yet to 
be answered (Beyersmann et al. 2017).

Studies on the influence of P. inhibens and TDA on bacterial communities of algae (Nannochloropsis salina, 
Thalassosira rotula and Emiliania huxleyi) have previously been conducted. Common for these studies is the 
discovery of Bacteriodetes and Proteobacteria being the dominating phyla.  Whether, the classes of 
Gammaproteobacteria, Alphaproteobacteria and Flavobacteria decrease or increases, when P. inhibens is 
introduced to the community, are not consistent (Dittmann et al. 2018; Haifeng Geng et al. 2016; Majzoub 
et al. 2019). This could indicate that the host and other members of the microbiome has an influence on 
which members are effected and how.  It was found that the presence of P. inhibens have an influence on 
the composition of the community over time, while when not present no statistically important differences 
occur. Furthermore the composition of community members colonising the host is found to be different 
from those free-living in the water (Majzoub et al. 2019). How the community is influenced and how rapidly 
it happens is a dose dependent manner. Higher doses causes a more rapid change in the community 
(Haifeng Geng et al. 2016; Dittmann et al. 2018).  
 
1.3.2 Bacterial communities of live feed 
The microbial community of organisms used for live feed in aquaculture have some common members, but 
are far from identical. 
The bacterial community changes over with the age of the culture and the accessible nutrition. As the 
bacterial community of the fish larvae changes with changes in feed, which is also the case for Artemia 
(Høj, Bourne, and Hall 2009; Biondi et al. 2017).  The bacterial community present with newly hatched 
Artemia are not the same found over time. Commonly found in association with Artemia are members of 
Gammaproteobacteria class and Plancomycetales. Vibrio alginolyticus has often been found related to 
Artemia, which is a well-known bacteria of the intestinal microbiota of fish (Høj, Bourne, and Hall 2009). 
ISHINO et al. (2012) investigated the bacterial community of rotifers Branchionus plicatilia sp., which 
compared to the above Artemia community was more phylogenetic widely distributed. Some still dominate 
more than others, the Alphaproteobacteria, with several members of the Roseobacter group present. While 
Gammaproteobacteria, Flavobacteria, Cytophaga and Bacteriodetes members were also found in the 
community. During the study, the found community members of rotifers were compared to the members 
of the production water. Here it was found that they do not correspond completely, but when rotifers were 
cultured before identification, they were more similar (Ishino et al. 2012). With this it is suggested that 
laboratory cultures communities might not fully reflect the natural abundance of the members.

The laboratory cultivation is also insecure when investigating the communities of microalgae. Though when 
produced in closed systems, it is possible to introduce contaminants to the culture. Not much is known 
about the bacterial communities of commercially used microalgae, but the knowledge of it is believed to 
facilitate the improvement of the culture reliability, such as controlling pathogens in aquaculture related 
productions (Biondi et al. 2017). With this perspective (Biondi et al. 2017) investigated the community of 
microalgae Tetraselmis suecica. An outdoor culture were sampled throughout the four seasons of the year, 
and a similarity of 70% were found between the summer and winter samples. The most abundant members 
of the community were members of the order of Flavobacteriales and the Roseobacter group. Other 
members found represented Gammaproteobacteria, Alphaproteobacteria, Cytophaga and Actinobacteria. 
 
1.4 The project 
Worldwide the aquaculture industry is challenged by the presence of bacterial pathogens, which are often 
transferred with the live feed into marine larviculture. This can lead to severe losses affecting the industry 
both in terms of yields and economic losses. A suggested solution is the use of probiotic Phaeobacter spp., 
however, little is known about how Phaeobacter spp. impacts the natural bacterial community of the live 
feed. Thus, the purpose of this study was to investigate how the probiont Phaeobacter piscinae S26 isolated 
from an aquaculture unit (Grotkjær et al. 2016) influences a mock community of bacteria isolated from the 
microalgae Tetraselmis suecica. The study looked at how S26 influenced the growth of the community 
microorganisms, if S26 changed the community structure and how this potentially affected the growth of 
the microalgae. In other words how is the interaction between the microalgae T. suecica and its natural 
bacterial community influenced when the probiont P. piscinae S26 is introduced.  
Experimentally the study was conducted by characterization of 16 bacterial strains isolated from the 
natural microbiome of the microalgae T. suecica. Following on, the strains were evaluated in their 
versatility of growth abilities under lab conditions, and investigated for their individual influence on each 
other and the microalgae. This resulted in a selection of strains for composition of microbial community, for 
use in a live feed model system, where the interactions of the community and the algae were assessed.

Bacterial strains used in this study were isolated from the natural microbiome of microalgae Tetraselmis 
suecica obtained from SELONDA Aquaculture SA, Greece. All of the 16 isolated bacteria were randomly

chosen from Marine Agar [Difco 2216, MA] plates, with the potential of more representatives of each 
species, based on colony morphology on MA plates. The probiotic strain used was Phaeobacter piscinae S26 
(Grotkjær et al. 2016; E. C. Sonnenschein et al. 2017). 
2.2 Bacterial cultivation 
Bacterial strains were stored at -80°C. Initially, incubation time needed for formation of single colonies was 
investigated, when recovered from freeze stock on MA.  
When recovered from freeze stock on solid media, the strains was streaked on MA and incubated at 25°C 
for 2, 3 or 5 days dependent on the strain (Table 1). Pre-cultures were made as overnight cultures by 
inoculating one single colony in 5 mL Marine Broth [Difco 2216, MB], and incubated at 25°C, 200 rpm.  
When recovered from freeze stock in liquid media, the strains were inoculated in 5 mL MB and incubated at 
25°C, 200 rpm for 2 days. Followed by a re-inoculation by transferring 50 µL to 5 mL new MB, which was 
incubated overnight, at 25°C, 200 rpm for 2 days. These conditions apply unless otherwise stated.  
2.3 Identification of bacterial strains 
2.3.1 Phenotyping  
The macro morphology was examined by observation of colony appearance on MA, including colour, size, if 
the appearance is clear or opaque and production of pigment. The micromorphology was examined by 
phase contrast microscopy (1000-fold magnification on Olympus BH2), by mounting 8 µL overnight culture 
onto a microscope slide, and observing motility and shape. 
Gram test was performed by adding a drop of 3% potassium hydroxide on a glass slide and mixing a colony 
into it. If slime formation was seen (indicative of DNA strands) this indicated a Gram-negative bacteria 
(Halebian et al. 1981). Strains with inconsistent results, phenotypic and genotypic, were controlled for the 
presence of L-alanine aminopeptidase using Bactident® Aminopeptidase [Merck 113301]. 
Presence of catalase activity was tested by adding a drop of 3% hydrogen peroxide on a glass slide and 
mixing colony mass into it. If formation of bubbles was seen this indicated a positive catalase activity 
(Taylor and Achanzar 1972). 
By smearing colony mass onto ready-made oxidase slides from BD BBLTM DrySlideTM Oxidase [231746, 
Becton Dickinson and Company, MD 21152 USA], the oxidase activity was tested. Development of 
blue/purple colour within 20 sec indicated a positive oxidase activity.

2.3.2 Genotyping 
Genotypic identification was completed through extraction of gDNA, PCR amplification of the 16S rRNA 
gene and Sanger sequencing of this. The assembled sequences were compared to the NCBI database. 
2.3.2.1 Extraction of genomic DNA 
Extraction of gDNA was performed by recovering strains on MA and an overnight pre-culture which of 1 mL 
was placed in a sterile Eppendorf tube (2 mL culture was used from those with low turbidity), centrifuged 
for 5 min at 13,000 rpm (minispin Eppendorf centrifuge). After discarding supernatant, the pellet was used 
further by following the protocol of Nucleospin Tissue Kit (Macherey-Nagle) for tissue and cultured cells, 
including all steps. Pre-lysis was performed overnight in Eppendorf ThermoMixer® C with continuous vortex 
at 300 rpm. Extracted gDNA was eluted and collected in an Eppendorf tube, using 100 µL elution buffer 
(Buffer BE) from the kit.  
Using DeNovix, the quality of extracted gDNA was measured (dsDNA). Until further use, the gDNA was 
stored at -20°C. 
2.3.2.2 PCR amplification of 16S rRNA gene 
Amplification of 16S rRNA by PCR were done in total 25 µL PCR reaction mix with 1 µL DNA template. (12.5 
µL TEMPase Hot Start 2x Master Mix K [VWR Life Science, 5200600-1250], 1 µL of each primer (10 µM), 9.5 
µL DNA grade water. Universal primers were used for amplification; 27F primer (5’ -AGA GTT TGA TCM TGG 
CTC AG-3’) and 1492R primer (5′-TAC GGY TAC CTT GTT ACG ACT T-3′) (Gram and Melchiorsen 2010). 
The PCR amplification was conducted in T100TM Thermal Cycler [Biorad] with 95°C for 15 minutes, followed 
by 35 cycles of three steps: 30 sec at 95°C , 30 sec at 51°C and 2 min at 72°C. Lastly, a final step of 5 min at 
72°C. 
PCR product were quality checked in a 1% (w/vol) agarose gel with 1X Tris Acetate EDTA buffer [Thermo 
Scientific; B49] at 90V, by mounting 7 µL PCR product and using 1 kb ladder [Thermo Scientific; SM0313] as 
fragment length reference. Gel were stained with 3 µg/mL Ethidium Bromide [Sigma Aldrich; E1510] for 15 
min and visualized by UV radiation in Gel doc [Biorad; Universal Hood II]. 
Until further use the PCR products were stored at -20°C.

Purification of PCR product was done by a modified procedure of the single-step PCR product purification 
by Werle et al. (1994). 10 U Exonuclease [Fisher Scientific; EN0581] and 5 U Fast Alkaline Phosphatase 
[Fisher Scientific; EF0651] were added to each PCR product and in T100TM Thermal Cycler [Biorad] incubated

for 15 min at 37°C, followed by an inactivation step for 15 min at 85°C. In a 1.5 mL safety-lock Eppendorf, 5 
µL of the purified PCR product was transferred to each of the two tubes, and 5 µL (5mM) of 27F forward 
primer added to one tube and 5 µL (5mM) 1492R reverse primer to the other.  
Samples were sent for Sanger sequencing [Macrogen Inc.]. 
2.3.2.4 Data processing 
Data processing was performed in CLC Workbench 8 by trimming raw sequences using the trimming tool, 
default parameters, and if necessary further trimmed manually. Forward and reverse sequence of each 
strain were assembled and the obtained consensus sequence for each 16S rRNA gene was compared to the 
NCBI 16S ribosomal RNA sequences (Bacteria and Archaea) database, by Nucleotide BLAST (BLASTn), and 
optimized for highly similar sequences (megablast).  
Using EMBL-EBI MAFFT (EMBL-EBI 2018), TreeHugger (Wernersson and Pedersen 2016) and FigTree 
(Rambaut 2018) with default parameters, a phylogenetic tree was constructed. The 16S rRNA gene for the 
probiont Phaeobacter piscinae S26 were included (E. Sonnenschein et al. 2017). A known distinct outlier 
from the strains was used for rooting of the tree, the 16S rRNA Chain A sequence from Escherichia coli K-12 
(NCBI accession 5ME0_A). Phylogenetic relationship was cross checked with the NCBI Taxonomy common 
tree tool.

2.4.2 Embedding test  
All the strains and Phaeobacter piscinae S26 were tested for their ability to grow when embedded in MA 
since this procedure was to be used for testing potential pairwise interactions between the strains.  
Strains were recovered in MB from freeze stock. Melted MA were cooled to 44°C and individually strains

25°C for 4 days, with daily inspection for growth.  
2.4.3 Inhibition assay  
The ability of strains to antagonise each other was determined using a so-called “Spot assay” where the 
target strain is embedded in an agar and the potential antagonist (outgrown culture) is spotted on top.  
Strains were recovered in MB from freeze stock. Melted MA was cooled to 44°C and the overnight culture 
was inoculated as a 200-fold dilution (approximately 5∙105 cells/mL final concentration). Plates were cast 
and dried for 15-20. Remaining cultures were spotted by mounting 5 µL overnight culture on top of the 
embedded agar, such that all strains were tested against each other.  Plates were incubated at 25°C, for 3 
days, with daily inspection of inhibition and induced growth zones.  
Vibrio anguillarum 90-11-287 (Skov, Pedersen, and Larsen 1995) were included in the assay as control for 
TDA production by P. piscinae S26

3.2.3 Pre-selection of strains  
Since the strains, within each genus, appeared similar, one strain from each of the six genera was selected 
for further studies. The strains that performed best in the media test, and in general were easy to work 
with in the laboratory, in terms of consistency in grow times and density, were chosen. Also, the macro 
morphology was evaluated as mix cultures would be a part of the further study, and thereby the ability to 
distinguish between the strains is important, if all genera should be included. If two or more strains of same 
genera were equally good, one was randomly chosen. 
All of the above resulted in the further use of the strains AS01, AS02, AS07, AS13, AS15 and AS16.

3.3.2 Mixed dual cultures  
A contaminant was found systematically in cultures with AS13, AS15 and AS16 present, in both 24 and 48 
hour samples. The contaminant was morphologically the same in all. It was possible to identify which 
colonies on the plates that were the contaminant and thereby still possible to determine cell density of the 
strains used in the mix cultures, though it should be kept in mind that this contamination could have an 
influence on the results. 
It was found that some combinations interact differently than when investigated as spot assay. Especially 
AS01 were influenced as it was inhibited below detection limit, after 24 hours by both AS02 and AS15, 
which was not the case in the spot assay.  
As expected, most strains were inhibited by co-cultivation with P. piscinae S26. The tolerance of AS07 
towards Phaeobacter supernatant as seen in the agar-assay, were not seen here as the growth was 
inhibited after 24 hours. In contrast, AS02 seemed to tolerate the presence of the supernatant, as it was 
not influenced in the time duration investigated. The remaining four strains were inhibited after either 24 
or 48 hours, as expected when comparing to the spot assay. Other combinations showed no inhibition or 
clear induction of one or the other strain (9.4Appendix D – Growth graphs of mix-cultures).  
No further inhibitions were observed in any of the strains. In all cultures with P. piscinae S26, it showed to 
be the dominating strain of the two involved. None of the other strain was found to be consistently the 
dominating, but AS01 always resulted in being that on less density in the cultures.

4 Discussion 
This study investigated the effect of the potential probiotic bacterium Phaeobacter piscinae S26 on a 
selected natural microbial community of the microalgae Tetraselmis suecica. Characterising  the 16 isolates 
resulted in six genera; Algoriphagus, Muricauda, Mameliella, Roseovarius, Oceanicaulis and Marinobacter, 
of which some have previously been found in connection with Tetraselmis suecica cultures (Biondi et al. 
2017). Evaluation of the isolates resulted in the selection of one strain of each genus, except Roseovarius, 
for a live feed model system setup.  
 
All six genera are known from various marine environments, found in the water column, tidal flat as well as 
sediments. More importantly they have been found in association with algae.  Algoriphagus sp. has been 
isolated from both green and brown macro algae, Acrosiphonia sonderi and Chorda filu, as well as the 
microalgae Dunaliella and Tetraselmis suecica (Biondi et al. 2017; Nedashkovskaya 2004). The finding of this 
==PAGE:43==
Josefine Hansen 
101577/S144493  
Page 43 of 66 
 
genus among the isolates is thus in agreement with previous findings. Literature on a negative or 
pathogenic effect of this genus towards Artemia, rotifer and fish has not been found.  
Species of the genus Muricauda has also been found in isolation of the bacteria community of T. suecica. 
Muricauda are only found in laboratory cultivated culture, but also during winter and spring in outdoor 
mass cultures (Biondi et al. 2017). The interaction of Muricauda and aquaculture related organisms have 
not yet been investigated, and it is unknown if the bacteria have a beneficial influence on the community 
and interactions, or it acts harmful. However both Muricauda and Algoriphagus have been found to 
antagonise algae, though this has not been proven towards T. suecica (Manset 2013; Shi et al. 2013).  
The knowledge of the genus Oceanicaulis is limited, and currently consists of solely two species. 
Oceanicaulis sp. has been found in association with the  Emiliania huxleyi and dinoflagellate Alexandrium 
tamarense (Zabeti et al. 2010; Strompl 2003). Whether it has an influence on its host is unknown. The 
knowledge extends to an isolate from a Demosponge having bioremediation abilities of the pesticide 
lindane (Loredana et al. 2017). Also capable of lindane degradation and characterised as a bacterial 
member of T. suecica in this study, is the genus Mameliella sp. (Loredana et al. 2017). The knowledge of the 
members of the genus is limited, thus the knowledge of its interaction in communities has not yet been 
studied. The genus is a member of the Roseobacter group (Z. Chen et al. 2015; Shao et al. 2015), and those 
of the 16 strain that is genetically closest related to Phaeobacter piscinae S26  
When characterising the strains AS04, AS05 AS07, all were found to be of the genus Mameliella, the initial 
Gram test wrongly indicated that the isolates were Gram-positive bacteria. The approach by Halebian et al. 
(1981) used for the Gram test here showed not to be sufficient in determination of bacterial classification. 
This supports that the method is suggested as an initial indicator of the bacterial classification and addition 
to the Gram staining, as faulty results also were observed in the development of the method. As control of 
these contradicting results, the presence of L-alanine aminopeptidase confirmed the genotypic result of 
these three strains being Gram-negative.  
Another genus previously isolated T. suecica is Roseovarius, which also have been reported found in 
association with other microalgae (Biondi et al. 2017). Roseovarius sp. has shown antibacterial activity 
towards fish pathogen V. anguillarum when tested as extracts in well diffusion and culture condition 
dependent (Bruhn, Gram, and Belas 2007). Roseovarius strains examined for the genetic potential of their 
antibacterial activity being due to the production of TDA, gave a negative result (Haifeng Geng et al. 2008). 
Roseovarius sp. is the only strain, in this study, of the six found genera which is known as marine pathogen. 
Roseovarius crassostreae is the causing agent of Roseovarius Oyster Disease, previously known as Juvenile 
==PAGE:44==
Josefine Hansen 
101577/S144493  
Page 44 of 66 
 
Oyster Disease, causing mass mortalities in populations of the Eastern Oyster (Maloy et al. 2007; Boettcher 
2005). Though being a well-known marine pathogen it has not been found to negatively influence finfish.  
Lastly of the six genera is Marinobacter.  The genus has been described as halophilic and has also been 
found in saline soil, and in connection with oil contamination, which led to the discovery of the ability to 
degrade hydrocarbons (Gauthier et al. 1992; Wang et al. 2015; Luo et al. 2015). Strains of the Marinobacter 
genus has several times been isolated from microalgae, including as a member of the community of T. 
suecica (Biondi et al. 2017). Members of the genus produce an unusual siderophone, Vibrioferrin, which 
facilitate the assimilation of iron in algae. Research on the addition of Marinobacter spp. to industrial algal 
production has proved an increased growth (S. A. Amin et al. 2009; Fuentes et al. 2016; Shandy. A. Amin, 
Parker, and Armbrust 2012; Cooper and Smith 2015). This mutualistic interaction is found with the algae 
Scrippsiella trochoidea, but could be of high value of applicable to other algae species. Induction of already 
present bacteria of T. suecica, resulting in an improved growth, could be of great interest to the industry.  
 
Testing the versatility of the strain in different media was done with the purpose of finding growth 
conditions close the natural environment, having mannose as carbon source. The findings of most strains 
only being able to form minor growth in media with mannose or glucose, and desire of a denser growth, 
the tested media was found not to be useful. For a more nutrient rich media Marine agar (MA) and ½YTSS 
were evaluated based on their nutritional components. The differences found were the presence of boron 
and tryptone present in ½YTSS and Peptone and bromide in MA. Further MA contains iron which is not in 
½YTSS and a necessity for Phaeobacter sp. to produce TDA (D’Alvise et al. 2016). Other than that, similar 
components including salts were present in both but in different yet still similar concentrations. The TDA 
production could be facilitated in ½YTSS by addition of iron. With the knowledge of the strains being able to 
grow on MA and in Marine broth (MB), it was chosen, to avoid adjustment of the ½YTSS media with iron. 
With the choice of media and presence of iron all strains were tested for their tolerance towards a TDA 
producing strain. Mameliella sp. were tolerant to S26 and being a part of the Roseobacter group (Zheng et 
al. 2010) and phylogenetic closest genus to P. piscinae S26, it could be suggested that this connection are 
partly causing the tolerance. As Mameliella does not form the brown pigment associated with TDA 
production (D’Alvise et al. 2016) this is not the cause for the tolerance. But the genetic similarity to 
Phaeobacter, could potentially be the key, if the genus have the same or similar abilities to prevent 
influence of TDA as Phaeobacter (Wilson et al. 2016). No previous studies have been found on Mameliella 
being able to produce or tolerate TDA.  
==PAGE:45==
Josefine Hansen 
101577/S144493  
Page 45 of 66 
 
The observed induced growth also in connection with the genus Mameliella, AS05 and AS07, could indicate 
that the strains produce a metabolite which is beneficial for the other strains. Since growth of most strains 
is induced, this indicates that a potential metabolite is produced by the Mameliella strain. The strains do 
not induce growth of those of the same genus. Those with highest induced growth when grown on 
Mameliella sp. embedded plates were one of the genetically most distinct, of the investigated strains, 
Muricauda. It can be speculated if the presence of Mameliella sp. in a community with the investigated 
strains also will facilitate growth. Further of interest from the inhibition assay is the presence of fuzzy edges 
when P. piscinae S26 is spotted on embedded Marinobacter, AS12 and AS16. This indicates that the strain 
has a tolerance towards lower concentrations of the compound diffusing into the agar, believed to be TDA. 
This tolerance is related to the minimal inhibitory concentration of the TDA towards the strains, which 
previously has been proven to vary dependent on species (Porsby et al. 2011).  
Looking into variations between the inhibition assay and when the selected strains were tested in dual 
cultures and bacterial communities, some strains did not act as expected. Growth of Algoriphagus, AS01, 
was supressed in the cultures which was not to be expected from the inhibition assay. This could indicate 
that the robustness of AS01 is dependent on culture conditions.  Also differentiating from the expectations 
is Mameliella, AS07, which was expected to have a tolerance towards P. piscinae S26. In contradiction, 
AS02, Muricauda sp. was able to grow in dual culture with the probiont, while AS02 was suppressed. These 
variations might be caused by the interspecific interaction which occurs when present in multi-cultures (W. 
Liu et al. 2016; Jonas S. Madsen et al. 2016). These interactions can enhance functions of one or several 
bacteria, and have several times been described to enhance the resistance towards stress and antibacterial 
compounds when living in mixed biofilm compared to single species biofilm (K. W. K. Lee et al. 2014; Jonas 
S. Madsen et al. 2016; Jonas Stenløkke Madsen, Sørensen, and Burmølle 2018; Røder et al. 2015).  
Differences on TDA production dependent on shaken or stagnant growth (Bruhn, Gram, and Belas 2007) 
were not observed, based on brown pigmentation, when testing the bacterial community at the two 
conditions. Thus, it cannot be concluded whether changes are dose dependent (Haifeng Geng et al. 2016; 
Dittmann et al. 2018).  
 
Investigating the influence of Phaeobacter piscinae S26 on the full live model system, it was found based on 
cell densities that some members were influenced; Algoriphagus were initially supressed, while Muricauda 
and Oceanicaulis were enhanced shortly compared to the control community. After the initial effect no 
significant influence were observed when compared to the final samplings. Thus, S26 did not influence the 
==PAGE:46==
Josefine Hansen 
101577/S144493  
Page 46 of 66 
 
members of the model community at long term.  In addition, changes between the individual members 
were observed within the two types of communities. 
Though not being significant, there was an initial decrease in the strains of Bacteriodetes phylum, while 
Proteobacteria were more stable over time. This is different from what was recently found in the studies of 
the influence of Phaeobacter inhibens on a marine diatom. Here members of Bacteriodetes were observed 
to increase over a 8 day period, while Proteobacteria decreased (Majzoub et al. 2019).  
The live feed model systems are based on an inoculation of all members at the same time, which could be 
an approach of batch production of the microalgae. Assuming the pathogen is not present initially it could 
be of great advantage to introduce the probiont as a preventive and prophylactic measure, and not as a 
treatment. This is suggested on the basis of a previous study determining the importance of introduction 
time of probiont and pathogen, contra the efficiency of it (D’Alvise et al. 2013). 
Based on previous studies it can be questioned whether it is the antimicrobial activity of Phaeobacter 
piscinae S26, which is actually responsible for the influence on the microbial community, or it is simply its 
presence. As introduced Phaeobacter sp. has been found to bring down mortality caused by Vibrio 
anguillarum in fish larvae, both when antibacterial active and when not (D’Alvise et al. 2012). Also, Majzoub 
et al. (2019) investigated the changes in community assembly when Phaeobacter sp. was present. They 
concluded that no significant difference of the assembly was seen when comparing the community with 
wildtype Phaeobacter, and a non-antibacterial strain. Instead, bacterial signalling and simple competition 
for the resources are suggested to be main influencers on the microbiome assembly. If this applies to the 
members of T. suecica bacterial community is potential, but this has to be further investigated.  
How the community is influenced is not necessarily species specific, but also strains specific. García Bernal 
et al. (2017) observed a significant shift of the bacterial community of Oyster, Crasostrea sikamea, in one of 
four tested groups, when treated with probiotic Streptomyces. Applying this to the result of this study 
indicates that other strains of Phaeobacter piscinae might influence the same bacterial community 
differently.  
Zhou et al. (2017) investigated the influence on the bacterial community in carp production by three 
commercial available probiotic products for water quality improvement, containing multiple bacterial 
strains. Here they found no significant differences on the control and the treated cultures, but the study 
revealed the importance of the competition between the bacterial from the product and the natural 
occurring bacteria in terms of the efficiency of the product. From this it can be stated that the influence of 
P. piscinae S26, could vary if other strain were introduced in the bacterial community.

How the microbial community and its host interact, and how the community influences the growth of the 
algae could also be observed from this study. Initially the presence of the community, both with and 
without P. piscinae S26, facilitated the growth of T suecica to a significant level compared to the control. 
This could indicate the community, initially could provide growth promoting compounds, varying widely 
from directly growth promoters, quorum sensing to facilitating the accessibility or assimilation of nutrients 
(Cooper and Smith 2015; Fuentes et al. 2016). Following the first two days no significant difference were 
observed. Is the community providing the growth promoter only because of it working on settling and 
assembling the community, or is it a growth promoter which could be triggered. In the case of the latter, 
this could be of great use in the industry, enhancing the yield over time, or lowering the production cost 
with a more efficient production.  
The initial influence of the P. piscinae S26 on both the microbial community and the T. suecica is of 
importance when structuring the addition of the probiont and the frequency of it.   
 
Speculations on the results of the live feed model system, and optimisation of the method is present. First 
of all the cell count of the members of the bacterial community could be incorrect. This is based on the fact 
that the medium for plating of the dilution series contains iron, and thereby enables production of TDA by 
Phaeobacter. Thus the influence of the probiont might be indicated to be higher than it actually is. 
Members with a relatively low or no tolerance towards TDA will not be countable from the dilution series, 
and only few members seem to survive the presence of the probiont. This also makes the comparison to 
the community without the probiont, questionable. Differences might be even smaller than observed. This 
insecurity could be avoided if e.g. ½YTSS media had been used.   
Characterisation of the contaminant is of high interest, due to its tolerance towards TDA production by 
Phaeobacter. It can be speculated if the cell density has an influence on the survival of the contaminant, 
due to the high inoculation level of it and as dose dependent influence on a community have previously 
been observed (Dittmann et al. 2018).  Additionally, this cell density made it difficult to count other 
bacteria on the plates of the dilution series, even causing some plates to be uncountable. Further, the 
colour of the contaminant caused the identification of AS15 and AS16, to be even more difficult when 
present on high density plates. Avoiding the issues of high densities on plates and components of media 
potentially influencing the results, the determination of the individual cell density, should be assessed 
differently. An approach previously found useful for microbial community studies is quantification by qPCR. 
Though this still implicates a detection limit (Dittmann et al. 2018).

For future studies an experimental setup like this live feed model system in general enables the 
investigations of the interactions in bacterial communities of microalgae. By this potential harmful or 
beneficial bacteria can be identified, and their influence determined. This could be useful in investigations 
on other microalgae than T. suecica used in this study, and their respective natural bacterial community 
members, especially with the focus of implementing beneficial bacteria to the community. As the influence 
of addition of probiotics can be investigated with the model system, the specific influence on the 
community of a member can also be investigated. This could potentially give knowledge on how the 
production of microalgae could develop. Overall the model system enables the study of observing bacterial 
communities with microalgae, as well as the ways of modifying the community by addition of external 
bacteria.  
The model system could further be developed on the factors of duration and dimension of it. How the 
organisms interact at longer duration might be needed if the results are meant to be applied to a 
production longer than 8 days. Algae for aquaculture are produced on much larger scale than the, in this 
study, 50 mL. This could potentially have an influence on the community and how it interacts. Upscaling the 
system, could give a more real simulation of the system.  
 
5 Conclusion 
In this study the influence of Phaeobacter piscinae S26, a potential fish larval probiotic bacterium, on the 
members of a selected, Tetraselmis suecica microalga related, community was investigated. A live feed 
model system was successfully established with the purpose of investigating this. The selected microbial 
community were formed on strain of the genera, Algoriphagus, Muricauda, Mameliella, Roseovarius, 
Oceanicaulis and Marinobacter. This community including the potential probiotic bacteria P. piscinae S26, 
had a significant effect on the growth of the microalgae T. suecica, enhancing it immediately after addition, 
but growth was not affected after 8 days. The presence of P. piscinae S26 in the bacterial community has an 
initial influence on the growth, by suppressing Algoriphagus sp. and enhancing Muricauda sp. and 
Oceanicaulis sp., but neither with any influence after 8 days. 
Thus, this study concludes that P. piscinae S26 is implementable in T. suecica cultures without causing 
significant changes to the microbial community or the algae itself, keeping the initial adaption of the 
community in mind in terms of how frequently the probiont is added.
