Abstract 
In recent years, construction design of tall wooden buildings has become more popular. These struc-
tures can be built with Cross Laminated Timber as the loadbearing and stabilizing system.  
The advantages of its use is e.g. the off-site prefabrication, the well-known thermal and fire proper-
ties, the lower density compared to other structural materials like concrete along with the sustaina-
bility aspect of using wood compared to concrete and steel.  
Wood being a combustible material is a concern for many people and its ability to contribute to the 
fuel load in a fire compartment is important to take into account when designing with wood struc-
tures. Often is the contribution to the fuel load limited by passive fire protection with non-
combustible gypsum plasterboards. 
The use of wood in high-rise buildings in Denmark is limited by the Danish Building Regulation, 
which require that if buildings with more than 4 stories shall be made of combustible materials, the 
design fire has to be treated as a parametric fire. This demand is the motivation for choosing to in-
vestigate combustible materials in parametric fires.  
The thesis will try to increase the knowledge about fire protection of CLT-elements by the use of 
gypsum plasterboards and Rockwool under long heating exposure in order to find a buildup which 
can have the same level of security regarding fire as non-combustible construction materials. 
The wood charring times and the performance of different fire protection buildups are investigated 
by the use of specimens with dimensions of 450x450 mm, which are heat exposed by a Heat Trans-
fer Rate Inducing System (H-TRIS). Twelve specimen types have each been tested twice. 
The test setup has made it possible to have a repeatable fire curve with easy equipment control. 
Some of the disadvantages were a limited maximum temperature, unevenly heat exposure and lack 
of failure of the gypsum plasterboards.  
Especially the lack of physical failure of the gypsum plasterboards is affecting the obtained results 
because a failure could lead to the possibility of shorter charring times than observed. The lack of 
fall-off is believed to come from the specimen size and assembly method.  
The results from the performed tests showed that the largest increases in charring times were either 
from adding additional gypsum plasterboards or making a thicker insulation layer. Furthermore a 
large increase in the charring time was observed when gypsum plasterboards were placed in be-
tween the insulation layer and the CLT-element compared to the ones where plasterboards only 
have been placed in front of the insulation layer. 
None of the test specimen succeeded in avoiding charring of the wooden surface and a concretely 
fire protection system for protecting CLT-elements in an entire parametric fire was not found. The 
best performing specimen types were the two types with four layers of each type of gypsum plaster-
board which had a charring time of approximately 275-280 minutes and the test specimen with one 
plasterboard layer in front of 45 mm of insulation and two plasterboards behind the insulation 
which was tested in the full test duration of approximately 360 minutes. 
The conclusion is that combustible gases are produced behind all the tested types of protection sys-
tems which after failure of the protection can lead to a contribution of the fuel load which has to be 
taken into account when making the fire design of high-rise buildings in wood.

1. Introduction and hypothesis 
1.1 
Background 
In recent years an architectural trend has started where the design and construction of tall buildings 
have been made with structural components comprised of engineered wood. Some of these wood 
products are Cross Laminated Timber (CLT) and Glued laminated timber (Glulam) [26].   
When using timber in structures some of the key advantages are:  
- 
Off-site prefabrication which can shorten the length of the construction period. 
- 
Well known thermal and fire properties. 
- 
Low density of timber provides lighter buildings, which can require smaller foundations.  
Furthermore, there is a sustainability aspect in the usage of wood and this makes it possible to cre-
ate carbon neutral structures [27].  
A life-cycle analysis developed to determine the amount of carbon dioxide and fossil fuel saved by 
various wood products from Oliver et al. [28] shows that there is great CO2 savings by avoiding 
steel and concrete and replacing it with wood. This is not only due to the bound CO2 in the wood 
itself, but mostly from avoiding the large fossil fuel consumption in the production of steel and con-
crete.   
In a world where not only global warming from the CO2 emission is an important topic but also the 
fact that the world’s increasing population is moving to central locations in capitals and cities, this 
is all relevant. The growing need of more homes in larger cities poses significant challenges as most 
of these cities have a huge underground infrastructure for the support of mobility in the city. This is 
where the advantages of the lighter timber structures can benefit to a development. The lighter 
structures make it possible to construct higher buildings because the building will transfer less ver-
tical load to the underground infrastructure. This creates also an opportunity to construct shallower 
foundations and it will then be possible to build on areas where heavier materials cannot be con-
structed [27].  
Wood is a combustible material which is a big concern when using it as building material especially 
in tall buildings. The concern lies in the fact that wood can contribute to the fuel load in buildings 
and increase the initial fire growth rate with the risk of compromising the fire protection systems. 
This may result in more severe conditions for occupants, fire fighters, property and neighboring 
properties [26].  
There are two often used methods of securing a fire compartment. One is an active method of fire 
protection by sprinkling and the other is a passive fire protection with non-combustible gypsum 
plasterboards. Water sprinklers effectively secure that the fire temperatures are kept under the wood 
ignition temperature. This method is mostly used in offices, schools, hotels and other large build-
ings. In housing constructions, it is not as accepted to use sprinklers because the continuously 
maintenance check is disturbing the privacy in the home and it can make a lot of damage in the case 
of erroneous activation of the sprinklers. It is therefore often chosen to use gypsum plasterboards as

fire protection in housing constructions, because of the easy maintenance and renovation of the ma-
terial [29].  
In the study of Hox [14] a full-scale test was made of a fire compartment consisting of a dorm room 
with connecting corridor in almost unprotected CLT-elements and equipped with sprinklers. It was 
shown that if the initial start of the fire is not suppressed by sprinkling, the fire will develop and 
result in a flashover. A room with combustible surfaces will have a fast fire with high temperatures. 
The test also showed that the fire would have higher fire energy than that from a standard fire curve 
ISO-834 [25]. Though the fire is fast and have high temperatures the test shows that wood has good 
insulation properties and that there have been measured large temperature differences through the 
cross section of the timber wall.  
The literature review by Brandon et al. [26] focuses on the contribution of wood based construction 
to compartment fires. This literature review includes 41 fire tests of compartments comprising of 
exposed or protected wood based constructions. One of these fire tests is the results from the above 
mentioned study by Hox [14]. Furthermore, the literature review contains 4 reference tests of com-
partments comprising of non-combustible steel frame constructions. The studies have shown that if 
the timber is encapsulated, the contribution from the wood to the compartment fire will be non-
existing or insignificant. The potential failure of the protection can however lead to fire contribution 
of the timber and eventually lead to a second flashover. It has also shown that the presence of un-
protected combustible surfaces can lead to an increased heat release rate, but it does not necessarily 
lead to increased temperatures in the fire compartment. For under ventilated fires the contribution of 
unprotected timber can lead to significant flaming combustions outside of ventilation openings, 
such as windows. The contribution of the timber can, but does not always lead to a delayed decay of 
the fire. 
It is however evidential that in order to avoid contribution from the combustible surfaces they have 
to be protected from the fire. In the bachelor’s thesis Ellegaard et al. [24] a case study of a six-story 
building were investigated from which a single fire compartment has been singled out. This was 
done in order to investigate which effects the surface material in the fire compartment and the open-
ing factor have on the development of a parametric design fire following the Danish national infor-
mation DS/INF 1991-1-2 [8]. The results are also compared to the standard fire curve ISO-834 [25]. 
The results showed that unprotected wood surfaces under a parametric design fire curve in a fire 
compartment have a much higher and longer temperature curve than the ones with gypsum fire pro-
tection boards. These fires also had higher temperatures than the standard fire curve ISO-834. The 
opening factor is also acting as a key factor for the development of the fire. A high opening factor 
gives a short fire with high temperatures, and a low opening factor gives a long fire with substan-
tially lower temperatures. For a fire compartment with protected surfaces and a low opening factor 
it has been shown that the parametric fire curve can have a lower temperature development than the 
standard fire curve ISO-834. This means that the temperature development through the fire protec-
tion will be lower and penetrate the fire protection slower, ensuring that the underlying structure is 
protected more sufficiently and for a longer time.

The choice of investigating the parametric fire curve as the dimensioning fire curve in tall wooden 
buildings in Denmark is due to the limitations of the Danish Building Regulations [1]. A prescrip-
tive approach is often used for fire safety in Danish buildings. This approach is based on the docu-
ment “Eksempelsamling om brandsikring af byggeri” [2], which contains collated examples of fire 
safety measures in buildings and prescribes a limiting height for constructions build with combus-
tible materials at 4 stories. If wood has to be used as structural material above 4 stories a perfor-
mance based approach is needed. When using a performance based approached a parametric fire 
curve has to be used as an alternative to the standard fire curve according to the Danish Building 
Regulations [1] guidance for section 5.3(1). This leads to the specific use of the parametric fire 
curve as the design fire for tall wooden buildings in Denmark [30].  
Despite the advantages of wood, it is not used in high rise buildings in Denmark which is not due to 
any legal restraints in the Danish legislations or restrictions in the fire regulations. It is due to the 
uncertainty of the economical differences to other more commonly used materials such as concrete 
and also by the lack of knowledge in the designing and execution phase of Danish actors [31].  
Even though Denmark does not have any wooden high-rise building yet, there is a great support 
from a wide range of actors in the Danish building industry such as industry associations, contrac-
tors and engineering firms and construction clients. It is important to continually increase the 
knowledge about wooden buildings in Denmark and make a better foundation in order to get the 
first wooden high-rise building in Denmark [31].  
This master’s thesis will try to increase the knowledge about the protection of combustible materials 
in parametric fires. The goal is to investigate if the wood surface can be protected in a way so it 
does not ignite and contribute to the fire load. If a combination of passive protection materials can 
secure this it would be a strong argument in order to have the same level of safety as a non-
combustible construction in high-rise buildings. 
The investigation is done by making small size samples (450 x 450 mm) and exposing them to a 
specific fire curve. The combustible material is CLT-elements because of the huge potential it has 
in the construction of tall wooden buildings. The CLT-elements will be fire protected by two types 
of gypsum plasterboards which are fastened directly to the CLT-element. One of the plasterboard 
types is then further investigated in different combinations with an air or insulating rock wool layer. 
The fire curve used for the testing is initially based on the curves from the before mentioned case 
study by Ellegaard et al. [24]. The curves have been modified in order to be replicated by the 
equipment (H-TRIS) in the DTU fire laboratory. When a parametric fire curve is used, the duration 
of the test has to include the heating phase as well as the cooling phase of the fire and this can lead 
to a very long heat exposure. The knowledge base of long time heat exposed fire protection is near-
ly non-existent and this is where the thesis will make an important contribution to the understanding 
of fire protection and the initial charring of wood. The investigated parameters in the tests will show 
how the different protection compositions perform compared to each other under heat exposure and 
for how long the protection can keep the surface temperatures on the wood below the charring tem-
perature.

1.2 
Project formulation 
The main research question for this master thesis to answer is: 
Can the use of fire protection systems containing boards (e.g. gypsum plasterboards) and per-
haps insulation, fire protect a combustible construction (e.g. CLT) in a parametric fire? 
To get a better understanding of what is relevant to know before working on the main question 
some pre-questions are asked to help with the boundaries and background for the understanding of 
the main question.  
The pre-questions are as follows: 
1)  What are the Danish legislations regarding fire for high-rise buildings? 
2) What are the fire properties of CLT-elements? 
3) What are the fire properties of gypsum plasterboards when protecting e.g. CLT? 
The first question will provide an understanding of which legislations there are in Denmark about 
high-rise buildings, e.g. how long a high-rise building should withstand fire and what temperature 
development one should use. 
The second question deals with the combustible material CLT-elements. It will give an understand-
ing of the critical features of the material, and what to be aware of under fire exposure. 
The third question shall give an overview of the fire properties of gypsum plasterboards as fire pro-
tection systems for a combustible material as wood.  
The scope for this thesis and for answering the main question is as follows: 
- 
Define room temperature profiles (developments) for different parametric fires according to 
Danish Standards.  
- 
Define the setup for fire experiments, e.g. how and what to observe during the test. 
- 
Define different fire protection systems, e.g.: 
o Plasterboards with different layers 
o Plasterboards of different types 
o Plasterboards with air behind or insulation   
- 
Perform experiments. 
- 
Analyse the results and conclude.

2. Fire legislations 
This chapter concerns the current fire legislations in Denmark. Chapter 2.1 is a summary of the 
Danish building regulations which contains the rules for the construction of buildings in Denmark. 
Chapter 2.2 is highlighting some key points from the European standards regarding structural fire 
design both generally and in timber structures. 
The chapter is written as background knowledge which is making a basis for some of the choices 
taken in the developed method in order to obtain the goal of the thesis.   
Eurocodes is a set of common standards within the European Union and are currently representing 
the standards for Danish construction design. They include standards for all building structures and 
materials.  
In order to adjust the Eurocodes to specific needs every country has national choices regarding na-
tionally applicable values, methods or complementary information which are specified in the coun-
try’s National Annex [32].   
2.1 
Danish Building Regulations 2015 
From the Danish Building Regulation [1] section 5.3(1) is the provision regarding structural factors 
in fire safety stated: 
“Construction products and building elements must be constructed such that people in or near 
the building can be brought to safety at ground level in the open air or to a safe place in the 
building, and such that the emergency services are able to rescue people, make satisfactory ar-
rangements for animals and carry out firefighting work.” 
The Danish Building Regulation [1] does not make any restrictions for the use of specific building 
materials or structural solutions in Danish buildings. It is only making function-based criteria re-
garding safety of people and animals and the secureness of firefighters’ work. 
In order to secure safe evacuation of people simulations can be made to estimate the time needed 
for evacuation. The needed time can then be used as a demand for the structures to keep their stabil-
ity and comply with the safety demand. However the time needed for firefighting work is not as 
easy to determine. This work can both include total fire extinguishing or fire extinguishing which 
ensure safety to the evacuation [33].  
Buildings are normally separated into fire compartments to ensure the limitation of the fire to a spe-
cific area in order to secure firefighting work only has to be concentrated on the fire extinguishing 
within a limited fire compartment. Due to the unknown time duration for this, it is a good idea to 
secure the structural stability throughout the entire fire [33].  
The guidance in the Danish Building Regulation [1] for section 5.3(1) is: 
“Load-bearing structures in traditional buildings may have a fire resistance as specified in 
Eksempelsamling om brandsikring af byggeri [Collated examples of fire safety measures in 
buildings].  
For information on the design of the load-bearing capacity of structures subjected to fire, see 
the Eurocodes listed in 4.2. Other fire loads may be used if they are treated as parametric fires

in accordance with DS/EN 1991-1-2, Actions on structures exposed to fire, with DS/EN 1991-1-

The guidance is referring to the use of “Eksempelsamling om brandsikring af byggeri” [2] in tradi-
tional buildings, which is a publication of prescriptive based solutions constructed on the basis of 
experience. In this context “traditional buildings” refer to buildings where the floor of the top floor 
is maximum 22 m above ground level. The publication is material neutral and refers only to the 
properties of the structures regarding their fire resistance e.g. failure, integrity and insulation time 
and the contribution to heat and smoke from the used materials to ensure the safety [30].  
“Eksempelsamling om brandsikring af byggeri” [2] shows (in Table 3.1) that for buildings in sever-
al storeys, where the height of the floor at the top storey is more than 9.6 m above ground level, the 
load bearing elements has to keep their load bearing effect for 60 or 120 minutes and they have to 
be made out of non-combustible materials. 
This publication is then limiting the use of combustible materials in high-rise buildings in Denmark. 
 
Furthermore the guidance gives an opportunity of using other fire loads as a performance based 
approach as long as they are treated as parametric fires in accordance with DS/EN 1991-1-2 [3], 
DS/EN 1991-1-2 DK NA [4] and the fire has been documented. There are no requirements to the 
material as long as the codes are fulfilled. This makes buildings build in Denmark with the use of 
combustible materials possible as long as the fire is treated as a parametric fire. 
2.2 
Eurocodes 
2.2.1 DS/EN 1991-1-2  
When making a structural fire design analysis the standard DS/EN 1991-1-2 [3] should be fulfilled 
with the related national annex DS/EN 1991-1-2 DK NA [4]. DS/EN 1991-1-2 [3] section 2.1(1) 
states that when making a structural fire design the following should be taken into account; selec-
tion of the relevant design fire scenarios, determination of the corresponding design fire and a cal-
culation of the temperature development within the structural members. 
Design fires 
According to DS/EN 1991-1-2 [3] section 2.3(2) the design fire should only be applied to one fire 
compartment of the building at a time, unless otherwise are specified in the design fire scenario. 
This often leads to a one sided fire exposure of the element which in this thesis will be applied to 
the tests so that the test specimens only get fire exposed from one side. 
It is stated in DS/EN 1991-1-2 [3] and DS/EN 1991-1-2 DK NA [4] section 2.4(4) that when design-
ing based on a parametric design fire the temperature analysis of the structural members should be 
made for the full duration of the fire, including the cooling phase.

2.2.2 DS/EN 1995-1-2 
Eurocode 5 has to be complied with when designing buildings with wood materials and in general 
civil engineering works in timber or wood-based panels joined with adhesives or mechanical fas-
teners in Denmark. The code only concerns requirements for mechanical resistance, serviceability, 
durability and fire resistance of timber structures. Other concerns such as thermal or sound insula-
tion are not considered in the scope of Eurocode 5. When the structures then are exposed to fire the 
standard DS/EN 1995-1-2 [5] should be used with the related national annex EN 1995-1-2 DK NA 
[6]. In this standard there is only dealt with passive methods of fire protection. The standard applies 
with structures that are required to fulfil certain demands such as avoiding premature collapse of the 
structure and limiting fire spread when exposed to fire. Active methods such as sprinkling are not 
covered by the standard. 
It is stated in DS/EN 1995-1-2 [5] section 2.1.1(2) that when fire compartmentation is required, the 
elements forming the boundaries of the fire compartment, shall be designed and constructed in such 
a way that they maintain their separating function during the relevant fire exposure. When relevant 
it should ensure no failure of integrity and insulation and that the thermal radiation from the unex-
posed side is limited.  
Parametric fire exposure is treated in DS/EN 1995-1-2 [5] section 2.1.3 and states that the load-
bearing function should be maintained during the complete duration of the fire including the decay 
phase, or a specified period of time. The reason for the use of the full fire duration is, as stated ear-
lier, caused by the greater penetration depth of the heat into the cross section for longer heat expo-
sure which results in more structural damage.

3. Theory  
In this chapter the behavior of wood and gypsum plasterboards under heat exposure are discussed. 
Chapter 3.1 regards timber structures and includes a definition of CLT-elements and how wood 
reacts to heat exposure.  
Chapter 3.2 is an overview on how and what to be aware of when using gypsum plasterboards as 
fire protection on wood.  
Chapter 3.3 regards the properties of gypsum plasterboards and how they react to heat exposure. 
Lastly in Chapter 3.4 is a review and walk through of the theory behind the parametric fire curve. 
3.1 
Timber structures 
3.1.1 Dimensions and build of CLT elements  
CLT panels are made of several layers of 
construction timber which mainly is 
composed of softwood. The layers are 
stacked crosswise, typically at a 90 de-
gree angle as shown in Figure 1. They 
are glued together which results in an 
element with wood grains in two direc-
tions just like plywood.  
The individual board thickness often 
varies from 10 to 50 mm and the individ-
ual board width can vary from 60 to 240 
mm [8, 9].  
CLT elements are usually fabricated in uneven number of layers with three, five or seven layers. 
The uneven number of layers allows the element to have a main and side strength direction. The 
biggest load acting on the element is typically placed in the direction parallel to the grain fibers of 
the outer layers. [36] For a wall structure this is often vertical due to the large load coming from the 
residual construction and the snow load above.  
The production size of the elements typically has a width of 0.6, 1.2 or 3.0 m and a length up to 18 
m. The size and form of the CLT elements is limited by the production, transportation and the erec-
tion possibilities [35].  
3.1.2 Wood reaction under heat exposure  
A large chemical part of wood consists of water. Other than water, wood is manly build up by long-
chained sugar-based polymers (carbohydrates) which are bonded together by lignin. The carbohy-
drates are manly composed of cellulose and hemicellulose which respectively make up 40-45 % and 
15-25 % of softwoods dry weight. The lignin content of softwood lies in the range 25-35 % of the 
dry weight. The rest of the wood content is minor amounts of extractives and inorganics.  
The extractives are a group of cell wall chemicals consisting of minor organic compounds. The ex-
tractives are e.g. responsible for the color, smell and durability of the wood. The inorganics is often

referred to as the woods ash content, which is an approximate measure of the mineral salts and other 
inorganic matter in the fibers after combustion at a temperature of approximately 575 °C. The inor-
ganic content is in most cases around 0.5 % of the dry weight. The inorganics are essential to the 
growth of the wood and they are absorbed from the ground through the roots and out in the tree. 
The inorganic content and composition is highly dependent on the environmental conditions in the 
life of the tree [37].  
When wood is exposed to heat e.g. from a fire it will undergo a number of stages of change. The 
wood temperature rises and when it reaches 100 °C the water in the wood will start to evaporate. 
When the temperature continues to rise, the wood will get into an endothermic pyrolysis phase 
where breakdown of the long-chained molecules will produce combustible gases. At a temperature 
of around 300 °C char is formed. The char is made of coal and if oxygen is present it will oxidize 
and leave only ash as an end product [11].  
The gasses produced from heating of wood manly consist of CO, CO2, H and hydrocarbons like 
methane, methanol and formaldehyde. In the beginning of the pyrolysis process carbon dioxide is 
the main product. When the temperature exceeds 300 °C a large part of the gas is hydrocarbons and 
when the temperature further increases to 500 °C and above, the hydrogen content of the gas is get-
ting more and more dominant for the increasing temperature.  
When the gas reaches the surface of the wood and comes in contact with the ambient air it reacts 
with the oxygen in an exothermic process and combusts [12].  
If a char layer is formed on the surface of an undamaged piece of 
wood there will be a heated layer behind with reduced strength 
and stiffness called the pyrolysis zone as illustrated in Figure 2. 
The char layer has a lower thermal conductivity than the un-
burned wood and it will therefore act as an insulating layer 
which prevents the heat in transmitting to the wood. After the 
charring layer has formed, the charring rate for large pieces of 
wood can then be assumed to be constant during the rest of the 
heating period of the fire [12].  
The ignition temperature for a wood surface is depending on the 
shape, the content of oxygen in the air, the possibilities of the air 
to flow and the duration of the heat exposure. The ignition tem-
perature for a plane surface is approximately 280 °C corresponding to a temperature where it is the 
general experience that dry structural wood will ignite if it is exposed for 5 minutes. The longer the 
heat exposure time the lower temperatures can cause ignition of the combustible gasses [12].  
3.1.3 Delamination of CLT 
Delamination of the individual layers is a significant factor when talking about CLT-elements. De-
lamination is a phenomenon where the individual layers of the CLT-element are falling off the ele-
ment during a fire. The delamination will lead to an increase of the fuel load and the fire develop-
ment inside the fire compartment. This occurs because of the increase of the burning wood surface.

3.2.2 Failure time 
There is no information in Eurocode 5 regarding the failure time of type F gypsum plasterboards.  
Normally the failure of cladding is due to decomposition of the gypsum plasterboard or to the pull-
out of the fasteners. It is important to secure a minimum penetration length of the fasteners into the 
wood in order to minimize the chance of fall-off due to pull-out of the fasteners [15].  
The use of either steel or wooden studs in assemblies has not proven to have any influence of the 
failure time of the gypsum plasterboards. [16] 
The paper Just et al. [17] states that with some exceptions the fall-off temperatures of gypsum plas-
terboards, Type F, for walls are between 600 to 850 °C.

have the following results on gypsum plasterboards fall-off:

For 2 layer assemblies the base layer will have much shorter fall-off times then the face lay-

thermal shock will create cracks in the base layer and thus weaken it. 
- 
Insulation in cavities will slightly increase the fall-off time of the plasterboards in front of it.

3.2.3 Cavities 
When an empty cavity is behind the gypsum plasterboards the cavity will be filled up with water 
vapor from the plasterboards which will have an influence on the heat transport through the cavity. 
The paper backing of the board will still pyrolyze as the temperature increases, but the lack of oxy-
gen inside the cavity will prevent the combustion of the combustible products from the pyrolysis of 
the wood. The combustion will first happen when cracks are forming in the gypsum plasterboard 
letting oxygen and heat enter the cavity [22].  
The article by Chen et al. [23] which contains the analysis of key parameters that affects the per-
formance of gypsum boards by the means of a mathematical model. The results showed that when 
insulation materials are used in the cavity between studs, the radiative heating of the un-exposed 
board will be impeded. This is depending on the thermal conductivity of the insulation and the heat-
ing of the un-exposed board can be delayed significantly.  
The construction technology update by Sultan et al. [18] comprising important factors in achieving 
the required fire resistance for walls and is based on results from an extensive industry-supported 
research project by the Institute for Research in Construction (IRC). It is stated that when having 
rock fiber insulation material in the cavity of an assembly it has been shown that if the insulation is 
installed tightly between the studs it could provide up to 60 % better fire resistance compared to 
those installed loosely.

3.3 
Gypsum plasterboard properties  
3.3.1 Content and dimensions of fire resistant gypsum plasterboards 
Gypsum plasterboards are comprised of non-combustible gypsum in the core and are paper-
laminated on each side.  
The paper provides a tensile strength to the board as gypsum has a low tensile strength. The gypsum 
core layer consists of mineral gypsum, chemically bound crystalized water and a small amount of 
free water, which is the key feature of the gypsum plasterboards fire resistance property.  
The first 75 % of the crystalized water is easier to remove than the last 25 %. When the gypsum is 
crushed and heated to remove 75 % of the crystalized water it is known as plaster of Paris. When 
water is re-added to this powder it is easy to mold into a desired shape. When the plaster has hard-
ened again it regains its hard structure and strengths [39, 40].  
Fire protection gypsum plasterboards are often produced with glass fiber additives. When glass fi-
bers are added it results in better fire resistant plasterboards. This is due to the ability of the fibers 
that helps to keep the plasterboard together when cracks start to form under heat exposure. The ef-
fect of the glass fibers is more influential in single-layer assemblies. The effect is also more im-
portant for binding low density plasterboards than for ones with high density [18].  
Fire protection plasterboards are often produced with a thickness of 15-15.5 mm, width of 900 or 
1200 mm and lengths of 2000-2500 mm [41, 42, 43].  
3.3.2 Heat and mass transport  
3.3.2.1 
Water 
When a gypsum board is heated up to around 100 °C the absorbed heat will drive off the water in 
the board. This process is a fire protection mechanism which delays the temperature rise in the gyp-
sum plasterboard and makes a steady temperature plateau on the unexposed side of the board. This 
plateau ends when the water is evaporated from the board. Then the temperature will start to rise 
again [19].  
The chemically bound crystal water in the gypsum plasterboard is 21 % by mass. The amount of 
free water is approximately 3 % by mass but is dependent on the ambient temperature and relative 
humidity in the room [20].  
During heating of gypsum plasterboards, the gypsum will undergo two endothermic decomposition 
reactions where the chemically bound water is removed under dehydration.

The high content of water in the gypsum results in an internal pore pressure inside the many pores 
of the board. When gypsum plasterboards are heated, the pore pressure will increase and the water 
vapor will start to migrate from the area with high pressure to areas with lower pressure. If the mi-
grated water vapor is transported to a cooler region it may condensate and release energy increasing 
the local temperature. When the heat continues to rise further in to the board the water will eventu-
ally evaporate again. This cycle will continue through the thickness of the board. The liquid water 
will give a relative larger thermal conductivity and increase the flux of heat conducted through the 
region [22].  
The numerical study by Wang [21] is evaluating the effect of mass (moister) transfer in gypsum 
plasterboards on its specific heat value in order to calculate the temperature in gypsum plaster-
boards in fire by the use of heat transfer analysis. Most heat transfer analysis methods do not in-
clude water movement and re-condensation of water inside the gypsum plasterboard. Wang [21] 
made a simplified method to convert the energy consumed to drive off the water as an additional 
specific heat to the gypsum plasterboard. An equation for a water content equivalent average addi-
tional specific heat has been modeled which both takes into account the chemically bond and the 
free water in the board. The study shows that for gypsum plasterboards affected by a parametric 
fire, the change of the fire load has little effect. The big influence comes from the rate of burning of 
the fire. The higher burning rate will give a higher equivalent additional specific heat from the 
moister movement in the gypsum plasterboard. 
Heat transfer through the gypsum plasterboard is a combination of both conduction through the sol-
id and convection and radiation through the pores. Gypsum plaster boards with low density have 
larger pores than boards with high density. If the pores in the boards are very small there is no space 
for natural convection and the transfer inside the pores will only be by radiation.  
The heat transfer through gypsum plasterboards can both be affected by temperature, density, mois-
ture content and porosity of the material. This sensitivity is making the determination of the effec-
tive conductivity of the material hard and a large variation between different types of plasterboards 
is possible [19].

3.3.2.2 
Material 
When the temperature rises on the surface of the plasterboard the paper laminates will start to burn 
away at the temperature range of approximately 200 °C to 350 °C [19].  
When the surface of the gypsum plasterboard is affected for a long time by heat, an ablation process 
starts to happen on the surface. In this process a thin layer of material on the surface is removed due 
to the bonding reduction. This is caused by the water loss and the material turning into anhydrate 
powder which sheds. The glass fiber in the core delays the process and for fiber reinforced boards it 
is first observed when the exposed surface is around 1000 °C. Ablation has a greater importance for 
thin boards compared to thick ones, as the percentage of material which has shed will be larger [19].  
3.4

For applying with DS/EN 1991-1-2 [3] annex A the standard DS/INF 1991-1-2 [8] is explaining 
how to apply the use of parametric fires. The parametric fire describes how a natural fire is expected 
to progress regarding compartment temperatures which is defined by the building design and usage 
and is based on a heat balance for a fire compartment. The standard complies with the Danish regu-
lation 5.3 paragraph 1 and the national annex DS/EN 1991-1-2 DK NA [4] [8].  
The standard DS/INF 1991-1-2 [8] gives a solution for determining the gas temperature in fire com-
partments based on the fire load, opening factor and the thermal properties of the boundaries. In

The thermal inertia can have a large variation depending on the materials used. Often the value 
1160 J/m2s½K for a standard fire compartment is used. This can be found for a Danish traditional 
masonry construction and standard fire testing ovens. For high insulating elements it should be tak-
en into account that the heat produced in the fire compartment has more difficulty leaving the com-
partment trough the surrounding structures which will lead to a more severe fire inside the com-
partment [33].  
By the use of parametric fires, the insulating structures are taken into account, but this effect will 
not appear for a standard fire test following ISO 834-1 [25]. When a standard fire test is executed it 
is following a predefined time-temperature curve. If the element then has a high insulating effect it 
will result in lower fuel used in order to get the same curve than an element with lower insulating 
effect. This results in an over estimation of the performance of insulating structures and it can lead 
to a false estimation of the behavior of the load bearing structure protected by insulation [33].

4. Method 
This chapter is explaining the method used in order to obtain the goal of the thesis; determining 
how different protection buildups perform compared to each other and for how long they can delay 
the initial charring time of the wood surface.  
Chapter 4.1, 4.2 and 4.3 are explaining the test setup, specimens and execution.  
The tests are executed by placing the test specimens in front of a Heat Transfer Rate Inducing Sys-
tem (H-TRIS). In order to observe what temperature and heat flux the object is experiencing in front 
of the H-TRIS an initial test setup is made to measure the temperature and heat flux development 
through time.  
The specimens for the CLT testing are built up by CLT-elements where gypsum plasterboards and 
insulation is fastened on the surface. The walkthrough of the execution is divided into three phases: 
before, during and after the testing.  
Chapter 4.4 regards the used fire curve and it takes the reader through the steps from the initial fire 
curve to the final used fire curve used in the tests. All raw data from the determination of the curves 
are seen in Annex A.

The H-TRIS is based on the use of a gas-fired radiant heating panel and the thermal loading is con-

- 
The burner intensity, BI. 
- 
The distance from the burner panel to the specimen, x.

4.1.1.3 
Thermocouple 
For this experiment there are used Type K thermocouples from OMEGA, in order to measure the 
temperatures in the tests. The used Type K thermocouples have bare wire and were 0.51 mm in di-
ameter and 2 m long. Information regarding limiting temperature range and limits of error are taken 
from the datasheet [47] and are: 
- 
Temp. range:  
-200 to 1250 °C 
- 
Standard error:  
2.2 °C or 0.75 % for temperatures above 0 °C (whichever is greater) 
Regarding the endurance of the thermocouples it is recommended that they are calibrated about 
every 20 hours of use when they have been exposed for longer times above 500 °C. It is also rec-
ommended that thermocouples are not used for more than 50 tests. These recommendations secures 
that ageing are not leading to large measuring errors [34].  
The thermocouple is made from a pair of wires with different metals. The temperature is measured 
at a welded junction between the two wires. The thermocouple is measuring a contribution of the 
thermal response from both the gas temperature by convection and the incident radiation. A smaller 
dimension of the thermocouple leads to more sensitive response to convection and thereby the gas 
temperature [34].   
Another important characteristic to notice about thermocouples is their response time. The thinner 
the diameter of a thermocouple is the faster the response. From the manufacturer’s website [48] it 
can be seen that the response time for a 0.51 mm thermocouple is under 0.1 second and is then suf-
ficiently precise for the long tests executed in this thesis.

4.1.1.4 
Heat flux meter 
A heat flux meter is a tool which is used in order to accurately 
measure the radiant heat flux which is directly exposed to a 
specimen. The meters are measuring the combined heat flux by 
incident radiation and convection to a water cooled surface. 
The contribution from convection is manly dependent on the 
temperature difference between the surrounding gasses and the 
sensing surface and the velocity of the surrounding gases. Oth-
er factors which can influence the measuring by the heat flux 
meter is the size and shape of the meter, its orientation and its temperature level which is near the 
cooling water temperature [49].  
The heat flux meter used in this thesis is of the Schmidt-Boelter type from Hukseflux which can be 
seen in Figure 9. The heat flux meter used is a SBG01-200 water cooled heat flux meter. It has a 
rated measurement range of 200 kW/m2 and is calibrated to 100 kW/m2. The measurements by the 
heat flux meter are more precise the closer the measured heat flux is to the calibrated value.  
The rated cooling water temperature should be in the range of +10 to +30 °C and it should have a 
flow of more than 10 l/h, preferably 30 l/h. The used pump in the tests provided a water flow of 
around 10 l/h.

4.4.2 Simulated curve by H-TRIS 
The test setup for this project is based on a heat flux exposure from an H-TRIS. The equipment re-
view can be seen in Section 4.1.1.1 and the control of the H-TRIS can be read in Section 4.3.1.2.  
The H-TRIS has a limited temperature rise and the burner panel has a maximum temperature of 
approximately 950 °C, which is not enough to simulate the chosen curves found in the case study 
by Ellegaard et al. [24]. It is then chosen to scale down the two curves from the case study and use 
these for the experimental testing.  
The minimum distance chosen from the H-TRIS to the test specimen was initially 10 cm but was 
later changed to 13 cm. The reason for this is written in Chapter 4.4.3.2. It is wanted to come as 
close as possible to the specimen to obtain the largest heat exposure as possible. The minimum dis-
tance is also chosen for the purpose that if some of the gypsum plasterboard is falling off in the test-
ing it needs space to fall down between the setup and the H-TRIS.  
It is important to notice that the closer the H-TRIS comes to the test specimen the more convective 
heat exposure there is from the heating panel of the H-TRIS. 
The H-TRIS is controlled by a computer program which is modeled to make 20 time durations 
where the burner intensity of the H-TRIS can be preset.

Curve use 
The resulting short temperature curve is deselected for the use in the testing of the CLT-elements in 
order to establish how the fire protection will react to a short high intensity fire. This reason can be 
seen from Figure 31 where it is seen that the maximum mean temperature never comes above the 
standard temperature curve. Furthermore it can be seen that the temperature is less than 200 °C after 
approximately 65.5 minutes for Test 3. For regular fire protection plasterboards it is commonly 
known that two layers of plasterboard is sufficient in protecting the underlying construction in one 
hour. Because the short temperature curve lies under the standard curve and has a fast cooling phase 
it is already expected that two layers of fire protecting gypsum plasterboard would be sufficient in 
protecting the CLT in the whole fire’s duration without the CLT contributing to the fire load on the 
fire exposed side. This was later proven right seen from the results of the testing of a short fire on a 
CLT-element protected by a double layer of Ultra Boards seen in Appendix K. 
In this thesis the short fire curve was used as testing fire in order to discover non-expected testing 
errors coming from the designed test setup. The short temperature curve is chosen in order to have a 
short testing time for the pretesting and saving time for other work on the thesis.

The first full-scale test is from Hopkin et al. [52] where the performance of structural insulated pan-
el systems was investigated in fires. In total four two-storey high fire compartments with a total 
height of 4.8 m and a floor plane of 4x3 m were tested.  
The structural insulated panels (SIPs) were made of Oriented Strand Boards (OSB) and two of the 
tests were insulated with expanded polystyrene (EPS) and two with polyurethane (PUR) as insulat-
ing material. The structure was protected by a single 15 mm layer of gypsum plasterboard type F. 
Based on the compartment geometry and available ventilation, which was consistent with a normal 
living area in an apartment, a fire density of 450 MJ/m2 was chosen in order to achieve a fire equiv-
alent to the standard fire ISO-834. At the end of testing, the fire was extinguished. The resulting 
time-temperature curves are illustrated in Figure 36 along with the standard fire curve and the pre-
dicted response according to the Eurocode parametric approach. 
 
Figure 36: Compartment time-temperature response for the four large scale fire tests. [52] 
The second full-scale test is from Frangi et al. [53] where the performance of CLT-elements in fire 
has been investigated. The full scale consisted of a three-storey building with a height of 10 m and 
an area of 7x7 m. The fire compartment tested in the building was a 3.34x3.34x2.95 m room on the 
first floor.  
The structure was made of 85 mm thick CLT-elements. Two of the walls were protected with a 
double layer of 12.5 mm gypsum plaster boards where the outermost layer was a fire proof gypsum 
board and the second was a standard gypsum board. The third wall had only a single layer of stand-
ard gypsum board and the fourth wall had no protection. Behind all gypsum plasterboard layers 
were 27 mm of rock wool. 
The fire load density used for the room was approximately 790 MJ/m2 and the compartment had 
two openings of about 1.0x1.0 m each which both was one quarter opened. At the end of testing the 
fire was extinguished. The resulting time-temperature curves were measured in the middle of the 
fire compartment and are illustrated in Figure 37 at the distances of 0.1 m, 0.74 m, 1.48 m, 2.22 m 
and 2.85 m from the floor.

When comparing the temperature curve found by the H-TRIS with the four summarized full-scale 
temperature curves, the H-TRIS temperature curve generally lies much lower than the temperature 
curve from the full-scale tests. The four full-scale curves has maximum temperatures in the interval 
of approximately 800-1200 °C where the simulated curve only have a maximum temperature of 
approximately 500-600 °C depending on whether the measurements were at the two thermocouples 
in the top or bottom. 
The full-scale curves are though much shorter in time length compared to the H-TRIS temperature 
curve. The first and second tests are around 60 min., the third is around 45 min. and the fourth is 
around 185 min where the H-TRIS temperature curve has a length of around 360 min. It is though 
not known for how long the four full scale tests would have continued to burn because they all were 
extinguished at the end of the test.   
All the full-scale tests are assumed to have good conditions of ventilation and larger opening factor 
than the minimum for the parametric temperature curve at 0.02 m½. This will lead to a shorter time-
temperature curve with higher temperatures than if the openings of the four fire compartments had 
been smaller or if the windows had been initially closed instead of open.  
All four tests have different temperature developments which all are dependent on the room geome-
try, fuel load and ventilation.   
The first test has not a very fast heating rate at the beginning but after 20 minutes it has the devel-
opment like a parametric curve with a peak around 45 min. which is less than the H-TRIS produced 
curve where the peak should have been around 95 min.  
The second curve has a fast heating rate at the beginning and then it starts to level out and has a less 
steep temperature rise until flashover happens at around 35 min.  
The third curve has a fast temperature rise which ends in a constant temperature plateau after ap-
proximately 15 min. This curve looks the most like the reduced H-TRIS curve which has a steady 
temperature plateau after approximately 22 min.  
The fourth curve which had the fuel load of a realistic residential living and two large openings 
shows good comparison of a short parametric fire with a fast temperature rise and high temperatures 
with a peak after approximately 23 min and after this a steady cooling phase. However, at the end of 
==PAGE:57==

the test is a temperature rise which occurred because of the ignition of the ceiling. This shows that 
wood elements can feed the fire in a later state of the fire and results in another rise of the room 
temperature. 
From the four full-scale tests are it seen that a parametric fire curve which takes into account the 
geometry, fuel load and ventilation condition of a fire compartment can be a good estimation on 
how the resulting temperature development would be inside a fire compartment.  
Overall it would have been preferable to have a little higher temperature in the H-TRIS produced 
temperature curve in order to get a little closer to both the initial fire curve from Section 4.4.1 and a 
natural fire temperature as produced in the full-scale tests. However, the temperatures cannot be 
higher due to limitations of the H-TRIS.  
The curve is accepted for the use in this thesis because it still produces temperatures which are well 
over the charring temperature of the wood at 300 °C. Furthermore is the goal for the thesis to inves-
tigate the fire protections reaction to long heat exposure and this is produced by the H-TRIS which 
has provided much longer heat exposure than the summarized full scale tests.

5. Results 
This chapter comprises all the results produced by the tests. 
Chapter 5.1 contains general observations from the tests. 
Chapter 5.2 contains all the mean recorded thermocouple temperatures in the different layers in 
each test. Furthermore individual observations to each test are noted down if any are observed. 
Chapter 5.3 contains the resulting initial charring times of the wood surfaces and the material losses 
for both the gypsum plaster boards and the CLT-elements. 
The tests are executed in a random order to ensure that if any H-TRIS produced differences had 
occurred it would have been randomly exposed to a specimen. This also ensures that the repeatabil-
ity is more valid for the two tests of the same kind because they are not executed directly after each 
other and maybe can have been exposed to the same H-TRIS produced fluctuation.  
The test log with dates of each executed test is seen in Appendix G. 
In order to note down the masses and dimension of the specimens along with comments before, 
during and after the tests a note scheme has been made. All the filled note schemes can be seen in 
Appendix H.  
During the post processing of the tests have a lot of pictures been taken. All pictures of the charred 
wood surfaces can be seen in Appendix J. 
5.1 
General observations from the tests 
The following points are general noted observation to the tests. 
- 
The paper covering the first layer of gypsum plasterboard ignited and burned away on all 
specimens within the first few minutes. In the following layers when the specimen was tak-
en apart it were noted that the paper was burned and laying in big pieces. 
- 
When a gypsum plaster board was fastened directly to the CLT-element, it was discovered 
that the vertical cracks between the wooden boards in the heat exposed layer acted as a 
chimney where the smoke rose from, see Figure 40. 
- 
The covering non-combustible plate which was placed to entrap the produced gases when 
hat channels were used was not sealed completely and gas leaked through the cracks as seen 
in Figure 41. The plate did make a difference because it was observed that when it was re-
moved, gas had buildup inside the cavity both when it contained an air layer or insulation. 
- 
A lot of smoke was still being produced after the shutdown of the H-TRIS and when the 
layers of gypsum plasterboard was being removed some specimens had a lot of smoke re-
leased just after removal of some layers, see Figure 42. 
- 
The first layer of gypsum plasterboard no matter the type had only minimal cracking, see 
Figure 43. For the subsequent layers had the Ultra Board huge shrinkage cracks which had 
no specific pattern (Figure 44) and the Secure Board had not as large but still big shrinkage  
cracks these were often horizontal (Figure 45). For both types of boards the cracks first 
started to form after the layer in front has been removed and the surface has been exposed to 
the ambient air temperature.

- 
It was observed that when the hat channels were removed the underlying surface would have 
suffered much less heat damage than the rest of the surface, see Figure 46. The hat channels 
themselves did not seem to have taken any damage from the heat exposure.  
- 
When the insulation was removed after testing it had become very soft and broke easy apart 
when handled. 
- 
Dehydration of the wood surface led to shrinkage of the individual wooden boards. The 
boards were restricted at the next perpendicular layer so it was observed that there was no 
shrinkage at the inner part of the heat exposed wood layer, see Figure 47. 
- 
It was observed that when the fire resistance of a build got better the charred area got small-

- 
Heating of the non-combustible plate at the test frame resulted in spalling of the board 
(Figure 48) which resulted in the need of changing both the top board and bottom covering 
board during the test phase. The change has been noted down in the note schemes for the 
test that were performed after the change of board.

6. Comparison and Discussion 
This chapter deals with the comparison and discussion of the obtained results from the tests. 
Before the discussions for the results is Chapter 6.1 comprised with an evaluation of the method 
used. The chapter deals with the test setup and the execution for obtaining a theoretical curve. 
Chapter 6.2 is a chapter where the different time-temperature curves are being compared on their 
development and performance during heat exposure. All the results are then reviewed and dis-
cussed. 
Chapter 6.3 is about the different mass losses measured after the tests and is discussing the conse-
quences they have for the performance of the specimens. 
6.1 
Evaluation of the method used 
6.1.1 Test setup, specimens and execution 
6.1.1.1 
Test setup 
One of the advantages in the use of the H-TRIS as the heating source is its repeatability of a heating 
curve. From all the above time-temperature curves are the two specimens of each type very similar 
this indicates that the heating has been very similar.  
Another advantage is its ability to simulate a specific heating curve. The curves are though limited 
to constant time steps which gives a rough approximated curve. It is possible to make the time steps 
smaller than the one used in these tests. A more precise curve was assessed not to be important be-
cause it was the long heat exposure which was the essential feature of the curve. 
The limitation for the use of the H-TRIS is that it has a limited maximum temperature which it can 
simulate. This makes a limit for its use in order to have tests with higher temperature compared to 
e.g. an oven test.  
From the tests were a lot of smoke observed leaving the cavity of the specimens both seen from the 
cracks between the boards and from the cavities between the hat channels. The leaving gases were 
sucked up through the exhausting hood. This is not assumed to would have happened for a real wall 
assembly because of tighter fitting with the floor structures above the wall. This higher smoke loss 
indicates also that there is a higher heat loss from the cavity compared to a real wall assembly. A 
tighter seal of the covering plate would then maybe have led to an earlier initial charring time than 
the results has shown. But it is not expected to give a large difference because it still was observed 
that some of the smoke and gases were trapped inside the cavity 
If a tighter assembly of the cover plate is made in future tests with this test configuration it is im-
portant to have more attention when the cover plate is removed. The tighter plate will give higher 
concentration of combustible gases inside the cavity which can ignite when oxygen from the ambi-
ent air is added from the removal of the covering plate. So be aware of the possible of ignition and 
the need for extinguish which can happen as seen for the tests with an air layer in Figure 61.

will then not be any fall-off due to gravitational forces.  
If the first layer had fallen off the specimens it is believed that the next layers would not have kept 
the protection function as long as the results has shown. From the post disassemble of the speci-

large cracking during a test. It is then believed that if the first layer of gypsum plasterboard has fall-
en off it would have resulted in large cracks in the next heat exposed plasterboard layer. These 
cracks would result in faster fall-off times of the next layer and heat penetration through the cracks 
would also lead to a faster temperature rise of the next surface behind the board. For the specimens 
with 3 and 4 layers of gypsum plasterboards directly after each other it would be possible that this 
phenomenon would repeat itself through the different layers.  
If a rock wool insulation layer is present behind the fallen off plasterboard it is assumed that this 
also would fall out. This assumption is based on the fact that the heated up insulation from the tests 
have softened during heat exposure and it would then not be rigid enough to stay in between the hat 
channels and thus fall out. 
Charring size 
Regarding the shrinking charring sizes on the specimens with increasing fire protection ability, sev-
eral factors are believed to have an influence. The different influences are coming from: 
- 
The heating profile of the H-TRIS heating panel. 
- 
Water movement from the center of the plasterboards and outwards. 
- 
Cooling by the ambient air around the specimen. 
- 
The heat shielding effect of the hat channels. 
The factor which has the biggest impact on this is believed to be the heating profile of the H-TRIS 
heating panel. The profile is assumed to be curved as shown in Figure 70 which results in a higher 
heating at the center of the specimen than at the sides. The smaller charring sizes are more elliptical 
than round which probably is caused by the convection where the heat is traveling vertical upwards. 
The effect of the heating profile is largest when the H-TRIS is very close to the test specimen.  
The curved heating profile will lead to a temperature difference between the inner and outer area of 
the specimen. This temperature difference will give a pressure difference in the gypsum plaster-
boards pore structure where the moister will travel outwards as shown in Figure 71. It will also 
travel further into the construction and into the layers behind.  
If a cavity is behind the plasterboard the moister will travel into the cavity and rise up and be 
sucked out by the exhausting hood. The longer period of water present in the plasterboard will pro-
long the cooling effect it gets from the evaporation of the water. 
A minor effect which can give a cooling effect to the boundary of the specimen is the ambient air as 
seen in Figure 71. The lower temperature of the surrounding air will provide a heat transfer from the 
specimen to the ambient air and then cool the surface down. It is though not expected to give a large

6.1.2 Curve determination 
The initial guess for the time-temperature curve by the mean of the heat flux curve is a good initial 
start guess for the temperature curve. The choice of leaving out the high percentages from the curve 
determination is also a good choice especially for very close distances to the test specimen. This is 
e.g. seen from the final reduced curve where the high percentages are not making any difference in 
the measured temperature in front of the specimen.  
The curve determination by iteration with the fraction the curve was off the theoretical curve is 
maybe not the most efficient way of determining a specific time-temperature curve. Especially for 
the cooling phase was it seen that it did not come as close as desired. A way to do this different is 
by reducing or increasing the correction factor for each iteration by a fixed value which maybe will 
give a faster approximation of the curve. The fixed value can be based on the difference between 
the theoretical curve and the initial curve determent from the heat flux equation.

6.1.3 Error sources 
Some of the error sources which could have an influence on the obtained results are: 
- 
The similarity between the theoretical and applied time-temperature curve  
- 
Buildup of the test specimen.  
- 
Placement of thermocouples. 
- 
Equipment measuring error. 
- 
Human error in test execution. 
- 
H-TRIS heating flow. 
- 
Number of tests is limiting the validation. 
In order to have a theoretical curve where it is possible to recreate and compare it to other types of 
heating equipment such as ovens and computational modeling, it is important that the simulated 
curve by the H-TRIS is as close as possible to the theoretical one. Here is an error in the use of the 
H-TRIS because it only models stepwise heating and not smooth curves. The approximation of the 
curve is also giving a minor error to the heat development inside the specimens. 
When building the test specimens there can be some variations in the insulation layer thickness 
which can lead to different performances. The insulation batts are split down the middle which can 
give a slight thickness variation over the surface because the insulation clumps together when split. 
This difference will be less if more tests are performed in order to have a better sample size for a 
determination of a mean value and the variation of the individual specimens. 
The placement of the thermocouples is giving an error because they not are fastened close to the 
measuring point. This gives the risk that the thermocouples can move slightly out of place when the 
next gypsum plasterboard or insulation layer is placed on top of them. This is especially an error for 
the specimens which have a faster heating and charring of the center because if the thermocouples 
have moved a little outwards it will result in a later measured time for the 300 °C. 
The thermocouples themselves have also measuring errors. They have a standard error and they are 
also affected by ageing when used repeatedly. For the thermocouples that are directly heat exposed 
by the H-TRIS there is an error in the fact that they are measuring a combination of both convection 
and thereby the gas temperature but also the incident radiation heat flux. The thermocouples are 
chosen small in order to have less influence from the radiation on the measured temperature. 
In the test execution there are chances for human errors. These can both be from typing in the 
wrong input data into the LabView program and the movement of the H-TRIS position during the 
test. The errors from moving the H-TRIS can e.g. be forgetting to move the H-TRIS at the end of a 
step.

The movement of the H-TRIS is inducing turbulent air flow in front of the heating panel. This is not 
seen as a big influence on the results due to the fact that the measuring is taken for every fourth sec-
ond and the turbulence is not expected to continue for very long. 
From the results of the individual curves in Appendix I it is seen that the H-TRIS in some cases 
have fluctuating heating flow which can affect the measuring in front of the specimens and for the 
curve determination. The fluctuation is not very large and is then not expected to give a difference 
in the measured temperature between the layers. 
An error in the validation of the results is that there only are two tests of each specimen type. The 
number is limiting the validation by statistical analyses and the tests can only be used to give an 
indication in which temperature range and duration the different layers in the different types are 
experiencing.

One of the first things that were noticed about the curves was the difference of the temperature de-
velopments behind the first gypsum plasterboard of the two different plasterboard types. All the 
tested Secura Boards have a decrease in its temperature curve, often was this observed around 550 
°C where it dropped around 50 °C. This can be seen in Figure 74 where all the temperature curves

the board. This results in a much faster dehydration and temperature rise in the board in front of the 
insulation. It is believed that this shock effect is the cause for the higher temperature before it is 
decreasing again.

6.3 
Discussion of the mass losses 
Mass losses from the gypsum plasterboards 
The mass losses from the gypsum plasterboards were very similar for each specimen type through 
the tests. Figure 83 is a diagram which illustrates the mean mass losses for all the tested specimen 
types. The first plasterboard layer has very little variation from specimen type to specimen type and 
the Secure Board has a mean mass loss of 22.8 % with a standard deviation of ±0.12 % where the 
Ultra board has a mean mass loss of 22.5 % with a standard deviation of ±0.29 %. The two plaster-
board types have then almost no difference in their mass losses when they are equally heat exposed. 
The amounts of mass losses are in good agreement with the theory which states 21 % chemically 
bound water and 3 % free water. This indicates that almost all the water has evaporated from the 
first layer of gypsum plasterboard. 
The different is larger for the next layers of gypsum plasterboard because some of the types have 
insulation in front of one or two layers of gypsum. For the insulation layer makes the hat channel a 
big difference because they protect the underlying piece of board and then the evaporation from that 
part which will give a lower measured mass loss of the plasterboards behind. This is especially seen 
for the specimens with 45 mm of insulation (1+2S-45I). For these specimens are there very large 
differences between the mass losses of the different layers of plasterboard. This is caused by the 
concentrated heat exposure to the center of the specimen where the outer area not will have experi-
enced the same evenly distributed evaporation over the surface. 
When the water has evaporated from the plasterboard it has the consequence that the temperature 
behind the board will increase rapidly. The longer the board can keep its water inside the board, the 
longer it will be able to protect the structure behind.

Mass losses from the CLT-elements 
The mass loss from the CLT-elements varies a lot both between the different specimen types and 
individual between the two tests of the same type. There are several factors which have influence on 
the mass loss of the CLT-element, where the mass loss manly consists of water vapor and combus-
tible gases. Some of the reasons are that: 
- 
The individual tests have been heat exposed in different time durations. The tests with the 
better fire resistance have been heat exposed for a longer time than toughs with lesser fire 
resistance. 
- 
The types with better fire resistance have experienced longer periods with temperatures over 
100 °C, where the water evaporates and the pyrolysis is happening. 
- 
The heating area is less for a better fire resistance buildup. 
The consequences for mass losses of the CLT-elements are that combustible gases are produced. 
This is even though the protection has not failed and the CLT is not directly heat exposed. These 
gases can after failure of the protection contribute to the fire load of the fire compartment. It is then 
important that the fire protection has as long failure time as possible in order not to let the gases 
which are being produced out and into the fire compartment where they can ignite and contribute to 
the fire load.

7. Conclusion 
This chapter is a conclusion on the obtained results from the work behind this master thesis.  
Chapter 7.1 is a conclusion on the method used for obtaining the results and some of the advantages 
and disadvantages for the used method.  
Chapter 7.2 is the final conclusion on the obtained results and regards the performance of the indi-
vidual fire protection buildups tested. It also contain the essential parts which are the charring times 
and what chances there are for the CLT-elements to contribute to the fuel load. 
7.1 
Method used 
The used method for this master thesis is mainly based on the use of a H-TRIS with a test specimen 
placed in front of it. The test setup had both advantages and disadvantages for the specific use in 
this project. In the following are listed some of the main advantages and disadvantages for the spe-
cific method used. 
Advantages: 
 
- 
Repeatability of a specific heating curve. 
- 
Easy control of equipment. 
Disadvantages: 
- 
Limited maximum temperature. 
- 
Unevenly heat exposure on the wood surface. 
- 
No observed gypsum plasterboard fall-off. 
The advantage of the repeatability of a specific curve is the most essential for the use of the H-
TRIS. This feature makes it possible to compare different tests and to have a base for the subse-
quent discussions. 
An easy control of the equipment is also an important advantage because it is limiting the human 
errors which can occur. The test setup can be controlled by one person and during the tests there is a 
limited amount of humanly work that has to be done, which only involves start of the equipment, 
moving the H-TRIS and stop of the equipment. A good consequent procedure before and after the 
execution phase, will secure a good constant flow through the tests. 
An important disadvantage to notice from the use of the H-TRIS is the limitation of its maximum 
temperature which it can expose to a specimen. For a long time-temperature curve it is not the max-
imum temperature that is the essential but the duration of the heat exposure and this is applied for 
the one used in this thesis. For a long heat exposure it is possible to get results with temperatures 
around 500-700 °C but if a fire with high temperatures for example over 1000 °C is needed it cannot 
be done with the H-TRIS in the DTU fire laboratory.  
The disadvantage from the unevenly heat exposure makes it hard to get a clear picture of the char-
ring time at the wood surface. More thermocouples are then needed in order to get a full picture of 
the heating over the entire surface. If an average temperature distribution is not needed it would be 
preferable to center the thermocouples even more than has been done in this thesis in order to obtain 
a more precise initial charring of the wood surface.

The lack of gypsum plasterboard fall-off compared with observed fall-off during full-scale tests has 
the disadvantage that the consequences of fall-off are not a part of the results from the executed 
tests. Fall-off can be important because it can lead to faster charring times and when the fire protec-
tion falls off the combustible gases behind and the direct heat exposure to the CLT-elements will 
make an unwanted contribution to the fuel load in a fire compartment. 
7.2 
Thesis results 
The purpose of this master thesis was to increase the knowledge base for the fire protection of com-
bustible materials in parametric fires. It was desired to investigate whether a combination of passive 
protection could result in the same level of safety as structures only made from non-combustible 
materials in a high-rise building. This was executed by investigating the performance of different 
fire protection buildups and if they could limit the contribution from the combustible material to the 
fuel load in a fire compartment. 
The fire simulated in this project was intended to be a parametric fire curve but has been scaled 
down in order to be produced by the H-TRIS. The heating is provided in a long time duration both 
with a heating and a cooling phase due to the critical conditions long fires apply to structures. The 
used curve simulated a fire with maximum temperatures of approximately 500-700 °C and with 
time duration of approximately 360 minutes.  
The parametric time-temperature curve was chosen in order to comply with the Danish Building 
Regulation and uses a performance based method in the determination of the fire safety in high-rise 
buildings. 
The knowledge base of long time heating exposure for fire protection has been extended due to the 
knowledge from this thesis. It gives an understanding on the performance of gypsum plasterboards 
as fire protection, the charring times of CLT-elements and the consequences it has.  
Overall 12 different types of buildups were tested with gypsum plasterboards where these also were 
in combination with an air gap or insulation. There have been tested 2, 3 and 4 layers of gypsum 
plasterboard directly fixed to the CLT-element and in combination with 25 mm of air gap or 25 mm 
or 45 mm of insulation. Each specimen type has been tested twice. 
From the results and comparisons of the different specimen’s fire resistances regarding charring 
times without gypsum plasterboard fall-off are the following concluded: 
- 
The influence of the number of gypsum plasterboards increases the temperature plateau 
from the evaporation of the water in the plasterboards which results in a large increase in 
charring times (approximately 104 % from 2 to 3 boards and 68 % from 3 to 4 boards).   
- 
25 mm air or insulation layer are not making a big difference compared to directly fixed 
plasterboards when regarding charring times (approximately 8-14 %). 
- 
The placement of gypsum plasterboards between the insulation layer and the CLT-element 
gives a significant increase in charring times compared to specimens where the plaster-
boards are only placed in front of the insulation layer (approximately 30 %).  
- 
An increase of the insulation thickness from 25 mm to 45 mm gives a large increase in the 
charring time (larger than 55 %).

Insulation behind the gypsum plasterboards has an influence on the evaporation of the water content 
of the gypsum plasterboard. The insulation layer results in a faster heating of the gypsum plaster-
boards and with that a quicker evaporation of the water. The faster evaporation results in a rapidly 
increase in the temperature behind the boards. This is a negative reaction for the fire resistance be-
cause it is the water that secures a low temperature in the protection buildup.   
The best performing fire protection buildups out of the tested ones were those with four layers of 
gypsum plasterboards and the one buildup with one layer of plasterboard in front of 45 mm of insu-
lation and two layers of plasterboards behind. These are very material heavy buildups and may not 
be the most cost attractive for the client.  
The four layers of plasterboards were both made with the Secure Board and the Ultra Board pro-
duced by Knauf. Both types showed the same level of protection of approximately 275-280 minutes 
before a mean charring temperature was observed at the surface of the wood.  
The type with the 45 mm of insulation was never measured to have charring temperatures at the 
four initial chosen thermocouples in the full duration of the test which was stopped after approxi-
mately 360 min. 
The three best performing specimens just mentioned had visible charring and they all had earlier 
heat and char formation in the center of the specimen after the tests. This was due to increased heat-
ing of the center of the wood surface compared to the four measured points. The center temperature 
of the three types showed that the difference of the four layers compared with the 45 mm of insula-
tion were almost identical which can lead to the conclusion that the performance maybe are only a 
little better for the insulated specimens than the specimens with only gypsum plasterboards. 
The goal of the thesis was to find out whether the protection prevents charring on the wood surface 
in an entire parametric fire however this was not obtained. All specimens experienced charring and 
with those combustible gases were formed. Combustible gases are already building up behind the 
fire protection even before the charring of the wooden surface and these can eventually increase the 
fuel load in the fire compartment. 
The failure time of the protection is maybe more important than the charring time when talking 
about contribution to the fuel load from the combustible materials behind. The failure will make it 
possible for the combustible gases to come in contact with the heat and oxygen in the fire compart-
ment and then ignite. Furthermore will the combustible material underneath be directly heat ex-
posed which would accelerate the charring and burning of the combustible material. A longer fail-
ure time is then also important when looking at protection abilities for different materials. 
Even though none off the tested buildups managed to protect the CLT-elements for an entire para-
metric fire curve is the information on how gypsum plasterboards reacts to long heat exposure still 
important. The information about the changes that can be made in order to prolong the fire re-
sistance when they are used is also important.

8. Perspective 
This chapter will highlight what this thesis has learned and how it can be used by others in order to 
come closer to the goal of learning more about CLT-elements and getting the first high-rise building 
with wood structures in Denmark.  
Some of the future work can build upon this knowledge from the thesis and come closer to a fire 
protection solution which can protect combustible materials in parametric fires and how it can be 
used in order to do it more general and useable for everyday engineers.  
8.1 
What can be used from the thesis? 
The most important information that can be used from this thesis is the resulting reactions from 
gypsum plasterboards as protection on CLT-elements. The results shows how different buildups 
with gypsum plasterboards reacts to long heat exposure and which adjustable parameters that can 
give the best effect when protecting CLT-elements. 
Furthermore the experiences from the development of a repeatable test method to determine the 
different reactions from fire protection of CLT-elements can be used as knowledge in order to fur-
ther develop a test setup. The test setup will be able to compare the results in this thesis with other 
types of protection materials then gypsum plasterboards. 
8.2 
What future work has to be done? 
Other time-temperature curves 
There has only been used one specific time-temperature curve for the tests in this thesis. This is 
limiting the knowledge for how gypsum plasterboards react to other types of heat exposure e.g. 
shorter fire curves with higher temperatures. 
It would be beneficial in the future if more parametric fires were tested on gypsum plasterboards 
with different time-temperature curves in order to investigate the changes in failure times and what 
charring times it would give. 
It is expected that the long temperature curve is the most critical for the structure but it would be 
preferable to base it on tests instead of expectations when specimens are exposed to a parametric 
fire curve.  
Statistical analysis 
In this thesis only two of each specimen type is tested, which is not enough tests to make a statisti-
cal basis for an evident conclusion on how gypsum reacts. It is more an indication of how the dif-
ferent types performed compared to one another and what the charring times are. 
It is then needed to make more tests in future research when a buildup is found to give the wanted 
results. The larger amount of tests will provide validation to the results and make a stronger argu-
ment for an approval of its use as fire protection on combustible materials.

Failure times of protection 
Fall-off is not observed in any of the tested specimens and this can lead to errors if it is not taken 
into account when looking at fire protection of any type of construction. Fall-off can lead to release 
of combustible gases and direct heat exposure to the underlying structure. Fall-off is often observed 
in full-scale tests. It is then important to take fall-off into account.  
Therefore future work has to make sure that the test setup is providing knowledge about whether or 
not there is a risk of the fire protection to fall off. This can maybe be done with larger testing sizes 
or another fastener pattern.  
Other materials for protection 
The gypsum plasterboard showed not full capability in protection the CLT-elements in the full dura-
tion of the parametric fire. It can be a possibility that other materials can be better as fire protection 
on CLT-elements and still be affordable in comparison.  
In future work it can be an advantage to investigate other materials in the use of fire protection and 
compare them to the performance of gypsum plasterboards. 
Horizontal tests 
This thesis has only looked at fire protection on vertical surfaces but a horizontal surface has anoth-
er resulting force from gravity and it is often observed that horizontal fire protection in the ceiling 
fails before the other surfaces. This is also caused by the larger heating from the uprising gases 
which are formed underneath the ceiling. 
It is therefore important in future work also to test horizontal buildups and how these will react to 
long time fire and if their reactions are far from those for a vertical structure.  
FEM-modeling 
Parametric fire curves are very different between fire compartments and that is why it could be an 
advantage to have a FEM-model tool which could predict the temperature development through a 
construction for any type of parametric fire curve. This can be done by using tests for calibration of 
a FEM-model. 
A future investigation on how to calibrate and make FEM-models for this specific use is also an 
important aspect which needs more investigation and can be used for future engineers in the every-
day design of fire protected structures.

Appendix K – Results from S-2U-0-01 
This appendix is compromising the results from the pre-test made with the short parametric fire 
curve. 
The results are seen in the diagram below were it is seen that no temperatures above 200 °C are 
measured at the surface of the wood which indicates that two gypsum plasterboards is sufficient in 
protection the underling CLT-element during the full length of the heating. 
 
There was no visible charring in the centre of the specimen as seen in the picture below. The char-
ring was seen in the bottom of the test specimen which indicated that heat got under the specimen 
and heated the bottom part more than the rest. The solution in order to fix this problem with bottom 
heating was to install a bottom covering plate on the test frame.
