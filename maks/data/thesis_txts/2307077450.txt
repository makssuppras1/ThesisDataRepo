Abstract 
The nutrient-rich anaerobically digested effluents were used for extracting water using 
forward osmosis (FO) process at the bench-scale and the FO process has also the potential in 
retaining the nutrients in the feed solution. The optimal draw solution (DS) evaluated from 
phase 1 experiments for the FO process to extract water for this specific project is found to be 
MgCl2 due to the minimal reverse salt flux and contribution to high osmotic pressure 
difference. The different feed solutions (FS) used for the FO process in the phase 2 experiments 
are potato starch wastewater, synthetic sugars, synthetic proteins, synthetic lipids, synthetic 
mixture, cattle manure (thermophilic), cattle manure (mesophilic) and swine manure. The 
performance of the FO-flat sheet membrane and the success criteria was based on the water 
flux, reverse salt flux and the nutrient rejection by the membrane. The average water flux is 
improved upon acidification of the FS using 6N sulphuric acid to reduce the pH of FS to 6.71. 
The highest average water flux (3.34 LMH) is achieved for swine manure as FS. The chemical 
and physical properties of the FS influence the average water flux achieved for each of the FS. 
The highest water flux achieved for swine manure is due to a combination of factors including 
low to moderately low total solids (TS), total suspended solids (TSS), total organic carbon (TOC) 
and dynamic viscosity of the FS. The lowest achieved water flux is in the case of cattle manure 
as FS. The water flux decline for all of the FS is majorly due to the dilution of DS. The water flux 
decline is also found to be due to the membrane fouling and concentration polarization (CP). 
The average reverse salt flux for all the FS is found to be lesser than 0.4 GMH for majority of 
FS except for cattle manure FS. The high reverse salt flux for cattle manure compared to the 
other tested FS is due to the presence of high amount of organic fibers (TOC) and total solids 
(TS). The methane yield is the highest for synthetic lipids FS (574 ml/gVS). An optimum FS for 
anaerobic digestion- forward osmosis (AD-FO) combined process is found to be synthetic lipids 
since the specific FS gives rise to a high average water flux (3.1 LMH) compared to some of the 
other tested FS. Phase 3 of the project involves modelling of water flux using MATLAB by de-
convoluting the effects of concentration polarization, structural parameter and mass transfer 
resistance. The results showed similarities with the experimental water flux from phase 2 for 
the majority of FS. Finally, the Aquaporin FO membranes could achieve an excellent nutrient 
rejection upon acidification. The total available ammonia nitrogen (TAN) rejections for 
majority of experiments were found to be higher than 90%. The orthophosphate rejections 
were higher than 99% for all the experiments. The membrane also showed the capacity to 
retain most of the nutrients in the FS. The TAN retention upon acidification is found to be 
higher than 80% for the majority of experiments. The phosphate retention varied significantly 
due to the capture of the ions in the fouling layer. The highest phosphate retention was found 
to be 83% in the case of cattle manure FS.

1. Motivation 
The ever increasing demand for clean drinking water and energy provides an impetus for this 
project. Safe drinking water is scarce, and the demand for clean and safe drinking water is 
skyrocketing day by day as the population of the world increases rapidly. The population of 
this world is expected to be growing  and estimated to be around 8.4 billion by mid-2030 and 
around 9.6 billion by mid-2050 [4]. According to ‘The United Nations World Water 
Development Report 2015’ [5] “Population growth, urbanization, migration, and 
industrialization, along with increases in production and consumption, have generated ever-
increasing demands for freshwater resources”. Water is the fundamental need for every living 
being and yet, the quest for finding a perennial source of fresh drinking water has not come 
to a standstill. 
For a sustainable world and a sustainable future, water and energy resources need to be 
efficiently managed in the context of ecosystem integrity and human well-being in a robust 
economy.  Water scarcity can be referred to an acute shortage of water regarding quantity or 
a lack of clean water in terms of quality [6]. Finding a reliable source, sustainable management 
and a robust cleaning and recycling is not impossible in the developing world, but it is 
considered to be time consuming and expensive [7][8]. Therefore, there is an imminent need 
to manage water in the most cost and energy-efficient way.  
It is a well-known fact that the water covers 71% of the Earth’s surface [9]. Only 2.5% is 
freshwater, and 98.8% of that freshwater is in the form of ice and ground water [10][11]. Most 
of the water are found to be brackish. To extract the pure water from brackish water, 
wastewater, activated sludge and anaerobically digested effluents, several technologies have 
been developed over the past few years. Some of the technologies include desalination, 
membrane distillation, reverse osmosis, membrane bio-reactors, membrane filtration and 
forward osmosis.   
Forward Osmosis (FO) technology has grown from being an academically proven technology 
to commercially viable technology over the past few years [12][13][14]. Companies such as 
Aquaporin A/S from Denmark have invested in developing the forward osmosis membranes 
for several applications [14][15][16]. The technology exploits the natural process of water 
uptake from the soil by plants and trees which involves low energy requirements, and this 
phenomenon is the foundation for the production of biomimetic FO membranes [16]. With 
the commercialisation of the forward osmosis membranes, water extraction from various 
sources and processes is expected to be cost and energy- efficient [17][18] [19]. 
Recent development in the membrane technology indicates that forward osmosis (FO) has a 
high potential for wastewater treatment, concentrating nutrients and producing high quality 
water. The effluents from anaerobic digesters include a nutrient-rich stream possessing 
nutrients such as Nitrogen, Phosphorus and Potassium. Forward osmosis is a proven 
technology to extract water from the anaerobically digested effluents and to up-concentrate 
the effluents [20]. By doing so, the nutrients can be retained in the feed of the FO setup. The 
diluted draw solution at the end of the experiment can be further used for water recovery

using reverse osmosis (or) membrane distillation (or) electro dialysis. Further, this enables the 
recovery of the salt used for preparing the draw solution which can be used for the

2. Project Objectives  
The main objective of this project is to evaluate the performance of forward osmosis (FO) fed 
with potato starch wastewater and various anaerobic effluents operated at thermophilic and 
mesophilic conditions in terms of water flux, reverse salt transport, and nutrients removal. 
Furthermore, the water flux in steady state conditions is decided to be modelled using 
deconvolution of ECP and ICP effects, and structural parameter of the FO membrane under 
specific conditions. The modelled water flux will be then compared to the experimental water 
flux.  
Other objectives of this project include:  
 Investigating different draw solutions for FO experiments  
 Characterizing the different anaerobic effluents  
 Calculating water flux and reverse salt flux for different anaerobic effluents  
 Finding correlation between effluent characteristics, water flux and reverse salt flux 
 Finding various parameters, process conditions and effluent characteristics that 
contribute to membrane fouling/scaling 
 Comparing and contrasting best effluents for FO-membrane based water extraction 
The project is divided into three phases: 
Phase 1: Performing standard FO experiments with calcein as the feed solution (FS) and 
different salt solutions as draw solution (DS) 
Phase 2: Performing FO experiments with different anaerobically digested effluents as the 
feed solution (FS) and the selected salt solution from phase 1 as draw solution (DS) 
Phase 3:  Modelling of water flux using MATLAB and comparing the results with the 
experimental water flux 
3. Background  
A well-known transport process is the process of osmosis. Osmosis is defined as the 
spontaneous net movement of solvent molecules through a semi-permeable membrane into 
a region of higher solute concentration, in the direction that tends to equalize the solute 
concentrations on the two sides of the semi-permeable membrane [21]. The semi-permeable 
membrane conventionally refers to selectively permeable membrane. Osmotic pressure is the 
driving force which helps in the transport of solvent molecules [22].  Another key for such 
process is the membrane. Among the first discovered membranes, biological structures paved 
the way for interesting research into this area. These led to the understanding of water 
transport across cell membranes [15].

3.1. An overview of types of Osmosis  
Three different osmotic processes namely reverse osmosis, pressure retarded osmosis, and 
forward osmosis work with the principle of net movement of water across a selectively 
permeable membrane [23][22].  
3.1.1. Reverse Osmosis (RO) 
Reverse osmosis (RO) uses hydraulic pressure to oppose and exceed the osmotic pressure of 
an aqueous feed solution. RO is used mainly in desalination applications to produce potable 
water from seawater. When it comes to the RO process, the opposing applied pressure is the 
driving force for mass transport across the membrane. The pressure applied need to be higher 
than the osmotic pressure. Reverse osmosis is one of the most widespread type of 
membrane-based desalination process [24]. When the brine solution is placed on the feed 
side of the membrane, RO process is capable of rejecting almost all colloidal or dissolved 
matter allowing only pure water to pass through the membrane and thus producing a 
concentrated brine and a permeate (pure water). A schematic representation of RO process 
can be found in Figure 1. The left side of the semi-permeable membrane where the pressure 
is applied represents the feed side and the right side represents the draw side or the 
permeate side.   
 
Figure 1: Reverse Osmosis. Source: [25] 
As mentioned earlier, the feed pressure needs to be fairly high for desalination or water 
purification. In seawater desalination, the feed applied pressure ranges between 55 to 68 bar 
[24] [26] [27]. An RO setup for desalination typically consists of [24]:  
1. Water abstraction 
2. Pre-treatment 
3. Pumping system 
4. Membrane separation unit 
5. Energy recovery system  
6. Post-treatment

3.1.2. Pressure Retarded Osmosis (PRO) 
In a pressure retarded osmosis (PRO), the osmotic pressure is converted into a hydrostatic 
pressure. In this process, the water diffuses into the more saline side which is under positive 
pressure, that is the osmotic pressure being more than the applied pressure [23]. When an 
increasing pressure is applied to the salt solution, the magnitude of the water flow will 
decrease until a point when the applied pressure and the osmotic pressure become equal. 
The phenomenon where there is a flow of water into a pressurized salt solution is called as 
PRO. The transition from reverse osmosis to pressure retarded osmosis to forward osmosis 
can be seen in the Figure 2. ∆P refers to the applied pressure and ∆π refers to the osmotic 
pressure. Since PRO is often used to convert osmotic pressure to hydrostatic pressure, this 
process can be used for generating electricity from seawater as well as fresh water [22] [23].  
 
 
Figure 2: Transition from RO to PRO to FO [29] 
The power that can be generated using PRO process per unit membrane area is equal to the 
product of the water flux and the hydrostatic pressure of the salt solution [30]. The 
disadvantage of PRO is the fact that when applied pressure is close to osmotic pressure, the 
water flux through the membrane becomes really low. Thus, to compensate the low flux, a 
very large membrane area would be required to produce power [30]. This in turn will result 
in high cost for this technology.  
3.1.3. Forward Osmosis (FO)  
As can be seen from Figure 2, ∆P for FO is almost zero, meaning that it is a naturally occurring 
process. In FO, the driving force is the chemical potential between the feed and the draw 
solutions [23]. Water diffuses to the more concentrated side of the membrane which is called 
the draw side of the membrane. This is the main advantage of using FO for water extraction

Other  advantages of FO include high rejection of a range of contaminants and lower 
membrane fouling propensity when compared to the pressure-driven membrane processes 
[23] [20]. A typical bench scale FO setup is shown in Figure 3. The left side is termed as the 
feed side, and the right side of the membrane is the draw side. The water flux is directly 
proportional to the concentration difference between the draw and the feed solutions [31]. 
The higher the difference, the better it is for the water flux to be high.

3.2. Types of FO membranes and different configurations  
The membranes to be used for FO needs to be dense, non-porous, and selectively permeable 
[22]. The quest to find appropriate membranes began with the experiments on bladders of 
pigs, cattle, and fish. Another research was carried out with collodion (nitrocellulose), rubber, 
porcelain and goldbeater’s skin [22] [32]. With the advent of 1960, the membrane technology 
leaped forward from laboratory scale to the industrial scale owing to the significant Loeb–
Sourirajan process. The asymmetric polyamide membranes are used in FO and PRO processes 
[33]. These membranes were touted as defect-free, high-flux and anisotropic membranes by 
nature [34][35]. Later, cellulose acetate hollow fiber membranes were used in FO process to 
desalinate sea water using  glucose as the draw solution [36][32].

higher when the support layer is thinner for FO process [45].

3.2.1. Plate-and-frame membrane module 
The first produced membranes were notably the flat-sheet membranes and the packing were 
done in a plate-and-frame module [46] [22]. Two end plates are taken and the membrane, 
feed, spacers and product spacers are layered or sandwiched together between two end 
plates [46]. In-between, there is a portion that passes through the membrane. This makes a 
way for the permeate collection. Plate-and-frame modules can be constructed in many 
different sizes, ranges, and shapes. They can be also manufactured in lab-scale as well as 
commercial scale. A schematic representation of typical plate-and-frame modules is shown 
in Figure 5. 
 
Figure 5: Early plate-and-frame design developed by Stern et al. [47] 
 
The plate-and-frame module suffers from some limitations. The module lacks sufficient 
membrane support and low packing density [22]. Lack of sufficient membrane support gives 
rise to low hydraulic pressure operating conditions. This, in turn, leads to high process control 
[22]. The packing density is crucial for a high membrane surface area. Further, it also leads to 
larger space requirements, making the capital and operating costs higher. It is often noticed 
during processes that there are leakages in the module owing to sealing issues [22][46]. 
Furthermore, monitoring the membrane performance and fouling characteristics are difficult 
with this module.

3.2.3. Hollow-fiber module (tubular module)  
The membranes were initially developed to be used as a flat sheet configuration. New 
technologies, however, cater to producing membranes in the form of thin tubes or fibers. This 
is called as a hollow-fiber module. This way of designing helps in producing very high 
membrane surface areas since the module is compact. The major drawback of this 
configuration is that the flux through the membrane is low compared to the other 
configurations [46]. The diameter of hollow fibers varies over a wide range, from 50 to 3000 
μm [46]. The fibers are preferably formed as a microporous structure so that the selective 
layer can be integrated either on the outside or inside surface. Many such fibers must then 
be packed into bundles and grooved into tubes to form a good membrane module. This 
further provides a good membrane surface area. This module is also called as a tubular 
module. Further, advantages of tubular membranes are that they are self-supported. Hence, 
they possess the ability to support high hydraulic pressure without getting deformed. The 
packaging density is relatively high compared to other configurations. Furthermore, tubular 
modules allow a free flow or in other words a good flow pattern that is required for FO [22]. 
Finally, according to [22] [50], hollow-fiber modules is touted as the most suitable for FO 
owing to the avoidance of a thick support. The different types of tubular membranes used for

3.3.3. Concentration Polarisation 
An important phenomenon which one needs to understand while dealing with FO membranes 
is the principle of concentration polarisation. This is really important in osmotically driven 
processes especially because of the membrane support layer [1]. Concentration polarisation 
(CP) leads to a reduction in the water flux across the membrane [58]. In most cases, the 
permeate water flux is found to be lesser than the expected values. It is verified that the 
effective osmotic pressure difference across the membrane is substantially lower than the 
osmotic pressure difference between the feed and the draw solutions [58]. In other words, 
the feed solution becomes more concentrated on one side of the membrane, and the draw 
solution becomes more diluted on the other side of the membrane leading to this 
phenomenon [1].  
The effect of concentration polarisation were studied and elucidated by numerous 
researchers and scholars in the past [22][68][15][58]. When water permeates through the 
membrane, the retained salts or other constituents such as contaminants or solute particles 
accumulate adjacent to the membrane surface giving rise to the effect of CP [58]. This is 
undesirable as it gives rise not only to a reduction in flux but also a requirement for additional 
applied pressure [22]. Further, CP can also promote scale formation or membrane fouling 
[58].  
The concentration polarisation can be external or internal depending on the type of the 
membrane. As discussed earlier, FO membranes possess a dense active layer and a porous 
==PAGE:26==

(also termed as ICP). This is because the accumulation of solute particles or the salt takes

could alleviate ICP. As discussed earlier Aquaporin membranes have thinner support layer 
compared to the other membranes.

3.4. Selection of draw solutions for FO processes 
An important precursor for selection of the draw solution is the high osmotic pressure of the 
draw solution. This sets the flow or transport of water molecules from the feed solution to 
the draw solution. The driving force being the osmotic pressure gradient, several other factors 
could also contribute to selecting the best draw solution for the relevant applications. Some 
of the characteristics a draw solution needs to possess are [51][1]:  
 The selected draw solution must require low energy for regeneration or re-
concentration and must be easily separated from the pure product water 
 The draw solute must be non-toxic 
 The selected draw solute must be chemically inert to the membrane 
 The solute particles in the draw solution should not impede with the performance of 
the membrane 
  It must have a high osmotic pressure at low concentrations  
  It must be highly soluble 
 It must be available at low cost  
 It must possess low viscosity  
 Low reverse solute diffusion (back diffusion of the draw solution to the feed solution)  
 Not adversely affected by contamination of ions from the feed solution  
 Minimal effect on internal concentration polarisation

3.5. Applications of FO in different scenarios: An overview 
The state of the art applications of FO includes desalination, wastewater treatment & 
reclamation, food processing, pharmaceutical products, power generation and some hybrid 
processes [58].   
3.5.1. Desalination 
Extracting fresh water from seawater has been a struggle since the advent of mankind. FO 
technology provides viable ways to extract freshwater from brackish water with the aid of 
organic draw solutions. FO is touted as a possible technology to replace RO technology [65]. 
The work done or the energy requirement in FO process to recover fresh water is much less 
compared to RO [58].  
 
Figure 15: Water extraction from seawater using Ammonia Carbon-dioxide FO process. (Source: [22][80]) 
3.5.2. Wastewater treatment and reclamation 
This application is very attractive both in the field of research and in the industry as it includes 
technologies that facilitate water reclamation in space [58][3]. FO has been reported to have 
the potential to sustainably treat wastewater sources and, in turn, produce high quality water 
[3]. However, often FO needs to be used in combination with RO to reclaim fresh water as 
well as to recycle the draw solution.  
3.5.3. Food processing  
In the food processing sector, up concentration of various fruit juices and energy drinks have 
been possible by FO technology [82][83]. An added advantage of using this technology is that 
the quality of the juices and extracts will be kept intact owing to the low temperature and 
pressure requirement for the operation of FO [58].  
3.5.4. Pharmaceuticals 
FO can also be used in pharmaceutical industries and drug production. FO allows 
concentration of the drug before being processed and produced. Further, it acts as an osmotic 
==PAGE:33==
Forward osmosis membrane based water extraction from anaerobic effluents  
 
33 
 
for controlled drug release which is beneficial considering the costs for conventional 
controlled drug release mechanisms [58].   
3.5.5. Power generation 
Another leap forward in closing the energy gap or to optimise the energy nexus is the use of 
FO technology in power generation areas. It should be noted that FO and PRO hybrid provides 
the best results for producing energy [30]. PRO is essential in recovering energy by utilising 
the variances in chemical potential. A layout of a simple power plant based on the hybrid of 
FO and PRO can be found in Figure 16.  
 
Figure 16: Schematic layout of a simple PRO power plant. (Source: [22]) 
 
3.5.6. FO-anMBR 
Water extraction from anaerobic effluents (digestates) from anaerobic membrane bio 
reactors is a state of the art technology which is in the developing stages. Forward osmosis 
can be utilised to concentrate the centrate (digestate) via batch and continuous operating 
conditions [20]. Further, it was reported by Hollow et al., that the combination of FO/RO 
technologies can deliver a sustained flux over a period of time under continuous operation 
[20]. A schematic diagram of the two step process to extract pure water from the centrate 
(used as feed) can be found in Figure 17. The centrate is concentrated using FO process where 
the water molecules diffuse into the draw solution utilized in FO. The RO unit used in the 
second stage enables to recover the pure water from the draw solution.

While FO process is a promising technology which allows up concentrating various feed 
solutions, in extracting pure water, it is often considered as a pre-treatment step or a first 
step in producing pure water [20]. Bench scale experiments have been conducted by 
Holloway et al., with constant feed solution concentrations and the concentration of draw 
solution being approximately 50 g/l NaCl [20]. The flow rates for both the solutions were in 
the order of 1.5 l/min [20]. The pH of the feed was constantly maintained at 7 to 8.   
When anaerobic digestion (AD), forward osmosis (FO) and membrane distillation are 
combined, the net result is the production of pure water and energy (biogas/methane). Thus, 
this is a promising closed loop solution. Further, the concentrated digestate after the FO 
process can be used as a fertilizer [3]. However, the challenges in developing this process 
include membrane scaling and fouling, continuous stabilisation of pH, temperature and 
continuous re-concentration of the draw solution (DS).  
3.6. Membrane fouling  
Membrane fouling occurs when certain unwanted materials and fibers accumulate on the 
active layer of the membrane [84] and fouling causes flux decline for FO process [85]. 
Although membrane fouling occurs at a slower place and in a less severe way for FO [85][86]as 
compared to the PRO or RO processes, fouling due to the presence of organic particles and 
fibers in the feed solution (FS) is inevitable. Various factors such as calcium binding, 
membrane orientation and the composition of the FS can contribute to the fouling in FO 
membranes. The fouling in the case of FO process for anaerobically digested effluents can be 
due to bio-film and organic fouling [82]. The organic fouling gives rise to a cake layer 
formation. The Fourier Transform Infrared (FTIR) and ATP bioluminescence assay (ATP) can 
be performed to quantify the organic fouling and the bio-film in the FO membrane [84][87]. 
When FO process is operated in a continuous mode instead of batch mode, using anti-foulants 
and cleaning solutions to clean the membrane periodically is advisable. This causes flux 
recovery and increases the usability and longevity of the FO membranes [84].

4. Materials and methods 
In this thesis, a series of theoretical modelling and experimental investigations were 
conducted with the aid of theoretical and experimental tools. As outlined in Section 2, the 
objectives of research were separated into two research phase. The first phase was focused 
mainly on quality control experiments on the FO process by means of performance 
characterization.  Some experiments were also conducted for comparison and validation of

With the aid of FO models developed in Phase I, various draw solutions were used in the

modelling of the water flux. 
4.1 Lab-scale FO experiments – Phase 1 
The FO experiments were divided into two categories namely Quality control (standard) 
experiments and the experiments with anaerobic effluents.  The quality check or the quality 
control experiments were also used to decide the draw solution for the experiments with 
anaerobic effluents. Further, the experiments served the purpose of modelling the optimal 
water flux across the membrane and to set the criteria or the standard for the membrane 
characterisation in terms of water flux, reverse salt flux and solute rejection by the

4.1.1. Quality control experiments

the fluorescence measurements. Additionally, this method can be used for different flow 
cells, feed and draw solutions. The standard experimental setup was constructed as shown in

the membrane from the draw solution to the feed solution.  The weight of the feed is 
measured for every five minutes. this was measured to calculate the water flux across the 
membrane from feed solution to the draw solution. Initial osmolarity of the bulk and the draw 
solution was measured using the Osmometer (Gonotec Osmomat 030 Cryoscopic). This was

4.2 Lab-scale FO experiments with anaerobic effluents

4.2.1. Experimental conditions 
The sample wastewater/effluent was defrosted well before starting the experiment so that 
the temperature of the feed and draw are the same. Since the variations in temperature 
affects the water flux, it was decided to start all the experiments with the same temperature 
[61][93]. However, it was not possible to construct a temperature controller for the apparatus 
in the limited time frame for the project. The pH of the effluent was checked and was made 
sure it was around 6.71. The effluents were acidified with 6N Sulphuric acid. It was proven 
that the change in pH has effects on the retention of dry matter (DM), total ammonia-nitrogen

free ammonia (NH3) as feed pH was lowered as presented in Figure 19. Further, it was proven

4.2.3. Operating procedure 
The membrane was cut exactly to fit the exact module size (0.0136 m2) and stored in MQ 
water exactly for 45 minutes before starting the experiment. The draw solution was decided 
to be 0.66 M MgCl2 based on the results from the standard tests from phase 1. 0.66 M MgCl2 
was chosen since the draw solution contributed to a lower reverse salt flux compared to the 
other tested draw solutions. The concentration of the MgCl2 was chosen to be 0.66 M to have 
similar osmotic pressure difference between the bulk draw and feed solutions compared to 
the other tested draw solutions such as 1 M NaCl and 1 M CH3COONa [70]. The draw solution 
(DS) was made only with MQ water for achieving better reproducibility of results. The feed 
solution (FS) was sieved thrice in a step-wise way using 1 mm, 250 µm and 125 µm sieves 
respectively. Sieving was done to avoid large particles and fibers to impede with the 
performance of the membrane by forming a cake layer within few hours of the experiment. 
Large fibers and particles in the FS could also clog the channel thus causing fault in the 
operation of the FO.

The draw solution was mixed well using a magnetic stirrer and left aside for some time. Care 
was taken to check the temperature since some of the dissolution reactions are exothermic. 
The feed and the draw solutions were always kept in room temperature. The pumps were 
checked if they were in working condition and since they are gearing pumps, the pumps 
operate only in one direction. The tubes were connected to the module as shown in Figure 
21. It was made sure that the flow in the feed side and the draw side of the module 
contributes to a counter-current flow condition. A spacer is placed in the module and made 
sure that the O-rings or the rubber rings were intact. The membrane is taken with a tweezer 
without touching the active layer and placed in the middle of the module. The module is 
closed by screwing first the cross directions and then the vertical and horizontal directions to

5. Results and Discussions 
The results and discussion section encompasses the following: 
 Results from phase 1 experiments in terms of the water flux, reverse salt flux and 
solute rejections for three different DS namely NaCl, MgCl2 and CH3COONa. The FS 
used for the experiments is 5 µM Calcein. These results set the standards for the phase 
2 experiments. 
 The DS was selected for the phase 2 experiments based on the results from phase 1 
experiments. The performance of the membrane in terms of average water flux is 
evaluated for different FS such as potato starch wastewater, synthetic sugars, 
synthetic proteins, synthetic lipids, synthetic mixture, cattle manure (thermophilic), 
cattle manure (mesophilic) and swine manure.  
 Factors contributing to variations in the observed water flux for different FS are 
discussed based on the chemical and physical properties of the FS. Further, a short 
description of membrane fouling is specified. 
 The reverse salt flux for the process is calculated based on the mass balance approach 
and the results are discussed. Furthermore, the results are compared with the quality 
standard results from phase 1 experiments.  
 The methane yield for different FS is compared with the water flux for each of the FS 
and the results are discussed. 
 The modelled water flux is compared with the experimental water flux 
 Finally, the rejection of nutrients namely TAN, TKN and Phosphate are presented and 
the results are discussed in detail.  
All the model calculations pertaining to the results and discussions for one of the FS can be 
found in appendix I.  
5.1 Discussions on water flux, Jw - Phase 1 
The results for the standard FO experiments for different draw solutions such as NaCl, MgCl2 
and CH3COONa can be seen from Table 6 and the comparison of the average water flux Jw 
(LMH), Js (GMH) and the ratio (Js/Jw) for the above mentioned draw solutions can be seen in 
the Figure 35. As it can be seen from the Figure 35 and the Table 6, the maximum average 
water flux that can be achieved with the Aquaporin FO membrane with 5 µM Calcein as feed, 
is when NaCl is used as draw solution (6.59 LMH and 6.2 LMH). However, the results also show 
that the maximum reverse salt flux is also the highest for NaCl (1.91 GMH and 1.18 GMH). It 
should be noted that a significant flux decline cannot be seen in the results because the 
duration of the standard experiments was only 215 minutes per experiment. The difference 
in the values of the average Jw and Js for the same experimental conditions might be due to 
the heterogeneity in the support layer parameters of the membrane [69]. The lowest reverse 
flux Js is achieved when MgCl2 is used as draw solution (0.67 and 0.75 LMH). However, the 
average water flux Jw when MgCl2 being used as draw solution, is also the lowest compared 
to the other draw solutions used. Whereas when sodium acetate is used as draw solution the 
results are in between the results for NaCl and MgCl2. Therefore, MgCl2 was chosen as a draw 
==PAGE:53==

solution to be used in the FO experiments for Phase 2 since MgCl2 showed lower reverse salt 
flux when anaerobically digested effluents were used as feed solution (section 5.2.5). Further, 
MgCl2 possesses the highest osmotic pressure compared to the other two draw solutions. The 
osmotic pressure for 0.66 M MgCl2 was measured to be around 49.16 +/- 1 bar whereas the 
osmotic pressure of 1 M NaCl and 1 M sodium acetate were measured to be around 41.87 +/- 
1 bar and 41.51 +/- 1 bar respectively. As already mentioned in the (section 3.1.3) osmotic 
pressure difference is the driving force for FO process and thus the draw solution (MgCl2) with 
the highest osmotic pressure at even lower concentrations were used in Phase 2 experiments.  
The hydrated radius of Mg2+ and Na+ are 0.428 nm and 0.358 nm respectively [111]. This 
proves why the reverse salt flux in the case of MgCl2 as DS is the lowest. The pore diameter of 
the aquaporin FO membrane when assumed to be similar to the pore diameter of a 
conventional FO membrane [112]  is around 0.25 to 0.3 nm. This shows why the FO 
membranes have the potential to reject the larger size molecules and ions such as Mg2+ and 
Na+.

5.2.1. Effect of operating conditions- Potato starch wastewater study case 
Figure 41 shows the water flux when Potato starch wastewater were used as FS A, B and C. 
The A, B, C of Potato Starch wastewater is explained in Table 8. The experiments for Potato 
SWW A, B and C as FS were conducted under different conditions as described in Table 4. In 
the case of Potato SWW A, as the water from the FS diffused to the draw side, the level in the 
feed reservoir reduced and since the bottle was closed and air tight, it created a suction effect. 
Even though a gas bag was connected to the feed reservoir, a vacuum was created in the 
bottle. This means that no significant amount of ammonia gas was produced. Furthermore, a 
small container bottle of 6N suIphuric acid was placed in the feed side of the bench scale 
apparatus, to capture ammonia gas. From the analysis of the sulphuric acid by the 
spectrophotometry method as described in section 4.3.3, it was concluded that there is no 
capture of ammonia. This further proves that there was no significant evolution of ammonia 
gas.  
The suction effect could also be caused by the temperature difference during day and night, 
since the experiments were conducted for 24 hours. This resulted in expansion and 
compression of air in the system [113]. The gas bag after 24 hours was found to be completely 
void. The average water flux of Potato SWW A (2.37 LMH) when compared with B and C (2.94 
LMH and 2.65 LMH) are significantly lower as a result of this phenomenon (as shown in Table 
10). Moreover, since this effect leads to a higher pressure on the feed and thus producing a 
positive trans-membrane pressure, the process is more likely to behave as PRO (as explained 
in section 3.1.2).  The Potato Starch Wastewater A1 FS was operated at co-current condition 
==PAGE:60==
Forward osmosis membrane based water extraction from anaerobic effluents  
 
60 
 
to compare the performance versus a counter current operation A2. The results from Table 
10 shows that the average water flux is higher for Potato SWW A2 than for Potato SWW A1, 
namely 2.06 LMH and 2.37 LMH, respectively. This can be explained by enhanced water 
diffusion from the FS to DS [89] when the system is operated in a counter-current mode.

is represented in the Table 14. As it can be seen from the Table 14, there is significant drop in 
osmotic pressure at the end of experiments for all the FS. The volume of water transported 
for the majority of experiments in phase 1 was approximately 1 L which leads to dilution of 
DS as water diffuses from FS to DS. This can be seen from the Table 9 of section 5.2. This

experiments.

of the experiment. An average of the modelled water flux at the steady state at the beginning 
and at the end of the experiments is represented in the last column of the Table 18.

Figure 50. It can be clearly seen that the modelled water flux and the experimental water flux

5.4. Discussions on TAN, TKN, Phosphorus and Phosphate rejections 
Nutrient rejections including Total available Ammonia-N (TAN), Total Kjeldahl- N (TKN), 
Phosphate and Phosphorus rejections were measured for each of the experiments in phase 
2. The rejection of TAN is calculated based on the initial concentrations in the FS and the final 
concentrations in the DS (Table 20). Results from the Table 20 shows that the TAN rejections 
were relatively similar for majority of the experiments. As compared to the conditions with 
acidification and without acidification, the TAN rejections are higher when the FS were 
acidified before the experiments. As equilibrium is shifted towards ammonium upon 
acidification as explained in Figure 19 of section 4.2.1, the rejection is enhanced in comparison 
with free ammonia. This can be evidently seen when the rejections of Potato SWW B are 
compared with Potato SWW C (Table 20).  
The rejections of TAN for the anaerobically digested FS is in agreement with the results for 
cellulose triacetate membrane as reported by Holloway et al., [20]. However, the rejections 
obtained in this study were found to be higher than the rejections achieved by S. Onoda et 
al., [117] which were equal only 60 %. It can be mentioned that for a semi-permeable 
membrane such as the Aquaporin FO membrane, the charge of the membrane is negative 
[14][118]. The Ammonia-N is positively charged and has a hydrated radius of 0.11 nm 
[111][119]. Even though the charge of the ion is positive, high rejections of TAN is achieved 
due to the radius of the ion. These results are also coherent with the results published by 
Oron et al., [120].  
The rejection of orthophosphate ions (PO43-) was calculated in the same way as it was done 
for TAN rejections and the results are tabulated in Table 15. The results show that the 
rejection is more than 99 % for all the FS. The results are in accordance with the published 
results of Holloway et al., [20] and S. Onoda et al., [117]. The orthophosphate ions are 
negatively charged and thus, the rejections are higher than the rejection of TAN. The pore 
size of the membrane is assumed to be around 0.25 to 0.3 nm as discussed in section 5.1. 
Furthermore, the hydrated radius of the ion is 0.49 nm [119] which validates the higher 
rejection for the phosphate.

5.4.1. Retention of nutrients 
One of the objectives of the project is to up concentrate the FS through FO. This means that 
the majority of nutrients are retained in the FS at the end of each experiment. The retention 
of the nutrients is calculated as the amount of nutrients retained in the FS at the end of each 
experiment. The retention of TAN and TKN are represented in Table 22 (found on page 
number 80). The retention of TAN in the FS was found to be more than 80 % for majority of 
FS except synthetic protein 2 FS. It should also be noted that more than 90 % of TKN is in the 
form of Ammonia-N as also observed by Holloway et al [20]. This can be seen from the Table 
22, where the contents of TAN and TKN in (g) are represented. It is also clear that the 
acidification improves the retention of TAN in the FS. This can be verified as the TAN retention 
in Potato SWW C is approximately 43%, whereas the TAN retention in Potato SWW B is 
approximately 86 % which is double the retention.   
In terms of Phosphate retention, the amount of orthophosphate in (g) for each of the FS at 
the beginning at the end of the experiments is presented in Table 15. It can be noticed that 
the retention of the phosphate improved upon acidification. This can be clearly seen when 
the phosphate retention for Potato SWW B and Potato SWW C are compared (approximately 
45% versus approximately 9%). In general, the retention of the phosphate ions is lower 
compared to the TAN. This can be due to the binding of phosphate ions to the cake layer 
formed on the Feed side of the membrane (membrane fouling). This was confirmed through 
the unpublished results of Carina Schneider et al., 2016 (verbal communication). The nutrient

Conclusion 
It was demonstrated from the experiments of phase 2 that the FO process has the potential 
for extracting water from the anaerobically digested effluents and capable of retaining the 
nutrients in the feed solution. The optimal DS evaluated from phase 1 experiments for the FO 
process to extract water for this specific project is found to be MgCl2 due to the minimal 
reverse salt flux and contribution to high osmotic pressure difference.  
The average water flux is improved upon acidification of the FS using 6N sulphuric acid to 
reduce the pH to 6.71. The highest average water flux (3.34 LMH) is achieved for swine 
manure as FS. The water flux decline for the FS is majorly due to the dilution of DS. The water 
flux decline is also found to be due to the membrane fouling and concentration polarization. 
High amounts of TS and TOC in the cattle manure FS leads to reduction in the average water 
flux achieved. The lowest achieved water flux is in the case of cattle manure as FS. The 
difference in average water flux achieved for synthetic proteins 1 and 2 (1.89 LMH and 2.82 
LMH) suggests that properties such as TS, TSS and TOC and presence of high amounts of 
organic fibers causes reduction in the water flux. Furthermore, the dynamic viscosity is found 
to be the highest for cattle manure (approximately 2 mPa.s) causing the reduction in water 
==PAGE:81==
Forward osmosis membrane based water extraction from anaerobic effluents  
 
81 
 
flux. The highest water flux achieved for swine manure is due to a combination of factors 
including low to moderately low TS, TSS, TOC and dynamic viscosity of the FS.  
Although the fouling on the membrane is found to be uniform in the case of all the FS, the 
degree and extent of fouling varied with different FS and this is due to the variation of physical 
and chemical properties for different FS. The average reverse salt flux for all the FS is found 
to be less than 0.4 GMH for majority of FS except for cattle manure FS. The high reverse salt 
flux (approximately 0.9 GMH) for cattle manure compared to the other tested FS is due to the 
presence of high amount of organic fibers (TOC) and total solids (TS) which causes membrane 
fouling and scaling and allows the solute from DS to pass through in small amounts towards 
the end phase of the experiment. The reverse salt flux is found to be negative in the case of 
synthetic lipids and sugars and close to 0 GMH in the case of synthetic proteins. This is due to 
the Mg deposition on the membrane in the membrane fouling layer. 
The methane yield is the highest for synthetic lipids FS (574 ml/gVS). An optimum FS for AD-
FO combined process is found to be synthetic lipids since the specific FS gives rise to a high 
average water flux (3.1 LMH) compared to the other tested FS. The second best FS for AD-FO 
combined process is found to be swine manure that gives rise to (392 ml/gVS methane yield 
and 3.34 LMH average water flux respectively).  
The modelled water flux performed by de-convoluting the effects of concentration 
polarization, structural parameter and mass transfer resistance in MATLAB, were similar to 
the experimental water flux obtained for majority of experiments except for one type of 
synthetic proteins FS (synthetic proteins 1). This validates the results obtained from the phase 
1 experiments.  
The phase 2 experiments showed that the aquaporin FO membranes could achieve an 
excellent nutrient rejection upon acidification. The total available ammonia nitrogen (TAN) 
rejections for majority of experiments were found to be higher than 90%. The 
orthophosphate rejections were higher than 99% for all the experiments. The membrane also 
showed the capacity to retain most of the nutrients in the FS. The TAN retention upon 
acidification is found to be higher than 80% for the majority of experiments. The phosphate 
retention varied significantly due to the capture of the ions in the fouling layer. The highest 
phosphate retention was found to be 83% in the case of cattle manure FS.

Future work and perspectives 
The research work can be extended in the future by combining the FO process with anaerobic 
digestion and reverse osmosis/electro dialysis/membrane distillation. This approach serves 
as a closed loop solution where energy production, water extraction and draw solution re-
concentration becomes possible. Furthermore, the concentrated feed solution can be used 
as a fertilizer as the nutrients are retained in the feed solution.  
Future research can be done by collecting the diluted draw solution from the phase 2 
experiments and removing the salt (MgCl2) from the DS using three different techniques 
namely, reverse osmosis, electro dialysis and membrane distillation. Comparative study can 
be made in terms of the economic viability and quality of the produced water (to check if the 
environmental constraints as per the Danish Ministry of Environment are met). The water 
extracted can be measured for the E. coli present, the nutrients present and the physical 
properties.  
The next step could be investigating the extent of fouling and to select the appropriate 
cleaning agents for membrane cleaning. Furthermore, the feed solution can be pre-treated 
with suitable reagents that facilitate fast sedimentation and the supernatant can be used for 
FO process. This increases the water flux and efficiency of the process.  
It is suggested to control the pH, and temperature during the process to provide constant 
experimental conditions during the FO process. Future research can also include translating 
the bench-scale experiment to a pilot scale combining with anaerobic digestion and reverse 
osmosis. The results from this project can be used as a guide for the pre-treatment of the 
effluents such as sieving and acidification. Furthermore, methods to apply this combined 
process in a continuous operating mode can be investigated in the future.  
The steady state model to evaluate the water flux can be extended to dynamic modelling to 
predict the water flux at different dilutions and osmotic pressure differences. A real time 
monitoring and controlling of various parameters in the pilot scale for a better and optimised 
operation of the process can be investigated. The flat sheet membrane can be replaced with 
hollow fibre membrane and the results can be studied.  
Finally, the idea from this project can be extended for the water extraction from effluents 
from beer breweries, diary wastes, bio-gas plants, cheese factories and food processing 
sectors.

Appendix E - Operating procedure for Quality control FO experiments 
in phase 1 
 
Operating Procedure for standard FO experiments: 
The membrane was prepared by:  
 
i. Cutting the membrane to fit the flow cell:  the membrane was placed on a 
suitable underlay with the support side facing the bottom.  
ii. Before the membrane can be used in a FO setup, it was important to ensure that 
the membrane is fully water saturated. Thus the membrane was soaked in MilliQ 
water for 45 minutes. 
 
The Test solutions were prepared as follows:  
iii. Draw: 1M NaCl or 0.66M MgCl2 or 1M CH3COONa is prepared by volume (first by 
taking the appropriate amount of salt and then filling up with water until the 
desired volume is reached. For each experiment, 1L for the draw reservoir was 
needed, and some extra volume to fill the tubes) 
                              Draw solutions were stored in room temperature in a closed reservoir.                                           
iv. Feed: 5 µM calcein solution: 5µM working solution was prepared with MilliQ 
water. Storage: Calcein stock and prepared feed solutions were stored in a closed 
reservoir at room temperature, away from light.  
 
Preparation of FO test set-up: 
 
v. Draw reservoir was filled with 1000g of a 1M NaCl or 0.66M MgCl2 or 1M 
CH3COONa solution and the weights are noted down on the report sheet. The 
draw solution has to be weighed in first, so the feed bottle can be tared and the 
scale shows the actual weight of the feed volume during the experiment. 
vi. The feed reservoir was filled with 1000g of a 5 µM calcein solution and the 
weight was noted down on the report sheet. 
vii. The tubing inlets and outlets were filled in the appropriate reservoirs, and pump 
heads, a conductivity probe was installed in the feed line directly after the 
membrane outlet, and the entire system was filled (feed and draw) by pumping 
at high speed. 
viii. At this point, the test cell was not included in the setup, instead the tubes are 
connected by a short tubing connector, since the membrane might get damaged 
by the high pumping speed. 
ix. Once all the tubes were filled, the pump speed was set back to 50.03 mL/min 
(tube inner diameter 4.8 mm).

x. The readily prepared membrane with active layer facing up was inserted into the 
CF042 test cell. Tweezers were used to take up the membrane from the petridish 
and the active area was not touched (area inside inner O-ring). The membrane 
was inserted with the edges aligned with the outer O-ring (as shown in Figure 
57).
