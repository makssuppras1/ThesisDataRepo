Development of a mechanical model for the Wavepiston device in waves and currents
by Auriane BUFFET
An updated version of the mechanical model of the Wavepiston device is proposed. It is now
modelled in working conditions, that is to say with plates moving in the waves and produc-
ing power. In order to do so, results from the Frequency Energy Tool (FET) are used. The
hydrostatic models for the buoys and the plate modules have also been changed. Calculations
problems in extreme sea state when the mooring chains and cables were either lowered down
onto the sea bed or on the contrary completely lifted have been dealt with, and the model can
now handle more critical regular sea states. The model can also be run in irregular waves.

1.1
Wave energy
Waves are a renewable energy source that is abundant, especially in Denmark, and yet little
exploited. Indeed, gross annual wave energy potential in the Danish part of the North Sea, has
been estimated to be approximately 30 TWh [9]. This can be compared to the production of pri-
mary energy of Denmark in 2018 that was of 161 TWh, with a share of almost 30% of renewable
energies [6].
(A) Attenuator
(B) Oscillating water column
(C) Point absorber
FIGURE 1.1: Different wave energy technologies from [3]
Wave energy has more advantages than only being plentiful, it is sustainable and very acces-

1.3
Motivation and objectives of the thesis
The central idea of this project is to update and improve the robustness of the model developed
in 2018 and described in [7] and [18].
The previous model is only taking into account the wave excitation force for the horizontal equi-
librium position calculation: the diffraction force and the viscous drag, which corresponds to
the case of static plates locked into position, and therefore not producing power. In this project,
the device is modelled in working conditions, with all the plates responding to the waves and
producing power, which is done including the other forces (inviscid and viscous hydrodynamic
resistance and PTO reaction force).
In the previous model [7], the calculations broke down in extreme sea states. In particular when
the chain linking the system to the anchor became too close to the sea bed or on the contrary
was completely lifted. This issue will be detailed further in the report and solutions will be
presented.
Irregular waves cases will also be considered, in order to study the system in more realistic
operational conditions.
The main contributions of this work are:
тАв The motion of the plates has been included in the model, that now represents the system
in working conditions;
тАв Improvement of the calculation of the buoyancy forces;
тАв Improvement of the robustness of the model in extreme sea states;
тАв Addition of a ямБxed anchor;
тАв Irregular waves can be handled by the model.
The wire connections between the plates of the device have been replaced with rigid beam
sections connected to each other with joints. These modiямБcations will also be discussed.
1.4
Approach
The approach used is a quasi-static approach. It is considered that the time scale of the deviceтАЩs
structural response to the waves forcing is much smaller than the time scale of the waves. At ev-
ery time step, the conямБguration of the system is found assuming that the system has reached an
equilibrium and is static. This is done by looping over the different nodes, solving an elastic or
inelastic catenary system between them. The loop continues until an equilibrium conямБguration
is found.

Assumptions
As explained in [15], [16] and [18], linear potential-ямВow theory in the frequency domain is used
to evaluate the wave loads on the plates. It means that the ямВuid is considered as incompressible,
irrotational, inviscid, that the time-varying quantities are oscillating sinusoidally and that lin-

around the edges of the plates are modelled via a constant quadratic damping coefямБcient. Other
assumptions regarding the structure of the system have also been made:
тАв Chain and cable elements have no bending stiffness;
тАв Chain elements are in-extensible;
тАв The seabed does not exert any horizontal force on the unlifted length of chain.

6 Discussion
The model presented in this report aimed at improving the robustness of the previous version
of the Visualisation tool. However, it still contains some shortcomings that must be pointed out.
Below is given a non-exhaustive list of these limitations.
As mentioned previously, the device is equipped with a storm protection feature. In extreme
sea states, the plates can be folded down thanks to a ямВip mechanism, in order to protect the
integrity of the structure. However, this is not included in the model. Therefore, the results
obtained for extreme sea states in this study might be over-evaluated.
When calculating the viscous drag, the value used for the drag coefямБcient in the FET is ямБxed to
CD = 10. Considering a ямБxed value for the drag coefямБcient, regardless of the sea state is reduc-
tive. Moreover, the value of CD might have to be re evaluated. (It was taken at CD = 1.18 in [7]).
In the model, the PTO and plate frequency responses are extracted from the FET output ямБles.
Then, the time series are reconstructed and used. As explained in Section 3.1, the PTO system
has been modelled in order to comply with the linearity assumption, but in reality some non-
linear effects exist. Those effects are therefore not taken into account in this model. The Time
Energy Tool allows to consider those non-linear effects and to produce time series of the results
generated by the FET. For now, those results are only available for a single plate in regular
waves, but a multiple plate version will be available for both regular and irregular waves in the
future [4].

7 Conclusion
7.1
Conclusion
This report has focused on presenting the improvements and modiямБcations made in the Visual-
isation tool.
After having presented the device itself and its characteristics, the general theory for the cables
geometry calculation, and for the various forces acting on the system has been derived. The
differences between the elastic and inelastic cable solutions, and the single and double ended
ones have been highlighted. Moreover, the new hydrostatic model has been introduced and a
start on the rigid beam model has been made. Then, the tool and the way the theory was imple-
mented in the code have been explained more in details, especially the modiямБcations made in
the single ended model. The main results obtained have been presented, both for regular and
irregular waves, and they have been discussed.
The updates and improvements made to the model include the modiямБcation of the buoyancy
calculation for the buoys and the plate modules. The buoys are now connected to the system
from its bottom instead of its centre. The plate of the EC is now taken into account in the hy-
drostatic model, allowing to have the plate equilibrium position more submerged than before,
which is more realistic.
Moreover, the plates are now considered into working conditions, whereas before it was not
moving relative to the rest of the module. Therefore, the calculations of the external forces
acting of the plate have been changed. Previously, only the diffraction and the viscous drag
forces were considered. Now, all the external forces described in Section 3.1 are accounted for
through the PTO reaction. This is done using the results from the Frequency Energy Tool, an-
other Wavepiston tool described in the FET documentation [14].

of the project. As mentioned before, the sea states with high wave height or wave period were
leading the calculations to crash, they are coloured in red. After implementing the changes de-
scribed in this report, the sea states presented in Table 7.2 are handled correctly by the tool (in
medium green).
