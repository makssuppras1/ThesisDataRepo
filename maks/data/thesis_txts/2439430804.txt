Contribution of intergenic mutations
to bacterial pathogenicity
Mikkel Anbo
Master’s Thesis, August 23, 2018
Supervisor: Lars Jelsbak
==PAGE:2==
Abstract
In this thesis, a bioinformatics approach coupled to functional genomics is applied to study the role
of a putative host-adaptive intergenic mutations in Salmonella Enteritidis. It is found that a SNP in
the folA promoter of S. Enteritidis leads to a 2.3-3 fold increase in gene expression, depending on the
growth media. The role of the folA promoter mutation is discussed, which likely relates to trimethorpim
resistance. The role of another putative host-adaptive intergenic mutation in P. aeruginosa, which
lies upstream of two genes, lptD and PA0596, which are related to cell wall and membrane, is also
studied using functional genomics. The intergenic mutation in P. aeruginosa is found to cause a 1.05
fold decrease in gene expression of the lptD promoter when grown in minimal media. The PA0596
promoter gene expression was very low during most of the growth conditions it was subjected to,
which prompted a search for compounds that could induce its expression. Two compounds, L-lactic
acid and cefotaxime, were found to induce the expression of the PA0596 promoter. It was also studied
if the intergenic mutation had any phenotypic impact, but none of the experiments indicated this.
While this is not the ﬁrst functional study of intergenic mutations, it is hoped that this thesis provides
additional evidence to suggest that nucleotide variations in intergenic regions are important for the
study of pathogenic bacteria and how they adapt to a host.

Introduction
Whole-genome sequencing of bacteria is a powerful tool which can be used to identify diversity in
microbial populations on a sequence level, in addition to this, it also provides unprecedented power to
unravel evolutionary processes [56]. Bacterial pathogens are known to evolve during colonization and
infection of humans [9], which can have a profound eﬀect on disease severity and antibiotic susceptibility
[25]. Developing a robust understanding of the molecular changes that result from host adaptation of
bacterial pathogens during infection is important for clinical treatment of infections [33].
Variations within protein-coding has for a long time been the genetic basis of evolutionary change,
and the vast majority of available information on genetic variation is from protein-coding regions [44].
While 85-90% of the bacterial genome consists of protein-coding genes, information about proteins
and the eﬀect of their activity is often insuﬃcient for explaining the relationship between genetic
variation and phenotype [44]. The remaining 10-15% of the bacterial genome consists of intergenic
regions, which comprise, in whole, or partially of both non-coding, and non-functional regions, such
as pseudogenes in varying states of decay [37], as well as functional elements like small RNA (sRNA)
genes and other regulatory control elements [7]. Many sRNAs are inﬂuential in the bacterial response
to many environmental stresses, like ﬂuctuations in temperature and pH as well as a plethora of other
stress responses [19]. The regulatory capacity of RNA molecules cover every stage of gene expression
as well as protein activity via highly diverse mechanisms [21].
Despite the fact that intergenic regions contain many important regulatory elements they are often, by
default, excluded from many databases and pipelines used for phylogenetic, epidemiological, or evolu-
tionary analyses [56]. Although, unlike protein coding genes, which show easily discernible patterns
of conservation shown by triplet-code structured sequences [44] as well as clear homology to proteins
in other bacterial genomes [7], untranslated sequences are more challenging to work with. One ex-
planation for this is is that there is no "oﬀ-the-shelf" methodology available to measure selection on
intergenic regions, since the standard approach for protein-coding sequence which looks at the ratio
between non-synonymous and synonymous mutations is invalid [56]. Poor annotation, low levels of
constraint, and a high frequency of indels are other factors that make intergenic regions technically
challenging to work with [56].
The goal of this thesis is to challenge the view that variations in protein-coding genes is suﬃcient
for elucidating the relationship between genetic variation and phenotype, and to provide additional
evidence that variations of intergenic regions are essential for the study of host adaptation. This is
done using a bioinformatics approach, to identify regulatory elements, followed by functional genomics,
to elucidate the phenotypic consequence(s) of intergenic mutations.
1.1
Project aim
The goal of this project is to provide additional evidence that polymorphisms of intergenic regions are
an integral part of host adaptation by studying the role of speciﬁc putative host-adaptive intergenic
mutations.
Datasets of intergenic mutations in pathogenic Salmonella Enteritidis and Pseudomonas aeruginosa
provide the foundation for this project. Intergenic mutations that do not hit predicted regulatory
elements are then omitted from these datasets, which leaves a set of intergenic mutations that can be
experimentally studied. At least one mutation from this dataset will be studied experimentally.
A four nucleotide insertion in an intergenic region in Pseudomonas aeruginosa will also be analysed
using strains that were previously constructed by the IMG group at DTU Bioengineering.
Finally, this project aims to elucidate the intergenic mutations’ impact on adaptation toward a
pathogenic lifestyle based on experiments as well as review of literature.

2
Theory
2.1
Intergenic regions
Prokaryotic genomes consist mainly of protein-coding genes and structural RNAs, where intergenic
regions (IGRs), typically thought to contain regulatory elements, only takes up a small portion of the
genome. The fraction of non-coding DNA in the majority of bacterial and archaeal genomes is between
6-14% [45]. The IGR is the region, or spacer, between protein-coding genes. The regulatory elements
found in IGRs can regulate almost all of the major processes in the cell, including transcription,
translation, and even protein activity [2].
2.1.1
Regulatory elements of transcription
There are three possible conﬁgurations of gene pairs, with respect to direction of transcription, unidi-
rectional, convergent, and divergent, seen in Figure 1. The IGR between gene pairs diﬀer by the types
of transcriptional regulatory elements they contain[40].
Figure 1: Possible gene arrangements
Three types of gene pairs with respect to direction of transcription. Diﬀerent genes are represented by coloured arrows,
the direction of the arrow indicates the direction of transcription.
The intergenic region between uni-directional genes, can include both a terminator for the upstream
gene and a promoter, as well as other signals such as an operator for the downstream gene. The
intergenic region between convergent genes exclusively contain terminators, and the intergenic region
between divergent genes exclusively contain promoters and other transcriptional factors.
The promoter is the region of DNA which initiates transcription of a gene. Most often, promoters
contain two highly conserved sequences, upstream of the transcription start site, which are recognised
by the sigma factor. One is the Pribnow box in the -10 region (10 bases upstream of the transcription
start site) of the promoter with the consensus sequence: TATAAT. The second one is in the -35 region
which has the consensus sequence: TTGACA. Diﬀerent sigma factors recognise diﬀerent -35 regions
and the most common sigma factor in E. coli is σ70. Most promoters vary slightly from the consensus
sequence, which has an eﬀect on the rate of gene expression. The transcription start site is typically a
T or C nucleotide, which is recognised by the RNA polymerase [52]. The features of the σ70 promoter
can vary quite a bit and can contain features such as the UP element and an extended -10 sequence.
The Up element helps in stabilizing the binding of RNA polymerase and is located upstream of the
-35 sequence. The extended -10 sequence, with consensus TGNTATAAT, is often found in promoters
without a -35 sequence.
Regulation of transcriptional initiation is mediated by regulatory proteins which act to increase or
decrease the gene expression by binding in, or near the promoter [52]. A transcriptional repressor
binds DNA at an operator sequence, which is located close to, or overlapping, the promoter and
prevents transcription of the DNA by the RNA polymerase. A transcriptional activator usually binds
upstream of the promoter at an activator site, where it facilitates binding of the RNA polymerase
to the promoter. Small molecules, called eﬀectors, can modify the activity of regulatory proteins in
negative and positive ways as well.
Page 2 of 58
==PAGE:7==
Transcription is terminated once the RNA polymerase encounters a transcriptional termination signal
in the DNA. There are two basic types of transcriptional termination signals: factor-dependent and
-independent termination, diﬀering by whether a protein is needed for termination or if the DNA
sequence alone is suﬃcient.
Factor-dependent transcriptional termination requires a termination factor, such as Rho in E. coli
which utilizes RNA translocase activity to dissociate the RNA polymerase from the transcript. The
mechanism of rho-dependent transcription termination involves recognition and binding of nonspeciﬁc
signals called Rho-utilization (rut) sites on the nascent RNA by Rho, followed by dissociation of RNA
polymerase from the transcript [42]. No single consensus rut sequence is thought to exist, which makes
bioinformatic prediction of Rho-dependent terminators diﬃcult [42].
The factor-independent (or intrinsic) terminator consists of two main elements, a GC-rich dyad which
forms the terminator hairpin 7-8 bp downstream of the transcripts 3’ end, followed by, a 7-8 uracil
rich region [42]. Since most factor-independent terminators have the same properties, they are easily
identiﬁed [52].
2.1.2
Small regulatory RNAs in bacteria
A large number of sRNAs have already been experimentally identiﬁed [46], and predicted in a wide
range of bacteria [19]. The sRNAs can be divided into several classes, which will be described in the
following paragraphs.
Antisense RNAs is a type of sRNA that regulates transcripts by basepairing, resulting in changes to
mRNA translation and stability [2]. Based on the genomic location, the antisense RNA can be divided
into two groups: cis-encoded and trans-encoded. The cis-encoded anti-sense RNAs are encoded on
the strand opposite the gene it regulates and has a region of perfect complementarity with its target
[2]. The trans-encoded antisense RNAs are genomically distant from their mRNA target and usually
only show partial complementarity to it [43]. Because of the limited complementarity of trans-encoded
antisense RNAs, the Hfq chaperone is often required to facilitate the interaction between sRNAs and
target mRNAs [19].
Base pairing between sRNAs and mRNAs can result in either positive or negative regulation of both
mRNA translation and or stability as seen in Figure 2. Positive regulation by sRNAs is often a result of
basepairing between sRNA and mRNA which disrupts formation of translation-inhibitory structures,
such as occlusion of the ribosome binding site (RBS) [43]. Negative regulation by sRNAs include direct
occlusion of the RBS, as well as degradation of sRNA-mRNA duplex by RNase E[30].

==PAGE:8==
Figure 2: Mechanisms of regulation by trans-encoded sRNAs
Figure from Richards et al., (2011) [43]. sRNAs designated as grey lines, and mRNA targets are designates as black
arrows. Complementary basepairing between sRNAs and mRNA is shown as series of thin lines between the two.
Protein-binding sRNAs are small RNA molecules which regulate protein activity by, for example,
competitive inhibition of the active site. An example of this is the 6S RNA which, in E. coli regulates
the RNA Polymerase by preventing promoter recognition [59].
Dual-function sRNAs are sRNAs which have multiple mechanisms of regulation. These sRNAs are
able to basepair to one or more regulatory targets, in addition to acting as mRNAs that encode small
regulatory peptides. An example of this is the SgrS sRNA in E. coli and Salmonella which regulates
sugar transport by repressing sugar transporter mRNAs, as well as encoding the SgrT protein that
inhibits the activity of the transporters [58][20].
Riboswitches is another class of sRNAs which can regulate genes at both a transcriptional and trans-
lational level [60]. Riboswitches regulate gene expression through changes in conformation which is
mediated by binding of small molecules or changes in temperature [43].
2.1.3
Other regulatory RNA molecules
TmRNA, also known as SsrA, or 10S RNA, is an RNA species which is able to act as both tRNA
and mRNA. In E. coli, ssrA encodes a small stable RNA molecule. The function of tmRNAs is to
release stalled ribosomes from mRNAs that do not have a stop codon [24]. The tmRNA then attaches
a 10 aminoacid tag to the defective protein, which is a signal for degradation recognized by certain
proteases [24]. In Salmonella Typhimurium, ssrA was discovered to be required for virulence [23].
2.2
Adaptive evolution
Host adaptation is an example of adaptive evolution which pathogens undergo in order to adapt to
the environment inside the host. Phylogenetic models are used to elucidate evolutionary relationships
between populations of bacteria, which enables inferences about evolutionary progression of mutations
[33]. Using phylogenetics to analyse populations of pathogenic bacteria, it is possible to identify the
genetic changes which are a result of host-adaptation and separate them from mutations that are a
result of genetic drift. [32]. A powerful indication, that a change in genotype and or phenotype is
caused by adaptation to a certain ecological niche, and not genetic drift, is parallel evolution, where
Page 4 of 58
==PAGE:9==
the repeated evolution of the same genotype occurs independently [62]. It is important to make this
distinction, since random genetic drift is a trait exhibited by all organisms.
Mutations in intergenic regions can have profound eﬀects on phenotype. One example of this is how
a SNP in a promoter of Salmonella Typhimurium modulates virulence in a chicken infection model,
contributes to serum resistance, and increases degradation of human complement factor B[15]. Mu-
tations to a sRNA gene in Salmonella have been shown to attenuate virulence in animal killing, and
aﬀect colonisation in a mouse infection model [14]. In Pseudomonas aeruginosa, several host adaptive
intergenic mutations that aﬀect metal uptake systems have been discovered, such as a promoter mu-
tation that aﬀects zinc acquisition [17], or promoter mutations which confer growth advantages in the
presence of haemoglobin [32].
There are diﬀerent types of mutations, some with a functional eﬀect and others silent. For protein
coding genes the impact of the mutation on the amino acid sequence, of the resulting protein, are said
to be synonymous or non-synonymous. Synonymous mutations are mutations to genes that do not
alter the amino acid sequence of the resulting protein. Non-synonymous mutations are mutations to
genes that do alter the amino acid sequence. For intergenic region such a distinction cannot be made,
by deﬁnition, and it is only possible to look at the type of mutation. Changes to the nucleotide se-
quence can be separated by whether the change aﬀects a single nucleotide, known as a single nucleotide
polymorphism (SNP), or if the length and composition of the nucleotide sequence is changed, which is
the case for insertion and deletion mutations, also known as indel mutations. Mutations that have an
impact on the pathogenicity of bacteria are known as pathoadaptive mutations.
2.3
Pathogenic bacteria
Pseudomonas aeruginosa is an ubiquitous environmental inhabitant and an opportunistic pathogen
which is capable of causing chronic infections in the airways of cystic ﬁbrosis patients [33]. Regular
sampling of CF sputum has made it possible to monitor the molecular evolution of P. aeruginosa as
it adapts to a lifestyle within the human airways [34].
Salmonella enterica is a gram-negative, rod shaped, and facultative pathogenic bacterium [31]. S.
enterica is a very diverse species which is composed of six subspecies: enterica, salamae, arizonae,
diarizonae, houtenae, and indica. S. enterica consists of more than 2600 diﬀerent serovars which can
be divided into typhoidal and non-typhoidal Salmonella (NTS) [13]. In this thesis there will be a focus
on Salmonella enterica subsp. enterica, serovar Enteritidis. Enteritidis is a pathogenic serovar of S.
enterica subsp. enterica with broad host-speciﬁcity and isolates of S. Enteritidis have been found to
cause infections in mice, colonize chicken tissue, and cause gastroenteritis in man [55]. Bacteremia
caused by S. Enteritidis is frequently associated with malnutrition, HIV infections or primary immune
deﬁciencies [26].

Project background
3.1
Experimental overview
The work can be split into three parts:
• Identiﬁcation of putative host-adaptive intergenic mutations hitting regulatory elements
• Investigating an intergenic mutation in Salmonella Enteriditis.
• Investigating an intergenic mutation in Pseudomonas aeruginosa
In part 1 of this project nucleotide variants in intergenic regions from multiple bacterial adaptation
studies were investigated. Web-based tools were used to identify regulatory elements within the inter-
genic regions. The goal of this task was to build a list of intergenic mutations in regulatory elements
that likely are a result of host-adaptation. One mutations found here was studied experimentally in
part 3.
Part 2 of the project was to experimentally determine the role of an intergenic mutation, in
Salmonella Enteritidis that was previously found in part 1. To analyse the eﬀect of this mutation,
which was found in multiple clinical isolates, the diﬀerence in gene expression of the mutant and the
wild-type intergenic region was investigated using transcriptional reporter fusions.
Part 3 of the project was the experimental study of an intergenic mutation found in a P. aeruginosa
promoter.In order to gain an understanding of the role of this mutation, which is found in multiple
clinical isolates of cystic ﬁbrosis patients, the diﬀerence between the mutated and wild-type intergenic
region was investigated using reporter fusions, and phenotypic assays.
3.2
Part 1
The identiﬁcation of putative host-adaptive intergenic mutations hitting regulatory elements was car-
ried out in diﬀerent ways depending on the organism used to create the dataset of intergenic mutations.
Since intergenic elements are rarely annotated on genomes, it was necessary to use web-based tools to
identify intergenic regulatory elements in the intergenic regions that were analysed.
3.2.1
Putative host-adaptive intergenic mutations in P.aeruginosa
First the number intergenic regions from a P aeruginosa study was narrowed down. This was done by
cross referencing 88 putative host-adaptive mutated intergenic regions from Khademi et al., (2017) [25]
with P. aeruginosa laboratory evolution studies, in order to ﬁnd study overlaps. In addition to this, i

Role of lptD (PA0595)
PA0595 encodes the LPS-assembly protein LptD [61]. LptD is part of the lipopolysaccharide transport
(lpt) system which is responsible for transporting LPS from the to the cell surface, where it modulates
the permability of the outer membrane, and prevents many toxic compounds from entering the cell
[3]. LptD is a β-barrel outer membrane protein which is essential for LPS transport to the cell surface
as seen in Figure 5 [49]. Synthetic peptidomimetic antibiotics which target lptD in Pseudomonas sp.
have been developed [53] [49].
Role of PA0596
PA0596 encodes the protein AmgK which is an aminoglycoside phosphotransferase [4]. AmgK is part
of a peptidoglycan recycling pathway which bypasses peptidoglycan de novo biosynthesis and is shown
to be responsible for the high intrinsic resistance of P. aeruginosa to fosfomycin as seen in Figure 6.
Peptidoglycan is one of the main components of the bacterial cell wall. This pathway in P. aeruginos
has only recently been completely elucidated by Borisova et al., 2017 [5]. Since this pathway confers
intrinsic reistance to fosfomycin, this pathway may serve as a novel target for antimicrobial therapy.

4.2.1
General maintenance of cultures and media
Overnight cultures are incubated at 37°C in LB for approximately 16-18h in 20 mL glass tubes with
200 rpm shake, unless otherwise speciﬁed. Appropriate antibiotic is used as a supplement to media to
ensure plasmid maintenance. For P. aeruginosa cultures with the ctx-lux plasmid, 50 µg/ml tetracyclin
is used for selection and maintenance. For E. coli cultures with the ctx-lux plasmid, 8 µg/ml tetracyclin
is used for selection and maintenance. For E. coli cultures with the pACYC-lacZ plasmid, 100 µg/ml
fosfomycin is used for selection and maintenance. Preparing agar plates with antibiotics is done by
adding the appropriate amount of antibiotic to the agar when the media is at 50-55°C. To store a
culture at -80°C, 1 ml of an overnight culture is added to 600 µl 50% glycerol in a freezer tube and
frozen at -80°C. Cells were regularly grown in Lysogeny broth (LB) and Mueller-hinton (MH), both
rich media, or ABT and MM, both minimal media. A list of media and the media composition can be
found in appendix. Strains, plasmids and primers are found, respectively, in Table 3, Table 2, Table 4.
4.2.2
Overlap extension PCR
"Outer" primers FwdfolaigrbamHI, RevfolaigrHindIII used to amplify folA intergenic region. SNP
introduced using overlap extension PCR with "inner" primers FwdCtoAfolasnp and RevCtoAfolasnp.
Two seperate PCR runs are needed to create the template needed for the ﬁnal PCR ampliﬁcation. The
two ﬁrst PCR runs uses one of the inner primers with one of the outer primers. For the third run, the
two fragments from the ﬁrst runs are used as a template to create a full fragment, where the SNP is
used. The general principle of overlap extension PCR is shown in Figure 9. Protocol can be found in
Appendix.

Two rounds of PCR, used to introduce a SNP to the template DNA. Blue or green arrows are primers, and the location
of the SNP is marked by a white box.

4.2.4
Construction of FolA reporter fusions
The intergenic region upstream of folA in S. Enteritidis P125109 was ampliﬁed by PCR with primers
"FwdfolaigrBamHI" and "RevfolaigrHindIII" using cell lysate as PCR template. Due to high annealing
temperatures, a temperature gradient between 70 and 64°C was used, and DMSO was added to the
reaction. PCR products were run on an agarose gel and bands of 250bp were extracted from the gel.
The C→A mutation was introduced on the intergenic region by overlapping extension PCR using
primer pairs "FwdCtoAfolasnp"+"RevfolaigrHindIII" and "RevCtoAfolasnp"+"FwdfolaigrBamHI".
Intergenic sequences were then integrated in the pACYClacZ plasmid. In individual reactions, both
intergenic regions and the pACYClacZ plasmid was digested with restriction enzymes HindIII and
BamHI. These were ligated, using NEB ligation calculator to determine the correct vector:insert molar
ratio. Digested pACYC was ligated as a control. Ligation mix was used to transform chemically com-
petent E coli cells as described previously. The sequence of the intergenic region was veriﬁed using PCR
and sanger-sequencing. Veriﬁed plasmids were puriﬁed from E. coli cells and used to transform elec-
trocompetent S Typhimurium KP1274 cells as previously described. Plasmid insert was veriﬁed with
PCR. Plasmids from S Typhimurium KP1274 were puriﬁed and used to transform electrocompetent
S. Enteritidis P125109 cells. Transformants were veriﬁed using PCR and stored in -80°C.
4.2.5
Chemically competent E. coli cells
DH5α cells were used to innoculate LB, which was incubated O/n at 37°C. OD600 was measured and 50
ml fresh LB was innoculated to OD600 = 0.05. The cells were grown at 37°C until OD600 = 0.4 −0.5
and then transfered to an ice bath for 30 minutes. Cells were then transfered to 8 cold 2ml Eppendorf
tubes and spun down at 8000 rpm for 5 minutes at 4°C. This was repeated 4 times in order to pellet
all of the cells. Supernatant was discarded and tubes were combined for a total of two 2 ml Eppendorf
tubes. Pellet was resuspended in 1 ml 50 mM CaCl2 and spun down (same centrifuge parameters).
The supernatant was discarded and the pellet was resuspended in 100 µl 50 mM CaCl2, 15% glycerol.
Aliquots of 50 µl were prepared and frozen in the -80°C freezer.
4.2.6
Electrocompetent Salmonella enterica cells
A Salmonella culture was inoculated in LB and incubated overnight at 37°C. 1 ml overnight culture is
used to inoculate 50 ml LB in a 250 ml Erlenmeyer ﬂask. Incubate ﬂask at 37°C with 200 rpm shake.
Growth is followed by measuring OD600. Cells are transferred to 2 centrifuge tubes and centrifuged at
6000 rpm (5860g) for 8 minutes at 0°C. Cells are constantly kept on ice. Each tube is resuspended in
25 ml icecold (autoclaved) 10% glycerol by gently pipetting up and down, centrifugation is repeated
as before. Glycerol washing is repeated two additional times. Finally, each tube of cells is resuspended
in 500 µl icecold 10% glycerol and aliquoted in 50 µl samples. Aliquots are stored at -80°C.

4.2.8
Transformation by electroporation
Previously prepared electrocompetent cells (Works with both E. coli and Salmonella enterica) were
thawed on ice and 1ml LB per sample was pre-warmed to 37°C. Electroporation tubes were chilled in -
20°C freezer. Everything is kept on ice prior to electroporation. 1 µl plasmid is added to competent cells
and transferred to electroporation tube which is placed in the gene-pulser slot. Cells are electroporated
by pressing the two buttons until a beeping is heard. Warm LB is quickly added to the cells and
transferred to an Eppendorf tube which is then incubated for 1-2 hours at 37°C. The time constant
of each electroporation is noted. LB + 8 µg/ml Tet was used to select for transformants. 3 plates
were made for each electroporation: 100 µl 10x dilution, 200 µl “neat”, and 200 µl concentrated sample
(centrifuged at 8k RPM for 2 min). Plates are incubated at 37°C. Transformants are re-streaked and
veriﬁed by PCR and or sequencing.

Transformation by heatshock
1-2 µl ligated plasmid is added to 50 µl chemically competent DH5α cells and incubated for 30 minutes
at 0°C. Cells are heat shocked at 42°C for 1 minute and then put back on ice for 2 minutes. Cells were
then then rescued with 500 µl LB and grown for 1 hour at 37°C. Cells are plated on selective medium
as described in section .
4.2.10
Spot assay
The expression level of the promoters was assayed on solid media. Both on LB and MM agar. The 4
reporter fusion strains were used with 3 replicates per run. Cultures were prepared in LB + 50 µg/ml
tetracycline and incubated overnight at 37°C. The overnight cultures were diluted 1:100 in fresh LB
and grown at 37°C for approximately 3-4h to reach mid-log phase. OD was measured and the cultures
were diluted to OD 0.5. Agar plates were prepared by drying them for 10 minutes and the position
of the spots were marked with a permanent marker according to “ﬁgure x”. Reporter.fusions with the
wild-type intergenic region was spotted on one side, and the reporter fusions with a mutated intergenic
region was spotted on the other side. The spots were left to dry completely before incubating them
upside down at 37°C. After overnight incubation of the plates, a GFP-visualiser setup was used to
take pictures of the plates. In order to minimize diﬀerence between plates, the same procedure was
followed, for taking pictures of the plates. Pictures were taken using the “rolling average” with the
average parameter set to “8” and three pictures were taken, in this way, of each plate. Pictures were
taken of the plates without the lid on. Quantiﬁcation of colony luminosity was done using ImageJ.

Initial setup and methods used in all plate reader experiments For all plate reader experiments,
where luminescence is measured, it is necessary to use black 96 well microtiter plate.
All plate-
reader experiments are run at 37°C for up to 24 hours with omnidirectional shake. OD600 as well
as luminescence is sampled every 5th or 10th minute and at the end of every experiment, the data
is exported to a text ﬁle.
For every diﬀerent layout of cultures in a microtiter plate, an R script
has been designed to read and process all data. Before inoculating the micro titer plate, the overnight
culture is washed twice by centrifuging the culture at 3600 g for 5 minutes, discarding the supernatant,
Page 14 of 58
==PAGE:19==
and then re-suspending the culture in fresh media. The OD600 of the culture is then measured, and
adjusted so the ﬁnal OD600 in the microtiter plate is 0.005, before incubation. Cultures are grown in

a breathable ﬁlm is placed over the microtiter plate and the plate is inserted into the plate-reader

The data that is read from the plate reader are data points of OD or luminescence which corresponds
to a certain time point and a well. OD and luminescence is not measured at the same time by the
plate reader, however the time diﬀerence between these measurements is less than a minute. In order
to compare the luminescence of diﬀerent cultures, the highest measured luminescence value is found
for a culture. This value is then normalized by dividing it with the OD600 that corresponds to this
luminescence value. This value is referred to as the "Lum / OD".

Motility assays Three diﬀerent motility assays was used: Swimming, swarming and twitching motility
assay.
Assays were done according to methods described by Filloux et al,.
(2014) [11].
Diﬀerent
concentrations of agar was used for the diﬀerent motility assays, 0.3% for swimming motility, 0.5% for
swarming motility, and 1.5% for twitching motility. MM agar was used for swimming and swarming
motilty assays.
To assay swimming motility, a colony was picked from a less than 7 day old agar plate with a sterile
toothpick or pipette tip. The toothpick (or pipette tip) was then used to stab halfway into the agar.
Plate was incubated upright at 30°C for 1-2 days and radial growth was measured.
To assay swarming motilty, 2.5 µl OD600 = 2 overnight culture was used to inoculate the center of an
agar plate. Plate was incubated upright at 37°c overnight, and phenotype was observed.
To assay twitching motility, a colony was picked from a less than 7 day old agar plate with a sterile
toothpick or pipette tip. Sterile toothpick or pipette tip was stabbed all the way through the agar to
the agar-plastic interface. To ensure inoculum had reached the bottom of the plate, the toothpick (or
pipette tip) was jiggled up and down a few times. Plate was incubated at 37°C overnight, and the
radius of the interstitial colony was measured.

in both LB and ABT media at 37°C. For each strain, 4 biological and 3 technical replicates were used.
Based on visual evaluation of growth curves, it was found that all cultures were growing in exponential
phase between log2 OD = −2.5 to log2OD = −1. In order to ﬁnd the growth rate of the culture a
linear regression is made on a log2 transformed growth curve between the previously mentioned OD
values. The slope of the resulting line is the growth rate. Any linear regression with a r2 less than 0.95
was discarded.

In order to systematically ﬁnd the growth rate from each well in the microtiter plate, a semi-log plot

control for variations in the duration of lag-phases for all biological replicates and at the same time

determined by ﬁnding stable linear growth in the semi-log plot. The interval -2.5 to -1 was determined
from growth curves in Figure 28 since all cultures, on both media, grew exponentially in this interval.
Figure 28: Semi-log plot of growth curves.
The growth rate was calculating by ﬁnding the slope of a linear regression between log2(OD) = -2.5 and log2(OD) -1.

for each well to ﬁnd the growth rate. Any linear regressions with an r2<0.95 was discarded. All linear
regressions made this way was found to have an r2 above 0.95, indicating that this is an eﬃcient way to
control for variable duration of the lag-phase. It also shows that the cultures are growing exponentially,
since the result of a logarithmic transformation of exponential growth is linear growth. This method
was only possible since all wells were inoculated at the same concentration of bacteria.

Discussion
In this thesis, the role of two intergenic mutations, from two widely diﬀerent backgrounds, were studied
experimentally. Both of these mutations are likely a result of host adaptation, which is why studying
them is important for elucidating the genetic basis for host adaptation.
The discussion has, like the introduction and results section, been split into three parts. The ﬁrst
part regards the set of putative host-adaptive intergenic mutations that hit regulatory elements. The
second part relates to the experiments that were performed to discover the role of a promoter mutation
in S Enteritidis. And in the third part, an attempt to elucidate the role of the intergenic mutation in
P. aeruginosa is made.
Page 36 of 58
==PAGE:41==
6.1
Part 1: Putative host-adaptive intergenic mutations that hit regulatory ele-
ments
The ﬁrst aim of this project was to ﬁnd putative host-adaptive intergenic mutations that hit regulatory
elements from a larger dataset of intergenic mutations, and to experimentally study at least one of
these in part 2.
Since many of the intergenic mutations in P. aeruginosa had already been analysed, an eﬀort was

It was later found that the list of intergenic SNPs by Jansen et al., (2015) was diﬀerences between

6.1.1
Prediction of regulatory elements
Regulatory elements in the intergenic regions were predicted using web-based tools. Strengths and
limitations of these tools are discussed here.
Promoter recognition. BPROM recognises bacterial sigma70 promoter sequences with 80% accu-
racy and speciﬁcity based on conserved sequences of E coli promoters. Which, i interpret to mean
that, in 20% of cases, BPROM fails to predict or incorrectly predicts a promoter. Another reason
why promoters may not have been predicted is the fact that alternative sigma factors, with diﬀerent
consensus sequences exist [52]. The sequence and structural proﬁle of promoters have also been shown
to diﬀer greatly between species [35], which is another factor to consider in relation to the use of
promoter recognition software.
Terminator recognition. Two diﬀerent recognition tools were used to identify terminators, ARNold,
and RegRNA2.0. Naville et al., 2011 presents ARNold, which combines two prediction algorithms,
Erpin and RNAmotif to predict Rho-independent transcription terminators with high speciﬁcity and
a higher sensitivity than the individual algorithms [39].
RegRNA2.0 analyses sequences using several diﬀerent algorithms and for terminator prediction, these
are: ERPIN, Rfam, and TransTermHP [6].
It is diﬃcult to talk about advantages or disadvantages of the two diﬀerent web-based services, and
some overlap seems to exist between them in terms of terminator prediction. According to Naville et
al 2011., TransTermHP has the best sensitivity and speciﬁcity of the previously mentioned algorithms,
however it is not able to analyse sequences below 74 nt, unlike the ARNold algorithm which would
indicate that a combination of the tools provides good coverage of diﬀerent terminators.
Recognition of other RNA motifs.
RegRNA2.0 is capable of identifying a wide range of in-
tergenic regulatory elements.

6.2
Part 2: Determining the eﬀect of an intergenic mutation in S. Enteritidis
The role of a putative host-adaptive intergenic mutation in S. Enteritidis, which was found in part 1,
was studied experimentally. The intergenic mutation is located within a predicted promoter in the -35
region of the folA promoter. The reason why this intergenic mutation was chosen over other intergenic
mutations, found in part 1, is discussed in this section. The role of the intergenic mutation, in relation
to host-adaptation should also become clearer.
Only a few of the mutations that were present in multiple isolates from S. Enteritidis were present in
multiple linages. The promoter mutation that was chosen is one of those, as described in section 5.1.3,
and as such is likely a result of a host-adaptive mutation and not natural genetic drift. Another reason
this particular intergenic mutation was chosen, is that the mutation likely eﬀects the expression of the
downstream gene folA, which is the target of some antibiotic therapies.
6.2.1
Role of the folA gene
The folA gene encodes a dihydrofolate reductase (DHFR) which is essential for maintaining the level
of folate cofactors necessary for the biosynthesis of nucleotide precursors and amino acids in bacterial
cells [51]. Antibiotics like trimethoprim and sulfonamide drugs aﬀect the biosynthesis of folic acid in
bacteria and are often used in combination [10]. Trimethoprim inhibits the activity of DHFR, and
resistance is usually associated with overproduction of DHFR or alterations to the DHFR enzyme
[10]. TMP resistance from promoter mutations that lead to an increased expression of DHFR have
previously been reported in E. coli [50] [51]. Smith et al., 1982 describes overexpression of DHFR that
is caused by variations in the -35 region of the fol promoter. Herrington et al., 2002 show a promoter
mutation, in the exact same position as the one found in this thesis, which leads to a change in gene
expression and phenotype [18]. The -34C→T fol promoter mutation described in the Herrington et
al., 2002 study, leads to an increase in DHFR activity.
6.2.2
Nucleotide variations of the folA promoter results in altered gene expression
The -35 region of the σ70 promoter has the consensus sequence TTGACA, which diﬀers from the -35
region of the wild-type S Enteritidis folA promoter TCGACG at two positions. The ﬁrst diﬀerence
is the -34T→C, and the other diﬀerence is the -30A→G. Previous studies have studied the eﬀect of
nucleotide variations in the σ70 promoter [38] [12]; these studies show that a -34T→C mutation of the
consensus σ70 promoter leads to a 22.7 fold decrease in expression and the -30A→G leads to a 1.9 fold
decrease in expression. The combined eﬀect of these mutations can be theorized to be the product of
these two mutations which is an overall 43.13 fold decrease in expression compared to the expression
of the consensus -35 region of the σ70 promoter.
The -35 motif of the mutant S Enteritidis folA promoter is TAGACG, which diﬀers from the wild-type
S Enteritidis folA promoter by a -34C→A mutation. Again this can be related to changes in expression
from the consensus σ70 promoter; the -34T→A leads to a 6.8 fold decrease in gene expression [38],
which combined with the -30A→G mutation can be theorized to be lead to an overall 12.92 fold
Page 38 of 58
==PAGE:43==
decrease in expression. Based on these two ﬁgures, the theoretical eﬀect of the -34C→A folA promoter
mutuation in S Enteritidis is a 3.3 fold increase in gene expression. This is remarkably close to the
diﬀerence in beta-galactosidase activity between the wild-type and mutant folA promoter described in
section 5.2.1, which showed a 2.29 fold increase on LB and a 2.98 fold increase in activity on ABT.
One reason why the results from this thesis might diﬀer from the theoretical ﬁgure is that multiple
nucleotide variations to the σ70 promoter might have a synergistic eﬀect on gene expression. The
standard deviation tied to the relative activity of diﬀerent -35 motifs, by Moyle et al., (1991) should
also be considered.
A transcriptomic analysis of the change between cells growing in LB and cells growing in minimal
media have previously been found to aﬀect the expression of 344 genes in E coli [54]. This most likely
explains why there is a diﬀerence in the the beta-galactosidase activity ratios between media.
6.2.3
Concluding remarks
It was possible to measure the eﬀect of a putative host-adaptive promoter mutation in S. Enteritidis
using reporter-fusions.
Relating to the aims of this thesis, not only did this show that intergenic
mutations can have a measurable eﬀect on gene expression, but it also shows that the ﬁrst part of
this thesis provides a list of intergenic mutations that likely have a functional eﬀect.
The results
found here are obviously not suﬃcient for elucidating the role of this intergenic mutation in relation
to host-adaptation, but it signiﬁes that this mutation should be studied further.
6.3
Part 3: Determining the eﬀect of an intergenic mutation in P. aeruginosa
The role of a putative host-adaptive intergenic mutation in P. aeruginosa was studied experimentally.
The eﬀect of a four nucleotide insertion in an intergenic region, on phenotype and gene expression was
investigated. This was a continuation of previous work done by the IMG group at DTU bioengineering.
6.3.1
Role of the PA0595//PA0596 intergenic region
A reason why this particular intergenic mutation is particularly interesting to study, can be found when
looking at the ﬂanking genes, and their function. As previously described, genes lptD and PA0596 have
an eﬀect on the LPS and the cell wall, which are located in the outer boundary of the bacterial cell.
The LPS and peptidoglycan are both essential components of P. aeruginosa and are known to have
an eﬀect on uptake[3], virulence and host response [41], and resistance to antibiotics [29].
6.3.2
Activity of lux reporter fusions
The activity of the diﬀerent reporter fusions was measured over long growth durations, and only
the peak luminescence, normalised by bacterial density, was used for comparison between diﬀerent
conditions.
Common for the lux reporters are a peak in luminescence during exponential growth
phase, followed by a decline in luminescence as described in section 5.5.1. This way of quantifying
lux reporter fusion activity was also used in Hermansen et al., 2018. Another way of quantifying lux
reporter fusion activity is to ﬁnd the highest ratio between luminescence and bacterial density, which
is diﬀerent from peak luminescence, as illustrated in Figure 31. The two measures of luminescence
activity peak at diﬀerent times, however the biological signiﬁcance of this is not known.
A third way would be to use the peak luminesence without normalising for bacterial density, however
that is out of the question since it is impossible to compare this value between isolates that grow
diﬀerently. Since the, normalised peak luminescence as a measure of reporter activity, is the standard
used at the IMG group of DTU bioengineering, it was chosen as the preferred method.

6.3.3
Alterations in gene expression as a result of the ins62 mutation
The eﬀect of the intergenic 4 nucleotide insertion on gene expression was measured using reporter-
fusion strains. This The eﬀect was measured on both rich and minimal media, and during planktonic
and colony growth. Many genes are expressed diﬀerently depending on whether cells are grown in LB
or minimal media in E. coli [54], or whether cells are grown in liquid or on agar plates [36].
Expression during planktonic growth
It was found that the four nucleotide insertion upstream of lptD signiﬁcantly lowered the expression of
the lptD promoter when grown on minimal media ABT. The location of the mutation, as described in
section 3.3, is downstream of the transcription start site, and does not hit the predicted lptD promoter.
No other regulatory elements were predicted in the PA0595//PA0596 intergenic region. Nucleotide
variations downstream of promoters have been shown to aﬀect transcription of a plant virus because
of transcription factor binding sites [16], but it’s diﬃcult to argue the relevance of this study due to
the vast genetic diﬀerence that are found between bacteria and plants (assuming the plant virus has a
plant-like genome). Three plausible explanations spring to mind. Either the location of the promoter
is not predicted correctly, a sequence recognised by a transcription factor which only binds when grown
in minimal media was disrupted or altered in some way, or the mutation enables the formation of an
RNA structure that aﬀects gene expression when grown in minimal media.
To further speculate: if the intergenic mutation aﬀects the binding of a transcription factor it would
either have weakened the binding of an activator, or strengthened the binding of a repressor. Regarding
RNA structures, the list of possibilities are almost endless (it includes anti-regulatory-elements, and
anti-anti-elements ad inﬁnitum) and the result of the mutation could have an eﬀect on both the
transcription and translation. Experimental evidence of either possibility would be ideal, however this
was outside the scope of this thesis.
Spot assay
Since it was not possible to measure the gene expression during colony growth, the spot assay method
should be modiﬁed in a way that controls for variations in luminescence at diﬀerent locations. One
example is to only measure the activity of a colony when it is in a certain location within the GFP-
visualiser.
6.3.4
Induction of the PA0596 promoter
Since it was not possible to distinguish between the activity of the wild-type PA0596 promoter (PWt)
and that of the control strain CTX-lux in section 5.3, a longer search was started to ﬁnd environ-
mental conditions where the wild-type PA0596 promoter is induced. The purpose of this is to ﬁnd an
environmental condition where the eﬀect of the intergenic mutation can be determined.
First it was tested if the presence of fosfomycin in the growth media induces the PA0596 promoter,
since the genes PA0596, PA0597 provide intrinsic resistance to fosfomycin as described by Borisova et
al., (2014). However it was not possible to distinguish the activity of the PWt reporter from that of
the control which means it does not induce the PA0596 promoter. From the results of the experiment
in section 5.5.1 the concentration of fosfomycin seemingly has an eﬀect on luminescence, growth rate
and duration of lag-phase, but since the reporter strains are not phenotypically diﬀerent, the biological
signiﬁcance of it is outside the scope of this thesis.
What followed was a large scale test of diﬀerent compounds using Biolog phenotypic assays to provide
a wide array of compounds to test. Two compounds signiﬁcantly induced the expression of the wild-
type PA0596 promoter, one an organic acid and the other a β-lactam antibiotic which provides the
foundation for further study to determine the role of the ins62 intergenic mutation on the expression
of PA0596.

6.3.5
Phenotypic eﬀect of ins62 mutation
The eﬀect of the four nucleotide insertion on phenotype was tested.
The antibiotic susceptibility,
motility and growth rate was measured.
Antibiotic susceptibility
The antibiotic susceptibility of the strains were measured since the role of genes lptD and PA0596,
as described in section 3.3 and 6.3.1, can eﬀect antibiotic susceptibility. No signiﬁcant changes in
antibiotic susceptibility could be found between PAO1 ins62 and PAO1 and there was a very high
variation between biological replicates, especially when fosfomycin was tested.
This high variation could have been caused by the high innate antibiotic resistance of P. aeruginosa, or
a high rate of mutations that lead to resistance of colonies over the course of the experiment described
in section 5.4.1. Another factor which could have caused this inconsistency in results could be the
plates were inoculated at a too high OD, or that stationary-phase overnight cultures were used to
inoculate the plate, rather than cultures in exponential growth-phase.
Motility assay
The motility of the strains were also measured, but no signiﬁcant changes caused by the ins62 mutation,
were found. Although not statistically signiﬁcant, the apparent diﬀerences in swimming motility, seen
in Figure 27 are most likely explained by variations in inoculum size which resulted in an outlier result
for PAO1.
The assays were rather diﬃcult and time consuming, and since no statistically signiﬁcant diﬀerence
could be found between strains, it was decided not to continue further with the motility assays.
Another thing to consider is that the eﬀect of the intergenic mutation could simply be too subtle, and
the resolution of the motility assays are not high enough to detect this. Additionally, the results from
the motility assays are probably more qualitative than quantitative as a result of the relatively low
resolution, and should exclusively be used to determine whether a strain is motile or not.
Growth rate
Finally, the impact of the intergenic mutation on growth rate was determined. Since the diﬀerence
between growth rate on LB was almost signiﬁcant at P=0.058, i tried calculating the diﬀerence in
generation time G, G = 1/k ∗60min/h where k is growth rate, based on Table 22. PAO1 had a
generation time of 112 minutes and ins62 had a generation time of 109 minutes when grown in LB.
When grown in ABT, PAO1 had a generation time of 77.9 minutes and ins62 had a generation time
of 81.8 minutes. Labauve et al., 2012 reports that PAO1 has a generation time between 25 and 35
minutes when grown in LB [27] which shows that the methods employed in this thesis to determine
growth rate are probably not all that accurate. The growth rate was determined using a microtiter
plate, which means the culture volumes are very low (less than 300 µl), and in order to get accurate
measures of growth rate, the growth rate should be determined in larger culture volumes.
6.3.6
Concluding remarks
An attempt was made to determine the role of the putative host-adaptive intergenic mutation in P.
aeruginosa, however a lot of questions remain unanswered. The intergenic 4 nucleotide insertion was
found to decrease the gene expression of the lptD promoter when grown on a minimal media for reasons
unknown. Since the activity of the wild-type PA0596 promoter could not be distinguished from the
background, it was ﬁrst necessary to ﬁnd a compound which induces the promoter in order to determine
the eﬀect of the intergenic mutation. While two compounds were found to induce the expression of
PA0596, it was regrettably not possible to test the diﬀerence between the wild-type PA0596 promoter
and the mutated PA0596 promoter when grown on media supplemented with one of these compounds.
Regarding the aims of the thesis, a lot of experiments were carried in an attempt to determine the role

==PAGE:47==
of the intergenic mutation, in P. aeruginosa, however further study is required to fully elucidate its
role.
7
Future prospects
While a lot of work was put into investigating the role of putative host-adaptive intergenic mutations,
much still remains to be studied. One mutation, found in section 5.1.2, was studied experimentally,
however 21 other intergenic mutations that likely are a result of host-adaptation in S Enteritidis and
1 for P. aeruginosa remains to be experimentally analysed.
Since it was possible to identify compounds that induce the expression of the PA0596 promoter, these
should be used to test the eﬀect of the intergenic mutation on gene expression.
Given that large genomic changes result from the adaptation to the airways of cystic ﬁbrosis patients
[33], it would be an interesting prospect and possibly more relevant to repeat the experiments described
in this thesis using a clinical isolate as a background rather than PAO1 to further elucidate the role of
the intergenic mutation.
Given the clear diﬀerence in gene-expression as a result of the folA promoter mutation in S. Enteritidis
the phenotypic eﬀect of this mutation should be experimentally studied. Speciﬁcally, the eﬀect of the
promoter mutation on trimethoprim susceptibility should be analysed.
8
Conclusion
The goal of this thesis is to provide additional evidence that nucleotide variations in intergenic regions
are integral to the study of host-adaptation. The eﬀect of two diﬀerent putative host-adaptive intergenic
mutations, from two diﬀerent species of bacteria were analysed by measuring changes in gene expression
or phenotype. The folA promoter mutation in S. Enteritidis was found to cause a 2.3-3 fold increase of
expression, depending on the type of growth media. The result found for the folA promoter mutation
also validates the use of predictive tools to determine which intergenic mutations to study.
The
intergenic 4 nucleotide insertion in the lptD promoter of P. aeruginosa was found to cause a 1.05
fold decrease in gene expression when grown in a minimal media. Two compounds, L-lactic acid and
cefotaxime, were found to induce the expression of the PA0596 promoter which provide a basis for
further study of this intergenic mutation. In conclusion, this thesis shows that mutations in regulatory
elements are valuable for the understanding of host-adaptive mutations.

new tube, which was used as template for PCR.
