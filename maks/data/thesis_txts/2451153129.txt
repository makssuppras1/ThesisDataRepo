Abstract
The increasing share of electric vehicles (EVs) on the market provide possibilities for signiﬁcant
GHG emission reduction. The current power system, with its growing renewable energy (RE)
integration, could encounter instabilities with high EV penetration. However, with coordination
and management such as vehicle-to-grid (V2G) services, EVs can become a major contributor
to stabilising and supporting the grid. Grid operators could exploit the EVs while they are
connected to electric vehicle supply equipment (EVSE) and charging is not requested by the
users. However, this availability might be aﬀected by the continuous technology development
seen on the market. Therefore, the purpose of this study was to explore EV availability for
V2G services that can be expected based on near and future technology outlooks.
The ﬁrst part of the study consisted of an exploratory study of real EV plug-in data from
residential chargers in France. Here, plug-in frequency, energy requirements of the users and
average plug-in session duration were established. In the second part of the study, a model
was created to simulate EV charging needs, using real plug-in data from six EVs as input. The
inﬂuence of large battery EVs and high charge power on plug-in patterns and the impact on
availability were investigated.
Analysis of the exploratory study revealed that EV users are unique, with diﬀerent plug-in
habits and requirements from the EV. Nevertheless, once a plug-in session occurs, most users
have a tendency of staying connected to the EVSE from afternoon to the following morning.
Model output showed that increasing battery capacity results in decreasing plug-in frequency,
as the EVs were able to drive further before charging was needed. Furthermore, higher charge
power caused a minor reduction in plug-in frequency. This was due to the EVs being able to
reach the desired capacity during each plug-in session and a lower minimum state of charge
(SOC), which initiated plug-in sessions and was implemented to represent user response to
high charge power. Availability analysis of the scenarios revealed that the worst case was large
battery EVs charged with low power. Plug-in frequency was low and there was no available time
for grid support as charge time lasted the entire plug-in session duration. The scenario with
the highest availability was small battery EVs supplied by high power EVSE. Charge time was
signiﬁcantly reduced and plug-in frequency was about 80 % compared to the reference scenario,
which represented the EVs in the exploratory study. Based on these results, the study concludes
that battery size and charge power have an inﬂuence on plug-in frequency and availability for
V2G services, in that increasing both reduces plug-in frequency and availability. However, if
the market oﬀers suﬃcient battery capacities and high power chargers to meet a high variety of
customer groups, one might see a wider range of availability due to increasing EV penetration.

1 | Introduction
1.1
Background and motivation
In order to limit the increase in global temperature to 2-2.4°C, climate models suggest that in
2050 emissions should be 50-85 % lower compared to 1990 [13]. In the modern power system,
the share of renewable energy (RE) is increasing and becoming an important contributor to
our society and temperature target [14–16]. In 2016, the Danish domestic electricity supply
consisted of 53.9 % electricity from RE. The energy mix was a combination of 37.5 % wind
power and 12.7 % biomass. The remaining 3.7 % were from solar energy, hydro and biogas [16].
The increasing share of renewable energy in the power system has resulted in greenhouse gas
(GHG) emission reduction in the power sector [15]. The transportation sector, on the other
hand, is the only major sector where emissions continue to grow. Car use, road and maritime
freight, the largest contributors to GHG emissions, are almost totally dependent on oil. Electric
vehicles (EVs) are one of the measures taken to shift the transport sector from a fossil-dependant
sector to an electricity-based sector. In addition, they can contribute to further climate change
mitigation, by reducing the need for fossil fuel, if coupled with a decarbonised power system.
The increasing share of EVs in the global/Danish market and the sector being coupled to

The evolving power system and the decarbonising of the transportation sector are not with-

the need for vehicle range to match travellers need, which hinders EV integration [18].

Problem statement
V2G service is a method to support the integration of RE. In order to determine possible EV
availability for V2G services, it is of interest to establish plug-in patterns and user requirements.
Imminent technology developments are anticipated to inﬂuence current plug-in habits. Thus,

current and future trends.

Battery technology
Today’s EVs are almost exclusively powered by lithium-ion batteries.
This technology has
made it possible to travel suﬃcient distances of typical driving patterns. Speciﬁc energy of the
batteries can be over 250 Wh/kgcell with a price of around 250 US $/kWh [17,23]. From 1990 to

Battery lifetime is highly inﬂuenced by several factors. Degradation can be caused by calendar
ageing and cycling aging. Calendar aging is mainly inﬂuenced by standing time, state of charge
(SOC) and temperature.
Cycling aging is inﬂuenced by cycle number, depth of discharge
(DOD) and charging rate [28]. Several studies have found that high average cell SOC accelerate
battery degradation, resulting in shorter cell lives. A higher average SOC maintained during
battery cycling leads to a faster degradation rate. Thus, storing a battery at full charge may
be considered misuse [28–30]. Keeping the battery SOC below 30 % have been discovered to
have a positive eﬀect on battery lifetime. When using uncontrolled charging, 80 % of the time
the battery SOC is above 90 %, which accelerates battery aging [29]. Smart charging might
limit battery degradation by setting an upper limit to SOC and shift the charge period. Thus,
this would lower the average SOC and reduce the time the battery SOC is high. Furthermore,
recent tests have shown that battery degradation with V2G is limited if the battery stays within
a SOC of 60 - 80 %. The impact on the battery becomes similar to normal AC charging [31].

Charging technology and infrastructure
In Denmark, a three-phase connection is dominant also for residential customers [32].
The
transmission system operates with a voltage of 400 kV, while the consumers receive an Alter-
nating Current (AC) at 230 V(between one phase and earth), with an allowed variation between
+/- 10 % [33,34]. As EVs use Direct Current (DC) for charging and discharging, the AC from
the grid needs to be converted using an AC/DC converter. This converter is a part of an EV
charger. The charger can be external to the vehicle (i.e. a fast charger) or it can be integrated
into the EV as an on-board charger. The on-board charger manages internal power ﬂow to
safely charge the EV battery. The external part of the charger, named Electric Vehicle Supply
Equipment, delivers power from the grid to the EV’s on-board charger. It is generally called
the "charger" and will in the remainder of this study be referred to as such [35].

2.3
Vehicle-to-grid concept
Unidirectional and bidirectional chargers can provide diﬀerent services to the grid.
While
unidirectional chargers can only provide load levelling by modulating the charge, bidirectional
chargers allow reverse power ﬂow from the EVs. Bidirectional chargers are also able to provide
services such as spinning reserve, peak power shaving, active power support, voltage regulation,
and harmonic ﬁltering. Both unidirectional (with active front-end converters) and bidirectional
chargers are able to provide reactive power support. With bidirectional chargers, when the EV
batteries are not charging but still connected to the network, energy can be provided to the grid
at high demand and thus enhance the eﬃciency of the grid; this refers to V2G technology [22,38].
Three elements are required to enable electricity ﬂow using V2G; (1) the EV must be connected
to the grid; (2) there must be a control or logical connection necessary for communication with
the grid operator; and (3) the vehicles must have on-board control and metering. Electricity
ﬂow is two ways, meaning the ﬂow is either from generators through the grid to the EV or from
the battery EV to the grid. The battery can discharge during peak hours when power is needed
and charge during valley time [21].
EV loads can be managed by smart charging using vehicle-grid integration. It can be done
by either the EVSE automated response to control signals that react to the grid and market
situations, by customers responding to price signals, or by a combination of the two while
still considering customers’ needs for vehicle availability. Smart charging is a way of optimis-
ing the charging process according to local renewable energy availability and distribution grid
constraints, as well as the preferences of drivers and EVSE site hosts [31].
Several potential beneﬁts have been deﬁned for a V2G system. These can be categorized as
technical, economic and environmental. It is considered a great value to society as it can oﬀer
a cheap and technically advantageous form of storage. Apart from the load control by smart
charging, V2G can provide other electricity grid services such as frequency control, spinning
reserves and peak shifting. Furthermore, it can oﬀer a potential reduction of investment costs
for further grid capacity, opportunity to create new revenue streams for utilities and vehicle

that V2G will co-evolve with technical improvements, such as batteries and storage, as well as

3 | State of the art
Prior research has been conducted with the aim of determining EV availability for grid support
and its ﬂexibility to provide ancillary services. Investigations have also been carried out to
ﬁnd out if today’s EVs can meet the driving range requirements of the users. Further, some
have studied driving and charging patterns in order to provide a guide for future charging
infrastructure. Diﬀerent approaches have been used, such as survey-based research, modelling
and simulation, and data driven approaches. This section provides insight into ﬁndings and
similarities of previously conducted studies. The studies are described under subsections with
common topics of interest. At the end of the section, a summary of the ﬁndings is presented.
3.1
Charging infrastructure
With the aim of estimating the potential change in electricity demand from the EV expansion
and enlighten considerations when constructing EV infrastructure in Korea, H. Moon et al.
conducted a survey which included two discrete choice experiments. The analysis of the survey
focused on consumers’ preferences of EVs, charging locations, types of EVSE and charging times
of the day. Firstly, based on choice probability, the share of newly registered EVs was estimated
to increase between 2.31-8.74 %. Secondly, the study found that consumers preferred charging
during the evening, but when introduced to both public and private EVSEs, the users preferred
charging at public EVSEs during the day. Furthermore, the demand for fast public EVSEs
only surpassed the demand for normal public EVSE during peak load periods. Thus, during
peak load time, consumers prefer charging using fast public EVSEs, which indicates that people
considered trade-oﬀs between price for charging and full charge time. Finally, H. Moon et al.
provided important political implications for policymakers to be considered when adjusting the
EV charging infrastructure [42].
D. Wang et al. also provided a paper with instructions for design and construction of charging
facilities. Based on driving data from the National Household Travel Survey (NHTS), a stochas-
tic simulation model was constructed in order to generate itineraries and charging load proﬁles.
Daily travel activities were based on start/end time, trip distance and start/end location. The
factors with an inﬂuence on charging load analysed in the report were battery capacity, daily
driving distance and oﬀ-peak price duration. The main ﬁndings of interest were that (1) EVs

that research is needed on 320 mile range BEVs and how much infrastructure is needed to
support the roll out of PEVs [45].

Smart charging services
In order to exploit EV load ﬂexibility, N. Daina et al. have investigated smart charging ﬂexibility
and proposed a random utility model for EV drivers’ activity-travel scheduling and charging
choices.
Data from two discrete choice experiments are implemented to the model, where
EV drivers evaluate charging options based on energy in the battery after charging, eﬀective
charging time and charging cost. The study found that the drivers prefer higher range levels
and that the majority prefer keeping the vehicle under charge when they are at home, as long
as charging does not induce delays. Only 40 % prefer charging as fast as possible [46].

Driving requirements and EV availability
Based on survey data collected in Denmark, Q. Wu et al. investigated driving requirements and
EV availability for grid services. Data from the Danish National Transport Survey was used to
ﬁnd driving distances and driving time periods. Q. Wu et al. found that the driving pattern
information needed in an EV integration study were initial SOC for the ﬁrst trip, energy used
per km, starting/ending time for the speciﬁc trip and location/destination. It was stated in
the report that energy used per km for a home passenger car is between 120 Wh/km and 180
Wh/km. The results showed that the average driving distance in Denmark was 29.48 km and
75 % of cars drive less than or equal to 40 km per day. The lowest percentile of EV availability
was 94.35 %, considering only driving time periods as the unavailable time periods. Moreover,
availability on weekends and holidays was higher than weekdays.
Q. Wu emphasised that
the driving pattern analysis could be improved by using real EV driving data and that when
investigating utilisation of EVs for providing regulation power, data of quarter time periods
should be used. For optimal EV charging studies in the energy power market, EV availability
data of one hour time period is needed [41].
In order to investigate EVs in the ﬂexible ramp market, Zhang and Kezunovic found it necessary
to estimate the power capacity of EVs based on stochastic driving behaviour. They made a
model of aggregated power capacity founded on EV availability, energy for charging/discharging
(how much energy the EV possesses) and EV charging/discharging power (determined by energy
and time duration).
Like Q. Wu et al. [41], Zhang and Keznuvic only considered the EVs
as unavailable during driving time periods.
Energy for charging/discharging was based on
information obtained from a survey conducted among EV drivers. This included accessibility
to charging infrastructure, willingness to plug the EV to the grid, time needed for EVs to be
fully charged, frequency that EV participates in the market and whether the user chooses to
leave in the middle of charging or after the EV is fully charged [48].

3.4
EV ﬂexibility
C. Develder et al. have conducted a quantitative analysis of ﬂexibility as demand response based
on two real-world data sets. The data sets included a charging at home ﬁeld trial in Flanders,
Belgium, and a large-scale EV public charging pole in the Netherlands. Demand response and
ﬂexibility were determined by the number of connected EVs, following arrival and departure
time, and how much charging could be shifted in time.
It was found that the charge near
home behaviour mainly provides ﬂexibility during night time, for around 15 hours. The charge
near work cluster has arrivals in the morning and departures late afternoon which results in
ﬂexibility for about 9 hours during daytime. The last cluster, park to charge, had short sojourn
times (total temporary stay connected to EVSE) around 3 hours and limited ﬂexibility [49].
Another research aiming at quantifying and analysing EV ﬂexibility is a study of data from
public charging infrastructure in the Netherlands. N. Sadeghianpourhamami et al. characterised
EV charging behaviours into three clusters; charge near home, charge near work and park to
charge, identiﬁed by arrival and departure times. Inﬂuence of weekdays and seasonal changes
were observed on arrival, sojourn and idle times (unproductive time while connected to EVSE).
Further, to quantitatively analyse the amount and duration of ﬂexibility during a day, measures
and an algorithm were proposed. Charging ﬂexibility characteristics were identiﬁed as amount,
time and duration of availability. The study found that the park to charge cluster, with the
largest number of sessions, had scattered arrivals and idle times around 48 min. The second
largest cluster, charge near home, oﬀers the longest idle times (10 h) with arrivals during the
evening. Charge near work, the smallest cluster, also has predictable arrival times (around
6-9 am) and idle times of 5 h 30 min.
N. Sadeghianpourhamami et al.
concluded that in
order to provide ﬂexibility to ﬁll the afternoon valley, charge near work should be targeted. To
further stimulate longer ﬂexibility duration to ﬁll this valley, the park to charge cluster should
be targeted. By targeting the charge near home cluster, ﬂexibility to ﬁll the night valley is
provided [50].

I. Pavic et al. investigated the impact of coordinated EV charging on ﬂexibility potential in

Personal vehicles resemble typical driving behaviour as

In a literature study by K. Knezovic et al., EV ﬂexibility service has been investigated and

attributes characterising ﬂexibility service: direction of power ﬂow, power capacity of charging
equipment and EVs, starting time, duration and location. K. Knezovic et al. observed that the
identiﬁed regulatory and policy barriers presented a greater challenge than the technology and
infrastructure due to large diﬀerences of distribution systems and regulatory frameworks across
Europe [52].
In an agent-based model, developed by Liu et al., the real amount of electricity that vehicles
can discharge to the grid was estimated. The study investigated the inﬂuence of battery size
and penetration rate of EVs on the potential capacity for V2G. Three charging patterns were
implemented in a model: uncontrolled charging, rapidly charging and V2G-smart charging.
A probabilistic algorithm was used to simulate charging decisions. Liu et al. found that the
increase of battery capacity lead to a minor increase of V2G potential, while an increase in
penetration rate highly aﬀected the increase in V2G potential capacity. The study also found
that to supply electricity to the grid, EVs must charge more during the valley period [53].

Multiple studies have been performed to uncover parameters that deﬁne and/or inﬂuence ﬂex-

4 | Charging load proﬁle
The purpose of this section is to deﬁne and describe factors that determine charging load proﬁles
and charging ﬂexibility. First, parameters used to describe charging load proﬁles, which in this
study are deﬁned by charging patterns, are characterized. Second, charging ﬂexibility is deﬁned
and factors found to have a large inﬂuence on ﬂexibility are identiﬁed. In addition, the section
describes how mean and standard deviations can be applied when analysing real data.
4.1
Charging patterns
To uncover the potential charging ﬂexibility of EVs, it is of interest to determine charging load
proﬁles that describe the requirements of EV owners. Other studies who have deﬁned charging
load proﬁles, use daily travel activities to determine a pattern. Travel activities were based
on start/end time, trip distance (energy use per trip) and start/end location [41,43,47]. Some
studies found that charging proﬁles were determined by start/end time of charge and energy
drawn per session [44,46]. In this study charging patterns are used to deﬁne individual charging
load proﬁles. Real data registering unique plug-in sessions can reveal tendencies of today’s EV
owners. The parameters used to determine individual charging load proﬁles for the purpose of
this study are as follows:
• Charging session; frequency and duration
• Energy charged per session
Charging session is deﬁned by frequency of plug-ins and duration of the plug-in each time a
user connects their EV to an EVSE. Frequency is derived from EV arrival, registered during
plug-in to the EVSE. Duration is calculated from the timestamps at plug-in and plug-out. One
can also determine the speciﬁc duration of charging if such timestamps are accessible. These
parameters describe how often the EV is connected to the EVSE and for how long it needs to
be charged, thus indicating available periods where the EV could be used for grid support.
Energy charged per session (Echarge) correlates with how much energy is required by the user.
Knowing the EV’s energy use (Euse), distance travelled in between charging sessions (D) can
be estimated using Equation 4.1. One can also calculate energy needed to cover the range
requirements of the user, thus how much energy the user requires when leaving the EVSE.
Energy charged per session can contribute to determine a user charging pattern and thereby
predict individual energy needs.

EV ﬂexibility for demand response has been determined by several factors like SOC when the

Some studies investigating EV ﬂexibility, found charging ﬂexibility to be identiﬁed by amount,
time and duration of availability, where amount implies the quantity of energy which can be
shifted. Time suggests the hour of a day the EV is connected to an EVSE and duration means
for how long [46, 50]. Studies have found these factors to be inﬂuenced by several technical
and user deﬁned parameters, such as battery size, charge speed, charge availability, as well
as temperature, driving behaviour and user preferences, as described in Table 3.1 section 3.

such as weather logging and meticulous user surveys, and are therefore considered outside the
scope of this study. Parameters deﬁning charging ﬂexibility are in this study described as:
• Charge time (Tcharge)
• State of charge (SOC)

available time in the afternoon/night but could increase potential grid services during working
hours. Fast public chargers could potentially reduce weekly sessions and EV availability for
grid support. This is because users might be able to fully charge their battery while running
errands or grocery shopping, and would no longer have incentives to connect their EV to the
EVSE during afternoon and night. This could decrease availability for ﬂexibility services both
during working hours and free time. As the data for this study is collected from residential
chargers, only at home charging will be evaluated in the remainder of the paper.

5.1
GridMotion and customer description
GridMotion is a project evaluating possible savings achieved by real-world EV users through the
implementation of smart charging and discharging strategies. The project is located in France
and operates in the timeline of 2017-2020. The main objective is to investigate to what extent
an electric driver’s electricity bill can be reduced without aﬀecting the transportation use by
shifting charging times to periods with lower electricity prices. Additionally, further savings
could be achieved by providing grid balancing services through a V2G system. GridMotion
oﬀers several services, the main ones being EV charge/discharge patterns, grid balancing both
bidirectional and unidirectional, demand charge and stacking of services and vehicles [55]. This
study will provide insight to which data is recorded and current customers’ charging patterns,
as well as develop a futuristic outlook of availability for V2G services assuming technology
development.
The EV ﬂeet consists of 50 "smart" unidirectional chargers (Nuvve PowerPorts) installed in
residential homes around France, and 15 V2G ﬂeet stations installed in the Paris area. When
an EV is connected, the charger communicates data such as timestamp of EV plug-in/plug-out,
power provided, power requested and power ﬂow. In addition, a Kuantic box dependant on
WiFi to store and transfer data is installed to measure SOC of the EV batteries. The "smart"
unidirectional chargers operate with a restricted charging timeline. Provided no alterations
have been made by the user, charging starts at 2 am and ends at 6 am, when the electricity
price is expected to be at its lowest. If the customer wishes to charge the EV at another time it
is possible to manually adjust charge time and requested an energy amount. All customers are
encouraged to connect their vehicle to the resident charging station whenever possible. Thus,

5.3.1
Error assessment and handling
Generally, when logging such large data sets, some errors might occur. It is important to be
aware of errors as they might inﬂuence the results in numerous ways. Lack of data might lead
to an incomplete plug-in session, which could lead to removal of the entire session. Additional
data, caused by equipment tests or users testing the EV connection, may lead to additional
plug-in sessions included in the data analysis and cause misleading results. This subsection
explains how errors in the data ﬁle are detected and handled. All data have been manually
checked in Excel to obtain a deeper understanding of how the data acts and is communicated.
A limitation of this process is the risk of human mistakes when assessing and handling errors.
The method is, on the other hand, determined to be the most accurate approach due to the
author’s limited experience in programming languages such as MATLAB.
The ﬁrst error detected in the data is that SOC, stored under "EnergyStored", is not always
updated for all EVs. By default, this column displays a constant 10 kWh, with a corresponding
33
==PAGE:52==
5. Data
value in "EnergyCapacity" of 20 kWh which is above the nominal energy capacity for a Peugeot
iOn. An example of this can be seen in Table 5.3 (iii.) Error data. For this reason, power ﬂow
to the EV, registered in the column "PowerFlow", will be used to calculated energy charged
per session. It should be noticed that when an EV is connected but not charging, "PowerFlow"
displays a value of ~47 W. This is not actual power obtained by the car, supplied by the grid
or the chargers’ own consumption, but a measuring error by the equipment.
The second error observed are momentary large peaks of power ﬂow, occurring outside standard
charging hours, during several sessions. This is explained by equipment tests performed by the
internal system of the EV and communication failure with the EVSE. Table 5.3 (iii.) Error
data displays an example of this under "PowerFlow". These errors have simply been removed
from the data. A third error found and similarly handled, is duplicated data that have been
observed and removed from the data ﬁle.
Another error detected is after separating the sessions into arrival (plug-in) and departure
(plug-out), some sessions are further split into multiple sessions.
This is because the data
measurements are logged with longer time intervals than the 10 minutes set to separate the
sessions. One of the reasons for the infrequent data logging could be WiFi disconnection or
temporary equipment failure. Such errors have been corrected by merging the split session into
one session.
A few "oﬀ-pattern" measurements, such as sessions occurring outside of commuting driving
patterns, have been observed lasting for about 30-60 minutes. Some of these have corresponding
power ﬂows indicating charging and it is concluded that these are due to the user manually
initiating the session. Others, however, are not subject to manual charging but are nevertheless
considered being real data, assuming the user follows the request by GridMotion to connect the
EV to the EVSE whenever possible.
There have also been a few measurements reporting EV sessions lasting for just a few minutes
or seconds during mid-day. Moreover, these measurements do not involve any power ﬂow that
could indicate that the user intended to charge the EV. Such data are assumed to be caused by
equipment tests and users testing the EVSE connection, and are thus removed from the data
ﬁle.

5.4.1
Method
In order to determine ﬂeet behaviour and charging patterns, an exploratory study has been
conducted. As described in section 4, session frequency and duration, as well as energy charged
per session are parameters used to determine a charging load proﬁle. Based on data from six
EVs in a six week period, average arrival time, departure time and duration of sessions, as well
as standard deviations, are established. Energy charged per session and available time after
charging are calculated in order to investigate EV availability for ﬂexibility services. Addition-

battery capacity can be while still being able to reach full capacity during a plug-in session.

5.4.3
Limitations
Every study has its confounding variables and limitations, and it is important to seek out
potential sources of bias. Bias can be deﬁned as any tendency which prevents unprejudiced
consideration of a question. Pannucci and Wilkins state that in research, bias occurs when
"systematic error [is] introduced into sampling or testing by selecting or encouraging one out-
come or answer over others" [57]. Potential sources of bias have been identiﬁed for several stages
of this project, from designing the "smart" charging system to interpret the received data.
One potential bias in the data is caused by the "smart" charging timeline. Aiming at reducing
customers’ electricity bills, charging is scheduled during a four hour period at night time, where
electricity prices are expected to be at its lowest. This could result in restricting the energy
provided to the EV, not charging the battery to its full capacity during a session. Consequently,
GridMotion customers might need to charge their EV more often than they otherwise would if
their battery had been fully charged each session. In eﬀect, the result might be more frequent
plug-ins. This will be reviewed by comparing the exploratory study to the base scenario output.
Another potential bias of the study is induced by GridMotion’s encouragement to its customers
to connect their EV to the EVSE whenever possible. Assuming the EV is used for commuting,
daily energy consumption could become easy to foretell and one might aspire to charge the EV

plug-ins than the user would otherwise plan for in their daily routines.
Furthermore, despite eﬀorts to reduce the inﬂuence of expectations and predictions during the

6 | Methods and model simulations
In this study, the eﬀect of technology development on charging patterns, and the inﬂuence on
availability, is investigated through simulation. The purpose is to understand charging ﬂexibility
that can be expected based on changes in EV charging behaviour. In order to induce changes
in user behaviour, a charging model is created using input plug-in data from real EVs in the
simulations. Three diﬀerent scenarios will be created and compared to a base scenario developed
from an exploratory study of real data. The section begins with a description of the model and
assumptions made under its construction. After this follows a description of the scenarios under
investigation along with explanations of speciﬁc input values used in the simulations.
6.1
Model description
This subsection includes a description of the model and an elaboration of the assumptions made
in the modelling process. The model is created in MATLAB, a high-performance language for
technical computing [58]. It simulates the charging need of commuting EVs which are charged
using EVSE typical for residential chargers. Model input is the speciﬁc EV chosen amongst
the EV owners that are a part of the GridMotion project. Each EV has unique data speciﬁed
per week, derived in the exploratory study, which can be found in Table B.1, Appendix B.1.
Input data to the model are an estimation of energy used per day and average session duration.
It is assumed that the EV users’ energy consumption remain unchanged despite technology
development in battery capacity and charging power.
The majority of the users’ days are
expected to be predictable, with continuous daily routines, and thus daily driving distance
remains the same. Furthermore, the model is based on the EV users being rational, charging
their EV as rare as possible and only when needed. Therefore, the simulation output represents
the theoretically lowest possible plug-in frequency. This might give deviating results compared
to the exploratory study output, where GridMotion encouraged their customers to plug-in
as often as possible.
However, this study takes a conservative approach to ﬁnd the lowest
availability that can be expected of the EVs.
Speciﬁc EV parameters deﬁned at the beginning of each simulation can be found in Table 6.1
along with descriptions and units. In the model, the EV is charged at constant power (Pcharge),
assuming constant current and voltage. In practice, both current and voltage can ﬂuctuate and
result in power output from the charger deviating from nominal power. This has been seen in
the data provided by GridMotion, but will not be implemented in the model created in this
study. Once nominal battery capacity (Qbattery,cap) is determined in each scenario, all EVs are

range of the battery can reduce cycling aging and expand battery lifetime [28–30] While this is
mostly discussed for large battery EVs, small battery EVs would also be aﬀected by cycle aging
as they might be charged more often due to lower theoretical range. It is expected that this
upper limit on capacity will become standard practice for all EVs and is therefore employed in
all scenarios simulated in the model.

in frequency and charge time that can be expected in the various scenarios. First, a general
description and analysis of the simulation output are presented. Extensive elaboration of the

in sessions for Base scenario are 77, while in the exploratory study there were 123 plug-in
sessions. The deviation could be caused by several reasons. First, the model is based on the
EV users being rational, charging their EV as rare as possible and only when needed. It is a
simulation of the theoretically lowest plug-in frequency that can occur with the input energy
consumption. In the real data, investigated in the exploratory study, GridMotion customers
are on the other hand encouraged to plug-in as often as possible. This could have caused some
additional plug-in sessions that would otherwise not take place. Second, another reason that
could aﬀect the frequency deviation is individual user behaviour captured in the exploratory
study. The exploratory study shows a standard deviation of 4.1 in plug-in frequency, which
might be explained by practical reason. The EV users might not drive an equal distance every
day and could have varied driving patterns.
Some may not use the EV every day or may
expect a longer trip the following day. In contrast, the model has assumed that the owners
operate their EV every day and have equal energy consumption making their charging pattern
predictable. Third, psychological factors could also be an argument for the frequent plug-ins in
the exploratory study. "Range anxiety" might cause the EV owners to charge their EV more
often due to the fear that the vehicle has insuﬃcient range to reach its next destination. The
simulation model is unable to capture such psychological aspects of individual users on a daily
basis but tries to impose it by varying minimum SOC. While minimum SOC triggering a plug-in
session is constantly 0.45 for Base scenario, the real plug-in data contains no information on
this. It could be higher or lower based on the individual user, which could also cause deviation
between the session occurrence. Fourth and last, in the model, initial SOC is set to 1 and is
equal in all scenarios. This means the EV owners simulated in the model have a full battery
capacity when the simulation period starts. The real EV owners, on the other hand, might
already have used their vehicle before the six week period where the data was obtained. Thus,
they could have had a lower initial SOC at the beginning of the six week period, which might
result in a higher plug-in frequency. This could also have an eﬀect on total energy charged
during the six week period, and would indicate deviating starting conditions between the real
EV users and the EVs simulated in the model. However, the energy charged during the six
weeks is quite similar. From the exploratory study, total energy charged for all six EVs in the
six week period was 691 kWh, while in Base scenario it was 687 kWh, listed in Table C.4 in
Appendix C. This demonstrates that initial SOC at the period start was quite similar.

Availability
Figure 7.7 presents and compares availability of the scenarios to what is considered zero- and
one-hundred percent availability. Zero availability portray no source that can provide ﬂexibility
services to the grid. 100 % availability suggests a grid-connected storage system, such as Lead-
acid batteries or Li-ion batteries, which purpose is to provide ﬂexibility services to the grid.
The time needed to recharge the storage system is not considered unavailable time as these are
ﬂexible periods which can be charged outside peak hours [61, 62]. Thus, as the batteries are
ﬂexible and always connected to the grid, a grid-connected storage system is 100 % available.
As can be seen, Scenarios 1 and 3 have zero and close to zero availability. Despite these being
unfavourable indicators for grid services, other advantages of the large battert EVs should not
be overlooked. First, lower emissions shifting the transport sector from a fossil-driven to an
electricity-driven sector, are a clear advantage of the electrically fuelled cars. If the energy
provided to the EV is supplied by renewable energy, even further emission reduction can be
obtained [17].
Second, the large battery capacities providing the users with longer driving
ranges are considered beneﬁcial for the user. The 1.1 % availability obtained in Scenario 3,
simulating large battery capacity and high charge power, amounts to about 2 hours a week.
Assuming a high EV market penetration with battery capacities of these sizes, one might expect
a noticeable availability assuming plug-in sessions occurs steadily throughout the week.
The scenarios with the highest availability, Base scenario and Scenario 2, are only a tenth of
the availability obtained with a grid-connected storage system. However, providing ﬂexibility
services to the grid using EVs comes with advantages. Some studies have found that grid-
connected storage systems are not considered economically feasible, even with TOU (Time of
use) support tariﬀs [61]. Others concluded that the present cost of battery storage would have
to be signiﬁcantly reduced to make the system feasible for investment [62]. With the trending
increase of EVs on the market, exploiting the existing and future availability could save costs
both for the customers and grid operators [31].
Despite the reference scenario, simulating small battery capacities supplied with low charge
power, having a signiﬁcant amount of availability, there are challenges to replace commercial
ICE vehicles with EVs. First, charge time is considered too long compared to the minutes
it takes to fuel a gasoline driven car.
Second, charge time combined with the range limit
determined by battery capacity gives ICE vehicles a clear advantage in long distance driving.
High power chargers, simulated in Scenario 2, could help reduce range anxiety and make EVs an
attractive option for transportation [59,60]. With an average charge time of about half an hour
as in Scenario 2.2, and anticipating increasing charge locations, EVs might also be competitive
in long distance driving. Furthermore, increasing battery capacities could reduce range anxiety
on long trips.

8 | Conclusion
Infrequent power output from RE and rising power demand from EVs present challenges to
the grid operators. However, the continuous EV penetration also contributes to the possibility
of EVs supporting the grid. Coupling the two sectors by using V2G services has been found
to reduce grid impact and could beneﬁt both grid operators and EV users.
An important
prerequisite is EV availability, in which grid operators can freely exploit the EV while it is
connected to an EVSE and charging is not requested by the user.
This is determined by
user plug-in patterns and aﬀected by several parameters such as battery capacity and charge
power. Continuous technology development could have an eﬀect on charging patterns, thus
future availability for V2G services might change. Few studies have been exploring time periods
available using real data and there is a lack of studies investigating future availability considering
trends in technology development. Therefore, this study has investigated the inﬂuence of large
battery EVs and increasing charge power on plug-in patterns, and the impact on availability.
The project consists of two main parts. First, an exploratory study of data from EVs in France
was conducted to determine plug-in patterns and user requirements. This was used to estimate
daily energy consumption for each individual EV and typical plug-in hours. The second part
explores the eﬀect on plug-in patterns when increasing battery capacity and charge power. For
this purpose, a model simulating charging events was created. This was done with an objective
to simulate actual user habits and create realistic model results. Four scenarios were separately
simulated in the model to mimic current and possible future plug-in patterns. These scenarios
were: (1) Base scenario which represented current EV technology, (2) Scenario 1 investigating
the eﬀect of increasing battery capacity, (3) Scenario 2 which studied the inﬂuence of increasing
charge power and (4) Scenario 3, a combination of Scenario 1 and 2, examining the eﬀect of
both increasing battery capacity and charge power. The output from the model revealed the
theoretically lowest possible plug-in frequency of the EV users and possible plug-in patterns.
Finally, the impact on plug-in patterns and grid availability was analysed.
The ﬁrst part of the study, analysing real EV data from France, revealed that the customers
tend to follow typical commuter plug-in patterns. Some of the key numbers obtained in the
exploratory study were: (i) average plug-in frequency per EV per week (3.3 sessions), (ii) average
session duration (11 hours), (iii) average energy charged per session (6 kWh), (iv) average arrival
time (around 7 pm) and (v) average departure time (around 6 am). The signiﬁcant standard
deviation found for the parameters revealed that the EV users are quite unique. They have
diﬀerent needs and travel patterns, indicated by the varying energy consumption and plug-in
frequency.

EV connected to the EVSE from afternoon to the following morning. Therefore, assuming a
high penetration of EVs, grid operators could expect signiﬁcant availability from EV owners
with access to residential chargers during afternoon and night, depending on charge power and
battery capacity.
In the second part of the study, the inﬂuence of large battery EVs and high charge power on plug-
in patterns, and the impact on availability, were investigated. The model simulations revealed
that increasing battery capacity results in decreasing plug-in frequency. This was because EV
users were able to drive further before charging was needed. Furthermore, increasing charge
power cause, in a lower degree, reduction in plug-in frequency. For the most part, this was due
to lower minimum SOC, initiating a plug-in session, which was set to simulate user response
to high power chargers. Estimations of availability for each scenario revealed the worst-case
scenario would be large battery EVs charged with low power. The customers had low plug-in
frequency and charging time took up the entire session duration, leaving no available time for
grid support. Best future outlook, in terms of availability, was small battery EVs charged with
high power EVSE. While high charge power induced a slightly lower plug-in frequency (80 %
compared to the reference scenario), it signiﬁcantly reduced the charge time. Thus, availability
was a little higher compared to small battery EVs charged with low power.
The purpose of this thesis was to explore EV availability for V2G services that can be expected
based on near and future outlooks of technology development. Although capacity usable for
V2G services was not investigated, other studies found penetration rate to be more eﬀective
to increase the capacity available for grid services than increasing EV battery capacity.
If
customers are presented with EV battery capacities able to meet their range requirements and
suﬃcient charge power to reduce charge time and lower the range anxiety, it might result in
increasing EV penetration. Furthermore, giving customers incentives to provide available EVs
and easy access to charging stations, signiﬁcant availability might be expected for V2G services.
8.1
Future work
This project has demonstrated diﬀerent eﬀects of battery and charging technology development
on plug-in patterns and availability.
Future work may include availability studies of other
locations such as workplace and public chargers. This might reveal a more distributed possibility
for V2G services during the day.
As this is a technical simulation and analysis, revealing
possibilities for the grid operator, the study might be widened by evaluating economic and
environmental beneﬁts. Investigating savings that can be obtained by expanding availability
for V2G services could provide EV owners with incentives to increase plug-in frequency.

8. Conclusion
The model was built simplistic and capture the theoretically lowest plug-in frequency needed.
Possible next steps may include further improving the accuracy of the model.
The EVs in
the model are charged at constant power, while in real life voltage and current ﬂuctuation
produce a varying power output. Therefore, to mimic real life charging, power variation could
be implemented. While general user response has been accounted for by varying minimum SOC,
the model is not able to capture individual user preferences and response to the technology
changes. Furthermore, only six EVs are simulated in the model. Thus, each EV owner has a
large inﬂuence on the result. Larger sample size in the model, with individual user responses,
could give normalized outputs representative for a wider user group. Also, the current model
assumes all customers have the same battery capacity and access to the same charge power.
In the future, it might be expected that users have diﬀerent EV capacities and charge powers,
which could be simulated in the model. This might reveal even distributed plug-in frequency
and availability for V2G services.
Another interesting extension could be to perform a sensitivity analysis of the model output.
A sensitivity analysis can determine how diﬀerences in the model input aﬀect the output.
Although the main parameters, such as battery capacity and charge speed, have been varied,
other inputs such as min./max. and initial SOC could result in divergent model output.

% --- OUTPUT PARAMETERS ---

% --- MODEL LOOP --- 
for d = 1:Period                              % Set simulation period 
     
    if (1<=d) && (d<=7)                % Energy usage and duration based on weekly values

        Energy_use_day = Sig(8,3);

        Energy_use_day = Sig(8,4);

D | Project plan
At project start, a project plan was created to identify milestones and activities and to estimate
the time consumption of the tasks at hand. A Gantt chart was created which described the
project schedule and is presented as a bar chart in Figure D.1. The vertical axis lists the tasks
that have been performed and the horizontal axis indicates the time intervals of the tasks. The
duration of the tasks is illustrated by the width of the horizontal bars in the graph, including
start and ﬁnish dates of the elements.
Furthermore, the tasks were separated into project
phases, illustrated with colours. Equal colours represent that the tasks are in the same phase.
In general, most tasks within the same phase were not performed individually and completed
before the next task was started. Instead, most tasks within a phase were performed at the same
time as the others, as most aﬀected each other. For instance, "State of the art" and "Deﬁning
parameters for analysis" was based on both theoretical background and ﬁndings in the study
of prior research, which again aﬀected what was needed to be described in the theoretical
background section.
Overall, the project schedule was upheld but subject to a few delays. "Data analysis" took
a while longer than expected.
It was caused by inexperience in MATLAB and unforeseen
challenges when handling the data ﬁle from GridMotion. This shifted the project plan a bit but
did not cause any major complications in the plan. "Aggregated results to large size battery
and scenarios" was actually the deﬁnition of the scenarios and the construction of the model
in MATLAB. This part took a shorter time than expected and thus the delay in the project
plan was reduced. The conclusion was written a week after the planned time period due to the
delay. However, there was safety time implemented in the plan to handle unforeseen delays so
this did not cause any complications. An overview of the noticeable schedule delays and task
revision is illustrated in Figure D.2 with red marks and writing.
