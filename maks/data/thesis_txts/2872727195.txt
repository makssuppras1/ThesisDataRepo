II. Abstract 
This master’s thesis has investigated, analysed, and discussed the effects of an air purifier on bedroom 
indoor environments – focusing on airborne particles – and the potential influence on sleep quality.  
An experiment was conducted involving 12 subjects (aged 19-56) and their bedrooms during a two-
week measuring period. The experiments were conducted from February to April 2025 in Denmark. 
An air purifier (Philips series 3000i) with a HEPA and active carbon filter was active for one week of 
the two-week experiment to investigate the possible improvement of the indoor environment and 
sleep quality. The indoor environment of the bedrooms was measured objectively by an ATMOcube 
and assessed subjectively via three surveys distributed during the measuring period. The quality of 
sleep was objectively measured by a Fitbit watch and assessed subjectively via the surveys. Further-
more, half of the subjects wore a secondary sleep-tracking device, the Muse S headband. However, 
comparisons conducted between Fitbit and Muse indicated a mismatch in data, potentially due to the 
different measuring methods. Consequently, Fitbit was used as the primary sleep-tracking device.  
A literature review was conducted on the correlation between the indoor environment (with a focus 
on airborne particles) and sleep quality. The literature review revealed that the level of PM2.5 posi-
tively correlated with both the time awake during sleep and light sleep. In addition, PM2.5 was found 
to negatively correlate with both REM and deep sleep. Furthermore, the review of literature suggested 
a negative correlation between the perceived sleep quality and the number of awakenings. 
 
Finally, it was concluded that the air purifier significantly removed PM2.5 from the bedroom indoor 
environment (𝑝= 0.001). Despite the limited sample size, a significant reduction in formaldehyde 
(CH2O) was also found with air purification (𝑝= 0.009). Additionally, the subjectively evaluated 
GSQS indicated statistically significant improvements (𝑝𝑝𝑎𝑖𝑟𝑒𝑑 𝑡−𝑡𝑒𝑠𝑡= 0.046) and borderline sig-
nificant improvements (𝑝𝑊𝑖𝑙𝑐𝑜𝑥𝑜𝑛= 0.057) in the subjects’ perceived quality of sleep. No other sig-
nificant effects were statistically found in the remaining investigated indoor environmental or sleep 
quality parameters.  
Although the findings of this study may serve as an initial investigation, the small sample size is 
important to acknowledge, since it may affect the representativeness of the study. Consequently, fur-
ther research and studies should be conducted on this topic.  
 
Keywords: indoor environment, air quality, particulate matter, PM2.5, air purification, sleep quality.

III. Resumé 
Dette kandidatspeciale har undersøgt, analyseret og diskuteret effekten af en luftrenser på indeklimaet 
i soveværelser – med fokus på luftbårne partikler – og den potentielle indflydelse på søvnkvalitet. 
Et eksperiment, der involverede 12 forsøgspersoner (i alderen 19-56 år) samt deres soveværelser i 
løbet af en to-ugers måleperiode, blev udført. Eksperimenterne blev udført fra februar til april 2025 i 
Danmark. En luftrenser (Philips serie 3000i) med HEPA- og aktivt kulfitler var aktiv i én uge af 
eksperimentet for at undersøge den mulige forbedring af indeklima og søvnkvalitet. Indeklimaet i 
soveværelserne blev målt objektivt med en ATMOcube og vurderet subjektivt via tre spørgeskemaer, 
som blev fordelt i løbet af måleperioden. Søvnkvaliteten blev målt objektivt med et Fitbit-ur og vur-
deret subjektivt via spørgeskemaerne. Derudover bar halvdelen af forsøgspersonerne en sekundær 
søvnmonitoreringsenhed, Muse S-pandebåndet. Sammenligninger mellem Fitbit og Muse viste dog 
uoverensstemmelser mellem data, hvilket muligvis skyldtes de forskellige målemetoder. Derfor blev 
Fitbit brugt som den primære søvnmonitoreringsenhed.  
Et litteraturstudie, der undersøgte sammenhængen mellem indeklima (med fokus på luftbårne partik-
ler) og søvnkvalitet, blev foretaget. Litteraturstudiet viste, at niveauet af PM2.5 korrelerede positivt 
med tiden, man var vågen under søvn, samt den lette søvn. Desuden korrelerede PM2.5 negativt med 
både REM-søvn og dyb søvn. Ydermere viste litteraturstudiet en negativ sammenhæng mellem den 
oplevede søvnkvalitet og antallet af opvågninger.  
 
Endeligt blev det konkluderet, at luftrenseren signifikant fjernede PM2.5 fra soveværelsets indeklima 
(𝑝= 0.001). På trods af det begrænsede datasæt blev der også fundet en signifikant reduktion af 
formaldehyd (CH2O) med luftrensning (𝑝= 0.009). Derudover indikerede den subjektivt evaluerede 
GSQS statistisk signifikante forbedringer (𝑝𝑝𝑎𝑖𝑟𝑒𝑑 𝑡−𝑡𝑒𝑠𝑡= 0.046) og forbedringer, der var tæt på 
signifikante (𝑝𝑊𝑖𝑙𝑐𝑜𝑥𝑜𝑛= 0.057) af forsøgspersonernes opfattede søvnkvalitet. Der blev ikke fundet 
andre statistisk signifikante forskelle i de resterende undersøgte indeklima- og søvnkvalitetspara-
metre. 
Selvom resultaterne af denne undersøgelse kan anvendes som en indledende undersøgelse, er det 
vigtigt at anerkende det begrænsede datasæt, da repræsentativiteten af undersøgelsen kan påvirkes. 
Derfor bør yderligere undersøgelser og forskning foretages om dette emne.  
 
Nøgleord: indeklima, luftkvalitet, partikler, PM2.5, luftrensning, søvnkvalitet.

1 Introduction 
Approximately one-third of a human life is spent sleeping. Hence, ensuring a healthy bedroom envi-
ronment is fundamental, as it affects sleep quality, health, and well-being considerably. Previous stud-
ies have indicated that an inadequate indoor environment may lead to poor sleep quality [1], [2], [3], 
[4]. Additionally, poor sleep may lead to several health issues – both physical and mental. 
Since a considerable proportion of buildings in Denmark are not equipped with a mechanical venti-
lation system in the bedroom, the effect of a versatile air purifier on both the indoor environment and 
sleep quality by removing particles from the indoor air was investigated.  
 
1.1 Hypotheses and Objectives 
This thesis hypothesised that the indoor environment – focusing on airborne particles – would be 
improved when introducing an air purifier by removing particulate matter from the indoor air. Addi-
tionally, the reduction of particulate matter might possibly improve the quality of sleep.  
In accordance with these hypotheses, the primary objectives were: 
- 
Investigate how sleep quality was affected by airborne particles (especially PM2.5) and the 
indoor environment in general.  
- 
Investigate if an air purifier improved the indoor environment. 
- 
Investigate if an air purifier improved sleep quality. 
Further details regarding the hypotheses and objectives will be described in Section 3.1. 
This led to the formulation of the problem statement:  
How is the bedroom indoor environment – especially airborne particles – affected by an air purifier, 
and does it influence the quality of sleep? 
 
This master’s thesis was intentionally delimited to perform a more comprehensive investigation of 
the topic within the timeframe. The investigation focused on the indoor environment primarily due to 
the academic field of my degree at DTU. Additionally, airborne particles (focusing on PM2.5) were 
more thoroughly described to fully investigate the effect of air purification. This focus was relevant, 
since previous studies have shown a correlation between particulate matter and the quality of sleep 
[2], [4], [5], [6]. The quality of sleep was also investigated; however, the medical or biological per-
spectives were limited as my academic background is not within these fields. Nonetheless, the 
==PAGE:13==
Page 13 of 88 
 
analyses and discussions of sleep quality were based on the experiment conducted as well as a review 
of existing literature.  
 
1.2 Methodological Considerations 
To investigate the problem statement, the scientific method was applied, and methodological consid-
erations were addressed. The indoor environment and quality of sleep were measured using Fitbit 
watches (actigraphy principles and photoplethysmography/PPG), Muse S headbands (electroenceph-
alography/EEG), and ATMOcubes (low-cost IAQ sensor). Not every subject wore the Muse headband 
due to limited availability; hence, the data obtained from the Fitbit watches served as the primary 
provider of sleep data. The bedrooms of the subjects were selected based on the use of natural venti-
lation to ensure better comparisons of the effect of an air purifier on the indoor environment. Addi-
tionally, the experiments took place in the colder months in Denmark and were not conducted simul-
taneously due to the limitation of measuring equipment; however, only separated by a minimal time 
difference. Furthermore, data from the experiment was collected via three surveys, and statistical 
methods were used to analyse the obtained data. In addition, a literature review of existing studies 
was used to obtain detailed knowledge about the topic and to provide a foundation for comparison 
and discussion. Nevertheless, there were some limitations regarding the experiment due to a rather 
small sample size, which will be addressed in Section 3.  
 
An experimental protocol for when to exclude data was formulated. If the experimental data from 
one of the measuring equipment experienced failure, the impacted data were excluded. If a subject 
reported being ill during the measurements, the sleep data were excluded. Additionally, weekend 
measurements were excluded due to possible changes in sleep patterns, daily routines, and behaviour. 
Further methodology of the thesis will be presented in Section 3. 
 
1.3 Structure of Thesis 
This section outlines the structure of this thesis. Literature will be reviewed in Section 2 to obtain 
theoretical knowledge about the topic and previous studies. The methodology, briefly addressed ear-
lier, will be further evaluated in Section 3. Additionally, the results and associated analyses will be 
presented in Section 4. The data and knowledge obtained throughout this study will be discussed in 
Section 5. Finally, the conclusions of this thesis will be presented in Section 6.

2 Literature Review on the Impact of Indoor Environment 
on Sleep Quality 
This literature review aims to identify current literature and studies regarding the impact of the indoor 
environment on sleep quality. Consequently, the literature review is divided into “Sleep” and “Indoor 
environment”.  
In subsequent sections, the existing studies will be evaluated and compared to the experiment con-
ducted. The comparison will provide a broader understanding of possible tendencies and inconsist-
encies that will be analysed and discussed in this thesis.  
 
2.1 Sleep 
Sleep is a fundamental part of life as the body restores energy and recovers – both physically and 
mentally [2], [3], [7]. Sleep affects health, the immune system, and memory, indicating the importance 
of this topic. Furthermore, the quality of sleep has a significant impact on learning ability and next-
day performance [2], [4], [8].  
The lack of sleep or impaired sleep can lead to severe health issues such as cardiovascular problems, 
high blood pressure, and depression [2], [7], [8]. In addition, external factors such as caffeine con-
sumption, medical conditions, exposure to electronic devices, professional obligations, etc., can in-
fluence the quality of sleep. Despite the evidenced impact on sleep quality, the bedroom indoor envi-
ronment is often neglected [3]. 
 
2.1.1 Sleep Stages  
A sleep cycle can be divided into two main stages: REM (rapid eye movement) and NREM (non-
REM), which is further divided into N1, N2, and N3 [4], [9]. One sleep cycle consists of REM and 
NREM (N1, N2, N3) sleep, and it is visualised in Figure 1.  
==PAGE:15==
Page 15 of 88 
 
 
Figure 1: One sleep cycle. 
The sleep cycle repeats itself approximately every 90 minutes throughout the night. The percentage 
of each sleep stage in a sleep cycle will vary throughout the night. In the first half of the night, deep 
sleep (N3) will account for a larger proportion of the cycle compared to the second half of the night. 
On the contrary, REM sleep will account for a larger proportion in the second half of the night com-
pared to the first half of the cycle [9].  
When learning or experiencing something new, the brain makes or strengthens neural connections. 
REM sleep is a crucial sleep stage, since it strengthens the important connections in the brain, which 
strengthens memory. In contrast, NREM sleep sorts and discards unnecessary connections to keep 
only the relevant neural connections. Accordingly, both the REM and NREM sleep stages are crucial 
for achieving restorative sleep and strengthening memory. To ensure both REM and NREM sleep, a 
consistent sleep schedule is essential [9]. 
 
REM sleep 
The REM sleep stage is where dreams occur, and the American National Sleep Foundation recom-
mends that this sleep stage accounts for 21-30% of the total sleeping time for adults (18-64 years) 
[10]. This sleep stage is characterised by low-voltage and mixed-frequency brain waves [4]. Muscle 
atonia occurs during this stage, even though the brain is active, the eyes rapidly move, and the respir-
atory rate and pulse vary [2], [4]. Usually, REM sleep occurs 80-100 minutes into the sleep [2].  
 
NREM sleep 
The NREM sleep stage can be divided into N1, N2, and N3. The N1 sleep stage is the lightest form 
of sleep that accounts for around 5% of the total sleep time [4]. The National Sleep Foundation 
==PAGE:16==
Page 16 of 88 
 
recommends that the N1 sleep stage accounts for ≤5% of the total sleeping time [10]. The N2 sleep 
stage is also light sleep, however, deeper than N1. This stage accounts for approximately 45% of the 
total sleep time. The N3 sleep stage is the deepest kind of sleep that accounts for around 25% of the 
total sleep time [4]. The brain activity during this sleep stage has a certain pattern, called delta waves, 
which have lower frequencies [2], [4]. The National Sleep Foundation recommends that the N3 sleep 
stage accounts for 16-20% for adults (18-64 years) [10]. It is particularly important to minimise awak-
enings during this sleep stage, since it could contribute to sleep inertia and temporarily reduced mental 
capacity [4].

stance, indicated by the sleep onset latency (SOL), number of awakenings > 5 minutes during sleep, 
wake after sleep onset (WASO), sleep efficiency (SE), and duration of sleep stages.

sleep quality, as seen in Table 1 [10]. The empty areas in the table indicate that there is no agreement 
regarding a recommendation.

The appropriate sleep duration may vary between individuals, since it depends on the specific person 
and their lifestyle. Thus, most people will feel rested and refreshed in the recommended sleep duration 
ranges mentioned above, as well as maintain a healthy sleep pattern [8].  
The lack of sleep or impaired sleep can lead to several health problems [8]. People lacking sleep may 
experience physical problems such as weight gain, high blood pressure, and an impaired immune 
system [9]. In addition, lack of sleep may cause mental health problems, since it affects mood, con-
centration, and performance [8], [9]. 
 
A study conducted in 50 bedrooms in Shanghai, China, examined the sleep quality during summer 
[2]. In this study, the data obtained from the subjects were divided into groups based on sleep duration: 
above or below 6 hours. Around 36% of the measured nights were less than 6 hours. The study indi-
cated that subjects who slept less than 6 hours were more sensitive to the indoor environment than 
the subjects who slept for more than 6 hours. The more sensitive subjects were more affected by an 
increase in temperature, relative humidity, CO2 concentration, and PM2.5 concentration. In this re-
gard, the study found that a sleep duration below 6 hours led to a decrease in sleep efficiency due to 
an increasing amount of time awake, as well as a decrease in the time in REM and deep sleep. Another 
==PAGE:18==
Page 18 of 88 
 
interesting finding was that with sleep above 6 hours of duration, the light sleep stage increased with 
higher PM2.5 concentrations [2]. This may suggest that the amount of deep sleep decreases when the 
concentration of PM2.5 increases.  
Additionally, the study investigated the Groningen Sleep Quality Scale (GSQS) and the Pittsburgh 
Sleep Quality Index (PSQI), which are subjective evaluations of sleep quality. The study found a 
negative correlation with GSQS and specific objective sleep quality parameters: TST, SE, REM sleep, 
and light sleep [2]. It is important to note that a lower GSQS signifies a better perception of sleep – 
hence, a negative correlation when comparing with the objective sleep quality parameters. Further-
more, the study found tendencies indicating that sleep durations below 6 hours resulted in higher 
PSQI scores. Additionally, the study suggested a positive correlation between PM2.5 and the PSQI 
score [2]. 
 
2.1.3 Key Findings: Sleep  
A sleep cycle consists of REM and NREM sleep. Deep sleep is more prevalent in the first half of the 
night, whereas REM sleep is more prevalent in the second half of the night. Accordingly, it is crucial 
to maintain a consistent sleep schedule to ensure both REM sleep and deep sleep are obtained. If the 
bedtime is considerably later than usual, the sleeping individual will gain less deep sleep than usual 
due to the circadian rhythm. Contrarily, if the wake-up time is earlier than usual, the REM sleep will 
be shortened. Both REM sleep and deep sleep are crucial parts of obtaining restorative sleep and 
strengthening memory. Additionally, it is beneficial for the quality of sleep to avoid awakenings dur-
ing the night. A study found that the number of awakenings negatively correlates with the perception 
of sleep quality (PSQ) [3]. 
The majority of adults require 7-9 hours of sleep each night to feel rested, get restorative sleep, and 
maintain a healthy sleep pattern [1], [8]. A study found that if the sleep duration was below 6 hours, 
the sleeping individuals were more sensitive to the indoor environment (temperature, RH, CO2, and 
PM2.5) compared to those who slept more than 6 hours [2]. The increased sensitivity among the 
individuals with sleep durations below 6 hours was found to increase wakefulness during sleep, lead-
ing to decreased sleep efficiency, and to increase the PSQI score. For the individuals with sleep du-
rations above 6 hours, the light sleep stage was found to positively correlate with PM2.5 concentra-
tions. Additionally, the GSQS score was found to negatively correlate with TST, SE, REM sleep, and 
light sleep [2].

2.2 Indoor Environment  
The indoor environment has been evidenced by several studies to have a significant effect on sleep 
quality [2], [4], [5], [6]. Insights into the indoor environment in bedrooms from relevant literature 
will be presented in this section. 
 
2.2.1 Air Quality 
PM 
An indicator of the indoor environment is the particulate matter (PM). Particles are typically divided 
by their different aerodynamic diameters. The smaller the particles, the more harmful they are due to 
their small diameter and their ability to enter the lungs and reach the lower airways. As stated in 
Section 1, the primary focus will be on fine particles (diameter ≤ 2.5 μm), however, coarse particles 
(diameter ≤ 10 μm) and very fine particles (diameter ≤ 1 μm) will be briefly addressed. Exposure to 
fine particles, PM2.5, during sleep can lead to sleep disorders and contribute to several health com-
plications such as lung cancer, chronic respiratory illnesses, and premature mortality [5]. 
 
The presence of particles in residential buildings is often associated with cooking, cleaning, smoking, 
burning candles, etc. Particle pollution can also originate from outdoor air pollution, such as traffic, 
pollen, wildfire, etc. Particles can be removed via ventilation, for instance by opening windows or 
trickle vents (natural ventilation), by mechanical ventilation, or by introducing an air purifier that 
circulates the existing indoor air through a filter. WHO’s global air quality guidelines (AQG) aim to 
protect world health by determining the necessary level of air quality [12]. Among others, WHO 
recommends the following concentrations of particulate matter, as seen in Table 3. The 24-hour con-

A study conducted in Taipei, Taiwan, from 2015 to 2021 investigated the impacts of PM2.5 on sleep

sleep, which affects the breathing and amount of oxygen to the brain during sleep [5]. The study 
indicates that an increase in PM2.5 exposure of 1 μg/m3 results in more light sleep (stages N1 and 
N2), less deep sleep (stage N3), less REM sleep, and an increased level of arousal. Hence, the study 
implies that a reduction in PM2.5 levels may improve both sleep quality and sleep efficiency [4]. On 
the contrary, a study of 10 subjects in Lisbon found a negative correlation between PM2.5 and N1 
sleep (part of light sleep) [6]. However, it should be considered that the sample size of the Portuguese 
study is rather small and that external factors possibly could have influenced the data. 
 
PM2.5 concentrations in bedrooms are generally lower compared to kitchens and living rooms, which 
are more exposed to sources of pollution. This statement is supported by a study conducted in Kuwait 
investigating concentrations of PM2.5 in bedrooms, kitchens, and living rooms. Nonetheless, due to 
the large amount of time people spend in the bedroom, the importance of healthy and favourable air 
quality intensifies [7].  
 
Another study conducted during sleep among 13 Portuguese students in university dorms investigated 
the influence of indoor air quality on sleep quality [3]. The study showed that 16% of the measure-

cated in Table 3 [12]. The average PM2.5/PM10 ratio from the investigated students was 0.82, indi-
cating a substantial proportion of fine particles in the bedrooms [3]. Another study, investigating Lis-
bon dwellings, found an average PM2.5 concentration of 15.3 μg/m3 and a PM2.5/PM10 ratio ranging 
from 0.72-0.84 [3]. The difference in PM2.5 concentrations in the university dorms and dwellings 
indicates a higher PM2.5 concentration in the dwellings.  
Another interesting finding among the Portuguese university students was that the activity level of 
the subjects during the night positively correlated with PM2.5 and PM10 levels. This may be due to 
particles from the mattress, pillow, and bed linen when occupant movement occurs [3]. The impact 
of movement during sleep has been investigated in another Portuguese study [13]. The study indicated 
that movements during sleep (by moving the bedding once) increased the concentration of PM

(especially PM10) by 56% on average. The concentration of PM rapidly increased after movement 
and declined gradually.  
 
The bedroom itself also greatly affects the indoor environment. This involves, among other things, 
room geometry, air distribution, mechanical installations, and orientation of the bedroom. Addition-
ally, human habits also influence the indoor environment [5]. A study conducted in 75 bedrooms in 
Denmark found that when a window or an internal door was open, the subjects’ perception of sleep 
quality was better compared to the window and door being closed [2]. Another study found that open-
ing windows or internal doors reduced the CO2 concentration due to the increase in available air 
volume in the bedroom [2]. However, the quality of sleep was only improved when the windows were 
open, supplying the room with outdoor air. Opening windows and relying on natural ventilation could, 
however, be a problem in some locations. A study found a correlation between OSA and particles 
originating from traffic [5]. This is worth considering if a bedroom is located close to a road with 
heavy traffic. Another study, analysing data from a medical research study (Multi-Ethnic Study of 
Atherosclerosis, MESA), supported this statement. The study discovered that an increase in annual 
PM2.5 exposure of 5 μg/m3 was linked to the probability of OSA being 60% higher [5].  
Another parameter affecting the indoor PM2.5 concentrations is the occupant’s smoking habits. A 
previous study has proven that indoor cigarette smoking leads to a three times higher concentration 
of PM2.5 [7]. Furthermore, wildfire also contributes to intensified ambient air pollution, which also 
impacts the possibility of OSA [5].  
 
CO2 
The concentration of carbon dioxide (CO2) can be used to indicate the air quality of a room. High 
indoor CO2 concentrations can indicate a poorly ventilated room or an excessive proportion of people. 
The primary source of CO2 pollution is humans. Based on a previously conducted experiment in the 
“Indoor climate, 12360” course at DTU, emission rates of CO2 from human beings were investigated. 
The experiment showed a range of emission rates ranging from approximately 20-75 L/h for a person, 
depending on whether the activity level was low or high, respectively [14]. Indoor CO2 can be re-
moved by ventilation – both naturally and mechanically. It is important to ventilate bedrooms, since 
high CO2 concentrations can cause headaches, exhaustion, disruptions of sleep, and negatively impact 
the next-day performance [1], [3].

2.2.2 Thermal Environment 
Temperature 
Temperature has a substantial impact on sleep, since fluctuations or non-preferable temperatures can 
cause awakening and impaired sleep. The temperature exposure should not be too low during sleep, 
since it may increase the number of awakenings and cause alterations in heart rate variability [4]. A 
drop in temperature may be associated with an increase in the level of PM2.5 due to less air movement 
and thus accumulation of particles in combination with fewer photochemical reactions that degrade 
particles. Hence, an increase in the level of PM2.5 could influence sleep quality by decreasing the 
amount of deep sleep and increasing the number of awakenings. On the contrary, high temperatures 
could reduce the levels of PM2.5 by convection (via air movement) and photochemical reactions that 
degrade pollutants. However, too high temperatures affect sleep quality negatively by impairing the 
sleep stages and sleep efficiency. Furthermore, it is important to acknowledge that meteorological 
changes and outdoor air pollution may influence sleep quality and cause disturbances [4]. 
 
The optimal temperature in a bedroom environment has been investigated in connection with relative 
humidity and clothing insulation during sleep. The study suggested that the temperature during sleep 
should be in the range of 17-28°C at 40-60% RH [1]. However, the recommended temperature by the 
Sleep Foundation ranges from 15.6-20°C [18]. Previous studies have indicated that the preferred tem-
perature for optimal sleep is approximately 19°C [6]. Nevertheless, the optimal temperature during 
sleep is also affected by several factors such as age, gender, air movement in the bedroom, clothing 
==PAGE:24==
Page 24 of 88 
 
insulation, etc. For instance, a study found that the female participants preferred bedroom tempera-
tures approximately 3°C higher than the male participants [6]. 
 
A study conducted among 8,611 participants in a sleep centre in Taipei found that a 1°C increase in 
the 1-month average temperature was associated with a 0.185% increase in the N1 sleep stage and a 
0.057% increase in the REM sleep stage. Additionally, the 1°C increase in temperature was also as-
sociated with a 0.243% decrease in the N2 sleep stage, and a 0.080 event/h decrease in the arousal 
index, indicating fewer awakenings during sleep [4]. 
On the contrary, a study conducted among 10 subjects in Lisbon found a significant negative corre-
lation between temperatures during sleep and REM sleep, measured by EEG [6]. However, due to the 
rather small sample size, this finding may have to be further validated by other studies. 
 
Relative humidity 
As indicated, temperature has a great impact on sleep quality. Similarly, the relative humidity (RH) 
influences sleep quality significantly. Elevated humidity levels can exacerbate sleep quality – espe-
cially in combination with thermally dissatisfying temperatures [3]. Additionally, higher humidity 
levels will lead to less light sleep [2]. On the contrary, a lower RH in combination with a low temper-
ature will result in a reduced amount of deep sleep. This is due to more awakenings during sleep, 
which is also influenced by the PM2.5 levels [4]. In contrast, a higher RH may contribute to a reduc-
tion of PM2.5 [4]. 
 
To achieve and maintain restorative sleep, the recommended RH level ranges from 30-60% [4]. As in 
the case of CO2, RH positively correlates with the number of people present. The study conducted on 
13 Portuguese students indicated a more humid bedroom when occupied by two persons compared 
to one [3]. With single occupancy, the measured mean level of RH was 47.3 ± 8.6%, while with 
double occupancy, the mean level was 57.5 ± 9.4%. Additionally, the study found a negative correla-
tion between perceived sleep quality and RH [3]. 
An increase in RH impacts sleep quality. The study conducted among 8,611 participants in Taipei 
investigated just that. The study found that a 1% increase in the 1-month average RH was associated 
with a 0.075% increase in the N1 sleep stage, a 0.068% decrease in the N2 sleep stage, and a 0.182% 
decrease in the sleep efficiency, indicating an increase in awakenings during sleep [4].

2.2.3 Lighting Conditions 
The optimal lighting condition for a sleep environment is complete darkness, since exposure to light 
during sleep can cause disruption of sleep and awakenings. Exposure to light with a shorter wave-
length on the visible spectrum – for instance, blue light – is associated with diminished sleep. For 
instance, a study found that exposure to blue light before bedtime resulted in a reduction of REM 
sleep and deep sleep. Conversely, light with longer wavelengths on the visible spectrum – warm-
coloured light – has been shown to enhance the quality of sleep as well as the perceived sleep quality 
(PSQI) and decrease sleep latency. In addition, exposure to light of 5-10 lux during sleep was found 
to result in a reduction of light sleep and an increase in time awake and in the REM sleep stage [1]. 
 
2.2.4 Acoustic Environment 
It is crucial to have a proper acoustic environment in the bedroom, since exposure to noise (especially 
intermittent noise) during sleep negatively affects sleep quality. The intermittent noise level in a bed-
room environment should be below 35 dB to avoid disrupted sleep. The perception of intermittent 
noise is usually more disturbing than continuous noise [1]. In addition, the Danish Health Authority 
recommends avoiding noise levels in the bedroom above 30 dB [11]. 
Performed studies suggest that noise generated from vehicles, aeroplanes, and trains is related to an 
increase in awakenings during the night, resulting in a reduced duration of actual sleep and thereby 
reduced sleep efficiency [1]. In addition, a study found a negative correlation between noise and the 
REM sleep stage. Furthermore, the noise level was found to be associated with the perception of sleep 
quality, indicated by the GSQS score [2]. 
 
2.2.5 Key Findings: Indoor Environment  
The indoor environment in a bedroom substantially influences the quality of sleep. Exposure to a poor 
indoor environment can contribute to several health issues and sleep disorders.  
A study found that PM2.5 positively correlates with light sleep and time awake during sleep. Con-
versely, PM2.5 negatively correlates with REM sleep, deep sleep, and sleep efficiency [4]. Interest-
ingly, a study found a positive correlation between the activity level during sleep and PM2.5 and 
PM10 – possibly due to particles being embedded in the mattress, pillow, and bed linen [3]. Similarly, 
another study found an average PM increase of 56% when the bedding was moved once, emphasising 
the particulate accumulation in the bedding [13]. Additionally, a positive correlation was found 
==PAGE:26==
Page 26 of 88 
 
between PM2.5 and the probability of OSA – specifically, a PM2.5 increase of 5 μg/m3 was linked to 
a 60% higher probability of OSA [5]. Furthermore, indoor smoking leads to three times higher PM2.5 
concentrations.  
Another influencing factor on the IAQ is the CO2 concentration, which was found to positively cor-
relate with the number of sleeping individuals in the bedroom and wakefulness during sleep. On the 
contrary, the CO2 concentration is negatively correlated with sleep quality, perceived sleep quality, 
perceived air quality, next-day performance, and deep sleep [3]. 
Regarding the thermal environment, a comfortable bedroom temperature and RH are crucial for ob-
taining good and coherent sleep. Studies have found both positive and negative correlations between 
bedroom temperature and REM sleep [4], [6]. In addition, the temperature was found to negatively 
correlate with light sleep and awakenings during sleep [4]. Furthermore, a negative correlation be-
tween RH and PSQ was discovered [3]. In connection, RH was found to negatively correlate with 
both light sleep and sleep efficiency [4]. 
 
The acoustic environment is an important factor when considering the quality of sleep. The noise 
level in bedroom environments should be below 30 dB to avoid sleep disruption, and the intermittent 
noise level should be below 35 dB [1], [11]. Additionally, continuous noise is usually perceived as 
less disturbing than intermittent noise [1].

3 Methodology 
This section outlines the methodological approach to the experimental field study conducted in this 
thesis among 12 subjects, as well as the approach to the literature review. 
 
3.1 Hypotheses and Objectives  
This thesis has investigated how the bedroom indoor environment is affected by an air purifier, and 
whether it influences the quality of sleep, as formulated in the problem statement in Section 1. Exist-
ing literature and experimental studies suggest there is a correlation between the indoor environment 
and sleep quality. However, there is only limited knowledge of the impact of an air purifier on sleep 
quality. Consequently, the scientific method was applied to investigate the problem statement. This 
led to the formulation of the two primary hypotheses: 
1. The first hypothesis was that the indoor environment – focusing on airborne particles – was 
improved when introducing an air purifier by removing particulate matter from the indoor air.  
2. The second hypothesis was that the reduction of particulate matter, due to the introduction of 
an air purifier, would improve the quality of sleep.  
 
To investigate the hypotheses, specific objectives were established and investigated throughout this 
thesis as previously described in Section 1.1. Based on the hypotheses and objectives, the expected 
outcome of this thesis was to locate possible tendencies regarding the indoor environment and sleep 
quality. This was expected to be proven by literature studies as well as the empirical data obtained 
from the experiment. From the gathered knowledge and data, it was expected that the air purifier 
would improve the indoor environment by minimising airborne particles and improving the quality 
of sleep.  
As stated in Section 1, this thesis was deliberately delimited to more comprehensively focus on the 
indoor environment – especially airborne particles. The quality of sleep was also investigated, how-
ever, not from a medical or biological perspective. 
 
The experiments conducted in this thesis were quickly established and initiated to ensure a compara-
ble external environment. Consequently, an initial project plan, including a time schedule, was devel-
oped and is presented in Appendix 1. Since a buffer time was already incorporated into the project

3.2 Literature Review 
A literature review on indoor environment (especially particulate matter) and the effects on sleep 
quality was conducted to identify relevant scientific studies and establish current knowledge regard-

3.3 Experiment 
12 subjects (aged 19-56) and their bedrooms were examined by measuring the indoor environment 
(with an ATMOcube) and the quality of sleep (with a Fitbit watch and a Muse S headband) for two 
weeks. The experiment was conducted from February to April 2025 in Denmark. An air purifier 
(Philips series 3000i) with a HEPA and active carbon filter was active for one week of the two-week 
experiment to investigate the possible improvement of the indoor environment and sleep quality.  
 
The subjects were selected based on several factors. The bedroom’s ventilation should be natural to 
make room for better comparisons of the effect of an air purifier on the indoor environment.

but in different rooms. This applied to subjects 5 and 9, where the bedroom of subject 5 was located

3.3.2 Intercalibration  
To ensure comparable results from the ATMOcubes, an intercalibration between the five ATMOcubes 
used was made. The Optical Particle Sizer (OPS) Spectrometer model 3330 from TSI was used as a 
reference for the intercalibration, as well as an Onset HOBO data logger, measuring CO2, tempera-
ture, RH, and other indoor environmental parameters. 
 
The HOBO measuring device was calibrated according to the manufacturer’s instructions. This im-
plied using nitrogen (N2) as a zero gas and CO2 (at 5000 ppm) as a span gas to calibrate the device. 
The five ATMOcubes were placed on a table in the same room and were calibrated by opening the 
windows and leaving the room for 30 minutes. Due to the complexity and cost of calibrating the OPS, 
the calibration was not done for this intercalibration test.  
Additionally, particles were manually generated with Topas’ Atomizer Aerosol Generator model ATM 
228 to measure a broader range of PM among the ATMOcubes. Similarly, CO2 was generated by a 
CO2 gas cylinder. 
The measuring devices (ATMOcubes, OPS, and HOBO) were left in the room for 24 hours, measuring 
every minute to measure the decay of particles and CO2, as well as variations of other indoor envi-
ronmental parameters.

3.3.3 Risk Assessment 
When conducting experimental work, there is a potential risk of unforeseen obstacles, errors, or com-
plications. To minimise the probability of unforeseen issues, the following was considered. 
==PAGE:38==
Page 38 of 88 
 
The equipment was tested beforehand to minimise the malfunction of a device. The air purifier was, 
for instance, tested to ensure that the noise was not noticeable. The filters for the air purifiers were 
cleaned with a vacuum cleaner before each experiment. The ATMOcubes were exposed to outdoor 
air once a week during the experiment to ensure that their self-calibrating function operated properly. 
The ATMOcube gathered data on a connected USB, making it impossible to check the measurements 
during the measuring period. This presented a risk if a device stopped recording during the two-week 
measuring period.  
 
Since not all 12 experiments were conducted simultaneously, a potential risk of external parameters 
influencing the results was present: for instance, a change in outdoor temperature. To minimise this 
risk, the experiment was conducted close to each other to ensure the same outdoor conditions (cold 
months). Furthermore, five experiments were conducted simultaneously to quickly obtain results. All 
12 experiments were not able to happen simultaneously due to the amount of equipment available. 
Additionally, a buffer time was planned to account for possible experimental complications. 
 
Another risk of this experiment was the different measuring methods of the Fitbit watch and the Muse 
headband. As previously stated, Fitbit and Muse use different measuring methods, which may provide 
different results regarding sleep. Before the beginning of the experiment, it was determined that the 
Fitbit watches would be the primary measuring device for sleep. However, some subjects wore both 
Fitbit and Muse to determine the possible differences between the measuring methods. The differ-
ences will be addressed, analysed, and discussed in subsequent sections.  
 
There are some limitations regarding the experiment conducted in connection with this thesis. Due to 
time constraints and the length of each experiment, it was limited to 12 subjects. This is why a liter-
ature review of existing studies was used to obtain detailed knowledge about the topic to discuss and 
compare the results of the experiment conducted. 
The experiments were conducted in each subject’s bedroom, which caused room-specific variations 
when comparing the results. Furthermore, the subjects had individual sleep habits which might have 
affected the results, as well as other influential factors such as age, gender, health, thermal properties, 
etc. Further limitations of the experiment will be discussed later in the thesis.

3.3.5 Statistical Analyses 
The experimental data have been statistically analysed using the software Excel, Maple, and Py-
thon/Spyder. To provide an overview and comparison of the collected data, box plots will mainly 
present the data. Afterwards, the data will be analysed statistically, and the following describes the 
statistical methods used.  
 
The dataset was tested for normality by the Shapiro-Wilk test. Subsequently, for interval/ratio-scale 
data that were normally distributed, the paired t-test was performed. Additionally, the non-parametric 
statistical method, the Wilcoxon signed-rank test, was performed on all the measured variables re-
gardless of the distribution of data and scale level. Hence, both parametric and non-parametric statis-
tical methods were investigated to ensure statistical robustness. To further ensure robustness, the sta-
tistical tests were two-tailed to account for possible effects in either direction due to the small sample 
size. The statistical analyses were performed using Python/Spyder, but will be briefly described in 
the subsequent sections.

The results from the experiments will be presented and analysed in this section. The data have been 
collected objectively by an ATMOcube, a Fitbit watch, and in some cases also a Muse headband, as 
previously described in Section 3. Furthermore, subjective data have been collected via three ques-

The indoor environmental data were organised to only include the time asleep among the subjects. 
The sleep duration was based on data from Fitbit, measuring when the subjects were asleep. In cases

including weekday measurements (Mon-Tue, Tue-Wed, Wed-Thu, Thu-Fri) to possibly ensure more 
consistent patterns in sleep habits and daily routines. Additionally, the sleep data of subjects who were 
ill during the measuring period were excluded to minimise potential sleep quality effects associated 
with the ill subjects’ health, applicable to subjects 5, 7, and 10. The collected (raw) results and anal-
yses of the 12 subjects are presented in Appendices 9, 11, 12, and 13.

-

4.5 Summary of Results 
In summary, the experimental data indicated the statistically significant effect of an air purifier on 
reducing the concentration of particulate matter and formaldehyde in the investigated bedrooms. No 
significant effects were detected on the objective sleep quality among the subjects, however, the sub-
jective GSQS score indicated borderline significant results for the Wilcoxon signed-rank test 
(𝑝𝑊𝑖𝑙𝑐𝑜𝑥𝑜𝑛= 0.057) and statistically significant results for the paired t-test (𝑝𝑝𝑎𝑖𝑟𝑒𝑑 𝑡−𝑡𝑒𝑠𝑡= 0.046). 
Despite the generally low concentrations of PM2.5 in the subjects’ bedrooms – most of which are 
below the WHO guidelines – the analysis indicated a positive correlation between GSQS and PM2.5. 
When investigating the GSQS questions further, two questions regarding tiredness after waking up 
and PSQ indicated the greatest improvements among the subjects’ subjective evaluations. The GSQS 
score on each question was significantly reduced when introducing air purification (𝑝= 0.006).  
 
The analyses of the results also suggested that PM2.5 in the measured bedrooms seemed to be emitted 
by indoor or outdoor sources and was not necessarily associated with the sleeping subjects. However, 
it was not possible to locate the exact sources of PM2.5 in this study. Thus, the measurements indi-
cated a predominance of fine (PM2.5) and very fine (PM1) particles in the subjects’ bedrooms during 
sleep. Additionally, the average level of PM2.5 was higher in the bedrooms located in the urban area 
==PAGE:75==
Page 75 of 88 
 
compared to the non-urban areas. Despite the differences in PM2.5 concentrations in the urban and 
non-urban bedrooms, the PM2.5 concentrations were approximately reduced by five to six times 
when the air purifier was active. Furthermore, the scatter plots and diagrams presented suggested a 
negative correlation between PM2.5 and SE. However, it was also indicated that both temperature 
and TVOC negatively correlated with SE, emphasising the importance of recognising the influence 
of other indoor environmental parameters. Finally, it is crucial to consider the small sample size of 
the experiment, which will be discussed in the subsequent section.

5 Discussion  
This section of the thesis aims to discuss, interpret, and reflect upon the observations, results, and 
analyses conducted throughout this thesis. 
 
5.1 Interpretation of Results 
The indoor environments among the 12 subjects indicated a significant improvement in the concen-
tration of PM when the air purifier was active. The subjects with high concentrations of PM were 
severely reduced when introducing the air purifier. Similarly, the subjects with already low concen-
trations were also reduced to even lower concentrations with an active air purifier.  
The analysis indicated that the subjects living in the municipality of Copenhagen (urban environment) 
were exposed to a slightly higher concentration of PM2.5 compared to the subjects living in a non-
urban environment – both with and without an air purifier. This could be due to more outdoor sources 
of pollution, for instance, traffic. On the other hand, particles also stem from indoor sources that are 
not necessarily location-specific, such as cleaning, cooking, smoking, etc. Consequently, it is im-
portant to acknowledge that the experiment gathered more data points with low PM2.5 concentrations 
and only a few with high concentrations, all relatively low when compared with the WHO guidelines. 
Nevertheless, subjects 6 and 12 reported in Questionnaire 3 that “The air felt less stuffy than usual” 
and “The air felt cleaner” respectively, both referring to the measuring week with an active air puri-
fier. However, a wider range of PM2.5 concentrations would be interesting to investigate in future 
studies.  
 
A general apprehension of introducing air purification is the noise level. The average noise levels 
measured in the two weeks did not show a significant difference, meaning the noise levels are rather 
unchanged when introducing an air purifier. Additionally, the subjects did not report any disturbances 
associated with noise except for subject 2. Subject 2 reported the occurrence of noise disturbances as 
being “more than usual” in both measuring weeks, which was possibly due to the complication with 
the specific air purifier that caused the air purifier to increase the airflow unexpectedly.  
The difference between the average noise levels in the two weeks was only 0.4 dB. The ATMOcube’s 
measuring range is, however, 30-100 dB (±2 dB). Consequently, some noise levels might have been 
below 30±2 dB where ATMOcube was not able to detect the exact level. Based on these measure-
ments, the active air purifier did not significantly affect the noise level. On the other hand, it is 
==PAGE:77==
Page 77 of 88 
 
important to consider that the air purifier was running at the lowest airflow rate (sleep mode). If 
another airflow rate were chosen in this experiment, the increased noise level caused by the increase 
in airflow rate would possibly result in a more remarkable difference and perhaps affect the sleep 
quality. Different airflow rates in combination with the associated generation of noise would be in-
teresting to investigate in future studies.  
 
Regarding the results of TVOC, interesting points surface. The concentrations of TVOC do not seem 
to be influenced by the air purifier. The active carbon filters used in the experiment are advertised to 
remove both formaldehyde and VOC [28], [29]. In this case, the ATMOcube measured the total con-
centration of VOCs: TVOC. Thus, the volatile organic compound, formaldehyde, was significantly 
removed by the air purifier. This may suggest that the filter of the investigated air purifier absorbed 
some VOCs, but not all. On the other hand, the accuracy of the ATMOcube measurements of formal-
dehyde (± 0.02 ppm or ± 20% m.v.) should be acknowledged. The measured concentrations of for-
maldehyde range from 0.00 to 0.03 ppm; hence, the given accuracy of ATMOcube may affect the 
results substantially. Nevertheless, it would be interesting to investigate both formaldehyde and 
TVOC further regarding air purification with an active carbon filter and use different measuring 
equipment than only ATMOcube.  
 
The remaining indoor environmental parameters should ideally remain unchanged throughout the 
measuring period. Regarding the thermal environment, both temperature and RH remained close to 
constant throughout the measuring period. The variations could be due to the variations in outdoor 
conditions. The concentrations of CO2 were also rather constant. Some subjects did, however, deviate 
from their usual number of sleeping people in their bedrooms throughout the measuring period. Nat-
urally, having one less or one extra bedroom partner causes a substantial difference in CO2 concen-
tration. Ideally, the number of people sleeping in the bedroom should be constant, however, it was 
not controlled in this experiment.  
Common for all subjects is that they have individual preferences regarding the indoor environment. 
For instance, the bedroom temperature might theoretically be appropriate, but if the subject prefers 
temperatures outside the theoretical range, the subject might feel uncomfortable. The individually 
ideal temperature may be influenced by factors such as age, sex, air movement in the bedroom, ther-
mal insulation of clothing or bedding, etc. Interestingly, the temperature was found to negatively 
correlate with SE, and the same applied to the correlation between PM2.5 and SE. This led to the 
==PAGE:78==
Page 78 of 88 
 
investigation of the possible correlation between temperature and PM2.5, which indicated a positive 
correlation. The analysis showed that PM2.5 was confounded with temperature. This indicated that 
several indoor environmental parameters may have affected the sleep – not solely attributable to 
PM2.5.  
 
The improvement of the indoor environment by air purification did not significantly influence the 
objective measurements of sleep among the subjects. Nevertheless, the calculated statistical effect 
sizes indicated somewhat an effect (however, not significant) on the number of minutes awake and in 
deep sleep when purifying the air. Both effect sizes implied a decreasing number of minutes with air 
purification. As stated in the review of literature, studies have previously shown that a reduction of 
PM2.5 results in less time awake and more time in deep sleep. Hence, the effect on time awake in the 
conducted experiment was expected. However, the effect of PM2.5 on deep sleep was not as expected. 
As indicated, the measured PM2.5 concentrations in the measured bedrooms were relatively low – 
the majority of data points were below the guidelines of WHO, even before introducing the air puri-
fier. Additionally, it was difficult to indicate tendencies when the data points were unequally distrib-
uted among the PM2.5 concentrations. However, the scatter plots indicated a negative correlation 
between sleep efficiency and PM2.5. It would be interesting to further investigate the possible tenden-
cies regarding PM2.5 and different sleep parameters with a broader range of PM2.5 concentrations. 
 
It is important to note that several factors may influence the quality of sleep, for instance, a shift in 
temperatures, humidities, personal factors, etc. Therefore, it would be interesting to investigate the 
effect of a larger improvement of PM2.5 concentrations and possibly control the other indoor envi-
ronmental parameters to keep them more constant. Additionally, a broader range of subjects would 
be valuable to investigate to explore the effects of an air purifier and possibly derive significant dif-
ferences in sleep with the introduction of air purification. Additionally, it may be difficult to compare 
sleep when factors such as age, gender, health, school/work obligations, and clothing insulation have 
an essential impact. The bedroom layout, thermal properties, and other room-specific variations may 
also influence the quality of sleep. This could promote the use of sleep centres to eliminate some 
room-specific variations. A new and unfamiliar sleep environment could, however, also influence the 
quality of sleep if the subjects are not as comfortable as in their own bedroom with the mattress and 
bedding that they are used to. This signifies the individuality of sleeping preferences and habits, which 
is a valuable factor to consider.  
==PAGE:79==
Page 79 of 88 
 
Another influencing factor in the monitoring of sleep is the measuring equipment. Measuring equip-
ment is rarely completely accurate at every measuring point. As stated, Fitbit watches are quite accu-
rate when detecting sleep, but less accurate in detecting and separating the specific sleep stages. In 
addition, the comparison of Fitbit watches and Muse headbands also indicated measuring inaccura-
cies. Limitations regarding the measuring equipment will be addressed in the subsequent section. 
 
The subjective results obtained by the surveys indicated, among others, both significant and border-
line significant reductions in GSQS when the air purifier was active, meaning an improved perception 
of sleep quality. If the statistical tests of GSQS were predetermined to be one-tailed, a significant 
effect could have been determined (𝑝< 0.029 for Wilcoxon signed-rank and paired t-test). However, 
since the two-tailed tests were predetermined, the potential result can only be discussed.  
Nevertheless, the GSQS results of the two-tailed tests are interesting, since a tendency regarding a 
positive correlation between PM2.5 and GSQS score was found. If the GSQS-PM2.5 correlation was 
extrapolated to include PM2.5 concentrations above the WHO guidelines, the GSQS scores would 
possibly exceed the score of 6, indicating poor sleep.  
When PM2.5 increased, the GSQS questions experiencing the largest increase were questions regard-
ing tiredness after waking up and PSQ. Interestingly, quotes by subjects 6 and 12 respectively (who 
had an active air purifier in the second measuring week) described similar tendencies: “I slept better 
compared to last week and felt more refreshed in the morning” and “I slept very well at the beginning 
of week 2 and felt more refreshed”. However, subject 12 did acknowledge a possible influencing 
factor on the sleep quality: “I was a bit too busy in week 1 compared to week 2”. The same applied 
to subject 9 (who had an active air purifier in the first measuring week): I was very tired during week 
2. However, I think that could also be due to other factors besides the indoor environment”.  
To summarise, both the indoor environment and sleep parameters investigated in this thesis are influ-
enced by several factors, which may make a direct comparison difficult. This leads to an assessment 
of the limitations of this study. 
 
5.2 Limitations 
When conducting experimental work, the associated limitations must be acknowledged and evalu-
ated. A crucial limitation regarding the experiment in this thesis is the small sample size. The 12 
subjects attending the two-week experiment represent a rather small sample of data and do not nec-
essarily give a representative insight into the correlation between sleep quality and indoor 
==PAGE:80==
Page 80 of 88 
 
environment. However, it may still help identify tendencies regarding the topic when comparing the 
collected data to existing studies identified in the literature review. Additionally, three subjects were 
excluded (representing the sorted dataset) due to illness during the measuring period, making the 
sample of data even smaller. The representativeness of this study could be improved by gathering 
data from a larger group of subjects, as also indicated by the statistical power analysis. Additionally, 
a larger sample size would be beneficial to minimise Type II errors. 
 
Another limitation of this experiment is the variations in outdoor conditions during the measuring 
period. The 12 experiments were not able to happen simultaneously due to the availability of meas-
uring equipment. Consequently, the outdoor conditions differed, which ultimately may have influ-
enced the indoor environment and the quality of sleep. Hence, it would have been ideal if the exper-
iments were conducted simultaneously. However, to minimise this risk of large outdoor variations, 
the experiment was conducted close to each other in the colder months to ensure similar outdoor 
conditions.  
 
Another risk of this experiment was the different measuring methods of the Fitbit watch and the Muse 
headband. As previously stated, Fitbit and Muse use different measuring methods, which may provide 
different results regarding sleep. Before the beginning of the experiment, it was determined that the 
Fitbit watches would be the primary measuring device for sleep. However, some subjects wore both 
Fitbit and Muse to determine the possible differences between the measuring methods. When com-
paring data from Fitbit and Muse, differences in especially time awake and REM sleep occurred. As 
indicated by studies reviewing the accuracy of several Fitbit models, the Fitbit may be inaccurate, 
especially in distinguishing between sleep stages [21], [23]. This constitutes a limitation that would 
be interesting to improve in future studies. 
Regarding the measuring equipment, an additional limitation is the lack of tracking of the measuring 
devices to prevent technical issues. This applied to both ATMOcube, Fitbit, and Muse. Furthermore, 
the ATMOcube’s accuracy represents a limitation which could be minimised by experimenting with 
measuring equipment with higher accuracy. Additionally, a previous study indicated that ATMOcube 
tends to underestimate the concentrations of PM1, PM2.5, and PM10, which was also indicated by 
the intercalibration conducted in relation to this thesis. Hence, the accuracy of the measuring devices 
is important to acknowledge.  
 
==PAGE:81==
Page 81 of 88 
 
Another limitation of this study is the possible change in behaviour. Even though the subjects were 
not informed when the air purifier was active to minimise bias, they might still have a change in 
behaviour. It is reasonable to think that some subjects may have ventilated the bedroom before bed 
more frequently than usual, knowing that their indoor environment is being measured. The same ap-
plies to sleep monitoring. Perhaps the subjects wanted to obtain a better sleep quality when monitor-
ing their sleep. This leads to another limitation: the ability of the subjects to track their own sleep data 
during the measuring period. Throughout the measuring period, the subjects were able to access the 
sleep data from their previous nights. For instance, if a subject obtained a low TST, they may be more 
likely to change their behaviour the next night. The subjects were not able to track the indoor envi-
ronment during the experiment, since the light indications on the ATMOcubes were turned off.  
 
5.3 Potential Innovations 
Potential innovation proposals have been elaborated upon following the work and reflections of this 
thesis. An innovation for implementing an air purifier in the bedroom may be to include it as an 
integrated part of the furniture.  
An idea is to have the air purifier integrated into the bed frame or headboard, supplied with a HEPA 
and active carbon filter. The air-purifying headboard would supply the sleeping individuals directly 
with clean air. However, noise and draught may be a problem, causing disturbances during sleep. 
Thus, the air purifier could be integrated into the bottom of the bed – potentially like an integrated 
bed bench. The airflow could be innovated and optimised to provide clean air directly to the sleeping 
individuals without causing disturbances from noise or draught. It could even provide the preferred 
temperatures to the individuals in order to provide the best conditions for achieving good sleep qual-
ity. This piece of air-purifying furniture could be produced as either an integrated part of the bed 
frame or as a bed bench that can be attached to an existing bed. However, the safety aspects of this 
innovation would need to be carefully considered. 
Similar ideas could be applied if combining lighting and air purification – for instance, an air purify-
ing pendant light. Additionally, the air purifier could also be an integrated part of wall art. These 
innovative ideas could implement the WHO’s guidelines and be automated to purify the air if the PM 
concentration exceeds the guidelines. While integrating air purification into bedroom furniture could 
be a potential innovation, several aspects regarding design, comfort, and safety are crucial to consider.

5.4 Future Studies and Recommendations 
Further investigation of the indoor environmental impact on sleep and the effect of air purification is 
necessary to potentially locate correlations between the effects of air purification on sleep quality. 
Recommendations for future studies have been identified and will be discussed in this section.  
 
Future research could investigate the correlation between the indoor environment, air purification, 
and sleep in more controlled environments to minimise bedroom variations. In this case, other factors 
could possibly influence the quality of sleep: for instance, the adjustment of sleeping in a new bed-
room with a different bed compared to what the subjects are used to. Some subjects might be unaf-
fected while others are affected by the new environment. Additionally, measuring all the subjects 
simultaneously would be beneficial to obtain similar outdoor conditions. However, this would require 
a more professional setup and a larger amount of measuring equipment. In summary, this may provide 
a better opportunity for comparison between the subjects when bedroom variations or outdoor varia-
tions are minimised.  
 
A predetermined sleeping schedule would also be interesting to investigate. For instance, to ensure 
that the subjects go to bed and wake up at the same time to investigate how their sleep stages differ-
entiate while their TIB remains similar. Regarding subjects, the selection phase could also be im-
proved. For instance, only subjects achieving a PSQI below 5 could be selected. Otherwise, focusing 
on subjects with airway problems would also be interesting to investigate the effects of air purification 
on this specific type of subject.  
 
An interesting point to further investigate regarding this topic is noise from the air purifier with dif-
ferent airflow rates and the associated impact on sleep. In this experiment, the air purifier’s sleep 
function was used, which is characterised by a low airflow rate and minimal noise. A higher airflow 
rate would result in an increased noise level. However, since the noise would be continuous, it would 
be interesting to investigate if the sleep quality would be affected. It could negatively affect sleep by 
causing disturbances, but it could potentially also eliminate intermittent noise by creating continuous 
noise.  
 
A recommendation for future studies is to use measuring equipment with the possibility of monitoring 
from a distance. This will be beneficial to quickly notice technical failures or other complications. In 
==PAGE:83==
Page 83 of 88 
 
this study, monitoring from a distance was not possible, resulting in the Fitbit failure of one subject 
and the ATMOcube malfunctioning of another subject.  
In connection, the subjects were able to access their Fitbit data gathered throughout each night. For 
future studies, it may, however, be beneficial if the subjects do not have access to their Fitbit data in 
order to minimise bias. Each morning, the subjects of this experiment would synchronise the data 
from the previous night. This implied that the subjects were presented with their quality of sleep. This 
could potentially create bias if they subsequently changed their habits or did something differently 
compared to what they usually do.  
 
Regarding recommendations for future studies, the preliminary work before setting up the experi-
ments should be considered to achieve the best possible outcome. In this study, the ATMOcubes were 
tested before the beginning of each experiment. The ATMOcubes also had to be calibrated once a 
week by exposing the devices to outdoor air. Correspondingly, the air purifiers were tested, and the 
filters were vacuumed before each experiment.  
 
The findings of this study suggest that even though PM was significantly reduced by the air purifier, 
the relatively low concentrations did not significantly show an effect on the objective sleep parame-
ters. Hence, it can be discussed whether an air purifier is necessary for bedrooms with already low 
PM concentrations (for instance, below the WHO guidelines).  
Despite the low PM concentrations, a significant effect on the subjects’ perception of sleep quality 
was found. In bedrooms with substantially higher PM concentrations, the implementation of an air 
purifier could possibly have a noticeable effect on the quality of sleep, however, it will need to be 
investigated.  
 
It is also interesting to compare air purification to mechanical ventilation. The clean air delivery rate 
(CADR) of the Philips air purifier was only provided at the maximum airflow rate (520 m3/h) [27]. 
Based on the standby and maximum power consumption (<2 W and 55 W), presented in Table 7, the 
lowest CADR was approximated, assuming that the power consumption in sleep mode is slightly 
higher (assumed to be 5 W) than in standby. Thus, the CADR is approximately 47 m3/h/person or 13 
L/s/person when the air purifier is in sleep mode. In comparison, the Danish Building Regulation’s 
(BR18) minimum requirement for ventilation in residential buildings is 0.3 L/s/m2 [42]. 
==PAGE:84==
Page 84 of 88 
 
Consequently, a mechanical ventilation system would require an increase in airflow to achieve similar 
airflow rates as the air purifier in sleep mode.  
 
Regarding future implementation in Danish regulations or standards, it may be difficult and expensive 
to establish for bedrooms and for the average person to monitor the PM concentration in the bedroom. 
The first step of implementation could possibly be in workplaces, where specific thresholds could 
serve as either recommendations or regulations. Nevertheless, no implementations should be made 
based solely on this study, as it would require more studies with a broader range of participants. In 
summary, multiple future studies could be conducted in addition to the recommendations and findings 
of this initial study.

6 Conclusions 
This thesis aimed to investigate the effect of an air purifier on the indoor environment of a bedroom 
– focusing on airborne particles – and the potential influence on sleep quality.  
Despite the limited sample size, it can be concluded that the Philips air purifier (series 3000i, with a 
HEPA and active carbon filter) used in the experiment significantly removed particles (PM1, PM2.5, 
and PM10) from the bedroom air when on the lowest airflow rate (𝑝= 0.001). Additionally, this 
study found a significant reduction in formaldehyde with air purification (𝑝= 0.009), however, no 
significant effect on the concentration of TVOC. The perceived quality of sleep, reported by the sub-
jects via the subjective evaluation of GSQS, indicated both statistically significant and borderline 
significant differences depending on the statistical method applied (𝑝𝑊𝑖𝑙𝑐𝑜𝑥𝑜𝑛= 0.057, 
𝑝𝑝𝑎𝑖𝑟𝑒𝑑 𝑡−𝑡𝑒𝑠𝑡= 0.046). Hence, the subjects perceived the sleep quality as being better with air puri-
fication. Similarly, a positive correlation between PM2.5 and GSQS score was indicated. Neverthe-
less, no significant differences were statistically found in the remaining sleep quality parameters 
among the subjects. This may be due to the generally low PM2.5 concentrations in the measured 
bedrooms – most of which were below the guidelines of WHO. Potential effects on sleep quality 
were, however, observed and will need to be investigated in future studies. Another interesting finding 
in this study was the relatively constant noise level throughout the measuring period, indicating min-
imal to no noise difference captured by the measuring device.  
 
It is important to acknowledge the limitations associated with this experiment, for instance, the small 
sample size. A larger sample size is necessary to possibly prove correlations between air purification 
and sleep quality. Additionally, a wider range of PM2.5 concentrations should be investigated to pos-
sibly measure a larger decrease in concentrations, which perhaps may lead to a measurable effect on 
sleep quality. Hence, further research and studies should be conducted on this topic; however, this 
thesis may serve as an initial study where the findings and recommendations for future studies can be 
applied.
