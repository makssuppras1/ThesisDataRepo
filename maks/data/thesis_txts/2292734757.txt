Summary
Bacille-Calmette Guérin (BCG) is a live, attenuated vaccine routinely used to immunize
humans against tuberculosis. There is evidence that BCG lowers infant mortality and
protects animals from unrelated pathogens. Little is known about immune response
to BCG in pigs. The goal of this project was to investigate the immune response to
the BCG vaccine to replicate this effect in animals.
Three days old piglets (n=32)
were assigned randomly to either control or vaccination group and injected with either
Sauton or BCG. 2.5 ml of blood was collected from each piglet before injection and
at 1, 8 and 21 days. Total mRNA was purified from the blood and the expression of
genes related to innate immunity, T-cell response and inflammation was measured using
RT-qPCR. No statistically significant difference was found in gene expression between
control and treatment groups at any time point. In the whole population the following
genes were expressed differentially over time: CD14, C3, IL1B, IFNA, CCL5, IL1RN,
TLR2, MYD88, IL1RAP, MX1, CD86, ITGAM, SLA-DBR, NOD2, TBX21.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
This thesis is a pilot study in a bigger project which aims to evaluate if the cross-
protective effects of the BCG vaccine in humans can be replicated in pigs to decrease
piglet mortality. Not much is known about BCG response in pigs. As their immune
system is more similar to human than mice they could be a good model to investigate
this process [1]. The aim of this project itself is to investigate the immune response
of young piglets to BCG vaccination, as the presence and type of the response could
indicate if this vaccine could be used in this context.
1.1
What is Bacillus Calmette-Guérin
vaccine?
Bacillus Calmette-Guérin (BCG) is a live, attenuated vaccine—currently the only vac-
cine against tuberculosis in use [2]. It was developed at the beginning of 20th century by
Albert Calmette and Camille Guérin by passaging Mycobacterium bovis in bile, glycerin
and potato medium until it had lost its virulence [3]. It was first administered to a
human in 1921 [3].
The use of the vaccine became more widespread after World War II. Due to tuber-
culosis epidemics after the war Scandinavian Red Cross Societies and UNICEF started
their campaign to vaccinate children against tuberculosis in the countries affected by
the war [4]. In 1974 BCG was included in World Health Organization’s expanded Pro-
gramme on Immunization and as for year 2004 around 100 million children worldwide
were receiving this vaccine yearly [5]. Even though the reported efficacy of BCG against
tuberculosis varies [5], over the years it has shown numerous non-specific, protective
effects in animals and humans.
1.2
Non-specific, protective effects of BCG
vaccine
Studies conducted in 40s and 50s in USA and UK have shown that BCG vaccination
lowers child mortality [6]. Later observational studies in Guinea-Bissau yielded similar
results. Both presence of a BCG scar and a positive tuberculin skin test were associated

with lower child mortality, even for children that were not exposed to tuberculosis [7, 8].
That effect could also be seen in randomized studies [9] and is stronger in females [10].
Other studies in humans seem to suggest that BCG vaccination may help to protect
children from helminths [11] and reduce cases of sepsis, bloodstream infections [9, 12]
and acute respiratory tract infections [13]. Studies in mice animals have shown that
BCG vaccine may protect against influenza [14], Babesia and Plasmodium parasites [15],
Candida albicans [16], Staphylococcus aureus [16] and Klebsiella pneumoniae [17]. BCG
vaccination also enhanced the cytokine and humoral response to influenza vaccination
[18]. Some studies suggest that BCG vaccination may lower prevalence of asthma [19]
and Hodgkin’s lymphoma [20]. BCG is also used in immunotherapy to treat bladder
cancer [21] and melanoma [22]. It is also considered as a potential therapy of type I
(autoimmune) diabetes due to its immunomodulatory properties [23].
1.3
The mechanisms of non-specific vaccine
effects
Two mechanisms may be responsible for non-specific effects of vaccines: T-cell mediated
heterologous immunity based on cross-reactivity of pathogens and “training” of innate
immune system [24].
1.3.1
T-cell mediated heterologous immunity
T-cell receptors are degenerated - one TCR can recognize multiple antigens [25]. TCR
interacts mainly with a MHC molecule presenting the peptide, not the peptide itself
[25]. Usually the interaction with the peptide is limited to only a few amino-acids, so
the rest of the peptide may be different and still bind the TCR [25]. The cross-reactive
T-memory cells remaining after immunization with one antigen may be activated by
an unrelated antigen during an infection with a different pathogen [25]. The memory
cells may also be activated directly by cytokines when binding leftover antigens from
previous infection or even without T-cell receptor stimulation [25]. Tissue damage by
viruses combined with cytokines may also activate some self-reactive memory T-cells
[25]. The direct type of cross-reactivity usually occurs between different viruses like
influenza and Epstein-Barr virus [26] and influenza and hepatitis C viruses in humans
[27] or lymphocytic choriomeningitis, Vaccinia and Panchide viruses in mice [28].
A study by Mathurin et al. [29] proposes that such mechanism may be responsible for
heterologous, CD4 T-cell-mediated protection from Vaccinia virus in BCG-immunized
mice.

1.3.2
“Trained” Innate Immunity
The innate immunity is the first, non-specific line of defense of the immune system
against pathogens. It consists of many cell types and mechanisms that non-specifically
destroy pathogens and assist the adaptive immune system in their detection and clear-
ance. For many years the innate immunity was thought to lack the ability to adapt to
pathogens. Recent studies have started to undermine this assumption—it seems that
innate immune system can also exhibit enhanced response when previously stimulated.
This mechanism is called “trained innate immunity” [30].
Studies on BCG provided a lot of information currently known about trained innate
immunity. This mechanism seems to be at least partially responsible for non-specific ef-
fects of BCG vaccine. Studies by Kleinnijenhuis et al. have shown that BCG vaccination
induces measurable, phenotypic changes in monocytes, the precursors to macrophages[16,
31]. Vaccination with BCG leads to increased expression of activation markers of mono-
cytes (CD14 and ITGAM), TLR4 and MRC1 [31]. Those changes persisted on monocytes
for up to one year [31]. Histone 3 lysine 4 trimethylation at IL6 and TNFA promotors
was also increased in monocytes of BCG-vaccinated mice [16]. This promotes the ex-
pression of those pro-inflammatory cytokines and is characteristic for trained monocytes
[16]. The training of monocytes seems to be dependent on NOD2 receptors, as NOD2
deficient mice showed no response [16].
Another mechanism in trained immunity is the Warburg effect. Upon activation
monocytes switch their metabolism from oxidation toward glycolysis by activation of
AKT-mTOR-HIF1α pathway [33]. This process may also make monocytes more suscep-
tible to activation by growth factors such as CSF1 [33].
Natural Killer cells, the innate immunity cells responsible for killing infected and
aberrant cells, can also produce a “memory” response. Experiments on mice shown that
after infection with mouse cytomegalovirus a new subpopulation of “memory” NK cells
that express higher levels of viral pattern recognizing receptors like LY49H [34] appears.

1.4
Early development of porcine immune
system
The immune system develops during the early stages of mammalian life. This must
be taken into account when doing experiments on such young animals. In mammals
a neonate possesses very little innate immunity and must be protected by maternal
antibodies acquired from mother in utero and/or by ingesting the colostrum, an antibody-
rich type of milk produced by the mother for a few weeks after giving birth. Pigs do not
get the maternal antibodies from the placenta and must absorb them through the milk
[36]. The uptake of the antibodies takes place during the first day of piglet’s life, as later
its gut closes, preventing the antibodies from passing into the bloodstream [37]. This
protection ceases after weaning, which usually performed when piglets are 2-3 weeks old.

The piglet does not produce it’s own antibodies during the first week of life, but after
that the amount of it’s own immunoglobulins increases steadily [38]. Other parts of the
piglet’s adaptive immune system also develop further over time: the amount of T-cells
and B-cells in the blood rises over first eight weeks of life, when the levels of monocytes
decrease [39].

1.5
qPCR
Real-time polymerase chain reaction (qPCR) is a method using PCR technique to mea-
sure the amount of DNA in sample. Combined with reverse transciption it allows for
measuring the gene expression at mRNA levels and is called RT-qPCR. The RNA used
for this method is usually extracted with a DNAse I treatment to remove any genomic
DNA. It is controlled for quality by measuring OD260/280 by spectrophotometry or by
measuring RNA Integrity Number (RIN) using electrophoresis. RNA of good quality is
usually considered to have RIN of over 5 [41] or OD260/280 1.8-2.0 [42]. The reverse tran-
scription is performed using primers with random sequence to cover all mRNA sequences
in the sample [42]. The amplified cDNA is then used in qPCR where the amplicon syn-
thesis is measured in real time using fluorescent dyes or probes. There are two main ways
of amplicon detection - intercalating dyes and probes. Intercalating dyes such as SYBR
green I bind to any double stranded DNA, which detects not only amplicons but also
primer-dimer pairs and other non-specific products and generates a higher fluorescence
when binding to longer products [43]. The specificity of the products measured by this
method can be assessed by measuring the melting curve of the sample by increasing the
temperature and measuring the fluorescence. Each amplicon has a characteristic melting
==PAGE:24==
6
1 Introduction
peak which can be distinguished by artefacts that melt in lower temperatures [43]. The
other method of detecting amplicons are hydrolysis probes such as the Taqman assay.
It uses marked probes that bind specifically to the amplicon and start emitting signal
after being cleaved by the Taq or Tth polymerase [43].
Fluorescence of the sample is usually reported by the machine as the Threshold
cycle (Ct) - the cycle when the fluorescence reached the predefined, above-background,
statistically significant fluorescence level [43]. The higher the Ct, the lower the expression.
That value can be then transformed into a quantitative results by constructing a standard
curve for the primer using dilution series. A good standard curve should have effeciency
of 90-100% and r2 value >0.98 [42]. 3 to 5 reference genes with stable expression should
be also used to correct the results for the differences in cDNA amount used and other
technical variations [44].
1.6
Hypotheses
Taking into the account the previous works on the mechanisms of the non-specific effects
of the BCG vaccination the following hypotheses were formed:
Hypothesis 1. BCG vaccination of neonatal piglets induces an systemic increase in
mRNA expression of genes related to inflammation, T-cell response and innate immunity.
Hypothesis 2. BCG vaccination of neonatal piglets induces elevated expression of genes
related to the phenomenon of ”trained immunity”, such as TLR4, NOD2 and CD14 in
blood.
The reasoning behind Hypothesis 1. was that the expression of those types of genes
could be an indication if the piglets do exhibit any non-specific reaction to the vaccina-
tion. The Hypothesis 2 was formed because trained immunity is thought to be the most
likely mechanism behind the non-specific effects of vaccinations.
Due to the results of the primary experiment another hypothesis was formed:
Hypothesis 3. Expression of certain immunity-related genes in blood changes over time
during the fist three weeks of live of a piglet.

cDNA synthesis

was mixed. For -RT master mix the volume of reverse transcriptase was substituted
with RNAse-free water. The genomic DNA-free RNA samples were mixed with 6 µl of
master mix or -RT master mix by pipetting and incubated in thermocycler at 42°C for

First, the primers (Sigma Aldrich, Germany) were diluted - 10 µl of each of the primers
(F and R) were mixed with 30 µl low-EDTA TE buffer (VWR) to achieve 20 µM solution.
5 µl of each primer was mixed together in and tube and filled up to 500 µl to create the
mix of all primers that would be used for pre-amplification.
The cDNA samples were first thawed on ice. 1 µl of TaqMan PreAmp Master Mix
(Applied Biosystems), 4 µl of low-EDTA TE buffer (VWR) and 2.5 µl of primer mix per
sample were mixed together and 7.5 µl of this mixture was added t the mini-eppendorf
tubes and mixed by pippeting with 2.5 µl of given cDNA samples. The tubes were then
incubated for 10 min in 95°C, then a desired number of cycles of 15 sec of 95°C and 4
min of 60°C, then put on ice.
3.5 µl of MilliQ water, 0.5 µl of reaction buffer (New England Biolabs, United States)
and 1 µl of exonuclease (New England Biolabs) per sample was mixed and then 4 µl
of it was added to all samples. The samples were mixed by pipetting and incubated in
thermocycler at 37°C for 30 min and then at 80°C for 15 min to remove any leftover
primers. The samples were then diluted 1:5 in 8 µl of low-EDTA TE buffer (VWR)
and frozen in -20°C until qPCR. The protocol for the Pre-amplification can be found in
Appendix F.

2.7.2
Statistical analysis
The data was analyzed in Graphpad Prism (Graphpad Software Inc., United States)
Software. As most of the groups fit the normal distribution the parametric tests were
used. The control and vaccinated groups were compared using unequal variances t-test.
The difference between gene expression in different time points was measured using
Repeated Measures ANOVA after removing the missing data (pigs # 4, 11, 12, 19, 22,
24, 29, 30, 31), as this model does not tolerate them. Due to the number of primers for
which the control and vaccinated groups were analyzed (n=51), the Bonferroni correction
(target p-value/number of comparisons) was used to adjust the statistical significance
threshold. The results with p<0.0098 were considered to be statistically significant.

CHAPTER 4
Discussion
Based on the current state of knowledge about the immune response to BCG in different
animals two hypotheses were stated at in the introduction of this thesis:
Hypothesis 1. BCG vaccination of neonatal piglets induces an systemic increase in
mRNA expression of genes related to inflammation, T-cell response and innate immunity.
Hypothesis 2. BCG vaccination of neonatal piglets induces elevated expression of genes
related to the phenomenon of ”trained immunity”, such as TLR4, NOD2 and CD14 in
blood.
The results of the experiment give enough evidence for both of the hypotheses to be
rejected, as both were based on the piglets showing changes in the expression of the
investigated genes in the vaccinated subjects in comparison to control. There was no
statistically significant difference between BCG-vaccinated and control group at any time
point. It can not be attributed to the method being not sensitive enough, as the changes
in gene expression were detected, only not between control and vaccinated groups, but
over time.
The method used to adjust the p-value threshold could also mask the differences,
as it is very conservative and may give false negatives. Even if the unadjusted p-value
threshold p=0.05 was used only 4 out of 51 investigated genes (MDA5, RIGI, SLA-DRB
and CSF1) showed statistically significant differences in expression. In all four cases the
difference was confined to only one time point and the other primers for RIGI, SLA-DRB
and CSF1 used in the experiment did not detect any significant differences in mRNA
levels. The statistical power could also be low due to the sample size, but it was an
aspect of the experiment that was hard to control due to the fact that the exact number
of live piglets birthed and the mortality of animals through the course of the experiment
can not be planned in advance. The analysis used also did not take into the account
such variables as sex and litter into account.
Those results do not mean that there are no differences in levels of the proteins
coded by those genes, as different parts of the immune system — such as some of the
pro-inflammatory genes [45, 46], interferons [47] and TLRs [46] — are regulated post-
transcriptionally.
There is a chance that the pigs just don’t have an immune response to BCG in
general, but there are proofs of response to BCG in pigs [48] and closely-related boars
[49]. Another reason for the lack of response may be maternal antibody interference,
which has been observed for different vaccines in humans [50] and is also present in pigs
[51]. An evidence to the contrary is that human neonates are routinely vaccinated at
==PAGE:52==
34
4 Discussion
birth when they are protected by the maternal antibodies absorbed during gestation and
do exhibit the protective effects of the BCG. The dose used might have been too low, as
in most experiments on BCG it usually higher. However, most of the research on BCG
response have been conducted on adult animals with fully developed immune system.
The route of immunization is an another factor that may influence the protective effects
of the vaccine [52, 53]. The studies on boars have been conducted using the oral route
of delivery [49, 54].
There are other evidences that the piglets respond poorly to the vaccination. The
pigs failed to form the BCG scar at the time of completion of the study. The scar might
have appeared later - in human infants it may take 6 months or longer to develop a
scarring, though it often appears after 12 weeks of vaccination [55]. Around 10% of
human children does not develop a BCG scar, especially when vaccinated at birth, but
it does not correlate with a lack of immune response to the vaccine [56, 57]. In a study of
persistence of BCG in the vaccinated pigs researched by this research group the pigs have
failed to form a BCG scar at the time of slaughter (20 weeks) [58, data not published].
The IFNG Release Assay conducted on the blood samples from the piglets enrolled in
the experiment as a part of a larger present study have also shown that the vaccinated
piglets exhibited low response to the tuberculosis antigens [58, data not published].
The lack of differences does not indicated the lack of protective effect of the vaccine
or lack of effect at all, as it might induce the changes in expression of different genes that
the ones investigated. Still, this result is quite surprising given the amount of examples
of the vaccine inducing an immunological effect in different adult and neonatal animals
in the literature.
Due to the results of the primary experiment another hypothesis was also created:
Hypothesis 3. Expression of certain immunity-related genes in blood changes over time
during the fist three weeks of live of a piglet.
The changes over time observed in the first part of the experiment were largely
proven to be highly statistically significant by the RM ANOVA test, so there is a hight
probability that they are true differences. The fact that the changes over time could
be seen in expressions detected by different primers amplifying the same gene further
strengthens this claim. This method also required removal of some data which without
doubt influenced the results, but most of the differences could be clearly seen when
comparing the control and vaccinated groups in the first part of experiment. Despite
this fact the results still support this hypothesis strongly.
Expression of most of the differentially expressed genes was falling - IL1B, IL1RN,
IL1RAP, TLR2, NOD2, CD14, ITGAM, MYD88 and C3 have all exhibited a decrease in
expression over time. The two cell markers in this group - CD14 and ITGAM are markers
of monocytes and macrophages [59, p.89, 104]. IL1B and IL1RN are also produced by
those cells [59, p.779] and IL1RAP expression is tied to IL1B [60]. TLR2 [59, p.86,]
and NOD2 are also present in macrophages. Though they may be some other reasons
for declining expression of those genes, the decrease in number of macrophages in blood
would be the simplest answer and would be consistent with results obtained by Grierson

4.1
Future work
As the study did not find any differences between the vaccinated and control animals
the results should be validated. Rerunning the whole experiment is not feasible given
the number of samples and investigated genes. Some of the samples could be rerun
with higher number of pre-amplification cycles - some of the low-expressed genes that
“escaped” the detection thresholds of the machine might become detectable then. The
primers that were found to produce low detection, efficiency or “bad” melting curves
could be validated on a different qPCR system using positive controls.

The levels of proteins encoded by the investigated genes could be measured using
ELISA or similar method, as mRNA levels may not reflect the actual protein levels.
Given that the results also suggest the reduction of macrophages in blood and increase
of T-cell levels performing a flow cytometry focusing on those two groups would be able
to confirm those findings.
The statistical tests used in this experiment were also lacking, as they did not take
into account such variables as sex and litter and RM ANOVA could not be used on
whole data set due to missing values. It would be best to use a mixed model to take
those variables and the missing data into account. Other, less conservative methods
than Bonferroni correction could also be used to adjust the p-value. A bigger sample
size would be a good choice for a future experiment as the sample size in this experiment
might result in too low statistical power.
The presence of the protective effects of the vaccination should also be verified, as
the results of this experiment may not be a good indicator of the presence of absence of
the non-specific effects. A challenge with an non-mycobacterial or viral pathogen and
analysis of survival and disease progression in the animals would be the best way to test
it, as unlike this experiment it would measure the direct effects of the vaccine.
4.2
Conclusions
In this project the expression of different genes related to innate immunity and T-cell
response in BCG vaccinated and control piglet groups was measured. The motivation
for that was investigating the immune response of piglets could indicate if vaccination
of pig neonates with BCG would be a feasible way of protection. The experiment was
conducted on three litters of pigs (n=32), which were randomized, assigned to one of
two groups and injected with either BCG or Sauton. The blood samples were taken
from piglets before the injection, and at 1 day, 8 days and 21 after the injection. Total
mRNA was purified from blood samples. cDNA was synthesized from mRNA and pre-
amplified. The expression of genes of interest related to T-cell response, innate immunity
and inflammation was measured using qPCR. No statistically significant differences in
expression between the vaccinated and control group were found at any time point, but
some of the genes have shown significant changes in expression over time. The lack of
difference between control suggests that the vaccination produced no immune response,
but the mRNA levels do not necessarily reflect protein expression and the vaccination
might have induce changes in expression of genes that were outside the scope of this
project. The changes in gene expression over time may indicate the decrease in number
of macrophages in blood and increase in number of T-cells.
The statistical methods used to analyze the data were not optimal, but more ad-
vanced test could not be used due to restrictions of software and time. Many primers
used in the experiment should be verified due as their detection was low or they were am-

APPENDIX B
RNA purification protocol
1. Centrifuge the PAXgene Blood RNA Tube for 10 min at 3000–5000 x g using a
swing-out rotor.
2. Remove the supernatant by decanting or pipetting. Add 4 ml RNase-free water to
the pellet, and close the tube using a fresh secondary Hemogard closure.
3. Vortex until the pellet is visibly dissolved, and centrifuge for 10 min at 3000–
5000 x g using a swing-out rotor. Remove the entire supernatant by decanting or
pipetting, and discard.
4. Add 350 µl Buffer BM1, and vortex until the pellet is visibly dissolved.
5. Pipet the sample into a 1.5 ml microcentrifuge tube. Add 300 µl Buffer BM2 and
40 µl proteinase K. Mix by vortexing for 5 s, and incubate for 10 min at 55°C in a
shaker–incubator at 400–1400 rpm. After incubation, set the temperature of the
shaker–incubator to 65°C for use in step 20.
6. Pipet the sample into a PAXgene Shredder spin column (lilac) placed in a 2 ml
processing tube, and centrifuge for 3 min at full speed (do not exceed 20,000 x g).
7. Carefully transfer the entire supernatant of the flow-through from the PAXgene
Shredder spin column to a new 1.5 ml microcentrifuge tube without disturbing the
pellet in the processing tube.
8. Add 700 µl of isopropanol (100%, purity grade p.a.), and mix by vortexing.
9. Pipet 700 µl sample into the PAXgene RNA spin column (red) placed in a 2 ml
processing tube. Close the lid gently, and centrifuge for 1 min at 8000–20,000
x g. Place the spin column in a new 2 ml processing tube, and discard the old
processing tube containing flow-through.
10. Pipet the remaining sample into the PAXgene RNA spin column (red). Close the
lid gently, and centrifuge for 1 min at 8000–20,000 x g. Place the spin column
in a new 2 ml processing tube, and discard the old processing tube containing
flow-through.
11. Add 350 µl Buffer BM3 to the PAXgene RNA spin column. Close the lid gently,
and centrifuge for 15 s at 8000–20,000 x g. Place the spin column in a new 2 ml
processing tube, and discard the old processing tube containing flow-through.

B RNA purification protocol
Note: Buffer BM3 is supplied as a concentrate. Ensure that ethanol is added to
Buffer BM3 before use.
12. Add 10 µl DNase I stock solution to 70 µl Buffer RDD in a 1.5 ml microcentrifuge
tube. Mix by gently flicking the tube, and centrifuge briefly to collect residual
liquid from the sides of the tube.
13. Pipet the DNase I incubation mix (80 µl) directly onto the PAXgene RNA spin
column membrane, and incubate on the benchtop (20–30°C) for 15 min.
14. Add 350 µl Buffer BM3 to the PAXgene RNA spin column. Close the lid gently,
and centrifuge for 15 s at 8000–20,000 x g. Place the spin column in a new 2 ml

15. Add 500 µl Buffer BM4 to the PAXgene RNA spin column. Close the lid gently,
and centrifuge for 15 s at 8000–20,000 x g. Discard the flow-through. Place the
spin column in a new 2 ml processing tube, and discard the old processing tube
containing flow-through.
Note: Buffer BM4 is supplied as a concentrate. Ensure that ethanol is added to
Buffer BM4 before use.
16. Add another 500 µl Buffer BM4 to the PAXgene RNA spin column. Close the lid
gently, and centrifuge for 2 min at 8000–20,000 x g.
17. Discard the processing tube containing flow-through, and place the PAXgene RNA
spin column in a new 2 ml processing tube (supplied). Centrifuge at 8000–20,000
x g for 1 min.
18. Discard the processing tube containing flow-through. Place the PAXgene RNA
spin column in a new 1.5 ml microcentrifuge tube, and pipet 40 µl Buffer BR5
directly onto the spin column membrane. Close the lid gently, and centrifuge for
1 min at 8000–20,000 x g to elute the RNA.
19. Repeat the elution step (step 18) as described, using 40 µl Buffer BR5 and the
same microcentrifuge tube.
20. Incubate the eluate for 5 min at 65°C in the shaker–incubator without shaking.
After incubation, chill immediately on ice.
21. If the RNA eluate will not be used immediately, store at –20°C or –70°C. Since the
RNA remains denatured after freezing and thawing, it is not necessary to repeat
the incubation at 65°C.
