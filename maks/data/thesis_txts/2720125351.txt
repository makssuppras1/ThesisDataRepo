Abstract 
Climate change induced temperature increases are causing the Greenland ice sheet to melt faster 
which alters the physical conditions in Arctic ecosystems. Efforts have been put into identifying key 
ecosystem components, termed tipping elements, and their thresholds towards environmental or 
ecological stressors. If such thresholds are crossed it can shift entire ecosystems into an alternate state 
– a phenomenon known as a cascading effect. One identified tipping element is the Greenland ice 
sheet that is currently melting at increased rates due to elevated temperatures in the Arctic. This could 
potentially reshape physical conditions in adjacent coastal waters. 
In this study, I investigated a prokaryotic community in a high Arctic fjord in NE Greenland in order 
to identify ecological stressors that might remodel it. Potential interspecies interaction on artificial 
marine snow was also investigated. In total, the cell abundance of six different prokaryotic taxa was 
enumerated from samples collected in the NE Greenland fjord, Ardencaple, in August 2022 or from 
ex situ incubation experiments with a natural microbial community from the Adriatic Sea collected 
in September 2022. Incubations were carried out either with or without marine snow produced from 
autoclaved diatom biomass (species Chaetoceros socialis). Samples were processed using CARD-
FISH and cells were enumerated from image analysis using the open software ACMEtool3.  
Bacteroidetes and Crenarchaeota were the two taxa with the highest abundance throughout the fjord. 
They were followed by Euryarchaeota and finally Alteromonas. No Vibrio was found in the 
Ardencaple fjord. In addition, my findings suggest that the prokaryotic community in the Ardencaple 
fjord is indirectly shaped by the vertical salinity gradient as an expression of freshwater input, 
probably from glacier meltwater. This could lead to a strengthened stratification in the fjord which 
will restrict the vertical transport of POM from the surface to the fjord interior and vis nutrient mixing.  
Furthermore, I found indications that Crenarchaeota makes up a considerable fraction of the nitrifier 
community at depth in the Ardencaple fjord, while Euryarchaeota may be a contributory player in 
ammonification. Bacteroidetes appeared inhibited by Alteromonas and Vibrio during incubations 
with marine snow. However, Alteromonas and Vibrio abundances was low or absent in the 
Ardencaple fjord, presumably due to temperature limitation, and their apparent role in interspecies 
interactions are for now not of significance.  
Overall, my findings demonstrate that climate change has the potential to change the microbial 
community considerably - with implications to POM transport and degradation in addition to overall 
nutrient cycling. Finally, the role of interspecies interaction should be taken into account in future 
studies evaluating the community’s response to environmental changes induced by climate change.

1 Introduction 
1.1 Increased freshwater inflow may alter carbon cycling in Arctic fjords 
Anthropogenic emissions of carbon dioxide (CO2), primarily from burning of fossil fuels, have 
increased the atmospheric CO2 concentration, and is at its highest within the last 800,000 years and 
far exceeds the natural multi-millennial variations (IPCC, 2021). This change has directly impacted 
the stability of the global climate, resulting in adverse effects such as extreme weather events, loss of 
tropical and boreal forests, as well as ocean acidification, deoxygenation, and increased stratification. 
Most pronounced are the effects observed in the polar regions, such as the Arctic (IPCC, 2021). 
The consequences of the ongoing climate change on the constituting elements of an ecosystem can 
be caused by one or more stressors and do not necessarily follow a linear response (May, 1977; 
Scheffer et al., 2001). One approach to predict the environmental and ecological consequences of 
climate change is to identify tipping elements and their associated tipping points. By definition, a 
tipping element is a key component in a system that can have multiple configurations. Changes in a 
tipping element configuration can happen as a response to one or more stressors, environmental or 
ecological, and occurs after crossing a threshold, the tipping point. This can then lead to consequential 
and even irreversible changes to the functions of a system (Duarte et al., 2012). 
Since the mid-1900’s the ice coverage of the Arctic Sea, in September, has decreased by ca. 40% and 
the rate of ice sheet loss has increased by a factor four within the 1900s (IPCC, 2021). This will cause 
environmental alterations as the Artic Sea ice and the Greenland ice sheet (GrIS) are considered 
global tipping elements due to their central role in i.a. water mixing and deep-water formation 
(Reigstad et al., 2008; Rudels & Quadfasel, 1991). Furthermore, evidence that the GrIS melting is 
reaching its tipping point has been demonstrated (Boers & Rypdal, 2021). In the fjords of Greenland, 
changes in melting rates has increased the freshwater discharge which can affect the fjord 
hydrography in one of two ways: (1) Meltwater from sea ice, glaciers, or ice sheets enters the fjords 
as surface runoff. This freshwater input reduces the surface salinity and thus water density. This 
intensifies the vertical stratification and restricts water mixing and POM export of particulate organic 
matter (POM) (Reigstad et al., 2008); (2) fjords with marine-terminating glaciers and ice bergs 
receive subglacial discharge that creates a buoyancy driven upwelling at the glacial outlet which 
enhances vertical mixing (Meire et al., 2017). As glaciers continue to melt, marine-terminating 
glaciers may retreat above sea level to become land-terminating glaciers. In such a scenario, the fjords 
could experience prolonged or permanent summer conditions with little to no ice cover and where 
wind and rotation are dominant mixing factors (Cottier et al., 2010). 
Vertical mixing is a key environmental factor in fjord ecology as it supplies the surface with essential 
nutrients that stimulate phytoplankton growth. Phytoplankton forms the base of the marine food web 
through the grazing food chain. However, earlier onset of the seasonal sea ice retreat has led to a 
temporal shift in the phytoplankton spring bloom that could affect e.g., the reproductive timing of 
grazing copepods (Daase et al., 2013). Additionally, the reduced nutrient supply from vertical mixing 
might result in a decrease in phytoplankton cell size which may further reduce the food web transfer

dynamic can thus cause a cascade effect through the marine ecosystem. 
1.2 Oceanic carbon pumps 
Apart from direct dissolution of CO2 and subsequent vertical transport via downwelling in the 
solubility pump, carbon in the ocean is also sequestered through the vertical transport of particulate 
organic matter (POM). Initially, phytoplankton fixate CO2 into biomass, which is subsequently 
funnelled through the grazing food chain. When the phytoplankton dies, the carbon ends up as i.a. 
POM that gradually sinks through the water column while at the same time being degraded and 
remineralised by microbial and zooplankton activities. The vertical transport of POM and 
remineralisation at depth maintains the dissolved inorganic carbon (DIC) gradient in the water column 
and is the core concept of the Soft Tissue Pump (STP) (Volk & Hoffert, 1985). In addition to this, 
some phytoplankton species also assimilate (bi)carbonate (HCO3-/CO32-) together with calcium ions 
(Ca2+) to form their hard calcium carbonate (CaCO3) shells. This further adds to the amount of carbon 
that can be transported vertically as POM and is termed the Carbonate Pump (CP) (Volk & Hoffert, 
1985). Collectively, the STP and CP will be referred to as the biological carbon pump (BCP) in this 
report (see Table 1 for overview of the different pumps described in this report). A total of 2.5±0.6 
PgC yr-1 of anthropogenic CO2 is estimated to be taken up by the ocean (Candall et al., 2021).

La Rocha & Passow, 2007). Additionally, POM sinking velocity is also highly dependent on initial 
phytoplankton cell size and general composition that influence the density of the POM (de La Rocha 
& Passow, 2007). A large fraction of the artic primary production and POM export is by diatoms 
(Agustí et al., 2020; Krause et al., 2018) that in addition are known to produce high concentrations of 
transparent exopolymer particles (TEP). TEP have shown to function as a form of glue for organic 
matter and thus promote aggregation that leads to faster sinking POM (Alldredge et al., 1993). The 
efficiency of the BCP in the Artic is thus likely to changes because of an altered phytoplankton 
community composition (Falkowski & Oliver, 2007). A simultaneous increase in allochthonous 
organic matter supply to the fjords via continuous glacial meltwater and river discharge, as predicted 
by the IPCC (2021), has the potential to alter the biogeochemical landscape within the fjords and 
adjacent coastal systems (Balmonte et al., 2020; Paulsen et al., 2017). These changes combined may 
further alter yet another pump, the Microbial Carbon Pump (MCP) (Jiao et al., 2010). 
1.2.1 The Microbial Carbon Pump 
The MCP is the newest addition to the BCP umbrella. It diverges from the other described pumps by 
not taking the vertical transport of carbon and appertaining DIC gradient into account. Instead, it 
focuses on the processes involved in microbial carbon cycling and its products and is estimated to 
sequester 0.3-0.7 PgC yr-1 (Legendre et al., 2015). Three pathways have been suggested for the 
microbially mediated carbon sequestration: i) viral lysis of microbial cells; ii) microbial cell 
production and proliferation; and iii) microbial degradation of POM (Jiao et al., 2010). Common for 
all three pathways is the apparent production of long-lived dissolved organic matter (DOM). This 
type of DOM can be of either high or low biologic value, which results in differences in turnover 
rates. Based on the turnover rates, an operational classification of DOM has been adopted and 
includes i.a., labile DOM (LDOM), which has an oceanic residence time of ~0.001 years, and 
refractory DOM (RDOM) with a residence time of ~16,000 years (Hansell, 2013). Additionally, 
DOM can be separated based on the C:N:P ratios: LDOM = 199:20:1; and RDOM = ~3,511:202:1 
(Hopkinson & Vallino, 2005). RDOM is thus both relatively more enriched in carbon and has a longer 
residence time compared to both LDOM and biomass (Redfield ratio 106:16:1). The MCP thus 
efficiently sequesters carbon in the ocean. However, a study by Arrieta et al. (2015) proposed that 
deep-ocean LDOC is too diluted to compensate for the metabolic cost of utilizing it, thus leaving it 
untouched. Regardless, the production of DOC via either microbial or other processes is a way to 
store carbon from the surface for prolonged periods of time. 
In addition to respiring organic matter, microorganisms also play a key role in other major nutrient 
cycles such as the nitrogen cycle that is often interconnected with the carbon cycle (Gruber, 2008). 
Changes in fjord hydrography could thus alter both the MCP via reduced POM export as well major 
nutrient cycling. Furthermore, POM acts as microbial hotspots where microbes are in close proximity 
to others which promotes competition for the available nutrients (Simon et al., 2002). Interspecies 
interactions in associations with POM could thus also play a major factor in a changing Arctic – does 
it depend on POM origin, composition, size? Knowing the microbial community composition and its 
response to environmental variables is thus important to predict the effect that climate change may 
have on the marine biogeochemical landscape and will therefore be the focus in this report.

2 Objectives and Hypotheses 
The aim of this study was to understand how the prokaryotic community in association with 
particulate organic matter interact with each other and the environment. This was to understand how 
a changing Arctic might shape the current prokaryotic community structure, which is an aspect in the 
marine carbon cycle that is still under heavy investigation. 
To approach the aim, I analysed samples collected in a high Arctic fjord in addition to an incubation 
study with a natural community from the Adriatic Sea. the data was interpreted to answer four 
hypotheses - two regarding the effect of environmental factors (a) and two regarding interspecies 
interaction (b): 
i.a) The cell abundance of the investigated taxa will change along the horizontal transect in the fjord; 
ii.a) Salinity will be the main driver for changes in the procaryotic cell abundance as seen in other 
studies (Balmonte et al., 2020);  i.b) The prokaryotic abundance will be higher when incubated with 
marine snow; and ii.b) Bacteroidetes will be the dominating taxa in incubations with marine snow 
due to its adaptations to a particle associated lifestyle (Fernández-Gómez et al., 2013).

3.2 Algae cultivation and marine snow formation 
The diatom Chaetoceros socialis (CCAP strain 1010/24, Scotland UK) was cultured in f/2 + Si media 
in accordance with the supplier (CCAP, 2022). The media was prepared with aged sea water (hence 
ASW) collected in the Adriatic Sea (salinity ~36‰). Cultivation was conducted at 23 °C (±1.5) with 
a 16:8 hours light:dark photocycle (~37 µmol photons m-2 s-1). Growth was monitored by OD 
measurements using a Hitachi U-2001 Spectrophotometer at 750 nm. The diatom culture was 
autoclaved at the end of the exponential growth phase and stored at room temperature (RT) until use. 
Initial cultivation attempts of C. socialis in sterilised Schott bottles with magnetic stirring (low rpm) 
resulted in low density cultures (Log(abs) <0.2, data not shown) that shortly after reaching the 
stationary growth phase, collapsed. After transferring the culture to bubble reactors it was possible to 
grow the culture in high density while still harvesting at the end of the exponential growth phase, thus 
assuming a high bacteria:algae ratio. 
Three 10L rolling tanks (0.1M HCl sterilised) were inoculated with a natural microbial community 
collected in the Adriatic Sea in September 2022 and added in a ratio of 1:10 L (natural microbial 
community : total tank volume). All particles were removed by vacuum filtration prior to inoculation 
(type ATTP, 0.8µm pore size, 47mm diameter, Merch Millipore, Germany). Two tanks also received 
autoclaved C. socialis culture in a ratio of 1:3 that would form the marine snow particles and function 
as food base for the natural microbial community. Finally, all tanks were topped with sterile filtered 
aged sea water (type GTTP, 0.22 µm pore size, 47mm diameter, Merck Millipore, Germany) until 
full. All tanks were then placed on a rolling table to form marine snow (salinity ~36‰, temperature 
23 °C (±1.5)). Samples were collected from each tank every 6 hours for 3 days, fixed in 2%

rolling tank with marine snow. The first round of treatment tests included ultrasonication at 37 kHz 
for 30 sec., 100 mM EDTA, or a combination of the two. EDTA showed to have the greatest potential 
to disperse cells, whereas ultrasonication displayed little to no effect (Figure 3). 
Based on these results, a series of EDTA concentrations were tested on new samples from the same

min at RT and subsequently vortexed for 3 min before filtration. The EDTA series was conducted in 
triplicates. The results showed that the bacterial cell count in the sample treated with 100mM EDTA 
caused the greatest difference in cell count compared to control sample (Tukey multiple comparisons 
of means, α≤0.1, n=15), and thus adopted into the protocol (Figure 4).

3.3 CARD-FISH, image acquisition, and processing 
To investigate the microbial abundance, Catalysed Reporter Deposition Fluorescence In Situ 
Hybridisation (CARD-FISH) was performed on both the Arctic and the incubation samples in 
accordance with the protocols described in Pernthaler et al. (2002) and Teira et al. (2004). In short: 
Cell material was fixed in 2% formaldehyde (final conc.), filtered onto wite polycarbonate filters 
(type GTTP, pore size 0.2 µm, diameter 25mm, Merc Millipore, Germany), and embedded in 0.1% 
agarose. Embedded filters were then labelled according to probe use and cut into 1/12 pie-shaped 
pieces. Bacterial cells were made permeable with a lysozyme mix while archaeal cells were made 
permeable with 0.1M HCl. Permeabilization was stopped by incubation in 0.01M HCl for 20 min at 
RT. Hybridization was conducted in the dark while incubating at 35 ℃ for 15 hours (see Table 3 for 
formamide concentrations). After hybridization, all filter slices were washed with a washing buffer 
(0.1% blocking buffer) at 37 ℃ for 15 min followed by incubation in Phosphate Buffered Saline with 
Triton X-100 (PBS-T) for another 15 min. Samples were amplified with AlexaFlour555 at 46 ℃ 
incubation for 15 min followed by a final incubation in PBS-T for 15 min (see Herndl (2018) for 
details of applied chemicals and concentrations). Samples were mounted onto glass slides with DAPI 
as counter stain to obtain total prokaryotic cell counts. To control for potential cell loss during the 
CARD-FISH procedure, one filter slice from each embedded sample was also mounted with DAPI in 
addition to a blank filter to control for contamination in the agarose.

4.1 CARD-FISH probe specificity 
The applied CARD-FISH probes (Table 3) covered the targeted taxa well. The EUB I-III probe mix 
covered a total of 88.5% of all sequence entries associated with bacteria while 0.1% covered sequence 
entries of Eukaryota (probe test, www.arb-silva.de). The Cf319a probe covered 45% of gene sequence 
entries within the phylum Bacteroidota. However, Bacteroidota is according to the NCBI taxonomy 
browser (Schoch et al., 2020) a phylum within the Bacteroidetes clade while Bacteroidetes is included 
as a taxon within Bacteroidota on www.arb-silva.de. Nonetheless, the probe covered 41.3% of the 
gene entries of Bacteroidetes which is near to that of Bacteroidota. The Alt1413 covered 56.5% of 
gene sequence entries in the family Alteromonadaceae and 38.8% of the gene sequence entries in the 
Colwelliaceae family. The GV probe covered 72.9% of the sequence entries within the Vibrionaceae 
family and 83.1% of the entries within the Genus Vibrio. The Crenarchaeota probes, Cren537 and 
Cren554, covered 16.2% of all gene sequence entries for Archaea and 44% of all entries in 
Crenarchaeota. The Eury806 probe covered 2.4% of gene entries in Archaea and 16.9% of the 
Candidatus phylum Thermoplasmatota. Upon further inspection, 82.6% of Marine group II was 
covered by the Eury806 probe which is affiliated with Euryarchaeota in the NCBI taxonomy browser 
(Schoch et al., 2020). I thus assume that the Eury806 probe covers Euryarchaeota well.  
The probe test software by www.arb-silva.de proved to be a useful tool for checking probe specificity. 
The user does however need to be aware of the taxonomy and cross reference with other taxonomic 
databases. Here I referenced the NCBI taxonomy browser (Schoch et al., 2020) which is just one of 
many available taxonomic databases but is not a taxonomic or phylogenetic authority in itself and 
needs to be kept in mind.

4.2 Environmental conditions in Ardencaple fjord 
The temperature profile of the Ardencaple fjord (Figure 5a) showed a three-layered system consisting 
of a relatively shallow and warm surface layer of ca. +1.5 ℃, a cold intermediate layer of ca. -1.5 ℃ 
penetrating ca. 150-200m into the water column and a relative warm bottom layer of ca. +1.5 ℃. The 
bottom layer at stations 10 and 11 was ca. 0.5 ℃ warmer than at stations 8 and 12 and was separated 
from stations 10 and 11 by a vertical thermocline. Salinity gradually decreased with depth from ~29 
at the surface to ~35 at 150m and below (Figure 5b). Water density followed the salinity profile. 
Nitrate (NO3-) and ammonium (NH4+) concentrations was on average 6.1±5.9 µM and 1.04±0.89 µM, 
respectively. While NO3- concentrations increased with depth, NH4+ concentrations were relatively 
low throughout the fjord except at stations 12 (Figure 5c & 5d). Deep chlorophyll max (DCM) was 
between 20 and 40m and reached up to 0.8 mg m-3 at stations 8 and 12 (Figure 5e). 
Average nitrite (NO2-) concentration was 0.031±0.03 µmol L-1 and peaked (0.1 µmol L-1) in the DCM 
layer at station 8. The average phosphate (PO43-) concentration was 0.68±0.17 µM and increased with 
depth. The average silicate (SiO2) concentrations were 6.69±2-33 µM. Surface PO43- and SiO2 
concentrations was both relatively depleted at stations 10 and 11. Oxygen (O2) had an inverse profile 
to salinity and decreased from ~400 µM at the surface to ~250 µM from 200m and below throughout 
the fjord.

5 Discussion 
The objective of this study was to investigate the effect of environmental factors on the prokaryotic 
community in a high Artic fjord and deduce potential interspecies interactions in particle-associated 
communities.  Prokaryotic cell abundance of six different taxa was enumerated using CARD-FISH 
from samples collected in the NE Greenland fjord, Ardencaple, and ex situ incubation experiments 
of natural microbial community either with or without marine snow. The natural community was 
collected in the Adriatic Sea. Four hypotheses were formulated, two regarding the Arctic community 
(a) and two regarding the incubation study (b): i.a) The cell abundance of the investigated taxa will 
change along the horizontal transect in the fjord; ii.a) Salinity will be the main driver for changes in 
the procaryotic cell abundance; i.b) The prokaryotic abundance will be higher when incubated with 
marine snow; and ii.b) Bacteroidetes will be the dominating taxa in incubations with marine snow. 
No significant difference in total or relative cell abundance for any taxa was observed between 
stations in the Ardencaple fjord (Figures 7 & 8) (one-way ANOVA, n=18, p≤0.05), thus rejecting 
hypothesis i.a. Both bacteria, Bacteroidetes, and Crenarchaeota correlated significantly with salinity 
and could be modelled based on it in a MLR model (Tables 4 & 5). This led to an initial confirmation 
of hypothesis ii.a. However, after consulting other studies, it was thought more likely that salinity had 
an indirect effect and water origin meltwater was the main predictor. Increased meltwater input was 
suggested to be able to increase POM attenuation in surface waters from strengthened stratification. 
Crenarchaeota and Euryarchaeota both correlated with NO3- and were as such believed to be active 
players in the fjord nitrogen cycle. However, ca. 30% of the variance between stations was not 
explained by the measured environmental variables from the Ardencaple fjord (Figure 6). It is 
therefore possible, that the prokaryotic community in Ardencaple was shaped by factors not included 
in this study. 
Although total abundance of all taxa was higher when incubated with marine snow (Figure 9), thus 
confirming hypothesis i.b, Bacteroidetes total and relative abundance was considerably reduced 
compared to the control tank. This rejected hypothesis ii.b. Furthermore, the cell specific growth rate 
of Bacteroidetes was the only one lower when incubated with marine snow than without (Table 6). 
A possible explanation of this was antagonistic interaction with Alteromonas and Vibrio. 
5.1 The prokaryotic community composition in the Ardencaple fjord may be defined 
by meltwater input 
Although Crenarchaeota and Bacteroidetes showed opposite depth profiles, their total and relative 
abundances were similar. Average total and relative abundance of Crenarchaeota and Bacteroidetes 
was 1.7∙104±2.5∙104 and 3.7∙104±4.1∙104 cells mL-1, respectively (Figure 7), and 6.4±6.8 and 7±4.2%, 
respectively (Figure 8). These abundance values are overall in good agreement with other studies 
investigating the microbial community in high latitude coastal and fjord areas (Amano-Sato et al., 
2013; Balmonte et al., 2020; Gómez-Pereira et al., 2010; Gutiérrez et al., 2015; Herndl et al., 2005; 
Zeng et al., 2009). The total abundance of Crenarchaeota and Bacteroidetes both significantly 
correlated with salinity and could furthermore be modelled based on it (Tables 4 & 5) but with 
opposite trends. While Crenarchaeota increased with increasing salinity, Bacteroidetes decreased. 
Where Bacteroidetes total and relative abundance decreased with depth, Crenarchaeota increased 
==PAGE:24==
Page 24 of 36 
 
(Figures 7 & 8) like salinity (Figure 5b). The apparent dependence on salinity could thus simply be 
an expression of depth. However, a study by Galand et al. (2008) from the western Canadian Arctic 
found higher relative abundances of Crenarchaeota in marine waters compared to riverine and 
coastal. This is further supported by the data of Gutiérrez et al. (2015) present in a study from a 
Chilean Patagonian fjord that. Here they only found high numbers of Crenarchaeota in samples 
collected during winter, coinciding with the seasonally lower freshwater input via meltwater. All of 
this align well with my findings of higher Crenarchaeota total and relative abundance at depth 
presented above. 
Moreover, other studies also showed an apparent link between salinity and Bacteroidetes abundance 
(Balmonte et al., 2020; Gómez-Pereira et al., 2010; Gutiérrez et al., 2015; Zeng et al., 2009). The 
study by Gómez-Pereira et al. (2010) demonstrated that the relative abundance of Bacteroidetes 
increased with latitude along a north Atlantic transect ending on the SE Greenland shelf. Relative cell 
abundance was in this study also calculated from total cell abundance obtained using CARD-FISH. 
However, they reported higher relative abundance of Bacteroides (up to 22%) than that from 
Ardencaple fjord. However, the depth distribution was similarly distribution (Figure 8). The higher 
relative abundance might be due to the sample site being further offshore than in this study. Indeed, 
the relative abundance of Bacteroidetes reported by Gutiérrez et al. (2015) was more like the one 
reported in this report. It was highest at the surface within the fjord (Chilean Patagonia) compared to 
the adjacent channel. This distribution was linked to glacial meltwater which was also the case in the 
study by Balmonte et al. (2020) from the Tyrolerfjord and Yong Sound in NE Greenland. However, 
they observed an increase in Bacteroidetes abundance with increasing salinity. This might have been 
due to differences in freshwater vs saltwater input. 
The study by Zeng et al. (2009) from the Kongsfjorden (Spitsbergen) showed high relative abundance 
of Bacteroidetes in surface and bottom waters throughout the fjord except at one station with apparent 
freshwater input. Here, Bacteroidetes only occupied a small fraction of the bottom water microbial 
community. This may be due to a restriction in vertical POM transport from a strengthened 
stratification (Reigstad et al., 2008). According to a study by Thiele et al. (2015) conducted offshore 
of Cape Blanc (Mauritania), the particle associated microbial community composition is a result of 
internal changes rather than de novo colonisation with depth. It is thus likely that an intensified 
stratification would diminish the abundance of Bacteroidetes with depth as they are generally adapted 
to a particle associated lifestyle (Fernández-Gómez et al., 2013; Pinhassi et al., 2004).  Thiele et al. 
(2015) also found higher relative abundance of Bacteroidetes on particles compared to free-living 
(13.7±10.5% vs 2.9±0.1%).  
Furthermore, a study by Bhatia et al. (2013) from SW Greenland reported high annual POC fluxes 
from the GrIC via meltwater which agrees with Paulsen et al. (2017) from Yong Sound (NE 
Greenland). Here, Paulsen et al. (2017) additionally showed that phytoplankton was the biggest 
source of organic matter for microbial growth which explains the significant correlations 
Crenarchaeota and Bacteroidetes had with phytoplankton (Table 4). Furthermore, the highest 
relative abundance of both taxa was observed at station 8 and 12 where two apparent phytoplankton 
blooms were measured (Figure 5e). I therefore argue that salinity indirectly dictates the microbial 
community composition and distribution in the Ardencaple fjord by restricting POM export from the 
==PAGE:25==
Page 25 of 36 
 
surface due to a strengthened stratification (Reigstad et al., 2008). The measured surface salinity in 
the Ardencaple fjord at the time of sampling was 29.2±0.1 PSU whereafter it increased to >31 PSU 
from the DCM layer and down (Figure 5b). This indicates that the fjord received freshwater 
discharge sometime before sampling. Even though this effect will mostly be seasonal it will probably 
become more intense and prolonged as global temperatures is expected to continue to increase toward 
year 2100 in most climate change scenarios (IPCC, 2021). Increased meltwater input might cause a 
shift in the microbial community towards an alternate state. Furthermore, a restriction in POM export 
and general water mixing strengthened stratification (Reigstad et al., 2008) could also affect major 
nutrient cycles and thus the biogeochemical landscape of the fjord. 
5.2 Intensified stratification could alter major nutrient cycles with negative feedback 
on the marine food web 
The total and relative abundance of Euryarchaeota (5.8∙103±6.2∙103 cells mL-1 and 1.6±1.8%, 
respectively) was overall in good agreement with other studies (Amano-Sato et al., 2013; Galand et 
al., 2008; Herndl et al., 2005) with few exceptions. Amano-Sato et al. (2013) sampled the Arctic Sea, 
pacific sector, and reported relative abundance of Euryarchaeota up to 15% from CARD-FISH 
obtained cell abundances. The high measurements were however limited to deep water and western 
Chucki sea summer water and were otherwise within the range of my measurements. Herndl et al. 
(2005) sampled a transect through the NE Atlantic up to the SE coast of Greenland. Interestingly, 
their relative abundance within the upper 200 m resembled mine (~2.5%) but was considerably higher 
in deeper water masses. Differences in relative abundance might be due to sampling area, like that 
discussed for Bacteroidetes (see section 5.1). This was also in agreement with Galand et al. (2008) 
that reported higher relative abundances of Euryarchaeota marine group II (MGII) strains in riverine 
water than marine waters. Salinity, or rather water origin, might affect Euryarchaeota in similar ways 
as that discussed above 5.1 section. 
 
However, Euryarchaeota total abundance only significantly correlated (Table 4) with NO3- whereas 
Amano-Sato et al. (2013) found that Euryarchaeota relative abundance correlated with NH4+ and 
temperature. (Table 4 and 5). This indicates that Euryarchaeota actively participate in the nitrogen 
cycling in the Arctic. The individual findings between this study and Amano-Sato et al. (2013) are 
however conflicting. The positive correlations with NO3- and NH4+ in mine and Amano-Sato et al. 
(2013) study, respectively, indicate that these nutrients are produced. Where NO3- could originate 
from nitrification it would simultaneously deplete NH4+ that will be oxidized into NO2- in oxygenated 
waters by i.a. ammonia oxidizing archaea (AOA) (see Hutchins & Capone (2022) for a review). This 
would ultimately imply a negative correlation with NH4+. The high NO3- concentrations (6.1±5.9 µM 
L-1) along with low NO2- and NH4+ concentrations (0.031±0.03 and 1.04±0.89 µM L-1, respectively) 
measured in Ardencaple (Figure 5c & 5d) indicate a system with active nitrification. However, all 
AOA known to date are associated with Crenarchaeota (Hutchins & Capone, 2022) making it 
unlikely that Euryarchaeota should have this role in the Ardencaple fjord. 
The highest relative abundance of Euryarchaeota was observed at station 12 (2.7±3.6%) where NH4+ 
concentrations were also highest (2.2±0.86%) (Figures 8 & 5d). Assuming the unequal distribution 
of NH4+ between stations caused the lack in correlation with Euryarchaeota, I might have seen similar 
==PAGE:26==
Page 26 of 36 
 
correlations as Amano-Sato et al. (2013). Had this been the case, a positive correlation with NH4+ 
could then suggest that Euryarchaeota are actively involved in NH4+ producing pathways such as 
ammonification (Hutchins & Capone, 2022). A phylogenomic study by Rinke et al. (2018) found that 
the Euryarchaeota marine group II (MGII) encoded high numbers of genes involved in i.a. amino 
acid and peptide degradation. Furthermore, they also found that most Euryarchaeota genera 
possessed gene segments coding for flagella and degradation of sugars, while lacking genes coding 
for chemotaxis. Furthermore, the study by Galand et al. (2008) reported higher abundance of 
Euryarchaeota in Arctic riverine and coastal waters compared to marine waters as well as on particles 
compared to free-living. These results combined with the high relative abundance of Euryarchaeota 
in the DCM layer (3.8±2.8%) (Figure 8) suggest that Euryarchaeota are active players in both the 
nitrogen and carbon cycle in the Ardencaple fjord and potentially in association with POM. The role 
of Euryarchaeota may thus become more pronounced in the future if the GrIS continues to melt 
because of climate change. 
Crenarchaeota also correlated significantly and positively with NO3- (Table 4) and is a more likely 
candidate for nitrification as all known AOA are affiliated with Crenarchaeota (Hutchins & Capone, 
2022). However, a significant correlation does not necessarily mean that there is a direct connection. 
This is e.g., seen with Crenarchaeota that correlated negatively with Chl-a (Table 4) which suggest 
that Crenarchaeota are responsible for parts of the POM degradation in the Ardencaple fjord. 
However, both the total and relative abundance, of Crenarchaeota, significantly increased with depth 
at all stations (Figures 7 & 8) while high Chl-a concentrations were only measured in the subsurface 
(Figure 5d). The apparent correlation is thus more likely to be due to their different distributions in 
the water rather than a connection. This is also supported by the Galand et al. (2008) study where the 
higher relative abundance of Crenarchaeota in marine waters suggests an adaptation to oceanic 
conditions that are generally more oligotrophic than coastal systems. However, they also discovered 
that the relative abundance of Crenarchaeota was highest in association with particles. The 
interaction between POM and both Crenarchaeota and Euryarchaeota would need to be investigated 
in future studies to clarify their respective roles in POM degradation. The increase in both NO3– and 
abundance of Crenarchaeota with depth (Figures 5c, 7, and 8) supports a potential correlation 
between Crenarchaeota and its role in nitrification. Aerobic nitrification is often conducted by 
autotrophic or mixotrophic organisms (Ward, 2008) which would mean that Crenarchaeota might 
also have a key role as primary producers in the Ardencaple fjord. 
Crenarchaeota and Euryarchaeota thus seem to be responsible for different key processes in the 
nitrogen and carbon cycle in the Ardencaple fjord. Furthermore, they both appear to be dependent on 
water origin and POM in a way that might favour Euryarchaeota in a scenario of high freshwater 
input. The biogeochemical landscape and nutrient flux might change accordingly. Furthermore, the 
potential strengthening in stratification (see section 5.1) might lock away the nitrogen species at depth 
from restricted water mixing. This would negatively impact phytoplankton and as such result in a 
potential cascading effect though the ecosystem. To confirm this, the exact metabolic roles would 
need to be investigated in subsequent studies. One approach could be in situ incubations to determine 
transformation rates, as well as genomic and transcriptomic approaches with focus on functional 
genes affiliated with nitrogen transforming pathways and processes.

5.3 Competitive interactions between bacterial taxa could affect degradation of 
particulate organic matter 
Alteromonas was found at lower abundance in the Ardencaple fjord than the other taxa 
(1.1∙103±1.9∙103 cells mL-1) and accounted for very low relative abundance (0.2±0.2%) (Figures 7 & 
8). The relative abundance was additionally lower than that reported by Jain & Krishnan (2017) from 
Kongsfjorden (Spitsbergen) where it accounted for ~10 and ~1%, respectively. They further found 
that Alteromonas was enriched in the free-living fraction compared to the particle associated fraction. 
However, this is the opposite of what I observed in the incubation study. This stands in contrast to 
the study by Thiele et al. (2015) where Alteromonas accounted for 13.6±12.2% and 1.6±0.6% of the 
microbial community was attached to particles and free-living, respectively, at 100-150m depth. 
Although all abovementioned relative abundances are higher than what I saw in the Ardencaple data, 
the findings by Thiele et al. (2015) is in relatively good agreement with my incubation study. Here I 
found higher total and relative abundances of Alteromonas in the tanks where algae was added 
(7.4∙106±7.8∙106 cells mL-1 and 6.7±4.7%) compared to the test tank (1.2∙104±1.3∙104 cells mL-1 and 
1.1±0.9%) (Figure 9 & 10). 
The two conflicting studies could be a result of the temperature difference. Firstly, the study by Thiele 
et al. (2015) was conducted off the NW coast of Africa where water temperatures are presumably 
higher than the Arctic. This would also be the case in the Adriatic Sea in September where the 
microbial community used in this incubation study was collected. Furthermore, the total abundance 
of Alteromonas in Ardencaple significantly and positively correlate with temperature (Table 4 & 5). 
Thus, the species composition that makes up the Alteromonas population in the Arctic might be 
different from that of lower latitudes which leads to the difference in attached vs. free-living lifestyles. 
The apparent correlation with temperature might further support a preference for warmer waters but 
this should be reinforced in additional studies. 
In my incubation study, relative abundance of Bacteroidetes was significantly reduced in algae treated 
tanks (0.2±0.3%) compared to the control tank (4.9±3.7%) (Figure 10). This is  despite a higher total 
abundance in the algae tank compared to the control (9∙104±8.9∙104 vs. 4.3∙104±4.5∙104 cells mL-1) 
(Figure 9). Furthermore, Bacteroidetes was the only taxa whose specific growth rate was lower in 
the algae treated tank compared to the control (Table 6). By comparison, the specific growth rate of 
Alteromonas and Vibrio was 2.75 and 1.65 times higher, respectively, in the algae infused tanks 
compared the control (Table 6). One explanation to this could be antagonistic interactions with 
Alteromonas and Vibrio.  
Interestingly, Long et al. (2003) showed that the antibacterial compound 2-n-Pentyl-4-Quinolinol 
(PQ) produced by marine Alteromonas significantly inhibited the respiration, growth, and motility of 
i.a. Bacteroidetes and Vibrio when grown on model particles. Another study by Long & Azam (2001) 
found that both Alteromonadales and Vibrionales were more prolific producers of inhibitory 
compounds as well as more resilient towards them. This could explain why Vibrio was apparently 
less affected by Alteromonas in the particle treated tanks. The study by Long et al. (2003) further 
found that the PQ did not diffuse from the model particle to the surrounding water, which explains 
why the apparent antagonistic effect was not observed in my control tanks (Table 6 and Figures 9 & 
10). However, in an incubation study by Pinhassi et al. (2004) with live phytoplankton cells, 
==PAGE:28==
Page 28 of 36 
 
Bacteroidetes was the dominating phyla. Whether this difference in the experimental outcome was 
due to the live vs dead algae biomass would have to be backed by additional research. Furthermore, 
the extent of PQ production in situ would also need to be investigated to evaluate the ecological role 
of this apparent antagonistic effect. 
Assuming that Alteromonas is currently limited by temperature in the Ardencaple fjord (Figure 5a), 
we might see an increase in their abundance as climate change progresses. The introduction and 
settlement of various lower-latitude marine phyla to the Arctic has already been observed (Chan et 
al., 2019). It is thus likely that similar trends will be observed for Alteromonas and Vibrio. How fast 
this introduction will occur, wherefrom, to what extent, and how the fraction between particle-
associated and free-living will be divided, is however difficult to predict. One approach could be to 
map out their current pan-Arctic distribution. Although efforts have already been put into this, e.g., 
through the studies referenced in this report, they each had their individual aims influencing what 
they measured. In addition, fundamental differences in methodology make it hard to compare. Finally, 
most studies investigating the microbial role in marine ecology and biogeochemical cycles have often 
disregarded unicellular eukaryotes, such as fungi. They would need to be included in such a study as 
their presence, abundance, and activity in marine systems have become increasingly evident in recent 
years (Breyer et al., 2022; Grossart et al., 2019; Grossart & Rojas-Jimenez, 2016). If the 
aforementioned was monitored on a regular basis, the Arctic microbial community response to 
climate change could more accurately be evaluated. 
5.4 Challenges and considerations regarding the experimental setup 
The experimental setup showed to have three drawbacks. Firstly, the enumeration of total prokaryotic 
cells identified via DAPI positive cells proved to be unreliable for the tanks with marine snow when 
larger water volumes were filtered. This was due to DAPI positive signals in the autoclaved algae 
biomass although no EUB338 I-III hybridized signal was observed. This indicates that the algae 
biomass was not a source of prokaryotic cell contamination to the rolling tanks. This is further 
supported by the fact that the initial total and relative abundance of the different taxa was similar 
between the test tank and the control tank (47.7, 0.8, 0.1, and 0% vs. 51.9, 0.9, 0.2, and 0% for 
Bacteria, Bacteroidetes, Alteromonas, and Vibrio, respectively) (Figure 10). This was even though 
initial relative abundance was calculated based on the total cells (DAPI) from the control tank. What 
rendered the DAPI positive signal in the autoclaved algae biomass, I am unaware of, as DNA should 
degrade at the temperatures applied during autoclaving (121 ℃ for 0.5-1h depending on water 
volume). Since this only proved to be a problem for timepoint 0 and 3 where larger volumes of water 
were filtered, I do not consider this to have had a significant effect on the results. However, it is 
something to keep in mind for future studies with a similar experimental setup and method of choice. 
Secondly, the use of vacuum filtration as a method for sterilising the aged sea water (ASW) proved 
to be inefficient. Therefore, it could have introduced up to 1.9∙104 cells mL-1 to the initial prokaryotic 
community in the rolling tanks (data not shown). This could account for up to 13% of the total initial 
cell abundance in the control tank and thus contributed to a considerable proportion of the total cell 
abundance in the tanks. However, the cells observed in the ASW were often very elongated compared 
to those observed in the natural community (data not shown). The cells could thus be excluded during 
enumeration with ACMEtool3 based on circularity and size. Indeed, when cells at T69 were counted 
==PAGE:29==
Page 29 of 36 
 
without excluding the elongated cells based on circularity and size, the total bacterial cell abundance 
was only ~2% higher than when they were excluded. Furthermore, most of the DAPI positive cells 
(>80%) in the aged sea water hybridized with the EUB338 I-III probe mix. This suggests that the 
relative proportion of cells from the aged sea water reduced over time in the tanks. I would argue that 
the total abundance in this report can still be used to calculate relative abundance from and deduce 
trends in community composition. However, the respiration measurements will have an unknown 
contribution from the remaining cells originating from the ASW. 
Finally, it proved impossible to collect intact marine snow particles due to the tank design. The tanks 
were designed as relatively flat cylinders with two large holes centred on each side of the cylinder 
fronts and a smaller hole at the edge on one side. When sampling, the tanks would therefore have to 
be removed from the rolling table and placed flat on a surface. This provoked an abruption in water 
motion causing the particles to disintegrate. Nonetheless, particle material was still collected from 
the bottom when allowed to sink. As such I assumed most of the prokaryotic biomass was from 
attached community. This is further supported by a similar incubation study by Kiørboe et al. (2003) 
using a natural bacterial community from the North Sea. Here the growth curve for the attached 
microbial community increased with time in a similar fashion and levels as mine (Figure 10). On the 
other hand, the ambient community in the study by Kiørboe et al. (2003) started at a higher abundance 
than the particle associated community and remained relatively stable throughout the experiment. The 
sampling method I applied thus worked for the purpose of this study, but a different experimental 
setup should be designed and implemented if the aim is to collect and study intact particles.

6 Concluding remarks 
Although not all hypotheses were confirmed, I demonstrated that the prokaryotic community in the 
Ardencaple fjord is indirectly shaped by the vertical salinity gradient as an expression of freshwater 
input, probably from glacier meltwater.  
Firstly, increases in meltwater discharge rate will probably lead to a strengthened stratification in the 
fjord which will restrict the vertical transport of POM from the surface to the fjord interior. This will 
limit the transport of organic matter with potential implications to the nitrogen and carbon cycle. This 
will primarily be a seasonal event but one that is expected to become more intense and prolonged as 
global temperatures continue to rise. 
Although it needs to be investigated further, I suggest that Crenarchaeota make up a considerable 
fraction of the nitrifier community at depth in the Ardencaple fjord and that Euryarchaeota might be 
capable of ammonification. Furthermore, the strengthened stratification will also restrict nutrient 
mixing from the fjord interior to the surface. This could ultimately lead to a marked change in the 
biogeochemical landscape of the Ardencaple fjord with implications on the remaining marine food 
web. 
Finally, the role of interspecies interaction needs to be considered in studies evaluating the community 
response to climate change induced environmental changes. This became evident as Bacteroidetes 
suffered from an apparent antagonistic interaction by Alteromonas and Vibrio in particle-associated 
settings. However, Alteromonas and Vibrio seemed restricted by presumably the temperature in the 
Ardencaple fjord. Therefore, their apparent interactions are, for now, not of significance. However, 
considering the apparent trend of non-native species introduction from lower to higher latitudes as 
water temperatures rises, they might become more pronounced in the future.
