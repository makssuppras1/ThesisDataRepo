Abstract
The production of glycoside conjugates is laborious, requiring substantial amounts of toxic chemi­
cals due to numerous protection and deprotection steps, and often with low yields. The production
of glycoside products with biocatalysts has therefore gained increased attention for the replacement
of extensive and inefficient chemical glycosylation. Glycosyltransferase Family 1 (GT1) enzymes
are high­potential candidates for this replacement owing to their promiscuous behavior towards a
wide variety of substrates and their ability to produce glycosides in a single step with high yields
using UDP­α­glycosides as donors. However, the knowledge of which enzymes can glycosylate
specific substrates to a desired product represents the biggest hurdle for industrial applications of
GT1s.
High­throughput screenings are essential in uncovering the acceptor specificity of these promiscu­
ous enzymes and are thus desirable.
Applying chromogenic assays for investigating enzymes involving glycosyl transfer offers a faster
and cheap method. This concept is based on applying a highly activated chromogenic CNP­β­
glycoside, from which the enzyme produces its natural UDPα­glycoside substrates by the reverse
glycosylation reaction. Additionally, the release of CNP by this reaction produces a yellow color
that can be detected in time by a spectrophotometer, providing both yields and rates for a given
reaction. Nevertheless, this method has only been reported in a single publication on one enzyme
a decade ago and has been ignored in the literature on glycosyltransferase assays.
In this work, the prospect of applying this method for the investigation of GT1s will be assessed.
Besides acceptor specificity, GT1s possess several uncovered phenomena of which hydrolysis
and dilution­induced deactivation were inspected by chromogenic assays with CNP­β­glycosides
and proved to detect these phenomena. Moreover, it was shown that GT1s could provide hetero­
stabilization to other GT1s against dilution­induced inactivation. The kinetic parameters for the 37
enzymes with UDP and CNP­glucose and CNP­galactose were found, which gave insight into the
activity of each glycosyl substrate where it was found that 81% of the enzymes were more active
on CNP­glucose than on CNP­galactose. Molecular dynamics simulations, along with the kinetic
parameters found in the reverse glycosylation reaction for UDP and each of the CNP­glycosides,
gave insight into the donor specificity of UGT1 and UGT144, where the ability to maintain a gg or
tg conformation of the hydroxymethyl side chain of glucose and galactose, respectively, reflected
the activity towards the particular CNP­glycosides. Furthermore, acceptor screenings of 47 accep­
tors with 37 enzymes were carried out and supplied with a method for estimating acceptor KM of
an enzyme from the high­throughput screening.

1
Introduction
Glycosylation is the conjugation of saccharides with other molecules, particularly other saccha­
rides, proteins, lipids, and secondary metabolites, by forming a glycosidic bond. The product of
glycosylation is referred to as a glycoside and encompasses countless compounds with essential
roles in biology and various chemical industries, e.g., pharmaceutics, cosmetics, and foods. In gen­
eral, glycosylation alters the physico­chemical properties of the glycosyl acceptor and governs its
functionalities, justifying the glycoside demand for various applications [1]. However, the chem­
ical synthesis of glycosides appears to be a difficult task. Carbohydrates, in combination with a
glycosyl acceptor, entail multiple glycosylation sites, which complicates the ability to control the
specificity and reactivity of the glycosylation process for the desired glycoside. To overcome this,
glycosides are usually produced by complex synthetic routes. These require several protection
and deprotection steps for regio and­ stereospecific control, and sometimes synthesis of precursor
compounds for mediating the glycosylation reaction [2]. Thus chemical glycosylation synthesis
usually requires extensive use of chemicals, which generates high amounts of waste and, from
a large­scale production point of view, conflicts with principles of green chemistry, where short
synthesis routes, minimization waste, and the avoidance of toxic reagents in the manufacturing
process of chemical products, are fundamental [3].
To that end, the interest in substituting these wasteful multistep synthetic processes with more
efficient methods by employing enzymes as biocatalysts to manufacture glycosidic products has
increased in the last 20 years. Enzymatic glycosylation is performed with unprotected sugar in a
single step under mild aqueous conditions. The reaction is facilitated by the amino acids in the
active site, which are also providing high regio and­ stereospecificity of the glycoside product.
Consequently, enzymatic glycosylation does not require the use of protection and deprotection re­
actions [2]. Thus the synthesis can potentially be performed by a single enzyme, in combination
with other enzymes or chemoenzymatically for the synthesis of more complex glycosides, and ulti­
mately minimize the number of synthetic steps, waste, and toxic compounds in the manufacturing
process [3].
The biocatalysts that perform glycoside synthesis are termed glycosyltransferases (GTs) and con­
stitute a superfamily that has been organized in the Carbohydrate­Active enZmes Database (CAZy)
into 115 families (as of January 19th 2023) based on sequence identity, tertiary protein structure,
and other biochemical characteristics [4]. Among the 115 families, the glycosyltransferase family
1 (GT1s) is the third largest in the CAZy database and is the enzyme family investigated in this
thesis. The GT1s are known for their promiscuous behavior, that is, their ability to accommo­
date a large variety of acceptor molecules, making them suitable candidates for synthesizing many
glycosidic compounds [5].
Despite the promiscuous feature of GT1s and the fact that they are among the most studied GTs,
there is a considerable lack of knowledge regarding which substrates a specific can glycosylate
and the reasons behind [5]. This limits the ability to utilize GT1s as biocatalysts for glycosylation
reactions in the chemical industry; hence, it is necessary to investigate GT1s further to uncover
their full potential as biocatalysts.
1.1
Glycosyltransferase family 1
The superfamily of GTs can employ various sugar donors for the glycosylation of an acceptor
molecule and are also classified according to the type of sugar donor they utilize [6]. GTs that use
nucleoside­diphosphate monosaccharides (NDP­sugars) as sugar donors are termed Leloir glyco­
syltransferases and are activated by the high­energy phosphodiester bond between the monosac­
charide and nucleoside, which detaches upon glycosylation with an acceptor molecule [5].
Another of the characteristics used to classify GTs is how they alter the sugar donor’s stereochem­
istry at the anomeric carbon in their reaction mechanism to yield a glycoside product. GTs that

GTs.[1]
changes the stereochemistry at the anomeric carbon of the sugar donor is classified as inverting
GTs. In contrast, GTs that maintain the configuration of the anomeric carbon is classified as re­
taining. Thus GTs that yield a α­glycoside upon glycosylation with an α­sugar donor and acceptor
is a retaining GT, while a GT that produces a β­glycoside is retaining, as shown in Fig. 1.1 [6].
The GT1s possess the inverting reaction mechanism and fall into the class of Leloir glycosyl­
transferases that employ α­NDP­sugars, where the nucleoside base is usually uridine. These en­
zymes are therefore classified as uridine diphosphate­dependent glycosyltransferases and termed
as UGTs. This abbreviation will be used throughout this thesis for designating the individual en­
zymes of the GT1 family [7].
Structure
GTs possess different structural motifs that have been classified into various topologies. The topol­
ogy of inverting GT1s is a GT­B fold, made up of two Rossmann­like domains at the C­ and N­
terminal of the protein responsible for the sugar donor and acceptor binding, respectively, with the
acceptor located above the sugar ring of the donor [5]. The domains are structurally similar and
connected by a flexible linker region that makes up a catalytic cleft where the active site resides
[1]. Each Rossmann­like domain consists of a repeating pattern of alternating α­helices and β­
sheets, with the β­sheets parallel to each other, ultimately creating an extended β­sheet surrounded
by α­helices, as shown in Fig. 1.2.
Figure 1.2: The GTB­fold of SlUGT5 (UGT 16), with the N­terminal domain shown in blue, the
C­terminal domain in red, and the linker region connecting the two in green. The Rossmann­like
structure of the N­terminal is shown with α­helices in red surrounding the yellow β­sheets forming
the extended β­sheet.
2
==PAGE:11==
A UGT will undergo several conformational changes following the induced­fit mechanism upon
binding the substrate molecules. The first step in this mechanism is binding the sugar donor in
the C­terminal domain, which causes the N­terminal to rotate toward the C­terminal and a flexible
loop on the C­terminal to cover the gap in between. This changes the conformation of the enzyme
from an open to a closed conformation, which gives rise to modifications of the substrate binding
pockets and enhances the affinity to the acceptor, which then attaches to the binding pocket in the
N­terminal domain [1, 8, 9]. The pyrophosphate moiety of the sugar donor will reside in between
the two domains and has the role of stabilizing the closed conformation by interacting with both
domains [1].
Reaction mechanism
Once a UGT is in the catalytically active conformation, the inverting reaction mechanism can take
place. The classical reaction mechanism of inverting GT1s resembles that of an SN2 reaction,
mediated by a catalytic base, usually in the form of a histidine side chain located in the active site
(Fig. 1.3) [1]. The catalytic base deprotonates the glycosylation site of the acceptor, which makes a
nucleophilic attack on the anomeric carbon. This forms an oxocarbenium­ion transition state while
the UDP molecule detaches and the β­glycosidic bond between the sugar and acceptor is formed
[8].
Figure 1.3: O­glycosylation reaction mechanism. The catalytic histidine abstracts a proton from
the acceptor, facilitating a nucleophilic attack on the anomeric carbon.
GT1s can glycosylate O, N, S, and C acceptor sites, sometimes with good regioselectivity on
an acceptor with multiple glycosylation sites. Where O has been proposed to follow a similar
reaction mechanism as described above [8], N, S, and C glycosylation use other distinct reaction
mechanisms [1]. C­glycosylation requires that the acceptor molecule is an aromatic compound that
has an electronegative group with a proton at the ortho or para position [8]. An oxocarbenium­ion
transition state can then be established by an amino acid acting as a general base by deprotonating
the electronegative group, which then withdraws electrons from the benzene ring, facilitating a
nucleophilic attack of the neighboring carbon atom on the anomeric carbon of the sugar donor[8].
N­ and S­glycosylations are believed to be more or less independent of deprotonation by a catalytic
base and depend more on the positioning of the acceptor relative to the donor and the electrostatic
environment in the active site [10].
For the experiments of this thesis, only acceptors that are prone to O­ and C­glycosylation are
used; thus, the relevant reaction mechanisms are the ones described above and shown for the O­
glycosylation in Fig. 1.3.
1.2
Substrate specificity
As mentioned, the key to unlocking the full potential of GTs as biocatalysts for industrial appli­
cations is to gain insight into their substrate specificity. The individual UGTs may be either quite
3
==PAGE:12==
specific or highly tolerant in terms of the substrate molecules they can glycosylate [8]. A prominent
feature of the GT­B fold, when comparing UGTs against each other, is that the motif constructed by
the amino acids in the C­terminal is always relatively conserved. At the same time, the N­terminal
has a more divergent motif [1]. This is believed to be the reasoning behind the UGTs acceptor
promiscuity and specificity towards UDP­sugars as the glycosyl donor [5].
Donor specificity
Although the conservedness of the C­terminal generates the recognition towards UDP­sugars as
the glycosyl donor, the individual UGTs may be able to host UDP­sugars of different glycosyl
groups or have a preference toward a specific glycosyl group [11, 12]. UGTs usually prefer UDP­
α­D­glucopyranose (UDP­Glc) as the glycosyl donor; however, it is known that UDP­N­acetyl­
α­D­glucopyranosamine (UDP­GlcNAc), UDP­α­D­galactopyranose (UDP­Gal), and other UDP­
sugar donors can be employed [13]. The governing factor behind the donor substrate specificity is
the ability of the amino acids in the C­terminal to interact with the sugar donor and contribute to the
stability of the oxocarbenium­ion transition state in the catalytic conformation [11]. This ability has
also been shown by Kim et al. [11] to be connected to the kinetic constants that reflect the catalytic
activity of a UGT, where mutants originating from two UGTs with specificity towards different
glycosyl donors proved to alter the specificity and kinetic constants in glycosylation reactions with
quercetin as the acceptor.
The stability of the oxocarbenium­ion transition state is pivotal for glycosylation and usually re­
quires that the side chain of the glycosyl group is in an energetically favorable conformation [14].
In this thesis, the donor specificity will be investigated with UDP­Gal and UDP­Glc as glycosyl
donors. The hydroxyl at the C6 position of galactose in free solution can exist in three different
staggered conformations gauche,gauche (gg), gauche,trans (gt), and trans,gauche (tg), whereas
that of glucose only can exist in gg and gt (Fig. 1.4) [14].
Figure 1.4: The gg, gt, and gg conformation and abundancy in free solution of galactose and
glucose. The terminology describes the relation of the O6­C6­C5­O5 dihedral angle first and
then the O6­C6­C5­C4 dihedral angle.[14, 15]
The different staggered conformations of the side chain contribute differently to the energy of the
oxonium­ion transition state. The gg and tg conformations of the hydroxymethyl provide the most
and least favorable transition states, respectively, while the gt conformation falls in between [14].
The C­terminal amino acids in the vicinity of the glycosyl group of the donor have been shown
by Quirke and Crich [14] to have a role in the orientation of the hydroxymethyl side chain when
the donor is bound. In their study, they investigated the orientation of the donor by inspecting the
donor site in 118 crystal structures from different glycosyltransferases with their respective hex­
opyranoside donor bound, where eight of the investigated crystal structures were inverting UGTs
with a preference for UDP­Gal, while 12 of the structures were inverting UGTs with a preference
for UDP­Glc. It was found that the glucose donors usually have the hydroxymethyl side chain in
4
==PAGE:13==
the gg conformation, whereas galactose donors have their side chains in the tg conformation This
suggest that the donor specificity of UGTs with UDP­Glc and UDP­Gal relies on the ability to
achieve these hydroxymethyl conformations.
Acceptor specificity
The acceptors that can be glycosylated in UGTs are hydrophobic with an aromatic group and re­
sides in a hydrophobic pocket constituted by a range of hydrophobic amino acids in the variable
N­terminal [10]. This range of hydrophobic amino acids has been shown to be highly variable and
never conserved in a sequence alignment analysis of 1356 UGT sequences that share a pairwise
identity of 20 to 80% [10]. Furthermore, only 1% of the amino acids at the positions establishing
the hydrophobic pocket contained hydrophilic residues, which reflects UGTs’ affinity towards hy­
drophobic aromatic acceptors.
In addition, the acceptor binding site, made by the conformational changes upon binding the gly­
cosyl donor, must also be shaped accordingly to the acceptor. Thus, the spatial arrangement of
the active site and hydrophobic pocket is also vital. UGTs are known for their plasticity, that is,
their ability to shape the active site and hydrophobic pocket in different ways, and believed to be
one of the main reasons behind their acceptor promiscuity [9]. Nevertheless, the governing factors
behind the dynamics of UGTs are still unknown, and acceptor specificity remains an uncovered
topic [1].
1.3
Other phenomena in UGT catalysis
While substrate specificity and reactivity play a significant role in forming a glycoside prod­
uct, other factors also influence the activity and outcome of in vitro glycosylation reactions with
UGTs. Among these factors are hydrolysis, dilution­induced deactivation, and reverse glycosyla­
tion, which are relatively overlooked compared to substrate specificity and reaction conditions.
Reverse glycosylation
UGTs are generally considered to run in the forward direction of the glycosylation reaction to form
a β­glycoside product Fig. 1.3. Yet the ability of UGTs to catalyze the backward reaction, that is,
the formation of UDP­sugar and an aglycon from a β­glycoside, has been proven and designated
reverse glycosylation (Fig. 1.5) [16]. The reverse glycosylation reaction is, however, not favored
in all reactions and dependents on the reaction conditions and the difference in pKa between the
aglycon of the β­glycoside and UDP (pKa (UDP) = 6.5); the lower the pKa of the aglycon the more
the equilibria is shifted towards reverse glycosylation.
Figure 1.5: The reverse glycosylation reaction is catalyzed by UDP, which to which the activated
β­glycoside is converted into UDP­sugar and the sugar aglycon.
As the reverse glycosylation reaction is dependent on low pKa of the released aglycon, the reaction
can be triggered by the use of activated β­glycosides which contains a small non­natural aromatic
aglycon, that drives the equilibrium in the backward direction [8]. With the formation of UDP­
sugars in this reaction by using activated β­glycosides, there has been an increased interest in using
the method for synthesizing expensive, commercially unavailable, and non­natural UDP­sugars
5
==PAGE:14==
and has gained [8, 16]. Furthermore, using activated β­glycosides is practical for synthesizing
a desired glycoside by the forward reaction to circumvent reverse glycosylation of the desired
product [8, 17]. Since reverse glycosylation of a glycoside product requires free UDP in solution,
which is released upon the forward reaction, an activated β­glycoside can thus be added to the
solution for consuming the free UDP to prime the forward reaction for the desired glycoside [8].
Hydrolysis
Hydrolysis of glycosidic compounds to sugar and aglycon is a mechanism associated with glyco­
sylhydrolases. However, this reaction has also been observed by UGTs where the UDP­sugar is
hydrolyzed into free UDP and sugar and is highly uncovered in the literature.
In unpublished data from the group of Enzyme Engineering & Structural Biology at DTU Biosus­
tain, this phenomenon has been proposed to take place in an experiment with 3,4,5­trichlorophenol
(TCP) and 30 different UGTs. The UGTs were incubated in HEPES buffer at pH 7, with UDP­Glc
and TCP for 10 min and 24 h and showed that 9 out of the 30 UGTs had lower yields or even no
yield after 24 h for four of the UGTs. This suggests that reverse glycosylation in combination with
the hydrolysis of UDP­Glc has taken place with the free UDP released upon hydrolysis catalyzing
the reverse glycosylation reaction to decrease the yield of glycoside product; the proposed reaction
is shown in Fig. 1.6. As hydrolysis is irreversible, the free UDP will alter the equilibrium towards
reverse glycosylation and continuously decrease the yield, where the degree to which the yield
has been lowered reflects the UGTs varying ability to hydrolyze glycosides. The thermodynamic
product is thus complete hydrolysis.
Hence, specificity investigations of UGTs toward acceptors relying on end­point measurements
might be biased depending on the capability of the enzymes to catalyze reverse glycosylation and
the hydrolysis of UDP­sugar. The methodology used for studying the specificity is therefore criti­
cal.
Figure 1.6: Reaction scheme for hydrolysis of UDP­Glc and reverse glycosylation of the TCP­
glycoside in PtUGT1. The hydrolysis reaction is mediated by the catalytic base (here shown facil­
itated by the catalytic dyad with Asp122), which deprotonates a water molecule in the vicinity of
the anomeric carbon, which then makes a nucleophilic attack on the anomeric carbon to hydrolyze
UDP­Glc. The presence of UDP then catalyzes the reverse glycosylation reaction.
Dilution­induced deactivation
Biologically, enzymes thrive in a complex cellular environment composed of other proteins and
biomolecules that interact to form a quinary structure that stabilizes the enzyme [18]. However,
6
==PAGE:15==
in the context of applying enzymes as biocatalysts for the formulation of glycoside products in in­
dustry and specificity studies, enzymes are usually secluded from their biological environment and
can lack this stabilizing quinary structure. Therefore, the chemostability of UGTs with an acceptor
is a practical concern.
In a few studies using GT1s for biotechnological applications, a non­linear behavior was observed,
with no activity observed at low enzyme concentrations [19, 20] The chemostability of 18 UGTs
at four different concentrations (10 to 80 mg L−1) and three acceptors at four different concentra­
tions (50 to 400 µM) has been investigated [21]. All enzymes displayed activity towards each of
the three acceptors; however, while it could be expected that doubling the enzyme concentration
would double the glycoside yield and exhibit a linear relationship, in numerous cases (15 out of 18
GT1 enzymes) another trend was observed. Yields at higher enzyme concentrations were either
maximized or showed a somewhat linear trend, while at low enzyme concentrations, no conversion
was observed.
This exposes the instability of GT1 enzymes at low concentrations, a term coined dilution­induced
deactivation (DID), and may reflect a lack of interactions in an environment diluted for other
biomolecules.
1.4
Chromogenic assays
The demand for understanding UGTs’ promiscuity and specificity towards various substrates for
applying them as biocatalysts requires high­throughput assays. Considering the many factors that
alter enzymes productivity and stability, such as pH, temperature, and those mentioned in Sec­
tion 1.3, the methodology used can, in many ways, alter the outcome. These methods often require
using various equipment and large amounts of solvents while also being relatively time­consuming
and expensive. Thus efficient ways to access knowledge about enzymes are attractive.
To that end, a method that relies on reverse glycosylation with a highly activated 2­chloro­4­
nitrophenyl­β­glycoside (CNP­glycoside) has been developed by Gantt et al. [17] and has shown
to apply to enzymatic reactions involving glycosyl transfer as in UGTs. The transformation of this
compound to UDP­Glc and CNP is exothermic and, thereby, thermodynamically favorable due to
the low pKa of CNP (5.43) with respect to UDP. Furthermore, the five resonance structures of the
corresponding CNP phenolate make it a stable compound and able to absorb light at 410 nm and
emit photons at 570 nm to yield a yellow color. This feature allows one to follow the glycosyla­
tion reaction in time by measuring the absorbance at 410 nm with a spectrophotometer and giving
insight into both initial rates and yields of the reaction.
This chromogenic assay method will be employed and developed in this thesis to investigate donor
specificity, acceptor specificity, acceptor kinetics, hydrolysis, and DID of various UGTs.
The donor specificity assays will look into the specificity of UGTs towards UDP­Gal and UDP­Glc
as substrates by solely relying on the reverse glycosylation reaction with a CNP­glycoside supple­
mented with UDP. The specificity towards either of the substrates is justified by the fact that if
an enzyme has specificity towards one of the UDP­sugars, then the reverse glycosylation reaction
will be thermodynamically favored, and the CNP­glycoside easily converted into a UDP­sugar and
CNP.
Acceptor specificity will be accessed by high­throughput screening of 47 different acceptor com­
pounds with UDP­Glc and CNP­Glc. This acceptor screening experiment will utilize both the
forward and reverse glycosylation reactions. The forward reaction will occur if the UGT has speci­
ficity towards a specific acceptor, releasing the UDP required to catalyze the reverse glycosylation
of CNP­Glc and yield CNP. The released CNP will thus correspond 1:1 with the amount of glyco­
side formed in the forward reaction and the spectrophotometric readout. However, this is under the
assumption that background hydrolysis of UDP­Glc does not occur. Thus, a control consisting of
only CNP­Glc and UDP­Glc with the screened UGT will be provided to detect this. Furthermore,
an experiment investigating the kinetics of GmUGT88E3 (UGT38) with eight different acceptors

will be performed through the same reactions.
To investigate hydrolysis, a UGT will only be provided with UDP­Glc and CNP­Glc and followed
over 8 hours. Thus this experiment depends on UDP to be released by hydrolysis to catalyze the
reverse glycosylation reaction and yield CNP.
The instability of UGTs upon DID experiment will, like the donor specificity assay, only proceed
by reverse glycosylation; however, in different concentrations of UGT.
Apart from these experiments, molecular dynamics (MD) simulations of the same UGTs com­

3.1
Assay Development
The general idea of using CNP­glycosides for generating UDP­glycosides from reverse glycosy­
lation catalyzed by GT1 enzymes has only been reported for OleD so far. Considering that this
method is, in principle, both generic and practical and was reported in 2011, it is surprising that it
has not been utilized on other GT1s.
Initially, a visual assessment with the reverse glycosylation reaction was conducted using a few
in­house available UGTs in a phosphate buffer at pH 6 and 9, where no significant visible differ­
ences were observed. Therefore it was decided to carry on a systematic kinetic assessment of all
in­house UGTs and to use pH 7.5 for all the chromogenic assays, as that is the physiological pH
for most plants’ cytoplasm, and most of the tested UGTs originate from plants.
On the CNP­glycosides side, solubility limitations prevented to obtain solutions, with concentra­
tions above 5 mM for CNP­Gal, and 10 mM for CNP­Glc.

duce between 500 to 700 data points per well. With 37 enzymes screened, this would be an enor­

from an experiment, and typical examples of the graphs obtained are shown in Fig. 3.1

3.2
Reverse glycosylation kinetics
As the reverse glycosylation reaction depends on the binding of two substrates, three complexes can
be formed in this reaction, two with either substrate and the ternary complex with both substrates
bound. This gives two reaction routes that lead to the production of UDP­sugar and CNP, as shown
in the reaction scheme in Fig. 3.2.
Figure 3.2: The reaction scheme for the reverse glycosylation reaction. The reaction can go by two
routes, with either of the substrates bound first. After binding of the second substrate, the ternary
complex will be formed, which will lead to the formation of the UDP­sugar and CNP products if
the reaction occurs. KM(UDP) and KM(CNP­glycoside) is the Michaelis­Menten constants for
the formation of the complex with either UDP or the CNP­glycoside bound to the enzyme. KM(P)
designate the Michaelis­Menten constant for the formation of the ternary complex.
Each complex is associated with a KM that reflects the affinity towards UDP or CNP­Glycoside.
The KM(UDP) and KM(CNP­glycoside), describe the enzymes inclination for forming a complex
with either UDP or CNP­glycoside when the enzyme has none of the substrates bound, while
KM(P) describe the tendency of the enzyme to form the ternary complex when it is bound with one
of the substrates. Considering the case where k−3 >> k5 and k−4 >> k5 yields the following
equations for the KM(UDP) and KM(CNP­glycoside)
KM(UDP) = k−1[E • UDP] + k3[CNP­Glc][E • UDP]
k1[E][UDP] + k−3[E • UDP • CNP­Glc]

UDP kinetics with CNP­Glc
The KM for UDP with CNP­Glc is, as stated above, an important parameter for the experiments
with acceptors and hydrolysis. The KM and kcat for the tested UGTs with varying UDP and constant
CNP­Glc concentration are shown in Fig. 3.5. The median of the KM values for UDP with CNP­
Glc is around 20 µM which indicates that half of the UGTs are saturated by 20 µM UDP. Thus these
enzymes should be active for the reverse glycosylation at low UDP concentrations and potentially
be proficient at detecting the conversion in the forward glycosylation reaction with an acceptor and
the hydrolysis reaction.
Figure 3.5: KM (in blue) and kcat (in cyan) of the 37 enzymes with UDP and CNP­Glc.
However, as the rate­limiting step is the formation of the ternary complex, and thus approximately
the kcat, this parameter is also important to consider in this regard. While it is seen from Fig. 3.5
that some enzymes exhibit high catalytic rates, these enzymes are also associated with relatively
high KM values. As the KM is defined as the concentration for which the observed rates are at
half of the apparent maximum velocity, this parameter will also go along with a higher kcat in
some cases. UGT41, UGT53, and UGT152 are three of such enzymes, and as seen, has the highest
catalytic rates while their associated KM values are also in the high end. These enzymes were the
most active in the type 2 RG experiment where the initial rates and yields at the two lowest UDP
concentrations prove that the enzyme is so active that the reaction has already stopped after a few
minutes Figs. B.23, B.35 and B.69.
On the contrary, Fig. 3.5 display that some enzymes have high KM values and low or almost zero
kcat (e.g. UGT2, UGT17, and UGT123). Such enzymes are poor catalysts for reverse glycosylation
with UDP and CNP­Glc as substrates.

MD simulations of UDP­glycoside donors
For glycosylation of a UDP­sugar with an acceptor to take place at the anomeric carbon, it is a re­
quirement that the oxocarbenium­ion transition state is stabilized. This stabilization was presumed
by Quirke and Crich [14] to be mediated by the orientation of the side chain at the C5 carbon as
described in Section 1.2, where the gg and tg conformations of glucose and galactose, respectively,
were associated with the higher stability of the transition state. Thus to validate the donor speci­
ficity found in the type 2 RG experiments, MD simulations of the 37 enzymes with UDP­Glc and
UDP­Gal without an acceptor molecule, were initiated.
UGT1 and UGT144 were selected on behalf of the observed kinetic parameters in Table 3.1. UGT1
proved to have a higher specificity towards CNP­Glc than CNP­Gal, while UGT144 was the most
specific enzyme for CNP­Gal. A visual assessment of the MD trajectories for these two enzymes
was thus conducted.
UGT1 simulations
The simulation of UGT1 with UDP­Gal, shows that the side chain switches conformations be­
tween gg and gt 4 times during the 20 ns. The hydroxyl group of galactose is surrounded by the
catalytic His32, Pro153, and Leu133 in the active side of UGT1, and Fig. 3.7 shows 4 frames of
the hydroxymethyl side chain of UDP­Gal upon the conformational changes in UGT1. Figs. 3.7(a)
and 3.7(b) represents UGT1 with UDP­Gal after 8 ns, where the first transtion happens. The hy­
drophobic Leu133 and Pro153 residues are at a distance of 5 Å and 4.2 Å away from the hydroxyl
group and move closer upon the conformational change to the gt conformer, while the polar His32
moves away from the hydroxyl group. This change in the amino acid distances from the hydroxyl
group and forces the transition to the gt conformation, by electrostatic repulsion of the hydrophobic
residues from the polar hydroxyl group.
The side chain stays in the gt conformation for 6 ns where the distance between the amino acid
residues remains around 4 Å, until the Leu133 residue moves away from the hydroxyl group and
creates room for the side chain to rotate back to the gg conformation, as seen in Figs. 3.7(c)
and 3.7(d). UDP­Gal changes back to the gt one more time for 2 ns before finally transitioning
back to the gg conformed, where it stays for the rest of the simulation. Importantly, the tg confor­
mation associated with high stabilization of the transition state for galactose was never observed.

Detection of dilution­induced deactivation (DID)
As stated in Section 1.3, DID is an unexplored phenomenon of UGTs and remains to be investigated
further. In that regard, it was decided to look into this phenomena by chromogenic assays with
CNP­Glc, with six UGTs diluted in a serial ½ dilution in twelve wells from 40 to 0.02 µg mL−1. To
investigate the effect of a quinary structure stabilizing the enzyme at low concentrations, two lines
of the 96­well microplate were constructed with UGT10 diluted 1/2 in each well, supplemented
with 2.5 µg mL−1 UGT38, and UGT38 diluted 1/2 supplemented with 2.5 µg mL−1 UGT10.
Fig. 3.10 display the yield percentage of CNP­Glc conversion, and log10 of the yield percentage,
produced in the chromogenic assay for each enzyme.
All of the enzymes exhibit dilution­induced deactivation to different extents, but the loss of linear­
ity was, nevertheless, observed at low enzyme concentrations. UGT53 appears to be most resistant
to this effect as activity is detected with as little as 0.2 µg mL−1. UGT10 and UGT43 show an
increase in yield at the lowest concentration, which is due to experimental errors or incorrect mea­
surements in the spectrophotometer. When neglecting UGT53, it is seen from the graphs with only
one enzyme and with only the yield percentage given, that the seems to be a superlinear relation­
ship between yields and enzyme concentration, while a break­off point appears after 2.5 µg mL−1,
where the enzymes are inactivated. Principally, the activity should show a linear trend when de­
creasing the concentration of an enzyme, however, this experiment shows that enzymes are inac­
tivated at low concentrations as found by Teze et al. [21] and Bidart et al. [19]. In the attempt to
uncover the presence of another enzyme would stabilize the enzyme and increase the yield, experi­
ments with the addition of UGT10 and UGT38 were performed. The concept of this experiment is
based on the break­off that is seen at 2.5 µM mL−1, which is the minimal concentration of enzyme
with activity. With the addition of 2.5 µM mL−1 the yields will increase accordingly. In the case
that the supplemented enzyme concentration does not provide additional stability, the same super­
linear trend of the yield as a function of enzyme concentration, but shifted upwards as indicated
by the red curve in Fig. 3.9. However, if the additional enzyme provides higher yields than the
2.5 µg mL−1 at low enzyme concentrations, the curve will become more linear, thus an indication
that the interactions between the enzymes increase the stability.
Figure 3.9

3.5
Acceptor assays
Chromogenic assays with CNP­glycosides are both cheap and fast, providing both yields and initial
rates in a short matter of time. Furthermore, as explained in Section 1.3, activated β­glycosides can
prime the forward reaction by consuming the free UDP released upon glycosylation of the accep­
tor, preventing reverse glycosylation of the glycoside product, thus potentially providing higher
yields and uncover the acceptor specificity for an enzyme. These features justify the interest in
using chromogenic assays for high­throughput acceptor screenings. Especially the time aspect is
superior compared to traditional screening methods, as a handful of different enzymes with a vast
collection of acceptors can be screened within a day.
Gantt et al. [17] reported the use of chromogenic assays for OleD and mutated variants with dif­
ferent CNP­glycosides and a few acceptors, and postulated that the use of CNP­glycosides for the
reverse glycosylation can be coupled with any NDP­sugar dependent enzyme. This postulate was,
however, never confirmed, but was proven to be true in this study where all of the UGTs showed
activity toward at least a couple of the given acceptors.

parts, with wells A1 to D11 containing 50 µM of acceptor and wells E1 to H11 10 µM. An example
of the output from an AS experiment of the 47 chosen acceptors, presented in Table 2.3, with UGT1
and UGT17 is shown Fig. 3.11(a) and Fig. 3.11(b).

released CNP in the reverse glycosylation reaction, the activity of the enzymes is also a reflection
of their activity towards UDP and the given CNP­glycoside. Thus, it reflects the glycosylation
activity against the proposed acceptor only as long as it is slower than the reverse glycosylation.
Hence, the enzyme activity in the reverse glycosylation at low UDP concentration is a determining
factor in this experiment. The determined kinetic parameters for the two enzymes found in the
type 2 RG experiment with CNP­Glc (Table 3.1), clearly reflect the acceptor activity for UGT1
and UGT17. UGT1 proved to have a relatively low KM for UDP at 8 µM and an above median kcat
26
==PAGE:35==
of 6 min−1, and therefore a higher activity in the reverse glycosylation at low UDP concentrations,
which evidently is mirrored in the higher activity for the acceptors. In sharp contrast, UGT17 had
the second highest KM for UDP at 321 µM and a kcat of 0.02 min−1, thus a very low activity for the
reverse glycosylation reaction which ultimately appears as a lower activity on the given acceptors
compared to UGT1.
Since the reproduction of UDP to UDP­Glc by the reverse glycosylation is required for the further
forward glycosylation reaction with an acceptor, the reverse glycosylation reaction becomes the
rate­limiting step for UGTs with high affinity towards an acceptor and a high KM for UDP, while
the forward glycosylation reaction with an acceptor is the rate­limiting step for UGTs with low
KM for UDP, which is ideal. Thus in order to yield true yields and rates with this method for an
enzyme and an acceptor, the kinetic parameters for UDP and CNP­glycoside are crucial factors.

Hydrolysis of UDP­sugars in UGTs is a limitation for the aspect of producing glycoside product.
With hydrolysis in combination with the reverse glycosylation reaction of an acceptor, the yield

acceptor by the reverse glycosylation reaction.

4
Conclusion
Chromogenic assays with CNP­glycosides serves as a fast method, providing both rates and yields,
while being dependent on the detection of the initial rate for the purpose of estimating kinetic pa­
rameters. The assays conducted on reverse glycosylation reaction, dilution­induced deactivation
inspection, acceptor screenings and hydrolysis were all able to detect activity of an enzyme. While
the hydrolysis experiment provided no insight into the hydrolytic rates of UGT5, UGT10, UGT70,
UGT75, UGT121 and UGT123, the method itself were able to detect hydrolysis of UDP­Glc.
The chromogenic assay for investigating of dilution­induced deactivation proved to detect this phe­
nomenon, and showed that UGT10 and UGT38 are stabilized at lower enzyme concentrations, in
the presence of each other.
The reverse glycosylation experiments offered kinetic parameters for 37 enzymes with UDP, and
gave insight into donor specificity with glucose and galactose by conducting experiments with
CNP­Glc and CNP­Gal. In total 8 out of the 37 enzymes had galactose as their preferred substrate
based on the respective kcat values found in the type 2 RG experiment with CNP­Glc and CNP­Gal,
and all UGTs were active on galactose except for UGT142, which was inactive on both CNP­Glc
and CNP­Gal.
The kinetic constants and rates from the type 2 RG experiment, classified UGT1 as being glucose
specific, and MD simulations showed that UGT1 were capable of preserving the glucose side chain
in the gg conformer, but was unable to transition to the tg conformation with galactose. On the con­
trary the type 2 RG experiment determined UGT144 to be galactose specific and MD simulations
dispalyed that UGT144 was able to maintain the tg conformation with UDP­Gal for the majority of
the simulation, while the gg side chain conformation with UDP­Glc was conserved. While there
seem to be a relation between the type 2 RG experiment and the literature upon stabilization of
the side chain conformer for the donor specificity of UGT1, the specificity of UGT144 towards
glucose and galactose donors is not reflected by this relation.
Importantly, the chromogenic assay allowed to investigate the specificity of 37 enzymes against 47
acceptors, making it the second largest dataset available on GT1 specificity, while being obtained
in a matter of weeks. In addition, the screening allowed for a rough estimation of the KM of active
enzyme­acceptor pairs.
