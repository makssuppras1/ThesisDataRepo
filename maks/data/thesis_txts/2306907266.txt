Abstract
This study aims to explore the study site (Grindsted, Danmark) within discharge
measurements and use existing data for developing, testing and applying a decision
support system (DSS) with a system dynamics (SD) framework for coupling of
groundwater- and surface water interaction. Although SD was used in previous
studies for water resources modeling (WRM), this study presents the novelty to
separate the groundwater-borne discharge into both sides of a stream. Within
this approach, it was already planned for further research to be connected, i.e.
meanders impacting on the groundwater-surface water interactions, coupling to
water quality.
Nine discharge measurements were conducted over a stream stretch of ca. 17.5
km, where the spatial variations of the stream have been explored. The discharge
measurement manifested spatial and, compared to former measurements, temporal
variations in the stream. The stream is in general groundwater gaining, whilst at
some stretches a losing stream occurs.
The DSS was developed using Vensim DSS 6.4 (2015), where a hydrological
model was built which uses precipitation and discharge data for simulating the
major processes associated with the hydrologic cycle: water creates either sur-
face runoﬀor it percolates to groundwater and discharges from/to the stream.
Furthermore, soil moisture was simulated within evapotranspiration.
Structural validity has been invetigated by testing diﬀerent scenarios, i.e. ex-
treme precipitation, climate change, land use change, soil conditions. The results
indicated that the model’s structure was robust on all kinds of scenarios, as the
consequences have been like expected.
A behavioral validity was used to test the DSS on Grindsted ﬁeld site. Here,
the model was able to capture the general trends of the observed data. However,
the ﬁtting of the peaks was weak, as the model typically over- or underestimated
these.
The results have been evaluated using the model performance indicators PBIAS,
NSE and RSR which indicated reasonable well results. Altogether, this study il-
lustrates the high potential, SD has in WRM.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Problem Deﬁnition
Nearly half of the citizens within the EU Member States are worried about water
pollution as an environmental issue regarding their economic standard (European
Parliament, 2016). Water scarcity, poor water quality and stress on ecosystems
are the result due to the extensive global exploitation of water resources with con-
tamination from industrial and agricultural sectors (Hinsby et al., 2008; Hightower
and Pierce, 2008; McKnight et al., 2012). Therefore, the commission of the EU
Water Framework Directive (WFD) (European Parliament, 2000) deﬁned water
protection by the management of water resources as one of the priorities in its
work (European Parliament, 2016).
An increasing trend can be seen for water management decisions that are based
on model studies (Scholten et al., 2007) and modeling tools have become progres-
sively more sophisticated. The conventional approach to water resources manage-
ment (WRM), however, has been to isolate the feedback dynamics of groundwater-
surface water interactions in such a way, that only the compartment are modeled
and managed separately(Dai and Labadie, 2001). While this approach does help
to ﬁnd answers for some important tasks, it fails to give fully long-term analyses
of the problem. However, both groundwater and surface water cannot be regarded
as isolated components but interact with each other in a variety of physiographic
and climatic ways (Sophocleous, 2002), thus creating a dynamic complexity, which
requires a modeling framework that is capable of capturing the dynamic interrela-
tionships of diﬀerent components and activities (Sehlke and Jacobson, 2005; Khan
et al., 2009).
System dynamics (SD) is therefore a modeling framework that has proven to
be robust, transparent and useful for developing appropriate management strate-
gies in WRM (Sehlke and Jacobson, 2005; Khan et al., 2009; Winz et al., 2009;
1
==PAGE:20==
MSc Jannik Anthonj
McKnight and Finkel, 2013). A SD-based WRM model will give stakeholders a
better understanding of how complex systems function through visualization and
computer simulation. It merits are the speed of model development and improve-
ment, the ability to simulate the interactions between the model’s components,
as well as its inherent ﬂexibility and transparency (Khan et al., 2009; Sehlke and
Jacobson, 2005). Within the conceptual category of hydrological models, SD mod-
els can help to design and simulate any process of concern in a watershed simply,
at the same time. They help support decision makers with economic, social and
political issues due to a watershed. Evaluation of the quantity and quality of both
surface and groundwater simoultaneously is therefore one of the requirements of
the WFD for assessing the risk contaminant sources may pose to ecosystems and
human health (Hinsby et al., 2008; McKnight et al., 2012).
The research presented thus aims to create hereby a framework which bridge
the gap by utilizing an approach for modeling the interconnection between ground-
and surface water at the stream corridor scale and furthermore will allow the cou-
pling of water quantity to a water quality module to better support sustainable
decision making at the catchment scale. Similar to previous studies (see Sehlke
and Jacobson (2005); Khan et al. (2009)), a SD framework is used to build a deci-
sion support system which simulates the hydrologic behavior of both ground- and
surface water. The novelty in this approach is that it separates the aquifer input
such that each side of the stream is simulate, which could be useful when coupling
to water quality issues, e.g. understanding the complex processes along meanders,
where streams potentially inﬂuence e.g. contaminant plumes (Nicolajsen, 2014).
Furthermore, the use of daily time steps has not been applied yet on WRM models
in SD.
1.2
Objectives
The overall aim is to develop a Decision Support System (DSS) for water quantity
in mixed land use catchments that can be coupled to water quality for assessing
e.g. the impact of multiple stressors along a stream corridor by use of a system
dynamics approach. The DSS has been applied to the Grindsted stream site. The
speciﬁc aims of this thesis are thus:
1. To explore the ﬁeld site and existing data and carry out stream ﬂow mea-
surements for hands-on understanding of the study site.
2. To develop a DSS which supports the management of water resources, where
SD is used to emulate the key hydrological factors. Furthermore, the possi-
bility to couple the water quantity module to water quality must be given.

2.1
Hydrologic models
Watershed modeling is situated at the centre of water resources planning. Tradi-
tionally, models are used to simulate a rainfall event to determine the contribution
of runoﬀit creates (Ghashghaei et al., 2013). Resulting from a precipitation event
in the catchment area, three kinds of ﬂows discharge to a stream (Winter, 1998):

Surface runoﬀ(=overland ﬂow) is generated as fast runoﬀ, if the soil is saturated
and thus no water can inﬁltrate anymore or the land cover no longer accepts
inﬁltration.
The surface runoﬀis the ﬁrst ﬂow which contributes to the
stream (Knudsen et al., 1986).
Inter ﬂow (=subsurface ﬂow) The amount of water which is not part of the
surface runoﬀis inﬁltrating into the soil. Here it either evapotranspirates,
percolates to the shallow aquifer or creates the subsurface ﬂow.
Base ﬂow (=groundwater ﬂow) The percolating water that recharges to the aquifer
then becomes base ﬂow to the stream. The base ﬂow regulates the stream
ﬂow also during phases between events. According to Dingman (2008), the
base ﬂow is usually assumed to be supplied by the groundwater circulation.

Groundwater and surface water interactions
From the very beginning of this project, a method for simulating groundwater dis-

can therefore be simulated in several ways. The Australian Government (2012)
tested ﬁve methods for quantifying the discharge between ground- and surface wa-
ter in ten ﬁeld studies in Australia. An overview of the methods is described in

2.1.2
Flow types and ﬂow rate
After Dingman (2008), generally four types of streams can be distinguished, which
are shown in ﬁgure 2.2 and described below (Australian Government, 2012; Winter,
1998):
Gaining streams receive water from the groundwater, which means the slope
of the water table slopes towards the stream. They are also called eﬄuent
streams. The discharging rate is determined by the hydraulic gradient (slope,
angle) of the water table and the hydraulic conductivity of the aquifer. In
eﬄuent streams, the water table in the stream is below the one of the aquifer.
Connected losing streams (= inﬂuent) occur if the water table in the stream
is higher than the groundwater table. The rate is controlled in the same
manner like in gaining streams - by the hydraulic gradient and the hydraulic
conductivity.
Disconnected losing streams can appear due to a layer with low permeability
beneath the stream bed.

2.2
System dynamics model framework
System Dynamics is a method of analyzing problems in which time is
an important factor, and which involves the study of how a system can
be defended against, or made to beneﬁt from, the shocks which fall upon
from the outside world.
Forrester (1961), cited by Ford (2010)
System dynamics (SD) was invented by Jay Forrester of the Massachusetts
Institute of Technology (MIT) in the 1960s for simulating business dynamics (Ford,
2010).
It is a methodology for studying and managing complex systems that
change over time. For focusing on the information feedback loops which increase
the dynamic behavior, computer modeling is used.
This helps in particularly
to understand the impact of time delays and nonlinearities in the system (Ford,
2010, 1999). Hereby, SD is a way to conceptualize physical processes in terms of
interconnecting various elements (Promio, 2011).

Approach
A SD model is built on an iterative way with steps of increasing complexity until it
ﬁnally simulates the natural system. According to Winz et al. (2009), the modeling
process can be therefore divided into four stages:
1. Building of a conceptual model.
2. Implementation of the conceptual model into a SD environment.
3. Testing the model through comparison with ﬁeld- or real world data.
4. Implementing and improving the model

The conceptual model is generally implemented using four diﬀerent entities,
stocks, ﬂows, converters and connectors, shown in Figure 2.3. Ford (2010, 1999)
recommends hereby to start with stocks as key variables, adding ﬂows and then
using converters to explain the ﬂows. A description of the entities was found in
Ford (2010, 1999):
Stocks represent storages. They accumulate the eﬀect of ﬂows and remain in
their current status if no ﬂows act on them. Furthermore, storages can be
used for delaying material ﬂow, if given a delay function.
Flows are the only way to change the status of stocks over the time. They rep-
resent material ﬂow in and out, as described by the arrow direction. If the
source/sink of the ﬂow is outside the system, a ﬂow is connected to the
boundary - represented as
in Figure 2.3.
Converters describe the ﬂows and dictate the rates at which processes operate.
Connectors deﬁne the relationships among diﬀerent components in the system
with the ﬂow of information. They are represented by single arrows.

2.2.2
Strengths
Stated in Winz et al. (2009), the strength of SD modeling can be summarized in
three diﬀerent categories:
Flexibility: SD modeling can be used for a wide range of applications. Multidis-
ciplinary projects support the use of qualitative and quantitative variables
in models.
Relationships between variables can be deﬁned on an ordinary scale. Diﬀer-
ent sub-models can be implemented and help increasing the interchangeabil-
ity and re-usability.
Established methodology: The dynamic nature helps users to quickly get an
understanding in the scope of the modeling and simulation process.
The models are built transparently, which is a crucial factor when involving
stakeholders for decision making, trust and acceptance.
Parameters do not necessarily need to be ﬁxed before the simulation, but
can be adjusted manually or dynamically.
Foresighting: Simulation allows the continuous testing of assumptions and sen-
sitivity analysis with few restrictions on problem presentation as long as
variables can be identiﬁed and relationships deﬁned.
2.2.3
Software
There is a large variety of softwares existing for system dynamic modeling. How-
ever, from the beginning of this study, the choice of software was between Ventana
System’s Vensim (Vensim DSS 6.4, 2015) and Simulink by The MathWorks, Inc.
(2016). The decision between both programs was made within a literature review.
Ford (2010) introduces SD software, including Stella, Vensim, Powersim, as
icon-based programs for modeling on the principles invented by Forrester (1961).
These programs are designed with the central focus on SD modeling, whereas other
programs like Simile, Simulink and GoldSim have not their central focus on SD
ideas. After consultation with the author (Ford, 1999, 2010), who described the
Simulink package as primitive and clumsy compared to other SD programs and
recommended Vensim as a platform, the decision was made to use Vensim DSS
6.4 (2015) as software. Furthermore, no studies were found on using Simulink for
water resources modeling and thus it was not seen as useful as program as Vensim
was.

3.1.2
Grindsted Engsø
Grindsted Engsø (Engsøen) is an artiﬁcial lake downstream of Grindsted town
which was landscaped in the early 1970s for providing a wastewater treatment
possibility for the Grindsted factory. However, this wastewater discharge ended in
1997 (Billund Kommune, 2016). It is not directly connected to Grindsted stream,
lying ca. 30m south of the stream but is fed by a channel. The water passes
through the up gradient lake and re-enters the stream down gradient via another
channel. The lake’s area is 30 ha with a mean depth of 1.55 m. Engsøen has,
compared to other Danish lakes, a low residence time with 39 days. The down
gradient channel was measured by Nielsen et al. (2014) with ca. 50 l · s−1 and
described by Petersen (2012) with ca. 30 - 40 l · s−1. Thus a signiﬁcant part of
the water balance is on the seepage from the lake to the river banks of Grindsted
stream (Billund Kommune, 2016).

Overview of the stream corridor
The investigated stream corridor has a length of ca. 17.5 km. In this area, several
potentially contaminant sources enter the stream, i.e. two ﬁsh farms, industrial
discharge, a contaminant plume and the discharge of a waste water treatment
plant. From the beginning of the study, the coupling of water quantity and qual-
ity should be enabled withing the modeling approach, and thus the contaminant
sources dealt as one of the motives for splitting the stream into diﬀerent reaches.
Furthermore, former researches indicated spatial variabilities of the hydrological
behavior within the investigated site.

discharge measurements (=DM) conducted during the ﬁeld work, described in

Reach01 can be found most upstream, whereas DM09 and Reach08 are downstream

This part was chosen to be presented ﬁrst, as it connects groundwater and surface

4.3.1.2
Evapotranspiration
Evapotranspiration (ET) is a term that summarizes all the processes involving
water in the liquid and solid phase at or near the land surface that becomes
atmospheric water vapor. It is an assembling of evaporation (=vaporization on
uncovered land or water bodies) and transpiration (=vaporization through plants).
Another process regarding ET is the sublimation which considers the vaporization
of water directly from the solid phase (Dingman, 2008).
Potential evapotranspiration
The potential evapotranspiration (ETp) was ﬁrst derived by Thornthwaite (1948),
assuming an area which is covered completely with growing vegetation and has
access to unlimited water supply. In this area, neither advection nor heat-storage
eﬀects occur (Dingman, 2008).
The ETp can be calculated using diﬀerent approaches, which are listed in table
4.2 compared by the type of data they use (Dingman, 2008). The most common
approach was derived by Monteith (1965) and has become known as the Penman-
Monteith equation (Dingman, 2008). However, due to the lack of data, a simpler
method was chosen for deriving the ETp.
Based on Thornthwaite (1948), Hamon (1963) estimated the ETp using a
temperature-based method, which is described in equation 4.5 (Dingman, 2008).

can be prevented.

of the aquifer. The hydraulic conductivity was set to be constant in each reach,
but could vary from reach to reach. It was left as a calibration parameter that was
allowed to range between 9 · 10−7 −10−4 m · s−1 for sandy layers (Duﬃeld, 2015).

4.8.3.1
OTT MF pro - Water Flow Meter
The OTT MF pro - Water Flow Meter is a current meter which capable to measure
ﬂow velocity and water depth. The ﬂow velocity is measured electromagnetic. It
can range between 0 −6.09 m · s−1 at a water depth of ≥3.18 cm. Its accuracy is
hereby 2−4 % of reading, depending on the ﬂow velocity (OTT Hydromet, 2015).
Furthermore, the current meter can measure the water depth ≤3.05 m with an
accuracy of 2 % in static, non-ﬂowing water. This is done by the absolute pressure
with single point calibration (OTT Hydromet, 2015).
The OTT MF pro - Water Flow Meter consists by a graphical user interface
(=GUI), where the measurement data can be monitored in real time and the results
be viewed and stored. This simpliﬁes the eﬀort with no paper work and can help
to improve the results (see Section 4.8.3.6).

4.8.3.3
Site selection
The selected sites should be as much as possible to a typical proﬁle shape. To
obtain the requirements of a suitable site, some guidelines are described by OTT
Hydromet (2015) which are slightly listed below:
1. As much straight run as possible.
2. Freedom of disturbances (pipe joints, sudden diameter changes, contribut-
ing/outgoing side-streams, obstructions).
3. No visible swirls, eddies, vortices, back-ﬂow or dead zones.
4. No areas immediately downstream of sharp bends or obstructions.
5. No areas with converging or diverging ﬂow.
6. No areas immediately downstream to sluice gates or places where channel
spills into a body of stationary water.

5.2
Model evaluation
A system dynamics model is typically evaluated using two criterions (Khan et al.,
2009; Winz et al., 2009; Ford, 1999, 2010). With the structural validity, the model’s
relations are tested. Diﬀerent scenarios can be tested on the model to demonstrate
the interactions between the components make sense. In particular, diﬀerent sce-
narios can be tested, i.e. changes in water levels, precipitation and climate factors.
Often, extreme scenarios are used for the structure validity (Khan et al., 2009;
Winz et al., 2009).
The second critereon is the behavioral validity, which refers to the simulations
ability to capture the observed system. Here, the calibrated model is tested on
the behavior in real conditions (right behavior for the right seasons), (Khan et al.,
2009)). This run, the base run, can then be compared to the runs of the structure
validity (Khan et al., 2009). The model evaluation was carried in three steps:
1. The structural validity was used for testing parameters and the sensitivity
of the model. In this validity analysis, estimated parameters have been used

2. The model was calibrated using the ﬁndings of the structure validity and the
existing data.
3. The model’s behavior was then tested by comparison of the observed dis-

the simulated values of the model.

Chapter 6
Conclusion
This study investigated the development of a DSS which is capable to couple water
quantity and quality across the groundwater-surface water interface.
Discharge measurements at in total 9 locations were successfully conducted and
gave an insight of the streams water quantity behavior. They have been compared
with existing data do get an overview of temporal and spatial variations in the
stream corridor. The study site was explored within the ﬁeld work to recognize
signiﬁcant changes regarding water quality and quantity, i.e. contaminant sources,
tributary in- and outﬂows and hydrological conspicuousnesses.
A DSS was developed using SD (Vensim DSS 6.4, 2015) which models the
water resources in the investigated area. The DSS comprises diﬀerent hydrological
pathways, i.e. surface runoﬀ, inﬁltration, evapotranspiration and percolation and
groundwater discharge to and from a surface water body. From the beginning of
the model devolpment, the focus was set in order to a possibility for coupling a
water quality module and thus the stream corridor was separated into 8 diﬀerent
reaches.
For testing the structure of the DSS was tested on diﬀerent on diﬀerent scenar-
ios, extreme climatic changes, diﬀerent hydraulic conductivities, land use change,
e.g.. Within a behavioral validity analysis, the DSS was tested of the accuracy it
can originated.
The application on the Grindsted stream site was conducted for the period of
28.06.2012 - 31.12.2013 and allowed comparison with existing ﬁeld data, collected
in the past. Although some data was not reliable the comparison to the ﬁeld data
showed reasonable results.
Altogether, the DSS can be concluded to be capable of simulating hydrological
behavior within a watershed. Furthermore, this study has proved SD to be useful
to determine hydrological trends and therefore to quantify hydrological discharges
to a stream.

Chapter 7
Future outlook
Regarding the assessment of water quality management across a stream corridor,
many things will need to be investigated. This chapter gives suggestions for further
reserach.
Coupling to water quality
Water quantity and water quality are inextricably linked, hence a coupling of
the water quantity module to water quality is needed. The DSS is constructed
to allow a coupling within this stream stretch. All known contaminant sources
can be coupled separably, and thus the water quantity module can be used to
determine the mass ﬂuxes. Furthermore the DSS allows with simulating surface
runoﬀthe coupling to diﬀuse contaminant sources, i.e. contamination caused by
agriculture and other land uses. The contaminant sources may be evaluated due
to their impact on the water quality on the stream corridor.
Determination of the hydrogeology
In order to eliminate uncertainties regarding the model perspective, an estimation
of the geological parameters at the study site is needed. Groundwater ﬂow and
geology are linked, and thus uncertainties in the one cause uncertainties in the
other. The depth of the aquifer is set with ca. 80 m. However, newish research
(GEOCON project, 2016) has resulted in a impermeable layer ca. 12 m which is
unknown where it occurs. Widespread groundwater potential maps could help to
determine areas of the stream losing and gaining groundwater.

Elimination of temporal variations
In this study, the groundwater-surface water interaction was estimated using Darcy’s
law. The groundwater equipotentials have been modeled based on one groundwa-
ter map by Orbicon (2016), and thus the temporal variations of the groundwater

APPENDIX
B
Derivation of the catchment parameters for
each reach
This section explains the handling of data with Esri ArcMap 10.1 (2012) for the
derivation of catchment speciﬁc parameters which diﬀer from one reach to another.
For the catchment parameters, four diﬀerent types of maps are provided for each
reach:
1. Land use with the diﬀerentiation between agricultural, urban and forestal
areas. The result are three values [%] of land use compared to the whole
catchment area.
2. The slope map gives the slope [%] for each point of the catchment area in
the reach. All slope values have been averaged for the calculations in the
model to ease the implementation in the model.
3. Flow length [m] is calculated for each point in the catchments and again, an
average value for the ﬂow length is used for further calculations.
4. The groundwater maps give a raster for the groundwater potential in the
area of one reach. The read out data is the average groundwater potential
100m north and south of the stream over the whole reaches area.
Derivation of the catchment of the reaches
The watersheds have been delineated with the Spatial Analyst Tool →Hydrology
which is provided by Esri ArcMap 10.1 (2012). Input data for the delineation of a
watershed is therefore a digital elevation model (DEM). A 1.6m grid of the Danish
DEM was found in Styrelsen for Dataforsyning og Eﬀektivisering (2016) and used
for the following calculations. All DEMs for the area of interest were ﬁgured out
and merged to one DEM with a range of
xUTM : 480, 000 −510, 000
yUTM : 6, 170, 000 −6, 190, 000
in the UTM32-ETRS89 coordinate system.
The watershed derivation consists of 5 steps:
Fill: Besides the x,y- coordinates, each pixel has a z-value. As a result of the
generation process, the z-value may be too high or too low. The Fill tool
removes sinks and peaks by the given trend of the surrounding surface values
(see ﬁgure B.1) (ESRI Inc., 2016c).

watershed-area of all reaches. Therefore, it was assumed, that all area which was
not classiﬁed to urban or forest is possibly agriculture.
As the land use map is not a raster, the raster-ﬁles of all reaches had to be
converted to polygon-shapeﬁles whereas the masking was then done using the
Analysis Tool →Intersect. For the derivation of the weights of the three sectors
of land use, the Spatial Analyst Tool →Zonal Geometry as Table was used, which
sums up all the areas which have been classiﬁed on the same land use.
The
whole catchment (see ﬁgure B.5) consists of an area of 138, 133, 954m2 with 77.22%
agriculture, 14.56% forest and 8.22% urban area. The resulting maps of the single
reaches can bee seen on the pages 96 - 110.

Slope
The average slope was calculated using the DEM, where Esri ArcMap 10.1 (2012)
uses the z-values for deriving the gradient between two pixel. This is done for the
whole catchment whereas the output is given as a slope map, where the average,
maximum and minimum can be read out.
Flow Length
The ﬂow length is a parameter, which indicates how far a raindrop has to ﬂow on
the surface until it reaches the outlet. It is often used for calculating the longest
way through a basin (ESRI Inc., 2016b). Input is the ﬂow direction map, where
an imaginary pathway is created and the length, depending on the pixel size is
calculated. Output was the maximum ﬂow length within a reach. 7
Groundwater
The groundwater map by Nielsen et al. (2014), described in Section 3.2 was used to
derive the groundwater potentials needed. A raster was created with its help and
the centerline was copied with respect to the distance from the stream. Afterwards
the stream’s copies have been cut to the length of the reaches and with extract
by mask a potential line could be created. As average values were needed for the
implementation in the model, the potential lines could be analyzed by using the
histogram function, whereas an average value was given.

APPENDIX
D
Deriving the surface water head
The hydraulic gradient analysis works with the head diﬀerence between the stream
and the aquifer. This section describes how the surface water elevation was com-
puted for each time step and which assumptions have been made for it. Finally,
the gradient for the entire stream and for each reach is set within some variation of
±0.04%, as a constant at 0.1%, independent of the stream’s discharge. This value
was taken for implementation into the model for the simulation of the surface
water elevation [m.a.sl.].
D.1
Creation of ratio curves
Ratio curves which relate the stream water elevation to the discharge at the gauging
station Eg Bro have been created. Data from 2012 - 2015 (provided by Orbicon
(2016)) was used, which tracked the surface water elevation and the corresponding
discharges. Considering all the data, one single ratio curve was not suﬃcient, and
thus the data was split up monthly to capture the diﬀerences in the elevation,
which mainly occurs due to plant growth in the stream.
The ratio curves have been created in Excel where the elevation [m.a.sl.] was
plotted against the discharge [L · S−1]. This curve was then analyzed by the trend
curve tool provided by the software, using logarithmic curves. Positive logarithmic
curves ensured that the head of the surface water was only allowed to increase if
discharge was higher, and conversely, to decrease if discharge was lower.
The accuracy of the ratio curve was set to two digits. Furthermore, the provided
tool show RSQ was used to estimate the accuracy (R2) compared to the original
data. The calculation is based on the Pearson product (Microsoft, 2016). As the
ratio curves were meant to be also part of the calibration, the accuracy was not
an elimination factor for not choosing ratio curves of the speciﬁc month. However,
for both May and June the ratio curves have been negative logarithmic and thus
could not be chosen for the implementation. Both have been replaced with ratio
curves from other months, i.e. May with April and June with July.

gradient had to be conducted.
