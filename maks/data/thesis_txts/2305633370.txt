Abstract
Increasing fuel prices and stricter rules of emissions have the recent years pushed
the research of alternative fuels in marine low-speed 2-stroke engines. A deeper
understanding of the combustion process is therefore essential.
The engines
which are used for propulsion in container ships, tankers ect. are very space
demanding and expensive to test due to high fuel consumption. The aim of
the thesis is therefore to develop a exible stand alone facility for experimental
testing of the combustion process in such engines. It covers a realistic gas com-
pression -and expansion process and hard requirements for the repeatability. At
the time of writing a system of that size for this specic purpose has never been
developed.
Two suitable systems have been identied during a thorough concept devel-
opment process. The systems are characterized as rapid compression expansion
machines (RCEM). Both systems incorporate a large hydraulic gas compressor
where the hydraulics provide a large degree of exibility. The main dierence
between the two systems are the driving concepts of the compressor. The rst
system is typied as the pneumatic driven RCEM and uses pressurized air which
is converted into uid power. The second system is typied as the mechanical
driven RCEM and uses stored kinetic energy from a ywheel which is converted
into uid power by a crankshaft mechanism.
The performance of the systems have been dynamically evaluated by zero-
dimensional zero-zone models written in Matlab. The results reveal that the
performance and the repeatability of the mechanical driven RCEM is the best.
However the systems do have a trade-o between size and performance where
the pneumatic driven RCEM is a more compact system. The nal recommenda-
tion, without considering any costs of the systems, is that the mechanical driven
RCEM is the most promising concept.

Resumé
Stigende brændstofpriser og strengere emissionsregler har de seneste år frem-
skyndet forskningen i alternative brændstoer for marine 2-takts motorer. En
dybere forståelse af forbrændingsprocessen er derfor essentiel. Motorerne der
bruges til fremdrivning af store containerskibe, tankskibe mv. kræver meget
plads og er dyrere at teste på grund af højt brændstoorbrug. Målet med denne
afhandling er derfor, at udvikle en eksibel og selvstændig facilitet til test af
forbrændingsprocessen i disse motorer. Det inkludere en realistisk kompression
-og ekspansionsproces og hårde krav til repeterbarhed. I skrivende stund er et
system i den størrelse, til det specikke behov, aldrig blevet udviklet.
To egnede systemer er blevet identiceret gennem et grundigt koncept -og ud-
viklingsforløb. Systemerne er karakteriseret som hurtig kompression ekspansions
maskiner (RCEM). Begge systemer inkludere et stort hydraulisk gas kompres-
sor stempel, hvor den hydrauliske olie giver god eksibilitet. Hovedforskellen på
de to koncepter er drivmåden. Det første system er kendetegnet som den pne-
umatisk drevne RCEM og bruger tryksat luft der konverters om til hydraulisk
energi. Det andet system er kendetegnet som den mekanisk drevne RCEM og
bruger oplagret kinetisk energi fra et svinghjul, som omdannes til hydraulisk
energi ved hjælp af en krumtap.
Ydeevnen af systemerne er blevet dynamisk evalueret med nul-dimensional
nul-zone modeller skrevet i Matlab. Resultaterne viser at ydeevnen og repe-
terbarheden er bedst for den mekanisk drevne RCEM. Det har vist sig at sy-
stemerne er et kompromis mellem ydeevne og størrelse, hvor den pneumatisk
drevne RCEM er mest kompakt. Den endelige anbefaling, uden at tage hensyn
til økonomi, er at den mekanisk drevne RCEM er det mest lovende koncept.

Preface
This thesis was prepared at the DTU Mechanical Engineering and in collabora-
tion with MAN Diesel & Turbo in fullment of the requirements for acquiring
an M.Sc. in Engineering. The thesis represents 35 ECTS points. The project
had been running from 1. August 2015 to 6. April 2016 and corresponds to
a full-time course of study with a stipulated time of 6 months. However, the
project period was extended 9 weeks due to 15 ECTS parallel activity.
The thesis deals with development of a rapid compression expansion machine for
investigation of the combustion process in large marine 2-stroke engines. The
eective time spend on the dierent project phases are: 2 months on concept
development, 2 months on concept 1 and 2 months on concept 2.
The thesis consists of two volumes:
• Volume One: Main report.
• Volume Two: Appendixes.
Lyngby, 06-April-2016
Casper Holte Nielsen

General background
The project is made in collaboration with Man Diesel & Turbo (MDT) and
is a part of the HERCULES-2 project (High Eciency R&D on Combustion
with Ultra Low Emissions for Ships).
The HERCULES-2 project is an EU-
project which consists of a joint venture between the two major two-stroke
marine diesel engine companies. The project is made up of 32 partners where
30 % are industrial and 70 % are universities and research institutions.
The goal of the project is "developing of a fuel-exible marine engine that is
optimally adaptive to its operating environment" and the work is divided into
4 work package groups. This thesis is a part of the work package group 2 which
consider "Multi fuel combustion". The aim of the work package is to develop a
spray combustion chamber (SCC).
The purpose of the SCC is to order a stand alone facility to test dierent fuels
in a combustion chamber which behave as realistic to a marine 2-stroke en-
gine. This includes visual access to realistic combustion process which gives the
opportunity to evaluate the dierent fuel characteristics. Furthermore, the ap-
plication must be able to test dierent engine control strategies, spray dynamics,
heat releases and emissions.

1.2
The low speed 2-stroke engine
The low speed 2-stroke engine is primarily sold for ship propulsion in large
container vessels, car carriers, tankers ect.
A minor fraction of the engines
are used in stationary power plants. The engines provide a power range from
1,560kW to 82,440kW. This corresponds to the power from 15 to 800 Volkswagen
TDI car engines. Ranging in bore from 30 to 95 cm, cylinder number from 4 to
12 and height from 5.9 to 16.2 m. A cross-sectional view of a G50ME-C engine
with pins to some of the main components are shown in gure 1.1.
Figure 1.1: Cross-sectional view of a G50ME-C engine.
Most of the engines sold for the ship industry today are electronically controlled.
This is a new trend compared to 10 years ago where all engines were mechanically
controlled by the cam-shaft. The new trend have opened up the opportunity
for tuning the exhaust valve and fuel injection timing to obtain even higher
eciency and less pollution. Traditionally the engines are powered by heavy fuel
oil (HFO). But the stricter rules of emissions today have pushed the industry to
use more exotic fuels as natural gas, LPG, methanol, ethane ect. At the time
of writing only a few dual fuels engines are in operation. However, the order
books tell that they will have a signicant breakthrough in the near future.
==PAGE:21==
1.2 The low speed 2-stroke engine

The engines do the complete cycle (intake, compression, ignition, exhaust) in
one revolution. The fuel is injected at very high pressure and temperature after
top dead center (TDC) where it immediately evaporates and self-ignites followed
by the combustion. This gives a pressure increase in the combustion chamber
and work is done on the crankshaft. The more exotic fuels, which are normally
used in the Otto-process, are injected in the same manner as the diesel fuels but
they are ignited with a small diesel injection.
The engines have longitudinal scavenging. This principle builds on air intake
ports (scavenging air ports) in the bottom of the cylinder liner and one large
exhaust valve centred in the cylinder head. Two or tree fuel valves are placed
around the exhaust valve. During the scavenging of the combustion chamber a
swirl is build. The swirl is very important for the engine performance since it
inuence the fuel and air mixing process.

The aim of this project is to develop a test facility for simulation of the combus-

2.1
Concept 1 - Pneumatic driven RCEM
Technical University of Munich (TUM) has developed a version of a pneumatic
driven RCEM where it is possible to adjust the piston speed without the need of
control valves. The compressor piston has a diameter of Ø220 and the system is
made for simulation of medium speed 4-stroke engines. A sketch of the RCEM is
shown in gure 2.3. A concentric driving piston (piston 1) moves in the opposite
direction of the compressor piston. In that way the system is balanced. The
pistons are connected with a hydraulic push rod which is also used to control
the machine.
Figure 2.3: TUM rapid compression machine [1].
The working principle is that energy stored on pneumatic pressure bottles is in-
stantaneously released and a piston drives rapidly to the TDC position. The pis-
ton compresses the gas charge inside the combustion chamber and the pressure
increases and temperature correspondent. Due to the relatively large masses of
the pistons the machine overshoots the steady state position and the pistons
bounce back resulting in a crankshaft like piston movement. The pistons will
continue to bounce until all the energy has been dampened away. In principle
the pistons work as ballistic masses. This means that no inuence on the piston
movement can be done as soon as the system is started.
Figure 2.4 shows a graph of the piston movement for the RCEM at TUM. The
red curve is the RCEM piston movement and the blue curve is for a real engine.
It is seen that the curves follow quite good near the TDC position which is
very essential in order to do good heat release experiments. A restriction in the
oil ow between the two pistons makes the opportunity to adjust the simulated
engine speed. The more the ow is restricted the more slow the piston movement

Concept 2 - Mechanical driven RCEM
A sketch of the Mechanical driven RCEM, proposed for concept 2, is shown in
gure 2.6. The system consists of a ywheel, a clutch-brake, an intermediate
shaft, a chain gear, a crankshaft, a driving piston which is connected to the

The working principle is that the fast spinning ywheel stores a large amount
of kinetic energy. The ywheel can be coupled to the rest of the system with a
rapid switching clutch. When the ywheel is coupled it spins the intermediate
shaft which drives the crankshaft through a chain gear. The chain gear reduces
the speed of the crankshaft to a level which matches the speed of a marine
two-stroke engine. The crankshaft is connected to the driving piston through
the crosshead. The driving piston is used to drive the hydraulic ow to the
working piston. After the crankshaft has turned 360o, the gas in the combustion
chamber has been compressed and expanded and the clutch is disengaged. A
fast switching brake integrated in the clutch is used to brake the shaft system
at BDC while letting the ywheel continue to spin.
The compression volume is adjusted by the start position of the working piston.
The ring side of the pistons are hydraulic connected to each other together with
a hydraulic gas accumulator. This part of the system has two purposes which is
cavitation precaution by pre-pressurizing the oil system and as a safety barrier
in case of failure in the combustion chamber.
In appendix A.8 the system is sketched with the option of changing the hy-
draulic gearing between the driving piston and the working piston. It gives the
opportunity to adjust the stroke to a desired length. Practically it is achieved
by having more driving pistons which can be coupled incrementally in and out
between tests. It gives the opportunity of changing the stroke bitwise.

3.2
Assumptions
In order to simplify the simulation some assumptions has been made:
1. No heat loss from oil volumes: The temperature dierence between the
oil volume and the ambient is very small. Considering the short time the
system is running this eect has been neglected.
2. All loss in the valves are transferred into heat in the oil: The orice pipe
and the by-pass throttles the ow resulting in an energy loss. Some of the
energy will radiate from the heated valve surface and some of the energy
will disappear into heat in the oil. This model simulates the worst case
scenario where all energy is transformed into heat in the oil.
3. Leakage free hydraulic pistons: The only leakage accounted in this model
is from the combustion chamber. This is done in order to validate if it has
signicant inuence on the repeatability of the piston movement.

3.5.2
Friction
The RCEM has 1 gas seal and 2 hydraulic seals. The gas seal is positioned at
the upper part of the working piston. And the hydraulic seals are positioned at
the lower part of the working piston and on the driving piston, respectively.
The friction is a hard size to predict. It depends on many factors such as the
seal type, lubrication ect. The friction originates from the seals which push out
against the cylinder liner. The force from the seals against the cylinder liner has
to be large enough to prevent gas and oil escaping from the chambers. Before
a relative motion of an object can begin the static friction force must be over-
come. For hydraulic cylinders the static friction force is time dependent. The
phenomena occurs after the piston has been at rest and the lubricant between
the seal and the cylinder liner is pushed out. If the cylinder has been at rest for
a while the force required to start the relative motion is higher and vice versa.
Several models for estimation of friction in pneumatic and hydraulic cylinders
are proposed in the literature. Today many methods refer back to the work
published in 1902 by R. Stribeck who studied friction in roller-bearings [14]. He
described the friction phenomena with the so-called Stribeck curve which divides
the friction into 3 regimes. Namely solid/boundary friction, mixed friction and
uid friction. In 1947 A. Tustin proposed a mathematical model for the friction
which is suitable for numerical models [15]:
Ff = FC + Fvv + Fse−fexpv
(3.45)
where FC is the Coulomb friction, Fv is the velocity dependent friction, Fs is the
stribeck eect and fexp is the exponential decay. The maximum static friction
is Fc + Fs.
This model does not include the time dependency of the static friction, backlash
eects and pressure dependent Coulomb friction. These eects are important

for the RCEM where the piston is accelerated strongly in the beginning after

4.2.5
Cavitation risk
When dealing with high hydraulic ow and large pressure drops there is always
a risk of cavitation. Cavitation is formation and collapse of vapour bubbles.
When the bubbles collapse it is associated with high release of energy which can
give high noise and material damages. Cavitation occurs in regions where the
pressure is lower than the oil vapour pressure. For a pressure reduction system
like the orice in the RCEM, the cavitation can occur even if the nal pressure
is above the oil vapour pressure. This is because the uid is accelerated through
the orice reaching the maximum velocity a little downstream of the orice. The
point of the maximum velocity is called the Vena Contracta. The increase in
uid kinetic energy costs a drop in the pressure and therefore a risk of reaching
the oil vapour pressure. Downstream of the Vena Contracta the uid kinetic
energy is converted back to pressure energy. It gives a pressure increase and
cavitation damages can potentially occur.
There does not exist any bullet proof method to evaluate cavitation. A simple
and common used way is to evaluate the cavitation index. It is given as the ratio
of the pressure dierential between the upstream pressure and the oil vapour
pressure to the pressure dierential across the orice.
Ci =
pupstream −pvapour
pupstream −pdownstream
(4.1)
W. Nurick [16] has experimentally found that cavitation occur for Ci < 1.45 in
spay atomizers with a sharp edged orice hole. [17] estimates that cavitation
occurs for a sharp edged orice in a pipe ow at Ci < 2 with a range of 1.8 −6.
The latter case is closest to the ow phenomena in the RCEM.

Based on the results it can be concluded that the performance of the RCEM
becomes better by introducing a hydraulic gearing between the pistons and by
increasing the masses of the pistons. Good performance and feasible geometrical
sizes are obtained with the parameters presented in table 4.3.
A cavitation evaluation is made which states that the system is probably exposed
for cavitation, but it will most likely not inuence the global results of the
simulation.
However, it is expected that cavitation must be anticipated by
making the components around the orice pipe interchangeable.
The sensitivity analysis reviled good results. The most sensitive parameters are
the combustion chamber start pressure and the oil temperature. It is found
that the pressure must be set within a tolerance of ±0.2bar which is reasonable
to obtain. Due to the sensibility of the oil temperature, it is concluded that a
cooling/heating system must be applied on the RCEM.

compensated for by increasing the initial ywheel speed. The conclusion
is that a ywheel of 100, 000kgm2 is feasible for simulating a G95ME-C
engine at 100% load.

Requirement 2: No piston movement between each shot. When verifying compu-
tational uid dynamics (CFD) of the combustion process it is essential to have
some accurate boundary conditions. The boundary condition such as scaveng-
ing -and exhaust pressure and temperature are not very well dened in real
engines because of the multiple cylinders and uctuating conditions. The best
way to ensure well dened start conditions, as swirl, combustion chamber start
pressure and combustion chamber start temperature, is by xing the piston at
BDC between each test.
In the pneumatic RCEM the piston stays at BDC
until the hydraulic by-pass valve opens. In the mechanical driven RCEM this is
achieved by the fast switching clutch-brake which provides only on compression
and expansion stroke. Both systems full the requirement satisfactory.
Requirement 3: Opportunity to create swirl. The conventional scavenging air
process in a marine 2-stroke engine is dominated by a swirling ow. The swirling
ow is established by a longitudinal pressure dierential between the angled
ports in the bottom of the cylinder liner and the exhaust valve in the top of the
cylinder head. The angled ports are uncovered by the piston at BDC. The swirl
has a large impact on the fuel-air mixing process and the combusted and un-
combusted zones. A realistic swirl is therefore essential when testing production
atomizers.
The optimal way to ensure a realistic swirl in the RCEM is by
longitudinal scavenging like for the engine. It can be achieved by a compressor,
a pressurized gas reservoir, a valve and a scavenge air box around the cylinder
ports. Since the RCEM is not continuous running, the size of the air compressor
depends on the desired injection receptivity.
The deeper evaluation of such
a system has not been conducted in this study and is left for further work.
However, it is concluded that both systems can full the requirement.

justment of the compression -volume, -pressure and -temperature. Both concepts
are 4 degree of freedom (DOF) systems which allow adjustment of the target
compression -volume, -pressure, -temperature and speed.
Hence the RCEM

7.2 Case study
77
7.2
Case study
In the initial project phase, some case studies have been dened. They are a
part of the task in the project denition in section 1.3 and is answered below.
How will change of combustion chamber geometry, for instance bore and stroke,
inuence the system? The pneumatic driven RCEM is less feasible for change
in combustion chamber geometry. This is because the masses in the system are
specially optimized for simulating one engine type. The considerations about
scaling the system from 50 cm to 60 cm bore showed that it was feasible but
the system performance decreased. See appendix B.3.3. The mechanical driven
RCEM is designed with the aim of simulating dierent engine types. This system
has shown excellent results for 50 cm and 95 cm bore engines. The engine types
in-between are not simulated. However, it is assessed that the mechanical driven
RCEM is able to simulate these engines too.
How will injection repetition rate as marine 2-stroke engine inuence the sys-
tem? The pneumatic driven RCEM will never reach as good injection repetition
as a marine 2-stroke engine. The time between the injections is determined by
the auxiliary systems for reloading of the system. The auxiliary systems are
not treated in details in this study. However, a best estimate will be about 3
minutes. In principle the mechanical driven RCEM could run with the same
injection repetition as a real engine. It will just require an enormous auxiliary
system for conditioning of air for the RCEM and cooling on the cylinder head
and liner. The conclusion is that the simplicity of the system is lost by increasing
the injection repetition rate as for a real engine.
How can full compression and expansion stroke be achieved?
The pneumatic
driven RCEM does not provide the latter. But the mechanical driven RCEM
can both provide a full compression and expansion stroke. This is achieved by
a crankshaft kinematic like in a real engine.
7.3
Model uncertainties
Both RCEM systems are simulated with so-called zero-dimensional models writ-
ten in Matlab and do not contain any consideration of the ow eld dimensions.
The systems are divided into relatively few zero-zone control volumes where
the state is assumed to be uniform within the volume. The largest uncertainty
is expected to be the ow ways between the driving cylinder and the working
cylinder for both systems.
==PAGE:96==
78
Comparison of pneumatic vs mechanical driven RCEM
The ow in the pneumatic driven RCEM is predicted with an orice correlation.
The correlation is valid for a straight pipe fully developed ow. This is not
the case in the RCEM where the ow changes its momentum when it passes
from one cylinder to the other. Hence the discharge coecient of the ow will
deviate. It is not expected that this will change the general behaviour of the
RCEM but it could introduce some errors of the chosen ow way areas (orice
pipe size). It must be kept in mind when designing the machine that the ow
passage ways must be made large enough to not slow down the RCEM. Another
estimate of the discharge coecient can be obtained by evaluating dierent
loss coecients for various ow way shapes proposed in the literature. Lastly
more sophisticated methods as computational uid dynamics (CFD) can be used
to obtain a detailed picture of the ow in the RCEM. The mechanical driven
RCEM has the same uncertainty about estimating the discharge in the pipes
but it does not inuence the performance in the same way as for the pneumatic
driven RCEM.
The clutch-brake model of the mechanical driven RCEM is aected by some
uncertainty within the assumed engaging and disengaging times. However, the
sensitivity analysis enclosed in appendix D.3.2 shows that the clutch engaging
time is not sensitive in the interval from 50ms to 400ms. The clutch disengag-
ing time and brake engaging time has not been investigated. In order to stop
the RCEM at 100 RPM the two operations must be accomplished in 300 ms.
The clutch-brake is normally used for punching machines where the shafts are
coupled and decoupled over one revolution. The chain gear on the RCEM gives
the clutch-brake 3.5 revolution to complete the same operation on the RCEM
at the same speed. The uncertainties are therefore not expected to cause any
large deviations.

Chapter 8
Conclusion
This section presents the most important conclusions based on the results in
this thesis.
The concept development reveal that the most feasible concept are a pneumatic
driven RCEM and a mechanical driven RCEM. Both concepts are characterized
as ballistic systems and can be adjusted between the runs by 4 DOFs. Fur-
thermore both systems use hydraulic oil for energy transfer between a driving
piston and a working piston which provide good exibility. The RCEM experi-
ences high force from the combustion and therefore large hydraulic piston areas
are needed to keep the uid pressure at an appropriate level. The trade o is
high ow. Hence it is not feasible to use hydraulic control valves to adjust the
piston movement.
A zero-dimensional zero-zone model has been developed for simulation the pneu-
matic driven RCEM. The model is used to verify the ballistic behaviour and
hard requirements of the repeatability. Hence it was necessary to include a few
model features as temperature and pressure dependent oil properties for the
bulk-modulus, density and viscosity, inclusion of predened air content in the
oil, Reynold's dependent ow through an orice, stick and slip friction model,
simplied heat release model for simulation of the combustion process, piston
blow-by and heat loss. Another state-space model has been developed for the
mechanical driven RCEM. The model basically contains the same features as
==PAGE:98==
80
Conclusion
mentioned above. However, the model has a few more control volumes and the
orice ow is substituted with two pipe ows. In addition to that, this model
includes the dynamic behaviour of a shaft system with a friction clutch-brake,
a chain gear and a crankshaft kinematic.
The pneumatic driven RCEM is the most compact system. The height of the
system is scalable with the stroke. The study has veried that the compromise
between the start pressure and temperature in the cylinder and stroke length
does not inuence the performance signicantly. It gives the opportunity to ad-
just the trade o between the size of the compressor/heating system for scavenge
air versus the size of the RCEM. The RCEM has been optimized to match the
relatively low frequency of a marine two-stroke engine. Hence it has been neces-
sary to increase the total masses of the pistons to 6 ton in order to bring down
the natural frequency of the system and avoid high hydraulic throttle loss and
cavitation. On the performance side, the system is able to do a full compression,
which is rather slow compared to a real engine, and a partly expansion stroke.
The compression and expansion stroke show good agreement with a real engine
near TDC where the expansion stroke is slightly better and long enough to cover
a full heat release at high load. The sensitivity analysis showed generally good
results. However, the most sensitive parameters are the oil temperature and the
start pressure in the combustion chamber. It is concluded that both parameters
can be controlled sucient with appropriate designed axillary systems.
The mechanical driven RCEM is less compact in size due to the large mechanical
components. However, the system can be tuned to cover the complete engine
programme from 50 cm bore to 95 cm bore. It is found that a working piston
with a hydraulic diameter of 80 cm will be sucient to cover the entire range.
Furthermore a 4 module build crankshaft is recommended. Each module consists
of a crank throw, a clutch-brake, a ywheel, a chain gear and driving cylinders.
The driving cylinders can be coupled incrementally in and out to cover dierent
engine stroke lengths. The initial investment cost of the system can be reduced
by only building one module covering a 50 cm bore engine. Furthermore, the
system can be extended with the last 3 modules to cover the entire engine
programme. The system has shown excellent performance results which cover
simulation of an almost perfect compression and expansion stroke similar to
marine two-stroke engines. Furthermore the system has shown to be a lot less
sensitive to parameter changes.
Overall, both systems full the project requirements stated in section 1.3. The
system with the best performance and the best repeatability is the mechanical
driven RCEM. If neglecting any considerations about the costs of the systems,
the mechanical driven RCEM is recommended as the most promising concept.

A.4.1
Introduction
A.4.1.1
Baseline
The baseline of this system consists of four hydraulic components. This include
a pump, a hydraulic cylinder, an accumulator and a control valve. The system
is show in gure A.2 in an ultra simple form.
Figure A.2: Baseline concept of solution 1.
When the system is at rest the accumulator is charged with nitrogen (N2) to
a desired pressure. When the pump starts the N2 in the accumulator is com-
pressed and energy is stored. The hydraulic accumulator works analogue to a
capacitor in an electrical circuit. When the accumulator is fully charged the 3/4-
way proportional valve is switched to the lower position and the piston moves
upwards. At this time the cylinder is positive loaded. While the piston moves
the position of proportional valve is adjusted which change the pressure drop
==PAGE:118==
8
Additional: Concept development
over the valve and hence the ow to the cylinder. At a certain time the propor-
tional valve closes and the piston stops at TDC. When the proportional valve is
switched to the top position the piston starts to move in the opposite direction.
The cylinder is now negative loaded. The cycle is over when the piston reach
BDT. Before another test can begin the accumulator must be charged to the
same level again.
A.4.1.2
Multiple pressure line system
A multiple pressure step system is sketched in gure A.3. By comparing the base
line concept and the multiple pressure concept it is seen that the 3/4-way valve
has been replaced by several 2/2-way valves and corresponding accumulators
which are charged to dierent pressures. The advantages is that the throttle
loss over the proportional valve is minimized.
Figure A.3: Hydraulic actuator driven by multiple pressure step lines.
In principle the system has four pressure lines namely the tank pressure (TP),
the low pressure (LP), the intermediate (IP) and the high pressure (HP). First
the pump is turned on and the accumulators are charged to a desired level.
When the accumulators are ready the LP valve opens and the piston starts to
move. The cylinder is now positive loaded. While the piston moves the ring
side pushes hydraulic uid into a low pressure accumulator. When the IP is
almost reached the LP valve starts to close and the IP valve starts to open. The
coupling between the IP and HP is done in the same manner. At the time when
the piston has reached TDC all valves are closed. The combustion process adds
additional energy to the system which is now recuperated in the accumulator
when the piston moves in the opposite direction. At this time the cylinder is
negative loaded.

A.5.1
Introduction
The rapid compression machine (RCM) is a well tested system. It can be bought
as a standard solution for piston diameters of up to Ø90 [25]. But most of the
machines are home-made. The RCM has been used for fundamental chemical
kinetic studies in many years [26].
A.5.1.1
Working principle
Energy stored on pneumatic pressure bottles are instantaneously released and
a piston drives rapidly to the TDC position. The piston compresses the gas
charge inside the combustion chamber and the pressure increases and tempera-
ture correspondent. The literature reports many experiments carried out with
RCMs, however the literature does not report much about how these machines
are constructed.
A.5.1.2
TUM test rig
Technical University of Munich (TUM) has develop more versions of rapid com-
pression machines (RCM). The largest one has a bore of Ø200 and a maximum
pressure of 200 bar. It is a machine made for simulation of four stroke medium
speed marine engines running 800 - 900 rpm. The facility includes swirl and
adjustment of the piston speed.
The basic of the TUM RCM is shown in gure A.27. Two concentric pistons
are aligned and move in opposite direction, a compression piston and a mass
balance piston.
This arrangement reduces vibrations and allows for optical
measurements.
In the beginning of the compression the inner and outer oil
chambers are closed by the compression piston. When the bypass valve opens
the compression piston is slowly moved until there is free passage from the
outer to the inner oil chamber. In that way the piston works as a fast switching
valve. The piston is now accelerated fast and decelerates again when the air
gets compressed in the combustion chamber. The injection of fuel is triggered
according to piston position or pressure level. The piston speed is controlled by
the driving pressure and the damping system in the bottom of the RCM [1].

A.8.1
Introduction
The system consists of a electrically or hydraulically motored crankshaft. On
the end of the crankshaft a big ywheel is mounted so constant rotational speed
over a crankshaft revolution is obtained. The crankshaft drives a number of
hydraulic cylinders which provides ow to the large hydraulic cylinder which
works as a gas compressor. A large bypass valve is installed so the ow can
bypass the large hydraulic cylinder.
The crankshaft can either be with linear arranged cylinders, radial cylinders or
a boxer layout. Depending on space and choice of balancing. The dierence
from a conventional combustion engine is that all pistons is pushed to TDC at
the same time. Furthermore, the crankshaft is reduced in size compared to the
simulated engine. Overall the crankshaft and the hydraulic cylinders work as a
large pump providing a periodic ow. The magnitude of the ow has exactly the
magnitude needed to drive the large hydraulic cylinder at the desired velocity.
The system is made such that the speed of the crankshaft is equivalent to the
speed of the simulated engine.
By having the same L/S-ratio (connecting rod / stroke) as the simulated engine
the areas on the hydraulic cylinders can be scaled in order to obtain a specic
stroke. When the crankshaft has pushed the hydraulic pistons to TDC the large
hydraulic cylinder is also in TDC position. In that way the frequency for the
crankshaft mounted hydraulic cylinders and the large hydraulic cylinder are the
same.

to friction loss. The resulting work delivered back to the driving chamber is 0.8
MJ. This means that 13 % of the energy needed for compressing the combustion

An undesired eect of the throttling is the increase in oil temperature, as shown

the performance of the RCEM, from shot to shot, as discussed in section 4.3.2.
It is therefore concluded that a cooling system is needed.

from the clutch signal is deactivated until the torque is 0 is called the dis-
engaging time. The disengaging time is used as a model input parameter.
4. In this state the clutch is disconnected by moving the plates fully away
from each other. This period is characterized by a slip behaviour. The
sinusoidal decrease of clutch torque is continued until Tc = 0.
Clutch/brake signal: o/o

for the clutch, the brake torque during the brake engaging time is charac-
terized by an sinusoidal increase in torque until the dynamic brake torque
Tbd is reached.
6. The brake torque drops to the loading torque Tb = TL and the brake plates
sticks to each other.
Clutch/brake signal: o/on
7. The brake plates stick against each other and the brake torque decrease
according to a sinusoidal function.
8. The brake plates slip against each other and the brake torque continue to
decrease according to a sinusoidal function until Tb = 0.
