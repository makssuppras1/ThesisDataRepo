Abstract
It is well known that vitreous enamel coatings can provide excellent protection against a wide array
of highly corrosive environments. But little work is available on the actual performance and failure
modes when subjected to highly corrosive environments, especially when defects are present in the
coating. The literature mainly deals with chemical attack on the surface of enamels or glass, which
to a large extent is done out of aesthetic considerations, more so than from a corrosion standpoint. In
this project ASTM G48-11 ferric chloride pitting test along with ASTM B117-11 salt spray testing,
was used to induce corrosion in enameled samples. Samples consisted of enameled low alloy steel and
stainless steel, using enamels both with and without adhesion promoters. Analysis was performed
using primarily SEM and EDS for chemical analysis. Computer modeling was used to model thermal
expansion and residual stress in the coatings, and combined with thermodynamic calculations to
explain corrosion failure seen on the samples. This approach to assessing the corrosion performance
and failure should make it possible to reliably tailor enamel coatings to a wide array of high corrosion
environments

Introduction
Vitreous enameling has been known to exist since the beginning of civilization. Throughout history,
vitreous enameling has been used to adorn jewelry and sculptures. With the industrial revolution,
the usage of vitreous enameling was attempted by industry. A suitable technique, however, was
not developed until 1840 in Germany. Due to exceptional corrosive resistance properties, vitreous
enameling soon became a commonly used surface technology in the early 20th century.
1.1
Vitreous Enameling; The Process in Broad Strokes
The practical process of enameling is fairly simple compared to other well known surface treatments
like plating or thermochemical hardening, this is also the reason why enameling have been used since
the Egyptians. Simpliﬁed, the enameling process consists of grinding glass to a ﬁne powder, called
a frit. Uniformly distribute the frit on the part to enamel, and burn the part for a short period at
elevated temperature typically in the range 700-800◦C.
A modern industrial enameling process commonly consists of the following 5 steps:
• Mixing of frit
• Milling of frit
• Coating
• Drying
• Firing
First the frit is made by smelting a basis glass typically consisting of oxides like; Na2O, CaO,
Al2O3, B2O3 and SiO2. The smelted material is quenched in water or in water-cooled rollers, and
ground into a rough powder.
Depending on which metal the enamel has to be applied on the basis frit can be mixed with
other components to promote wetting and adhesion abilities of the enamel. One of the most used
is Cobalt oxide (CoO). CoO has a distinctive blue color which is characteristic for many enamels
and further helps the adhesion of the enamel. This is what is reﬀed to as a adhesion promoter.
When the desired pigments, adhesion promoters and thermal expansion coeﬃcient controlling
elements have been added the enamel is milled into a very ﬁne powder, which allows the enamel

a very ﬁne powder it can also be applied electrostatically, like it is know from powder coating with

1.3
Literature Review
The ﬁrst thing that will strike any newcomer to the ﬁeld of vitreous enamels, is the relative scarcity
and or availability of new publications and reference material within this ﬁeld. Most major de-
velopments can be dated back to the 1930’s - 1960’s, after which interest in the ﬁeld seams to
sharply decline. Among the most notable contributions during this time is the work of Dr. A. I.
Andrews: Porcelain (Vitreous) Enamels[3](1935), in collecting and unifying the available knowledge
and observations of the time in one holistic book. The book was later revised in 1961 again Dr.
A. I. Andrews to include the many diﬀerent developments during the intervening years. The book
received its 3. and latest revision in 2011 by Dr. Silvano Pagliuca and Mr. William D. Faust, new
preparation and application techniques that had become available since 1961, among other updates
to the material. As a whole the this book provides a corner stone in the understanding and practical
workings of vitreous enamels, and is a such the primary reference work in this report.
Another central work is the work is: Nature of Adherence of Porcelain Enamels to Metals (1959),
by B. W. King, H. P. Tripp and W. H. Duckworth. This is arguably one of the most extensive and
systematic investigations into the requirements for attaining adherence between enamel coating an
metal substrate. The work lead them to formulate a set of fundamental criteria for achieving ad-
herence, which was tested on 27 diﬀerent substrate metals. Their conclusion forms much of the
scientiﬁc rational for the experiment in this report. The ﬁnal conclusion of their work being the 4
following statements:
(1) The bond between enamels and metals can be chemical in nature.
(2) Good adherence requires that the enamel at the interface be saturated with an oxide
of the base metal, and this oxide must be one which, when in solution in the enamel, will not be
reduced by the metal.
(3) Good adherence of enamels to metals appears to be the result of metal-to-metal bonding
between the atoms in the base metal and metallic ions in the enamel when certain chemical and
thermodynamic requirements are met.
(4) The oxides commonly known as adherence promoters help to establish and maintain the
conditions necessary for good adherence without contributing directly to the bond developed.

2.2
Glass to Metal Adhesion
The central function of the metal to enamel coating system, is the nature of the bond between
metal and glass/enamel. If preformed correctly the bond strength between the enamel layer and
the metal substrate can exceed the yield strength of the enamel itself. Although this adhesion is at
the core of all vitreous enamels, it is sadly often the most misunderstood part of vitreous enamels.
On the most principal level three types of bonds can be responsible for the adhesion 1) Van der
Waals forces 2) Mechanical bonds 3) Chemical bonds. Van der Waals forces is by far the weakest
in the range of 2 to 4 kJ mol−1 per atom pair [6] whereas covalent bonds can be up to 100 times
stronger, the strengths of these bonds have been calculated to be in excess of 10 GPa[7]. So in
the case of chemical bonds the interface strength can end up being extremely strong. Compared
to the bulk tensile strength of a annealed glass at 50 MPa under compressive load[3, p. 791][7].
The maximal strengths of a pure mechanical bond would be lower than that of a chemical bond, at
approximately 35 MPa. The result of this is that if the bond between metal and enamel is chemical
in nature it is very unlikely that clean breaks at the interface would occur but rather in the bulk of
the coating.

Ever since the ﬁrst enamels was applied to metals, i has been observed that the addition of certain
oxides to the enamel improves the adhesion between enamel and metal. The most common of these
oxides is cobalt oxide, nickel oxide and to some extent copper oxide, where cobalt oxide is by far
the most used and is responsible for the characteristic blue color found in many enamels.

in B. W. King in 1959[7]. "The oxides commonly known as adherence promoters help to establish
and maintain the conditions necessary for good adherence without contributing directly to the bond
developed". Some of the science behind this deceptively short statement will now be elaborated.

electronic structure as seen on Figure 2.4. This would explain why maintaining interface saturation

2.4
Chemical Analysis
Preforming an exact chemical analysis on an enamel coating is a challenging prospect, because of
several factors. For one, most enamels are nearly completely amorphous, maybe with some slight
crystalline precipitations. This means that many of the analysis methods that rely on crystal in-
teraction, like diﬀraction spectroscopy e.g. X-ray diﬀraction (XRD) or Electron Diﬀraction, are of
limited use. These techniques are mostly used in the study of precipitation in glass-ceramics.
The combination of the high electrical resistivity (1010-1014Ω·m)[3, p. 799] and typical coating
thickness of <50 µm, excludes the use of electrochemical analysis methods e.g. polarization curves,
for study of the enamel coating. Electrochemial analysis is thus limited to the study of defects in
the coating.
This leaves the methods that relies on direct interaction with the electrons of the constituent ele-
ments such as X-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS), X-ray ﬂuorescence (XRF), Glow-discharge
optical emission spectrometry (GDOES) and Energy-dispersive X-ray spectroscopy (EDS). For the
scope of this report EDS is the chosen analysis method, due to the availability with Scanning Elec-
tron Microscopes (SEM) with EDS capability making it well suited for the study of cross sectional
studies of the enamel.
Test experiments with GDOES was preformed but due to the high number of diﬀerent elements
comprising a typical enamel, and the lack of calibrated references this method proved unsuitable.
EDS relies on an incoming beam of charged beam of particles, in this case electrons, directed at
the material being analyzed. If they are of suﬃcient energy (typically between 5-15 keV) there is a
chance that they might hit an orbiting electron as seen in Figure 2.6 displacing an orbiting electrode
resulting in higher energy electrons to decay to a lower energy state, while emitting a characteristic
electromagnetic radiation (X-ray). The energy of the X-ray radiation is detected, allowing element
identiﬁcation as the diﬀerent elements have diﬀerent characteristic energy ﬁngerprint. Combining
this with the relative intensity it is possible to get the percentages of diﬀerent elements in the sample
in either wt%(weight percentage) or mol%(Atomic faction).

Milling of the Frit
After the out-gassing of the molten glass, it is quenched on an aluminum block. The rapid cooling
ensures that little to no crystal precipitation occurs, and incorporates a large amount of internal
stress. The resulting glass blob, however, is diﬃcult to brake down to the ﬁne particles needed for a
good enamel frit. The relative high hardness of the enamel of 480-630 HV[17, p. 663], means that
regular ceramic laboratory mortar and pestle is ill suited to breaking up the larger enamel pieces,
and might shatter as a result.1 The most common method of breaking up enamels in industry, is by
quenching the molten glass between water cooled rollers[3, p. 374], resulting in a thin sheet of glass
that is easily broken. Following the quenching the enamels ﬂakes need to be milled to a consistent
smaller particle size, which is done by use of a ball mill ﬁlled with water and ceramic media[3, p.
391]. This process is well suited for fabrication large quantities, whereas for the fabrication of small

4.1
Adhesion
In the following section, the testing procedure for judging the adhesion will be described and the
parameters involved in achieving proper adhesion.
There are diﬀerent ways for ranking adhesion, but directly measuring the bond strength, as stated
in Section2.2, is almost impossible since strength of chemical bonds at the interface might exceed
the yield strength of the bulk enamel.
This combined with the diﬃculty in mounting a sample in a rig since this inevitably relies
on the strength of the mounting adhesive. Resulting in that direct measurements are rarely done.
The more common practice is by indirect evaluation of the adhesion quality by deformation
test. There are diﬀerent ways to preform this, the most commonly used in evaluating otherwise
identical coatings e.g. thickness, substrate metal and enamel type, is ASTM B916 [18] this test uses
a ball shaped weight to make an indent on a enameled sheet and then evaluating the adhesion by
the amount of enamel renaming on the surface in the following way.
• 0 = No adherence—only shiny metal showing, and enamel may be ﬂaking oﬀbeyond the
impact area.
• 1 = Poor adherence—very little glass adhering, less than 10 %, and only at the outer edges
of the impact area.
• 2 = Fair adherence—glass particles adhering in at least 25 to 30 % of the impact area.
• 3 = Good adherence—glass particles adhering in at least 50 to 60 % of the impact area.
• 4 = Very good adherence—glass particles adhering in at least 70 to 80 % of the impact area.
• 5 = Excellent adherence—glass particles covering almost all of the impact area, at least 90 %.
The problem with these adherence tests is that they require very similar samples in order to make
reproducible results. It is noted that variables as layer thickness, geometry and thickness of base
stock may reduce adhesion e.g. thicker enamel layers gives worse adhesion compared to thin layers,
and the same for thickness of stock material. Considering the forces involved in the falling weight
test, it becomes clear that these observations are due to higher net forces on the enamel coating
resulting from the higher second area of momentum(I) in thicker coatings. And that when a thicker
base stock is used there is less possible deformation resulting in more force on the coating.
But the general method of evaluating the level of adhesion by judging the enamel remaining
on the sample after deformation remains valid and substantiates the claim that the interface bond

4.3
Corrosion Tests
The following section deals with the corrosion tests performed on the enamel coatings. This serves
both to highlight the strengths as a technical coating and to illustrate some of the diﬃculties and
considerations involved in tailoring a coating for a given application. The two main corrosion tests
performed are salt spray test ASTM B117-11[23] and pitting test ASTM G48-11[19].
The salt
spray test was performed to test general corrosion resistance in an accelerated environment. Pitting
test was initially chosen as a way to compare the performance of the enamelled coating to that of
stainless steel, which, in many cases, is the main competitor to enamels in terms of applications.
This is clearly evident from how most enamelled cast iron cookware was displaced from the marked
by stainless steel alternatives in the 70’s and 80’s. This makes it interesting to look at how the
performance of enamel compares to a 316L stainless steel. It is not that uncommon to enamel
stainless steel to improve the visual appearance as mentioned in section4.1.1. Furthermore, there
could also be signiﬁcant advantages to enamel stainless steel to improve chemical resistance and
high temperature performance. And with a thermal conductivity 0.11 - 1.26 W/ (m · K) for enamels
and between 12.11 to 45.0 W/ (m · K)[3, p. 797] for stainless steels, it would also serve as an eﬃcient
thermal barrier.

Chapter 5.
Conclusion
Chapter 5
Conclusion
In conclusion it was possible to use ASTM G48-11 ferric chloride pitting test along with ASTM
B117-11 salt spry tests to induce corrosion in the samples. When using cobalt bearing enamels on low
alloy steel(ST 37) all samples showed excellent corrosion resistance, and a very strong resistance to
under-creep corrosion. On fractured enamel it was found that crevice corrosion could initiate when
cracks bisected the coating, but that corrosion would eventual stop due to blockage by corrosion
products.
Enameling on stainless steel(316L and TP347H) was found to signiﬁcantly reduce the cor-
rosion resistance compared to untreated stainless.
It was found that the presence of adhesion
promoting oxides e.g. cobalt oxide(CoO) would result in a rapid oxidation of chromium in the steel.
This resulted in the formation in a intermediate oxide layer between enamel and the stainless steel
substrate drastically reducing adhesion. Excellent adhesion was attained on stainless steel when
using an enamel free of adhesion promoting oxides.
Due to the higher expansion coeﬃcient of the stainless steel compared to the low alloy steel,
the samples exhibited high residual thermal stresses. These stresses was modeled using COMLSOL
showing high tensile stress along sample edges. This combined with depletion of chromium in the
steel due to both thermal oxidation and reaction with oxides in the enamel lead to highly accelerated
corrosion.
5.1
Further studies
Going forward it would be very interesting to include tests of corrosion performance in high tem-
perature high corrosion environments. Where enamels coatings are far superior to paints, and far
cheaper than ceramic coating. Potentially providing both corrosion protection, increased wear re-
sistance and while also acting as a thermal barrier coating. This could potentially be of great use
in various kinds of exhaust, smoke treatment systems or for use as a coating in high temperature
chemical/catalytic reactors.
