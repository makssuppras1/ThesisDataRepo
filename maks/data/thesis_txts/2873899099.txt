2. Abstract 
 
Predator–prey interactions are central to ecosystem stability, yet in the North Sea they are 
increasingly shaped by climate-driven warming and human exploitation. The lesser sandeel 
(Ammodytes marinus), a keystone forage fish, plays a vital role in supporting cod (Gadus 
morhua), haddock (Melanogrammus aeglefinus), mackerel (Scomber scombrus), and whiting 
(Merlangius merlangus). This study investigates how climate change is altering predator–prey 
dynamics by combining 26 years of trawl survey data, satellite-derived sea surface temperature, 
habitat information, and stomach content analysis. Findings reveal contrasting species 
responses: cod remain strongly dependent on sandeel, showing reduced spatial overlap and 
northward shifts into areas where sandeel are scarce, making them increasingly vulnerable to 
food limitation under continued warming. Haddock shares considerable spatial overlap with 
sandeel, yet diet samples did not consistently show sandeel consumption, suggesting that while 
they may occasionally feed on sandeel, their broader diet reduces their sensitivity to sandeel 
declines. In contrast, mackerel and whiting demonstrate greater resilience through dietary 
flexibility and broader spatial distributions. Mackerel track warming waters northward, while 
whiting maintain stability through generalist feeding and wider habitat use. Overall, results 
indicate that climate-induced warming is restructuring predator–prey interactions in the North 
Sea, with important implications for ecosystem functioning and fisheries sustainability. 
Building on these findings, this study hypothesizes that cod will continue to face increasing 
food limitation due to their strong dietary dependence and limited adaptability, whereas 
haddock, mackerel, and whiting will show greater resilience through flexible foraging 
strategies and wider spatial ranges. It is further hypothesized that sea surface temperature is the 
dominant driver of these shifts, surpassing the influence of fishing pressure or other 
oceanographic factors, and that under future warming scenarios cod will face the greatest 
ecological risk compared with more adaptable predators. These insights highlight the need to 
integrate climate-driven dynamics into ecosystem-based fisheries management and to 
safeguard keystone forage species critical to maintaining North Sea food web stability.

3. Introduction 
 
A review highlights that the North Sea is among the most intensely studied and human-
impacted marine ecosystems worldwide, undergoing rapid warming and structural shifts 
because of combined fishing pressures and climate change (Sguotti et al., 2022). In recent 
decades, several drivers like rising sea surface temperature (Rijnsdorp & Peck, 2009), shifting 
oceanographic patterns (Keil et al., 2020), and intensifying uneven exploitation (van der 
Reijden et al., 2018) have been altering North Sea biological dynamics. At the heart of this 
research is the lesser sandeel (Ammodytes marinus), According to the Scottish Government 
(2023), the sandeel is the most abundant and important forage fish in the North Sea, with a 
critical role in North Atlantic marine food webs. Recognized as a keystone species, the sandeel 
plays a crucial role in transferring energy through the North Sea food web, supporting 
commercially important fish such as cod (Gadus morhua), haddock (Melanogrammus 
aeglefinus), mackerel (Scomber scombrus), and whiting (Merlangius merlangus), as well as 
seabirds and marine mammals (Scottish Coastal Forum, n.d.). 
 
This study investigates how climate change specifically, the persistent sea surface temperature 
of North Sea waters is influencing predator–prey dynamics between sandeel and these four 
major predators. The changing conditions of the oceans may alter temporal overlap between

Environmental variability is a natural feature of North Sea ecology; however, recent decades 
have brought accelerated warming, with average sea surface temperatures rising by 
approximately 0.3°C per decade (European Environment Agency, 2024) Combined with 
ongoing fishing pressure these changes have the potential to disrupt the balance of predator–
prey relationships in the region. Such trophic shifts are critical to monitor, as they underpin 
ecosystem health and maintain the functional symbiosis between prey and predators. 
Ultimately, this balance ensures sufficient fish stocks for future harvests. To safeguard both 
ecological resilience and fisheries sustainability, this pelagic relationship must be carefully 
managed and, where necessary, restructured (European Environment Agency, 2024). 
 
3.1 Background and Significance 
In the global context, the North Sea ecosystem lies within the temperate zone. In such temperate 
marine systems, the spatial, temporal, and compositional patterns of prey availability can 
strongly influence predator distribution and overall foraging success. The primary prey species 
==PAGE:8==
8 
 
in this study, the lesser sandeel (Ammodytes marinus), inhabits sandy seabed habitats, spending 
its life cycle spawning and burrowing. Notably, sandeel exhibits a narrow thermal tolerance 
and limited adaptability to warming ocean (Baumann et al., 2019). When sandeel abundance 
declines, the resulting impacts cascade throughout the food web, a process known as a bottom-
up trophic effect. Historically, cod (Hüssy et al., 2016) and haddock have shown strong 
dependence on sandeel as a food source, whereas mackerel and whiting display greater dietary 
flexibility (ICES, 1999). 
 
Over the 12 years of time span, data display together with successive and instantaneous changes 
in sandeel population and distribution, with notable anomalies such as the sharp biomass drop 
in 2018 (ICES Data bank). This event surprisingly coincided with a marked decrease in 
predator-prey spatial overlap coefficient across all four predators. Among them, most notably 
is cod which face compounded pressures from reduced sandeel availability and habitat loss 
driven by warming waters. Other predators like whiting appear less dependent toward sandeel. 
The key challenge lies in understanding the degree to which climate change is reshaping these 
predator–prey interactions, and whether such changes are likely to persist, intensify, or reverse 
in the future. Without this understanding, fisheries management risks relying on outdated 
assumptions about species distributions, prey availability, and ecosystem resilience.  
3.2 Research Objectives 
This thesis aims to address the following objectives  
1. Quantify spatiotemporal shifts in the distribution of sandeel and its four key predators 
(cod, haddock, mackerel, whiting) over the past 26 years. 
2. Assess spatial overlap between predator and prey distributions using robust statistical 
measures, with emphasis on identifying periods of strong or weak coupling. 
3. Examine dietary relationships through stomach content analysis to determine the 
relative importance of sandeel in predator diets. 
4. Evaluate environmental drivers particularly sea surface temperature and their influence 
on predator and prey distribution patterns. 
5. Forecast potential future dynamics under continued warming scenarios to inform 
ecosystem-based fisheries management. 
By combining spatial ecology, trophic analysis, and climate science, this thesis not only 
advances our understanding of predator–prey relationships in the North Sea but also offers 
practical insights for managing marine resources in an era of rapid environmental change.

4.2 Data Sources and Preprocessing 
 
4.2.1 Survey and Catch Data 
The main datasets of predator biomass complied by downloading data from the DATRAS 
(Database of Trawl Surveys) by ICES website and sub-domains [ices.dk] across European 
waters which is specially based on NS-IBTS (North Sea International Bottom Trawl Survey). 
The DATRAS data is scripted by the readExchange function. The predator data products type 
is Haul metadata (location, haul duration, total haul weight, gear, depth – “HH”), length-based 
catch data (fish species, length class, number caught at each length- “HL”), age-based catch 
data (“CA”).  
 
This data repository from 2000-2025 with hauls duration ranges 26-34 minutes focused on 
quarter one datasets (Q1) of every year covering eight border countries near the North Sea 
coastline. Besides, the gear type named GOV – Grand Ouest Vertical trawl which is designed 
for capture a wide range of demersal fish. Individuals were classified as adults using European 
Union minimum landing size thresholds as practical proxies for length-at-maturity: cod ≥ 35 
cm, haddock ≥ 30 cm, whiting ≥ 27 cm, and mackerel ≥ 30 cm (North Sea recommendation), 
following EU fisheries regulations (European Commission, 2023 and Krag, 2009). Herewith 
the determined prey Sandeel survey DATRAS data has ensured from Norwegian Sandeel 
Spawning Survey-NSSS by from relevant time window 2012-2024 using a dataset 
encompassing 4813 hauls mentioning haul range from 0-16 minutes covering southern and 
central North Sea sandeel banks. This survey is specially designated to trace the sandeel 
abundance and biomass estimation across sandeel banks.

4.2.4 Spatiotemporal Model Fitting 
After verifying accurate mesh knots, a spatiotemporal model has been introduced using 
Spatiotemporal distribution Modelling (sdmTMB) function. The fundamental variable was 
logging based density as a function of depth which a relevant biological covariate influencing 
cod distribution. To secure positive skewed continuous measurements for cod biomass model, 
the Tweedie distribution (with a log link) was introduced. This distribution is particularly 
suitable for ecological count data and secure continuous variation for positive responses of data 
that are based on the Poisson-Gamma framework. The formula structure further contains both 
spatial and spatiotemporal random fields that designated as independent and identically 
==PAGE:13==
13 
 
distributed (IID) across years. The year was treated as a temporal grouping factor. In next the 
sanity function has initiated to confirm successful convergence. The model check showed no 
problems, meaning the error estimates are reliable. 
 
4.2.5 Prediction Grid and Visualization 
To replicate spatiotemporal predictions, a grid was generated over the North Sea longitudes 
from –2° to 10° and latitudes from 50° to 60°. This grid was rendered for each year in the study 
period from 2000 to 2025, and average depth value linked to the predictions to secure real 
biomass prediction. This study covers one hundred equally spaced points for each direction 
like latitudinal and longitudinal to make a grid. Along the X-axis (longitude), there are about 
120 evenly spaced points, starting at -2 and ending at 10 and the latitude-Y, almost cover one 
hundred points spaced evenly starting at 50 and ending at 60. So, when all these points 
combined, the grid size is 120×100=12,000 which covering 10000 locations between 
mentioned geographical coordination. This predicts values like biomass at many points across 
the area, giving a detailed map of how things vary spatially. 
 
A predict function from sdmTMB was used to initiate log-biomass values (estimated), which 
were exponentiated to return to the response scale (biomass). A faceted plot was fabricated by 
using ggplot2, which helps to show the prey and predators biomass by year. The visualization 
exposed dynamic shifts revealed in spatial hotspots of all predators, with higher predicted 
biomass appearing to shift slightly across the North Sea over the 25-year period. The use of a 
logarithmic colour scale (viridis: plasma) enabled effective contrast across high and low 
biomass regions. A year wise biomass summary has been plotted to understand how biomass 
changes over the time. Here, also added spatiotemporal centre of gravity-(COG) in vector field 
that displays for how the cluster of biomass changes its direction due to response of climate 
change or another environmental variable. This terms also collaborate the year wise climate 
driven habitat change.

5.2 Prey abundance and Size Structure 
The prey species, the lesser sandeel (Ammodytes marinus) in the North Sea typically reaches a 
maximum length of about 25 cm, with average mature sizes around 14.7 cm (MarLIN, 2025). 
The size spectrum shows multiple age classes including young (newly recruited) and adult 
sandeels. The younger sizes mentioned represent recent recruits or younger cohorts, while the 
bigger size classes correspond to mature individuals. This wide range of size shows remarkable 
ecological significance as they served as prey for a diverse group of predators residing at 
different trophic levels. They are also known for its schooling behaviour and seasonal growth 
patterns appear as a key forage fish in the North Sea ecosystem. The smaller size sandeels are 
targeted by small piscivorous fish, sea birds and juvenile fish and larger size groups are 
consumed by larger demersal and pelagic predators like cod, whiting, mackerel, and haddock.

5.3.1 Sandeel: Strong Negative Depth Response 
Sandeel is a benthic and sandy-bottom dwelling species, showing a strong and negative 
response to depth. The estimated depth effect coefficient calculated as -0.23±0.01 which is log-
transformed, explaining that the abundance of sandeel shows declined relation with increasing

6. Discussion 
This research overall highlighted the predator-prey interactions between a vital forage fish prey 
sandeel with four ecologically and commercially significant predators mentioning cod, 
mackerel, whiting and haddock. In this direction the main integration is spatiotemporal 
modeling, diet analysis and environmental data, particularly sea surface temperature. The 
findings ultimately reveal the common pathways in how predator species are in their reliance 
on sandeel and how climate change may be altering these relationships. 
 
6.1 Predator–Sandeel Relationships 
Cod showed the strongest link to sandeel out of all four predators. Stomach content analysis 
revealed that sandeel made up 20–30% of the diet of cod and mackerel in at least one sample. 
Haddock had no sandeel in its diet, and whiting only had it present in lesser amounts. This, 
combined with the high spatial overlap between cod and sandeel (Bhattacharyya Coefficient 
usually above 0.75, peaking at 0.89 in 2015 and 2020), means cod populations are highly 
vulnerable to changes in sandeel numbers or distribution. Haddock also shared a lot of space 
with sandeel but did not show them in their diet samples. This could mean they feed on sandeel 
sometimes, but data did not capture it. More likely, haddock’s broader diet makes them less 
affected by sandeel declines. Mackerel fed mostly on pelagic fish, like sandeel, herring and 
sprat, and sometimes shared habitat with sandeel. As sea temperatures rise, mackerel and 
sandeel are both shifting north, but not always in the same way. This could mean fewer chances 
for mackerel to feed on sandeel in future years. Whiting, the most flexible feeder, had the 
lowest and most inconsistent overlap with sandeel (BC = 0.45–0.81). Feeding on crustaceans 
and bottom-dwelling prey sometimes sandeel as data claimed, they seem the least dependent 
on sandeel. 
 
Climate change is affecting these relationships differently. Cod are moving north at about 4.3 
km per year to stay in cooler water, while sandeel can tolerate warmer temperatures but remain 
tied to sandy seabeds. If cod and sandeel end up in different areas, cod could face food 
shortages, especially in new northern habitats.  
 
 
6.2 Common Patterns and Climate-Related Stress 
A particularly striking pattern across all four predators occurred in 2018, when spatial overlap 
with sandeel dropped sharply, aligning with a known crash in sandeel biomass that year. This 
appears to have been a “bottom-up” event where something affected the prey first potentially 
==PAGE:39==
39 
 
caused by disturbed sandy seabeds, unusually warm sea temperatures, extreme weather, or poor 
spawning success. Because sandeels are a vital food source in the North Sea, such declines can 
ripple through the ecosystem, and the shared drop in predator–sandeel overlap during 2018 
underscores their leading role in maintaining the region’s marine food web. 
 
6.3 Fishing Pressure: The Hidden Hand 
While environmental variables have been discussed in brief which show dominant character in 
this study. But a hidden concept cannot be ignored, that is the fishing pressure. Overfishing of 
all prey and predators, especially cod and sandeel, has reduced population resilience. This 
anthropogenic scenario makes these species more vulnerable beside the warming sea surface. 
Interestingly, the North Sea has the blessings of nearly 90 numbers of sandeel banks where 
sandeel are most abundant, such as Elbow spit and Borkum. This renowned habitat zone 
witnessed comparatively heavy fishing pressure especially the spawning season when fewer 
young sandeels are produced. That also leads to a shortage of food for earnest predators. Due 
to the heavy fishing time, it can heavily disrupt sediment structure that further hampers the 
spawning growth and survival capacity 
At the same time, even though cod fishing has been reduced through stricter quotas in recent 
years, it may still slow down the recovery of cod stocks, especially because cod are also 
struggling with habitat loss caused by climate change. So, both fishing and environmental 
changes together are making it harder for cod and other species to bounce back. 
 
6.4 Synthesis: Connecting Objectives, Results, and Management Meaning 
The main goal of this study was to determine how the ecological relationship between the four 
main North Sea predators cod, haddock, mackerel, and whiting and the important forage fish, 
sandeel (Ammodytes marinus), is being shaped by climate change, specifically the continuous 
increase in sea surface temperature (SST). This was accomplished by combining three 
complementary methods: spatial overlap analysis (to determine the frequency of predator and 
prey sharing habitats), stomach content analysis (to measure dietary reliance on sandeel), and 
biomass modelling manifested that warmer sea surface temperatures are pushing both prey and 
predators northward. As an example, Cod and Mackerel displayed extraordinarily strong 
supports. In a word, climate change is really reshaping the habitats. Together, these techniques 
demonstrated distinct variations in each predator's response to sandeel availability 
fluctuations. According to these findings, predators' reactions to changes in their prey are 
species-specific and influenced by both ecological characteristics and environmental tolerance. 
==PAGE:40==
40 
 
This means that future climate-driven changes in the distribution of sandeels will have an 
uneven effect on the North Sea food web. These three results all support each other and help 
us understand the bigger picture of how these species interact—and how climate change is 
affecting them. 
Together, these results indicate that climate-induced changes in sandeel distribution are 
reshaping predator-prey interactions in complex ways. Notably, some species, such as cod, 
appear particularly sensitive to these changes, while others, including mackerel and whiting, 
exhibit greater flexibility in their response. This differential vulnerability underscores the 
importance of considering species-specific behavioral and ecological traits when assessing the 
impacts of environmental change on marine food webs. 
 
6.5 Fisheries Management Implications  
This research can be particularly important for making better decisions in fisheries 
management.  
• 
The first implication is to be for the Cod fish which is dependent on sandeel as study shows. 
This predator species is highly under pressure from warming seas. To help cod recover, we 
must protect sandeel habitats and consider cod as feeding needs when setting fishing rules.  
• 
Managers or other decision makers should think about limiting sandeel fishing in key areas, 
especially their banks. Sandeel fisheries often operate in the same areas where predators 
rely on them for food. If too many sandeels are caught—especially during weak years—it 
can create food shortages for predators.  
• 
Other flexible species which have not shown pragmatic changes like cod as their 
temperature range is more specious than the cod, but as sea surface temperature increases 
day by day it will be an alarming situation for other predators like whiting, haddock. Their 
shifting behaviour means we need dynamic, multi-species management—not just separate 
rules for each species. 
In summary the findings of this thesis strongly support the original research goals. By 
combining where species live, what they eat, and how they overlap, we get a full picture of 
how climate and human activity are changing the North Sea food web. These insights are 
essential for protecting vulnerable species and making smarter decisions in fisheries 
management going forward.

7. Conclusion 
What pops up from this investigation is a coherent picture: Sandeel is more than just a prey 
species—it serves as a key foundation of the North Sea food web, supporting a wide range of 
predators and ecosystem functions. From this 36-year sequence reveals in a single expression 
cod is impacted most, haddock somewhere adjusts, mackerel try to change its foraging strategy 
and whiting continues with resilience. The fate of the cold-water species like cod caught in a 
warming trap-gradually compressed its spatial footprint that was the consequence of thermal 
constraint. 
The above figures advocate this dissertation, from spatiotemporal biomass maps, to overlap 
index graphs, to stomach content synopsis- all corroboration leads to the same conclusion: the 
upcoming future of predator species in the North Sea is strongly linked to the survival of 
sandeel. Nevertheless, whose own future is increasingly threatened by climate change and 
human pressures such as fishing. 
 
In a wider context, where SST continues to trend upward at the rate of 0.03℃ per year, which 
is a meaningful outcome of habitat shifts. From the standpoint of mackerel and whiting they 
are both able to adapt and persist. It is worth mentioning that cod and haddock may encounter 
greater challenges due to their reliance on specific prey like sandeel and limited habitat options. 
To protect this balance, management must shift from species-specific strategies to ecosystem-
based approaches that consider: 
• 
Predator–prey interactions 
• 
Habitat requirements 
• 
Environmental forecasts 
• 
Fishing pressure hotspots 
Resilience in the North Sea will depend on maintaining the integrity of its foundation species 
and right now, that means protecting the sandeel. For fisheries management, this trajectory 
signals an urgent need to reevaluate stock assessments, marine protected area boundaries, and 
adaptive quotas considering shifting species distributions. Without substantial mitigation of 
greenhouse gas emissions or large-scale adaptation in fishery practices, cod may vanish from 
large parts of its traditional range, leaving a vacuum in both ecosystem structure and human 
economies.
