Abstract
This thesis, developed in collaboration with Energy Machines, aims to create a comprehensive pool of energy
building simulation results, focusing on space heating, domestic hot water, and space cooling. Three prevalent
Danish building archetypes, each with a total surface area exceeding 10,000 m², were selected for analysis across
residential, office, retail, logistics, educational, and hotel segments. Key input parameters of the building
envelope and occupancy patterns were extensively researched.
The in-house model that was used for the
energy building simulation was validated through IDA ICE, ensuring realistic simulations. A total of 1500
simulations per segment, with randomly generated parameters, were conducted to reflect the variations in
building energy demand accurately.
The results form a robust database for assessing energy performance
and guiding future projects.
This thesis emphasizes the importance of understanding occupancy patterns
and building conditions, demonstrating that careful parameter selection significantly influences simulation
accuracy.
Future recommendations include detailed validation of Air Handling Units (AHUs), expanding
building archetypes, broadening the input parameter library, and incorporating future weather files to account
for climate change. This research contributes valuable insights into the energy demands of different building
types, supporting the goal of energy efficiency in Denmark’s building sector.

Introduction
As we face the pressing challenges of climate change and energy resource management, the drive for energy
efficiency has never been more vital. Buildings, as significant energy consumers, play a key role in this effort,
particularly in Denmark where a diverse building stock presents both unique challenges and opportunities.
The current building stock is extensive and largely consists of structures with poor energy performance. As
a result, it holds significant potential for energy and environmental savings.
The goal of this thesis is to
develop a comprehensive pool of energy building simulation results in partnership with Energy Machines. It
focuses on fundamental areas including space heating, domestic hot water, and space cooling (both power and
energy). The objective is to create a model that closely resembles the Danish building stock for potential
projects of the company. My thesis project’s main objective is to perform a comprehensive analysis of the
variations in building energy demand. To achieve this, three building archetypes with a total surface area
greater than 10,000 m² were chosen, each of which represented the most typical geometric types in Denmark.
A thorough investigation was conducted into all crucial input parameters for energy building simulations
across different segments: residential, office, retail, logistics, educational, and hotel. For each segment, we
established ranges and distributions of these parameters. The energy model was then validated, comparing it
with IDA ICE. By applying extreme scenarios to these input parameters realistic simulations and results were
ensured. To accurately reflect the Danish building stock, extensive research was conducted to link the year of
construction, building segment, and building condition. This involved analyzing historical data and current
building conditions to establish correlations. A total of 1500 simulations were executed for each segment, were
the input parameters were randomly generated for each scenario from the given ranges and distributions.
The subsequent steps include simulation processes and the systematic storage of results, ultimately forming an
extensive library of energy consumption data. The resulting data pool provides a robust foundation for energy
performance assessments and future building projects, offering valuable insights into the energy demands of
different building types under various conditions.
2.1
Energy Machines
Energy Machines is a leader in the design, implementation, and operation of integrated energy systems for
buildings and communities. Unlike energy solutions that rely on burning fossil fuels or biomass to generate
heat, Energy Machines systems efficiently move heat from the ground, air, and waste heat sources using heat
pumps.
By integrating leading low-carbon energy technologies like geothermal energy storage, heat pumps, and heat
recovery into a single system via software, Energy Machines’ fully electric solutions enable both brand new
and century-old properties to be heated and cooled with 100% renewable energy, minimal energy waste, and
an eliminated carbon footprint.
EM Simulate enables data-driven, modular design of integrated energy systems with predictable outcomes.
By creating a digital twin of any building, engineers can scale and run multiple scenarios to compare the
performance of different system configurations. The energy model has the power to simulate detailed energy
demands for each hour of the year, for up to twenty-five years of operation. This combined with location-based
weather data, building properties, and energy usage factors enables highly optimised energy system designs.
Energy Machines combined heat pump-and-chillers are the heart of our integrated energy systems, providing
properties with simultaneous heating, cooling and domestic hot water. The integrated system operates in a
continuous energy cycle, storing heat energy underground during summer and extracting it during the winter.
The configuration may be BTES (Borehole Thermal Energy Storage)-based, where energy is stored in bore-
holes, or ATES (Aquifer Thermal Energy Storage)-based, where energy is stored within natural underground
aquifers.

Literature review
One could argue that this thesis serves as an extensive literature review. Throughout the project, not only was
there a comprehensive examination of similar projects conducted previously, but there was also an in-depth
investigation into occupant and segment-related aspects of building energy codes and standards. The first part
of this investigation will be presented in this section, while the latter will be discussed later in the report.
This chapter seeks to present state of the art by summarising publications relevant to the creation of energy
building simulation data pools or address certain steps of the process. Throughout the thesis, these articles
formed the strategy to optimize the thesis development. The way they will be presented is a small summary
and then important key points of the article.
The article by Martin Heine Kristensen et al.[1] was the most influential during the thesis. It presents a novel
approach to enhancing the predictive accuracy of urban building energy models through hierarchical calibration
of building archetypes. The authors address the common challenge in urban building energy modeling (UBEM)
of balancing model complexity and accuracy. The proposed framework introduces a hierarchical multilevel
method for calibrating archetype physics-based BEM (Building Energy Models) parameters using Bayesian
inference. This approach assumes exchangeability among archetype buildings, meaning they are considered
a homogeneous sample.
By leveraging independent time series datasets, the framework collectively pools
individual building parameter estimates towards a common archetype, capturing variability and correlation.
This method enhances robustness against outliers, improving out-of-sample prediction reliability compared to
models based on simple aggregation.
A case study on Danish residential single-family dwellings from the 1950s demonstrated the framework’s
effectiveness in identifying shared archetype parameters and accounting for heterogeneity in the training data.
The archetype framework is envisioned as a central calibration tool for urban-scale building energy modeling,
where archetype models serve as manageable components for complex urban areas. However, its applicability
for such large-scale use requires further validation in future research.
Key points of the article include:
• Archetype Definition and Calibration: The paper emphasizes the importance of avoiding oversimplified
archetype definitions, which can lead to significant predictive errors.
Using archetypes significantly
decreases the number of buildings in a building stock, as they represent groups of topologically identical
ones. Moreover it reduces computational load. The hierarchical calibration method helps to propagate
uncertainties and refine the parameters used in energy models, ensuring a more robust and accurate
representation of urban energy use.
• Bayesian Inference and Multilevel Modeling: The framework utilizes Bayesian inference to manage and
propagate uncertainties during the calibration process. This multilevel modeling approach allows the
pooling of data from various buildings, thereby reducing the impact of outliers and improving the gen-
erality of the model.
• Minimize uncertainties: The Morris method, sensitivity analysis technique, was applied to define the
most influential parameters. Applying distribution and ranges to the influential parameters was also
exhibited.
• Case Study and Validation: The method is demonstrated using a case study from the Danish residential
building stock, involving 3-hourly energy use measurements from 50 buildings. The model’s predictive
capability was validated against 100 out-of-sample buildings, showing promising results with a normalized
mean bias error (NMBE) of 2.9% and a coefficient of variation of the root mean square error (CVRMSE)
of 7.8%.
This study enabled me to understand the importance between model complexity and accuracy.
Building
archetypes are vital to create a energy performance simulation data pool. Deciding also on the most influential
parameters is also key to the realization of the project.

By applying aggregation

Ballarini and Corrado [3] present a novel methodology for assessing the energy consumption of building stock.
The focus is on creating a comprehensive approach that accounts for the complexities and variations within
building stocks, improving the accuracy and reliability of energy consumption estimates. The authors pro-
pose a multi-step methodology that integrates detailed data collection, building archetype development, and
simulation models. The process is designed to handle large datasets and diverse building characteristics.
• Data Collection: Comprehensive data collection is the first step, involving gathering information on
building characteristics, energy consumption, and climatic conditions. This includes data on building
geometry, construction materials, usage patterns, and existing HVAC systems.
• Building Archetypes: The methodology emphasizes the development of detailed building archetypes.
These archetypes represent typical buildings within the stock and are defined based on common features
and energy consumption patterns.
• Simulation Models: Advanced simulation models are employed to predict energy consumption. These
models take into account the detailed data from the archetypes and apply various scenarios to assess
potential energy savings and impacts of different interventions.
• Validation and Calibration: The methodology includes rigorous validation and calibration steps, using
real-world data to ensure the accuracy of the models and simulations.
The work of Jaume Palmer Real et al. (2022) [4] proposes a methodology for defining building archetypes
based on data-driven techniques. It employs mixed effects modeling to account for both fixed and random
effects, thereby capturing the variability within and between buildings. This approach enhances the accuracy
of energy demand predictions for building populations. The article emphasizes the significance of archetypes
in understanding and predicting building energy performance.
Archetypes provide a standardized way to
categorize buildings, allowing for more accurate energy consumption forecasts and policy assessments. The
mixed effects modeling approach used in the study captures both fixed effects, such as building type, and
random effects, like occupant behavior and weather conditions, which vary across different buildings.
The article by Jesper Kragh and Kim Bjarne Wittchen [5] presents two typologies developed under the EU-
financed TABULA project. The aim is to enhance energy performance assessments and facilitate the formu-
lation of energy-saving policies for residential buildings in Denmark. The typologies are categorized into two
types of models: real example models and average designed models. These models encompass three main
building types: single-family houses, terraced houses, and blocks of flats, each representing nine periods of
construction, reflecting different building traditions and insulation standards. The average designed models
are based on data from the Danish Energy Performance Certification Scheme, providing a robust framework
for calculating various energy-saving scenarios.
The study validates the energy balance model for the residential building stock against official Danish energy
consumption statistics from 2010, revealing a deviation of less than 4%.
This demonstrates the model’s
accuracy and its potential for supporting national energy policy and planning.
This study was very important during the thesis, since it focused on the danish residential segment providing
with valuable insights.
The article "Parametric Energy Performance Analysis and Monitoring of Buildings - HEART Project Platform
Case Study" [6] explores advanced methodologies for assessing and improving the energy performance of
buildings. This study is part of the HEART (Holistic Energy and Architectural Retrofit Toolkit) project,
funded by the EU Horizon 2020 program, which aims to enhance the energy efficiency of existing buildings
through deep retrofitting.

The research utilizes parametric simulation and model calibration to evaluate a wide range of energy perfor-
mance outcomes, considering user behavior and varying operating conditions from the start. The case study
focuses on a multi-storey residential building in Bagnolo del Piano, Italy, built in 1985 and currently under

Key aspects of the HEART project include:
• Parametric Simulation: This approach generates diverse building energy performance scenarios, allowing
for a comprehensive understanding of potential outcomes and the impact of different variables on energy
use.
• Model Calibration: Employing robust and scalable techniques to ensure that the simulations align closely
with real-world data, thereby improving the reliability of the results.
• Energy Performance Monitoring: The HEART platform integrates continuous monitoring to validate
and adjust the models over time, ensuring sustained energy efficiency improvements.
Even though this study addresses one specific building, it focuses on a parametric investigation of an energy
performance simulation. Giving ranges in input parameters and expecting to investigate the differences in the
energy performance is the core for this thesis as well.
The article by Brock Glasgo et al. [7] examines the accuracy of residential building energy simulation tools
by comparing simulated loads to actual measured loads at the circuit level.
The study highlights signifi-
cant discrepancies between simulated and actual performance, underscoring the need for improved calibration
and validation methods in residential energy simulations. This research aims to enhance the reliability of
simulations used for energy code compliance, building operation optimization, and performance verification.
The work of Anastasios Ioannou and Laure Itard [8] explores how different building parameters and occupant
behaviors affect the energy performance and thermal comfort of residential buildings.
Key findings of the study include:
• Building Parameters: The study identifies the most critical building-related parameters affecting energy
consumption, such as the window U-value (thermal transmittance), window g-value (solar heat gain
coefficient), and wall conductivity. These factors significantly impact the building’s heating requirements,
especially in colder climates.
• Occupant Behavior: When incorporating behavioral parameters like thermostat settings and ventilation
rates, the study found these factors to have a more substantial impact on energy consumption than
the building parameters. This highlights the importance of considering occupant behavior in energy
simulations.
• Thermal Comfort: For thermal comfort, measured using the Predicted Mean Vote (PMV) index, the
study found that metabolic activity and clothing insulation were the most influential factors, with ther-
mostat settings also playing a significant role.
• Simulation Tools: The research utilizes Monte Carlo sensitivity analysis to assess the impact of various
parameters. This method helps in understanding the range of potential outcomes and identifying which
parameters need more accurate input data to improve simulation reliability.
This study emphasizes the need for comprehensive sensitivity analyses in the design phase to better predict
actual building performance and ensure occupant comfort. The use of Monte Carlo to change the combination
of the building envelope and the occupancy behaviour parameters is significant. This method was also used
in this thesis.
The article by José Eduardo Pachano and Carlos Fernández Bandera[9] outlines a systematic approach for
calibrating building energy models using measured data. The process involves several steps:
• Data Collection: Gather detailed information on building geometry, systems, and operational schedules.
• Initial Model Setup: Create a preliminary model using software like EnergyPlus.

• Simulation and Comparison: Run simulations and compare results with measured data.
• Sensitivity Analysis: Identify key parameters affecting discrepancies.
• Parameter Adjustment: Adjust parameters iteratively to reduce differences.
• Validation: Validate the calibrated model against additional data sets to ensure accuracy.
This multi-step process aims to enhance the precision of energy performance predictions and support better
decision-making in building energy management. It is an example of how to process discrepancies during the
validation of a single simulation or the variation analysis.
The article "DECODE: Data-driven Energy Consumption Prediction leveraging Historical Data and Environ-
mental Factors in Buildings" [10] presents a model for predicting building energy consumption using historical
energy data, occupancy patterns, and environmental factors such as temperature and humidity. The study
introduces a Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) model, a type of recurrent neural network well-suited for time
series forecasting.
The selected features include occupancy, temperature, calendar, humidity, and historical energy consump-
tion. Data pre-processing involved aligning different sampling rates (energy data at 1 minute, occupancy at
10 minutes, and temperature/humidity at 30 minutes) to a common 10-minute interval using interpolation
methods.
The dataset was divided into training (70%), validation (15%), and testing (15%) sets in chronological order to
maintain the temporal sequence of the data. The LSTM model was trained alongside other machine learning
models like Linear Regression (LR), Decision Tree (DT), and Random Forest (RF) to predict future energy
consumption.
The performance of the models was evaluated using standard metrics such as Mean Absolute Error (MAE),
Mean Squared Error (MSE), and Root Mean Squared Error (RMSE).
The DECODE model leverages advanced machine learning techniques to provide accurate energy consumption
predictions, aiding in effective energy management and optimization in buildings.
Finally, this thesis relied on a research project of Aarhus University, BeReal [11]. The goal of this research
project was to create an online software application that supplements the SBi application Be15, which is
used to predict building energy consumption. BeREAL targets the "performance gap" between predicted and
actual energy use, managing uncertainties by allowing a range of input parameters and providing results as
confidence intervals. It automates numerous calculations, aligning with industry guidelines on incorporating
uncertainties.
BeREAL highlights ten key input parameters that most significantly affect energy consumption and utilizes
precise weather data from Aarhus University, which can deliver hourly data from the past 45 years for specific
locations. This real weather data is crucial, as it can explain up to 20% of the variation in a building’s energy
consumption.
The application serves two main purposes: during the design phase, it helps create realistic energy consumption
forecasts and identifies critical factors; once a building is operational, it evaluates whether actual energy
consumption is acceptable and identifies reasons for high consumption levels.
The project resulted in a reliable, user-friendly application that has received positive feedback and is mainly
used in the design phase. A lack of core energy data has limited its use in analyzing operational buildings.

Methods
4.1
Objectives
From the aforementioned publications, it can be noted that significant research has been conducted on creating
energy models models that resemble part of the building stock, especially on the residential segment. A lot of
emphasis has been given on building archetypes, mitigating the impact of uncertainties and the validation of
the energy building simulation model. However, I have not encountered a study that specifically differentiates
between various segments using distinct input parameters, nor one that underscores the critical relationship
between the year of construction and the building envelope.
The overall aim of the thesis is to create an extensive database of energy consumption data for buildings across
different segments.
• How does the energy demand in space heating, space cooling and domestic hot water vary depending on
different input parameters of energy building simulation, parameters describing the building envelope and the
occupancy patterns?
• How these parameters vary across different segments? How the building use and patterns influence the
energy building demand?
• What are the key drivers of energy demand variations among different types of buildings, and how do these
drivers differ across different segments?
• The input parameters are specified with min, max (and mode) values, following a specific distribution. How
does the choice of input parameter ranges impact the distribution of energy consumption outcomes across
different building segments?
• How do the results of the simulations using the EM in-house tool compare with empirical data from real-world
buildings, and what are the implications for the accuracy and reliability of simulation models?
4.2
General approach
Energy demand refers to the energy that is required to be supplied to the building in order to keep the
zone setpoint from an idealized heating/cooling system, while taking into account hourly losses (transmission,
ventilation, infiltration), hourly heat gains (internal heat gains – people light, equipment, solar heat gain) and
including AHU heat recovery.
A data pool of energy building simulation results across different segments is the goal of this project. To
achieve that, extensive investigation was carried out into the input parameters that affect building performance
simulations. Input parameters that affect the building envelope and describe the occupancy patterns were
researched. A range of values about each input parameter in every segment was gathered in summary tables.
An in-house tool to execute energy building simulations was developed from the company at the same time as
this thesis, which was EnergyPlus based. The accuracy and the functionality of the tool was validated through
consecutive execution of simulations and comparing the results to IDA ICE. Testing the tool with extreme
scenarios calibrated the maximum values of the input parameters. Consecutively, ranges and distributions for
each input parameter were defined.
After validating the in house tool and forming each input parameter, a script is developed where the inputs
are the number of iterations and the distribution and the range of each input parameter. Random values
are generated in each iteration that reflect the input parameters. A building performance simulation is then
executed for each iteration, and the results are stored for each simulation and in a summary excel file for easier
overview.
Reviewing the results, there are consecutive reruns of the variation analysis, in order to resemble the danish
building stock and match future projects of the company. The link between the building condition and the
year of construction is researched. The final results are validated through experience and relevant studies.
In general this thesis, focuses on investigating input parameters, the accuracy of a single energy building
simulation and forming a data set close to the danish building stock.

dividing each area to strike an optimal balance between precision and computational efficiency. Increasing
the number of zones improves accuracy but also extends simulation time. Conversely, simulating the entire
building as a single zone is unrealistic. The goal is to find the best combination that ensures precise results
without excessive computational demands. It is decided that each building has a specific use, it represents one
segment and each floor will be divided to zones according to its geometry type. The division of zones will be
defined in the next section.

Window to wall ratio
The window fraction represents the percentage of glazing in a building, which is vital for ensuring adequate
daylight access and providing views. However, excessive glazing can lead to overheating during the summer
months. The window area has also higher heat loss than the rest of the wall due to higher U-values and
thermal bridges.
In Denmark [14] [15], it is estimated that before the 1960s, window areas in residential
buildings covered approximately 15% of the façade area. In recent years, this proportion has increased. In

U-values
U-values are a measure of the thermal performance of building elements, indicating how much heat is transmit-
ted between the inside and the outside of a building. The lower the U-value, the better the material performs
as an insulator, contributing to greater energy efficiency and thermal comfort within the building.

4.6
Building envelope
In this project, we will be varying the U-values of several key building components, including the exterior wall,
the roof, the slab, and the windows. These variations are crucial for understanding how different construction
materials and building techniques impact overall energy performance. The materials used in exterior walls,
such as brick, concrete, or wood, significantly impact their U-values. Modern insulated walls typically have
much lower U-values compared to older constructions. Roofs are another critical component, with materials
ranging from traditional thatch to modern insulated metal or asphalt shingles. The level of insulation installed
in the roof cavity plays a significant role in determining the U-value. The insulation beneath concrete slabs
can vary greatly, affecting the overall U-value of the floor. This is especially important in minimizing heat loss
to the ground. Windows are often the weakest point in a building’s thermal envelope. Advances in glazing
technologies, such as double or triple glazing and low-emissivity coatings, have led to significant improvements
in their U-values.
The U-value of a building element is influenced by several factors, most notably the type of material used
and the year of construction. Historically, building regulations and energy requirements have evolved, leading
to stricter standards and improved insulation properties in newer constructions. The first set of energy re-
quirements for buildings in Denmark, known as BR61, marked the beginning of regulated energy performance
standards. Since then, these requirements have been progressively tightened to improve energy efficiency and
reduce heat loss through building envelopes.

4.6.2
G-values
The G-value, also known as the solar heat gain coefficient (SHGC), represents the fraction of solar heat that
passes through the glazing and enters the interior of a building. A higher G-value indicates that more solar
heat is transmitted, while a lower G-value means less solar heat is allowed in. A building’s needs for heating
and cooling are directly impacted by the G-value. Higher G-values, for example, can be advantageous in colder
locations since they let more solar heat in, which lessens the need for artificial heating. Lower G-values, on
the other hand, are preferred in warmer areas to decrease heat gain and lower cooling loads.
The type of glazing and any coatings that are applied to the glass can have a considerable impact on the G-value
of windows. Single glazing typically has G-values ranging from 0.70 to 0.85. These windows allow a substantial
amount of solar heat to enter, which may not be ideal for energy efficiency in most modern buildings. Double
17

glazing, on the other hand, typically has a coefficient of 0.50 to 0.70, which improves thermal insulation while
reducing solar heat gain as compared to single glazing. Lastly, G-values for triple glazing are frequently in the
range of 0.35 to 0.60. By minimizing heat transfer, these windows reduce solar heat intake and offer superior
insulation.
4.6.3
Infiltration
Infiltration refers to the energy losses caused by the unintended entry of cold outdoor air through cracks and
gaps in the building’s exterior. Although this process is unintentional, it is inevitable and has a considerable
effect on the building’s energy efficiency. Infiltration happens through the building’s envelope and interacts
with internal airflow patterns, usually measured in air changes per hour (ACH).
Newer buildings have lower infiltration rates thanks to improved air-tight construction methods. Conversely,
older buildings typically experience higher infiltration rates, resulting in greater energy losses and reduced
thermal comfort.
Infiltration rates can vary widely: new buildings generally range from 0.1 to 0.2 ACH, while buildings in poor
condition can have rates as high as 0.5 to 0.6 ACH.[16], [17].

4.8
DHW consumption
Domestic hot water (DHW) consumption is a crucial parameter in building energy performance, and it varies
significantly based on building type, usage patterns, and occupancy. This variability makes it one of the most
challenging aspects to standardize in literature and practical assessments. DHW consumption refers to the
amount of hot water used within a building over a year. It is typically measured in cubic meters per square
meter per year (m³/m²/year).
In residential buildings, DHW consumption is approximately 0.26 m³/m²/year.
This higher rate reflects
the frequent use of hot water for activities such as bathing, washing dishes, and laundry.
DHW energy
consumption accounts for a significant portion of total heating energy, typically around 15-30%. In newer,
more energy-efficient buildings, this can rise to about 50% of the total heating energy consumption due to
better insulation and lower space heating demands. For office buildings, the DHW consumption is significantly
lower, approximately 0.07 m³/m²/year. This lower rate is due to the limited use of hot water, primarily for
handwashing and cleaning purposes. Offices do not typically have the same level of hot water demand as
residential settings. Other building types, including educational, retail, and logistics buildings, generally have
even lower DHW consumption rates, around 0.03 m³/m²/year. These buildings use hot water mainly for
sanitary purposes and occasional cleaning, resulting in lower overall consumption compared to residential or
even office buildings [95],[96].
• Residential: 0.1 to 0.4 m³ per m²
• Hotel: 0.3 to 0.7 m³ per m²
• Logistics: 0.01 to 0.1 m³ per m²
• Retail: 0.01 to 0.003 m³ per m²
• Educational: 0.05 to 0.02 m³ per m²
• Office: 0.02 to 0.15 m³ per m²
It is important to note that the energy consumed by a domestic hot water (DHW) system encompasses not only
the energy required to meet the annual hot water demand but also the energy lost through the recirculation
system.
4.9
Ventilation
4.9.1
Ventilation flow rate
Ventilation flow rate determines the amount of outdoor air introduced into the building, which is essential
for maintaining acceptable indoor air quality. Adequate ventilation helps in diluting and removing indoor
pollutants, including volatile organic compounds (VOCs), carbon dioxide (CO2), and other contaminants, thus
ensuring a healthy indoor environment. Ventilation impacts the thermal comfort of occupants by influencing
the indoor temperature and humidity levels. High ventilation rates can help in cooling during hot periods by
25
==PAGE:32==

4.9
Ventilation
increasing the amount of cooler outdoor air brought into the building, while in colder periods, it may require
more heating energy to maintain comfortable indoor temperatures.
The ventilation flow rate significantly affects the energy consumption for heating and cooling systems. In cold
climates, higher ventilation rates increase the load on heating systems as more cold air needs to be heated to
maintain indoor comfort levels, while in hot climates, ventilation can either increase or decrease cooling loads
depending on the outdoor air temperature.
In order to execute a reliable building performance simulation, accurate modeling of ventilation flow rates
is fundamental. Ventilation rates affect the calculation of heating and cooling loads, energy consumption,
and indoor environmental conditions.
Ventilation rates must meet local building codes and standards for
ventilation and indoor air quality.
Denmark follows European standards for ventilation in buildings, such as EN 16798-1:2019 [32], which aligns
with the general European approach to ensuring adequate ventilation.
The Danish Building Regulations
(BR18) also emphasize energy efficiency and indoor climate, requiring similar ventilation rates:
• Residential: 0.0004 to 0.001 m³/s per m²
• Hotel: 0.001 to 0.0025 m³/s per m²
• Logistics: 0.0003 to 0.001 m³/s per m²
• Retail: 0.001 to 0.003 m³/s per m²
• Educational: 0.001 to 0.0025 m³/s per m²
• Office: 0.001 to 0.002 m³/s per m²
Office buildings require higher ventilation rates to maintain air quality for workers. Logistics buildings, such as
warehouses, typically have lower occupancy but still require sufficient ventilation to ensure safe working condi-
tions. Retail environments, with high and variable occupancy, need high ventilation flows as well. Educational
facilities, such as schools and universities, need to ensure good IAQ for students and staff.
The provided ventilation flow rate ranges serve as guidelines for establishing the possible ventilation flow
rates in the Danish building stock. Standards such as EN 16798-1:2019 [97] and ASHRAE 62.1 [40] offer
recommendations to ensure adequate air quality. However, it is important to note that these standards are not
always mandatory and can vary based on specific building characteristics and usage. Buildings constructed
before the implementation of these standards or those with unique ventilation needs may operate with lower
or higher ventilation flow rates. For instance, older buildings may have different structural and functional
requirements, leading to variations in ventilation performance. Additionally, some buildings may necessitate
higher ventilation flow rates due to high occupancy levels or specific indoor activities.
From other standards and various articles and literature, the final ventilation flow ranges that were used in
this project are:
• Residential: 0.0003 to 0.0015 m³/s per m²
• Hotel: 0.0005 to 0.002 m³/s per m²
• Logistics: 0.00015 to 0.001 m³/s per m²
• Retail: 0.001 to 0.004 m³/s per m²
• Educational: 0.0015 to 0.005 m³/s per m²
• Office: 0.0007 to 0.002 m³/s per m²
Even when unoccupied, buildings can accumulate various pollutants, such as volatile organic compounds
(VOCs) from building materials, furniture, and cleaning products. Keeping ventilation flow at lower levels
when there are no occupants, helps to dilute and remove these pollutants. Consequently, there is a minimum

4.9.2
Heat exchanger efficiency
Heat exchanger efficiency determines how effectively the system can recover heat from exhaust air and transfer
it to incoming supply air. This efficiency significantly impacts the energy required for heating or cooling the
supply air. Typically, the efficiency of heat exchangers ranges between 70-90%, depending on factors such as
airflow rates, the condition, and the design of the exchanger.
Literature indicates that modern heat recovery systems in buildings can recycle between 60-95% of waste
energy, which is a promising efficiency range for various applications [98] These efficiencies are provided by
manufacturers and are highly dependent on the specific design and operational conditions of the heat exchang-
ers.
The Climate Machines utilize a cross-flow plate heat exchanger that operates in two stages, achieving a heat
exchanger efficiency of up to 90%. This high efficiency is significant for reducing the energy required to heat or
cool the supply air, thus enhancing the overall energy efficiency of HVAC systems. The design and operational
stages of this heat exchanger are optimized to maximize heat recovery from the exhaust air to the incoming
supply air, contributing to more sustainable and cost-effective building operations.
4.9.3
Natural ventilation
In this project, natural ventilation is not taken into account since the windows are considered always closed.
Therefore, the building’s ventilation is solely reliant on mechanical systems. Effective use of natural ventilation
can reduce the reliance on mechanical cooling systems, thereby saving energy.
4.10
Heating and cooling setpoints
Heating and cooling setpoints in buildings refer to the desired temperature ranges at which heating or cooling
systems maintain indoor comfort. These setpoints can vary depending on factors such as building occupancy,
season, climate, and individual preferences. In general, a typical indoor set temperature for cooling is between
22-26 °C with an average of 24 °C. In hotter climates this parameter might be lower and in cooler ones higher.
Too low values can lead to higher energy consumption. On the other hand, a typical indoor set temperature
for cooling is between 20-22 °C. Too high values can lead to higher energy consumption.
In this project the heating and cooling setpoints are fixed, remaining constant throughout the year. In addition
to the use of an ideal HVAC system, it means that the operational temperature of the zones has a lower limit
the heating setpoint and an upper the cooling setpoint. No heating and cooling setbacks are used.
In the testing process, it was observed that if the heating and the cooling setpoints are less than 2 degrees
apart, then cooling was needed in the winter days, something that is not really realistic. For example, in his
example the heating and cooling setpoint is selected at 22 °C and 23 °C, something that could be realistic
according to occupant patterns, in an office with high internal gains. According to the energy demand results
of the simulation, in this week of March, while the ambient temperature is around 5 °C, cooling is needed,
something that is not realistic. To avoid this, it is ensured that the difference between the two setpoints is
minimum 2 degrees. This approach reduces the likelihood of unnecessary cooling or heating demands based
on slight fluctuations in indoor and outdoor temperatures, while still providing adequate comfort for building
occupants.

4.12
Schedules
Except from the value of internal gains such as occupancy, lighting and equipment, applying schedules to the
energy building simulations is equally important. The different segments are modeled in order to understand
their energy usage which is influenced by occupancy patterns and equipment operation. Buildings have varying
occupancy patterns throughout the day. Applying schedules allows the simulation to mimic real-life usage, such
as turning on lights, heating, and cooling systems when occupants are present and reducing energy consumption
during unoccupied periods. Different building systems and equipment, such as HVAC (heating, ventilation,
and air conditioning) systems and lighting, can operate on schedules in an energy building simulation. By
incorporating these schedules into the simulation, you can accurately model the behavior of these systems
and their energy consumption.
Considering that this projects focuses on the energy consumption across
different segments, different schedules were created so as the segment use is realistically depicted. The following
schedules categories were investigated:
• Occupancy schedule Occupancy presence in the building. It is the most important schedule since it
helps simulate when spaces are in use, influencing the operation of lighting, HVAC systems, and other
equipment.
• Lighting schedule Specifies when lighting systems are turned on and off within the building. It considers
occupancy patterns to ensure that lights are only active when needed, optimizing energy usage.
• Equipment schedule Determines the operating hours of various equipment within the building, such as
office equipment, kitchen appliances, and other electrical devices. This schedule helps model the energy
consumption associated with equipment usage.

4.12.1
Monte Carlo
The sources indicate a broad range of inputs due to the complexity of capturing occupant behavior accurately.
This variability necessitates a detailed variation analysis. In building performance simulations, numerous input
parameters contribute to the overall outcome, often leading to similar results through different combinations
of these parameters. A statistical approach is essential for addressing the uncertainties associated with these
input parameters, enabling a more comprehensive understanding and accurate simulation outcomes.
The Monte Carlo method fundamentally employs random sampling to investigate the potential outcomes of
a system or process.
By generating random inputs from defined probability distributions, it captures the
uncertainty and variability of each variable. Conducting numerous simulations, each with a distinct set of
33
==PAGE:40==
Establishing a simulation based data pool for energy consumption data
4.12
Schedules
randomly generated inputs, builds a detailed picture of the range of possible outcomes.
This approach is particularly effective for energy building simulations, which require numerous input parame-
ters such as weather conditions, occupant behavior, and equipment performance. These parameters can vary
significantly and unpredictably. Monte Carlo methods address these uncertainties by creating random samples
from probability distributions, enabling a thorough exploration of various scenarios. This probabilistic method
generates a broad array of scenarios, allowing for the evaluation of the likelihood of different outcomes and
identifying the most probable performance metrics and associated risks. Random sampling was used which
is the most basic sampling technique and works by generating a random number and scaling it to the target
variable via its probability distribution. [7]
By running many simulations with diverse inputs, Monte Carlo methods prevent reliance on specific assump-
tions or a single set of inputs. This results in more robust and reliable predictions of building energy perfor-
mance, capturing the full range of potential behaviors.

5.4.3
Distributions
In the variation analysis each input parameter is assigned a certain distribution. The choice of the distribution
depends on the nature of the parameter being modeled and the available data.
• Square distribution (uniform distribution)
This distribution assigns equal probability to all values within a specific range and has a constant
probability density within the range and zero probability outside of it. It is applied when there is no
specific information available about the parameter’s distribution other than the range within which it is
expected to lie.
• Normal distribution
Useful when the parameter is symmetrically distributed around a mean value and deviations from the
mean follow a bell-shaped curve.

6.2
Results: Residential Segment
The residential sector is characterized by low ventilation flows, low internal gains, and diverse occupancy
patterns, which significantly influence its energy consumption profile. Heating consumption is a major energy
component in residential buildings, particularly in regions with cold climates, such as Denmark. The need
to maintain comfortable indoor temperatures during the winter months leads to substantial energy use for
heating systems.
In Denmark, cooling systems are not universally applied in residential buildings due to the temperate climate,
where natural ventilation and passive cooling strategies often suffice during the warmer months. However, as
climate change leads to increasing summer temperatures, there may be a growing need for efficient cooling
solutions in the future.
Domestic hot water (DHW) consumption is a significant energy driver in residential buildings. Activities such
as bathing, cooking, and cleaning require a constant supply of hot water, making it essential to implement
efficient water heating systems.

Using firgures 60 and 61, key characteristics of the influence of the input parameters could be mentioned.
Increasing U-values, which represent thermal transmittance, predictably leads to higher heating consumption
due to increased heat loss through the building envelope. In contrast, cooling consumption remains steady or
slightly decreases with higher U-values, as less heat is retained within the building.
Floor height significantly impacts heating consumption, as taller ceilings increase the volume of space that
needs to be heated, resulting in higher energy demands. This effect is particularly notable in the logistics
segment.
The window-to-wall ratio (WWR) also influences energy consumption. An increased WWR can lead to higher
heating and cooling demands due to greater heat loss and gain through windows.
Setpoints for heating and cooling play a crucial role in determining energy consumption. A higher heating
setpoint results in increased heating consumption to maintain a warmer indoor environment, while a lower
cooling setpoint increases cooling consumption to achieve cooler indoor temperatures.
Internal gains from equipment and occupants affect both heating and cooling. High internal gains reduce the
need for heating, as they contribute additional heat to the building, but increase the cooling load. Excessive
internal gains can even necessitate cooling in winter, which may render the simulations unrealistic.
Ventilation flow is one of the most influential parameters. High ventilation flow rates lead to increased heating
consumption due to the need to warm incoming cold air, while significantly reducing cooling demands by
bringing in cooler outside air.
Infiltration primarily affects heating consumption.
Higher infiltration rates lead to greater heat loss and
proportionally higher heating demands and lower cooling ones.

Discussion
This thesis is structured into four main parts. First, it provides an extensive literature review of the chosen
input parameters for the variation analysis. Second, it involves the testing and validation of the simulation tool
to ensure its accuracy and reliability. Third, it details the execution and customization of the variation analysis
for each segment of the study. Finally, it presents the interpretation of the results. This holistic approach to
energy building simulation encompasses a comprehensive investigation, addressing every aspect of the process
rather than focusing on a single component. With the goal of generating a comprehensive database of Building
Performance Simulations for representative buildings in the Danish building stock, specific guidelines were
established to access the progress of the project.
7.1
Literature review
The research of literature in the sector that this thesis emphasizes not only exhibited the present state of art,
but optimized the process of this thesis. There were articles focusing more on the development of building
archetypes, the input parameters or the whole process. There was no publication found that addressed different
segments or linked the relationship between the year of construction and the building condition.
7.2
Input parameter literature review
In this step, the key input parameters for energy building simulation were addressed individually for each
segment. These parameters were divided based on whether they were influenced by building condition or by
usage and occupancy profiles. Extensive research was conducted to understand their potential values.
The studies highlighted the importance of archetypes. To better represent the Danish building stock over
10,000 m², three geometric types were used: rectangular, courtyard, and L-shaped.
An optimal balance
between accuracy and computational efficiency in the number of zones per floor was investigated.
Standards, articles, and relevant literature were researched for each parameter to map their possible representa-
tive values. It became evident that occupancy patterns significantly influence parameter values, with different
usage scenarios presenting unique characteristics. For example, high occupancy necessitated higher ventilation
flow to ensure indoor environmental quality. The interrelationships between parameters were interesting and
distinguished the different segments.
Notably, substantial differences in some values were observed during the research. For instance, office equip-
ment power consumption ranged from 4W/m² to 17W/m². This discrepancy could be due to the lower value
being typical in open-plan offices and the higher one in desk areas with computers and other equipment. When
modeling the floor into zones based on orientation, the lower value was more appropriate for the simulation
tool used, although the higher value was not incorrect. This example underscores the importance of applying
a critical filter to understand the reasons behind these value ranges.
Forming the distribution of the input parameters was challenging.
The presented tables summed up the
findings from the research. Afterwards, extreme scenarios were executed for each segment, to ensure that
the combination of internal gains and low cooling setpoint results into realistic cooling loads in the winter.
Repetitive simulations were run until the maximum internal gains were tailored. Crossing the values higher
than the maximum values in the initial table, the distributions were created with the remaining values.
As the distributions and their characteristics were formed, it became clearer which values were most applicable
to the simulation approach. This careful review and understanding ensured that the parameters used in the
simulations were both accurate and relevant to the specific conditions and uses of the buildings being studied
and the simulation approach.

7.4
Execution and customization of the variation analysis
A notable distinction in the variation analysis approach of this project compared to existing literature is the
segmentation of input parameters by segment and building condition. This method creates more realistic
scenarios by considering both the building usage and envelope. Additionally, the parameters are not linked
linearly; instead, ranges are provided for each parameter. This approach reflects the reality that older buildings
tend to share certain envelope characteristics with other buildings from the same construction period, while
newer buildings share characteristics with those built more recently.
For instance, it is unlikely that a building with poor U-values (indicating poor insulation) would also have low
infiltration rates. By applying ranges based on building condition, the analysis captures a broader spectrum of
scenarios. This enhances the realism of the generated data pool, providing more accurate and diverse scenarios
for simulation.
After establishing the distributions and ranges for all segments, executing the variation analysis proved to
be highly challenging. Initial rounds of analysis revealed that while the results of individual simulations were
reliable, the aggregated results for a single segment were not. Initially, the number of iterations across building
conditions was the same. However, the resulting distribution curves for heating and cooling demands were
unrealistic. For example, the majority of simulations showed space heating demand below 20 kWh/m². This
occurred because the distribution of heating in well-conditioned buildings is very narrow, typically ranging
from 20-25 kWh/m². In contrast, buildings in medium and poor condition exhibit greater fluctuations, with
values normally ranging between 20-100 kWh/m².
To address this discrepancy, research was conducted on the number of buildings in each segment and their
construction years to accurately represent the Danish building stock.
The number of iterations was then
adjusted accordingly. This adjustment resulted in distribution curves that aligned more closely with findings
from previous projects, experiences, and literature.
The next step involved tailoring the total surface area to align with potential projects of Energy Machines.
By focusing on the geometry, multiple rounds of variation analysis were conducted to match the company’s
preferences. This step was crucial to ensure the project’s higher usability and success within the company.
Finally, the results indicated substantial sensitivity to the input parameters. Understanding their influence
was key to the final presentation of the results, highlighting the importance of precise input data in achieving
realistic and applicable outcomes.

7.6
Limitations
There was a lot of discussion around the selection of the HVAC system. Simple HVAC uses the EnergyPlus
is Ideal Loads Air System, providing hot/cold air to meet heating and cooling needs directly, with mechanical
ventilation loads calculated for each zone. It is easier and quicker to set up, requiring less detailed input and
knowledge of HVAC systems. Energy consumption for heating and cooling is determined post-process using
simple seasonal efficiency factors. This method is best for preliminary design stages, quick assessments, or
when detailed HVAC modeling isn’t necessary. Detailed HVAC, on the other hand, models HVAC systems
in full detail using EnergyPlus air and water-side components linked on a schematic layout. This approach
requires more effort to set up and a deeper understanding of HVAC systems. It provides a more accurate and
detailed calculation of energy consumption, considering all components and their interactions. Detailed HVAC
is ideal for detailed design stages where accurate modeling of HVAC systems is crucial.
In this project the infiltration, the heating and the cooling set-point schedule are considered constant. There
was no investigation of heating and cooling setback when the building is unoccupied. This might have led
to higher energy consumption. Especially in segments such as office spaces that are not used in the night
or the weekend, heating and cooling systems could have been unnecessarily running during these unoccupied
periods, resulting in higher energy consumption and increased utility costs. Implementing heating and cooling
setback strategies, where the set-points are adjusted to reduce energy usage during unoccupied hours, could
have helped mitigate this issue.
For example, during nights and weekends when office spaces are typically unoccupied, the heating set-point
could be lowered in winter to reduce the need for heating, and the cooling set-point could be raised in summer
to minimize cooling demand. This would allow the building to operate in a more energy-efficient manner by
aligning HVAC operation with occupancy patterns.
Additionally, incorporating infiltration schedules that vary based on outdoor conditions could provide a more
accurate representation of air leakage and its impact on heating and cooling loads. By accounting for factors
such as temperature differentials and wind speeds, the simulation could better predict the actual energy
requirements for maintaining indoor comfort levels.
Overall, considering dynamic schedules for infiltration, heating, and cooling set-points, with adjustments for
unoccupied periods, is essential for accurately modeling energy consumption and identifying opportunities for
energy savings in building simulations.

Conclusions and Recommendations
The research conducted in this thesis has underscored the critical role that buildings play in the broader context
of energy consumption. By focusing on the diverse building stock in Denmark, we have been able to develop a
comprehensive data pool of energy simulation results that are both robust and reflective of real-world condi-
tions. The collaboration with Energy Machines has been instrumental in achieving these outcomes, providing
valuable insights into the energy demands of various building types under different scenarios. Summarizing
the most important sections of this project:
• Detailed investigations into crucial input parameters for energy simulations across different segments
(residential, office, retail, logistics, educational, and hotel) were conducted, establishing ranges and
distributions for these parameters.
• Extensive research into the historical data and current building conditions allowed for accurate correla-
tions between the year of construction, building segment, and building condition.
• The energy model was validated against IDA ICE, ensuring that the simulations and results are realistic
and reliable.
• A total of 1500 simulations for each building segment were successfully executed, providing a robust
foundation for assessing energy performance and informing future building projects.
Looking forward to recommendations for future work, my thoughts focus on improving the used model, expand
the input parameter libraries and taking into account more parameters. More specifically:
• Validation of Air Handling Units (AHUs): A detailed validation of the AHUs should be conducted.
This involves not only verifying their performance within the energy models but also ensuring that their
real-world operation aligns with simulated results. This can be achieved through on-site measurements
and comparing these with the simulation data.
• Expansion of Building Archetypes: While the current study focused on three building archetypes, future
research could expand this to include more diverse building types and configurations. This would further
refine the model and make it applicable to an even broader range of buildings.
• Expansion of the input parameter library: One of the significant outcomes of this thesis was the estab-
lishment of a comprehensive set of input parameters for energy building simulations. These parameters,
encompassing various building segments, were crucial in achieving realistic and accurate simulations.
However, the current study provides a foundation, and there is considerable scope for expanding the
input parameter library to enhance the robustness and applicability of the energy models.
• Incorporation of Future Weather Files: To enhance the accuracy and relevance of the simulations, it is
recommended to incorporate future weather files that account for projected climate changes. This will
provide a more comprehensive understanding of how buildings will perform under different future climate
scenarios.
