Department of Wind Energy
MSc in Engineering
Wake effect impact on aggregated modelling and active power control
by Ilias PLIOTAS
Over the past few years, the wind energy penetration into the electricity grid has
been continuously increasing. This is due to the willingness of many countries to
become independent of fossil fuels. Denmark, for example, has committed to a 100%
fossil fuel free power production by 2050. In addition, current market trends indicate
that over the course of the next few years wind energy is expected to expand even
more.
This will undoubtedly create new challenges for the electric power systems. The
transmission system operators have already, and in the future are expected to re-
quire even more the Wind Power Plants to provide ancillary services into the sys-
tem. Therefore, there is a need for simulating the active power output of a wind
farm more accurately and in less time.
This project deals with the wake effect and how this phenomenon affects the esti-
mated active power output of a wind farm. The possibility of using an aggregated
model in order to represent a wind farm as a lumped model is also investigated.
Two major scenarios are developed, in respect to the incoming wind. The ﬁrst one
considers only static wind speeds, namely wind speeds that are not changed over
time. The second one uses real life wind data from an operational wind farm. For
both scenarios, a number of different cases have been considered, depending on the
wake model and the wind farm model that has been used.
The analysis is realized by making use of the software package Matlab/Simulink.
Numerous simulations are carried out and the results show that the wake effect has a
signiﬁcant impact on the total active power output of the wind farm. More precisely,
in all investigated cases the no wake scenario overestimates the active power output.
Additionally, the aggregated model shows signiﬁcantly high accuracy compared to
the detailed one with the simulation time required being greatly reduced.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Background
Over the past decades, energy production based on fossil fuels has been evidently
responsible for several negative consequences. The degradation of the environment
and its detrimental effects on the population throughout the life cycle of the fuel,
from extraction until combustion, followed by several byproducts, have been inves-
tigated and addressed by many researchers. Consequently, many countries have
been taking measures, trying to mitigate global warming and climate change in gen-
eral [1]. One of the key measures adopted, is the commitment to change the energy
production share from fossil fuel based to a more renewable one, with Denmark be-
ing the vanguard of it. It is indicative that since 2012, through an Energy Agreement
[2], Denmark has been bound to achieve an energy share that consists of 35% re-
newable sources by 2020, as well as to cover 50% of electricity consumption by wind
energy. In addition, a long-term goal has also been set, targeting a fossil fuel free
energy supply by 2050. As far as the current mix of renewable energy sources in
Denmark is concerned, the electricity generation is dominated by wind power, but
it also consists of electricity generated by hydroelectric power, photovoltaic cells,
biogas and biomass.
In the light of the Danish governments’ attempts for an independent fossil fuel en-
ergy share [3], it is a well-established fact that there has been a great expansion of
wind power since 1990. More speciﬁcally, according to Energinet.dk, the Danish
Transmission System Operator (TSO), wind power covered only 2% of the Danish
electricity consumption in 1990, whereas in 2016, it covered approximately 37.6% of
it [4]. The same trend is also shown over the past few years. In 2011, wind turbines
(WTs) produced roughly 9700 [MWh] (28% of the electricity consumption), while in
2016 they produced more than 12700 [MWh] [4], [5]. Although wind energy is sub-
ject to wind conditions and thus the production is varying throughout the years, the
aforementioned ﬁgures clearly show that the targets for a different, more renewable
energy mix in Denmark are feasible.
The big driver behind the growth of wind energy investment, however, is the de-

1.2
Problem Formulation
The increased wind energy penetration in the current, designed for conventional
power plants, electricity grids is to undoubtedly bring some challenges in the up-
coming years.
As there is a gradual replacement of the conventional units, the
requirements and grid codes have been updating by the power system operators.
Therefore, in addition to the minimum technical and functional requirements that
wind power plants (WPPs) have to meet, they also have to provide ancillary ser-
vices that once only the traditional power plants did. In Denmark, the technical
regulations for WPPs are introduced in [8], where ancillary services such as active
power control are also included. Taking these requirements as well as the projection
for more wind energy in the future into consideration, it is of high importance to be
able to simulate a wind farm’s behaviour as accurately as possible when it comes to
power system studies. Moreover, when the studies involve not only a local or even
a national system, but an international or European one, computational time and
power needed for these simulations become crucial as the corresponding number of
wind farms is increased.
Another challenge that WPPs will face due to the increased size and number of wind
turbines, is the accuracy of the wind speeds that are used for power system studies.
In order for a WPP to be able to participate, for example in active power control, its
active power output and thus the rotor effective wind speeds of the wind turbines
must be accurately estimated. As phenomena such as the wake effect have an im-
pact on the rotor effective wind speed of each individual wind turbine, wind speed
measurements taken from a single wind turbine in a wind farm, or even a meteo-
rological mast located outside of it, may not be representative for the whole wind
farm.

Objectives
The main objective of this thesis is to investigate the impact of the wake effect on the
active power control of wind farms. A set of active power control studies are thus
performed in order to quantify the wake modelling effects on the available power
of a wind farm. In addition, the extent to which an already developed aggregated
model, representing a wind farm as a lumped equivalent, provides acceptably accu-
rate results in respect to active power output is studied.
The ultimate goal of this project is to contribute to the following:
• Evaluation of the wake effect on active power output of a wind farm in power
system studies.
• Decreased simulation time and computational power required for these stud-
ies through the use of an aggregated model.
1.4
Scope and limitations
In the course of this work, two major scenarios are considered in respect to wind
speed and direction. The ﬁrst one considers static wind speeds and directions, while
the second one is realized by using real wind data, thus including stochasticity. A
number of different cases, that are to be analyzed further in the project, are then held
for each scenario.
Throughout this work, the following limitations and assumptions are considered:
• Two simple and robust wake models are used, without getting deep into their
details or trying to use more advanced models for higher accuracy, for exam-
ple, ones using computational ﬂuid dynamics (CFD).
• The project is limited to the investigation of a wind farm’s behaviour with a
layout identical to the one of the Horns Rev-I wind farm.
• The study focuses on three different wind turbine sizes. More speciﬁcally the
reference wind turbines DTU 2-MW, NREL 5-MW and DTU 10-MW are con-
sidered.
• The ﬁrst of the two major scenarios considers only static winds, without taking
into account its stochastic nature. There will be therefore deliberately chosen
wind speeds and directions for the simulations, which will be perpendicular
to the ﬁrst row or column of the wind farm’s layout.
• The data used for the second major scenario, the one considering stochastic
winds, is limited to a time period of one hour of operation, in one-second in-
tervals, due to large simulation time required.
• The wind directions used for this time period are limited to eastern wind direc-
tions. Therefore, there is no investigation held for stochastic winds from other
directions.
• Due to high computational cost and simulation time, only one row of the wind
farm is analyzed during the scenario of stochastic winds.

2.1
Aggregated Modelling in Literature
As discussed in the introduction of this project (Chapter 1), the electrical energy
produced by the wind has been continuously increasing over the past years and
subsequently, the number of wind turbines in a wind farm has raised. Therefore, it
is reasonable to pursue developing simulation models that are able to describe wind
farms’ behaviour accurately when it comes to power system studies. Although a
wind farm model, that includes a detailed representation for each wind turbine,
would result to the expected outcomes, it would also be highly demanding in terms
of complexity, computational cost and simulation time. In order to face this issue,
researchers apply aggregated modelling practices, in which a wind farm is repre-
sented by one, or a group of, equivalent models. More speciﬁcally, according to
[9], three different representations can be implemented in order to aggregate a wind
farm.
• Single unit representation
• Cluster representation
• Compound representation
In single unit representation, the wind farm is represented by a single equivalent
model of a wind turbine. Then the electric power output is re-scaled in order to
adapt to the capacity of the entire wind farm. In the study [10], Akhmatov & Knud-
sen introduced a single unit aggregated model of a large scale offshore wind farm,
in order to investigate power stability. The suggested model consists of an one-
machine equivalent with MVA rating and active power output the total sum of the
different wind turbines. They also recommend the use of a multi-machine equiv-
alent model, especially in cases when the wind distribution over the wind farm is
irregular.
A comparison between a single unit representation of a wind farm and the detailed
modelling of it for power studies has been undertaken in [11]. The results show
that the author’s model represents an acceptable approximation of the detailed one,
using signiﬁcantly less simulation time and computer power.
Cluster representation means the development of groups that consist of wind tur-
bines that face the same wind. An equivalent model then represents each group,
with the power rating and active power output to be equal to the capacity of each
formed cluster. The number of the groups depends on the accuracy that is required
for the simulation. For example, in [12], a cluster representation is used to model the
==PAGE:24==
6
Chapter 2. Literature Review
dynamic response of a wind farm. Each row of the wind farm sees the same wind
speed and therefore the wind turbines of each row are grouped into a cluster and are
represented by an equivalent model. The proposed aggregated model has proven to
reduce the complexity and simulation time, without compromising accuracy.
Compound representation is a combination of the single and cluster modelling. In
[13], Fernandez et al. suggested a wind farm equivalent model that is based on a
dynamic simpliﬁed model of each individual wind turbine that represents the me-
chanical torques. Then, all the different models for the mechanical torques, are ag-
gregated and inserted into an equivalent generator model. Although each wind
turbine faces a different wind speed, the proposed model decreases the complexity
of modelling the entire wind farm at the Point of Common Coupling (PCC).
During the course of this project, the simulations will mainly focus on single unit
representation, and more precisely will be adapted to the model developed in [11].
2.2
Wake Effect in Literature
When operating, wind turbines extract kinetic energy from the wind, causing a
deﬁcit in the average downstream wind speed, along with increased level of tur-
bulence. This phenomenon’s aftermath is a decreased total power output of a wind
farm due to the wind speed loss compared to the case where all wind turbines see
the same wind speed as the most upstream one. In addition increased loading for a
number of the wind turbines in the wind farm exists.
Due to the severe impact of the wake both in power production and the lifetime of
a wind farm, several models have been developed in an attempt to calculate it accu-
rately. One of the ﬁrst tools that were able to model single wakes, was introduced
by Jensen [14] and Katic et al. [15]. It uses an empirical equation that is based on a
balance of momentum. The model assumes a linearly expanding wake downstream,
only depending on the distance behind the rotor. The Jensen model has been widely
used for wake modelling, due to its simplicity and robustness.
In the late 1980’s, Larsen [16] developed a simple wake calculation procedure, which
is based on the Prandtl’s boundary layer theory. The model uses closed-form solu-
tions for both the width and the mean speed proﬁle in the wake and in its initial
version was only able to calculate single wakes. The Larsen model was ﬁrst imple-
mented in the module-based software package WindPRO [17]. A later version of
this model [18], accounted for the fact that the earlier one was unable to provide re-
sults for multiple wake conditions. This was achieved by re-deﬁning the boundary
conditions making use of the results of full scale experiments.
In 2006, Frandsen et al. [19] demonstrated an analytical model for the prediction of
wind speed deﬁcit in large offshore wind farms, assuming a linear expansion of the
wake area. The proposed model consists of three distinct regimes, namely multiple
inline wake interaction, the wake limitation due to the ground and adjacent wind
turbine rows and their corresponding wakes and the equilibrium or inﬁnite scenario,
in which the ﬂow within the farm is balanced with the boundary layer over the wind
farm.

2.3
Active Power Control of Wind Farms in Literature
As the regional increase in the percentage of the electricity that is generated by wind
has been increasing, ongoing research tries to improve the integration of large wind
farms into the existing electric power grid in order to increase its reliability and
stability. In Denmark, Energinet.dk already requires a number of ancillary services
to be provided by WPPs over 11 [kW] [8]. One of these required ancillary services
is the active power output control (APC), including the provision of grid balancing
services, namely primary frequency control (PFC) and automatic generation control
(AGC).
Aho et al. have developed and compared two different APC controllers in [25], [26],
the APC torque controller and the APC blade pitch controller. The former provides
power control through the power electronics without altering the blade pitch control
system, whereas the latter provides power control by changing the speed reference
set point in the blade pitch controller. The study [27] proposes two different active
power control schemes that are able to address the control at an entire wind farm
level. The ﬁrst scheme is based on adaptive pole placement control and has proven
to be signiﬁcantly effective in regards to frequency regulation. The second proposed
scheme consists of fuzzy gain-scheduled PI control and as the results have shown, it
is able to act and recover quickly in case of a frequency event.
2.4
Wake effect impact on modelling
Although extensive research has studied aggregated modelling of wind farms as
well as more effective ways to integrate wind power into the existing electric power
system, the vast majority does not take wake effect into consideration. In most cases,
this results to an overestimation of the active power output of a wind farm. Zhang
and Wang in [28] have tried to model different wind farms for power system studies,
considering the wake effect. Their outcome has revealed that the wake effect should
be considered in such analyses, otherwise the resulted active power output will be
higher than the real one.
The above arguments show that there is a gap in the literature as far as the direct
impact of the wake effect in a wind farm on different aggregated models and active
power control is concerned. That was what gave rise on the idea of investigating the
speciﬁc topic, in an attempt to understand this connection.

3.1
Wind turbine data
Wind turbines can be categorized into two classes, in respect to their speed control
ability; namely as ﬁxed-speed wind turbines (FSWT) and as variable-speed wind
turbines (VSWT). The advantages of the VSWT over the FSWT are notable. Their me-
chanical rotor frequency is decoupled by the electric grid frequency, through power
electronics. This enables the rotational speed of the wind turbine to continuously
adapt to the wind speed in such a way as to always operate in the highest possible
aerodynamic efﬁciency. In consequence, a higher annual energy production com-
pared to FSWTs is achieved, in which the wind turbine is designed to achieve its
maximum efﬁciency in a speciﬁc wind speed. For the aforementioned reasons and
despite the fact that VSWTs carry increased costs and losses (due to the power elec-
tronics interface), they have become the most dominant type of wind turbines over
the past several years [29]. Therefore, it was decided that VSWTs would be used
for the current work. Their operation can be summarized in Figure 3.1, where the
output active power (P), the rotational speed (ω), the pitch angle (θ) and the power
coefﬁcient (Cp) are depicted as a function of the wind speed (U).
Two different operational zones are deﬁned, depending on the wind turbine’s be-
haviour. The ﬁrst zone, the optimization zone (A-C), is deﬁned for wind speeds be-
tween the cut-in and the rated one. In this region, the wind turbine operates in such
a way that its power output is maximized depending on the speciﬁc wind speeds.
This can be achieved by setting the other characteristics accordingly. More speciﬁ-
cally, the pitch angle is kept constant to its optimal value, while the rotational speed
increases until it reaches its nominal and then it is kept steady (B-C). As a conse-
quence, the power coefﬁcient starts decreasing (B-C).
On the other hand, for wind speeds higher than the rated one and up to the cut-out
wind speed (C-D), the power output is kept constant to the wind turbine’s rated one.
This is called the power limitation zone. To achieve this, the pitch angle is increased,
so that the rotational speed is kept to its nominal value causing the power coefﬁcient
to keep decreasing.

3.2.3
Pitch controller
The pitch controller is responsible for changing the pitch angle of the blades when
the power output of the wind turbine is required to be controlled. The difference
between the generator speed at a speciﬁc time and its nominal value is calculated,
providing the pitch angle reference. As the pitch angle is subject to several physical
constrains, for example the rate of change of the angle, the actual pitch angle is
compared to the reference one within the Actuator. In addition, in order to realize
the time that is needed to change the pitch angle in reality, a servomechanism is
used, consisting of a time constant of Tservo.
Figure 3.9: Pitch controller block diagram of a VSWT [33]

within all operational zones. In fact, as shown in Figure 3.1, the pitch angle only

3.2.4
Active power controller
The active power control block is in charge of making sure that the measured, or
output, active power follows the reference one. In this project, the reference active
power is provided by a maximum power point tracking (MPPT) look up table when
a down-regulation of the wind turbine is not required, whereas when this is a case,
the power curve of the wind turbine is used. The controller has as input the error
between the reference and the measured active power while through a PI controller
the reference current is provided in the system. However, as in this work the voltage
throughout the system is considered to be constant to 1 [pu], the reference current
will be equal to the reference active power that the system will need to follow.
The active power control block is then connected to a simple ﬁrst order ﬁlter, whose
time constant represents the mechanical dynamics of the wind turbine when the
power reference changes. It should be mentioned that the power electronic inter-
face’s as well as the generator’s dynamics are considered to be signiﬁcantly faster
than the mechanical ones, and thus are neglected.

Wind farm modelling
The model of a VSWT that was described in Section 3.2, was further developed in

3.3.1
Detailed model
The wind farm power output was ﬁrst calculated using the detailed wind farm
model. This considers the total number of wind turbines in the wind farm (n number
of wind turbines) and their corresponding wind speeds and directions. Each wind
turbine produces a power output, depending on the speciﬁc wind input. The total
output power of the wind farm is then calculated by adding all the power outputs
produced for each wind turbine. The model is depicted in Figure 3.13.
Figure 3.13: Detailed wind farm model
As for each wind turbine, the model is used with a different wind input, it represents
the behaviour of the wind farm in a more realistic way. This relies on the fact that
the distances between two wind turbines in a wind farm may vary from hundreds to
thousands of meters. Consequently, the effective wind speed of each wind turbine
may be subject to signiﬁcant variations in such distances. However, as the model
simulates each wind turbine separately, the computational time and power will also
be increased compared to the aggregated model.

Chapter 4
Case studies and Results
This Section of the project describes the investigated case studies and scenarios along
with their corresponding results. In order to evaluate the impact of the wake effect
on the active power output and control of a wind farm, two major scenarios are cre-
ated. The ﬁrst one is the case of a static incoming ambient wind, which means that
both wind speed and direction are considered to be constant along the simulation pe-
riod. The second scenario considers dynamic incoming wind, namely wind speeds
and directions that alternate in time. The latter is realized by using a time series
of Supervisory Control and Data Acquisition (SCADA) data from the Horns Rev-
I wind farm, provided by DTU Wind Energy Department, which includes, among
others, the effective wind speed and the produced as well as the estimated active
power of each wind turbine for four hours, in an one-second intervals.
4.1
Scenario I: Static wind speed and direction
The initially investigated scenario does not take into account the turbulence of the
ambient wind and uncertainty of its velocity and direction. Instead, it considers a
steady incoming wind (both in terms of velocity and direction) to the wind farm. For
this scenario, two main cases are examined. In the ﬁrst one, the wind farm produces
active power equal to the available one for the corresponding wind speed. On the
other hand, the second scenario involves a down-regulation strategy of the wind
farm. This means that it is allowed to produce only a fraction of the total available
power, by applying delta or balance active power control on it.
4.1.1
No power set-points case
As discussed during the introduction of this Section, the ﬁrst case that has been
investigated is the case where no active power control strategy of the wind farm is
involved. This means that throughout the simulations, there will be no alteration of
the reference power that the wind turbines have to follow. The impact of the wake
effect on the total active power of the wind farm is evaluated through a series of
simulations which involves the following cases:
• No wake effect
• Aggregated static wake effect - Jensen wake model
• Aggregated static wake effect - Larsen wake model

By observation of the results contained in Figure 4.3, a number of important points
can be noticed. First and foremost, it is clear that the estimation of the power out-
put in the case where no wake is regarded in the simulations is signiﬁcantly higher
than the ones where the wake effect is considered. In fact, for medium wind speeds
(around 7 [m/s]) the difference between the two power output estimations exceeds
50%.
Moreover, it should be underlined, that not only the wind speed, but also its direc-
tion plays an important role on the behaviour of the active power output. Although
when no wake is considered the power output is constant for a speciﬁc wind speed,
regardless the wind direction, this is not the case with wake. Instead, it can be no-
ticed that in the case of northern winds, the output power is signiﬁcantly higher than
the ones where the wind is coming from another direction. This is expected, because
of the row choice. For northern winds, there is only one row of wind turbines af-
fecting the wind speeds at the location of the turbines in question and thus the wind
speed deﬁcit is lower than in the case where 6 upstream rows of wind turbines exist
(southern winds). On the other hand, for eastern and western winds, the amount of
upstream turbines that have an impact on the row of wind turbines as a whole, will
be the same. Consequently, the power output for these directions is equal.
Another important point that can be extracted is the fact that the aggregation models
seem to be highly accurate compared to the detailed ones. The highest differences
occur for very low wind speeds, where the aggregated models and especially the
Jensen one, overestimate the wind speed deﬁcit on the tested wind turbines, which
results to a lower power output. In fact, for non-northern winds, the average wind
velocity estimated by the Jensen model is below the cut-in of the DTU 2-MW wind
turbine (3 [m/s]) resulting to a zero estimated production.

Wake effect impact on power output
In the previous subsection, the resulting power outputs for the different studied
cases were presented. By observing the results, it is instinctively understood that
when no wake is considered in the calculations, the power output of the wind farm
is overestimated compared to when a static wake model is used. This subsection
provides a quantiﬁcation of the wake’s impact on the power output. In order to
realize this, a sensitivity analysis is carried out for all three wind turbines sizes.
During this, a number of simulations is held for a wider range of wind speeds and
their corresponding estimated wind farm active power outputs are compared to the
==PAGE:54==
36
Chapter 4. Case studies and Results
one when no wake is included in the calculations. More speciﬁcally, wind speeds
from 7 [m/s] up to the cut-out wind speed of 25 [m/s] are considered with a step of
0.1 [m/s]. It has to be noticed that very low wind speeds near the cut-in are avoided,
as this can result to zero power output estimation, as previously shown.
Then, the percentage decrease of the active power output of the wind farm is calcu-
lated using the following formula:
Power decrease = Pno wake −Pwake
Pno wake
·100
(4.4)
Where Pno wake is the active power output estimation of the wind farm when no
wake is considered and Pwake is the one provided by the model when the wake
effect is included in the calculations.
Figures 4.7 and 4.8 show the percentage deduction of the estimated active power
production of the wind farm when the wake is considered in the calculations. More
speciﬁcally, they illustrate the case where the Jensen and Larsen static wake models
are used along with a northern/southern and a western/eastern wind direction.
As it can be seen, the percentage decrease of the active power is noticeable when
the wake effect is considered. This means that the estimated active power output
will be signiﬁcantly lower than in the no wake case, especially for wind speeds that
are below the wind turbines’ rated ones. While in the optimization zone the "loss"
of active power remains roughly steady, a range where the percentage decrease is
getting smaller is observed in all cases. This can be explained as after the rated wind
speed, the active power estimation of the no wake case (Pno wake) remains constant
and limited to 1 [pu]. Instead, the wind velocity resulted by the wake models are
below the rated ones and consequently the power output (Pwake) is less than 1 [pu].
After a certain wind speed, which depends on the wind direction, the wind turbine
size and the wake model that is used, this reduced wind velocity becomes equal
or higher than the rated one. In turn, the estimation of the active power output
considering the wake becomes equal to the rated one, remaining constant for the
rest of the simulations. From this wind speed until the cut-out one, the wake effect
does not have an effect on the total active power output of the wind farm.
Another important outcome that can be observed is the effect of the wind turbine
size on the total active power output of the wind farm. DTU 10-MW wind turbine
results to a higher power deﬁcit than the NREL 5-MW and DTU 2-MW one. This
means that the wake caused by this wind turbine is stronger than the others’. In
other words, when the DTU 10-MW wind turbine is investigated in the speciﬁc wind
farm layout, it is expected to produce less active power, percentage-wise, than the
smaller ones. This effect can be mainly observed for wind speeds below the rated
ones and especially for the case that the Jensen wake model is used.

Power set points case
The last investigated case of this Scenario considers the wind farm to take part into
active power control. The realization of this case is developed by down-regulating
each wind turbine of the wind farm at the same time. More speciﬁcally, the reference
active power which each turbine’s power output has to follow is limited to either a
certain percent of the available active power or to a speciﬁc amount of active power
in [pu] depending on whether delta or balance control is used. Thereafter, the be-
haviour of the wind turbine for the cases that the wake effect is or is not considered
into the calculations is compared. The comparison is carried out in respect to mainly
the estimated active power output, but also the pitch angle and rotational speed of
the wind turbine’s rotor. The implementation of this study case is achieved by using
the block diagram described in Section 3.2.6 in the wind turbine model.

thus cannot increase more, the pitch angle changes to a higher value. On the other
hand, when the power set-point increases, the pitch angle decreases. The rotational
speed reacts to this change by decreasing its value. However, as the pitch angle can
further decrease until its optimal value (0 [deg]), the rotational speed returns at its
steady state value, namely 1 [pu].

turbines.

calculated using the dynamic wake model (re-calibrated Larsen model) using
the effective wind speed of the ﬁrst wind turbine of the row. The power output

As it can be seen, the aggregated model’s active power output estimation follows
the ﬂuctuations in active power production, but with a delay of several hundred
seconds. This can be explained by considering the distance between the wind tur-
bines and the time that it takes for a particle to move from the most upstream wind
turbine to the one in question.
In order to solve the delay problem, a simple methodology is used that is based on
[35]. This is to apply a time delay td to the instant when the considered volume of air
passes the downstream location tdown. Therefore, this will be equal to tdown = tup+td,
with tup being the instant when the air passes the upstream location. The time delay
td is estimated as: td = ∆x/mean(U∞,down), where U∞,down is the rotor effective wind
speed at the downstream wind turbine.
Additionally, not only has the time delay to be considered in the estimated active
power output, but also in the measured one. This is because, the active power that
is included in the SCADA data for the ﬁrst seconds of the data set and for the down-
stream wind turbines, is the result of winds in the past. As there is no information
about these winds and consequently, no estimation of the active power can be made,
the SCADA data is also cleared for this time period. This procedure results to Fig-
ure 4.16. It can be observed, that up to a point the active power output is gradually
increased. This is because the wind reaches the location of each downstream wind
turbine at a different time instant. Therefore, until roughly 650 [s], which is the mo-
ment when the wind reaches the last wind turbine of the row (WT #74 ), less than
10 wind turbines contribute to the total active power output. After this point, all 10
wind turbines of the row contribute to the active power production. It is noticeable
that the estimated power output now follows the ﬂuctuations of the active power

Chapter 5
Conclusion and Future work
The main objective of the current project is to evaluate whether the wake effect has
to be taken into consideration for power system studies. As wind energy has been
continuously growing over the past years and future projections show that this trend
will continue in years to come, it is of utmost importance to be able to simulate the
behaviour of a wind farm as accurately as possible. This is because TSOs will re-
quire that the WPPs are to be able to provide ancillary services to the electricity grid,
something that so far only conventional power plants did. Although the wake effect
has been extensively studied within other scopes, such as for aerodynamic studies,
it is usually not taken into account when it comes to power systems. Another ob-
jective of this thesis, is to evaluate an already developed aggregated model, which
considers the wind farm as a lumped equivalent. In this Chapter, the results and
conclusions are summarized, while possible future work is suggested.
5.1
Simulation scenarios - a summary
In this work, a number of simulation cases are developed in order to assess whether
and to what extend the wake effect is important to be included in power system
studies. Two major scenarios are created in respect to the incoming winds:
• The ﬁrst scenario considers static incoming winds, which means that no turbu-
lence or stochasticity is taken into consideration. In other words, a deliberately
chosen wind speed and direction is used for each case and is kept constant
throughout the simulation period.
• The second scenario includes the stochasticity of the wind and its turbulence,
as real wind data is used as measured in Horns Rev-I wind farm, which is the
wind farm under investigation.
Several cases are developed for each scenario, including a no-wake case and cases in
which the wake effect is considered, calculated by two static wake models, namely
the Jensen and Larsen ones and a dynamic one. Moreover, a case study where the
wind farm is required to participate in active power control (both delta and balance
control) is investigated for the static case scenario in order to evaluate the wake’s
effect on the results.
Besides the accuracy of the simulation results, the computational time required is
considered and investigated in these cases. This is considered important, as with the
increased number of wind farms in the grid, the need for studying their behaviour
for power system studies both accurately and swiftly has been escalated. Therefore,

the second objective of this work is to evaluate an already developed aggregated
model, which considers the whole wind farm as a lumped equivalent. The power
output of this equivalent model is then re-scaled to match the actual size of the wind
farm. To this end, the cases in which the wake effect is considered are compared
with each other in terms of these parameters.
5.2
Discussions and conclusions
This project provides a number of interesting outcomes both concerning the rela-
tionship between the wake effect and the power system studies and about the use of
aggregation modelling in order to represent a wind farm.
First of all, for all the investigated cases, it is clear that the wake effect has a signif-
icant impact on the active power output of the wind farm. More speciﬁcally, both
static and dynamic incoming winds to the wind farm are studied, showing a notice-
able deﬁcit of the total active output power (exceeding 50% for some wind speeds)
for the cases where the wake effect is included in the calculations.
Another noteworthy result of this work is that for static winds, the degree in which
the power output is affected by the wake effect is directly dependent on a number
of parameters, such as the wind speed, the direction and the wind turbine size.
This is the outcome of a number of simulations that consider three different refer-
ence wind turbines, namely the DTU 2-MW, NREL 5-MW and DTU 10-MW ones
located in a layout identical to the one of the Horns Rev-I wind farm. The distance
in-between the wind turbines is kept constant to 7 rotor diameters, which is equal to
the distances in the reference wind farm. However, as wind turbines with different
rotor diameters are used, this is changed accordingly. Section 4.1.1 illustrates the
dependence of the wake effect impact on active power output on wind turbine size.
It is shown that for larger WTs, or larger rotor diameters, the consideration of the
wake effect in the calculations results to a higher deﬁcit in the active power. As far
as the wind speed is concerned, the results in Section 4.1.1 also show that for the spe-
ciﬁc wind turbines, the higher deﬁcit in the active power caused by the wake effect
is when they operate in the optimization zone, after which the deﬁcit is gradually
diminished. The direction of the wind and its impact on the wake effect is also
discussed throughout this project. The impact of the wake effect is shown to alter
according to the direction from which the wind is coming. Northern and southern
winds are proved to cause a more signiﬁcant wake and consequently lower power
output than the western or eastern ones. This conclusion is based on the speciﬁc
wind farm layout due to the different number of columns and rows it consists of as
well as the equidistant wind turbines. Further investigations are necessary in order
to assess if this is also valid for other types of layouts.
In addition, the use of aggregation modelling as a tool to represent the wind farm’s
behaviour is evaluated. This project shows that for the speciﬁc scenarios that are
investigated in it, an aggregated model representing the wind farm as a lumped
equivalent ([11]) is able to provide results of high accuracy. This is assessed by com-
paring the active power output of the wind farm with the one calculated by a de-
tailed model, which simulates each individual wind turbine of it. The results show
that the suggested aggregated model can provide an estimation of the power output
of the wind farm in a fraction of the time required by the detailed model, without
compromising the accuracy of the results. More speciﬁcally, the aggregated models

require approximately 70 times less time when the whole wind farm is simulated
and 9 times less when only one row of it is considered. Additionally, the mean
error between the results of the aggregated models and the detailed ones is calcu-
lated and is always less than 4.5%. It is also shown that for the case the wind farm is
required to provide active power control, and for static winds, the aggregated model
is able to follow the reference output power very well.
5.3
Future work
This work presents the ﬁrst investigated stage of a very complex problem, namely
the impact of wake effect on the power system studies. Its outcome supports the
belief that this phenomenon should be included in the calculations of power system
studies. However, in order to be able to prove its importance and generalize its
applicability, a number of research points can be further investigated.
Investigation of different wind directions
This project only considers wind directions that were perpendicular to the ﬁrst row
or column of wind turbines in the wind farm for the scenario of static winds and
eastern winds for the one using real data. A wider range of wind directions to be
studied is suggested, so that the wake effect is analyzed in greater detail. Both the
use of deliberately set wind directions and real wind data is advised.
Investigation of the effect to different wind farm layouts
In this work, the investigation is limited to a speciﬁc wind farm layout, namely the
one of the Horns Rev-I wind farm. However, in order to be able to gain a general
understanding of how the wake effect affects power system studies, different wind
farm layouts should be also studied, from simpler to more complicated ones.
Investigation of the wake effect in different wind turbine sizes
This thesis analyzes the wake effect impact on three reference wind turbines in the
case that the wind speed and direction is constant throughout the calculations. How-
ever, only one reference wind turbine is considered for the real data, the one whose
characteristics are similar to the real wind turbine’s ones. It is therefore suggested
that deeper research is undertaken that concerns real wind data extracted from wind
turbines of different sizes.
Wake effect impact on power set-points changes
The wake effect’s impact is also discussed in the case that the wind farm is required
to take part in active power control. However, this is only in the scope of this project
for the static wind scenario. Therefore, it is suggested that future research is focused
on cases where power set-point changes exist under stochastic wind speeds.
Wake effect impact on different ancillary services
Further investigations should also focus on whether and in what way the wake effect
inﬂuences the results of other ancillary services that a wind farm is required to pro-
vide to the electricity grid. For example, the effect on the inertia control is suggested
to be in the purpose of future work.
