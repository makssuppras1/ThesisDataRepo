Abstract
The diﬀerences in the socio-economic experiences of both genders, often simply termed the gender gap, is an histor-
ical issue which has gained increasing attention in modern times. Studies of inequality across diﬀerent areas have
long been undertaken. Organizations such as The World Economic Forum and The World Health Organization
have built systems of reports and rankings to quantify and recognise the gender gap. Often, they focus on particular
aspects of inequality such as health, education, economic participation or political empowerment [30]. Whilst the
speciﬁc causes of these inequalities can vary between diﬀerent countries, it is important to note that access to health
institutes, schools and universities, work places and cities will inﬂuence the above mentioned aspects accordingly
[31]. In the literature we can ﬁnd that women and men have diﬀerent mobility patterns [23], and from global
statistics it is visible that women have limited access to transportation, and use cars much less frequently, which
can limit their access to opportunities [27].
Although we have some understanding of the gender gap in mobility, we currently lack worldwide quantitative
analysis of the issue. With the increasing use of mobile phones and big data, we can begin to analyse statistically
these diﬀerences in mobility. This study will focus on understanding gender gaps in four fundamental areas of
mobility: entropy, radius of gyration, number of unique visited places, and number of new visited places, using
a large-scale data-set consisting of the GPS traces of 100 000 individuals worldwide. Speciﬁc attention is paid to
factors of age, country of origin, urban area of origin. Correlations between perception of safety and the gender
gap in mobility are also investigated.
Using big-data to quantify the gender gap has the potential to improve
understanding of the issue, and to provide a background for designing better policies, cities, transport solutions,
and much more.

Introduction
The ﬁelds of humanities and social sciences have shown that cities are not designed in a gender neutral way [68].
Even in daily mobility, women and men have diﬀerent experiences. One of the key factors limiting women’s mobility
is the security and amount of physical or sexual crimes in an area [33]. Women’s mobility is also strongly related to
the locations of bus stops, the amount of lighting on the streets, and crowding of an area. With regards to speciﬁc
patterns, women due to their household responsibilities mostly engage in trips with many purposes and therefore
many stops [34]. Considering that urbanisation oﬀers many possibilities to reduce gender inequality; such as access
to higher education or skilled work for both women and men, the limits in mobility (often between rural areas and
urban ones) is an especially crucial issue. Therefore through understanding human mobility with a focus on gender
diﬀerences it is possible to verify if women and girls can truly beneﬁt from the opportunities oﬀered them within a
city.
In order to study the issue, reliable data is necessary. To understand general inequality issues we can rely on orga-
nizations like The World Economic Forum and The World Health Organization which build reports and rankings
for quantitatively measuring and recognising gender diﬀerences. In the ‘Global Gender Gap Report 2020’ [30],
we can ﬁnd information about inequality in the following areas: 1) Economic Participation and Opportunity 2)
Educational Attainment 3) Health and Survival 4) Political Empowerment per country. This data is collected from
statistics such as labour force participation or enrolment in primary education, and not from individual user input,
and as such provides an objective viewpoint. The report gives information about general trends across both the
world and within particular countries, but is lacking more precise information and analysis of the causes. Another
way to study the issue, often used in research is to use data derived from surveys [35- 37]. This can be more precise
but encounters its own diﬃculties, in that it is highly subjective and very limited in quantity. Therefore the use
of mobile data was a major milestone which allowed researchers to perform global analyses with high precision, to
understand individual behaviours. The following thesis is therefore unique in using very precise, individual GPS
traces, whilst also having a worldwide reach.
In describing the concept of human mobility, several factors have shown themselves to be valuable [1],[5],[7],[9]. They
include quantitative descriptions of how many places a person has visited (in total or just counting new places), how
often they visited certain locations, and the distances involved in travelling to each of those places. In this study,
the following four factors will be used to deﬁne gender diﬀerences: median entropy per month, radius of gyration,
number of new places visited in a month and median number of unique places visited per day. Entropy captures the
degree of predictability characterizing a time series. The radius of gyration measures the typical distances covered
by individuals. In research conducted at the Center for Complex Network Research at Northeastern University in
Boston [18], and based on data from 50,000 individuals, it was found that the radius of gyration follows a fat-tailed
distribution, which indicates that most individuals’ daily activity is limited to a region of 1 to 10 km, although
there are a few users who regularly cover hundreds of kilometers. It was also proven that individuals who travel
less have a smaller entropy, so they are easier to predict, whilst those with a larger radius of gyration have higher
entropy and therefore are less predictable. Two other factors are the number of unique places visited, and num-
ber of new places visited. These factors also relate to entropy, as new locations are less predictable than unique ones.
In recent years studies of human mobility have gained signiﬁcant interest. Several research papers described modern
methods in this ﬁeld and new ﬁndings. In the paper ‘Gender gaps in urban mobility’ [23] research conducted on
anatomized mobile phone users over a period of 3 months showed that the average radius of gyration of women is
1.09 km shorter than that for men. Also, women’s mobility is characterized by a smaller entropy when compared to
men, with an average diﬀerence of 0.26, which can be interpreted such that women distribute their trips among a
few highly preferred locations, whilst men distribute their trips among many locations with an almost equal prob-
ability. Accordingly, it can further be shown that women are more frequently found in their most visited locations.
In addition the study found that women travel to fewer unique locations than men on average by 8.58 per month.
This could be related to the data found in the CIVITAS Policy Note [27] prepared by European Union, which
was part of the documentation for the program “Strategy for equality between women and men 2010-2015”. They
conducted a survey entitled “Attitudes on issues related to EU transport policy” in 2007, where it was found that
men more often use cars while women more commonly use both public transportation and walking. Men mostly
travel for business purposes and therefore cover longer distances, whilst women travel predominantly for purposes
such as shopping, escorting, or personal business, and therefore cover mostly short distances. This has inﬂuence
on the labor market, since women are more often forced to choose part time or low paid work so as to be located
closer to home. We can see this diﬀerence in the European statistics where according to Eurostat, in the EU-27,

4.1
Scope
The goal of this study is the analysis of human mobility with a focus on gender diﬀerences based on four key factors:
entropy, radius of gyration, number of new and number of unique places visited using a global large-scale data-set.
It will aim to to answer the following questions:
• Is there a diﬀerence in the overall mobility for women and men?
• Does the gender gap vary in diﬀerent countries?
• Does the gender gap exist across all age groups?
• Is the gender gap the same for rural and urban areas?
• What features are important to predict the four key factors?
• How does perception of security vary between genders?
• Is the perception of security inﬂuencing daily mobility?

Background Literature
This chapter will present an overview of the recent, relevant studies in the ﬁelds of gender inequality in mobility
and big data for tracking human mobility. It will include a short introduction to the physical modeling of social
phenomena, and an overview of the statistical analysis of human mobility. It will provide an introduction to topics
related to geo-referenced data (big data) on human mobility, urban mobility, socio-economic factors and mobile
phone position tracking. The ﬁnal part of the chapter focuses on socio-demographic indicators which could inﬂuence
human behavior and magnify the gender gap in mobility. The purpose of introducing this question is to deepen the
understanding of the whole issue. It is important to observe how economical factors can inﬂuence human behavior
on many levels. One of the considerations will be access to public transport, which should be not only seen as a
limitation in mobility, but as a big factor in how well humans can use the opportunities oﬀered them in big cities
to develop their potential. Understanding of all these socio-economical issues are necessary to be able to interpret
the results and make meaningful conclusions.
Key words used to ﬁnd the following literature are: human mobility, mobility patterns, gender gap in mobility,
tracked human mobility, human dynamics, urban mobility.
5.1
Modeling of social phenomena
The idea that much of the world’s phenomena are of statistical origin is so widely accepted that many disciplines
have merged with statistics, building separate disciplines on their own, for example statistical physics. Statistical
models are being used in interdisciplinary topics related to chemistry, medicine, information technology, computer
science, etc. Following this path, statistical methods began to emerge as a tool for analysing experiments con-
ducted in sociology and widely understood social sciences. From there the interest in using statistical modeling
in social phenomena rose exponentially [2]. Using models developed based on physical laws for understanding so-
cial dynamics, where basic variables are not particles but humans, and every individual interacts with a limited
number of peers, seemed to be very useful for scaled and universal sociological research. More advanced statistical
models could deepen understanding of human behavior and technological development, and help to scale up models.
The developing understanding of the quantitative properties in the collective characteristics of a large number of
people, for example birth and death rates or crime prediction, was a big step in the process of introducing statistics to
social sciences [3]. Building on top of that many researchers looked into interactions between individuals, especially
to understand phenomena related to common experience, mass reactions and group behaviour [1].
5.1.1
Pedestrian behavior
The behaviour of pedestrians has been studied using empirical methods since the 1950s [4]. As an important aspect
of crowd behaviour this can be used in such ﬁelds as emergency policy or marketing. The phenomena of collective
motion can be also observed in panic motion and vehicular traﬃc.
The very ﬁrst models to describe pedestrian motion were based on gas and ﬂuid ﬂow dynamics, with the diﬀerence
that individuals were assumed as a granular structure which occupy a volume that cannot be penetrated by others [5]
, [6]. Models used discrete time and space, with areas divided into cells. The movement of a individual pedestrian is
a biased random walk; models can be computed eﬃciently but they do not describe precisely the dynamics observed
in reality. The complex phenomenology is limited mostly because of space discretization. Therefore, models where
individuals move in continuous space begin to play an important role [7],[8].
5.1.2

Mobility at the level of individuals
With increasing accuracy of tracking individuals it was found that human trajectories are comparable to some
animals, for example albatross movements [14] or those of monkeys [15]. All of these ﬁndings used the fact that
human trajectories can be approximated by a Levy ﬂight — a random walk for which step size follows a power-law
distribution (y = xk) with the exponent k ≤2. Besides some controversial assumptions, the main ﬁnding was that
both humans or animals travel only short distances most often, but occasionally travel very long distances. Other
similarities were found in the collective movement of pedestrians showing synchronization [16], similar to ﬂocks of
birds [17].

5.3
Models for human mobility
With the rise in interest in human mobility, various models were proposed to accurately describe individual mobility
patterns or general population ﬂows. Most of them used distance as a primary factor to analyse movements and
interactions between places. Those models started to play an important role in understanding socio-economical
questions. With the development of more sophisticated mathematical methods such as hidden Markov models and
diﬀusion processes gender and economical factors behind population movement were further analyzed.
5.3.1
Spatio-temporal scales
To model various types of movements, diﬀerent metrics were developed. One of the more general metrics is jump
length - the distance an individual travels in a given time period. It was especially convenient initially, when the
resolution of the measurements were not highly accurate. Regardless of the chosen resolution and constraints, jump
length is typically denoted as a euclidean distance between r(t) and r(t+dt) - i.e. the distance between locations
at time t and location after time dt. Models are used to show distributions of ∆r within particular populations, to
show probabilities that a random individual will travel a distance r from the starting point in the time dt. In ‘The
scaling laws of human travel’ [20] probabilities P(∆r) deﬁned for the trajectories of dollar bills and the distribution
of jump lengths was observed to follow a power law P(∆r) ∼r−(1+β) , with exponent β = 0.59 , independent of
the size (in population) of the entry point of a bank note (see Fig.5).

5.5
Gender diﬀerences in mobility patterns
In many everyday tasks, it is possible to notice gender diﬀerences. In the context of mobility, women more often
engage in multi-purpose, multi-stop trips where they focus on many chores. Taking into account various economical
and cultural aspects this could be explained by for example the fact that women are still on average less well paid
than men and are therefore forced to use public transport, instead of directly driving by car [23].
5.5.1
Urban mobility
Many cities were not designed with a focus on gender equality, therefore experiences for men and women whilst
moving around can be very diﬀerent. Lack of security and the fear of various types of violence in public areas
and when using public transportation are important factors which limit the everyday movement of women [24].
However, urbanisation simultaneously oﬀers possibilities to reduce this gap; many wealthy countries change the
design of cities to be more woman friendly, paying more attention for example to pregnant women’s needs. [23].
On the other hand, urbanisation can also increase inequalities by for example reinforcing geographical segregation.
Mobility could play an important role in reducing such segregation. Understanding the diﬀerences between genders
in urban mobility is key to a better understanding of the situation that many women cannot always fully beneﬁt
from the opportunities oﬀered them by cities.

5.6
Gender inequality index
Gender inequality is a commonly used measure for political purposes or research.
Since each country has it’s
own culture and history, it is rather challenging to ﬁnd a way of encompassing everything. Nevertheless there are
several worldwide rankings which capture the inequality of each country by chosen characteristics. One of the most
popular is the ”Global Gender Gap Report 2020” [30] written by The World Economic Forum, which focuses on

5.6.1
Global Gender Gap Report 2020
The World Economic Forum has been using the Global Gender Gap Report since 2006 in order to build a quantitative
base for the inequalities in Economic Participation and Opportunity, Educational Attainment, Health and Survival,
and Political Empowerment [30]. The goal is to deﬁne gender gaps, observe the change over the time and perform
the necessary actions to narrow it. In 2020 the report informed that there is still a 31.4% average gender gap that
remains to be closed globally. Although we can see positive improvement in 101 out of 149 countries from last year,
the most critical regions - the Middle East and North Africa would need 140 years to close the gap at the current
rate.

5.7.1
Safety perception
Although scores representing safety within a given location can be collected and analysed in reports such as the one
from the Ramboll Foundation, the whole phenomenon is complex and more diﬃcult to understand. In the studies
conducted by the faculty of Spatial Sciences at the University of Groningen [42] the research team investigated
perceptions of safety in Jakarta and Kuala Lumpur through: 1. Spatial conﬁguration and socio-cultural constructs
as external factors and 2. Individual characteristics as internal factors. They used methods of combining on-street
surveys of 383 participants, qualitative analysis of video recordings of the chosen neighbourhoods, and space syntax
analysis of eight street network models. The on-street surveys reported the participant’s perceptions of safety when
walking on a particular street. The videos provided the context of how the streets were used, inferring the socio-
cultural constructs of the study area. The computational analysis provides insights on how spatial conﬁgurations
correlate with safety perception.

One of the key ﬁndings is that understanding of safety perception in relation to mobility needs to be investi-
gated on a multidisciplinary level and by combining various factors. Most of the research, especially for urban
planing, focuses on a limited part of the phenomenon and therefore provides fragmented perspectives and inade-
quate solutions. As an example, in Jakarta and Kuala Lumpur, separated train carriages for women are provided
on the commuter line, but safety during the walk to the train station and from train to destination is not considered.
The main ﬁnding was again that women are more likely to report negative perceptions of safety as compared
to men. In Jakarta, an internal-external mechanism is related with individual risk acceptance and expectations
of risky behaviour from other user groups (such as pedestrians being wary of motorcyclists), and how users of
diﬀerent modes negotiate and share the space. In Kuala Lumpur, an external-internal mechanism is seen where an
individual’s expectation of presence of other pedestrians and the perceived image of the area dictates travel choice.
Furthermore, in Jakarta, the perceptions of safety are signiﬁcantly correlated with walkability potential, i.e. the
more walkable an area is the more likely respondents reported a non-negative perception of safety.
5.7.2
Predicting safety perception
Research in social sciences has shown the existence of a correlation between the visual appearance of a city’s neigh-
borhoods and the behavior of pedestrians or liveliness of the area [44], [45]. For a long time, due to the limited
amount of data mostly collected in street surveys, the research was limited to chosen areas and did not provide
an overview. More recently, however, online data collection methods where humans evaluate images, using experts
[48] or crowdsourcing [47], have increased the availability of urban perception data. Although this increased the
range of research, the resolution was still limited (even the most ambitious crowdsourcing eﬀorts [47] were limited
to less than 10 images per square mile). This was a big constraint in developing a deep understanding of the human
perception of safety. In the paper ‘Streetscore - Predicting the Perceived Safety of One Million Streetscapes’ [46]
team demonstrated a prediction algorithm trained using generic image features and the scores of perceived safety
from a crowdsourced study. They evaluated that the typical high scoring image contains for example: suburban
houses, trees or lawns; while the typical low scoring image contains empty streets, fences, and industrial buildings.
They used image feature extraction to quantify the variance of the features and associate their perceived safety.
The ﬁnal output was a tool allowing creation of perception maps for 27 cities across the United States, using images
densely sampled from Google Streetview, at 200 images per square mile.
Another computational method was used for ﬁnding the correlation between liveliness and safety perception in
research described in ‘Are Safer Looking Neighborhoods More Lively? A Multi-modal Investigation into Urban
Life’ by a research team from Italy [49]. Using a convolutional neural network trained on a data-set of Google
Street View images and images scored using a crowd-sourced visual perception survey they estimated scores of

7.4
Random Forest
The general idea behind a random forest is to build multiple decision trees and merge them together to generate
greater accuracy and stability [63]. The method is based on training several decision trees in parallel with bootstrap-
ping (random sampling with replacement) followed by aggregation; together called bagging. This method ensures
that individual decision trees are trained in parallel on various subsets of the training data-set, using diﬀerent
subsets of the available features. Each individual decision tree in the random forest is therefore unique, and so the
overall variance is reduced.
7.4.1
Hyper-parameter Tuning
To optimise the performance of a random forest, hyper-parameters like number of decision trees in the forest and
the number of features considered by each tree when splitting a node can be tuned. Usually, a trial and error
approach is required initially, since the most optimal hyper-parameters are unknown at the beginning of analysis
[64]. In order to ﬁnd the best combination of parameters, the model evaluates performance on a training set. This
can lead to over-ﬁtting, and so cross validation is used. A common method for cross validation is K-Fold CV, where
the data is split into K number of subsets, or folds. The model is then iteratively ﬁt K times, each time training
the data on K-1 of the folds and evaluating on the Kth fold. In this work there are 100 iterations, with 3 folds, each
time using diﬀerent model settings. Then a comparison of all models is made, and selected parameters are applied
to train on the full training set, before being evaluated on the testing set.
The Random Search Cross Validation package in Scikit-Learn can be used as a ﬁrst approach for deﬁning a range
of hyper-parameters. In this method a grid of hyper-parameter ranges is generated. Random combinations of the
parameters from the grid are chosen and evaluated on the K-folds from the training data set. Most often the grid
contains ranges for the following parameters:
• Number of trees in the forest.
• Max number of features considered for splitting a node.
• Max number of levels in each decision tree.
• Min number of data points placed in a node. before the node is split.
• Min number of data points allowed in a leaf node.
• Method for sampling data points (with or without replacement).
After choosing a narrow range of parameters through Random Search, Grid Search with Cross Validation can then
be used, where it is possible to explicitly specify every combination of settings to try.

second number in the new list. The algorithm repeats this process n times.

9.2
Random forest
A random forest is an ensemble learning method for classiﬁcation or regression based on constructing many decision
trees and by using bootstrapping; ﬁnding the class that is the mode of the classes (classiﬁcation), or the mean
prediction (regression) of the individual trees. It can be used for ﬁnding feature importance and deﬁning if particular
variables have an inﬂuence on a given dependent variable. In the following study the random forest is performed
not with the goal of obtaining precise predictions, as it is often used in research, but instead to ﬁnd out if gender
plays a signiﬁcant role in building a model for entropy. For each variable two models will be built: one with gender
as one of the variables and one without. By comparing the mean square error obtained, it can be deﬁned if gender is
a signiﬁcant variable. The following sub-chapter will present the methodology used in this study and the obtained
results.
To build the model related to entropy the following variables were used:
• Urbanization level, height, and weight per person were taken directly from the data set.
• Age was calculated from the year of birth given in the data set.
• Country and gender was converted to a numerical representation without an arbitrary ordering (one-hot
encoding).
• Scores from ‘Gender Inequality Index’ were added.
• The index from ‘Global Gender Gap Report 2020’ for Education, Economy, Politics and Health per country
was added.
• The median entropy per user was set as a target (also known as the label).
The data was then converted into arrays and split into training and testing sets with a ratio 1:4.
Using this data three models were built: a base model without tuning of the hyper parameters, a model with
random search training and a ﬁnal model where the parameters were tuned by a grid search with cross validation.
For the second and third model the tuned parameters were:

11.1
Results
It was shown that for all four key factors (entropy, radius of gyration, number of unique places visited, and number
of new places visited) the distributions for women and men diﬀer. In Figure 23 a comparison of the medians for
entropy was shown, with a clear indication that women have a lower median. Similar results for radius of gyration,
number of new locations and number of unique locations are shown in the appendices A,B and C respectively. It is
important to observe how various variables can inﬂuence human mobility, and so all four key factors were analysed
with respect to age group, height and weight, urbanization level, country of residence etc. To discover more about
the socio-economical aspects inﬂuencing mobility, data from both the Human Development Index and Gender In-
equality Index was included. The main conclusion drawn was the existence of a visible gender gap, meaning that
gender is a signiﬁcant factor inﬂuencing mobility. However, mobility remains a complex phenomenon, with further
detailed analysis required for a more complete understanding.
Statistical tools including linear models and random forests were used, in order to quantify the importance of factors
inﬂuencing mobility. For the linear model, data was grouped both by country and the median of a given factor per
county. Across each of the key factors diﬀerent variables were shown as being of most importance. As an example;
for the case of entropy, economical variables showed most importance, whereas for radius of gyration it was apolit-
ical variables, and for the number of places visited it was simply age. To compliment and further investigate these
correlations, random forests were constructed using data grouped into individual users. At ﬁrst gender appeared
not to be inﬂuential, but after careful analysis it was shown that height and weight were being used as proxy for
gender, which was found to have inﬂuence across all four key factors.
Mobility is inﬂuenced by many factors; our lifestyle, access to public transport or cars, the country and culture
we live within. Among those, safety also seems to play an important role. In Chapter 10, the inﬂuence of per-
ception of safety on the gender gap in mobility was investigated. It was shown that women more often consider
safety when walking, and always chose safer areas to rest than men. The diﬀerence varies with time-of-day, but in
Figure 80 it can be clearly seen that the medians of safety scores are generally higher for women. These consid-
erations are likely to play an important role in mobility, as they can place constraints on where people are able to go.
Overall the work proves the existence of and quantiﬁes the gender gap in mobility across the four key factors:
entropy, radius of gyration, number of new places and number unique places. The phenomenon of mobility cannot
be explained simply through statistical analysis, and would require more focused research across a broad range of
ﬁelds including political and social sciences. Nevertheless the work in this thesis shows that a phenomenon which
until now had only been considered in local and speciﬁc cases is in fact global and broadly signiﬁcant.
11.2
Novel work
Mobility as a phenomenon is not a new topic for research, however this work has aimed to expand on existing
understanding in multiple ways. This research is focused speciﬁcally on the inﬂuence of a gender gap, in contrast
to previous studies which have included myriad factors, of which gender is just one. This focus allowed for a more
thorough understanding of the issue of gender diﬀerence, and to observe it in a larger context. Another novelty
of the work presented here is the scale of the data used as an input. With the rapidly increasing amount of data
which can be collected from users, this research was able to be undertaken on a global scale, with input from a
wide range of geographically varied locations, and as such produced results within a global overview with very high
resolution. Further additional elements of this work when compared with previous studies include: the inclusion
of data from the Human Development Index and the Global Gender Gap Index, considerations of the height and
weight of participants, and a division of the cases for walking and resting when considering perception of safety.

11.3
Bias
Despite the huge amount of available data, and eﬀorts to include a diverse range of ages, urbanisation levels,
countries etc., there remains inherent biases. Most obviously is that the majority of data was collected through
users mobile phones, which immediately constrains users to those at a certain ﬁnancial level and living in a particular
way. With the progression of globalisation, it is expected that cultures become more universal and lifestyles more
similar across the world. Nevertheless it must be remembered that there exists many other groups of people, such
as those with disabilities excluding them from the use mobile phones, people living in traditional ways (for example
the Amish), who have decided to abstain from much of modern technology, people living in remote areas without
access to those technologies, people without the ﬁnancial possibility of inclusion, and many more. Eﬀorts to better
include these groups in wider studies could add a lot of understanding to the phenomenon of mobility.
11.4
Validity
A complete validation of the kind of results presented here is not possible, however comparisons to the previous,
smaller scale studies can be made in order to verify conclusions. Many previous studies have suggested that women
are more limited in mobility when compared to men. This has mostly been attributed to diﬀering daily schedules,
tasks etc. The larger number of women in lower paid work or who do not work have also been given as inﬂuencing
factors. Lastly, the often increased vulnerability of women on public transport or in public spaces has been shown
to have an eﬀect. It was shown previously that all of these factors result in both a smaller radius of gyration and a
smaller number of visited places for women, which agrees with the conclusions drawn from this analysis.
11.5
Limitations
The fundamental limitation of this work is the complexity of human mobility as a phenomenon, making it very
diﬃcult to capture suﬃciently in a short research project. Statistical analysis can provide a high level overview,
but it does not allow for an understanding of the complete phenomenon, nor captures all of the numerous nuances.
In addition, when working with data related to humans, individual behaviors must be accounted for if a complete
picture is to be provided, and the use of statistical analysis by it’s very nature excludes this.
It is important to highlight that gender was self reported, which can bring some inaccuracy. In addition gender was
assumed to be a binary variable, which does not reﬂect the real world precisely. An extension of these deﬁnitions
can be considered for further work.
11.6
Further work
A full understanding of the gender gap in mobility is important and valuable for reducing gender inequalities more
generally. Therefore further work should include a wider and more complete analysis. For example, in understanding
the perception of safety it would be valuable to track people’s movements, so that we could observe how women
and men design their chosen paths, or look more closely at the design of lighting along walking routes or at bus
stops, to ﬁnd out what could be improved. For understanding which factors play a role in mobility for a given user
variables such as their education, work, and salary should be added. In this way more limitations on mobility could
be understood. Another interesting research avenue would be to examine causation relations, for example can lower
education levels be partially described by limitations in mobility (e.g. to get from a village to the city) or rather is
limited mobility a result of low education. Personally, I would like to continue work on this topic with collaboration
from the social sciences, in order to add a deeper understanding of human behavior both as individuals and within
a society. Further consultation with for example urban planning departments could be used for understanding how
gender diﬀerences inﬂuence the design of the places in which we live, work, and socialise.
11.7
Summary
Understanding diﬀerences in experiences between genders has in recent years become an important objective across
many disciplines. Quantitative analysis of the gender gap in mobility can help to direct both political decisions
and urban designing trends towards building a more equal world. Mobility limitations should not be viewed only
as diﬃculty with transport, but as a barrier which limits access to opportunities. This is therefore a fundamental
issue, since the promotion of for example university places or job positions for women are only superﬁcial gestures,
if access due to mobility remains unequal. The possibilities which have been created in the last decade through the
rapid advancement of computing power and data science techniques mean that quantitative evidence for the gender
