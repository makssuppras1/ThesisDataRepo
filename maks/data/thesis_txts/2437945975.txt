Abstract
In this master project, a single-family house designed for NZEB target and
equipped with water-based ﬂoor heating and cooling system was studied. By
dynamic simulations of the building in Advanced IDA-ICE, the performance
of self-regulating ﬂoor heating strategy was compared to those of conventional
control systems, which comprised of individual room controllers such as dead
band thermostats and pulse-width modulation controllers. The performance
was assessed by taking into consideration energy consumption, thermal envi-
ronment and return water temperature as criteria for the comparison. Second
part of the project concerned cooling, where a self-regulating ﬂoor cooling
system was designed for meeting Category II in EN 12521. Consequently, a
preliminary analysis of a ground loop heat exchanger, which was supposed to
be coupled to the ﬂoor cooling system, was conducted using COMSOL Multi-
physics.
The dynamic simulations demonstrated that a self-regulating ﬂoor heating
with variable supply water temperature based on outdoor reset function, plus
a weather compensation controller and two diﬀerent mixing loops, consumed
6.1% more than a system with pulse-width modulation controllers. Thermal
environment was also slightly worse with 93.7% of time in Category II against
95.5%. However, the remarkable advantage of the self-regulating system was
the return temperature, which was estimated to be about 7◦C lower. As a con-
sequence, through an optimisation process, a ﬂoor heating system with pulse-
width modulation controllers was simulated with variable water temperatures
around 25◦C and a faster response. The system reduced its energy-weighted
return temperature down to 23.5◦C, without signiﬁcantly eﬀecting thermal
comfort.
The investigation of self-regulating ﬂoor cooling indicated that without
using venting by opening window, the needed space cooling to avoid overheat-
ing was 3 times higher compared to a system with both means. Furthermore,

venting and ﬂoor cooling at supply water temperature of 23◦C was already
suﬃcient for achieving Category II at least 95% of the time. Finally, the anal-
ysis of the ground loop heat exchanger showed that a total pipe length of 74
m would be needed to meet the demand and that the average cooling power
would be decreased by 40% after 10 years of operation.
In conclusion, it can be said that the ﬂoor heating system performed bet-
ter in terms of energy and thermal comfort when controlled by pulse-width
modulation room controllers, and therefore, such control strategy is recom-
mended. By optimising supply temperature and controller response, the issue
of high return temperature can be solved without compromising thermal com-
fort. With respect to cooling, ﬂoor cooling should be used as complementary
mean to avoid too high energy consumption. In this way, a ground loop heat
exchanger should be capable of providing enough free cooling. However, this
system needs to be properly designed and operated.

loop heat exchanger

CHAPTER1
Introduction
1.1
Background
In order to counteract climate change and move towards a fossil fuel-free
society, the European Union had established ambitious goals for reducing the
energy used in buildings, which accounts for around 40% of the total energy
consumption in Europe [25]. According to EPBD (Energy and Performance of
Buildings Directive), all new buildings should be nearly-zero energy buildings
(NZEB) by the end of 2020 and all new public buildings should be nearly-zero
energy buildings by 2019 (European Commission 2010). A NZEB is deﬁned as
a building that has a very high energy performance, in which the low amount
of energy required should be covered to a very signiﬁcant extent by energy
from renewable sources, including energy from sources produced in situ or
nearby.
Figure 1.1: Deﬁnition of Nearly-Zero Energy Building
However, the Member States are responsible for the practical application
of the deﬁnition stated of NZEB and have to document this in their national
plans. In last decade, Denmark has tried to guide Europe, by setting the re-
quired energy frame for NZEB to 20 kWh/m2 per year for residential buildings
and 25 kWh/m2 for public buildings (taking into account energy for heating,
cooling, ventilation and domestic hot water). Therefore, to meet these strict
==PAGE:16==

requirements, very energy-eﬃcient heating and cooling systems are required.
An example of these systems is ﬂoor heating and cooling systems. It is well
known that low temperature heating is beneﬁcial in terms of eﬃciency of heat
generations (boilers, heat pumps, etc.), and in addition for ﬂoor heating, it
creates an uniform thermal indoor environment [21]. However, when it comes
to ﬂoor heating (cooling) systems, which have a signiﬁcant thermal mass, the
delay time between the heat supply and the response in indoor temperature is
consistent. For this reason, the convectional control techniques cannot adapt
to dynamic changes. In regard to this matter, the concept of self-regulating
ﬂoor heating (cooling) becomes interesting.
Low temperature heating (cooling) systems that utilise large internal areas
to supply energy require surface temperature of only few degrees above the
desired room temperature. Therefore, even a small diﬀerence between ﬂoor
surface and room temperature results in a large potential for self-regulation of
the heat supply to the room. For well-insulated houses with heating demand
of 10 to 20 W/m2, the self-regulating eﬀect corresponds to 30-90% of the aver-
age heat ﬂux [23]. Moreover, such system operates entirely without any active
control system, whereas a typical ﬂoor heating system today use thermostatic
control systems that regulate the mass ﬂow of warm water to each individual
room. The cost of sophisticated temperature sensors, valves and so on are sig-
niﬁcant in comparison with the cost for the entire ﬂoor heating system. Such
value in some cases is estimated to be 25-40% of the total investment cost for
heavy ﬂoor heating [16].
In regards to cooling, if a high-insulated envelope certainly reduces the
space heating demand, it might create problems with overheating during sum-
mer or when the solar gains are consistent. When passive means like venti-
lation by window openings or shading are not suﬃcient, mechanical cooling
is required. Now, since ﬂoor system is a high-temperature cooling system, it
yields higher eﬃciency for reversible heat pump and chillers. Especially, as
the ground temperature often is around 10 ◦C over the year, it is possible to

2.1
Control strategies of ﬂoor heating systems
In the following section, the recent technology and research concerning control
strategies of ﬂoor heating systems are presented.
2.1.1
Conventional controllers
In general, the common method for operating a ﬂoor heating system is the
continuous mode of room temperature. In such mode, the heat ﬂux to the
concrete slab is regulated to maintain the room temperature within chosen
limits.
These modes are on-oﬀcontrol and PI (proportional and integral)
[6]. In Denmark, most controllers for ﬂoor heating are on-oﬀcontrollers with
ﬁxed temperature [18]. Depending on whether the system supplying heat to
the system can deliver water at an appropriate temperature between 35 and
45◦C, either a direct supply from the manifold is used or a shunt is used which
prevents too high ﬂow temperatures. Even though such high supply temper-
atures are probably not needed in well-insulated dwellings, occupants often
complain of too low temperature in the rooms. [33].
When looking at existing solutions on the market, return limiter type
FJVR by Danfoss automatically controls the return temperature from ﬂoor
heating pipes. This limiter works as a self-acting P-band regulator. Other so-
lutions provided by Danfoss are control strategies that use either thermostat
in a reference room or individual thermostat in each room (such as BasicPlus,
FH-CWx or WT-x 1), which work with PID (proportional, integral and diﬀer-
ential) controllers [2]. Other thermostats (programmable thermostat) allow
the user to setback the room temperatures when the room is not in use to im-
prove eﬃciency and reduce operating costs. More advanced PID thermostats
can measure and memorise the response time of the heating system and com-
1www.heating.danfoss.com
==PAGE:20==
2 State of the art
pensate for slow response of the system by turning the system on earlier in
order to reach the room set-point at the desired time [3].
In 2001, Olesen emphasised the advantage of using both central and indi-
vidual room control. The central control regulates the supply water tempera-
ture according to the outside temperature (outdoor reset controller) whereas
the room control regulates the water ﬂow rate or water temperature individu-
ally for each room, according to the room set-point [22]. Jesper et al. (2003)
also suggested to use a variable supply temperature based on outdoor temper-
ature rather than a constant temperature ﬂow [18].
Nevertheless, the most recent ﬂoor heating systems in buildings use pulse-
width modulation controller (PWM). PWM is an on/oﬀcontroller strategy
in which the time duration ’ON’ (valve open) is modulated. This duration is
called duty cycle. The duty cycle is calculated based on certain time period
(e.g. 1 hour) and signal received from the room sensor. This signal depends
on the type of sensor used, such as P (proportional) or PI (proportional +
integral). If, for example, at the beginning of the cycle (tick time) the signal
received by the PWM controller is 0.5, it means that the valve will open for
50% of time within that cycle. This strategy implies a small supply of heat to
the slab for more or less short periods according to the room temperature.
It can also be mentioned that more advanced PWM controller for ﬂoor
heating system adjust the duty cycle with one cycle, providing a better re-
sponse and overall performance.
2.1.2
Self-regulating strategy
Peter Weitzmann simulated the temperature during cooling season in oﬃce
buildings integrated with TABS, which intrinsically imply the self-regulation
eﬀect. In his research, two diﬀerent control strategies were applied: constant
water supply all day and circulation of water only during night. Despite the
fact that the two strategies yielded very small diﬀerences in indoor temper-
ature, the conclusion was that the system was able to keep the oﬃce with
comfortable thermal environment [34]. Moreover, in his PHD’s thesis, Weitz-
mann mentions that buildings with low energy consumption might have a very
large degree of self control, yielding no need for control systems. This would
decrease the price of the ﬂoor heating sytem [33].

2.1.3
Predictive control method
In the matter of predictive control method (PCM), several research have been
conducted over the years. In the following, some of them are presented.
In 1999, Lee et al. addressed the problem of delay time of ﬂoor heating
(Ondol heating) by proposing a predictive control by means of artiﬁcial neu-
ral network, and it concluded that it was performing better than a normal
2-position on/oﬀcontrol. The predictive control determined the proper hot
water supply on/oﬀtime (pump operation) by learning the thermal charac-
teristics of apartment buildings and the availability of control parameters [19].
In another study, predictive control of intermittently operated ﬂoor heating
system was developed by Cho et al. (2003). Through computer simulations
and experiments, the energy performance of a conventional control strategy
was compared to that of a predictive control one [6]. First step of the method
was to estimate the hourly outdoor air temperature based on expected maxi-
mum and minimum temperature (Th and Tl) and their time-of-day occurrence
(th and tl), respectively. Secondly, the rate of the boiler heat output had to be
determined. Consequently, a heat distribution pattern was assumed. Then,
an empiric formula of the building heating demand in function of the out-
door temperature was obtained by a simulation program. Finally, with all
8
==PAGE:23==
2.1 Control strategies of ﬂoor heating systems
the aforementioned information, the heat supply cycle was calculated. The
investigation showed that the predictive control strategy was able to save be-
tween 10% and 12% during winter and even more during mild-weather days
compared to a conventional control strategy.
Privara et al. applied a predictive controller in order to control the tem-
perature of a university building, which was able to take into account both
weather forecast and thermal capacity of the building. The controller was
tested during the heating season and showed an increase of energy saving of
17-24% compared to a conventional controller, still maintaining the heating
comfort of the building. In [26], Sasic Kalagasidis A. stated that several years
of experience in Swedish buildings showed that by using predictive controls
with forecast control of building heating systems, energy saving of 10-20%
could be achieved while maintaining stable indoor temperatures.
In 2011, Karlsson et al. developed a predictive control method to deter-
mine the optimal supply ﬂuid temperature in the case of concrete embedded
water-based ﬂoor heating [17]. The method consisted of obtaining the forth-
coming supply water temperature through a numerical optimisation based on
a prediction of the upcoming heat demand. The method relied on an elemen-
tary response function, which was obtained either from a numerical control
volume model (Simulink Model) or a simpliﬁed 2-node lumped model. He
concluded that, ﬁrstly, the accuracy of the simpliﬁed model was surprisingly
good compared to the detailed numerical method, and secondly, that the op-
timised supply temperature was relatively steady over time and experienced
shift only when there were rapid changes in heat gains. It also proved, that
disregard from rapid changes, by applying an average value of the supply tem-
perature, the indoor temperature deviated approximately 1◦C compared to
the optimised case.
Indeed, in 2008, Karlsson had simulated the performance of the self-regulating
method with either a constant supply temperature and a variable supply tem-
perature (predictive control method based on the past 4 days) for a detached
single-family house. He had concluded that the solution with constant supply
temperature had performed better from a thermal comfort point of view in
comparison to the solution with pre-calculated supply temperature [15].

2.2
Ground loop heat exchanger
Since ground temperature at a certain depth remains nearly constant over
the year, there is huge potential for cooling and heating. A ground loop heat
exchanger consists of pipes, mostly horizontal, immersed in the soil at relative
short depth (e.g. 2-4 m) from the surface. Such system is therefore diﬀerent
from a borehole, in which the pipes are vertically placed and reach consider-
able lengths. However, both systems are usually coupled to a heat pump for
increasing its eﬃciency. Several researches can be found on this topic. In the
following only few will be mentioned.
Florides et al. described several types of ground heat exchanger. Thanks
to a literature review, they recommended a minimum depth of 1.5-2 meters,
and a pipe spacing of minimum 30 cm. They ﬁnally indicated that an hori-
zontal loop should have 35-60 m per kW of heating or cooling [11].
In [20], Naili et al. conducted experiment of an horizontal ground loop
heat exchanger in Tunisia. It was found that a loop of 25 m buried at 2 m
was able to cover 38% of the total cooling demand of a tested room of 12 m2
ﬂoor area. Though, such heat exchanger was coupled to a heat pump.
In 2006, Esen et al. investigated the eﬀect of depth of pipe trenches in the
heating season. Pipes were placed at 1 and 2 meters, which yielded an energy
eﬃciency of ground-coupled heat pump of 2.5 and 2.8, respectively. Then, by
means of numerical simulation, it was found that the exergy eﬃciency was
considerably decreased by increasing the reference outdoor temperature [10].

Methodology
The performance of self-regulating ﬂoor heating system was determined by
comparison with other control strategies.
The criteria for the comparison
were the following three:
• energy consumption (space heating and cooling)
• thermal comfort (hours below set-point, overheating, etc.)
• return temperature from manifold and/or from ﬂoor heating zone circuit
Return temperature was also considered in the comparison because lower
values are extremely important from the perspective of using renewable sources
such district heating and heat pumps.
To estimate the performance of the ﬂoor heating/cooling system, dynamic
simulations were used.
The main simulation tool used to conduct the in-
vestigation was IDA Indoor Climate and Energy (IDA-ICE). Subject of the
investigation was a single-family house which met the NZEB requirement for
Denmark. This building was previously designed as assignment for the course
11116 - Sustainable Buildings at DTU, and it was used as starting point for
the investigation. The project was basically divided in three diﬀerent parts:
comparison of ﬂoor heating model of IDA-ICE, investigation of performance
of control strategies and ﬁnally, analysis of ﬂoor cooling with ground loop heat
exchanger. More details are provided in the following sections.
3.1
Validation of ﬂoor heating system
Before modelling the controllers, it was necessary to assess whether the IDA-
ICE model of the ﬂoor heating/cooling system was fairly suitable for the inves-
tigation. Since the IDA-ICE ﬂoor construction consists of a one-dimensional
==PAGE:28==
3 Methodology
simpliﬁed model, it was decided to compare its results with more detail simu-
lation tools, Heat2 and COMSOL. The ﬂoor heating system of only one room
was considered. Firstly, a detailed steady-state simulation in Heat2 permitted
to determine the heat transfer coeﬃcient between average water temperature
and average temperature of the concrete slab. Then, by means of COMSOL, a
3-D model of the ﬂoor heating loop for one room was created, which provided
information on the concrete slab dynamic behaviour and outlet temperature.
Results were then used for further analysis in the project.
3.2
Investigation
After comparing the ﬂoor heating system, the controller strategies were tested
in Advanced IDA-ICE. Advanced IDA-ICE diﬀers from its standard mode due
to the possibility of creating objects, making diﬀerent combination of default
objects, etc. In general, it is therefore possible to work with a larger amount
of parameters and variables, as well as post-processing more detailed informa-
tion.
The following control strategies were included:
1. dead-band thermostat
2. ideal PI controller (PI)
3. pulse-width modulation controller (PWM)
4. self-regulating with constant supply water temperature
5. self-regulating with variable supply water temperature
All controllers, except for PWM, could be found as default objects in Ad-
vanced IDA-ICE. However, with regards to PWM, the controller object had
to be created by means of another programme: IDA Translator. This tool
allowed to change the code of a predeﬁned object of IDA-ICE and obtain a
controller with the desired functionality.
Consequently, the ﬁrst approach of the investigation was to fully under-
stand how the controllers worked. To do so, the ﬂoor heating system of only
one room was considered. The system was then run considering three diﬀerent

scenarios, which represented few days in winter and spring. The same system
was then operated for the entire heating season. The next step was to test the
ﬂoor heating system on the entire building. Consequently, the ﬁndings of the
comparison were used to optimise the system, with aim of fulﬁlling the three
criteria previously mentioned.
With regards to ﬂoor cooling, the self-regulating system was simulated

CHAPTER5
Model comparison
In this chapter the IDA-ICE model was compared to more detail programmes:
Heat2 and a 3-D simulation tool, COMSOL Multiphysics. The chapter was
divided in four main sections.
In the ﬁrst section, a Heat2 simulation of
ground/ﬂoor construction interface was presented, showing the actual temper-
ature and resistance of the soil during heating season. Secondly, Heat2 was
used to determine the heat transfer coeﬃcient from water to concrete slab
to use in the IDA-ICE model. Consequently, an analysis of the dynamic be-
haviour was conducted by comparing IDA-ICE with COMSOL. The aim was
to obtain information on how to model the ﬂoor heating system in IDA-ICE
in the best possible way, as well as understating possible limitations of the
simpliﬁed ﬂoor heating model.
For investigating the ﬂoor heating system mode, only the utility room was
considered.

5.2.3

Now, after calculating the heat transfer coeﬃcient Hwater−slab, it was necessary
to verify that the simpliﬁed 1-D model of IDA-ICE represented the dynamic
behaviour of the concrete slab with fair approximation, which was believed to
be very important for the control strategies. To do such veriﬁcation, COM-
SOL Multiphysics was used. This tool allowed to create a 3-D model of the
ﬂoor construction and ﬂoor heating system, in which the position of the pipes,
beginning of the loop and its length could be taken into account.
However, the COMSOL model was limited to the ﬂoor construction, with-
out the possibility of modelling room or room controller of the ﬂoor heating.

Because of that, the following solution was adopted: a dynamic simulation
of 2 days was run in IDA-ICE and its results (room temperature and volume
ﬂow rates) were used as boundary conditions in COMSOL.

In the following section, the functionality of each controller are described.
6.2
Functionality of controllers
6.2.1
Dead-band thermostat
The dead-band thermostat considers two limits, usually called "High" and
"Low" limit. The controller works as follow: when the measured room tem-
perature drops below the lower limit, the heating system is switched ON. The
system will be switched OFF only when the room temperature becomes larger
than the high limit. By looking at common products on the market, ther-
mostat controllers for ﬂoor heating system can have a dead-band of ±0.2◦C1
which, considering desired room temperature of 20◦C, means lower and higher
set-points of 19.8◦C and 20.2◦C.
Figure 6.6: Dead-band of thermostat
6.2.2
PI controller
PI controllers modulate the controller variable over time (e.g. water ﬂow rate).
However, these are usually not used to control the valve actuator of a ﬂoor
heating loop, due the fact that the valve takes time to change position, yield-
ing a bad response of the system. Nevertheless, it was decided to test the
performance of ideal PI controller which can control the mass ﬂow rate of the
system accordingly to the signal given by the controller itself.

6.6
Performance of control strategies for whole building
To conclude the chapter, simulations of heating season for the entire building
were performed for each controller strategy.
With regards to operating conditions, several considerations were done.
In single-family houses and apartments, unnecessary high ﬂow rates are ex-
perienced due to the fact that plummer and technicians set the pressure of
operation of pump to high levels (even 4-6 m), in order to ensure that rooms
are supplied with enough heat. So, volume ﬂow rates were established accord-
ing to Appendix C.3, which represented a poor operation of the system. This
yielded a total ﬂow of 4.3 m3/h and pressure drop of 4.8 m. Note: good
practice of design ﬂoor heating consists of determining the ﬂow rates based
on the heating load of the room and water temperature diﬀerence of 5-10◦C.

egy with variable supply temperature was considered (SRv*). The approach
of establishing outdoor reset curve for SRv* was to obtain an amount of hours
below set-point similar to those of conventional controllers (e.g. thermostat),
giving therefore the best energy-wise outcome. Instead, conventional control
strategies were still simulated with supply temperature of 40◦C.

set-point (corresponding to Category III) were considered as parameter to
assess the thermal environment of the NZEB [28]. Energy-weighted return

CHAPTER9
Discussion
Most relevant ﬁndings as well as methods used are discussed in the following
sections.
9.1
Floor heating/cooling model
The analysis of dynamic behaviour of the concrete slab in Section 5.2.3 indi-
cated that the one-dimensional model of IDA-ICE followed relatively closely
the detailed 3-D model created in COMSOL, especially for low supply tem-
peratures.
Nonetheless, the average heat ﬂux error for the case with high
supply temperature (40◦C) was overestimated in IDA-ICE by 12.1%. Now,
since Hwater−slab is equivalent to a resistance which is about ﬁve-ten times
smaller than the resistance given by the upper part of the ﬂoor construction,
the reason for such deviation is probably the heat transfer coeﬃcient h of the
ﬂoor surface. This coeﬃcient is accurately calculated in IDA-ICE considering
its convective and radiative part, as well as taking into account the heat bal-
ance of the room (air and surface temperatures). Instead, this coeﬃcient was
given as a reference value in COMSOL. Moreover, the heat ﬂux is strongly
depended on ﬂoor surface temperature, and indeed, the deviation between the
two models was quite small in the case of low temperatures (23◦C).
Concerning return temperatures, the diﬀerence was about 3-3.5◦C for the
case high supply temperatures. One could think that Hwater−slab might be the
cause, however, this would not explain why return temperatures in the other
case (23◦C) were more similar. Instead, another explanation could be that
the simpliﬁed 1-D formula of IDA-ICE (see Section 5.2) does not taken into
account the fact that the slab temperature is actually not constant over the
ﬂoor construction. On the other hand, the detailed model considers this local
"3-D eﬀect" of having at the end of the loop a lower slab temperature. In fact,
for constant operation of the ﬂoor heating (self-regulating case), the average
water temperature would become closer to its supply (or return) temperature,
==PAGE:124==
9 Discussion
yielding a more uniform slab temperature.
In conclusion, all the investigations were conducted using a heat transfer
coeﬃcient of 29.5 W/m2K which was calculated assuming laminar ﬂow, and
since considerably higher ﬂow rates were actually used in Chapter 6, it is
believed that the results from IDA-ICE fairly represented the a real ﬂoor
heating system.
9.2
Performance of control strategies
9.2.1
Case-scenarios
The investigation at room level in three diﬀerent case scenarios (A, B and C)
provided a clear understanding of controller functionality. In general, it can
be said that the self-regulating strategy performed better than conventional
controllers when the supply water temperature was optimised. In this matter,
the variable self-regulating strategy with outdoor reset controller was found
to be more valuable, giving some extra energy saving.
Overshooting of conventional controllers for case-scenario B (opening win-
dow) could be avoided by implementing an extra function for room sensors:
when a sudden temperature drop in the room occurs (which can only be caused
by opening window), the system could be turned oﬀfor a certain period (e.g.
10-20 minutes), after which, it could switch on or oﬀwhether the measured
temperature is still below the set-point or not. Such feature is already used
for radiators, thus it could be easily added to ﬂoor heating controllers.
Nevertheless, when the heating season was considered, self-regulation start-
ed showing limitations: either space heating or unmet heating hours were con-
siderably larger by constantly supply low water temperatures. Only by using
variable supply temperatures with a further optimised outdoor reset function
(SRv3), it was possible to achieve values comparable to dead band thermostat
and PWM controllers. It is reasonable to think that if a supply temperature
function can be easily found for few days, when outdoor temperature and so-
lar radiation vary within wider ranges over very long periods, determining an
optimal seasonal function is much more complex.
110
==PAGE:125==
9.2 Performance of control strategies
9.2.2
Performance of the building
After testing one room, the investigation was extended to the entire dwelling.
The variable self-regulating strategy ended up performing poorly with respect
to conventional controllers. Optimising one supply temperature for the whole
building (SRv*) resulted in 11.5% of extra energy compared to PWM and a
worse thermal environment with 7.1% of time in Category III.
Introducing a second mixing loop to supply rooms with smaller heating
loads gave an unexpected small improvement of only few percentages. Per-
haps, the small beneﬁt was due the fact that just two mixing loops were not
enough to solve the issue of having rooms with quite diﬀerent heating loads.
But, using more than two mixing loops (two manifolds) was not considered as
practical solution, because of their high cost. Finally, in the latest model, a
weather compensation controller was in charge of turning oﬀthe system based
on solar radiation and cloudiness. Such system still ended up consuming about
6.1% more compared to PWM.
One possible solution, which was unfortunately not considered in this
project, could be to design the building in a way that rooms had quite similar
heating loads. For example, in this speciﬁc case, one could have reduced the
ventilation rates of critical rooms down to acceptable values (0.3 l/s/m2 in BR
15), and see if the self-regulating strategy (supplying these critical rooms at
lower temperature) would have gained an extra beneﬁt compared to making
the change for PWM. Moreover, more complex solutions such as predictive
control method (PCM) were not considered due to their complexity, but it
is believed that they could contribute to increase the performance of the self-
regulating system.
Despite of what was just said, two other withdraws of a self-regulating sys-
tem have to be taken into account with regards to occupants. Firstly, users
would certainly want to turn on/oﬀthe system in speciﬁc rooms, and a cen-
tralised controller would not give this possibility. Secondly, common situation
in danish dwellings is to have very warm ﬂoor surface in the bathrooms, for
standing on in bare feet, and this would require an extra manifold only to
supply those zones, which would not be very convenient due to its cost as
previously mentioned.

9 Discussion
9.3
Return temperature
Return temperature has also to be considered in the overall evaluation of
the system. Results of Section 6.6 showed that the energy-weighted return
temperature went down to 21.82◦C with self-regulating strategy. Such return
temperatures would be very valuable for the district heating network and when
using heat pumps.
In the second part of the optimisation of the systems, supply temperature
for operating with PWM controller was lowered down to 25◦C. As expected,
this yielded more hours below the set-point, because of the lower surface tem-
perature that was reached. Though, thermal environment plot of Figure 7.8
indicated that a PWM with faster response, and a complementary outdoor
reset controller, would improve the thermal environment, giving only 1.5% of
diﬀerence compared to a system operated with 40◦C.
In this matter, it could also be relevant to test modern PWM controllers,
that adjust the duty cycle, with lower water temperatures of 25-28◦C. Such
controllers may eﬃciently perform even with lower temperatures. Moreover,
if one considers that users usually like to have a higher room set-point of
21-22◦C, the slightly worse thermal performance given by using lower supply
temperatures would still yield operative temperatures within Category II or
even Category I, and so suitable.
9.4
Floor cooling
Investigation of ﬂoor cooling systems (FC) indicated that overheating issues
could be solved by constant supply of water at either 19◦C or up to 23◦C if
venting was also applied. This last approach, however, gave a much more con-
tained space cooling of 4.5 kWh/m2 (3 times less energy compared to FC 19).
Such limitation could be quite problematic when venting by opening window
is not possible, like for example in case of buildings aﬀected by urban noise or
pollution.
The system was then tested and optimised to fulﬁl category II during
cooling season. What could be seen is that the thermal comfort was already
achieved (above 95%) for the model with venting and FC at 23◦C. When using
temperatures lower than 22◦C, the fraction of hours in Category III actually
grew, due to overcooling. This issue could be easily solved by implementing

an outdoor reset. Also, combined solutions of ﬂoor heating and cooling are
already available on the market, which both use PWM as control strategy.
Even though the performance of self-regulation was not compared in this case,
it is expected that ﬂoor cooling controlled by PWM would have consumed
somewhat less.
A further consideration can be done with regards to danish occupants who
usually desire room temperatures of 19-20◦C in master bedrooms at night

15-16◦C, and therefore, there would be necessity to verify if the ground loop

9.5
Ground loop heat exchanger
The investigation of the ground loop heat ex-changer was limited due to time
constraints and unexpected problems in obtaining convergent solution for a
detailed 3-D model of the loop in COMSOL. It is believed the 3-D model
would have been very beneﬁcial in understanding the capacity and limitation
of the system.
From this perspective, the simpliﬁed model only gave the
change to roughly estimate the cooling potential. Indeed, the results could be
aﬀected by the fact that the temperature decrease as the water runs through
the loop was not taken into consideration. Moreover, as already explained
in Section 8.5, the system was constantly operated over the year due to the
model and method limitations.
Nevertheless, the preliminary analysis indicated that there would be need
of approximately 74 m of pipes to ensure to meet the cooling demand for the
ﬁrst year. Such length would result in a trench length of around 19 meters,
which would easily feet in a typical plot area of 700 m2. This estimation was
somewhat on the conservative side since the inlet temperature corresponded
to the model with operation at 21◦C (the thermal comfort was already met
for supply water of 22◦C).
Anyway, after simulating for 10 years, the average cooling power over the
period was decreased by 40%, with a total energy balance of -942.6 kWh/m.
Such problem could be avoided by using a more constraint input of heat to
the ground, or for example by using a wider pipe spacing. In this ﬁeld, both
experimental work and simulation could useful for better understanding how

CHAPTER10
Conclusion
The ﬁnal investigation provided information on the performance of a self-
regulating ﬂoor heating system for a nearly-zero energy single-family house
over the entire heating season. Results showed that the most optimal self-
regulating system, with variable supply water temperature, ended up consum-
ing 6.1% more than a ﬂoor heating system with individual room controllers.
Thermal environment resulted to be about 93.7% of the time in Category II of
EN 15251, whereas a system with pulse-width modulation controllers yielded
95.5% in the same category. For these reasons, a self-regulating ﬂoor heating
system does not seem very promising, and thus individual room controllers
such PWM are recommended.
Nevertheless, signiﬁcant beneﬁt of self-regulating ﬂoor heating system was
with regards to return temperatures, which were decreased down to 21-22◦C,
considerably lower than those of other strategies. This indicated that low sup-
ply temperatures are required in order to decrease return temperatures. As
consequence, by optimising either controller settings of pulse-width modula-
tion controllers and using supply water temperature based on outdoor reset
function, it was possible to decrease the return temperature of almost 6◦C
(23.5◦C) while achieving slightly worse thermal comfort.
Moreover, self-regulating ﬂoor cooling was tested in the summer period.
A combination of venting by opening window and ﬂoor cooling with constant
supply of water at 22-23◦C was found to be the most convenient solution to
avoid overheating and also meet Category II in EN 15251. Such systems had
a space cooling between 4.5 and 9.4 kWh/m2. Consequently, a preliminary
analysis of a ground-loop heat exchanger conducted in COMSOL indicated
that a total pipe length of about 74 m would be needed. Its energy balance
over ten years resulted to be -942.6 kWh/m, with a decrease of average cooling
power of 40% at the end of the period. This indicates that the system has
to be properly designed and operated to avoid decrease of eﬀectiveness over
==PAGE:130==

time.
10.1
Further investigations
Future work could consider self-regulating ﬂoor heating/cooling system com-
bined with advanced predictive control methods, mainly to reduce the energy
consumption. Moreover, if one wants to apply self-regulating strategies, it
could be relevant to see how diﬀerent designs of the building aﬀect the perfor-
mance of system.
However, great eﬀort should be made on lowering supply/return tempera-
tures of conventional ﬂoor heating systems, since it would be signiﬁcantly ben-
eﬁcial when using renewable sources such as district heating and heat pumps.
There is need for documentation regarding performance of using modern pulse-
width modulation controllers at lower temperatures. From this perspective,
self-learning controllers could optimise the supply water temperature in time
while ensuring acceptable thermal comfort.
Lastly, investigation on ground loop heat exchanger combined with ﬂoor
cooling and used to pre-heat supply air of ventilation system during winter
is believed to be extremely relevant. The development of free and low cost
cooling systems could easily become a standard solutions for optimised NZEB.
Experimental work in a single-family house would provide documentation on
performance, feasibility and robustness of the system.

APPENDIXB
B.1
Modelling pulse-width modulation in IDA-ICE
Pulse-width modulation controller is not included in the standard/advanced
version of IDA-ICE. However, in the latest version 4.8, a time modulator is
available, called ’MODCTR’. In this default object, the duty cycle is calculated
by a P-controller (proportional), which sets the portion of time cycle when the
OutSig is 1 (valve opened). To deﬁne the proportional controller, two limits
(high and low) have to be given. The part of the original code regarding the
duty cycle can be seen in the following ﬁgure.
Figure B.1: Default object ’MODCTR’ - ﬁrst part of original code
==PAGE:142==
B
Now, this controller was not going to work as the PWM controller needed
for the investigation, since a P-controller cannot reach the room set-point and
only ﬂuctuate between two values (high and low limits). Therefore, it was
decided to change the original code of the object by means of IDA Translator,
which uses NMF (Neutral Model Format) language. The idea was to erase
the ﬁrst part of the code which was not necessary, and substitute the variable
which represented the duty cycle (OutSig) as an external variable (PIOutsign).
See Figure B.2. Consequently, this external variable was given by connecting
the MODCTR object to a default PI-controller from IDA-ICE.
Figure B.2: IDA Translator interface - new code of PWM controller
Once the object was created in IDA Translator, it was added to IDA-ICE
by using ’Extend IDA application’. A default PI-controller was then connected
to the PWM object. See Figure B.3. Finally, in order to make the water ﬂow
to the ﬂoor heating system controlled properly, the default valve object had to
be change with another valve ’Liquid ﬂow controller’ (Note: it can be found
in the right palette under Plant). See Figure B.4.
