Summary
Osteoarthritis (OA) is a multi-facted and heterogeneous disease affecting approximately 600 million
people worldwide. Despite extensive clinical trials, no disease-modifying OA drugs (DMOADs) have
yet succeeded. This leaves patients with no treatment options beyond pain management and lifestyle
modifications. Recent data-driven analyses of independent knee OA cohorts have identified distinct
molecular subgroups driven by Inflammation, Low Tissue Turnover, and Structural Damage, which
may respond differently to treatment, reinforcing the concept of treatable endotypes. These endotypes
were identified through clustering-based analyses of large panels of biochemical markers, which are im-
practical for routine clinical application due to the extensive patient recruitment efforts required and
high operational costs.
This thesis aimed to develop a predictive model for stratifying knee OA patients into these endotypes by
leveraging data from independent knee OA cohorts in past discontinued clinical trials. First, biomarker
data from two independent cohorts were merged to form a more representative, unified dataset. Then,
biomarker panel refinement was performed through iterative k-means clustering to identify a minimal
yet relevant set of biomarkers for use in prediction models to stratify the endotypes of OA. The per-
formance of the models was evaluated in an external OA study. Finally, endotypes were predicted in
prior treatment studies to assess whether endotypes that biologically match with the mode of action of
a drug exhibited differential treatment response, thereby supporting the concept of treatable endotypes
of OA.
The results demonstrated that a reduced biomarker panel of six biomarkers could classify OA endo-
types with 89% accuracy using multinomial logistic regression. In contrast, multiple linear regression
for predictions of overlapping endotypes exhibited high errors and poor model fit. The performance
of the multinomial logistic regression model suggested potential applications in optimizing clinical trial
recruitment. In an exploratory subgroup analysis of treatment effects within predicted endotypes from
three independent treatment studies, insignificant differential treatment effects were observed. How-
ever, the subgroup analyses showed indications of less disease progression and a slight improvement
in endotypes where the drug mode of action aligned with molecular characteristics of the endotypes,
supporting the concept of treatable endotypes in future clinical trials.
The findings highly depended on consistent biomarker measurement ranges, equipment stability, and
overall data quality. These factors currently limit the model’s generalizability and applicability in clin-
ical practice. Future research should focus on ensuring data quality and integrating predictive models
into clinical trial designs with proven treatment effects to facilitate direct validation of prediction models
for real-world trial recruitment applications.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Osteoarthritis (OA) is one of the most prevalent chronic joint diseases, affecting around 600 million
people worldwide[1, 2]. The disease is characterized by the progressive degeneration of joint cartilage,
leading to pain, stiffness, and reduced mobility.
However, the burden of OA extends beyond joint
deterioration, severely diminishing patients’ quality of life, increasing the risk of comorbidities, and
imposing substantial economic strain on healthcare systems and society in general[3].
Despite the
profound socio-economic impact of OA, therapeutic advancements for OA have been slow, with current
treatment options focusing only on pain relief and lifestyle modifications, as no disease-modifying OA
drugs (DMOADs) have yet shown clinical eﬀicacy. This lack of successful clinical trial outcomes is
mainly due to the heterogeneity of OA[1]. This has led to the emergence of hypotheses regarding distinct
subpopulations of OA, with heterogeneity attributed to different biological signals and etiologies[4]. The
biological signals of OA can be effectively captured by relevant biomarker measurements, unraveling
the molecular processes in the human body that lead to tissue turnover and inflammatory conditions,
accelerating disease progression[5].
Through decades, Nordic Bioscience has focused on substantial biomarker development for investi-
gating disease progression and changes in the extracellular matrix (ECM)[6] in a variety of diseases,
including OA[5]. The ECM is the network of proteins and molecules surrounding and supporting the
cells and tissue in the human body[6]. Understanding the changes in the ECM can lead to invaluable
insights into personalized medicine.
For OA, contributing factors beyond what can be measured through biomarkers are age, sex, body
mass index (BMI), and lifestyle choices[7]. Generally, females show higher prevalence, symptom severity,
and different biochemical outcomes than males[8, 9]. Such biological and clinical differences may influ-
ence disease-modifying treatments, emphasizing the consideration of sex-specific differences in patho-
physiology and patient stratification.
In the context of OA heterogeneity, the term endotype denotes subtypes of OA defined by specific
pathophysiological mechanisms. Recognizing such endotypes may prove vital in bridging the scientific
gaps in OA research and developing targeted treatment options tailored to these subgroups.
The field of OA has evolved considerably in recent years. Earlier research predominantly focused
on clinical and basic scientific aspects of disease progression. However, data generation and model-
ing advancements have shifted the emphasis toward bioinformatics, computational statistics, machine
learning, and various artificial intelligence (AI) tools, such as clustering and image analysis[7, 9, 10,
11].
Recent data-driven analyses of two independent heterogeneous OA cohorts - specifically, knee
OA (KOA) cohorts - have consistently revealed three distinct endotypes, namely Low Tissue Turnover,
Inflammation and Structural Damage[7, 12] through clustering of large panels of biomarkers. The en-
dotypes are driven by specific biological signals identified through serum- and urine-based biomarker
measurements. In the context of OA, biomarkers serve as quantitative measures reflecting underlying bi-

ological processes and alterations in the human body and are invaluable for endotype characterization[5].
The findings underline that the umbrella term ”osteoarthritis” fails to capture the entire spectrum of
distinct biological mechanisms that drive disease progression within the OA population. This lack of
molecular distinction may dilute the effectiveness of potential treatments in clinical trials if recruitment
is not optimized. However, applying this knowledge in clinical settings remains a challenge, as existing
methods for endotype identification, such as clustering, are resource-intensive and retrospective in the
sense that they require substantial recruitment of patients to acquire cohorts large enough for meaning-
ful clustering. This thesis leverages data from four independent studies to address these challenges and
explore the potential for targeted treatment through the predictive modeling of endotypes.
1.1
Endotyping
By quantifying biomarkers, scientists can elucidate the molecular mechanisms that give rise to ob-
servable clinical manifestations of a disease[13], namely endotypes. Endotyping may enable a more
nuanced understanding of disease heterogeneity by directly linking biological signals to clinical out-
comes. In other medical fields, such as asthma, endotyping has facilitated promising progression in
endotype-specific treatment options, with improved patient quality of life[14, 15], underscoring its clin-
ical potential. Within the realm of OA, the integration of endotyping emerged in 2021[16, 17] and has
since proven to be a critical area of research. By dissecting the complex molecular landscape of OA
captured by biomarkers, endotyping aims to identify subpopulations that may respond differently to
targeted interventions in clinical trial settings. Presently, clinical trial recruitment for OA relies on
radiological findings of the affected joint and patient-reported outcomes rather than molecular char-
acteristics. Biomarker measurements offer significant advantages over conventional medical imaging
for endotyping. While imaging techniques provide structural information, these techniques are costly,
time-consuming, and show diﬀiculties in detecting early-stage OA[18, 19]. Biomarker assays can capture
the dynamic molecular processes, offering a more direct and sensitive assessment of disease activity[18].
However, comprehensive biomarker panels can also be resource-intensive in cost and labor. Identify-
ing a smaller, representative biomarker subset that accurately reflects the endotypes of KOA makes it
possible to streamline the recruitment process for clinical trials without compromising the quality of
molecular distinction while facilitating eﬀicient and cost-effective patient stratification in a clinical trial
context. Therefore, this thesis includes an effort toward biomarker panel refinement to address these
challenges.
Until now, endotypes in KOA have been defined by analyzing biofluid data through computational
methods such as common factor analysis, k-means clustering, and deep learning-based methods[7, 11,
12, 16]. While computational cluster-based endotyping has been a promising tool for understanding
the heterogeneity of OA, it faces some limitations in clinical application. First, the methods are com-
plex and require skilled professionals for the interpretation of results. Second, current methods rely on
long-term patient recruitment to gather large-scale data sets and are, therefore, retrospective, making
them unsuitable for in-clinic use. Clustering methods are not designed for individual-level applications,
which may be critical for stratifying patients during trial recruitment and identifying patients whose
endotype aligns with the mode of action (MoA) of the given therapeutic drug. This highlights the need
for a clinically feasible approach to endotyping and the relevance of the work presented in this thesis.

1.2
Prediction Model for Endotyping
A key consideration in this thesis is the clinical feasibility of individual-level predictions of endotypes
through predictive modeling over population-based clustering. Integrating biomarker-based predictive
modeling for endotyping into routine medical workflows at the individual level may enable a faster
and more clinically feasible recruitment strategy for clinical trials. Predictive modeling offers an eﬀi-
cient alternative for clustering-based endotype stratification by combining patient-specific data. Such
data includes patient-specific clinical records, including sex, age, and BMI, which are known to have
a direct impact on KOA[7, 9]. For example, post-menopausal females exhibit a higher disease bur-
den, more extreme severity, and comorbidities[20], which can more eﬀiciently be captured by methods
that go beyond clustering. Therefore, to ensure the capture of such differences, this thesis focuses on
supplementary sex-stratified assessments. This approach helps determine whether endotyping models
maintain accuracy across diverse patient groups while reducing potential bias in patient stratification.
This data-driven approach can enhance clinical trial recruitment and accelerate the development of
targeted treatment options for OA.
This thesis explores a novel approach to KOA endotyping by integrating biomarker data into predictive
models. The models aim to stratify KOA patients into previously identified endotypes - Low Tissue
Turnover, Inflammation and Structural Damage - based on serum biomarker profiles and selected clini-
cal covariates, enabling streamlined in-clinic recruitment for clinical trials on an individual level. This
work leverages four independent studies to address the challenges of OA heterogeneity. The studies in-
clude one observational study and three interventional clinical trials. The interventional trials assessed
the eﬀicacy of three drugs: Oral Salmon Calcitonin and MIV-711, both targeting bone remodeling[21,
22], and UBX0101[23], aiming to clear out aging cells that can cause inflammatory conditions in the
human body. Despite initial promise, these studies were discontinued as they could not demonstrate
significant eﬀicacy in the overall population. This underscores the need to investigate whether distinct
endotypes may have responded differently to treatment and perhaps had shown benefit of treatment
in a subpopulation. Applying data from these studies provides a unique opportunity to refine patient
stratification methods and explore the potential for targeted therapies by employing tools from the field
of data science.
1.3
Aims of Project
This thesis aims to address the limited clinical applicability of existing endotyping methods and to
facilitate the application of molecular endotyping in clinical trials through predictive modeling using
an optimized set of parameters. The project aims to:
• Integrate heterogeneous KOA cohort studies to create a larger, unified dataset for analysis.
• Identify and reduce biomarker redundancy to optimize a minimal, effective biomarker panel for
endotyping.
• Develop, implement, and evaluate a predictive model for endotype stratification in independent,
unseen KOA cohorts.

By achieving these objectives, the thesis aims to advance data-driven methodologies for KOA clinical

APPROACH
APPROACH was a two-year longitudinal observational study, with intermediate visits
at the initialization of the study and at month 1, 6, 12 and 24. The study was completed in 2021,
enrolling N = 297 participants from five existing European observational cohorts[24].
Participants
were selected using a predictive model[25] designed to identify individuals with an increased likelihood
of KOA progression within the length of the study. The study aimed to identify and validate phenotypes
of KOA through medical imaging and biomarker analysis. Key inclusion criteria for the study included:
• Predominantly KOA of the joint connecting the shin and thighbone (tibiofemoral joint)
• Diagnosis of primary knee osteoarthritis (no underlying cause of disease)
• Knee pain, and an additional three of the following:
– Above 50 years of age (Table B.6)
– Less than 30 minutes of morning stiffness
– Crepitus (crackling/popping sensation) on active motion
– Bone tenderness or swelling of the knee
– No palpable warmth

SMC
SMC was a two-year, phase III interventional study completed in 2010, with N = 1176 par-
ticipants enrolled. The study investigated the safety and eﬀicacy of the drug Oral Salmon Calcitonin
(sCT), an anti-resorptive drug that targets bone tissue[22], for managing KOA against a placebo (Pla).
Half of the population received sCT, and the other half received Pla. The patients were monitored
during intermediate visits at baseline and at months 1, 6, 12, and 24. Inclusion criteria for the study
were:
• Aged 51-80.
• Kellgren-Lawrence (KL) grade of 2 or 3 in the target knee (knee with most severe clinical mani-
festations) (See Appendix A.1, Table A.1 for KL classification).
• Joint space width (JSW) of ≥2 mm in target knee. JSW is an indicator of loss of articular
cartilage (Table B.7). Low JSW indicates the advancement of OA.

3.2
Data Pre-processing
The datasets used in this thesis were preprocessed before acquisition for this study, but a summary of
the preprocessing steps is provided.
Outlier Trimming
Winsorization was employed to mitigate the potential influence of extreme out-
liers in the datasets. Winsorization is a statistical technique that limits extreme values by replacing
them with non-outlier values to reduce their impact while preserving the data distribution[28]. Values
falling below the lower threshold (25th percentile minus 1.5 times the interquartile range (IQR)) and
exceeding the upper threshold (75th percentile plus 1.5 times the IQR) were replaced by the respective
2nd and 98th percentile values. This approach restricts disproportionate effects of extreme observations,
preserving the robustness of downstream analyses.
Batch-effect Corrections
Batch effects refer to unwanted technical and biological sources of vari-
ations in data arising from inconsistencies in experimental conditions. Factors causing batch effects
include batch handling, discrepancies in equipment, or differing environmental conditions[29].
The
data from the studies considered in this thesis include biomarkers measured at different points, which
introduces the possibility of observed differences being attributable to technical variations rather than
biological phenomena. Systematic deviations attributed to differing measurement dates of the same
biomarker were modeled using linear mixed effects and subtracted from the winsorized, log-transformed
biomarker values to address batch effects. This correction process yielded adjusted values where tech-
nical variations attributed to differing measurement dates were reduced.

missing data. Missing data may arise from insuﬀicient samples, human errors, or participants dropping
out of the study. The missing data were assumed to be missing completely at random (MCAR)[30],
strengthening the argument for imputation[30, 31]. Missing data due to patient dropout (which may be

3.3
Clustering
Previous studies of the APPROACH[7] and SMC[12] have provided indications of the existence of KOA
patient subgroups defined by endotypes driven by Structural Damage to bone and cartilage, connective
tissue Inflammation, and Low Tissue Turnover through the application of k-means clustering. The
analyses were performed on independent, heterogeneous KOA cohorts[7, 12]. These findings directly
informed the use of k = 3 clusters for both k-means clustering and Fuzzy k-means clustering, recapitu-
lating the previously identified endotypes of these cohorts.
3.3.1
k-Means Clustering
The algorithm k-means is a density-based, unsupervised learning method for partitioning data points
from a dataset into k distinct clusters[36]. Each data point is assigned to the cluster with the nearest
mean by iteratively maximizing the intra-cluster similarity and minimizing inter-cluster similarity. The
algorithm begins by randomly selecting k initial centroids, representing cluster centers. The distance
between each data point and each centroid is then computed stepwise, assigning data points to the
nearest centroid. A commonly used proximity metric is the Euclidean distance[36]:
d(i, j) = ||xi −cj||2
(3.2)
where xi is the i’th data point, and cj is the j’th cluster’s mean. The centroids are iteratively updated to
the mean of the data points in each cluster. The algorithm iteratively repeats the process of reassigning
data points and updating centroids until all points are assigned to a cluster and cluster assignment has
stabilized. In this study kmeans from the stats package in R was used for clustering[37].
Optimization of Cluster Initializations
k-means can be influenced by the random initialization
of centroids, potentially leading to variability in results. Therefore, the nstart parameter[37, 38] was
optimized by running the k-means algorithm 100 times with varying nstart values for 50 random seeds.
The nstart value determines how many initial clustering configurations the algorithm generates[39].
The nstart yielding the smallest average total within-cluster sums of squares (WCSS) across runs was
selected for configuration of the k-means algorithm before obtaining cluster labels, ensuring consistent
and stable performance (Appendix C.1, Figure C.1 and C.2).
Endotype Labels
As the data did not include true endotype labels, the endotype labels used through-
out this thesis were obtained through k-means clustering to recapitulating previous findings in AP-
PROACH and SMC[7, 9, 12].

The Fuzzifier m
The fuzzifier parameter m in FkM is a critical component that determines the
level of fuzziness in the clustering process[41]. The parameter takes values in the 1 ≤m < ∞range,
where values closer to 1 produce clustering behavior similar to conventional k-means, and higher values
yield softer membership degrees and higher degree of overlap between clusters. Selecting a proper value
of m is important. However, research has not found a best practice for selecting the fuzzifier. Upon
developing the FkM algorithm, Bezdek et al. developed a proposition for the interval of 1 < m ≤5[41,
44]. Later, Chan & Cheung (1992) [45] proposed an interval of m ∈[1.25, 1.75]. Wu (2012) proposed an
interval of m ∈[1.5, 4][46]. These are all propositions, and none are widely acknowledged for validating
the outcomes of fuzzy clustering[44]. Therefore, the presentation of the FkM application in this thesis
will apply the default metric, the Fuzzy Silhouette (FS)[47] from fclust[48], checking different values
of m for the fixed value of clusters, k = 3. The parameter m is then selected as the one maximizing the
FS (Appendix C.2, Figure C.4 and Figure C.5).

3.4
Biomarker Panel Selection
A fundamental part of predictive modeling is identifying a relevant set of features. In this thesis, consid-
erable effort was devoted to identifying an optimized, minimal, and effective biomarker panel, retaining
essential information supporting the differentiation of the three endotypes in KOA, Inflammation, Low
Tissue Turnover, and Structural Damage[7, 12]. This feature engineering approach helps avoid the
inclusion of parameters that add limited information while increasing the operational costs associated
with extensive biomarker measurements. In addition, employing a refined biomarker panel enhances
clinical applicability and feasibility. In large-scale clinical trial processes - potentially screening thou-
sands of patients - measuring a smaller, well-selected set of biomarkers streamlines patient assessment
and contributes to cost-effectiveness and operational eﬀiciency.
To achieve an optimized biomarker panel, k-means clustering was combined with the Adjusted Rand
Index (ARI) (Section 3.4.1) to evaluate cluster stability of varying biomarker sets across iterations.
This data-driven approach facilitated a systematic refinement, supporting the differentiation of KOA
endotypes.
3.4.1
Adjusted Rand Index
The ARI is an improved version of the Rand Index (RI), correcting for agreement between two partitions
that may occur by random chance. Both the RI and the ARI assess the similarity between two partitions,
P and Q, of a feature set X by determining partition agreement. However, RI does not account for
the possibility of random partitions by coincidence. To address this, the ARI normalizes the RI by
subtracting the expected similarity under random partitioning, scaling it relative to the maximum
possible agreement beyond chance. The ARI is defined as[49]:
ARI =
Index −Expected Index
Maximum Index −Expected Index
(3.8)
where
• Index is a representation of observed agreement between partitions P and Q (Rand Index).
• Expected Index quantifies the level of agreement between two partitions if assigned randomly. It
describes the expected value of RI under the null hypothesis that P and Q are independent and
random[49, 50].
• Maximum Index is the theoretical upper bound of agreement achieved between P and Q.
ARI is bounded between -1 and 1. A value of 0 indicates complete disagreement in the partitioning
of P and Q, while 1 indicates perfect agreement. Negative values are not of interest, as these indicate
a partitioning worse than what could be expected by chance[50]. The ARI was utilized as a cluster
validity metric to evaluate the similarity of partitions of the same data set. It was also applied to

3.4.2
Iterative Biomarker Panel Selection using k-Means Clustering
To systematically refine the biomarker panel, reduce redundant biomarkers, and increase clinical feasibil-
ity, k-means clustering was applied, leveraging the ARI to evaluate the impact of individual biomarker
removal in an iterative setting. This approach is valuable for identifying an optimal biomarker panel
that can be applied to accurately predict patients’ endotype.
Baseline Clustering for Similarity Assessment
To enable clustering validation using ARI, a
baseline clustering was established for comparison across iterations using k-means (k = 3), representing
each of the three endotypes considered in this work. This baseline cluster partitioning was based on
all available biomarkers in the APPROACH + SMC datset (n = 797) with 10 overlapping biomarkers
(Table 2.2).
Setup of Iterations
The iterations of the iterative k-means clustering algorithm proceeds as follows:
1. Sequentially remove each biomarker in the current set, one at a time:
a) Perform k-means clustering to partition the data.
b) Calculate the Adjusted Rand Index (ARI) between the clustering of the reduced biomarker
set and the baseline clustering.
2. Identify and remove the biomarker that yields the highest ARI in the current iteration, indicating
minimal impact on clustering stability.
3. Use the reduced biomarker set for the next iteration.
Upon terminating the algorithm, the biomarker removal that yielded the largest decrease in ARI while
reducing the biomarker panel served as a criterion for determining the reduced biomarker panel. The
rationale for excluding the biomarkers with the highest ARI in each iteration is based on reducing
redundancy while preserving cluster similarity. A substantial reduction in ARI following a biomarker
removal indicates that it contributed unique information essential in distinguishing clusters (i.e., endo-
types). The algorithm terminates when three biomarkers are left, balancing the reduction of redundant
biomarkers and clustering performance. Reducing beyond this threshold risks losing information essen-
tial for distinguishing endotypes.

When working with compositional data, such as the membership degrees derived from the FkM clus-

3.7.2
Refinement of Model Fit
Biomarker data are often complex[12], and linear models may not be appropriate to capture the complex
nature of the biomarkers fully (Figure B.1). The following describes the inspection initiatives taken to
improve the fit of the MLR models.
Generalized Additive Models
To address non-linear relationships in the linear model, Generalized
Additive Models (GAMs) were incorporated to identify quadratic terms to continuous explanatory
variables with a non-linear relationship with the response variable to improve model fit[57, 60]. The
GAM replaces the linear form of a regression model with the sum of smoothing functions estimated
using a non-parametric smoother. All quadratic terms added to the models were identified by exploring
the visual outcomes of the gam function in R[61] by examining the confidence intervals of the curvatures.
A quadratic term was included for variables that showed non-linear trends and/or the 95% confidence
intervals were too narrow to fit a straight line, meaning that the predictor should be explained through
a quadratic term.
Interaction Terms
The interaction terms identified for the linear models were determined using
trees by the tree function in R[62]. This function detects optimal data splits, revealing interactions
where the effect of one predictor on the response variable depends on the value of another predictor[57].
Model Selection
Once potential quadratic terms and interaction terms were identified and included
in the model, their significance was assessed using an F-test via the drop1 function with test = F[57].
This function systematically removes each term one at a time, comparing the full model’s fit against
reduced models in an ANOVA-like framework. Terms with the lowest F-test values were iteratively ex-
cluded in a stepwise manner, retaining only statistically significant (p < 0.05) predictors that explained
the most variation, ultimately improving model fit[63].

3.8
Concept Evaluation
To further evaluate the concepts of classification and predictions of membership degrees, the MCLR and
MLR models were applied to an external dataset from the Unity OA study (Section 2.1). This was done
to obtain a sensitivity analysis of the models, where endotype clusters and membership degrees are ob-

The concept evaluation should not be considered a direct model validation but a demonstration of
the models’ practical applicability in correctly determining patients’ disease subtypes and potential
overlap. This is interesting as it is interesting to assess if an endotype that biologically matches the
MoA of a clinical trial drug can potentially hold information on the treatment effect. The demonstra-
tion should eventually prove if either modeling concept can be clinically applicable for in-clinic patient
stratification, optimizing recruitment while powering clinical trials to subgroup analyses, and aiding the
search for differential treatment options for KOA patients.
3.9
Assessment of Treatment Outcomes
To finalize the investigations of this thesis, an exploratory assessment of treatment effect in the pre-
dicted endotypes within the treatment arms of the three interventional studies - SMC, Medivir, and
Unity - was conducted. The analysis was based on the primary outcome measures specified for each
study (Section 2.1). Although these clinical trials failed to demonstrate a significant treatment in the
overall population, this hypothesis-driven analysis explores whether responses may differ between the
predicted endotypes. Importantly, this investigation is purely exploratory, as it was conducted post-hoc.
Therefore, the results should be interpreted cautiously, not as definitive evidence of endotype-specific
treatment effects. If trending differences in treatment response between endotypes are observed, this
may suggest that certain endotypes could represent treatable subpopulations when biologically matched
with the drug’s MoA. Such findings could guide the future of clinical trials, informing the development
of precision medicine approaches and aligning patient recruitment with drug MoA (Table 3.2).

The model accounted for key clinical covariates, including treatment, age, BMI, sex, and the baseline

primary outcomes. Additionally, an interaction term between treatment and endotype was included to
assess whether treatment effects varied across endotypes, allowing for separately estimating treatment
effects within each endotype. Biomarkers were not included in the treatment effect assessment as they
were fundamental to endotype prediction and not part of primary outcome measures.
Estimated marginal means (EMMs) were computed with emmeans[65] in R to evaluate treatment ef-
fects within each endotype. EMMs are predicted means derived from the linear models used to analyze
changes in primary outcomes. Unlike raw means, which can be influenced by differences in baseline
values or other factors, EMMs adjust for these variables, providing a clearer comparison of treatment
effects. By holding all other predictors constant, they ensure that the estimated treatment effect re-
flects differences due to treatment itself rather than other sources of variation. This approach allows
for a more accurate interpretation of the treatment effects within the endotypes. Subsequently, pair-
wise comparisons between endotype groups were performed, adjusting for multiple testing using Holm’s
correction[66]. Holm’s correction is well-suited for pairwise comparisons by controlling the family-wise
error rate (FWER), detecting false positives (Type I errors), while maintaining the ability to reduce
false negatives (Type II errors)[67].

4.2
Predictive Modeling Results
This section outlines predictive modeling results for stratifying the molecular endotypes in KOA patients.
For the MCLR model, a stratified training set of the APPROACH + SMC dataset (70%) and a test
set (30%) was used to ensure equal representation of endotypes across both subsets. The MCLR model
was used to model the relationship between the six biomarkers derived from biomarker panel selection,
select clinical variables, and the endotypes. Model performance was evaluated using standard metrics,
including accuracy, precision, recall, and F1 score, to assess the predictive capability of the biomarker
panel.
In addition to the categorical classification, continuous fuzzy membership degrees, derived from the
fuzzy k-means clustering process, were transformed using the logit transformation and incorporated
as response variables in alternative models. These transformed membership degrees were analyzed to
evaluate their predictive power and the utility of the transformation in capturing subtle associations
between biomarkers and endotypes. This dual approach provided a comprehensive understanding of the
predictive potential of the stratification of KOA patients through different approaches. The two methods
to capture endotype-specific patterns in a predictive model were evaluated in terms of performance in
a concept evaluation context, of which the best model was selected for explorative analysis of endotype-
specific treatment effects.

model (Table 4.13). The evaluation was performed after applying the inverse transformation to the
predicted membership degrees, ensuring comparable scales. The R2 values indicated the proportion
of variance explained by the models, with Low Tissue Turnover exhibiting the highest explanatory
power (R2 = 0.777, R2
adj = 0.773) followed by Structural Damage (R2 = 0.760, R2
adj = 0.756), and the
Inflammation model (R2 = 0.671, R2
adj = 0.664). These values suggested that while models were able
to capture a major portion of the variation, some variance remained unexplained. The MAE values,

from 0.073 (Structural Damage) to 0.094 (Inflammation). The RSME value showed that the Structural
Damage model had the lowest error (RMSE = 0.099), while Low Tissue Turnover had the highest
error (RMSE = 0.125). This showed that while the Low Tissue Turnover model explained a larger
proportion of variance compared to the models for Inflammation and Structural Damage, it also had
higher prediction errors (Figure 4.6, 4.7 and 4.8). These results aligned with the model diagnostics for
each model, showing suboptimal model fit (Appendix E, Figure E.3, E.6, and E.9). After using the

SMC
The ANOVA comparison of the modeling of endotype-specific treatment effect compared to the treat-
ment effect of sCT versus placebo in the overall population suggested no statistical evidence of endotype-

to exhibit less worsening in the treated group compared to placebo over two years (Table 4.17 and Fig-

effect, as the CIs did not include zero. When stratified by endotype, Low Tissue Turnover exhibited
a significant effect, however speculatory as there were no significant differences between the endotypes
(Table 4.17, Figure 4.15 and Figure 4.16).

CHAPTER 5
Discussion
This chapter discusses the steps taken in this project to develop a predictive model for stratifying
treatable molecular endotypes in knee osteoarthritis patients. It addresses the key limitations of the
data, methods, and research approaches used to acquire the results in this thesis.
5.1
Data Quantity and Quality
A key limitation of this study was the small dataset. Despite merging data from the APPROACH and
SMC studies, the final dataset included only 797 patients, representing a limited subpopulation. This
challenge is common in osteoarthritis research, where clinical trial cohorts are typically small[68], and
assembling large cohorts is a time-intensive process[69]. This contrasts the nearly 600 million people
worldwide living with OA[1]. Additionally, patient recruitment for the clinical studies followed eligibility
criteria to ensure homogeneity, which, while necessary for the clinical trial design, inherently reduced
the diversity and representativeness of the broader OA population. Consequently, the demographic and
clinical characteristics may not fully reflect the molecular characteristics of the wider OA population,
ultimately limiting the generalizability of the findings (Table B.6 and Table B.7). Beyond population
size and diversity, small datasets pose general challenges for predictive modeling. Limited data may
increase the model’s susceptibility to noise, increase the risk of overfitting, and fail to capture the full
variability of large, diverse populations, leading to reduced predictive performance on unseen data.
Another critical consideration regarding clinical trial data is the variability in measurement ranges
between datasets (Appendix B, Table B.2, B.3, B.4, and Table B.5). Such discrepancies may arise from
technical factors, including changes in laboratory equipment over time, and biological and environmental
factors. Differences in sample handling procedures further contribute to variations that are diﬀicult
to control. Given these challenges, inconsistent measurement ranges present obstacles in predictive
modeling, where data quality directly impacts the outcome. To eliminate such discrepancies, the data
was scaled to limit the impact of extreme observations and acquire comparable data scales, which is
beneficial for understanding the biomarkers’ impact on endotypes relative to each other and for well-
configurated data for machine learning tasks.
Missing Data, Outlier Trimming and Imputation Risks
Batch effects and missing data present
challenges when working with small datasets, as they require careful handling to preserve as much
of the natural and biological variability as possible. While the data in this thesis underwent batch
effect correction, imputation for missing values, and outlier trimming to mitigate these issues, each
method may introduce uncertainties. Computational batch effect correction is intended to reduce non-
biological variations, yet it remains diﬀicult to ensure that only non-biological effects are removed
without distorting true biological signals. Likewise, trimming extreme outliers while improving data
==PAGE:60==
5.2 Clustering-Based Endotype Labels and Membership Degrees
51
consistency may remove some biologically meaningful variability in the data, which may seem extreme
in a data-scientific context. Imputing missing values while preserving the data size is most reliable
when data are MCAR, as adhering to the assumptions of data MCAR helps ensure that imputed values
do not bias the result and approximate actual biological variation[30]. Adherence to these principles is
crucial for the integrity of predictive modeling since all these elements are methods for manipulating
data. Thereby, some natural biological variability may have been concealed, impacting the outcomes of
subsequent analyses, which is a considerable limitation.
5.2
Clustering-Based Endotype Labels and Membership Degrees
While clustering-based endotyping has improved the exploration and refinement of the understanding
of patients with KOA, clustering-based endotyping poses some limitations.
Sensitivity to Data Quality, Preprocessing and Parameter Tuning
Data quality and prepro-
cessing are vital in obtaining meaningful clusters for endotyping. The data differences in scales, such
as CTXI and PRO-C1, between biomarkers require scaling to avoid feature dominance (Appendix A).
Feature dominance may lead to disproportionate influence in clustering and biased predictions if not
handled appropriately. However, scaled values are less intuitive in terms of interpretation. Additionally,
preprocessing is essential, as extreme outliers may distort clusters, thereby increasing risks of wrong
cluster assignment. However, in data from the real world, especially biological data, outliers may be
explainable by biological factors, which are disregarded by outlier trimming, potentially causing infor-
mation loss. Finally, parameter tuning is crucial for the success of clustering-based algorithms. In terms
of k-means and FkM, the choice of three clusters was directly informed by previous findings[7, 9, 12].
Thus, the number of clusters was not challenged in this study. However, the validity of k-means clus-
tering can be influenced by the nstart parameter, which was optimized in this study to ensure stable
results (Figure C.2, Figure C.1 and C.3). Similarly, the fuzzifier parameter m in FkM was optimized
using the fuzzy silhouette score[47], given the ambiguity in the literature regarding best practices for
tuning m. While k-means and FkM were chosen due to interpretability, computational eﬀiciency, and
previous application in the context of OA endotype research[7, 9, 12], alternatives such as DBSCAN
or Gaussian Mixture Models (GMMs) could have been used.
DBSCAN is more robust to extreme
observations and can detect non-spherical clusters, potentially enabling better capturing of underlying
biological variability[70]. However, DBSCAN requires specific tuning of the cluster size and number
of points in a cluster, challenged by the high dimensionality of biomarker data. GMMs could have
provided uncertainty estimates in cluster assignments through its probabilistic approach[71]. However,
such approaches assume Gaussian distributions, which is challenging given the observed distributions
of biomarker data (Figure B.1). These approaches could be further explored in the future, regarding
potential advantages for KOA endotyping.
Data-Driven, Not Clinically Defined
Another limitation is that the endotype labels are produced
computationally and purely derived from patterns in data. No clinical professionals have specifically
determined that the patients used in the studies have the endotypes they have been labeled with, nor
have they approved the overlaps in endotypes. However, the concept is largely accepted by the OA field.
Though computational cluster-based endotyping - both through hard labeling with k-means and the
==PAGE:61==
5.3 Biomarker Panel Selection
52
exploration of overlapping endotypes with Fuzzy k-means (FkM) - has enabled a more nuanced view
and contributed to advancements within OA research[4], as it allows for a better understanding of the
multiple facets of the disease, not only in general but in the individual. However, the limitations of these
methods should be considered. This thesis enforces using k = 3 clusters, aligning with the previously
identified endotypes, thereby directly forcing patients to belong to one of the clusters to recapitulate
previous findings[7, 12]. While this approach ensures consistency and reproducibility, it may overlook
additional biological heterogeneity or other clinically relevant distinctions to refine patient stratification
further, which could have been identified through partitioning into additional clusters.
5.3
Biomarker Panel Selection
The biomarker panel selection process used to identify and reduce biomarker redundancy resulted in
a reduction from 10 to six biomarkers, based on those overlapping between the four studies. This ap-
proach was important, as substantial biomarker measurements are costly and resource-intensive. The
final selection of biomarkers for distinguishing the endotypes of KOA was achieved through an itera-
tive k-means clustering approach, leveraging the ARI to assess cluster stability across iterations. The
final biomarker panel was determined at the steepest ARI decline, ensuring a balance between feature
reduction and clustering coherence (Figure 4.1 and Table 4.1).
Data Limitations and Panel Composition
Since the biomarker panel selection process was based
on the APPROACH + SMC dataset, which was relatively small and homogeneous, uncertainties remain
regarding the generalizability of the selected biomarkers. Greater demographic diversity in the dataset
could have influenced the panel composition, due to the biological variability of individuals. Besides,
the biomarker panel included only inflammatory and bone remodeling biomarkers, and no cartilage
remodeling biomarkers, otherwise known to be a significant factor in KOA due to the degradation
of cartilage over time (Table 4.2). The biological variability captured in this dataset may not fully
reflect key molecular characteristics of KOA, which is crucial for defining a robust biomarker panel,
incorporating all the main drivers of KOA through bone- and cartilage remodeling and inflammatory
conditions. Another limitation is related to the previously mentioned variability in measurement ranges.
If measurement stability fluctuates or changes over time due to technical factors and advancements in
assay development, the suitability of the biomarker panel could be affected negatively. As with other
data-driven approaches, the outcome of the biomarker panel is highly dependent on data quality. Due
to the observed variations in measurement ranges of biomarkers and the small sample size, there is a
risk of the biomarker panel being sub-optimal (Table B.2, B.3, B.4 and B.5). However, based on the
radar charts for the APPROACh + SMC data, and the independent datasets Medivir and Unity, the
biomarkers of the reduced biomarker panel appear to be able to describe the main characteristics and
separate endotypes (Figure 4.2, D.1, and D.2).
Balancing Generalizability and Information Loss
The unreduced panel of biomarkers was al-
ready constrained to overlapping biomarkers across all cohorts to ensure generalizability in downstream
analysis. The APPROACH, SMC, Medivir, and Unity cohorts originally included biomarker measure-
ments from 19, 11, 14, and 12 biomarkers, respectively (Table 2.1). Consequentially, the restriction of
only applying those overlapping between the datasets may have excluded biomarkers that could have
==PAGE:62==
5.4 Biomarker-Based Predictive Models for Endotyping
53
contributed valuable information in the biomarker panel selection procedure. Nevertheless, given that
endotyping for KOA patients was originally clustering-based[7, 9, 12], the iterative clustering approach
used here offers an innovative and meaningful strategy for identifying biomarkers that play an important
role through clustering-based feature selection that accounts for the biomarkers providing information
to retain meaningful clusters defining the distinct endotypes. Ultimately, streamlining and refining a
representative biomarker panel for clinical application in KOA diagnostics, patient stratification, and
clinical trial recruitment holds substantial value, as it reduces the financial and operational demands
associated with extensive biomarker measurements. Such demands could be further decreased with an
even smaller biomarker panel, however, further restricting the panel may eliminate vital information
about biological processes, which are important for determining the disease progression.
5.4
Biomarker-Based Predictive Models for Endotyping
A key consideration in this study was the development, implementation, and evaluation of a predictive
model for endotype stratification in KOA patients. The reasoning for choosing a predictive model over
previous clustering-based endotyping, was the clinical feasibility of individual-level predictions of endo-
types. Predictive modeling enables endotype stratification based on a single patient’s serum samples,
leveraging the streamlined biomarker panel from the iterative clustering process to directly classify the
patients to one endotype. This approach may facilitate immediate in-clinic recruitment for clinical trials
if the predicted endotype aligns with a drug’s mode of action (MoA), reducing the time and resources
required for patient recruitment. In contrast, clustering methods - while useful for initially identifying
subgroups across larger populations - are retrospective because they would require substantial prior
patient recruitment and testing efforts to define endotypes and plan subsequent trials on a population
level. Predictive modeling thus provides a potentially more feasible path for patient-specific endotype
classification, accelerating the trial process and paving the way for precision medicine discovery in OA.
Choosing appropriate modeling approaches for predicting treatable endotypes in KOA patients relied
on several factors. First, the number of observations and features was small. Such circumstances allow
for simpler models, as small datasets do not require many computational resources. Second, complex
models may tend to overfit if trained on small datasets, as the advanced underlying operations can lead
to memorization rather than generalization, reducing model reliability on unseen data. This is partic-
ularly relevant for deep learning models, which typically require large datasets to extract meaningful
patterns and would introduce undesirable interpretability challenges in a clinical setting, where disease
management and patient care are prioritized.
Furthermore, model transparency is crucial in a clinical and regulated context. Predictive models
used for patient stratification in clinical trials must be interpretable, reproducible, and explainable
to support medical decision-making and meet regulatory standards. Black-box models lack the trans-
parency needed for such applications, making simpler methods—such as logistic regression or linear
regression—preferable for endotype prediction.
This thesis explored two different modeling approaches: a multinomial logistic regression model for
classification (MCLR) and multiple linear regression (MLR) models for predicting membership de-
grees. However, the model diagnostics of the MLR models showed suboptimal model fit, violating the
==PAGE:63==
5.4 Biomarker-Based Predictive Models for Endotyping
54
assumptions of linear regression even though substantial efforts for optimizing model fit were made (Ap-
pendix E). The MCLR model for classifying OA endotypes showed consistently good performance in
the test set and the concept evaluation showing accuracies of 0.8917 and 0.8171, respectively (Table 4.4
and Table 4.14). Oppositely, the MLR models showed high errors in terms of MAE and RMSE as well
as clear problems predicting membership degrees in the concept evaluation, especially for Inflammation
and Low Tissue Turnover (Table 4.16 and Figure 4.11, 4.12, and 4.13). These findings indicated that
the MLR models were too simple to capture the complexity of the data, though these complex patterns
were attempted to be eliminated through optimizing model fit using quadratic terms and interactions.
Classification of Endotypes
The MCLR model applied in this thesis for endotype prediction showed
promising predictive abilities, with an accuracy of 0.89 and 0.81 for the APPROACH + SMC test set
and the concept evaluation on the unseen Unity dataset, respectively. Class-wise metrics showed results
that adequately reflected the accuracies, both in the overall population and when stratified by sex
(Table 4.4, 4.5, and 4.6). In the concept evaluation of the MCLR model, a decrease in accuracy was
expected due to differing measurement ranges of the biomarkers included in the biomarker panel and
the technical implications of applying a model trained on one dataset to predict another (Table 4.14).
Adding to this, the Unity dataset used for concept evaluation was a much smaller cohort, capturing the
variability of the real world to a lesser extent. Even though the model demonstrated decent performance,
several factors should be considered for its future clinical feasibility. First, the model essentially aims to
reproduce the endotype labels generated from density-based k-means clustering. The endotype labels
are computationally generated rather than directly annotated by clinical professionals, which directly
informs that there is no actual ground truth. Secondly, the model was trained on a small dataset of 797
patients, predominantly Caucasian, postmenopausal females, which may introduce bias in biomarker-
based signals compared to other demographic groups. This was specifically observed in the estimate
of the MCLR model (Table D.1), inflating the probability of males belonging to Structural Damage,
compared to Low Tissue Turnover or Inflammation in comparison to females, which could likely be
due to the high imbalance in representation of sexes (Table B.1). This could limit the model’s ability
to capture biological variations in patients dissimilar to those in the training data, thereby reducing its
generalizability.
Regarding data limitations, the precision of laboratory equipment is crucial for the long-term suc-
cess of biomarker measurements’ applicability in a clinically feasible endotype classification model. The
measurement range of biomarkers must remain stable over time. If the measurement ranges and vari-
ability of the biomarkers in the panel were to show extreme differences in the future, the scaling of
patient data required for classification may yield incorrect outcomes. Additionally, assay development
and biomarker precision must be stable and resistant to environmental effects to prevent the need for
frequent model retraining. Frequent retraining may become expensive, as acquiring sensitive, regulated
data, such as serum samples, is time-consuming and requires appropriate permits. Acknowledging the
limitations while recognizing the opportunities of the endotype classification model, there are clear
incentives for considering biomarker-based classification for the future of clinical trial recruitment of
treatable molecular endotypes.
Misclassifications and Overlapping Endotypes
The misclassified patients from the MCLR model
exhibited interesting patterns (Figure 4.4 and Figure 4.9). The biomarker measurements of these pa-
tients suggested overlaps among biomarkers that typically distinguish endotypes. This aligned with
==PAGE:64==
5.4 Biomarker-Based Predictive Models for Endotyping
55
previous studies indicating that KOA endotypes are not necessarily clear-cut, but a continuum of
mixed pathologies, reinforcing the importance of the analyses where MLR models were employed to
predict proportional membership degrees to each of the three endotypes. The findings from the MLR
modeling approach found that model optimization procedures, by including interactions and quadratic
terms, helped models approximate MLR model assumptions (Appendix E). However, there were still
characteristics indicating sub-optimal model fit for all three membership degree models. These issues
led to diﬀiculties in predicting membership degrees of the KOA patients. Specifically, extreme member-
ship degrees were challenging to predict in the APPROACH + SMC dataset, as indicated by collected
metrics showing high errors compared to the proportional nature of the membership degrees, ranging
between 0 and 1 (Table 4.13). Nevertheless, the predictions revealed that more than half (431 out of
797) of the patients showed a primary membership degree (≥0.51) to either Inflammation, Low Tissue
Turnover or Structural Damage compared to the 572 patients with a ”true” primary assignment to
either of the endotypes as obtained from FkM.
Applying the MLR models to the Unity OA cohort in the concept evaluation, the findings showed
that the predicted membership degrees of Inflammation and Low Tissue Turnover exhibited high de-
viations from the true membership degrees, implying that the model struggled to predict membership
degrees in unseen data (Figure 4.11, 4.12, and 4.13). Only 32 out of 175 patients were predicted to
have a primary endotype assignment, of which the majority belonged to Structural Damage, which also
showed the best performance relative to the other two endotypes (Table 4.16 and Figure 4.11).
A limitation of the approach was that model diagnostics of the initial MLR models for each endotype
indicated suboptimal model fit, which may have been amplified when applied to unseen data. Factors
such as differing measurement ranges and patient variations likely contributed to poor generalizability.
In addition to this, the models were based on the entire spectrum of membership degrees for the
respective endotypes. Had the models been trained only on extremes, the outcome could have been
different. However, training only on high and low membership degrees would disregard patients with
higher overlap, biasing the outcome. Moreover, the compositional nature of membership degrees and
their relationship to biomarker levels may be too complex for the MLR model to capture effectively,
particularly when applied to unseen data subject to uncertainties. Additionally, the membership degrees
used in MLR were derived from FkM. While FkM is widely applied in biomedical fields of research[43],
it presents challenges in optimization due to the absence of well-established best-practice guidelines
for tuning its hyperparameters[46].
The diﬀiculty in optimizing FkM could have introduced noise
into the membership degree predictions, complicating the applications of MLR for modeling endotype
associations and making them subject to uncertainties in terms of the reliability of the membership
degrees. This suggests that MLR may be too simplistic to model the relationships between clinical
variables, biomarkers and membership degrees adequately, limiting its potential application in clinical
environments.
Given the importance of deepening the understanding of the multi-faceted nature of KOA, future
studies could focus on refined methodologies for membership degree predictions that could combat the
limitations of MLR models.
Feasibility in a Clinical Context
The prediction models lack some feasibility in a clinical context in
terms of individual-level predictions as a consequence of the observed instability of measurement ranges
and variations of biomarkers. The issues were mitigated in this thesis by z-score scaling of populations.
==PAGE:65==
5.5 From Endotypes to Treatable Endotypes
56
As a consequence of variability and uncertainties in equipment when assessing samples, predicting new,
independent observations may be wrong compared to large-scale populations, introducing the risk of
wrongful classifications in future applications on an individual level. Scaling makes the prediction model
less feasible on an individual level as it requires knowledge of the mean and standard deviation of a
population to correctly scale and thereby make predictions through the approach considered in this
thesis. However, dealing with such scaling issues as a consequence of variability in measurement ranges
across biomarkers is out of the scope of this project, and therefore, the results are a proof of concept.
Choosing between MCLR and membership degree prediction using MLR depends on the intended
use case. If a strict classification is required in clinical decision-making, MCLR provides a clear-cut
assignment of patients into predefined endotypes. However, if the goal is to account for patient hetero-
geneity and overlapping endotypes, MLR provides a more flexible framework, albeit with challenges in
predictive accuracy, given the complexity of biomarker data and the computation of membership de-
grees. Moreover, one may argue that if a maximal membership degree determines the patient’s primary
endotype, in terms of endotype to MoA matching, classification would be a well-suited model choice.
Both methodologies were appropriate choices considering the sample size and their computational effec-
tiveness. Had more data been available, more complex algorithms, such as multi-layer neural networks,
could have been applied. However, the computational time of training and testing would increase rad-
ically compared to the simpler yet effective approaches considered in this thesis. All in all, one of the
main takeaways from the predictive modeling approaches is, that the models can never exceed the ac-
curacy of the biomarker measurements, and data-driven approaches remain sensitive to the input data.
Better prediction models and their clinical feasibility rely heavily on the quality of the measurements
conducted in the laboratories.
5.5
From Endotypes to Treatable Endotypes
While predictive modeling of endotype associations provides a framework for stratifying patients based
on biomarker-driven characteristics, an equally important aspect is to determine whether these endo-
types are not only distinct but potentially also treatable endotypes. Clinical trials in KOA thus far
have yielded negative or inconclusive results, largely due to the assumption that all patients within
the clinical trial represent a homogeneous population. Given the growing evidence of molecular and
pathophysiological heterogeneity in KOA[7, 72], such broad analyses may obscure clinically meaningful
treatment effects that only emerge within specific endotypes, raising the relevance of the subgroup
analyses conducted in this thesis.
The treatment effect was assessed in the three studies, SMC (Oral Salmon Calcitonin vs. Placebo),
Medivir (MIV-711 vs. Placebo), and Unity (UBX0101 vs. Placebo), based on the endotypes predicted
using the MCLR model.
Based on the MoA of the drugs tested in the studies, endotype matches
were hypothesized for more meaningful assessment (Table 3.2). The p-values indicated no significant
effects when including endotype-specific effects. Thereby, none of the patients in either of the endotypes
showed a significant change in the primary study outcome. (Table 4.17, 4.18, and 4.19). However, by
further inspection of the EMMs comparing placebo to treatment in each study, some indicators of less
worsening and more improvement were observed for some groups based on primary outcome measures
==PAGE:66==
5.6 Future Perspective
57
(Table 3.2). However, the primary outcome measures may be severely influenced by the placebo ef-
fect, the subjectivity of questionnaire-based outcomes (NRS and WOMAC), and the uncertainties of
radiographic assessment to determine JSW[73, 74] and thereby overall disease progression. The find-
ings, though speculative and merely explorative, could be pointing towards further exploration of the
need for personalized medication, where optimization of patient recruitment through biomarker-based
endotype prediction, could be beneficial. In addition to this, it may underline that biomarkers are more
objective in terms of determining the progression of KOA, compared to subjective questionnaires and
other metrics fraught with uncertainty.
Concept Evaluation over Validation
It is with great consideration that the approaches of this the-
sis eventually led to an evaluation of the concepts rather than a direct validation. The aforementioned
limitations of computationally obtained endotype labels and membership degrees, enforcing three en-
dotypes, is a major factor regarding this decision. To directly validate the model, first, it would require
that the biomarker panel was derived from a more diverse and much larger population. In addition, it
would require that there is much less variability in biomarker measurements over time to acquire more
trustworthy results. As a consequence of the uncertainties about the biomarker panel and the compu-
tationally obtained endotype labels, neither the MCLR nor MLR models can be validated for clinical
use. Both models required scaling of features for model training to mitigate feature differences. The
scaling was done on the entire population, which, considering the biomarker measurement ranges and
variability, makes scaling of new, single observation diﬀicult. None of the clinical studies in this thesis
were powered for subgroup analysis. This was clear, as the studies initially failed and were discontinued
as a result of no eﬀicacy in the overall populations, which, at the time of conducting the trials, were
assumed to be homogeneous. To obtain clinical trial studies, the clinical trials would have had to be
structured in a completely different way, with prior assumptions of heterogeneity in KOA, and primary
endpoints being defined specifically for each of the respective endotypes. In that way, studies would be
powered for subgroup analyses[75], comparing treatment outcomes between groups based on biomarker
profiles, relevant demographic variates, and perhaps clinical outcome measures such as JSW, WOMAC,
and NRS, though subject to subjectivity.
Most importantly, to be able to validate the concept of this work, it would require an interventional
clinical trial demonstrating an actual treatment effect in a specific subgroup and a clinical validation
going beyond the current acknowledgment of OA endotypes.
5.6
Future Perspective
This study highlights the potential of data-driven biomarker-based predictive modeling for endotype
classification in KOA. However, further research is needed to refine and validate the findings, ensuring
broader applicability and future clinical utility.
Data Diversity and Size for Model Generalizability
Future studies could aim to incorporate
larger, more diverse cohorts. This could be done by recruitment of a new cohort or merging data from
previous cohorts. This could ensure a more balanced demographic and molecular representation.
To incorporate data-driven approaches and machine learning models in a clinical trial recruitment
setting, more data may be necessary to obtain models that apply to the general KOA population.
==PAGE:67==
5.6 Future Perspective
58
Acquiring more extensive datasets could allow for more complex modeling approaches that can better
handle non-linearity and naturally occurring biological variations in biomarker data.
Measurement Standardization and Stability
The success of biomarker-based prediction models
relies heavily on the consistency and reproducibility of biomarker measurements. To support the reliabil-
ity of the future of biomarker-based OA studies, it is of high importance that laboratory technicians and
scientists explore the possibilities of stabilizing equipment for biomarker measurements in the future.
Translating Findings to Precision Medicine
One of the primary drivers of all the research in OA
is to break down the pathophysiological causes driving the disease and potentially discover a person-
alized medication that targets endotypes that match the drug’s MoA. Future studies should evaluate
whether molecular endotypes identified through the data-driven predictive approaches presented in this
thesis correlate with differential treatment responses based on predictive endotyping based clinical trial
recruitment, preferably in clinical trials with evident treatment effects.
==PAGE:68==
CHAPTER 6
Conclusion
This study aimed to develop a predictive model for stratifying treatable molecular endotypes in KOA
patients, leveraging iterative biomarker panel selection for feature selection.
Subsequently, two dif-
ferent approaches for predictive modeling were applied. One classifier for obtaining direct endotype
labels and another approach for modeling membership degree to endotypes, aiming to capture the more
complex relationships between endotypes in KOA patients. The findings demonstrated that a reduced
biomarker panel of six biomarkers, obtained through an innovative approach employing k-means and
the Adjusted Rand Index to assess clustering stability, could effectively retain the characteristics of
the endotypes Inflammation, Low Tissue Turnover, and Structural Damage. The predictive modeling
approaches showed that the classification model demonstrated strong predictive performance with an
overall accuracy of 81% in unseen data, while the predictions of membership degrees exhibited large
residual errors when applied to unseen data.
This study contributes to advancing clinical trial recruitment in OA through predictive modeling, with
the goal of enabling subgroup analyses for continued search for precision medicine. By identifying key
biomarkers for molecular endotype stratification, this approach provides a structured method for refin-
ing biomarker panels while preserving biological relevance. The integration of a refined biomarker panel
with a probabilistic classification model underscores the potential of data-driven endotype stratification
to improve clinical trial recruitment and support the discovery of personalized treatments for KOA
patients.
However, several limitations must be considered. The data set, though carefully curated, was small
and homogeneous, posing limitations for the generalizability of the findings, making this work a proof-
of-concept with potential for future validation.
Additionally, variability in biomarker measurement
ranges across data sets poses challenges for long-term model stability. Addressing these issues through
more diverse cohorts and standardized assay protocols will be essential for ensuring the clinical appli-
cability of biomarker-based endotyping based on predictive modeling.
The broader implications of this work extend beyond KOA. The methodology could be adapted to
other diseases characterized by molecular heterogeneity, paving the way for biomarker-driven classifica-
tion in several diseases and allowing for a more objective disease assessment than the currently applied
questionnaires subject to subjectivity and uncertainty. Future research may focus on external validation
and refinement of equipment for biomarker measurements and refinement of biomarker panel selection
for optimization of clinical trial protocols while assessing the clinical feasibility of integrating predictive
modeling into clinical workflows and validating this practice in clinical trials with proven effects in a
specific endotype.
==PAGE:69==
6 Conclusion
60
In conclusion, this study underscores the value of biomarker-based data-driven endotyping for KOA
while highlighting its potential to advance precision medicine through predictive modeling approaches.
While challenges remain, the findings provide a foundation for future research aimed at translating
biomarker-driven patient classification to clinical applicability. Continued efforts to increase data vol-
umes, validate models, and improve biomarker measurements will be key to realizing the full potential
of these approaches in a clinical setting.

Appendix: Multiple Linear
Regression
Appendix E focuses on the model selection for the logit-transformed membership degree vectors obtained
from the FkM algorithm as of Section 3.3.2. The following figures and tables aim to show the process
of model selection for each of the three response variables for the reader to understand the reasoning
behind the finally selected models for the membership degree predictions shown in Section 4.3. Before
delving into the outcomes of the modeling of the membership degrees separately, the initial model for
all three endotypes takes the form
Y ∼β0+β1C1M + β2C3M + β3VICM+
β4CTXI + β5NMID + β6PRO-C1+
β7Age + β8BMI + β9Sex
(E.1)
where Y is either of the three endotypes, Structural Damage, Inflammation or Low Tissue Turnover.
E.1
Structural Damage
The initial model for modeling the Structural Damage endotype showed strong curvature and violation
of the assumptions of a linear model.
To accommodate the issues of the initial model, smoothing functions of the Generalized Additive
Model (GAM) (Figure E.1) and a tree-based representation of interactions using the tree-function in
R (Figure E.2) was employed to identify potential quadratic terms and interactions to improve model
fit.
As a result of the GAM, quadratic terms were added for biomarkers C1M, VICM, CTXI, NMID
and PRO-C1 due to the non-linear trends. The tree analysis for Structural Damage showed that NMID
was the primary splitting feature, playing an important role in determining the membership degrees of
this endotype. Additionally, it was seen that PRO-C1 also differentiated the data.
