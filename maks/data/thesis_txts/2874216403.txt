Sammenfatning
Smoltens migration fra ferskvand til saltvand udgør en kritisk fase i havørredens (Salmo
trutta) livscyklus, hvor fisken er særligt udsat for både høj prædationsrisiko og miljømæs­
sigt stress. Mellem 2022 og 2023 blev der ved Varbjerg Havn i Danmark etableret et kun­
stigt stenrev, verdens første såkaldte “smoltrev”, med det formål at forbedre levevilkårene
for smolt ved at tilbyde øget strukturel kompleksitet, skjulesteder mod rovdyr og bedre
fødemuligheder. Dette speciale undersøger, om revet har øget smolttilstedeværelsen
under deres havvandring ved at sammenligne undersøgelser fra før etableringen i 2022
med tilsvarende undersøgelser efter etableringen i 2025.
Data blev indsamlet gennem natlige snorkeltransekter, en omkostningseffektiv metode,
der muliggør direkte visuel observation af fisk og habitat forhold i de timer, hvor smolt
er mest aktive. Undersøgelsen bestod af to delstudier: 1) En før/efter­sammenligning
baseret på identiske transekter på hver side af revet, og 2) En undersøgelse af rumlig
variation i smolttilstedeværelse, undersøgt på begge sider af revet samt på den omkring­
liggende sandbund. Dataene blev analyseret for at beregne andelen af transekter med
smolt, og statistiske test blev anvendt til at vurdere forskelle i tilstedeværelse mellem
studierne, revzonerne og i relation til habitatvariabler som dybde og afstand til kyst.
Resultaterne viser en signifikant stigning i smolttilstedeværelse i 2025 sammenlignet med
2022, hvilket understøtter hypotesen om, at revet allerede bidrager til forbedrede lev­
evilkår under smoltens migration. Studie 2 viste desuden høj smolttilstedeværelse på den
nærliggende sandbund, hvilket indikerer, at revet kan have udvidet det samlede tilgæn­
gelige habitat. Selvom effekten er tydelig, befinder revets økologiske udvikling sig stadig i
en tidlig fase, og der forventes en gradvis stigning i både biodiversitet og biomasse, indtil
økosystemet når en tilstand, der minder om naturlige stenrev.
For at supplere feltobservationerne blev der udviklet en simpel deterministisk popula­
tionsmodel, der simulerer de potentielle langsigtede effekter af revet på smoltens over­
levelse og produktion. Modellen inddrager vækst i bæreevne, variable prædationsrater
samt rekruttering både med og uden tæthedsuafhængighed og estimerer, hvordan revet
over tid kan øge smoltbestanden sammenlignet med et scenarie uden rev.
Dette speciale viser, at kunstige stenrev kan understøtte migrerende fiskearter ved at
øge den strukturelle kompleksitet i kystnære habitater. Det anbefales, at overvågningen
fortsættes, eksempelvis ved brug af akustisk telemetri og tagging, for at opnå en mere
detaljeret forståelse af fiskens tilstedeværelse og udnyttelse af revstrukturerne samt for
at kvantificere revets beskyttende effekt. Resultaterne bidrager til den voksende evidens
for, at habitatrestaurering med kunstige rev kan være en effektiv metode til at styrke be­
standene af anadrome fisk.

Abstract
The migration of smolt from freshwater to the marine environment represents a critical
life stage for sea trout (Salmo trutta), during which they experience high predation risk
and environmental stress. Between 2022 and 2023, an artificial stone reef was deployed
at Varbjerg Harbor, Denmark, designed to be the worlds first smolt reef, with the aim
of improving habitat conditions for smolt by providing structural complexity, refuge from
predators, and increased feeding opportunities. This study investigates whether the reef
has enhanced smolt presence during migration by comparing pre­deployment survey from
2022 with post­deployment surveys from 2025.
Data were collected from fieldwork through night snorkeling transects, a method that of­
fers cheap, direct visual assessment of fish presence and habitat conditions during peak
smolt activity hours. Two studies were conducted 1. A pre/post comparison using iden­
tical transects on either side of the reef, and 2. A spatial use study by examining smolt
presence across reef sides and adjacent sand bottom. Data were processed to determine
proportions of transects with smolt presence, and statistical tests were applied to assess
differences in smolt presence between studies, reef zones, and habitat variables such as
depth and distance to shore.
Results show a significant increase in smolt presence in 2025 surveys compared to 2022,
supporting the hypothesis that the reef provides improved habitat conditions during mi­
gration. Study 2 revealed that smolt presence was also high in the adjacent sand bottom
areas of the reef, indicating the reef have expanded the overall habitat in which smolt
can utilize. While these results suggest that the reef is already benefiting smolt, the reefs
ecological maturity remains in its early stages, and further increases in biodiversity and
biomass are expected over time until the reefs ecology mirrors that of a natural stone reef.
To complement field observations, a simple deterministic population model was devel­
oped to investigate potential long­term effects of the reef on smolt survival and production.
It implements carrying capacity growth, variable predation rates, and recruitment with and
without density dependence, the model simulates how the reef could increase the smolt
population over time compared to a non­reef scenario.
This thesis demonstrates that artificial stone reefs can support migratory fish species
by enhancing coastal habitat complexity. Continued monitoring, incorporating acoustic
telemetry and fish tagging, is recommended to better understand the presence, utiliza­
tion of the reef structures and to quantify the reefs protective benefits, to guide future
reef designs. The results contribute to growing evidence that habitat restoration through
artificial reefs can be an effective conservation method for sustaining anadromous fish
populations.

Introduction
Stone reefs are complex underwater structures that provide hard substrate for macroal­
gae and benthic organisms, creating habitats that provide refuge and feeding opportunites
for fish. In Danish waters, natural marine reefs are part of the Natura 2000 network and
designated as protected areas, because of the ecosystem services they provide (Støttrup
et al. 2014).
During the last century, intensive stonefishing, the extraction of stones and boulders,
has resulted in the removal of cavernous reefs, hard substrates, and associated bio­
genic structures (Støttrup et al. 2014). This degradation increases average depth and
reduce light availability for benthic vegetation, which diminishes habitat complexity and
biodiversity. Although stone fishing of large boulders has now ceased in Denmark, its
consequences have degraded important coastal habitats, reducing their capacity to sup­
port healthy fish populations (Støttrup et al. 2014). One promising way to mitigate this
impact is to restore the habitats through artificial reefs, which aim to reintroduce structural
complexity and restore ecological functions.
In 2022­2023, an artificial stone reef, the worlds first smolt reef, was deployed at Varbjerg
Harbor on the northern coast of Funen, Denmark (DTU Aqua 2021). The reef was de­
signed to provide shelter and suitable foraging habitat for juvenile sea trout, called smolts,
during their migration from freshwater to the marine environment.
Sea trout are an anadromous species native to Danish freshwater and coastal ecosys­
tems. At 1­3 years of age, they undergo smoltification and migrate during spring from
rivers, into coastal waters. This life stage is associated with high mortality due to preda­
tion by birds, larger fish, and marine mammals. By providing crevices, shaded areas, and
over time a colonized surface of macroalgae and mussels, the artificial reef is expected to
improve survival by offering refuge from predators and access to invertebrate prey (DTU
Aqua 2021).
The aim of this study is to investigate whether the establishment of the artificial stone
reef at Varbjerg Harbor has improved habitat conditions for smolt during their migration.
This is assessed by comparing smolt presence between 2022 (pre­deployment) and 2025
(post­deployment), supplemented by an additional study that investigated whether smolts
remained near the reef structures or also utilized adjacent sand bottom. Field data were
collected using night snorkeling transects, a cost­effective and direct visual method that
observes fish presence and habitat conditions during peak smolt activity. To complement
the field observations, a simple population model was developed to examine potential
long­term effects of the reef on smolt survival and production.
The central hypothesis is that artificial reef increases structural complexity, provides refuge
from predation, and improves food availability, thereby supporting a higher proportion of
transects with smolt presence compared to pre­deployment conditions.

Background
2.1
Sea Trout Life History
Brown trout (Salmo trutta) is a fish species native to Europe, with a natural range that
stretches to the western part of Asia and the northern part of Africa. It is the most common
salmonoid species in Denmark, and one of the most diverse in terms of morphologies, life
history, and migratory strategies (Ferguson et al. 2019; Boel 2012). They can utilize both
freshwater and marine environments at different stages in their life cycle. Within a pop­
ulation, the life strategies can vary; some individuals choose to spend their entire life in
native freshwater streams and lakes, while others adopt an anadromous life strategy with
feeding migrations to the ocean (Kristensen 2019). Individuals who choose to migrate are
called sea trout. Regardless of which strategy they adopt, when mature, the individuals
usually return to their native stream to spawn, although straying to other rivers does occur.
This enables brown trout to form locally adapted populations, more capable of navigating
the habitat compared to a foreign trout (Kristensen 2019).
Figure 2.1: Life history of Salmo Trutta.
The life­cycle of Salmo trutta begins after mature adults have converged in rivers and
streams to spawn during autumn or winter on hard substrates such as gravel and small
rocks. The fertilized eggs development is temperature dependent, and alevins hatch in
early spring when the water warms up and survive by feeding endogenous. When the
yolk sac is absorbed fry will emerge from the gravel and start feeding exogenously by
hunting small prey (Kristensen 2019). The parr then faces its first decision: Should I stay
or should I go? Or in other words, reside or migrate?. The parr that stay in the stream
becomes brown trout. The parr, that chooses to migrate to the sea will face another
decision, do I migrate now, or wait another year?. When they decide to migrate they

undergo a process called smoltification and become smolt and eventually sea trout, and
during the marine phase focus on growth, before returning to their native river or stream
to spawn as adults 2.1. (Kristensen 2019). In elaboration to figure Figure 2.1 there exists
two types of residence (Ferguson et al. 2019):
1. River­Resident: Resident within 1st or 2nd order tributary.
2. Lake­Resident: Spawn and spend their entire life cycle within the same lake.
The river­resident trout spends its entire life cycle within rivers or small tributaries. They
perform localized dispersal movements but do not undertake extensive migrations. Lake­
resident trout, display both horizontal and vertical movements within the lake, as a shift be­
tween spawning and feeding habitats seasonally or even diurnally (Ferguson et al. 2019).
The migratory life histories include three main types of directed seasonal movements.
1. Fluvial­adfluvial: Migration to a larger tributary or main stem of the river.
2. Lacustrine­adfluvial: Migration to a lake.
3. Anadromy: Migration to an estuary or sea.
Some Salmo trutta follow a fluvial­adfluvial migration, where they stay entirely within fresh­
water. These individuals move downstream into larger rivers to feed and grow, before
returning upstream to spawn. Others follow a lacustrine­adfluvial migration, where they
hatch in small streams flowing into a lake and then migrate into the lake. Lastly, there
are the anadromous migrators; these trout migrate to the sea after smoltification and feed
in marine environments, then later return to freshwater to spawn. In some populations,
this migration stops in estuaries or fjords instead of the open ocean, a behavior termed
semi­anadromy or partial­anadromy (Ferguson et al. 2019).
The fry that choose a resident strategy become territorial and develop a brownish color.
The individuals that choose to migrate from freshwater to the marine environment have
to invert their osmoregulatory processes to excrete ions instead of retaining them. This
physiological transformation, called smoltification, changes the fish’s color from brown to
silver. The mean age of migrating smolts in Denmark is 1.8­2.2 years, and usually takes
place in spring during April­May, depending on latitude and river temperatures (Kristensen
2019).
Most current freshwater Salmo trutta populations are from anadromous ancestors, as a
result of marine barriers after the retreatment of ice from the glacial period. Which shows
that the anadromous strategy is not a fixed trait and can change over time and even within
the lifetime of individuals. Formerly anadromous Salmo trutta can adopt a freshwater life
history, where older individuals stop migrating (Ferguson et al. 2019). All migratory life
histories are fundamentally similar in terms to what drives the migration, but differ in the
choice of destination. This difference in life histories raises a question, what drives some
individuals to migrate in the first place, and what determines their destination? (Ferguson
et al. 2019).
2.1.1
Why Migrate?
Migration is a coordinated movement of several individuals from one habitat to another.
The migratory behavior is an adaptation for avoiding resource depletion, competition and
utilize seasonal peaks in resources elsewhere (Boel 2012). In many rivers Salmo trutta
populations show characteristics of optional migration, with some migrating while other re­
main as residents. The migration is a trade off between the potential benefits and costs.

Migration downstream within a river system, a lake, or to the sea increases feeding op­
portunities, both in terms of quantity and quality (Ferguson et al. 2019). Anadromous fish
do a majority of their feeding and growth in saltwater, and are predominant where the
sea is more productive than freshwater habitats (Boel 2012). This results in faster growth
and ultimately a larger body size at maturity, greater energy stores, higher fecundity and
production of offspring and overall better fitness. Studies have shown the the contribu­
tion of juvenile production in a river was higher for anadromous individuals compared to
river residents (Ferguson et al. 2019). Adversely migration increases energy expenditure,
physiological stress, risk of predation, parasites and diseases, where the costs increases
with the habitat destination. Predation in lakes is generally higher than rivers, and even
higher at sea than in fresh water, where predation is a major mortality factor shortly after
smolts reach the sea (Ferguson et al. 2019).
Studies show that there exist a sex bias in Salmo trutta populations, with females be­
ing dominant among fluvial­adfluvial, lacustrine­adfluvial, and anadromous migrants, and
males among the residents (Ferguson et al. 2019). Since female reproductive success
(number of eggs) is directly linked with body size and their fecundity increases expo­
nentially with length, the balance between costs and benefits of migration for females is
significantly different compared to males as any additional growth gained by migrating
translates into more offspring. Larger females can attract males, obtain and defend better
spawning sites in more substrate sizes and excavate deeper nests. Male reproductive
success is less size dependent and is typically limited by access to mates. Achieving a
large size can be a benefit in attract and defend mates, but even small males can produce
millions of sperm and adopt a sneaking tactic to fertilize eggs. Males therefore have less
to gain from extra marine growth and often mature as residents (Ferguson et al. 2019;
Boel 2012)
Although offspring of migratory or resident salmo trutta can adopt different life histories
from those of their parents, there is a often a tendency to follow the same strategy. Fe­
male offspring produced by anadromous mothers rarely expressed residency with only
2%, while the percentage of males was 41%. Both male and female parr produced by
resident mothers is significantly more likely to show residency. The relationship between
the mother and her offspring life­histories reflect genetic effects, with offspring inheriting
migratory alleles from their mother (Ferguson et al. 2019). The decision to migrate or re­
main resident can also be linked to individuals physiological conditions such as metabolic
rate, lipid storage and energetic state, where these conditions can be influenced by abi­
otic environmental factors such as temperature and water flow and biotic factors such
as food availability. The number of migrants in both lacustrine­adfluvial and anadromous
can be increased by reducing food availability for individuals. In relation to food availability
higher juvenile density also increase the likelihood of migration, because of competition
of resources (Ferguson et al. 2019). A key abiotic factor in the migratory decision is tem­
perature, both the absolute and variation in temperature. Temperature is linked to food
availability, metabolism, feeding activity and lipid storage and function as a stressor on
the migratory decision. A study done on the effects on Salmo trutta migrations made ex­
perimental manipulations of temperature, food removal and cortisol administration, where
cortisol should mimic a physiological challenge. The study found that smaller individuals
and individuals in poorer condition were more likely to migrate and migrated earlier (Fer­
guson et al. 2019).
Migration is influenced by a combination of genes and environmental conditions. Each

individual has a genetically determined threshold, and migration will occur if their phys­
iological condition falls below this threshold, if above the individual stays and matures.
Within a population individuals have different threshold values which are likely to follow
a normal distribution. The mean threshold will differ among populations and sexes and
the choice between migration or residency depends on both the physiological condition
and threshold value. This value can also explain full residency or full migration, if all in­
dividuals are above or below their thresholds, they all stay or all migrate (Ferguson et al.

This first threshold determines the individual decision to migrate, and are not linked to
size, but rather feeding conditions, where poor feeding conditions are likely to result in
migration. Another threshold relates to the timing of migration, and is often size related.
If an individual have not reached its size threshold, it may delay migration to a later age.
Survival during migration is also correlated with size, so fish in poor condition will delay in
order to grow more (Ferguson et al. 2019).
When the decisions of if and when to migrate have been taken, the next decision is
to where. The final feeding destination appears to be decided when migration begins.
Where anadromous and lacustrine­adfluvial individuals can migrate both through other
lakes and rivers without evidence of stopping, and since both mortality and energy ex­
penditure are increased with migration, it is expected that they will not continue their mi­
gration unless they are programmed that way. The juvenile metabolic requirements and
growth rate, shows importance in determining if the individual remains resident or be­
come fluvial­adfluvial or anadromous. A low metabolic rate result in individuals to remain
in their natal tributaries as their metabolic needs is satisfied with enough food. Individu­
als with higher metabolic needs, migrate until the destination maintains their growth. The
migration distance may also be influenced by energy status, shorter distance migrants
are more lipid depleted than long distance. Fish with higher energy depletion terminates
their migration at the earliest increased feeding opportunity. Studies have suggested that

2.1.2
The Journey Home
An out migration also requires a return migration for spawning, generally to the individ­
uals natal river. They use a strategy called natal philopatry or homing, which results in
distinct genetic structuring between populations. Homing is an evolutionary mechanism
where individuals increase their fitness by increased likelihood of finding suitable partners
and habitats during reproduction and the development of local adaptations. It is a com­
plex behavioral and physiological process, that are not fully understood, but shows that
individuals from a population have great accuracy to return to their natal environment,
often the same stretch of river where they hatched. Several mechanism effects its suc­
cess where juveniles are imprinted to the cues of their natal river and the use of earth’s
magnetic field during long distance migration (Källo 2023).

Historical Status of Smolt Population in Danish Waters
In Denmark all rivers, streams, lakes and brackish wetlands have been influenced by
human activities. This anthropogenic impact have affected most fish stocks (G. H. Ras­
mussen 2007). Since there are no natural obstacles in Danish river systems, it is assumed
that most river systems had brown trout populations after the last glacial period, which mi­
grated from areas in Europe (Gorm Heilskov Rasmussen and Stig Pedersen 2017). 887
river systems in Denmark originally had wild trout populations with an estimated annual
smolt production of 2.6 million. In 1900 the number of river systems reduced to 250, and
the lowest recorded number in 1960 with only 176 rivers systems that spawned trout,
with a smolt production of 0.177 million (G. H. Rasmussen 2007). This reduction was
the cause of human impacts that led to habitat loss, due to river regulations, migratory
barriers, untreated sewage and agricultural runoff that polluted streams and reduced the
water quality and oxygen levels. This decline have since been mitigated by improving
sewage treatment and pollution, efforts in restoration such as removing barriers and habi­
tat improvement (Gorm Heilskov Rasmussen and Stig Pedersen 2017). Surveys by DTU
Aqua confirm that that the wild trout population is rising, especially in river systems with
restoration efforts (DTU Aqua n.d.). By 2014 the trout population had more than doubled
to approximately 371 systems (Gorm Heilskov Rasmussen and Stig Pedersen 2017). An­
other study found that the number of streams had increased to 406. This recovery shows
the importance of successful habitat restoration, improved accessibility, spawning oppor­
tunities, pollution control and fishing restrictions can have on reviving trout populations
(Höjesjö et al. 2017).

Historical Status Funen
In the late 20th century, the streams on Funen were in poor shape, blocked by mill

2.4
Predation
2.4.1
Predation Pressure During Migration
When smolt migrate from freshwater to the marine environment, they experience high
mortality. The environment they enter is unknown and different from their natal streams,
exposing the smolt to new stressors and predators (Dieperink, S. Pedersen, and M. Ped­
ersen 2001). The smolts undergo osmoregulatory changes and changed metabolic de­
mands in order to manage the shift from freshwater to saline marine conditions. These
changes, while important for their success, temporarily affect their swimming performance
and ability to avoid predators. The maladaptive anti­predatory behavior hypothesis, sug­
gest that osmotic stress, together with the presence of predators, induces a stress re­
sponse that affects the normal anti­predatory behavior, and increases the risk of predation
within 24 hours of exposure to salt water (Dieperink, Bak, et al. 2002). Smolt migrations
often concentrate the fish through confined areas, such as river mouths, estuaries, and
shallow coastal waters that attract and concentrate predators. These shallow waters can
also force the smolts close to the surface making them visible to avian predators. Partic­
ularly cormorants and grey herons are known to take advantage of the narrow passage­
ways, resulting in high predation rates.
Telemetry studies in Denmark and Scandinavia have shown that there is heavy preda­
tion on migrating smolt during early marine migration, with most predation occurring within
the first 48 hours after entering saltwater (Dieperink, S. Pedersen, and M. Pedersen 2001;
Jepsen, Skov, et al. 2014). Their vulnerability to predation is influenced by origin and size,
where the hatchery reared smolts experience higher mortality compared to wild smolts.
Hatchery smolts, having spawned in a protected environment, lack experience with preda­
tors, while wild smolt have developed greater environmental awareness and behavior to

avoid predators, and demonstrate higher survival rates during migration (Dieperink, S.
Pedersen, and M. Pedersen 2001). Larger fish have higher probability of survival, due
to increased swimming performance, osmoregulatory ability and sensory perception. If
predation only removes fish that would have died from competition for food or habitat, it
has little impact on the population. But for smolt, where marine mortality is density inde­
pendent, any extra loss of smolts directly reduces the number of adults returning to spawn

2.4.2
Piscivorous Fish Predation on Smolt
Smolts are subjected to predation from a variety of piscivorous fish, with some predators
adapting their behavior to exploit this seasonal food source. Research on predation by
pikeperch and pike on smolts during their downstream migration through a reservoir found
shifts in predator behavior. Female pikeperch, spent more time near the outlet during the
smolt run in May, compared to other times of the year. Of the 20 tagged smolt monitored,
four were eaten by pike in the river, 12 smolts reached the reservoir, were six got eaten by
pike, five by pikeperch and only one smolt made it to the sea. The study also suggest that
the majority of female pikeperch and a few female pike have adjusted their behavior to hunt
smolt during the smolt run, where analyses of the stomach contents of 46 pikeperch and
193 pike, during spring, showed that trout smolt were important prey and constituted 70%
of the total pikeperch consumption and 30 % of the pike consumption (Jepsen, Susanne
Pedersen, and Thorstad 2000).
2.4.3
Mammal Predation on Smolt
Several mammal species are known to prey on smolt in Denmark, including otters, mink,
and seals. These predators can affect trout populations particularly during migration and
after stocking. Otters are opportunistic predators, while their general diet consist of prey
that are easy to catch, they are known to prey on trout, especially after stocking. A study
in Trend Å, found that 65 % of stocked brown trout, were eaten within weeks, most likely
by otters. Another study in Skals Å, only showed trout made up 1­8 % of the otters food
consumption, but the days after stocking this consumption increased to 33 %. The results
suggest that stocked trout are vulnerable to otters especially if other prey is limited, but
wild trout are less affected (Jepsen, Skov, et al. 2014). The American mink, an invasive
species in Denmark, is known to prey on fish, including trout. Studies suggest that trout
only make up a small part of the mink’s diet and have no significant impact on wild trout
populations (Jepsen, Skov, et al. 2014). Seals typically hunt in saltwater, but have been
observed to enter rivers during trout spawning seasons, where harbor seals have been
documented catching sea trout 20 km upstream. Grey seals, which are larger are known
to hunt sea trout, and international studies suggest that seal predation can significantly
impact migrating salmonoid in confined areas and passages (Jepsen, Skov, et al. 2014).

Stone Reefs
2.5.1
What Are Reefs and Where Do They Come From?
Reefs are some of the worlds most species rich habitats, reefs in temperate waters are
home to a large amount of benthic species. And provide substrate for epifouling organ­
isms to settle and grow, they increase available hiding spaces for mobile organisms, im­
pact predator­prey relationships and further increase habitat heterogeneity, compared to
a sandy bottom. Reefs found in temperate sandy bottom environments, can more than
double the local biodiversity and increase the local biomass up to a hundred fold (Coolen
2017). In Europe, natural reefs are recognized for their importance for biodiversity and
the European Union has defined natural reefs as protected habitats in its Habitat Directive
(Coolen 2017). The natural stone reefs in Denmark were formed during the last glacial
period, were the glacial movements and melting were responsible for depositing materials
that form the seabed. Erosion due to changes in water levels following the glacial period,
shaped the reefs that we know today (Dahl et al. 2016).
Based on their origin, reefs can be classified into biogenic, geogenic, and anthropogenic,
from this point on referred to as artificial reef. Biogenic reefs are formed by the accu­
mulation and structural activity of living organism, especially sessile reef building organ­
isms. These include a diverse range of taxa, such as calcareous algae, corals, polychaete
worms, oysters, mussels, barnacles, bryozoans, and sponges. These organisms secrete
calcium carbonate or other structural materials that form complex three dimensional habi­
tats over time. Biogenic reefs are often highly productive and support rich biological com­

munities. In Danish and other temperate waters, examples include blue mussels beds.
(Coolen 2017).
Geogenic reefs, on the other hand, are of geological origin and are composed of non liv­
ing mineral substrates. These typically include rocks, boulders, and gravel beds. These
hard bottom features contrast with the predominantly soft, sandy or muddy seabeds of the
region, providing critical habitat for macroalgae, invertebrates, and fish species (Coolen
2017).
Artificial reefs are man made structures placed in the marine environment intentionally,
to enhance habitat and biodiversity, or accidentally, as in the case of shipwrecks or dis­
carded infrastructure (Coolen 2017). Ecologically, these structures function similarly to
natural reefs. When well designed or opportunistically colonized, artificial reefs can sup­
port ecological functions such as fish aggregation, larval settlement, and macroalgae
growth, contributing to local biodiversity (Coolen 2017). The motivation for constructing
artificial reefs other than the ecological functions can be to promote recreational, commer­
cial, and socioeconomic benefits, such as diving, sports fishing, enhancing commercial
fisheries or even tourism and coastal development. In some cases, artificial reefs are
secondary outcomes of infrastructure installations, such as coastal defense structures,
harbor piers, navigation buoys, floating pontoons, offshore wind turbine foundations, or
oil and gas platforms (Dahl et al. 2016).
2.5.2
Stone Reefs and Habitat Degradation
In Denmark and parts of Scandinavia, many natural stone reefs were heavily exploited
as sources of construction material throughout the 20th century. Large boulders were
removed for use in buildings and infrastructure (Interreg Baltic Sea Region 2024), with an
estimated 8.3 million m3 of stone removed, resulting in the loss of approximately 55 km2
of reef habitat, roughly the size of Fanø island (DTU Aqua 2023). By 2010, seabed boul­
der extraction was banned, but the historical removal had already caused severe habitat
degradation (Dahl et al. 2016).
This extraction flattened and destroyed the complex three­dimensional structures, leading
to reduced shelter and surface area for marine life. Macroalgae forests lost substrate to
attach to, and the deepened seabed reduced light availability for benthic vegetation. As
plant biomass declined, it was replaced by opportunistic algae that further degraded habi­
tat quality. The loss of hard substrate also meant the disappearance of biogenic structures
such as mussel (Støttrup et al. 2014). When large boulders were gone, smaller stones
became more vulnerable to storms and currents, accelerating erosion. These changes
shifted habitats from complex reef systems to bare sand or gravel bottoms, a transforma­
tion correlated with declines in biodiversity and a simplified ecosystem (Casabona et al.
2024). The loss of macroalgae also reduced carbon sequestration and oxygen produc­
tion. The degradation of stone reefs has had significant consequences for fisheries and
biodiversity. Their loss has reduced recruitment and local stock sizes of reef­associated
species, affecting both commercially and non­commercially fished species. Trophic dy­
namics have shifted as predatory fish lost important prey, and historical fishing grounds
for lobster, cod, and other valuable species disappeared (Støttrup et al. 2014).
Reef restoration has been shown to reverse some of these impacts. In Denmark, the
“Blue Reef” project at Læsø Trindel added 60,000 of m3 boulders to a historically mined
site in 2008. Four years later, monitoring showed a 6–8 fold increase in benthic fauna
biomass and a 4–6 fold increase in individual abundance compared to pre­restoration

of anoxic events (Støttrup et al. 2014). The physical presence of reefs also attenuates

2.5.4
Colonization Timeline and Ecological Maturity of Artificial Stone
Reefs
While artificial reefs can be deployed to restore habitats, the speed at which they reach
ecological maturity is not fully known. Understanding the timeline for species coloniza­
tion and community development can help assess their short and long­term potential and
value compared to natural reefs. Newly deployed reefs start as bare rocks and initially
host very low biodiversity. Over time, they are gradually colonized by a range of ma­
rine organisms, but this process occurs at different rates depending on the type of flora
and fauna. The speed of colonization is influenced by factors such as temperature and
closeness to natural habitats. Tropical reefs often experience faster colonization and suc­
cession compared to temperate reefs (RGV Reef 2025).
In general short term development happens fast, with microbial biofilm, that coat the stone
surfaces within hours and days. During the first weeks to months after deployment, the
biofilm attracts plankton and microscopic larvae of marine invertebrates and algae begin
to grow on the surfaces creating the first generation of reef inhabitants (RGV Reef 2025).
A study done in temperate waters in Chile found that after 3 months, the majority of the
reef was still bare rock, but barnacles covered 11 % and by 8 months after deployment, a
higher variety of invertebrates and algae had appeared and settled on the reef structures.
Over the first 1­2 years the species richness continues to increase, as more slow growing
sessile invertebrates begin to establish themselves and the community shifts to include
bigger structure forming invertebrates. After 22 months the artificial reef in Chile was
dominated by macroalgae and had attracted macro­invertebrates such as crabs, shrimp,
sea stars and different mollusks (Toledo et al. 2020). After the two years artificial reefs
typically have a resident community of mobile invertebrates similar to natural reefs. A
study done in a subtropical estuary on an oyster reef restoration found the species com­
position and biomass of benthic invertebrates had developed and resembled natural reef
conditions after 20­22 months (Jud and Layman 2020).
Other studies have indicated that under favorable conditions an artificial reef can achieve
similar biodiversity and biomass after 3­5 years. However, slow­recruiting or long lived
species may be underrepresented on young artificial reefs, and require 10+ years to match
the same presence and abundance of a natural reef. A long­term study in the Red Sea
found that a decade after deployment the reef still had lower coral species and cover com­
pared to natural reefs in the same region (Perkol­Finkel and Benayahu 2005). Another
study that investigated trophic complexity, to investigate if the food web of an artificial reef
resembled that of a natural reef, found that at least 8 years was needed before nutrient
cycling, predator­prey and host­parasite dynamics mirrors that of a natural reef (Moore
et al. 2023).

Materials and Methods
4.1
Data collection: Night Snorkel Transects
Objective: The purpose of the night snorkel transects is to systematically count small
sea trout (smolt), cod, and flatfish to assess their presence in relation to the stone reef in
front of Varbjerg Harbor.
The fieldwork was carried out during late April and May, aligning with the migration period
of smolt. Two separate studies were set up using night snorkeling transects. Study 1, is
a replication of the surveys done in 2022 pre­deployment of the reef. where the dates of
the transects was kept consistent with previous dates to facilitate reliable comparisons.
A total of 40 transects are established, 20 on each side, see Figure 4.1a. Study 2, was
conducted to investigate how the smolt used the reef, and if they ventured away from the
stones. A total of 40 transects was done, 10 on each side, and 20 transects in the middle
of the reef see Figure 4.1b. All transects were conducted at night as smolt are more
active and easily observed during this time. The snorkel transects begin after sunset and
conclude before 02:30 AM, to ensure uniform sampling conditions. The transects are
perpendicular to the reef and spaced seven meters apart. The shallowest transect start
at a depth of approximately 50 cm near the coast, with subsequent transects extending
offshore to a depth of approximately 3 meters at a distance of 140 meters from the shore.
Each transect measures 25 meters in length and is marked by buoys at both ends, secured
with anchors.

4.1.1
Preparation and Execution
During the daylight hours preceding the survey, the transects were deployed in prepara­
tion for the night’s data collection. Here the selected transects was distributed across both
shallow and deep areas each night to prevent bias in the dataset. To facilitate navigation,
bouys were placed at both ends of the planned transects, providing snorkelers with clear
orientation points. All equipment, including underwater lights and data sheets, was tested
and prepared before nightfall. Before participating in data collection, all snorkelers went
through training to ensure accurate observations. Each participant completed 10 prac­
tice transects in daylight, including four at deep water sites where diving was necessary.
Additionally, two practice transects were conducted at night to familiarize snorkelers with
low light conditions. A key component of training involves developing an accurate per­
ception of distances underwater, ensuring that fish are counted only within one meter on
either side of the transect, therefore the training transects involved a 2 meter long object,
so the participants got a feeling for distances underwater at different depths. All training
transects was timed, and additional training transects were conducted until the participant
were consistent in completing a transect at approximately 5 minutes interval.

Two snorkelers conducted the survey each night, with one person stationed on land to
record observations and monitor safety. The snorkeler conducting the transect carefully
approaches the starting buoy, with minimal disturbance of the reef, and signals readiness
using a three blink flashlight signal. Upon receiving confirmation from the person on land,
the snorkeler begins a slow, 5 minute swim along the transect, counting only the fish
observed within the designated survey area. To minimize disruption, transects was always
surveyed in sequence from the shallowest to the deepest, and alternating between the
reef sides. Once the transect is completed, the snorkeler sends a two­blink signal, and
the data is immediately recorded upon returning to shore. Between each transect, a 20­
minute waiting period is done before the next transect begins, ensuring that fish behavior
is not disturbed. If two consecutive transects are less than 21 meters apart, an additional
5 minute delay per missing seven meters was done. The process got repeated throughout
the night until all planned transects have been completed or the 02:30 AM cutoff time was
reached.

4.2
Data Processing and Statistical Methodology
This section describes the data processing steps applied to the field survey datasets
from 2022, 2025 and Study 2. Data were prepared in MATLAB, and all statistical meth­
ods included, such as test for normality, homogeneity of variances, parametric and non­
parametric tests, correlation analyses, and logistic regression follow the guidance pre­
sented in Discovering Statistics Using SPSS by Field (2009).
4.2.1
Tidal Correction
Depth measurements for transects T1 to T40 were recored on May 20’th between the
14:30 and 16:15, while depths for transects M1 to M20 were measured May 26’th at ap­
proximately 16:00. Because tidal fluctuations can cause discrepancies between the mea­
sured depths and the actual depth during night transects, a tidal correction was applied.
Tidal height data were obtained from the Danish Meteorological Institute (DMI) tide tables
for the relevant location and dates (Danmarks Meteorologiske Institut 2025). The tidal
height for each transect’s exact date and time was estimated under an assumption of a
linear change in water level between the four tide vales provided daily by DMI. From these
values, a tidal change per minute was calculated, which was then used to interpolate the
tidal height for each specific transect time. The recorded depth measurements were then
adjusted by adding or subtracting the difference between the tide height at the measure­
ment time and the tide height at the transect survey time. This correction ensured that all
depth data reflected comparable tidal conditions, improving the consistency and accuracy
of depth related analyses across the datasets.
4.2.2
Statistical Analysis
Data from transect surveys were collected from three datasets. 2022 pre­deployment
survey, 2025 post­deployment survey, and 2025 Study 2, survey. Data included species
counts (smolts, flatfish, cod), transect ID’s, depth measurements, distance to shore, as
well as the date and time of each transect. All data were imported into MATLAB and for­
matted for analysis.
Transects were classified into Side A, Side B, and Middle M for spatial comparison within
and between the datasets. Species counts were transformed into binary presence/ab­
sence variables. Where transects with observed (counts > 0) got assigned a value of 1
(presence), and transects with (counts < 0) got assigned 0 (absence). This was performed
separately for smolts and flatfish across all datasets. For each species and dataset, the
proportion of transects with presence was estimated using binomial distribution, with 95
% confidence intervals (CI). Proportions were calculated for the overall presence for each
dataset and presence for each spatial category. Fischer’s Exact Tests were used to test
for significant differences in species presence between reef sides (A vs B, A vs M, B vs
M) and across surveys years and study designs (2022 vs 2025) and (2025 vs Study 2).
This test was chosen because the subgroup sample sizes were small, were Fisher’s Ex­
act Test provide accurate results regardless of sample size.
To identify potential habitat associations influencing smolt presence, two variables were
considered, average transect depth and transect distance from shore. Descriptive statis­
tics mean and median values, were calculated separately for transects with presence
and absence. This descriptive comparison provided an overview of how presence varied
along depth gradients and spatial distance to the shoreline. Before conducting statis­
tical comparisons of the two variables between transects with and without presence, it
was assessed whether the assumption of equal variances between groups was met. To
evaluate this, Levene’s Test for homogeneity of variances was used separately to each

21
==PAGE:32==
variable, across each study. The results of this test helped determine whether a para­
metric or non­parametric statistical approach should be used for further analyses. Based
on the outcomes of the normality and variance assessments, a dual testing strategy was
chosen.
1. Mann­Whitney U Test: Non­Parametric
2. Two­sample t­test: Parametric
Although Levene’s Test showed most groups, expect for the depth variable in study 2,
did not differ significantly in variance (p>0.05), and t­test are valid under those conditions,
the sample sizes were small. Therefore, the Mann­Whitney U test was selected as the
primary test for interpretation across all comparisons. The t­test was still included as a
supplementary analysis in cases where Levene’s test supported equal variances, provid­
ing an additional perspective on group differences.
To evaluate whether transect depth and distance to shore were significantly associated
with species presence three complementary statistical approaches were applied to each
variable across the three datasets.
1. Point­Biserial Correlation
2. Spearman Rank Correlation
3. Logistic Regression
These three methods were selected to ensure a robust understanding of the variables
influences. Point­Biserial and Spearman correlations provided insights into linear and
monotonic trends, while logistic regression allowed for probabilistic interpretation of the
relationship between variable gradients and presence.

Across all three datasets (2022, 2025 and Study 2), mean and median depths were con­

depths, with mean depths of 121.41 cm versus 161.24 cm, and median depths of 115.48
cm versus 169.6 cm for transects with and without smolts, respectively.
In 2025 the mean and median depths were slightly shallower in transects where smolts
were observed, but did not reach a significant difference (Mann­Whitney p = 0.545, t­test
p = 0.416). In contrast, there was evidence of a statistically significant relationship be­

tically significant difference in mean depths according to the t­test (p = 0.023), while the

unequal variances between groups (p = 0.0125), the assumption of equal variances re­

Discussion
This study investigated whether the implementation of an artificial stone reef could in­
crease the presence of smolts in a coastal marine environment. Across the surveys, smolt
presence was higher in 2025 than in 2022, suggesting that the reef provided improved
habitat conditions, likely through increased structural complexity, refuge from predators,
and enhanced foraging opportunities.
In 2022, before reef deployment, smolts showed a significant preference for shallow and
nearshore transects. This distribution likely reflected the limited shelter and feeding op­
portunities available in the featureless study area, making nearshore waters the most
favorable habitat. In contrast, the 2025 post­deployment survey observed smolts across
a wider range of transects, with no significant relationship between smolt presence and
either depth or distance to shore. This shift indicates that the reef reduced smolts depen­
dence on nearshore areas by providing additional habitat features, enabling a broader
use of the surrounding environment.
Study 2, conducted independently and close to the same dates as the 2025 study. Was
designed to validate the effect of the reef and explore how smolts interacts with the sur­
rounding habitat, it aimed to assess if the smolts remained close to the reef structures
or if they also ventured into the adjacent areas without stones. The study showed a high
presence of smolt in the middle transects, which were located over sandy bottom between
the side reefs. This suggest that smolt also utilize the nearby open habitats, potentially
as feeding grounds, which indicates that the reef not only attract smolt, but also facilitate
a broader habitat use in the surrounding area, perhaps due to an overall improvement
of habitat quality, safety and resource availability. Study 2 also showed a correlation be­
tween depth and smolt presence, as well as a tendency for smolts to be located closer to
the shore, similar as the patterns observed in the 2022 survey. This suggest that when
smolt are away from reef structures, they tend to remain in shallow, nearshore waters,
perhaps as a strategy to optimize foraging while reducing predation. Their silvery col­
oration can provide camouflage against the water surface, making them less visible to
avian predators, and reduced maneuverability for larger predators in the shallow water.
Together, the studies show a consistent narrative, that the artificial stone reef appears to
have increased the presence of smolt and their behavior, by enhancing habitat conditions
that meet their ecological needs during migration and entry into the marine environment.
The increased presence of smolts following the reef’s implementation, combined with ev­
idence of broader spatial use of the surrounding area, suggests that the reef not only
serves as an attractor but also enables smolts to safely expand their habitat use beyond
the immediate reef structures. These findings highlight the potential of artificial reefs as a
valuable tool in habitat restoration efforts aimed at supporting sea trout populations during
vulnerable life stages, by creating environments that promote both refuge and functional
connectivity across coastal zones.
6.0.1
Attraction VS. Production
Artificial reefs are commonly used with the aim of increasing fish abundance at deploy­
ment sites, either by attracting individuals from surrounding areas or by enhancing fish
production. However, do the reef structures produce new fish biomass or simply redis­
tribute existing individuals. This is an important distinction in evaluating the ecological
34
Impact of Artificial Smolt Reefs
==PAGE:45==
value of the artificial reef (Bohnsack 1989). Two primary hypothesis, can explain the in­
crease in fish presence at artificial reefs (Brickhill, Lee, and Connolly 2005).
1. The attraction hypothesis
2. The production hypothesis
The attraction hypothesis suggest that artificial reefs primarily serve as aggregation sites,
concentrating fish from nearby habitats as a result of their complex physical structure. This
behavior is driven by the fish’s natural tendency to seek out structured environments. This
hypothesis suggest, that the total biomass in the ecosystem remains unchanged and the
reef simply redistributes fish. Attraction may result in increased predation efficiency for
predators, and increased fishing pressure, under the false assumption of higher produc­
tion, which can potentially counteract any ecological benefits (Bohnsack 1989; Brickhill,
Lee, and Connolly 2005). Adversely, the production hypothesis suggest that artificial
reefs enhance the carrying capacity of the environment by creating new habitat that sup­
ports functions, such as feeding, refuge and reproduction, ultimately resulting in higher
fish populations. Multiply reef mechanisms support this hypothesis. Including provision
of additional food through the development of substrate organisms, improved feeding ef­
ficiency, leading to faster growth rates. Shelter from predation and increased recruitment
success, by offering suitable habitat, that was otherwise unavailable (Bohnsack 1989;
Brickhill, Lee, and Connolly 2005).
In the context of the results, the higher presence of smolts in 2025 from both studies,
where Study 2 also showed distribution away from the reef over sandy bottom. Suggest
that the reef help increase production rather than aggregating fish. The absence of cor­
relation between smolt presence and distance to shore in 2025, combined with the high
smolt presence in both the new structures and the surrounding area, indicates that the
reef has improved overall habitat quality, and supports the idea that mechanisms such as
refuge from predation and improved food availability have increased, which have resulted
in the behavioral shift and potentially increased production. However attraction and pro­
duction are not mutually exclusive, while the reef may promote the production of some
fish, they can attract and concentrate other fish.
6.0.2
Why a high Presence on sand bottom?
The results from Study 2, revealed that smolt presence was not only found at the reef
structures but also in the middle of the reef. This behavior indicates that the ecological
influence of the reef are not limited to its physical structure but also extend to the surround­
ing area. These results are consistent with previous findings from estuarine and coastal
studies on juvenile salmonids, that show smolt use the structured habitat for refuge, but
regularly venture into the adjacent featureless area for foraging. Where this behavior is
a trade­off between feeding opportunities and predator avoidance, as the open area of­
fer more prey, and the structured habitat provide shelter (Sharpe et al. 2019; Dumbauld,
Hosack, and Bosley 2015).
6.0.3
Why no increase in Presence for flatfish across the studies?
Across the three surveys (2022, 2025, and Study 2), flatfish presence showed no signifi­
cant increase after the reef was deployed. The proportions of transects with smolt pres­
ence remained stable across the surveys, and statistical comparisons revealed no signifi­
cant differences between the surveys or reef sides 5.2. These finding indicate that flatfish
may not experience the same benefit from the added structural complexity as smolts.
This can be explained by the behavior and predator avoidance strategies of flatfish. Ju­
venile flatfish rely on spatial refuges in shallow water rather than structural hiding spots

==PAGE:46==
and their predation pressure is less effectively reduced by structural complexity than by
shallow water depth. A study on predation risk for juvenile flatfish found that predation
risk for winter flounder decreased sharply in shallow habitats below 1 m, regardless of
physical complexity. Their coloration and burial behavior in sandy substrates allow them
to avoid detection by predators even in structural simple environments. This camouflage,
where flatfish can use the sand bottom as refuge and shelter is a contrast to species
like smolts, with their silvery color and higher activity that make them more visible and
therefore more dependent on physical refuge from predators. Flatfish also forage in open
sandy or muddy habitats, where the reef may not enhance, the availability of their feeding
grounds, flatfish also settle and remain in habitats that provide suitable sediment grain
size for burial and ambush feeding, suggesting that their spatial distribution is influenced
by sediment rather than reef structures (Manderson et al. 2004).
Observer bias may also have contributed to lack of increase in presence, as flatfish re­
main motionless and blend in with the seabed, where the smolt are more mobile and
conspicuous, this makes them more difficult to spot during transects, which could have
lead to an underestimation of flatfish abundance.
6.0.4
Observer Bias: Night Snorkeling Method
The use of night snorkeling transects as a method of surveying presence of reef associ­
ated fish species can introduce bias, primarily due to the influence of the observer on fish
behavior and detection. Fish can alter their natural activity in response to human pres­
ence, some fish can be scared and evade, while others are drawn toward the torch light.
Both behaviors were observed during the fieldwork, where certain individuals approached
the snorkeler’s torch, while others actively avoided and fled the transect area. This can
affect and potentially alter presence estimates. Previous studies using remote camera
systems have shown that fish often avoid snorkelers, leading to underestimations from the
visual surveys (Lindfield, McIlwain, and Harvey 2014). The experience of the snorkeler
also plays a role. In the case of this field work, an inexperienced snorkeler might over­
look flatfish, hidden in the sand or accidentally double count fish that swim around and
orbit the snorkeler, over­ or underestimate transect width and incorrectly count fish that
are outside the transect parameters, or leave out counts of fish that should have been
included. Studies have documented observer variability in species identification, transect
width estimations, and swimming speed (Emslie et al. 2018). The physical presence of
the snorkeler can also cause disturbance to the reef. Kicking at the surface can create
turbulence and noise that might cause fish to flee the transect area. Sediment can also
be suspended, especially in shallow transects where the snorkeler is close to the bottom.
Environmental conditions can also limit the accuracy and consistency of the transects.
Weather conditions can cause increased turbidity and reduce visibility, waves can make
it difficult to follow the transect lines as they disorient the snorkeler and potentially direct
the observer off course.
To reduce bias and minimize disturbance during this study, measures were implemented.
Transects training was conducted both during the day and night, across shallow and
deeper transects. These exercises increased consistency, as swimming speed was timed
to match the duration of five minutes per transects. and the participants became familiar
with the method. During the training transects a 2 meter long object was used to help
the snorkeler get a feel of the visual estimation of the transect widths at different depths.
In addition to training, environmental conditions were also carefully considered. Surveys
were conducted on nights with relatively calm weather and clear visibility. To further limit
potential reef disruption, after each transect, observers alternated between reef sides,

and a minimum waiting period of 20 minutes was done before conducting the next tran­
sect. To minimize potential disturbance from previous transects, each new transect was
positioned at least 21 meters away on the same reef side. In case where this spacing
was not possible, and the distance was less than 21 meters, an additional delay fo 10
minutes was used for every 7 meters to allow the reef to settle and fish return to normal
behavior. Regardless of the mentioned precautions and training exercises, it should be
acknowledged that the elimination of bias cannot be completely ensured. The presence
of snorkelers, affect fish behavior to some extent, and environmental variability such as
water clarity and wave action can further influence fish counts. While the method can pro­
vide insight into the spatial presence of the reef associated fish, and their behavior and
habitat use, it may not produce a fully accurate estimate of the fish presence. As such,
the results should be interpreted with an understanding of the method’s limitations, and
should be supplemented with acoustic tracking or remote video observations to validate
and perhaps also expand upon the findings observed through snorkeling transects.
6.0.5
Future Potential of the reef
The results of this study, combined with the literature knowledge of artificial reef coloniza­
tion dynamics, indicates that the reef is still in an early to intermediate stage of its eco­
logical development. From figure 6.1 it is shown that short­term colonization have taken
place, with marine organisms having established themselves on the reef. The observed
presence of macroalgae, crustaceans, and other macroinvertebrates species align with
the successional patterns described for other artificial reefs studies within its first couple
of years since 2022­2023 (Toledo et al. 2020; Jud and Layman 2020). Even though no
analysis of macroalgae coverage and invertebrate species and other sessile organism
took place. High variety of marine organisms was observed during the fieldwork, and the
stones was almost entirely covered in algae and other plant species, as well as the re­
ported presence of two cods, compared to the pre­deployment survey of 2022, where zero
cod was observed. This shows that the reefs ecological stage already provides structural
refuge and foraging opportunities for smolt and other fish. During snorkeling trips in the
adjacent areas it was observed that those rocks had a higher cover of sessile organisms,
especially blue mussels, which was less dominant on the smolt reef at Varbjerg. So even
though the reef is already a functioning habitat, it has not yet reached maturity, but if
the the colonization continues at the rates observed in similar systems, the reef could,
within the next few years, approach levels of biomass and species richness comparable
to those of nearby natural reefs (RGV Reef 2025). However, before the reef achieves full
ecological maturity, where the community composition, functional diversity and its food
web approaches or is identical to those of natural reefs, is likely to require a longer time­
frame, and based on other long­term studies, this establishment can take up to a decade
or even longer (Perkol­Finkel and Benayahu 2005). Overall the current and future poten­
tial of the reef looks promising as can already be seen in the increase in both presence,
and spatial behavior of the smolt fish.

Simple model of the reefs effects on smolt
The smolt reef model developed in this study is a deterministic population structure used
to investigate how the deployment of an artificial stone reef at Varbjerg could potentially in­
fluence smolt survival and annual production over time. It incorporates ecological factors
such as growth of the habitats carrying capacity, as the reef matures, changes in pre­
dation rates as the habitat increases in complexity and smolt density. Recruitment from
returning adults with and without density dependence. While this model is simplified, its
objective is to provide an illustrative overview for looking at the potential long­term effects
of the reef and complement the empirical observations.

Several assumptions have been made to simplify the model. It treats the coastal zone as

is assumed to be constant, with only the predation rates fluctuating as a function of reef

recruitment follows a Beverton­Holt function in the density­dependent scenario, where re­
cruitment increases with spawner abundance, but plateaus at high densities.
These assumptions can lead to limitations, as environmental variability, extreme events,
and fluctuations in predation, carrying capacity, or smolt production are not reflected,
resulting in perhaps unrealistic population trajectories. Many parameters are based on lit­
erature values or estimates rather than site specific data, limiting its predictive accuracy.
Despite these limitations, the model in the context of this study translates into qualitative
ecological expectations, such as the assumption that reef structures reduce predation and
increase the habitats carrying capacity, and investigates scenario comparisons between
reef and no reef effects, and between density and non density­dependent recruitment. It
showcases the potential benefits of the reef under simple assumptions both short­ and
long­term on the smolt population. This model should only be seen as a way to show the
concept of the reef and not as a predictive forecast on smolt production.

Conclusion
This study investigates whether the implementation of an artificial stone reef at Varbjerg
Harbor could improve habitats conditions for sea trout smolts during their migration from
freshwater to the marine environment. By using pre­deployment data from 2022, and
post­deployment surveys from 2025, supported by an independent Study 2 conducted in
the same period, the patterns in smolt presence relative to depth, distance to shore, and
habitat use was asssessed.
The findings indicate that the reef has had a significant positive effect on smolt pres­
ence. In 2022 smolts were confined to shallow, nearshore transects, suggesting that in
the absence of structures and refuge, the shoreline provided the best combination of shel­
ter, food and predator avoidance. By 2025, smolt presence increased across the study
area, with no significant relationship between presence and depth or distance to shore.
This shift implies that the reef’s three dimensional complexity, offering crevices and other
hiding opportunities, enabled smolts to expand their use of the habitat, and reduced their
need on nearshore areas.
Study 2 confirmed this expanded habitat use, by showing high smolt presence not only
at the stones but also in the middle transects over sandy bottom. Where these open
habitats are likely used as foraging grounds, with the reef providing nearby shelter from
predators. Both Study 2 and the 2022 survey found a correlation between smolt presence
and nearshore shallow waters when fish were away from the reef structures, likely due to
camouflage benefits, food availability, and reduced access for large predators.
The consistent patterns across datasets suggest that the reef has enhanced habitat qual­
ity both within and beyond its physical structure. This improvement appears to support
the production hypothesis for artificial reefs, whereby new habitat increases the systems
carrying capacity and contributes to population growth, rather than simply attracting in­
dividuals from other areas. While attraction cannot be fully dismissed, the distribution of
smolts in the studies suggest ecological benefits that supports the production hypothesis
mechanisms.
The study also shows the value of combining field surveys with conceptual ecological
modeling. The deterministic smolt population model provided a simplified view of how
reefs changes in both carrying capacity, predation rates, and recruitment could influence
long­term population trajectories. This combination of empirical field data and theoretical
modeling improves the interpretation of field results.
Overall, the results support the use of artificial stone reefs as a habitat restoration method
for increasing sea trout populations. By creating structurally complex environments that
facilitate a broader and safer use of the coastal zone. Continued monitoring over multiple
years, coupled with tagging and telemetry studies, will be essential to fully quantify the
reef’s contribution to smolt production.
