Abstract 
 
This thesis investigates the potential of two novel materials, Witepsol H15 and 
gelatin methacryloyl (GelMA), to fabricate micropyramids (MPs) for oral drug delivery.  
The research meticulously optimizes fabrication methods to achieve precise control over 
the MPs geometry, ensuring accurate replication of the mold dimensions.  The study also 
explores the stability of the MPs under various storage conditions, finding that those stored 
in the fridge and freezer exhibit excellent stability over two months, while those stored at 
room temperature show signs of degradation.     
 
Furthermore, the research delves into the drug loading capacity and release kinetics of these 
MPs, using curcumin as a model hydrophobic drug for Witepsol H15 MPs and Rhodamine 
B as a model hydrophilic drug for GelMA MPs.  The findings demonstrate the potential of 
these materials for controlled and sustained drug release, with Witepsol H15 showing 
temperature-dependent release and GelMA's release rate being modulated by crosslinking.  
The results suggest that these MPs could improve drug bioavailability and therapeutic 
outcomes.     
 
By elucidating the size, stability, drug loading capacity, and release kinetics of these novel 
MPs, this thesis provides valuable insights for the design and optimization of future oral 
drug delivery systems.  The research contributes to the advancement of oral drug delivery 
by exploring the potential of Witepsol H15 and GelMA MPs for enhanced drug delivery.

1 Introduction 
This thesis investigates the potential of two novel materials, Witepsol H15 and 
gelatin methacryloyl (GelMA), for the fabrication of microparticles designed for oral drug 
delivery. By recognizing the limitations of conventional oral dosage forms, this research 
explores the capacity of these materials to load both hydrophilic and hydrophobic drugs, 
facilitating their targeted and sustained release within the gastrointestinal tract. 
Witepsol H15, a semisynthetic lipid that melts at body temperature, is hypothesized to 
enable sustained drug release upon reaching the physiological environment of the body. On 
the other hand, GelMA, a biocompatible and biodegradable hydrogel, not having a behavior 
that responds to body temperature, will be used to achieve controlled drug release through 
the manipulation of its crosslinking density. The study will encompass the fabrication and 
characterization of microparticles with pyramidal shapes composed of each material, which 
from now on we will call micropyramids (MPs), evaluating their drug loading capacity, 
release kinetics, and stability under simulated gastrointestinal conditions. In vitro studies 
will assess the performance of these MPs in delivering both hydrophilic and hydrophobic 
model drugs, with a focus on achieving sustained release profiles. 
This research intends to contribute to the advancement of oral drug delivery systems by 
demonstrating the feasibility and efficacy of Witepsol H15 and GelMA MPs for enhanced 
drug delivery. The results show potential to improve therapeutic outcomes by optimizing 
drug bioavailability, minimizing side effects, and promoting patient adherence through less 
frequent dosing.

2 State of the Art 
 
2.1 Oral drug delivery 
 
Oral drug delivery is a cornerstone of modern medicine due to its patient 
convenience and acceptance [1]. The non-invasive nature of swallowing a pill or capsule 
significantly increases patient compliance, especially when compared to parenteral routes 
of administration, which are often associated with pain and discomfort [2]. This, paired 
with the flexibility of oral dosage forms – ranging from simple tablets and capsules to more 
complex systems, like microcontainers and microparticles – makes oral delivery a highly 
adaptable approach for a wide range of therapeutic needs [3].  
However, the journey of a drug from ingestion to systemic circulation is full of challenges. 
First, the drug must navigate the dynamic environment of the gastrointestinal tract, where 
factors such as pH, enzymatic activity, and motility can considerably affect its solubility, 
stability and, ultimately, its absorption [4]. Moreover, even after a successful absorption, 
the drug faces the obstacle of first-pass metabolism, where a notable portion may be 
metabolized by the liver before reaching its intended site of action [5] 
These challenges have spurred the evolution of innovative drug delivery systems targeted at 
improving bioavailability and therapeutic efficacy. Controlled-release formulations, for 
instance, can modulate drug release over time, securing a sustained therapeutic effect while 
minimizing side effects [6]. Microcontainers and microparticles, with their ability to 
encapsulate and protect drugs from degradation, offer an optimistic approach for delivering 
sensitive molecules like peptides and proteins, which are particularly susceptible to 
enzymatic degradation in the GI tract [7]. Furthermore, targeted delivery systems can 
bypass the harsh conditions of the upper GI tract, improving drug absorption and 
minimizing systemic side effects [8]. 
In order to make progress in the field of drug delivery and improve patient outcomes, it is 
fundamental to understand the mechanisms and capabilities of these novel oral drug 
delivery systems. By dealing with the restrictions of traditional oral dosage forms, these 
technologies have the possibility to hugely transform the way in which we administer 
medications and treat a wide range of diseases [9] 
 
2.2 Microparticles and microcontainers for oral drug delivery 
Microparticles and microcontainers have proved to be promising drug delivery 
systems to overcome some of the challenges of the oral route of administration, and 
upgrade the therapeutic outcomes of several drugs[10]  
On one hand, microparticles are small particles, typically ranging from 1 to 1000 μm in 
size, that can be produced from a variety of materials, including polymers, lipids, and

proteins [11]. They can encapsulate drugs within their matrix or adsorb them onto their 
surface. On the other hand, microcontainers are essentially hollow microparticles with a 
defined cavity that can be filled with drugs [12]. Usually, these are made from 
biodegradable polymers, and can be engineered to release drugs in a controlled and 
sustained way [13]. 
Microparticles and microcontainers have the ability to increase the solubility of poorly 
soluble drugs by encapsulating them in a hydrophilic matrix, which constitutes one of their 
major benefits for oral drug delivery [14]. Furthermore, these systems can protect drugs 
from the harsh environment of the GI tract, preventing degradation and ensuring that a 
sufficient amount of the drug reaches its target site[15]. This protection is especially 
important for sensitive drugs, like proteins and peptides, that are susceptible to enzymatic 
degradation in the GI tract [16]. Besides, another important benefit of these systems is their 
ability to control drug release: by modifying the composition and structure of 
microparticles and microcontainers, researchers have been able to achieve sustained drug 
release, lowering the frequency of administration and improving patient adherence [17]. 
Moreover, these systems can be customized for targeted drug delivery if they are 
conjugated with specific ligands that bind to receptors in the desired area of the GI tract 
[13]. 
In spite of the diverse advantages, the use of the mentioned systems in oral drug delivery 
implies some challenges. Firstly, scaling up the manufacturing process to fabricate these 
systems on a large scale can be complex and costly [18] 
Secondly, in order to guarantee biocompatibility and avoid any potential toxicity, the 
selection of materials used for production needs to be carefully made [19]. Lastly, the 
regulatory approval process for these types of systems can also be rigorous and time-
consuming [20]. Nonetheless, we believe that the potential benefits of these systems for 
improving drug delivery and therapeutic outcomes outweigh the obstacles. 
When designing effective microparticle and microcontainer systems for oral drug delivery, 
the size and shape of these particles can seriously influence their drug loading capacity, 
release kinetics, and biodistribution [21].  
 
The size of microparticles significantly influences their behavior and effectiveness. Larger 
microparticles, such as those exceeding 200 µm, can exhibit slower gastric emptying rates, 
potentially delaying drug absorption [22]. Conversely, microparticles smaller than 10 µm 
tend to have reduced absorption and increased elimination due to rapid intestinal 
transit[23]. Furthermore, microparticle size plays a crucial role in controlling drug release 
rates, with larger particles generally exhibiting slower release profiles [24]. 
The shape of microcontainers plays a crucial role in their interaction with the mucosal layer 
of the colon, influencing drug absorption. Christfort et al., (2020) compared cylindrical, 
cubic, and triangular microcontainers loaded with Amoxicilin and found that those with

corners and edges, such as the cubic and triangular shapes, exhibited greater mucoadhesion 
than the cylindrical ones. This enhanced mucoadhesion can lead to improved drug 
absorption [4].  
 
 
2.3 Examples of microparticles and microcontainers in oral drug delivery: 
Microparticles and microcontainers have been successfully used to deliver a wide 
range of therapeutic agents, including small molecule drugs, proteins, peptides, vaccines, 
and even genes[12].  
For example, they have been employed to improve the bioavailability of poorly soluble 
drugs, like Acyclovir, by protecting them from degradation while raising their solubility. 
Other mentioned drugs are Griseofulvin (antifungal), Ibuprofen (NSAID), Paclitaxel 
(anticancer), Cyclosporine A (immunosuppressant) [13]. 
In the case of proteins and peptides, Microcontainers/microparticles provide protection 
from enzymatic degradation in the GI tract and can even enable targeted delivery to specific 
cells or tissues [25]. One promising application is oral delivery of vaccines. For instance, 
CTB (Cholera toxin B subunit (CTB) as a potential vaccine antigen that can be delivered 
orally using chitosan microparticles. CTB is a non-toxic portion of the cholera toxin that 
can induce an immune response against cholera [16]. 
Finally, these systems have demonstrated potential in gene therapy, where they can be 
utilized to deliver genes to specific cells in the GI tract for therapeutic purposes. Verma, P. 
et al. (2021) discuss the potential of nanoparticles for delivering genes to intestinal cells for 
treating inflammatory bowel disease (IBD) [26]. 
 
2.4 Thermoresponsive microparticles 
Thermoresponsive microparticles are emerging as a very interesting tool in drug 
delivery due to their ability to react to temperature changes [27]. These particles, typically 
made from polymers with a lower critical solution temperature (LCST), undergo a phase 
transition when the temperature rises above the LCST [28]. This shift can trigger changes 
in the microparticle (shrinking, swelling, or altering its permeability) making controlled 
drug release possible [29]. 
This thermoresponsive behavior provides different advantages for drug delivery. It allows 
for targeted drug release at locations with elevated temperatures, like tumor sites, and also 
enables controlled release rates by adjusting the polymer composition and LCST [30]. This 
can improve the bioavailability of drugs with short half-lives or poor solubility and 
minimize side effects by concentrating drug release to the desired area [31]. 
Thermoresponsive microparticles are generally implemented in diverse fields, like cancer 
therapy, treatment of infectious diseases, wound healing, and vaccine delivery [32].

2.5 Witepsol H15 
 
 
Witepsol H15 is a semisynthetic lipid composed of a mixture of triglycerides of 
saturated fatty acids, primarily lauric acid, with smaller amounts of myristic and palmitic 
acids. These fatty acids are derived from vegetable sources - mainly coconut and palm 
kernel oils.  
It exists as a solid at room temperature but melts at body temperature (33.5 - 35.5 °C), 
making it suitable for use as a suppository base, carriers in capsules, ointments, creams and 
binders in tablets.  
Witepsol H15 is recognized for its stability, compatibility with various drugs, and ease of 
use in pharmaceutical manufacturing [33]. 
 
2.6 Gelatin methacryloyl (GelMA) 
 
Gelatin methacryloyl (GelMA) is a versatile hydrogel derived from gelatin that has 
gained prominence in drug delivery because of its biocompatibility, biodegradability, and 
ease of modification - and it is inherently safe for biological applications since it comes 
from a natural material [34]. Based on the research of Luo et al. (2019), one of the key 
applications of GelMA in drug delivery is the creation of microneedles (MNs) [34]. These 
MNs are designed to penetrate the skin's outer layer (stratum corneum) without causing 
pain, delivering drugs directly to the underlying tissues. Their investigation has focused on 
using GelMA MNs for sustained drug release, where the drug is released gradually as the 
GelMA degrades, offering a controlled and extended therapeutic effect. 
Beyond MNs, GelMA's applications in drug delivery extend to several areas. It can be used 
to create 3D-printed scaffolds that encapsulate drugs, providing targeted and sustained 
release within a specific tissue or organ [35]. Moreover, GelMA-based nanoparticles’ 
ability to encapsulate drugs enhances their stability and cellular uptake, while GelMA-
incorporated wound dressings can provide a sustained release of drugs that promote healing 
[36]. Also, injectable GelMA hydrogels involve another minimally invasive approach, that 
allows direct administration of drugs into tissues with controlled release profiles [37]. 
The release rate of drugs from GelMA MNs can be controlled by adjusting the degree of 
crosslinking in the GelMA. Crosslinking is a process that creates chemical bonds between 
the polymer chains in GelMA, making the material more resistant to degradation. By 
controlling the crosslinking density, it is possible to fine-tune the rate at which the GelMA 
degrades and, therefore, the rate at which the drug is released [38].

3 Aims and Hypothesis 
 
 
This master thesis aims to develop and characterize novel micropyramid-based drug 
delivery systems for oral administration. Focusing on GelMA and Witepsol H15, the 
research will optimize fabrication methods for precise control over micropyramid geometry 
and investigate their capacity to load and release hydrophilic and hydrophobic model drugs. 
Drug release profiles will be evaluated under various conditions to assess thermoresponsive 
behavior and controlled and sustained release characteristics. Furthermore, the thesis will 
explore modulating drug release from GelMA MPs by adjusting the degree of crosslinking. 
This research will contribute to a deeper understanding of these systems and their potential 
for controlled oral drug delivery, identifying key parameters for future validation and 
development. 
 
 
THE HYPOTHESIS ARE THE FOLLOWING: 
 
H1: Witepsol H15 MPs will exhibit temperature-dependent release of the encapsulated 
hydrophobic drug (Curcumin), with increased release rates at higher temperatures.  
H2: GelMA MPs will demonstrate controlled release of the encapsulated hydrophilic drug 
(Rhodamine B), and the release rate can be modulated by controlling the degree of 
crosslinking within the GelMA matrix.

4.2 GelMa preparation protocol  
The traditional preparation method of GelMA was put forward by Van Den Bulcke 
et al.  in 2000 [39] . In general, this method involved a direct reaction between gelatin and 
methacrylic anhydride (MA). 
The preparation process (Figure 1) involves the following steps: Firstly, gelatin is dissolved 
in phosphate-buffered saline (PBS, pH = 7.4, 50°C) to create a 10% (w/v) solution. After 
the gelatin is completely dissolved, MA is added to the mixture (0.8 ml per g of gelatin) 
and stirred at 500 rpm for 2 hours. Secondly, the reaction is stopped by diluting the solution 
with PBS at a 1:5 ratio. After 30 minutes, the diluted solution is transferred to a dialysis 
membrane (12-14 kDa) against distilled water for 5-7 days (40°C with continuous stirring). 
The water is changed several times a day to remove unreacted MA and byproducts. 
Finally, the solution is freeze-dried. This is done by placing it in a freezer at -80°C 
overnight. Then, the frozen solution is transferred to a freeze dryer and lyophilized until a 
dry, white porous foam is obtained. This usually takes several days. Store the lyophilized 
GelMA in a freezer (-20°C) until further use.

4.3 Mold preparation protocol  
For the MPs fabrication, a silicon master, featuring a 2D array of regular square 
pyramidal cavities, was produced using anisotropic potassium hydroxide (KOH) etching of 
silicon. An inverse replica was fabricated in polymethylmethacrylate (PMMA) by hot 
embossing. These two steps were carried out by a third party in a clean room environment. 
To enable the fabrication of MPs with two different sizes, two separate inverse PMMA 
replicas were fabricated. The first yielded MPs with a base of 100 μm and a height of 70 
μm (will be referred to as "100 μm pyramids"), while the other produced MPs with a base 
of 50 μm and a height of 35 μm (will be referred to as "50 μm pyramids”). 
The final mold was prepared by pouring and curing polydimethylsiloxane (PDMS) on the 
PMMA replica as follows: 15 mL of PDMS base is mixed with 1.6 mL of curing agent in a 
50 mL tube using a spatula. To eliminate air bubbles, the mixture is degassed in a 
desiccator under vacuum for 40 minutes. 
The PMMA replica is then assembled by securing it in the holder. This involves screwing 
together three pieces: a top piece with a square opening (sealed with Parafilm), the replica 
itself with the cavities facing upwards, and a flat bottom piece. The assembled replica is 
placed in a larger Petri dish to contain any spills. The degassed PDMS mixture is carefully 
poured into the PMMA replica, filling the cavities. 
Finally, the entire setup is transferred to an oven and cured at 40°C overnight. After curing, 
the PMMA replica is disassembled from the holder. The newly formed PDMS mold is then 
carefully peeled away. Both the PMMA replica and the Petri dish are then cleaned with 
water and dried thoroughly, ready for reuse.

4.4 Casting of Micropyramids  
The fabrication of MPs using the prepared PDMS molds involves a casting process 
that varies slightly depending on the chosen material: GelMA or Witepsol H15. 
4.4.1 Lipid Preparation: 
Prior to casting, the lipid Witepsol H15, which is provided in a solid phase by the 
manufacturer, needs to be melted. An appropriate amount of the lipid is placed in an 
Eppendorf tube and heated in an oven at a temperature above its melting point (35.5°C) for 
a sufficient period to ensure complete liquefaction. 
4.4.2 GelMA Preparation: 
A pre-weighed amount of GelMA, synthesized according to the previously 
described protocol, is placed in an Eppendorf tube. To this, 20% (w/v) of Milli-Q water is 
added. The tube is then placed in an oven at 40°C overnight to allow the GelMA to fully 
hydrate and form a homogenous solution. 
4.4.3Casting Procedure: 
Once the chosen material (either the melted lipid or the hydrated GelMA solution) 
is prepared, it is deposited, using a pipette , onto the face of the PDMS mold containing the 
array of pyramidal cavities (Figure 2). Using a spatula, the material is carefully and evenly 
spread to ensure complete filling of all cavities, avoiding the formation of air bubbles or 
voids. Once all cavities are filled, the mold is placed in a refrigerator for 15 minutes, 
allowing the material to dry or solidify completely. After the drying/solidification process 
is complete, the MPs can be carefully removed from the molds.

4.5 Removal methods 
Two distinct methods were employed to remove the fabricated MPs from the PDMS 
molds. 
4.5.1 Adhesive Method 
This method utilizes an adhesive tape (e.g., carbon tape) to extract the MPs from the 
mold cavities. Since the MPs are oriented with their bases facing down in the mold, the 
adhesive tape is applied to the base of the pyramids. Upon removal of the tape, the MPs 
remain adhered to the tape, now oriented with their apexes facing upwards. This method is 
beneficial for microscopic examination, as they are presented in an easily accessible 
orientation on the tape surface. 
 4.5.2 Double-Layer Method 
This method involves a sequential casting procedure. Once the initial layer of MPs 
has completely dried or solidified within the mold, a second layer of the same material is 
cast on top. This creates a supporting layer that facilitates the removal of the entire 
structure. It is crucial to ensure that the first layer is completely dry before applying the 
second layer. After the second layer has dried or solidified, the entire structure can be 
carefully peeled away from the mold. This method is suitable for applications where the 
MPs are required in bulk or where a supporting layer is beneficial for further processing or 
handling. 
4.6 Mold cleaning 
Following the demolding of the MPs, the PDMS molds are placed in a suitable tube. 
Isopropanol is added to the tube until the molds are completely submerged. The tube is then 
sealed with a lid and placed in a sonicator bath. Sonication is performed at low temperature 
for one hour to effectively remove any residual material adhering to the mold surfaces. 
After sonication, the molds are carefully removed from the isopropanol, which now 
contains the dislodged material. The molds are then placed on absorbent paper to facilitate 
the evaporation of any remaining isopropanol. This drying step ensures that the molds are 
thoroughly cleaned and free of any contaminants, rendering them suitable for subsequent 
casting cycles 
4.7 Characterization and Optimization 
 
4.7.1 Micropyramids size measurements 
To ensure the consistent fabrication of MPs with precise dimensions, a systematic 
approach was employed to determine the optimal volume of casting material required. This 
involved an iterative process of casting MPs using various volumes of material and 
==PAGE:18==
Microparticles fabrication for oral drug delivery 
 
17 
 
examining the resulting structures using a HITACHI TM3030Plus scanning electron 
microscopy (SEM). This examination aimed to identify any incomplete filling of the mold 
cavities or the formation of undesired shapes. The iterative process was repeated until an 
optimal volume of material was identified, ensuring the complete filling of the cavities 
while minimizing material waste and maintaining the desired well-defined pyramidal 
geometries. 
The fabricated MPs placed on carbon tape, were imaged using a SEM with an accelerating 
voltage of 15 kV. The magnification was adjusted as needed for each image. ImageJ 
software was used to analyze the acquired images and determine the average size of the 
MPs. To calculate the base size, images were taken from the top, while images at a 90° 
angle were used to calculate the height. 20 MPs from each mold size (100 μm and 50 μm) 
were measured to calculate their average volumes. 
Knowing the dimensions of the replica molds, the obtained volumes were compared with 
the theoretical volumes to verify complete filling of the pyramids. In addition to the volume 
measurements, a visual inspection was performed to corroborate the use of the correct 
amount of material. This inspection ensured that the pyramids were well-defined, with 
sharp corners, free of holes or pores, and that the entire mold was filled with pyramids, 
leaving no empty spaces. 
 
4.8 Stability Test Microparticles 
To assess the stability of the synthesized MPs, a comprehensive evaluation was 
conducted to confirm their ability to maintain designed characteristics and properties over a 
defined storage period. To determine the impact of storage conditions on stability, the study 
compared particles stored in three different environments: freezer, refrigerator, and room 
temperature. This comparison allowed for the identification of the optimal storage 
conditions for preserving the integrity and functionality of the MPs. 
Samples were analyzed weekly for two months using a scanning electron microscope 
(SEM) to observe any morphological or structural variations. 
Stability was evaluated using parameters including morphological integrity, surface 
characteristics, and particle size. Morphological assessments included signs of deformation, 
curved shapes, or other significant structural changes. Surface properties were examined for

4.9.3 Model drugs  
Curcumin as a Model Drug for Witepsol H15 Micropyramids 
Curcumin, a hydrophobic drug, is a suitable choice for the Witepsol H15 MPs for 
several reasons. Witepsol H15 is a lipid-based material, and curcumin, being lipophilic, 
exhibits good solubility in lipid formulations [40]. This ensures that curcumin can be 
effectively incorporated into the Witepsol H15 matrix without compromising the structural 
integrity of the MPs. Curcumin possesses inherent fluorescence properties, allowing for 
easy tracking and visualization during experiments [41]. This is crucial for assessing the 
distribution and release of the drug from the MPs. Curcumin's behavior in the Witepsol 
H15 MPs could be indicative of how other poorly water-soluble drugs would behave in this 
delivery system [42].  
This can be extrapolated to understand the potential of Witepsol H15 for delivering a wide 
range of lipophilic drugs, including those with therapeutic applications such as antibiotics, 
anticancer drugs, and anti-inflammatory agents [43]. 
Rhodamine B as a Model Drug for GelMa Micropyramids 
Rhodamine B is an appropriate choice for the GelMa MPs for several reasons. 
GelMa is a hydrophilic material derived from gelatin. Rhodamine B, being a hydrophilic 
dye, is compatible with the GelMa matrix, ensuring proper encapsulation and distribution 
within the MPs [39]. Rhodamine B is a fluorescent dye, enabling easy visualization and 
tracking during experiments [44]. 
Rhodamine's behavior in the GelMa MPs could be indicative of how other hydrophilic 
drugs would behave in this delivery system. This can be extrapolated to understand the 
potential of GelMa for delivering a wide range of hydrophilic drugs, including those with 
therapeutic applications such as proteins, nucleic acids, and even cells [45].

4.11 Controlling Drug Release from GelMA Micropyramids via Crosslinking 
Due to the lack of thermoresponsive behavior observed in Witepsol H15 MPs, to 
gain control over the drug release rate from GelMA MPs, the degree of crosslinking within 
the GelMA matrix was modified, based on Luo et al. (2019) findings [34]. This study 
demonstrated that the release rate of drugs from GelMA microstructures can be modulated 
by adjusting the crosslinking density. 
To achieve this, a photoinitiator (PI), called 2-Hydroxy-2-methylpropiophenon, was 
incorporated into the GelMA formulation, and the MPs were exposed to UV light to induce 
crosslinking. Two PI concentrations were investigated, 1% and 2% (w/w), relative to the 
mass of water used in the GelMA pre-solution (4.4.2 GelMA Preparation:). The extent of 
crosslinking is directly proportional to the UV exposure time: longer exposure times result 
in higher crosslinking densities and, consequently, slower drug release. 
Several methods were explored to optimize the photocrosslinking process: 
4.11.1 Method 1 
The PI was added to the diluted GelMA before casting. This solution was then 
exposed to UV light for varying durations. After UV exposure, the drug was added, and the 
standard casting process was followed. 
4.11.2 Method 2 
The PI was added to the GelMA with drug solution, which was then cast into the 
molds. The samples, while still in the mold, were exposed to UV light for various 
durations. Finally, the MPs were removed from the mold. 
 
4.11.3 Method 3 
 
The PI was added to the GelMA with the drug solution and was exposed to UV 
light for different time intervals before proceeding with the casting process. 
==PAGE:22==
Microparticles fabrication for oral drug delivery 
 
21 
 
 
4.11.4 Method 4 
The PI was added to the GelMA with drug solution, which was then cast and the 
MPs were removed from the mold using the double layer method. The MPs layers (without 
the mold) were then exposed to UV light for different durations. 
This systematic investigation of different photocrosslinking strategies allowed for the 
identification of the most effective method for controlling the drug release rate from 
GelMA MPs. The optimal method will be selected based on its ability to achieve the 
desired release kinetics while maintaining the structural integrity of the MPs

Drug Loading Capacity (DLC) 
 
Witepsol H15 pyramids 
To determine the DLC of our Witepsol H15 pyramids, we analyzed the curcumin 
content of the pyramids using a plate reader. Based on a calibration curve generated with 
known curcumin concentrations, we determined that the pyramids contained an average of 
0.04 mg of curcumin. To express this as a DLC, we divided this value by the total mass of 
the pyramids: 
0.04 mg / 30 mg = 1.3 μg/mg 
==PAGE:32==
Microparticles fabrication for oral drug delivery

This value of 1.3 μg/mg represents the amount of curcumin loaded per milligram of 
Witepsol H15. 
Tollemeto et al. (2024) demonstrated that curcumin can achieve full dissolution and 
homogeneous distribution within Witepsol H15. Therefore, we could suppose that the 
curcumin is also fully dissolved and homogeneously distributed within our Witepsol H15 
pyramids [33]. 
However, the solubility of curcumin in Witepsol H15, although higher than its solubility in 
water (approximately 0.00575 mg/mL), is not unlimited. 
It is possible that the initial concentration of 1 mg/mL used in our fabrication exceeded the 
solubility limit of curcumin in Witepsol H15, even in the melted state, or that as the lipid 
solidified, the solubility of curcumin decreased, causing some of it to precipitate. This 
could have led to a lower-than-expected DLC. 
It is also possible that not all of the curcumin dissolved completely in the lipid at the 
beginning, even with heating. Some curcumin particles may have remained suspended in 
the molten lipid without truly dissolving. As the lipid cooled and solidified, the solubility of 
the curcumin in the lipid would have decreased further. This could have caused additional 
curcumin to precipitate out, contributing to the lower-than-expected DLC. 
The particle size of the curcumin used can influence its dissolution rate, with smaller 
particles generally dissolving more readily due to their larger surface area. Adequate 
mixing during the fabrication process is also crucial to ensure proper dissolution. 
Insufficient mixing could lead to uneven distribution and incomplete dissolution of 
curcumin [46]. 
 
Despite the ability of Witepsol H15 to load curcumin, as demonstrated by the obtained 
DLC value (1.3 μg/mg) , further optimization by adjusting parameters such as the initial 
curcumin concentration, ensuring complete dissolution, and controlling fabrication 
variables could potentially lead to improved drug loading.

80% of the Rhodamine B already released at the initiation of the experiment. Complete 
release was achieved within approximately 30 minutes. This outcome indicates that a 2-
second UV exposure is insufficient to establish a controlled release system, as the majority 
of the drug is released almost immediately. 
4-second UV exposure: This condition demonstrated a more gradual release pattern, 
commencing with approximately 50% release and slowly increasing to 60% during the first 
hour. Following this initial phase, the release rate increased but still maintained a controlled 
behavior compared to the 2-second exposure. 
6-second UV exposure: This condition displayed the lowest initial release, starting around 
20% and gradually increasing to slightly over 40% during the first hour. Similar to the 4-
second exposure, the release rate accelerated after the first hour while retaining a controlled 
profile. 
The results unequivocally demonstrate that increasing the crosslinking time correlates with 
a slower and more sustained release of Rhodamine B from the GelMA MPs. The 2-second 
UV exposure serves as a clear indication that inadequate crosslinking results in rapid and 
uncontrolled drug release.  The contrary, the 4-second and 6-second exposures exhibit more 
desirable release profiles, characterized by slower initial release and a more controlled 
overall pattern. 
This ability to modulate the release rate by adjusting the crosslinking time highlights the 
potential of Method 3 for achieving controlled drug delivery. While longer UV exposure 
times might further enhance the control over the release profile, this method presents a 
practical limitation. The viscosity of the GelMA mixture increases significantly with 
exposure time, making it challenging to cast the solution into the molds for durations 
exceeding 6 seconds. This constraint restricts the range of crosslinking conditions that can 
be explored using Method 3. 
Method 4: 
 
In this method, the PI was added to the GelMA/Rhodamine B solution for casting, 
and the MPs were removed using the second layer method ( 
4.11.4 Method 4). The obtained MPs layers were then exposed to UV light for 
different durations (5, 10, and 15 minutes) to investigate the effect of crosslinking time on 
Rhodamine B release.

The 5-minute and 10-minute UV exposures exhibited similar initial release profiles, starting 
slightly above 40% and following a comparable trend. In contrast, the 15-minute exposure 
began with a very low release, around 5%, followed by a rapid increase to almost 50% 
between 15 and 30 minutes. After the initial 30 minutes, all three conditions displayed a 
very slow increase in Rhodamine B release, forming a plateau and indicating a significantly 
reduced release rate. This plateau effect suggests that a high degree of crosslinking might 
hinder the diffusion of Rhodamine B from the MPs. Therefore, it is essential to find a 
balance where the exposure time allows for a controlled and sustained release without 
excessively impeding drug diffusion. 
Results in Method 4 suggest that crosslinking the MPs layers after removal from the mold 
provides a more effective means of controlling drug release compared to the other methods. 
This could be attributed to several factors, including uniform crosslinking, reduced 
interference from the mold, and mainly, due to the possibility of exposing the MPs layers to 
longer crosslinking times. This flexibility in exposure time allows for greater control over 
the degree of crosslinking and, therefore, over the drug release kinetics.

The observation that the 15-minute exposure resulted in a very low initial release, followed 
by a rapid increase, further supports the notion that a high degree of crosslinking might 
initially hinder drug diffusion. However, the eventual catch-up with the other conditions 
indicates that the drug is still able to diffuse, albeit at a slower rate. This highlights a 
potential trade-off between achieving a low initial burst release and ensuring sufficient drug 
release over time. 
Overall, Method 4 proved to be the most effective approach for investigating the impact of 
crosslinking on drug release from GelMA MPs. This method offers the flexibility to 
explore a wider range of crosslinking conditions without the limitations encountered in 
previous methods. By crosslinking the MPs layers directly, it is possible to achieve a more 
controlled and predictable release profile. This method warrants further investigation and 
optimization to fine-tune the release kinetics for specific drug delivery applications, 
particularly by exploring the balance between crosslinking density and drug diffusion. 
Results in Methods 3 and 4 showed that the release of Rhodamine B from GelMA MPs can 
be controlled by varying the duration of UV exposure, which is consistent with the findings 
of Luo et al. (2019). In both cases, increasing the crosslinking time resulted in a slower 
release of the drug. This is likely due to the fact that crosslinking makes the GelMA matrix 
denser and less porous, which hinders the diffusion of the drug out of the matrix [34].  
This ability to control drug release from GelMA MPs has several important implications for 
drug delivery applications. First, it allows for the development of sustained-release drug 
delivery systems, which can provide a more consistent therapeutic effect over a longer 
period. This is particularly beneficial for drugs that have a short half-life or that require 
frequent dosing [13]. Second, it allows for the localized delivery of drugs within the GI 
tract. By controlling the crosslinking density of the GelMA matrix, it is possible to design 
MPs that release the drug in a controlled manner, potentially at specific regions within the 
GI tract. This can help to reduce side effects and improve the efficacy of treatment [51]. 
Finally, this delivery method can be used to deliver a wide range of drugs, including small 
molecules, proteins, and nucleic acids [45] This versatility makes it a promising platform 
for the development of new and innovative drug delivery systems.

6. Conclusion 
The results demonstrated the successful fabrication of MPs with precise dimensions 
using both GelMA and Witepsol H15. The MPs were fabricated with specific sizes of 50 
µm and 100 µm, a size range that ensures timely gastric emptying, provides adequate 
residence time in the intestine for absorption, and allows for controlled drug release.     
The fabrication method employed in this research is not only straightforward but also 
exhibits scalability, a significant advantage over other fabrication methods for oral drug 
delivery systems that often face challenges with poor scalability. 
The stability of the MPs was evaluated, with those stored in the fridge and freezer 
exhibiting excellent stability over two months. However, MPs stored at room temperature 
showed signs of degradation over time, with GelMA MPs being more stable than Witepsol 
H15.    
The drug loading capacity (DLC) for curcumin in Witepsol H15 MPs was found to be 1.3 
µg/mg, indicating that Witepsol H15 can effectively load curcumin. The DLC for 
Rhodamine B in GelMA MPs was found to be 32.5%, suggesting that GelMA is an 
effective material for loading and delivering Rhodamine B. Further optimization could 
potentially lead to improved drug loading capacity for both materials.  
Witepsol H15 MPs exhibited a controlled release of curcumin at 37°C, achieving complete 
release within 90 minutes, which suggests their potential as an effective drug delivery 
system for controlled release at physiological conditions. GelMA MPs showed a rapid 
release of Rhodamine B at 37°C, with almost 90% released within the first 30 minutes. 
However, this rapid release was improved by adjusting the crosslinking, highlighting the 
potential of this method for achieving controlled drug delivery. The release of Rhodamine 
B from GelMA MPs can be controlled by varying the duration of UV exposure, with longer 
exposure times resulting in slower release. 
In conclusion, this thesis successfully developed and characterized MPs -based drug 
delivery systems using GelMA and Witepsol H15. The findings on size, stability, drug 
loading capacity, and release kinetics can inform the design and optimization of future drug 
delivery systems using these materials.

7. Future Prospects 
This thesis has demonstrated the potential of Witepsol H15 and GelMA MPs for 
oral drug delivery, but further research is needed to fully optimize and validate these 
systems for specific drug delivery applications.    
Optimizing drug loading: Further research could focus on optimizing the drug loading 
capacity of both Witepsol H15 and GelMA MPs by adjusting parameters such as the initial 
drug concentration, ensuring complete dissolution, and controlling fabrication variables. To 
gain a better understanding of the drug dissolution and precipitation behavior within the 
matrix, techniques like XRPD, Raman spectroscopy, and confocal microscopy could be 
employed. Separately, conducting solubility studies to determine the maximum solubility 
of the drug in the chosen material at different temperatures could help optimize the drug 
concentration used in the fabrication process for improved drug loading.    
Investigating different drugs: The current study focused on curcumin and Rhodamine B as 
model drugs. Future studies could investigate the encapsulation and release of other drugs, 
including vaccines, small molecules, proteins, and nucleic acids.    
In vivo studies: The current study was limited to in vitro experiments. Future studies should 
evaluate the in vivo performance of these MPs using cell or animal models to assess their 
efficacy and safety for oral drug delivery.    
By addressing these future prospects, the research can further advance the development of 
Witepsol H15 and GelMA MPs as promising platforms for oral drug delivery, potentially 
leading to improved therapeutic outcomes for a wide range of diseases.
