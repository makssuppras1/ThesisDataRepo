Abstract
This thesis investigates a LIDAR-assisted wind turbine controller (LiAC) that uses upstream wind mea-
surements to enable proactive pitch control, implemented in the HAWC2 simulation environment via
Python. Turbulence coherence and wind advection are analyzed using LES data to understand how to
design a low-pass filter and how to accurately estimates its delay. Actuator dynamics are also modeled
to assess system latency. Simulations show that the LiAC reduces rotor speed variability and structural
loads compared to a baseline controller, supporting ongoing efforts in the CONTINUE project.

Introduction
The control system is a crucial component of modern wind turbines, significantly influencing their be-
havior and performance. The primary objective of the control system is to maximize energy production
while ensuring that both ultimate and fatigue loads remain within the design limits [10]. One promising
approach to enhancing controller performance is the LIDAR-assisted controller (LiAC), which refines
the controller’s actions by using preview information about incoming wind, estimated through LIDAR
technology.
Traditional controllers operate based on a feedback loop, where the effect of the incoming wind on the
turbine is evaluated, and the turbine’s position is adjusted accordingly. While effective, this approach
introduces a delay in system response, which can lead to excessive loading. In contrast, LiAC systems
can foresee the incoming wind, allowing the turbine to adjust its position before the wind reaches it.
This proactive response minimizes loading on the structure, particularly fatigue loads, thereby reducing
the need for raw materials, lowering CO2 emissions, and cutting costs. An intuitive analogy to illustrate
the paradigm shift introduced by LiAC technology is proposed by Schlipf [18]: traditional controllers are
similar to a person riding a bike, controlling it only by feeling the impacts of the road on the wheels,
whereas in LiAC, the person can also use their vision to avoid obstacles before they occur. This thesis
is part of the CONTINUE project, a collaboration between DTU and Vestas, which focuses on the
development of a LiAC [23].
The starting point of this project is to investigate how preview wind speed estimates from the LIDAR
can be effectively integrated into the control loop. A major challenge in doing so is assessing the quality
of the LIDAR estimates, particularly under conditions with significant vertical wind shear, which affects
the accuracy of the advection speed assumption. To address this, an in-depth study using a Large
Eddy Simulation (LES) dataset is carried out to analyze the relationship between shear and advection
and to evaluate how reliably the LIDAR can predict the incoming wind field.
A second important factor in LIDAR-assisted control is timing. Specifically, the time delay between
when the wind is measured and when the appropriate control action should be applied. This delay
depends not only on the physical properties of the turbine but also on the wind signal itself, particularly
the low-pass filter applied to the LIDAR measurements. As such, a portion of this work is devoted to
analyzing signal behavior in the frequency domain to develop analytical expressions for the effective
delay introduced by filtering and actuator response.
The turbine response is simulated using HAWC2 (Horizontal Axis Wind turbine simulation Code, 2nd
generation), developed by DTU Wind. This tool enables time-domain aeroelastic simulations that in-
clude detailed structural and aerodynamic dynamics. The controller is implemented in Python, and
special attention is given to integrating it smoothly with HAWC2 via the hawc2lib interface.
Two LIDAR configurations are examined in this study. First, a simple idealized wind preview is used
to validate the methodology and build the simulation framework. Then, a more realistic LIDAR system
based on DTU’s Turbulent Field Estimator (tfe) is analyzed, which better replicates real-world LIDAR
behavior. The impact of each system on key performance indicators, such as load reduction and rotor
speed regulation, is evaluated and compared to results from existing literature.

Modern wind turbines are devices that convert the kinetic energy of the wind in electrical energy,
by exploiting the lift force generated by their blades [14]. Figure 2.1 provides a simplified graphical
representation of a wind turbine, highlighting the most important quantities for controlling purposes:
• V0 incoming wind
• θ pitch angle: using hydraulic or electric actuators, the blade can rotate around its longitudinal

• Ωrotational speed
• M aerodynamical moment: generated by the lift and drag forces of the blades, it is counter

• T thrust force: horizontal loading on the turbine generated by the lift and drag forces
Modern wind turbines controlling action can be divided in 3 regions, as depicted by Figure 2.2, each
corresponding to a specific range of incoming wind speeds [3].
• Region 1: before cut-in speed, no power generation as the wind speed is too low.
• Region 2: between cut-in and rated speed, maximum rotor efficiency through torque controlling.
Also called partial load region.
• Region 3: between rated and cut-out speed, pitch control to maintain nominal power and rated
rotational speed. Also called full load region.
For higher wind speeds, the turbine is stopped to avoid mechanical damage, so there is no power
production.

Wind Evolution
The first step in applying LiAC technology is to assess the accuracy of the LIDAR in estimating the
incoming wind and to characterize the relationship between advection speed and turbulence within a
dynamically changing wind field. The key question to address is: given the incoming wind front on a
plane perpendicular to the observer at a certain x-distance, how does the wind evolve in the direction
of the advection speed?
There are several techniques that can be used to quantify wind evolution. One of the most impor-
tant concepts is Taylor’s frozen turbulence hypothesis [22]: turbulence is assumed to stay constant as
it moves downstream with the average wind velocity. Mathematically, it can be expressed by Equa-

2.3
LIDAR technology
Compared to traditional controllers that only react to the wind hitting the turbine, the newly developed
controller has the ability to anticipate incoming wind. This is achieved by using Light Detection and
Ranging (LIDAR) technology. The system emits a beam of coherent light toward a target and detects
the backscattered light. If the target is moving, the frequency of the returning light shifts due to the
Doppler effect, with the shift being proportional to the speed of the target. By directing a LIDAR system
at the aerosols in the air, the incoming wind velocity can be measured [21].
For control applications, LIDAR systems can be either Continuous Wave (CW) or Pulsed, and they can
be positioned on the ground, on the nacelle behind the rotor (nacelle-mounted), or on the hub in the
spinner (hub-mounted). In the CONTINUE project and in this thesis, the focus is on a hub-mounted
Pulsed LIDAR. The optimal firing sequence for LIDAR measurements and the techniques to recon-
struct the wind field are still under investigation, so for the purpose of this study, these parameters will
be assumed based on the findings of [24]. Ideally, the LIDAR system is capable of measuring and
reconstructing the incoming wind speed on a plane perpendicular to the wind direction, at a specific
distance upstream of the turbine. However, the LIDAR’s estimate is not a perfect representation of the
actual incoming wind. Therefore, great attention is given into assessing how reliable these measure-
ments are for control purposes, and determining which components of the signal should be filtered out
using appropriate techniques.
This thesis utilizes HAWC2’s Turbulent Field Estimator (tfe) tool, which simulates LIDAR measurements
and extracts key parameters. Specifically, it provides an estimate of the rotor average wind speed
(RAWS) at a define preview distance. This parameter is then fed into the LiAC controller to enhance
its control strategy.

3.4
Findings
In this chapter, the wind evolution in a LES dataset representing turbulent flow over flat terrain was
characterized. The so-called ’Schlipf’s recipe’ was adapted and applied to compute the time lag, co-
herence, and coherence bandwidth between signals at different streamwise locations. An average
behavior across the control volume was identified: the flow is more turbulent and less coherent near
the ground, exhibits maximum coherence around hub height (150 meters), and coherence decreases
again toward the top of the domain.
A possible explanation for this trend lies in the y-direction velocity component shown in Figure 3.1.
The relatively strong lateral flow at low and high altitudes may reduce the coherence between turbulent
structures, while around hub height, where the V -component is negligible, coherence remains high.
Overall, this study was useful for establishing a reliable method to estimate the threshold frequency
f0.5, which indicates the coherence limit between two wind signals separated by a distance ∆i along
the advection direction i. The approach, adapted from [19], is summarized below and will be referred
to throughout this work as the ’modified Schlipf’s recipe’:
1. Extract the wind preview signals at two spatial location. For a LiAC one signal is the actual
RAWS, the other the LIDAR estimate of the RAWS. The signals must be sufficiently long to
ensure a robust frequency-domain analysis.
2. Estimate the time lag between the signals by identifying the peak of the sliding Pearson correla-
tion.
20
==PAGE:29==
3. Time-align the signals by shifting one of them according to the estimated lag.
4. Compute the coherence between the two aligned signals using Equation 2.7. If multiple signal
pairs are available (e.g., from different realizations), use the averaged coherence formula from
Equation 3.3.
5. Determine the threshold frequency f0.5 as the frequency at which the coherence drops to 0.5.
This procedure is a key step in the development of a LIDAR-assisted control (LiAC) system, as it
provides a quantitative assessment of the quality of the LIDAR wind preview. Practically, it allows the
designer to determine the appropriate cutoff frequency for the low-pass filter used to process the LIDAR
signal before it is used in the control algorithm.

HAWC2 and coupling framework
To perform the aeroelastic simulations needed to evaluate the controller performance, a coupling frame-
work was developed between HAWC2, the Python library hawc2lib, and a custom Python script. This
chapter describes the structure of HAWC2 and hawc2lib to provide the reader with an understanding
of how the software operates, as well as the capabilities and limitations of the developed simulation
framework.
4.1
HAWC2 structure
HAWC2 models the dynamic behavior of wind turbines by integrating structural mechanics, aerodynam-
ics, control systems, and environmental conditions. A key feature of HAWC2 is its modular architecture,
which allows for the integration of custom control strategies through Dynamic Link Libraries (DLLs) [5].
HAWC2’s simulation framework comprises several interconnected modules:
• Structural Module: utilizes flexible multibody dynamics, modeling components such as blades,
tower and drivetrain to accurately captures large deflections and nonlinear behaviors.
• Wind model: characterizes the incoming wind and tower shadow effects. It supports the use
of a Mann turbulence box, which simulates a 3D turbulent wind field based on Taylor’s frozen
turbulence hypothesis. Each Mann box is randomly generated from a specified seed, allowing for
the creation of different but statistically similar turbulence fields by using different seeds.
• Aerodynamic Module: based on Blade Element Momentum (BEM) theory, enhanced to account
for nonlinearities.
• Control Module: integrates user-defined control algorithms via DLLs, allowing for flexible imple-
mentation of turbine control strategies.
The controller of the turbine is coded within several DLLs (’Basic DTU Wind Energy controller’ respon-
sible to compute the torque or pitch demands, ’servo with limits’ to model the pitch actuator dynamics,
etc.). Essentially, these components work as ’black boxes’ which take particular inputs from the HAWC2
core, perform the required operations, and output the quantities back to the HAWC2 core. This is re-
peated for every time step of the simulation. The interface between the program and the user is a ’.htc’
file, which specifies the necessary modules and values. This is the file that needs to be modified for
changing the simulations parameters, such as wind speed or controller gains.
To simulate a LIDAR-assisted CPC in HAWC2 one need, on principle, the following DLLs:
• ’LIDAR.dll’: responsible to compute the LIDAR estimate of the RAWS
• ’feedforward control.dll’ : representing the feedforward control block, uses the LIDAR RAWS to
estimate ˙θFF
• ’modified DTU Wind Energy controller.dll’: modification of the basic controller to allow to insert
the feedforward pitch component as described in Equation 2.16
However, the DLLs need to be coded in Fortran or in C/C++, and their nature of black boxes makes
it difficult to test and implement them. Hence, this work focuses on implementing these operations
in a custom Python code, thanks to the hawc2lib library. Moreover, the ’modified DTU Wind Energy
controller.dll’ is already being developed [7], so this work focuses on Implementing only the first two
components.

HAWC2lib library
Using this library one can run HAWC2 simulations within a Python environment [6]. Essentially, in this

• run the HAWC2 simulation step by step
• add sensors that records a specified variable, for example simulation time or the 3D wind vector

• set a desired variable to a specified value and send it back to the HAWC2 core. Note that such

Filter
This chapter focuses on signal processing and control theory, with particular attention to the develop-
ment and modeling of a filter. The objective is to demonstrate how a filter can be both modeled and
implemented in code, and how to estimate the delay it introduces. In the end, estimates for both Tfilter

5.4
New buffer time
In this chapter, mathematical estimates were provided for two key delay components: Tfilter, which rep-
resents the time shift introduced by the filtering process, and Tactuator, which accounts for the dynamics
of the pitch actuator. While the estimate for Tactuator is relatively accurate, the estimation of Tfilter is more
uncertain. This is because the time delay introduced by the filter depends on the characteristics of the
input signal, and therefore cannot be represented as a constant across all conditions.
To account for this uncertainty, as well as other unmodeled sources of delay, it is useful to introduce
an additional delay term, Tadditional. This leads to the updated buffer time expression shown in Equa-
tion 5.31:

specific LIDAR system being implemented. This will be detailed in the following chapter.

Developed controller
The developed controller can now be introduced. In this work, two different LIDAR systems have been
used, referred to for simplicity as ’plane’ and ’tfe’. The first system, ’plane’, is a basic wind estimation
setup. The second system, ’tfe’, is discussed in the following chapter.
After introducing the LIDAR system used in this chapter, the focus shifts to evaluating the performance
of the developed controller. To do so, 10-minutes simulations are performed, and several performance
indicators are extracted. Note that the simulations begin with a transient phase that is required for
numerical stability and initialization, which is removed from the data before extracting any metrics.
The total duration of each simulation is long enough to allow for a full 10-minute steady-state period.
The main performance indicators considered are the design loads and the rotor speed. These are
compared against a baseline controller where only the feedback loop is active.
The design loads under analysis are defined as follows and are referred to as ”channels”:
• Tower-base loads: moments at the base of the turbine tower. These can be in the fore-aft di-
rection (aligned with the wind, y-direction)(TbFA) or in the side-to-side direction (perpendicular to
the wind, x-direction) (TbSS).
• Blade-root moments: these can occur in the flapwise (BRMflap) or edgewise direction (BRMedge)
of the blade.
The term ”design loads” includes two categories: extreme loads and fatigue loads [2].
• Extreme loads are defined as the maximum load value reached on each channel during the
simulation, multiplied by a safety factor. Since the analysis focuses on the relative change in
loads with respect to the baseline, the safety factor is set to 1.
• Fatigue loads are evaluated using the Damage Equivalent Load (DEL) approach. For each chan-
nel, the load time series is processed using a rainflow counting algorithm, which extracts a single
equivalent load value R that represents the damage from a 10-minute simulation. When multiple
turbulence seeds are used, a cumulative equivalent load Req is calculated using Equation 6.1. In
this formula, m is the W¨ohler exponent, which depends on the material of the component. For
the DTU 10 MW wind turbine, the tower can be considered made out of metal, so m = 4, while
the blades are composite with m = 10.

6.2
Online coherence updating
The final unknown that must be defined before running the 10-minute simulations is the cutoff fre-
quency of the filter. Since this value depends on the incoming wind conditions, it cannot be known in
advance. To address this issue, a measuring phase is introduced between the transient phase and
the steady-state simulation. During this phase, both the RAWS measurement and the LIDAR preview
are collected, and the coherence is calculated in order to estimate f0.5. This ’coherence updating’ pro-
cedure allows for estimating the cutoff frequency without any ’a priori’ information, that is the goal of
this LIDAR implementation. The challenge then becomes determining how long this measuring phase
should last to obtain a sufficiently accurate estimate of the coherence.

for six different seeds. A missing value indicates that the coherence remained below 0.5 across all
frequencies. As one can see, too short measurements do not allow for a reliable estimate, which
appears to stabilize across seeds from 300 seconds onward around the value of 0.12 Hz. This value will
be used in the following for the ’plane’ LIDAR. It should be noted that even the full 10-minute simulations
do not converge to a single f0.5 value across all realizations, highlighting the signal-dependent nature

6.3
Parameters Optimization
In this controller implementation there are several input parameters:
• ∆y: the preview distance at which one places the LIDAR measurement is an essential parameter
for a LIDAR assisted wind turbine controller. Unfortunately, due to the use of the Mann box, char-
acterized by the frozen hypothesis, the ’plane’ LIDAR estimate is independent on this parameter.
Hence ∆y = 100 m will be constant for all simulations.
• f0.5: due to its dependency on the wind, it is measured using the ’online coherence update’
method discussed above.
• Tadditional: this additional time used to compute the buffer time, introduced in the previous chapter,
can be optimized to obtain a higher performing controller.
• reduction in FB gains: in theory, a known disturbance can be effectively compensated if its in-
fluence on the system output is known and invertible [19]. This implies that a controller could
operate using only the feedforward loop, provided the FF block is sufficiently accurate. To explore
this concept, the feedback gains can be reduced, thereby increasing the relative contribution of
the FF component. This approach can also be interpreted as ”making space” for the feedforward
action. A well-tuned feedback controller typically bases its action on the proportional and integral
terms, as shown in Equation 2.4. When a feedforward term is added, as in Equation 2.16, it is
reasonable to expect that the contribution of the original FB terms must be reduced accordingly to
maintain a balanced control response. To do so the multiplication constant p or ’poles multiplier’,
is introduced to modify the proportional and integral gain. In particular, the new proportional gain
is K′
p = pKp and the new integral gain is K′

The parameter p significantly influences the controller performance. Reducing p leads to substantial
load reduction up to a value of 0.3. Decreasing it further weakens the feedback action excessively,
resulting in poor controller performance. In such cases, the turbine’s emergency braking system is
activated, increasing the blade pitch to induce stall. This behavior is evident in Figure 6.6, where the
simulation with p = 0.5 closely follows the baseline, while the simulation with p = 0.1 fails during the
transient phase: the pitch angle saturates at its upper limit, and the rotor speed drops to nearly zero.
Returning to the 2D optimization, the value p = 0.5 emerges as the optimal choice across all four
load channels. It effectively reduces both tower-base side-to-side and blade-root flapwise loads, while

TFE
The second LIDAR system analyzed in this work is the Turbulent Field Estimator. This is a proper
model of a LIDAR system, developed within the CONTINUE project, that accurately simulates a hub-
mounted pulsed LIDAR. The goal of this chapter is to demonstrate that the ’tfe’ can be integrated in the
developed HAWC2-python framework, and explain the necessary modifications to account for the new
LIDAR system.
7.1
Turbulent Field Estimator
The implementation in HAWC2 of this new LIDAR follows the same logic of the other ’blocks’ discussed
above: instead of developing an ad hoc DLL, the block is implemented in hawc2lib, making it more
readable and easier to use. The system takes the following inputs:
• beam pattern: as the LIDAR system obtains the wind estimate by firing laser beams to the aerosol
in the air, the position and firing sequence of such beams is an essential parameter. They are
defined by:
– half-cone opening angle
– azimutal angle of the beam
– range, that represents the focus distance measured from the rotor center
These parameters are often optimized to improve the accuracy of the LIDAR wind estimation. For
the ’tfe’ implementation proposed in this work, the scanning pattern was selected based on the
supervisor’s recommendation and the work proposed in Figure 7.1. Note that since the LIDAR is
hub-mounted, it rotates with the turbine, allowing for a complete scanning of the upwind area.
• measured plane location: equivalent to the ∆y parameter previously defined. In particular, the
tfe measures the wind within a cylinder having its center axis aligned with the wind direction, a
radius roughly the size of the rotor radius and height expressed in seconds. The resulting system
is portrayed in Figure 8.4 in the Appendix. The LIDAR essentially averages the wind within this
domain, projecting its average velocity on a plane at the middle of the cylinder.
• LIDAR parameters: characteristics of the LIDAR system itself, related to the averaging operations
performed by the LIDAR when extracting a measurement.
From a more practical point of view, implementing this new LIDAR is quite straightforward, due to the
’block nature’ of the developed framework. The ’plane’ LIDAR block can be substituted with the ’tfe’,
which outputs a RAWS estimate every 0.1 seconds. The estimate is then inserted into the FF block, to
be filtered and used to calculate d ˙
FF/dt. As described in the next section, the signal produced by the
’tfe’ is different from the signal obtained by the ’plane’, so some adjustments must be made.

7.2
Required modifications
To begin the analysis, the time history of the ’tfe’ estimate is compared to both the actual RAWS signal
and the ’plane’ estimate, as shown in Figure 7.2. It is evident that while the ’tfe’ preview captures
the general trend of the wind speed, it is noticeably noisier and more oscillatory compared to the
’plane’ preview. In particular, the ’tfe’ estimate overshoots several peak values. This behavior is clearly
visible in Figure 7.3, where the RAWS, ’plane’, and ’tfe’ estimates are time-shifted to be in phase and
normalized with respect to the RAWS.
The next step involves applying the modified Schlipf’s recipe. First, the lag is computed using the sliding
Pearson correlation coefficient. Then, the coherence between the RAWS signal and the lag-corrected
LIDAR preview is evaluated to extract f0.5. This lag can also be compared to the lag predicted by the
frozen Taylor hypothesis, computed as ∆y divided by the mean wind speed. When averaged over six
seeds, the lag from the ’plane’ estimate is within 0.5% of the Taylor-based lag, whereas the ’tfe’ lag
is 4.17% shorter, equivalent to a difference of 0.28 seconds. This deviation is significant and could
suggest a less accurate estimation from the ’tfe’ method.
Furthermore, the coherence curve illustrates the difference in quality between the RAWS and the ’tfe’
wind estimate. Unlike the ’plane’ estimate, the ’tfe’ coherence never reaches a value of 1 and drops
below 0.5 more rapidly, further emphasizing the limitations of the ’tfe’ preview.
The coherence updating can be implemented for the new LIDAR system: after the transient state, a
measuring phase is introduced, during which the RAWS and the LIDAR preview are recorded. Then,
following the modified Schlipf’s recipe, the cutoff frequency f0.5 is computed and used in the filter. To
validate this approach, it is necessary to assess the reliability of the f0.5 estimate as a function of
the duration of the measuring phase, as illustrated in Figure 7.4. As shown, measuring phases shorter
than 300 seconds often result in missing data points, indicating that the correlation between RAWS and
the ’tfe’ preview remained consistently below 0.5. This impacts the standard deviation, which appears
artificially reduced on the left side of the plot. To ensure a reliable f0.5 estimation, a conservative
duration of 600 seconds was selected for the measuring phase.

8
Conclusion
8.1
Summary
This thesis presented the development, implementation, and evaluation of a LIDAR-assisted collective
pitch controller for wind turbines, integrating real-time upstream wind measurements into the pitch
control strategy. The main goal is to improve turbine performance by reducing structural loads and
rotor speed variability, ultimately contributing to the reduction of material use and CO2 emissions in line
with the CONTINUE project’s broader objectives.
The key contributions are the following:
• Coupling Framework: a novel simulation setup was developed by integrating HAWC2 with a
Python-based controller via the hawc2lib library. This flexible framework allowed real-time extrac-
tion and processing of wind signals, enabling prototyping of advanced control strategies without
relying on complex DLL implementations.
• LES Data Analysis: a comprehensive analysis of wind field evolution using a LES dataset en-
abled the characterization of advection delays and coherence decay across different heights and
preview distances. The ’modified Schlipf’s recipe’ was successfully adapted to estimate the co-
herence bandwidth f0.5, which serves as a suitable cutoff frequency for the low-pass filter applied
to the LIDAR signal.
• Filter Design and Delay Quantification: the low-pass filter and actuator dynamics were modeled
analytically. A simple expression for estimating the time delay introduced by the filter was pro-
posed and validated over different simulations, achieving sufficient accuracy for control purposes.
The actuator was modeled as a second-order damped system, and its frequency-dependent de-
lay was estimated with relatively high accuracy.
• Controller Implementation: two LIDAR configurations were modeled and tested in the developed
framework, a simple preview and the DTU-developed Turbulent Field Estimator.
The former
one allowed to establish the framework and understand its criticalities, while the latter enable to
evaluate the performance and limitations of the tfe. A 2D optimization was performed to evaluate
popt and Tadditional,opt. While the former has a clear optimum value, the additional delay is less
impactfull on the overall loads. When using the ’plane’ LIDAR the gain scaling factor can be set
to 0.5 and the additional delay to -0.5 seconds, while when using the ’tfe’ the optimal parameters
are popt = 0.7 and Tadditional,opt = 0 seconds.
Finally, the load reductions achieved by the two developed controllers can be compared to the values
reported in the literature, as shown in Figure 8.1, essentially summarizing Figure 2.7, Table 6.1 and
Table 7.1. The developed controllers demonstrate reductions in DEL that are generally in line with
those found in previous studies. The only notable deviation is the rotor speed RMS for the tfe-based
controller, which is significantly higher than the other RMS values. However, it is important to note that
fluctuations in rotor speed are not a major concern for turbine operation and are not the primary target
of LiAC systems. Therefore, this deviation is acceptable.

The results confirm the hypothesis that preview-based control can yield measurable performance im-
provements over traditional feedback-only systems. By anticipating incoming wind disturbances, the
turbine can proactively adjust its pitch, leading to a reduction in fatigue loading.
However, a key challenge in the system is the accuracy of the LIDAR preview and the limited ability to
compensate for its errors using the tuning parameter Tadditional. This issue is illustrated in Figure 8.2,
which shows three instances of the feedforward control block in action. At each reference time, the
RAWS estimate from the ’tfe’ LIDAR is passed through a low-pass filter and the feedforward wind
estimate is obtained by time-shifting the filtered preview by the proper Tbuffer. The resulting value is
compared with the actual RAWS to compute the wind speed error ∆ws. For simplicity, actuator dynam-
56
==PAGE:65==
ics are neglected in this example, so the buffer time is defined as TTaylor −Tadditional. In an ideal case,
a perfect LIDAR estimate would result in no difference (∆ws = 0). Alternatively, any consistent offset
could be corrected with a fixed Tadditional. Unfortunately, the computed ∆ws varies between reference
locations and includes both positive and negative values. While these three instances are not statis-
tically significant on their own, they provide valuable insight into why tuning Tadditional did not lead to
significants reductions in DEL.
This brings to the second problem: dealing with unknown or partially known signals. Many of the key
quantities, such as filter-induced delay, are inherently signal-dependent, meaning their values can only
be accurately determined after the signal has been observed. While it is relatively straightforward to
measure these effects ’a posteriori’, estimating them ’a priori’ is significantly more difficult. This is fur-
ther complicated by the frequency-dependent nature of both the wind disturbances and the controller’s
response. Although pre-simulation ’measuring phases’ can help estimate some parameters, a degree
of uncertainty is unavoidable. In this context, simpler models and equations with minimal tuning re-
quirements proved to be more robust and practical than more precise but input-sensitive approaches.
Another important limitation is the narrow scope of the controller’s validation. The LiAC was primarily
optimized and tested at a wind speed of 15 m/s and a fixed turbulence intensity. Although brief tests
at other wind speeds showed that the controller remains functional under varying input conditions, its
performance is not consistent across the full operational range. Crucially, there is no guarantee that the
optimized parameters, Tadditional,opt and popt, remain optimal under different wind conditions. Additionally,
it is worth noting that a systematic tuning of the baseline controller’s gains was not performed at the
outset of this study. As a result, it remains possible that more suitable gain values, better suited for
feedforward integration, could improve performance. Finally, the transition between Region 2 (torque
control) and Region 3 (pitch control) was not explicitly considered. This transition is a key challenge
in controller design, and careful treatment is essential if the proposed framework is to be applied in
practice.
Finally, the incoming wind was simulated using only one turbulence intensity, 10%, which is relatively
low compared to the standard wind classes defined in Table 8.4 in the Appendix. In theory, a well-
designed LiAC should outperform a standard controller in turbulent conditions, meaning that higher
turbulence levels could result in even greater load reductions relative to the baseline. However, as
previously discussed, the current controller may not be optimally tuned. Therefore, its behavior under
increased turbulence remains uncertain, and no conclusions can be drawn without further testing.
8.3
Future work
The results of this thesis suggest several promising directions for future research:
• Varying wind conditions: as discussed, the controller was primarily validated at a wind speed
of 15 m/s with a turbulence intensity of 10%, and only briefly tested across a broader range
of wind speeds. To assess the practical applicability and robustness of the controller, a more
comprehensive evaluation under varying wind speeds and turbulence levels is necessary.
• Optimization of the ’tfe’ configuration: with the framework now established, the firing sequence
of the ’tfe’ LIDAR can be refined. Modifying this sequence may improve the quality of the wind
speed estimates and enhance the controller’s performance.
• Realistic incoming wind fields: a major limitation of the current framework is the use of a standard
Mann turbulence box, which lacks temporal evolution in the incoming wind. As highlighted by the
LES study, wind evolution significantly affects LIDAR measurement accuracy. Although 4D Mann
boxes (with time as the fourth dimension) have been proposed [9], their integration into HAWC2
is more complex and remains an open challenge.
57
==PAGE:66==
• Individual Pitch Control (IPC): one of the key advantages of LIDAR-assisted control is its potential
to support individual pitch control, where each blade is pitched independently. Enabling IPC
would require extending the LIDAR estimate to extract additional flow characteristics, such as
vertical and horizontal shear at the preview plane, in addition to the RAWS estimate.
8.4
Final remarks
This work advances LIDAR-assisted wind turbine control by introducing a flexible simulation platform
and a systematic method for integrating real-time wind preview into control algorithms. The proposed
controller showed clear benefits in simulation and lays a strong foundation for future experimental and
theoretical developments in the field. As the wind energy sector continues to scale up turbine size
and improve efficiency, predictive sensing technologies like LIDAR-assisted control will be crucial to
improve power production while maintaining economic performance.
Within the CONTINUE project framework, this work established a direct path between the LIDAR’s
firing sequence and the resulting damage-equivalent loads, enabling recursive system optimization.
One can say, the loop is closed.
