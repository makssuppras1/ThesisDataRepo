Abstract
Urban drainage systems are increasingly challenged by deep uncertainties arising from climate change,
urban development, and evolving regulatory requirements. Traditional planning approaches, which typ-
ically rely on deterministic assumptions and fixed design standards, often fall short in addressing such
uncertain futures. This thesis explores the application of the Dynamic Adaptive Policy Pathways (DAPP)
approach to urban drainage planning, using the Hellerup catchment in Gentofte Municipality, Denmark,
as a case study. The objective is not to identify an optimal solution for the catchment, but to evaluate how
DAPP can be applied to improve planning under uncertainty and to assess its advantages and limitations
compared to conventional methods.
The study focuses on two key performance indicators: compliance with sewer service-level standards
in terms of flood protection, and stormwater treatment performance under different climate scenarios.
Future climate uncertainty was represented using five climate factor (CF) trajectories derived from the
Danish Meteorological Institute (DMI) and Spildevandskomitéen (SVK). Hydraulic simulations were
performed in MIKE+ to evaluate the current drainage system, as well as two strategic adaptation options:
a centralized “Seabed-Tunnel solution” and a more decentralized “No-Tunnel solution.” Adaptation tip-
ping points were identified by analysing when each solution fails to meet performance thresholds under
future climate conditions.
The results show that the current system is already non-compliant with service-level requirements and is
highly sensitive to increasing rainfall intensities. While the ”Seabed-Tunnel solution” ensures compliance
under moderate scenarios, it reaches its limits under more extreme conditions unless supported by addi-
tional local measures. In contrast, the ”No-Tunnel solution” demonstrates similar flood performance but
greater long-term adaptability and flexibility to support decentralized and nature-based drainage strate-
gies. However, it lacks scalability in terms of treatment performance due to spatial constraints. The study
further reveals that stormwater treatment targets, such as the 90% annual treatment rate, may need recon-
sideration under future climate conditions, as maintaining them becomes increasingly difficult without
major infrastructure upgrades.
By integrating quantitative modelling into the DAPP framework, this thesis demonstrates how adaptive
planning can be integrated in the urban drainage context. It highlights that while hydraulic models offer
valuable insights into system behaviour, simplifications and strategic prioritization of uncertainties and
actions are essential to keep modelling efforts feasible. The DAPP approach proved especially useful in
mapping adaptation pathways, visualizing tipping points, and exploring the dynamic robustness of differ-
ent strategies. However, its application requires careful adaptation to context, stakeholder engagement,
and clear objectives, especially given the resource demands of running multiple simulations.
This thesis concludes that incorporating adaptive planning principles such as DAPP can significantly
enhance the resilience of urban drainage systems by preparing for a range of plausible futures, rather than
relying on fixed-scenario planning. The approach is particularly valuable for municipalities navigating
climate uncertainty, long infrastructure lifespans, and evolving policy landscapes.

Introduction
Urban drainage systems face significant future challenges and uncertainties driven by climate change,
rapid urbanization, ageing infrastructure, and increasing regulatory and societal demands. More frequent
and intense rainfall events, combined with expanding impervious surfaces, are expected to increase urban
flooding, sewer overflows, and pollutant loads to receiving waters, threatening both public health and the
environment (Dong et al., 2017; Babovic et al., 2018). To address these challenges drainage systems
need to be adapted and designed in a way to be ”future-proof”, meaning creating robust and resilient
systems, which can withstand or recover from a wide range of influences in the future. In this study an
urban drainage system in Hellerup is considered. Under awareness of this future challenges the local
municipality Gentofte published a strategy paper, called the Wastewater Plan which contains objectives
and strategies to adapt the drainage system to a safe, efficient and resilient system. In order to meet this
objectives the drainage system will undergo substantial structural changes in order to meet the targets set
out in the current Wastewater Plan 2022–2032 (NIRAS, 2025; Gentofte Kommune, n.d.[b]). NIRAS a
Danish engineering consultancy, in consultation with Novafos (utility company) and the municipality, has
developed catchment plans for the various sub-catchments, outlining a proposed sequencing of actions
to be implemented over time in order to achieve the municipality’s long-term objectives (NIRAS, 2025;
NIRAS, 2023b).
As is common in urban drainage planning, systems are typically designed for long lifespans and are
expected to meet municipal objectives over several decades. Decisions regarding urban drainage in-
frastructure must therefore be made in the context of various sources of uncertainty, including climate
change, urbanisation, and evolving legislation, each of which can have a significant impact on system
performance over time (Babovic et al., 2018; Dong et al., 2017; Haasnoot et al., 2013).
To ensure that urban drainage systems continue to meet their objectives under changing conditions, it is
essential to incorporate these uncertainties during the planning and design phases. Traditional planning
methods are often characterized as “predict-then-act” approaches, which aim to forecast the most likely
future scenario and design systems accordingly (Babovic et al., 2018). In urban drainage, this typically
involves applying safety factors to increase the robustness of the system against various uncertainties.
In Denmark, for example, the Wastewater Committee (Spildevandskomiteen) publishes recommended
climate factors, used as part of the safety margin, based on regional extreme rainfall models. These factors
vary depending on the return period and projected future time horizon and are widely used in system
dimensioning by multiplying them with rainfall inputs for hydraulic simulations (Arnbjerg-Nielsen et al.,
2023; Gram-Hanssen et al., 2023).
However, selecting a single set of climate factors for system design places a great deal of trust in a spe-
cific future scenario. If reality deviates from this scenario, systems may fail to meet their performance
requirements. In fact, reality does not unfold along a single, predictable path but can develop in many dif-
ferent and sometimes unexpected directions. This condition is referred to as a state of deep uncertainty,
where not only the probabilities of future events are unknown, but even the structure of the underly-
ing processes may be unclear (Marchau et al., 2019; Haasnoot et al., 2013). In such cases, traditional
planning approaches often fall short. Without knowing which scenario will most accurately reflect fu-
ture developments, this can lead either to over-dimensioning (with significant financial implications) or
under-dimensioning (with inadequate performance outcomes) of drainage systems (Marchau et al., 2019).
To better manage deep uncertainty, adaptive planning approaches such as Dynamic Adaptive Policy Path-
ways (DAPP) have emerged. These approaches emphasize flexibility, continuous learning, and adjust-
ment over time, allowing systems to be modified as more information becomes available or as conditions
change. Instead of relying on a single forecast, adaptive approaches focus on identifying tipping points

and planning sequences of actions that can shift depending on how the future unfolds. This marks a shift
away from traditional approaches of static robustness, which typically involve increasing safety factors
to cope with uncertainty, toward dynamic robustness, where systems are explicitly designed to adapt over
time (Babovic et al., 2018; Marchau et al., 2019).
The DAPP approach originally emerged in the field of climate adaptation and water management in the
Netherlands. It was first formalized by Haasnoot et al. (2013) and builds upon earlier work in adaptive
policymaking and robust decision-making (Haasnoot et al., 2013). A new generation of climate scenar-
ios necessitated changes in regulations and adaptation plans. In response, the Dutch Delta Programme
developed DAPP as a structured method to support decision-making under deep uncertainty in terms of
long-term water management of the Rhine Delta. The main objectives of this project were the protection
of the Netherlands from flooding and the ensureance of adequate supplies of freshwater for generations
ahead. From there on the Dynamic Adaptive Policy Pathways (DAPP) approach started to be increas-
ingly used as well in urban drainage projects to help cities adapt to climate change and sea-level rise.
It got applied in diverse urban settings such as the Thames Estuary (UK), Wellington (New Zealand),
Singapore, London, and Can Tho (Vietnam), but with a majority of cases related to sea-level rise and
storm surge protection (Haasnoot et al., 2013; Haasnoot et al., 2023).
This thesis explores how the Dynamic Adaptive Policy Pathways (DAPP) approach can be beneficial
in a municipal urban drainage project in Hellerup, Denmark. Specifically, it analyses how the DAPP
framework can enhance the adaptability and dynamic robustness of urban drainage systems when fac-
ing future uncertainties. A quantitative approach is applied by integrating hydraulic simulations using
MIKE+ software to assess the performance of different drainage system solutions under a range of climate
and regulatory scenarios. Based on the research background and problem framing, the study addresses
the current lack of integration of long-term uncertainties in conventional planning processes, which often
rely on static robustness and safety margins based on single future projections.

Research background
2.1
Uncertainty in urban drainage planning
Uncertainty is a major challenge in the design, operation, and management of urban drainage systems.
It arises from factors such as climate change, rainfall variability, data limitations, model imperfections,
and system defects. Effectively addressing uncertainty is crucial for building reliable, resilient, and sus-
tainable urban drainage systems. Uncertainty can result from data limitations or data quality, from the
complexity of hydrological models but also from structural or systemic sources like defecting pipes or
ageing infrastructure (Lian et al., 2020; Fu et al., 2022). In addition to these, there are significant uncer-
tainties regarding how external conditions may evolve in the future. Some of the key future uncertainties
relevant to urban drainage systems are outlined in the following.
2.1.1
Future uncertainties and their effects in urban drainage
Future uncertainties in urban drainage arise from both environmental and socio-economic developments
and can amplify flood risks, increase infrastructure costs and therefore affect the robustness, cost-effectiveness
and resilience of drainage systems in the future. Due to that, the management and incorporation of these
uncertainties is essential to ensure long-term system performance and avoid lock-ins, over-dimensioning,
or maladaptation (Kool et al., 2023; Babovic et al., 2018; Marchau et al., 2019).
Among the most critical sources of uncertainty is climate change. It directly affects rainfall patterns,
sea levels, evaporation rates, and groundwater conditions, which are all connected to the performance
of drainage infrastructure (Climate Change (IPCC), 2019; Arnbjerg-Nielsen et al., 2023). In particular,
projections indicate an increase in the intensity and frequency of short-duration extreme rainfall events,
which puts existing combined and separated systems under stress (Babovic et al., 2018; DMI, 2025; Kool
et al., 2023). These changes are difficult to quantify with certainty. Even when climate models agree on a
general trend (e.g. more intense rainfall), they differ in the magnitude and regional manifestation of these
trends (Haasnoot et al., 2013; Marchau et al., 2019). Furthermore, current dimensioning guidelines often
rely on historical rainfall data, which may no longer be valid under non-stationary conditions (Arnbjerg-
Nielsen et al., 2023).
Urban densification and land use change also introduce uncertainty. As cities grow and new impervious
surfaces are added, runoff volumes increase and drainage systems are placed under additional pressure.
In a study published by Yassine et al. in 2022 the effect of urbanization and the associated land-use
change on the surface runoff was analysed for Cairo in Egypt. It was found out, that an increase of im-
pervious surfaces in combination with poor urban planning can lead to a surface runnof increase of 180%
according to their modelling. This would account for more than three times the effect, climate change
had on the runoff during the same observation period (Yassine et al., 2022). Another study conducted in
Barcelona by Medina et al. came to similar results. Urban development can significantly worsen urban
drainage performance, even without considering climate change (Medina et al., 2020). Besides the effect
on the runoff, as well the vulnerability and exposure to flooding of certain areas can change with future
densification which would increase the flood risk as well. However, the pace and nature of urban de-
velopment can vary widely depending on economic trends, political priorities, and societal preferences,
making long-term forecasting difficult (Babovic et al., 2018; Kool et al., 2023). In addition, changing
societal expectations about regulations, environmental protection, and the role of nature-based solutions
may have an effect on drainage planning in the future (Marchau et al., 2019; Babovic et al., 2018).
Legal frameworks can also change over time, affecting how urban drainage systems are planned, op-
erated, and financed. For example, new legislation may require stricter service levels, more frequent
flood protection, or enhanced stormwater treatment. Modern legal frameworks are increasingly moving

toward performance-based planning, which sets outcome-oriented criteria (such as flood protection lev-
els or water quality targets) rather than prescribing specific technical solutions. However, most policies
are hybrid, combining performance-based elements with traditional regulations to allow flexibility while
ensuring compliance. As well the role and responsibilities of institutions, e.g. utility companies or mu-
nicipalities, in the development of urban drainage infrastructure can change in the future (Kool et al.,
2023; Rosa et al., 2020). Besides that the regulations on the integration of climate adaptation and sus-
tainable drainage solutions (e.g. green infrastructure) might evolve in the future. A shift from traditional
stormwater management approaches, including grey infrastructure and centralized approaches, to more
local and holistic approaches like using sustainable urban drainage systems (SUDS) can already be ob-
served worldwide. Besides the environmental and climate challenges that increase the demand for such
solutions the policies and legislation plays as well an important role in the adaptation in implementation
of them (Rosa et al., 2020).
Those future uncertainties do not act in isolation. They interact and reinforce each other, often amplifying
risks and complicating urban drainage planning, operation, and financing. Their combined effects can
lead to greater flood risks, higher costs, and increased pressure on infrastructure. Rapid urban develop-
ment and the increase of impervious surfaces, combined with more intense rainfall from climate change,
increases the risk of urban flooding dramatically (Ren et al., 2019). So there is a need of planning and
designing urban drainage systems efficient and holistically, by taking into account possible future devel-
opments and uncertainties and avoiding poorly planned and inflexible systems that are less able to cope
with the combined pressures of increasing climate change, urbanization and other future uncertainties
(Yassine et al., 2022).
2.1.2
Deep uncertainty
Decision makers and planners in urban drainage face unknowns about climate, urban growth, regulations,
and technology that are difficult or impossible to quantify. This state is referred to as ”Deep uncertainty”
in the literature (Babovic et al., 2018; Sadr et al., 2018). As mentioned in the introduction current planning
standards are not including these future uncertainties and may fail under unexpected conditions, leading
to insufficient flood protection or unsustainable investments (Babovic et al., 2018; Marchau et al., 2019).
Addressing deep uncertainty requires robust, flexible strategies that perform well across a wide range of
possible futures, rather than optimizing systems for a narrow band of conditions. Robustness means that
a strategy is able to reliably perform well under a range of potential futures. The wider the range, the
more robust is the system (Marchau et al., 2019; Babovic et al., 2018). In a paper published by Babovic
et al., two variations of robustness are discussed. Static Robustness, which is defined as ”the range of
possible futures within which a system can deliver a desired level of reliability”. Designing a system,
that can perform well in a wide range of futures, can easily lead to over dimensioning of the system by
using high safety factors, which makes the projects more expensive. In contrast to that, the concept of
dynamic robustness got introduced, which is a key principle of the DAPP approach. It is described as ”a
system’s ability to adapt to changing conditions”(Babovic et al., 2018).
2.2
Introducing the DAPP approach
To design systems that can adapt to changing conditions the many different possible future developments
need to be acknowledged first of all. Then the system needs to be tested against impacts of some of the
possible futures and a plan needs to be set up to guarantee a standard of reliability for as many futures
as possible. This is typically done by using approaches that are part of the so called ”Decision making
under deep uncertainty” (DMDU) framework. They typically try to offer different adaptation pathways
as different sets of actions to implement in the future. Long term actions are prone to be postponed into
the future, when more information is available. In general the aim is to keep the system flexible and avoid
”lock-ins”, so actions that reduce the flexibility drastically are avoided. Another important point is the
iterative nature of the approach, so pathways and plans need to be re-evaluated on an iterative basis when
more information becomes available in the future (Babovic et al., 2018; Haasnoot et al., 2023; Marchau

et al., 2019).
One widely used approach for planning under deep uncertainty is the Dynamic Adaptive Policy Pathways
(DAPP) approach. Its main objective is to develop multiple sequences of actions over time, each with its
own dependencies, both on preceding actions and on the system itself. The DAPP approach combines
two adaptive planning methods: Dynamic Adaptive Planning and Adaptation Pathways. Building on the
concept of the Adaptation Tipping Point, the approach shifts the focus away from predicting a single
future scenario. Instead, it aims to identify the conditions under which a system will no longer meet its
objectives. The timing of these system failures depends on which future scenario unfolds (e.g., climate
change projections), and is typically identified through multiple simulations, often computationally in-
tensive. To address potential performance gaps or system failures, additional actions are planned. Each
of these actions is evaluated over the planning horizon to determine when it reaches its limits, and when
a follow-up measure becomes necessary. This process results in the construction of a pathway: a se-
quence of actions mapped out over time. Multiple pathways, each representing a different strategy or set
of measures, can then be compared using a multi-criteria analysis. In addition to cost and mitigation per-
formance, typical evaluation criteria include flexibility, potential lock-ins, and adaptability of the actions
over time (Babovic et al., 2018; Marchau et al., 2019).
2.2.1
Original DAPP approach by Haasnot et al.
The original Dynamic Adaptive Policy Pathways (DAPP) approach, developed and published by Haas-
noot et al. in 2013, consists of a ten-step process designed to support decision-making under deep uncer-
tainty. The first step involves defining the system’s characteristics, specifying the objectives for future
development, and outlining any current or potential future constraints. This results according to Haas-
not et al. in a ”definition of success,” which sets indicators and targets for evaluating the performance
of actions and pathways later on, and for assessing the ”sell-by dates” (tipping points) of actions. This
step also identifies major uncertainties relevant to the decision-making problem, which are not limited to
future conditions but can also include uncertainties related to data or models (Haasnoot et al., 2013).
In step 2, the system is analysed in more detail by comparing the current system and possible future system
solutions against the in step 1 defined objectives. The aim is identifying its vulnerabilities and oppor-
tunities, using transient scenarios. Vulnerabilities refer to weaknesses or bottlenecks where the current
system, without any interventions, will fail to meet the defined objectives at a certain point in the future.
Opportunities, on the other hand, include existing areas or infrastructure that could contribute positively
over time towards achieving the desired outcomes. This analysis can by done either qualitatively in form
of expert judgment and stakeholder knowledge or it can be supported by using computational models
(Haasnoot et al., 2013; Babovic et al., 2018; Haasnoot et al., 2023).
If the current system is projected to fail in meeting the objectives, interventions become necessary. These
actions to meet the ”definition of success” from step 1 are identified in step 3. In step 4, each of these
proposed actions is assessed individually to determine when it will reach its limitations under different
future scenarios. This step is iterative in nature, as new actions can reveal further vulnerabilities or
opportunities, requiring ongoing refinement (Haasnoot et al., 2013; Babovic et al., 2018; Haasnoot et al.,
2023).
Once the actions and their interactions are well understood, Adaptation pathways are developed in step
5. These represent sequences of actions over time, and multiple combinations are possible depending on
stakeholder preferences, contextual relevance, and logical constraints. In practice, a single action within
a pathway may represent a group of related measures, meaning not every individual intervention needs to
be depicted explicitly (Haasnoot et al., 2013; Babovic et al., 2018; Haasnoot et al., 2023). The resulting
pathways can be visualized in different formats, with the most common being the so-called Pathway Map
or Metro Map, an example of which is shown in fig. 2.1.

If an action reaches its limit it is visualized by a so called ”Adaptation Tipping Point” (ATP). The timing
of an ATP, or its ”sell-by date,” is scenario-dependent, meaning it can vary based on different future
scenarios. When an ATP is reached, a new or additional action is necessary. This shift to a new action or
pathway is visualized by a transfer station (Haasnoot et al., 2013).
After the pathways are generated and visualized, preferred pathways can be selected in consultation with
stakeholders, while less favourable or less feasible pathways may be discarded (step 6). Subsequently,
contingency actions can be identified and implemented to ensure that the preferred pathways remain
achievable and to enhance their robustness (step 7). Based on the results of the previous steps, a final
Adaptive plan can be developed and implemented in step 8. After implementation, the system should be
monitored continuously to evaluate whether its development aligns with the expectations and structure
of the dynamic plan (Haasnoot et al., 2013).

by significant path dependencies and long-term impacts of early-stage decisions (Babovic et al., 2018;

2.2.2
DAPP in the Danish context
In the Danish context, the DAPP approach was tested by the Danish Coastal Authority (DCA) in two
pilot areas, as well as in a case study conducted in another Danish city by Kool et al. The DCA study
aimed to assess the applicability of the DAPP approach for climate adaptation and flood risk reduction
within a municipal context in the towns of Vejle and Assens. In both cities, as well as in the study by Kool
et al. the primary focus was on flood risks resulting from storm surges and fluvial flooding from local
watercourses. Pluvial flooding was only considered in the latter (Kaija Jumppanen Andersen, 2020; Kool
et al., 2023). Following initial meetings and consultations, the municipalities, together with the DCA and
experts experienced in the DAPP methodology, decided to adopt a predominantly qualitative approach,
supported by quantitative inputs. It was concluded that a fully quantitative DAPP implementation would
be difficult for Danish municipalities due to resource constraints and data availability. Consequently,
the methodology was adapted to better fit the local planning context, allowing for a more exploratory
application that could be adapted to municipal needs (Kaija Jumppanen Andersen, 2020).
Compared to the original stepwise approach by Haasnoot et al., an additional step was introduced prior to
the system description. This so-called step 0 involves a preliminary reflection on expectations regarding
both the planning process and its outcomes. Given that the DAPP approach in the Danish context places
greater emphasis on stakeholder integration, initial discussions with key actors are also included at this
early stage. Following this, the process continues with the conventional step 1: System Description (Kaija
Jumppanen Andersen, 2020).
The second step of the original approach, which traditionally includes a detailed problem analysis under
transient scenarios, is treated differently. In the adapted process, vulnerabilities and opportunities are
explored in a more qualitative manner, primarily through stakeholder workshops and discussions. This
step is now split into two sub-steps: 2A and 2B. In step 2A, a catalogue of flood risk-reduction ideas
is developed via brainstorming and categorization by various stakeholders. Step 2B involves reviewing
existing plans and long-term visions for urban development in the area (Kaija Jumppanen Andersen,
2020).
Step 3 merges the outcomes of step 2A and 2B by identifying how flood risk mitigation measures can
align with broader urban development goals. This step is considered critical, as it aims to integrate flood
protection measures holistically into urban planning and prevent conflicting developments. As in the
original DAPP framework, this step helps to filter viable or well-fitting measures from less suitable ones
(Kaija Jumppanen Andersen, 2020).
In step 4, a dynamic measure map is created, similar to step 5 in the original DAPP process. This involves
assigning actions to future time points, identifying interdependencies, and visualizing relevant pathways.
The result of this step is referred to as a “DAPP light map”, as the level of detail remains relatively low
at this stage. The study concluded that simplifying the map by grouping individual actions into broader
action sets or development directions improves its readability and usefulness, as excessive detail can
undermine its function (Kaija Jumppanen Andersen, 2020).

In the adapted step 5, a Multi-Criteria Analysis (MCA) is used to evaluate and compare different path-

Second, MCA enables the inclusion of diverse criteria beyond costs, such as ecological impacts, risk re-

these criteria, which are then weighted according to their relevance (Kaija Jumppanen Andersen, 2020).
Finally, an additional step was introduced beyond the original final step of the DAPP framework. This
concluding step involves the development of a concrete action plan, outlining short-, medium-, and long-

2.3
Uncertainties in hydraulic modelling
The use of hydraulic models in urban drainage planning is quite common around the world and in most
cases they are the main method for dimensioning the drainage systems (Abd-Elhamid et al., 2020; Rosa
et al., 2020; Medina et al., 2020). Therefore it is important to incorporate those uncertainties in hydraulic
simulations, to make their results reliable and enable resilient long-term planning. The state of the art
of including future uncertainties in urban drainage planning in Denmark is using uncertainty factors.
Recommendations about methods and values to use are published by the Danish waste water committee
(SVK = Splidevandskomiteen) in their reports. The SVK initially distinguished between uncertainty
from the calculation methods and data uncertainty and summarized those two in the ”Safety factor”. In
their more recent publications they introduced as well an uncertainty factor for climate change: ”Climate
factor” and a factor for densification, representing consequences of urban development in the catchment.
Those factors are included in the design rainfall intensities, which work as the input for dimensioning the
drainage system (Arnbjerg-Nielsen et al., 2023; Gregersen et al., 2014a; Mikkelsen et al., 1999).
2.3.1
Climate Change
In the context of climate change, precipitation changes are among the most critical factors influencing
pluvial flood risk. To integrate projected changes in precipitation into hydrodynamic models, so-called
climate factors are commonly applied. Climate factors describe the relative increase (or decrease) in pre-
cipitation intensities or volumes expected under future climate conditions, typically based on comparisons
between historical and projected periods (DMI, 2024; Arnbjerg-Nielsen et al., 2023).
The general formula for calculating the climate factor is given by:
CF = it0+∆t,T,dur,location
it0,T,dur,location
(2.1)
The CF expresses the ratio between the rainfall intensity of an event with a given return period (T),
duration (dur), and location at a future time (t0+∆t), and the rainfall intensity of the same event at the
current time (t0). These climate factors are multiplied with CDS storms, which are commonly used as rain
input for hydrodynamic models like MIKE+, to project future rainfall. (DMI, 2024; Arnbjerg-Nielsen
et al., 2023).
The Splidevandskomiteen (SVK) in Denmark is, among other things, working on understanding extreme
rainfall patterns and the influence of climate change on them. To do this, the committee has developed
updated regional models based on a combination of long-term observational data and climate model out-
puts. In their latest publication, Skrift 32, they analysed rainfall data from 1979 to 2019 across Denmark,
applying statistical methods to capture regional variations in extreme rainfall events. Additionally, they
incorporated results from high-resolution regional climate models (HCLIM-3 at 3 km and HCLIM-12
at 12 km resolution) to evaluate how extreme precipitation events are expected to change in the future
(Arnbjerg-Nielsen et al., 2023; Mikkelsen et al., 1999).
Rather than relying only on direct climate model projections, the SVK applies corrections to historical
extreme rainfall statistics based on observed trends over time and subsequently derives climate factors

2.3.2
Other uncertainties
Besides climate change, the other factors of uncertainty like urbanization, growing population and the
connected land cover changes should be as well considered in the hydraulic modelling. This can be done
by identifying projection of population growth and the urban development along with the changes in
impervious cover and land use (Kourtis et al., 2021). Urban development has a significant impact on
the hydrological response of urban catchments, due to the increase of impervious surfaces. This can be
incorporated in hydraulic models for instance by increasing the runoff coefficients according to future
development projections (Abd-Elhamid et al., 2020).
2.4
Adaptation strategies to improve urban drainage system robustness
In addition to the use of planning approaches that better incorporate future uncertainties and focus on
adaptability and flexibility also structural measures and implementations need to become more reliable
and resilient. The current urban drainage infrastructure, consisting of pipes, basins overflows, tunnels,
treatment plants, etc., which is commonly dimensioned to a certain level of service, needs to undergo
changes to be better prepared for changing future conditions (Sadr et al., 2018; Dong et al., 2017). Two
studies from (Dong et al.) and (Sadr et al.) evaluated and compared the robustness of green versus grey
infrastructure under future uncertainties from several sources. Both came to the result, that green in-
frastructure generally offers higher adaptability and effectiveness for resilience improvement, especially
under future uncertainties. Strategies robust in sustainability were identified to be generally also robust
in reliability and resilience. Sadr et al. found out, that hybrid solutions that combine green with gray
infrastructure are more robust and balanced, especially compared to conventional gray-only strategies,
which often lack sustainability due to unbalanced economic, environmental, and social performance.
Dong et al. highlighted as well the importance of combining both, green and grey to achieve optimal
and sustainable urban drainage infrastructure. Sadr et al. analysed permeable pavement in driveways,
bio-retention planters applied to residential roads and rain garden applied to residential roofs. Rain gar-
dens were found to be the most robust green infrastructure option for sustainability, and also performed
very well in terms of reliability and resilience(Source). Bio-retention planters and permeable pavement
also provided robustness, but their effectiveness was generally proportional to the amount of impervious
area they disconnected. However, permeable pavement was less effective due to high costs and limited
operational benefits, especially in scenarios with high urban creep. In the study published by Dong et
al. green roofs and permeable asphalt was analysed. Building 20% of the total area as green roofs led to
about a 30% increase in system resilience, while 20% permeable pavement led to about a 33% increase,
on average across future scenarios (Dong et al., 2017; Sadr et al., 2018)
In the context of sustainable urban drainage infrastructure LID (Low impact development) is a commonly
used technique for local flood mitigation. It combines structural and non-structural measures which
reduce flood risk on the one hand while also promoting the ecology by using nature-based solutions.
Besides the already mentioned green infrastructure solutions, that are part of LID, Rain gardens, bio-
retention cells and permeable pavements there are further options, e.g. Swales or rainwater harvesting
tanks/cisterns (Kaykhosravi et al., 2018). LIDs can be implemented in hydraulic model software (SWMM

or MIKE+) via dedicated LID modules, which allow the user to allow to specify the type, location, and
area of SUDS/LIDs within a catchment (Kaykhosravi et al., 2018; DHI, 2025b). Otherwise LIDs can be
implemented directly by decreasing the percentage of impervious surfaces. This method assumes that
LIDs intercept, infiltrate, or delay runoff, effectively making part of the area behave as pervious (She
et al., 2020).

For several years the goal of the municipality Gentofte was to separate the stormwater from the wastew-

decades. Due to that, local infiltration on the property level is only possible in few areas. Regardless, in
general only few residents decided to voluntarily connect their rainwater to the new separate system, so the
combined system is not as relieved as planned and suffers still from overload during rain events (Gentofte
Kommune, n.d.[a]; Rambøll, 2022). Therefore, the municipality decided to change their strategy from
”semi-separation” to full separation to eliminate all CSOs and flooding with combined wastewater. This
strategy was published in the current wastewater plan 2022-2032. This plan sets a framework for the
long-term development of the Urban drainage network of Gentofte in the future. The main feature of this
plan is the full separation of the entire sewer system of Gentofte Municipality within 30 years (Gentofte
Kommune, n.d.[a]; Rambøll, 2022).

Research aim and objectives
3.1
Key research questions
Based on the research background and the problem statement, the following research question has been
formulated:
How can the DAPP approach enhance the adaptability and dynamic robustness of urban drainage
planning under future uncertainties, while keeping the planning process transparent and practical?
This question addresses the current challenge of insufficient integration of future uncertainties into urban
drainage planning. It also targets an existing research gap regarding the application of the DAPP ap-
proach in small-scale, local urban drainage systems, where further research is needed to understand how
the approach can be structured and applied effectively. Additionally, it acknowledges the potential com-
plexity of the DAPP approach as a barrier for practical implementation in municipal contexts (Babovic
et al., 2018; Marchau et al., 2019).
To answer this research question, the study focuses on a case area in Hellerup, a district in the Gentofte
municipality located in the northern suburbs of Copenhagen. This leads to the following case-specific
research question:
How can urban drainage planning in Hellerup benefit from the DAPP approach by integrating
adaptive decision-making into long-term infrastructure planning?
This study explores how the DAPP approach can be incorporated into a local drainage planning and
to what extent it offers advantages over conventional planning approaches, particularly with regard to
climate adaptation. The research builds on the original DAPP framework but also considers the modifi-
cations applied in the Danish municipal context. While not every step of the DAPP process is followed
strictly, its core principles guide the development of the methodology and structure of this study. The
main focus lies in identifying key uncertainties and incorporating them into future scenarios, as well as
developing and testing quantitative models of the drainage system and proposed adaptation measures
under these scenarios. This will be carried out using the MIKE+ software developed by DHI.
From this focus, the following sub-question emerges:
What are the key uncertainties that need to be considered when planning the future drainage system of
Hellerup/Gentofte?
As quantitative modelling and simulation are state-of-the-art in urban drainage planning (Abd-Elhamid
et al., 2020; Rosa et al., 2020; Medina et al., 2020), this study examines how such methods can be inte-
grated into the DAPP framework. A central challenge is selecting a model structure that balances detail
with computational efficiency, as DAPP-based planning often involves a large number of simulations to
explore a wide range of future conditions (Babovic et al., 2018). Therefore, the following sub-question
is addressed:
What level of model complexity is appropriate for small-scale applications, and how can this complexity
be reduced while still producing meaningful results?
With the help of quantitative modelling, the performance of the drainage system solutions can be assessed.
Therefore the following question is also investigated:
How do different climate and regulatory scenarios impact the performance of the current drainage system
and the proposed solutions?

3.2
Objectives of the study
A key underlying assumption of this study is the separation of the existing combined sewer system into
two networks, one for stormwater and one for wastewater. This separation strategy serves as the founda-
tion for all future planning in this study. It is treated as a fixed measure and is included in every solution
pathway explored (Gentofte Kommune, n.d.[b]).
Maintaining the required service level, both under current and future climate conditions, is used as a
quantitative performance threshold in the MIKE+ simulations. The system is considered to fail when this
service-level is no longer achieved during a design rainfall event under future climate scenarios. In this
thesis, these standards serve as the basis for evaluating system performance. To maintain the service-level
as well under future climates, climate factors are used to simulate future climate conditions. The defined
service levels (5- and 10-year return periods) are treated as thresholds, and any simulated exceedance of
these thresholds under future climate conditions is a failure to meet the required level of service (Gentofte
Kommune, n.d.[b]).
Another key planning strategy considered in all pathways is the implementation of rainwater purification
measures. All proposed solutions include a treatment step before stormwater is discharged into the Øre-
sund. In consultation with Novafos, the municipality decided that at least 90% of the annual stormwater

4.1
MIKE+ and provided models by NIRAS
For the assessment of the system performance the software MIKE+ is used. MIKE+ is a flexible system
designed for modeling and planning water distribution networks, wastewater and stormwater collection
systems, and river networks. It offers comprehensive tools for hydraulic network modeling and includes
various specialized modules. The hydraulic network modeling elements and properties module represents
all the real network elements like, nodes, pipes and canals, basins etc. MIKE+ includes a variety of
simulation capabilities which enables the user to model e.g. Water Distribution (WD), Collection System
(CS), Rainfall-Runoff (RR) or Water Quality (WQ). Model data can be manually entered, imported from
external sources, or graphically digitized on the map (DHI, 2025a; DHI, 2025b; DHI, 2023).
For this thesis MIKE+ models of the current drainage system and a possible future design of the drainage
system (”Seabed-Tunnel solution”) were provided by NIRAS. These models already included the 1D
hydraulic network with its drainage elements and the input data for the Rainfall-Runoff model e.g. infil-
tration and catchment parameters.
The model of the current system consist of a combined drainage part and a part where a stormwater
system was already implemented. In areas of the combined system, all catchments, including street
catchments, are connected to the combined sewer network. In areas where the stormwater system has
been implemented, street catchments are connected to the stormwater system, while remaining residential
catchments continue to discharge into the combined sewer network (”semi-separation”). Wastewater
contributions are represented by load points for each catchment connected to the combined system.
The model of the ”Seabed-Tunnel solution” represents the future plan of the stormwater system. In this
system the street catchments as well as the residential catchments are connected to this stormwater system,
so a full separation of the entire catchment is simulated. All the infrastructure dimensions and locations,
e.g. pipes, basins, pumps etc., are based on calculations conducted by NIRAS.
For assessing the system performance 1D hydrodynamic simulations of flows and water levels in urban
drainage and wastewater collection networks were performed. Rainfall-runoff calculations are performed
using a simple Time-Area method, relying on a simple treatment of hydrological losses and runoff routing
via the Time-Area curve. The impervious areas and the infiltration parameters were given and predefined
Time-area curves were used. As a rainfall input for the Rainfall-runoff model in MIKE+ Chicago Design
storms were generated. These rain events were then imported in MIKE+ as a dfs.0 file (DHI, 2025a;
DHI, 2025b).
Running the 1D hydrodynamic simulations computes water levels at nodes (e.g. manholes, basins, etc.)
as well as discharge for pipes and functional elements as pumps, weirs, orifices, valves (DHI, 2025b). In
this thesis the following simulation outputs were used:
• water level above ground (node flooding)
• water volume above ground
• link discharge
• pump discharge
• weir discharge
The output parameter node flooding is calculated as the difference between the node water level and the
ground level at that node. For nodes of the type “Manhole,” MIKE+ allows three different cover types.

4.2
SVK-Spreadsheet for generating CDS events
An excel spreadsheet published by SVK was used which allows the user to generate Chicago design
storms (CDS) as rainfall inputs for hydraulic modelling software as MIKE+. A CDS transforms the
statistical information from an Intensity-Duration-Frequency (IDF) curve into a single, symmetric rain
event centered around a peak intensity. The total volume of the CDS corresponds to a block rain of equal
return period and duration. IDF-curves are a commonly used tool for urban drainage design, as they are
depicting the relation between the duration of an rain event and the average intensity for a given return
period. The Spildevandskomiteen (SVK) developed them in Denmark, out of the statistical analysis of
collected rain gauge data of their network of gauges across Denmark. The recorded rainfall events were
analysed in terms of their frequency and intensity, enabling the generation of regional IDF curves. These
curves represent the average rainfall intensity associated with specific return periods and event durations,
and form the basis for standardized design rain events used in urban drainage planning. (Arnbjerg-Nielsen
et al., 2023; Harremoës et al., 2005; Gregersen et al., 2014b; Andersen et al., 2022).
The spreadsheet includes input fields for the specification of rainfall curve characteristics. These include
the coordinates of the project area, the return period of the rainfall event, and the chosen safety factor.
Based on these parameters, the regional IDF curve is computed. In addition, parameters relevant for
pipe dimensioning, such as the CDS rainfall duration, the discretization time step, and an asymmetry
coefficient, must be defined in order to generate a CDS rainfall event. The resulting CDS rainfall is
represented as rainfall intensities per discretization interval and can be exported for use in MIKE+ as a
rainfall input (Spildevandskomiteen, 2024).

land-use data at a resolution of 1 meter for its rainfall-runoff model. Users can instantly access detailed

5.3
Challenges and system constraints
5.3.1
Challenges in the drainage system
The design as a combined system leads to significant challenges during heavy rainfall, when the system
exceeded its capacity. In such cases, combined sewage overflows through manholes, flooding streets,
buildings, and other infrastructure. Additionally, the system releases excess wastewater through the out-
let points directly into the Øresund (CSOs). This discharge can degrade water quality, affecting both the
aquatic ecosystem and human activities like swimming. An environmental impact assessment by RAM-
BØLL (2022) evaluated water quality in Gentofte municipality, revealing that the northern Øresund was
in moderate condition, based on indicators like chlorophyll levels, eelgrass health, benthic organisms, and
chemical status. This area is strongly impacted by emissions from western Sjælland’s coastline, including
CSO discharges from Gentofte municipality and Hellerup (Gentofte Kommune, n.d.[b]; Rambøll, 2022).
Beyond the water quality and flood risk concerns, many pipe sections are old and no longer watertight,
leading to wastewater infiltration into the groundwater or, conversely, groundwater intrusion into the
drainage system, increasing the volume of water sent to treatment plants (Rambøll, 2022).

Due to climate change, both rainfall intensity and the total amount of precipitation are projected to in-
crease. The Danish Meteorological Institute (DMI) provides future climate projections through its Cli-
mate Atlas, offering municipal-level insights into expected precipitation trends under different Repre-
sentative Concentration Pathway (RCP) scenarios. The low-emission scenario (RCP 2.6) aims to limit
global warming to 2°C above pre-industrial levels, while the medium-emission scenario (RCP 4.5) rep-

fossil fuels, leading to severe warming and climate impacts (DMI, 2025; Climate Change (IPCC), 2014).
Each of these projections carries uncertainty, visualized in Figure 5.9, where the rectangle dimensions
represent confidence intervals and the horizontal line inside marks the median estimate.
DMI’s projections extend to 2100, divided into three time periods:
• Early century (2011–2040): A 16% increase in annual cloudburst events under RCP 2.6, rising to
21% under RCP 8.5.
• Mid-century (2041–2070): A 27% increase under RCP 2.6 and 38% under RCP 8.5.
• Late century (2071–2100): The frequency of extreme rainfall events is expected to surge by 71%

The findings highlight a critical trend: short, intense rain events (cloudbursts), are projected to become
significantly more frequent. These extreme events exert the greatest pressure on the drainage system and

Drainage system under the ”Seabed-Tunnel solution” proposed by

5.4.1
Stormwater system
To align with the goals of the current wastewater plan (2022–2032) of Gentofte Municipality, NIRAS
developed a set of possible actions and a meaningful sequencing for their implementation. The core
strategy focuses on the separation of the remaining combined system in Hellerup 3 and the full-separation,
so the connection of road and roof water to the stormwater system, of the currently “semi-separated”
area in Hellerup 1. Unlike the current system, where separated rainwater is discharged untreated into
the Øresund at the end of the Hellerup Tunnel (Lille Strandvej), the separated stormwater must now
undergo treatment to remove nutrients and environmentally hazardous substances before discharge. Due
to the high-density urban development along the Hellerup coastline, local implementation of common
treatment solutions (e.g., rainwater sedimentation basins) is challenging. Furthermore, the municipality
has not provided specific areas to facilitate such local solutions (NIRAS, 2025; NIRAS, 2023a; NIRAS,
2023b).
To address these constraints, NIRAS and Novafos proposed the construction of a partly underwater rain-
water tunnel, referred to in this thesis as the ”Seabed-Tunnel”. This tunnel will convey stormwater col-
lected from Hellerup 1 and Hellerup 3, starting at a shaft in Hellerup Harbour and ending at a second
shaft at Tuborg North, adjacent to an existing pumping station. At Tuborg North, stormwater will un-
dergo treatment using filter technologies designed to meet municipal water quality objectives before being
discharged into the Øresund. In addition to the major components, such as the rainwater tunnel and treat-
ment infrastructure, several supplementary minor solutions are part of this plan. These include the reuse
of existing pipes from the combined system, the construction of new pipes, the installation of new pump-
ing stations, and other necessary modifications to facilitate the full separation of the Hellerup catchment.
The plan was developed based on detailed analyses and calculations by NIRAS, which assessed system
capacities, treatment requirements, and spatial planning considerations (NIRAS, 2025; NIRAS, 2023b).
The hydraulic capacity of the system to handle additional rainwater following the complete separation
was assessed through hydraulic simulations in MIKE+. The treatment performance, ensuring compli-
ance with municipal standards, was evaluated through long-term simulations (LTS) using the SUMBA
software. SUMBA, which functions as a box model, uses long-term rainfall series as input and produces
output in terms of discharges and corresponding infrastructure dimensions, such as basin volumes and
treatment capacities. The resulting design specifications, including treatment infrastructure dimensions
and required inflow capacities, were then integrated back into the MIKE+ model to perform system-level
simulations (NIRAS, 2025).

there are no other treatment pumps in the system. At the KP81 structure in Lille Strandvej a pump with
a capacity of 280 L/s pumps the water to the shaft at Hellerup Harbour and from there it gets conveyed
further to Tuborg. The excess water will be discharged in the Øresund from KP81 via either the coastal
emergency outlet (UR1) or the seabed outlet (UR2). The treatment rate gets not only influenced by the
pumping rate but as well by the connected retention volume, which works as a delay and enables later
treatment. The newly implemented retention volume in Tuborg totals 4,300 m³ and consists of the tunnel
itself as well as two associated shafts, one located at the tunnel inlet in Hellerup Harbour and the other
at the outlet in Tuborg North. No additional retention volumes were established elsewhere in the system
(NIRAS, 2023a).
5.4.3
Wastewater system
In connection with the separation of Hellerup 3, an assessment has been made on whether the combined
system can be reused for wastewater, with the conclusion that there will be challenges with self-cleaning
ability due to lower flow, meaning only a few pipes will be suitable for reuse. So as well new additional
wastewater pipes have to be constructed. The separation project will also lead to changes in existing
outlets and overflows from the project area to Øresund. Most of the currently existing CSOs can be
closed after the separation project, since the system no longer exceeds its capacity during rain, while
some will remain as emergency overflows. In the thesis only the stormwater system will be considered
(NIRAS, 2023a; NIRAS, 2025). Therefore the changes in the wastewater system will not be described
as detailed as the stormwater system.

planning context, described in Chapter 2 (Haasnoot et al., 2013; Kaija Jumppanen Andersen, 2020).
Several modifications were made to adapt the approach to the specific requirements of urban drainage
planning. Notably, the evaluation of adaptation pathways and the formulation of a preferred adaptive
plan were not included, as these were beyond the primary scope of this study. An overview of the applied
methodological steps is presented in fig. 6.1.

6.2
Context for decision-making
The first step of the methodology is the understanding of the decision context. This includes a thorough
description and analysis of the catchment area in Hellerup, focusing on its land use, existing drainage
system, and key boundary conditions as well as an understanding of current challenges and projected
future developments and challenges. To define the planning objectives, the municipal goals outlined in
the Wastewater Plan 2023–2032 were taken into consideration. Based on these overarching objectives,
the specific aims and research questions of this study were developed. A detailed description of the
catchment area and its boundaries is provided in chapter 5, while the objectives and goals of this thesis
are outlined in chapter 3 .
6.3
Adaptation tipping points: conditions and thresholds
As a second step, the conditions for so-called Adaptation Tipping Points (ATPs) are defined. In this
context, time is used as the reference axis, meaning that an ATP is the specific point in time when an action
or system configuration fails to satisfy the defined performance objectives. These objectives are derived
from the generally recommended service-levels in Denmark and policy goals set by the municipality.

The drainage system in Hellerup can be divided into two distinct stages: the current system (pre-separation)
and the future system (post-separation). In its current configuration, Hellerup 3 operates as a combined
sewer system, while Hellerup 1 is considered ”semi-separated”. System failure in the current configura-
tion is defined as the inability of the system to convey runoff from a design storm, resulting in flooding
and/or combined overflows into the Øresund. The return period of the design storm used in the analysis
depends on whether the analysis concerns the stormwater or the combined system.
In the future stage, after full separation of the catchment, only the stormwater system contributes to
flooding and outlet discharge, as wastewater and stormwater flows are entirely separated. System failure
in the future context is defined by two criteria. First the inability to manage runoff from a design storm
under future climate conditions, leading to flooding, and second falling below the 90% annual treatment
rate set by the municipality. Both types of failure represent Adaptation Tipping Points (ATPs) in the
system, i.e., points beyond which the current configuration no longer meets defined objectives.
Before full separation is implemented, CSOs may occur, discharging untreated combined sewage and
stormwater directly into the Øresund. These events are considered especially harmful from both envi-
ronmental and public health perspectives (Rambøll, 2022). As separation progresses, CSOs are expected
to reduce in frequency and volume. Once separation is complete, CSOs are eliminated by definition,
shifting the focus of system performance assessments toward stormwater-only flooding and compliance
with the stormwater treatment requirement, in particular, the 90% annual treatment objective.

Future uncertainties
To develop future scenarios, future changes must be identified and systematically analysed (Marchau et

the following three factors affecting future urban drainage systems are considered most important and
therefore analysed in the thesis:
• Climate change,
• Urban development, and
• Legislative and regulatory changes.

the projected impacts of climate change on future rainfall patterns. Nevertheless, changes in groundwater

As a third uncertainty, future changes in legislation can significantly impact the urban drainage system.
Legislative changes could either tighten requirements, for example, by enforcing stricter service levels or
mandatory green infrastructure, or relax existing standards in response to shifting political or economic

Climate factor data management
To incorporate the effects of five different climate scenarios and three return periods over a full projection

6.4.4
Legislation scenarios
Legislation changes will be incorporated in two different ways. One change for each of the performance
indicators, node flooding and outlet discharge (treatment rate). In both scenarios the selected legislation
change in this thesis will change to more loose policies. A loosen of the policies for the treatment rate is
chosen because the current policies decided by the municipality are quite strict and a loosening of them in
the future is seen as a possible decision in consultation with NIRAS. The current goal of the municipality
is to treat 90% of the annual discharged water before in enters the Øresund. In this thesis a loosening of
this goal to 85% of the annual water is considered as a legislation change scenario. The methodology
how this is realised and simulated in the thesis is explained in section 6.5.3.
The flooding of nodes, is linked to the service-level of the pipes. The current commonly used service-level
for stormwater pipes is 5 years. In the future this standards could shift in both directions, either loosen
or stricten. In this thesis a loosening of this standards is considered to represent a more decentralized
future urban drainage development, focusing on LIDs and adaptation to more frequent flooding instead of
large-scale grey planning approaches that intend to store more water and transport it out of the catchment.
Therefore a reduction of the service-level for the stormwater system down to 4 years will be simulated.

measures will be included as additional or alternative solutions as pathways in the DAPP approach.

6.5.1
General procedure of assessing the system
After the future scenarios were created the CFs were used to generate CDS rains with the spreadsheet.
The MIKE+ simulations for assessing the current system performance were then conducted with the
different design storms by gradually increasing climate factors. The model results were analysed and
the system performance assessed according to the performance criteria and their conditions of failure.
As long as the system was performing good the CF could be increased and the process started from the
beginning. This iterative process continued until the system’s limits were reached and the tipping point
was found. In terms of node flooding, the first simulation run in which flooding at a node occurred, was
the tipping point. In terms of the treatment rate the tipping point was the point in the future were the
required treatment rate was undercut for the first time. This point in the future was expressed by the
climate factor used for the generation of the currently used CDS. With this CF the corresponding years in
the future were calculated each referring to another climate scenario. The stepwise process used to assess

Catalogue of additional measures considered for the ”Seabed-Tunnel solution”
To address localized flooding at a small scale, Low Impact Development (LID) measures and small-scale
grey infrastructure were implemented in MIKE+. One such measure were rainwater barrels installed
adjacent to private residences. These barrels were modeled indirectly by introducing a new catchment
parameter class to represent residential catchments with buildings equipped with such storage systems.
These catchments typically include impervious surfaces, such as roofs of houses, garages, or sheds. To
simulate the water retention effect of rainwater barrels, the initial loss parameter was increased from the
default value of 0.6 mm to higher values (e.g., 10–20 mm). This adjustment reflects the volume of rainfall
retained before contributing to surface runoff. For instance, an initial loss of 20 mm corresponds approx-
imately to 1 m³ of storage per 50 m² of roof area. Accordingly, a house with a 150 m² roof area would be
equivalent to a 3 m³ rainwater tank, a relatively large tank size. However, given that these measures were
only applied in a limited number of catchments, such dimensions can be considered feasible, especially
with potential municipal support. The chosen storage values are not derived from specific literature but
are pragmatic estimates intended to reflect realistic tank capacities. It was assumed that tanks are fully
emptied before each rain event, and potential partial filling (e.g., from household use for garden watering)
was not accounted for. Hence, the modeled purpose of the tanks is solely stormwater retention, not water
reuse.
A second option involved modifying the surface of road catchments. In MIKE+, this was represented
by reducing the impervious fraction by 20% to simulate the installation of swales or raingardens along
roadsides. These green infrastructure elements are designed to increase infiltration, reducing the volume
of runoff entering the stormwater network. For simplicity, the infiltration capacity of these elements is
not modeled explicitly, but their effect is approximated by assuming that the portion of rain, falling on
those structures infiltrates naturally, like on a pervious area.
A third, more intensive intervention consists of constructing underground basins near critical nodes.
These basins can be realized either by enlarging existing manholes or adding separate subsurface struc-
tures, with appropriate basin geometries defined in MIKE+. While this measure is more effective for
mitigating localized flooding, it is also more complex and costly and is therefore considered a secondary
option.

6.6
Development and assessment of alternative solutions
6.6.1
No-Tunnel Solution
To avoid constructing the tunnel from Hellerup Harbor to Tuborg North, an alternative solution was
developed that decentralizes stormwater treatment across three outlet points within the catchment. This
configuration is based on a preliminary screening by NIRAS, referred to as the “HSE Scenario 2” (NIRAS,
2023a). In this scenario, referred to as the ”No-Tunnel solution”, stormwater treatment facilities are
implemented at Lille Strandvej, Hellerup Harbor, and Tuborg North.
At Lille Strandvej, the existing pumping structure KP81 remains in place, maintaining its function with
both coastal and seabed overflows. Unlike the “Seabed-Tunnel solution,” which includes an additional
pump transferring water to the tunnel’s starting point at Hellerup Harbor, the alternative model features a
new pump with a capacity of 165 L/s that represents the treatment at Lille Strandvej, which subsequently
discharges into the Øresund. Consequently, the connection from Lille Strandvej to Hellerup Harbor was
deactivated in the model. This treatment unit is designed to process stormwater from Hellerup 1 and parts
of the Aurehøjvej sub-catchment.
At Hellerup Harbor, a new retention basin with a volume of 2,900 m³ was implemented, in line with
NIRAS’ design for this scenario (NIRAS, 2023a). This basin is equipped with a treatment pump rated
at 60 L/s and an overflow pump for excess discharge, which bypasses treatment and discharges directly
into the Øresund. The inflow to this facility primarily originates from the Sofievej and Sankt Peders Vej
sub-catchments.
At Tuborg North, the outlet is connected only to the Tuborg Parkvej sub-catchment under this scenario.
Accordingly, the treatment capacity was reduced to 60 L/s, based on NIRAS’ calculations (NIRAS,
2023a).
In addition to the distributed infrastructure, this solution offers a potential reduction in project construc-
tion time. According to NIRAS’ implementation plan (table A.1), the tunnel construction would require
approximately four years. By omitting the tunnel, these four years could be saved, allowing for an ear-
lier project completion in 2052 instead of 2056. This earlier readiness is considered in all subsequent
simulations. A map illustrating the modified infrastructure layout and the locations of the decentralized
treatment facilities is presented in the following figure.

6.6.2
No-Tunnel Solution with LIDs
To explore an alternative to traditional grey infrastructure solutions, a scenario relying primarily on low-
impact development measures was analysed. The focus was on rainwater barrels that private property
owners could realistically install. Each property was assumed capable of accommodating a rainwater
tank of approximately 2 m3, a sizeable but feasible capacity. Within the MIKE+ model, rainwater tanks
were implemented for 276 roofs across the catchment. For context, the total Hellerup catchment area
includes roughly 3570 buildings, as determined using SCALGO data (Scalgo, n.d.) and analysed with
QGIS.
The design threshold for this measures was a 5-year CDS event with a climate factor (CF) of 1.49. The
rainwater tanks were implemented in the MIKE+ model which contained the existing drainage system
including previously implemented local flood mitigation measures (CF 1.3 mixed additional measures,
appendix A.4.3). Because the original local flood mitigation measures also included rainwater tanks, but
implemented indirectly through changed infiltration parameters, these were removed before introducing
the new tanks. The new rainwater tanks were directly implemented per catchment, with an important
Future-Proofing Urban Drainage
45
==PAGE:54==
modification: they were equipped with a throttle pipe to continuously discharge water, thereby keeping
the tank available to accommodate peak rainfall. In contrast, the initial rainwater tanks lacked this feature,
simply storing the first millimetres of rainfall until the tank is full. This provided only minimal flood
mitigation due to bad peak rainfall timing. With the new method, the rainwater tanks have a higher
probability to cut the peak of the rain event and therefore show a much larger effect on flood mitigation.
The tanks were not implemented on building-level but as combined basins at a catchment scale or across
multiple catchments connected to the same drainage node. For instance in a catchment containing of 6
buildings a basin with a volume of 12 m3 in total was implemented as an additional manhole with with
the specific volume set by depth and diameter. Runoff from the catchment was directed into this tank
and then discharged via a throttle pipe (3-10 cm diameter, depending on the number of buildings) into
the main stormwater system. When the inflow into the basin exceeds the outflow through the throttle
pipe, the basin starts to fill up until reaching a weir positioned near the tank’s top. Once full, excess
water spilled over this weir into the stormwater network. The throttle pipe’s diameter was optimized to
achieve maximum storage efficiency and peak flow attenuation. In total 35 retention tanks were strategi-
cally placed experiencing high loads or under-dimensioned capacities. A detailed map illustrating these
locations is included in the Appendix (fig. A.18). Overall, 106 catchments containing 276 roofs were
equipped with rainwater tanks. The implementation of the rainwater tanks will start directly after the
”No-Tunnel solution” is completed in the year 2052 and is assumed to take 5 years, so will therefore be
completely implemented in 2057. The following figure illustrates the workwise and the implementation

6.7
Design and evaluation of pathways
The conducted simulations identified the Climate Factors (CFs) at which the drainage systems including
certain actions begin to fail. In this context, failure is defined as the system no longer meeting the required
performance criteria, namely, the service level and treatment rate. Within the framework of the Dynamic
Adaptive Policy Pathways (DAPP) approach, these CF values represent the system’s tipping points. By
applying different climate scenarios, each CF can be translated into a specific year, which varies depend-
ing on the scenario. Based on this, adaptation pathways can be developed as sequences of actions or
measures, where transitions from one action to the next occur before a tipping point is reached under a
given climate scenario. Besides the tipping points as well the implementation times for the actions are
being considered, to estimate a meaningful point of time to switch to another action. In this thesis, the
selected actions are aligned with the defined performance criteria. Separate sets of actions were evaluated
for addressing flooding and treatment performance, resulting in distinct pathways for each criterion. The
resulting adaptation pathways were visualized using pathway maps, a method introduced in Chapter 2,
which are considered the state-of-the-art tool for presenting DAPP outcomes. These maps were created
for each climate scenario and for both performance criteria individually. A summary of the considered
actions for the flood mitigation and as well for the treatment performance and the pathway maps are
shown in the results part under section 7.6.

Additional measures to comply with a 5-year CDS under a CF of 1.49
Since the simulated water volume above ground under CF 1.49 conditions (106 m3) is significantly lower
and the flooding is concentrated around local bottlenecks, it was assessed whether this volume could be
realistically mitigated through local-scale flood retention measures. Therefore further simulations were
conducted in MIKE+ following a trial and error approach. The possible measures out of section 6.5.3
and several combinations were analysed, and their effectiveness was evaluated based on the reduction
in water volume above ground. In general, the approach prioritized measures with lower construction
impact and minimal nuisance to the public.
An overall commitment scenario, where all residents of Hellerup 1 implement rainwater tanks on their
properties, was assessed. This was simulated by adjusting the initial loss for all residential catchments as
mentioned in section 6.5.3. Various levels of initial loss were tested (5 mm, 10 mm, 15 mm, and 20 mm),
Future-Proofing Urban Drainage
60
==PAGE:69==
corresponding to different tank sizes. For example, an initial loss of 10 mm represents approximately a
2 m3 tank for a 200 m² roof area. Applying 5 mm or 10 mm of initial loss had no effect on flood reduction.
A minor improvement was observed at 15 mm. Only the 20 mm scenario showed a significant reduction
in flooding. However, achieving 20 mm of initial loss would require a rainwater tank of approximately
4 m3 for a typical 200 m² roof area, which is considered unrealistic on a large residential scale.
Therefore, a combination of locally appropriate low-impact development (LID) solutions, subsurface
basins, and selective pipe enlargements was applied to reduce flooding under a 5-year CDS event with
a climate factor (CF) of 1.49. A detailed description and mapping of the implemented measures are
provided in the appendix A.4.4. Through the implementation of these measures, the water volume above
ground could be fully eliminated for a 5-year CDS under a CF of 1.49. It should be noted that these
measures could be further expanded or additional implementations introduced to address flooding under
even higher CF values. However, simulations using a CF of 1.72 revealed significantly larger volumes
of water above ground and showed that flooding also begins to affect areas in Hellerup 3. This indicates
that at such high CFs, localized interventions may no longer be sufficient. Instead, more extensive and
systemic changes, such as the construction of larger retention and treatment infrastructures, could be
required, especially to as well achieve flood risk mitigation for rain events with higher return periods
For this reason, no additional adaptation measures are proposed within the scope of this thesis. However,
the above-ground water volumes under higher climate factors (CFs) were still calculated and are discussed
for reference. Notably, by designing the system to perform up to a CF of 1.49, flooding can be avoided
throughout the planning horizon until 2123 under all climate scenarios, except SVK-High. Under the
SVK-High scenario, further measures would be required to maintain service-level compliance beyond
the year 2083, which corresponds to CF = 1.49. If no additional measures are implemented, flooding will
occur under higher climate conditions, reaching an above-ground water volume of 310 m3 by 2123 (CF
= 1.72).

8
Discussion
The focus of this study is on the application and incorporation of the DAPP approach into urban drainage
planning and to evaluate its advantages and disadvantages compared to conventional planning approaches.
The aim was not to find a solution for the urban drainage project in Hellerup but to use this case study to
identify general key uncertainties in urban drainage projects and incorporating them into future scenarios,
as well as testing how quantitative simulations (MIKE+) can be included in the DAPP approach. So the
thesis followed a more exploratory approach, than a solution finding approach.
8.1
Interpretation of the key findings
8.1.1
Future uncertainties and the DAPP approach
The basis for the calculations of the performance of the different drainage system scenarios and their tip-
ping points are the different future scenarios. The identified future uncertainties of the drainage system
where then included into future scenarios. Here the focus of this thesis primarily was on the implica-
tions of climate change on the water balance particularly on the rain events. In general planning in urban
drainage can reach a state of deep uncertainty, where the future is so unpredictable, that stakeholders can-
not agree on models, probabilities, or even the set of possible future scenarios affecting urban drainage,
such as climate change, urbanization, or policy shifts (Babovic et al., 2018; Sadr et al., 2018). Based
on this knowledge it has to be emphasized, that the selected future scenarios as well as the actions con-
sidered in this thesis are not predictions of how the future will unfold, but rather represent a range of
possible responses to uncertain and changing conditions. While the scenarios and actions are mapped
out in advance, the actual future may differ from any single scenario, so the DAPP approach emphasizes
flexibility and monitoring of the actual future development. This means that DAPP is not about choosing
a single scenario, but about preparing a set of robust, adaptable strategies that can be deployed as needed,
making urban drainage systems more resilient to deep uncertainty and a wide range of possible futures
(Babovic et al., 2018; Marchau et al., 2019; Haasnoot et al., 2013). The range of future scenarios and
adaptation actions considered in this thesis can be of course expanded by far more possible scenarios and
actions, for instance, different legislation changes, combinations of grey and green infrastructure as an
adaptation solution etc.
8.1.2
Climate factors
The used climate factors to represent the effect of climate change on the rainfall intensities were derived
from different sources and are based on different calculation methods. Both CF values were used in the
spreadsheet provided by the SVK to generate CDS events. Based on this, the question arises if it is ac-
ceptable to apply DMI’s climate factors within the SVK spreadsheet to generate future rainfall events,
given that the two approaches rely on different baseline rainfall intensities. The median accumulated
rainfall intensity over one hour during the reference period (1981–2010) in DMI’s dataset, for a given
return period, is lower than the accumulated intensity of a one-hour CDS event with the same return
period generated by the SVK spreadsheet (using a climate factor of 1). This discrepancy is primarily
due to differences in the underlying data sources. DMI’s lower baseline values likely result from the use
of bias-corrected, gridded rainfall data to calibrate their climate models, whereas SVK relies on point
measurements from rain gauges, which typically capture higher localized intensities compared to the
spatially smoothed gridded datasets. These differences are recognized and acknowledged in this thesis
to ensure transparency regarding the methodological choices. Nevertheless, since climate factors repre-
sent relative changes between future and baseline conditions, the application of DMI’s climate factors to
the SVK-based rainfall intensities is considered acceptable and appropriate for the purposes of scenario
development (DMI, 2024; Arnbjerg-Nielsen et al., 2023; Gregersen et al., 2014a).

8.1.3
Performance of the drainage system
Sewer service-level compliance in the face of climate change uncertainty
In this thesis simulations of the current drainage system were conducted, to analyse the performance of
the status-quo as well as simulations of possible future solutions to analyse the future development of the
performance. All the simulations were conducted under different future scenarios to first of all identify
how the performance varies between them and as a second step see how mitigation actions could increase
the performance under the different scenarios and also identify how they differ between them. To analyse
the sewer performance in terms of flooding the required service-level of the drainage system was used a
a quantitative threshold of performance.
The results show, that the current combined drainage system in the Hellerup 3 sub-catchment, is not
complying with the service-level standards by far. Under design events the system is heavily overloaded,
leading to water above ground in many parts of the catchment, emphasising the urgent need for adaptation
and long-term infrastructure upgrades to ensure future resilience.
Because of the awareness of the poor condition of the drainage system in Gentofte Municipality, a de-
cision was made in the previous Wastewater Plan to “semi-separate” the drainage system in Hellerup 1,
meaning that only the stormwater from roads was diverted into a new stormwater system, while runoff
from residential areas remained connected to the combined system. In the current Wastewater Plan pub-
lished in 2022, the municipality formally decided to shift from road-water separation to full separation
throughout the entire Hellerup catchment (Gentofte Kommune, n.d.[a]). Within just a few years, the strat-
egy was fundamentally changed. However, this shift now brings considerable challenges: many of the
pipes that were originally dimensioned for a semi-separated system are undersized for the increased loads
of a fully separated system, resulting in hydraulic bottlenecks and difficulties in maintaining sewer com-
pliance (NIRAS, 2023b). These issues could potentially have been avoided with a more forward-looking,
flexible planning approach that accounted for future uncertainties. Applying adaptive frameworks such as
the Dynamic Adaptive Policy Pathways (DAPP) approach could have supported a more robust transition,
reducing the risk of such mismatches between infrastructure design and future system requirements.
The proposed “Seabed-Tunnel solution” by NIRAS, which aligns with the municipality’s updated sepa-
ration plans, was evaluated in terms of its performance under various future scenarios. The results clearly
revealed the mentioned service-level compliance issues in the Hellerup 1 area. As this system has already
been constructed and pipe dimensions cannot be changed, additional measures are required in these areas
to ensure compliance. Example measures were implemented in the MIKE+ model, successfully maintain-
ing compliance with the service-level up to a Climate Factor (CF) of 1.3, considered a standard threshold
in urban drainage planning. However, under more extreme climate scenarios such as SVK-High and DMI
RCP-8.5, the “Seabed-Tunnel solution” would reach its tipping point within the planning horizon, need-
ing further interventions to uphold service-level standards and prevent surface flooding (see: figs. 7.18
and 7.19). Conversely, under lower climate scenarios like DMI RCP 2.6 and 4.5, the system appears over-
dimensioned for the current planning horizon until 2123 (see: table 7.5). These findings underline the
significant impact that different future climate scenarios can have on the drainage system’s performance,
and on flood risk. This highlights the importance of incorporating a wide range of future conditions into
the urban drainage planning process to ensure robust and adaptive system design.
A set of local, small-scale measures (”CF 1.49 mixed mitigation measures”) (see: table A.3) was imple-
mented at critical locations within the Hellerup 1 catchment. These adaptations successfully postponed
the tipping point of the “Seabed-Tunnel solution” until the end of the planning horizon under the DMI
Future-Proofing Urban Drainage

==PAGE:87==
RCP-8.5 scenario, and until 2083 under the SVK-High scenario (see: fig. 7.19). This suggests that the
tunnel-based solution has a certain degree of flexibility and adaptability, enabling it to remain functional
even under more severe climate scenarios. However, it was also observed that with further increasing
climate factors (beyond CF = 1.49), water volumes above ground began to appear at an increasing num-
ber of locations across the catchment, indicating a more widespread exceedance of the system’s capacity
(see: figs. 7.11 and 7.12). Whether the system could be further adapted to maintain performance under
such conditions was not investigated within this thesis. Nevertheless, it is expected that such adaptations
would be significantly more complex and costly, particularly if the tunnel itself reaches its design limits.
This highlights that while the “Seabed-Tunnel solution” is adaptable in the face of localized exceedances,
its flexibility decreases when the exceedance becomes widespread. Once critical infrastructure such as
the tunnel approaches its capacity limits, the system’s overall adaptability is substantially reduced. To
gain a deeper understanding of the tunnel’s behaviour and that of the connected infrastructure, it would be
beneficial to simulate the system under more extreme rainfall events. This would help assess the actual
flood risk and explore the potential for further adaptation to mitigate these risks effectively.
Given that fundamental legislative and strategic changes in urban drainage have occurred repeatedly in
the past, such as the revision of separation plans in Gentofte Municipality (Gentofte Kommune, n.d.[a])
or the development of the Cloudburst Management Plan in Copenhagen (City of Copenhagen, 2012), it
is reasonable to assume that similar shifts will take place in the future. In this thesis, the hypothetical
adjustment of the service-level requirement from a 5-year to a 4-year design storm already demonstrated
a considerable impact on system performance. A broader shift in perspective, from traditional, fully
engineered systems toward more adaptive, “living with water” approaches such as Sustainable Urban
Drainage Systems (SUDS), could potentially lead to a further relaxation or redefinition of service-level
expectations. In this context, adaptation to more intense precipitation and runoff should not rely solely on
expanding grey infrastructure but also emphasize reducing flood risk through the integration of nature-
based solutions and strategies that replicate natural hydrological processes. This paradigm shift has the
potential to create more resilient urban drainage systems that can flexibly respond to a wide range of
future conditions. From this perspective, it could be argued that the “Seabed-Tunnel solution”, as an
example of a highly engineered, centralized infrastructure, may limit such flexibility. By focusing on
conveying stormwater out of the catchment and into the Tuborg area via a tunnel, this solution reinforces
a grey infrastructure lock-in and distances the system from the principles of a more decentralized and
adaptive water cycle management. Consequently, opportunities for transitioning towards a nature-based
or hybrid approach may become constrained, reducing the ability to integrate future shifts in strategy or
policy.
Due to the potential lock-in effects and the limited flexibility associated with the tunnel, as well as other
drawbacks such as high construction costs, resource consumption, and environmental impacts, not ex-
plicitly assessed in this thesis, an alternative scenario that avoids the tunnel was developed and evalu-
ated. Simulation results showed that, in terms of service-level compliance, the “No-Tunnel solution”
performs similarly to the tunnel-based approach under future climate scenarios (see: figs. 7.18 and 7.19).
Avoiding the tunnel could offer several advantages: reduced construction and maintenance costs, lower
resource consumption, shorter implementation time (estimated at four years in this thesis), and most
notably, increased flexibility to adapt to future changes (climate, legislation, etc.) to avoid under- or
over-dimensioning. Moreover, it keeps the option to shift toward more sustainable water management
strategies by keeping stormwater within the catchment and supporting a more natural water cycle. As
part of this alternative pathway, the implementation of rainwater barrels on private properties was con-
sidered as an alternative for the originally proposed small-scale grey infrastructure measures. These Low
Impact Development (LID) solutions are aligned with municipal restrictions on infiltration due to high
groundwater levels and have shown comparable flood reduction performance to the grey infrastructure
interventions. Although private property implementation introduces certain challenges, these LID mea-
sures represent a promising decentralized alternative and offer the potential to be integrated not only with

the No-Tunnel solution but also in combination with the tunnel-based scenario. This underlines the value
of hybrid approaches that combine centralized infrastructure with distributed, nature-based solutions to
enhance system adaptability and sustainability.
In this thesis the service-level of the drainage system was chosen as the performance threshold to esti-
mate whether the drainage system is performing in alignment with the requirements. Consequently, the
simulations were conducted using design storms with return periods of 5 and 10 years. This methodolog-
ical choice emphasizes compliance with sewer design standards rather than addressing objectives such
as comprehensive flood risk assessment and flood mitigation, which would typically involve simulations
of more extreme events, such as cloudbursts or storms with higher return periods. Nonetheless, the use
of design storms still enabled an assessment of system performance under different future scenarios and
facilitated the identification of tipping points. The simulation results clearly show that when the service
level is exceeded, indicated by water appearing above ground, this situation would be exacerbated under
more extreme rainfall conditions. Thus, even though larger events were not directly simulated, it can
be inferred that flood impacts would scale accordingly. Furthermore, the simulations revealed key bot-
tlenecks in the system, locations where capacity is exceeded, which would remain critical under more
intense rainfall. To more accurately evaluate the flood extent and associated risks under future climate
scenarios, additional simulations using higher return periods and a coupled 1D-2D modeling approach
could be employed. This would allow for a more spatially representation of surface flooding and provide
valuable insights into risk hotspots. Such analyses offer an opportunity for future research to enhance
understanding of flood dynamics under deep uncertainty and support more resilient drainage planning.
Stormwater treatment performance under climate change uncertainty
The analysis of outlet discharges from the current drainage system highlighted the critical issue of com-
bined sewer overflows (CSOs) and untreated stormwater discharges. In particular, the combined outlet
U17 and the stormwater outlet UR2 exhibited high discharge volumes during a 10-year design event (see:
fig. 7.7). The under-dimensioned combined sewer system leads to CSOs occurring more frequently than
in a system designed to meet service-level standards. Furthermore, the nearby positioning of the two
key outlets creates a localized pollution hotspot during heavy rainfall events (see: fig. 5.8). Already in
the simulated baseline year 2025, discharge volumes varied considerably between the different climate
scenarios, indicating the sensitivity of the system to changing rainfall patterns.
To address these issues, the current wastewater plan of Gentofte Municipality sets the objective of treating
90% of the annual stormwater volume. This requires treatment infrastructure to be installed before any
discharge into the Øresund. However, due to limited space within the Hellerup catchment, the “Seabed-
Tunnel” was proposed as a solution to transfer stormwater to Tuborg, where sufficient space for treatment
is available. Thus, the primary reason behind constructing the tunnel is to enable compliance with the
treatment target. Nevertheless the results showed that the event-based treatment rates during the simulated
design events are considerably lower than the targeted 90% annual treatment rate. As a result, during
rainfall events that exceed the system’s treatment capacity, significant volumes of untreated stormwater
are discharged, potentially compromising water quality despite meeting annual averages.
It is important to note that the 90% treatment requirement was established based on current climate condi-
tions. Unlike the service-level for sewer design, the impact of future climate change on treatment capacity
has not been incorporated into this target. This is clearly illustrated by the simulation results: under the
SVK-Standard climate scenario, the event-based treatment rate already falls below the threshold in the
very first year after the treatment becomes operational (see: fig. 7.13). As rainfall intensities increase
with climate change, the treatment rate declines further over time. This underscores a major gap in cur-
rent planning approaches, namely, the absence of climate change considerations in stormwater treatment
strategies. If this remains unaddressed, the volume of untreated stormwater discharging into the Øresund
will continue to rise, counteracting the original purpose of the tunnel.
On the other hand, the results demonstrate that a slight reduction of the annual treatment rate from 90% to

==PAGE:89==
85% can have a considerable impact on system performance under future scenarios. With the exception of
the SVK-High scenario, all other scenarios remain above the revised threshold throughout the planning
horizon (see: fig. 7.13). In practical terms, this means that a small relaxation of the treatment target
would significantly reduce the required treatment capacity, along with the associated costs and resource
and planning demands. This is largely because maintaining a high treatment rate becomes increasingly
challenging as rainfall intensity grows. This finding opens up a broader discussion on the necessity
and environmental relevance of the strict 90% threshold. While this target aims to minimize pollution
discharged into the Øresund, it is worth questioning whether such a high rate is still justified in a future
where achieving it could require disproportionate investments. If the environmental benefits of the last
few percentage points are marginal, particularly under extreme rainfall conditions, it may be appropriate
to reconsider the threshold.
However, if the municipality remains committed to the 90% target despite future challenges, additional
investments in treatment infrastructure will be essential to maintain compliance as climate change pro-
gresses. With an increase in the treatment flow from the initially designed 310 L/s to 430 L/s, achieved by
adding two extra treatment units, the system’s performance can exceed the 90% annual treatment thresh-
old under the three lower climate scenarios (see: fig. 7.15). This highlights that, due to the available space
in Tuborg, the system offers the potential for future capacity upgrades, making it adaptable to changing
conditions and uncertainties. If the 90% treatment rate is intended to be upheld throughout the entire
planning horizon, it would be advisable to install a higher treatment capacity, such as the two additional
units, as part of the initial implementation of the full “Seabed-Tunnel solution” project.
In contrast, the “No-Tunnel solution” is significantly limited by spatial constraints, which restrict the
installation of additional centralized treatment units. As a result, its treatment performance starts at a
lower level and continues to fall well below the 90% threshold across the planning horizon (see: fig. 7.16).
When considering only centralized treatment strategies, the ”Seabed-Tunnel solution” which enables full
transport and treatment of stormwater at Tuborg, demonstrates greater adaptability and can be considered
more robust and resilient under deep uncertainty.
However, if future developments in urban drainage shift toward decentralized treatment concepts or if
the strict 90% requirement is revised, the ”No-Tunnel solution” could offer greater long-term flexibility.
Avoiding the tunnel keeps more decision space open, allowing adaptation toward alternative, decentral-
ized strategies that align with nature-based and distributed water management principles. Constructing
the tunnel early on effectively locks the system into a centralized strategy, limiting the potential for such
a transition. Nonetheless, the simulation results showed that the “No-Tunnel solution” would fall below
even an 85% annual treatment threshold under higher climate scenarios (see: fig. 7.16 and ??), indicating
that more adjustments or relaxed targets would be necessary to maintain compliance.
Decentralized stormwater handling (property-scale rainwater barrels) was also tested, but their effect on
treatment performance was found to be minimal. This is partly due to the simplified implementation
in MIKE+ (see: fig. 7.21). However, the primary purpose of these LID measures was to temporarily
retain water and reduce pressure on sewer capacity, not to directly improve the treatment rate. With
more targeted design, decentralized systems could potentially contribute more meaningfully to stormwa-
ter treatment, though this was not explored in detail in this thesis.
8.1.4
Evaluation of the different solutions
Since the primary objective of this thesis was not to identify the most preferred adaptation pathway or
sequence of actions for the catchment, a Multi-Criteria Analysis (MCA), which is typically a core com-
ponent of the DAPP methodology, was not conducted. Nevertheless, the analysed solutions were briefly
assessed based on key decision-making criteria such as adaptability and robustness, which together con-
tribute to the overall resilience of the system under uncertain future conditions. In summary, the “Seabed-
Tunnel solution” would introduce a strong dependency on a centralized, engineered approach, thereby
limiting the system’s ability to evolve towards alternative strategies in the future, such as decentralized

==PAGE:90==
concepts, nature-based solutions, or “living with water” approaches. In contrast, the “No-Tunnel solu-
tion” showed similar performance with regard to service-level compliance and offers greater adaptability
to a broader range of future scenarios, making it more dynamically robust and potentially more resilient
overall. However, in terms of stormwater treatment, the centralized tunnel system connected to Tuborg al-
lows for more scalability, as the available space offers flexibility for additional treatment units to be added
in response to increasing rainfall intensities. The No-Tunnel approach, constrained by limited space in
the Hellerup catchment, lacks this scalability in its current form. Nevertheless, if future planning would
as well integrate decentralized retention and treatment measures, the adaptability of the No-Tunnel solu-
tion could be enhanced. Overall, the results suggest that while the Seabed-Tunnel solution offers greater
flexibility within a centralized treatment framework, the No-Tunnel solution provides higher adaptability
and dynamic robustness in the broader context of urban drainage system resilience.
8.2
Evaluation of the DAPP approach
A central objective of this thesis was to evaluate how the integration of the Dynamic Adaptive Policy
Pathways (DAPP) approach can enhance urban drainage planning, particularly regarding adaptability
and dynamic robustness under future uncertainties. This chapter critically assesses the application of the
DAPP approach in the context of the Hellerup drainage system.
One of the key advantages of incorporating frameworks like DAPP into urban drainage planning is the
explicit focus on deep uncertainty and its implications for long-term infrastructure performance. Despite
growing scientific evidence that urban drainage systems, due to their long design lifespans, are exposed
to a wide range of unpredictable future developments, current planning practices often remain based
on a “predict-then-act” paradigm. Within this paradigm, single-scenario forecasts, supported by safety
factors, dominate planning processes, while the variability and uncertainty of future conditions are largely
overlooked. The DAPP approach challenges this traditional mindset by promoting the exploration of
multiple plausible futures and integrating uncertainty explicitly into planning decisions (Babovic et al.,
2018; Marchau et al., 2019).
The initial step of the DAPP process is the identification of key uncertainties relevant to the planning
context. In this thesis, such uncertainties were identified through an in-depth analysis of the catchment’s
characteristics and influencing factors. While stakeholder workshops are typically involved to define
these uncertainties, this thesis followed a more research-based approach due to time constraints. Nonethe-
less, the process of thoroughly understanding the catchment context was proved to be essential for con-
structing meaningful future scenarios. Unlike conventional applications of the DAPP approach, which
often rely on qualitative and stakeholder-driven methods, the methodology in this thesis incorporated
a quantitative modelling approach using MIKE+. This created new challenges: translating qualitative
uncertainties and adaptation options into quantitative model inputs, boundary conditions, and hydraulic
structures. For example, modelling every design variant of a stormwater system or implementing LID
measures such as rainwater barrels required simplifications and approximations. Similarly, incorporat-
ing certain future drivers, such as urban densification or sea level rise, into the hydraulic model proved
difficult and time-intensive. These modelling limitations raise questions about the reliability of quantita-
tive results, especially when key components must be represented in a highly abstracted manner, as was
the case with the simplified implementation of LID measures in this thesis. The analysis in this thesis
also demonstrated that different types of simplifications, such as the representation of rainwater barrels,
can lead to varying results in terms of their performance and functioning, thereby influencing the overall
flood response of the system in significantly different ways.
Despite these challenges, the inclusion of quantitative simulations adds considerable value to the DAPP
process. While qualitative assessments and stakeholder input help to build a shared understanding and
identify relevant actions and conditions for tipping points, the integration of hydraulic models can pro-
vide evidence-based insights into system behaviour. It is recommended, however, that prior to building
and simulating numerous scenarios, a pre-selection of key actions and uncertainties be made, for instance
Future-Proofing Urban Drainage
82
==PAGE:91==
through participatory processes, to reduce modelling complexity and ensure focus on the most impactful
aspects. In this thesis, the primary uncertainty explored was the effect of climate change on rainfall in-
tensities, which was identified as a highly sensitive and influential factor. Other uncertainties were either
considered less significant or excluded due to limitations in time and modelling complexity. Similarly,
only a limited set of adaptation actions were implemented and assessed in the simulations. This subset
represents only a small fraction of the theoretically infinite set of possible actions and levels of detail that
could be considered. Again, this highlights the value of involving stakeholders early in the DAPP process
to prioritize critical uncertainties and actionable measures that are most relevant to local objectives and
constraints.
Another relevant point in the evaluation of the DAPP approach is the role of modelling software, in
this case, MIKE+, for representing future uncertainties and adaptation actions. In this thesis, MIKE+
was applied in a 1D configuration without the 2D surface flow module, which proved to be efficient in
terms of simulation runtimes, typically ranging from 30 seconds to 8 minutes depending on the simu-
lated system. This setup enabled a large number of simulations within a reasonable timeframe, making
it suitable for scenario-based planning. This observation also relates directly to one of the sub-research
questions of this thesis: What level of model complexity is appropriate for small-scale applications, and
how can this complexity be reduced while still producing meaningful results? In this context, the chosen
model complexity, 1D network modelling without 2D surface coupling, proved sufficient to answer the
main research questions, particularly regarding service-level compliance and tipping points under vari-
ous climate scenarios. While more detailed modelling (e.g. full 1D-2D integration) would offer a deeper
spatial insight, especially in terms of flood risk assessment. However, the added complexity and signifi-
cantly longer simulation times would not necessarily lead to proportionally more meaningful results for
the scope of this project. Therefore, for small-scale DAPP applications focused on system performance
thresholds, a simplified model can be both appropriate and effective. Implementing a broad range of
future uncertainties and adaptation actions in MIKE+ can be time-consuming and technically demand-
ing. The high level of hydraulic detail, may exceed the needs of exploratory, strategic planning. In such
cases, tools like SCALGO Live may offer advantages. SCALGO allows for fast scenario testing and eas-
ier representation of surface-based adaptations such as LID or nature-based solutions (SCALGO, 2024).
Ultimately, the choice of modelling software and its level of complexity should align with the purpose of
the assessment. If the goal is to evaluate system performance at a detailed hydraulic level, MIKE+ is in
advantage. However, when a broader, less technically detailed screening of surface and land-use scenar-
ios is sufficient, simpler tools like SCALGO may be more appropriate. This highlights the importance
of balancing detail with efficiency, particularly in DAPP applications, so that simulation outputs remain
actionable and informative without becoming prohibitively complex or resource-intensive.
A key disadvantage of the DAPP approach is that its implementation can require substantial resources,
including extensive data collection, continuous monitoring, and active stakeholder engagement, factors
that may present challenges for smaller municipalities or organizations with limited capacity. In the urban
drainage context, applying DAPP typically involves running numerous simulations using hydrological
models such as MIKE+, which can be both time-consuming and computationally demanding. This com-
plexity increases further when accounting for multiple uncertainties, hazards, and potential adaptation
actions (Babovic et al., 2018; Haasnoot et al., 2023).
Based on the experience gathered in this thesis, it is essential to adapt the DAPP approach to the spe-
cific context of the project. While DAPP provides a structured, stepwise framework for exploring and
evaluating adaptive strategies, it is not a one-size-fits-all methodology. Each project requires a careful
consideration of its unique objectives, scale, data availability, and institutional setting. It is therefore
important that stakeholders define in advance what the purpose and expected outcomes of applying the
DAPP approach are. This includes deciding on the appropriate level of detail in the model outputs, the
number and type of uncertainties and hazards to be included, the scope and scale of possible actions,
and which stages of the DAPP process should be emphasized. Research such as this thesis, and related
Future-Proofing Urban Drainage
83
==PAGE:92==
studies, such as the adaptation of DAPP for the Danish municipal context, can serve as valuable guidance
for municipalities, utilities, and consultants. They provide insight into practical interpretations of the
approach and illustrate how the individual DAPP steps can be adjusted to fit specific planning challenges
and institutional frameworks.
It finally should be mentioned, that a fundamental characteristic of the DAPP approach is its iterative
nature. DAPP is designed as a cyclical process where decisions are revisited and updated as new in-
formation emerges and as conditions evolve, rather than being a one-time, static plan. This involves
regularly monitoring key indicators and signals, assessing whether adaptation thresholds or triggers are
being approached, and then adjusting actions or pathways accordingly (Babovic et al., 2018; Marchau
et al., 2019).
8.3
Scope and limitations
While this thesis provides valuable insights into the application of the DAPP approach for urban drainage
planning, several limitations should be acknowledged that influence the scope and interpretation of the
results.
First, the DAPP framework acknowledges uncertainty through the use of scenario-based planning and
adaptive actions. However, in the context of this study, the implementation of future uncertainties and
adaptation measures within the MIKE+ model required substantial simplifications. The hydraulic mod-
elling of complex interventions, such as stormwater retention or Low Impact Development (LID) solution,
was necessarily abstracted due to software limitations and time constraints. As a result, the representation
of actions and uncertainties does not reflect their full technical detail or spatial complexity.
Second, the analysis focused primarily on the impact of climate change on rainfall intensities, as this was
identified as the most influential and feasible uncertainty to quantify and implement in MIKE+. Other
important climate-driven uncertainties, such as rising groundwater levels or sea level rise, were excluded.
This was due both to the complexity of integrating these drivers into the model setup and the associated
increase in simulation scope and computational demand. Consequently, the results likely underestimate
the future pressures on the drainage system, particularly in low-lying or coastal areas.
Third, the study considered only pluvial flooding and did not assess potential interactions with coastal
processes such as storm surges. This limits the applicability of the results in assessing compound flooding
risks, which may become increasingly relevant in coastal municipalities like Gentofte under future climate
conditions.
Additionally, system performance was evaluated using a service-level threshold based on return periods
(e.g. 5- and 10-year design events). This performance-based approach is in line with current engineering
practice but does not capture the full extent of flood risk under more extreme rainfall events, such as
cloudbursts or rare compound events. As such, the assessment focused more on service-level compliance
than on flood risk mitigation.
Finally, the adaptation pathways analysed in this thesis assumed a direct transition from the current system
to either the “Seabed-Tunnel” or “No-Tunnel” solutions. No intermediate stages or phasing of measures
were modelled, although in reality, such transitions typically occur incrementally. This limits the ability
to assess the sequencing, feasibility, or timing of specific interventions, which are central to adaptive
planning in practice.
Despite these limitations, the thesis demonstrates how the DAPP approach can be meaningfully applied
even with modelling constraints, and it highlights the need for further research into the integration of a
broader range of uncertainties and staged interventions in urban drainage planning.

Conclusion
This thesis investigated the application of the Dynamic Adaptive Policy Pathways (DAPP) approach in
the context of municipal urban drainage planning, with a case study focused on the Hellerup catchment
in Gentofte Municipality, Denmark. The primary objective was to assess how the DAPP framework
could enhance the adaptability and dynamic robustness of urban drainage systems under conditions of
deep uncertainty, such as those posed by climate change, regulatory shifts, and urban development. The
study aimed not to give an optimal engineering solution but to explore how future uncertainties could be
integrated into the planning process, how the DAPP methodology could be practically applied at the local
scale, and how hydraulic modelling tools such as MIKE+ can support this process. A central concern
throughout the thesis was to maintain a planning approach practical and feasible.
One central outcome of this thesis was the identification of climate change, particularly the intensifica-
tion of rainfall, as a critical uncertainty influencing the future performance of the drainage system. The
system’s sensitivity to different climate scenarios was clearly demonstrated: while higher-end scenarios
(e.g. RCP 8.5 or SVK-High) pushed systems toward early tipping points, lower scenarios led to signif-
icant over-dimensioning. This emphasized the importance of designing systems that can adapt across a
wide range of possible futures.
Among the three evaluated system configurations, the existing combined system already showed insuf-
ficient performance under current conditions. The “Seabed-Tunnel” solution, though effective in the
short term, showed limited long-term adaptability and risked locking the municipality into a centralized,
inflexible path. In contrast, the “No-Tunnel” solution demonstrated greater flexibility and adaptability,
particularly by allowing for future shifts toward decentralized, nature-based strategies. However, it faced
limitations in treatment capacity due to spatial constraints.
The treatment performance analysis further underlined that rigid targets, such as a fixed 90% annual treat-
ment rate, can reduce system adaptability. Even modest adjustments (e.g. to 85%) significantly improved
feasibility under future conditions. Ultimately, the findings highlight that system resilience depends not
only on technical performance but also on institutional and spatial flexibility to adjust strategies over
time.
This thesis demonstrated that the DAPP approach, though resource and time demanding, offers consid-
erable advantages in urban drainage planning. It promotes a shift from deterministic, prediction-based
design to a flexible, adaptive strategy that embraces uncertainty and long-term change. In practice, the
integration of MIKE+ hydraulic simulations within the DAPP framework provided quantitative insights
into system behaviour under various scenarios. However, challenges such as data demands, model com-
plexity, and stakeholder involvement must be managed carefully. For small-scale or resource-constrained
projects, simplified models and tools like SCALGO Live could support early screening and strategic pri-
oritization. The thesis also highlighted the value of adapting the DAPP process to the specific planning
context. Not every DAPP step needs to be followed strictly; instead, the methodology should be adapted
based on project scope, available data, and stakeholder needs. This makes the DAPP approach a flexible
framework rather than a static method, suitable for both general-level strategy development and detailed
engineering analysis. A final critical observation is that DAPP must be implemented as an iterative,
ongoing process. Only through regular updates, monitoring of indicators, and re-assessment of tipping
points can the long-term resilience of urban drainage systems be ensured.
The findings of this thesis offer a compelling case for integrating DAPP into urban drainage planning. In
an era where the effects of climate change and urbanization are accelerating, relying solely on traditional,
deterministic planning approaches is increasingly careless. DAPP enables planners and decision-makers
to think in terms of pathways, tipping points, and strategic flexibility. The study further shows that even
Future-Proofing Urban Drainage
85
==PAGE:94==
in a small municipal context like Hellerup, the approach is applicable and valuable, particularly when
adapted to the local institutional, technical, and environmental setting.
This thesis also calls for a different perspective on urban drainage infrastructure investment. Performance
under a single design scenario is no longer sufficient to justify long-term investments. Instead, systems
should be evaluated across a range of plausible futures, and their ability to adapt must be a core consider-
ation. This does not mean avoiding engineered solutions, but rather combining them with LID measures
including them both in an adaptive framework that has a higher ability to withstand changing conditions
in the future.
For municipalities, consultants, and researchers, the results suggest that early identification of uncertain-
ties, flexible modelling strategies, and iterative decision-making structures are key methods of resilient
urban water systems. Ultimately, this study contributes to link the academic adaptation theory with the
practical infrastructure planning, showing how a structured yet flexible framework like DAPP can help
cities build urban drainage systems that are not only compliant today but also resilient tomorrow.

A.1.1
Artificial basin in a 1D MIKE+ model
By default, the surface area of this artificial inundation basin increases exponentially, from the surface
area of the manhole to up to 1000 times that value. This is intended to simulate how water spreads onto
the natural surface, such as a street, during flooding. The basin starts expanding at a depth of 0.25 m
below the ground level and continues increasing until it reaches the defined threshold. A scheme of this

A.2
Climate factors
A.2.1
Climate factor data management
As a first step, the interpolated annual climate factor values were rounded to two decimal places. This
rounding led to many consecutive years sharing the same climate factor values, typically with a new
value only every three to four years. To verify that this rounding does not significantly affect the results,
a sensitivity analysis was carried out by comparing CDS inputs and simulation outputs for values that
differed only beyond the second decimal digit. The outcome of this test showed that such small differences
had no significant impact on either the CDS hyetograph or the model results. Additionally, climate factors
across different climate scenarios often converged to similar values for the same return period, resulting in
further redundancy. These overlaps were analysed in Excel, and this first reduction step already brought
the total number of simulations down to 197.
In a second reduction step, similarities between CDS events across different return periods and climate
factor combinations were examined. This was based on the principle that CDS rains are generated using
the same rainfall distribution logic and differ only by the input parameters (peak intensity, duration, return
period) (Arnbjerg-Nielsen, n.d.). For instance, a CDS storm with a 5-year return period and a climate
factor of 1.3 may be hydrologically similar to one with a 10-year return period and a factor of 1.1. By
Future-Proofing Urban Drainage
92
==PAGE:101==
calculating and comparing the total accumulated rainfall of each CDS event, these overlaps were identi-
fied. An initial threshold of 0.4 millimetres per rain event was applied to group CDS events with similar
rainfall characteristics. This clustering reduced the number of distinct CDS events to 109, and thus the
number of required simulations to 109. Further sensitivity analysis was conducted to evaluate the influ-
ence of small differences in accumulated rainfall depth (in millimetres) on the resulting flooding outputs
in MIKE+. The analysis showed that the threshold for distinguishing between different CDS events could
be increased without significantly affecting the simulation results. Specifically, increasing the threshold
to 1 mm still resulted in comparable flooding outputs and would reduce the number of required simula-
tions to 43. If the threshold were increased further to 2 mm, the number of distinct simulations could be
reduced to just 23, while maintaining a meaningful differentiation in scenario outcomes.
These reductions were initially driven by data similarity, but the final selection of simulation runs will
also take into account which outputs are most relevant for system performance evaluation. This includes
considerations such as system objectives, key performance indicators, and first insights from preliminary
simulations. These aspects will be further described in the subsequent sections. Nevertheless, the applied
reduction and clustering process highlights which simulations are the most efficient to perform in terms
of representing the widest range of scenarios and time periods.
