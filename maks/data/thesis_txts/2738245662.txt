Abstract 
Skeletal muscles are a part of the musculoskeletal system responsible for correct body 
function. It is responsible for movement and the protection of internal organs. Damage to those 
can be, therefore, very dangerous for human life. Tissue engineering aims to develop 
representative tissue constructs to allow a better understanding of pathologies and provide the 
possibility of testing treatment methods in vitro. Manufacturing such scaffolds is a complicated 
process for which one of the biggest challenges is obtaining material formulations that can be 
printed using 3D manufacturing techniques such as Direct Ink writing DIW and, at the same 
time, support the maturation of myoblast cells by recapitulating Extracellular Matrix (ECM).  
The primary goal of this research was to investigate the feasibility of generating 
specialized anisotropic gel substrates capable of guiding the formation of anisotropic skeletal 
muscle myotubular tissues. 
Substrate 
formulations 
were 
based 
on 
transparent 
carboxymethylated nanofibrillated cellulose (cNFC) and maleimide functionalized cNFC 
(cNFC-mal), as the previous research successfully utilized the cNFC-Gelatin formulations to 
obtain scaffolds supporting oriented myotubular tissues [1]. In this study, cellulose nanofibers 
were integrated with Collagen (Col), Matrigel, and RGD to enable attachment and 
differentiation of C2C12 cells.  
Firstly, we investigated the behavior of scaffolds formulated of cNFC/Col on TCPS 
substrates, revealing that cNFC does not bind readily to common tissue culture substrates and 
collagen adhesion was insufficient to support scaffold adhesion due to swelling of cellulose 
fibers (Figure B1). Introducing poly-d-lysine coating of TCPS did not provide better adhesion 
of cNFC/Col formulations. Consequently, we functionalized cNFC with maleimide to enable 
crosslinking of cellulose fibers with Dimercaptosuccinic Acid (DMSA). This modification 
resolved the delamination issues by preventing delamination for over 2 weeks. 
Cell studies with C2C12 cells revealed that scaffolds made of maleimide functionalized 
cNFC and Collagen support initial cell attachment but result in non-homogenous cell 
distribution.  Further modifications of scaffold formulations with Matrigel failed to generate 
scaffolds with confluent cell coverage. We also investigated cNFCmal/RGD scaffolds that also 
supported initial adhesion but myotube formation was negligible. We concluded that lack of 
organized myotube formations was caused by formulations being too soft to provide a stable 
support for the cells.  
Bioprinting instead of 3D micro-extrusion was investigated as an alternative leveraging 
the rheological properties of studied formulations. Using this method, we successfully obtained 
scaffolds with a homogeneous distribution of fluorescent beads during preliminary studies.    
To conclude, during this research, we successfully resolved the scaffolds delamination 
issue however, we did not obtain anisotropic gel substrates capable of guiding the formation 
of anisotropic skeletal muscle. The future should include further modifications and 
optimizations of ink formulations to improve scaffolds stiffness. On top of that the already 
described ink formulations should be investigated in bioprinting experiments with cells.  
Keywords: Nanofibrillated cellulose, Carboxymethylation, Extrusion-based bioprinting, 
Skeletal muscle, Tissue Engineering

1. Introduction 
Direct Ink Writing is becoming one of the main tools for producing multi-material 
scaffolds in the tissue engineering field.  This 3D printing technique is applicable for micro-
extrusion 3D printing and bioprinting in an automated manner. One of the biggest challenges 
encountered during developing tissue scaffolds is the choice of biomaterials in the ink 
formulations. In both cases, the material must present specific rheological properties that 
would allow for generating scaffolds that obtain stable geometries with high precision while 
recapitulating the extracellular matrix (ECM) to allow cell adhesion, proliferation, and 
maturation. Therefore, most of the inks currently used for DIW are characterized by shear-
thinning behavior. Bioprinting also requires ink to sacrifice cell viability during printing to shear 
stress applied upon extrusion. Therefore, most bio-inks rely on very soft formulations with 
storage modulus in the range between tens to hundreds of Pascals [1], [2].There are no 
specific limitations for 3D micro-extrusion as the cells are not inside the ink. However, as the 
cells are usually seeded on top of extruded substrates, those scaffolds need to support cell 
adhesion and differentiation, which are influenced by the mechanical properties of the 
substrates on which the cells are seeded. The stiffness required depends on the cell type. One 
specific type are muscle cells, which usually require stiffness around 15kPa for optimal 
adhesion and to form myotube formations [1], [4]. Most biomaterials do not contain a full 
spectrum of the required properties, what leads to the development of biomaterial composites.  
Previous research demonstrated anisotropic gel substrates capable of guiding the 
formation of anisotropic skeletal muscle myotubular tissues based on carboxymethylated 
cellulose nanofibers and gelatin [1]. The ink formulations for creating those scaffolds required 
enzymatic crosslinking and had limited long-term myotube adhesion. Moreover, cNFC itself 
didn’t support cell adhesion and showed a tendency to delaminate from  common tissue culture 
substrates [3]. 
This study aimed to utilize the formulated cNFC from the previous research to create 
scaffolds based on it with Collagen, Matrigel, and RGD as alternatives to gelatin in hopes of 
obtaining scaffolds that will also present to influence myotube formation and orientation. 
Throughout the study, we investigated obtained substrates and tried to prevent delamination 
of our scaffolds and enhance cell adhesion and myotube formation. To do so, we conducted 
multiple experiments, leading to several modifications of ink formulations. In the first part of the 
study, 3D matrices of cNFC/Collagen were created and studied for their substrate and cell 
adhesion to identify potential issues. Then, we tried to coat TCPS with poly-D-lysine to improve 
scaffold attachment.

The second part of the study focused on maleimide-functionalized CFCs. Maleimide 
functionalization allowed for the crosslinking of cNFC inside the scaffolds with DMSA. 
Collagen, RGD, and Matrigel were investigated as cell-binding motifs in scaffolds based on 
cNFC-maleimide.

Observations of C2C12 behavior on our engineered scaffolds gave insights into cell 
adhesion, proliferation, and differentiation on our scaffolds. Moreover, it identified the influence 
of different properties on cell interactions. The last part of the study investigated the potential 
of utilizing the studied ink formulations in bioprinting.

2. Background 
2.1 Biomaterials suitable for use as inks in direct-ink-writing tissue 
models 
Tissue Engineering is a multidisciplinary aiming to develop specific tissue substitutes 
to repair or replace lost biological functions[5]. 3D scaffolds are created to resemble ECM and 
guide cellular growth and differentiation. To develop such scaffolds, biomaterials must 
recapitulate native ECM structures[6]. In general, scaffolds can be made out of natural 
materials or synthetic polymers[7]. The material choice is highly dependent on specific cell 
demand. Moreover, to create the scaffold, the material “liquid-phase” needs to be 
characterized by specific rheological behavior, allowing for its extrusion and after extruding 
needs to formulate a stable scaffold. For those reasons, most scaffolds formulations are made 
of hydrogels. Hydrogels are made of polymer chain networks that encapsulate high water 
concentrations. Hydrogels can be made of both natural and synthetic biomaterials[8]. Created 
gels can mimic the ECM environment and are widely used in biomedical applications. 
2.1.1 Direct Ink Writing 
 
DIW is one of the most commonly used methods AD techniques for fabricating tissue 
scaffolds. The method relies on extruding an ink material through a nozzle allowing for the 
fabrication of controlled networks layer-by-layer [9]. It employs one of three extrusion 
mechanisms. Those are pneumatic, piston-based or screw-based[10]. The pneumatic system 
applies air pressure to ink for the extrusion. It’s a simple method that can be easily adjusted 
for different viscosities of inks however the control over the amount of extruded material is 
limited. Piston piston-based system, on the other hand, gives great control and precision over 
material deposition but is limited to low viscous inks[10], [11]Screw-Based Systems are less 
common and not widely used in tissue engineering.  
 
State-of-the-art shows that DIW presents a major limitation for bioprinting 3D structures 
and is limited to 2D structures and 3D structures not thicker than a few hundred 
micrometers[12]. This comes from the restrictions for the printed material as the mechanism 
applies pressure on the liquid phase to extrude it through a narrow nozzle. The perfect material 
should decrease its viscosity with applied pressure, making it shear-thinning at the same time 
allowing a precise extrusion to maintain the engineered shape of the print while not damaging 
cells with shear stress in the nozzle. Therefore most bioinks rely on liquid like hydrogels. 
Creating a thicker scaffold with such properties is challenging as the mechanical properties 
are too weak to maintain shape due to gravitational forces [13]. Removing cells from the ink 
would allow for a higher viscosity margin of the liquid phase while the cells could be seeded 
onto printed scaffolds.   
2.1.2 Common ink biomaterials 
 
The most commonly used materials for 3D printing are ECM-derived proteins as they 
provide the closest resemblance to the native ECM. Some of the most commonly used are 
collagen, gelatin, Matrigel, and fibrin[14], [15]. On the other hand, there are synthetic polymers 
that might not resemble native ECM as well as ECM-derived proteins but are biodegradable, 
biocompatible, and have easily tunable mechanical properties. Some of the most commonly 
applied synthetic materials in tissue engineering are PLA, PGA, PEG, and PLGA[16].

2.1.3 Cellulose 
 
Cellulose is a biopolymer that is naturally found in bacteria, plants, and animals making 
it the most abundant biopolymer on earth[17]. Easy access to cellulose combined with its 
proven biocompatibility and biodegradability results in its increasing use in healthcare 
applications[18]. It consists of chains made of glucose molecules connected by β(1→4) 
glycosidic bonds. Those chains are arranged linearly. This arrangement allows hydrogen 
bonding between hydroxyl groups on adjacent chains making it insoluble in most solvents. On 
top of that, it results in the formation of microfibrils[17][18]. In literature, cellulose is often 
divided based on its length into CNC (2~20nm diameter and 100~600nm length) and MFC ( > 
1µm). Another group is NFC which can also be distinguished from MFC based on the diameter 
being in nanoscale. To obtain cellulose nanocrystals (CNC) breakage of microfibrils is 
necessary  [13], [19]. For creation of CNC acid treatment is mostly described approach[20].  
 
The fibrillar nature of cellulose is a desirable characteristic as it mimics ECM as the 
main of components such as fibronectin and collagen have similar characteristics and those 
proteins are responsible for cellular adhesion and migration[21]. In addition to that inks with 
fibular components present with thixotropic behavior. Thixotropy gels are thick under static 
conditions and more fluidic with applied physical stress which is a necessary mechanism for 
creating viscous shear-thinning inks[22].  
 
Cellulose these days finds many applications in 3D printing in biomedical applications 
from wound dressings that promote healing through drug delivery systems to creating flexible 
and biodegradable cellulose sensors[23], [24], [25], [26]. However one of the issues of 
cellulose for cell and engineering applications is light diffraction by the cellulose fibers leading 
to limited transparency which limits its usability in applications where optical microscopy is 
required. Different studies showed that carboxylation under optimized conditions proved to 
allow for the creation of carboxymethylated nanofibrils[27], [28], [29]. The previously mentioned 
study successfully obtained transparent fibers by using this method. For this by controlling the 
reaction degree through adjustment of the polarity of mixed alcohol reaction solvents to obtain 
fibers with lengths under the length of visible light [1]. There were documented alternative 
methods to resolve the transparency issue such as chemical oxidation. However, cellulose 
oxidation leads to breakage of the fibers to sizes that are not sufficient for leading the cell 
alignment[30], [31].  
 
Carboxymethylation resolves the transparency issue however, cNFC still doesn’t 
contain any cell-binding motives. It also tends to delaminate from the tissue substrates like 
TCPS therefore, it cannot be used to support the formation and culture of myotubs.  
2.1.4 Maleimide 
 
Maleimide is a highly reactive vinyl monomer that found its applications in bioconjugate 
chemistry[32], [33]. Maleimide compound is characterized by a double carbon (C) bond which 
is a result of w carbonyl C=O groups. The presence of double bonds in the maleimide structure 
makes it highly reactive with nucleophiles such as thiol groups. A simplified mechanism of the 
thiol-maleimide reaction is presented in Figure 3.  
==PAGE:11==
 
Figure 3 Visualization of the thiol-maleimide coupling mechanism. Created with Biorender 
Thiol maleimide reactions are widely described in the literature[34], [35]. The reaction 
is pH dependent as from pH 6.5 to pH 7.5, maleimide reacts selectively with thiols while beyond 
7.5 pH free primary amines may compete with thiols[36].  This reaction is classified as a 
Michael addition click reaction. This reaction proved to be useful in applications that vary from 
small molecule synthesis to specific polymer modifications for biological systems to 
functionalization of material surface coatings[37]. 
 
2.1.5 Collagen 
Collagen is the most abundant protein of ECM, accounting for up to 30% of total protein 
in the human body. It’s the main structural component that provides tensile strength and 
integrity to tissues. The majority of the collagen found in a body is one of 3 types which are 
types 1, 2, and 3[38]. Collagen is characterized by three parallel polypeptide strands forming 
a triple helix formation. Collagen type 1 also presents a fibular structure caused by 
fibrillogenesis. Fibrillogenesis occurs in two steps nucleation and fiber growth[39].  
==PAGE:12==
 
Figure 4 Schematization of a collagen α chain triple helix segment. (a) Crystal structure of a collagen triple helix, 
(b) View down the axis with the three strands depicted in space-filling, ball-and-stick, and ribbon 
representation[39]. 
Collagen type 1 is the most used out of the three in biomedical applications. It is 
characterized by low immunogenicity and as an ECM-derived biomaterial that contains cell-
signaling domains[40]. Moreover, collagen gels can be crosslinked without chemical 
modifications.   
Although collagen is the structural component in native tissue collagen gels show 
mechanical weakness and tend to have a low resistance to enzymatic degradation[41]. 
Therefore literature shows different approaches to overcome those limitations. The more 
popular include modification of collagen crosslinking methods or using collagen in composite 
inks with other biomaterials such as alginate and others[40], [42], [43], [44]. 
Due to its availability and great biocompatibility Collagen has been successfully 
employed in number of biomedical applications. It was shown to support cell viability in 
regenerative tissue constructs [45].  
2.1.6 Matrigel  
 
Matrigel is a protein mixture that has been extracted from Englebreth-Holm-Swarm 
mouse sarcoma cells [46]. It was first described in the 1980s and was documented to contain 
major components of many tissue basement membranes. Its main components are Collagen 
type IV, laminin, and entactin [47].  
Since the 80s, many studies have been conducted using Matrigel. Their results showed 
that Matrigel can be a very beneficial component of substrates for 3D and 2D cell culture. It 
was widely used for supporting the cell development[48].

2.1.7 RGD 
 
RGD is a peptide consisting of a sequence made of Arginine, Glycine, and Aspartic 
Acid. This sequence can be found in proteins belonging to the native ECM. Moreover, the 
literature shows that this sequence motif is crucial for cell adhesion as it binds to cell integrin 
receptors[49]. Integrins are cell-specific transmembrane receptors found on the cell surface. 
They are responsible for the binding of the cell to ECM and for cell-cell interactions [50].  
 
 
Figure 5 Scheme of the RGD peptide binding onto an integrin. Shows influence of RGD-peptide binding and 
possible ways into the cell[51]. 
 
Therefore, RGD finds application in several fields such as surface modifications of 
culture dishes for better adhesion or drug delivery systems[52]. On top of that, it was 
successfully used for functionalizing hydrogels to provide a cell-friendly environment allowing 
for their attachment and growth [53], [54].  
2.1.8 TCPS 
TCPS stands for Tissue culture polystyrene, a material used to create most of today's 
lab equipment for cell culture. Its properties as a cell substrate create a stable environment 
that promotes cell growth and proliferation. This makes it  Its also transparent what is an 
important factor in cell examination [55].  
TCPS surface coatings are often employed to obtain the desired surface attachment 
for cells [56]. However, TCPS is very rigid and hydrophobic, which can cause poor interactions 
with soft hydrogels.

2.1.9 Poly-D-Lysine 
 
Poly-D-lysine and Poly-L-lysine are synthetic polymers characterized by a strong 
positive charge [57]. They are composed of d-lysine and l-lysine amino acids, respectively. 
Those compounds have been widely described as coating membranes to improve cell 
adhesion. Both do not influence signaling pathways but enhance electrostatic interactions, 
leading to cell attraction [58].  
 
Figure 6 Chemical Structure of d-lysine and l-lysine and its polymers.  [59] 
Polymers were applied in both surface coatings and in the functionalization of tissue 
scaffolds by being directly into the formulations and showed improvement of cell attachment 
and proliferation [58], [60]. Both compounds have been successfully utilized in biomedical 
applications, but poly-d-lysin is not degraded by cellular proteases [61].  
The working mechanisms of poly-d-lysine suggest that it might be used for improving 
attachment not only for cells but for negatively charged scaffolds. 
2.2 Skeletal Muscle Tissue 
 
Skeletal muscles comprise around 35% of body mass and are a part of the 
musculoskeletal system responsible for correct body function. It is responsible for movement 
but also for the protection of internal organs  and postural stability [62]. Skeletal muscle itself 
is composed of several functional units. The sarcomere is the basic contractile unit of a 
myocyte and is composed of two proteins: actin and myosin [63]. Those proteins are 
responsible for muscular contraction. Anatomically, skeletal muscle tissue is composed of 
several myofibers. Those comprise thousands of myofibrils, which represent muscle cells with 
mentioned proteins [64].   
==PAGE:15==
 
Figure 7 Visual presentation of Skeletal muscle anatomy. [62] 
 
The development of muscle tissue starts from pluripotent stem cells [65]. The body 
activates these precursor cells, which, under the influence of signaling factors, develop into 
more specific myogenic progenitors. From there, cells transform and become myoblasts. 
Fusion of those myoblasts leads to the formation of so-called myotubes [65]. The ECM 
environment strongly influences this process [66]. Those myotubes, with time, mature to form 
myofibers, which, in groups, create a skeletal muscle.  
 
Figure 8 Visualization of Myofibers development path [67] 
To develop in vitro models of skeletal muscle tissue, many factors need to be 
considered. The final model would have to mimic mechanical forces to replicate contractions 
and more. Firstly, however, a scaffold imitating natural ECM needs to be created to allow 
muscle fibers to develop. For that, the scaffold must allow cell adhesion, proliferation, and 
differentiation and to do that it needs to . It also would need to support cell culture for enough 
time for myofibers to develop.  
2.2.1 C2C12 Cells 
C2C12 are myoblast cells derived from the thigh muscles of C3H mice that underwent 
crush injury. They are a common choice in studies concentrating on the regeneration of 
muscles and their biology. They first were described in 1977 in the work of Yaffe and Saxel, 
who developed the cell line found in a 2-month-old C3H mouse following a crush injury 
[68]C2C12 is highly adaptable to culture conditions and can differentiate into myotubes by 
==PAGE:16==
fusion of myoblast cells [69]To form myotubes, the C2C12 cells first differentiate from 
myoblasts into myocytes and finally form myotubes [70]. This differentiation pathway is 
essential for creating skeletal muscle tissue models.  
 
Figure 9 Schematic design of simplified C2C12 differentiation mechanism. GM = growth medium; DM = 
differentiation medium [70] 
Observations of the creation of muscle tissues give a great insight into muscle growth 
and regeneration mechanisms. It also allows to study myopathies and what causes them 
[71]This makes C2C12 cells an exciting choice for studying myogenesis. Currently, C2C12 
cells are widely used for different applications in Tissue engineering.  
Different studies were conducted on C2C12 seeded on and within hydrogel substrates 
to investigate the influence of the substrate on cell behavior. For C2C12 seeded on hydrogel 
scaffolds, some of the most common issues were relatively fast delamination of formed 
myotubes from cells seeded on substrates [72]. The scaffold's stiffness also significantly 
impacts tissue adhesion and differentiation, while hydrogel morphological cues have been 
documented to influence myotube orientation [72]. The C2C12 cell viability and spreading have 
shown a direct connection with their seeding density. It is caused by cadherin located in cells. 
These cell-cell adhesion molecule signals cause them to aggregate, which is important during 
myogenesis for the fusion of the cells [73]. 
2.2.2 Immunostaining 
Immunostaining is a technique relying on specific cell binding properties of cells to allow 
attachment of fluorescent stains. A cell-dependent antibody is selected to bind to the cell of 
interest to visualize particular proteins in the studied samples. It was first described in 1941 by 
A.Coons  [74]. Currently, there are several techniques for immunostaining, such as the 
originally described Immunohistochemistry or western blotting. In Immunofluorescence, 
tissues must be fixated to preserve their morphology before introducing the primary antibody. 
Then, depending on the research interests, different staining or secondary antibodies can be 
implemented to mark specific targets. This method allows for visualization under the 
fluorescent microscope and quantification of cell studies, providing information on many 
aspects like cell-substrate interactions and cell differentiation [75].

3.2 Project Overview 
This project establishes a series of experiments based on micro-extrusion 3D printing 
and bio-printing using composite inks based on transparent carboxymethylated nanofibrillar 
cellulose (cNFC) and collagen. The thesis aims to assess whether cNFC and collagen can be 
used to create anisotropic gel substrates that influence the alignment of muscle myotubular 
tissues. To test that hypothesis, a range of concentrations of cNFC in composites were tested, 
and several modifications were applied to the system. The main stages of this study were ink 
preparation and modification, assessment of gel formation mechanical properties such as 
adherence to the tissue culture substrates, and the assessment of the ability to support cell 
adhesion and myotube formation by the created scaffolds. 
 
Figure 13 Schematic overview of the experimental procedures and assessment points. Created in Biorender 
First, we synthesized the carboxymethylated nanofibrillated cellulose, which we 
functionalized with maleimide in later studies. We then prepared ink formulations based on the 
cNFC mentioned with different cell-binding motifs. Afterward, the inks were used for micro-
extrusion 3D printing to create scaffolds that were then crosslinked under different conditions 
depending on their content. The created scaffolds were then optimized to present long-term 
adherence to the TCPS substrate. Lastly, scaffolds that achieved that property were then used 
in cell studies, and an assessment of their influence on cell attachment and support of myotube 
formations was made.

3.3 Material Synthesis 
3.3.1 cNFC synthesis 
The synthesis process of Nanofibrillated Cellulose Fibers was prepared according to 
the previously published protocol with minor modifications [1].  
To summarize, the 80 g of 10% concentrated cellulose pulp was dispersed in 780 ml 
Isopropanol:Ethanol solvent mixture with a 2:1 volume ratio (IPA:EtOH (2:1)). To homogenize 
the mixture Ultra-Turrax homogenizer equipped with an S25N-18G-ST set was used at 10k 
rpm for 10 minutes. The CMC dispersion was transferred to a 3-neck round flask and heated 
to 35˚C. This solution underwent alkaline treatment upon reaction with 100ml of 6% sodium 
hydroxide solution which increased the reactivity of the cellulose and caused the fibers to swell 
for 30 minutes. Subsequently, the suspension was heated to 45˚C and the carboxymethylation 
reaction was performed by adding 5,64g of monochloroacetic acid (MCA) in 30ml of IPA:EtOH 
(2:1) to the reaction flask, to introduce the carboxymethyl groups into the cellulose structure. 
The pH was adjusted to 7 by adding 50 mL of a 10% acetic acid stock solution and then the 
solvents were filtered to obtain solid semi-wet content material. Finally, the product is dialyzed 
against DI water with 14kDa cutoff dialysis tubing for 3 days before freeze-drying. 
 
Figure 14 Graphic scheme of carboxymethylated cellulose nanofibers synthesis 
3.3.2 cNFC-mal synthesis 
To create maleimide functionalized cNFC fibers, 2 g of freeze-dried fibers were 
transferred into a round-bottom flask equipped with a magnetic stirrer. Appropriate amounts of 
100 mM 2-(morpholine-4-yl)ethane-1-sulfonic acid buffer (MES; pH 5.5) is added to the fibers 
to create 1% w/v solution, then left on the stirrer until obtaining a homogenous clear solution. 
Subsequently, 3.99g DMTMM was added to the reaction as a coupling reagent before the 
addition of 127,2 mg NH2-maleimide (176.60 g/mol). The mixture was left stirring at RT 
==PAGE:22==
overnight, then dialyzed against 150mM NaCl and DI water for 2 days each with 10-14 kDa 
cutoff dialysis tubing. The material was then freeze-dried and stored at -20°C. 
 
Figure 15 Graphic scheme of cNFC-maleimide coupling reaction 
The efficiency of coupling reactions was confirmed using nuclear magnetic resonance 
(NMR: Bruker BioSpin GmbH) and Fourier transform infrared (FTIR: IRAffinity-1S, Shimadzu) 
spectroscopy methods.  
4.3 Hydrogel Ink Formulations 
4.3.1 Ink Preparation 
In the first part of the study, the formulations of cNFC at different concentrations were 
investigated with a fixed concentration of collagen to study the effect of cNFC content on the 
differentiation of C2C12 muscle myoblast cells. All operations were performed under a bench 
in sterile conditions.  
Unless otherwise specified, all inks with cellulose fibers, including cNFC and cNFC-
mal, were prepared in DMEM+1%P/S  together with 10% NaHCO3 and 10% HEPES buffer 
solutions that were used to adjust the pH of the mixture upon collagen addition the next day. 
Appropriate amounts of fibers were left in DMEM, NaHCO3, and HEPES buffer overnight to 
swell to obtain a homogenous ink formulation. The following day, the precursor inks were 
mixed with a sterile metal spatula until a homogeneous mixture was obtained. From this point 
on, all tools and mixtures were placed on ice to avoid the polymerization of the collagen upon 
its addition. After adding the collagen, the mixture is thoroughly mixed with a spatula and 
loaded into pre-chilled 3ml plastic syringes. 
Before the 3D printing, the inks were transferred to a pre-cooled centrifuge and 
centrifuged for 5 minutes at 5000 RPM to remove all air bubbles entrapped during the ink 
preparation process.  
The inks with the Matrigel were prepared according to the presented protocol. Matrigel 
was added at the same time as collagen in an amount equal to 10% of the total volume in the 
ink formulation making it ~0.1% final concentration.  
==PAGE:23==
In order to enhance cell attachment, the biologically active peptide sequence (RGD) is 
grafted on the cNFC-mal via the maleimide-thiol coupling reaction. Acetyl-C-(AEEAc)-
(AEEAc)-GRGDSP peptide (CASLO ApS, Denmark) dissolved in PBS was added into the 
cNFC-mal to obtain inks with 2 mM and 4 mM RGD peptide concentrations in final ink 
formulations. No buffers were added for those samples and pure cNFCmal samples, and 
DMEM was adjusted to match the desired cNFC concentration. 
For reference samples with collagen and without cNFC the volume reserved for cNFC 
was filled with DMEM and the ink was centrifuged in the eppendorf tube and transferred to a 
special 1ml glass syringe sterilized beforehand.  
4.3.2 Crosslinking Methods 
To crosslink cNFC and Col mixture, scaffolds were transferred into an incubator set at 
37˚C immediately after printing and kept there for 40 minutes for collagen crosslinking. 
Afterward, DMEM + 1% P/S solution was added on top of the prints to block the drying of 
hydrogels until cell seeding. 
In the case of prints containing cNFC-mal and Collagen/Matrigel, the prints were 
crosslinked in the incubator the same as cNFC and Col prints however, they were secondary 
crosslinking of cNFCmal in the meso-2,3-Dimercaptosuccinic acid (DMSA) with various 
concentrations dissolved in DMEM + 1% P/S or depending on the experiment.  
Printed scaffolds containing just cNFC-mal or cNFC-mal and RGD were crosslinked in 
DMSA solutions in DMEM + 1% P/S immediately after printing without prior incubation. The 
DMSA concentration was calculated based on print size to match a specific molar ratio 
(Appendix A).

4.6 In-vitro characterization studies 
4.6.1 Cells culture 
The C2C12 immortalized mouse myoblast cell line is used for all cell-related studies. 
Cells were cultured in a growth medium containing DMEM and 10% fetal bovine serum (FBS) 
with 1% antibiotics (Penicillin/streptomycin,P/S) in an incubator set at 37˚C and introduced 5% 
CO2. Throughout the project, the passage number of the cells used in the experiments varied 
from 20 to 35. 
Prior to seeding with cells, the printed and crosslinked substrates were washed in 
DMEM+1%P/S to remove the excess DMSA. Afterwards, the DMEM was removed and a 
C2C12 growth medium solution with cells was introduced to the prints. The prints were kept in 
the incubator set at 37˚C and 5% CO2 for 1 day to grow, then the medium was changed to 
C2C12 differentiation medium (DM) containing  DMEM with 2% horse serum (HS) and 1% P/S. 
The differentiation media was exchanged every 2 days. 
After 10 days of incubating in DM the cell seeded on substrates were fixed unless 
specified differently. For fixing the cells they were washed 3 times with PBS. Subsequently 
they were fixed and permeabilized in a 4% paraformaldehyde (PFA) and 0.1% Triiton-X100 
solution in PBS at RT for 20 minutes. The wells with cells were again washed 3 times with PBS 
and stored in fridge before immunostaining. 
4.6.2 Immunostaining 
For the staining, cells were incubated with a primary solution consisting of Alpha 
Sarcomeric Actinin Monoclonal Antibody (EA-53) in 1:400 dilution in 1% BSA in PBS overnight 
at 4°C. The next day after washing with 1% BSA in PBS 3 times secondary solution made of 
Goat anti-Mouse IgG (H+L) Highly Cross-Adsorbed Secondary Antibody, Alexa Fluor Plus 488 
1:250, DAPI (4',6-diamidino-2-phenylindole, dihydrochloride) 1:1000 and Alexa Fluor 647 
Phalloidin 1:200 in 1% BSA solution in PBS is later added and incubated for 2 hours at room 
temperature. Samples were then washed with 1% BSA solution 3 times and with PBS 2 times.  
4.6.3 Cell Imaging and Analysis 
To monitor the phenotypical changes, the cells were imaged on days 0, 3rd, 6th, and 
10th of Differentiation using a brightfield microscope (ZEISS) with 3 different magnifications

(4x, 10x, 20x). Fluorescent images of stained samples were taken using an AxioVert 135 
(ZEISS) microscope. 
Image analysis was performed using ImageJ software to quantify the results of 
myotube formations. For that, a random square on the stained sample was selected, and 
measured length and width for each myotube present in the selected area. The obtained data 
was then exported to Excel (Microsoft) and plotted.

6. Discussion 
The primary objective of this study was to explore the feasibility of creating anisotropic 
gel substrates that could effectively guide the formation of anisotropic skeletal muscle 
myotubular tissues. The substrates mentioned were based on transparent cNFC-maleimide 
with Collagen, Matrigel, and RGD as cell binding motifs. The study focused on examining the 
delamination of printed scaffolds from the TCPS surface and cell adhesion to those substrates. 
A series of modifications to ink formulations were applied to improve those qualities.  
6.1 cNFC/Col scaffolds 
The study conducted that all ink formulations that were based on both cNFC and 
cNFCmal presented the ability to obtain regular flat substrates.  
The study showed that scaffolds made of cNFC-Col composite inks did not provide 
sufficient adhesion to the TCPS well plate. The control of Col 0.4 presented with nice adhesion 
to the substrate while increasing the content of cNFC made the adhesion worse. This shows 
that cNFC does not present binding to the TCPS itself. Moreover, the crosslinked collagen is 
insufficient to provide attachment to the well plate. We believe that the primary mechanism 
contributing to the delamination was the swelling of cellulose fibers. As the material swells, it 
pulls the gel formation from the substrate. In the case of cell studies, the microscopy showed 
the initial attachment of the cells to each of the printed substrates. However, we couldn’t do a 
control without collagen as the cNFC was not crosslinked and would be just washed out in 
DMEM. Therefore, we couldn’t assess the influence of cNFC on cell adhesion. On the other 
hand, collagen 0.4% has been confirmed to be cell-friendly. Based on the comparison of 
different concentrations of seeded cells, a significant difference between 5k/cm2 and 10k/cm 
was observed. Meanwhile, increasing the amount of seeded cells to 20k/cm2 did not show 
much difference. From those observations, we decided 10k/cm2 would be an optimal cell 
seeding density for future experiments. One of the issues encountered during our first printing 
experiment was the very fast drying of the printed substrates. It was caused by the dry 
environment under the hood where the printer was located. To prevent that, we decided to 
place a MiliQ water in between prints to limit the drying of the samples.  
6.2 cNFC-Col scaffolds on PDL-coated substrate 
Poly-D-lysine is a widely used polymer in Biomedical research. It is composed of a D-
isomer of the amino acid lysine, making it strongly positively charged due to the protonation of 
the amino groups in its lysine residues [76]. It is proven that poly-D-lysine improves cell 
adhesion. However, in our experiment, we tried to use it to bind cNFC to the culture surface. 
cNFC, as mentioned before, holds a strong negative charge from ionizable carboxyl groups. 
We wanted to test whether that combination might lead to electrostatic interactions with PDL 
that will improve cNFC-Col scaffold adhesion. 
The experiment results showed no improvement and, for some conditions, deterioration 
in the surface adhesion of tested scaffold formulations. We did not find any documentation of 
poly-d-lysine being used in such a manner, so we cannot compare our results. However, we 
can speculate that our failure to observe any positive results might be caused by a lack of 
fragile interaction between PDL and cNFC that was insufficient to oppose forces causing the 
delamination. 
==PAGE:46==
6.3 Functionalization of cNFC fiber with maleimide 
To continue the study, we needed to prevent the gels from delamination from the TCPS 
substrate. We believed that swelling of cNFC-s was the main reason for scaffold delamination. 
To limit the swelling, we decided to crosslink cNFC in addition to crosslinking collagen. To 
enable crosslinking the cNFC, we functionalized the fibers with maleimide so they could be 
chemically crosslinked.  
 
We coupled Maleimide onto cNFC according to a previously formulated protocol. 
Spectroscopies indicated that we successfully coupled NH2-maleimide onto cNFC, resulting 
in the functionalization of the fibers. We obtained a degree of substitution of 4%, from which 
we could estimate the maleimide molarity in our printed scaffolds. To crosslink the cNFC 
maleimide, we decided to use DMSA. The Dimercaptosuccinic Acid contains two thiol groups, 
so it can react with two free maleimides, which would crosslink our fibers.  
However, the literature did not broadly describe DMSA's use for crosslinking, so we 
conducted a study to ensure its ability to crosslink our fibers. The first study, in which we used 
DMSA in a PBS solution, showed that DMSA could be utilized for cNFCmal crosslinking, as all 
samples except the ones without DMSA crated stable gels. We also observed a limit to DMSA's 
solubility as in wells that the crosslinking solution was in a 1:50 (maleimide/DMSA) molar ratio, 
which oscillated around 3,5 mg/ml of DMSA, it did not dissolve. The same samples were then 
observed for signs of delamination. All samples crosslinked in a 1:50 ratio delaminated after 
removing the crosslinking solution and adding PBS. This was most likely caused by 
undissolved DMSA left inside the well because of its attachment to the printed scaffold, which 
dissolved in new PBS, causing second crosslinking. However, all prints crosslinked in other 
conditions showed no signs of delamination for three weeks. 
A delamination study was performed under the same conditions as the ones for the 
cNFC/Col test to validate that we obtained improved surface adhesion due to cNFC 
functionalization and crosslinking. This study confirmed that our new modified scaffolds adhere 
to the TCPS and have not shown signs of delamination for over two weeks.  
Throughout this study, we also observed the behavior of the scaffolds crosslinked in 
different maleimide/DMSA molar ratios. An example of calculations showing the DMSA 
concentrations in crosslinking solutions in reference to the molar ratio is shown in Appendix A. 
While scaffolds based on cNFCmal/Col looked similar for each condition except for those not 
treated with DMSA, the cNFCmal formulations differed based on the molar ratio in which 
crosslinking was performed. Firstly, cNFCmal crosslinked in 1:1 condition showed a rougher 
surface and visibly looked thicker than conditions treated in 1:5 and 1:10 ratios, indicating that 
more swelling occurs when we apply a 1:1 ratio. We assume that the degree of reaction is not 
the same for those conditions. Several factors might cause that. First is that the theoretical and 
practical ratios differ as we don’t know the exact volume of the print, and during calculations, 
oscillations were applied; however, the stoichiometry should be 2:1, so we still should have an 
excess of DMSA. Another reason that we believe might be true is that the DMSA only has 
access from the top of the print therefore, it needs a significant excess of DMSA because, 
without it, the reaction speed is much slower, allowing cellulose fibers to swell.  
Furthermore, it is worth mentioning that control samples of cNFCmal without collagen 
left in DMEM+1%P/S without DMSA formed gel repeatedly. We conclude that the amino acids 
in the DMEM induced the crosslinking.  
==PAGE:47==
In conclusion, we managed to obtain the scaffolds without the delamination issue, 
which can allow us to create scaffolds that can support an extended culture of myotube 
formations in the future.  
As the delamination issues were resolved, the next part of the study concentrated on 
improving cell attachment and myotube formation. 
First, we seeded  C2C12 cells on formulations established during the delamination 
studies and analyzed their behavior. As expected, cells on formulations composed only from 
cNFCmal did not attach to the printed substrate. We, therefore, confirmed that cNFCmal lacks 
binding motifs.  
The formulation of cNFCmal/Col showed attachment of the cells for every tested 
condition. The observed attachment was not homogeneous and resulted in cell aggregate 
formations, mainly in the middle of the scaffolds. Identical behavior cells presented on scaffolds 
printed with pure collagen shown in Figure 20. From that comparison, we can assume that the 
presence of cNFC doesn’t cause the behavior of the cells, but it is caused by the nature of the 
cells. The C2C12 are documented to seek cell-to-cell contact, especially at lower densities, as 
it is amenable for contact-mediated signaling pathways [77]. Those interactions are proven to 
be strongly promyogenic [77]. This phenomenon led to the formation of “islands” of cells on 
the printed substrates, while parts of the scaffolds did not have any cells attached to them. 
Also, the sides of the scaffolds were always unoccupied by the cells, but that can be explained 
by the close presence of TCPS, which cells prefer as their substrate. We can conclude that 
the proliferation of cells attached to the printed substrates is negatively influenced as cells on 
TCPS obtained full coverage of the entire well.  
We also consider the possibility that with C2C12 migration towards each other, they 
pull collagen with them as Collagen and cNFC are two different phases, which would explain 
why there is no adherence to some of the gel areas. However, we could not test this 
hypothesis. In future studies, fluorescent staining of the collagen could resolve whether that 
hypothesis is valid. 
Immunostaining images were taken after ten days of differentiation. Unfortunately, 
noise influences the resolution of stains, which is typical for staining cells on gels. The 
fluorescent agents get absorbed by the gel, which, in our case, did not allow for cell counting 
as the resolution did not allow for the identification of single nuclei, so myoblast fusion couldn’t 
be studied nevertheless, staining allowed for the analysis of obtained myotube formations.  
The “island” formations were visible (Figure B3.1 and B3.2), especially on actin-α 
staining. That phenomenon is an unwanted feature and should be eliminated in future work. 
One of the possibilities is seeding cells already in amounts that would cover the entire 
substrate.   
The myotube formations were only present on the island formations and were forming 
in some of its parts. Therefore, their density must be considered when comparing the 
geometries of obtained myotubes on printed scaffolds to those of TCPS control.  
The comparison of the lengths of the obtained myotubes showed that on cNFCmal 
4%/Col0.4% scaffolds, we obtained myotubes with an average length similar to those of the 
TCPS control except for the one not treated with DMSA, which was characterized by 
==PAGE:48==
significantly shorter myotubes. Similarly, myotubes on cNFC2%/Col0.4% scaffolds were 
considerably shorter. While in the width of myotubes, we did not observe any notable 
differences. The length of myotubes formed on all of the cNFC/Col scaffolds differed drastically 
even on the same samples, and similar behavior was observed in the TCPS control. 
These observations align with the fact that stiffness influences the formation of 
myotubes, as soft substrates do not provide sufficient mechanical cues for differentiation, 
leading to shorter and less organized myotubes [78]. To validate our observation, the 
rheological properties of cNFCmal were tested.  
The rheology results showed that cNFCmal formulations are very soft, with a storage 
modulus of tens of pascals. There is a visible trend of an increasing storage modulus while 
increasing cNFC concentrations however, the difference is negligible in our range. A significant 
difference is visible with increased DMSA content in crosslinking solution where the exact 
formulation of cNFCmal 4% jumps from 70Pa storage modulus to 1126Pa. This result is still 
not enough to provide differentiation cues for the muscle cells [79]. The previous study in which 
cNFC/gelatin led to the formation of oriented myotubes achieved a storage modulus of 15kPa 
[1].  
Those results also explain why the cell adhesion was poor. Nonetheless, we still 
achieved myotube formation, which shows promise in future work where similar formulations 
might be tested with stiffness modifications. 
6.4 cNFCmal/Col/Matrigel formulations  
 
The cNFCmal/collagen formulations showed promising results even though cell 
attachment was not optimized. We decided to increase the base amount of cNFCmal to 6% 
and add Matrigel to increase the number of cell-adhesive motifs and recapitulate the ECM 
network to a higher degree. The study also tested the influence of different concentrations of 
DMSA in the crosslinking solution. 
This study showed that Matrigel potentially improved cell attachment. However, the 
formulations of cNFCmal 6%/Col 0.4% did not allow the cells to adhere to the surface, which 
did not comply with previous studies. This stops us from making assumptions about Matrigel's 
influence while comparing to cNFCmal 6%/Col0.4% formulations. On top of that, cells did not 
attach in the cNFCmal/col/Matrigel formulation crosslinked crosslinking solution counting up to 
1:5 maleimide/DMSA ratio. The TCPS control showed excellent adherence and differentiation, 
so the cells did not cause the issue. From the results we obtained, we could assume that there 
was a problem with the collagen used in our formulations. This study should be repeated to 
investigate whether increased cNFCmal concentration or Collagen issues caused the cell 
behavior. Unfortunately, due to lack of time, we were unable to repeat this study. 
6.5 cNFCmal/RGD formulations  
 
To further study the possibilities of cNFC-maleimide applications, formulations with 
cNFCmal/RGD were investigated. Free maleimides bind to the RGD, which creates non-
protein scaffolds. Non-protein scaffolds are of interest as they minimize the risk of immune 
reactions, their degradation is tunable, and they are easy to fabricate.  
==PAGE:49==
This study used two concentrations of cNFC mal (4% and 6%). RGD in amounts of 
2mM and 4mM were added to each ink, resulting in 4 formulations in total. First, the NMR  
spectroscopy returned the result shown in Figure 33. The degree of substitution of RGD 
differed from 0,75% in cNFCmal 6/ RGD 2mM formulation to 3% in cNFCmal 4/ RGD 4mM 
and cNFCmal 6/ RGD 4mM meaning that 75% of dree maleimides were occupied in those 
formulations.  
The scaffolds were prepared for seeding, but most of the samples were delaminated 
after 1 day of culture. The only condition without signs of delamination was cNFCmal 6/ RGD 
2mM crosslinked in a 1:1 ratio. Also, with partial delamination in some of the samples, 
cNFCmal 6/ RGD 4mM and cNFCmal 4/ RGD 2mM remained adhered to the surface. All other 
conditions delaminated. The delamination of samples prepared in 1:5 and 1:10 solutions 
indicates that maleimide prevents delamination by crosslinking the fibers and reacts with the 
TCPS. We believe so because in cNFCmal 6/ RGD 2mM in 1:10 condition where only 18,75 
% of maleimide is occupied by RGD when the DMSA is added, the scaffold delaminated, 
suggesting there was no maleimide to attach to TCPS. At the same time, in 1:1, no signs of 
delamination were visible, suggesting that all DMSA reacted, but there was still free maleimide 
to bind to the surface. 
Conditions that did not delaminate were used in cell studies, the results of which can 
be seen in the immunostaining pictures in the result section. The cells, similarly to experiment 
with cNFCmal/col, showed migration towards each other (visible on some of the images from 
immunostaining); however, except for the clamps of cells, we did not observe “island” 
formations, which might support the theory of cells pulling collagen. In the case of RGD, it is 
bound to cNFCmal therefore, cells couldn’t do the same. The cells presented sharp ends 
visible on brightfield microscopy, which suggested that the cell's adhesion was even worse 
than in the case of formulations with collagen as a cell-adhesive motif. 
Immunostaining for those printed substrates revealed a near lack of myotube formation. 
The phalloidin bonded mostly to round-shaped cells (dead). This observation suggests that as 
a cell matures and transforms into a myotube, the cell adhesion is too weak to support it. 
Therefore, it delaminates as it causes the mechanical forces to pull from the scaffold. The 
myotubes that were observed were measured and compared to the TCPS. In the comparison, 
the myotubes on cNFCmal/RGD were shorter and thinner, which proves that the substrate 
cannot support the long-term culture of myotubes. To ensure that the observed trend was 
caused by delamination and not slow myotube formations, the same conditions were cultured 
for 20 days in different media and then immunostained. The results obtained were very similar 
to those from 10 days of differentiation.  
In the end, we did obtain cell adhesion, which proved the interaction of RGD with 
C2C12 integrins and the poor attachment could also be caused by scaffolds being too soft. 
 
6.6 Bioprinting  
 
We could not create a scaffold to guide the formation of anisotropic skeletal muscle 
myotubular tissues. The main reason is that the gels were too soft for the cells to adhere to 
and redeem differentiation cues properly. Therefore, we decided to try to place the cells inside 
==PAGE:50==
the ink for the bioprinting, the storage modulus needs to be low not to compromise cell viability, 
which would change the property that is our problem into the desired one.  
Firstly, bioprinting would mean that the cNFCmal will be crosslinked in the presence of 
the cells therefore, a cytotoxicity assay was conducted. The results showed that the DMSA is 
non-cytotoxic in the range of up to 1mg and becomes cytotoxic in a concentration between 
1mg/ml and 3mg/ml. We didn't need to be above 0.7mg/ml for crosslinking of our scaffolds, 
which was the concentration we used in the 1:10 molar ratio condition for the biggest scaffold. 
Therefore, we should be able to crosslink our prints without damaging the cells. 
The printing with the fluorescent beads showed that the ink preparation method also 
applied to the previous experiment obtained a homogeneous solution, resulting in the print 
having an even distribution of beads within the scaffold. The scaffolds obtained were also in 
the 200-300 µm range, which was proved using a z-stack on confocal microscopy.  
The following steps would include optimization of printing conditions such as bio-ink 
formulations and printing parameters and printing with live C2C12 cells with concentrations 
around 10 mln/ml, but we couldn’t continue this study due to lack of time.

8. Conclusion and Future work 
This study explored the feasibility of creating anisotropic gel substrates using cNFC-
maleimide composites combined with collagen, Matrigel, and RGD motifs to guide skeletal 
muscle myotubular tissue formation. 
Initially, we conducted experiments using cNFC-Col formulations, which revealed their 
poor adhesion to well plates made of TCPS. We believe that the swelling of cNFC fibers 
caused the delamination of the scaffolds, along with other cues. The first modification applied 
with the intention of improving surface adhesion was a poly-D-lysine coating of TCPS. This 
approach failed to provide a stable long-term adhesion of scaffolds, likely due to insufficient 
electrostatic interactions. 
In the second part of the study, cNFC was functionalized with maleimide and 
crosslinked using DMSA in different molar ratios of maleimide to DMSA.  This resulted in 
obtaining scaffolds of cNFCmal/Col with two crosslinked phases that presented a stable long-
term adhesion to TCPS in optimized conditions. 
Cell studies that we conducted on scaffolds incorporating cNFCmal and Collagen as 
cell binding motives supported attachment of the C2C12 cells but exhibited cell aggregation, 
causing a non-uniform adhesion and formations of cell “islands”. Moreover, the myotube 
formations were only visible on the mentioned C2C12 aggregates, causing significantly fewer 
myotube formations than those on the TCPS substrate. Myotube analysis on different scaffolds 
also presented a trend that lower scaffold stiffness resulted in shorter myotubes being formed.  
On top of that, no signs of myotube orientation in relation to print direction were visible, which 
also can be explained by the low storage modulus of formulations used. 
 
We applied several modifications to improve cell attachment and myotube formations, 
including adding Matrigel and RGD to the cNFC-mal-based scaffolds and optimizing them. The 
addition of Matrigel supported cell adhesion, but its effect on improving the ability of 
cNFCmal/Col to support myotube formation could not be validated, potentially due to collagen 
quality issues.  cNFC-maleimide formulations functionalized with RGD allowed the creation of 
protein-free scaffolds that supported initial C2C12 adhesion however, their cell adhesive 
abilities were insufficient to support myotube maturation. 
In conclusion, we resolved the delamination issues of cNFC-based scaffolds. Still, we 
did not manage to obtain scaffolds capable of guiding the formation of anisotropic skeletal 
muscle myotubular tissues, as previous studies showed for cNFC/gelatin-based formulations.  
Future work should continue the micro-extrusion 3D  approach with seeding cells or apply 
bio-printing instead. For the continuation of micro-extrusion 3D printing, the focus should be 
on enhancing scaffold stiffness so printed substrates can provide better adhesion and 
influence the orientation of forming myotubes. On the other hand, the properties of formulations 
tested throughout our studies are desired for bioprinting, making the application of this method 
an interesting alternative to investigate.
