Preface
This project is the culmination of my 35 ECTS point Master Thesis carried out at Topsøe
in Lyngby and is the final project of my Master’s degree in Chemical and Biochemical
Engineering from DTU. My thesis was supervised by Martin Andersson and Hanne
Falsig, and I am sincerely grateful for their support and guidance in the project. I would
also like to thank the Inorganic Analysis department at Topsøe for their hospitality in
taking me on as a project student. I am also very grateful to fellow Master’s student
Elise Despesse for discussion and feedback near the end of my project.
The thesis is divided into five sections. The first section aims at introducing hetero-
geneous catalysis as a technology for nitrous oxide abatement with a focus on zeolite
catalysis. The second section describes the basic concepts of density functional theory
as a modeling tool in heterogeneous catalysis. Leading up to the results of the project,
the third section presents the methodology and the modeling carried out in the project,
and the fourth section presents and discusses the results with an emphasis on previous
studies carried out for the nitrous oxide decomposition reaction. Lastly, the fifth section
summarizes the main findings and suggests directions for future studies.

Abstract
Iron containing zeolites are used industrially for NOx abatement technologies and have
shown great promise for a number of other catalytic reactions such as decomposition of
nitrous oxide and oxidation of metane to methanol. This thesis investigates the stability
of extraframework iron and the free energies of nitrous oxide decomposition on different
iron sites in the BEA zeolite with the most popular computational chemistry method
today, density functional theory.
Relative stabilities of hydroxylated monomeric iron, [Fe-OH]+, oxylated monomeric iron,
[Fe-O]+, divalent iron Fe2+ and two oxygen bridged dimeric iron complexes, [Fe-(µO)-
Fe]2+ and [Fe-(µO)2-Fe]2+ were examined depending on the aluminum siting in the BEA
zeolite. One highly stable Fe2+ located in the six membered β-ring was found, while
[Fe-OH]+ was found to be nearly equally stable at all investigated sites in the zeolite.
Two [Fe-O]+ sites stabilized at an aluminum atom in the six membered β-ring were
found to be significantly more stable than the remaining seven that were investigated.
Nitrous oxide decomposition was investigated on the most stable [Fe-OH]+, [Fe-O]+,
Fe2+ sites and a [Fe-(µO)-Fe]2+ sites. [Fe-OH]+ and [Fe-O]+ are found to have good
nitrous oxide adsorption and relatively low O2 removal barriers, and are therefore likely
to be active in nitrous oxide decomposition at industrial conditions. Fe2+ is found to
bind nitrous oxide well and to desorb O2 at reasonable temperatures, but the highly
stable geometry of an oxygen adsorbate suggests that dissociation of a second nitrous
oxide molecule on the site will be unlikely. The oxygen bridged dimer was found to have
reasonable nitrous oxide adsorption but high barriers for O2 desorption, suggesting that
the site will not be active for direct nitrous oxide decomposition at industrial conditions.
The dimeric site is interesting candidate as an active site in the ammonia selective
catalytic removal of nitrous oxide, in which ammonia will remove adsorbed oxygen atoms.
This thesis sheds light on what is likely to be the active site during direct nitrous oxide
decomposition and potentially what the role of reductants could be in the selective
catalytic reduction of nitrous oxide.

Introduction
The ability of catalysts to improve the rate of a chemical reaction without being con-
sumed in the reaction is used today in many applications from conversion of a raw
material into a product to production of food and removal of pollutants from exhaust
gases.
Catalysis has for more than a century been closely linked to the major challenges in
the world.
In the 1910’s, the world was facing a shortage of resources for ammonia
production during World War 1, and the development of a catalyst for ammonia synthesis
allowed for an alternative way of producing ammonia. Named after its inventors, the
Haber-Bosch catalyst is now described as one of the greatest scientific breakthroughs in
modern history, as it allowed for fertilizer to be produced from abundant resources. One
could argue that world’s problems today are much greater. With heavy industrialization
throughout the 20th century and a lack of focus on the effect of greenhouse gases in the
atmosphere, we stand today with the immense burden on our shoulders of human-caused
climate change. It is now accepted that drastic measures need to be taken soon to reduce
emissions of greenhouse gases. To be able to act, affordable and scalable technologies
that can solve the problem are needed. Similar to the ammonia shortage of the 1910’s
catalysis is likely to play a major role in the future remediation of climate change.
Nitrous oxide is the forgotten villain of climate change with an estimated greenhouse
effect of nearly 300 times higher than that of carbon dioxide in addition to ozone deplet-
ing effects[4]. Nitrous oxide is primarily emitted from agriculture and nitric acid plants,
and the potential use of ammonia as a marine fuel is an additional source of nitrous
oxide emission to the atmosphere that should be addressed[5]. While catalytic nitrous
oxide decomposition has been studied for many decades[6] there has been low attention
on nitrous oxide in the atmosphere, and as a consequence abatement technologies have
not yet been developed to their full potential.
Towards the goal of developing a commercial nitrous oxide decompositon catalyst, a
theoretical understanding of the interaction of nitrous oxide with catalytic material can
be extremely helpful. Being the most popular electronic structure tool, density functional
theory is known for its low computational cost compared to accuracy. This project uses
density functional theory calculations to study a nitrous oxide decomposition catalyst.

1.1
Heterogeneous Catalysis
A catalyst is defined as a material that increases the rate at which a chemical reaction
approaches equilibrium, without being consumed in the process[7]. Heterogeneous cat-
alysts are solids which catalyze liquid or gas phase reactions, and are the most widely
used type of catalyst in industry. A number of different aspects make heterogeneous
catalysts practical to use, perhaps the most important ones being their applicability
at high temperatures and pressures, and that no separation of catalyst and product is
needed[7][8].
A catalytic reaction can be visualized as a cycle in which the catalyst interacts with
reactants in a number of different steps and regenerates in the end when products leave
the catalyst. The underlying concept of the catalytic process is that a catalyst does not
change the overall energy difference of the reaction but changes the intermediate energies
in the reaction. Between reactants and products, a catalyst provides a pathway in which
intermediates are stabilized such that energy barriers between intermediate steps are
lower than without the catalyst. Figure 1 illustrates the effect of a catalyst in lowering
the activation energy of a reaction.[9]

The overall mechanism of a heterogeneous catalytic reaction is adsorption of one or more
reactants to the surface followed by reaction on the surface and subsequently desorption
of products. Two mechanisms, based on how a second reactant adsorbs to the surface,
are typically distinguished between.
In the Langmuir-Hinshelwood mechanism, both
reactants adsorbs to the surface and subsequently recombine on the surface and desorb.
In the Eley-Rideal mechanism, one reactant adsorbs to the surface, and the second
reactant binds directly from gas phase to the adsorbed reactant[9].
The chemical bonding relevant to a catalytic reaction is typically categorized as either
physisorption and chemisorption. Physisorption is a weak long range attractive interac-
tion between two atoms or molecules caused by their electron clouds forming induced
dipoles, and is typically described occurring at a bond length of 3-10 ˚A[10]. Chemisorp-
tion occurs at a shorter range, typically 1-3 ˚A[10], and involves an overlap of electron
clouds of two molecules or atoms[9].
A fundamental aspect in catalysis is the balance between reactants binding too strongly
and reactants binding too weakly. If a catalyst binds reactants too weakly, there will be
a high fraction of unoccupied sites and as a consequence a low reaction rate. Intuitively,
reactants have to be on the surface for the reaction to proceed. Meanwhile, if a catalyst
binds reactants too strongly, the reactants will not react on the surface and instead
occupy active sites. In that case the surface will be completely covered by a specific
reactant that is unable to leave the surface. The concept that a the highest rate of a
catalyst is obtained when reactants bind neither too strongly nor too weakly is known as
the Sabatier principle[7][8][9]. The Sabatier principle is often illustrated as the reaction
rate as a function of adsorption energy of a number of different catalysts for a specific
reaction, in which a volcano-plot as shown in Figure 2 is obtained. The implications of
the Sabatier principle on catalyst design is that one is interested in finding a material
that is at the top of the volcano curve.

Figure 2: Illustration of the Sabatier principle. Figure from Medford et al.[2].
Designing an optimal catalyst for a reaction is much more complex than knowing what
metals are active in the desired reaction. Metals can form a vast amount of different
sites, which can potentially participate in the catalytic reaction. In the case of a metal
surface, there are different possible facets, as well as kinks, steps and vacancies that can
potentially catalyze the reaction, and to get the maximum performance of a catalyst,
one should know what sites are the most active in the reaction[9].
Since heterogeneous catalytic reactions take place on the surface of a material, only
surface atoms are directly involved in the reaction. It is therefore fundamental to catalyst
design to maximize the surface area of the catalytic material, such that maximum amount
of exposure between reactant and catalyst is achieved. This is typically achieved by
dispersing the catalyst material on a porous support, such that the catalyst is typically
present in clusters ranging in size from individual atoms to nanoparticles[7][8][9]. Some
of the most frequently used catalyst supports in industry are zeolites.
1.2
Zeolites in catalysis
Zeolites are a group of microporous materials consisting of TO4 units, where T is typically
either silicon or aluminum. The TO4 units are arranged such that two neighboring T
4
==PAGE:20==
atoms are connected with an oxygen atom. Over 200 different zeolite types are reported
by the international zeolite association, some of which can be found in nature[11][12].
Figure 3 shows four different zeolites ordered according to the diameter of their microp-
ores, and illustrates the diversity in zeolites frameworks that can be made simply from
different arrangements of TO4 units.
Figure 3: Four different zeolites and their largest ring. Only framework atoms are shown.
Figure from Pour et al.[3].
Zeolites are known by their excellent cation exchange abilities, allowing catalytically ac-
tive metals to be incorporated in extra-framework positions. By substituting framework
Si, having a charge of 4+, with Al having a charge of 3+, a charge deficiency occurs in
the zeolite framework. This charge can be compensated by a monovalent cation located
in an extra-framework position immediately outside the Al atom. The more aluminum
atoms that are substituted into the framework during the zeolite synthesis, the bigger
a charge imbalance is created which can allow for cations of higher valences or polynu-
clear cationic clusters to be accommodated in extraframework positions. The function
of a zeolite as a catalyst can be altered depending on what cation is located in the ex-
traframework position. The simplest case is that the cation is H+, which results in the
zeolite being a Brønsted acidic catalyst[12].
Iron containing zeolites have relevance to a number of different reactions, in particular
involving NOx abatement reactions and hydroxylation of methane to methanol and
benzene to phenol.
Since Fe(+) is not stable, iron is typically found in oxylated or
5
==PAGE:21==
hydroxylated form and is likely to form iron oxide clusters such as dimers, oligomers or
potentially hematite[13][14].
The iron nuclearity of the Fe-BEA zeolite prepared by different methods was studied
systematically by Høj et al.[15], who found that the iron distribution depended only on
the iron loading and not the preparation method. At iron loadings lower than 1.2 weight
percent, iron was present as monomers, whereas at intermediate iron loadings of 2.1-5
weight percent, a mix of monomers, dimers, oligomers and hematite were present.
1.3
Catalytic nitrous oxide decomposition on Fe-BEA
The challenge of decomposing nitrous oxide - a relatively unreactive molecule - lies in
finding a material that is able to bind nitrous oxide, subsequently break the N-O bond,
while at the same time not binding oxygen atoms so strongly that they are unable to
recombine on the surface and subsequently desorb. The main classes of materials that
are being studied for nitrous oxide decomposition are spinels, zeolites, perovskites and
hexa-aluminates with zeolites and mixed-oxides being the most promising so far[6][16].
Among investigated materials for nitrous oxide decomposition iron and cobalt BEA
usually perform the best[16].
The Fe-BEA zeolite has shown promising results as a
catalyst both for direct nitrous oxide decomposition, but also the selective catalytic
reduction of nitrous oxide with ammonia[17][18].
Two different mechanisms have been reported in the literature for direct nitrous oxide
decomposition. Both mechanisms start with dissociation of a nitrous oxide molecule on
the active site:

Pirngruber’s

the most active for the reaction, which was later supported with kinetic measurements
by P´er´ez-Ramirez et al.[19]. Li et al.[23] suggested based on DFT results that dimers
are the active site on the Fe-ZSM-5 zeolite with reasonable nitrous oxide dissociation
barriers and oxygen removal, while monomers potentially contribute at sufficiently high
temperatures. Furthermore, Heyden et al.[24] have pointed to [Fe-OH]+ sites as being
active when water and nitric oxide are presence in the reactant gas. In addition, some less
abundant sites have been considered for the reaction, including two isolated Fe2+ sites,
which is found in the FER zeolite but is also likely to be found in the BEA zeolite[25].
Furthermore, [Fe-(µO)(µOH)-Fe]+ has been suggested as active site by Kiwi-Minsker et
al.[26]
Despite several decades of studies in nitrous oxide decomposition, important questions
about the nature of active site in nitrous oxide decomposition on zeolites and about the
effect of other gases on the decomposition reaction still remain unanswered.

Density Functional Theory
This section describes the basic underlying assumptions of density functional theory with
the goal of providing enough background to illustrate the uncertainties in the density
functional theory method in this project.
2.1
Kohn-Sham density functional theory
Computational methods based on quantum mechanics have become an indispensible
tool to evaluate adsorption energies and reaction barriers of chemical reactions on solid
catalysts. Electronic energies of many-particle systems can in theory be determined by
solving the time-independent Schr¨odinger equation, depending on the Hamiltonian, ˆH,
and the wave function, ψ.
ˆHψ = Eψ
(2)
ˆH describes the contributions to the total energy of a quantum mechanical system,
while the wave function is a purely quantum mechanical concept but can be understood
as implying the probability of finding a particle at a specific coordinate. However, an
exact solution is only known for very simple quantum mechanical systems. As one of
the biggest contributors to computational quantum chemical methods, Walter Kohn,
expressed in his Nobel Prize lecture in 1999[27]:
”In general the many-electron wave function ψ(r1 ,...,rN) for a system of N electrons is
not a legitimate scientific concept, when N>N0 , where N0≈103.”
The development of computational methods to describe chemical reactions therefore
lies in imposing a series of approximations on the Schr¨odinger equation to obtain an
equation that can realistically be solved for a system involving many particles[28]. His-
torically, the development of nearly all quantum chemical methods used today relies
on two fundamental approximations: The Born-Oppenheimer approximation and the
Self-Consistent-Field approximation.
The Born-Oppenheimer approximation is the uncoupling of nuclear motion and elec-
tronic motion, justified by the mass of electrons being much lower than the mass of
nuclei. The electronic position can be envisioned to adjust instantaneously to a change
in a nuclear position, and as such the Born-Oppenheimer approximation leads to a sepa-
9
==PAGE:25==
rate electronic Schr¨odinger equation which can be solved independently from the nuclear
one at fixed nuclear positions[28].
The Self-Consistent-Field approximation is an approximate way of obtaining electron-
electron repulsion energies.
Here, the electron-electron repulsion is estimated as the
sum of the repulsion of each electron with a field consisting of the N-1 other electrons.
The Self-Consistent-Field approximation requires an iterative scheme for the electronic
structure to converge. Another name often used for the Self-Consistent-Field energy is
the Hartree energy, EHartree[28][29].
Kohn-Sham density functional describes the total energy of a quantum-mechanical sys-
tem as the sum of the kinetic energy of non-interacting electrons, Ekin(ρ), the external
potential, Eext(ρ), the Hartree energy, EHartree, and the exchange-correlation energy,
EXC(ρ). By obtaining energies as functionals of the electron density, ρ, the Schr¨odinger
equation is reduced from involving N coordinates to describe the complete wave function
of N electrons to only three coordinates. The exchange-correlation energy is formally all
energy not described by the first three terms, and as such density functional theory is
simply a reformulation of the Schr¨odinger equation at this point[27].
Etot(ρ) = Ekin(ρ) + Eext(ρ) + EHartree(ρ) + EXC(ρ)
(3)
The first approximation within density functional theory is that the exact functional to
describe the exchange-correlation energy is not known, and thus approximate functionals
to describe the exchange-correlation energy must be used[28][29].

While the exchange-correlation energy is a small contribution to the total energy, it
accounts for most energy in chemical bonding[30]. The simplest approach to obtain the
exchange correlation energy is to assume that the electron exchange correlation energy
only depends on the local density. This is leads to the Local Density Approximation, in
which the exchange-correlation energy is obtained from[28][29]:

Exchange-correlation functionals that are more accurate than Generalized Gradient Ap-

ent of the electron density. For the purpose of describing catalytic reactions, Generalized
Gradient Approximation methods are often used and the uncertainty of the method is
kept in mind[29]. The uncertainty is described as approximately 0.1-0.2 eV[9].
In this thesis, the revised Perdew-Burke-Ernzenhofer, revPBE, functional is used, which
is known to accurately obtain chemisorption energies[31][32].
2.3
Basis functions
Another important decision in density functional theory modeling is the choice of basis
functions to represent the wave function. The type of basis functions used for a calcula-
tion is typically chosen based on how the electronic orbital is expected to look and what
is computationally efficient[29]. This thesis used plane wave basis functions, which are
based on the periodic electronic structure of crystals, which are convenient since zeolites
are crystalline solids.
Ideally an infinite amount of basis functions would be used, and an exact representation
of the actual wave function could be achieved, but obviously this is impossible. The
basis set size is therefore defined by a cutoff value, which depends on the system being
studied[28].
Since only valence electrons are of interest to chemical bonding, it is a reasonable ap-
proximation to freeze core electrons when solving the Kohn-Sham equations[28].
As
such pseudopotentials have been used in this thesis to represent ionic cores of hydrogen,
nitrogen, oxygen, silicon, aluminum and iron.

2.4
Dispersion
Density functional theory is known to accurately predict electronic structures and en-
ergies of bonds of a chemical nature, such as metallic, covalent and ionic bonds. These
bonds are caused by overlaps of electron clouds of two species, and in that case the
attractive and repulsive forces can be described mostly by local effects between the two
electron clouds. However, one of the major shortcomings of density functional theory
is the inability to predict non-local long range interactions, and for systems where ph-
ysisorption is important and additional dispersion correction needs to be added[33].
Several approaches to including dispersion in density functional exist. Some methods
include the dispersion correction as a term in the exchange-correlation functional, some
include an additional dispersion potential in the Kohn-Sham Hamiltonian while some
include an additional dispersion energy independently of the Kohn-Sham equations,
known as the C6 type dispersion corrections[33].
Dispersion is particularly important for zeolites, since a molecule located in a zeolite
channel will experience physisorption with the surrounding zeolite framework. In this
project, the semi-empirical Grimme’s D dispersion correction was used, a C6 based
dispersion correction. Grimme’s D dispersion energy contains two element-dependent
parameters and one functional-dependent parameter[33].

Computational model of the Fe-BEA zeolite
This section introduces the BEA zeolite model used in the project and explains, based
on the structure of the BEA zeolite and empirical rules, the reasoning behind the choice
of extraframework iron sites that are investigated.
3.1
BEA zeolite model
Zeolites naturally consist of silicon and oxygen, and their properties of stabilizing metal
cations comes from impurities in the framework[12]. A charge deficiency of -1 occurs by
substituting one aluminum into the framework, which allows for a monovalent cation to
bind to the framework near the aluminum atom. In the case that two aluminum atoms
are located in the framework, a charge deficiency of -2 occurs, and a divalent cation can
be stabilized in an extraframework position. This project used a model of the BEA zeo-
lite consisting of 96 T-atoms, with either one or two aluminum atoms in the framework.
Substituting one aluminum atom into the framework instead of a silicon atom results
in a Si/Al ratio of 96, and two aluminum atoms an Si/Al ratio of 48. The BEA zeolite
structure can be seen Figure 5.

3.1.1
Monovalent iron complexes
The stability of monovalent iron complexes in the BEA zeolite framework is likely to
depend on the location of aluminum in the framework. To fully describe effects of the
local framework geometry on the stability of monovalent iron complexes, each T-site
substitution of aluminum should be considered. The BEA zeolite has nine distinct T-
sites, which are conventionally given the name T1-T9.
This project uses a different
T-site classification based on the geometry of the BEA zeolite to avoid ambiguity in the
given names of sites. Here, the nine sites are divided into three types of sites based
on their location in the T16 unit.
R sites are the four most central sites forming a
cross, B sites are the four sites forming a square around the R sites, and W sites are
the eight sites in outermost positions in the T16 unit. Figure 6 shows the T16 unit and
examples of B, W and R sites. Appendix 1 shows a list of all T-sites with the notation
used in this project as well as the notation used by the International Zeolite Association.
Figure 6: T16 unit, and examples of B, W and R -type T-sites. Red is oxygen atoms
and yellow is silicon atoms.

3.1.2
Divalent iron complexes
The number of possible divalent cation exchange sites is significantly reduced by a set
of empirical rules that apply for all zeolites. The most famous rule is L¨owenstein’s rule,
which states that Al-O-Al linkages are not possible in the zeolite lattice, which means
that the maximum Si/Al ratio is 1. Takaishi’s rule states that only one aluminum atom
is allowed in a five-membered ring[34], while Dempsey’s rule states that aluminum atoms
in the framework seek to have the maximum distance between each other[13][35]. Since
two aluminum atoms cannot be located in a five membered ring, a divalent cation is
unlikely to be located in a five membered ring. This means that only the six membered
ring, known as the β ring is likely to stabilize a divalent cation, and therefore only the
six membered ring has been investigated as a potential location of a divalent iron cation.
The location of β rings in the BEA zeolite is shown in Figure 7.

Computational details
The DFT calculations were carried out as plane wave calculations on Quantum Espresso
6.3[36] on DTU’s High Performance Computer. Nuclear coordinates were modidied on
the Atomic Simulation Environment graphical user interface[37]. Ionic cores were rep-
resented with PAW pseudopotentials[38].
Exchange-correlation energy was described
with the revised Perdew-Burke-Ernzenhofer functional[32] and dispersion was described
with the Grimme D dispersion correction[39]. Brillouin zone was sampled at the gamma
point. The Broyden-Fletcher-Goldfarb-Shanno algorithm[40] was used for geometry op-
timization, and convergence was assumed when the energy varied with less than 10−4 eV
between two steps in the geometry optimization. A Gaussian smearing of 0.01 was ap-
plied. Self-Consistent-Field convergence was assumed when the energy varied with less
than 10−8 between steps. Spin polarization was applied on iron, and for calculations of
molecules in vacuum on oxygen. DFT calculations of molecules were carried out in a
20x20x20 unit cell.
The adsorption energy ∆Eads was defined as:
∆Eads = Ezeolite−molecule −Ezeolite −Emol
(6)
In which, Ezeolite−molecule is the calculated energy of adsorbate-zeolite complex, Ezeolite

were neglected, so that the entropy of adsorption, ∆Sads, was defined as:
∆Sads = Sads −Smol
(7)

species, whose adsorption on the zeolite lead to Sads. To determine Gibbs free energies,
a pressure correction of gas phase species, N2O, N2 and O2 was included, such that
Gibbs free energies were determined from:

Results and discussion
This section describes the results of DFT modeling in this project with a focus on the
stability and activity of active sites.
4.1
Stability of extraframework iron in Fe-BEA
With the aim of understanding the geometry of iron complexes in the BEA zeolite, and
what sites are most stable, the adsorption of monomeric and dimeric iron complexes to
aluminum framework sites in the BEA zeolite were investigated.
4.1.1
Fe2+
Energies of three different divalent iron sites relative to the lowest energy site are shown
in Figure 9.
The first has aluminum atoms located at R sites, the second has both
aluminum atoms at W sites, and the last has an aluminum atom at an R site and one at
a W site. With an energy of 0.64 eV and 0.89 eV, respectively, lower than the two other
sites, the iron site where both aluminum atoms are at R positions is the most stable.
The lower energy can likely be attributed the stable coordination of iron as a slightly
distorted square planar position.
4.1.2
[Fe-OH]+
Geometries and energies of the most stable [FeOH]+ geometries stabilized at aluminum
atoms located at three different T-sites relative to the lowest energy site are shown
in Figure 10. Evidently, most geometries have energies that are within the assumed
uncertainty of 0.2 eV. The only geometries that have slightly higher energies are the
ones located in five membered rings. These results suggest that stability of [FeOH]+
is only slightly dependent on the local framework geometry, and that several different
[FeOH]+ sites are likely to be found in an Fe-BEA zeolite.

zeolite, as these sites are not poisoned by water. Furthermore, they describe that an
FeOH site could be formed by reaction of NO with a poisoned Fe(OH)2 site. In addition,
Heyden et al. point to the most likely reaction path being that a nitrous oxide molecule
is decomposed on [Fe-OH]+ such that [O-Fe-OH]+ is formed, and the adsorbed oxygen
atom is removed by reaction with nitric oxide or nitrogen dioxide.

5
Conclusion
These results provide insight into what is the active site during direct nitrous oxide
decomposition.
By the use of DFT calculationns, relative stabilities of iron complexes and free energies
of nitrous oxide decomposition in the BEA zeolte were examined. It was observad that
the location of [Fe-OH]+ at extraframework positions in the BEA zeolite depends only
slightly on the T site location of aluminum in the framework, with several T sites being
equally stable. More significant differences between relative stabilities are seen for [Fe-
O]+, in which two T-sites are significantly more stable than the remaining seven. The
speciation of nearby iron sites as two isolated [Fe-O]+ was found to be significantly
less stable than an bridged dimer, [Fe-(µO2)-Fe]2+, suggesting that iron is likely to
form dimers. Similarly, nearby [Fe-OH]+ complexes are likely to recombine at elevated
temperatures by to form a double oxygen bridged dimer, [Fe-(µO)-Fe]2+, by desorption
of water.
O-terminal end adsorption of nitrous oxide and adsorption of oxygen species on different
extraframework iron sites was also investigated with DFT. The free energies of adsorption
revealed a hydroxylated iron monomer, [Fe-OH]+, and an oxylated monomer, [Fe-O]+,
to be interesting sites for nitrous oxide decomposition due to reasonable nitrous oxide
adsorption and a low barriers for O2 desorption. A divalent iron cation located near two
framework aluminum atoms was found to have interesting adsorption energetics, with
reasonable nitrous oxide adsorption and a reasonable O2 desorption barrier, but con-
siderations on the binding of the second nitrous oxide molecule suggested that nitrous
oxide dissociation is unlikely to occur on the site. An oxygen bridged dimeric iron site
was found to have high barriers for O2 desorption, suggesting that high temperatures
are needed for the reaction to occur, or alternatively a reductant such as ammonia could
remove adsorbed oxygen species at lower temperatures.
5.1
Future perspectives
The findings in this thesis open up for a number of different theoretical and experimen-
tal directions to go in. Firstly, a complete description of nitrous oxide decomposition
38
==PAGE:54==
requires knowledge on barriers of the reaction, and the first step from here is likely
to determine energy barriers of the dissociation of both nitrous oxide molecules on the
five investigated sites. Once barriers are obtained, a theoretical kinetic study could be
extremely beneficial to understand what steps are rate determining at different sites
depending on temperature and partial pressures of gas species.
Furthermore, a kinetic study would likely unveil which sites are most likely to be active
at industrial conditions, and here experimental studies could be extremely useful to
supplement the theoretical work. Since site specific spectroscopy techniques, such as
M¨ossbauer spectroscopy of EXAFS, can be used to quantify weight fractions of specific
extraframework sites in zeolites, one could try and correlate nitrous oxide decomposition
activity with the weight fraction of a specific site, e.g. [Fe-O]+ monomers in a series of
prepared Fe-BEA zeolites with the goal of determining what site is active in the reaction.
Similar studies have been carried out for other NOx reactions, one example being Høj
et al. If one could conclude a difference in reactivity between monomers and oligomers
and be able to point to a type of site as accounting for the reactivity in Fe-BEA and
perhaps more generally iron containing zeolites, this would be extremely useful in the
design of an optimal iron containing zeolite catalyst for nitrous oxide decomposition.
With the goal of developing an abatement technology, a natural step from studying
the direct nitrous oxide reaction would be to investigate the effect of nitric oxide and
nitrogen dioxide on the stability of active sites iron sites and the mechanism of nitrous
oxide decomposition, as these gases are likely to be found along with nitrous oxide. A
simple approach would be to complement the work carried out by Heyden et al. for
nitrous oxide decomposition in the presence of nitric oxide for the Fe-ZSM-5 zeolite with
a similar study on the Fe-BEA zeolite with dispersion corrected periodic DFT rather
than cluster models, such that confinement effects of nitrous oxide in the zeolite channel
are included. Furthermore, ammonia selective catalytic reduction of nitrous oxide is a
topic with very limited theoretical work, and extending the work done in this project
to ammonia selective catalytic reduction would be highy useful in explaining what iron
species account for nitrous oxide decomposition activity in the presence of ammonia.
