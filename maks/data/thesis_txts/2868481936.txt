1
Introduction
1.1
Background
Room acoustics addresses how sound propagates in enclosed environments, ultimately impacting
speech clarity, musical fidelity, and listener comfort. While standard texts e.g. (Jacobsen, Poulsen,
Rindel, Gade, & Ohlrich, 2011) often describe a diffuse-field model, most real-world rooms deviate
significantly due to varied absorption, complex surfaces, and non-uniform materials. These non-
homogeneous conditions can diverge from idealized theories, highlighting the need for more robust
methods that capture the true directional and absorptive behavior of room acoustics.
1.2
Motivation
Conventional acoustic measurements, such as those guided by ISO 3382 (ISO 3382-1:2009 Acoustics
— Measurement of room acoustic parameters — Part 1: Performance spaces, 2009), place micro-
phones away from boundaries under the assumption of a sufficiently diffuse field in the main listening
area. In rooms with non-uniform absorption, however, strong local effects near walls or absorptive
panels may not be captured by these standard positions. Important directional reflections or inho-
mogeneous decay paths can be overlooked, potentially limiting the accuracy of conventional rever-
beration metrics. To better address such localized phenomena, advanced computational and signal
processing methods, notably spherical harmonic analysis and wavenumber approaches suggested by
(Nolan, Fernandez-Grande, Brunskog, & Jeong, 2018) offer deeper insights into the angular distri-
bution of energy. By employing these tools, room acoustic design and measurement practices can be
refined to account for complex sound field decay patterns and boundary effects in real-world spaces.
1.3
Problem Statement
Existing room acoustic characterization methods may fail to capture the nuanced behavior of sound
fields in non-homogeneous rooms, especially when measurements rely solely on standard ISO 3382
positions. This research investigates how near-surface measurements differ from traditional central
positions in such environments. By quantifying these differences, we aim to reveal the limitations of
prevailing measurement standards and to propose more comprehensive methods tailored to complex
acoustic conditions.
1.4
Objectives
The primary objectives of this research are to:
1. Develop an analysis tool that utilizes spherical harmonics to process and interpret impulse
responses in non-homogeneous rooms.
2. Compare near-surface measurements with ISO 3382 recommended positions using simulations
in the Treble software environment (Treble Technologies ehf., 2024b).
3. Investigate energy flow patterns and acoustic behavior via wavenumber and spherical harmonic
analyses, focusing on local absorption effects.
4. Evaluate the impact of non-homogeneous absorption on key room acoustic parameter, rever-
beration time (T20).
1
==PAGE:6==
5. Propose recommendations to enhance acoustic characterization methods for complex (non-
Sabine) environments.
1.5
Research Questions
To fulfill these objectives, the study addresses the following core questions:
1. How do near-surface acoustic measurements in non-homogeneous rooms differ from those rec-
ommended by ISO 3382?
2. What insights do these differences provide regarding the limitations of standard measurement
approaches in complex acoustic fields?
3. How effective is spherical harmonic analysis in quantifying directional energy decay and isotropy
in non-homogeneous spaces?
4. How does a non-homogeneous absorption distribution influence the T20 parameter?
5. What specific improvements can be recommended to current measurement standards to capture
the complexities of non-homogeneous acoustics more reliably?
1.6
Scope and Limitations
This research relies on simulation-based analysis using Treble to model non-homogeneous rooms with
uneven absorption distributions. The main scope and limitations are:
• Simulation Environment: All analyses are performed in Treble. No physical experiments
are undertaken, limiting direct real-world validation.
• Measurement Positions: We compare near-surface measurements against ISO 3382 positions
to investigate local absorption effects.
• Frequency Range: Analysis focuses on 125 Hz to 1 kHz, covering only partial standard third-
octave bands relevant to room acoustics.
• Spherical Harmonic Expansion: The expansion is truncated at a practical order to balance
accuracy with computational cost.
• Simulation Accuracy: Accuracy depends on how well Treble models geometry and material
properties. Minor deviations can affect results.
• Generalizability: Findings stem from specific simulated cases and may not directly extend
to all non-homogeneous rooms.
• Validation: In the absence of physical measurements, validation relies on theoretical consis-
tency checks and comparison with established literature written by (Nolan et al., 2018).

Thesis Structure
The thesis is organized into the following chapters:
1. Introduction: Describes the background, motivation, problem statement, objectives, research
questions, and overall thesis structure.
2. Literature Review: Surveys relevant work on room acoustics, impulse response measurement

By examining near-surface versus standard measurement positions in rooms with non-uniform ab-
sorption, this thesis investigates how boundary proximity influences acoustic metrics and field distri-
butions. The findings may help refine practical measurement approaches for real-world spaces that
deviate from diffuse-field assumptions, offering broader insight into capturing local and directional
phenomena.

Literature review
2.1
Overview of Room Acoustics
Room acoustics is the study of how sound behaves in enclosed spaces, influenced by factors such
as room geometry, surface materials, and furnishings.
Understanding these factors is crucial for
designing spaces with optimal acoustic performance, affecting speech intelligibility, music quality,
and the overall auditory experience.
2.1.1
Sabine vs. Non-Sabine Rooms
Traditionally, room acoustic analysis has relied on the Sabine equation, which assumes a homogeneous
distribution of sound absorption and a diffuse sound field (Bies, 1995). However, many environments
do not satisfy these assumptions due to varied surface materials, complex geometries, or other non-
homogeneities. As a result, Sabine’s model can yield inaccuracies in predicting reverberation time
or speech intelligibility, particularly in non-Sabine settings. (Prawda, Schlecht, & Välimäki, 2022)
3
==PAGE:8==
2.2
Impulse Response Measurement in Room Acoustics
Impulse responses (IRs) are central to characterizing the acoustic properties of a space.
An IR
captures how a room responds to an acoustic impulse, combining reflections, absorption, and scat-
tering into a time-domain record that can be transformed into multiple acoustic metrics. ISO 3382
standards (ISO 3382-1:2009 Acoustics — Measurement of room acoustic parameters — Part 1: Per-
formance spaces, 2009) prescribe guidelines for measuring reverberation time, clarity, and other
parameters, typically calling for microphone positions in or near the center of the room. While such
standard positions facilitate repeatable measurements across laboratories, they may not adequately
capture local phenomena near absorbing or reflective surfaces.

For acoustic analysis, several key properties make spherical harmonics particularly useful:

In room acoustics, these properties enable us to decompose measured or simulated impulse responses
into directional components. The order n effectively determines the spatial resolution of the analysis,
with higher orders capturing more detailed directional characteristics of the sound field. This math-
ematical framework is particularly valuable for analyzing non-homogeneous rooms, where sound en-
ergy may not be uniformly distributed (Rafaely, 2015). This background connects to our subsequent
analysis, where we use spherical harmonic decomposition to study differences between near-surface
and ISO 3382 measurement positions in non-Sabine rooms.
Application to Sound Field Analysis:
Compared to planar or linear microphone arrays, spher-
ical arrays sample the acoustic field in every direction, capturing the full three-dimensional distribu-
tion of the incoming wavefronts (Fernandez-Grandé, 2016). Expanding the measured sound field into
spherical harmonic coefficients Am
n then facilitates (i) identifying predominant directions of arrival,
(ii) differentiating low-order modes such as monopoles or dipoles from higher-order modes indicating
complex reflection paths, and (iii) deriving diffuseness or isotropy metrics.
Practical Considerations:
Despite these advantages, spherical harmonic analysis demands accu-
rate, regular geometry for the microphone array, strict positioning tolerances (e.g., mic sphere radius
known to sub-millimeter precision), and reliable regularization. Tikhonov regularization, guided by
the L-curve, is commonly used to invert the underdetermined or ill-conditioned system of equations
mapping from measured pressures to spherical harmonic amplitudes (Hansen, 2024).

2.5
Energy Flow and Acoustic Parameters
Although parameters such as clarity C80 and full reverberation time T60 are commonly used in
practice, this research focuses specifically on T20 (i.e., the decay interval from −5 dB to −25 dB).
Under uniform absorption, classical Sabine and Eyring formulas can approximate T20 effectively.
However, in non-uniform cases, local boundary absorption may produce strong directional reflections
that invalidate diffuse-field assumptions. Thus, while C80 or T60 might also offer insights, the present
work concentrates on T20 and wavenumber-based analyses to address anisotropic or multi-phase decay
structures. This follows the approach of Nolan et al. (Nolan et al., 2020), where T20 was likewise
investigated to capture detailed decay behavior.
2.6
Identified Gaps and Rationale for this Study
While significant progress has been made in understanding sound fields in reverberant spaces, notable
gaps remain:
1. Near-Surface vs. Standard Positions: Most established guidelines (ISO 3382) focus on central
or mid-room positions, potentially overlooking localized boundary phenomena.
2. Non-Homogeneous Absorption: Many numerical or experimental studies concentrate on ho-
mogeneous reverberation chambers or assume even absorption distribution. Real rooms often have
patchy absorptive treatments (walls, ceilings, corners), leading to anisotropic fields that remain
under-characterized by classical metrics.

3. Time-Resolved Angular Analysis: The short-time wavenumber approach proposed by Nolan
et al. (Nolan et al., 2020) demonstrates how isotropy evolves over the decay, but has not been ex-

To address these gaps, this thesis develops a simulation-based analysis tool leveraging wavenumber

• Demonstrate where and how partial absorption significantly affects directional decay.
• Clarify whether near-surface measurements yield new insights missed by standard ISO placements.
• Validate whether time-segmented isotropy indicators capture these localized effects more effectively

• Investigate how energy-decay curves (EDCs) and T20 analyses reveal frequency-dependent multi-

3
Methodology
3.1
Research Approach
This study employs a simulation-based approach using Treble to model non-homogeneous rooms. The
room geometry and material properties are first designed in SketchUp, then imported into Treble
for acoustic simulation. The primary focus is on comparing impulse responses measured near room
surfaces with those at standard ISO 3382 positions, analyzing differences in acoustic energy flow and
isotropy. All simulated IRs are post-processed in MATLAB via a custom wavenumber analysis tool,
which computes spherical harmonic expansions, isotropy metrics, and short-time segmentations.
Meanwhile, the T20 values (reverberation times from -5 dB to -25 dB) are extracted directly from
Treble’s acoustic results to complement the wave-based indicators. Collectively, these approaches
help elucidate how selective absorption treatments, particularly near boundaries, can alter both the
room’s directional decay patterns and its overall reverberation characteristics.
3.2
Simulation Environment: Treble
Treble is an advanced acoustic simulation platform that combines wave-based and geometrical solvers
to capture sound propagation in enclosed spaces. At lower frequencies, it relies on a discontinuous
Galerkin (DG) scheme to solve the full wave equation in the time domain, accounting for phenomena
such as diffraction, scattering, and modal behavior. This approach ensures highly accurate modeling
of complex or low-frequency interactions that simpler energy-based methods might overlook. For
higher frequencies, Treble’s geometric solver (based on image-source and ray-radiosity techniques)
simulates reflections and scattering under standard ray assumptions, then hybridizes these results
7
==PAGE:12==
with the wave-based data. Users can import room geometries (e.g. from SketchUp), assign material
properties (absorption and scattering), and define source-receiver configurations. Treble then outputs
detailed impulse responses and acoustic metrics, including reverberation times and directional energy
distributions. Because it can accurately capture phenomena like diffraction and multi-slope decays,
Treble is particularly suited to investigating non-Sabine environments with localized, angle-dependent
absorption, making it central to the simulations and analyses performed in this thesis (Treble Tech-
nologies ehf., 2024a).
3.3
Development of the Analysis Tool
An analysis tool was developed in MATLAB to process and interpret the simulated impulse responses
obtained from the Treble simulations. This tool is critical for transforming raw time-domain pressure
data into meaningful spatial and frequency-domain representations, following the wavenumber-based
approach of Nolan et al. (Nolan et al., 2018). Its workflow converts raw impulse responses into
frequency-domain data, performs coordinate transformations for spherical harmonic decomposition,
solves the ill-posed inverse problem of estimating the wavenumber spectrum (using regularization
techniques), and computes spherical harmonic coefficients alongside isotropy indicators to assess the
spatial uniformity of the sound field. By integrating these operations into a single, coherent pipeline,
the tool provides a systematic method for evaluating non-homogeneous (non-Sabine) room acoustics,
facilitating comparisons of measurement positions and revealing complex acoustic behaviors otherwise
hidden in time-domain data.
3.4
Theoretical Foundations
This section outlines the core mathematical principles behind the analyses in this thesis. The ap-
proach closely follows Nolan et al. (Nolan et al., 2018), who introduced a wavenumber-based frame-
work for measuring isotropy in reverberant spaces, adapted here to a simulation-based context.
Subchapters 3.10–3.11.2 which address ill-posed inverse problems, Tikhonov regularization, the L-
curve method and residual analysis via the normalized cumulative periodogram (NCP), draw on Per
Christian Hansen’s lectures “Intro to inverse problems” (Hansen, 2024) and define the core equa-
tions, variables, and reasoning that underpin subsequent acoustic analyses. Initially, the framework
is presented in a steady-state perspective, paralleling the multi-source validation in Chapter 3.15.1
where isotropy trends from Nolan et al. (Nolan et al., 2018) are reproduced. This confirms the reli-
ability of the plane-wave decomposition and wavenumber-based isotropy approach. In section 3.14,
the method is extended to a time-segmented formulation, following the subsequent study by Nolan
et al. (Nolan et al., 2020), capturing how partial absorption and early reflections evolve throughout
the decay. This time-dependent approach underpins Cases B, C, and D, providing detailed insights
into non-diffuse conditions characteristics in a rectangular room with non-homogenous absorption
distribution.

3.10
Ill-Posed Inverse Problem
While the above formulation is straightforward in continuous theory, in practice we only have a
finite set of microphone measurements {p(rm)}. Estimating P(k, θ, ϕ) from limited data becomes an
ill-posed inverse problem:
1. Underdetermined: We attempt to reconstruct a continuous function P(·) from discrete sam-
ples.
2. Sensitivity: Small errors (e.g., simulation inaccuracies or limited sampling) can yield large
instabilities in the solution.
Recognizing this ill-posedness is crucial. Even with simulation data (i.e., with minimal noise), the
finite sampling of the wavefield means we must regularize the solution to avoid non-physical spikes
in P.

may cause the sound field to become more or less isotropic in specific time windows. In essence, it
exposes transient anisotropy that is invisible to a purely steady-state or single-time-window analysis.

4.3
Additional Simulation Parameters
• Frequency Range: Each scenario is simulated in Treble using octave or third-octave bands from
63 Hz up to 8 kHz. For post-processing, we focus on center frequencies at 125, 500, and 1000 Hz.
• Time Discretization: A sampling frequency of 32 kHz ensures adequate temporal resolution for
the impulse responses. After exporting the IRs, optional gating or manual time-cropping can be
applied in MATLAB if needed.
• Short-Time Snippet Analysis: The IR data are segmented into short (100 ms) Hann-windowed
snippets at chosen instants (t = {0.05, 0.10, 0.50, 0.75} s).
This time-dependent wavenumber
approach follows Nolan et al. (Nolan et al., 2018), enabling analysis of how isotropy and directional
decay evolve over time.
• Tikhonov Regularization: Plane-wave coefficients are estimated using Tikhonov regulariza-
tion with an L-curve search for the optimal parameter. This stabilizes the inverse problem of
reconstructing the wavenumber spectrum from discrete IR data.
• Boundary Conditions: Surfaces are modeled using frequency-dependent impedance, accounting
for both reflection and absorption effects.
• Execution of Simulations: Each case (B, C, D) is run independently in Treble, generating IRs
at each microphone location on the spherical array. These IRs are then exported for wave-based
and spherical-harmonic analysis in MATLAB, as outlined below.

5.1
Overview of Post-Processing
Before detailing each scenario, we summarize the post-processing pipeline:
1. Windowing: Each IR is segmented into short windows (100 ms Hanning window) centered at
t = 0.05, 0.10, 0.50, 0.75 s to capture early, mid, and late parts of the decay.
24
==PAGE:29==
2. Frequency-Domain Conversion: An FFT is computed for each time-windowed IR at the
relevant frequencies (125–1000 Hz).
3. Plane-Wave Inversion (Tikhonov): A plane-wave matrix W is constructed for each fre-
quency/time slice, and the wavenumber coefficients are solved via Tikhonov with an L-curve
to pick λ.
4. Spherical Harmonic Expansion: We expand the resulting wavenumber spectrum into spher-
ical harmonics, obtaining Am
n and isotropy indicator i(k).
5. Visualization: Graphs include (i) isotropy vs. time curves, (ii) 3D wavenumber scatter/heatmaps,
(iii) 2D unwrapped spherical maps, and (iv) bar charts of spherical harmonic coefficients. Ad-
ditional graphs such as the original spherical harmonics coefficients and order summation,
L-Curve plots, 3D wavenumber scatter plots and the NCP residual plots are found in the
appendix 8.2 for reference.

5.4
Energy Decay Curve (EDC) and T20 Analysis
In addition to the spatio-temporal wavenumber approach, we also investigate the energy decay in each
scenario by examining the energy decay curve (EDC) and T20. Specifically, we filter each impulse
response IR into frequency bands ( 125 Hz, 500 Hz, 1000 Hz), compute the Schroeder reverse integral,
39
==PAGE:44==
and average across microphones in linear (power) domain. This yields a mean EDC vs. time that
indicates how quickly energy decays at each frequency. By plotting the EDC in dB scale, we can
identify key slopes, multi-slope transitions, and other irregularities. Comparing near-surface (NS) to
ISO (central) positions reveals whether local boundaries accelerate or modify the energy decays.
5.4.1
Method Overview
Following standard room acoustics practice, each IR is (i) optionally gated to remove pre-arrivals
or noise, (ii) band-pass filtered (1/3-octave center frequencies at 125 Hz, 500 Hz, 1000 Hz), and (iii)
transformed into an EDC using the reverse cumulative sum of |p(t)|2. The final EDC is normalized
to 0 dB at t = 0. To obtain a mean EDC across multiple receivers, we first average all EDCs in linear
domain, then convert to decibels. Six final plots are produced, we then interpret whether the decay
is single-slope or multi-slope, noting how absorption and boundary proximity alter the low vs. high
frequency decay.

6
Discussion
In this chapter, we synthesize the findings from §5 and relate them to the broader objectives and
literature presented in chapters 1–2. The following sections address the core questions posed in §1,
assess the validity of the spherical-harmonic approach under non-homogeneous absorption, and con-
siders the strengths and limitations of near-surface measurement strategies compared to conventional
ISO 3382 positions.
6.1
Interpretation of Key Findings
1. Differences Among Cases B, C, and D:
All cases reveal persistent anisotropy, whereas par-
tial absorption from a suspended ceiling (case c) or combined ceiling/wall absorbers (case d) reshapes
the early energy and slightly shifts spherical harmonics order modes in some time windows. These
patterns suggest that overhead and wall treatments primarily mitigate certain directional paths while
leaving others (notably lateral surfaces in rectangular plans) relatively unaffected.
2. Near-Surface vs. ISO Positions:
Although both positions show similar overall reverberation
times within each of their respective scenario, near-surface arrays consistently capture boundary-
specific effects more prominently during the early stages of the decay (as seen in the spherical
harmonics order sums of chapter 5.3). Even with overhead or wall absorption (cases c and d), near-
surface microphones detect slightly higher pressure levels and reflection intensities from adjacent walls
(as seen in chapter 5.2), whereas the centrally located ISO array underreports these local boundary
influences. Similarly, in the EDC and T20 analysis (Section 5.4), near-surface positions often exhibit
more pronounced undulations in the energy decay curve. In case d, for instance, near-surface T20
drops more sharply at mid frequencies e.g., 500 Hz due to direct interaction with the absorptive wall,
whereas the ISO position in the room center completely overlooks this effect.
3. Frequency Dependence:
The transition from 125 Hz (longer wavelengths) to 1000 Hz (shorter
wavelengths) underscores how partial absorption modifies the decay in different frequency bands. In
43
==PAGE:48==
cases c and d, the ceiling and wall panels are more effective at mid/high frequencies in principle, but
grazing-angle reflections can produce an extended reverberant tail and multi-slope EDC. Thus, T20
cannot be interpreted purely on the basis of an absorption coefficient at that frequency because the
wavefield geometry also matters (2D/3D wave propagation).
4. Multi-slope decays:
The emergence of multi-slope decay in cases c and d reinforces that
designers and acousticians should interpret EDT, T20 or T30 with caution in such rooms, as a single
parameter may not reflect the true multi-phase reverberant decay. In practice, multi-slope decay can
complicate attempts to characterize the room with a single reverberation time parameter due to the
fact that the early decline of the EDC is oftentimes faster than the latter part of the EDC decline in
a double slope case.
6.2
Consistency with Existing Literature
Comparisons to Nolan et al. (Nolan et al., 2018) confirm that the plane-wave plus spherical harmonic
approach is robust for quantifying anisotropy in both near-surface and ISO configurations.
Our
multi-source validation scenario (§3.15.1) broadly reproduced their finding that more sources yield
higher isotropy. In addition, the distinct absorption distributions in cases c and d highlight how the
“decaying field” analysis from Nolan et al. (Nolan et al., 2020) can be adapted to expose time-varying
directional phenomena unique to non-homogeneous surfaces.
6.3
Practical Implications
In many real spaces with partial or patchy absorption, near-surface measurements reveal critical
boundary-driven reflections that standard ISO 3382 positions often overlook. Our simulations indi-
cate that local damping modifies directional decay more strongly at mid/high frequencies, especially
close to walls or overhead panels, underscoring the value of time-segmented analysis and appropri-
ately chosen frequency bands. While Treble’s wave-based solver provides detailed modeling, these
findings remain to be validated experimentally, and expanding spherical harmonic orders or shorten-
ing time windows could further clarify rapid directional changes. We therefore recommend at least
one microphone placement near absorbent boundaries, along with careful regularization and array
geometry, to capture the anisotropic phenomena typical of non-homogeneous rooms. The following
conclusion summarizes these results, addresses the research questions, and outlines potential avenues
for practical validation and enhanced measurement strategies.
7
Conclusion
This chapter synthesizes the key outcomes of the study, evaluates how effectively the research ob-
jectives and questions (outlined in §1) have been addressed, and offers final remarks on potential
extensions and wider implications for room acoustics.
7.1
Summary of Findings
The analyses in Chapter 5 demonstrate that partially absorptive (non-Sabine) rooms do not conform
to a single-slope diffuse-field model; instead, directional reflections and multi-slope decays emerge
when absorption is localized (e.g., on one ceiling panel or wall). Time-resolved plane-wave inversion
and spherical harmonic expansions revealed persistently anisotropic behavior across the tested fre-
quency bands, indicating that room geometry and boundary treatments can substantially shape the
44
==PAGE:49==
angular distribution of energy. Specifically, near-surface (NS) placements recorded higher early reflec-
tion levels and sharper mid-frequency decays than ISO (center) positions, highlighting local boundary
effects that conventional guidelines might overlook. Meanwhile, energy decay curves (EDCs) and T20
calculations reinforced these conclusions by showing multi-slope or even triple-slope reverberation in
the mid/high frequency bands of partially treated cases. Though overhead or wall absorbers were
effective at damping certain paths and frequencies, alternative reflection paths often preserved a
later, slower decay phase. Fully reflective scenarios exhibited a more uniform single-slope EDC, but
still retained strong directional components at all frequencies. In summary, both wave-based and
energy-based analyses converged on the same core finding: non-uniform absorption triggers spatially
and temporally distinct decay characteristics, captured by combining near-surface measurements,
short-time frequency analysis, and standard T20 metrics.
7.2
Contributions to the Field
This research contributes to non-Sabine room acoustics by implementing Nolan et al.’s wavenumber-
based isotropy framework in Treble, integrating short-time windowing, plane-wave inversion, and
spherical harmonic analysis within a single MATLAB pipeline. By demonstrating that ISO 3382
positions can overlook important boundary driven reflections, it highlights how near-surface arrays
can more effectively diagnose problematic reflections. Furthermore, by adapting time domain seg-
mentation to partial overhead or wall absorption, the study reveals how specific angular components
are selectively damped, shaping the decay field’s isotropy in non-homogeneous environments. Col-
lectively, these findings deepen our understanding of partial absorption and its impact on wavefield
behavior, illustrating how selective absorption reshapes both the angular and temporal characteristics
of reverberant fields and advancing the broader study of room acoustics and architectural acoustic
design.
7.3
Answers to Research Questions
Recalling the five core questions from §1, we can address them in turn:
1. How do near-surface measurements differ from ISO 3382 positions?
They reveal stronger boundary reflections and pressure build-ups, more pronounced local absorp-
tion effects, and minor EDC irregularities (undulations) when facing a boundary.
2. What insights do these differences provide regarding limitations of standard measure-
ment approaches?
Standard central placements may overlook boundary-driven directional anomalies in rooms with
partial absorption. This underscores the value of including at least one near-surface measurement
in non-homogeneous spaces, either to detect localized issues or to complement central data.
3. How effective is spherical harmonic analysis in quantifying directional energy decay
and isotropy?
Spherical harmonics, combined with plane-wave inversion and Tikhonov regularization, robustly
isolate directional components and provide a stable measure of isotropy over time. The harmonic
order sums reveal which modes dominate at different time-frequency bins, and the isotropy indi-
cator i(k, t) succinctly tracks how uniform the acoustic energy distribution is.
45
==PAGE:50==
4. How does a non-homogeneous absorption distribution influence T20?
Unevenly distributed absorption can lead to multi-slope decays and frequency-dependent varia-
tions in the energy decay curve. This often causes T20 to deviate substantially from diffuse-field
predictions, particularly in frequency bands where boundary absorption or grazing reflections are
significant.
5. What specific improvements can be recommended to current measurement standards?
In non-homogeneous or partially absorptive spaces, we advise supplementing ISO guidelines with
near-surface arrays to capture boundary-driven directional anomalies, particularly grazing-angle
reflections that can prolong the energy decay. Additionally, employing short-time, wave-based
analyses can help detect multi-slope decays or other localized phenomena. Together, these steps
go beyond conventional T20 or T30 metrics, providing a more complete characterization of the
acoustic environment.
7.4
Limitations and Future Work
While the simulation-only context and the 100 ms window limit the scope of the results, future
investigations may include physical measurements in real non-homogeneous environments, verifying
how effectively they capture boundary effects in practice. Using 50 ms (or even shorter) windows
could reveal faster reflection dynamics, though at the cost of frequency resolution and inversion
stability. Mapping anisotropy across multiple sources or employing advanced optimization routines
might further refine measurement guidelines.
Ultimately, the techniques demonstrated here can
be extended to diverse architectural contexts and specialized array geometries, and we hope these
results will stimulate new approaches to measuring and modeling partial absorption in realistic spaces,
improving both the accuracy of acoustic predictions and the efficacy of targeted treatments.
7.5
Final Remarks
In closing, this thesis underscores the importance of where and when impulse response data is col-
lected in rooms with a non-homogeneous absorption distribution. By merging wave-based simulation
(Treble) with short-time plane-wave inversions and spherical harmonic expansions, we showed that
partial overhead or wall absorption significantly alters directional energy flow, especially in near-
surface regions that conventional ISO guidelines might ignore. In summary, bridging advanced wave-
based simulation with spherical harmonic analysis provides a clear view of how non-homogeneous ab-
sorption shapes the sound field’s directional decay. Near-surface measurements detect local boundary
phenomena early on, complementing standard ISO positions. As room acoustics research continues
to emphasize complex geometry and partial treatments, such multi-faceted measurement strategies
become all the more essential.
