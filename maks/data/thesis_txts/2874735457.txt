Abstract
Traditional hearing aids improve hearing in quiet environments but struggle in multi-speaker set-
tings due to overlapping speech and non-linear noise. We propose a visual-attention-driven se-
lective amplification method: a wearable camera captures the scene, an eye-tracker records gaze,
and face recognition identifies speakers. These cues feed a state-space model simulating attention
dynamics; an observer binarizes attention to guide a beamformer in adjusting target direction and
gain. Using the dataset from Dorszewski, Fuglsang, and Hjortkjær 2024, the approach achieved
significant improvements (p < 0.001, Cohen’s d up to 8 in Recall). The inferred mean gaze-
switching time among active conversation partners is 16 seconds, suggesting a link between gaze
behavior and auditory attention.

Introduction
1.1
Background
Hearing aid devices and cochlear implants have made significant strides over the past few decades,
achieving remarkable technological breakthroughs and substantially improving the quality of life
for the hearing impaired. In simple acoustic settings, such as quiet indoor conversations or single-
source environments, current dual-channel hearing aids can effectively restore hearing to some
extent through frequency band compression and mapping. This includes adjusting gain at specific
frequencies to enhance speech clarity and using noise suppression algorithms to filter out back-
ground noise. However, in more complex scenarios, especially multi-source acoustic environments
like a ”cocktail party,” or in situations where the sound sources and noise are not additive and can-
not be separated by pure dual-channel audio methods, the performance of dual-channel hearing
aids is less effective. Specifically, in a cocktail party, multiple speakers talk simultaneously, with
their voices overlapping and interfering, making it difficult for traditional devices to accurately
separate and enhance the target speech. Similarly, in environments like traffic or concert venues,
noise may mix with speech signals in a non-linear way, further challenging the processing capabili-
ties of existing technology. This is primarily because dual-channel audio cannot effectively handle
non-additive noise, such as convolved or modulated noise, due to limitations in frequency band
compression algorithms and limited frequency domain resolution. These constraints often lead to
distortion or the omission of crucial audio information in complex acoustic environments, thereby
affecting the user’s auditory experience and communication effectiveness (Armstrong et al. 2022).
Many previous studies (e.g. Van Veen and Buckley 1988) have enhanced the effectiveness of hear-
ing aids by introducing beamforming technologies. This approach is highly effective for preserving
spatial cues and suppressing noise, significantly improving speech clarity and the auditory experi-
ence for users in noisy environments. For example, in a multi-person conversation, beamforming
can focus on the direction of the target speaker while suppressing interfering sound sources from
other directions.
Furthermore, the application of various machine learning algorithms has further optimized the
performance of beamforming. For instance, Fernandez et al. 2022 have adopted spatial cross-
correlation matrix matching, which adaptively adjusts the beamforming weight parameters by an-
alyzing the correlation between signals received by multiple microphones, thereby more precisely
enhancing the target sound source and suppressing noise. This combination of machine learning
not only achieves significant progress in objective metrics but also receives positive feedback from
users in subjective hearing tests, demonstrating better comfort and naturalness.
Unlike these methods, which rely on multi-channel audio signals themselves and the phase dif-
ferences and relative physical positions of audio signals used in beamforming as key cues, other
multiple modalities of information can also serve as important supplementary cues. For example,
in the pioneering work by Hjortkjær et al. 2025., advanced decoding methods were used for audi-
tory attention decoding (AAD) from electroencephalogram (EEG) signals. This provided precise
and real-time neuro-driven cues for the beamforming microphone array, enabling dynamic adjust-
ment of the microphone’s directionality based on the listener’s brain activity. This significantly
improved speech clarity and target source separation in complex acoustic environments.
This project mainly elaborates on and implements a model at the software level. This model uses
a wearable camera and an eye-tracker to collect visual data, which serves as an input for a state-
space equation. Specifically, the wearable camera captures real-time image information of the

user’s environment, while the eye-tracker precisely records the user’s gaze points and eye move-
ment trajectories, obtaining visual input data. This data is integrated and fed into a state-space
model that imitates human attention mechanisms using state variables, for example, by simulating
the cognitive process of selectively focusing on specific areas or objects in complex scenes. Fur-
thermore, the model introduces an observer to binarize the output, converting continuous attention
states into discrete ”conversation partners” or ”background participants” labels to simplify subse-
quent analysis and application. The entire process aims to more accurately model and replicate
the dynamic characteristics of human speech attention, providing a powerful tool for research in
related fields.
In summary, this project primarily aims to:
• Process and analyze gaze data and face recognition data.
• Implement multiple state-space models to simulate attention accumulation and switching,
enabling correct classification of conversation partners (interlocutors).
• Optimize the parameters of the implemented models.
• Validate the models by comparing them with baseline models.
1.2
Proposed Methodology
This project presents a novel methodology that enhances selective amplification by integrating vi-
sual cues, specifically eye-gaze tracking and face recognition, as indicators of auditory attention.
Instead of using EEG signals as in previous work by Hjortkjær et al. 2025, this Master’s project
adopts a state-space modeling approach to pursue a similar objective through a different method-
ological path. Instead of using neural signals, this new system employs two visual inputs: eye-gaze
tracking and face recognition.
Dataset
This project is based on the dataset created by Dorszewski, Fuglsang, and Hjortkjær
2024, in which eye-gaze tracking data and face recognition data used in this project are included.
The eye-gaze tracking data is captured via sensors on a pair of glasses, providing a direct source
for attention decoding. Concurrently, a face recognition algorithm processes the visual signal from
a mounted camera to identify and discriminate individuals from the background, leveraging the af-
fordability and efficiency of modern techniques. By fusing the data from eye-gaze tracking and
face recognition, we establish a robust, alternative source for attention decoding. This combined
visual input, when paired with movement tracking data, serves as a crucial hint to steer the beam-
former, which automatically enhances the desired target signal.
State-space model(SSM)
SSM is widely used in many fields as filters, control algorithms, and
biological models since it was introduced in Kalman 1960. It has also been used in auditory atten-
tion decoding to enhance the performance (Aroudi, Taillez, and Doclo 2020). A state-space model
is used as the algorithm simulates the mechanism of attention accumulation in this project, which
gives the prediction for deciding whether the people in the region of vision are part of the cur-
rent conversation or not, and gives a gain for the steering of the beamformer accordingly. Leaky
integrator, as one specific type of SSM, have been proven to be effective in modeling attention
mechanism in neuron and the corresponding decision making process (Mitani et al. 2013). Sin-
gle and double layer leaky integrator is used in this project to model the attention accumulation
process.
The parameters of the state-space model are optimized and evaluated using the dataset by Dorszewski,
Fuglsang, and Hjortkjær 2024, and corresponding properties of the model are analyzed and dis-
cussed.

2.1
Assumptions and Concepts Clarification
A fundamental assumption underlying this project is that the object being gazed at by the subject
corresponds to the object of interest for the subject. This assumption is supported by prior re-
search, such as Le et al. 2025, which demonstrates a strong correlation between gaze direction and
attentional focus.

2.1.2
Mechanism
The dialogue recognition system employed in this project is built upon a core assumption: dur-
ing conversations, the Subject exhibits a clear visual attention preference. More specifically, the
Subject typically maintains gaze on their conversation partners, while rarely directing attention
toward background participants. This behavioral pattern has been consistently observed in prior
research across social psychology and human–computer interaction, where humans demonstrate a
strong gaze bias during natural dialogue. A detailed analysis of our dataset further confirms that
this assumption holds true within the present study.
Based on this assumption, our system processes video frames of the subject sequentially. First, a
target detection algorithm identifies all participants in the field of view, recording their bounding
box positions and dimensions. In parallel, the system integrates gaze coordinates collected by a
calibrated eye-tracking device. When the Subject’s gaze coordinates overlap with a participant’s
bounding box in a given frame, the system infers that the participant is being visually attended to.
At this point, an is_gazed label is dynamically assigned. This binary variable indicates whether
gaze behavior is present.

2.2
Dataset
The dataset used in this study was originally introduced by Dorszewski, Fuglsang, and Hjortkjær
2024, comprising egocentric videos for face recognition analysis together with calibrated eye-gaze
tracking data collected by the authors. In total, it contains 68.9 hours (approximately 6.2 million
frames) of egocentric video, segmented into 877 conversation clips with an average duration of 4.7
minutes. Of these, 52 hours include calibrated eye-gaze tracking data. The dataset is hierarchically
organized into sessions, participants (i.e., subjects wearing the egocentric camera and eye-tracking
device), blocks, and conversations, where each participant contributes 20 conversations per ses-
sion, and each session is further divided into five 25-minute blocks. Building on this structure,
the present study conducted a comprehensive data analysis, followed by offline model develop-
ment and optimization. For model training and validation, the dataset was further partitioned, with
detailed information on segmentation and utilization provided in the subsequent chapter.
2.3
Eye-gaze behavior analysis
Before building the entire system, it is important to first evaluate the dataset confirming that the
underlying statistical properties of the gaze behavior in this dataset satisfy the assumptions of the
system. Specifically, the bounding boxes of recognized faces in the collected videos, together with
the eye-gaze tracking data, are examined using the proposed methods in order to verify whether
meaningful relationships exist between these two sources of information.
This section provides a detailed description of the data preprocessing procedures and analytical
methods, aiming to systematically investigate the potential association between gaze targets and
speaker identity. Prior to constructing the analysis framework, a comprehensive evaluation of the
dataset is essential to ensure the validity and reliability of subsequent modeling. Such an evaluation
not only facilitates the identification of potential biases or noise but also provides data-driven
insights for model optimization.
Specifically, we systematically examined the facial bounding boxes detected in the collected videos
alongside the concurrently recorded eye-tracking data. Statistical analyses were then employed to
characterize the distribution of gaze patterns and to assess whether meaningful correlations exist
between gaze behavior and speaker identity. These analyses further evaluate the practical util-
ity of gaze patterns in distinguishing between speakers or in revealing predictable trends under
specific interaction contexts. Based on these in-depth investigations, a robust data foundation is
established, providing critical prerequisites for subsequent model construction, feature engineer-
ing, and performance evaluation, thereby ensuring the scientific rigor and reproducibility of the
study.

2.4.2
Baseline Model: A Reference
The baseline model serves as a benchmark for evaluating the performance of other models, ad-
hering to a simple and direct principle: when the input is 1, indicating the subject (an individual
wearing glasses and eye-tracking equipment) is fixating on a target person, the model classifies
that person as a conversation participant. When the input is 0, indicating the subject is not fixat-
ing on the target person, the model classifies them as a non-participant. This mechanism provides
a benchmark whose classification accuracy is used to measure the relative performance of other
models, offering a reference for subsequent model optimization and comparison. Specifically,
the baseline model provides a performance lower bound; any newly proposed model must sig-
nificantly outperform this baseline to be considered of practical value. This comparative method
clearly quantifies the improvement in a model’s ability to determine participation status.

This mathematical expression clearly reflects the deterministic nature of the model’s decision: the

them from a participatory state. This parameter-free, non-learning design makes the baseline model
highly transparent and reproducible, providing a stable and reliable point of comparison for more
complex attention recognition models.
2.4.3
Linear Integrator: the basic model of the project
The core model of this project is a state-space model, specifically a Leaky Integrator. By intro-
ducing a ”leak” term, this model effectively simulates the decay characteristics of a system’s state,
enabling a more accurate description of dynamic system behavior. For instance, in neuroscience,
leaky integrators are commonly used to model the changes in a neuron’s membrane potential, where
the leak term represents the natural dissipation through ion channels.

The Linear Integrator (LI) model is defined as a single-layer system designed to simulate the dy-
namic accumulation and decay of human attention during conversations. It combines a linear in-
tegrator with a gated binary output function. Specifically, the model captures a subject’s sustained
focus on potential conversational participants through an integration process: when a person is fix-
ated upon, their corresponding attention integral value gradually increases over time. Conversely,
when the fixation is interrupted, the integral value enters a decay phase, decreasing exponentially.
This mechanism is analogous to short-term memory or attention span in neuroscience. The model’s
core parameters are: b, the integral leak time constant, which controls the rate of attention decay
(a larger b value indicates slower decay and a longer-lasting attention span); and θ, the decision
threshold, which determines the integral level required for a target person to be identified as an
active conversational participant. For instance, in a multi-person conversation, if someone is fix-
ated upon for several seconds, their integral might quickly exceed θ, leading the system to classify
them as a participant. A brief glance, however, might cause the integral to decay too quickly and
remain below the threshold, so they are not included.

Bayesian Optimization
In this work, Bayesian Optimization (BO) is employed to fine-tune model parameters following a
preliminary coarse grid search. Bayesian Optimization is a global optimization strategy engineered
for problems where the objective function is expensive to evaluate, lacks an analytical form, or has
no available gradient information. It is particularly well-suited for engineering applications, such
as acoustic design, where each function evaluation may correspond to a time-consuming simulation
or a physical experiment.

Advantages and Limitations
The primary advantages of Bayesian Optimization include:
• Sample Efficiency: It is highly effective at finding the optimum of expensive black-box
functions with a minimal number of evaluations.
• Principled Trade-off: The acquisition function provides a natural mechanism for balancing
the exploration-exploitation trade-off.
• Uncertainty Quantification: The surrogate model provides not only predictions but also a
measure of uncertainty about those predictions.

dimensions, such as those in acoustic engineering, Bayesian Optimization remains a powerful and
practical choice.

Experiment Setup
This chapter outlines the experimental design and methodological framework of the study. It de-
tails the dataset restructuring, relative eye-gaze analysis, model development, attention-switch sim-
ulation, parameter optimization, and model evaluation procedures.
3.1
Dataset Partitioning
The dataset provided by Dorszewski, Fuglsang, and Hjortkjær 2024 is originally segmented into
training, validation, test_seen, and test_unseen subsets. However, given that the methodology
employed in this project does not rely on machine learning models, such granular partitioning is
deemed unnecessary. Consequently, the dataset is restructured into two primary subsets: training
and test.
The training subset is utilized to calibrate the models under predefined evaluation metrics, while
the test subset serves as the benchmark for assessing the models’ performance. This streamlined
partitioning ensures simplicity and aligns with the specific requirements of the proposed method-
ology.
3.2
Relative Eye-gaze Analysis
Following dataset splitting, relative eye-gaze data is processed to extract relative gaze vectors
through the calculation of vectors between each pair of data points, as described in subsection 2.3.1,
which are then used to test the hypothesis that eye movements correlate with the identity of the
gazed-at object, i.e. whether the gaze is directed towards subject’s focus of attention.
By examining relative gaze positions, this study investigates whether these positions follow a spe-
cific distribution, assessing central tendency, dispersion, and the presence of multiple peaks—
while also testing the prior hypothesis that conversation participants receive significantly more
gaze than non-participants. In particular, increased focus is anticipated toward individuals who
are speaking or reacting, as opposed to passive bystanders, suggesting that gaze dynamics reflect
underlying social interaction patterns.
The experiment is set up with controlled comparisons along two dimensions to systematically
explore and validate the hypotheses.
3.2.1
Consistency Check Across Data Splits
This experiment validates the dataset used—the full dataset, the training set, and the validation
set—to ensure a consistent distribution under the conditions of both conversation participants and
non-participants. This is achieved by examining whether these data subsets share similar charac-
teristics in key variables such as gaze position and participant status. Through this consistency
check, it is ensured that the results obtained from subsequent training and validation on these sets
are reliable, thereby avoiding overfitting or bias and guaranteeing that the model can reproduce
similar performance on other unknown datasets, such as in practical application scenarios or on
newly collected data, demonstrating stable performance.
3.2.2
Analysis of Relative Gaze Distribution by Number of Active Partners
The second experiment analyzes whether any formal differences exist in the distribution of rela-
tive gaze positions for different numbers of dialogue participants (e.g., dyadic conversation, group
discussion, etc.). It is hypothesized that detectable features are present; for instance, gazes might
be more concentrated and frequent with fewer people, while more dispersion or a turn-taking pat-
tern might be followed with more people. By comparing these distributions, an exploration is
Conversation Partners Recognition from Eye-gaze with State-space Models
17
==PAGE:26==
conducted into whether different strategies could be adopted for different numbers of conversation
participants—such as adjusting model parameters or designing specialized classifiers—to optimize
the accuracy and robustness of gaze prediction.
3.3
Attention Switch Simulation
A key objective of this study is to examine whether the model can correctly classify recognized
faces while both attention and conversational context change dynamically. To test this, we simulate
attention-switching scenarios by concatenating data from different conversations.
The dataset from Dorszewski, Fuglsang, and Hjortkjær 2024 provides the necessary structure for
such simulations. Conversations are organized by session, participant (the individual wearing the
egocentric camera and eye-gaze tracker), block, and conversation. Here, a block contains a set
of 5-minute conversations, and multiple blocks constitute a session. By merging conversations
within a block, we simulate a natural setting in which participants enter and exit a discussion over
approximately 25 minutes.
In this setup, eye-gaze data and face recognition bounding boxes are combined sequentially. For
participants who are not present in certain conversations, missing data are represented as empty
inputs (binary zeros in the model), allowing the offline models to process all sequences consistently.
Using this procedure, 805 conversations were concatenated to form approximately 160 extended
25-minute blocks. Conversations lacking eye-gaze data were excluded to maintain data integrity.
This mechanism provides a controlled yet realistic environment to evaluate model performance
under dynamic attention shifts and varying conversational participation.

Model Optimization
Following the initial development of the models, the subsequent step of parameter optimization be-

different architectures are both meaningful and fair. In this section, we present the methodologies
employed to explore the parameter space, with a particular focus on balancing model efficacy
against computational efficiency. The goal is to identify parameter configurations that maximize
performance while avoiding unnecessary complexity or overfitting.
We begin with the basic model introduced in subsection 2.4.3, which provides a simple yet ef-

yses between the optimized single-layer and double-layer configurations further highlight the ad-
vantages and limitations of each approach, offering a nuanced understanding of their respective
capacities.
Through this structured progression from the simple to the complex, the optimization process not
only improves model performance but also provides valuable interpretative insights into the role
of individual parameters in shaping system dynamics.

5
Discussion
5.1
Main Results
The results presented in section 4.4 demonstrate that the leaky integrator provides an effective
framework for modeling attentional states from gaze-tracking and face-recognition inputs. Specif-
ically, this study evaluated the relationship between gaze switching and attentional shifts among
interlocutors in multi-party conversational settings. In a controlled simulation where participants
frequently alternated gaze across multiple interlocutors, eye-movement recordings indicate that
the leaky integrator accurately captures both the temporal dynamics of gaze transitions and the
allocation and transfer of attentional resources.
A two-layer leaky-integrator architecture—where the first layer encodes attention inputs for in-
dividual interlocutors and the second layer integrates combinations of interlocutors—was com-
pared with a single-layer model. While the two-layer model exhibited longer response latencies
for single-object events and lower performance on certain pointwise metrics, it demonstrated im-
proved stability and generalization across trials. This likely reflects the hierarchical integration of
inputs, which smooths trial-level variability but introduces a trade-off with responsiveness. Con-
versely, the single-layer model attained marginally higher mean performance on some metrics
but displayed larger dispersion, indicating a trade-off between peak performance and robustness.
These findings suggest that the choice between single- and double-layer architectures depends on
application priorities and constraints, highlighting complementary strengths of the two designs.
From a cognitive science perspective, the proposed single layer model offers an interpretable, low-
complexity state-space representation of attention. It reproduces attention-state transitions and
identifies a characteristic time constant of approximately 20 seconds, consistent with fixation du-
rations observed in multi-party conversational contexts. Moreover, its computational efficiency
enables real-time operation on resource-constrained platforms, supporting both theoretical inves-
tigation and practical applications in human–machine interaction.
Meanwhile, the double-layer leaky integrator introduces a new yet intuitive method by applying
the leaky integrator to the combination-based attention state rather than attention to individual
interlocutors. The combination-based attention-state model is more robust to the temporal dynam-
ics of gaze switching and more sensitive to the allocation of attention among interlocutors. This
suggests a potential mechanism for how attention is allocated across conversation partners in an
overall ”attention map” in the brain. We obtained a time constant of approximately b ≈1.65 s
for the first leaky integrator and b ≈1.58 s for the second. While the single leaky integrator time
constant directly relates to the mean gaze-switching time. Here the first time constant doesn’t have
a intuitive meaning, while the second time constant provides insight into the dynamics of the at-
tention state at the level of object combinations, suggesting how long it takes on average for the
combined attention state to shift.
5.2
Limitations
Despite these encouraging results, several methodological and representational limitations should
be acknowledged. First, the state-switching mechanism relies on a specific data-preprocessing
pipeline designed to maximize experimental control and reproducibility. This fixed construction
may bias the estimated time constant toward dataset-specific patterns, limiting generalization to
other conversational scenarios or tasks of longer duration.
Second, the study employed a binary input encoding (gaze vs. non-gaze), which simplifies the
model but discards continuous information such as precise gaze-to-target distances and motion

Euclidean distance) did not yield improvements in predictive accuracy or robustness under the
current architecture and hyperparameter configuration. These negative findings do not preclude
the potential benefits of distance representations, which could encode fixation strength, duration,
or dynamic changes and thereby improve generalization in more complex scenarios. Realizing this
potential is likely to require more sophisticated model architectures, careful selection of distance
measures, and systematic parameter optimization—directions that were beyond the scope of the
present study.
Another reason why using the gaze-to-target distance as an input may fail in this project is the
mentioned concatenation mechanism, which might create a ”jumping gap” when switching be-

5.3
Future Directions
Addressing these limitations suggests several avenues for improving the model’s generalization
and practical utility. Increasing the diversity and scale of training data may enhance performance,
though gains are likely constrained by the alignment between the chosen time constant and the
user’s gaze-switching cadence.
A promising avenue involves developing a dynamic relevance estimation module that continuously
evaluates the correlation between input features and the current attentional state. Analogous to
human attentional modulation driven by novelty and task relevance, this module could adaptively
reweigh inputs or adjust model parameters. For instance, upon detecting a contextual change (such
as the appearance of a novel face), the system could lower the threshold for state transitions or
reduce the leaky integrator’s time constant, increasing sensitivity to salient events and improving
responsiveness.
Complementary efforts in multimodal sensor fusion—integrating physiological and behavioral sig-
nals, as well as auditory cues such as sound-source identification—could yield more comprehen-
sive and robust attentional-state estimates. Multimodal fusion allows cross-validation of attention
estimates, mitigating the impact of noise in any single modality and enhancing overall system
robustness.
Finally, the gaze-based attention classifier developed here could be incorporated into larger ar-
chitectures, for example as an input or supervisory signal to neural networks, providing a modu-
lar building block for systems that require efficient, dynamically responsive attention estimation.
Collectively, these directions offer a roadmap for extending the current work toward more gener-
alizable and practically deployable attentional-state modeling frameworks.

Conclusion
This project successfully demonstrated the efficacy of a leaky-integrator model in capturing the
dynamics of attentional shifts during multi-party conversations. By analyzing eye-movement data
from a simulated environment, we found that this model effectively relates gaze switching to the
allocation of attention among interlocutors. Furthermore, our investigation of a novel two-layer
architecture revealed a crucial trade-off: while a single-layer model offers faster responses, the two-
layer design provides substantially improved generalization and stability, making it better suited
for complex, dynamic scenarios.
The primary contribution of this work is an interpretable and computationally efficient state-space
model of attention. From a cognitive science perspective, the model identifies a characteristic time
constant of approximately 20 secs for gaze fixation, offering a quantitative parameter consistent
with observed human behavior. A time constant of 1.6 seconds was also identified for changes in
attention with respect to conversation partners, highlighting a faster dynamic that complements the
longer fixation process and offering a quantitative handle for future model refinement. For applied
engineering, its simplicity and low computational cost make it a viable tool for developing real-
time, attention-aware human–machine interaction systems, even on resource-constrained devices.
In conclusion, this study validates the leaky integrator as a foundational tool for modeling con-
versational attention. Although the current implementation has limitations, the model provides a
robust and interpretable baseline. The principles and architecture established here serve as a mod-
ular building block for future research, paving the way for the development of more adaptive and
multimodally-informed systems capable of achieving a deeper and more practical understanding
of human attentional states.

Traditional hearing aids improve hearing in quiet environments but struggle in multi-speaker settings due to
overlapping speech and non-linear noise. We propose a visual-attention-driven selective amplification method:
a wearable camera captures the scene, an eye-tracker records gaze, and face recognition identifies speak-
ers. These cues feed a state-space model simulating attention dynamics; an observer binarizes attention to
guide a beamformer in adjusting target direction and gain. Using the dataset from Dorszewski, Fuglsang, and
Hjortkjær 2024, the approach achieved significant improvements (p < 0.001, Cohen’s d up to 8 in Recall).
The inferred mean gaze-switching time among active conversation partners is 16 seconds, suggesting a link
between gaze behavior and auditory attention.
