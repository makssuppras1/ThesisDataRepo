4 Discussion 
InfecSous diseases pose a notable challenge in aquaculture. Disease outbreaks caused by ﬁsh 
pathogens such as Vibrio anguillarum cause detrimental yield and economic losses every year. 
While eﬀecSve, anSbioSc treatment may contribute to the development of anSbioSc resistance 
and, therefore, should be replaced by sustainable alternaSves. Vaccines are one such alternaSve 
for disease prevenSon, but their applicaSon is limited. AlternaSve anSbioScs are needed, 
parScularly for ﬁsh larvae, which sSll have undeveloped immune systems, and probioScs oﬀer a 
promising alternaSve.  
This project aimed to invesSgate the inhibiSon potenSal of microbiomes from live feed culture, 
speciﬁcally those from roSfers (Brachionus plica6lis) and Artemia (Artemia salina), against the 
ﬁsh pathogen Vibrio anguillarum. Two fracSons, akached and free-living, were tested for each 
host. All tested microbiomes showed complete growth inhibiSon of the V. anguillarum strains 
NB10 and 90-11-286. While the inhibitory eﬀect of Artemia microbiomes (A_A and A_F) 
decreased as sample concentraSons decreased, roSfer microbiomes showed complete 
inhibiSon of V. anguillarum even at the highest sample diluSon. AddiSonally, three bacterial 
strains isolated from the inhibitory microbiomes, which were idenSﬁed as Vibrio ﬂuvialis, 
Cobe6a sp., and Pseudomonas pu6da, also showed inhibitory eﬀects when tested as pure 
cultures against V. anguillarum. These ﬁndings emphasize the inhibitory potenSal of natural 
microbiomes and highlight their prospect for applicaSon in disease management within 
aquaculture.  
The observed diﬀerences in inhibiSon strength of Artemia and roSfer microbiomes could be 
explained by the diﬀerent starSng inocula. Artemia samples showed much lower CFU counts 
than the roSfer samples. A more similar starSng inocula of the sample groups would have likely 
diminished the observed inhibiSon potenSal diﬀerences. The Artemia samples were sterilized 
with bronopol, resulSng in lower CFU counts (and thus lower starSng inocula), and also explains 
the lower richness. Altogether, these factors may have contributed to the reduced inhibitor 
potenSal of the more diluted Artemia samples.  
==PAGE:48==
 
48 
In this study, Artemia microbiomes (A_A and A_F) were dominated by the genera Cobe6a, 
Exiguobacterium, and Pseudomonas (in decreasing relaSve abundance). Exiguobacterium has 
repeatedly been reported as a dominant member of Artemia microbiomes. Studies reported 
this genus in microbiomes from commercially available Artemia franciscana94, as well as in 
tested Artemia salina nauplii 95. Cobe6a was the most abundant genera in all Artemia 
microbiomes, reaching over 95% relaSve abundance in the free-living Artemia microbiomes. 
While Cobe6a was not frequently reported as one of the most dominant taxa, one study that 
looked at the microbiome composiSon of Artemia sinica under diﬀerent probioSc treatments, 
also found Cobe6a to be the most abundant genus in there no treatment Artemia sample 96.  
While our Artemia sample microbiomes were dominated by Cobe6a, many studies reported 
Vibrio as a dominant genus94,97–99, which was only present in very low abundances in the 
Artemia samples of this study. This low abundance of Vibrio, as well as the general low richness 
recorded in the Artemia samples, could be explained by the bronopol treatment, which has 
been shown as an eﬀecSve sterilizaSon method to reduce Vibrio bacterial counts100. 
RoSfer microbiomes (R_A and R_F) were mostly dominated by Vibrio, Pseudoalteromonas, 
Marinomonas, and Glutamicibacter. Especially the presence of mulSple Vibrio species, both 
non-pathogenic and potenSal pathogenic strains, in the roSfer microbiomes is in accordance 
with other studies that recorded Vibrio to be part of the roSfer core microbiome101–103. 
ComposiSonal variaSons across the samples were further conﬁrmed by disSnct clustering of the 
diﬀerent sample groups (A_A, A_F, R_A, R_F) shown in the NDMS plot, and the staSsScal 
signiﬁcance was determined by the PERMANOVA analysis. This showed that both the host 
(Artemia or roSfer) and the fracSon (akached or free-living) inﬂuence the microbial 
composiSon. Notable microbial composiSon diﬀerences between roSfers and culture tank 
water were previously shown, were almost half of the found core members of the roSfer 
microbiome were signiﬁcantly less abundant in the culture water samples103. Another study, 
while not directly comparing rearing water and zooplankton microbiomes, but rather 
invesSgaSng the eﬀects of diﬀerent enrichment methods on these sample groups, found that 
diﬀerent genera were most abundant in the Artemia nauplii samples compared to rearing water 
==PAGE:49==
 
49 
samples104. This suggests that diﬀerent sample fracSons, host associated and free-living, can 
harbor disSnct microbial composiSons. Zooplankton are known to provide microhabitats that 
are rich in nutrient and carbon sources105, which associated bacteria can beneﬁt from. Some 
bacteria have evolved speciﬁc mechanisms for the akachment to host surfaces. For example, 
metagenomic analysis of zooplankton-associated bacterial communiSes have revealed mulSple 
genes that encode for proteins involved in akachment of the cells to saccharide based surfaces 
like chiSn106, with is major component of crustaceans exoskeleton, including that of Artemia.  
AddiSonally, a shin in the microbial composiSon between pre and post-assay samples was 
observed. Both Flavobacteriaceae and Rhodobacteraceae were dropped from the ten most 
abundant families in the post-assay samples. This observed shin is likely explained by the 
culSvaSon in IOCG media for 33 hour, which favored fast-growing bacteria of other families. 
The agar-based inhibiSon assay showed that isolates idenSﬁed as Cobe6a sp., Vibrio ﬂuvialis, 
and Pseudomonas pu6da were able to inhibit V. anguillarum.  
Cobe6a amphimic6c and Cobe6a marina strains isolated from corals have previously been 
shown to produce secondary metabolites with anSmicrobial acSvity against other Vibrio 
species: Vibrio vulniﬁcus, Vibrio parahaemoly6cus, and Vibrio harveyi107. Therefore, it could be 
speculated that similar metabolites produced by our Cobe6a isolates could have caused the 
observed inhibiSon of V. anguillarum.  
Some Cobe6a strains reportedly are also able to produce biosurfactants capable of interfering 
with the quorum sensing (QS) of Vibrio anguillarum108. The regulaSons of bioﬁlm formaSon, 
toxin producSon, moSlity, and metabolism are inﬂuenced by the QS system, whereas this 
inﬂuence diﬀers depending on the V. anguillarum strain109. This indicates another potenSally 
beneﬁcial trait of Cobe6a in terms of probioSc potenSal.  
Some members of the Pseudomonas genus were previously reported for their probioSc 
potenSal. Pseudomonas ﬂuorescens AH2, for example, showed inhibitory acSvity against V. 
anguillarum when tested under iron limitaSon in vitro and was also successfully tested in 
vivo110. Pseudomonas aeruginosa PA1 is known to produce and secrete pyoverdine, a 
==PAGE:50==
 
50 
ﬂuorescent siderophore, which showed inhibitory eﬀects on the growth of V. splendidus, V. 
parahaemoly6cus, V. alginoly6cus, and V. harveyi111. 
Given the high relaSve abundance of Cobe6a and Vibrio ﬂuvialis in the inhibitory microbiomes 
and their inhibitory potenSal, these taxa likely contribute largely to the observed inhibitory 
eﬀects of the tested microbiomes. Moreover, the interacSon between the microbial community 
members may lead to synergisSc eﬀects and further enhance the inhibitory eﬀect.  
The high inhibitory eﬀect of the invesSgated live feed culture microbiomes indicated the 
potenSal for uSlizaSon as a probioSc treatment for disease prevenSon in aquaculture. However, 
it is crucial to consider the microbial composiSon of these microbiomes. The roSfer 
microbiomes included a variety of Vibrio species, some of which are potenSal pathogens. Vibrio 
ﬂuvialis, which also showed inhibiSon of V. anguillarum in the agar-based inhibiSon assay, is a 
known ﬁsh pathogen and foodborne human pathogen112. Vibrio diabolicus and Vibrio 
mediterranei are pathogens in certain marine mollusks113–116. AddiSonally, Vibrio alginoly6cus 
was isolated from the inhibitory roSfer microbiomes and represents another opportunisSc 
pathogen for a wide range of aquaSc animals and humans112. This underlines the need for 
cauSon when considering natural microbiomes as probioScs and highlights the importance of 
invesSgaSng microbiome composiSon to reassess their probioSc potenSal. Without criScal 
evaluaSon, their applicaSon could introduce not only beneﬁcial microbes but also potenSal 
pathogens.  
Further invesSgaSon is also needed to elucidate the underlying inhibitor mechanisms. Due to
