Abstract
In contrast to conventional energy sources, wind and solar energy plants face intermittent gen­
eration and uncertainty due to their reliance on prevailing weather conditions in their respective
locations, with wide ranging effects for economics and planning of these plants.
This thesis conducts generation simulations for economical analysis of several wind and solar
farms located in Europe. The study considers the high volatility of electricity prices in the Day­
Ahead market, and how this prices affect different onshore technologies and photovoltaic plants.
Within the onshore technologies, the specifications on specific power and hub heights are taken as
main variations between the farms. As methods, the study analyses the power generation of the
VRE facilities as well as the comparison between a more traditional Levelized Cost of Electricity
(LCOE) with novel metric the Cost of Valued Energy (COVE) and Return on Investment (ROI) is
evaluated.
Hence, several generation simulations for the different technologies of these plants are performed
through Correlations of renewable energies tool (CorRES), where time­series generation outputs
are analysed and further used for revenue and profitability assessments, along with the calcula­
tion of the cost and profitability related metrics. For demonstration, a range of cases studies are
performed, including seven distinct technologies and seven locations, utilizing historical data for
validation purposes and future spot prices scenarios for the lifetime assessment of the plants.
This case study reveals a misalignment in investment decisions when utilizing the different prof­
itability metric Return on Investment ROI and standardize used metric LCOE. The introduction of
COVE aims to be utilized as interface metric between the two. The analysis concludes with a direct
alignment between LCOE and ROI assessment in profitability, indicating solar PV as the optimal
investment choice in one main future scenario namely R1, and a direct alignment between COVE
and ROI for a variation of the main scenario R1, where onshore wind farms with Specific factor
199 emerge as the optimal investment. An additional assessment based on technology results in
a slightly better alignment of COVE with ROI ranking tendencies compared to LCOE. However,
the improvement is not significant. Therefore, it is not sufficient to conclusively validate COVE as
a superior metric for profitability assessment.

Introduction
Energy consumption is witnessing a steady and continuous rise, fueled by the growing demand
from various sectors and industries. This upward trend in electricity use has been observed over
the years, signifying a generalized and sustained increase in global energy needs.
In the last century, the exponential growth in energy demand, driven by industrialization and au­
tomation in various sectors, has made electricity an indispensable necessity, becoming a basic
commodity for our society. In response to this growing demand, the world’s energy infrastructure
has undergone substantial expansion, resulting in a significant increase in total installed capac­
ity. At the same time, the energy sources driving this transformation have continuously evolved,
adapting to the changing needs of the modern world.
Renewable energies made their entry into the global power system in 1978 with Wind energy farms
and 1983 with Solar energy plants, and ever since, their production has witnessed a steady and
continuous growth year by year. As of 2021, the share of wind energy reached 2.71%, while solar
energy accounted for 1.53% of the total energy consumption. In contrast, conventional sources like
coal, oil, and gas remained dominant, holding shares of 25.2%, 29%, and 22.88%, respectively, in
the global energy mix. [1]
Figure 1.1: Introduction of VREs in the energy system [1]
Renewable Energy Historical Background
In the 20th century, the environmental movement’s rise and escalating concerns about fossil fuel’s
adverse effects on the planet sparked renewed interest in renewable energy sources. However, it
was during the oil crisis (1970s) that the focus on renewable energy gained even greater signifi­
cance, prompting substantial investments by governmental bodies in research and development.
This newfound emphasis on energy independence spurred the advancement of alternative energy
sources, marking the beginning of the renewable industry and later introduction to our electricity
markets, as can be seen in Fig. 1.2.
In the wake of the Paris Agreement, the relevance of renewable technologies gained further pop­
ularity. Governments worldwide escalated their investments in the development of renewable en­
ergy technologies, with the aim of sidetrack energy­related CO2 emissions and moving towards a
more sustainable energy future. [2, 3]

Despite the challenges renewables faced initially in competing economically against well­established
conventional energy sources, a significant decrease in costs has been witnessed in recent years,
particularly in wind and solar energy, enabling the widespread adoption and expansion of these
technologies.
European Internal Energy Market
Since the liberalization of European power markets in the late 1900s, the need for a market that
could ensure security of supply while fostering competitive competition for generators became
imperative. To address this, the Internal European Energy Market was established, aiming to
provide electricity services to customers effectively.
The European Internal Energy Market encompasses a diverse range of companies, each assigned
distinct roles within the system. These roles include generation organization, where power plants
are managed, and trading bids and offers for energy prices. The responsibility for managing the
supply and demand prices falls under the purview of the Market Operator, while the Transmission
System Operator oversees the physical transmission and distribution of electricity. To effectively
govern and regulate such a complex and extensive organization, regulatory bodies play a piv­
otal role in ensuring that all processes align with the defined framework and adhere to regulatory
guidelines.
The Energy Markets are divided into futures markets, Day­ahead market and Intra­day market.
All of then clear before the actual time of physical delivery in the Balancing market, which ensures
power supply­demand balance. The Day Ahead Market works with bids and offers for the following
day, and serves as a trading platform, where buyers and sellers participate, reflecting supply and
demand for specific time and location slots. Section 2.2 will discuss the mechanisms involved in
the functioning of this market.
Wind and Solar Technological progress: Cost reduction tendencies, support
schemes and increase in investments
In the scope of PV technology, recent developments have been noteworthy. In particular, the
levelized cost of energy (LCOE) has seen a substantial decline, dropping by an impressive 88%
since 2010 and further reducing by 13% compared to 2020.
In addition, the overall increase in capacity factor is another achievement, rising from 13.8% in
2010 to 17.2% in 2021. These improvements can be attributed to the continuous improvements
introduced in PV technologies, which drive higher efficiency and performance.
Similarly, onshore wind technologies have not lagged behind in their progress. They have achieved
an impressive 15% decrease in LCOE compared to 2020, making them a very competitive tech­
nology. In fact, at a staggering 69 GW of installations, onshore wind technology now has a lower
LCOE than the cheapest conventional fuel plant. These cost and efficiency optimizations demon­
strate the growing competence and viability of renewable energy sources in the global energy
landscape. [4]
Focusing mainly on European countries, a total of 17.4 GW of wind energy will be installed in 2021,
of which 11 GW correspond to EU­27 countries. In total, onshore and offshore wind power plants
covered 15% of the energy demand in the same year. Currently 64% of the total installed capacity
in Europe is concentrated in 5 countries, including Spain, France and Germany. With respect to the
former, it is expected that an additional 20 GW of onshore wind capacity will be installed by 2026.
With this data, we are able to reflect the significant progress in wind energy installations projects
across European countries. These is also reflected on the quantities and decisions towards green
technologies. These investments are on the course of reaching 1.55 trillion euros by the end of
2023 globally, surpassing previous 1.3 and 1.1 trillion in 2022 and 2021. [5, 6]

1.1
Motivation
In light of the increasing investment trends and a growing emphasis on cost reduction, companies
find an increasingly attractive proposition to embark on new Variable Renewable Energy (VRE)
projects. These initiatives are anticipated to yield significantly greater benefits for facility owners
and investors, surpassing the wind and solar projects realized to date.
In contrast to conventional energy sources such as nuclear or hydro power, solar and wind energy
plants face intermittent generation and heightened uncertainty due to their reliance on the prevail­
ing weather conditions in their respective locations. Consequently, these renewable energy plants
are exposed to a relatively higher level of risk compared to conventional facilities. Another uncer­
tainty for the creation of new plants is future spot prices. As more renewable capacity is introduced
through new installations and re­powering, prices will presumably decrease, causing these new
plants to reduce their revenue per MWh.
However, despite aforementioned risks, a key driving force behind significant investments in these
technologies is the remarkable drop in costs per unit of energy generated.This cost reduction trend
serves as an incentive for investors to capitalize on the great potential of these renewable sources.
Therefore, assessing different approaches for selecting the best investment decision is going to
be one of the main motivations in this thesis.[8]

1.2
Research Question
Having reviewed the global overview presented in the previous sections, some questions arise.
Given the assumption that Wind and Solar farms are financially feasible and yield profits,
• How does the profitability of these projects vary when modifying the characteristics of the
plants? In particular, which specific technologies demonstrate superior performance in terms
of income?
With this background, the question of whether conventional wind farms thrive exclusively in high­
wind­speed sites or whether they are also profitable in locations with medium and low wind speeds
will also be asked.
• Is LCOE a direct indicator of profitability? and are there other metrics which are more suitable
for assessing profitability?
• Do the wind turbines with higher capacity factor provide the highest revenues and profit?
Added to these questions, and with the statements of the profitability of the renewable source,
• How would the same­scale solar photovoltaic farm perform under the same scenarios?
1.3
Report Outline
Throughout the upcoming chapters, this report will undertake a comprehensive examination and
analysis of the profitability and viability of European onshore wind and photovoltaic plants, taking
into account current prices and cost estimations towards 2050.
• Chapter 1 will provide an overview of the pertinent topics relevant to this study.
• In Chapter 2, the definition of metrics and a discussion of specific related works will be pre­
sented, serving as a foundation for a deeper understanding of the project’s specifications.
• Chapter 3 will encompass the methodology throughout the project, detailing the procedures
implemented to achieve generation output estimations for the plants, as well as comprehen­

2.1
Metric Definition
In the context of VRE plants, this report is based on several key metrics regarding generation and
costs. A concise introduction to these metrics is provided in this section: to assess the simplified
cost required to generate 1 MWh of electricity, the Levelized Cost of Energy (LCOE) serves as
the primary metric. Additionally, another metric with the same units, EUR/MWh, the Cost of Val­
ued Energy (COVE), will be explored in greater detail, encompassing considerations beyond plant
costs. An interface metric called Value Factor (VF) relates the last two. The fundamental costs of
a plant can be categorized into two components: Capital Expenditures (CapEx) and Operational
Expenditures (OpEx). CapEx represents the initial investment made at the project’s inception and
comprises equipment costs, balance of system expenses and financial expenses. This large fig­
ure is of significant importance for wind and solar projects. On the other hand, OpEx includes both
Fixed and Variable operation and maintenance costs, typically calculated on an annual basis.The
Revenues of the plants refer to the gross income from direct participation as generators (sellers) on
the Day Ahead energy market, and the overall profitability to the difference between the expected
total revenues and the overall total costs.
When mentioning total costs or revenues, it refers to the discounted cumulative sum of all relevant
costs or revenues incurred throughout the entire lifetime of the project. A simpler and and useful
metric to assess profitability is the Return on Investment (ROI),as it represents the percentage of
returns or profits generated by a plant over its lifetime compared to the initial costs required to
establish it. A higher ROI indicates a more financially viable and profitable project.
2.2
Market Clearing Price and Volume
Electricity prices can be hard to forecast and tend to experience significant fluctuations. This un­
predictability is due to the fact that energy markets operate under dynamic conditions, influenced
by various factors that impact supply and demand. As a result, prices can vary widely over time.
The bids from generators are aggregated to a supply curve by the market operator, with offers
from the buyers that want to purchase electricity. These offers and bids correspond to a specific
period, and in the case of the Day Ahead Market, this predefined period is of 1 hour. The bids for
generation are then ranked from lower to higher price, and the demand offers from higher to lower.
This method gives the name of the merit order curve. The intersection of the supply and demand
curves gives two results, the market clearing price and the market clearing volume. Renewables
have very low marginal costs, as compared to conventional facilities, the fuel costs are 0, as the
sun and the wind resource does not involve costs. Therefore, they are found at the bottom of
the curve. For the non VRE, the lowest costs are usually allocated to Nuclear. For low (or non­
existing) carbon permit prices, the running costs of a coal plant are typically lower than those of a
combined cycle gas plant (CCGT). For extraordinary load peaks, diesel or gasoline fueled plants

2.3
State of the Art
Integration of Renewable Energies in the Power System
As evident from the Merit Order Curve, the presence of a high penetration of zero marginal cost
variable renewable technologies, usually leads to market clearance at a lower price, in the case
the conventional plants do not alter their capacity volumes. While this may seem advantageous
for these technologies, as they generate revenue without incurring fuel costs, the increasing pen­
etration of such technologies can undervalue their own contributions, leading to what is known as
the ”cannibalization effect”. This phenomenon has been studied in depth, as exemplified by [11],
where a time­series econometric analysis explores the absolute (unit revenues) and relative (value
factors) cannibalization effect of solar and wind technologies, as well as the cross­cannibalization
effects between these technologies. The study uncovered that wind and solar technologies in Cali­
fornia are impacted by this phenomenon. The high penetration of wind energy decreases the value
factor for wind itself, while solar penetration can also decrease the value factor for wind. Similarly,
the value factor for solar energy can be affected by the high penetration of solar and wind energy.
Consequently, understanding the VRE generation within the grid where a plant will operate during
its lifetime is significant from a plant­level perspective. For further investigation, plant allocations
could be explored in different power systems, considering those with high VRE shares and others
with lower penetration, to better comprehend the implications of the cannibalization effect.
Cost of Valued Energy
The conventional approach of average annual generation does not adequately account for the rapid
fluctuations in energy value, particularly when dealing with variable renewable energy sources
(VREs) characterized by dynamic generation patterns. Overlooking the timing of production can
lead to the omission of crucial information regarding the impact of price variations and supply­
demand peaks on the power system. On the contrary, the cost of value energy presents an op­
portunity to achieve more precise cost estimates per MWh by factoring in the influence of price
fluctuations during periods of high supply and demand. This metric would then allow for a more
accurate assessment of the economic viability and true value of energy generation in VRE systems
than LCOE. [12]

Renewable Electricity Contracts (RECs)
In the early stages of renewable energy development, these projects faced challenges in attract­
ing sufficient investment due to higher costs and uncertainties. Recognizing the importance of
transitioning to green and sustainable energy sources, governments implemented subsidies and
financial incentives to support the growth of renewable energy projects. These subsidies aimed
to make renewable energy more economically attractive and viable for investors, enabling the in­
dustry to scale up. Nowadays, the decreasing costs and favorable market conditions have made
renewable energy projects competitive in Europe, even without public financial support. Energy
markets alone can deliver a significant portion of the desired renewable capacity. However, na­
tional support schemes are still utilized to stimulate further investment in renewables, especially
with the European Green Deal’s ambitions.
The most common European RECs in 2021 are the Feed­in Premium (FIP) floating and Contracts
for Difference (CfD). In the past, Feed­in Tariffs (FIT) were the predominant support schemes for re­
newables. Nevertheless, European countries aim to design flexible support schemes that respond
to changing production costs and avoid abrupt changes in order to maintain investor confidence,
and help optimize renewable energy potential and minimize costs for consumers. [7, 13]
Wind Technology in Low Wind Areas
When designing a new wind power plant, it is essential to choose the best technology to suit the
wind regime of the plant. Wind regimes according to the International Electro­technical Commis­
sion (IEC) are classified into four:
• Class I: High wind speed site, mean wind speed of 10 m/s.
• Class II: Medium wind speed site, mean wind speed of 8.5 m/s.
• Class III: Low wind speed site, mean wind speed of 7.5 m/s.
• Class IV­S: Very Low wind site, mean wind speed of 6 m/s or lower.
At present, most of the wind facilities are located in Class I and II areas, leaving the lower wind
speed sites III and S free for new installations. These locations, having a lower average wind
speed, are less desirable for the current turbine designs used in Europe, due to their low Capacity
Factor. The development of low­wind turbines would be beneficial for the realization of these
projects in zones III and S, as by decreasing the cost of this new technology very desirable LCOE
values could be achieved, which would help the investment decision of these projects. [14, 15]
One of the ways in which turbines are currently being optimized for low wind areas is by increasing
the rotor diameter, thus decreasing the Specific Power and increasing the output per MW installed.
With increasing blade length, the initial investment cost also increases, but with the negative CapEx
trend on wind technology, it could become a viable investment option. [7]
A study on the competitiveness of particular wind turbines designed for very low wind regimes has
been conducted in Northern and Central Europe. The study found that these Low Wind Turbines
not only resulted in reduced transmission costs but also generated greater revenue compared
to conventional turbines in areas with strong wind energy penetration at certain periods. This
phenomenon led to increased profitability for low­wind turbines compared to conventional turbines,
due to lower spot prices during these high­wind energy moments. In contrast, the advantage of
the low wind turbine lies in its higher generation during low VRE generation periods, allowing it to
fetch better prices for the electricity it produces. [16]

VRE energy plant simulations ­ CorRES
Correlations in Renewable Energy Sources (CorRES) is a tool created by Department of Wind and
Energy Systems used for the simulation of time series of wind and solar energy generation. Its
main use is in the integration studies of wind and solar systems, but it has applications for both
plant­system level analyses. [17]
For the simulation of solar photovoltaic, pvlib is the selected tool. As input it is possible to intro­
duce different tilt angles, orientation angles and specific selection of inverters and panels. When
running the simulation, the equipment is chosen as default. It uses the ERA5­Land meteorolog­
ical datasets, from which it obtains DNI and DHI. For the wind standard simulations, high­wind
resolution data from Global Wind Atlas is linked to the software, allowing for a more accurate rep­
resentation of the wind resource. As input, CorRES requests the locations with coordinates of
the plants to be simulated, the installed capacity, the efficiency, wind speed shut down and start
speeds, and the selected power curve. From this simulations, we are able to obtain the time­series
energy production of the plants in p.u., as well as time series meteorological data such as hourly
DHI and wind speeds to complete the analysis. [18]
Day Ahead Auction Prices
For spot prices, historical data from the energy charts of Fraunhofer Institut ISE energy charts are
used as a reliable source. The time­series data is given for most countries hourly, and scaled to
UTC time for alignment of time zones. [19]
For the future scenario analysis, the day­ahead electricity prices are derived from the Balmorel
energy system optimization model, as described in REALISE D2.2. [20] The Balmorel Model is
run as part of the REALISE project, and the outputs of the project are the inputs used in this study.
This model enables the study of energy system development towards 2050, specifically focusing
on the northernmost European countries. With its ability to model multiple energy sectors including
power generation, transport, heat, and gas, as well as their couplings (e.g., utilizing heat, gas, and
electric vehicles for power system storage), the Balmorel model provides a reliable framework for
system analysis. The model incorporates various inputs such as projected mean costs of gen­
eration, storage, and heat technologies (onshore and offshore wind, solar PV, fuel, heat pumps,
and electrolyzers), along with market prices like natural gas and CO2 taxes. In conjunction with
the CorRES model, the Balmorel model utilizes these inputs to accurately capture the variability of
variable renewable energy (VRE) technologies. [21]
Utilizing these initial data, the development of electricity demand and generation for the years 2025,
2035, and 2045 is optimized, as well as the expansion of transmission lines between electricity
zones.

5.5
Value Factor ­ VF
To easily see the relationship between the LCOE and COVE, we use the Value Factor (VF), The
value factor reflects the effect of the electricity prices on the unit cost of energy produced. This
metric gives us an easy insight on the over and underestimation of costs when calculating the cost
of electricity.
The VF are visually represented on a heat map. Each scenario has different VF results. However,
they all show similar behaviours. The solar technologies have lowest value factors within the range
of 0.67 to 0.69 consistently for all scenarios, remarking that by taking into consideration the future
expected spot prices, COVE suggests a much higher costs than LCOE, which maintains the solar
technology as the cheapest.
When looking more into details, we notice a decrease on costs with respect to conventional LCOE,
on the French onshore farms, and a slight decrease on cost for the onshore with highest hub height
turbines for South Germany. The Value Factor (VF) is intricately tied to the price ratio observed
in each price zone, signifying that a VF below 1 indicates that the facility generates electricity at
prices lower than the standard price prevailing in that specific location. Consequently, this leads
to a diminution in the overall value of the energy produced by the facility.
Examining the results, we find that, with the exception of onshore wind turbines in regions char­
acterized by high Global Horizontal Irradiance (GTI), such as France and South Germany with
significant solar photovoltaic influence, all the plants generate electricity at prices below their op­
timal spot prices, resulting in a depreciation in the value of the energy generated. A consistent

Revenues: Support Scheme
On the base case, the plants´s income came exclusively from Day Ahead Market participation. In

average value from the awarded prices for onshore wind Farms in 2021 [7]. Ensuring profitability
with certainty involves setting the CfD price higher than the calculated LCOE or COVE. By estab­
lishing a CfD price above the LCOE or COVE, we secure a reliable means of attaining profits and
maximizing profit.
Result
From the lifetime total revenues, we obtain the highest total revenue for the same turbine technol­
ogy and location as in scenario R1. The total amount corresponds to 328.13 MEur. This amount
is greater than any achieved on base scenario R1 and R19, and only slightly lower than the one
obtained in the extreme scenario. The lowest revenue comes from onshore farm in Belgium, with
turbine technology of SP277­HH100. On the contrary to the base cases, this revenue does not
correspond to a solar farm. Regarding the latter, solar farms seem to get a much higher revenue
by taking this support scheme, duplicating and even triplicating the amount, as it is seen in France.
France had a total revenue for direct market participation in R1 of 31,33 MEur and with the CfD,
the plant would make 99.83 MEur.
From a profitability perspective, the plant that receives the largest profit is the expected: onshore
wind farm SP199­HH150 in Belgium. A total lifetime profit of 167 MEur is achieved by taking this
green subsidy, compared to the 52,74 MEur from scenario R19.
When looking at the profitability, the solar plants take the lead with the largest ROI in all locations.
Notably, Northern Germany and Denmark stand out as the most attractive investment sites, offering
a consistent 100% ROI for the six onshore plants.
An intriguing observation is that, except for the highest wind speed location, the least profitable
wind technology is the SP335­HH150. Additionally, onshore plants with taller hub heights exhibit
lower return rates compared to those with HH100 turbines. This suggests that taller hubs may only
be justified for very high wind sites.
Overall, all energy projects get a positive return on investment by utilizing the support scheme,
making it a favorable decision for new plants aiming to start operations.

6
Discussion and Conclusion
6.1
Discussion
One of the limitations encountered during the realization of this projects, was the fact that there was
no way to optimize generation, due to the unpredictability of variable renewable energy generation.
The integration of energy storage could be a promising possibility for large­scale generation, as it
would provide the plants flexibility in their generation and allow the times for electricity injection to
be done at the expected high electricity price timings. Moreover, as a different source of incoming,
the participation for capacity markets would also offer increased revenues.
With regard to the specific locations seen in this report, a more dramatic variation in the onshore
and solar plant portfolio, such as offshore locations or countries with higher GTI like Spain or
Italy, could lead to significant variations in the results. For this project, the choices were limited
to countries where future scenario prices were available, and the cost data from different sources
did not align well with the simulated generation results. Therefore, the choice of study of these
alternative locations was finally excluded.
Regarding the VF results, there is a consistent undervaluation observed for solar technology across
all future scenarios. A valuable addition to this analysis would have been to calculate the Value
Factor for historical scenarios as well, to determine if this undervaluation of the technology is also
present in past data.
Finally, a notable observation from the results is the limited impact of electricity prices on the COVE
when using the pr methodology with normalized future electricity prices. When calculating pr using
the price­demand methodology with historical data, the linear correlation reveals that the relation­
ship is not precisely one­to­one with the normalized spot prices. The COVE calculations based on
this pr methodology could potentially offer valuable insights into whether the price ratio model aligns
closely with Return on Investment (ROI) tendencies. However, due to data access limitations for
future scenario demand and Variable Renewable Generation, as well as missing historical data for
certain countries, the pr price­demand modelling was excluded from the scope of this study.
6.2
Conclusion
To conclude the realization of this Thesis, the questions aimed to get answers to are answered
accordingly to the results obtained.
This thesis addresses research questions on the profitability of VRE, using seven locations with
different meteorological conditions and within seven different technologies, six onshore wind tur­
bines, and solar photovoltaic technology. In the Preliminary Case Section 5.1, the data given for
future scenarios analyses are validated against historical data. The utilized are the hourly elec­
tricity spot prices and hourly generation. The results obtained from the 5­year validation confirm a
strong correlation between the overall revenue from historical spot prices and the future expected
spot prices. The Base Case uses the future electricity prices as foundation to assess the 25 year
lifetime of the 49 plants.
Thanks to these results, we were able to conclude that a good agreement between the technology
and the location of the facilities plays a key role in the overall success of the project, as the varia­
tions in profit are not subtle. With regard to the correct interpretation and utilization of the metrics,
we conclude that the LCOE does give a good insight on whether the VRE project will be successful
and provide income, by choosing the lower side values from the same technology, but the lowest
LCOE does not imply the highest profits, and vice versa. The alternative profitability metrics used
Simulation and economic analysis of VRE plants
53
==PAGE:62==
in this project are ROI and COVE. ROI is valuable in project assessment as it indicates positive
profits. However, its context dependence requires consideration of income and cost expectations,
and its profit range is not defined, making it insufficient as a sole basis for VRE project profitability
assessment. Notably, the calculation complexity of ROI involves price of electricity expectations,
which may not always be available or reliable for investors’ decisions. On the other hand, LCOE is
simpler to assess at the plant level, relying solely on plant­specific cost and generation estimates,
not the entire system. COVE is evaluated similarly to LCOE, with lowest and highest LCOE values
corresponding to lowest and highest COVE values in each scenario. Although in future scenario
R4, COVE R4 exhibits a direct correlation with ROI’s R4 choice for the best plant in terms of prof­
itability, this alone is insufficient to conclusively establish COVE as a superior metric for profitability
assessment. It is important to note that LCOE also demonstrates a direct relationship with ROI
R1, unlike COVE R1. This indicates that both the COVE and the LCOE metrics possess certain
correlations with ROI decisions in different scenarios, necessitating further investigation to deter­
mine their overall effectiveness in measuring profitability assessments. Nevertheless, it highlights
a necessity for transitioning from solely relying on LCOE to additional metrics that offer better align­
ment with the overall plant profitability. Incorporating such metrics will enable more accurate and
comprehensive assessments, facilitating informed investment decisions, and maximizing returns
for VRE plants.
Regarding the relationship between high energy output of the technologies and high acquisition
of revenues and profits, the SP199­HH150 technology exhibits the highest overall AEP in most
locations, together with the highest income, but its profitability is not straightforward. For scenarios
R4 and R19, maximum profits are observed in the plant located in Belgium. However, in France,
the same technology results in the largest negative profit among all onshore and solar facilities.
Similarly, locations with similar meteorological conditions like South German and Polish facilities
also show significant negative losses. Therefore, the lower specific power turbine with hub height
150 would not be a suitable investment for locations with low and very low wind sites, but it proves
to be worthwhile in medium and high wind sites.
6.3
Future Work
Observing the influence of different price scenarios on revenues and profitability assessments, con­
ducting analysis for additional future scenarios would give a broader overview of their impact on
the assessments. Additionally, the incorporation of alternative support schemes within the studied
scenarios would also contribute towards a richer understanding of the role these support schemes
play in future power plants. A second open point of this thesis report would be a potential opti­
mization for COVE calculation, as it could offer a more significant impact metrics for investment
assessment.
To finalize, the incorporation of Low Wind Turbine technology as well as offshore wind turbines
inside the analysis would be valuable of compare the impact of this technologies on very high and
very low wind locations.
