Abstract
The energy consumption and CO2 emissions from the building and construction sector have recently
reached an all-time high. The operational energy demand for heating, cooling, lighting, and equipment in
residential and non-residential buildings accounted for a staggering 30% of the total energy consumption
in 2021. Thus, the development of high-performance buildings with sustainable building designs and
operational solutions should be of great focus to limit and prevent further global warming and climate
change.
Bovieran Danmark is a Danish company that designs and builds senior homes. The vision of Bovieran is
to create an environment where community and safety meet a climate that contributes to the well-being
of its residents. The design includes a large, heated atrium serving as a winter garden with plants and
trees, combining the health beneﬁts of a senior housing community and nature by offering a common
semi-outdoor space. However, the heated atrium poses an issue, as the energy consumption used to heat
the large space is very high.
The aim of this thesis was to identify and evaluate the energy savings potential of different design and
operation parameters to optimise the energy performance while also ensuring a satisfying thermal com-
fort of the large, heated atrium in the Bovieran design. The atrium was heated solely by mechanical
ventilation and had a total yearly heating consumption of about 184,000 kWh from September 2021 to
August 2022.
Through a literature review, different design and operation parameters affecting the building performance
of the atrium were identiﬁed. This included orientation, U-value, building geometry, heating setpoints,
and schedules. A survey was used to identify occupant behaviour and gather information on the residents’
perception of the atrium. A post-occupancy evaluation was performed based on logged data on the en-
ergy performance and indoor climate of the atrium to create a representative building energy model of the
atrium using building energy modelling software IDA Indoor Climate and Energy. The building energy
model was used to perform building performance simulations to identify the energy savings potential of
the different design and operation parameters. Additionally, the indoor climate of the atrium was evalu-
ated relative to the simulated thermal comfort by means of the number and share of comfort hours within
the period of use. The different parameters were ultimately evaluated in a comparative study, including
the energy savings potential, the thermal comfort, and a combined cost per comfort hour variable.
The study showed signiﬁcant potential for optimisation of both the energy performance and thermal com-
fort of the atrium. The case with the highest energy saving potential decreased the heating consumption
by around 105,500 kWh, corresponding to a 57% decrease. In contrast, the best-performing thermal com-
fort case had an increase of 1,247 comfort hours, corresponding to a 50% increase, while also increasing
the share of comfort hours within the period of use from 44% to 88%. To ensure the optimisation of both
the energy performance and thermal comfort of the atrium, the best performing design parameters could
be combined with the best performing operation parameters.

1 Introduction
1.1 Background
The energy consumption and CO2 emissions from the building and construction sector reached an all-
time high in 2021 despite an increase in energy-efﬁciency investment and lower energy intensity. The
immense increase in global gross ﬂoor area the past years has outpaced the otherwise huge increase of
investment in building energy efﬁciency which has lead to the increase of energy consumption and CO2
emissions.
The Paris Agreement is a legally binding international treaty on climate change adopted by the United
Nations, UN, at the Climate Change Conference in Paris in 2015 (COP21). The goal of the treaty is to
combat climate change by substantially reducing the emission of greenhouse gasses such as CO2 in order
to limit global warming by ensuring a global temperature rise of below 2°C, preferable 1.5°C, above pre-
industrial levels. To achieve this long-term goal, the UN aim to reach the global peaking of greenhouse
gas emission as soon as possible to achieve a climate neutral world by 2050.
According to the 2022 Global Status Report for Buildings and Construction released at the latest Climate
Change Conference in Egypt, COP27, the building and construction sector accounted for 34% of the
total ﬁnal energy consumption in 2021 and about 37% of the energy and process-related CO2 emissions.
Out of the 34%, the operational energy demand for heating, cooling, lighting, and equipment in residen-
tial and non-residential buildings covered 30%. The operational demand increased with 4% from 2020
to 2021, being the largest increase in the past 10 years. As a result of this, the CO2 emission, related
to the operational energy demand, also increased by about 5%, accounting for 27% out of the 37% en-
ergy and process-related CO2 emissions in the building and construction sector. Therefore, the building
performance of existing and new buildings must be improved and optimised to reduce the energy con-
sumption and CO2 emissions in order for the UN to be able to fulﬁll the Paris Agreement. To do so,
high-performance buildings characterised by sustainable and resilient design, satisfying indoor climate,
zero or nearly zero greenhouse gas emission and energy-efﬁcient design and performance are needed.
While high-performance buildings are required in order to limit and prevent further global warming
and climate change, more homes to live in are also in demand due to an increasing world population.
The world population is expected to increase by nearly 2 billion people by 2050 and thereby reach
a projected population of 9.7 billion. Thus, the population will be almost four times larger than the
estimated population in 1950, just 100 years earlier.
Especially, the demand for homes that address to elderly is rising as the number of elderly will increase
signiﬁcantly in the coming years while people will also live longer [1]. From 2011 to 2020, the number
of Danish people over 65 years old increased by 24% from 934,000 to 1,156,000 people according to
the Danish Ministry of Health. A large rise in the number of 70-74 years old is mainly due to the large
post-war generations, as a result of high birth rates in the years around the end of the Second World War,
as well as the general increase in life expectancy. According to Statistics Denmark, the percentage of

==PAGE:14==
citizens over 65 years old will continue to increase towards 2050 with a total of 14% [1]. In 2040, that
will mean that every fourth Danish person will be over 60 years old [2].
Therefore, the demand for residences that address to elderly, their needs and behaviour also commonly
known as senior homes is rising. The term senior home can be used to describe different types of resi-
dences addressed to the elderly. Senior homes typically include easy accessibility and low maintenance.
However, many senior homes are also listed as senior housing communities with an increased focus on
community.
Senior housing communities are very relevant as loneliness amongst the elderly can lead to mental as well
as physical health consequences such as depression, stress, high blood pressure and even cardiovascular
diseases [1]. According to the Danish National Health Proﬁle 2017, 4.7% and 6% of Danish men and
women, respectively, over 65 years old felt unwanted, and in 2018, approximately 1/3 of all deaths due
to suicide was committed by people over 65 years [1].
Studies show that living in a senior housing community with others can have a positive effect on the
quality of life and thus the health and well-being of a person as it remedies loneliness [2]. A new report
from COWI carried out for Realdania also estimates that every fourth citizen between the ages of 50 and
75 years has expressed a genuine interest in living in a senior housing community [3] [2].
However, people are not only just looking for somewhere to live, they are also looking for a home that
is sustainable, safe, and healthy. This comes as Danish people spend in average 80 to 90% of their
life indoors. Therefore, in order to provide the best prerequisites for health and well-being, a healthy
and comfortable indoor climate is crucial [4]. Poor air quality, uncomfortable temperatures, displeasing
daylight or bad acoustics can cause discomfort and lead to fatigue, headache, and allergic symptoms,
and in the worst case more serious diseases such as pulmonary and cardiovascular diseases and cancer.
Particle pollution and moisture are some of the factors that have the most adverse effects on the indoor
climate [5].
Moreover, it has also been a common assumption for centuries that visual contact with plants or other
natural elements might stimulate psychological well-being. Many studies indicate that contact with
nature generates extensive psycho-social, cognitive, and physical health and well-being beneﬁts such as
improved attention, lower stress, and better mood [6].

eration parameters in order to optimise the energy performance while also ensuring a satisfying thermal

In order to achieve this, the following objectives and research questions were examined.
1. Perform a literature review to identify design and operation parameters that affect the building perfor-
mance of the atrium.
What design and operation parameters can optimise the energy performance while also ensuring a satis-

2. Perform a survey to identify occupant behavior and perception of the atrium.

4. Perform building performance simulations to identify the energy saving potential of the identiﬁed

Is it possible to establish a comparative evaluation method to compare the effect of the different parame-

2 Literature Review
2.1 Building Modelling and Building Performance Simulation
Building Information Modelling (BIM) is commonly used in the Architecture, Engineering, and Con-
struction (AEC) industry to perform holistic design processes. BIM can facilitate design projects from
the design phase to the operation phase, including the construction phase. It is used to create and manage
data to optimise the design process and project. By using BIM, it is possible to create a BIM model that
is a digital representation of the building in question [7] [8].
However, in recent years, a new method focused on the energy performance of buildings, also known
as Building Energy Modelling (BEM), has gained considerable interest in the AEC industry. BEM
is a computer-based mathematical model created based on fundamental physical principles and sound
engineering practice. Where BIM is generic and uses a holistic approach, BEM is dynamic and is used
to simulate building performance. The building performance simulation allows for a better understanding
of the relationship between the design and the performance [9].
The AEC industry uses BEM to evaluate and optimise the energy performance and the indoor climate
of new buildings and retroﬁt designs. A building’s design, operation settings, and control strategies can
be optimised based on the simulations. BEM is also used to validate, and document buildings’ energy
performance and indoor climate in compliance with regulations, building codes, and certiﬁcations [9].
A BIM model includes data on the 3D geometry of the building, building constructions and material prop-
erties, types of spaces, and types of HVAC systems. However, as BEM is used for Building Performance
Simulation (BPS), a BEM model also needs inputs on the heat loads, including schedules, component
efﬁciencies, operation settings, and control strategies for the HVAC systems, as well as deﬁned thermal
zones, to perform the energy performance simulation. These inputs are combined with information about
the location in question and the local weather. Based on this, the BEM software calculates thermal loads,
system responses to those loads, and the resulting energy consumption along with related metrics of the
indoor climate [9].
BEM can give quantitative insight into the energy performance and indoor climate (thermal, lighting,
and acoustic). Information on this can be used to evaluate variables of interest that can be relevant for the
design, construction, or operation phases. Thereby, BEM can be used for new and already established
designs. However, recent studies continue to highlight that there are still often signiﬁcant discrepancies
between the predicted simulated building performance and the subsequent measured building perfor-
mance [10].
2.1.1 Energy Performance Gap
The difference between predicted and measured energy performance is known as the energy performance
gap. For example, a study performed between 1995 and 2002 revealed that the measured energy con-
sumption was often twice as much as predicted. However, more recent studies have suggested that the
measured energy consumption can be up to 5 to 10 times higher than the predicted [10].
6
==PAGE:19==
There are multiple possible reasons for this discrepancy; some are related to the design phase, some to
the construction phase, and some to the operation phase. In the design phase, one of the main causes of
discrepancy between the simulated and the measured building performance is inadequacy arising from
incorrect assumptions and simpliﬁcations of the model. A lack of information or knowledge of the
building design, operation, settings, or use imposes beliefs that may deviate from the actual situation and
not correctly reﬂect the in-use performance and, thus, cause inaccuracy.
As for simpliﬁcations, it is essential to determine the appropriate level of detail to ensure a valid model
while avoiding unnecessarily time-consuming modelling and simulation. The level of detail can be de-
termined by performing sensitivity analyses. However, discrepancies might still occur after the design
phase. Some of the main causes of discrepancy in the construction phase occur when the building, or
parts of it, is not built as speciﬁed in the design phase or when the quality of work is not satisfactory
or sufﬁcient. Additionally, discrepancies may also occur in the operation phase. For example, a lack of
monitoring and feedback after taking the building into use can cause wrongful or inefﬁcient settings and
controls of the technical services not to be identiﬁed and modiﬁed. Inappropriate user behaviour can also
lead to discrepancies as well as if the use of the building is changed.
Several measures are feasible to reduce the energy performance gap between the simulated and measured
energy performance of a building. First, an integrated design process could eliminate discrepancies in
all three phases: design, construction, and operation. An integrated design process refers to a high-
performance, holistic process where the design of a product or construction is done in close collaboration
and with consideration for the other disciplines from the start of the design phase [11]. This would
include close collaboration between architects, engineers, contractors, facility managers, and possibly
occupants. Through ongoing communication among the involved parties, discrepancies and uncertainties
could be corrected.
Furthermore, the energy performance gap could also be reduced by using sensors and meters in buildings
to monitor and log the actual building performance after taking the building into use. This is also known
as Post-Occupancy Evaluation, POE [12]. The logged data could then be used as feedback for corrections.
Hereby, any wrongful or inefﬁcient settings and controls of the technical services could be identiﬁed and
corrected to ensure the most optimal and energy-efﬁcient operation of current designs. Additionally, the
data could also be used to optimise future either identical or similar designs.
2.1.2 Calibration
As previously mentioned, BEM and other BPS software can also be used for already established designs,
either to demonstrate the building performance or to optimise the design and the building performance,
e.g. future identical or similar designs. With information on the built-as design and measurements on
the actual building performance, it is possible to tune a model and the simulated building performance to
match the actual building and the measured building performance. Thus, by calibrating the model, it is
possible to reduce discrepancies and deviations, thereby increasing the accuracy and creating an adequate
model that properly reﬂects the actual building performance. Therefore, the better the calibration, the
better the prediction. However, even after calibration, the simulated and the measured data might still
not match, and any predictions may be inaccurate. Therefore, the model and the simulated building
performance must always be validated.
7
==PAGE:20==
2.1.3 Validation
To validate a building energy model and determine the energy performance gap, the heat transfer co-
efﬁcients of the building envelope of the BEM model and the actual building can be used to compare
the simulated energy performance with the measured energy performance. The energy performance of
a building is strongly related to the heat transfer coefﬁcient of the building envelope, as it indicates the
amount of heat removed and provided to the space and is strongly correlated to the heating demand.
Therefore, the heat transfer coefﬁcient is a very good parameter for validating a building energy model.
2.2 Introduction to Atria
An atrium is a central open space in a building, often extending several stories in height and serving as
a focal point for natural light and ventilation. Atria have been used in architectural design for centuries,
dating back to ancient Rome, where they were a common feature in public buildings and private homes.
In modern architecture, atria are used in various building types, including ofﬁce buildings, hotels, shop-
ping centres, and residential buildings. They are often used as a means of creating a sense of openness
and connectedness within a building and can also serve as a means of natural ventilation and light [13].
Atria have been the subject of much academic research, focusing on their impact on energy consumption,
natural ventilation, and the well-being of building occupants.
One study found that atria can signiﬁcantly reduce energy consumption in buildings by providing natural
daylight and ventilation, reducing the need for artiﬁcial lighting and mechanical cooling. Another study
found that atria can improve indoor air quality by increasing the amount of fresh air circulating in a
building, which can also positively impact the health and well-being of building occupants [14].
Research has also shown that atria can positively impact building occupants’ social interactions and
productivity. For example, one study found that employees in an ofﬁce building with an atrium reported
higher levels of satisfaction and greater communication and collaboration among coworkers [15].
While atria can have many beneﬁts, they also have some potential drawbacks. One potential issue is that
it can be more challenging to control the temperature, which might lead to overheating and a poor indoor
climate. Additionally, the large open spaces in the atria can make it difﬁcult to control sound levels,
which can be a problem in certain types of buildings such as libraries or ofﬁces.
2.2.1 Atrium Geometry
The geometry of an atrium and the spacial relation to its adjacent building is a crucial parameter when
designing an atrium. This is especially the case when the atrium is heated, as the geometrical interre-
lationship between the atrium and its adjacent building dictates the thermal transmission between them.
Therefore, during the heating season, the building contributes a heat gain to the atrium through its adja-
cent surfaces. Apart from thermal transmission, the interrelationship also plays a signiﬁcant role in the
utilisation of heat gain from solar radiation. As atria typically have large glazed surfaces, controlling
the solar heat gain can be challenging, as it can be beneﬁcial during winter as it decreases the heating
demand; however, it can also be disadvantageous due to possible overheating during summer.

In relation to the shape and interrelationship between the atrium and its adjacent building, the shape

the compactness of a building and describes the relationship between the building envelope area and the
volume of a building by a ratio as seen in Figure 2.3. Thus, buildings with lower shape factors have
smaller building envelope areas in proportion to their volume and, thus, lower heat losses during the heat-

through the envelope to maximise energy performance. Danielski et al. [18] investigated the importance
of shape factors in multi-storey apartment buildings with heated atria and found that the atrium should
be designed to minimise the shape factor of the whole building.

2.3 Energy Performance Analyses of Atria
Contrary to regular building standards, Guidance for designing energy-efﬁcient atrium buildings is still in-
adequate. However, some parameters inﬂuencing the energy performance of atria are presented through-
out the literature. For example, Bajracharya [20] states that the energy performance of an atrium is
dependent on the following factors: (i) geographical location and orientation; (ii) ratio of atrium size to
adjacent building size; (iii) type and function of the atrium; (iv) thermal nature of adjacent spaces; (v)
adaptation of passive cooling and heating strategies; (vi) envelope construction and operation hours; and
(vii) temperature setpoint and equipment efﬁciency.
Additionally, a study by Landsberg et al. [21] investigated the design strategies for energy-efﬁcient
atrium spaces. The study analysed the impact of a wide range of design strategies on the energy perfor-
mance of atria by ﬁeld monitoring and BEM. The analysis was divided into two optimisation strategies
being Orientation and Envelope and HVAC Systems. The orientation and envelope optimisation counted
parameters as glass orientation, glass conductance, internal mass, glass shading coefﬁcients, and glass
area, among others, while the HVAC system optimisation counted parameters as thermostatic control,
atrium heat recovery, night ventilation, alternative HVAC systems, and reduced outdoor air quantities,
among others. The study concluded that the energy performance of atria highly depends on the climate
and geographical location, which is why the ﬁnal recommendations are based on four different locations
in the USA with different climates.
Some of the parameters affecting the energy performance of atria found in the literature were used for
optimisation in this study. The parameters were divided into design and operation parameters and are
elaborated in section 3.6 and section 3.7, respectively.

2.4 Indoor Climate of Atria

2.4.1 Thermal Comfort
The American Society of Heating, Refrigerating and Air-Conditioning Engineers, ASHRAE, has stated
thermal comfort as "That condition of mind which expresses satisfaction with the thermal environment
and is assessed by subjective evaluation" [22]. D’ Abriosio et al. [23] classify the main aspects affect-
ing the human perception of thermal comfort into environmental and personal factors. The four main
environmental parameters affecting thermal comfort include air temperature, radiant temperature, air ve-
locity, and relative humidity, while clothing insulation and metabolic heat are the personal parameters.
Thermal comfort has been considered the most important parameter inﬂuencing overall satisfaction with
Indoor Environmental Quality, IEQ [24]. Moreover, explicit performance regression can be noticed due
to thermal discomfort [25]
However, no standards for thermal comfort in atria have yet been produced, so proper evaluation of ther-
mal comfort is not explicitly deﬁned. Consequently, evaluating thermal comfort in atria varies throughout
the literature.

ranean climate with the intention of predicting summer comfort in the space. The simulated results were
analysed, and the thermal comfort was evaluated using the parameter Index of Comfort, Ic, which ex-
pressed the degree of comfort inside the atrium. The index of comfort represented an integration of the
overheated period along the occupied period as seen in Equation 2-1:

of building for warm or cold seasons [27].
A more recent study by Sokkar et al. [28], examining different types of atrium conﬁgurations and opera-
tion strategies in an ofﬁce building, presented a method of comparing the thermal comfort performance
of the simulation cases. The study used the Adaptive Thermal Comfort model, ASHRAE Standard 55
and EN 15251, to evaluate the thermal comfort of each simulation case. The adaptive model can be ap-
plied to naturally ventilated buildings if the following conditions are met: (i) the space in question must
be equipped with operable windows that open to the outdoors and can be readily opened and adjusted

occupants are engaged in near-sedentary physical activities, with metabolic rates ranging from 1.0 to 1.3

2.4.2 Natural Ventilation
The optimum atrium design utilises natural ventilation to reduce energy consumption and is considered
an effective strategy for improving indoor air quality and thermal comfort. Since it mainly depends on the
air movement between the outdoors and the indoors, achieving comfortable thermal conditions requires
higher ventilation rates than improving indoor air quality does [28].
The pressure difference between the outdoor and indoor environments, caused by wind and buoyancy-
driven forces, results in air movement through the atrium. Natural ventilation due to buoyancy-driven
force occurs when the indoor temperature is greater than the outside temperature; however, only if a lower
level inlet opening and a higher outlet opening are present along with heat sources creating temperature
differences between the inside and outside environments. The buoyancy-driven force occurs due to the
pressure difference due to air having different densities at different temperatures. Some of the different
variables that increase natural ventilation are ambient parameters such as outdoor temperature, wind
direction and speed, and solar intensity [14]. Solar radiation is the most considerable factor of discomfort;
however, it also has the main role in creating thermal stratiﬁcation in the atrium. As a ventilation driving
force, wind is important for increasing the stack effect. Wind-driven natural ventilation is especially
effective in areas with relatively high wind speeds, which is why atrium buildings in rural areas would
generally experience better natural ventilation than similar buildings in dense urban areas.
The design of the atrium also plays a signiﬁcant role regarding the efﬁciency of the natural ventila-
tion. Some of the most inﬂuential atrium components and conﬁgurations include opening characteristics,
atrium geometry, roof properties, fenestration properties, and materials. The opening characteristics
include the number of openings, size, position, and location, with the opening size having the most sig-
niﬁcant role. For example, in temperate climates with buoyancy-driven ventilation, the upper openings
should be larger than the lower inlet openings to enhance airﬂow through the atrium. Additionally, the
temperature stratiﬁcation in the atrium decreases signiﬁcantly when increasing the size of the openings.
Ventilation control systems can play a vital role in stabilising the impact of unpredictable and extreme
ambient variables in atria, thus enabling the achievement or maintenance of a desired environmental qual-
ity. One way this can be achieved is by taking advantage of wind in buoyancy-driven natural ventilation
while simultaneously preventing any negative effects on airﬂow patterns in the atrium through intelligent
control for ventilation openings [14].
2.4.3 Humidity and Moisture Emission
In any mechanically ventilated space, the relative humidity, RH, is an important parameter of indoor air
quality, IAQ. The RH of the indoor air is heavily correlated to the comfort of humans as too low RH,
below 25%, can feel uncomfortably dry and cause discomfort in the mucous membranes. At the same
time, too high RH, above 60%, can feel uncomfortably wet [30]. However, no regulations are made
for RH in atria, as these are usually less conditioned and less intended for staying in longer periods.
Furthermore, too high relative humidities may also result in condensation forming on cold surfaces,
which can cause mould, corrosion and moisture-related deterioration.
It is a common practice for atria to have some vegetation to contribute to the connection between the
semi-outdoor space and the outdoors. However, the atrium in Bovieran has a vast amount of plants and a
13
==PAGE:26==
pond contributing to moisture being emitted into the air. Due to the heavy vegetation in the atrium, some
parallels can be drawn to greenhouses that share many similar properties with atria. Ma et al. [31] studied
the greenhouse water evaporation characteristics. The study aimed to ﬁnd the water evaporation through
regression analysis of different indoor environmental factors. The evaporations in the greenhouse com-
prised water evaporation, soil water evaporation, and plant transpiration, with the latter being the most
difﬁcult to measure due to it being a complex physiological process. The factors inﬂuencing soil water
evaporation are meteorological conditions, including solar radiation, air temperature, ground tempera-
ture, relative humidity, and wind speed. According to their experimental measurement data, evaporation
and transpiration were mostly affected by the indoor air temperature, the soil temperature, the indoor
humidity, and the indoor light given as radiation intensity. The study used tomato plants as their case and
found different regressions for the amount of water emitted to the air by evaporation and transpiration by
relational expressions using the average indoor temperature, average indoor relative humidity, average
soil temperature, and average indoor light as radiation intensity. According to the regressions, water
evaporation increases when air temperature, radiation intensity, and soil temperature increases, while
it decreases when the relative humidity of the air increases. The relational expressions can be seen in
Appendix E, as Equation E-1, Equation E-2, and Equation E-3.
2.5 Windows
There is an increasing demand for Nearly Zero (NZEB), zero (ZEB), and plus energy buildings. Elec-
tricity generation contributes to the emission of greenhouse gases, which produces global warming and,
thereby, climate change. A NZEB and a ZEB are deﬁned as buildings with very high energy performance
and, thus, a low energy demand.
Windows account for a large part of the heat gain and heat loss in a building. Therefore, energy-efﬁcient
windows are important to reduce and limit the heating and cooling demand. The thermal glazing proper-
ties of a window are described by the thermal transmission coefﬁcient, U-value [32].
The thermal transmission coefﬁcient of a window, commonly known as just the U-value, is a measure of
how much heat is transmitted through the window. The U-value of a window includes the U-value of the
glazing and the frame, respectively. A U-value of 1 indicates a heat ﬂow of 1 W per square metre surface
area for every degree difference between the two sides of the window, also deﬁned as W/m2K. The lower
the U-value, the better the insulating capacity of the window and the higher the thermal efﬁciency. A low
U-value is always preferable. However, in some cases, it is of higher priority than others [33].
2.6 Orientation
The orientation of a building describes the placement of the building on a site, including the position-
ing of entrances, windows and glazing. Usually, these design decisions are restricted by logistics, the
surroundings of the site, and the view.
However, the orientation of a building, including the placement of windows and glazing, has a signiﬁcant
impact on the building performance and energy efﬁciency of a building. The orientation of a building
inﬂuence daylight illumination, solar heat gains, and air ﬂows from natural ventilation, which determines
the need for electrical light, heating and cooling, and mechanical ventilation. Therefore, the orientation
14
==PAGE:27==
of a building is an important parameter to reduce and limit the energy consumption of a building [34].
It is possible to exploit solar radiation and solar energy to maximise solar heat gain using passive and ac-
tive solar strategies. Passive solar strategies include collecting, storing, distributing, and controlling solar
heat gains using the building components of a building, such as windows, shading devices, roofs, ﬂoors,
and walls. Examples of passive solar strategies are: Implementing large, south-facing windows to max-
imise solar heat gain, implementing building materials that absorb and slowly release heat, manipulating
the building geometry and design to ease natural ventilation, and proper selection of windows and glaz-
ing to facilitate the objective in question. Active solar strategies include capturing and storing solar heat
and energy using mechanical or electrical means such as solar thermal systems and solar photovoltaic
systems [35].

3 Methodology
This thesis was performed as a mix of qualitative and quantitative research methods. The research in-
cluded a literature review, survey conduction, data collection, data processing, and data analysis.
This thesis addressed energy saving potentials of a heated atrium and was performed as a case study.
A literature review and a questionnaire survey were carried out to identify relevant theory and research
as well as key observations. Based on measurements as well as considerable amounts meetings and
workshops with stakeholders*, a data collection and processing were performed to build and calibrate
a BEM model of the case study in order to perform descriptive research of the energy performance of
the atrium. The calibration of the BEM model resulted in an extensive preliminary analysis in order for
the model to perform representative predictions of the energy performance of the atrium. Exploratory
research was performed to predict the effect of different variables on the energy performance of the
atrium. Data analysis was performed to study the relationship between the variables and the energy
performance of the atrium to identify energy saving potentials.

3.1 Building Performance Simulation Software
IDA Indoor Climate and Energy, IDA ICE, is a building energy modelling software. IDA ICE 5.0 beta

indoor climate of the atrium in order to perform the exploratory research.
The IDA ICE software is a state of the art building simulation tool, which is able to perform whole-year
simulations of multi-zone models with detailed thermal indoor climate and energy performance outputs.
The physical models of IDA ICE reﬂect the latest research and are some of the most trusted models
available [36]. Additionally, the software is highly customisable and allows for very speciﬁc needs
in regards to conﬁgurations of ventilation systems, controllers, and other matters. The latter was very
important for the project, as the current operation and settings were to be accurately modelled, which
was done by creating macros and graphical scripts inside of IDA ICE.

3.2.4 Infiltration
Inﬁltration is the unintentional air ﬂow or the air leak of outdoor air into a building. Inﬁltration usually oc-
curs through cracks in the building envelope and through open windows and doors. Thereby, inﬁltration
is related to the air tightness of the building envelope.
In IDA ICE, air leakage can be modelled as depended on the actual wind pressure or as a given ﬁxed in-
and exﬁltration rate. In this case, the air leakage was modelled as depended on the actual wind pressure.
Thereby, air leaks were automatically modelled for all external walls.
In accordance to the the material provided by Bovieran Danmark, the air tightness of the building was
deﬁned as 1 L/s per square metre ﬂoor area at a pressure difference of 50 Pa.
3.2.5 Full Mixing
In the actual building two sensors located in the atrium logged the measured data used for the calibration
of the reference model. One of the sensors was placed in the lower part of the atrium indicating one zone
and the other sensor was placed in the upper part of the atrium indicating a second zone.
20
==PAGE:33==
The atrium obtained a large solar heat gain during summer and a large heat loss during winter due to its
large glazing area. Thus, thermal stratiﬁcation occurred frequently in the atrium due to its large volume,
which complicated and restrained full mixing of the air.
The average temperature difference between the upper and the lower zone in the atrium was 1.5°C with
the highest differences occurring during summer. The maximum temperature difference was 8.4°C.
The supply air was provided through fabric ducts from FabricAir, which were placed just under the
ceiling, one along each side of building A and C, and one along the center of the roof. Each fabric duct
is perforated along the bottom and has several jet outlets to ensure a uniform distribution of fresh air.
Eight manilla fans were installed in between the trusses under the glazed roof to provide mixing of
the air to avoid too large temperature differences between the upper and the lower zone of the atrium.
However, the fans were only set to turn on when the measured temperature difference of the upper and
the lower zone was above 3°C. This temperature difference setpoint was, however, able to be changed by
the board members. In IDA ICE, full mixing in the atrium zone was assumed for the reference model,
as a simpliﬁcation of the manilla fans in the actual atrium. Therefore, an average air temperature of the
upper zone and the lower zone was used to calibrate the model.
Full mixing refers to an assumption, that the air within a volume is evenly distributed, and thus, the
air quality including the temperature, humidity, and pollution is the same everywhere. Therefore, no
thermal stratiﬁcation occurs due to a completely mixed air volume. It is most often preferable and
desirable to avoid thermal stratiﬁcation and obtain full mixing in a space or building to ensure a uniform
and comfortable indoor climate.

3.2.9 Heat Balance
The heat balance of the apartments and the heat balance of the atrium was established as a part of the
calibration of the reference model. The heat balance is representative of the heating season, where
natural ventilation was not used. The heat balance includes both latent heat and sensible heat. A visual
presentation of the heat balances can be seen in Figure 3.9.
Figure 3.9: Illustration of the Heat Balance [38]
Additionally, the heat balance equation of the apartments and the atrium can be seen in Equation 3-1 and
Equation 3-2, respectively.
Heat Balance of the Apartments:
QH,AHU +QH,FloorHeating +QI +QS = QT,Outside +QT,Apartments/Atrium
(3-1)
Heat Balance of the Atrium:
QH,AHU +QI +QS +QT,Apartments/Atrium = QT,Outside +QE
(3-2)
where Q is the symbol for energy, and H stands for heating, I for internal loads, S for solar energy, T for
transmission, and E for evaporation. The left side of the equations indicates the heat gains of the space,
and the right side indicates the heat losses of the space.
Heating
The apartments and the atrium gained heat from heating with the respective heating systems. For the
apartments, this included heating from AHU and ﬂoor heating; for the atrium, this included only heating
from AHU.
Internal Loads
The apartments and the atrium also gained heat from internal loads, including occupants, lighting, and
appliances. The latter two are also referred to as equipment. See subsection 3.2.10 for more details.
27
==PAGE:40==
Solar Energy
The apartments and the atrium gained heat from from solar energy through the glazing in windows and
doors. This included diffuse and direct solar radiation to the space as well as absorbed solar radiation
from the glazing.
Transmission
Additionally, a heat transfer between the apartments and the atrium occurred by transmission through the
shared surfaces. The direction of the heat transfer was dependent on the air temperature of the two spaces.
The space with the highest temperature experienced heat loss, and the space with the lowest temperature
experienced heat gain. Thereby, when the indoor air temperature in the apartments was higher than the
indoor air temperature in the atrium, the apartments gained heat from the atrium, and vice versa. Usually,
the indoor air temperature in the apartments was higher than the indoor air temperature in the atrium.
Both the apartments and the atrium also lost heat to the outside by transmission through the building
envelope. As the building envelope of the atrium was largely just glazing, the heat loss of the atrium
from the building envelope was large.
Evaporation
Furthermore, the atrium also had a heat loss from the evaporation of water in the pond, the plants, and
the irrigation of the plants.

3.2.11 Model Validation
The reference model was calibrated according to measured data of the indoor climate and the energy

was representative.

3.2.12 Climate
Location
Copenhagen, Denmark, was used as the location of the model as this was the city in closest proximity out
of the options available in IDA ICE, though the actual building was located in Ishøj, Denmark. However,
the straight line distance between the two locations is just about 15 km. Thereby, the assumption was
acceptable.
Weather Data
Denmark is located in the Northern Hemisphere and has a temperate climate. The summers are mild to
warm with an average temperature of 16°C, and the winters are cool to cold with an average temperature
of 0.5°C. Denmark has an average of four hours of sun a day with the highest average in the spring and
during summer. Moderate precipitation is evenly distributed over the year, and the main wind direction
is West to Southwest [40].
A custom-made weather ﬁle provided the weather data for the simulations. The weather ﬁle was supposed
to be created based on measurements from a local weather station at A/B Bovieran Ishøj. However, due
to irregular and unrealistic measurements, the weather ﬁle was created based on measurements from a
local weather station at the Technical University of Denmark, DTU, Lyngby, Denmark. The straight line
distance between Lyngby and Ishøj is just about 20 km, so the weather conditions in Lyngby for the spe-
ciﬁc period were very similar to those of Ishøj according to data measured by the Danish Meteorological
Institute, DMI [41].

3.3 Simulation Settings
3.3.1 Simulation Period
The actual building was taken into use in September 2021. Therefore, indoor air temperature, relative
humidity, and energy consumption measurements only date back to then. However, as it is of interest to
simulate the energy consumption for all months and all seasons to gain a complete understanding of the
building performance, the simulation period is set to be from the 1st of September 2021 to the 31st of

3.3.2 Zone Model Fidelity
The zone model ﬁdelity for both the apartments and the atrium was set to energy.
The advanced zone model ﬁdelity climate with stratiﬁcation was tested for the atrium as it considers
the thermal stratiﬁcation effect by including a vertical temperature gradient. However, due to limited
computing power and processor speed, this was not possible.

3.4 Data Processing
3.4.1 Indoor Climate
The indoor air temperature and the relative humidity in the atrium measured by the two sensors located in
the upper and lower zone of the atrium were extracted from NV Embedded, a cloud-based control system
used by WindowMaster [42]. The data was comprised in a Comma Separated Values (CSV) ﬁle. The
ﬁle included information on the date and time [DD-MM-YYYY HH:MM:SS] and the relative humidity
[%] and indoor air temperature [°C] for the upper and lower zone, respectively. The two sensors logged
measured data at the same time every time either the indoor air temperature or the relative humidity
changed within either of the zones. Thus, from the 1st of September 2021 to the 31st of August 2022,
one full year, the sensors logged data 193,362 times. As the data were not logged periodically, some
hours had signiﬁcantly more measurements logged than others.
The indoor climate data set was processed to join it with the outdoor conditions data set. The indoor
climate data set was ﬁltered according to the date and time to contain only one measurement per hour
per day. The changes in the measured indoor air temperature and relative humidity within the same
hour were found to be of insigniﬁcant size. Therefore, it was assumed that the ﬁrst measurement within
each hour was representative of the indoor climate conditions within that hour. Thus, the data set was
ﬁltered by retaining the data of the ﬁrst measurement within each hour and neglecting all the following
measurements for that same hour. However, as the measured data were only logged when a change in
the indoor air temperature or relative humidity occurred, the hours that did not experience any changes
were not represented. To account for this, the measured data for the previous hour on the same day were
duplicated to represent the hour without data. In addition, in cases where the ﬁrst hour of the day was
missing, the measured data for the following hour was duplicated.
3.4.2 Outdoor Conditions
The outdoor air temperature and the relative humidity measured by the local weather station at DTU
were also extracted and comprised into a CSV ﬁle. The measured data were logged periodically with one
measurement per hour. The ﬁle included hourly measurements of outdoor air temperature [°C], relative
humidity [%], average wind direction [°], average wind speed [m/s], direct normal radiation [W/m2], and
diffuse horizontal radiation [W/m2].
3.5 Evaluation
This study was performed to optimise the energy efﬁciency of the atrium while ensuring a healthy and
comfortable indoor climate. Cases with either different design or operation parameters were set up
and tested. To evaluate the building performance of each of the cases, two key performance indicators,
KPIs, were established, one targeting the energy performance and one targeting the indoor climate. Both
the different design and operation parameters, as well as the two KPIs, were determined based on the
literature review and the post-occupancy evaluation.
36
==PAGE:49==
3.5.1 Energy Performance
With logged measurements of the energy consumption for space heating of the atrium, this was chosen
as the energy performance KPI. The optimisation of the design and the operation was done to lower the
energy consumption of the atrium, mainly consisting of space heating. As the only means of heating the
atrium was through the AHU, the evaluation of the energy performance for the different cases was based
on a comparison of the simulated heating consumption of the AHU for the atrium. To a high degree,
the fan energy remained the same from case to case. Therefore, this was not included in the heating
consumption for the AHU. Additionally, the heating consumption of the presented results was rounded
to the nearest 500 kWh to ease the comparison process.
Cost
To provide an additional understanding of the energy performance and the saving potentials of the atrium,
the operational cost for heating of the atrium was included in the study. The electricity price assumed for
the operational cost was 3.4 DKK/kWh. The electricity price was determined as the average electricity
price of the average electricity price for the 4th quarter of 2021 and the 1st, 2nd, and 3rd quarter of 2022
[43]. Though, the electricity prices are ever changing, the relationship between the operational cost and
heating consumption is linear.
3.5.2 Indoor Climate
There are no Danish standards or Danish legislation that states any requirements for the indoor climate
of an atrium, including thermal comfort, air quality, view, acoustics, humidity or daylight conditions. All
parameters are important to provide the best prerequisites for a healthy and comfortable indoor climate.
However, only thermal comfort was examined to evaluate the indoor climate in the following analysis.
As documented in subsection 3.2.9, the moisture emission and humidity in the atrium were essential to
the calibration of the reference model. However, as the moisture emission and humidity of a space are
very dependent on the temperature in that space, the accuracy of the simulated building performance was
expected to be lower when changing and varying the return air temperature setpoint, as it would cause
a high uncertainty. Therefore, the humidity conditions of the atrium were not included in the following
analysis but were only used for calibration of the reference model. In addition, the CO2 levels in the
atrium were not included in the evaluation of the indoor climate in the atrium. According to BR18, the
CO2 level of a space should not exceed 1,000 ppm [44], and the average CO2 level in the atrium was
about 400 ppm (see subsection 4.1.2). Therefore, as the CO2 level in the atrium did not pose an issue at
any time, it was not evaluated.
Thermal Comfort
The Bovieran is a senior community housing and is designed for elderly people. Additionally, the atrium
is designed for sedentary and light physical exertion. Therefore, as it is quite common for the elderly
to have poor blood circulation due to a decline in physical activity or insufﬁcient diet, obtaining good
thermal comfort in the atrium is important. As described in subsection 2.4.1, thermal comfort is seen as
one of the most important parameters affecting IEQ, which is why it was chosen as the indoor climate
KPI.
37
==PAGE:50==
Comfort Hours
To evaluate the thermal comfort of the atrium for the different cases, the comfort hours parameter was
used as per Sokkar et al. [28]. However, a different range of the operative temperature for comfort hours
was assumed. Contrary to the study by Sokkar et al., the adaptive comfort model was not used as a means
of assessing thermal comfort, as the mean monthly outdoor temperature should be a minimum of 10°C
for it to apply, which was not the case in this study. Additionally, the atrium was heavily conditioned
by mechanical ventilation for most of the year, which does not comply with the adaptive comfort model.
The temperature range was instead determined according to the Danish Building Code 2018, BR18, the
Work Environment in Denmark, and the Danish Energy Saving Plan issued by the Danish Government
in the Fall of 2022.
BR18 states that in rooms where people stay for a longer period of time, it must be ensured that during the
intended use and activity, a healthy and comfortable indoor climate can be maintained. More speciﬁcally,
overheating can be documented with a limited amount of hours above a speciﬁc operative temperature.
For non-residential buildings, a maximum of 100 hours above an operative temperature of 26°C and 25
hours above an operative temperature of 27°C is usually acceptable. For residential buildings, where it
is possible to change your clothing level and open windows to create natural ventilation, a maximum of
100 hours above an operative temperature of 27°C and 25 hours above an operative temperature of 28°C
is usually considered to comply with the provision [45].

more sensitive to temperatures, the lower limit of the operative temperature range of comfort hours in
the atrium was determined as 19°C, and not 18°C as stated by the Work Environment in Denmark.
Thus, the operative temperature range of comfort hours was determined to be 19-27°C. Besides the
number and share of comfort hours, the different cases were also evaluated on the number and share of
hours below 19°C and above 27°C, respectively.

both minimised the heating consumption and maximised the number and share of comfort hours.
Therefore, the heating consumption per comfort hour, or cost per comfort hour, was determined for
each of the different cases. Using this information, it was possible to identify the cases and, thus, the
parameters that were the most energy efﬁcient in regard to comfort, meaning the ones that ensured the
lowest heating consumption per comfort hour.

3.6.1 Orientation
By obtaining a higher solar heat gain through the windows during winter, it is possible to limit the
heating demand and, thereby, reduce the heating consumption. To exploit the radiation from the sun
and maximise the solar heat gain, the large glazing facade of the atrium should face South as the facade
would then receive the most direct solar radiation throughout the day. However, the orientation of the
large glazing facade was not a ﬁxed parameter in the design. Out of 24 buildings in Sweden, 15 of them
were facing North, just 5 of them were facing South, 4 of them were facing East, and none of them was
facing West. As for Denmark, the large glazing facade of all six buildings was facing North. It was not
clear whether the orientation of the buildings was based on logistics, view, solar radiation, or daylight.
Therefore, a parametric study of the orientation of the building, more speciﬁcally, the large glazing
facade, was performed to determine the optimal orientation for minimising the energy consumption.
However, simultaneously, a satisfying and comfortable indoor climate in the atrium was also important
to provide the best prerequisites for stays and activities in the space. Therefore, the parametric study also
considered the indoor climate, more speciﬁcally the indoor operative temperature, in regard to avoiding
or limiting overheating. As there were no speciﬁc requirements or standards for the indoor climate in the
atrium, the energy consumption was weighted higher than the indoor climate, meaning some overheating
would be acceptable.
In total, four simulation cases with different orientations were compared in subsection 4.2.1. The four
orientations chosen were the four cardinal directions being North, South, East, and West to get a broad
comparison. Additionally, the heating consumption for atrium heating was found for all orientations of
the large glazing facade with a 10° interval by doing a parametric run in IDA ICE. The results of the
parametric run can be seen in Figure 4.5.

As stated in subsection 2.2.1 the shape factor needs to be as small as possible to achieve the best energy

3.7 Operation Parameters
From the survey (see Appendix D), it was evident that people generally found it too cold for stays in
the atrium during winter with the current temperature setpoints as presented in Table 3.5. There are two
reasonable options if the residents ﬁnd the temperatures too cold for staying in the atrium. The ﬁrst
option is to lower the heating setpoint even further to an absolute minimum. By lowering the return air
temperature setpoint, it is possible to limit the heating demand during the colder months and, thereby,
reduce the heating consumption. The second option is to raise the heating setpoint and achieve increased
thermal comfort, consequently increasing the heating consumption. Bovieran states on its website that
the minimum temperature in the atrium is 10°C during winter to allow the plants to rest. However, the
boards of each individual Bovieran have the ability to adjust the return air setpoints as they choose. This
results in unresolved estimations of the return air setpoint, with the aim of making a compromise between
the thermal comfort of the atrium and the heating consumption during winter.
Therefore, as a parameter, the return air setpoint of the atrium was tested with the intention of optimising
the operation of the Bovieran design. All simulation cases can be seen in Table 3.10 but will be further
elaborated in the following subsections.
3.7.1 10°C and 20°C Heating Setpoint
As Bovieran Denmark states that the minimum temperature in the atrium is 10°C; this heating setpoint
was used for a whole-year simulation. This was done to determine the operational setting with the lowest
heating consumption for the current design.
Additionally, a 20°C setpoint was used for a whole-year simulation. The 20°C setpoint was chosen with
the expectation that the operative temperature would be above the lower limit of the operative comfort
range being 19°C. This was done to ensure the largest share of comfort hours and to determine the
operational setting with the highest heating consumption for the current design.
The simulated heating consumption of the two simulation cases, referred to as 10°C Setpoint and
20°C Setpoint, can be seen in Table 4.8.
3.7.2 Period of Use
By having varying heating setpoints throughout the colder months, chosen by the board of each Bovieran,
the period of use is not clearly deﬁned. As it was a common opinion that the atrium was too cold for
stays with the original setpoints, it is relevant to explore a more well-deﬁned period of use, which makes
it more transparent for the residents when they can exploit the atrium.
The simulated heating consumption of each month with the 20°C setpoint was analysed to see which
months had the highest heating consumption. The most consuming months in order are December, Jan-
uary, February, and November, as seen in Figure 3.15. Thus, thermal comfort and energy savings could
be compromised by lowering the return air setpoint during the most energy-consuming months. Having
a setpoint below 20°C yields poor thermal comfort and too cold temperatures for stay, while having a
setpoint above 10°C results in excessive heating consumption. Therefore, having an air temperature set-
point anywhere between 10°C and 20°C is unfavourable from a combined thermal comfort and energy
performance point of view. However, by lowering the heating setpoint to 10°C in the coldest months,

3.7.3 Day and Night Schedule
As previously described, the heating consumption was highly correlated to the temperature difference
between the desired indoor and outdoor air temperature. The smaller the temperature difference, the less
energy is needed to heat the air. This principle is what the day and night schedule optimisation was based
upon. As the outdoor air temperatures are generally colder during the night, the return air temperature
was set to 10°C from 21:00 in the evening to 04:00 in the morning and 20°C from 04:00 to 21:00 to allow
for more comfort hours within the occupancy schedule from 06:00 to 21:00. The AHU provides fresh
air at a rate of 15,000 m3/h corresponding to around 1 ACH, which is why in theory all the air should be
changed in one hour, depending on the Air Change Effectiveness (ACE). Therefore, the air temperature
in the atrium should be able to reach 20°C before the occupancy schedule starts at 06:00. The day and
night schedule was applied to the two optimisation cases 7 Month Period of Use and 9 Month Period of
Use, creating two additional cases, 7 Month Period of Use D/N and 9 Month Period of Use D/N. The
schedule was only applied for the period of use with the heating setpoint of 20°C and not the rest period
with the heating setpoint of 10°C. Once more, when evaluating the share of comfort hours, presented
in Table 4.9, for the two optimisation cases, the occupancy schedule does not apply to the rest period,
which is why these months were not accounted for in the total hours in use.

3.7.4 Outdoor Temperature Control
The outdoor temperature control optimisation is built on the same principle as the day and night schedule.
However, instead of having a ﬁxed schedule for lowering the heating setpoint, this optimisation adjusts
the setpoint based on the actual outdoor air temperature. This way, the atrium can be heated to 20°C
when the outdoor air temperature is high enough to avoid excessive heating consumption. If the outdoor
air temperature is too low, the atrium will be heated to the minimum heating setpoint of 10°C. This
was implemented in IDA ICE by substituting the return air schedule with a linear segment controller
component having the ambient air temperature as input. The linear segment controller (marked with a red
square in Figure 3.16) functions as a piece-wise proportional controller and is able to adjust the heating
setpoint based on the outdoor air temperature. In the simulation, an outdoor air temperature below 10°C
resulted in the heating setpoint being 10°C, while an outdoor air temperature above 10°C resulted in the
heating setpoint being 20°C as seen in Figure 3.16. This way, the temperature difference between the
outdoor air temperature and the desired air temperature in the atrium will never exceed 10°C, resulting
in a rather minimal heating consumption while still obtaining good thermal comfort when the outdoor
air temperature allows for it. The optimisation case is referred to as Outdoor Temperature Control, and
the simulation results are presented in subsection 4.3.4
Noteworthy is that the current Bovieran design does not have the piece-wise proportional controller for
controlling the return air setpoint. However, outdoor air temperature sensors are installed at Bovieran
Ishøj, which is why it seems like a reasonable implementation and a possible way of controlling the
setpoints.

4.1.1 Energy Performance

the energy efﬁciency and the energy saving potentials of the atrium were studied.
The simulated yearly heating consumption of the atrium was 183,500 kWh, a difference of 500 kWh a

South, East, and West all had the smallest energy saving potential, together with Enclosed, 9 Month
Period of Use, and 9 Month Period of Use D/N. The by far largest energy saving potential was achieved
with 10°Setpoint.
Most of the cases also concurrently increased the yearly number of comfort hours by a little or a lot,
and thus, lowered the cost per comfort hour. However, Enclosed, Enclosed Moderate U-value, and 10°C
Setpoint reduced the yearly number of comfort hours, either slightly or signiﬁcantly, and 10°C Setpoint
received by far the fewest comfort hours.
Contrary, 7 Month Period of Use and 7 Month Period of Use D/N as well as 9 Month Period of Use and
9 Month Period of Use D/N, all achieved by far the largest yearly number of comfort hours.
Based on the yearly heating consumption and the yearly number of comfort hours, the highest cost per
comfort hour was required with North and Reference. The lowest cost per comfort hour, equivalent to
45% of the highest cost, was achieved with 10°C Setpoint. Enclosed Low U-value and Low U-value
received the second and third lowest cost per comfort hour, equivalent to 51% and 52% of the highest
cost.
Several of the cases minimised the yearly heating consumption and maximised the yearly number of
comfort hours, and thus, achieved a minimal cost per comfort hour. Thereby, these cases included the
parameters with the highest energy saving potential and energy to comfort efﬁciency.
Low U-value and Outdoor Temperature Control ensured both two out of the four lowest yearly heating
consumptions and two out of the four lowest costs per comfort hour as well as two out of the eight highest
yearly numbers of comfort hours.
7 Month Period of Use and 7 Month Period of Use D/N ensured two out of the eight lowest yearly heating

5 Discussion
This chapter critically discusses the methodology used and the ﬁndings of this study. In addition, the
sections comprise critical observations from the study and provide alternative methodologies and recom-
mendations for future research.
5.1 Design and Operation Parameters
This study examined the yearly operating cost of heating the large atrium. 14 out of the 16 cases reduced
the yearly heating consumption and, thus, the annual operating cost. For the cases where only the heating
setpoint or period of use were changed, the capital cost would remain the same. However, some cases
included a signiﬁcant change in design, which would introduce an additional cost. Furthermore, some
of the cases also included changes in heating setpoints and period of use, which would affect the use
patterns of the occupants as well as their yearly cost.
Some cases also increased the number and share of hours above 27°C. However, according to the survey,
the residents rarely or never experienced the temperature in the atrium as too high. The highest number
of additional hours above 27°C was 196 hours. However, the median was just 4 additional hours.
5.1.1 Thermal Transmission Coefficient
Even though the operating cost was signiﬁcantly reduced when using windows with a moderate or low
U-value instead of a high U-value, the capital cost of windows with a moderate or low U-value is also
substantially higher than the capital cost of windows with a high U-value. This is due to the window’s
composition, as windows with a lower U-value include a more expensive gas ﬁlling, glazing type, frame
material or additional glazing. Therefore, the difference in capital cost of the windows must be held
up against the yearly operating cost saving to determine how long it would take to reimburse the cost
difference. With that information, it can be decided whether it would be beneﬁcial to use the better but
also more expensive windows. Additionally, the study showed that the windows in the roof had the most
signiﬁcant effect on the yearly heating consumption due to the large area and the placement. Therefore,
it would be most beneﬁcial to improve the U-value of the windows in the roof.
However, by lowering the U-value, an additional glazing layer is usually necessary, going from a double-
pane window to a triple-pane window. Thereby, the weight of the windows also increases, which would
affect the load-bearing structure, so reinforcement of the structure could become necessary. A reinforce-
ment would also entail a higher capital cost and, most likely, include aesthetic changes to the design,
either by a larger load-bearing structure or more load-bearing structures such as columns. However, in
Bovieran Frederikssund, the windows in the roof were triple pane glazing due to local regulations, and
seemingly, the load-bearing structure was not reinforced. However, this would have to be conﬁrmed with
Bovieran Denmark.

5.1.2 Orientation
As previously stated in subsection 3.6.1, it was not clear what the reason behind the choice of the ori-
entation of the existing buildings, and more speciﬁcally, the large glazing facades in the atria, was. As
for the design parameters, the least invasive and most inexpensive to change was the orientation of the
building, as there is no immediate additional cost to rotating the building. Based on the ﬁndings from
the study, the most beneﬁcial orientation of the large glazing facade was south. However, most of the
Swedish and all of the existing Danish Bovieran are facing north/northeast. Possible reasons for the large
glazing facade not facing south could be the surroundings of the speciﬁc sites. This could include both
the desired view and daylight in the apartments and the atrium, as well as the location, size, and shape of
the site regarding the position of the building, parking lot, driveway, and entrance.
5.1.3 Building Geometry
From the literature review, it was found that the energy performance is dependent on the geometry and
interrelationship between the atrium and its adjacent building. An enclosed atrium was the most optimal
geometrical design regarding energy performance, and therefore, the energy-saving potential of an en-
closed atrium was investigated. However, though the energy performance improved, it also raised some
potential issues for the natural ventilation during summer. The maximum operative temperature in the
atrium increased due to the openable windows in the glazed facade being exchanged with a building,
worsening the natural ventilation. As described in subsection 2.4.2, for natural ventilation to occur, both
a lower-level inlet and a higher-level outlet opening must be present. Therefore, the efﬁciency of the
natural ventilation of the enclosed atrium should be further investigated, e.g. through CFD simulations.
As for the design of the atrium, the survey strongly indicated that the view out of the large glazing
facade was very signiﬁcant for the perception of the quality of the atrium and that if it was removed, the
perception of the quality of the atrium would be very deteriorated. This could indicate that the market
value of a design with an enclosed atrium would be lower than the existing open atrium in the original
Bovieran design. However, the residents could be status quo biased, as their opinion could be skewed
due to their comfort of knowing what they already have.
Furthermore, changing the building geometry of the atrium would also require an additional cost. To
facilitate a new design, new documentation in drawings and calculations would have to be produced.
Therefore, this additional cost should also be evaluated according to the yearly operating cost saving.
The proposed design of an enclosed atrium would, however, also include an additional building, which
could ensure a minimum of another 10-12 apartments. Thereby, an additional proﬁt would also be
included. Furthermore, the yearly operating cost per apartment would also decrease due to the additional
apartments. However, with an enclosed atrium, it would be possible to increase the area of trees and
plants to the entire space of the atrium, as it would be possible to move the common house into the
new building. This would ensure more green space for the residents to enjoy and improve the view of
the apartments placed by the common house. Furthermore, with an enclosed atrium, the orientation of
the building would also not affect the heating consumption. Therefore, an enclosed atrium offers more
ﬂexibility as to the placement of the building on speciﬁc sites.

5.1.4 Heating Setpoint and Period of Use
Changing the heating setpoint of the supply temperature in the atrium would not entail an additional cost.
However, as the atrium has a large volume of around 15,000 m3, it required a lot of heating to obtain good
thermal comfort, as presented in chapter 4. Having to condition such a large space and provide heating
through mechanical ventilation in the colder months comes with excessive heating consumption. As seen
in section 4.3, the heating setpoint signiﬁcantly impacted the heating consumption. Therefore, to reduce
the heating consumption, the heating setpoint was lowered. However, by lowering the heating setpoint,
the number and share of comfort hours were also decreased, which affected how often the atrium was
suitable for stay, and, thus, the use patterns of the occupants.
By the application of Personal Comfort Systems (PCS), the temperature requirements for the whole zone
would be more relaxed [47]. PCS target to condition only the occupied zone of a space, contrary to
conventional HVAC systems, conditioning the whole building volume. Heating PCS includes means like
air sleeves, heated seats, and heat lamps. The survey showed that two-thirds of the residents would stay
more often and for longer periods during winter if heat lamps were installed in the areas where they
stayed the most. As seen in Figure 4.6, the heating consumption was more than halved and reduced by
over 100,000 kWh, with a heating setpoint of 10°C rather than the original heating setpoints. Therefore,
the implementation of PCS and the effect on thermal comfort when having low heating setpoints could
be investigated in future research.
Using an outdoor temperature control, a low heating consumption and cost per comfort hour were ob-
tained, and a high number of comfort hours was achieved. However, as the outdoor temperature varies
a lot throughout the year in Denmark, even during the day, the heating setpoint changed accordingly,
which caused the indoor air temperature in the atrium to vary a lot, too. Thereby, the distribution of
comfort hours during the start and the end of the heating season were quite dispersed and not necessarily
coherent, which would affect the occupants’ use patterns.
On the contrary, deﬁning a period of use by implementing a schedule to the heating system also signiﬁ-
cantly reduced the heating consumption while ensuring a designated period of use where the temperature
was kept constant too. Thereby, signiﬁcant temperature variations in the atrium were avoided. However,
this also meant that a certain period during the coldest months of the year was deliberately not suitable
for stay. By also including a day and night control, the heating consumption was reduced additionally.
Though, it eliminated some of the comfort hours in the early morning and late evening. However, accord-
ing to the survey, most residents used the atrium in the middle of the day and not in the early morning or
late evening. Thus, the effect on the use pattern of the occupants would be limited.
5.2 Limitations of a Case Study
The study of the energy performance and the thermal comfort of atria was tested for a speciﬁc case.
This entails that both design and operation optimisation of the atrium is not necessarily apposite to all
types of atria. Contrary, only directly applicable to the speciﬁc design of Bovieran Ishøj. The tendencies
in the results may only apply to atria with similar building geometry, size, interrelation to adjacent
buildings, internal properties, orientation, and geographical location. This presents limitations regarding
the comparison between other atrium buildings around the world and the ﬁndings of this study.
72
==PAGE:85==
The building performance simulation used a weather ﬁle with data from September 2021 to August 2022.
This was used to calibrate the model, which is why the actual energy performance and thermal comfort
might not directly reﬂect future years.
5.3 Evaluation of Indoor Climate
The method of evaluation used for the indoor climate was based on ﬁndings from the literature review.
However, no regulations nor standards on indoor climate in atria exist. Consequently, the evaluation
method was selected in conformity with the practices used in some of the reviewed literature. Comfort
hours were used to compare the thermal comfort of the different simulation cases. However, the assumed
operative temperature range of the comfort hours was based on non-standardised methods, making the
temperature range of the comfort hours disputable. Thus, future research could include further inves-
tigation of the temperature range of comfort hours. This could include investigating how sensitive the
number and share of comfort hours are to a slightly smaller or wider temperature range and/or time frame,
or whether or not the temperature range of comfort hours should be adjusted according to weather and
season as the thermal clothing insulation (CLO) is usually lower during summer than winter, and thus,
also making the occupants more sensitive to temperature changes.
Future research could also include investigating the perceived thermal comfort of elderly people in atria.
A total of 30 existing Bovierans, six in Denmark and 24 in Sweden, are built, allowing for great poten-
tial for POE and comparative analysis. Furthermore, by conducting surveys on the perceived thermal
comfort in many similar atrium buildings, it would be possible to interpret and compare the responses to
the measured data. That way, qualitative and quantitative research could potentially standardise an ade-
quate method of evaluating thermal comfort in atria, which could potentially include deﬁned temperature
ranges and time frames of comfort hours.
5.4 Comfort Hours vs. Climate Change
As previously stated in section 1.1, the development of high-performance buildings, including sustainable
building design and operational solutions, are required to limit and prevent further global warming and
climate change. With an increasing interest in these types of housing with a large, heated atrium, an
issue of comfort vs. climate arises. How high of a cost per comfort hour is reasonable? Is a high
heating consumption acceptable as long as it ensures an equivalent high thermal comfort? If not, what
should the maximum allowable heating consumption or number of comfort hours be then? Future work
could include a study of the balance between the heating consumption and the number of comfort hours,
which could potentially lead to standards or recommendations of how to facilitate thermal comfort while
limiting the heating consumption.
5.5 Conditions of Plants and Trees
In the optimisation process of the operation of the atrium, the impact of changing the heating setpoints
was investigated. Bovieran stated that the minimum temperature in the atrium was 10°C to allow the
plants to rest, which is why this was assumed to be a reasonable minimum heating setpoint. However,
the length of the rest period was not speciﬁed in terms of the survival of the plants. As the atrium
73
==PAGE:86==
comprised 1,800 plants, details regarding the dormancy of all the individual plants were too extensive a
feature to include in the study. Apart from the length of the rest period, the temperature setpoints also
varied between 10°C and 20°C during the day with the day and night schedule as well as the outdoor
temperature control. The high and frequent temperature ﬂuctuations in the atrium may possibly damage
or harm the plants, as they can be subject to osmotic stress, which disrupts normal cellular activities or
even causes plant death [48]. However, the extent of the pertinence of this concern was examined, as
plant biology on an individual level was too extensive to include in this study.
Lowering the heating setpoint in the atrium signiﬁcantly affected the heating consumption, which is why
utilising other plants could be of interest. Furthermore, using plants suitable for temperate climates,
allowing for the heating setpoint to be lowered even further during winter, could yield a signiﬁcant
decrease in heating consumption. However, this would indubitably mean that the Bovieran design would
abandon its concept of imitating the French Riviera.
5.6 Moisture Emission
To determine the moisture emission in the atrium, certain assumptions were necessary. During visits to
Bovieran Ishøj and Bovieran Frederikssund in the winter months, it was evident that a lot of the residents
were airing out and opening their windows towards the atrium.
This undermines the assumption that all the air supplied to and extracted from the atrium occurred
through the AHU of the atrium. However, it was difﬁcult to predict how big of an impact this had
on the moisture emission, as the individual opening schedules of the windows facing the atrium were
unknown.
This could give reason to exploit alternative methods which are not based on the same assumptions. The
atrium space also included a pond with a relatively large surface area, which is why some parallels can
be drawn to load estimations for a natatorium. The 2011 ASHRAE Handbook on HVAC Applications
states that the rate of evaporation from a pool can be estimated by Equation 5-1:
wp = A
Y ·(pw −pa)·(0.089+0.0782·V)
(5-1)
where wp is the evaporation of water [kg/s], A is pool surface area, Y is latent heat required to change
water to vapour at surface water temperature [kJ/kg], pw is saturation vapour pressure taken at surface
water temperature [kPa], pa is saturation pressure at room air dew point [kPa], and V is air velocity over
water surface [m/s] [49].
The moisture emission used for the simulations was explicitly applicable to the heating setpoints within
the period, as it was calculated using the measured humidity levels and air temperatures. However, as
the moisture emission was only entirely accurate for the reference model, the results of the optimised
models were somewhat skewed.
As presented in subsection 4.1.2, the sensible heat required to obtain humidity levels in the reference
model for it to comply with the measured is of great magnitude. This entails that the accuracy of moisture
load estimation is of great importance. A more accurate way of implementing the moisture emission
74
==PAGE:87==
would be to make instantaneous estimations based on the simulated conditions in the building energy
model. A way of implementing Equation 5-1 into a macro in IDA ICE could be investigated to make
instantaneous moisture estimations in the model. The implementation of Equation 5-1 in IDA ICE would,
however, also require some assumptions as the application of the formula is meant for natatoria. Though,
some similarities can be drawn to the atrium of the Bovieran design. Additionally, the moisture emission
estimation of plants in greenhouses presented in subsection 2.4.3 could be explored further to be able to
make instantaneous estimations in IDA ICE.
EQUA, the developers of IDA ICE, were currently working on releasing an Ice Rinks and Pools extension
to IDA ICE. This extension aims to allow the user to have open ice and water surfaces in a zone. The
model is supposed to account for both mass and heat transfer between the ice or water surface and the
zone. This extension could possibly resolve the issue of having the same moisture emission for both the
reference and optimised models by calculating instantaneous mass and heat transfer during simulation.
As the moisture emission rate relies on many factors, including the temperature of the air as described
in subsection 2.4.3, the moisture emission used when optimising in subsection 4.3.1 was to some degree
inaccurate. This being due to the modelled air temperatures in the atrium being different from the mea-
sured air temperatures on which the emission rates were based on. When lowering the heating setpoint
in subsection 4.3.1, the moisture emission rate would, in reality, be lower than what was used in the
model. As the sensible heat taken from the air, as a result of the moisture emission, was of great magni-
tude, and the moisture emission rate would decrease when lowering the heating setpoint, the true heating
consumption could possibly be even lower than what is presented in subsection 4.3.1. Apart from less
water evaporation, as a result of the lowered air temperatures, the 1800 plants in the atrium would also
go dormant and be able to rest. During colder periods, the plants need less water due to their dormancy,
which is why a reduction in irrigation of the plants in the atrium would occur [50]. The reduced amount
of water supplied to the plants would also lead to less evaporation, meaning less sensible heat is taken
from the air, resulting in even greater energy savings potentials.
As the moisture emission rate proﬁles were only determined for October to March, the proﬁles for the
remaining months were assumed to be the proﬁle of March, as this yielded the highest emission rates.
However, the moisture emission rate would, in reality, to some degree, be higher during the warmer
months. The increased moisture emission during summer would result in more sensible heat being
removed from the atrium air. This suggests that the simulated air and operative temperatures were slightly
higher than what the temperatures would be in reality. As the moisture emissions for the summer months
were unknown, it is difﬁcult to predict how much of an impact this source of error had on the simulated
results.
The accuracy of moisture emission estimations is arguably of great importance for both the simulated
energy performance and indoor climate, which is why this should be a great focus area for future research.

5.7 Measured and Simulated Temperatures
The measured and simulated air temperatures of August, which were the highest temperatures obtained,
can be seen in Figure B.1 and Figure B.2, respectively. The simulated maximum air temperature in the
atrium for the reference model was 33.1°C, while the maximum average between the measured lower and
upper zone was just 29°C. The maximum air temperature in the lower and upper zone was 27.2°C and
32.7°C, respectively. The upper zone was close to the maximum simulated air temperature of 33.1°C.
However, as well-mixed air was assumed in the model, the average air temperature between the two
measured zones is a more appropriate comparison. The average maximum air temperature between the
two measured zones was about 4°C higher than the simulated maximum air temperature. This complied
with the, in section 5.6, mentioned moisture emission in the model being less than what it would be
in reality during the warmer months. This suggests that the simulated maximum air temperatures and
the operative temperatures for the optimised models, to some degree, were higher than what they would
actually be.
The measured maximum air temperatures in the upper and lower zone of the atrium were somewhat
low, considering the large glazed envelope area. This could result from very efﬁcient natural ventilation
during the summer months. The energy report provided by MOE stated that natural ventilation needs to
be able to provide 50 L/(s·m2), corresponding to around 20 ACH. If this was to be true, all air inside the
atrium should, in theory, be changed every 3 minutes when the natural ventilation was the most efﬁcient.
This suggests that the maximum measured air temperatures inside the atrium and the maximum measured
outdoor air temperatures should correlate to a high degree. To get an adequate comparison, the average of
the 25 highest measured average air temperatures of the two zones was compared to the average of the 25
highest measured outdoor air temperatures. A difference of less than 0.1°C was found, which supports the
strong correlation. Both measured averages were around 28.7°C. However, it should be mentioned that
the air temperature inside the atrium varied by about 7°C between the upper and lower zone on average
for the 25 highest measurements. This suggested high stratiﬁcation occurred in the atrium at high indoor
air temperatures. As the occupied zone was at the same height as the lower zone sensor, it was relevant
to investigate the measured air temperatures from this sensor. The average of the 25 highest measured
air temperatures in the lower zone was around 26.5°C, with the highest measured air temperature being
27.3°C. With a large glazed envelope area, higher air temperatures would be expected due to high solar
gain, as seen in Figure 4.1. However, the highest measured air temperatures in the lower zone were
lower than the highest measured outdoor air temperatures, although no mechanical cooling of the space
was provided. This seemed counterintuitive, considering the large solar gain through the glazed envelope.
However, this could be a result of the evapotranspiration of the large number of plants, as they contributed
to a lot of sensible heat being removed from the space. Another reason could be the large thermal mass
in the atrium, which provided passive cooling during the day due to the thermal mass being cooled to
lower temperatures during the night. Having a large thermal mass would result in fewer temperature
ﬂuctuations and could aid in lowering the air temperature during summer by the mean of passive cooling
[51]. The thermal mass implemented in the atrium zone in the building energy model only accounted for
the deck access as presented in subsection 3.2.8. However, concrete columns supporting the deck access,
as well as metal trusses supporting the glazed roof, may also have an impact on the ﬂuctuations of the
simulated atrium air temperatures. Thus, a way of accurately implementing thermal masses as internal
76
==PAGE:89==
masses in the building energy model should be of great importance for future investigations, as this might
yield more accurate simulated air temperatures, along with operative temperatures.
Additionally, ﬁeld measurements of the operative temperature could be carried out using a black globe
temperature sensor. These measurements could then be used to validate the simulated operative temper-
atures, which underlie the evaluation of the thermal comfort in the atrium.
5.8 District Heating Penalisation
Bovieran Ishøj received a penalty of 70,000 DKK from their district heating company for 2022. This
was due to the return water temperatures of the heating coil in the AHU being too high. The district
heating companies charge an annual penalty if the temperature difference between the supply water and
the return water is too small. This happens when the water is not sufﬁciently cooled. This implied that
the heat transfer between the heating coil and the supply air in the atrium was insufﬁcient. As a result,
the district heating was supplied at a temperature of 65°C, and the penalty was charged when the water
was not being cooled by 30°C.
One way of efﬁciently cooling the water supplied by district heating is to increase the heat transfer area of
the heating coil. However, this would require changing the heating coil, which might not be an option in
the existing AHU due to practical reasons. The return water temperature could be a focus area in future
research with the interest of reducing the penalty from the district heating company. The difference
in supply and return temperature of the district heating in the heating coil can be found by making a
graphical script in IDA ICE, which could give detailed insight into the conditions that inﬂuence the heat
transfer efﬁciency.

6 Conclusion
This thesis aimed to identify and evaluate the energy-saving potential of different design and operation
parameters to optimise the energy performance while also ensuring a satisfying thermal comfort of the
large, heated atrium in the Bovieran design.
From the literature review, the following design parameters were identiﬁed as having a signiﬁcant effect
on the energy performance and the thermal comfort of an atrium; the orientation of the atrium, the
thermal transmission coefﬁcient of the building envelope of the atrium, and the building geometry of the
atrium. Furthermore, the following operation parameters were identiﬁed; the heating setpoints and the
schedules of the heating system of the atrium.
Another important ﬁnding from the literature review included the importance of representative building
energy modelling and building performance simulations to minimise the energy performance gap be-
tween the predicted and the actual building performance. This study was performed as a post-occupancy
evaluation which is a valuable tool for reducing the energy performance gap.
The literature review also provided essential knowledge on established comparative evaluation methods,
including a deﬁnition of comfort hours. Therefore, the yearly number of comfort hours was used to
evaluate thermal comfort.
From the logged measured data, it was clear that the heating consumption of the atrium was of great
signiﬁcance. Therefore, the energy performance of the atrium was evaluated according to the yearly
heating consumption.
The survey’s main ﬁndings included that the occupants stayed in the atrium fewer times and for shorter
periods during winter than summer and that the vast majority of the occupants found the temperature
in the atrium too low for stays during winter. However, the survey also stated that the occupants would
stay more times and for longer periods in the atrium if the temperature were higher. In addition, many
of the occupants wished to increase the heating setpoints in the atrium during winter to improve their
experience of the atrium and their perception of its quality.
However, the survey also indicated that the vast majority of the occupants rarely or never found the
temperature in the atrium too high during summer. Therefore, based on the literature review and survey
ﬁndings, the comfort hours were deﬁned by a temperature range of the operative temperature of 19-27°C.
Furthermore, based on the survey, a period of use from 06:00 to 21:00 was assumed. Therefore, only the
share of comfort hours within this period was accounted for.
As for the design of the atrium, the survey strongly indicated that the view out of the large glazing facade
was very signiﬁcant for the perception of the quality of the atrium and that if it was removed, the percep-
tion of the quality of the atrium would be very deteriorated. However, according to the literature review
ﬁndings, an enclosed atrium was the most optimal geometrical design regarding energy performance.
A representative building energy model of the actual building and atrium was created based on provided
documentation and logged measured data of the energy performance and indoor climate.
78
==PAGE:91==
Based on the identiﬁed design and operation parameters with energy-saving potentials, 16 simulation
cases with different parameters and variations of these were investigated. The simulated building perfor-
mance of each of the 16 cases was then compared to the building performance of the reference case and
evaluated regarding the yearly heating consumption, the yearly number and share of comfort hours, and
the cost per comfort hour.
The study showed that the heating setpoint in the atrium had the largest effect on the building performance
and, thus, the largest energy-saving potential of all the parameters investigated.
The study also showed that deﬁning a period of use during the warmer months, where the heating setpoint
was 20°C and a rest period during the coldest months, where the heating setpoint was 10°C, also had one
of the largest energy saving potentials as well as one of the highest energy efﬁciencies regarding the
thermal comfort.
As for design parameters, the study showed that a low U-value of the glazing in the atrium had the highest
energy-saving potential. It also showed that the glazing in the roof had the most signiﬁcant effect on the
heating consumption of the atrium rather than the glazing in the facades. Furthermore, a low U-value
was also very energy efﬁcient, ensuring one of the lowest heating consumption per comfort hour.
Additionally, the study showed that the orientation of the large glazing facade in the atrium had the lowest
impact on the heating consumption. However, of the four cardinal directions, it was evident that south
was the most beneﬁcial orientation. The same energy-saving potential was observed when orienting the
large glazing facade towards the south and enclosing the atrium.
To ensure optimisation of both the yearly heating consumption and the yearly number of comfort hours,
and thus, the cost per comfort hour, the best-performing design parameters could be combined with the
best-performing operation parameters.
