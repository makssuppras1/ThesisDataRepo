Abstract 
In recent years, the attention on microplastic pollution as an environmental pollution problem has 
increased, but more recently nanoplastics have also raised concerns. Micro and nanoplastics are 
found in high concentrations in both freshwater and seawater environments. One of the main 
differences between the two environments is the salinity of seawater, which is why this work sat 
out to investigate salinity’s influence on the agglomeration and uptake of nanoplastics. A short 
termed test design for the uptake and depuration test was based on findings in a literature review 
on micro and nanoplastics. Furthermore, three different plastic types of plastic particles, 
polystyrene (PS), polymethylmethacrylate (PMMA), and polyethylene glycol (PEG) coated gold 
nanoparticles, were found relevant for characterisation and uptake testing.  
DLS characterisations of z-average, polydispersivity index and zeta potentials analysed the 
importance of the surfactant Tween20 and the surface functionalization with a carboxyl acid group 
on the stability of nano polystyrene and PMMA particles over a salinity gradient from 0 ‰ to 32 ‰. 
The non-stabilised and non-functionalized particles of both types showed increasing agglomeration 
with increasing salinities. The addition of surfactant to the particles decreased the agglomeration 
degree. The effect of the carboxyl acid group was particle specific and increased agglomeration of 
PS considerably in contrast to the PMMA. Particles with both surfactant and functionalization were 
very stable and displayed very little agglomeration for all salinities.  
Images taken of copepod Acartia tonsa after 24 hours exposure to fluorescent PS (55nm) particles 
with a confocal microscope verified uptake of nanoplastics particles in the test organism. 
Quantitative detection of uptake and depuration by the very small test organism required low 
detection limits, and therefore for analysing the influences of salinity on uptake a model particle 
with an inner core of gold which allowed for ICP-MS quantification and an outer polymerisation with 
PEG was chosen. Characterisation showed that the particle was unaffected by salinity and time, 
which made it suitable for the baseline study. Fast initial uptakes were seen in all three salinities 
tested (8 ‰, 16 ‰ and 32 ‰). Body burdens were slightly higher in 8 ‰ test setup due to an 
unintentionally higher concentration, but residual body burdens after a 24 hour depuration phase 
were similar in all cases. Salinity alone does not seem to have an effect on uptake, but more tests 
would have to be conducted in order to confirm this.

1. Background 
Plastic pollution in the aquatic environment has become a well-recognized environmental problem 
(C. J. Moore et al. 2001; Derraik 2002; Thompson et al. 2004; Browne, Galloway, and Thompson 
2007; Charles James Moore 2008; Andrady 2011). The scientific focus, especially in the last 
decade, has been on occurrence, fate, and effect of microplastics (MP) (<5mm) mainly in the 
marine environment (Cole et al. 2011; Andrady 2011; Zarfl et al. 2011; Wright et al. 2013; Dekiff et 
al. 2014). As a consequence of the ubiquitous MP pollution and the consecutive weathering as well 
as the emerging use of nanomaterials, nanoplastics (NP) must be present as well. However, there 
is currently no way of quantifying NP pollution (Andrady 2011). NPs are presumably either a result 
of spill of engineered nanoplastics (ENP) (primary source) or a result of disintegration of larger 
plastic debris (secondary source) (Andrady 2011). The disintegration of larger plastic debris into 
MP and NP make plastics available for ingestion for the smaller marine biota such as the primary 
producers and consumers.  
A common denominator for approximately 80 % of marine plastic debris is that it is of land based 
origin (Andrady 2011). The majority of terrestrial plastics found at sea is thought to have been 
transported there by rivers (Lechner et al. 2014), substantiated by very high observed 
concentrations of MP in rivers like for example the Danube (Lechner et al. 2014) and the Thames 
(Morritt et al. 2014). Moreover, recent studies have revealed high quantities of plastic particles in 
lakes within the vicinity of heavily urbanised areas (Faure et al. 2012; Eriksen et al. 2013). One 
apparent difference between freshwater environment and seawater environment is the higher 
salinity in seawater. Salinity is important for the environmental behaviour of nanoplastics as it is 
believed to affect the state and rate of aggregation and agglomeration of colloids in dispersion. 
Higher salinity will result in more agglomeration of NP, and it most likely has an effect on the 
availability for uptake by organisms. Salinity also implies a higher density for seawater compared to 
freshwater, and thus a slightly larger range of plastic particles can stay suspended, which could 
suggest a difference in how the biota is exposed in freshwater and sea water, respectively. A 
literature search on occurrence in freshwater and sea water respectively, revealed no significant 
difference in concentration ranges of plastics found in the two environments. It is therefore 
important to investigate the environmentally-relevant differences affecting fate and behaviour for 
MP and NP between freshwater and sea water, hereunder especially salinity, and what influence 
they might have on fate and effect of NP.  
1.1 Aim 
The overall aim of this study is to investigate the potential uptake of NP particles by aquatic biota 
over a salinity gradient.  
 
In order to do this a literature review was performed on MP, NP and nanoparticles to 
examine fate, behaviour and effects, and to come up with recommendations in regards to 
test designs and applicability of different plastic particles for uptake and depuration tests.  
 
A test was set up to evaluate the behaviour of two types of plastic (polystyrene (PS) and 
polymethylmethacrylate (PMMA)) in different salinities and the influence of surface 
functionalization and/or surfactant on their size distribution.  
 
Lastly, an uptake and depuration test with the marine copepod Acartia tonsa was carried 
out to investigate whether the uptake of a stable suspension of NP would differ in three 
different salinities, and to evaluate on the appropriateness of the model particle.

2 Introduction 
This chapter will provide a background on MP and NP as well as an overview of the state of the art 
within nanoparticles and NP uptake and depuration testing. Moreover, it will include theory of 
stability of suspended NP and bioconcentration principles. 
2.1 Plastic debris  
Plastic is an inexpensive synthetic material with a vast variety of applications. It is strong, versatile, 
lightweight, durable, and corrosion resistant (Ivar do Sul and Costa 2014). As plastic has high 
thermal insulation values (Thompson et al. 2009), and can prove a barrier against oxygen/moisture 
it is very suitable for consumer packaging meant for disposable single-use purposes as for 
example packaging for food and drinks. Single-use packaging accounts for one third of the total 
global plastic production  (Selke 2003; Andrady and Neal 2009), and as a result of poor recycling 
and current littering behaviour (Thompson et al. 2009) plastic constitutes 50-80 % of marine debris 
(Barnes et al. 2009). Riverine transport and surface runoff are the most probable routes of 
transport of plastic debris into the marine environment (Lechner et al. 2014; Sadri and Thompson 
2014). Alternative routes are through sewer overflows and fly-tipping (waste deposed off directly 
from flights into the oceans) (Sadri and Thompson 2014). Additionally, approximately  18 %  of the 
marine plastic debris can be ascribed to wear and tear of fishing gear and unintended disposal of 
plastic material by the fishing industry (Andrady 2011). Some of the most commonly used plastic 
types for packaging are low- and high-density polyethylene (PE), polystyrene (PS), polypropylene 
(PP), polyvinyl chloride (PVC) and polyethylene terephthalate (PET) (Andrady 2011; Ivar do Sul 
and Costa 2014). Because PS is widely used in single-use packaging among a wide range of other 
applications it is a plastic type that is very likely to be found in the environment. The density of 
polystyrene is 1.06 g/cm³. Besides from single-use packaging plastic, PS is used for numerous 
applications that allow them to end up in the environment, eg. consumer products or medicine 
where it ends up in wastewater streams (Fendall and Sewell 2009). Other types of plastics are 
widely used industrially e.g. polymethylmethacrylate (PMMA). PMMA is a clear acrylic plastic 
material known to have the greatest surface hardness of all the thermoplastics, why it is sometimes 
used instead of glass. Among other things it is also used in signs, paints and as the core material 
in plastic optical fiber (Webopedia 2015), which also makes it likely to be found in the environment. 
Cole et al. (2013) found acrylic plastics to be in the top five most frequently found plastics types in 
the English channel. Its density (1.19 g/cm³) is slightly higher than that of PS, and it is also more 
hydrophilic than PS (Phosphorex 2015). Lastly, polyethylene glycol (PEG), a polyether compound, 
is an example of a plastic type that is used in a wide range of applications from industrial 
manufacturing to medicine (Chemindustry 2015). It has a density between 1.12-1.21 g/cm3, and it 
is perfectly soluble in water. PS, PMMA and PEG are all different types of plastics illustrating the 
vast application variety and sources for plastic pollutions. 
2.2 Micro- and nanoplastics 
Plastic debris in the range between  333 µm – 5 mm is commonly referred to as MP (Arthur and 
Baker 2009), although the exact size definition of MP is still under debate and various definitions 
exists (see Figure 1). Literature generally agrees that there are two main sources (Arthur and 
Baker 2009). The primary sources of MP are those intentionally manufactured for either direct or 
indirect use, e.g. for use in consumer products such as facial scrubs or in industrial abrasives 
(Arthur and Baker 2009). Secondary sources, conversely, are a result of weathering and 
disintegration of larger plastic debris (Thompson et al. 2004; Ryan et al. 2009). Factors that will 
==PAGE:11==
3 
 
most likely accelerate the light-initiated oxidative as well as physical degradation process are wave 
action, high surface temperatures and UV-B radiation accelerates, conditions which are all often 
found on beach environments (Arthur and Baker 2009; Andrady 2011). Such conditions will 
ultimately result in a weakening of the plastic, followed by embrittlement and micro-fractions 
(Andrady 2011) generating powdery plastic fragments in various sizes such as MP and NP. In 
water the disintegration of plastic debris can be retarded by lower oxygen concentration and 
temperatures as well as biofouling (Andrady 2011). Fouling by biomass on MP was confirmed by 
Moret-Ferguson et al. (2010) and several studies have suggested that fouling by biofilm on the 
surface of plastic debris can cause negative buoyancy (C. J. Moore et al. 2001; Derraik 2002; 
Thompson et al. 2004; Barnes et al. 2009; Browne, Galloway, and Thompson 2007), thus causing 
even light-density plastics to slowly submerge. Upon sinking, the plastics will be exposed to lower 
temperatures and limited light, which provides poor environment for microorganisms and thus 
negate the fouling and cause the plastic particles to re-emerge up into the surface layers. The 
reversible process of biofouling and the sedimentation of higher density plastics contribute to a 
distribution of plastics throughout the water column exposing both pelagic as well as benthic biota.  
2.2.1 Definitions 
According to the European Commission a nanomaterial is “a natural, incidental or manufactured 
material containing particles, in an unbound state or as an aggregate or as an agglomerate and 
where, for 50% or more of the particles in the number size distribution, one or more external 
dimensions is in the size range 1–100 nm” (Kato, 2012). Currently, no definition exists for NP, but 
in this study NPs are defined as plastic particles with a size < 1 µm. MP, on the other hand, is often 
defined according to Arthur and Baker (2009) as less than 5 mm.  However, the size gap that 
follows this definition is unfortunate (can be seen in Figure 1), which is why a definition for MP of 
1µm-1mm is suggested and used in this study. Often the lower limits of the size defintion have 
been influenced by the lower dection level which often equaled the net size of which it was 
collected.  
2.2.2 Occurrence 
MP have been reported in high concentrations both near remote islands and along shorelines of 
heavily populated areas (Barnes et al. 2009), and even around Antarctica (Zarfl and Matthies 
2010). They have been found throughout the entire water column (Cole et al. 2011), in sediments 
(Dekiff et al. 2014; Claessens et al. 2011), on beaches (Faure et al. 2012), and in the deep-sea 
sediment (Van Cauwenberghe et al. 2013). Very large concentrations are found in gyres, 
especially in the North Pacific gyre (C. J. Moore et al. 2001). Moreover, MP is reported found in 
remote mountain lakes (Free et al. 2014), in the great European rivers and estuaries worldwide 
(Yonkos et al. 2014; Sadri and Thompson 2014). The extent of the MP pollution might indicate the 
state of eventual NP pollution. Literature moreover indicates that there is a similar extend of MP 
pollution in freshwater  (Wegner et al. 2012; Eerkes-Medrano, Thompson, and Aldridge 2015). 
The method of sampling seems to be of great importance when trying to quantify the 
environmental concentrations of MP. Norén (2007) found a large difference in particle 
concentration when using an 80 µm mesh compared to a 450 µm mesh. In fact, the concentrations 
were up to 100,000 times higher with the 80 µm, suggesting that the mesh size is of great 
significance. Furthermore, it underlines the assumption that the smallest fractions are the most 
abundant. This is a strong indication that NP therefore might be present in very large quantities, 
but currently there is no quantification of it.

2.2.3 Environmental concerns 
There are potentially three harmful effects related to MP and NP for marine and freshwater biota 
connected  to ingestion; (1) physical effects related to ingestion similar to those found for 
macroplastics, (2) toxic response from leaching plasticizers and, (3) toxic response to adhered 
pollutions that absorbs after ingestion (Cole et al. 2011).  
Firstly, MP has been shown to be ingested by marine biota mistaking it for natural prey as the 
particles occupies the same range as the vast majority of zooplankton or indiscriminate feeding 
behavior (Cole et al. 2011). NP, however, is a lot smaller than most natural prey (slightly smaller 
than nano- and picoplankton), which may be the reason why marine biota is less likely to mistake 
NP for natural prey. Nonetheless, as NP aggregates easier in seawater than in freshwater the 
probability might be greater in seawater. Alternatively, nanoplastics might enter the cells of biota 
and fauna by means of endocytosis as well as the possibility of passive ingestion in for example 
filter feeders (Ward and Kach 2009; Andrady 2011). Here, the risk is that the nano-sized plastics 
particles will be able to not only enter the cells, but also that it might carry adhered pollutants with it 
into the biota. Wright, Thompson, and Galloway (2013) speculates that ingestion could cause  
blockage of digestive tract and abrasions when ingested by small organisms such as zooplankton. 
Browne et al. (2008) showed translocation of MP within the haemolymph of M. eudlis after 3 days 
of exposure and presence of MP in the circulatory system after 48 days. In another study of MP 
uptake and depuration von Moos, Burkhardt-Holm, and Köhler (2012) studied whether industrial 
high-density PE (HDPE) fluff (non-uniformly shaped grains Gaussian distributed between 0− 80 
μm) were are ingested and translocated into cells and tissue of M. edulis. The HDPE was 
observed to enter both the gills and the digestive gland, which resulted in a strong inflammatory 
response in the mussels. Other effects could be reduced feeding due to satiation and starvation 
(Derraik 2002), however there is still a knowledge gap in regard to chronic effects of MP and NP 
exposure, why the list could easily be longer (Van Cauwenberghe et al. 2013). 
Secondly, the disintegration of plastics may allow plastic additives, also known as plasticizers, to 
leach out of the matrix, which is especially of concern if it happens after ingestion by marine biota 
(Cole et al. 2011). Many of the plasticizers are suspected of being toxic, endocrine-disruptors, 
carcinogenic, to have effects on growth, fecundity, sexual development, as well as morbidity, and 
mortality (Oehlmann et al. 2009; Cole et al. 2013). Beside plasticizers, the toxicity for NP might 
also be related to particle toxicity (Besseling, Wang, Lürling, et al. 2014), and could depend on 
characteristics such as particle size, polymer type, and age of the plastic particles. 
Lastly, another concern regarding MP pollution is the influence it will have on co-contaminants 
such as persistent organic pollutants (POPs) or metals. In recent years, more literature have 
focused on the sorption and desorption processes of organic pollutants on plastics (Teuten et al. 
2007; Teuten et al. 2009; Bakir, Rowland, and Thompson 2014; Lee, Shim, and Kwon 2014; 
Wang, Shih, and Li 2014; Velzeboer, Kwadijk, and Koelmans 2014; Koelmans, Besseling, and 
Foekema 2014). Several studies have shown the potential for a carrier effect of organic pollutants 
by MP (e.g. in Mato et al. 2001; Teuten et al. 2007; Zarfl and Matthies 2010; Arthur and Baker 
2009; Derraik 2002), and yet little is understood about the factors influencing the sorption and 
desorption (Bakir, Rowland, and Thompson 2014). A less investigated topic is the carrier-effects of 
NP. There is therefore a great need to improve the understanding regarding ecotoxicity testing of 
NP.

2.3 Uptake of nanoplastics and nanoparticles 
There is still a lack of scientifically based standards within ecotoxicity testing of manufactured 
nanomaterials, and a prevailing consensus that existing methods and test guidelines need 
modifications in order to work in the case of nanomaterials (Handy, Van Den Brink, et al. 2012). 
Some studies suggest that methods based on substance classifications are the most pragmatic 
solution (Stone, Johnston, and Schins 2009). Whereas other suggests that each test should based 
on consideration of the most important properties of the tested material at hand, hydrophilic or 
hydrophobic and so on, and where properties are alike similar setups should be used (Handy, 
Cornelis, et al. 2012).  
2.3.1 Uptake tests with nanoplastics 
The type of the tested plastic particle is important for the test setup as the physiochemical 
properties differ and affect how the particles behave in aquatic environments. Moreover, it is 
important to have a particle that represent what is actually found in the environment in order to 
mimic the environmental fate and effect of NP most realistically. For plastic the density of the 
particles is significant as it greatly influences where the particles can be found. High-density 
plastics have a greater tendency to settle in contrast to light density plastics, which have an 
influence on which range of aquatic biota the plastics is available for ingestion to. Moreover, 
surfactant, surface functionalization and concentration ranges might also influence the behaviour.

In some of the studies concerning uptake and depuration of nanoplastics, PS particles have been 
used (Wegner et al. 2012; Besseling, Wang, Lu, et al. 2014). Wegner et al. (2012) exposed the 
mussel Mytilus edulis to different concentrations (0, 0.1, 0.2, and 0.3 g/L) of nano PS (30 nm). The 
water was sampled every 30 min and analysed with a UV-VIS spectrophotometer in order to 
determine the actual concentration of nano PS during the bioassay. The measurements showed a 
decreasing concentration of the nano PS in the water but only in the setups with M. edulis present. 
An examination of the particle settling velocity confirmed that the decrease in concentration was 
caused by M. edulis removing the particles and not by settling. In seawater most of the particles 
had aggregated to a size of 1 µm within the first 16 min, and at the end of the bioassay particles 
were found to have a diameter of 3,518 nm (Wegner et al. 2012). Microscopy was used for visual 
detection of the nano PS in the tissue of the mussels. The mussel was observed to have an 
increased production of pseudofeces and to have a reduced filtering activity, which over long-term 
exposure therefore could eventually lead to starvation.  
Besseling, Wang, Lürling, et al. (2014) investigated the ecotoxicological effects at two different 
trophic levels for two freshwater aquatic species of nano PS (70 nm, sodium dodecyl sulphate as 
surfactant), specifically on the green alga Scenedesmus obliquus and freshwater crustacean D. 
magna. Besseling, Wang, Lürling, et al. (2014) chose PS because it is very widely applied 
commercially and moreover because it had already been used in earlier toxicity tests. They tested 
both pristine and aged dispersions of nano PS, in order to determine the influence of the age of the 
particles on the ecotoxicological effects. A mix of the different particles; pristine particles, pristine 
particles with kairomone, aged particles, and aged-filtered particles were used. Besseling, Wang, 
Lürling, et al.  (2014) showed a reduced population growth as well as a reduced chlorophyll 
concentration in the S.obliquus. For the D. magna the results indicated a reduced body size and 
severe modifications in the reproduction. The number and body size of the neonates reduced, and

the occurrence of neonate malformations rose significantly. Effect concentrations were in the range 
between 0.22 and 103 mg nano-PS/L, malfunctions were observed from 30 mg of nano-PS/L and 
up. Besseling, Wang, Lürling, et al. (2014) notes that such concentrations are higher than those 
currently reported for both marine and freshwater, but that they might occur in sediment pore 
waters.  
Nano PS (55 nm) was also used in a study investigating the potential binding capacities onto a thin 
film of cellulose of neutral and negatively charged nanoplastics with a simple fluorescence method 
(Bhattacharya, Chen, et al. 2010). Cellulose, being the primary structural component in cell-walls of 
green plants, several forms of algae and the oomycetes, is therefore very relevant when 
investigating the fate and effect of nanoplastics in the environment. Two different types of nano PS 
were used, one neutrally charged coated with Tween-20 and another negatively charged and 
functionalized with carboxylic groups. Bhattacharya, Chen, et al. (2010) found a medium binding 
affinity onto cellulose for both types of NP. For the neutrally charged particles the adsorption on the 
cellulose film decreased with increasing salinities, which according to Bhattacharya, Chen, et al. 
(2010) could be due to the aggregation of the neutral NP and electrostatic repulsion between the 
negatively charged NP.  
In a similar study, the adsorption onto a model cellulose film and to two types of algae Chlorella 
and Scenedesmus for both positively and negatively charged nano PS (20 nm, amidine and 
carboxyl latex respectively, concentrations between 0.08-0.8 g/L) was found to favour the positively 
charged over negatively charged nano PS (Bhattacharya, Lin, et al. 2010). Bhattacharya, Lin, et al. 
(2010) wanted to imitate natural condition which is why no sonication of the dispersion were 
performed, and so the nano PS formed agglomerates in the dispersions. For the positively charged 
amidine PS an average zeta potential of 106 mV was detected. For the negatively charged 
carboxyl PS the average zeta potential was -40.4 mV. The bioassays found that the nano PS 
adsorbs to the algae and possibly physically block-off the light and air flow, hindering the algal 
photosynthesis, indicating the adsorption on the algae promoted ROS production.

2.3.2 Uptake and depuration of surface functionalised gold nanoparticles 
Alternatively, other nano particles with a surface polymerisation can be used as a model particle. 
Skjolding, Kern, et al. (2014) used gold nanoparticles as they are easily quantified at low detection 
limits both chemically and by transmission electron microscopy (TEM). It is a rather inert material 
and will have a limited dissolution in water. Moreover a well-defined synthesis allows gold 
nanoparticles in different sizes and with different functional groups to be produced. For uptake 
studies low background concentrations in the aquatic environment as low toxicity also plays a role. 
Skjolding, Kern, et al.(2014) investigated uptake and depuration by the freshwater crustacean D. 
magna of gold nanoparticles, and the effect of size (10 nm and 30 nm), dispersant (citrate (CIT) 
and mercartoundecanoic (MUDA)) as well as the effect of feeding. They showed a fast initial 
uptake for all the tested particles, with fastest rates observed for the 10 nm MUDA particles. A 24 
hour depuration in clean media showed no clear trend of depuration, however higher residual body 
burdens were observed for both types of 10 nm particles compared to the 30 nm particles. The 
addition of food did not impact the body burdens significantly but the depuration was observed to 
increase, which Skjolding, Kern, et al. (2014) notes as an important finding for long term tests 
where the addition of food is a requirement for the survival of the test animals. Moreover in a 
preliminary test it was found that the uptake and uptake rates did not statistically differ for test 
organisms exposed over 24, 48 or 72 hours.

sum of energy made up of the potential energy contributed to by the solvent, VS, (usually 
negligible) and the attractive and repulsive potentials, VA and VR (Malvern Instruments Limited 
2011a).

Steric stabilization is obtained by chemically functionalizing or coating the particles with polymers 
or proteins in order to neutralize the surface charge and thus preventing the particles of forming 
agglomerates. Complete steric stabilization suggests a surface charge of zero or close to zero  
(Jensen, Pojana, and Bilanicová 2014). Where steric stabilization depends on the specific surface 
of the particles, the electrostatic stabilization of the particles depends on the surrounding media. 
The pH, ionic strength and composition of the media all affect the electrostatic double layer and 
when one or more are modified it cause alterations of the Stern layer at the surface of the particle. 
The electrostatic double layer at the particle surface is a result of the surface charge and the strong 
attraction of counter ions in the interfacial region. Around the Stern layer a layer depleted in the 
counter ions found in the Stern layer will form, called the Guy-Chapman layer  (Jensen, Pojana, 
and Bilanicová 2014). This layer is less firmly associated and more diffuse, and thus also 
sometimes referred to as the diffuse layer.  
2.4.1 Zeta potential (ζ-pot) 
The ζ-pot is defined at the slipping plane between the Guy-Chapman layer and the surrounding 
media, and has the magnitude of the potential gap between the Stern and Guy-Chapman layer 
(see Figure 2) (Jensen, Pojana, and Bilanicová 2014). The size of the ζ-pot for a given colloid 
system can give an indication of the stability. The rule of thumb is that a colloid suspension is 
highly stable at ζ-pot values larger than |30| mV, but can however be observed to be metastable at 
lower ζ-pot values. When the ζ-pot is larger than |30| mV the repulsive potential causes the 
particles to stay dispersed (Jensen, Pojana, and Bilanicová 2014). 
 
Figure 2. Illustrates the electrostatic double layer at the particle surface, a the Stern layer and the Guy-Chapman 
layer (diffusive layer) (Hartmann et al. 2014) 
The ζ-pot is influenced by the pH of the media, and will have a point where the ζ-pot will pass 
through 0 mV. This point is referred to as the isoelectric point. At and just around the isoelectric 
point the suspension will be unstable, whereas the suspension above and below the isoelectric 
point should be stable (Malvern Instruments Limited 2011a). Also the conductivity of the media will 
have an effect on the ζ-pot, as the thickness of the electrical double layer is influenced by the 
concentrations of ions and the ionic strength of the media. Increasing the ion strength of a solution 
will cause the double layer to compress.

pH wihtin the animal could affact the aggregation of the nanoparticles and thus the uptake and 
translocation into the animal.

2.4.2 Size distributions  
Given nanoparticles tendency to agglomerate or aggregate when dispersed in liquids, the size of 
dispersed nanoparticles is often given as a mean size. Many different methods are available for 
determining primary particle size, for example high-resolution imaging techniques such as 
transmission electron microscopy (TEM) and scanning electron microscopy (SEM), and other 
techniques like disk centrifugation (DC) and nanoparticle tracking analyse (NTA) (Jensen, Pojana, 
and Bilanicová 2014). In this work, the mean Z-Average, which is the cumulants mean of the given 
size distribution (Malvern Instruments Limited 2011b), was characterized by means of a Malvern 
Zetasizer Nano ZS. The Z-Average is therefore sensitive to large particles or agglomerates. The 
Zetasizer Nano ZS uses Dynamic Light Scattering (DLS) to measure the size of particles and 
macromolecule (often in the submicron region). The DLS, also sometimes referred to as Quasi-
Elastic Light Scattering (QELS) or Photon Correlation Spectroscopy (PCS), measures the diffusion 
of the nanoparticles dispersed in a liquid moving under Brownian motion (Malvern Instruments 
Limited 2015b) and relates it to the hydrodynamic diameter, d(H), by means of the Stokes-Einstein 
equation. The Brownian motion of the particles is random and caused by the collisions with the 
solvent molecules. For larger particles the movement is slower, as the impact from the solvent 
molecules will have a smaller effect, whereas they will have a large effect on smaller particles 
making them move faster. When measuring the Brownian motion it is important with a known 
stable temperature of the suspension. The temperature has an influence on the convection 
patterns which can affect the Brownian motion of the particles, as well as the viscosity of the media 
(Malvern Instruments, 2000). When a laser light is beamed into the suspension the Brownian 
motion of the particles to be sized will cause the light to scatter in different intensities, from which 
the velocities of the particles can be estimated (Malvern Instruments Limited, 2015). An intensity 
size distribution can be made, where the width of the curve is a parameter called the Polydispersity 
Index (PDI). The PDI can lie between 0.05-1 and the PDI of a sample will tell something about how 
==PAGE:20==
12 
 
monodisperse or polydisperse the sample is, and thus it DLS an appropriate technique. Higly 
polydisperse solutions (PDI > 0.7) (Malvern Instruments Limited 2011b) will result in unreliable 
values because the large aggregates will have a large influence on the size distribution and thus 
might screw it towards the large particles, and generally an indication that samples are unsuited for 
DLS characterisation.  
Also samples with a high degree of sedimentation will affect the outcome of the size analysis, as 
they settle out as the particle size increases. The larger the size of the aggregates the higher the 
velocity caused by gravity. The more stable a sample is, the more reliable is the Z-average. 
However, it is still a size distribution and not an accurate measurement of the primary particle size.  
2.5 Bioaccumulation principles 
Bioaccumulation is the accumulation of a contaminant in an organism in any case of exposures 
routes (Bour et al. 2015), that it both direct exposure, which refers to exposure from the 
surrounding environment (e.g., water, sediment and air) or in this case direct uptake of the 
“contaminant”, as well as exposure from contaminated food. Moreover, bioaccumulation is also the 
magnification of contamination degree over the food chain. Bioaccumulation is measured as a 
bioaccumulation factor (BAF), and it expresses the ratio of contaminant concentration in the test 
organism to the concentration in the environment. BAF is often determined in field experiments. It 
should be noted that no distinction is made on the two exposure routes (Bour et al. 2015). 
Bioconcentration, on the other hand, excludes uptake of the contaminant via ingestion of 
contaminated food and solely considers the accumulation from the direct exposure. It is quantified 
by the bioconcentration factor (BCF), which again is defined as the ratio of contaminant 
concentration in the test organism to the concentration in the environment, often synonymous to 
the surrounding water, at equilibrium. As it requires the absence of food it can only be determined 
in laboratory experiments.  
Assessments of BAF and BCF must only include the truly absorbed contaminants and not include 
particles sorbed on the exoskeleton of the test animals nor particles that are immediately 
depurated again, which is why test animals must be rinsed and transferred to an environment 
without contaminants and allowed to depurate (Bour et al. 2015).

2.5.1 Acartia tonsa 
The copepod Acartia tonsa is a fairly common zooplankton which was originally found in the Indo-
Pacific region. Today, it can however be found in both marine and brackish waters along the North 
American Atlantic coast line and European coast lines (ADW 2015).  A. tonsa are most commonly 
found in shallow depths between 0 – 50 meters, and has been found to inhabit salinities between < 
5 ‰ and up to 30 ‰, but has been observed to tolerate a salinity range  in laboratory setups from 
nearly 0 ‰ to 52 ‰ (Calliari et al. 2008), and it can therefore be described as being a euryhaline 
organism. The female animals are larger than the males, and their size (0.5 – 1.5 mm) allows for 
them to be visually counted (ADW 2015). They feed on the nauplii of other copepods, which is the 
first stage larva of certain crustaceans, in addition to phytoplankton (Jensen 2010). It exercises 
both suspension-feeding on non-motile prey as well as active capture of motile prey. Where the 
lower size limit of what they might intentionally capture and ingest is 2-4 µm (Jensen 2010), they 
might very well ingest much smaller particles as a consequence of the suspension-feeding.

In this chapter, a description of all materials and method used for the experiments in this study are

3.1 Test media 
Filtered sea water (0.2 µm MiliQ filter) provided by DTU Aqua was used. It was stored in the dark 
at 4˚C. For use in the bioassays the sea water was diluted with an USEPA soft media, Lumbriculus 
Media, made by diluting USEPA medium media in MiliQ water 1:1. 
3.2 Nanoplastic particles 
In the initial study of size and zeta potential over a salinity gradient for five types of PS and four 
types of PMMA nanoparticles (nominal size: 100 nm, Phosphorex Inc., Hopkinton, MA, USA) with 
and without surface functionalization and surfactant (COOH and Tween20) were tested. From now 
on polystyrene is referred to as PS, surfactant (Tween20) is denoted T and surface 
functionalization (COOH) is denoted with C. The same annotation is used for the PMMA particles.  
Additionally, fluorescent PS (green, excitation/emission: 450/500 nm) was characterised and used 
to qualitatively determine the uptake of NP in the test organism by use of confocal microscope. All 
NPs were supplied as 1% solid suspensions in de-ionized water with a sodium azide concentration 
of 2mM. 
To accommodate the analytical issue of quantifying plastic uptake chemically, PEG5000 coated Au 
nanoparticles, with a nanosized gold core and a polymerized surface of PEG was used (1mg/mL) 
for the uptake and depuration study in order to make a quantitative assessment of uptake of NP in 
the test organism. The coating with PEG makes it a suitable model particle for NP uptake. This 
particle was synthesized at DTU Nanotech. Two different batches of the suspension were used.  
3.3 Characterisation – size distribution and zeta potential

with media, yielding a concentration of 1 mg/L. The media was prepared as a salinity gradient 
setup containing ten different salinities from freshwater (0‰) to brackish water (10.9 ‰) and 
seawater (33 ‰). The NPs were added directly prior to measurements. Both zeta potential, z-
average and polydisperse index (PDI) was measured.

3.4 Cultivation of Acartia tonsa 
Eggs in suspension from December 2014 were supplied from DTU Aqua (250.000 eggs mL-1). The 
A. tonsa supplied by DTU Aqua were acclimatized to the lower salinities in the test setup prior to 
the bioassay. This was done by allowing the eggs to hatch in the desired salinities and letting the 
animals develop in that environment for a period of at least 3 weeks. 10 µL of egg suspension 
were added to 400 mL of media (8‰, 16‰ and 32‰ solutions) in 500 mL bluecap flasks. 2000 µL 
of an alge Rhodomonas Salina suspension were fed to each flask.  For the first four days no 
aeration were applied to prevent the bubbles to destroy the sensitive eggs or newly hatched 
animals. The cultivation took place in a room with a constant temperature of 20 ± 1˚C under a 16:8 
hour light-dark cycle. Hatching was observed in small quantities in all salinities; however, the 
hatching percentage was very low. This could be due to the fact that the eggs were more than 3 
months old. The animals were fed every 2-3 days with between 4-6 mL of R. Salina suspension. 
The media was replaced once due to the development of degrading green algae on the bottom and 
in the suspension making the media unclear.  
3.5 Cultivation of Rhodomonas salina 
The Rhodomonas culture was provided first by DTU Aqua and later by DHI, and it was grown in 
preheated (80 ˚C) sea water (33‰) containing a nutrient mix for that exact purpose under constant 
aeration and constant light at a temperature of 20 ± 1˚C. Whenever possible the culture was 
inoculated every second day, and a certain amount was transferred to a blue cap flask and kept in 
the fridge.

number of dead animals using a light table. After 48 hours they were counted again.

excitation/emission: 460/500 nm) were left for 24 hours under 8/16 hours dark light circle with no 
aeration and no food source. After which one animal was investigated under a Leica DM600B CS 
(Confocal System SP5) microscope with a GFP/RFP filter, to see whether or not it was possible to 
confirm an uptake of particles by A. tonsa.  
3.7 Uptake and depuration test with Acaria tonsa 
As for the acute toxicity test, three stock solutions with a volume of 2 L were prepared. 1 L was 
transferred to a measuring flask where 1 ml PEG-Au nanoparticle suspension was added yielding 
a concentration of 1 mg/L. Four 50 mL beakers were prepared with approximately 25 mL of test 
media and 5 animals for each of the sampling times (1, 2, 4 and 24 hours) for both uptake and 
depuration in addition to four control beakers. In a total 36 beakers were prepared, see a visual 
setup in Figure 5. The 16 beakers setup for the depuration part of the setup had the stock solution 
changed after 24 hours to clean media. At each sampling time the animals in the four beakers 
were sacrificed and transferred to four analytic vials in which they were dried.  Furthermore, 5 mL 
of the test media was sampled from three of the four beakers for the uptake part of the setup. Here 
it should be noted that the water was not sampled from specifically in the bottom or top of the 
media, rather where it could be avoided to include any animals in the water samples. The beakers 
were covered with laboratory film and stored in the dark at 20±1˚C for the entire duration of the 
test. Test media containing the nanoparticles and dead animals were carried out as blank tests in 
order to estimate the amount of particles sorbing to the exoskeleton of the test animals. 
After sampling, the sacrificed animals were left to dry in the analytic vials, after which the samples 
were prepared by dissolving both biomass and gold. This was achieved by adding 0.5 mL of 
concentrated nitric acid and 1.5 mL 37 % hydrochloric acid (1:3), resulting in a total of 2 mL aqua 
regia in each vial. 24 hours later, the samples were diluted with 8 mL of MiliQ water. For samples 
containing water the dissolution was done after evaporating the water over night on a heating 
device, which resulted in the formation of salt crystals in the bottom of the vials. Aqua regia, in the 
same proportion as before, was added onto the crystals. The vials were ultra-sonicated for three 
minutes to ensure that the crystals were all present in the acid and not attached to the glassware. 
Each stock solution was also sampled to establish the actual gold concentration (triplicates), and 
the procedure was as for the rest of the media samples.

In this section the outcome of the laboratory experiments including particle characterisation, acute 
toxicity of the fluorescent particles, the qualitative assessment of uptake by confocal microscope 
images of A. tonsa after exposure to fluorescent polystyrene, as well as the quantitative uptake 
and depuration tests for the PEG-coasted nanoparticles over the salinity gradient will be presented. 
It should be noted that for the initial particle characterisation of the PS and PMMA particles, the

4.1 Characterisation   
The initial test was done in order to verify that agglomeration occurs in an increasing degree

(43.1 nm), which was due to the result of one sub-run that gave an z-average of 221.8 nm with a 
PDI value of 0.34. The rest of the runs in that particular run all had stable PDI values below 0.23.

4.1.2 Z-average and PDI after 96 hours 
The influence of time on the state of agglomeration was tested by measuring the z-average as well 
as PDI again after 96 hours, and the z-average data is indicated by squares in Figure 6. The 
general trend for all plots a)-h) in Figure 6 is that time results in an increase of z-average. In 
general the PDI values have increased over time. Furthermore, an elevated number of the particle 
types had PDI values above 0.5 (Figure 7). 
4.1.2.1 Polystyrene  
The z-average of PS was slightly higher for all times with mean z-averages from 93.6±2.3 nm to 
689.9±14.2 nm with PDI ranging from 0.20-0.57. The overall standard deviation for the 
measurements also increased, the relative standard deviation were however all below 8%. The 
largest difference over time for the PS particles all included was observed for the PS T (Figure 6c). 
The mean z-average for PS T were all >296 nm, where the largest z-average for t=0 hours was 
249 nm. Z-average increases until the salinity of 10.9‰, after which it decreases steadily. At the 
salinity 10.9 ‰ an especially high z-average of 1432.7± 48.0 nm was observed. This particular 
point also had the highest PDI (0.76). The PDI indicates a high degree of agglomeration, but as the 
values is above 0.7, it is a poor indication of average size. The development of z-average over the 
salinity gradient for PS C at t = 96 hours is very similar to that of t = 0 hour, except for the z-
average at salinity 2.4 ‰ where the measured z-average is 1153.7± 81.1 nm. For PS C T the z-
average is almost as stable as right after mixing, except for a slight increase in z-average with 
increasing salinities.  
4.1.2.2 PMMA 
For the PMMA the samples only became more polydisperse over time with PDI values between 
0.97 and 1 for all samples except the vial with salinity of 0‰ (PDI=0.18). The mean z-average 
values were all >1422 nm suggesting high degree of agglomeration. Again the particle with 
surfactant, in this case PMMA T, was the one where time had the largest observed effect on z-
average (Figure 6d). The maximum z-average observed was for the salinity 2.4‰ with a z-average 
value of 2875.3±387.5 nm (t= 0 max=513.6±45.4 nm). In general the z-average values were 
around 1 µm for PMMA T after 96 hours with an average PDI of 0.7. Which suggests that time 
highly influences the degree of agglomeration. For the most part of the PMMA C z-averages time 
has had an increasing, however minimal, effect resulting in z-average values of between 
119.1±20.1 and 539.8± 168.8 nm with an average of 226.2 nm. PDI values were between 0.24-
0.67 with an average of 0.35. In the case of PMMA C T for t = 0 hours and both PS CT, the 
observed z-average PMMA C T was very stable and showed a slight increase in z-average over 
the salinity gradient. The largest observed z-average for this time step was only 327.3±4.9 nm. The 
PDI values were all below 0.5 with an average of 0.34.

5 Discussion 
To be able to conclude on the work done in this study, there are several things that need to be 
considered. For instance what outcome can be taken from the initial particle characterisation of the 
different particles, the relevance and shortcomings of the chosen particles for the uptake and 
depuration test, the feasibility of the test setup, the chosen test organism, and the quality of the test 
results. Furthermore, it is important to identify what other environmental factors that need to be 
included when investigating the differences between freshwater and seawater environments for 
uptake studies. All of which will be discussed in the following.  
5.1 The consequence of the choice of particle 
The initial characterisation of the different types of PS and PMMA indicated that surfactant and 
surface functionalization is of great importance for the behaviour and fate of NPs in aquatic 
environments. The degree of agglomeration and consequently also to some extent the degree of 
settling of the plastic particles differed from type to type. This was expected, as surfactant is added 
with the exact purpose of stabilising the particle, why lesser agglomeration should occur with 
particle dispersed with surfactant. The surface functionalization appeared to depend on the specific 
type of particle, as increased agglomeration was observed for the functionalised PS in contrast to 
less agglomeration for functionalised PMMA.  
Size wise it is not possible to directly compare the z-average from PS to PMMA due to the fact that 
measured PDI values were very high for the PMMA (>0.7). However, the results clearly indicate 
that PMMA showed higher tendency to form larger agglomerates than PS in all types of aquatic 
environments as a function of increasing salinities. Furthermore, PDI and z-average indicated that 
the agglomerates were formed almost immediately after the introduction of salinity in the media. 
The density of the plastic could also be important in this context, as it is important for the fate and 
behaviour of the particle. Gravitational forces could have caused the PMMA particles to settle, 
which would have resulted in the high z-average and PDI. PMMA has a higher density than PS, 
which could be a contributing factor to why the PMMA in general had higher values of z-average 
and PDI compared to PS. On the other hand, PMMA is described as being more hydrophilic than 
PS, and should therefore be expected to be better dispersed.  
In regards to surface functionalization and the particle specific effects, it was observed that PS C 
showed that highest agglomeration of all the particles with much lower PDI values than that of 
PMMA. The low PDI values indicate monodisperse size distribution, meaning that it was not only a 
few particle that agglomerated but the majority. This suggests that the surface properties of the 
particles are really important properties in regards to fate of the particle. It would therefore be very 
relevant to examine what kind of surface charge is found on particles in the environment, and to 
identify if there are general trends regarding surface charge and occurrence of single particles 
versus larger agglomerates. As mentioned in the introduction, the surface charge as well as the 
salinity was found to have an influence on the adsorption onto cellulose film, and Bhattacharya, 
Chen, et al. (2010) observed negative correlation between adsorption onto cellulose with 
increasing salinities for neutrally charged polystyrene particles. Therefore, the surface 
functionalization might have an influence on whether the particles are taken up, adsorbed on the 
exoskeleton or settled out as a result of agglomeration.  
==PAGE:41==
33 
 
There will most likely exist a great variety of particles in the environment, however, if the surface 
charge has an influence on the behaviour, there might very well be a correlation between the 
surface properties and the location in the water column. Based on the results from this study, 
negatively charged PS particles and PMMA (particles that both exhibited a high degree of 
agglomeration) would be less likely to be found in the upper parts of the water column as the 
probability is that they will settle due to gravity, and therefore perhaps less likely to exist in a nano 
scaled form. 
For the purpose of testing the uptake as a result of salinity-induced agglomeration, the non 
functionalised and non stabilised PS would have been a very good choice, as it showed a linear 
increase in z-average for increasing salinities with very low and stable PDI values (<0.4). Using 
this sort of particles would mean that the influence of size on uptake could be investigated, and it 
would be possible to show whether or not salinity-induced agglomeration would result in better 
availability for ingestion for the biota. This would requires that uptake was unaffected by salinity 
alone. However, there is currently no way of quantifying PS through analytic techniques, unless 
they are somehow labelled with e.g. fluorescence. In this connection it should be noted that 
Associate Professor Anders Egede Daugaard at DTU Chemical Engineering in relation to this 
study attempted to use Thermo Gravimetric Analysis-Infrared detection (TGA-IR) to quantify PS 
particles taken up by D. magna but that the detection limits prevents this method for application in 
uptake studies with small marine species such as crustaceans and copepods like A. tonsa. The 
amount of plastic that the animals are capable of taking up is too small, and would thus require a 
substantial amount of test animals and an effective method to isolate the plastic and remove the 
biomass. Cole et al. (2013) used acid, alkaline and enzymatic digestion techniques to isolate MP 
taken up by marine biota, and were able to do so without destroying the MP.  
An alternative solution could have been to use fluorescent PS, and in that way being able to 
quantify the amount of PS accumulated by the measured fluorescence intensity. However, the 
fluorescent PS particles did not exhibit the same agglomeration behaviour across the salinity 
gradient as was seen for the non functionalised and non stabilised PS particles. In fact, they were 
observed to be relatively stable and very similar to PS C T (t = 0 hour). It turns out that the particles 
came in a Tween20 suspension, which can explain why they were relatively stable and well 
dispersed and only displayed limited agglomeration. After three days some degree of 
agglomeration was observed, but still not in the same extent as PS, maybe caused by a dilution of 
the surfactant. The surfactant and resulting minimal agglomeration makes the fluorescent PS 
particles less fit as an indicator for uptake of plastic particles as a result of salinity-induced 
agglomeration. As for a model particle for uptake differences only caused by salinity it is also a 
poor choice because it is not stable over time. Moreover, fluorescence has been observed to be 
affected by agglomeration. In a study with silver nanoparticles the wavelengths broaden with 
increasing size (Singh et al. 2013), suggesting that particles with high degrees of agglomeration 
are unsuitable for this method of quantification. As the fluorescent light only seems to be affected 
by the size of the silver nanoparticles it is most likely not a particle specific effect and will therefore 
also be relevant for the PS particles and other nanoparticles. The qualitative assessment of uptake 
in this study with the fluorescent PS particles cannot determine whether the particles are actually 
inside the test animal or if actually ingested then depurated immediate after, but should simply be 
used to verify uptake like it was done in the study by (Setälä et al. 2014). To make a quantitative 
analysis of the uptake using fluorescent particles either image analysis of the pixels in the pictures 
==PAGE:42==
34 
 
taken with for example a confocal microscope should be made or a standard curve for the 
fluorescence as a function of agglomeration specific to the particular particle must be made to 
account for the particle specific agglomeration effect on the fluorescence. The pixel analysis would 
require very high dissolutions to be able to detect single particles and one would have to find a 
solution to the agglomeration induced alterations to the emitted light. Cole et al. (2013) actually 
quantified uptake of 20 µm fluorescent polystyrene. After the fluorescent MP had been internalized 
in the copepod Temora longicormis and released by enzymatic digestion, the particles were 
enumerated by counting them which requires very high microscope resolution before it could work 
for NP with non agglomerated sizes. In relation to NP it is also important to determine whether the 
enzymatic digestion technique has an impact on the fluorescence and the agglomeration degree. 
The PEG5000-Au nanoparticles were chosen as a model particle for the uptake and depuration 
test based on the inner gold core, as it could easily be quantified by ICP-MS analysis even at very 
low concentration. In this study a standard curve for gold concentration by no-gas quantification on 
the ICP-MS got as low as 0.1 µg/L. The characterisation showed z-average of the particles was 
unaffected by the salinity, thus are the PEG5000-Au nanoparticles a poor indication for the uptake 
of plastic particles affected by salinity induced agglomeration. The stability of the particles seems 
to be uncoupled with the zeta potential, as the zeta potential suggests that they should be highly 
unstable. This observation leads to the conclusion that the particles must be sterically stabilised. 
As a result of the stability across time and salinity for the PEG5000-Au particles, they work 
excellent as model particles for a base line study for testing whether uptake across the salinity 
gradient depends on other factors than size of the particles. The polymerisation of the surface 
justifies the use of a nano gold particle, as the surface is all the animals will encounter, and 
therefore the assumption is that they will not be able to differentiate these particles from other NP 
particles. Furthermore, gold has low background concentrations in the aquatic environment and 
generally exhibits low toxicity in contrast to silver nanoparticles.  
5.2 The feasibility of the test setup 
Before evaluating the results from the uptake and depuration test it is relevant to discuss the 
feasibility of the test setup. A setup where A. tonsa (n=20, in four replicates) was tested after 1, 2, 
4 and 24 hours (uptake phase), and subsequently animals were transferred to clean media and 
again sampling occurred after 1, 2, 4 and 24 hours (depuration phase) was chosen. No stirring or 
sonication was applied, as well as no cleaning of the exoskeleton and no feeding during the test.  
A 24 hour uptake and a 24 hour depuration setup was chosen based on experience from the study 
by Skjolding, Kern, et al.(2014) where no statistical difference was found between the uptakes from 
the 24, 48 and 72 hour test setup. The short-term test design was also chosen based on the fact, 
that feeding should be avoided in order to keep the complexity of the setup at a minimum. Both 
uptake and depuration was shown with this setup in all three salinities, which is why this setup 
appears to be feasible for this specific purpose. However to test the feasibility a longer test setup 
would have to be tested in order to determine whether or not the body burdens reached a steady 
plateau for uptake or if the residual body burdens would decrease further.   
Due to the size of the A. tonsa it was very challenging to ensure that they were properly cleansed 
before being sampled, which is normally the procedure in order to avoid particles sorbs onto the 
exoskeleton of the test animal to interfere with the measured uptake. The reason was it was very 
difficult to sample the animals and transferring them to the test vials, because of the risk of 
==PAGE:43==
35 
 
destroying the animals and getting them stuck in the finely meshed nylon net used for transferring 
from test vials to the analytical vials. Therefore a compromise was made. The animals were not 
cleansed before they were transferred to the vial for testing. Instead a dead control was made. Five 
animals were killed with ethanol and then placed in a stock solution in order to determine the 
concentration of NPs sorbed onto the exoskeleton. As the dead controls showed concentration 
<0.0 µg/L, the NP sorbed to the exoskeleton was neglected for the tested kind of particles. 
Considering the sterric stabilisation of the particles it is not surprising that no sorbtion on 
exoskeleton was observed. It would however be interesting for future studies to test the difference 
of adsorption for different particles in order to determine if the adsorption was affected  by surface 
functionalization as seen in the studies by (Bhattacharya, Lin, et al. 2010; Bhattacharya, Chen, et 
al. 2010). This was however outside the scope of this study.  
The size of the test animal also raised another issue, as it meant a very small amount of biomass 
for the chemical analysis. Five animals per beaker is a procedure similar to that of ecotoxicity 
testing with D. magna. The size of D. magna, however, is much larger and thus provides a better 
basis for the quantification method. With an average dry weight for A. tonsa of 5 µg/organism  
(KOK, 2015) compared to a dry weight of 88 ± 9 µg/ organism for D. magna  (4–5 days old) 
(Skjolding, Winther-Nielsen, and Baun 2014) yields a difference of approximately 80 µg per test 
organism per beaker, which is a significant difference in biomass. For future testing an increased 
number of A. tonsa would be preferred, for example in the range of 25 animals per beaker yielding 
a total of 100 animals per time step, which would increase the biomass and thus also most likely 
decrease the uncertainty of each time step.  
The use of the freshwater crustacean D. magna is fairly common within ecotoxicity testing and it is 
a part of the base-set of aquatic organisms for acute toxicity testing. For this study the salinity 
tolerance of D. magna is not high enough, hence, it was not an option. However, the issue with the 
small amount of biomass would have been addressed if daphnia would have been used, or 
alternatively a larger number of A. tonsa as just proposed. D. magna exhibits a different feeding 
trade, as they eat all through the water column including scavenging from the bottom of the beaker, 
why the bioaccumulation also would have been very interesting to address for this specie. For 
particles with a high degree of agglomeration and gravitational settling benthic species such as the 
M. edulis or other sediment living organisms would have been an appropriate test animal. In this 
setup the PEG5000-Au particles showed little signs of agglomeration (stable z-average and stable 
PDI), which is why the pelatic A. tonsa seems very suitable for this test setup.  
5.2.1 Water phase concentrations 
To ensure that the test organisms are in contact with the nanoparticles and thus in chance of 
actually ingesting them, the particles have to be in suspension. In this setup, the initial 
characterisation of the particles led to the assumption that the stable PEG5000-Au particles would 
stay suspended. If the particles had a large degree of settling caused by for example gravitational 
forces working on agglomerates, some sort of force e.g. sonication or stirring would have to be 
applied to the beakers to keep the particles suspended. As done with MP setup in the study by 
(von Moos, Burkhardt-Holm, and Köhler 2012) where aeration from both top and bottom was used 
to keep the plastics suspended. Stirring or sonication have also been used to keep the particles 
suspended  (Browne et al. 2008; Cole et al. 2013) (It should be noted, that the sonication was not 
applied during the bioassay but only in the preparation phase), however in uptake and depuration 
studies with sensitive test animals those techniques cannot be applied. For A. tonsa even too 
==PAGE:44==
36 
 
intense aeration (bubbles) can hurt or even kill the organisms. Beside the settling, absorption to the 
glassware could also hinder the availability for ingestion of the test organism, which is not a nano 
specific problem but something to consider for all hydrophobic substances (Handy, Van Den Brink, 
et al. 2012). Therefore it is essential for the test setup, that not only the nominal concentration but 
also the actual exposure concentration throughout the setup is controlled or at least quantified. In 
this setup water phase concentrations were sampled simultaneous with the test organisms, in 
order to determine the actual exposure concentration. However nothing was done to control it, due 
to the characteristics of the test particle (stable z-average). The measured concentrations for the 
water phases for all three setups were analysed, and the concentration in the 8‰ setup showed to 
be three times higher than the water phase concentration of the other two test setups, which was 
already a suspicion when that particular stock solution was much darker coloured (see Appendix 
9.6). There can be two explanations for why this happened. Either, an error was made and more 
than 1 mL of the PEG5000-Au nanoparticle suspension was added to the measuring flask when 
the stock solution was prepared. Or the second batch of PEG5000-Au nanoparticle suspension, 
which was only used for that particular stock solution, had a higher concentration than the batch 
used for the two other test setups. Time limitations was the reason that no further investigation of 
this issued was made.   
The Au concentration mean for the 8‰ setup appears to increase over time, but statistically the 
means cannot be said to be different from one another (p>0.05, F<Fcrit, ANOVA single factor). For 
the two other test setups the concentration seems to increase from the concentration in the stock 
solution, especially in the 16 ‰ test, which should not be possible. The background levels in both 
water controls and test animal controls were insignificant and cannot explain the increase. It could 
perhaps be due to the inconsistency in the sampling procedure, where the main concern was to 
avoid the test animals. Therefore the water phase samples could reflect concentration differences 
throughout the test vials. However, as the PEG5000-Au nanoparticles were very stable in the 
suspension, this is most likely not the reason. The water phase samples were heated prior to 
sample preparation with aqua regia, which as reported led to the formations of salt crystals in 
proportion relative to the salinity content of the samples (example can be seen in Appendix 9.6). 
Hence, a larger formation of crystals was developed in the water phase samples from the 32 ‰ 
than for e.g. 8 ‰. Despite ultra-sonication of the samples and the addition of aqua regia, the 
crystals were still present in some extent when the samples were analysed. There is a possibility 
that some of the gold was captured in the crystals and that the analysis therefore underestimated 
some of the concentrations. This could also be a contributing factor to why a much larger 
concentration was seen in the stock solution in the 8 ‰ setup, as less crystals developed in that 
salinity.   
It would have made more sense if the nominal concentrations had been observed to decrease over 
time confer paragraph 2.5, as particles taken up or settling out would result in a concentration 
decrease like it was observed in the study with the blue mussel by Wegner et al. (2012). Mussels 
filter relative large quantitative of water which explains their observation. A decrease in water 
phase concentration was not observed or could at least not be supported by the data and huge 
deviations. Furthermore, the settling would not occur with this model particle and perhaps the five 
A. tonsa are unable to remove an amount significant enough to have an influence on the water 
concentration. In this study the nominal concentration for the setup was aimed at 1 mg/L. In the 
study by Skjolding, Kern, et al.(2014) a concentration of 0.5 mg Au/L was used and they succeed 
==PAGE:45==
37 
 
in showing uptake. Therefore the concentration range for this setup seems to be reasonable, but 
the environmental concentration ranges yet needs to be quantified in order to properly conclude on 
the appropriateness of the exposure concentration. So far no one has been able to quantify the 
environmental concentrations of nanoplastics, which is why nothing conclusive could be drawn on 
this matter.  
That the A. tonsa are able to take up NP can be supported by the image taken of the fluorescent 
polystyrene under a confocal microscope in Figure 11b. The image showed what looks like 
fluorescent particles in the internal organs of the A. tonsa and are in agreement with similar images 
taken with fluorescent micro-sized particles in several other marine species (Cole et al. 2013; 
Setälä, Fleming-Lehtinen, and Lehtiniemi 2014). The acute toxicity test of the fluorescence PS 
particles showed increasing mortality with increasing salinities. The number of dead animals did 
however not seem to differ statistically from the number of dead animals in the control vials, which 
is why nothing conclusive can be said on the toxicity of the fluorescent PS towards A. tonsa. 
Nevertheless, this concentration was in the range of effect concentrations observed in the study by 
Bhattacharya, Chen, et al. (2010) on D. magna after exposure to 70 nm PS particles and yet it was 
significantly lower than the maximum effect concentration. A second study should probably be 
made to see if the data reproducible.  
5.2.2 The outcome of the uptake and depuration test 
When evaluating the uptake and depuration results, it is important to reflect on the concentration 
differences between the three test setups. Instead of the test being a baseline test of uptake of 
plastic particles as a function of salinity, it also became a test of concentration differences. It 
should be noted that no dead animals were observed in the test, and the particles were considered 
to exhibit no toxicity.  
Firstly, all three setups showed a fast initial uptake, which is in accordance with other uptake 
studies (Skjolding et al. 2014). The depuration happened to a great extent within the first hour, and 
steady residual body burdens remained the following 24 hours. The largest uptake observed in the 
8 ‰ test setup suggests that the concentration influences the uptake, but considering the standard 
deviations it is hard to conclude that the uptake differs within the first hour between the setups. For 
the second sampling, two hours after test start, the means for 8 ‰ and 16 ‰ increased and a 
slight decrease was observed for 32 ‰. Here it is important to notice that the standard deviations 
exceed the mean in both the case of the 8 ‰ and 32 ‰. Particularly, the standard deviation for 8 
‰ is huge with a relative standard deviation of 103 %. For all three setups the concentration in the 
third sampling cannot be said to be different from the second sampling. As described in the result 
paragraph, an increase in concentration was observed at the sampling 24 hours after test start for 
all three setups. Like for the first sampling, the concentration mean at 24 hours after test start was 
higher for the 8 ‰ setup, but due to the large standard deviations of all three setups, the 
concentrations could not be concluded to be different from one another. That the uptake would 
depend on the concentration seems quite reasonable, as more particles are available for ingestion 
as well as passive uptake through filtration.  
Depuration occurs almost immediately after transfer to clean media in all cases. Again this 
corresponds with the findings observed in the study by  Skjolding, Kern, et al. (2014), where it is 
suggested that the decrease in body burdens illustrates the purging of the gut. In the image with 
the fluorescent particles all the particles are present in what looks like the gut and no translocation 
==PAGE:46==
38 
 
was observed. Therefore it seems reasonable that the concentration would decrease drastically 
after purging of the gut content. The residual body burden appears to find its range within the first 
couple of hours and stabilises there the next 24 hours for all three setups. It is interesting that the 
residual body burdens seem to be alike for all three setup despite different uptake rates, which 
indicates that the residual body burdens unlike the body burdens after 24 hours appears to be 
independent of the exposure concentration. In the Skjolding, Kern, et al. (2014) study, they suggest 
that the daphnia retain some of particles in the gut as a consequence of no feeding. As the test 
animals in the uptake and depuration study were not fed for over 48 hours, it could be the same 
mechanism that resulted in the residual body burdens. Skjolding, Kern, et al. (2014) found that the 
residual body burdens decreased when feeding was added to the test setup. The effect of feeding 
was not tested in this work, but it would be interesting to investigate if the observation also would 
be seen for A. tonsa. If so, then the residual body burdens from this work could represent worst 
case scenario, based on the assumption that adding food to the system would not also increase 
the uptake more than it would increase depuration rates.  
The expectation was that similar uptake and depuration rates would be found in all three salinities, 
as salinity was presumed to have no influence on uptake of stable and well dispersed particles by 
the euryhaline A. tonsa. It appeared that there was a slightly higher uptake in the 8 ‰ setup, which 
was probably due to the higher exposure concentration. If the concentration had been the same, 
the uptake would have been expected similar or perhaps higher in the salinities more natural to the 
marine copepod. However, there is a slight indication of a higher uptake in the 16 ‰ compared to 
32 ‰, perhaps indicating that the A. tonsa despite being euryhaline experiences slightly better 
living conditions in the 16 ‰ test setup. No biological endpoints were noticed, and it would be 
interesting to investigate the potential influence of salinity on normal feeding behaviour with R. 
Salina, a so-called baseline test for this baseline test.  
None of the R2 values for the uptake and the depuration phases for all three setups were >0.5, 
indicating that no truly good model fit was obtained for neither of the tests. The relatively small 
sample size (n=20) and thus small amount of biomass for each time step and high relative 
standard deviations partially explains the low R2 values. More data points would naturally have 
improve the dataset on which a fit is made. A bigger spread of the time steps for example over 1, 4 
and 8 hours instead could perhaps also have given a better fit, but for practical reasons it was not 
done like this. Specifically, the model fit for the 8 ‰ would probably have been a better fit if not for 
the third sampling point. Had it been treated as an outlier a model fit could be obtained in 
Graphpad, yielding an uptake rate in the depuration phase of 0.46±0.12 mg kg-1dw h-1 and a 
depuration rate of 0.07±0.02 h-1, both are given with ±SE. The goodness of fit was still very poor 
with a R2 value of 0.06. Due to the poor fit, none of the computed rates are considered reliable.  
5.3 Environmental relevance 
When looking at the test setup and the results in a bigger perspective, there are certain limitations 
that cannot be ignored. First of all the test is designed very simple in order to be able to conclude 
on the salinity effect, why other factors are excluded, hereunder food sources and natural organic 
matter (NOM).  
The dependency of the primary size of the NP was not investigated, nor if size affected uptake. 
The different particles used for characterisation, evaluating the influence of salinity on size 
distribution, and uptake test had primary sizes range from 55 nm (PEG5000-Au) to approximately 
==PAGE:47==
39 
 
120 nm (PMMA). The size of the particles could influence whether the biota take up the particles 
through passive filtration or selectively ingest them mistaking them for prey. The PEG5000-Au are 
relative small compared to plankton and ingestion of them could therefore easily by a result of 
passive uptake by filtration. If that was the case similar uptake rates all three salinities for the 
stable particle would be expected, except of course for the setup with elevated concentrations. 
From an environmental perspective, it is hardly relevant to examine particles with an artificial 
surfactant if the source of the NP is disintegration from larger plastics. Here it must be presumed 
that any surfactant will disappear with time and in the disintegration process, whereas, if the 
particles originate from industrial sources and are let directly in the aquatic environments there 
might very well still be surfactant on the particles at first, but again it is presumed that this will only 
be relevant right at the time of let out. In regards to the functionalization of the surface of the 
particles, it would be relevant to investigate the interaction between differently charged particles, 
salinity and NOM, and whether or not an interaction would influence the fate and behaviour of the 
NP. Organic matter could potentially coat or adhere to the surface of the plastic particles, which 
could impact the fate and behaviour of the plastic particles in respect to sorption of POPs and 
metals as well as the buoyancy, perhaps making the plastics less available for ingestion for biota. 
This study used a mix of filtered seawater (0.2µm) and an artificial media, and thus neglects any 
influence by NOM. Realistically, very few environments would have no or close to no NOM, except 
for maybe pristine oceanic waters. The focus is purely to investigate the effect of salinity on the 
availability of NP for ingestion, which is why the interaction between NP and NOM was not 
included. However, the author recognises the content of NOM is another apparent difference found 
in freshwater versus open seawater.  According to Hartmann et al. (2014) studies have found NOM 
to have both a decreasing effect as well as boosting effect on different nanoparticles suggesting 
that the interaction is rather complex and most likely dependent on the surface properties of the 
nanoparticles.  
Under the assumption that the agglomeration observed with increasing salinities is in fact is 
aggregation, it would be interesting to investigate ecotoxicity and carrier potential of NP compared 
to the MP counterpart. As aggregates in contrast to agglomerates decreases the specific surface 
area compared to when the plastic occurs as singles particles, the overall sorption capacity of NP 
must therefore decrease in seawater compared to freshwater, as a result of the higher aggregation 
in seawater. This could suggest that in relation to ecotoxicity and carrier potential of e.g. POPs that 
NP might be a larger problem in freshwater compared to sea water. It is unknown if aggregates 
and agglomerates have the equivalent physiological impact as the primary nanoparticles (Andrady 
2011). The higher density for seawater compared to freshwater allow more plastic particles to stay 
suspended, which results in different exposure in freshwater and sea water, respectively, for the 
aquatic biota. However if the elevated content of NOM in freshwater compared to seawater has 
comparable effects on agglomeration as salinity then the ecotoxicity and carrier potential of NP 
would be similar in the two environments. A salinity induced agglomeration could perhaps result in 
less translocation of NP, as the increasing size into sizes in the micro range hinders uptake by 
endocytosis. However, agglomeration could result in larger availability for ingestion for a larger 
range of biota including larger animals than copepods and crustaceans.  
The content of nutrients is another important environmental factor. Rivers and lakes, and partially 
also estuaries, have higher content compared to pristine oceanic waters, why a higher interaction 
between organisms and plastics are to be expected. In nutrient rich waters the likelihood or in other 
==PAGE:48==
40 
 
words the competition between plastics and zooplankton is less pronounced, why ingestion of 
plastics are less probable than under nutrient poor conditions, given that the predator exerts non 
discriminating ingestion. And as discussed it could have an impact on both uptake and depuration.  
For analytical purposes stable dispersion of nanoparticles and controlled environments are often 
preferred in order to generalise and standardise the test and results mostly. Skjolding, Kern, et al. 
(2014) points out that comparison and generalisations of different nanoparticles are difficult as a 
result of the lack of scientific standardized test procedures with the large variety of test organism 
used an the wide range of different tested nanoparticles. From this work the results indicate that 
there is still a long way ahead before any standardisation can be made in regards to testing of 
nanoparticles in general, and way to large varieties within the types of MP and NP for it to be done 
within the field as well.

6 Conclusion 
This work sat out to investigate the potential uptake of NP particles by aquatic biota, more 
specifically A. tonsa over a salinity gradient. 
Following recommendations for uptake and depuration test designs with nano plastic particles are 
based on the literature search as well as experiences made based on the uptake and depuration 
test done in this study:  
 
Characterisations have to be made in order to learn the degree of agglomeration for 
subsequent estimation of the location of the particles in the water column. Moreover, the 
surface charge will provide indications for whether or not the particles are likely to sorb to 
the exoskeleton or the internal intestine of the test organism.  
 
For unstable particles the nominal concentration needs to be determined over time.   
 
The type of test animal (pelagic versus benthic) as well as feeding traits should be 
considered in relation to the particles that needs tested, to ensure exposure throughout an 
uptake and depuration test.  
 
Biomass was inadequate as uncertainties were large and the measured concentrations 
very close to detection limits, which is why the number of very small test organisms need to 
be increased or carefully considered in relation to quantification method.  
A large initial characterisation of two types of plastic, PS and PMMA, with and without surfactant 
and surface functionalization found that the tested surfactant (Tween20) in general had a 
stabilising effect on size distribution which reduced over time. The simultaneous use of surfactant 
and surface functionalization caused the best stabilisation of both type of particles, and the particle 
size distributions were observed stable over the salinity gradient and over time. The effect of 
surface functionalization with a carboxyl acid group appears to be particle specific as it has 
significantly different effect on PS and PMMA particles.  
For the purpose of testing the uptake as a result of salinity-induced agglomeration, the non 
functionalised and non stabilised PS was found to be a good model particle, due the a linear 
increase in z-average over the salinities gradient with very low and stable PDI values (<0.4). First 
fluorescent PS was tested as a proxy for PS, however, the fluorescent PS particles only displayed 
limited agglomeration and the quantification method was questionable. The PEG5000-Au particles 
used in the uptake test were unaffected by the salinity, and they were stabile across time and 
salinity, which made them poor model particles for the uptake of plastic particles affected by 
salinity induced agglomeration, but very appropriate as a model particle in the baseline study for 
uptake across the salinity gradient unaffected by agglomeration.  
The conclusion on whether uptake differed due to salinity alone was that there was not statistically 
difference between the uptake of PEG5000-Au particles for the two out of three test setups (16 ‰ 
and 32 ‰), hence the exposure of the PEG5000-Au particle appear to be uniform across the 
salinity gradient. Unintentionally, a concentration difference was observed in the 8 ‰ setup, which 
was why no conclusion could be made in regard to baseline uptake in that setup. However similar 
residual body burdens were found across all three salinities. The conclusion was that salinity alone 
was not found to have an effect on uptake, but more tests would have to be conducted in order to 
confirm this.

7 Future Work 
It would be interesting to investigate the uptake of particles that are not stable over a salinity 
gradient, in order to determine whether the uptake is influenced by the degree of agglomeration 
induced by the salinity gradient. It would give insight to whether effects are expected to be the 
same in two relative different environments. Moreover, it would be interesting to look into which 
degree MP results can be related to the fate and effect of agglomerated NP.  
Moreover, it would be interesting to investigate whether different nutrient contents such as for 
example seen in freshwater, estuarine and pristine oceanic waters would have an influence on 
uptake as a result of competition. This would help enlighten whether the large concentrations of 
MP (thereby possible also NP) observed and in the great oceanic gyres poses a higher risk for 
biota than MP in the other two environments and thus where the greatest need for intervention is.  
In a societal perspective, it would be interesting to introduce salinity or to increase the ion strength 
of waste water stream to allow nanoparticles to agglomerate and then hopefully settle out. Or to 
introduce some sort of cellulose membrane for the nanoparticles to sorb to and thereby avoid 
outlet into the environment.

During the uptake and depuration test with PEG5000-Au nanoparticles a couple of obsertions were

Firstly, there was a clear difference in colour in the three stock solutions, which can be seen above

other stock solutions.  
Secondly, the samples reacted differently to the addition of the aqua regia. In Figure 16A b) three 
replicates (U4 A, B and C, U:uptake phase, 4: times in hours after test start, Letter: the replicate) 
for one of the three salinityssetups in the uptake and depuration tests can be seen. Again, there is 
a clear difference in colouring of the samples. The first one being almost completely clear, the 
second  being very dark yellow and the last one a shade in between.  
Lastly, a great amount of crystals formed in the analytic vials as a consequnce of heating on saline 
water samples. An example of this can be seen in Figure 16A c). Aqua regia has been added to 
the sample, which has not yet undergone ultra-sonification. The size of the crystals seemed 
logically to be correlated to the salinity content.
