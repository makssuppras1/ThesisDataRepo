Abstract
The main aim of this project has been to develop a rate-based model capable of describing
the heat and mass transfer in an absorption tower using a MEA-VRA-H2O-CO2 solvent.
The models developed in this thesis have been based on a rate-based model developed by
Gabrielsen et al.
As a Ô¨Ārst step toward the main aim of the project, the ordinary diÔ¨Äerential equations
(ODEs) central in the original model of Gabrielsen, were derived. This lead to a slightly
changed model, with one the eight ODEs changed to a new form, which was settled as
the basis model of this work.
The computational implementation of the basis model was then extended with a pinch
convergence routine in order to provide the basis model with the stability needed to handle
the required extensions made in the Ô¨Ānal additive model. Two extrapolative methods were
also implemented to investigate whether such an approach could lead to faster and even
more stable solutions.
Finally, was an additive model developed by extending the basis model with a simplistic
implementation accounting for the changed mole ratios that the addition of additives
causes.
The outcome of this thesis have been that the overall three-stage rocket of this thesis
was partly successful. The new basis model, developed in this project, proved to have a
behavior that was similar to that of the original model. This model was then successfully
extended with a pinch convergence routine making it more robust to changes of the
operational conditions. The results of the extrapolative methods did not immediately
prove to have the desired accelerating properties as intended, but further studies of their
behavior should be conducted before any decisive conclusions can be made. The added
robustness, provided by the the pinch convergence routine, was utilized in the development
of an additive model that accounted for the changed mole ratios of the solvent components
==PAGE:4==
when VRAs are added. However, updating the mole ratios to account for additives did
not seem to give the additive model any improved simulation capabilities, regarding VRA
containing additives, compared with the original model.

Chapter 1
Introduction
This thesis investigates how the inclusion of the so-called ‚ÄôVapour Reducing Additives‚Äô to
the well-known MEA-H2O-CO2 solvents, could be implemented into a rate-based model
simulating mass and heat transfer in an absorption tower. The work is based of on a
model already developed by Gabrielsen et al [7].
The motivation for developing models capable of simulating absorptions, or desorptions
towers, is two-folded. For one thing, the development and optimization of solvents use-
ful for absorbing various gaseous components, have been described as a marathon [16],
where a lot of experiments need to be conducted in order to make progression. If some
of these could be omitted by instead conducting computational simulations, the solvent
development could be a lot less expensive. Secondly, by developing these models from
underlying, physical correlations and subsequently validating them against experimental
data, it becomes clear what underlying properties of the system that are the most essen-
tial for the capabilities of the solvent.
In order for a rate-based model to be able to describe how mass and heat transfers from
one phase to another, it needs to account for a complex sequence of diÔ¨Äerent chemophys-
ical properties that are involved in the Ô¨āuid dynamics of a counter current absorption
tower. To come about modelling such a convulted system, a range of the approxima-
tions and simplifations need to be taken. Techniques of post combustion capturing of
CO2 waste gasses have luckily been around for a long time, and so have the the theoret-
ical groundwork lying behind these. The tremendous endeavour to develop a rate-based
model capable of simulating CO2 absorption processes is therefore only possible due to the
contributions of the many scientists that have worked with the topic through out centuries.

2.1
Carbon capturing
Carbon capture technologies is often associated with two categories; CCS (Carbon Cap-
ture & Storage) and CCU (Carbon Capture & Utilization) [6]. The main idea of both
concepts is to capture CO2 released from heavily polluting point sources, e.g. various
types of power plants or CO2 releasing production facilities, so that the CO2 is restrained
from the atmosphere. The ‚Äôstorage‚Äô in CCS comes from the fact that the separated CO2
is stored in a secluded reservoir, which most often is a deep underground, geological for-
mations, with a porosity and permeability needed for CO2 to penetrate the formation.
At these depth levels will CO2 undergo to liquid state and it‚Äôs therefore possible to store
large quantities of it in this fashion. The produced quantities of CO2 are however at a
magnitude so that more geological storage sites have to be found. Furthermore, due to
the risk of CO2 to release from the storage site, is geological sequestration still associated
with skepticism [27]. An alternative could be to chemical bind CO2 as carbonates through
reaction with metal oxide. However, the reaction is highly energy demanding, which is
why industrial implication still remain elusive [18].
CCU may oÔ¨Äer a carbon capturing solution that could provide some economic incentive
since the goal here is to use the captured CO2 for the production of other products [4].
One such problem could be carbon-neutral fuels, or CCU-fuels, where the combustion
process is reversed, by reacting CO2 with hydrogen to produce synfuels. However, due to
the high energy requirements for producing both the needed hydrogen and the synfuel as
well as the energy requirement for the CO2 capture itself, this isn‚Äôt the most eÔ¨Écient way
of limiting the net CO2 emissions [11].
No matter which further fate there is chosen for the captured CO2, the capturing process

==PAGE:15==
is central. Here, post combustion carbon capture technologies have long been regarded as
a viable option, when it comes to reducing CO2 emissions from power plants. Though this
technology isn‚Äôt ready for the biggest power plants, it is still the technology that comes

the game have long been dictated by the use of alkanolamine-CO2-H2O systems, where
CO2 is chemically bound to the aqueous alkanolamine solvents [15] (see more about this

CO2 gas, is that it is reversible and temperature dependent. This entails that control of
the temperature provides a handle to shift between absorption, an exothermic process,
and desorption, an endothermic process, of CO2. Another key feature is the selectivity of
the CO2-amine reaction, which minimizes the absorption of the Ô¨āue gas components like
methane [22].
The work done in this work have been limited to the modelling of absorber. A further
detailed discussion of the behaviour of the alkanolamine-CO2-H2O system in the absorber
is given in section 2.2.3.
2.1.2
Use of Vapour Reductive Additives (VRAs)
One of the great challenges for CO2 capture using alkanolamine solvents is the that the
operational costs of the technology is so high. Along with the creation of the pressure
drop needed in the absorber, the regeneration of the amine solvent to its lean state in the
reboiler, are what causes the high energy consumption resulting in the high operational
costs [20].
To tackle the latter process, the use of vapour reducing additives (VRAs) have been
suggested in the CERE institute of DTU (article describing this will be published doing
Page 6 of 82
==PAGE:17==
2019). The function of these additives should be to reduce the vapour pressure of wa-
ter and alkanolamine through dilution, which reduces the driving forces for vaporization.
This should in turn reduce the energy requirements for reboiler aswell as for subsequent
condensation and drying processes.
Previous studies performed at the CERE institute of DTU have investigated the eÔ¨Äects
of VRAs, speciÔ¨Ācally urea and ethylene glycol (MEG), on the performance of MEA-H2O-
CO2 systems. Experiments performed on a pilot plant have suggested that the addition
of 30 wt% have a negative impact on the absorbing capabilities of the absorber compared
with a pure MEA-H2O-CO2 (30-70 wt%) solvent. The choice of the VRA have also been
found to be of importance. When MEG was chosen, it was found that the absorption rate
was too low to keep up with the feeding rate of CO2 resulting in accumulation of CO2 in
the column. For urea there absorption rate was steady through out the column, but still
had too low net absorption compared with pure MEA-H2O systems.
The column height to loading and temperature proÔ¨Āles achieved in these experiments
have been used to validate the rate-based models developed in this work. The hope is to
develop a model that accounts for the additives in a satisfactory way.
2.2
Modelling absorption towers
The fundamental function of an absorption tower is substance transfer from one phase to
another. Such a task involves three general problems involving; 1) equilibrium conditions,
2) rate conditions, and 3) Ô¨āow conditions [3].
Problem 1 is based on thermodynamics, as substance transfer only take place if the equi-
librium between the two phases favour the substance transfer and if there is a deviation of
this equilibrium between the phases. For heat transfer, deviation from equilibrium means
the presence of temperature gradient between the phases. The heat capacities of the in-
volved phases are the other inÔ¨āuencing factor on the heat transfer. PT Ô¨āash calculations,
where the goal is to describe the mere outcome of mixing two or more components, are
examples of calculations merely based on equilibriums.
Problem 2 is based in kinetics, and any transfer of either mass or heat, follows the general
rate-expression:
[Transfer] = [Transfer coeÔ¨Écient] x [area] x [driving force]
Page 7 of 82
==PAGE:18==
The driving force is either a concentration or a temperature gradient, and is an expres-
sion of the deviation from equilibrium conditions. Though the equilibriums still are the
fundamental driving force, these problems are also concerned with how the phase transfer
takes place, i.e. description of the mass/heat Ô¨āuxes through out the absorption process.
Problem 3 involves problems of a more mechanical nature involving the phase Ô¨āows. Pres-
sure gradients along the column can arise as a result of the mass and heat transfer and
the mechanical interaction between the two Ô¨āows can cause unwanted implications.
The model explored in this work is hence a rate-based model in that it calculates the
phase transfer rate in discretised volumes of the column.
2.2.1
The chemical reactions
When a chemical reaction takes places between the absorbant and a component of the
liquid phase, this have a drastic eÔ¨Äect on the absorption behaviour bye enhancing the
driving forces of the absorbant (see section 2.2.3).
When deciding which reactive component of the solvent there is to be used, its along with
the usual considerations [20], mainly a trade of between the eÔ¨Äectiveness of the absorption
rate and loading capacity of CO2 versus the operational cost, due to the energy expense
of regenerating the lean solvent by breaking the base-CO2 bond in the reboiler, of doing
so [12]. Which way to lean this balance depends on the circumstances, e.g. if the purpose
is to clean Ô¨āue gasses from power plants, where the partial CO2 pressure is relatively low,
then a quite eÔ¨Äective, reacting agent is needed.
In this context it have become conventional to divide the selection of suitable alka-
nolamines into three chemical categories; the primary, the secondary and the tertiary
alkanolamines. The primary alkanolamines have long been regarded as the most eÔ¨Äec-
tive for Ô¨āue gas cleaning due to their high absorption rates. However, they also exhibit
large heat of reactions and have limited loading capacities [1]. For primary and secondary

CO2 per mole alkanolamine, aswell as smaller heat of reactions. Their low absorption
eÔ¨Äectiveness, however, makes them ineÔ¨Écient for Ô¨āue gas cleaning. An option in between
these extremities, are the sterically hindered alkanolamines, deÔ¨Āned as "a primary amine
in which the amino group is attached to a tertiary carbon atom, or a secondary amine in

The absorber model developed by Gabrielsen et al is an extension of the absorber model
developed by Pandya [21]. The end goal of these models are to simulate an absorption

by Pandya, a key feature to do so is to be able to describe the temperature variations

in the absorption process as the physical properties associated with the Ô¨āuxes. If the
temperature is not controlled, the equilibrium curve could cross the operating line which
would result in a pinch situation where the driving force for the mass Ô¨āuxes become zero.
It is also of paramount importance that the bulk gas temperatures is ensured to be above
the dew point to ensure that fog formation is prevented. The bulk liquid Ô¨āow rate can be

number of ways, as already described, and the two-Ô¨Ālm theory was used in this case. In
addition to the assumptions already described, the following assumptions simplify the

‚ÄĘ The system is adiabatic as it approximately is in industrial set ups.

2.4
Computational implementation
The main function of the MatLab program that Gabrielsen wrote, based on the rate-based
model described in the previous sections, is to solve the Dirchlet boundary value problem.
The Dirchlet boundary value terminology refers to the fact that boundary conditions have
Ô¨Āxed values, which is the eight parameter values in the gas and liquid inlets. The program
does so through the inherent MatLab function ‚Äôbvp4c‚Äô, a general boundary value problem
solver, which again calls a subfunction that‚Äôs setting up the ODEs based on the values
recieved from the main script.
The ‚Äôbvp4c‚Äô code utilizes a collocation method for the numerical solving of the ODEs
[14]. The code is set up to divide the interval of integration into a mesh of points which
provides a set of subintervals. An error of the numerical solution for each subinterval is
estimated and evaluated based on a given tolerance criteria. If the tolerance criteria isn‚Äôt
satisÔ¨Āed, the mesh size is increased and the new set of solutions is found. This is repeated
until a solution is found that satisÔ¨Āes the tolerance criteria for each subinterval or until
a maximum mesh size is reached in which case the solver is abandoned with the solution
not satisfying the tolerance critera. The iterative method requires the points of the initial
Page 25 of 82
==PAGE:36==
mesh as well as an estimated solution to each of these to be provided. This can be done
with the built-in routine ‚Äôbvpinit‚Äô which, from a provided mesh size and set of parameter
values, sets up a set of equally spread mesh points all with the same parameter value.
Overall, the main functions of the main script is to:
1. Set up of the tower speciÔ¨Ācation, e.g. height and cross sectional area
2. Calculation and set up of the initial values of the eight parameters solved for, serving
as the boundary conditions
3. Set up of an initial guess for the bvp-solver
4. SpeciÔ¨Ācation of the properties for the bvp-solver, most importantly the maximum
allowed mesh size for the solution array and the error tolerance between the inputted
and the calculated boundary values
5. Calling the bvp-solver, ‚Äôbvp4c‚Äô, which returns a solution array containing the solu-
tions of the eight ODEs for each grid point of the column
6. Vizualization of the solutions

Limit analysis of the concentration gradients
It can be somewhat elusive to interpret the ODEs governing the concentration gradients,

Implementation of the pinch convergence routine
In order to make the model more stable, better initial guesses had to be fed to the inherent
bvp-solver. As mentioned before, the inherent MATLAB "bvpinit" routine sets up at data
structure that contain initial guess based on the inputted initial values for each of the
Page 58 of 82
==PAGE:69==
eight parameters solved for. This initial guess is simply formed by creating a solution
where the the parameter values at each grid point is equal the initial parameter value, i.e.
keeping the parameters constant throughout the column. This initial guess corresponds
to nothing at all happening in the column, which is suÔ¨Écient for easier cases, but as seen
from the stress test, this is clearly not good enough for more diÔ¨Écult cases.
Therefore, a successive substitution routine, based on similar iterative procedures used
for Ô¨āash calculations [19], was implemented. The main idea is to approach the solutions
in steps where a solution is calculated at each step and then used as an initial guess for
the next step. In algrebraic terms, the procedure can be wrtitten as below where x is the
solution vector and f is the bvp-solver.
x = f(x)
x(k+1) = f(x(k))
The trick is then to change the conditions, for which a solutions is found at each step,
in such a way that the new solution is not far from the initial guess used at that step.
Since the mass transfer coeÔ¨Écient controls the mass transfer, this coeÔ¨Écient provides just
the kind of handle needed to do so. By multiplying the mass transfer coeÔ¨Écient with a
damping factor, őī, having a start value just above 0 and going up to 1, the system is
damped so that mass transfer is slowly increased. By increasing the damping factor there
is "pinched" toward the Ô¨Ānal solution so to speak. Hence, having a damping factor close
to 0 corresponds to have such a little mass transfer that the constant initial guess given
by the "bvpinit" routine is good enough to get started. The procedure is Ô¨Ānished when
őī = 1 corresponding to using the full value of the mass transfer coeÔ¨Écient. The Ô¨Āgure

Step size
Once the pinch convergence routine provides stable solution, the time it spends on doing
so becomes the next issue to address. One way to make the routine faster is by increasing
the step size, ‚ąÜ, of the damping factor increments and hence limiting the number of times

Chapter 5
Part III - The additive model
As previously described the the hope of adding VRAs to the well-known CO2-alkanolamine-
H2O is that it can result in energy savings by reducing vapour pressures doing the solvent
regeneration. It is of importance that this addition do not decrease the performance of
the absorber too much. The results from preliminary experiments do not look too promis-
ing (internal experiments at the CERE institute at DTU, article will be published doing
2019). However, if a predictive model could be developed that accounts for the addition
of VRAs, then it could be further investigated whether an optimal solvent mixture, that
includes VRAs, exist.
In this work a simplistic model have been developed, which is validated against experimen-
tal data from a pilot plant scale experiment with an urea-MEA-water solvent (0.3-0.3-0.4
wt%). The purpose of this have been to 1) investigate whether a model taking the ad-
dition of VRAs into account can give improved predictions compared with the original
model, and 2) to investigate which properties in the model that is most sensitive to the
inclusion of VRAs.

Conclusions and future work
Though it was possible to achieve a good Ô¨Āt between the experimental loadings (for
experiments conducted with additive containing solvents), it was more challenging to do
so for the liquid temperature proÔ¨Āles. In general the high sensitivity of correlation model of
Billet and Schulte toward the operational conditions, made Ô¨Ātting against experimental
data challenging but it was possible to reach a good Ô¨Āt for the reference experiment.
Therefore it is possible that the poor Ô¨Āt against the temperature proÔ¨Āles is caused by the

of VRAs.
Though it could clearly be seen that the mole ratio of water was signiÔ¨Ācantly reduced
compared with the original model, both models behaved somewhat similar, indicating that
change of the mole ratios alone do not encompass the eÔ¨Äects of adding VRAs. Variation to
a few of the correlations governing the chemophysical properties, the liquid heat capacity,
the liquid density and the Henry‚Äôs constant, did not change the predicted curves much.
This could however also be a reÔ¨āection of the insuÔ¨Écient way these correlations were
extended to account for VRAs, since they are semi-empirical expressions developed for
MEA-water solvents.
Due to the above reasons, it was not possible to develop a rate-based model, extended to
account for VRAs in a simplistic fashion, nor was it possible to gain much information
about the which properties that is most sensitive to the inclusion of VRAs, though it
doesn‚Äôt appear that the model is very sensitive to changes to the additives.
Future work should be focused at further Ô¨Ātting the experimental data with simulated
curves to gain certainty whether the model‚Äôs actual capabilities regarding simulation of
VRA containing solvents. The obtainment of semi-empirical correlations of the chemo-
physical properties for a VRA-MEA-H2O-CO2 system should also been conducted to
verify whether correct description of these could improve the performance of the model.

Chapter 6
Conclusion
Overall was the three-stage rocket of this thesis partly successful. While reviewing the
original model by Gabrielsen, a new basis model was developed which behavior was found
to be similar to that of the original model. This model was then successfully extended
with a pinch convergence routine making it more robust to changes of the operational
conditions. The added robustness was utilized in the development of an additive model
that through a simplistic approach accounted for the mole ratios of the solvent components
when VRAs are added. However, updating the mole ratios to account for additives did
not seem to give the additive model any improved simulation capabilities, regarding VRA
containing additives, compared with the original model.
