Abstract
The market share of electric vehicles (EV) around the world has been in steady rise over
the past years, and its impact is seen on the grid, public infrastructure and emissions.
From the later, operational emissions pose many questions, yet have seen a simplistic
approach in their estimation until recently, when studies have focused on marginal carbon
intensities (MCI).
The purpose of this thesis is to analyse the operational emissions of electric private ve­
hicles in the danish grid and the underlying opportunities they present. The first step
consisted in analysing the effect of using either marginal or average values to calculate
the carbon footprint of EVs. Tomorrow, a company focused on data­driven environmen­
tal solutions, provided both timeseries and with the use of a linear optimisation model it
was concluded that using average carbon intensities instead of marginal can lead to a
30% emission underestimation. A key factor in reducing the MCI value, and therefore EV
emissions, in Eastern Denmark (DK2) was found to be marginal imports from Sweden,
mainly composed of hydro generation.
Secondly, this study explored the impact of utilising different charging methods in DK2 and
compared overall emissions to such of an internal combustion engine (ICE). The results
displayed that EVs can save at the minimum 63% of emissions compared to ICEs, and up
to 78% using MCI smart­charging. In addition, the misestimation of EV emissions by the
ACI meant its smart­charging scenario saved around 10% less compared to the optimal
MCI smart­charging. Two key factors in emission reduction were found to be charging
frequency and charging window (both size and time of the day), while charger’s rated
power had a limited effect. Having more frequent charging sessions and longer charging
windows allowed for the lowest final emissions. However as overall EV numbers rise,
models like the one presented may become void as the large load in the system will have
an effect on the marginal emissions.
After analysing emissions based on assumptions and average values, an algorithm was
developed to provide average and aggregate results for Frederiksberg Kommune. This
algorithm took vehicle user behaviour data and accounted for range anxiety, to derive
emissions from EV users according to three types: home, public and work chargers. This
last block found that home chargers under immediate charging presented the largest emis­
sions of the three. Oppositely, under MCI smart­charging this trend turned around and
home chargers were the lowest emitting subset. This supported the idea of having long
charging sessions. According to the findings and group sizes, Frederiksberg current EV
fleet emits 366 tonnes of gCO2eq but could save an extra 45 tonnes. Public chargers were
studied under shortened charging windows and it was found that a two hour in the after­
noon could lead to an 8% decrease in savings.

CHAPTER1
Introduction
1.1
Context
The primary energy consumption of the world reached a new maximum of 162,194 TWh
in 2019, with just under 26 TWh of those associated to electricity generation [1]. The
immense energy dependency today’s society experiences has brought with it a series of
environmental impacts and challenges that have caught the attention and concern of the
many.
An example of these are Green House Gases (GHG), mainly originated from the com­
bustion of fossil fuels, which is progressively leading to the rise in temperature of Earth’s
atmosphere, known as greenhouse effect. In the electricity sector, the challenge of reduc­
ing GHG emissions is being met through Renewable Energy Sources (RES), which are
able to generate energy without relying on fossil fuel combustion. However, with impact
limited to the electricity grid and while its presence still remains low, new challenges are
appearing due to its unpredictable nature and lack of flexibility, which hinder the stability
of the power system where generation and consumption must always match.
Traditionally, demand has had little to no flexibility, hence supply has had to adapt to follow
the daily and seasonal trends the load displays. However, in the last couple of years, the
ability for demand to adapt and partially change its shape has risen. This has introduced
a term into the electric grid called demand­side management, which is defined as the
modification of energy consumption through various means to the benefit the power grid.
As an example of this, which serves as coupling of the electricity and transport sectors,
the Electric Vehicle (EV) has come into picture and can potentially provide that flexibility
RES lack, while reducing emissions of the transport sector. EVs have a battery pack that
can be charged to later power, through an electric motor, its wheels. This opens up a
broad range of possible scenarios regarding uses, benefits and drawbacks of EVs and
therefore, in the same manner as RES, provide no perfect solution; its integration must
be dealt with properly to transition to a sustainable future.
A simple benefit from EVs comes from the lack of combustion in the vehicle’s motor, which
reduces emissions within cities, leading to an improvement in air quality. EVs, however,

do have emissions associated to their operation phase, as the electricity used to charge
their batteries has a certain carbon footprint. On the other hand, the main problem holding
back the EV from reaching its maximum potential is the available range in the battery

are improving and hopefully will reach a point where they can be comparable to Internal

ICEs? This will be one of the key points of this thesis.
Multiple studies can be found regarding life­cycle emissions of EVs compared to those
from Internal Combustion Engines that have arrived at diverse results. In [2] it is stated
that, while end­of­cycle emissions are still unclear for the EV, emissions associated to
manufacturing processes are on a general basis higher for EVs compare to ICEs. Al­
though EVs are at a disadvantage due to the production of the battery pack, this disparity
may narrow with time as more experience is gained on the underlying processes, and
technology matures.

studying emissions of EVs, as such depend on type and size, as well as the emissions
derived from the charging process of the battery. The later is dependent on the coun­
try under study as each has its own energy mix, resulting in differing carbon footprints
(see Figure 1.1 above). Because of this, results from EV studies diverge from one an­
other depending on the implemented methodology, the country under investigation and
the assumptions made. It is worth noting that the use­phase emissions of ICE are more
homogeneous on an international scale, as combustion of petroleum does not differ no­

Currently in Denmark there is a total of 31,000 EVs, [5], small value compared to the
2030 objective that foresees a surge to just under a million, [6]. At the present level
of penetration, net emissions from the charging of EVs are not as significant as other
sources, and are therefore quantified using average emission values as seen in [7] and
[8].

As displayed in Figure 1.2, EV market share is growing in scale and this technology is
becoming a notable source of pollution. As the International Energy Agency (IEA) stated
in its 2020 Global EV Outlook, [6], global electricity demand from EVs can grow up to 550
TWh by 2030, compared to the 80 TWh seen in 2019. In addition, it gave insight on the
inferior level of emissions EVs were having due to a lower carbon intensity of the electricity
systems compared to ICEs emissions levels. It must be pointed out that calculations in the
EV Outlook use average annual carbon intensities of generation (including transmission,
distribution and charging losses).
An important aspect when comparing the life­cycle of different energy sources or its emis­
sions is the extent of the study. Generally there is no specification of the scope of the
study, as long as the same is applied to all for a fair comparison. Figure 1.3 shows an
example of a life cycle assessment based on different scopes for EVs.

The final decision will be based on the available data and time. Nevertheless, two different
studies analysing the same topic under the same scope may yield differing results, due
to assumptions and data available.
In this project, well­to­wheel (or grid­to­wheel for EVs) will be studied. This has been
chosen because it provides the correct basis for EV and ICE comparison. For EVs, grid
(transmission and distribution) losses, charger efficiency and EV vehicle consumption will
be accounted for, with the later in a more simplified manner compared to the displayed in
Figure 1.3. The same approach will be performed for ICE, using general emission rates
and data from technical reports to account for upstream (well) emissions.
As found during literature review, the task of emission quantification has seen a simplistic
approach in many cases, and average values such as the Average Carbon Intensity (ACI)
on an hourly and yearly basis have been the norm. This time­series is calculated as
the hourly (or yearly) average electricity mix times the corresponding emission factor of
each technology, and it depicts the state of the overall system emission­wise. In other
words it represents the average kgCO2eq/kWh of electricity consumed at the point of final
consumption. However, in the last decade a deeper analysis of the emissions of EVs has
been performed by many institutions and new insight has been gained.
In these new studies the focus is shifted towards marginal emissions. A new time­series
emerges named Marginal Carbon Intensity (MCI), which is defined in [9] as the incre­
mental change in carbon emissions as a result of a change in demand. This signal helps
quantify the emissions of charging an EV at a certain hour of the day by focusing on the
generation units that react to this increase in demand. MCI models can be grouped into
three different methods, according to [9]:
4

==PAGE:17==
• Single Marginal Plant MCI: This simplistic approach reduces the complexity of the
problem by estimating the marginal power mix to be composed uniquely by Com­
bined Cycle Gas Turbine power plant. The MCI is then calculated using the emission
factor of such technology, plus system losses.
• Empirical Approach MCI: Empirical approaches use historical data­sets and linear
regression models to estimate the average response each generation technology
has to changes in demand. As any empirical approach, its accuracy is highly corre­
lated to the quality of the data available. This method will be later explained in more
detail.
• Merit Order Based MCI: This last method divides power plants into base­load and
marginal, with the later being the ones able to adjust their output to balance changes
in demand. With these, a second merit order curve is composed based on price or
utilisation level of marginal units, which provides the final signal as a result.
In [10], the use MCI forecasts relying on short­term load forecasts is proposed as an
emission saving approach. In addition, after a thorough comparison of its empirical MCI
with the system’s ACI, its findings show that the use of average values in EV emission
quantification leads to a major underestimation of the real values.
Ultimately, the use of annual or hourly granularity, and marginal or average in the energy
sector is a matter of the objective behind the study and the time available to develop
it. The use of annual average mix, straightforward and simple, can be useful for quick
country to country comparisons. On the other hand, when interpreting marginal demand
with the use of average values, hourly or annual granularity has little difference, per [11].
Here the same conclusion was reached as in previously mentioned papers; the use of
average values leads to major underestimation of EV emissions.
The appropriate accounting of emissions is the key for a sustainable roll­out of EVs, not
only due to the correct understanding of its impact, but also because it can lead to a min­
imisation of emissions thanks to smart­charging. Smart­charging is defined as the charg­
ing of EVs in an optimised manner through data analysis and communication. The idea
behind this is that the charging schedule will follow a certain signal, to maximise or min­
imise an objective function, subject to certain constraints. Examples of this is CO2 based
charging, grid congestion minimisation and renewable use maximisation. The scope of
this thesis is limited to CO2eq emissions minimisation, therefore the other two will not be
mentioned further on.
The impact of CO2 smart­charging will be linked to the number of hours available for
charging, their marginal carbon intensity and the energy demand. Natural time limitation
(due to behaviour of drivers) presents a new term called temporal flexibility, which defines
the amount of time charging can be postponed while ensuring the charging needs are
met. Theoretically, for any charging event, more temporal flexibility means larger emission
savings, although this ultimately depends on the evolution of the MCI during the charging

The last main variable in EV studies is the energy demand. This is dependant on factors
such as EV specifications (battery capacity and charging limitation) and drivers’ behaviour
(daily driving distance and parking duration). In this project, it is expected to be a deciding
factor on the potential savings smart­charging can achieve.
On behaviour of EV users, simplistic methods can be seen where a distinction is made
between home and work periods, with different charging scheme proposals, [8] [10] [11].
Another common solution are empirical approaches, where real data is available from
transportation surveys, [2] or EV dynamometer, [12]. Finally, specific models can be ob­
tained from institutions, such as NREL’s BLAST­V [13], that develops charging curves
based on EV model, charging infrastructure and trips, and can provide good estimates of
consumption, [7].
Any assumption or approach in this regard can be valid enough, but also be prone to errors
or open to improvement. Studies using dynamometer usually have scarce data, which
may lead to noise, transportation surveys are mainly done on ICE users, and complex

The first step in this project is fully understanding how the MCI signal used works and
how it is derived. This chapter will first provide some background and then will proceed
to explain and analyse Tomorrow’s MCI model. Finally, a recap of the data provided by
the company for this project is presented. This includes past data of Denmark’s energy
system as past energy mix, carbon intensity and import/export breakdown, obtained from
Tomorrow’s API, electricityMap [3]. All these data­sets came on an hourly basis and will
be explained thoroughly after the model has been presented.
As previously mentioned, the rising EV penetration is deriving in an increase in electricity
demand whose emissions cannot be accounted by the ACI. Indeed emissions depend
on many factors related to the power system’s state. Ultimately, one simply multiplies
the average or marginal electricity mix times the corresponding emission factor of each
technology to obtain the ACI or MCI of the system at a specific hour; in other words perform
a weighted average. To comprehend how this electricity mix is formed, an understanding
of the merit order curve is indispensable.
The merit order curve is constructed by ranking generation units (or imports) based on
their bidding price in ascending order. Units are then brought online to satisfy demand
based on this curve, leading to a cost minimisation. This is known as ”economic dispatch”
and, ideally, bids reflect the cost of producing one more unit of electricity such power plant
has. Usually, the cheapest generation units tend to be RES, as these do not have the
extra operational costs conventional power plants have due to fuel consumption, and are
therefore called first. Figure 2.1 shows an example of a merit order curve, which displays
the pattern mentioned.
Marginal Carbon Intensity for Electric Vehicle Charging ­ Benchmarking and Scaling
9
==PAGE:22==
Figure 2.1: Example of a merit order curve, source [14]
These definitions imply that as more demand is added to the system, more expensive
and polluting energy sources will be brought up. The cheapest power plant with spare
capacity at any given time in the system is called the Marginal Power Plant. This already
speaks of the inconsistency behind the use of average values in this field, as many power
plants in the average energy mix are already at full capacity. Average values reflect on
which resources (and how much of each) have been used for the current demand level,
whereas marginal focus on what is currently available.
In addition to this, from a stability perspective, in any power system there must always
be a balance between power generation and consumption. Usually, demand has a lack
of flexibility, hence, whenever an imbalance in either occurs only certain generation units
are able to react. These units tend to be coal, oil, gas, biomass or hydro power plants
as, opposite to RES, they are capable of varying their power output at desire subject to
certain ramping rates.

Before digging into the model, it must be mentioned that there is no common ground
on how marginal emissions should be calculated. This is partly due to the absence of
a truth­signal; not one measuring device that can provide a definite value of how much
an increase in consumption will emit. Moreover, the reduced amount of flexible load in
power systems in the last decades means there has been no imperative need of calcu­
lating marginal emissions in a precise manner for countries or international organisms,
translating in a lack of official or accepted guideline today. Therefore, one cannot eval­

By solving equation 2.2 one can infer on the 1st order marginal origin of a given country; 1st
referring to just neighbouring countries. However, this is only the first step, as the marginal
generation of a neighbouring country may not originate within its own electricity system,
but in an another area where the mix might differ. Therefore, the problem presented is
not as straightforward. To solve it, a method named flow tracing is used, which follows
the chain of imports to obtain the n­th marginal origin.
1. For every area under analysis, the origin of marginal electricity is estimated using
equation 2.2.
2. A flow conservation equation is written for each country, meaning a change in im­
ports in an country yields an equal variation in demand in that same country.
3. The system of equations is solved, providing the answer to the origin of marginal
electricity for each studied area.
The solution is a matrix of as many rows and columns as countries studied, with values
ranging from 0 to 1 representing the ratio of marginal demand covered by that country.

next paragraph dives in­depth into this.
As a final note, the extended version of the MCI correlation matrix showed an inverse
correlation between hydro power consumption on the average mix and on the marginal
mix; periods with larger hydro power generation at the current system state saw lower
marginal consumption covered by hydro. Hydro power plants are usually linked to a water
reservoir, which can be considered its ”fuel” tank. Inherently, water in a reservoir has its
own marginal cost; the less water remaining in the reservoir the more costly it is to spend
one more unit of water. Therefore, as more energy is coming from hydro at a certain point
in time, less will be available at the margin, explaining the inverse correlation between the
ACI and the MCI. Another plausible explanation is the capacity limit of the power plants
itself and the interconnection cables between Sweden and Denmark.

the 15 minute marks would be rounded to the nearest timestep, while MCI was expanded
into the smaller granularity by simply using the corresponding hour value. Therefore, each
hour in the MCI was repeated 4 times in the model.

be homogeneous every 4 timesteps, the model might decide to charge at a lower power
output during those instead of charging in less timesteps at higher power. Despite this
leading to the same total emissions, efficiency of the charger will drop, meaning more
energy demanded. By charging at rated power (as long as the remaining battery capacity
allowed for it) this problem was solved.

3.4
Assumptions
Before digging into the definition of the study cases, the general assumptions of the project
are presented here. Each case has its own assumptions too, which are described in each
section.
A major assumption in this project was the charger’s efficiency, η. In reality, this depends
on the power output at any given time and the EV state of charge, among other. [22]
found that at power output close to rated, efficiency could reach values over 90%, while,
as power output reduced, it could drop down to 20%. On the other hand, [23] focused
on efficiency test method for EV chargers and found that as SOC levels surpassed a
certain threshold, the power output of the charger dropped significantly, therefore taking
a significant amount of time to charge from 80­100%.
This thesis’ scope focuses on the emission side of EV roll­out, hence efficiency was made
constant at 90% as a trade­off between the maximum possible efficiency, over 90%, and
the drop in power output experienced near full charge. Future works may focus on improv­
ing the model by adding a variable charging efficiency. Nevertheless, the presented linear
optimisation model charged at rated power if possible so below 90% charger efficiency
occurrence was low.
Moreover, depending on the type of charger and type of vehicle, maximum charging power
and battery size, Pch and Bcap respectively, will vary greatly throughout the population. To
avoid major complications, this study will part from an initial base case and then develop
sensitivities to gain insight of the importance of these two variables. A battery size of

22kW), [25]. The final decision was 11kW as it is expected to be the most common AC
(slow) chargers, source [26].
In addition, an important aspect regarding EVs is their energy consumption. In the same
manner as ICE, their laboratory efficiencies differ from the so­called ”real­world” efficiency.
The chosen value was 0.2kWh/km, as despite the fact that it will always depend on the

4.1
Empirical comparison of CO2 signals
One of the most important aspects of this project is the analysis of EV integration, its poten­

besides one of the sensitivities, will occur during this span. According to the IEA’s 2020

on this information a night charging window from 19:00 to 6:00 was chosen. This period
contains the average maximum and minimum of the MCI, so ultimately it will provide a
theoretical maximum and minimum of EV emissions.
This project’s first RQ is focused on understanding how much better the MCI is compared
to the ACI, in EV emission quantification. As an initial step, the immediate charging model
will be used to charge a fixed amount of energy throughout the night, for a whole year.
Later, emissions related to the resulting charging schedules will be calculated with both
signals and compared. This will allow for a true quantification of the misestimate of the
ACI.
Secondly, smart­charging will be performed for the same periods using both MCI and ACI.
Again, related CO2eq footprint will be calculated with the MCI. This alongside immediate
charging will provide a comparison of EV impacts against ICE in Denmark, RQ2. As an

extension, price based smart­charging using DK2­Elspot prices is presented. Overall, the
evaluation of the charging schemes will be composed of emission, total cost, and charging
distribution.
An important assumption in this section is that the days since the last charging session
of an EV and the energy demanded per charging session follows a linear relationship.
In real life these are truly related, but may not show such a simple relationship, as daily
trips will vary depending on user and day of the week. In this thesis the assumption is
made based on the 43.4km driven per day and user in Denmark and an EV consumption
of 0.2 kWh/km, so each day the battery will be discharged 8.68kWh. The same one year
period will be analysed for each energy demand/day(s) between consecutive events, so
implicitly it will serve as a sensitivity study.
Thirdly, a set of sensitivity analysis are proposed in order to study the impact of various
variables on emissions. These will test the assumptions explained in the previous sec­

rated power study, a one year simulation per Pch value will be performed, for an energy
demand of 34.72kWh (4 days since the last charging session). Pch will focus on Type 2
EV chargers, from 3.7kW to 22kW, as this is expected to be the most common charger
type for home charging.
Finally, the charging window sensitivity will provide insight of the potential savings each
plug­in hour and the effect of a larger or shorter windows have on emissions. A large scale

two months and computation time would have been notably high.
4.2

Up to this point, this thesis has developed simulations based on general informed as­
sumptions, using data relevant to the whole of Denmark. This use case presents a spe­

is an urban area with a population of just over 100,000 residents, source [34], and just
under 500 EVs [33] (≈1.6% of the total personal vehicle fleet). As an urban area, it has
a high population density and a below average daily driving distance.

combine a large dataset obtained from the Danish National Travel Survey (TU) with an
empirical model to obtain the energy demand and charging window duration.
These will be inputs to the smart and immediate charging models, presented in Section
2.1, in order to provide an estimation of how many emissions the average EV user in

Frederiksberg can save and the yearly emissions of the whole Kommune. This will there­

performed in order to assess the impact of a reduce charging window in public infrastruc­

The general approach used was to perform a series of simulations and average out re­
sults. This idea stems off the principle known as Statistical Ensemble, where a large
number of sets or collections, assumed macroscopically identical, are studied and to­
gether represent the probability distribution of the whole system. In this case, the system
is Frederiksberg population.
4.2.1
TU survey and synthetic data
The Danish National Travel Survey is a survey that gathers travel patterns among the
Danish population [35], whose first generation started in 1975 at a small case. Currently,
data collection is done via web and telephone interview in a continuous manner, as part
of a 3rd generation that started back in 2006. A large number of variables are drawn from
this, however not all are relevant to this thesis. The important features are listed below:
• Day: Unix time of the day the interview sampled.
• Day type: Trip day type (normal weekday, Friday/weekday before holiday, weekends
and other special dates). This variable is important in the later imputation of agents.
• Parking conditions at home/occupancy: States whether the agent has parking con­
ditions at the location of interest that could allow for continuous, uninterrupted charg­
ing.
• Session weight: Weighting factor that quantifies how much a specific agent repre­
sents the population’s behaviour. It is scaled such that one year’s weights add up
to the annual average day traffic.
• Departure and Arrival time­stamps: Time stamps corresponding to the departure
and arrival of each trip during the day.
• Departure and Arrival purpose: States the location from where the car departed and
the location where the car arrived to for each trip.
• Trip length: Distance driven in each trip, in kilometers. The sum of all distances
driven by a user in its daily activities was assumed to be the daily average driving
distance of the agent.
This dataset was provided as a csv with over 7,500 entries, also called agents. An agent
represents a one day behaviour of a person living in Denmark, that at some point in their
day went into and/or out of Frederiksberg throughout the day. However, as the study­
case focused on the population living in Frederiksberg, this shrunk down to 719 agents
only. Therefore, to have a larger pool of agents to pick from and account for the session
weights provided, a synthetic dataset was developed by co­supervisor Fábio Hipólito.
Marginal Carbon Intensity for Electric Vehicle Charging ­ Benchmarking and Scaling
35
==PAGE:48==
This generated agents according to the weights and allowed for a fine approximation of
Frederiksberg, under the assumption that the survey is a fair representation of the overall
population.
In addition, the synthetic set took into account the type of days the previous and next
days were for each agent. This allowed for the imputation of two important outputs: the
previous day last arrival and next day first departure. The former was used for all agents
charging at night, as it will be explained later. The final size of the entire dataset was
approximately 600,000, which reduced down to just under 240,000 agents when filtering
for inhabitants of Frederiksberg.
4.2.2
Agent selection
Consequently, a large number of agents must be studied in order to avoid noise from
spoiling the results. Another option is to perform multiple simulations with fewer agents
and average out the results. Both these methods should eliminate the effect of outliers. It
was finally decided to proceed with 3 simulations of 1000 agents per day (mainly due to
time limitation), with each simulation performed on 2019 MCI data.
In reality 1000 agents per day is indeed an oversample of the current EV penetration in
Frederiksberg (475 EVs). However, the sample will be partly formed by public charging
agents. Analysing only the population of the municipality means underestimating occur­
rence of charging events from public charging at Frederiksberg, as competition for these
spots from users not living within the municipality is overlooked.
Ultimately, the objective of this chapter is to provide an average estimate. Therefore as
long as a big enough sample is used to smoothen out noise, results should be valid.
Future works may focus on developing a different simulation that accounts users living in
and just parking in the municipality.
From the synthetic dataset, the 1000 agents must be picked every day. Agents may be
imputed in a variety of ways. Two key factors are the type of day under analysis, as people
behave differently on weekdays, weekends and holidays, and the previous and next day
type, outbound traffic will vary depending on this. Note that the variables explained in
4.2.1 that define the type of day of the agent include such information. The final output
of this function provided the necessary information of the agent behaviour to perform the
optimisation.

CHAPTER6
Conclusion
The rise of EVs in today’s society has brought with it many questions and possibilities. A
few years ago this was reflected in the introduction of marginal emission studies, which
could help introduce EVs (and other flexible demands) to the grid in the most emission­
friendly manner. This project aimed at understanding such marginal carbon intensities
and why the should be used in electric vehicle studies. In addition, through a set of study
cases, a deep analysis of the potential impact of EVs emission­wise was performed. Ulti­
mately, this is heavily dependent on the zone’s grid, type of EV and vehicle utilisation. The
later, was integrated by means of a large dataset of real driving behaviour. This meant
prioritising EV integration in society by meeting users travelling demand.
The first research question this study focused on was: RQ1: How do EV emissions quan­
tify and compare based on MCI and ACI?. The correct estimation of emissions inherently
allows for an optimal integration via smart­charging. This thesis determined that the use
of the ACI to measure emissions of electric­mobility could underestimate true emissions
up to 30%.
With the use of a smart­charging model, EVs charging schedules were optimised following
both MCI and ACI, and compared to immediate charging. It was found that any smart
scheduling led to improved results compared to immediate charging. However, the use
of the MCI as a charging signal provides 20% more emission savings than ACI.
The second research question proposed was: RQ2: How do EVs and ICE compare
emission­wise and what factors can help increase savings?. It was found that regard­
less of the charging method, EVs in DK2 may save at least 63% of emissions compared
to ICE (using immediate charging) and up to 78% (MCI smart charging), so the transition
to electromobility will overall be positive in the short to medium run.
For the second part of RQ2, this project developed some sensitivity analysis and found
that the most determining variables were the charging windows’ day location and duration.
Moreover, to a lesser extent the charging power and the charging frequency proved to be
quite significant. To obtain minimised emissions, frequent MCI smart­charging with long
charging windows at night and 22km should be applied.

The rest of the project was focused on answering RQ3: What are the potential emission
savings for the average Frederiksberg EV driver? And the whole Kommune?. Behavioural
data was used in the model and it was found that the average user in Frederiksberg can
save up to 2.6kgCO2eq per charging session, or 262.17kgCO2 per year, if they decided to
change from immediate to smart charging. These results correspond to home chargers,
who benefited from the longest temporal flexibility of all and the lowest MCI of the day.
The other subsets studied showed less savings; 35.20kgCO2eq and 42.09kgCO2eq per year
for public and work users respectively.
Accounting for the % each subset represents of the whole dataset and the current EVs
in Frederiksberg, the overall EV fleet emissions were found at 366,254kgCO2eq under im­
mediate charging. This already means savings of around 65% considering the ICEs they
substitute. These savings can be further increased to 69% by implementing MCI smart
charging in the Kommune’s EV users.
Moreover, through the study of the public parking limitations, it was found that a significant
impact was observed in the afternoon. While potential savings in public infrastructure due
to MCI smart charging are not as notable as those at home, late afternoon savings reach
the magnitude of 10%, compared to the 2% if the time limit is present.
Despite the notable savings obtained from using the MCI smart­charging, the future of
EV integration should not only depend on this. A potential side­effect of having a very
large load following the MCI is the rebound of carbon intensities levels. Therefore, a fast
updating MCI using feedback of the EV load already assigned is crucial. Nonetheless, as
Denmark’s EV fleet increases, the possibility of integrating its demand in the day­ahead
by means of an aggregator can help reduce related emissions and help guide future grid
investments.
As a final, more personal comment, the project saw many changes through its develop­
ment, that were expected, and had their own ”contingencies”. For example, agent data
from the TU survey arrived around a month later than expected, while other tasks such
as data processing was finished earlier than expected. This left extra­slack according to
the Gantt diagram. To tackle this extra effort was dedicated to the empirical comparison
in the meantime, which was finished earlier. From this I have learned that having a time
plan (regardless of the final accuracy) is very important, as it helps with status report half­
way through the project. Nonetheless, if I went back to the start of the project I would
better define the methodology and specify thoroughly the overall objectives, as part of the
project focused on reducing the scope.
6.1
Future work
While this thesis has looked into the emission side of EV integration, this matter is far
more complex. As a building block on top of the work presented, the combination of grid
constraints with emission reduction should be approached. This may come in the form
Marginal Carbon Intensity for Electric Vehicle Charging ­ Benchmarking and Scaling
65
==PAGE:78==
of extra constrains or a multi­objective optimisation model that accounts for the state of
the system and decides when charging is preferred. In addition, future models could
introduce uncertainty, as ultimately in real life the MCI signal and other grid state values
are forecasts.
On the MCI model’s side, as it has great potential in countries such as Denmark where it
differs greatly with the ACI, some work can be done to improve the geographical granu­
larity. Currently, the MCI is calculated on a national level or zone splitting based on large
areas. In reality, some deviations can be seen within those regions, as consuming energy
in the city of Copenhagen compared to consuming energy in the north of Zealand does
not have the same carbon footprint. This would mean taking more precise values within
their dataset, such as temperature, which is taken on a national average.
Moreover, as mentioned previously aggregators are a useful concept within EV integra­
tion. Ideally they could be able to group a large number of EVs and make offers in the
day ahead market, maybe even provide flexibility within the grid (something that has been
already studied). This requires of in­depth analysis based on user acceptance and overall
behaviour, while still being open to a high uncertainty.
