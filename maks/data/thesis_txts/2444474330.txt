Preface
The presented psychoacoustic experiments were performed with the participation of Nille Ellise
Kepp, audiologist, currently writing her PhD at the University of Copenhagen (KU) and with the
collaboration of Decibel, an association for children and adolescents with hearing loss.
Copenhagen Regional Ethics Committee regarded the study as having no negative consequences
or risks for the study participants, as the ﬁtting of the cochlear implant and hearing aids was not
modiﬁed. Any information apart from age, gender and type of technology was disregarded from
this study.
The study has been completed in the period between 1-8-2018 and 1-2-2019, as part of the
requirements of the MSc in Acoustics Engineering at the Technical University of Denmark (DTU).
All subjects were tested in the Audio Visual Immersion Lab (AVIL) in the department of Acoustics
of the Technical University of Denmark (DTU). All provided informed consent and received com-
pensation for their participation in this study through Decibel association [or Nille Ellise Kepp?].
This thesis was born with the interest of Decibel association for testing low-frequency hearing
(below the values tested of a regular audiometry test) in children with bilateral cochlear implants
and bilateral hearing aids. They wondered if a possible hidden residual hearing would be related
with the high variability in their speech in noise results. That seed grew into another two interesting
psychoacoustic questions: is their pitch perception and spatial localization skills related to the high
variability in speech in noise?
Taking this as a starting point, a Virtual Reality based gaming interface combining three tests
i.e.
pitch ranking, absolute spatial localization and residual hearing, was developed and tested
in a group of children with bilateral cochlear implants and bilateral hearing aids (note that the
participants were not the same as the ones of the speech in noise data explained before). The main
ideas for the experiments are shared with Nille Ellise Kepp and Jeremy Marozeau (supervisor of
the project). As future lines, it would be interesting to measure the speech in noise of the same
children that perform this test, in order to see if there is any relation between the results.

Abstract
This study investigates diﬀerent psychoacoustic aspects of children aged 6 to 8 years old, using a
Virtual Reality (VR) based gaming interface. A pitch ranking, spatial localization and residual
hearing experiment are tested in the same environment, motivated by their eﬀect on speech intel-
ligibility in noisy situations. VR is used in its full immersion mode i.e. head mounted display,
loudspeaker based 3D audio and interaction by the user using a hand-held joystick. The audio
stimuli processing, experiment automatization and data storage were done through Matlab. The
graphic environment and the user interaction were tracked by Unity.
Firstly, the relation of the spectral range in pitch perception was studied through a constant stim-
uli pitch ranking test, with fundamental frequencies at high-frequency (F0HF) and at low-frequency
(F0LF) spectral regions, ranging from twelve to one semitone diﬀerence. The results suggest com-
plex stimuli, due to the harmonic density, adds prominent peaks in the temporal envelope of the
signal. This may have been helpful for Normal Hearing (NH) subjects that could resolve them, and
detrimental for Cochlear Implant (CI) users, that could not [1, 2]. Therefore, the expected beneﬁt in
pitch perception from temporal cues for CI users was reduced [3], showing a similar pitch threshold
for both high and low frequencies. Hearing Aid (HA) users had lower thresholds at low frequen-
cies than at high frequencies, showing the beneﬁt of their acoustic low frequency hearing. Finally,
the semitone diﬀerence showed signiﬁcance among all three groups, consistent with literature [4].
The semitone threshold was lowest for NH subjects for both stimuli, followed by the HA users at
low frequencies and CI users at high frequencies, where they showed a lower threshold than HA users.
Secondly, Spatial localization was tested using narrowband noise stimuli centered at high-
frequencies (HFN), low-frequencies (LFN) and using broadband (WBN) noise combining both. The
spectral range and presented azimuth positions seemed to inﬂuence the spatial localization. In gen-
eral, for narrowband signals the RMS error was bigger at LFN than at HFN, although the diﬀerence
between both was smaller for HA users, probably due to their acoustic low frequency hearing, that
was more helpful than the information by the HA users at HFN. The general increase of error at
LFN could partially be due to the smaller bandwidth present, thus, eliciting a lower loudness and
interaural temporal cues. Therefore, as expected, the larger bandwidth of WBN stimuli was helpful
for spatial localization for the three groups, creating the smallest localization error, consistent with
literature [5]. Whereas CI users and some HA users had frontal lateral confusions, the NH subjects
did not, showing a better frontal accuracy than hearing impaired, probably due to the delay and
gain diﬀerences introduced by the bilateral hearing techologies. In addition, the visual accuracy of
the subjects was similar across hearing groups.
Finally, a low-frequency hearing detection test was performed in order to relate it with the
pitch perception and spatial localization skills, using frequencies below the lowest measurement in
audiometric tests i.e. 125 Hz. Few CI users seemed to detect the presented low-frequency stimuli.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Aristotle once stated that “Humans are social beings". In order to socialise, humans communicate
mainly by speech. Therefore, the sense of hearing plays an important role in social life. In a world
with 466 million people aﬀected by disabled hearing loss (HL), from which 34 million are children1,
this becomes a major concern. Social situations with multiple people talking at the same time are
very common in daily life. In order to follow a conversation in these cases, the target source needs
to be separated from the noisy background [12]. This process is very challenging for people with
hearing loss. Auditory cues such as pitch, spatial localization and low-frequency acoustic hearing
(residual hearing) have been proved helpful in speech in noise situations [13]. Although the pitch
and spatial localization performance of adult Cochlear Implant (CI) users and Hearing Aid (HA)
users has been widely studied, less information is known about the performance of children. This
becomes important as more and more children are implanted and given hearing aids in early child-
hood, due to the expansion of awareness and knowledge about hearing loss and the increase in
accessibility of the devices [4]. Furthermore, the use of new technologies such as Virtual Reality
serve as a motivational platform for testing children, keeping their interest and attention through
interactivity and playfulness.
1.1
Background
Before reporting previous studies in pitch perception, spatial localization, residual hearing followed
by current applications of Virtual Reality in children, a brief introduction to the concepts of sen-
sorineural hearing loss, hearing aids, cochlear implants and Virtual Reality is given.
1.1.1
Sensorineural hearing loss
This study focuses on sensorineural hearing loss. This type of hearing loss is created due to a damage
in the inner ear, i.e. due to partial or total loss of hair cells2. These are placed on top of the Basilar
Membrane (BM), placed inside the cochlea, that moves along with the movement created by the
acoustic wave that enters the ear. When the BM moves, the hair cells act as transducers from the
movement of the travelling wave to neural impulses (electrical) that are sent to the brain. The IHC
are the responsible organs that transmit the acoustic information towards the auditory pathway.
The Outer Hair Cells (OHC) amplify the sound selectively (called active mechanism), providing a

sharp frequency tuning. Sensorineural hearing loss often occurs when OHC are damaged, the active
mechanism decreases, which leads to nonlinearities in the Basilar Membrane. Thus, the frequency
selectivity is reduced. The frequency selectivity of a healthy cochlea can be modelled as a bunch of
ﬁlters along the BM, each tuned to a speciﬁc Characteristic Frequency (CF). The loss of OHC could
be pictured as the auditory ﬁlters getting broader [15, 14, 16]. While the active mechanism would
amplify the sounds diﬀerently depending on the input level, thus performing a compression, the loss
of the active mechanism would also get reduced, thus, reducing frequency selectivity and the the
dynamic range. The broaden of the auditory ﬁlters is also not symmetric, which produces upward
spread of masking, i.e. some frequency components may be masked even though the components
are above the absolute threshold of the hearing impaired. The absolute threshold (minimum audible
level) increases i.e. reduced sensitivity to weak sounds. In addition, forward masking is prolonged
i.e.sounds presented before the stimuli can reduce the perception the stimuli. Also, the distinction
between short and longer signals is deteriorated. Finally, the help of the second ear to the total
perceived sound is reduced. This has various consequences, such as making both the localization of
sounds and the suppression of the background noise a cumbersome task.

considered.

1.1.2.1
Hearing aid
A Hearing Aid (HA) is an electronic assistive device designed for improving hearing loss up to
moderate levels (see Table 1.1). Its basic functioning can be explained starting from a microphone
(see (1) in Figure 1.1) that picks up the sound signal. This signal is then processed, ﬁltered and
optimized to provide audibility in critical frequencies (see (3)).
Then, this signal is sent to a
receiver (loudspeaker, see (2)) which converts it back into an acoustic signal. This is powered by
a small battery see (3). Almost all the hearing aids have an adjustable gain that simulates the
compression in the healthy cochlea. Most HA contain algorithms to reduce environmental noise
and acoustic feedback. Sometimes these algorithms misinterpret stimuli and classify them as noise

1.1.2.2
Cochlear Implants
The Cochlear implant (CI) is a neural prothesis used to restore hearing in patients with severe to
profound sensorineural hearing loss (see Table 1.1). It attempts to bypass the external ear, middle
ear and hair cells of the inner ear, stimulating directly the ﬁbers via electrodes placed in the cochlea.
Its functionality is based on the fact that even if the transduction mechanism is damaged (e.g. hair
cells are non working), the neurons do not totally atrophy, and thus can be electrically stimulated,
producing a sensation of sound [14].
Figure 1.2: Cochlear implant sketch (www.cochlear.com).
Figure 1.2 shows the cochlear implant system. The system consists on a microphone (see (1)
in Figure 1.2) that takes the external sound, sends it to a speech processor (1) which divides the
spectral information into diﬀerent ﬁlters (or channels). From the processor, the information is sent
via radio-frequency to a receiver implanted behind the ear (see (4) in Figure 1.2), which in turn
3

1.1. BACKGROUND
February 1, 2019
sends pulses of electrical current to the electrode array inserted through the round window into the
cochlea duct (see (7) in Figure 1.2). This information is transmitted in two ways. On the one hand
the transmission is related to the corresponding active electrode (place coding, as the electrodes
follow a tonotopic organization) while on the other to the amplitude and time patter of stimulation
on the individual electrodes (temporal coding). This is done by short biphasic current pulses (20 to
400 microseconds per phase), delivered at a pulse rate of around 800 pulses per second (pps) [19].
There are diﬀerent speech processing strategies. Most of them discard the ﬁne structure and
encode the coarse features [20, 21]. One simple way to illustrate the common speech processing
being made in the CI could be found in the way simulations are created. Loebach et al. [22] de-
scribed the processing as dividing the spectrum into frequency bands, deriving the envelope from
each band, replacing the spectrum in each band with the carrier signal (type of noise, e.g. white
noise or sinusoid), modulating the carrier with the amplitude of each envelope and reassembling the
bands. Some of the perceptual challenges encountered in cochlear implant users in comparison with
NH peers are the bigger amount of spread of excitation of electrical current in the stimulation of the
neurons. This decreases the frequency selectivity, increasing the excitation pattern. In addition, the
dynamic range with electrical stimulation is smaller than that of the normal activation of auditory
nerve ﬁbers through excitation of inner hair cells.
Anu Sharma concluded that the optimal time for cochlear implantation is within the ﬁrst 3.5-4
years of life, and best before the second year of life, when central auditory pathways show the
maximum plasticity to sound stimulation. Children that become deaf before learning the language
and are early implanted with a cochlear implant show better results in spoken language than the
ones that are implanted later. This can be due to the capacity of the brain to reorganize during
childhood, partly because of the development in synaptic plasticity. The eﬀect of the time of the
implantation reveals that there is a sensitive period for cochlear implantation in early childhood
[23].

Virtual Reality
As described by Burdea [24], “Virtual Reality can be deﬁned as a simulation in which computer
graphics are used to create a realistic-looking world, creating a sensation of immersion. [...] this
world is not static but responds to the user’s input, creating interaction." Immersion is based on
creating an illusion of an alternate reality [25]. Perception of reality is created through our senses
i.e. sight, hearing, touch, smell and taste [26]. Many of those senses can be simulated by Virtual
Reality. The most common are sight, hearing and touch.
For presenting the sound, 3D audio technology is needed. In order to perform the auralization,
diﬀerent techniques exist. Vorlander et al described it as three blocks [27]:
• Sound generation: Vibration, friction, combustion...
• Sound propagation: Free ﬁeld, room, junctions, dumping...
• Sound reproduction: Headphones (binaural), Multichannel loudspeaker setups...
In the case of sound ﬁeld based reproduction, multichannel loudspeaker setups are used, that re-
produce sounds from diﬀerent locations, creating interaural diﬀerences by the head an torso.
The interaural diﬀerences can be also artiﬁcially simulated, by adding time delays or level dif-
ferences to each ear. The resulting ﬁltered signal is called Head Related Transfer Function (HRTF).

1.2.1
Pitch perception
Pitch is a subjective attribute, deﬁned as the attribute of the auditory sensation in terms of which
sounds can be ordered on a scale extending from low to high [29]. For a pure tone, pitch would be
its frequency; for a complex tone, pitch would be the fundamental frequency. A change in pitch is
useful for speech perception as it helps with the prosody [30], speaker identity, voice emotion, and
separation from target speech from noisy background [13]. Also, a change in pitch leads to a sense
in musical melody [31].
There are two main theories about the mechanisms used for pitch perception, Place theory and
Time theory. There is a third one, spectro-temporal theory, that claims that pitch perception is the
result of the combination of both place and time cues.
• Place theories are based on the stimulation of diﬀerent places along the BM that each fre-
quency components provokes. The auditory-nerve ﬁbers with characteristic frequencies are
stimulated, producing neural spikes.
This theory assumes that the Auditory System uses
these characteristic frequencies and the amount of activation in each channel to extract pitch.
However, there are some phenomena that are not explained by this theory (as an exclusive
strategy). One of these is Missing fundamental phenomena (where the pitch of a sound (F0
in case of complex sounds) is not present in the signal but is perceived [32, 33]. In addition, a
low correlation between FDLs and frequency selectivity, measured using psychophysical tuning
curves (PTCs) the two. It was concluded that frequency discrimination was not closely related
to frequency selectivity, suggesting that place models alone were not adequate to explain the
data [34].
• Time theories state that the input sound generates diﬀerent patterns of vibration at diﬀerent
places on the basilar membrane. These vibrations then create synchronous ﬁring of auditory-
nerve ﬁbers, due to the phase locking i.e. ﬁbers ﬁre exactly when the basilar membrane wave-
form has a certain phase value (see section ?? for more detail). The time intervals between
successive ﬁrings, called inter-spike intervals (ISI), occur at integer multiples of the waveform
period, and pitch is assumed to be extracted from these intervals (as seen in autocorrelation
models [35]). Temporal models have the advantage that they can use information from un-
resolvable components as well as resolvable components in the stimulus, thus accounting for
the missing fundamental phenomena [36, 37].
• Some theories argue that both mechanisms may work together, referred to as spectro-temporal
theories. The spectro-temporal theories assume that information from both low harmonics
5
==PAGE:17==

February 1, 2019
and high harmonics contribute to the determination of the pitch. The initial place/spectral
analysis in the cochlea is followed by an analysis of the time pattern of the neural spikes
evoked at each CF [31, 38]
Sensorineural hearing loss is associated with the partial or total loss of OHC (see Section 1.1 for
more details), resulting in a lower frequency selectivity. The umbrella of each auditory ﬁlters could
be seen as broader, and thus less frequency components get resolved than in a normal hearing (NH)
system. The mechanism for detecting pitch from unresolved harmonics is based on the periodicity
of the envelope created by the unresolved harmonics. For hearing impaired, this is a useful cue for
detecting pitch. It has been studied that the threshold for detecting a change in pitch (F0DL) wors-
ens with the presence of low numbered harmonics for hearing impaired people, whereas for normal
hearing people the F0DL for a complex tone is usually smaller when low numbered harmonics are
present. It has been argued that the output of the auditory ﬁlters could be more complex with low
number harmonics, with more prominent peaks. Hence, when analysed with broad ﬁlters would be
more challenging to analyse spectrally [1].
Zeng et al. used the advantage that CI have of testing the place and time cues independently, as
the electrodes can be stimulated with diﬀerent rates and in diﬀerent electrode placements. By in-
creasing the stimulation rate, i.e. simulating the eﬀect of a pure tone, they showed that the increase
in temporal pitch stops somewhere around 300 Hz [39]). The high-rate deterioration observed in
electric hearing could be due to the mismatch between place and rate of stimulation [40, 21]. This
could be due to the fact that place–frequency cues for the fundamental are generally poor due to
poor spectral resolution and envelope temporal cues for the fundamental are only salient at the
lower frequencies [41].
Research in pitch perception has shown that in the majority of cases, CI perform worse than NH
and HA users [40]. As an example, Looi et al. studied a group of adult CI users compared to HA
users in their pitch direction discrimination [42]. The tones they used were separated one octave,
half octave and a quarter octave apart, ranging from 98Hz until 740Hz, sung by a female and male.
CI users achieved an accuracy of 68%, 64% and 51% respectively, whereas HA users showed 90.2%,
83.7% 74.7% respectively. A recent study also looked at the diﬀerences between pitch direction
discrimination for NH, bilateral HA and bilateral CI users [43]. They stated that for a diﬀerence
of less than 4 semitones (from 880 Hz to 1122 Hz), NH subjects scored with an accuracy of 90% to
100%, HA with 70% to 100% and CI from 70% to 85%.
Looi and Radford studied the pitch ranking in a group of children with unilateral CI, unilateral
HA, Bimodal stimulation (BMS) and compared it with NH results [4].
They used sang vowels
ranging from 98Hz until 740Hz. At 3 semitones diﬀerence, CI scored with a mean of 67.45 (std =
14.54), HA users with a mean of 79.11 (std = 19.74), BMS with a mean of 63.99 (std = 19.99) and
NH with a mean of 88.79 (std = 11.03). They stated that the participants with acoustic residual
hearing scored better than the ones with electrical stimulation.
Leal et al. conducted two pitch perception tasks with adults using 29 Nucleus CI24 devices,
20 using the ACE strategy and 9 using the SPEAK strategy. The ﬁrst task was a same/diﬀerent
comparison of 12 pitch pairs. The second task consisted of eight paired musical excerpts where
the listener was asked to state whether the pitch became higher or lower, and where this change
occurred. For the same/diﬀerent comparison, the mean score was 90% correct, with 69% of subjects
scoring above 88%. In the higher/ lower task, subjects averaged 74% correct, with around half of

Stalinski et al. [46] also studied pitch ranking in a group of normal hearing (NH) children of

Each of theres studies used a diﬀerent spectral range, thus it makes it diﬃcult to compare their
results (despite Looi et al., that they used the same stimuli in both of their studies). Also, they
diﬀer in the stimuli type, some containing sang vowels and others piano notes. It is clear from the
previous studies that CI users perform worse than HA users and NH subjects.
1.2.2
Spatial Localization
Spatial localization accounts for the combination of the sounds received in both ears to perform
a spatial map in 3D space, and thus be able to detect and discriminate between sound sources at
diﬀerent positions. As Moore stated, “Two ears are deﬁnitely better than one" [51].
By cross-comparing intensity of arrival (also called Interaural Level Diﬀerences, ILD, as seen in
Figure 1.3), time of arrival (through the Interaural Time Diﬀerences, ITD, as seen in Figure 1.3)
and signal to noise ratio between both ears, a signal can be located. The head shadow and the
ﬁltering created by the shape of the pinnae and the reﬂections of the body help in the perception
of the direction of the sound [52].
ITD and ILD depend on the spectral content of the stimuli, Rayleigh [53] introduced a concept
called the Duplex Theory, where he explained that if the stimuli has low frequency (LF) content,
its wavelength is long compared to the size of the head. Thus, it will bend around the head without
being absorbed or shadowed by the head. The diﬀerence between both ears will rely in their phase
7
==PAGE:19==
1.2. LITERATURE REVIEW
February 1, 2019
(thus time) diﬀerence, ITD. If the stimuli has high frequency (HF) content, thus small wavelength
compared to the head, it will be shadowed by the head and thus the amount of energy arriving to
the next ear will be reduced, leading to a intensity diﬀerence between ears, ILD. Thus, ITD are
useful for stimuli with LF content whereas ILD are useful when the stimuli has HF content, thus,
with a broadband signal both cues can be used by the listener [54].
Figure 1.3: ITD (A) and ILD (B) representation. (A) An ITD occurs when the distance to one ear
is longer than to the other (red path is shorter than the blue path because the sound waves need to
travel around the head). (B) An ILD occurs when the level reaching one ear is higher than to the
other, this is created by can the shadow created by the head and torso (shown as a shadow on the
back of the head) [8].
When the auditory system is impaired, ITDs and ILDs get aﬀected by the fact that the spectral
and temporal resolution are decreased [14]. In addition, they introduce delays and gains that can
distort the interaural diﬀerences, e.g. by the loss of head shadow eﬀects due to gain diﬀerence
between ears. In the case of most Cochlear implants, the stimulation rate at the diﬀerent electrodes
is constant, and this stimulation does not correspond to the signal properties. Interaural Level
Diﬀerences (ILD) are preserved reasonably well in CI (although the mismatches in Automatic Gain
Control between devices can lead to misinterpretation of the ILDs). The Interaural Time diﬀer-
ences get more aﬀected due to the lack of low-frequency ﬁne-timing performed in CI [21]. Thus,
the ﬁne structure of the signal gets distorted. For hearing aid users, through the loss of the active
mechanism at the BM, the time that it would take the travelling wave to arrive to a certain place
in the BM may change.
Spatial localization is usually measured in two diﬀerent ways: (1) in terms of accuracy i.e. ab-
solute spatial localization, or of (2) acuity i.e. Minimum Audible Angle. The ﬁrst accounts the
capacity of localizing source positions e.g. the extent to which a child has been able to develop a
spatial-hearing “map.” It is often given as the error rate (Root Mean Square) for each position, or
averaged across positions or subjects. On the other hand, acuity The second is a relative measure
of discrimination among source positions, given by their angular diﬀerence [5].

Cochlear implanted children. In one study, they tested bilaeral CI users and NH children in a set
up of 15 loudspeakers separated 15◦apart, ranging from -70◦to 70◦. They found that NH children

1.2.3
Residual hearing
In listeners with moderate hearing impairment, the outer hair cells are usually damaged, but fre-
quency resolution is still provided by the passive vibration patterns of the basilar membrane. In the
case of the profund hearing impairment with a cochlear implant on, as the listening is made through
electrical stimulation with no involvement of the basilar membrane, the frequency resolution is even
poorer. Even if in speech in quiet cochlear implants can understand speech, when background noise
is present their performance drops down. Residual hearing accounts for a low-frequency acoustic
hearing, that is preserved in some cases of hearing loss. It has been proven beneﬁcial, mainly for
detecting pitch (F0) in situations with backgrdound noise [64].
Henry et al. [65] found that the spectral resolution was improved for the residual hearing CI
users, mainly in noisy background situations. Stickney et al. [66] suggested that poor pitch percep-
tion was the reason for the bad performance of CI users when a target sound was presented in a
competing talker background.
Residual hearing has also been proven beneﬁcial for spatial localization. Loiselle et al. [67]
studied a group of CI adults with hearing preservation, and tested the eﬀect on wideband (WB),
high frequency (HF) and low frequency (LF) content stimuli with and without their CIs. They
saw an improvement in spatial localization, measured as Root Mean Square (RMS) error for the
subjects with hearing preservation.

1.3
Research objectives
The presented literature shows the diﬀerences between NH children and children with HA and CI
in three psychoacoustic aspects. Generally, best performance has been shown for NH, followed by
children with HA and then by children with CI. Although the relation of the pitch and spatial
localization skills has been proved to be related with speech in noise results, this has been mostly
studied in adults. As the number of CI implanted children as well as the number of children with
hearing aids is growing, there is a need to understand their perception. In addition, it has been
noted that pitch and spatial localization tests are done always in independent test environment
conditions, making it diﬃcult to relate the results between them. Furthermore, not many auditory
test methodologies using Virtual Reality have been found in literature.
Therefore, the objective of this study is the combination of the aforementioned three psychoa-
coustic aspects using a children-friendly test environment through Virtual Reality technology. Sound
ﬁeld reproduction is chosen for auralization, which will be performed through a multichannel loud-
speaker array. This provides an ecologically valid environment, in which the perceived interaural
diﬀerences for the subjects are non artiﬁcially created. Children are expected to maintain the cog-
nitive eﬀort for the length of the test, by ﬁnding the motivation through the interaction and the
visual stimuli [24, 48]. Finally, [Merging the three tests in one same test environment gives the
possibiltiy to relate the same auditory maturity level at both pitch and spatial localization results,
which is important in case of the children, where the plasticity develops very fast [23].
The aims of this study are twofolded. Firstly, the results of pitch perception, spatial localization
and residual hearing will be tested and compared to existing literature and then the impression
produced by VR as a test platform on the children will be brieﬂy described.
• Comparison between the three hearing groups in the diﬀerent psychoacoustic tests
1. In pitch perception, spectral region will make CI users and HA users to perform better
i.e. lower threshold at LF than at HF, by making use of the temporal cues and by making
use of their low-frequency acoustic hearing, respectively. NH will be unaﬀected by the
spectral region.
2. In spatial localization, Spectral region of the narrowband signals will aﬀect CI and HA
users inversely, whereas will not have an impact in NH subjects. CI users are expected
to have better performance (less error) when narrowband signals at high-frequencies are
present, making use of ILD cues. HA users re expected to have better performance (less
error) when narrowband signals at low-frequencies are present, making use of their low-
frequency acoustic hearing. Finally, Normal hearing subjects are not expected to have
diﬀerence between the presented narrowband spectral regions. Finally, spectral region
covering both narrowband regions will be helpful for localization.
3. The users with residual hearing will show better results in pitch ranking and in spatial
localization.
• VR technology will have a positive eﬀect on children. It will engage and motivate them to
perform attentively, by using its playfulness.

2.4.3
Experimental procedure
After the General experimental procedure (see Section 2.3), the subjects were instructed with the
concept of pitch. This was achieved by showing sung examples, or making comparisons between
female and male voice pitch either as animals (e.g. pig or bird), or as body positions (e.g. down-

Their main task was to move the helicopter together with the direction of the pitch. For moving
it up, they had to pull the joystick towards their body, whereas, for moving the helicopter down
they had to push the joystick towards the front. They were forced to respond even if they were in
doubt.
In order to accelerate the test process and thus keep the motivation and concentration of the chil-
dren, a timeout of 4 seconds after the oﬀset of the sound was set. In the case that subject did
not respond in that time, the next stimuli would be presented and the time-outed trial would be
discarded from the analysis.
The experiment was designed as a constant stimuli method with Two Alternative Force Choice
(2AFC) procedure.
2.4.4
Data Analysis
The response variable consisted in the hit rate of the subject (i.e. if direction was upwards, and
the response was upwards, then that single trial was considered correct). The data was ﬁtted to
a psychometric function.
It is a representation that relates a change in physical stimulus with
a perceptual response (number of correct responses). A cumulative gaussian function was used
for the curve [77]. The method of Two Alternative Forced Choice (2AFC) was used. Therefore,
two options were presented i.e. up or down. A chance of a correct response with two options is
1
number of alternatives = 1
2 = 0.5 i.e. 50% correct. Therefore, the guessing rate was set at 50 %. The
optimal threshold is given by the steepest point on the psychometric function, i.e. its gradient [9].
At this point, the largest change in correct responses is given for a very small variation in level.
The lapsing rate was taken into account when optimizing the ﬁtting of the data, accounting for
stimuli-independent errors [78]. The ﬁtting was performed with the use of Psygniﬁt Toolbox in
Matlab (see [77] for more details about the Bayesian inference used for modelling).
A measure of response time was calculated for each individual. This value was taken from the
start of the stimulus onset until a response was given by moving the joystick. Then, the values were
averaged across subjects.
Due to the timeout of 4 seconds applied (see Table 2.2), even if the subjects were instructed to
select an option, the system would pass to the next trial after the time window set in the timeout.
This data was discarded.

2.5.3
Experimental procedure
Before following the General experimental procedure (see Section 2.3), the subjects were invited to
look at the control PC-screen, in which the visuals seen from the VR glasses were shown. Their task
was to explode the balloon that they thought was producing a sound. One of the experimenters
was inside of the lab, performing the test for the children to understand the task visually before
starting to explain by words. This was a nice way to show that they needed to try to be visually
accurate when pointing to the exact center of the balloons. This was made to measure their visual
accuracy and see if there were diﬀerences between hearing groups.
In the beginning of every trial, the subject was asked to look to a green square in the front
(0◦) and click with the joystick, to ensure the same starting position for every trial. The green
square would change color if the cross-hair of the subject was correct. Once the green square was
clicked, it would disappear and then stimuli would be reproduced. Then the subject would be forced
to choose one of the 15 balloons shown. A timeout of 8 seconds was chosen for the subject to respond.
An adaptive test using a 15AFC procedure was chosen for the test, as the ﬁfteen balloons were
always visible. The choice of an adaptive procedure was create a dynamic test suited to the capa-
bilities of each hearing group.
Six diﬃculty levels were chosen, starting from Level 1 with ∆α = 90◦as the easiest level and
ﬁnishing at Level 6 with ∆α = 15◦as the most diﬃcult. As an example, in the ﬁrst diﬃculty level,
a loudspeaker would be reproduced, and in the next trial, the next loudspeaker would be played 90◦
separated from the ﬁrst. In order to set a random right-left direction, all possible combinations of
three loudspeakers separated by ∆α were set per each of the six levels. As an example, in the ﬁrst
diﬃculty level with ∆α as 90◦, this three combinations were set: -105:-15:75, -90:0:90 or -75:15:105.
Then, one of the combinations would be randomly chosen e.g. the second (-90:0:90). Then, the
central loudspeaker, e.g. 0◦and one lateral loudspeaker (randomly chosen either -90 or 90). Once
the pair of loudspeakers were chosen, its order was randomized e.g. in the ﬁrst trial a lateral loud-
speaker would be reproduced and then the central one, or viceversa. With this randomization a
possible right-left predictability was aimed to remove. In order to consider a response as correct, a
margin of error of ±45 ◦was allowed around the reproduced loudspeaker (more suitable for hearing
impaired subjects, see Section 1.2.2 for more details).
Then, as a part of the adaptive procedure using one up, two down method, two consecutive
correct responses would allow the subject to continue to the next diﬃculty level. The diﬃculty
levels were deﬁned according to the angular separation (∆α) between consecutively reproduced

Chapter 4
Discussion
This section discusses the results of the three experiments presented in the previous chapter, with
respect to the hypotheses described in Section 1.3. In addition, it compares the response time in
the ﬁrst two experiments. It concludes with a qualitative impression gathered from the children
regarding the VR gaming setup.
4.1
Pitch ranking
The ﬁrst hypothesis mentioned in Section 1.3 stated that CI and HA users would perform with lower
thresholds at fundamental frequencies centered at low-frequencies (F0LF) than in high-frequencies
(F0HF), and that NH subjects would perform similarly despite the spectral region. The presented
F0LF signal ranged between 110 Hz and 220 Hz (chords A2 and A3, < 300 Hz) and F0HF between
739.99 Hz and 1480 Hz (chords F5#-F6#, > 300Hz). Firstly, an improvement for CI users was
expected due to the use of temporal cues, as the temporal pitch has been shown not to provide pitch
information for frequencies above 300 Hz [39, 82]. However, the results show that the threshold at
F0LF is slightly higher than at F0HF (4.94 and 4.73 semitones, respectively, as seen in Table 3.1).
This can be explained due to the complex nature of the sounds i.e. fundamental frequency with har-
monics. Thus, although the fundamental frequencies were in the low-frequency region, its harmonics
covered a wide spectral range. The studies of pitch perception where the temporal cues would elicit
pitch up to 300 Hz may not be applied in this case, as pure tones or variation in stimulation rate
contain fewer harmonic components than complex stimuli [39]. As explained in Section 1.2.1, due
to the poor place resolution of CI users, the envelope created by low-numbered harmonics could be
more confusing than helpful [1, 2]. Therefore, it may be that the the pitch at F0LF got confounded
in the complex pattern of non-resolved prominent peaks created by the low-numbered harmonics of
low-frequency content.
However, the spectral region did not show signiﬁcance whereas semitone number did. A pre-
vious study by Looi and Radford measured pitch ranking at 6 and 3 semitones in a group of CI
children with a sung vowel stimuli with the F0 in the spectral region from 98Hz to 340Hz [4]. In
our study, F0LF stimuli was in the same spectral region, although the timbre was a piano note.
For a diﬀerence of 6 and 3 semitones, their results from CI participants showed 76.6% and 67.45%
accuracy, whereas in our study they performed with 72.22% and %66.66, respectively (see Table A1
in Appendix 5.1). Note that the age of NH subjects tested was a bit higher for NH in their study.
In another study, the same researcher tested pitch ranking in adult CI users, and they found that
adult CI, for 6 and 3 semitone diﬀerence performed with 51.8% and 64.3%, performing worse than
35

CI users in our case [42].
Secondly, for hearing aid users it was expected to be easier to detect semitone diﬀerences at
F0LF than at F0HF, due to their low-frequency acoustic hearing preservation. Indeed, HA users
performed with an average threshold of 2.8 semitones at F0LF compared to 8.86 at F0HF (see
Table 3.1). For F0LF, the results at 6 and 3 semitones are in line with those of Looi and Radford,
who when testing pitch ranking in HA children at 6 and 3 semitones they found an accuracy of
88.54% and 79.11%, whereas in our study they performed slightly better, with 90% and 80% [4].
The same researchers tested pitch ranking in adult HA, and found that at 6 and 3 semitones they
they performed with 83.7% and 74.7%, worse than the HA children in our experiments [42]. The
presentation order may have had inﬂuenced the drop seen in the results of HA users in 6 semitones
at F0HF (see Figure 3.1). That could be due to the eﬀect of the start of a new block i.e. the blocks
of semitones were divided in two diﬃculties i.e. from 12-7 and 6-1. Therefore the change between
the blocks might have aﬀected the HA users.
Lastly, NH subjects were expected not to perceive diﬀerences between spectral regions. How-
ever, it can be seen that the performance at F0LF is better than at F0HF. This could be due to
the higher harmonic density present at low-frequency region in F0LF, that although detrimental
for CI users, seems beneﬁcial for NH subjects [1]. Due to the narrower auditory ﬁlters, the low and
middle-numbered harmonics presented at low freuencies could be resolved. However, this ﬁnding
did not show signiﬁcance, whereas semitone number did. Looi and Radford tested the pitch ranking
in NH children in 6 and 3 semitones, and found an accuracy of 94.58% and 88.79% [4], slighltly
higher than for our measurements (90.90% and 80.95%, respectively). Note that the age of the NH
subjects they tested was higher than the NH children in our experiments, being a possible inﬂuence
in the better results. As a comparison of the performance at F0HF, Stalinski et al. [46] studied pitch
ranking in NH children at the age of 8 to 11 years, with stimuli centered at 880 Hz and semitone
diﬀerences from 4 to 0.1 semitones. The stimuli they used is comparable to the F0HF presented in
this study. They found out that for 1 semitone, their performance was above 83%. In the case of
the participants in our study, they performed with 71.42% of accuracy for 1 semitone at HF. The
fact that Stalinski used lower semitone diﬀerences than 1 semitone (they tested up to 0.1 semitones)
may have contributed to a training of the subjects and thus could have aﬀected to the better results.
The average response time is smaller for CI users for both stimuli types, although the diﬀerence
with NH children is small at F0LF (2.13 and 2.10 s, respectively). The average and variability of
the response time increased for both hearing impaired groups at F0LF, whereas it decreased for
NH subjects. One option for the decrease in NH subjects could be the presentation order, as HF
were presented in the beginning, and it could have taken time to understand the task. Although a
training session was presented before the main test session, the intervals for the training session were
ﬁxed in an octave distance. However, the main test session gradually decreased the intervals. This
could have been distracting at the beginning, thus, explaining the decrease at F0LF seen in NH. The
increase of response time at F0LF for CI users could be due to the confusion created by the high
harmonic density present in F0LF sounds, in which they had to apply more eﬀort to understand the
pitch. However, this would not explain the performance of HA users at F0LF, were HA users were
assumed to use their acoustic low-frequency hearing. In addition, a possible explanation for both CI
and HA users could be the repetitive nature of the constant stimuli presented, that may have been
tedious to follow for some subjects. Note that hearing aid users were the youngest group on average.
From the cognitive perspective, Andrews et al. found that in behavioural tests, the cognitive

4.2
Spatial localization
The second hypothesis assumed that the spectral region inversely aﬀect CI and HA users, while
not exerting an eﬀect on NH subjects. Firstly, CI users were expected to beneﬁt from Interaural
Level Diﬀerences (ILD) cues at high-frequency narrowband signals (HFN). HA users were expected
to beneﬁt from acoustic hearing preservation. Indeed, the results show that average RMS error and
variability is smaller at HFN than at LFN, as seen in Figure 3.3.
Secondly, HA users were expected to perform better at LFN than at HFN due to the use of
acoustic low frequency hearing. Table 3.2 conﬁrms the case while Figure 3.3 shows that the variabil-
ity of RMS error also decreases towards LF. Van den Bogaert found an improvement in adult HA
users when using their spatial localization without their HA devices for high-frequencies, showing
the detrimental of the HA devices [60]. This may be an explanation for the high variability found
in HF stimuli for HA. Although the average RMS error value is higher at HFN than at LFN, the
diﬀerence is small. One of the explanations for the poor performance of HA users at LFN could
be the smaller spectral width at LFN than at HFN (see Figure 2.6 in section 2.5.1). In addition,
LFN could be perceived as less loud than HFN. In order to overcome loudness dependencies, the
presented Sound Pressure Levels (SPL) were roved ± 4 dB. However, no distinctions in the gain
depending on the spectral region were applied. The equal loudness contour curves show that low
frequencies at the same SPL level as high frequencies are not perceived as equally loud (see [83]
and Figure A1 in Appendix 5.1). Therefore, the combination of a lower perceived loudness and
the smaller bandwidth presented in LFN may have aﬀected to the lower performance of all hearing
groups at LFN. The best practice would probably have been to loudness match the stimuli by the
subjects [84]. However, this required an extra time, that added to the existing time, would be
excessive for children.
The same notion holds for the reduced performance of NH at LF, both as an increase of RMS
error and variability (seen in Figure 3.3). Other studies have addressed the eﬀect of spectral region
on localization. Kuehnle et al reported an improvement in NH children at low frequencies compared
with high frequencies [58]. However, the bandwidth they used in both stimuli was the same i.e. two
octaves, not as in the case of this study. Bess et al [85] tested pure tone localization at 3kHz and
37

February 1, 2019
at 500 Hz, and stated that low frequencies were better localized. However, the stimuli in our case
is not comparable to pure tones.
The combination of both ITD and ILD elicited by the broadband (WBN) signal was expected
to help with spatial localization. Indeed, WBN stimuli creates the smallest averaged RMS error and
variability among all the stimuli types for the three hearing groups. For CI users, an averaged RMS
error of 36.69◦was found at WBN. This ﬁndings are in line with the results from Grieco-Calub
and Litovsky [55] and Van Deun et al. [59]. They found an average RMS error ranging from 19
to 56◦and from 13◦to 63◦, respectively. Another preliminary study using VR for localization was
performed by Simoes-Franklin. They elicited the diﬀerent sound positions through Head Related
Transfer Functions (HRTF) instead of presenting the sounds in free ﬁeld, as in our case [7]. They
found RMS errors around 60◦, higher than our ﬁndings (average of 36.69◦). This could be due to
the artiﬁcally created ITD and ILDs.
For HA users, an averaged RMS error of 35.55◦was found at WBN. Van den Bogaert [86] tested
adult HA users using a broadband steady state noise, with an average spectrum of a dutch male
talker (VU-noise [87]). They found an RMS of around 10◦in the subjects. This is bigger for the
HA users tested in this study, but the comparison is not so straightforward due to the diﬀerence in
stimuli and in age, as our participants were children.
For NH children, an averaged RMS error of 15.16◦was found at WBN stimuli (see Table 3.2).
This is in line with studies from Grieco-Calub and Litovsky, that reported RMS errors from 9◦to
29◦[55] and of Godar et al, that reported RMS errors from 1.4◦to 38◦[56]. Van Deun et al. [59],
reported smaller averaged RMS errors of 10◦, 6◦and 4◦for children on the age of 4, 5 and 6 years
old. When using VR for spatial localization, Sechler et al found that average RMS for NH adults
was bigger than the results we found in children, as they found 23.81%. It has to be noted that
instead of free ﬁeld, they used HRTF through headphones [6].

averages the errors from all directions presented in azimuth (right-left), so the direction dependant
information per angle is lost. The results of CI children are in agreement with data of Litovsky et al.
[5] shown in Figure 4.1. They argued that the errors committed by CI users and NH subjects were
diﬀerent. They showed that the CI users perceived frontal positions as coming from the laterals,
while NH subjects performed with the best accuracy at frontal positions.

Finally, the presentation order of the angular positions was designed assuming that the separa-
tion of consecutively reproduced loudspeakers would aﬀect the error. It was assumed that it would
be more diﬃcult to distinguish between two loudspeakers separated by 15◦than by 90◦. Thus, the
error in localization would increase with the decrease of angular distance between loudspeakers. This
was not the case, as the eﬀect was inverse i.e. RMS decreased with decreasing loudspeaker separa-
tion (see Figure A2 in Appendix 5.1). The design of this procedure was motivated by the creation of
an adaptive procedure that would dynamically change the angular separation between consecutively
reproduced loudspeakers (see Section 2.5.3 for more details about the design). However, two factors
were not taken into account:
• Angle dependent localization abilities for NH subjects are diﬀerent than for CI and HA users
e.g. frontal sounds being easier to detect than laterals. This can be seen in Figure 3.2), where
frontal directions seem to be perceived as coming from the laterals. One reason could be the
directionality of the hearing devices, that depend on their microphone pattern and position
together with the diﬀerences in gain and delay between bilateral devices were not taken into
account. This became a problem due to the angular separations chosen. As explained in Sec-
tion 2.5.3, 6 angular separations (called diﬃculty levels) were set, starting from big angular
separations until small angular separations. The ﬁrst diﬃculty level would reproduce loud-
speakers separated 90 ◦, and due to the right-left randomization seeked, three combinations
would be set: -105:-15:75, -90:0:90 or -75:15:105. Even if the right-left direction was random-
ized, a central loudspeaker would always be reproduced. As two correct responses were needed
to continue to the next level, CI users (and some HA users) would be disadvantaged in compar-
ison with NH users, due to their poor frontal direction localization. This can be visually seen
in the last angular that the subjects were able to discriminate (see Figure A2 in Appendix 5.1).
• In addition, an allowed error of 45◦throughout all angular separations was set for all the levels.
This was too small for frontal sounds (that would be perceived as very laterals e.g.> 45◦),
and instead would make angular separations placed close together easy to perform correctly
and continue tot the next level. Therefore, the adaptive procedure that aimed to increase the
diﬃculty did not work as expected due to the constant allowed 45◦of error.
Chan et al. also used an adaptive procedure for testing spatial localization, but divided the azimuth
hemisphere into sectors, separated by left-right as the simplest, and decreasing the angle separation.
It worked for CI users, as even if the frontal sounds were perceived in the laterals, the trial would
be considered correct in their case [28]. They did not set any allowed error as in our case. In order
to get a better design in our case, the allowed error could be taken out, and the loudspeaker array

Chapter 5
Conclusions
Throughout the study, the eﬀect of diﬀerent stimuli parameters in three experiments were inves-
tigated and compared with existing literature.
This section summarizes the highlights of each
experiment and suggests possible improvements and extensions to the completed work.
The eﬀect of the pitch at diﬀerent spectral ranges was aﬀected by the use of complex stimuli,
where the harmonic density adds prominent peaks in the temporal envelope of the signal. This
may have been helpful for NH subjects that could resolve them, and detrimental for CI users, that
could not. Therefore, the beneﬁt expected from ITD cues for CI users was reduced, showing a
similar pitch threshold for both HF and LF. HA users had lower thresholds at F0LF than at F0HF,
showing the beneﬁt of their acoustic low frequency hearing. Finally, the semitone number showed
signiﬁcance among all three groups. The semitone threshold was lowest for NH subjects for both
F0HF and F0LF, followed by the HA users at F0LF and CI users at F0HF, where they showed a
lower threshold than HA users.
The spectral range and presented azimuth positions seemed determinant for spatial localization.
In general, for narrowband signals the RMS error was bigger at F0LF than at F0HF, although the
diﬀerence between both was smaller for HA users, probably due to their acoustic low frequency
hearing, that was more helpful than the information by the HA at HF. The general increase of error
at F0LF could partially be due to the smaller bandwidth present in the F0LF signal, thus, eliciting
a lower loudness and interaural temporal cues. Therefore, as expected, the bigger bandwidth of the
broadband stimuli was helpful for spatial localization for the three groups, creating the smallest
RMS error. Whereas CI users and some HA users had frontal/lateral confusions, the NH subjects
did not, showing a better frontal accuracy than hearing impaired, probably due to the delay and
gain diﬀerences introduced by the bilateral hearing technologies. In addition, the visual accuracy
of the subjects was similar across hearing groups.
Few CI subjects seemed to detect the presented low-frequency stimuli in the Experiment 3,
residual hearing. One could say that a better perception of pitch at LF spectral region was seen
for those subjects, whereas no clear inﬂuence was seen in spatial localization. However, it is diﬃ-
cult to make comparisons between the performance for pitch ranking and spatial localization and
attribute an eﬀect. In addition, two HA users were found not to hear to the low-frequency stimuli
acoustically. Interestingly, the sensitivity for detecting visual stimuli without the presence of sound
was calculated and it was seen that the visual sensitivity was similar across hearing groups, showing
that distractions aﬀected all the hearing groups.

5.1
Future lines
One objective of this study was to compare the speech in noise performance of children with diﬀerent
types of hearing. As this data was not available, a further investigation of the speech in noise skills
of the children, through a test such as HINT, would be interesting, in order to see the weight and the
correspondance of pitch direction perception, spatial localization and low-frequency hearing on the
results. In addition, as all these tests were performed in silent conditions, incorporating background
noise could be a further step, e.g. adding diﬀerent bandwidth widths, envelope shapes and levels to
the already existing test.
As the settings from the subjects were not modiﬁed, the loudness may have been diﬀerent per
subject and stimuli type [50]. As future lines, a loudness matching procedure could be applied to the
diﬀerent stimuli [91]. This option was considered at the initial part of the design of the experiments,
but was discarded due to the big amount of experiments already present and the need to be fast in
time for the children not to get tired. In this way, the loudness dependent factors could be reduced
more than using the loudness balancing performed for Experiment 1 (pitch ranking) and the use
of the same SPL level (although roved ±4 dB) in Experiment 2 (spatial localization). In addition,
it may be that the SPL levels used for testing low-frequency hearing were not enough for eliciting
low-frequency hearing in some subjects. However, this could induce a vibration perception in the
body instead of a heard stimuli.
For the pitch ranking test, a shorter test in which the subject would interact with the sound was
proposed for some children. This would make the ﬂying experience more immersive. In addition, a
discriminative task to ﬁnd the JND in pitch would be interesting to create, in which the cognitive
eﬀort used to understand if the direction was upwards or downwards would be removed. Also, it
would make it easier to explain the concept of pitch i.e. clicking if the stimuli is diﬀerent does not
require to understand if it is lower or higher. In the present study, once the concept of upward pitch
was understood, the understanding of the movement needed to do with the joystick in order to move
the helicopter accordingly was sometimes diﬃcult. This movement was better understood by older
children. Therefore, a simpler task would eliminate doubts about cognitive eﬀects in the results. In
terms of timbre, Galvin et al studied diﬀerent timbres of instruments, and stated that piano timbre
was less eﬀective than other instruments [92]. A possible study could combine diﬀerent timbres and
relate the spectral components to their pitch ranking performance.

For the spatial localization test, the width of the narrowband signals was not the same, thus,
the results at diﬀerent spectral positions are blurred with the possible eﬀect of bandwidth. In order
to avoid this, the chosen stimuli should have the same relative bandwidth (i.e. 2 octaves, as used
by Kuehnle et al [58]). As future work, the eﬀect of bandwidth width in the diﬀerent spectral
regions could be an interesing response variable to measure, as the eﬀects of bandwidth could be
separated with the ones of spectrum (and thus, diﬀerent uses of ILD and ITDs). In case of want-
ing information about all loudspeaker positions, then a constant stimuli test would be a better
solution than an adaptive one. In this way, although it would be a longer test, balanced results
would be obtained, and thus, easier to compare with other studies using constant stimuli procedures.
In addition, an improvement to the current adaptive procedure could be made by using an
adaptive procedure as Chan et al. designed [28]. They divided the localization sphere into sectors
separated by certain angular separations, as in our case. However, they did not set a margin of
error, as was done in this study. Their allowed error was implicit in the sector width chosen. As
this sector decreased, the margin of allowed error decreased as well. In this way, in the easiest level,
a CI or HA user that would feel the sounds totally lateralized would perform correctly, and the
level diﬃculty would indeed increase as the sectors became smaller. This would make the subjects
at least be able to continue to the next angular separation. An interesting approach to create the
sectors could be to take into account the best areas that the directional microphones pick in order
to create the diﬀerent diﬃculty levels. However, this test would be diﬀerent for each subject and
more diﬃcult to perform for NH users, as the best hearing areas are diﬀerent for a normal hearing
person than for a hearing impaired, mainly because of the eﬀects of the microphone directionality
and interaural gain and delay diﬀerences that they apply.
It is acknowledged that the setup used in this project i.e. anechoic chamber and array loudspeak-
ers, is not available everywhere, and requires a certain investment and maintenance. A cost-eﬀective
alternative would be to reproduce the tests showed in this study using artiﬁcially elicited HRTFs,
recorded from a Head and Torso Manikin, with and without hearing aids with diﬀerent microphone
directionalities. Then, these HRTFs could be presented through headphones to NH and HA users.
For presenting them to CI users, a simulation of the audio processing at CI could be applied [22]
and presented to CI users through Direct Connect method, as Chan et al [28] presented in their work.

APPENDIX E
Calibration
The calibration between the Real Environment and Virtual Environment consisted in three main
steps shown in Figure A1. The explanation is roughly guided by the work done by Ahrens et al.
[93].
• The three trackers (three red dots in the ﬁrst subplot of Figure A1) created a plane in RE and
VE (white and yellow triangles in second subplot, respectively). The RE plane was taken as
a reference. The normal of the VE plane was then calculated and rotated until it was equal
to the one of RE plane, as seen in the third subplot. Orientation was achieved.
• A RE reference point (one of the red points) was chosen and aligned to a point of the VE
plane (see third subplot).
• The angle from the ﬁxed point to each of the other two points was taken for VE and RE
(see A and B in fourth subplot). Then, the diﬀerence between the angles for each point was
calculated, and the VE points repositioned to the angle diﬀerence(see ﬁfth subplot).
The calibration was continuously updating, and re-calibrating in case of a bigger error than 2 cm.
