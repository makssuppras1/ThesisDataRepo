Abstract
Streamers are thin ionized plasma channels that form the early stages of lightning dis-
charges.
In this project, electron avalanches and potential avalanche-to-streamer transitions in
the atmosphere of Primordial Earth are studied, and along with background ionization
as the basic propagation mechanism of streamer discharges, the motion of electrons is
modeled with a particle-in-cell (PIC) Monte Carlo Collisions (MCC) code.
This code performs simulations in three atmospheric compositions suggested for Pri-
mordial Earth of approximately 3.8 Ga ago: two for an oxidizing atmosphere, N2:CO2 =
80%:20% and N2:CO2:H2O:H2:CO = 80%:18.89%:1%:0.1%:0.01% (suggested by Kast-
ing), and one for a reducing atmosphere, H2O:CH4:NH3:H2 = 37.5%:25%:25%:12.5%
(i.e. the gas mixture used in the Miller-Urey experiment); or simply N2:CO2, Kasting
and Miller-Urey for reasons of convenience.
In order to make comparisons, the code runs simulations in a fourth atmosphere too,
which represents Modern Earth: N2:O2 = 80%:20%; or simply air.
All four gas mixtures use two different ambient electric fields, 1.5Ek and 3Ek, and
three different background ionization densities, 103 cm−3, 104 cm−3 and 105 cm−3.
From all 24 mixture-field-background combinations, the conditions appear to be
adequate for 18 of them, favoring the production of a bidirectional streamer from an
initial electron avalanche. The six cases that do not achieve this goal are all for Kasting
and N2:CO2 gas mixtures with an ambient electric field of 1.5Ek and for all three levels
of background ionization.
After just half a nanosecond and when the ambient field is 3Ek, the number of
electrons with energies above 100 eV in air and in Miller-Urey seems to have already
increased significantly; this is in correspondence with the fact that the simulations of air
and Miller-Urey have already reached a streamer discharge until that time step.
In all other gas mixtures and for both ambient fields, plus with any background ion-
ization level, the energies of the electrons are limited between 100 eV and the ionization
energies of their gases, for up to 1.85 ns.
Furthermore, Miller-Urey is the gas mixture that reaches to a streamer stage first
having the fastest front velocities compared to all other mixtures. Air comes second,

then Kasting and last the N2:CO2 gas mixture with the slowest front velocities and the
least energetic electrons.
Finally, from all atmospheric compositions that were tested as possible candidates for
representing the Primordial Earth’s atmosphere, streamer discharges in the Miller-Urey
gas mixture, along with their characteristics (such as electron energies, front velocities
and maximum electric field), are found to be closer to (and sometimes even to resem-
ble) their corresponding streamer discharges and their characteristics in today’s Earth
atmosphere, air, than any of the two cases of an oxidizing Primordial atmosphere; i.e.
N2:CO2 and Kasting’s gas mixture. As streamers are thought to have existed on Pri-
mordial Earth, this result may hint that the Primordial atmosphere was similar to the
reducing Miller-Urey mixture.

1.1
Lightning
The ancient Greeks believed that lightning was a weapon of Zeus used for destruction.
This interpretation seems reasonable, if we consider how spectacular and frightening
lightning looks, plus how powerful it is. However, who would have thought that it may
be responsible for the opposite, too, i.e. creation? The Miller-Urey experiment suggests
that lightning may have contributed to the origin of life on Earth through the formation
of amino acids [1, 2, 3, 4, 5]. In any case, lightning is a phenomenon that fascinates
people and challenges scientists. Understanding how it works may reveal bigger secrets
than we even imagine; knowledge not only about our past, i.e. sparking life on Primordial
Earth, but also about our future if we learn how to control it (e.g. its applications could
improve human life significantly).
While researchers investigate lightning now on Earth or other planetary bodies [6, 7,
8, 9, 10, 11, 12, 13, 14], I investigate here its occurrence in the past. In this work, I focus
on the early stages of lightning in the atmosphere of Primordial Earth, the so-called
streamers (Section 2.2) which are thin ionized plasma channels, formed by an initial
electron avalanche and driven by the ambient electric field.
This project intends to study these early stages of lightning on Primordial Earth
and compare with Modern Earth, by simulating streamer discharges in its atmosphere.
Earth is approximately 4.54 Ga (billion years) old [15]. After the Late Heavy Bom-
bardment (LHB) had stopped approximately 3.8 Ga ago, life had probably appeared by
approximately 3.5 Ga ago, according to evidence such as stromatolites and microfossils
found in prehistoric sediments [16, 17, 4]. So, it has been proposed that life on Earth,
presumably, began during this period (i.e. between 3.8 and 3.5 Ga ago) [18, 4]. Before
this period, it seems improbable for life to have had a sufficiently large chance to initi-
ate, due to the large number of impactors hitting Earth repeatedly and vaporizing its
oceans, keeping the planet not favorable for life [19, 4].
Different conditions, such as the gas mixtures suggested for Primordial Earth and
the ambient electric field, are tested for that era (3.8 Ga - 3.5 Ga ago), in order to
examine which conditions favored the production of streamer discharges as precursors
of lightning in the atmosphere of Primordial Earth. Additionally, avalanche properties

2.2
Streamers
Streamers are thin ionized plasma channels, which penetrate into the background gas,
and propagate under the influence of an electric field. These are discharge modes which
may form when a medium, such as air, is exposed to a sufficiently large potential differ-
ence over a sufficient distance. Thus, free electrons accelerate, collide with gas molecules
and, provided sufficient energy, ionize the background gas molecules. This process con-
tinues as a chain reaction, creating electron avalanches (Townsend avalanches) [20, 21,
22] and residual ions, and thus the initial electron-ion patch used at the beginning of
each simulation, as can be seen in Figure 2.1 and in Figure 2.5. Streamers form when
the size and density of the avalanche grows enough (after approximately 18-20 effective
ionization lengths [23], when the avalanche starts from a few electrons [24, 23]) to cause
space charge effects and distort the electric field. Such streamers constitute the early
stages of lightning.

We have positive streamers, if they propagate towards the direction of the ambient
electric field, and negative, if they propagate opposite to it. Typical speeds that both

Streamers in nature
Theoretically (i.e. if the conditions are right), in nature, streamers may be found in
general anywhere atmospheres exist; e.g. on planets (such as Earth, Venus, Jupiter,
Saturn, Neptune and Uranus [6, 7, 9]), moons (such as Titan [14]), exoplanets and
brown dwarfs [8, 9]. However, this is not always the case, because there are several
parameters and conditions that determine the existence of streamers, such as the gas
composition, the altitude, the atmospheric density, and of course the ambient electric
field which affects the streamer velocities to a great extent.

As can be seen in Figure 2.2, other streamer examples are the TLEs (Transient

in higher altitudes, and include sprites, blue starters, blue and gigantic jets, halos and
elves.

Sprites [11, 12] are large-scale electrical discharges which occur high above a thun-

Blue jets [11, 12] are flashes propagating upwards from clouds, are brighter than
sprites and, as implied by their name, are blue.
Blue starters [11, 12] are considered to be a weaker form of blue jets; they appear to

Gigantic jets [11, 12], obviously, are much bigger than blue jets and reach much

Elves [11, 12] are rapidly expanding (up to 500 km across) rings of light that last
less than one millisecond and occur high above energetic cloud-to-ground lightning of
positive or negative polarity.
They take their name as an acronym for Emission of
Light and Very Low-Frequency perturbations due to Electromagnetic Pulse Sources, in
relation to the mythological creatures of William Shakespeare’s ”A Midsummer Night’s
Dream”. As their name suggests, they appear when an electromagnetic pulse (EMP),
caused by a discharge from an underlying thunderstorm, propagates into the ionosphere.
Until 1999, sprite halos [11, 12] were mistaken for elves. Halos, however, are diffuse
disk-shaped glows that, apparently, precede sprites and propagate downwards, from
around 85 km to 70 km altitude and last for approximately one millisecond.
All these upper atmospheric optical phenomena are directly linked to the lightning
and streamer discharges.
TLEs occur in the middle and upper parts of the atmosphere, well above the usual
lightning we typically observe from the ground; so, always above thunderstorms, which
means that lightning activity will be on-going in the lower atmosphere [13].
On April 2, 2018, the Atmosphere-Space Interactions Monitor (ASIM) [10], as an
international mission of the European Space Agency (ESA), was launched with desti-
nation the International Space Station (ISS), having as one of its main objectives to
observe the TLEs from above; i.e. from the ISS while flying with it. More specifically,
ASIM is now attached outside the European Columbus Laboratory on ISS, from where
it monitors thunderstorms continuously from above and sends data and images back to
Earth.
Interestingly, one could say that the main correlation between streamers and light-
ning, is that during the initial nanoseconds of its life, lightning passes from the streamer
stage first, before becoming the actual lightning as we know it. This means that in any
lightning event we may witness, we should remember from now on that a streamer has
preceded it.

2.2.3
Streamers in industry
Six examples of applications that use streamers are: Tesla coils, ozone generation, air
purification, plasma-assisted combustion, guided streamers and plasma actuators. The
plasma produced by streamers is strongly non-equilibrium, since it is not in thermody-
namic equilibrium; the temperature of the electrons is much higher than the temperature
of the heavier species (i.e. ions and neutrals), because the electrons have much higher
energies. Consequently, chemical reactions may take place in a gas/substance without
heating it.
Plasma medicine takes advantage of this attribute by the use of guided
streamers [26], also known as plasma ”bullets”, which are meant to help in wound treat-
ment [27].
Plasma medicine can be subdivided into three main categories:
• Non-thermal atmospheric-pressure direct plasma for medical therapy [28]: a new
innovative approach in medicine for wound healing, with a variety of other appli-
cations, such as topical treatment of skin diseases with microbial involvement, and
even treatment of cancer diseases.
• Plasma-based bio-decontamination and sterilization [29]: nonthermal plasma, and
especially atmospheric pressure plasma, kills and inactivates microorganisms effi-
ciently. Due to the moderate gas temperatures (near or at room temperature),
the so-called cold plasma is ideal for use with heat-sensitive materials such as
polymers.
• In addition to therapeutic and sterilization applications, physical plasmas are also
used in plasma-assisted modification of bio-relevant surfaces [30]: an effective and
economical surface treatment technique for many materials; the big advantage is
that the surface properties and biocompatibility can be enhanced selectively, while
the bulk attributes of the materials remain intact.
Besides plasma medicine, plasma-assisted applications have started rising in aerospace
engineering too. One example is the integration of flight control and flow control using
plasma actuators [31]: actuators that are developed for aerodynamic flow control and
impart force in a similar way to ionocrafts; i.e. using electrohydrodynamics to provide
lift or thrust, without requiring combustion. It seems to be advantageous to use such
plasma-assisted applications, due to the low average power consumption and the absence
of any moving parts.

2.3
Collisions
As free electrons are accelerated by the electric field, they collide with molecules of the
ambient gas. More specifically, electrons gain energy from the external electric field and
dissipate it by collisions to the neutral gas molecules. The collisional transfer of this
accumulated energy to the neutral gas molecules occurs by elastic and different types of
inelastic collisions. In the elastic collisions, the total kinetic energy is conserved, while in
the inelastic collisions we have loss of the total kinetic energy due to inelastic processes.
2.3.1
Cross sections and scattering
In this work we assume an isotropic model of scattering. This means that I employ the
integral cross sections [32, 33, 34, 35, 36, 37, 38, 39] that I have received by S. Dujko
(personal communication, February 12, 2019), which are the momentum-transfer cross
sections in their nature. For example, the elastic cross section is the same as the elastic
momentum transfer cross section. This approximation is valid for lower electron energies
(lower than 20 eV for gases considered in this work) and has been tested many times
in the past ([40] and references therein). The isotropy of scattering may be assumed
due to the fact that numerous elastic collisions generally randomize directions of electron
motion, and hence, one could not see any significant effects of introducing the anisotropy
of scattering.
For higher electron energies (> 20 eV), however, this physical picture is not valid
anymore and the use of an anisotropic model of scattering is required. In such case, one
needs to use the differential cross sections and the corresponding total cross sections,
which can be obtained by integrating the differential cross sections, at the given electron
energy. This is one of the biggest problems in modeling, and two of the main reasons
[40] are:
Firstly, the lack of reliable cross sections for electron scattering in the literature;
experimental differential cross sections are not as broadly available as integrated cross
sections, because measuring differential cross sections is much more demanding than
measuring integrated cross sections.
Secondly, when implementing an anisotropic model in a Monte Carlo collision (MCC)
module, an additional random number is required, plus an additional table look-up
(interpolation) per collision event; thus, the computing cost of the collisions increases
significantly.
Nevertheless, despite the fact that experimental data ([41] and references therein)
clearly show that the angular scattering of electrons becomes forward as the incident
kinetic energy increases (> 20 eV), it is quite common to find studies ([40] and references

2.3.2
Types of collisions
The collisions that are implemented in the code of the simulations fall into four main
categories [44, 45, 14]:
• Elastic collision: an electron hits a molecule elastically, with such low kinetic energy
that it changes only its own direction of motion without losing or transferring any
energy to the molecule. The total kinetic energy of the two bodies remains ideally
the same.
• Excitation: an electron hits a molecule with enough energy in order to merely
excite it. Consequently, in this type, the energy of the incident electron decreases.
We can distinguish between rotational, vibrational and electronic excitations.
• Electron impact ionization occurs when a free electron hits a neutral atom or
molecule with sufficient kinetic energy to ionize it; the impacting electron loses
energy. In this case, the free electron as it knocks another electron out of its orbit,
it converts the initially neutral atom to a cation; a positively charged ion.
Electron impact ionization from now on will be referred to simply as ionization for
reasons of convenience.
• Electron attachment essentially plays the opposite role than ionization. It is also
known as electron capture ionization and takes place when an incident electron
attaches to a neutral atom or molecule to form a anion. In this work, we consider
the dissociative electron attachment and three-body attachment.
Any type of attachment from now on will be referred to simply as attachment for
reasons of convenience.
2.4
Breakdown field
Breakdown field (Ek), also known as critical electric field, is the exact value of the electric
field at which ionization and attachment are equal. This value is needed to determine
how high the ambient electric field Eamb has to be, compared to the breakdown field Ek
(e.g. 1.5Ek or 3Ek), in order to cause sufficient (for streamers creation) ionization in the
simulations, thus overpowering the effect of the attachment which would otherwise slow
down or prohibit to some degree the transition of an electron avalanche to a streamer.
Initially, the breakdown field is given in Td; Townsend (Td) is the physical unit of
a density reduced electric field E/N and 1 Td is equal to 10−21 V·m2. The gas density
N has units of m−3. Therefore, the product between E/N (in V·m2) and N (in m−3)

2.5
Dominant propagation mechanisms
Each streamer comes into existence from an initial electron avalanche, and evolves and
propagates through different types of collisions. From these, propagation by electron
impact ionization, photoionization by UV or Bremsstrahlung photons, and background
ionization are the main mechanisms. Especially the last two are essential for the creation
of positive streamers. More specifically, at the negative front (Figure 2.1), electrons
diffuse more since they are attracted by the anode, and they propagate by electron
impact ionization assisted by photoionization and background ionization, whereas the
positive front (Figure 2.1) totally depends on photoionization and background ionization,
since it needs a source of free electrons to propagate towards the cathode.
2.5.1
Photoionization
In air and other N2:O2 mixtures, the most commonly accepted mechanism regarding the
propagation of positive streamers, is photoionization. This mechanism starts when free
electrons are accelerated by the high electric field that exists at the positive front. These
energetic electrons excite nitrogen with energies above the ionization energy of oxygen.
As a reaction, the excited nitrogen deexcites by emitting an ultraviolet (UV) photon
which hits oxygen with sufficient energy to ionize it. The result is an initially neutral
oxygen molecule that becomes a positively-charged ion, plus a liberated electron; the
one that was knocked off its orbit by the incident UV photon. Thus, new free electrons
propagate towards the direction of the electric field, helping the positive streamers grow
longer and faster [46, 47]. Therefore, new electrons can be created not only directly,
due to electron impact ionization, but also indirectly, due to the ionization of oxygen
molecules by photons emitted from excited states of nitrogen [48].

air (both nitrogen and oxygen are present), is the following [47, 49, 48]:

2.5.2
Background ionization
Apart from photoionization, another mechanism contributing significantly to the prop-
agation of positive streamers (and also helping negative streamers) is background ion-
ization [46, 47, 50, 51].
This mechanism works as electron impact ionization (described in Section 2.3.2),
but the initial free particles that hit the molecules and ionize them, come from sources
relevant to the background. Three are the most possible sources: cosmic or solar radi-
ation, radioactivity from radioactive materials, and remaining ionization from previous
discharges in a repetitive discharge mode [46, 47, 50, 51]. From the first two sources,
the background ionization is typically in the range of 103-104 cm−3 in ambient air [46,
47, 50, 51].
The combination of both photoionization and background ionization in a simulation
would be an ideal scenario able to represent the actual conditions more realistically, at
least in air.
2.6
Primordial Earth’s atmosphere
There are two types of atmosphere that bifurcate scientists when they try to express a
widely accepted theory regarding the Earth’s atmosphere during its early years. These
types are the reducing and the oxidizing atmosphere.
In 1962, H. D. Holland [52, 53] suggested that Primordial Earth experienced both
types. In the beginning, the very early Earth of approximately 4.5 Ga ago underwent
the reducing type containing almost no oxygen, but massive amounts of volcanic gases
released to the atmosphere due to magma oceans covering the surface prior to crust
formation. As Earth was cooling down rapidly radiating its heat into space and reaching
less than water boiling temperature (water was slowly becoming present), its atmosphere
eventually started making the transition to the next stage, the oxidizing type; then,
dioxygen (O2) would still be absent, but the number of oxygen-containing compounds
just started rising [54].

2.6.1
Reducing atmosphere
A reducing atmosphere contains no significant amounts of free oxygen (O2) or other
oxidizing gases, and hence the conditions for oxidation to occur are not favorable [55].
More specifically, such an atmosphere is essentially an atmospheric condition that not
only prevents oxidation due to the lack of big amounts of oxygen and other oxidizing
gases or vapors, but also comprises reducing gases such as carbon monoxide and hydro-
gen, plus compounds such as hydrogen sulphide, which would normally be oxidized by
oxygen if it was present [56].
Since the atmosphere has a primary role in theories regarding the origin of life, it
is crucial to know what the Earth’s atmosphere was composed of, approximately 3.8
Ga ago; after the LHB had stopped. Thus, the Miller-Urey experiment [1, 2, 3, 4, 5]
demonstrated that many biologically important organic compounds, including amino
acids and sugars, could be created by spark discharges in a H2O:CH4:NH3:H2 reducing
atmosphere. However, it is discussed whether methane and ammonia were present in
Primordial Earth’s atmosphere, or at least in significant amounts. Volcanic gases were
being oxidized making N2 the major nitrogen gas and CO2 the main gas containing
carbon [54, 4].
2.6.2
Oxidizing atmosphere
First and foremost, an oxidizing atmosphere of a planet or a moon is such that oxidizes
immersed (surface) compounds. Volcanic gases may be oxidized, too. Typically, this
term (i.e. oxidizing atmosphere) corresponds to an O2-rich atmosphere; the Modern
Earth’s atmosphere is a representative example.
Although most of the oxygen that
exists in the atmosphere of our planet is created by biological photosynthesis, oxidation
may also result from photochemistry caused by solar UV radiation.
In this case, a
representative example is the oxidized Martian surface [57]; the atmosphere of Mars is
an oxidizing atmosphere consisting mainly of CO2, N2 and Ar (plus traces of H2O and
other gases), but the OH radicals and oxygen atoms produced by photolysis, result in
surface oxidation on Mars and the formation of O2, O3 and H2O2. More specifically,
because the solar UV radiation penetrates into the ground due to the lack of strong UV
absorbers in the atmosphere, highly oxidizing compounds can be found on the Martian
surface, including free radicals such as hydroxyl (OH·), perhydroxyl (HO2·), atomic
oxygen (O·) and superoxide (O−
2 ), and also oxygen ions and other oxidizing molecules
such as oxygen (O2), ozone (O3), and hydrogen peroxide (H2O2) [58].
As for the case of Primordial Earth, numerous scientists [59, 52, 60, 54, 61] have
suggested that the Early Earth’s atmosphere consisted mostly of N2, CO2 and H2O,

with just small quantities of H2 and CO, and almost no CH4 or NH3, since volcanic
gases were being oxidized. Therefore, according to them, a quite probable type for the
Primordial Earth’s atmosphere seems to be the oxidizing type.
2.7
Simulation code
All of the above-analyzed terms are modeled in order to run simulations under the Pri-
mordial Earth’s (and the Modern Earth’s) conditions, as realistically as possible. For
this reason, information regarding how the model works is provided in the following para-
graphs; the presented equations derive from the work that has been done by Chanrion
and Neubert [45] using the same main parts of the code.
2.7.1
Physical model
In this model, the simulation domain represents part of an atmosphere (i.e. the chosen
gas mixture for a specific case) and has cylindrical symmetry. So, for each individual
electron, there are three coordinates in velocity space (vr, vθ, vz) and only two spatial
coordinates, r and z, with the latter to be aligned parallel to the electric field, as in
Figure 2.1; i.e. vertically between the anode and the cathode.
Initially, a charge-neutral electron-ion patch (Section 2.7.4) is placed at the center
of the domain. The electric field accelerates free electrons and makes them move ac-
cordingly inside the domain. As the electrons interact with neutral molecules of the gas
mixture, the corresponding types of collisions from Section 2.3.2 take place; e.g. a free
electron may hit a neutral molecule with sufficient energy to ionize it leaving a positive
ion behind, plus a new free electron which may do the same to another molecule, as
a chain reaction. Ions are considered immobile for such short time (few ns) that the
simulations last, and thus after being created they remain stationary throughout the
duration of a simulation. The model is then able to trace all the possible interactions
happening between electrons and the ambient gas and to keep track of the electron
evolution in time [45].
The ionization of the ambient gas is considered weak in order to be able to ignore any
effects to its density and temperature due to perturbations. Although electromagnetic
fields are generated due to the discharges, in this project only the electric field (main
source of non-linearity in the model) is taken into account; not the magnetic field [45].

2.7.2
PIC-MCC method
A particle-in-cell (PIC) code is used in order to simulate particles that move freely
within a grid of a defined number of grid points. Monte Carlo collisions (MCC) is a
simple method for modeling particle collisions. It can be coupled with the PIC method
to obtain the PIC-MCC algorithm [62] used in this project. This combination implies
that all the under investigation different types of particle collision take place inside
a simulation domain of chosen spatial dimensions. So, the PIC-MCC code works by
looping over all source particles in this predefined spatial domain (with a size of 1.25
mm × 14 mm for the case of this project), testing each particle for a collision, and
if a collision (one of the four types described in Section 2.3.2) occurs, performing the
appropriate action. Here, in our Monte Carlo model, we assume that the ambient gas
is stationary and unmodified by the collisions [45].

3.1
Introduction
This chapter connects all the theory discussed in the previous chapter, to the final

3.2
The PIC-MCC model
In all simulations of this project, a 2.5D PIC-MCC code is used. It has previously been
used by Chanrion and Neubert [45] and by Köhn et al. [14], and I use it for this project
too, after doing the necessary modifications and additions.
Initially, a charge-neutral electron-ion patch (Section 2.7.4) is placed at the center
(z0=7 mm) of the simulation domain, as in Figure 2.5, with initial electron density
ne,0 of 1020 m−3 and initial electron avalanche size λ of 0.2 mm (Section 2.7.4.1). This
domain, in which particle collisions take place, is of cylindrical symmetry consisting of
two spatial coordinates, r and z, and three coordinates in velocity space, vr, vθ and
vz, for each individual (super)electron [65, 14].
More specifically, as in [14] and in
Figure 2.5, the cylindrical dimensions of the simulation domain are Lr = 1.25 mm and
Lz = 14 mm, with a number of discrete points of Nr = 150 and Nz = 1200 in r– and
z–direction, respectively. On these grid points, the initial ambient electric field Eamb is
applied pointing downwards (i.e. towards the cathode as in Figure 2.1), as also described
in the simulation diagram in Section 2.7.3.
At the boundaries (z = 0, Lz), the Dirichlet boundary conditions [45, 14] Φ(r, 0) =
0 and Φ(r, Lz) = Φmax = Eamb · Lz are used, and at the boundaries (r = 0, Lr), the
Neumann boundary conditions [45, 14] ∂Φ/∂r = 0 are used. Electrons that pass over
the boundaries are not considered anymore; they are written off.

mosphere for Primordial Earth (Section 3.3), N2:CO2=80%:20% and N2:CO2:H2O:H2:CO

3.3.5
Implementing propagation mechanisms
As described in Section 2.5, the most important propagation mechanisms taken into
account for this project are photoionization and background ionization. It is also men-
tioned that the first is a process well understood only for atmospheres that contain both
nitrogen and oxygen. Apparently, an issue arises regarding the implementation of the
photoionization process in the selected atmospheric compositions for Primordial Earth,
but this problem is dealed in the following paragraphs.
==PAGE:52==
32
3 Simulation set-up
3.3.5.1
Photoionization
Generally, researchers (from [67, 68, 69, 70, 46, 7, 47]) agree that photoionization (Sec-
tion 2.5.1) is the main propagation mechanism of positive streamers in air and facilitates
the negative streamers. However, according to the same sources and especially [47], the
traditional model suggests that in the case of an atmosphere in which either nitrogen
(N2) or oxygen (O2) would be absent, photoionization should stop completely, because
it depends on the ratio between the two. Consequently, this leads to the conclusion
that, since both of the atmospheric compositions I investigate do not contain oxygen
(O2), photoionization, even if present, we would still not know how it happens and thus
we would not be able to simulate it. Interestingly, it has experimentally been proven in
[7] that positive streamers can exist in the atmospheres of Venus or Jupiter-like planets
(CO2:N2 = 96.5%:3.5% and H2:He = 89.8%:10.2%, respectively), implying that maybe
photoionization plays some contributing role there. However, scientists still do not know
how exactly photoionization works in different than N2:O2 atmospheres, and also accord-
ing to [7, 47], photoionization is unlikely to exist on Venus. So, since to the extent of
our knowledge it is not known how photoionization works in our atmospheres so far (i.e.
in N2CO2 and in Kasting’s), this forces us to totally exclude it from the simulations of
this project in both oxidizing atmospheres here, and for comparison reasons in all other
gas mixtures as well, searching for other realistic ways of positive streamer propagation
to substitute it.
3.3.5.2
Background ionization
Due to reasons explained in the previous paragraph, photoionization cannot be used.
Thus, background ionization is employed instead. Based on the theory in Section 2.5.2,
typical levels of background ionization are between 103 cm−3 and 104 cm−3 Initially, this
range has been chosen for our simulations, too.
Another background ionization level that is considered, is of the magnitude of 105
cm−3, because, although I excluded photoionization due to the lack of concrete knowl-
edge or at least possible theories about how it works in other than air-like gas mixtures,
one cannot say with absolute certainty that photoionization might not happen at all in
different (than air) gas mixtures [67, 68, 69, 70, 46, 7, 47]. Thus, by using the latter
background ionization level, which is higher than the natural levels of 103-104 cm−3, I try
to ensure I cover such a hypothesis under which both processes, i.e. photoionization and
background ionization, are present and both contribute to the streamer propagation.

Summary
Here, I briefly remind the reader of some key points regarding what is set up in this

In this project, the PIC-MCC model is implemented initially for two oxidizing atmo-

fields (Eamb) are calculated in Table 3.1, Table 3.2 and Table 3.3; i.e. ambient fields of
1.5Ek and 3Ek.
As for streamer propagation mechanisms, in all cases photoionization is not used,
but background ionization of three different levels (Section 3.3.5): 103 cm−3, 104 cm−3
and 105 cm−3.

CHAPTER 5
Conclusions and outlook
As the title reveals, what was researched in this project, are streamer discharges in
the atmosphere of Primordial Earth. Early stages of lightning, the so-called streamers
(Section 2.2) are thin ionized plasma channels that penetrate into the background gas
and propagate under the influence of the ambient electric field. The field accelerates
the electrons of the under study gas mixture causing them to collide with the molecules
of the ambient gas, and thus to be involved into different types of elastic and inelastic
collisions (Section 2.3.2). Provided that the cross sections of the gases are available,
the PIC-MCC method (Section 2.7.2, Section 3.2) was employed in order to simulate
such collisions in a 2.5D (two spatial coordinates and three in velocity space) simulation
domain of cylindrical symmetry and predefined size.
Between different atmospheric compositions suggested for Primordial Earth’s atmo-
sphere, three were selected to work with in this project. The following two are both
examples of an oxidizing atmosphere, N2:CO2 = 80%:20% ([59, 52, 60, 54, 66, 4, 61] and
references therein) and N2:CO2:H2O:H2:CO = 80%:18.89%:1%:0.1%:0.01% suggested
by Kasting [66], [4]; for reasons of convenience they are simply called as ”N2:CO2” and
”Kasting”, respectively. The third atmospheric composition represents a reducing at-
mosphere and is the gas mixture used in the Miller-Urey experiment [1, 2, 3, 4, 5], that
is H2O:CH4:NH3:H2 = 37.5%:25%:25%:12.5%; for the same reasons this composition is
simply called ”Miller-Urey”.
A fourth and last atmospheric composition that was used is Modern Earth’s, in order
to be able to compare the Primordial Earth’s atmospheres to it, and generally to make
comparisons between all four of them. The gas mixture used is for artificial air [71]
N2:O2 = 80%:20%, or simply air.
Initially, photoionization was intended to be included to all four atmospheric compo-
sitions as the dominant propagation method of positive streamers and one that facilitates
the negative ones as well. However, due to lack of explicit knowledge on how it works
in other than air-like gas mixtures, three out of four mixtures of this project could not
include it. For reasons of consistency, it was finally excluded from all four gas mixtures.
Instead, it was substituted by another method that would serve the exact same propaga-
tion goals, background ionization. Trying to mimic the effects of photoionization through
background ionization, natural background density levels were taken into account; 103
==PAGE:86==
66
5 Conclusions and outlook
cm−3 and 104 cm−3. Another, higher level of background ionization was used, 105 cm−3,
considering the hypothesis that if with the natural levels, photoionization could be ”re-
placed”, then with an even higher one, the model would simulate conditions under which
both photoionization and background ionization are present and both contribute to the
streamer propagation and generally to the results of the simulations.
One last key condition that had to be decided and to be included into the code, is
the value of the ambient electric field; two values were selected. I started simulations
with 1.5Ek and continued with a higher ambient field of 3Ek; the breakdown field Ek
(Section 2.4) has a different value for each gas mixture, as can be seen in Table 3.1,
Table 3.2 and Table 3.3, and it corresponds to cases when ionization and attachment
rates are equal (Figure 3.3, Figure 3.4, Figure 3.6).
The obtained results from the simulations are for electron density and electric field,
along with avalanche/streamer properties such as electron energy distribution, maximum
electric field at positive and negative fronts, front velocities and positions.
In all cases, regardless of the combination, it has been observed that the maximum
electric field at the positive fronts is steadily higher than the one at the negative fronts.
This makes sense considering how the electrons behave at the streamer heads; they
diffuse more at the negative front, but they concentrate more at the positive, causing
electron multiplication due to more collisions there and thus higher electric field (more
effective ionization there).
All results confirmed another collective remark; as it was observed, all combinations
using Miller-Urey mixture propagate much faster and achieve to create streamers sooner
than using any other gas mixture.
Second comes air, then Kasting and last is the
N2:CO2. This order is maintained in all other results as well, and sometimes in pairs;
e.g. Miller-Urey and air contain the most energetic electrons in the simulations, even
with energies higher than 100 eV (when the ambient electric field is 3Ek and after 0.5
ns), whereas the most energetic electrons in Kasting and N2:CO2 are limited between
their ionization energies (Table 4.1) and 100 eV (in both ambient fields and up to 1.85
ns).
The above-mentioned order could be interpreted considering that less energetic elec-
trons get attached more easily to gases with big cross sections, such as H2O, the percent-
age of which corresponds to the majority in the Miller-Urey gas mixture ratio. Thus, in
Miller-Urey, if more energetic electrons remain, this means that they propagate faster
and make a streamer production occur faster.
In addition, as expected, by raising the ambient electric field from its initial value of
1.5Ek to 3Ek, no matter of what other parameters have been set, in all cases, higher am-
bient field caused faster movement (i.e. higher front velocities), higher electron energies,
and less time was required to observe a transition from an avalanche to a streamer.

Regarding the background ionization, it seems that in all gas mixtures, the higher its
level, the higher the front velocities, the electron energies, and the time for an avalanche-
to-streamer transition decreases.
As it was compared and analyzed by observing the figures of the previous chapter
called ”Simulation results” (and in the Appendix where extra figures can be found),
background ionization plays a significant role for the positive streamers and facilitates
the negative ones as well, something that was needed especially in this project in which
photoionization could not be implemented, as explained in the ”Theory” and the ”Sim-
ulation set-up” chapters (especially in Section 3.3.5).
In addition, from the background ionization levels that were tested (103 cm−3, 104
cm−3 and 105 cm−3), a general conclusion that could be extracted here, is that the higher
the background density, the sooner the simulations experience a transition from an initial
electron avalanche to a streamer. This quick transition is even more noticeable when
the ambient electric field is of higher value too, i.e. 3Ek instead of 1.5Ek, which means
that a combination of high background ionization level and strong ambient field would
almost secure a streamer production during the very first nanoseconds of a simulation
for all gas mixtures (Table 5.1).

If no increase happens, another way to achieve the same result (i.e. streamers in
these two gas mixtures as well, while the conditions are not favorable), could then be
to leave the conditions as they are, but to wait for a longer time to see if the expected
streamers will occur provided enough time; in simulations this is not an ideal solution
though, since it increases the computational cost (sometimes even memory errors) and,
obviously, the waiting time (from days to even weeks) for a simulation to be completed.
Maybe the ideal solution would be if we could include photoionization too, in the
gas mixtures of this project, while leaving the background ionization as it is; within its
natural density levels. This combination would probably give us bidirectional streamers
even in cases such as these exceptions.

. After taking into consideration the electron density, the maximum electric field, the
electron energies, the front velocities and positions, and generally all the results of the
streamer characteristics that were used, observed and analyzed in this project, from
the three gas mixtures that tried to represent the Primordial Earth’s atmosphere, i.e.
N2:CO2, Kasting and Miller-Urey, the results of the latter mixture are those closest
to the corresponding results for air, and thus for Modern Earth. This certainly does
not consist a proof that the gas mixture used in the Miller-Urey experiment could be
the right suggestion as the most probable Primordial atmospheric composition on our
planet, because Earth now compared to Earth back then have many differences. How-
ever, regardless of their differences, focusing instead on something they have in common
that concerns us all, i.e. life, today it has evolved in many different forms and can be
found almost anywhere we look for it on our planet, but back then, before life would be
able to appear, some critical conditions that would favor it first had to be ”set” right,
starting maybe with at least one; the streamers. If so, then maybe this could be another
confirmation of the Miller-Urey experiment and another reason why it was considered
successful by many, while trying to replicate conditions, which, through streamer dis-
charges in the atmosphere of Primordial Earth similar to those in today’s air, might
have led to the initiation of life.
.
. Apart from the consideration about simulations with a higher background ionization
level, something else that could be regarded as future work, is a Paschen curve, be-
cause we could then observe how the breakdown voltage depends on the product of gas
pressure and minimum breakdown distance [75].

Ancient Greeks believed lightning was Zeus’s weapon of destruction. This inter-
pretation seems reasonable, considering how spectacular and frightening it looks, 
plus how powerful it is. Who would have thought it may be responsible for the 
opposite too, i.e. creation? The Miller-Urey experiment suggests that it may have 
contributed to the origin of life on Earth through amino acid formation. In any 
case, it is a phenomenon that fascinates people and challenges scientists. 
While researchers investigate lightning now on Earth or other planetary bodies, 
we investigate its occurrence in the past (in Primordial Earth’s atmosphere ≈ 3.8 
Ga ago), focusing on its early stages, the so-called streamers; thin ionized plasma 
channels, formed by an initial electron avalanche and driven by the ambient elec-
tric ﬁeld.
