Abstract
Frequency stability in today’s power systems has become more critical than ever before
due to the growing contribution of renewable energy sources. This situation has arisen
because of the electro-mechanical decoupling between renewable generation sources and
the main grid caused by their connection through power electronic converters. Until now,
power system stability has relied heavily on large plants providing synchronous inertia
by utilising the kinetic energy stored in their rotors to counteract disturbances in system
frequency. In recent years much research has been carried out on additional services that
renewable generators can provide to contribute to system frequency stability. This MSc
thesis analyses the provision of synthetic inertia from wind power parks. Although this
service is only required to date by one transmission system operator in Canada, it is
deemed an issue so important that it is listed as one of three main focus topics in the
research and development plan for ENTSO-E 2015-2017.
The overarching goal of the thesis is to design two synthetic inertia controllers that
adhere to the grid code requirements from two different countries and then to utilise them
to aid in the integration of high levels of wind power penetration in a test power system.
The simulation software used is the Real Time Digital Simulation platform and the chosen
test system is the IEEE 9 Bus Power System. The controllers are designed using this
platform for a full converter wind turbine generator and are validated in isolated testing
at a rated wind speed of 12 m/s, and at a low wind speed of 7 m/s. A scenario analysis
is then carried out during which the penetration of wind power in the test system is
increased in steps of 10% until frequency stability can no longer be sustained. The role
of synchronous inertia in maintaining frequency stability is also explored through the use
of synchronous condensers. Finally the economic aspect of inertia is discussed, using the
real-world example of the Bornholm island power system.
This thesis identifies that, for the power system in question, the maximum penetration
of wind power that can be integrated while maintaining system stability is 50%, which also
involves the installation of 3 synchronous condensers. With regards the synthetic inertia
controllers, a df/dt triggering mechanism was found to be superior to a df mechanism
due to its lower deceleration and hence more efficient delivery of the service. The study
concluded that as synthetic inertia is introduced to more grid code requirements around
the world, further specification is needed on the characteristic of the response and, more
importantly, on the control during the recovery period of the service. Finally, the reduction
in turbine lifetime caused by the increased loading that occurs in the provision of synthetic
inertia is recommended as the most important area of further research, as without this
key input, the full economic assessment cannot be carried out.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Electricity is one of the most widely used commodities in the world today, and its share of
global final energy consumption has doubled over the past forty years [3]. Electric power
systems form the backbone of the production, delivery and use of electricity. Therefore,
efficient and reliable system operation is of the utmost importance to ensure efficient trans-
mission of electricity from generation to load centres. While the technical, economic and
market challenges associated with power system operation have historically been well un-
derstood, the large-scale introduction of modern variable renewable energy sources brings
a new wave of operational challenges [4].
Demand for electricity generally follows daily and seasonal patterns. On a domestic
level, this pattern sees a low demand for electricity during the night and increases as people
wake and activity ramps up. Peaks and troughs in demand for electricity reflect the daily
routines of users, the best example of which is the dinner-time peak which occurs in western
Europe between 6pm and 8pm every day. Despite recent advances in battery storage of
electricity, the fact remains that currently there is no economically efficient way to store
electrical energy at scale [5]. Therefore, electricity must be instantaneously generated as
it is required. This is to say that in an alternating current AC power system, the power
generated by power plants must match the power drawn by consumers, plus the system
losses, at any given moment in time. A system imbalance is defined as a mismatch between
the power being drawn from the system and the power being fed into the system. The
result of an imbalance is the deviation of the frequency of the power system from its optimal
value of 50 Hz (or in some countries 60 Hz.) As all power system components (including
generators, transformers, overhead lines and even loads) are designed and optimised for
operation strictly at 50 Hz, frequency stability is one of the most important aspects of
power system operation.
To account for the importance of a stable system frequency, generators in a power
system are equipped with control mechanisms that allow them to respond to changes
in system frequency both independently and on demand.
In traditional power plants
this takes the form of governor droop control.
This allows a generator to increase its
output by a certain percentage for each Hz that the system frequency drops below its
nominal value and decrease its power output by the same percentage when the system
frequency exceeds its nominal value [6].
When wind turbine generators (WTGs) were
first installed in the 1980s, they were synchronously connected to the power system just
like traditional power plants.
However with breakthroughs made in power electronics,
modern WTGs can now operate at multiple speeds as they are not electro-mechanically
2

coupled with the system [7].
This has the benefit of much greater efficiency in WTG
operation, but unfortunately disables them from providing a natural frequency response
[8]. This means that as traditional synchronous generators are gradually phased out for
asynchronous renewable energies, the total amount of inertia in a power system drops and
in the moments following a mismatch in generation and consumption in a power system,
the rate of change of frequency (ROCOF) increases [9].
So the challenge relating to inertial response and frequency stability in low inertia
systems is perhaps one of the greatest challenges facing power system operators in coming
years, and one of the biggest barriers to furthering the integration of renewable energy
sources into the power system. However, over the past decade important research has
proved the ability of WTGs to provide services to enhance frequency stability, namely
their ability to provide a form of emulated inertia which is also called synthetic inertia.
Synthetic inertia is defined as the fast delivery of additional power by a WTG relative to
a frequency deviation in the grid [10]. This capability, while still in its infancy in terms
of practical experience [11], is offered by several turbine manufacturers today [12].
The motivation of this work lies in the fact that, following over a decade of research,
synthetic inertia is becoming a required capability for Wind Power Parks (WPPs) around
the world. This transition from academic research to commercial implementation is deeply
interesting, and has lead to the main focus of this thesis being placed on the design of
synthetic inertia controllers to adhere to current and future grid code requirements, and
to test the extent of which the provision of synthetic inertia can aid in the integration of
wind power into the power system. Of course, it is well understood that synthetic inertia
alone cannot solve the frequency stability issues of power systems with large penetrations
of renewable generation and so therefore synchronous condensers (SCs) are also included
in this study. As previously mentioned, the goal of the study is to design synthetic inertia
controllers to be installed in a WPP to aid in the integration of high levels of wind power in
the test system. The first controller adheres to the Canadian transmission system operator
(TSO) Hydro-Quebec, as this is the only place where synthetic inertia is a requirement for
WPPs. The second controller is designed to adhere to the future requirements that are
currently being defined by the TSO in the UK, National Grid. The reason two controllers
were designed is to allow for the comparison of the different requirements being demanded
of WPPs by the two TSOs, and to identify areas in which further specification is needed.
Along with the economic assessment, this work aims to offer results which can aid in the
future planning of low inertia power systems.

Chapter 1. Introduction
1.1
Task List
In line with the objective of the thesis to identify the maximum penetration of wind
power that can be achieved in a power system through the use of synthetic inertia and
synchronous condensers to achieve frequency stability, the following tasks have been com-
pleted:
1. Summarise the research that has been done in the field of synthetic inertia and the
use of synchronous condensers to enhance frequency stability in low inertia systems.
2. Present and discuss grid code requirements related to synthetic inertia worldwide.
3. Design two different synthetic inertia controllers that adhere to different grid code
requirements.
4. Test and validate the controllers at rated wind speeds and low wind speeds.
5. Create an appropriate test system in which to install a WPP with synthetic inertia
capabilities.
6. Perform a scenario analysis for increasing levels of wind power penetration to identify
the upper limit which can be integrated utilising both synthetic and synchronous
inertia to achieve frequency stability.
7. Perform an economic assessment of the provision of synchronous and synthetic inertia
from various technologies.
1.2
Structure
This thesis is structured as follows:
• Chapter 1 introduces the topic of frequency stability and inertial response. It also
outlines the major objectives of the thesis and the methodology being undertaken.
• Chapter 2 presents the State of the Art regarding frequency stability, synthetic
inertia provision by WTGs, and synchronous condensers.
• Chapter 3 presents the current grid code requirements pertaining to synthetic inertia,
and discusses work being done by various TSOs in this area.
• Chapter 4 describes the design and validation of the two synthetic inertia controllers.
• Chapter 5 presents the IEEE 9 Bus System and the scenario analysis in which the
penetration of wind power is increased until the system stability limit is broken.
• Chapter 6 offers a discussion on the preceding chapters and a judgement on the
synthetic inertia controller designs.
• Chapter 7 contains the economic assessment of synthetic and synchronous inertial
technologies and presents real world frequency statistics from the Danish island
power system of Bornholm.
• Chapter 8 presents the conclusions of the work and discusses the most important
areas of further study.

Chapter 2
State of the Art
This chapter explains the role of generator inertial response in electrical power systems.
This begins with a brief outline of frequency stability as a component of power system
stability. The concept of inertia is then introduced and the contribution of wind turbine
generators and synchronous condensers to system inertia is discussed.
2.1
Principles of Frequency Stability
The classical definition of power system stability is ”the ability of a power system to regain
an equilibrium state after being subjected to a physical disturbance” [1].
Figure 2.1: Definition of Power System Stability as presented in [1]
Figure 2.1 defines the three key aspects of power system stability; rotor angle, fre-
quency, and voltage stability. While all three elements are essential to secure power sys-
tem operation, power systems with large penetrations of non-synchronous generation are
most likely to experience problems with frequency stability, hence this is the element cho-
sen for study in this thesis. Frequency stability fundamentally involves the instantaneous
balancing between the total generation and total demand in a power system, minimising
deviations in system frequency from the ideal value of 50 Hz in Europe. [13]
Stages of a Frequency Deviation
Given the large scale of modern day power systems, and the quantity and size of generators
and load centres, it is impossible to maintain a perfect frequency signal of 50 Hz. Allowable

ranges of frequency signals are defined by national TSOs. In the Central European (CE)
grid, the allowable frequency range is defined as 50 Hz +/- 0.02 Hz [14], whilst in the
Nordic power system and the Irish and UK power systems, the allowable frequency ranges
is 50 Hz +/- 0.1 Hz [15]. Deviations outside of this acceptable range are deemed to be
frequency events. These are caused by disturbances in the system such as the loss of a
generator or transmission line, or the disconnection or connection of a large load centre.
Figure 2.2: Stages of a Frequency Deviation [1]
Figure 2.2 illustrates an exaggerated under-frequency event in a power system.
It
shows the frequency signal declining from 50 Hz to 49.2 Hz following an incident. The
whole deviation can be broken down into four sections. As can be seen in Figure 2.2,
the system operates normally within the allowable frequency range for the first 8 seconds.
Then at t=8s, a system imbalance occurs.
Stage one is titled the frequency drop as this is when the biggest drop in frequency
occurs, in Figure 2.2 this lasts from t=8s to t=10s. Here it is caused by consumption
being greater than production. During this stage, the ROCOF is determined by the total
system inertia. Synchronous generators react naturally to this imbalance and increase their
output depending on their inertia constant in order to reduce ROCOF. As this critical
stage depends on system inertia, it is the period which is focused on throughout this study.
Stage two features the Primary Frequency Response (PFR) and here lasts from t=10s
to t=30s. The lowest point of frequency that is reached during the transient is called the
frequency nadir, and here is equal to 49.2 Hz. During PFR the governor droop controls
in synchronous generators react to the power imbalance and in this case increase their
production according to their droop setting characteristics. The lower the droop value of
a generator, the more it changes its power output to react to an imbalance such as this.
The aim of PFR is to restore balance between generation and consumption levels [16].
Stage three features secondary control. This action is performed by TSOs or by au-
tomatic generation control (ACG). The purpose of this section is to restore the system
6

frequency to within its acceptable range and then to dispatch sufficient power generation
to alleviate the primary reserves, enabling them to be ready for the next frequency devi-
ation. The final form of control is called tertiary control. This is less critical than the
other reserves as it deals with the rescheduling and dispatching of generators by the TSOs
in the coming hours to account for the event that has just occurred (e.g. if a large load
has just been connected then more generators must be dispatched in the coming hours to
ensure that generation can continue to meet consumption.)

generation sources and system inertia are presented.

2.2.2
Inertia from Different Generation Sources
As discussed in the previous section all electrically coupled machinery contribute to system
inertia [18]. This is because their rotor shaft is synchronously coupled to the stator field.
However, in the case of renewable generation sources such wind power or solar PV, there is
no such electro-mechanical coupling. They are instead interfaced with the grid via power
electronic converters, which in the case of WTGs allows them to operate with variable
speed, and hence their rotor speed is decoupled from the system frequency.
In power
systems with high penetrations of non-synchronous generation, traditional generators have
already begun to be displaced by renewable alternatives. This poses the greatest threat
to power system stability during the inertial response phase of a frequency event [19].
Practically speaking, more renewable penetration means a ’lighter’ power system with
less inertia which experiences a bigger and steeper frequency drop following a frequency
disturbance. Without taking measures against this problem, the integration of renewable
energy in future power systems will be limited in order to ensure secure power system
operation. Fortunately there are measures that can be taken to counteract the effects of
low system inertia, which are explained in the coming sections.
2.3
Synthetic Inertia from WTGs
Research into the area of inertial response from WTGs is long established with publications
dating back over ten years [13].
Over the last decade this topic has evolved from a
research area, into a turbine requirement [20], to an area with newly published practical
implementation experience [11]. There are distinct challenges posed by the usage of WTGs
to enhance system frequency stability [21], [22], and as with all new technologies, multiple
opposing methodologies have been proposed to enable synthetic inertia. Each of these are
discussed in detail in the following sections.
2.3.1
Approaches to Inertial Response Emulation
With regards the approaches taken to emulate an inertial response from a WTG, there are
two main methodologies that have progressed successfully from abstract design through to
physical implementation, while other more novel mechanisms are discussed in literature.
The two more realistic methods are presented below.
’Hidden Inertia’ Rotor Energy Extraction
This approach has long been discussed in literature [13] and has been given many different
names including ’Hidden Inertia’, ’Synthetic Inertia’, and ’Emulated Inertia.’ These names
all reflect the fact that type 3 and type 4 WTGs offer no ’natural’ inertial response as
they are electrically decoupled from the grid. This approach has recently seen practical
implementation on a commercial scale by turbine manufacturers Siemens [23], Enercon
[24] and GE [25].

The basis of this approach is that the WTG operates along its normal maximum power
point tracking (MPPT) curve, however when a frequency event is triggered, either by a
frequency deviation or a threshold ROCOF value [21], an additional signal is sent to the
power reference [26]. This overrides the internal MPPT signal and instead determines
the reference power output of the WTG by a combination of the derivative of the system
frequency and WTG speed reference. This is done in practice by increasing the electrical
torque in the generator, causing the WTG main shaft to slow down and allowing inertial
energy stored in the turbine rotor and shaft to be extracted [27] [28] [29]. The rotational
power is described by the expression P = T · ω. Where ω is the rotational speed and T is
the torque [1].

response is temporary in nature and has the drawback of a recovery period as illustrated

Delta Control
The principal behind delta control is that the WTG continuously operates at a delta rela-
tive to its maximum available power signal [33]. The proposed value of this power reserve
delta ranges from 5% [29], 10% [34] to 20% [35] [36] of rated power and is implemented us-
ing a varying pitch angle. This is easily implemented using a frequency sensitive adaptive
gain through which the reference power signal passes [34]. For normal operation the gain
remains between 0.8 and 0.9, and increases up to 1 for under frequency events to release
reserve power into the system.
This approach has the obvious benefit of no recovery period, as the WTG operates
with a reserve prior to the frequency deviation. This reserve could be reset following the
frequency event at a point when the system frequency has been appropriately restored
[37]. However, an equally obvious disadvantage is that it is an uneconomical solution [38],
meaning that it is unfeasible and unlikely to be implemented in the near future [34]. This
is due to the fact that most systems with high penetrations of wind power still feature
lucrative feed-in-tariffs (FiT) for wind generation, where any curtailment of wind power
is very costly to the system operator [39]. Furthermore, findings in Gonzalez-Longatt et.
al [21] demonstrate that while delta control offers a smoother overall power response to
an under-frequency event, the ’hidden inertia’ approach gives a faster response due to a
higher power output increase over time, resulting in a lower system ROCOF and higher
nadir following a frequency event, which is the primary goal of inertial response.
Other Novel Approaches
Rotor energy extraction and delta control are not the only approaches to enable synthetic
inertia from WTGs. In [18] Licari proposes extracting stored energy from the DC-link
capacitors to use for inertial response. Large amounts of energy are stored in the DC-
link capacitors of a full-converter WTG during normal operation. The control action is
implemented by reducing the reference voltage of the grid-side controller proportional to
the frequency deviation signal. While this is an interesting concept it is not chosen to be

implemented as the voltage across the DC Link is critical to the machine and should not
be compromised.
Another novel method is proposed by Ehrlich and Wilch in [34], through which the WTG
rotor actually accelerates before decelerating and releasing inertial kinetic energy. The title
Kinetic Energy Control II (KEC) is given to this. This approach is based on the fact that
kinetic energy stored in the rotating masses of a machine increases as the square of its
rotational speed. Therefore, the faster the rotor is spinning when the response is triggered,
the more kinetic energy that can be delivered. While this paper shows that in comparison
to the regular ’hidden inertia’ approach, this method gives a higher frequency nadir and
a quicker overall recovery time for the given parameters, it results in a significantly higher
ROCOF, an important goal of inertial response [34].
2.3.2
Differences Between Inertial Response from WTGs
and Synchronous Machines
The literature has proven that multiple approaches are available to enable a synthetic
inertial response from WTGs [6]. However, an important point discussed by Ruttledge in
[40] is that inertial response from WTGs must be considered as distinctly different from
that of synchronous generators, and for this there are four main reasons.
1. Following the deceleration of the WTG main-shaft when extracting kinetic energy,
acceleration must occur for the turbine to regain normal MPPT operation [26]. If
the WTG is operating above rated wind speed when the frequency deviation occurs,
this acceleration can simply be achieved by reducing the pitch angle of the blades.
However, if the WTG is operating below rated wind speed, a recovery period is
required in which the WTG significantly reduces its energy output to the grid [17].
This is a drawback as the inertial response results in some energy costs for the grid.
2. Despite the fact that control measures allow WTGs to react to frequency deviations
and temporarily supply additional energy to reduce ROCOF and increase nadir,
there is no denying that this response is still electrically decoupled from the grid,
and therefore perhaps not as reliable in power system security terms [19].
3. Thirdly, as the inertial response is a result of a control scheme, the characteristic of
the response is subject to the tuning of the designer. This could be beneficial as it
provides additional flexibility that traditional plant cannot. In [41] Ruttledge out-
lines different operational scenarios during which different gain values could be found
through bespoke response optimisation to offer a tailored power system response to
frequency deviations.
4. Finally, in terms of the difference between an emulated inertial response (EIR) from
WTGs and the inertial response from synchronous generators, it must be noted that
an EIR from WTGs varies depending on geographical location and wind speed and
therefore the total potential inertial response of this type is constantly changing with
time [42]. This poses a huge challenge for TSOs to constantly monitor system inertia
requirements in real time.

2.3.3
Challenges Relating to Inertial Response of WTGs
Just as there are distinct characteristics of the inertial response emulated by WTGs, there
are also distinct challenges. These are summarised below.
Rotor Angle Stability
Rotor angle stability is defined as ”the ability of synchronous machines of an interconnected
power system to remain synchronised under normal operating conditions and after being
subjected to a disturbance. It depends on the ability to maintain and restore equilibrium
between electromagnetic torque and mechanical torque of each synchronous machine in
the system between approximately 3 to 5 seconds following a disturbance [1].” Rotor angle
stability is particularly important for areas with large interconnections, such as those in
the CE grid, and it is also of growing importance as the level of synchronous inertia in
power systems starts to fall. Given that the inertial response phase of frequency stability
and rotor stability are effected during the same critical period, it is considered what impact
synthetic inertia has on rotor angle stability. The use of the service for rotor angle stability
enhancement in particular has not yet been the subject of much research. However, as
discussed in [43], the clearing time for faults at a transmission level in the CE grid is
in the range of 100 ms, therefore in order to affect rotor angle stability the activation
time of the synthetic inertia must be in the range of 50 ms. This value has not been
achieved in practice. Furthermore, findings in [44] show that synthetic inertia causes a
slight deterioration in the critical clearing time for transient stability. Through eigenvalue
analysis it was found that incorrect tuning of the synthetic inertia controller gains can lead
to a degradation in oscillation damping in the system. It was concluded that a properly
tuned synthetic inertia controller should not negatively affect rotor stability, however this
is subject to further research [45].
Triggering Mechanisms and Frequency Measurement
One of the biggest challenges that remains in synthetic inertia is the identification of the
best triggering mechanism for the controller.
This is largely due to the fact that the
inertial response controller is solely concerned with system frequency, and this parameter
is itself inherently noisy and difficult to measure [46].
This is because frequency is a
derived signal, meaning that it cannot be directly measured but must be calculated using
the three phase voltage signal at the point of common coupling (PCC) between the WTG
and the grid. The voltage signal is noisy and therefore the frequency signal must be at
least as noisy as the voltage signal. The biggest danger associated with this relates to
the accidental triggering of inertial response, which under certain circumstances could be
harmful to system frequency stability, and hence should be avoided at all costs.
Recent work has been completed by Loughborough University to identify the optimal
triggering mechanism for emulated inertial responses from WTGs. Gonzalez-Longatt com-
pares three various mechanisms: a continuously operating control, an under-frequency
trigger, and a maximum frequency gradient trigger [47]. A controller that is continuously
operating, i.e. without a triggering mechanism, is criticised for being unrealistic in that
it continuously extracts kinetic energy from the WTG rotor for any change in system
frequency. Furthermore, every grid code in publication allows for a certain dead-band
around optimal system frequency (due to the fact that absolute power balance is impossi-
ble to continuously maintain [48]). Out of the two remaining triggering mechanisms, the
12
==PAGE:26==
Chapter 2. State of the Art
under-frequency comparator is shown to be quicker with an activation time of 250 ms,
than the maximum gradient trigger which takes 450 ms to activate [47]. However, what is
even more interesting and beneficial is that the under-frequency trigger provides the best
dynamic response in terms of a higher frequency nadir and also the biggest delay before
it is reached [47]. This in turn allows more time for governors in traditional synchronous
generators to respond and aid in system frequency recovery. For this reason, an under-
frequency trigger is utilised for the design of inertial response controllers in this thesis,
where the grid code of the system in question does not stipulate otherwise.
Speed Control
While active speed control of WTGs during inertial response was ignored in much of the
early literature [27], it was still acknowledged as a necessity before practical implementa-
tion of the controller could take place [26]. Speed control is a critical element to include
as this area poses the largest threat to the WTG operational safety. As previously dis-
cussed, during inertial response kinetic energy is extracted from the WTG rotor causing
deceleration. If too much kinetic energy is extracted then the rotor will fall below its
minimum rotational speed and stall [28]. In order to avoid damaging the WTG during
the provision of synthetic inertia, it should be ensured that the WTG is never allowed to
decelerate below its minimum rotational speed [49]. There are numerous ways proposed
to implement this.
The simplest and most commonly discussed approach to implement speed control is
through the use of an adaptive gain. A linear weighting of the inertial response signal
from 1 at full speed to 0 at 40% of rated machine speed is proposed in [35]. A more novel
approach is proposed in [50]. This paper presents a combined approach to inertial control,
rotor speed control and pitch angle control for low, medium, and high wind speed modes.
The focus is on maximising the inertial response of a doubly fed induction generatorDFIG
turbine for every wind speed. For low wind speeds only rotor speed control is utilised.
For wind speeds above rated wind speed, where the turbine pitches its blades to remain
at rated power output, pitch control is used to regulate the inertial response during the
recovery period. The benefit of this is that no power is drawn back from the grid during
the recovery as the WTG can be accelerated again simply by pitching the blades. In terms
of speed control, [50] proposes the inclusion of the upper and low speed limits of the WTG
during the calculation of the inertial response controller gain K, which then ensures that
the turbine always gives the maximum possible response, without exceeding its upper and
lower limits. While the approach presented in this paper is novel and interesting, it is
outside the scope of this thesis to implement a coordinated control strategy for rotor and
pitch control across three wind speed ranges.
Practical Implementation
While the research area of synthetic inertia is well established, the first papers discussing
practical experience with the service have only recently been published. In [11], operational
experience from a WPP in Quebec with two years of data is discussed. The 138 MW WPP
consists of 60 ENERCON type 4 WTGs each with a synthetic inertia controller. The
paper shows that the overall inertial emulation of the wind farm works as expected, which
means that the WTGs are capable of responding to large, short-duration under-frequency
conditions in the grid with an increase in their active power output [11]. However it is also
noted that inaccuracies can occur due to the aggregation of control functionality from a
13
==PAGE:27==
Chapter 2. State of the Art
turbine to a park level. It must be considered that each WTG across a WPP operates with
a different rotational speed, and can often be subject to a different wind speed. Hence each
WTG has a unique quantity of inertia to offer to the system. If this is all simply averaged
across the WPP, then certain turbines could see more damage in terms of increased loading
than others [11]. Another observation is made regarding the difference in WTG response
depending on power output. The output is divided between lower power band (), upper
power band (UPB) and nominal power band (NPB), and it is shown that following a
frequency event, the recovery period for operation in the UPB is deeper and longer than
that of the LPB response. The paper finally notes that the practical experience gained
in Quebec should not only be utilised by turbine manufacturers to improve their control
mechanisms but should also be studied by TSOs to fine-tune the requirements placed on
WPPs to provide synthetic inertia.
2.4
Synchronous Condenser Inertial Response
If inertia emulation (IE) from wind turbine generators is a new demand being placed on
a relatively new technology, then inertial response from synchronous condensers is quite
the opposite. SCs have played an essential role in power system stability for over half a
century. Their primary tasks have been reactive power compensation and voltage stability,
which is achieved through power factor control or excitation current control [51].
A synchronous condenser is identical in design to a synchronous machine, except that
the main shaft is not connected to anything and therefore is available to spin freely [52].
They are useful machines for grid stability enhancement as they increase the overall short
circuit capacity of the systems in which they are installed [53]. This in turn improves the
stability of weak interconnections [54], facilitates system protection and even improves the
operation of power electronic devices [55].
However, in recent years an increasing amount of research has been carried out on
the capability of SCs to improve the frequency stability of power systems with low inertia
through inertial response [56], [57], [58]. Because of the synchronous nature of SCs, inertia
is an inherent feature of the machines and therefore the frequency and inertial response
offered by them is natural and does not require supplementary control element unlike
WTGs [48]. Kinetic energy stored in the rotating main-shaft of an SC is continuously
available to the grid which can be either released or absorbed for system frequency regu-
lation during disturbances [59]. For example, during an under-frequency event like a loss
of generation, an SC will release kinetic energy to the grid to act against a steep ROCOF.
Likewise, during an over-frequency event such as a loss of load, an SC will speed up to
absorb the surplus power in the grid to act against a runaway frequency [51].
In [60], system frequency response enhancement using SCs is simulated and tested on
the South Australian power system. Several interesting and noteworthy insights are made
here. The initial and unsurprising finding is that as the number of synchronous condensers
in a system increases, so too does the frequency nadir. The ROCOF decreases, and the
point in time at which the nadir is reached following a frequency disturbance is delayed
[60]. However, what is more interesting is that these system benefits appear to saturate
beyond a certain level of SCs, which is to say that beyond a certain installed capacity,
the additional inertia brought about by new SCs has little to no benefit.

Current Grid Code Requirements
A grid code is a document drafted by the TSO of each transmission system in the world.
It outlines the requirements that must be met by each generator wishing to connect to
either the transmission or the distribution system [14]. The requirements vary according
to the rated power output of the machine in question and the voltage level at which that
generator wishes to connect. Grid code requirements include specifications for generator
behaviour in the areas of, for example:
• Active Power Output
• Low Voltage / Fault Ride Through
• Reactive Power Capabilities

clarification.

3.3
Future ENTSO-E Inertia Requirements
No TSO in Europe has placed requirements on WPPs to provide synthetic inertia to date.
This is due to the fact that the countries with the largest penetrations of renewable energy
are also well interconnected to neighbouring systems, meaning that frequency stability has
not been a major concern of the power system operators in the CE grid. Major exceptions
to this are Ireland and the UK, which are more isolated than the rest of the European
grids.
Nonetheless ENTSO-E, the European Network of Transmission System Operators for
Electricity, have indicated that inertial response capabilities will be mandatory for many
WPPs [28]. In the ENTSO-E draft connection codes published in 2012 [63], it was stated
that all WPPs with a rated output power greater than or equal to 50 MW, or with a
connection point greater than or equal to 110 kV, must provide synthetic inertia for the
purposes of supporting frequency stability in the given system. This requirement is to be
placed on both onshore and offshore WPPs in the coming years [10].

3.5
Synthetic Inertia Studies for ROCOF Analysis
in the Irish Power System
While no requirements have been placed on WPPs in Ireland to provide synthetic inertia
services to date, it is widely recognised and understood that Ireland is one of the first
power systems to experience the operational problems associated with high system non-
synchronous penetration (SNSP), due to the isolated nature of the system and the high
instantaneous penetration of wind power at any given time [66]. As a result of this, studies
are currently being carried out to identify possible solutions to the problems associated
with the ROCOF increases that arise in low inertia systems.
It is pertinent to note here that the technical abilities of power system components to
improve the initial stage of a frequency event (i.e. increase the frequency nadir or decrease
the ROCOF) fall under the service of Fast Frequency Response (FFR). Therefore, while
FFR is the broad service which is discussed in much of the literature where different
technologies are compared, it should be understood that synthetic inertia is one of the
control mechanisms which enables the delivery of FFR by WTGs.

attention was given in the report to asynchronous technologies that could help to prevent

mitigation of ROCOF events [67].

ROCOF Solutions Project Phase 2
The final report of the second phase of the ROCOF Alternative Solutions Project, car-
ried out by the Irish TSO EirGrid, was published on March 31st 2016 [68]. The aim of
this phase was to determine the volumes of synchronous and/or synthetic inertia required
to maintain ROCOF at 0.5 Hz/s. A base case model was created that was free from op-
erational constraints such as must run inputs or reserves. This model was unconstrained
with the exception of an SNSP limit set to 75% and a ROCOF set to 1 Hz/s. Studies
were then carried out to achieve a ROCOF of 0.5 Hz/s firstly by adding only synchronous
19
==PAGE:33==
Chapter 3. International Grid Codes for Inertia Requirements
inertia, then by adding only synthetic inertia, and lastly by using a combination of the
two.
It was identified that synchronous inertia is a clear solution to maintaining ROCOF
within +/- 0.5 Hz/s. Studies indicated that a system inertia of at least 20,000 MW.s needs
to be retained for the majority of dispatches to achieve this ROCOF goal. This equates
to approximately 12,000 extra MW.s of synchronous inertia to be added to the 1 Hz/s
base case [68]. The addition of further constraints such as minimum reserve requirements
reduces the amount of additional synchronous inertia required.
It was also found that synthetic inertia could form an alternative solution to the ROCOF
goal of +/- 0.5 Hz/s. However, there were distinct challenges associated with this approach.
In particular the performance of synthetic inertia devices was found to be highly sensitive
to the characteristics of the response, particularly the response time and device ramp rate.
According to the report, in order to meet the ROCOF goal the following criteria must be
satisfied:
1. A response time no greater than 100 ms following the start of the event.
2. The active power injection must be fully achieved within 200ms after the device begins
to respond.
3. A suitable form of control is required to prevent unintended adverse system issues
during the frequency recovery.
4. A minimum of +/- 360MW of supplementary synthetic inertia would need to be
available.
5. Synthetic inertia response is required for both high and low frequency events.
It was concluded that ROCOF target value of +/- 0.5 Hz/s could also be achieved
utilising a combination of synchronous and synthetic inertia technologies from the list
outlined in the Phase 1 report, however as previously mentioned this is highly dependent
on the characteristic of the synthetic inertia response. The report highlighted the need
for a TSO led demonstration project to deepen the understanding of synthetic inertia
capabilities of the technologies in question and to create detailed requirements for synthetic
inertia response characteristics.

WTG Full Converter Model in RSCAD
Within RSCAD , the software tool associated with the RTDS platform, there are pre-
installed models for each of the four different types of WTGs. As the trend with WTGs in
Western Europe continues with an emphasis on bigger rotor diameters, bigger generators
and offshore locations [69], it was decided that the appropriate choice of WTG for this
project is a type 4, full converter model.
The model is taken as presented from the
RSCAD library, the appropriate modifications are made to the controllers with the aim of
emulating an inertial response for a given frequency deviation. The turbine is then scaled
up to an appropriate park size model. This is installed in the test power system for the
purposes of inertial response testing simulations.
The type 4 WTG model included in the RSCAD library is a variable speed, direct-drive,
permanently-excited, 2 MW wind turbine. It is titled PMTST550 and is designed as a 2.5
MVA, 50 Hz system. There is also an identical 60 Hz model available. The model can be
scaled simply by using the interface transformers provided within the model. Back-to-back
3 level voltage source converters (VSC) are used to transfer power from the permanent
magnet synchronous machine (PMSM) to the AC system. Standard control systems are
included for the pitch control and rotor speed control which are based on a basic speed
vs power characteristic that is used to track the maximum available power output at
any given time. Further details on specific components of the full converter model used
throughout this project can be found in this manual [70]. This includes details on VSCs,
PMSM, and the interface transformers.
4.2
Design Criteria for Synthetic Inertia Controller
As previously discussed in Chapter 2, synthetic inertia provision involves the extraction of
kinetic energy from the WTG rotor. This is done by supplying an additional power signal
that pulls the WTG away from its MPPT curve, decelerates the rotor, and then allows the
WTG to return to normal operation when the the response has been given. The control
trajectory is shown in Figure 4.1.
21
==PAGE:35==
Chapter 4. Controller Design & Validation
Figure 4.1: Synthetic Inertia Control Trajectory relative to MPPT Curve
Figure 4.1 Shows the relationship between WTG power and rotational speed for varying
wind speeds, and the MPPT curve which intersects each of these and their maximum point.
Since all simulations for this study are carried out at a rated wind speed of 12 m/s, only
this curve is relevant here. Point ’A’ indicates the starting point where the WTG operates
along its MPPT curve. When the synthetic inertial response is triggered, an additional
power signal is generated which causes the WTG to operate at point B, with a higher
output power and a lower rotational speed. In order for the WTG to return to its starting
point, it must first operate at point C, with a lower output power and lower rotational
speed, from which point it can accelerate to regain its original operating point at A.
This describes the fundamental operation of the controller, however it is also important
to outline specific design objectives for the controller which are listed below:
1. The IE controller should cause an increase in the power output of the given WTG
proportional to the rate of change of frequency or absolute frequency as detected by
the WTG.
2. The response should only occur for under-frequency deviations. No response should
be given by the IE controller for over-frequency deviations, nor should a response be
visible during the recovery phase of a frequency drop when the frequency remains
below the optimal value of 50Hz.
3. The response should take the form of an additional power signal that is summed
with the optimal power signal prior to the calculation of the machine speed. This
ensures a return to stable and optimal operation of the WTG when zero output is
given by the IE controller.
4. The nature of the power increase and duration of response should fulfil the criteria
as outlined in the grid code requirements for WTGs in the country in question.
5. A form of speed control is to be present in the IE controller to prevent this additional
22
==PAGE:36==
Chapter 4. Controller Design & Validation
action from causing the WTG to decelerate below its minimum operational speed.
This is a protection mechanism for the mechanical components of the machine.
6. The total combined power output of the WTG from the power output set point and
the additional power from inertia emulation should not exceed 1.1 p.u. as outlined
in [19].
7. The controller parameters should be tuned as such to minimise the recovery time
and to avoid a secondary dip in system frequency during this recovery phase where
possible.
8. Finally the response of the IE controller should be reproducible and consistent for
comparable frequency deviations over time.

4.4.1
Validation Testing Methodology
The purpose of this section is to describe the validation of the synthetic inertia controllers.
The RSCAD template for a full converter WTG is shown in Figure 4.5. This is a simplified
single line diagram of the WTG template, to illustrate that it features a WTG model
connected to an infinite bus operating at 33 kV and to an RL load.
Figure 4.5: RSCAD Full Converter WTG Template
Validation testing is carried out at turbine level. Figure 4.5 shows the WTG connected
to an infinite bus and an RL load. Given the nature of the infinite bus, if either of the
pre-programmed faults had been utilised during testing, the infinite source would react to
the fault regardless of the synthetic inertia controller present in the WTG. For this reason,
the synthetic inertia controllers (also referred to as the inertia emulation controllers) were
fed a dummy trigger signal to assess whether they reacted correctly to different frequency
events. For the Canadian controller, the dummy input consisted of a frequency slider since
the controller is df triggered. For the GB controller, a df/dt slider was used. Given that the
scope of this work only involves the analysis of under-frequency events, the first validation
test was a simple over frequency (or positive df/dt in the case of the GB controller) test to
ensure that the controllers ignore all activity above 50 Hz. Following this the controllers
were tested at both rated wind speed and below rated wind speed to test the operation of
the speed controller. These are described in the following sections.
4.4.2
Validation Testing at Rated Wind Speed
The lowest frequency for which generators in Denmark are required to remain connected
to the power system and operate normally is 47 Hz [71]. The frequency threshold at which
the synthetic inertia controller should be triggered in the Hydro-Quebec grid code is 49.5
Hz [69]. Therefore the tuning of the controllers was carried out to maximise the response
given for a frequency deviation of 0.5 Hz, while ensuring an appropriate response could
still be maintained for a frequency deviation of 3 Hz.

System Stability Limit Scenario
The 50% penetration scenario, as previously mentioned, incorporates 160 MW of wind

was tested to the limits of its stable operation, a final test scenario was then carried out,
with the aim of identifying whether more than 82 WTGs could be incorporated into the
system in steady state system. At such high levels of wind power penetration, it is required
to start the simulation with the synthetic inertia control disabled and the WPP scaling
factor with a maximum value of 70. Once the system has started up successfully, the WPP
scaling factor can be increased until the appropriate number of WTGs are simulated. In
order to reach 60% wind penetration, 192 MW wind power must be incorporated.This
amounts to approximately 104 WTGs.
By slowly increasing the WPP scaling factor, then re-dispatching G2 to provide the
remaining necessary power to achieve demand generation balance, it was found that the
highest penetration of wind power that could be achieved in the system under stable steady
state operation was 184 MW, or 57.5% of the total system demand. It is worth noting
that decreasing the MVA capacity of G2 to maintain a constant volume of primary reserve
in the system inhibited any penetration increase of wind power in this scenario. Therefore,
for the purposes of this study the MVA capacity of G2 was left unchanged. Furthermore,
when exposed to the loss of G1, the frequency response characteristic of the system was
unstable, resembling the black line in Figure 5.12. This indicates a lack of synchronous
inertia in the given scenario, and while it was solved in previous scenarios by increasing
the quantity of synchronous condensers in the system, the system is now unfortunately
saturated in terms of the quantity and capacity of synchronous condensers. It can hence
be concluded that the maximum feasible penetration of wind power in this power system
is between 50% and 57.5%.

Chapter 6
Discussion
The aim of this chapter is to reflect on the studies and analysis carried out during the sim-
ulation phase of this project, and then to relate them back to the overall thesis objectives.
This includes a final assessment of the controller performance and a sensitivity analysis on
the tuning of the controllers, a reflection on the various grid code requirements presented
throughout the report, and finally summary of the role played of synchronous condensers
in this project.
6.1
Reflection on Grid Code Requirements
Following the design, validation of testing of two synthetic inertia controllers which adhere
to different grid code requirements, the compelling conclusion that can be drawn is these
requirements can all be met. Both absolute frequency deviation and ROCOF triggers have
been proven to work, and it has been proven that a WPP can be controlled to increase its
power output relative to a frequency deviation for time periods in the range of 5-10 s.
However, the more valuable outcome of the studies carried out during this project lies
in the identification of specifications lacking from current grid code literature surrounding
synthetic inertia. These include:
• Activation Time: This specification itself also depends on the goals of the TSO in
question regarding the use of synthetic inertia. If, as described in the Irish case in
Chapter 3, the service is being used to prevent ROCOF events, then extremely fast
activation and delivery times are required, e.g. 100 ms and 200 ms respectively [68].
However, as shown in the scenario analysis in Chapter 5, if the goal is to reduce the
frequency nadir that occurs following a frequency event, then much larger activation
and delivery times can still provide valuable results. Therefore further clarification
is required from TSOs as to the aim of synthetic inertia in the power system in
question and, following this, appropriate values for activation and delivery times
should be given.
• Power Injection: Figure 3.1 indicates a requirement to increase power production
by a maximum of 5.4% relative to nominal power output. However the Canadian
grid code stipulates simply a 5% increase in power output for 10s [20]. The context
of the power injection should be clearly stated in all grid codes. While a power
increase relative to rated WTG power is simpler to implement as it is a constant
45
==PAGE:59==
Chapter 6. Discussion
value, a power increase relative to current, or pre-fault, power output is perhaps
more feasible to implement for WTGs operating below rated wind speed.
• Recovery Period: This is the most important specification for synthetic inertia that
has not been sufficiently detailed in any grid code. The results of the scenario analy-
sis from Chapter 5 show that without any requirements placed on the control of the
recovery period, a secondary frequency event can easily be caused by WTGs, espe-
cially at high penetrations of wind power. It is recommended that both the duration
of the recovery period and the magnitude of the allowable recovery power dip be
specified by the TSO in the synthetic inertia requirements. With this information,
OEMs could incorporate a distributed recovery period into their synthetic inertia
controllers and mitigate the possibility of causing a secondary frequency drop.
6.2
Assessment of Controller Performance
While the primary objective of this project is not to design two synthetic inertia controllers
for direct comparison (especially considering the two controllers were designed with as
many of the same components as possible for the sake of homogeneity) it would nonetheless
be unacceptable not to make some final comments regarding the performance of each
controller.
It is clear that in the majority of scenarios explored in Chapter 5, the best performing
controller was the GB controller. The inherent difference in the controllers lies in their
triggering mechanisms, and it can be concluded that df/dt is a more suitable triggering
signal that absolute frequency deviation for the purposes of synthetic inertia. The GB
controller, particularly in scenarios of penetration from 10% - 30%, was able to provide
a higher peak power injection with a lesser total deceleration and a lower total volume
of kinetic energy. This resulted in both a better frequency improvement and a smaller
recovery period for the GB controller. The reason for this is that the ROCOF value is
highest immediately following the frequency event, in this case the loss of G1. Therefore
the GB controller produces its peak power output as soon as possible following the event,
where it is most beneficial in increasing the frequency nadir. Comparatively, the Canadian
controller with a df trigger, produces its maximum power output when the frequency de-
viation is highest, and is therefore slightly slower and less effective than the GB controller.
However it is also important to recognise the role of tuning in the controller performance,
this is discussed in the following section.

of the range of operation of the controllers. The scenario chosen for the sensitivity analysis
was 40% wind penetration, as this deals with high volumes of power which is helpful to
illustrate sensitivities, and it does not experience the complications in power output seen
in the 50% scenario.

The Economics of Inertia
7.1
Introduction
The aim of this chapter is to take the work done during the simulation phase of the
project and introduce a real world context to it. One of the original objectives of the
work was to determine whether it was cheaper to utilise synthetic inertia from wind power
or synchronous inertia from synchronous condensers to enhance the frequency stability
of a small power system. However, it soon became clear that the question, both from a
technical and an economic perspective, is not one or the other, but rather how can the
two work together to provide a technically and economically viable solution?
This chapter examines the island power system of Bornholm, an island off the coast of
Denmark. It was chosen for analysis as it is a small power system with a high penetration
of wind power. The system frequency on the island is analysed for one year to quantify
the deviations that occur. The data is then used to make an economic assessment of the
feasibility of using synthetic inertia from wind power on the island to assist in frequency
control.

7.5
Economic Assessment
While the literature surrounding synthetic inertia is mature, there are still many aspects
that are unknown, particularly in relation to the economics. REServices was the first
study that investigated wind and solar based grid support services at an EU level. It
has provided technical and economic guidelines and recommendations for the design of a
European market for ancillary services, as well as for future network codes within the Third
Liberalisation Package [77]. It covers the topic of synthetic inertia through the assessment
of the new ancillary service Fast Frequency Response, defined as the injection of active
power in response to a grid frequency deviation within 2s [78]. A major component of
the REServices work was to carry out an industry survey to gather industry opinion on
the additional costs associated with enabling WTGs to provide supplementary ancillary
services for frequency and voltage support. Through this consultation, a utilisation cost
for FFR was estimated to be 0.16 €/MWh [79]. As FFR isn’t a service that is currently
provided by any WPPs, the actual costs incurred through additional maintenance from
the provision of FFR is unknown. Therefore, this figure is purely a speculative figure from
industry on the reduction of WTG lifetime caused by additional loading during synthetic
inertia response. A more concrete figure is provided for the investment cost associated
with the software upgrade required to enable FFR in WTGs. This is presented as 2,000
€/MW [66].
Application of Costs to Bornholm Test Case
As previously stated, the frequency measurements were taken at Aakirkeby substation,
to which a 6 MW WPP is attached. Therefore, if this WPP was to provide FFR through
synthetic inertia, the capital costs are easily estimated to €12,000. Furthermore, if the
WPP was to react to all 444 frequency deviations in the given period, by providing addi-
tional active power to the value of 10% of the pre-event power for a duration of 10 seconds,
given a cost of 0.16 €/MWh, then the operational costs would only amount to the negli-
gible value €0.03 per year. If, pending further research, the utilisation cost of synthetic
inertia is in fact this low, then the economic case for this service is very positive. It is
unlikely that other technologies would be able to compete directly with it on this basis,
however they could be more favourable in terms of flexibility and other factors.
Synchronous Condenser Costs
Comparatively, the standard capital cost for a synchronous condenser is in the range
of 350,000 €/MVA. If a direct comparison were made, it is clear that due to much lower
investment costs, synthetic inertia from WTGs appears economically favourable over syn-
chronous inertia from SCs. However, performing a direct comparison of the two services
is not balanced for the following reasons:
• The responses from each machine is not the same. As shown in Chapters 5 and 6,
the response characteristic from WTGs and SCs is different, where under current
53
==PAGE:67==
Chapter 7. The Economics of Inertia
control designs SCs are better suited to reducing instantaneous ROCOF and WTGs
are more capable of improving frequency nadir.
• Comparing a synchronous condenser with another technology purely on the basis
of its inertial response is not fair, as firstly an SC provides multiple other system
benefits including voltage support and short circuit power enhancement. Secondly
the lifetime of an SC often exceeds 40 years, at least double that of a typical WPP.
• The REServices project has made significant progress in estimating the costs associ-
ated with enabling renewable energies to provide ancillary services. However, for the
case of synchronous condensers where the capital costs are already known, studies
are needed to estimate the distribution of costs across the various and ever increasing
number of ancillary services which SCs can provide.
It should also be noted that investment decisions in power system components are
location specific, hence a comparison between inertial response technologies would also
need to be carried out for location specific cases. Therefore, instead of attempting to
place one technology above the other, it is perhaps more useful to estimate the combined
cost of both technologies, that if installed could enable a WPP to give an overall inertial
response more similar to that of a synchronous condenser.

Summary
This chapter introduced the island power system of Bornholm, and gave an analysis of
frequency deviations there over the course of a year.
Capital and utilisation costs for
provision of synthetic inertia from a WPP on the island were given and the total costs
were calculated. The same was done for a synchronous condenser, however it was concluded
that a direct economic comparison between synchronous and synthetic inertia cannot be
made as there is a difference in the services provided and also the economic lifetime of the
two investments. However, it is worth noting that a more valuable economic assessment lies
in the comparison of fast frequency response costs from different asynchronous generation
sources. This was done for primary frequency response as part of the REServices project
[39] for a case study of wind power and solar PV in the Iberian peninsula. The methodology
involved running economic dispatch scenarios with realistic penetrations each generation
source and then enabling frequency response from wind and solar PV. The dispatch model
that was used was WILMAR and the cost comparison is done based on the final system
costs that WILMAR calculates for hourly dispatches over an entire year. As the work was
in early stages the paper focuses more on the methodology and makes no solid conclusions
on costs and potential savings of using renewable generation sources for primary frequency
response. Nonetheless more work is needed in this area, particularly the comparison of
FFR provision from WTGs, battery energy storage systems (BESS), aggregated EVs,
demand side management and flywheels.

Chapter 8
Conclusions
This chapter summarises the most important findings of the work carried out for this MSc
thesis. In particular conclusions can be made with regards the following:
Regarding the Provision of Synthetic Inertia by WTGs:
• This project has proven the ability of WTGs to provide a synthetic inertial response
by increasing their active power output relative to a frequency deviation for a short
period of time.
• Two different controllers were designed and incorporated into WTG rotor control,
including a speed controller to protect WTGs from deceleration below a rotational
speed of 0.4 p.u.
• The controllers were validated for operation at rated wind speed of 12 m/s and a
lower wind speed of 7 m/s. Low wind speed validation was carried out to ensure
the correct operation of the speed control element which prevents the WTG from
decelerating below 0.4 p.u.
• During testing the controller with the df/dt triggering mechanism (GB) outper-
formed the controller with the df triggering mechanism (Canada) as it was able to
provide a higher peak power output with a lower overall volume of energy, resulting
in a lower deceleration and hence a smaller recovery period.
Regarding the Scenario Analysis:
• Simulations were carried out on a 9 Bus test system for the loss of one generator for
scenarios of wind penetration increasing from 10% to 50%.
• The first scenario in which a synchronous condenser is required for stable system
operation to be achieved following the loss of G1 is 40% wind penetration.
• The final scenario which provided acceptably stable results in terms of system fre-
quency response and generator active power output was the 50% scenario.
This
involved the integration of 160 MW of wind power, and the installation of 3 syn-
chronous condensers located in each corner of the system to avoid inter-area oscilla-
tions. It is understood however, that this is a particularly severe case for the 9 bus
system, where following the loss of G1 only a single synchronous generator and a
single wind farm remain. In larger systems with more generators and a more diverse
reserve contingents it is likely that more wind power could be integrated.

==PAGE:69==
Chapter 8. Conclusions
• While the synthetic inertia controllers failed in their aim of reducing the instanta-
neous system ROCOF following the loss of G1, this was instead achieved by the
installation of synchronous condensers in the system. The improvement in ROCOF
achieved by the SCs was significant; 0.15Hz/s when the first SC was installed in the
40% scenario, and a further 0.395 Hz/s when the final two SCs were installed in the
50% scenario. This leads to the conclusion that by combining the responses of SCs
and synthetic inertia from WTGs, a significant enhancement in system frequency
stability can be achieved.
• The re-tuning of controller gains and time constants for each scenario enabled ef-
fective provision of synthetic inertia. But it must be considered as an important
conclusion that under these designs the management of real-time synthetic inertia
provisions on a large scale would pose a significant burden to system operators.
Regarding Grid Code Requirements for Synthetic Inertia:
• This project has proven that the various grid code requirements for synthetic inertia
published by TSOs globally can be met. However, significant further specification is
required before the widespread participation of WTGs in this service can begin.
• These include specific values on activation and delivery time, power injection val-
ues, and in particular the characteristic of the recovery period and how it can be
distributed to avoid the occurrence of a secondary frequency dip as happened in
several of the scenarios analysed.
Regarding the Economic Aspects of Inertia:
• This project initially intended to carry out a comprehensive economic evaluation of
the provision of synthetic inertia from WTGs, however this could not be completed
as the utilisation cost (the cost of additional loading caused by synthetic inertia
provision that reduces WTG lifetime) is to date unknown.
• Figures from the REServices project gave a WTG capital cost for software upgrades
to enable the service of 2,000 €/MW, and from industry consultation a vague esti-
mation of 0.16 €/MWh was given for a utilisation cost.
• While exact costs are unknown, the economic outlook for the provision of synthetic
inertia from wind seems favourable as power systems that experience problems with
frequency deviations commonly already feature large penetrations of wind power.
• It can be concluded that economic comparison of synthetic inertia from WTGs and
synchronous inertia from SCs is complex as they provide complimentary rather than
competing services. SCs also provide a full suite of system stability services that
must also be included in the evaluation. In addition, the long operational life of an
SC leads to an investment recovery period that may be inherently different for the
two technologies.
Regarding the Bornholm Power System:
• Frequency on the island power system of Bornholm was studied for the period of
June 2014 - July 2015, during which time a total of 444 frequency deviations occurred
outside the allowable frequency range of the Nordic system of 49.9 Hz - 50.1 Hz.

8.1
Further Study
Following the work carried out as part of the MSc thesis the following topics are suggested
as the most important next steps for research in the field of synthetic inertia:
• It is proposed that the most important research area moving forward is the effect of
increased loading on WTG rotors due to the provision of synthetic inertia leading
to a reduction in WTG lifetime.
• Further research is also required on the control of the recovery period during syn-
thetic inertial response, as no optimal strategy has yet been suggested in literature.
• While only under-frequency events were included in the scope of this thesis (and
most literature surrounding synthetic inertia) it is important to note that a loss of
load or islanding situations leading to over-frequency events are also important and
should be studied further.
• While to date battery technology has been too expensive to utilise for services such
as fast frequency response, now that prices are beginning to fall it would be valuable
to conduct research on the suite of ancillary services that could be provided by a
combination of WTGs and BESS.
• A method of comparing synthetic inertial response from various generation sources is
needed. This comparison should incorporate the technical and the economic aspects
of all sources, ideally it would also extend to include all ancillary services and would
assist in the planning of future power systems with high penetration of asynchronous
generation.
• Finally, it is proposed that Bornholm is an appropriate test bed for the first two
items of research on this list as it has a high penetration of wind power and an
appropriately unstable frequency signal. This would enable DTU and its industrial
partners to stay at the forefront of research in the area of synthetic inertia and low
inertia power systems.

Detailed Controller Design
This appendix offers a more detailed description of the synthetic inertia controllers, util-
ising the actual RSCAD controller configuration.
Controller to Fulfil Hydro-Quebec Grid Code Requirements
There are four main blocks that form the controller to satisfy the grid code requirements
for Hydro-Quebec; an MPPT torque signal calculation block, the frequency measurement
and response calculation block, the speed control block and the optimal speed signal
calculation block. The construction of each of these blocks is discussed in detail below.
MPPT Block
The MPPT block is shown in Figure A.1. This block takes in both the optimal speed
signal and the real-time speed measurement. The difference between these two signals is
then passed through a PI controller and the result is the torque signal that is sent to the
rotor with the aim of minimising the difference between the actual and optimal speed of
the turbine rotor. This is a pre-existing block in the RSCAD model, however it is included
here as the PI control parameters were changed during the implementation of the inertial
response control. A problem arose as the PI controller was very finely tuned meaning
the response from speed deviation to torque actualisation is very fast - this inhibited
the response time of the inertial response that would begin to recover after less than 2
seconds. Therefore to enable the inertial response to deliver the full power increase and
for the appropriate time frame, the proportional gain was reduced from 0.01 to 0.001 and
the integral time constant was increased from 0.5s to 3s.

Inertial Response Block
The grid code requirements in Hydro-Quebec stipulate that an inertial response must
be delivered for frequency deviations larger than 0.5Hz. Therefore an under-frequency
triggering mechanism is implemented in this controller. The magnitude of the frequency
deviation then dictates the strength of the inertial response. This is calculated using a
wash-out filter. This is then passed through a limiter. The lower value of the limiter is not
significant as for an under-frequency event the washout filter calculates a response that
is also negative. The upper value of the limiter of 0 is critical however, as this ensures
that no inertial response is given for an over frequency event. While it can be argued that
inertial response from wind turbines will be essential in very low inertia systems in the
future, under frequency events are prioritised here. The removal of this limiter would of
course enable this controller to respond to both positive and negative frequency deviations.
The total inertial response is represented by the signal IE and is shown in Figure A.2
Figure A.2: Core of Inertial Controller
It is worth noting that while in the figure the parameters for the washout filter are
initialised to a gain of 0.5 and a time constant of 2s, through empirical testing these values
were optimised to a gain of 2 and a time constant of 12s during isolated validation, and
for each of the scenarios the controller must be tuned for a new WPP size. The resulting
response shape of the controller is discussed in Chapters 5 and 6.
Optimal Speed Signal Calculation
The optimal speed signal is calculated according to the block in Figure A.3. This is the final
stage of the inertial response controller where the response is summed to the current power
signal and the resulting optimal speed set-point for this total power signal is calculated.
While originally the inertial response signal was summed to the torque signal IACRQ
shown in Figure A.1, it was found that summing instead to the power signal gave more
control over the duration of the response, and furthermore ensured a steady recovery to
an optimal operating point as the IE_add signal simply becomes zero when the frequency
is restored to 50 Hz.
