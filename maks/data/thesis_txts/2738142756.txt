Abstract 
 
This master’s thesis systematically reviews literature to identify and analyze incentives 
and models that promote sustainable urban mobility. The research aims to identify, 
categorize, and evaluate various incentives and models that promote sustainable urban 
mobility, focusing on financial, policy-based, operational, and informational incentives. 
 
A big concern about urban transportation is that contributes significantly to greenhouse gas 
emissions, posing environmental challenges, especially in developing countries. In this 
dissertation the importance of sustainable urban mobility to mitigate these impacts is 
highlighted, by examining existing research on incentives and models. Key questions are 
answered, including identifying effective incentives and models, understanding their 
implementation methodologies, and analyzing their effectiveness across different country 
contexts. A systematic literature review methodology was adopted, employing a rigorous 
two-phase approach. The first phase involved identifying relevant papers from high-ranking 
journals using predefined keywords, focusing on sustainable urban mobility. In the second 
phase, a detailed content analysis extracted and categorized data based on economic, social, 
and environmental criteria. The review was conducted with a time spam on literature from 
2018-2024, ensuring contemporary relevance. Descriptive analysis revealed a significant 
increase in research on sustainable urban mobility post-2020, influenced by the COVID-19 
pandemic and global sustainability efforts. The content analysis identified four primary 
clusters of models: Behavioral and User-Centric Models (BUCM), Simulation and 
Infrastructure Models (SIM), Decision Support Models (DSM), and Data-Driven Energy 
Models (DDEM). Each model addresses specific urban challenges, providing tailored 
solutions for diverse urban contexts. 
 
The study highlights the varying effectiveness of incentives across geographic and socio-
economic contexts. The three categories are developed countries, developing countries and 
emerging economies. Developed countries benefit from advanced financial and regulatory 
mechanisms, while developing countries rely more on financial and policy-based 
incentives. Emerging economies focus on infrastructure development and public awareness 
campaigns. The research emphasizes the need for scalable and adaptable mobility solutions 
and suggests future research areas, including the role of emerging technologies in urban 
mobility.

1. Introduction  
1.1. The concerns about urban transportation 
Greenhouse gases and the accompanying phenomenon contribute as endogenous 
factors to climate change. All gaseous components of the atmosphere that contribute to the 
greenhouse effect are collectively referred to as greenhouse gases. They absorb long-
wavelength terrestrial radiation and re-emit thermal radiation, heating the surface. Certain 
gases, such as ozone, are also semi-transparent to solar radiation, as a result of which they 
absorb a part of it, contributing to a certain extent to the cooling of the earth's surface 
(Pathak, 2015). 
In presents days, urban transportation has a variety of advantages and disadvantages 
for society. On the one hand, transportation constitutes a fundamental human need that 
permits social interaction. Interestingly, transportation produced approximately 5% of all 
jobs in the European Union (EU) and also a gross value added of 5%, in 2016. However, 
the continuous increase in the traffic levels have turned the transportation sector into a 
major contributor to the amount of the total greenhouse gases (GHGs). In the EU, the 
transport sector had shown an increase by more than 28% in absolute terms since 1990 and 
was responsible for almost 23% of the total GHG emissions in 2021 (Kraus et al., 2021). 
From the total transportation emissions, almost 71% is caused by the road transportation 
with the vehicles producing more than 60% of these emissions. Although engines in recent 
years have become more efficient and emit less pollutants, this has nevertheless been offset 
by an increase in the volume of cars, but also by engines that are becoming more and more 
powerful. Therefore, about 11% of the total GHG emissions are caused only by automobile 
traffic, showing that society’s car dependency contributes significantly in high GHG 
emissions. Since greenhouse gas emissions are the primary cause of global warming, 
transportation is essential to achieving the Paris Agreement's target of limiting the rise in 
global temperature to less than 2 oC (Shokoohyar et al., 2022).  
In the case of developing countries, also known as low- and middle-income 
countries (LMICs), the demand related to urban mobility is rising with 90% of growing 
urban populations predicted to settle in Asia and Africa by 2050 (Brockerhoff, 2014). 
Currently, 92% of the 7 million air pollution-related fatalities worldwide are attributed to 
LMICs (Rosenberg, 2016). Despite owning only 47% of the world's registered vehicles, 
they bear 90% of the 1.25 million traffic-related deaths (Adeloye et al., 2016; Organization, 
2015). Moreover, sedentary lifestyles brought on by increased motorization contribute to 
the increase of non-communicable diseases in metropolitan settings. The 56.9 million 
NCD-related fatalities that occur annually in LMICs account for 80% of this phenomenon's 
unequal global impact (Oms, 2011). It is crucial to explore urban transport policy measures 
in cities already facing high burden of mortality, morbidity and inequity.

[2] 
 
Overall, the concern about urban transportation is a global problem, which can be 
solved with the appropriate models and incentives. 
1.2. The importance of sustainable urban mobility 
As a result of the previously discussed problem, it is crucial to promote sustainable 
transportation principles to preserve mobility and manage transportation in a way that is 
socially, environmentally, and economically responsible. In order to reduce global warming 
and advance social well-being, sustainable transportation ideas are essential and are 
especially critical in city settings. However, the definition of sustainable urban 
transportation is still up for debate and lacks consensus, much like the idea of sustainability 
in general (Kraus et al., 2021; Shokoohyar et al., 2022). 
While there are numerous different ways to define sustainability the Brundtland 
report introduced the following definition: Sustainability is the preservation of human life 
in the future. More operational definitions were emerged as a result of the lack of practical 
use of this definition. The vast majority of these categorize the idea into aspects related to 
the economy, society, and environment. Intersections exist between these three dimensions, 
including social cohesion (socio-economic), economic efficiency (environmental 
economic), and environmental responsibility (social–environmental). Sustainability is the 
integration of all the parts that have been discussed, and none of the three dimensions can 
be dominant. There is an argument that the environmental dimension is the most important 
by many authors, however sustainability is defined as the equitable assessment of social, 
environmental, and economic considerations (Kraus et al., 2021; Mohapatra et al., 2023). 
 
In addition, there is no agreed-upon definition of sustainable transportation either. 
Nevertheless, the majority of definitions uphold the significance of the three and 
incorporate social, environmental, and economic goals. The most well-known idea was 
created by Banister to enhance the operationality of sustainable transportation. Below is a 
list of his concept's elements (Kraus et al., 2021): 
• The promotion of a modal shift, meaning that the car travels need to be replaced by 
more environmentally transportation modes, such as bicycles and public 
transportation. Improving the infrastructure for walking and bicycling, encouraging 
multimodality, restricting the number of spaces available for parking cars, and 
raising the cost of using the roads constitute all ways to encourage shifting. 
• A decrease in the number of journeys and a reduction in the amount of the distance 
that must be traveled. The measures to avoid traffic are linked to urban planning. 
• By introducing shared vehicle ownership and low emission vehicles can lead to 
increased efficiency levels for transportation systems. 
• Digitalization, such as smart applications, or mobility as service solutions that 
display numerous intermodals offers and multimodalities for passenger 
==PAGE:10==
 
 
[3] 
 
transportation. Hence, a range of mobility options is provided for consumers to 
select from, promoting a modal shift away from the usage of cars. 
 
For a sustainable mobility paradigm, these actions can be incorporated into the 
Avoid–Shift–Improve (ASI) framework. The ASI framework offers a comprehensive 
approach to addressing transportation challenges and promoting sustainable mobility by 
focusing on three key strategies (Pan et al., 2010): 
1. Avoid: Avoid using land-use and urban planning strategies that enhance proximity 
of residential areas to offices and essential industries in order to minimize 
movement. These can be achieved through encouragement of mixed-use 
development, increase in the use of Information Communication Technology (ICT) 
so as to reduce physical movements and policies which discourage unnecessary 
trips. 
2. Shift: Improve public transportation systems by making them more attractive, 
accessible and reliable enough to cause a shift from private cars towards other forms 
of sustainable transport such as bicycles and walking, building extensive cycling 
networks and pedestrian-friendly environments. Low fares, good quality service 
delivery, and safe infrastructure will play a critical role in this approach. 
3. Improve: Cleaner technologies like electric or hydrogen powered automobiles 
need to be adopted. These will optimize traffic management systems, improve road 
network efficiency through smart mobility solutions. Adoption of this strategy must 
also include better vehicle standards alongside promoting renewable energy usage 
amongst vehicle owners themselves. 
Different dimensions of mobility exist, such as urban and rural; however, this 
master thesis analyzes the urban dimension. Urban mobility poses unique challenges and 
opportunities due to higher population densities, greater availability of public transport 
options, and more significant potential for walking and cycling infrastructure (Mohapatra 
et al., 2023).  
In the context of urban mobility, specific actions under the ASI framework may 
include the implementation of zoning regulations to encourage high-density, mixed-use 
developments to reduce travel distances. Also, the expansion and the upgrade of the public 
transportation network to be more convenient and reliable. Another action can be investing 
in electric bus fleets and infrastructure for charging electric vehicles (Pan et al., 2010). 
Sustainable urban transportation is crucial because it reduces traffic jams, enhances 
air quality, and fosters economic growth and poverty reduction. If action is not taken to 
guarantee environmental preservation, there will be a rapid increase in motorization in 
developing countries. The demand for personal mobility must be balanced with the 
==PAGE:11==
 
 
[4] 
 
significant social, economic, and environmental consequences of motorization, which 
present a significant challenge for developing nations. Thus, developing countries face a 
critical challenge in formulating policies that address high-priority socio-economic 
development issues while lowering the social and environmental costs associated with car 
ownership and use. In order to develop environmentally friendly transportation 
technologies and policies, developing nations borrow concepts and lessons from the 
strategies and experiences of developed countries. Long-term plans need to take 
transportation sustainability into consideration, and cars need to be a key component of 
these plans. They play a central role in achieving sustainability, which may stand alone or 
complement the national development plans or master plans already that are prepared. 
1.3. Objectives of this review 
The primary objective of this systematic literature review is to comprehensively 
analyze and synthesize existing research on the sustainable urban mobility. The following 
research questions (RQs) have been formulated to guide the systematic inquiry: 
RQ1: Based on a systematic literature review, which are the incentives and practices that 
focus on sustainable urban mobility? 
RQ2: Based on a systematic literature review, which are the models that can assist 
sustainable urban mobility? 
RQ3: Based on a systematic literature review, what is the modelling methodology? 
RQ4: Based on the analysis, how the incentives change according to the different country 
characteristics?  
RQ5: Based on the incentives, what are the challenges for each incentive? 
 
RQ1 aims to identify and categorize the incentives and practices highlighted from 
different studies that have been used to promote sustainable urban mobility, drawing from 
a broad range of literature. By drawing from a broad spectrum of literature, this inquiry 
will uncover successful strategies employed across different urban contexts. The goal is to 
compile a comprehensive list of financial, policy-based, operational, and informational 
incentives that have proven effective in encouraging sustainable transportation behaviors. 
This analysis will help in understanding the diverse approaches taken globally to address 
urban mobility challenges. 
RQ2 intends to investigate the numerous models that have been developed to 
facilitate sustainable urban transportation, offering a thorough analysis of their structures 
and operations. This inquiry will shed light on the innovative frameworks and 
methodologies that have been designed to enhance urban mobility. The focus will be on 
identifying models that integrate different aspects of the Avoid–Shift–Improve (ASI) 
==PAGE:12==
 
 
[5] 
 
framework, aiming to provide a holistic understanding of how these models contribute to 
sustainable urban transport systems. 
RQ3 focuses on the methods and techniques utilized in creating and implementing 
sustainable urban mobility models. By examining the strategies and tactics employed in 
the development of these models, this inquiry will provide insights into the best practices 
and methodological approaches in the field.   
RQ4 aims to provide insights about how the characteristics of each country can 
affect the types of incentives, which can be used. By analyzing factors such as economic 
status, cultural context, political environment, and existing infrastructure, this inquiry will 
reveal how incentives need to be tailored to fit specific national and local conditions. 
Finally, RQ5 synthesizes the findings from the identified incentives, aiming to draw 
comprehensive conclusions about the effectiveness and the challenges of these strategies 
on sustainable urban mobility. Answering these questions ensure a thorough exploration of 
the topic, assisting in the development of a deep understanding of the existing situation.

2. Methodology 
In this Master thesis, a systematic literature review was conducted to select and analyze 
the papers. This kind of review is the most well-know. It transparently and methodically 
collects, analyzes, and synthesizes research findings, frequently with the use of 
predetermined criteria. In order to achieve this, this method primarily follows the Preferred 
Reporting Items for Systematic Reviews and Meta-Analyses (PRISMA) guidelines to 
ensure scientific rigor, as these are adapted in more than 60,000 reports already. The 
PRISMA guidelines provide a framework for reporting systematic reviews and meta-
analyses. These guidelines help ensure clarity, transparency, and completeness in research 
reporting. They are widely adopted across various fields of research to enhance the quality 
of systematic reviews and meta-analyses. The PRISMA guidelines are constituted from 4 
steps: identification (to systematically identify all relevant studies on a given topic), 
screening (to filter out studies that do not meet the basic inclusion criteria), checking for 
eligibility (to assess the full texts of the screened studies for eligibility)  and the inclusion 
of selected papers (Kraus et al., 2021; Shokoohyar et al., 2022). 
The methodology is developed through two phases. The first phase is constructed in 
the following way: The first step is focusing on identifying based on topic related keywords 
of relevant papers, articles and studies, which were published in highly ranked journals, 
using reference managers. The second step is dealing with analyzing of their abstracts and 
full body texts. 
In the second phase, the remaining papers underwent a detailed content analysis, where 
economic, social, and environmental criteria relevant to sustainable urban transportation 
projects were extracted and categorized. Descriptive analysis was performed to identify 
trends and distribution of papers over time. The selected studies were then systematically 
analyzed to identify and evaluate various models and incentives for promoting sustainable 
urban mobility, ensuring a comprehensive synthesis of the research findings. 
In Figure 1, the suggested methodology is displayed. The following sections go into 
further depth about each phase.  
Evaluation of internal validity, or the search for methodological bias, is a crucial step 
in performing systematic literature reviews. To ensure the reduction of bias, the review 
process adhered to the PRISMA checklist.

2.1. Material Comprehensive Research 
For the systematic literature review, the first step of identification is the selection of 
the databases, the definition of meaningful key words and setting a time horizon of the 
published papers. Additionally, to maintain consistency and ensure the inclusion of high-
quality research, only review papers written in English were included. This language 
criterion was set to facilitate a uniform analysis process, as English is the predominant 
language of scientific discourse in this domain. This comprehensive and systematic 
approach to identifying relevant literature was designed to provide a robust foundation for 
the subsequent stages of the review process. 
==PAGE:15==
 
 
[8] 
 
 
For the database selection, IEEE Xplore and Mendeley were used. The Institute of 
Electrical and Electronics Engineers (IEEE) is a leading professional association dedicated 
to advancing technology for the benefit of humanity. IEEE Xplore digital library is a 
research database for discovery and access to journal articles, conference proceedings, 
technical standards, and related materials on computer science, electrical engineering and 
electronics, and allied fields. IEEE Xplore provides web access to more than 5 million 
documents from publications. Mendeley is an academic social network and reference 
management tool that promotes online collaboration, research discovery, and organization 
for researchers. It lets users effectively handle references and build bibliographies. The 
selected papers, articles and studies were verified on Scopus. By verifying the sources on 
Scopus, you can confirm that the most relevant and high-quality research have been cited, 
which enhances the credibility and rigor of the research. 
 
For the second step of identification, the main keywords for obtaining data were 
defined. Keywords such as "sustainable urban mobility," "mobility incentives," 
"transportation sustainability," "urban transport policies," and "models sustainable 
mobility" were identified as crucial for the search. The selection of these keywords aimed 
to ensure that the literature evaluation captures a wide range of studies and methods 
relevant to improving urban transportation systems. They also cover the broad and 
interconnected themes of sustainability and urban mobility. Through the use of these 
focused keywords, the study seeks to find best practices that may be modified and used in 
various urban contexts to improve sustainable mobility outcomes, as well as a variety of 
incentive methods that have been put into place globally and assessed for success.  
 
To capture the latest advancements and trends, the review focused on papers with 
a time span of 6-year time frame from 2018-2024. With this initial query 220 papers were 
collected out of the 293 total. 
 
2.2. Selection of Papers 
The remaining papers had to be examined through an analysis procedure as they did 
not all address the assessment of sustainable urban transportation. A screening phase was 
refined, in which each abstract was reviewed to determine the relevance and scope of each 
paper. Therefore, an acute keyword analysis on the abstract of all articles in the dataset was 
also applied. The scope for each paper was identified and papers that didn’t fit the criteria 
were excluded from the systematic literature review. Specifically, papers that lacked a focus 
on sustainable urban mobility, or that did not present any incentive models or practices, 
were filtered out. The papers that were subjected to the second selection phase were 91. 
The papers that advanced to the second selection phase were then subjected to a 
more detailed examination. The overall subject focus of the articles served as the primary 
eligibility criterion. Upon reviewing all 91 papers, only those that presented a 
==PAGE:16==
 
 
[9] 
 
comprehensive analysis of essential components of sustainability assessment, such as 
models or practices, were included in the final version. Papers with excessive specificity 
and those with criteria that did not indicate a sustainability factor were removed.  
Following this last round of rigorous screening, 25 papers remained. These selected 
studies form the foundation of the systematic literature review, offering valuable insights 
into effective incentives for sustainable urban mobility and guiding future research and 
policy development in this critical area. 
 
2.3. Data Extraction and Analysis 
 
One of the most important steps in conducting an extensive literature review on 
incentives for sustainable urban transportation is extracting relevant information and data 
from chosen studies. During this procedure, the selected articles are carefully examined to 
identify important details including the kinds of incentive models that were employed, the 
context in which they were used, the techniques that were employed, and the results that 
were reached. Key data points include descriptions of financial incentives, policy 
instruments, technological innovations, and behavioral interventions designed to promote 
sustainable transportation practices. 
 
A standardized template was used to extract relevant information from each 
selected study. This included details such as authors, publication year, geographical focus, 
type of incentives or models discussed, and key findings. The extracted data were 
categorized into themes based on the types of incentives (financial, policy-based, 
operational, informational) and models (behavioral, simulation, data-driven, decision 
support). 
 Furthermore, it is crucial to gather data regarding the success or failure of these 
incentives, the difficulties they faced, and their efficacy. Through systematic organization 
and classification of this data, the study can combine and evaluate the information to 
identify trends, make connections, and provide new understandings. This methodical 
approach offers an extensive understanding of the current library of research and facilitates 
the formulation of informed conclusions and suggestions for promoting sustainable urban 
transportation. 
The first step for the data analysis is the descriptive analysis, to identify the temporal 
and geographical distribution. By analyzing the distribution of selected papers over time, 
trends and shifts in research focus related to incentives for sustainable urban mobility can 
be identified. The second step is the content analysis which aims to identify and categorize 
various incentives discussed in the selected studies. This included direct monetary support, 
==PAGE:17==
 
 
[10] 
 
pricing strategies, regulatory policies, infrastructure improvements, and informational 
campaigns. The second step also includes the analyzation of different models used in 
sustainable urban mobility planning. This involved evaluating their theoretical foundations, 
empirical evidence, practical applications, and effectiveness in different urban contexts. The 
last step is the comparative analysis, in which there is the comparison of the types and 
effectiveness of incentives across different geographic and socio-economic contexts. This 
helped identify which incentives work best in developed, developing, and emerging 
economies. In addition, the challenges associated with each type of incentive were analyzed, 
providing insights into the practical difficulties and barriers faced in implementing 
sustainable urban mobility strategies.

• To identify the correlation between models and different characteristics of 
the urban areas  
• To evaluate the effectiveness, the flexibility and the scalability of various

• To identify the strengths, weaknesses, and practical implications of 
implementing these models

3.2.1.1. Behavioral and User-Centric Models 
 
This type of model aims to understand and forecast the user behavior and 
preferences regarding urban mobility services. This kind of model can help with 
developing incentives for the sustainable urban mobility, by optimizing mobility services 
based on user data. 
 
XGBoost for Shared Micro-Mobility Choices 
XGBoost for Shared Micro-Mobility Choices is a powerful machine learning 
algorithm with high accuracy and performance in handling large datasets. XGBoost can 
determine the importance of various features (e.g., price, convenience, availability) 
influencing user decisions. The use of this model can have important implications with 
regard to the regulation and management of shared micro-mobility services (Ren at 
el.,2023). 
For achieving sustainable urban mobility, this model can examine which incentives 
(e.g., discounts, promotions) have the potential to attract more users shared micro-mobility 
services. By analyzing user data, it assists in creating incentives that are specifically 
customized to the requirements and preferences of various user groups by evaluating user 
data. 
MaaS Adoption Models (Mobility as a Service) 
 
Mobility as a Service (MaaS) integrates mobility services related to public 
transportation (such as taxis, automobile rentals, car sharing, bicycles, motorbikes, and 
scooters) into a single platform that can be accessed via a smartphone. MaaS increases the 
possibility for lowering traffic in cities and purifying the air while assisting users and 
mitigating the negative environmental effects of driving (Higueras-Castillo at el, 2022).  
 
The adoption model provides insights regarding identifying barriers to the adoption 
of integrated mobility services and suggest targeted incentives (e.g., subscription discounts, 
integrated payment systems) to overcome these barriers and encourage user uptake. It can 
be used to promote sustainable modes of urban travel. 
Behavioral Models for Sustainable Mobility Adoption 
 
The goal of these models is to comprehend the social and psychological elements 
that affect people's decision to adopt sustainable modes of transportation. They contribute 
==PAGE:26==
 
 
[19] 
 
to the creation of focused interventions and rewards that encourage the use of walking, 
bicycling, and public transportation. They can help design behavioral incentives (e.g., 
awareness campaigns, community rewards) that encourage users to adopt sustainable 
transport modes. 
3.2.1.2. Simulation and Infrastructure Models 
LUTI Models (Land Use/Transport Interaction Models) 
 
LUTI are spatial interaction models, which use theoretical foundations from 
locational theories, urban economics, and regional economics. Therefore, these models 
combine the principle of land suitability, as a result of the interaction between economic 
production factors. Using this model, the choice of location is determined by exponential 
or logarithmic models based on the method of utility maximization (Gavanas et al.,2018).   
There is a suggestion for integration of LUTI models into sustainable urban 
mobility plans.  This has as a result, that LUTI models can simulate the impact of land use 
policies and infrastructure investments on transportation patterns. Hence, they support the 
creation of land use incentives that support sustainable urban transportation, such as zoning 
laws and transit-oriented development.

Multilevel Transport System Models 
 
The multilevel transport system (MTS) models take into consideration interactions 
between different transport modes and geographic levels and provides a comprehensive 
view of the transportation system, helping planners and policymakers make informed 
decisions. They are essential in improving the effectiveness, accessibility, and 
sustainability of urban mobility and at the same time can support the development of 
effective incentives and policies to effectively encourage the use of public transportation 
and other active transportation options.

Bicycle Traffic Models 
Simulates and improves the movement of bicycle traffic in cities. These approaches 
are essential for organizing and creating bicycle infrastructure that encourages riding as an 
environmentally friendly form of transportation. Through this model, it is possible to create 
infrastructure incentives—such as bike-sharing programs and designated bike lanes—to 
encourage riding as a sustainable method of transportation. 
3.2.1.3. Decision Support Models 
 
Decision Support Models assess the efficacy of different incentives and policies 
using decision support systems, aiming to ensure that the resources are used effectively.  
Promethee Method (Preference Ranking Organization Method for Enrichment 
Evaluations) 
 
Multi-criteria analysis techniques are a useful tool for the management of 
transportation development. They enable the collection of gathered data and the discovery 
of invisible trends from it, aid in the formulation of strategic decisions, and expedite and 
simplify the procedure for making decisions. MCDM metods can be divided into two basic 
==PAGE:28==
 
 
[21] 
 
categories: Multi-Attribute Decision Making (MADM) and Multi-Objective Decision 
Making (MODM). They differ mainly in the number of alternatives. Within the MADM 
methods, six subgroups can be observed: Outranking methods, Interaction-based methods, 
Pairwise comparison methods, Distance-based methods, Utility-based methods, and Other 
methods. Within Outranking methods, the most popular methods are: PROMETHEE, 
ELECTRE and ORESTE. Promethee Method involves the calculation of preference 
indices based on multiple criteria, requiring an extensive understanding of its flexibility 
and application in balancing various sustainability goals (Torbacki, 2021). 
 
Urban mobility plans can be ranked and prioritized using a variety of factors with 
the use of the Promethee approach. This model can assist in creating all-inclusive incentive 
packages that address numerous aspects of sustainable transportation (e.g., integrating 
financial, infrastructure, and regulatory incentives). 
Policy and Incentive Evaluation Models 
 
Policy and Incentive Evaluation Models are essential tools for promoting 
sustainable urban mobility. The purpose of these models is to evaluate the effectiveness of 
various incentives and policies for sustainable urban mobility, by using Multi-Criteria 
Analysis (MCA), Cost-Benefit Analysis (CBA) and Scenario Analysis. In addition, these 
models provide policymakers with insights to improve existing policies and also to 
optimize the allocation of the resources. 
 
3.2.1.4. Data Driven Energy Models 
 
Data-Driven Models for E-Mobility 
 
Data-Driven Models use data to evaluate and optimize various solutions. In this 
study, data-driven models for e-mobility focus primarily on electric vehicle solutions. By 
supporting decision-making for the implementation of services and infrastructure for 
electric vehicles, these models guarantee the economical and efficient use of resources. 
These models can help in the development of infrastructure incentives, such as charging 
stations, and financial incentives, including tax credits and subsidies that encourage the 
adoption of e-mobility, as a more energy-efficient form of transportation. 
Renewable Energy Systems Models for Urban Mobility 
 
These models incorporate renewable energy sources, such as wind and solar, into 
systems for urban mobility. These models evaluate the feasibility and impact of renewable 
energy in reducing reliance on non-renewable fossil fuels and encouraging eco-friendly 
modes of transportation. To lower the carbon footprint of urban mobility, incentives for the 
==PAGE:29==
 
 
[22] 
 
integration of renewable energy sources, such as grants for solar-powered EV charging, 
can be developed.

3.2.3. Discussion of Model Challenges 
 
Each of the models that have been mentioned before, provide a lot of benefits and 
insights about the sustainability in urban transportation. However, there are challenges and 
considerations regarding their application.  
For BUCM the data privacy constitutes one of the biggest challenges, as collecting 
and analyzing personal data raises concerns about data security. Another element that may 
cause issues for these models is consumer acceptance of new technologies, which is 
impacted by factors like perceived value, trust, and comfort. Another challenge can be the 
complexity of integrating new mobility solutions in the existing transport infrastructure, 
requiring a significant investment. Lastly, these models must ensure that they can be scaled 
to accommodate growing urban populations and expanding service areas is essential for 
long-term success. 
For the SIM, the biggest challenge is the complexity of the integration of these 
models with the existing urban planning and transportation systems. One of the challenges 
associated with these models is their adaptation to changing urban dynamics, such as 
changes in transit preferences, population density, and technological improvements. 
Furthermore, cooperation amongst several stakeholders is necessary for the 
implementation of these models, which can be troublesome. Once more, scalability is an 
==PAGE:32==
 
 
[25] 
 
issue with these models since they can grow to meet the needs of expanding service regions 
and increasing urban populations. 
The challenges of scalability and adaptability are also part of DSM. Nonetheless, 
the most significant obstacle facing these models is the availability and quality of the data, 
since precise decision-making requires reliable and complete data. Furthermore, the 
integration of these models with current urban planning and transportation systems is quite 
complicated. 
The DDEM can face hard challenges in the policy support. These models must have 
strong policy support and regulatory frameworks. The later, are necessary to encourage the 
adoption of renewable energy in urban transport. Also, the technical feasibility must be 
taken into consideration and also the public acceptance. 
 
3.3. Content Analysis of Incentives 
This section aims to methodically identify, classify, and comprehend the diverse 
incentive types that are being used or proposed in various contexts. Below the objectives 
are presented: 
• To identify and classify common incentives for the sustainable urban mobility. 
• To provide insight into the effectiveness of the different incentives. 
• To compare the types and effectiveness of incentives in various geographic and 
socio-economic contexts. 
• Provide insights and recommendations for policymakers, urban planners, and 
transportation authorities. 
3.3.1. Description of Incentives 
 
By reviewing and analyzing the literature, several common themes and types of 
incentives, which are used to promote sustainable urban mobility, have been revealed. The 
four types of incentives are: financial incentives, policy-based incentives, operational 
incentives and informational incentives. The goal of these incentives is to reduce the use 
of private cars, to encourage the use of sustainable transport modes, and improve the 
overall efficiency and environmental sustainability of urban transport systems. 
 
These papers present several financial incentives, including direct monetary 
support, pricing strategies and cost-reduction for end-users. Direct monetary support 
includes subsidies, for example the financial assistance provided to support shared mobility 
projects, ensuring they are financially viable and attractive to users. Also, grants can be a 
direct monetary support, like in the case of Philippine cities, where the government 
supported financially sustainable transportation projects, such as public transport 
improvements and pedestrian infrastructure (Bellen, 2018). The pricing strategies 
==PAGE:33==
 
 
[26] 
 
constitutes of congestion pricing, distance-based pricing, and time-of-day pricing. 
Congestion pricing is a strategy to promote the use of alternate forms of transportation, by 
charging cars to use specific routes during peak hours in order to lessen traffic congestion. 
Distance-Based pricing uses fees depending on the distance traveled to encourage shorter 
trips and the usage of public transit. The use of variable pricing based on the time of day 
to control demand and lower peak traffic numbers, so encouraging off-peak travel, is 
another financial incentive.  Additionally, there are tax incentives, reduced fares, and 
subscription discounts available to encourage end users to cut their expenses. 
 
Policy-based incentives across these papers include national and local policies, 
strategic planning initiatives, regulatory frameworks, and collaborative governance 
structures. Collaborative governance includes policies that encourage the collaboration 
between public authorities and private mobility service providers to create a cohesive urban 
transport ecosystem. The goal of the policy-based incentives is to create an environment 
that is suitable to sustainable urban mobility.  By implementing these policies, governments 
and organizations can promote the adoption of sustainable transportation options, improve 
service quality, and ensure the long-term viability of sustainable urban mobility initiatives. 
As can be seen in the Table 3.5, the policy-based incentives are mentioned in 7 out of the 
11 papers. 
 
In these papers the operational incentives are presented, which include 
improvements to infrastructure, service quality, and convenience for users. The purpose of 
these incentives is to increase the appeal and usability of sustainable transportation 
solutions for daily use. Adoption of sustainable transportation options is greatly aided by 
improvements like complete EV charging infrastructure, bike lanes designated for cycling, 
guaranteed parking for sharing cars, and pedestrianization. These incentives are designed 
to enhance the accessibility and overall user experience of sustainable transportation modes 
by emphasizing operational improvements. 
 
The last type of incentives from the systematic literature review are the 
informational incentives. Informational incentives are a powerful tool in promoting 
sustainable urban mobility. Cities can effectively encourage the public to adopt eco-
friendly transportation solutions by utilizing incentive programs, mass media, internet 
platforms, community participation, and educational initiatives. As a result, there may be 
major positive effects on the environment, the economy, and society, making cities more 
sustainable and livable.

Emerging Economies  
In emerging economies (e.g., China, Southeast Asia), a combination of financial, 
policy-based, operational, and informational incentives is essential for promoting 
sustainable urban mobility. 
In emerging economies, the financial incentives are mainly discounts and subsidies 
for using public transport and EVs, and reduced fares for ride-sharing services. The goal 
of these incentives is to lower the cost of sustainable transport options, making them more 
accessible. Financial incentives, such as discounted fares for public transport and subsidies 
for purchasing EVs increase affordability and adoption rate of sustainable transportation 
options. In China rapid growth has occurred in EV sales due to substantial government 
subsidies and incentives. Another way to promote sustainable transportation is by lowering 
the prices for ride-sharing services to make them more competitive with private car use. In 
the case of Beiijing, reduced fares for ride-sharing have increased its popularity (Waqas, 
2018). 
Policy-based incentives include policies promoting public transport safety and 
convenience, and for EV charging infrastructure to ensure the long-term viability of 
sustainable transport initiatives. The promotion of public transport can be achieved with 
policies that ensure the safety and convenience of public transport users, such as improved 
safety measures and enhanced service quality. In cities like Shanghai, higher ridership has 
been achieved with the implementation of policies focused on public transport safety 
(Waqas, 2018). Another incentive is the integration of sustainable transport in urban 
planning with incorporating sustainable transport options into urban planning processes, 
such as dedicated lanes for buses and bicycles. Cities like Seoul have integrated bus lanes 
and cycling paths into their urban planning achieving higher attractiveness for public 
transport modes. 
The development of comprehensive charging networks, improved service quality 
for ride-sharing, and safer pedestrian crossings constitutes the operational incentives in the 
countries with emerging economies. An example of operational incentive in emerging 
economies is the establishment of a wide network of charging stations for electric vehicles 
to support the adoption of EVs by making charging convenient and accessible. Creating 
safer pedestrian crossings is also another operational incentive, which encourages walking 
by making it safer for pedestrians. In cities like Bangkok, safer pedestrian crossings have 
improved walkability.

3.3.3. Discussion of Incentives Challenges 
Incentives for sustainable urban mobility vary in their impact and challenges across 
developed, developing, and emerging economies. While financial incentives and 
regulatory frameworks are effective in promoting adoption, challenges such as resource 
limitations, policy implementation, and infrastructure development need to be addressed. 
The challenges vary across geographic and socio-economic contexts. 
In the developed countries, the financial incentives can face equity issues. 
Congestion pricing or similar mechanisms can affect individuals with lower incomes, that 
rely on car travel during peak times. Hence, these incentives can unfairly burden 
disadvantaged groups. Another problem for the financial incentives is the high cost, due to 
subsidies for the sustainable transport options, that can be financially burdensome for 
governments. The market saturation can be also a challenge, as in highly developed 
markets, additional financial incentives may have diminishing returns as the market for 
EVs and car-sharing services becomes saturated. 
The policy-based incentives in the developed countries face challenges, including 
the complex coordination, political resistance and regulatory compliance. The review show 
that aligning national and local policies, as well as coordinating between various

Infrastructure development, maintenance and upgrades and user adaptation are

The implementation of informational incentives in the developed nations is a

unsure how to measure the effectiveness. With the informational campaigns, it is 
challenging to keep the public continuously engaged and informed about sustainable 
transport options requires ongoing effort and resources. At the same time, measuring the 
direct impact of informational campaigns on behavior change can be difficult. The cultural 
resistance can be also a problem, as users with deeply ingrained car-centric cultures will 
resist to a sustainable change. 
The developing countries often have limited financial resources to provide financial 
incentives. Prioritizing funding and securing external support to provide substantial 
subsidies for sustainable transport options are crucial but challenging. Additionally, there 
is a strong reliance on outside funding due to the unpredictability of grants and financial 
assistance from foreign organizations. Sustaining long-term profitability requires 
sustainable funding strategies. The economic volatility and swings experienced by 
developing nations affect the availability and constancy of financial incentives, making it 
challenging to continue providing ongoing support. 
The operational incentive necessitates large expenditure in the development of new 
infrastructure, such as bike lanes and pedestrian zones, which can be a big hurdle in 
locations with limited resources. Furthermore, sustaining good service quality in 
sustainable transport services such as public transportation can be difficult because it calls 
for ongoing management and investment, which is difficult in developing countries. 
Policy-based incentives need to have regulatory enforcement, making it challenging for 
developing countries with weak enforcement mechanisms and lack of regulatory oversight. 
Also, in the developing nations ensuring that policies are effectively implemented at the 
local level can be difficult, especially in regions with weak administrative capacities.

There some challenges in the informational incentives in the developing countries,

developing nations, it is challenging to ensure that informational campaigns reach all 
segments of the population, including marginalized and underserved communities. An 
additional challenge is overcoming cultural preferences for private car use and promoting 
sustainable alternatives requires sustained and multifaceted approaches. Designing 
effective informational campaigns in areas with lower literacy and education levels, that 
are accessible and understandable to everyone can be challenging. 
Countries with emerging economies have challenges regarding the economic 
variability and balancing the priorities for the financial incentives. These countries exhibit 
frequently economic fluctuations, which can impact the availability of funds for subsidies 
and discounts, leading to inconsistencies in the support provided to sustainable transport 
initiatives. Another challenge is to balance the priorities of the investments. There must be 
a balance between the investments in sustainable transport and with other critical needs, 
such as healthcare, education, and infrastructure development. 
The challenges with the policy-based incentives in the emerging economies are 
similar with the developing countries. Policy enforcement is the biggest challenge, as 
regions with weak enforcement mechanisms cannot ensure the compliance with new 
policies.  Integrating new sustainable transport policies with existing systems and practices 
can be complex and also maintaining consistent policy direction despite political changes 
can be challenging. 
The infrastructure investment is an issue about the operational incentives in 
emerging economies. Developing and maintaining new infrastructure, such as 
comprehensive charging networks and safer pedestrian crossings, requires significant 
investment and planning. New infrastructure is difficult to be developed in cities with high 
urban density and also high service quality and reliability for ride-sharing and other 
sustainable transport services. 
The informational incentives in emerging economies face challenges, such as 
effective communication, cultural change and sustained effort.  Continuous effort is needed 
to keep the public informed and engaged about sustainable transport options, which is 
challenging. Promoting cultural change towards more sustainable travel behaviors is slow 
and requires persistent and multifaceted approaches.

4. Conclusions and Discussions  
 
 
In this systematic literature review on sustainable urban mobility incentives and 
models several keys insights are revealed. In the academic interest, it can be noticed that 
there is a notable increase in sustainable urban mobility, especially post-2020, likely driven 
by the COVID-19 pandemic and a global emphasis on sustainability and climate action. 
The systematic literature review has a geographical and institutional focus, as the research 
is conducted mainly by institutions and regions with strong commitments to sustainability 
primarily. European capitals like London, Paris, and Amsterdam, along with Nordic 
countries, show significant contributions in this area. The descriptive analysis shows that 
the majority of the studies are conducted by small research teams, typically consisting of 
3-4 authors, indicating a collaborative yet focused approach in this field. 
 
In this review, a classification of four primary clusters of 10 models in total were 
identified from the 14 selected papers. The Behavioral and User-Centric Models (BUCM) 
focus on understanding and forecasting user behavior to optimize mobility services. 
Another cluster is Simulation and Infrastructure models (SIM), which use spatial and 
interaction theories to plan and improve urban transport infrastructure. There is also 
Decision Support Models (DSM), which can help evaluate the effectiveness of various 
incentives and policies using multi-criteria analysis. Lastly, the Data-Driven Energy 
models analyze and optimize electric mobility solutions and renewable energy integration. 
 
For the incentive’s categorization, four main types of incentives to promote 
sustainable urban mobility were classified. The first type is the financial incentives, which 
includes subsidies, pricing strategies, and cost reductions. Another type of incentives, 
which was presented, is the Policy-Based incentives. National and local policies, regulatory 
frameworks, and strategic planning are some examples from this type. The Operational 
incentives focus on creating reliable sustainable mode choices for the users with 
infrastructure improvements and service quality enhancements. The last type is the 
Informational incentives, which focus on informing the public with educational incentives 
and public awareness campaigns.  
 
The effectiveness of incentives varies across geographic and socio-economic 
contexts. The three categories are developed countries, developing countries and emerging 
economies. Developed countries benefit from advanced financial and regulatory 
mechanisms, while developing countries rely more on financial and policy-based 
incentives. Emerging economies focus on making sustainable transport options accessible 
through infrastructure development and public awareness campaigns. Developed countries 
face challenges in the Operational and Informational incentives, as building comprehensive 
networks for EV charging, bike lanes, and other infrastructure requires significant 
investment and planning and in car-centric cultures users may resist changes toward more 
==PAGE:46==
 
 
[39] 
 
sustainable options. Developing countries have limited resources and economic instability, 
as fluctuations in the economy can impact the availability and consistency of financial 
incentives. Other challenges for developing countries and emerging economies are the 
weak enforcement mechanisms and lack of regulatory oversight hinder the effectiveness of 
policies. 
Copenhagen has been a pioneer in adopting renewable energy solutions within its 
urban mobility framework in the recent years. The city's strong commitment to 
sustainability and innovative energy policies makes it an ideal case study for combining 
Data-Driven Energy Models (DDEM) with Financial and Policy-Based Incentives. Using 
Renewable Energy Systems Models for Urban Mobility, the dependence on fossil fuels can 
be reduced by incorporating renewable energy sources into the transportation network. For 
example, solar panels and wind turbines can be used to power EV charging stations and 
public transport systems. With Data-Driven Analysis model optimization of the energy use 
can be achieved, by analyzing the energy usage patterns to optimize charging schedules 
and distribution. In combination with these models, financial incentives and policy-based 
incentives can be used. For the financial incentives, grants to support the development of 
renewable energy infrastructure for urban mobility and subsidies to encourage the purchase 
of electric vehicles by reducing the initial cost. The policy-based incentives can be 
mandates for renewable energy integration and regulations to support EV infrastructure. 
The purpose of these incentives is to ensure that a significant portion of urban transport 
energy comes from renewable sources and the availability of charging stations to support 
EV adoption. The combination of Renewable Energy Systems Models for Urban Mobility 
with Financial and Policy-Based Incentives in Copenhagen demonstrates a robust approach 
to achieving sustainable urban mobility. By integrating renewable energy into the 
transportation system and providing financial and regulatory support, Copenhagen can 
successfully promote EV adoption and reduced carbon emissions. 
A topic for future suggestion would be the scalability and adaptability of mobility 
solutions, as they have been highlighted in this dissertation as two of the biggest challenges 
and they are crucial for sustainable mobility solutions to be effectively implemented across 
diverse urban environments. The research area would be the scalability of successful 
models, first by analyzing case, in which studies cities have successfully implemented 
scalable urban mobility solutions and then by identifying factors contributing to scalability, 
such as infrastructure, technology, and governance. The next research area would be the 
adaptability to local contexts, by examining how mobility solutions can be tailored to fit 
different urban characteristics, including population density, geographic layout, and 
existing infrastructure. 
Another suggested research topic for further investigation would be the role of 
emerging technologies in urban mobility. The focus would be how Artificial Intelligence 
(AI) and Machine Learning (ML) can optimize traffic management systems, predict user 
==PAGE:47==
 
 
[40] 
 
demand patterns, and personalize transportation services to enhance efficiency and user 
satisfaction. By analyzing real-time data, AI and ML can dynamically adjust traffic signals, 
reduce congestion, and improve route planning, making urban travel smoother and more 
efficient. The study could also examine the autonomous vehicles and how they can 
potentially impact the urban mobility. Autonomous vehicles promise to revolutionize 
transportation by reducing human error, enhancing road safety, and providing mobility 
solutions for the elderly and disabled. Investigating the integration of AVs into existing 
traffic systems, their potential to reduce traffic congestion, and their effects on public 
transportation networks could provide valuable insights into the future of urban mobility.  
Another research area could be to study the role of IoT in creating smart traffic systems, 
real-time data analytics, and infrastructure maintenance. This technology can be 
instrumental in predictive maintenance of infrastructure, ensuring that roads, bridges, and 
public transport systems are kept in optimal condition, thus preventing costly breakdowns 
and disruptions. Furthermore, IoT can facilitate seamless communication between vehicles 
and infrastructure (V2I), leading to more coordinated and efficient traffic flows. Exploring 
these technologies could reveal how they synergize to create a more integrated, responsive, 
and sustainable urban mobility ecosystem. The study could examine case studies of smart 
cities implementing these technologies, assess the challenges and opportunities they 
present, and provide recommendations for policymakers and urban planners on leveraging 
these innovations to improve urban transportation systems.
