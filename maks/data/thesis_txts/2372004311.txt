1.  Introduction and Motivation 
 
1.1 Introduction to Bioprinting 
Bioprinting is a recently new field within tissue engineering that can be described as a robotic additive 
Biofabrication that has the potential to build or pattern organ-like structures [1].  Though Tissue Engineering as 
a field rose in the 1990’s, the field have risen rapidly the last 10 years, with multiple companies and several 
commercial products [2]. Tissue Engineering aims to restore tissue and organ function by employing strategies 
from both biology and engineering, to solve clinical problems. Problems such as the functional failure of tissues 
and organs, which is a severe and costly healthcare problem, due to the limited availability of donors [3]. 
Though artificial prostheses and mechanical devices solves certain clinical problems, they are not ideal, 
because they are subjected to mechanical failure upon long-term implementation. Secondly, artificial 
prostheses and mechanical devices rarely fully integrate with the host tissue, and can trigger a host immune 
response damaging both the implant and the healthy tissue around the implant [2]. 
Tissue Engineering revolves around two main strategies: transplantation of tissue grown in vitro consisting of 
an artificial matrix with cells and growth factors, or in situ regeneration of tissue relying upon a combination of 
an artificial matrix and growth factors as a guiding template to induce host cell regeneration of the tissue in 
vitro. 
Both strategies typically involve multiple components including cells, a physical template and a combination of 
growth factors.  
 
1.1.1 Materials 
Biomaterials are used to develop scaffolds, which provide a template for cells to organize and restore structure 
and function to damaged or dysfunctional tissues. Such biomaterials can simultaneously be used to supply 
nutrients, drugs and bioactive factors that direct specific tissue growth. Ideally the biomaterial should possess 
the physico-mechanical properties suitable for the intended application and be compatible for further 
functionalization with bioactive factors [2]. These materials are derived from both natural and synthetic 
sources, and possess a range of structural and functional properties that accommodate tissue engineering. 
Natural materials are advantageous due to their inherent biological recognition through receptor-ligand 
interactions, cell mediated proteolysis, and low toxicity. Protein based natural polymers include collagen, 
gelatin, silk, fibrin, elastin and soybean. Another class of natural polymers is polysaccharides that contain 
monomers linked together by O-glycosidic bonds to form polymers. Multiple polysaccharides such as Alginate 
and chitosan have been successfully used in tissue engineering [4]. Though natural materials also suffers from 
multiple limitations, which include purification, cost, immunogenic responses, lack of mechanical properties 
and processability [2]. 
Many synthetic materials have been designed and fabricated for tissue engineering, such as biodegradable 
synthetic polymers, which allows for controlled mechanical properties and degradation kinetics, easy process

ability into custom shapes and structures and easy modification of the material. The vast majority of synthetic 
polymers belongs to the polyester family, which includes poly(glycolic acid), poly(lactic acid) and their 
copolymer poly(lactic-co-glycolide). Whereas synthetic polymers with photo polymerizable groups are based 
on poly(ethylene glycol) and poly(acrylates), which upon exposure to UV radiation and cross-linking agents, 
these polymers can cross-link to form three-dimensional networks [5]. 
Combinations of natural and synthetic polymers have particular advantages compare to the individual 
counterparts, which allows for changing or optimizing natural material properties for the certain application. 
 
1.1.2 Scaffold Design 
Scaffolds act as the synthetic analog of the natural extracellular matrix (ECM). The role of scaffolds is to 
recapitulate the normal tissue development process by allowing cells to formulate their own 
microenvironment [6]. The scaffold provides the necessary support for cells to attach, proliferate and maintain 
their differentiated function and regeneration of new tissue. So the scaffold must have certain characteristics 
such as: a three-dimensional highly porous structure with an interconnected pore network to support 
cell/tissue growth and diffusion of nutrients and metabolic waste; biodegradable or bioresorbable with 
controllable degradation and resorption rates to match cell/tissue growth; suitable surface chemistry for cell 
attachment, proliferation and differentiation; mechanical properties to match those of the tissue at the site of 
implantation; and easily processed to form a variety of shapes and sizes [2]. 
 
1.1.3 Method for Bioprinting 
 Bioprinting is a process that uses a computer-controlled 3D printing device to accurately deposit cells and 
biomaterial into precise geometries with the goal being the creation of anatomically accurate biological 
structures, such as 3D tissue constructs. Bioprinting devices have the ability to print cell aggregates, cells 
encapsulated in hydrogels or viscous fluids, or cell seeded microcarriers, all of which falls under the term 
“bioinks”, whereas Bioprinting also includes deposition of cell-free polymers that can provide mechanical 
structure or a more hospital environment [5]. 3D Bioprinting is gaining prominence in tissue engineering 
because it offers a straightforward method for fabricating 3D constructs containing complex geometric 
distributions of cells, biomaterials and growth factors, which makes it a promising tool for the development of 
functional tissue [7]. 
A major advantage of this technology is its ability for simultaneously 3D printing various cell types in defined 
spatial locations, which makes this technology applicable in tissue engineering to meet the need suitable for 
transplantation suitable organs or tissue [8]. 
Computer-assisted designs (CAD) inspired by biological structures, can be used to guide the placement of 
specific types of cells and materials into precise, planned geometries that mimic the architecture of actual 
tissue construction, which can be subsequently matured into functional tissue constructs or organs [5].  
Bioprinting as a process can be divided into three separate technological steps: Preprocessing, Actual printing 
and post processing. Preprocessing is creating a blueprint of the targeted tissue or organ design, using imaging 
and CAD techniques. Actual printing is done using the robotic Bioprinter, dispensing cells and biomaterials.

During post processing the created construct must undergo cell proliferation tissue remodeling, and 
maturation in a “bioreactor”, a special designed chamber, that accelerates tissue maturation [9].  
 
1.2 Goal and Scope of this project 
In this project, the initial goal was to modify and print biomaterials using a standard Fab@Home, an extrusion 
based Bioprinter, assembled in a prior Special Course.  Since Bioprinting is a method used as a tool, promising 
biomaterials were chosen by inputs form interesting 3rd party. Professor Jörg Kutter at Copenhagen University 
was interested in the Bioprint capabilities of Thiol-ene resins, especially printability. PhD Student Janko Kajtez 
was interested in the printability and preparation of Gelatin Hydrogels. Both materials were chosen to be 
tested for Bioprinting. In order to stabilize the construct during printing, the materials had to be cured using 
UV, to initiate crosslinking. This required modification of the assembled Bioprinter, to allow controlled UV 
curing. Secondly, correct composition of both biomaterial had to be established.

2. Theory 
 
2.1 Bioprinting 
Bioprinting methods are generally divided into three different fields: Inject printing, Extrusion-based deposition 
and Laser-Induced Forward Transfer. All three methods have their advantages and disadvantages.  
 
Inkjet printing is also called drop-by-drop 
Bioprinting, is a non-contact strategy based on 
deposition of bioink in drops, to form a final 
multilayer construct. Drops of a defined in volume, 
in picoliter range, are generated by pressure pulses 
induced by thermal or piezoelectric changes [10]. 
The drop-by-drop approach relies on being able to 
quickly polymerize or stabilize the printed material 
place, so that subsequent droplets can be added to 
the growing structure. The requirement for a fast-
gelling materials places a limitation on the types of 
materials that can successfully be applied, and as 
the printable droplets are typically small volumes, 
scaling up to fabricate a large organ structure might 
be difficult. Though the small droplet volume allow 
high-resolution printing of advanced structures. 
Laser-Induced Forward Transfer (LIFT) technology is 
comprised by a laser beam that is pulsed at desired 
time and lengths and a donor material “ribbon” 
comprised of the printable material. This ribbon is 
supported on a transport layer, such as gold or 
titanium, which absorbs the laser energy and 
transfers it to the ribbon [5]. The transfer is 
controlled by the laser beam pulses that target 
precisely defined positions on the energy-absorbing layer. Based on the absorbed energy, defined sized 
droplets of bioink is formed, and deposited. This method allows for printing high viscosity materials and high 
cell densities at very good solutions, however is limited by the high costs and lack of suitability to print large 
constructs [10].  
Scaffold-free Bioprinting is based on aggregates or rods compromised solely of cells, that are printed in 
geometric patterns and allowed to fuse over time to form larger constructs. The strength of this method lies 
within its high cellularity, though preparation time, printing speed and building materials volumes currently 
limits its scalability [5].

Pneumatic-based system utilizes 
pressurized air using valve-free or valve-

valve-based configurations allow for more

for higher precision. Pneumatic-driven 
EBB requires sterilization of the air

Pneumatic-driven systems do not 
generate smooth extrusions of semisolid 
or solid bioink, and requires another liquid 
or gel medium to deliver the bioink

added, bioink can easily attach on the

Mechanical microextrusion systems 
utilizes piston or screw driven

the nozzle, whereas screw-driven may

for dispensing bioinks with higher 
viscosities. However, screw-driven 
configurations can generate larger pressure drops along the nozzle, which can harm the loaded cells. 
Mechanical-driven systems provide better printing abilities of semisolid and solid inks [9].

2.2 Thiol-ene Click Chemistry 
Modern novel photopolymerization monomer systems have been developed which are based on thiol-ene 
resin chemistry, involving free radical step growth rather than chain growth polymerization. These systems, 
especially oligomeric thiol-ene materials, produce polymers having high conversion and dramatically reduced 
polymerization shrinkage. Because this process occurs in the pre-gel stage, it does not contribute to  shrinkage 
stress. They also have high cross-link density and improved mechanical properties [13].

Thiol-ene (TE) Resins have been proposed as alternative materials to PDMS for rapid prototyping of 
microfluidic devices [14], and possibility for photo-cross-linkable hydrogels [15]. The thiol-ene reaction has 
many properties in common with the extensively used biomaterial (Meth) acrylate (MAA), such as spatial and 
temporal control of polymerization, solventless formulations, and good mechanical properties. It displays rapid 
curing in the presence of little or no photoinitiator, low volume shrinkage, delayed gelation point, little or no 
sensitivity to oxygen and low polymerization shrinkage stresses [16]. Based on ‘click chemistry’, TE resins are 
well suited for both UV-induced radical step-growth polymerization where both sulfhydryl (thiol) and allyl (ene) 
containing monomers are consumed in stoichiometric ratios with no byproducts [14]. Furthermore, the unique 
alternating step-growth mechanism lead to well-defined homogenous polymer networks that impart excellent 
optical clarity, a narrow glass transition and low permeability to common solvents.  Moreover, because the 
thiol-ene reaction belongs to the family of click reactions, denoting a set of powerful specific highly reliable 
“lock-and-key” reactions. Both the polymerization and the material properties of thiol-ene systems make them 
very suitable for microfluidic devices fabrication [16].

 2.3 Hydrogels 
Hydrogels have received significant attention because of their high water content and related potential for 
many biomedical applications. The high water content allows for movement of nutrients and oxygen, for which 
to sustain survival for cells. Hydrogels are polymeric structures tied together as water-swollen gels by: covalent 
cross-linking, ionic forces, hydrogen bonds, hydrophobic interactions, polymer crystallites physical 
entanglement of individual polymer chains or a combination of the above-mentioned interactions [13]. 
Covalent cross-linked hydrogels are usually synthesized by bringing together small multi-functional molecules 
such as monomers and oligomers, and reacting this to form a network structure or crosslinked with larger 
polymer molecules. Such cross-linking achieved by a reaction between two chemical groups, initiated by a 
catalyst, such as photo-polymerization or by radiation cross-linking. Serval methods for forming cross-linked 
hydrogels, such as (meth-)acrylated hydrogel, are based on free radical reactions [15].  
An alternative method, achieved through chemical cross-linking calls for direct reaction of polymer that have a 
small molecular weight, with a di-functional or multifunctional cross-linking agent. This agent usually links 
multiple longer polymer chains through is di-functional or multi-functional groups [13]. Such as the thiol-ene 
chemistry that relies upon the defined dimerization of a double bond with a thiol group and network formation 
occurs, which recently have seen a growing popularity, through methods like the Michael addition of dithiol 
compounds.

The physical behavior of biomedical hydrogels is dependent on their dynamic swelling and equilibrium in in 
aqueous solutions. The water within the swollen hydrogels is bound to the polymer chains by either polar 
interactions or hydrophobic interactions. Most solutes can diffuse into and through the swollen hydrogels only 
within unbound or free water channels. In general the swelling properties can be controlled by copolymerize 
basic hydrophilic monomers with less hydrophilic monomers. Such processes can lead to a wide range of 
swellable hydrogels, into families with high water content, rapid swelling and superabsorbent hydrogels.  
Knowledge of the swelling characteristics of a polymer is of utmost importance in biomedical and 
pharmaceutical applications since the equilibrium degree of swelling influences: the solution of diffusion 
coefficient through the hydrogel, the surface properties and surface molecule mobility, the optical properties 
and the mechanical properties [13].   
In the field of Biofabrication, hydrogels is predominantly used as bioinks, since they allow for embedding cells 
in an environment that closely mimics the natural ECM [15]. Though hydrogels have been employed as 
injectable scaffolds or as delivery systems for a variety of tissue engineering applications. Hydrogels allow for 
homogenous drug or cell distribution with any shape or size, and though natural hydrogels such as fibrin or 
collagen have been used, the physical mechanism is more difficult to control with natural hydrogels. 
 
2.3.1 PEG Hydrogels 
One of the most prominently used polymers for hydrogel preparation is biomedical applications is 
Poly(ethylene glycol) (PEG) Hydrogels, because of hydrophilicity and biocompatibility [15].  
Hydrogels of Poly(ethylene oxide) (PEO) and Poly(ethylene glycol) (PEG), have received increasing attention 
recently for biomedical applications because of the non-toxic behavior of PEG. This hydrogel have been used in 
cell scaffolds, adhesive medical application and delivery vehicles with promising results [19].  
Though PEG is compositional homogenous, nontoxic and tunable with respect to chemical and mechanical 
properties. However PEG like other synthetic systems, inherently lacks biologically active sites which limits 
proliferation, migration and organization of incorporated cells [20].  
 
2.3.2 Collagen Hydrogels 
Collagen is the most prevalent protein in the connective tissue of animals, and constitutes approximately 25% 
of total body protein in humans. The molecular subunit of collagen, called tropocollagen, is a rigid rod with a 
molecular weight ~300 KDa. Tropocollagen assembles into larger structural units called fibers [18]. There are 
four commonly used types of collagen: Type 1 collagen is the type commonly found in skin, bone and tendons, 
type 2 is most common in cartilage, type 3 is abundant in vascular structures and type 4 collagen is found in the 
basement membrane that separates epithelial and mesodermal tissue.  
Collagen is used as a biomaterial because of its widespread abundance in nature and ease of modification. The 
abundance of hydroxyl, amine and carboxylic acid groups allows simple cross-linking between and within 
==PAGE:15==
15 
 
collagen units. Self-cross-linking can be induced by dehydration when exposed to high temperatures (105 C) 
[5]. Exposure to glutaraldehyde produces collagen with firm covalent cross-links and elastic behavior. 
Gelatin is an irreversible denatured and hydrolyzed form of collagen that is commonly used in pharmaceuticals, 
and used in bioinks to replicate the interpenetrating network architecture of the extracellular matrix [21].  
 
2.4 Thiolated Gelatin  
Injectable gelatin hydrogels formed with bio-orthogonal click chemistry are cell-responsive ECM mimics for 
biomaterials applications. Gelatin polymers with pendant norbornene can quickly crosslink with thiol groups 
upon curing, allowing for high viability of encapsulated cells, establishment of 3D elongated cell morphologies 
and biodegradation when injected [22].

Native Gelatin have poor adhesive properties, but one strategy to improve adhesion of polymers is the concept 
of thiolation. Thiolated polymers, designated thiomers form disulfide bonds between the free thiol groups of 
the polymer and the alkene norbornene [24]. Hydrogels can be formed by cross-linking soluble polymer chains 
covalently into insoluble networks that may be more appropriate for applications requiring extended material 
stability. Covalent polymers can be formed via either radical mediated polymerization or bio-orthogonal ‘click’ 
reactions. Radical mediated polymerizations are initiated by radicals, which are generated from initiators 
excited by appropriate initiation energy source, such as photons or heat. These radicals can propagate across 
multiple vinyl moieties and as a result, these chain-growth polymer networks formed by radical mediated 
polymerization usually contain heterogeneous cross-links.  Photopolymerization is one that permits control 
over the polymerization because the initation and termination of cross-linking reaction can be modulated by 
light irradiation [25]. Photopolymerization requires the input of ultraviolet light for cross-linking, which needs 
dose optimization to balance toxicity to cells with hydrogel mechanical properties and limits the method to 
settings where rapid UV illumination is possible [22].

3.2.2 Floodlight control 
FabIntrepreter was written in C++ by students of the Cornell 
University’s department of Mechanical & Aerospace 
engineering [23]. The system works by creating a “FAB” 
files, which is files with the printing instructions for the 
Fab@Home [28]. This file encodes the tool settings, 
controls the fabrication parameters such as deposition rate, 
and the tool movement through what it defines as paths. 
The FAB file is constructed in a XML files, separated in two 
different sections. The first section defines parameters for the print and references the tool intended for 
printing. The second section defines the print itself by dividing it into paths and points. A path is layer printed 
with a specific tool or material, and consists of points that describe the path with the material will be 
deposited.  The Tool is a defined combination of a specific extruder setup, such as a 2 ml Syringe, which is 
loaded with a specific material such as thiol-ene. The tool will define the conversion parameter settings into 
mechanical steps for the motors, such as the conversion deposition rate of material into steps of the extruder 
motor.  
Each path describes a printing movement for a specific 
material / tool, so each time printer would change 
material to print with, a new path would be initiated, 
and its print settings would be loaded. The amount of 
paths would indicate how many times the printer 
changed tool, whereas the amount of points would 
indicate amount of movement for the specific path.

3.3 Thiol-ene Ink preparation 
Thiol-ene Ink was prepared were greatly inspired by experiments conducted at Copenhagen University [14].   
To alter viscosity of the Thiol-ene ink, oligomerization were tested at various stoichiometric imbalances, where 
the materials were pre-curing were conducted, or in the case of no oligomerization the material were only 
mixed before printing.  
The photoinitiator Lucirin TPO-L was dissolved and stored in a 10% weight to weight solution of Ethanol. The 
stored photoinitiator in the glass vile, was wrapped in tinfoil, which protects it from UV light, which degrades 
the photoinitiator over time. 
The due to small amount printed per experiment, the Thiol-ene ink was prepared in Eppendorf 4 mL tubes, 
which could fit approximately 5 g of thiol-ene ink. This amount would allow two fillings of the syringe, and with 
a syringe lasting three runs, allowing for a maximum of six different settings per stoichiometric ratios.  
The Stoichiometric balanced ratio was prepared by mixing the two momomers in a ratio of 4:3. Pentaerythritol-
tetrakis(3-mercaotopropionate) (“Tetrathiol”) has a molecular weight of 488.66, and Triallyl-1,3,5-triazine-
2,4,6(1H,3H,5H)-trione (“Triallyl”) has a molecular weight of 249.27. With a total mixture of 5 g, the mixture 
comprised of 2.98 g Tetrathiol and 2.02 g Triallyl. The Eppendorf tube was wrapped in tinfoil to remove the 
exposure to light and UV. 1% weight to weight photoinitiator was added, and was mixed vigorously, and then 
vortexed to remove bubbles in the viscous materials, arising from the mixing. This would lead to a 
photoinitiator concentration in the solution of 0.1%.  
For Stoichiometric imbalanced was prepared by designating different ratios, such as 1:20 Triallyl to Tetrathiol, 
but conducted in identical methods. In the case of a ratio of 1:20, a mixture of 0.149 g Tetrathiol was mixed 
with 2.02 g Triallyl in an Eppendorf tube and 1% weight to weight photoinitiator was added. The mixture was 
stirred vigorously while curing for 30 seconds under a 365 nm UV light. The Eppendorf tube was wrapped in 
tinfoil to protect from further UV exposure, 2.831 g of Tetrathiol was added and the mixture was stirred 
vigorously again without exposure to UV, and at the end vortexed to remove bubbles.

4.1 Fab@Home printability 
All tests using thiol-ene inks showed the Fab@Home printer capable of printing with layers of 0.2mm and quite 
accurate positioning within the XY field. But due to the human required calibration before each print, all first 
layers were not of similar thickness. The XY dimensions of the printed structures were not affect, and the 
structures could be reproduced accurately.  
The belt driving the Z-axis had some problems with lifting the plate, due to a loosening belt that repeatedly 
need refastening. If the belt was not fasten enough, it would introduce a small interval, where the motor would 
rotate the gear without moving the plate itself, creating an error in the print, by not lowering the plate the 
calculated distance.  
During printing small pauses could be observed, when the printed reach a point positions, where the printer 
changed direction. This could lead to a continues flow of material from the printer while pausing, affecting the 
accuracy of the print.  
When starting upon a new path, a change in when the push-out 
action was conducted, could also be subjected to variation, which 
would lead to a prematurely or delayed extrusion of material.  
When the printer reached the end of a path, the push-back action 
could also be delayed, which would lead to material kept being 
extruded, when the needle moved into the UV protector, or 
prematurely, so little or no material would be extruded is the last

The UV protector effectively shield the needle from being exposed to light, which could be observed by

4.3 Gelatin Ink printability 
The Gelatin Ink test was first used to determine the behavior of gelatin and the possibility to use it as a 
printable hydrogel. Later the cell viability tests were conducted using the gelatin ink, to determine the cyto-
compatibility of functionalized gelatin. 
 
4.3.1 Gelation of gelatin  
There were remarkably difference in the behavior of the gelatin dependent on both the temperature and the 
concentration. With a 5% concentration of gelatin, the solution would continue to be fluid regardless of the 
dissolving had been done under 20 or 50 °C. The solution of 10% gelatin and a temperature of 20 °C the 
solution would also continue to be fluid, but the same solution subjected to treatment of 50 °C, would solidify 
when reaching room temperature again. This would indicate that heating would affect the gelation of the 
gelatin and could affect the possibility to use gelatin for printing and even just mixing materials into the 
solution. With high concentrations of gelatin at 20%, the solution would solidify regards of the temperature of 
treatment was 20 or 50 °C. This would indicate that high concentrations of gelatin could be impossible to 
manipulate with subjecting it some kind of continues heating.  
 
4.3.2 Functionalized Gelatin using Norbornene, NHS and EDC 
When the photo initiator was mixed into the functionalized GelNB and thiol solution, it quickly became cloudy. 
This indicated that the some cross-linking did occur, though the material did not become solid or even 
noticeable more viscous. When the photoinitiator was added into the simple solution of only Norbornene, EDC, 
NHS and Thiol, the cross-linking also occurred in this situation, suggesting that the Thiol cross-linked with free,

5 Discussion 
In this chapter, the problems and solution of using the Fab@Home as a Bioprinter, as well as using thiol-ene 
and Functionalized gelatin as bio inks for Bioprinting will be discussed and assessed from different perspectives 
to get a broader perspective of the problems experienced during printing and ink processing in Bioprinting. To 
have a broad discussion, on these issues, it is important to note the Fab@Home does not utilize the same 
control files know as G-code, as standard printers, rather it uses FAB files, which does not allow as much 
parameter settings or control of the printer. Secondly the two materials chosen, was being assessed upon they 
print ability and not they biological applicability, though other work have shown promising results in these 
regards. 
 
5.1 Printability of the Fab@Home 
The conversion of the Fab@Home, was clearly successful, which the results of effectively printing with UV 
curable materials also showed. The precision of the movement of the printer and speed did not become a 
limiting factor for the resolution of the printed structures. Though Fab@Home have a limited precision, 
especially in the Z-direction, this error-induced effect did not influence the printed structures greatly.  
One major problem of using the Fab@Home as the framework for bioprinting, is the setup using acrylic plates. 
This setup is rather huge, compared to the possible print area, which takes up a lot of space. This problem led 
to making it impossible to have the Fab@Home inside a LAF bench, allowing for actually printing cell laid 
hydrogels. The acrylic plates which creates the skeleton of the Fab@Home is prone to contamination and 
overlaps the separated areas in a LAF bench, risking contamination of the whole LAF bench. Modern 3D 
printers are often much smaller, which would allow for putting them inside a LAF bench, or even some newer 
Bioprinter are built inside a LAF bench. 
 
5.1.1 Floodlight as UV source 
The method of using a standard UV floodlight as source a mechanical setup to control the exposure is typically 
know from the commercial products. However, this method also poses some problems regarding overexposure 
onto parts of the print already cured or not intended to be cured. The floodlight will expose the whole plate 
each time the curing process is conducted, which could lead to over exposing the cells with UV light. 
Secondly it becomes impossible to manipulate level of curing of the material, which otherwise could be used to 
create more or less stiff materials. With a floodlight it is impossible to control the amount of UV light a specific 
part of the structure would be exposed to, so the only method to change the stiffness of the materials would 
be to change it molecular structure or through Stoichiometric imbalance. 
And with the current setup, using a standard commercial floodlight for private use, the amount of energy it 
induces per 𝑚𝑚2 is not determined, and the level of energy is much lower than normal floodlight for 
laboratory use. This leads to longer exposure time, and more flow out of the material when printed, which 
distorts the structure. 
==PAGE:33==
33 
 
5.1.2 Extrusion control 
The Fab@home does only allow the deposition rate to be changed throughout the printing, which leads to 
problems when less material needs to be extruded at specific site of the print, such as when lines cross each 
other. As it can be seen in the prints, the amount of material extruded, does not only relate to the deposition 
rate, but is also effected by the Push-out and Push-back actions. Moreover, because the timing of when each 
action is conducted may vary due to program FabIntrepreter, the prints may not be completely identical, even 
with the same parameters. Other programs running on the PC at the time might affect the FabIntrepreter the 
CPU of the PC and its timing. This timing affect the pauses between each line of code is executed, and can 
induce a delay in when an action or a movement is done. This delay creates a shift between to actions 
conducted at the same time, such as moving to a specific point while the Push-out action is executed. 
Even though the Fab@Home is a micro-extrusion based system, it does not allow for any more control than a 
pneumatic based system, since it does not allow changes of deposition rate. And opposite the pneumatic 
system, the Fab@Home does not have almost instant pressure delivery, which results in a great time interval 
from when the extruder begins to exert pressure, until it begins printing. 
As observed in 4.2.2, greater viscosity leads better control of flow of material, but a greater viscosity also leads 
to more friction against the needle when material passes through. This affects the possibility to change needle 
diameters, because a different needle diameter also would change the friction dramatically, as described in 
2.1.1.  
Only one 3 dimensional structure was printed, to be able to compare the results. Though it would be expected, 
that different structures required different parameters for successful and high resolution prints. Simply 
because different structures would lead to different demands of the material behavior and print parameters.  
 
5.2 Printability of thiol-ene 
The behavior of the thiol-ene ink would change considerably with the different levels of pre-oligomerization. 
This would most likely be due to the change in viscosity which would allow for the material to keep its structure 
until cured. But it also changed the time lag, from when pressure was applied to the material and extrusion of 
the material would begin. This is most likely due to the change in compressibility which would change the 
amount of pressure absorbed into the material before extrusion. The effects could be observed by seeing that 
the best results were obtained with a low ratio and an accurate deposition rate.  
The Push-out and Push-back actions also proved important in increasing resolution of the prints, where too 
little material pushed out, would lead to a lack of material in the beginning of each path, while too much 
material would lead to very wide and unfavorable lines in the beginning. The appropriate levels for these 
actions also changed, when the ratio changed, as it can be seen in the results. Smaller amount of material 
needs to be pre-primed with the push-out action, when the oligomerization reaches a lower ratio.  
Due to the nature of oligomerization, a certain threshold exists for how low a ratio can be utilized. As the ratio 
approaches 1:3, risk of gelation of the material, where chain-link polymerization occurs rises.  
 
 
==PAGE:34==
34 
 
5.3 Printability of gelatin 
Due to the high viscosity of the gelatin, structure could easily be printed and retain its structure. The Printed 
gelatin showed great sensitivity to temperature, both affecting its solubility, gelation and cross-linking ability. 
Higher temperature would lead to greater solubility, but also possible gelation and what can be assumed 
norbornene disassociation from the gelatin.  The tests clearly showed requirement of dialysis, because 
unbound norbornene also would bind to the thiols, which makes the process both more time consuming and 
more expensive to conduct. The solution would not be easily stored due to its tendency to gelation, which is 
why previous work have used freeze drying to store it in a powder state [22].  
The method of using 4arm-PEG5K-SH was not printable due to its almost instantaneously cross-linking and its 
tight crosslinking. Because the preparation of the inks were done in a laboratory illuminated by normal lights 
and had windows, everything was exposed to an ambient level of UV radiation. When using Poly(ethylene 
glycol) dithiol or thiol-ene inks, the required amount of UV radiation was high enough, to which the ambient 
exposure did not interfere. But the results from using 4arm-PEG5K-SH indicates that the required amount of 
UV radiation is much smaller. The only way to avoid this problem, would be to either use a room equipped with 
UV free lights and windows covered in UV protective filters, or cut down the amount of photoinitiator 
significantly.  
 
5.4 Viability of gelatin embedded cells 
The photograph shown in 4.3.4 clearly shows live cells in the functionalized gelatin, with not observable 
difference between the use of photoinitiator or not. This shows that the solution is not directly toxic to the 
cells, and that the use of 1% photoinitiator does not change the toxicity of the solution. This would seem 
promising, due to photoinitiators generally being a problem when using cross-linkable hydrogels. 
These results should be view with certain amount of skepticism, because the results only shows the amount of 
live cells. A better method for obtaining much more precise results, would be to use live and death staining, 
where Ethidium homodimer III is used, which will color the cells red, if they die. This method would be able to 
provide a ratio between live and death cells, which could determine the level of cytotoxicity of the hydrogel. 
This type of test would be more precisely called live cell staining.  
Since the reheating results in failed cross-linking, the effect of the tightness which the functionalized gelatin 
would cross link were not possible to be determined. How tightly the hydrogel is cross-linked, also affect the 
suitability for different cells, so the results of the cell viability tests would also depend on which type of cells 
used in comparison to the hydrogel. To further investigate material, certain rheology tests would have to be 
conducted, to determine the behavior and capabilities of the material. 
The current method of only waiting for 1 hour before testing also excludes the possibility to determine any 
effect due to the cells being exposed to UV radiation. Generally cells experience DNA damage when exposed to 
enough UV radiation, which depends on both the level radiation, wavelength and type of cell. Though this 
effect is very probable, it would only be possible to be tested during prolonged tests, out of the capabilities for 
this project.

6 Conclusion and Outlook 
 
6.1 Conclusion 
In this thesis, the 3D printing using a modified Fab@Home printer using biomaterials intended for bioprinting 
with the focus of using UV cross-linking have been carried out. The initial aim of the project, which was to print 
UV cross-linkable biomaterials using the Fab@Home, was resolved. The UV floodlight installed on top of the 
Fab@Home proved capable of cross-linking the printed materials, though due to it much lower radiation 
energy exposure, which resulted in longer curing intervals. The Fab@Home also proved quite capable of 
printing sub millimeter resolution, with precise movement of all three axis. 
The reason for printing thiol-ene inks and modified gelatin, was to investigate already know material’s 
capability to be used in bioprinting. Thiol-ene inks proved quite capable, though required extensive changes to 
both print parameters and ink stoichiometric balance to acquire a reasonable print resolution.  Whereas 
modified gelatin proved quite capable as 3D printable material. 
Important parameters for produce  acceptable printed structures, were determined to be deposition rate, 
Push-out and Push-back function for regulating build up pressure, and viscosity. All three parameters showed 
remarkable impact to the resolution of the 3D printed structures. Tests also showed the effects of each 
parameter were affected by the other parameters.  
Modified gelatin proved to be very sensitive to UV radiation from external sources, which proves the 
requirement of specific UV free environments for printing certain UV cross-linkable materials such as modified 
gelatin. Especially the use of 4arm-PEG5K-SH as the thiol cross-linked with modified Gelatin or GelNB, showed 
very strong sensitivity to UV radiation. Which in combination to the tight cross-linking reaction, raising the 
viscosity dramatically, made 4arm-PEG5K-SH an unusable thiol for printing. 
To test Cytotoxicity, immediate cell-viability was tested, using live cell staining. Which showed cells surviving in 
the modified gelatin, with no changes regards of exposure to photoinitiator. These results and the printability 
of modified gelatin makes it a very promising hydrogel and 3D printable biomaterial.  
 
6.2 Outlook 
The main aim for any future work should be to decide whether to focus of the mechanical control and 
structure of functional Bioprinter or improving the available bio inks for bioprinting. Regardless the avenue 
chosen, much more data needs to be collected and analyzed, and aspects need to be greatly improved of each 
area.  
The Fab@Home as a platform for Bioprinting, is greatly restricted due to inability to change parameters during 
the print, and dynamically change the printing behavior while printing. If this wanted to be changed, a great 
possibility would be to convert the current system to utilize procedural machine code, such a G-code, which is 
utilized by most 3D printers. G-code systems allow for change parameters, but also change the amount of 
material between each point in the print, which would greatly enhance the resolution of the printing.  
==PAGE:36==
36 
 
A more common problem for bioprinting, especially micro extrusion printing, is the calibration of a Bioprinter 
to a specific material. This process is similar to the work done with thiol-ene inks, which can be extensive and 
exhausting to acquire reasonable results. Especially since most researchers in the field of biomaterials and 
especially tissue engineering, does not have much experience robotics and computer-aided manufacturing. To 
overcome this issue, research should be focused upon Bioprinters capable of self-calibrating or easing the work 
of calibrating, using sensors and signals analysis. Another path for overcoming this problem could be to make 
extrusion systems that are less sensitive to the material behavior, such as friction and compressibility. This 
could also be done through mechanics such as vibration and sensing pressure, which could determine when 
the threshold pressure had been reached. 
For the second avenue, improving modified gelatin using Norbornene, would require much more testing to 
actually confirm that the modification actually went as intended through characterization, and to determine 
the behavior of such bio inks in regards to cell compatibility and material properties.  
The optimization of the thiolated gelatin would be focused upon multiple areas to achieve better bio ink. The 
primary focus should be to establish the correct composition to achieve a survivable rigidity of the hydrogel 
that could sustain survivability for neural stem cells. The second area, which could be focused upon, is the 
correct balance between UV radiation and Photoinitiator, to neither exert DNA damage as well as making the 
ink toxic for cell due to the photoinitiator.
