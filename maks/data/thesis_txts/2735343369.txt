Abstract
In light of environmental sustainability concerns and climate change, the construction industry must pivot towards
creating buildings that are adaptable to new challenges and ready for any future changes. A critical aspect of
this adaptability is the choice of ventilation systems. While centralized ventilation systems (CV) are commonly
preferred, decentralized ventilation (DV) systems offer greater flexibility and adaptability.
This research thoroughly investigates two scenarios for an office building, comparing the use of Centralized
Ventilation (CV) and Decentralized Ventilation (DV), to evaluate flexibility and other potential advantages.
Simulations conducted with IDA ICE revealed that Decentralized Ventilation (DV) systems installed on an office
floor are more energy-efficient than Centralized Ventilation (CV) systems. Furthermore, the elimination of CV
ductwork in favor of DV systems can result in a total vertical space saving of 9.9 meters within the building (95
total height). This space saving equates to almost three additional floors, assuming a slab-to-slab height of 3.8
meters.
Moreover, Decentralized Ventilation (DV) systems demonstrated superior efficiency through reduced energy
consumption, not only based on weather data for 2023 but also in projections for 2050 and 2080. However, it
was observed that CV systems performed better in terms of supplying cool air.

Summary
The study centers on the significance of building adaptability, a critical aspect in response to climate change and
sustainability demands. Among various technologies, ventilation systems are highlighted for their ability to adjust
and respond to anticipated changes, emphasizing their pivotal role.
This study examines two distinct scenarios for an office building, each employing a different ventilation technology:
one scenario implements a Centralized Ventilation (CV) system, while the other utilizes a Decentralized Ventilation
(DV) system. Each scenario has been analyzed across three different weather time frames: 2023, 2050, and 2080.
For this analysis, a 95-meter-tall building located in Kongens Lyngby, Denmark, was chosen.
A range of
methodologies was applied to devise the ventilation systems, employing both Danish and European Standards to
specify the minimum air supply required for each office space, bathroom, and corridor, as well as to determine the
appropriate duct diameters for the Centralized Ventilation (CV) system. System Air Cad software was then used
to select the Air Handling Unit (AHU). Subsequently, IDA ICE 4.9.9 software facilitated simulations to measure
energy consumption and ensure compliance with indoor climate and CO2 levels.
For Decentralized Ventilation (DV) equipment, with collaboration with Air Master, a Danish company specializing
in DV devices for various airflow requirements, was sought. Ultimately, IDA ICE was also utilized to evaluate the
energy consumption of DV systems and to conduct a comparative analysis of both systems.
Ultimately, the results indicated that Decentralized Ventilation (DV) systems are more energy-efficient, though they
underperformed in air cooling capabilities. Nonetheless, a significant advantage of DV systems became apparent
through the conservation of vertical space within the building, achieved by eliminating the ductwork required for
Centralized Ventilation (CV) systems: earning extra floors.

Introduction
In the contemporary construction industry, there is an increasing demand to anticipate the several changes that
may transpire through out the lifetime of a building’s existence.
Factors such as varying weather conditions,
climate change, the functionality of the structure, incorporation of new technologies, and more, collectively fall
under the umbrella term of adaptability. This is due to the importance of reducing high CO2 emissions in our
environment, and construction industry plays a big role in this environment pollution.
According to the ”Architecture 2030” platform [1], ”the built environment is responsible for about 42% of annual
global CO2 emissions.
Of those total emissions, building operations are responsible for approximately 27%
annually, while the embodied carbon of just four building and infrastructure materials; cement, iron, steel, and
aluminum, are responsible for an additional 15% annually.”
Therefore, these percentages are quite critical and a cause for concern.
The European Union has developed a
classification system ”EU Taxonomy” to establish a common framework for determining which economic activities
can be considered environmentally sustainable. This aims to create a standardized set of criteria for assessing the
environmental sustainability of economic activities, particularly in the context of climate change mitigation and
adaptation.
The EU Taxonomy extends obviously to the construction sector.
Within the taxonomy framework, economic
activities related to construction need to meet specific environmental sustainability criteria to be considered as
contributing to the EU’s environmental objectives.
These criteria encompass factors such as energy efficiency,
resource use, and greenhouse gas emissions. Here comes the ”provision of EU-level technical guidance on adapting
buildings to climate change” [2].
This guide sets criteria for construction and renovation projects. Essentially, the following steps are required to
provide substantial contributions to climate adaptation in buildings:
1. Screening to identify climate risks that may affect the performance of the building in the future over its expected
lifetime.
2. Where the screening shows a risk for climate hazards, a Climate Risk and Vulnerability Assessment (CRVA) is
conducted.
3. Assess the adaptation solutions (both physical and non-physical) to reduce the identified risk.
4. Adaptation solutions that substantially reduce the most important risks are finally implemented.
The criteria mentioned above underscore the significance of forecasting the future needs of a building and ensuring

its capability to adjust to various potential situations.
Furthermore, the emphasis lies not only on making a

The EU is also working on creating an EU model for digital building logbooks for new and existing buildings.

A study made in Stuttgart (Germany) called: Why are adaptive facades not widely used in practice? Identifying
ecological and economical benefits with LCA.” [4]. This research study demonstrates the advantages of incorporating
an adaptive facade into a building. It was discovered that a facade equipped with a decentralized ventilation rather

4.1
Adaptive ventilation: decentralized ventilation systems
In any building there are two types of mechanical ventilation.
Centralized ventilation (CS) systems and
decentralized. Centralized ventilation systems utilize a single, large unit to distribute air throughout a building
via extensive ductwork, offering uniform air quality control and potentially more efficient operation at scale but
requiring significant space and maintenance.
Decentralized ventilation (DV) systems, on the other hand, consist of multiple small units located throughout
a building to independently control the air in specific areas, providing greater flexibility and energy efficiency
due to reduced ductwork but potentially leading to higher initial costs and more complex maintenance requirements.
The adaptability of DV manifests through:
4

Spatial Flexibility: Decentralized systems can be installed in specific areas as needed without the requirement
for extensive ductwork. This makes them ideal for retrofitting into existing buildings or spaces where traditional
ducted systems would be impractical or too invasive.
Customized Ventilation: These systems allow for the ventilation to be tailored to the needs of individual rooms
or zones within a building. This means that areas with higher occupancy or different usage patterns can receive
more ventilation, improving comfort and air quality where it’s most needed.
Energy Efficiency:
By avoiding the energy losses associated with long duct runs in centralized systems,
decentralized systems can be more energy-efficient, especially in buildings where not all areas require constant
ventilation. They can operate independently, only when and where needed, reducing overall energy consumption.
Ease of Installation and Expansion: The modular nature of decentralized systems allows for easier installation
and scalability.
Buildings can start with what is necessary and expand or adjust the system as needs change
without major renovations.
Resilience and Redundancy: In decentralized systems, the failure of one unit does not impact the entire
building’s ventilation.
This redundancy ensures that ventilation can continue even if individual units require
maintenance or repair, enhancing the building’s overall resilience.
Integration with Smart Building Technologies: Decentralized ventilation systems can easily integrate with
smart building technologies, allowing for automated control based on real-time data (such as CO2 levels, humidity,
and occupancy). This adaptability not only improves indoor air quality but also optimizes energy use.
Aesthetic Versatility: Without the need for extensive ductwork, decentralised systems offer more flexibility in
architectural and interior design, allowing for a more seamless integration into the aesthetic of a space.
In summary, the adaptability of decentralized ventilation systems stems from their flexibility in installation
and operation, energy efficiency, and ability to provide customized ventilation solutions. These attributes make
them particularly suitable for modern, dynamic environments where occupant comfort, energy conservation, and
architectural integrity are paramount.

Motivation and prerequisites
The motivation of the project is to compare the two ventilation systems (centralised and decentralised) and prove
that decentralised are able to be adaptable in terms of energy consumption through out the time (climate change)
and in terms of space savings as the duct work would be remove mostly. Additionally, prove their flexibility. The
primary emphasis of the project will be on office buildings.

Problem statement
The aim of this project is to conduct a comparison between a flexible ventilation system (Decentralised Ventilation)
and a traditional, non-flexible ventilation system (Centralised ventilation).
The problem statements are:
• Do we see actually an improvement in energy consumption and total costs of the installment of decentralised
ventilation system flexible technology rather than the centralised ventilation in office buildings?
• How significant is the advantage of implementing a decentralized ventilation system in a office building,
especially considering the impact of climate change over several years?
• By eliminating the ductwork associated with traditional centralized ventilation, do we gain more floor-to-floor
space, or perhaps even add extra floors?

5.2
Danish standard DS EN 16798-2
The Danish standard DS EN 16798, part of the European Standard (EN), relates to the indoor environmental
quality, specifically focusing on the performance criteria for the indoor environment in terms of air quality, thermal
environment, lighting, and acoustics within buildings. This standard is designed to ensure that buildings provide
comfortable, healthy, and productive indoor environments for their occupants while also considering energy
efficiency.
DS EN 16798 is relevant to architects, engineers, and building designers involved in creating or renovating buildings
to ensure that they meet high standards of occupant comfort and energy efficiency. It applies to a wide range of
buildings, including office spaces, residential buildings, schools, and healthcare facilities.
In the context of ventilation systems, the standard is particularly pertinent as it sets the criteria for ventilation
that not only ensures a healthy and comfortable indoor climate but also considers the adaptability of systems to
respond to the changing needs of the building and its occupants. Adhering to this standard can guide the design
and implementation of systems that enhance occupant comfort, health, and productivity, as well as the building’s
energy performance.

The first scenario examines the more commonly encountered traditional ventilation approach: the Centralized

10.1
Brief introduction to CV
A centralized ventilation system is a single, unified system designed to provide ventilation to an entire building
from a central location. It typically consists of a network of ducts that distribute fresh air throughout the building
and remove stale air from it. This system is controlled centrally, allowing for uniform air quality and temperature
control across all areas of the building.
Centralized systems are often used in larger buildings due to their efficiency in managing air flow on a big scale.
They can incorporate filters, heat recovery units, and other technologies to improve air quality and energy efficiency.
However, the installation and maintenance of the extensive ductwork (figure 13) required can be complex and costly.
The heart of a centralized ventilation system, the AHU (figure 12), is responsible for drawing in fresh outdoor
air, filtering it, and then heating or cooling it as needed before distributing it throughout the building. It may also
control humidity levels.
Figure 12: Air Handling Unit (AHU)
16
==PAGE:21==
Mohamed Filali Yachou

Thesis
Figure 13: Ductwork of a centraliced ventilation system
Filters within the AHU remove particles and contaminants from the air, ensuring that the indoor air quality is
maintained.
Fans in the AHU and throughout the system ensure that air circulates efficiently. Supply fans push conditioned
air into the building, while exhaust fans remove stale air.
Coils within the AHU adjust the temperature of the air. Heating coils warm the air in colder months, while cooling
coils reduce the air temperature during warmer periods.
The heat exchanger is a crucial component in many centralized ventilation systems, especially those designed
with energy efficiency in mind. It plays a vital role in recovering energy from the exhaust air and using it to
pre-condition the incoming fresh air.
Depending on the climate and the specific needs of the building, the system may include devices to add moisture
to or remove moisture from the circulated air.
Vents and grilles are the points of interaction between the ventilation system and the building’s occupants.
10.2
Dimensioning
The ventilation system is design to comply with the climate class II and is dimensioned based on DS EN 16798-2 [6]
table B.6 according to a low polluted building, see figure 14. The ventilation system is designed only to maintain
the air quality in zone and will not provide cooling to the zone. However, the ventilation air will be either heated
or cooling a bit since a cooling and heating coils are placed in the air handling unit. This will ensure that the
ventilation air is better fitted for the temperature in the zones. All the meeting rooms and office spaces will be
provided with VAV ventilation and the bathrooms in the core and corridor will be provided with CAV, due to a
cheaper solution and the areas purpose without longer occupancy.

In certain areas, the cores utilize the ”CAV” type system, while the rooms employ the ”VAV” approach. The
difference between CAV (Constant Air Volume) and VAV (Variable Air Volume) systems lies in how they manage
airflow and temperature control within a building’s heating, ventilation, and air conditioning (HVAC) system. CAV

Whereas, VAV adjust the volume of air being distributed to a space based on the temperature demand or occupancy,
allowing for more precise control over environmental conditions.

Routing of ducts

Second scenario: Decentralised Ventilation (DV) System
The second scenario examines the adaptable ventilation system: Decentralized Ventilation System in Building 313.
A decentralized ventilation system represents a modern approach to ensuring fresh air circulation and maintaining
indoor air quality across different spaces within a building. Unlike centralized systems that manage air distribution
from a single, large unit, decentralized systems employ multiple, smaller units located strategically throughout the
building, directly within or adjacent to the spaces they serve.
11.1
Dimensioning
The concept entails fitting each zone or room with DV (Decentralized Ventilation) units incorporated into the
facade. The building features ample facade space, making it an ideal location for the installation of DV systems.

Incorporating decentralized ventilation equipment into the facade of a building offers several significant benefits,
particularly in enhancing the efficiency and adaptability of the building’s ventilation system. One of the primary
advantages is the ability to draw fresh air directly from the outside, which ensures a continuous supply of clean air
and improves indoor air quality by reducing the reliance on recirculated air, which can carry pollutants.
Additionally, decentralized ventilation systems are designed to regulate airflow based on the specific conditions
within each room. This means that the system can adjust the volume of air intake and exhaust in response to
changes in room occupancy, temperature, and humidity levels.
For example, if a room becomes crowded, the
system can increase airflow to maintain comfortable conditions, whereas it can reduce airflow in unoccupied spaces
to conserve energy.
All the devices shown in Figure 28 are connected to a central control system, which could be housed within a single
shaft of the core. This setup allows for adjustments to any of the devices from one consolidated location.
In the decentralized system, the space needed from the slab to the drop ceiling decreases. In the centralized system
(first scenario), the ductwork required a clearance of 400 mm for supply and another 400 mm for exhaust, totaling
800 mm. With additional ducts included, the overall space needed was 920 mm. In our scenario, we only require
250 mm for the ventilation equipment plus 120 mm for other installations like fire safety components, piping, etc.,
bringing the total height from the drop ceiling to the slab to 370 mm. This results in a space saving of 550 mm.
More than half meter of saving.

Conclusion
This study has demonstrated that incorporating decentralized ventilation systems into the facade of an office building
offers significant benefits in two key areas: Firstly, it has been established that the extensive ductwork required by
Centralized Ventilation (CV) systems can be eliminated and replaced with a smaller number of ducts with reduced
diameter. For this building, which spans 25 floors with 18 of them dedicated to office space (as illustrated in figure
9), the total increase in free height would be 0.55 meters per floor for 18 floors, resulting in an aggregate gain of
9.9 meters. This adjustment enables the maintenance of a floor-to-ceiling height of 2.58 meters, like to what was
previously achieved with CV systems. n the construction sector, gaining an additional two or nearly three floors
translates into a significant financial advantage, making stakeholders more inclined to adopt such measures.
Figure 36: To the left section with CV system, and to the right section with DV system
Figure 37: Total height space saved by using DV
Furthermore, Decentralized Ventilation (DV) systems have been shown to offer greater energy flexibility. This is
because they bring air directly from the outside into the indoor environment, eliminating the need for extensive
ductwork and thus reducing energy losses. However, it has also been established that DV systems are not fully
equipped to provide cool air. For 2023, DV AM 300 gave a value of 1428.9 kWh for heat exchange whereas CV
has given a value of 1649.7 kWh. For air cooling, CV showed to supply with a less amount of energy consumption,
especially in the future weather conditions (2050 and 2080). Currently proved, the primary area for enhancement
in Decentralized Ventilation (DV) systems lies in their cooling capabilities.
Indeed, Decentralized Ventilation (DV) systems represent adaptable technologies. When implemented within the

Discussion and further work
The efficiency of the Centralized Ventilation (CV) system could potentially be enhanced by incorporating a radiant
ceiling. This modification would reduce the necessary height between the floor and the false ceiling, potentially
diminishing the advantages offered by Decentralized Ventilation (DV) systems. Additionally, certain areas of the
floor plan, such as the core sections, might not receive adequate air supply. Consequently, the optimal solution
could involve a hybrid approach, merging CV with DV systems. This would involve using a smaller Air Handling
Unit (AHU) capable of delivering airflow to specific areas, like staircases in the core, particularly important for
maintaining pressurization in these areas to meet fire safety standards.
It would have been advantageous to construct a detailed model of the entire building in IDA ICE to facilitate a
comprehensive evaluation, rather than analyzing it by dividing the space into zones corresponding to individual
rooms. Unfortunately, the author’s equipment lacked the capacity to handle such extensive requirements. Moreover,
it appears that IDA ICE may not be fully equipped to simulate large-scale projects effectively. It might have been
prudent to utilize a different software solution for this purpose.
It’s worth noting that certain fans do not operate optimally in windy conditions. Our model building, standing
at 95 meters, utilizes axial fans, which are particularly challenged by wind – a notable consideration in Denmark,
where wind conditions are prevalent.
Testing these fans within a wind simulation model to evaluate their
functionality would have been beneficial.
Decentralized Ventilation (DV) systems are integrated into the facade.
Exploring in greater detail how these
devices can be incorporated into the facade while preserving an aesthetically pleasing architectural outcome, without
compromising the design, would have been intriguing. Additionally, conducting a research study on the disassembly
and assembly of DV devices to evaluate the building’s adaptability to changes in its use (such as converting office
spaces into residential areas) would present an interesting area of investigation.
