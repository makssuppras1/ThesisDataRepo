Structure of thesis
This master’s thesis is structured into two main sections: the core content and the supplement-
ary material. The core content is organised in a format similar to traditional research articles,
comprising an abstract, introduction, method, results, discussion, and conclusion. The supple-
mentary material provides additional details and data supporting the main findings. Below is a
brief description of each section.
INTRODUCTION This section introduces the main research question: ”In what ways can
LCIA methodology be applied to practically assess off-site biodiversity within the construction
sector, considering its impact on planetary boundaries?” together with the subquestions ”What
influence does the change to mainly biogenic materials have on biodiversity?” and ”Does the
approach in the doughnut-BIOTOOL have the correct foundation?”. Providing background on
the key concepts and current challenges in the field.
METHOD The method section details the approaches used to link the planetary boundary
of biosphere integrity to life cycle impact assessment endpoint indicators. It covers the process
of allocating the share of safe operating space, the selection and setup of case studies, and
improvements to the recent doughnut-BIOTOOL.
RESULTS This section presents the findings from the various methodologies and case studies.
It includes a comparison of different LCIA methodologies, an analysis of the contribution of
various construction materials and energy sources to ecosystem quality, and an evaluation of the
impact on the planetary boundary for biosphere integrity. Additionally, it discusses the effects
of the improvements made to the doughnut-BIOTOOL.
DISCUSSION The discussion section synthesises the literature and the study’s contributions,
provides recommendations based on the findings, and identifies areas for future research. It
critically examines the relevance of certain impact categories, methodological choices, and the
representativeness of the study’s data.
CONCLUSION This section summarises the main findings of the thesis, highlighting how off-
site biodiversity is currently covered in LCIA, together with their link to the planetary boundary
of biosphere integrity. It also outlines the limitations of the study and improvements to future
off-site biodiversity assessments.
SUPPLEMENTARY MATERIAL The supplementary material is divided into several parts,
providing additional information on the background introduction, methods, results, data and
results in Excel, SimaPro CSV files, and lastly our improved doughnut-BIOTOOL Excel.

Abstract
The intersection of climate change and biodiversity loss poses a significant threat to the well-
being of current and future generations. This study explores how Life Cycle Impact Assessment
(LCIA) methodologies can assess off-site biodiversity within the construction sector, linking these
assessments to the planetary boundaries (PB). Critically examining the current LCIA methods,
the study highlights their strengths and weaknesses in accurately capturing biodiversity impacts,
ensuring a correct foundation for the industry.
Through a series of case studies, this research evaluates the impacts of various construction
materials and energy sources on off-site biodiversity. The findings indicate a significant oversight
of key impact categories affecting biodiversity in current Life Cycle Assessment (LCA) practices.
This study demonstrates how the buildings transgress the PB by linking the ecosystem quality
endpoint categories of ReCiPe, Impact World+, LC-IMPACT and a PB-LCIA to the PB for
biosphere integrity.
By advancing the integration of biodiversity into LCA, this thesis contributes to a more holistic
approach to environmental sustainability, ensuring that the shift towards biogenic materials does
not inadvertently harm biodiversity. The findings support the development of more sustainable
building practices that align with both climate and biodiversity goals.
Through the proposal of improvements to current methodologies and practices, this study aims
to inform the need for a more comprehensive framework for evaluating biodiversity impacts in
the construction sector.

1
Introduction
Today we face the interlinked emergency of climate change and the loss of biodiversity, threaten-
ing the well-being of current and future generations induced by current and previous generations
[WWF, 2022]. This is quantified in the Planetary Boundaries (PB) Framework, first introduced
in 2009 by Rockstr¨om et al. [2009]. It defines a safe operating space (SOS) within the PBs to
maintain the Holocene’s stable state. The Holocene Epoch is a relatively stable period of 11,700
years [Steffen et al., 2015]. In the latest update to the PB framework, six out of the nine PBs are
now transgressed [Richardson et al., 2023]. Two of the nine PBs, climate change and biosphere
integrity are considered core boundaries. If these boundaries are significantly altered, it could
cause the Earth System to enter a new state.
Climate change as an impact measurement is already well adopted in industry and worldwide,
and thresholds relating to the PBs are already being established [Reduction Roadmap, 2023].
Meanwhile, industries have not yet adopted biosphere integrity as an impact measurement. In
the Danish building sector, there has been a shift towards using biogenic materials due to their
lower climate change impact [Gottlieb et al., 2023]. However, this shift in materials does not
consider other potential impacts, such as effects on biodiversity or pollution. The environmental
impacts of a system are commonly estimated through Life Cycle Assessment (LCA). Within
the LCA method, Life Cycle Impact Assessment (LCIA) is the main step to calculate impacts
related to a functional unit for different impact categories (IC) [Hauschild et al., 2018]. Biosphere
integrity is arguably represented at the endpoint level of LCIA through ecosystem quality (EQ).
LCIAs are continuously being advanced to include biodiversity in all aspects as defined by
the United Nations (UN) and the Intergovernmental Platform on Biodiversity and Ecosystem
Services (IPBES) [United Nations (UN), 1992; IPBES, 2019]. There are several LCIA methods,
but only a few are operational for covering biodiversity loss [Crenna et al., 2020]. Even so,
these methods only target three of the five direct drivers of biodiversity loss as defined by
IPBES [2019]: land use, climate change, and pollution [Sany´e-Mengual et al., 2023; Winter
et al., 2017]. Accumulating these actualities to the UN definition of biodiversity, current LCIA
methods only calculate species diversity (species richness), thus lacking genetic diversity and
ecosystem diversity [Winter et al., 2017]. Making it important to question the validity of current
LCIA methods’ ability to accurately measure biodiversity impacts.
Measuring the impact on biodiversity is a topic that is still in its early stages in the construction
sector and is currently not included in the Danish Building Regulations (BR) [BR18, nd]. As a
result, companies do not have clear definitions and regulations in this field, making it difficult to
account for the relationship between climate change, biodiversity, and their business activities
[ConTech Lab, nd]. However, from 2019, the new Environmental Product Declaration (EPD)
standard includes indirect measurements for biodiversity impact [DS/EN15804:2012+A2, 2019].
The previous indicator for Global Warming Potential (GWP), GWP-total, has been separated
into GWP-luluc, GWP-fossil, GWP-biogenic, and GWP-total. The new standard also segments

Nevertheless, the direct impact on biodiversity is still not measured. These changes to EPDs are

In 2023, with the publication of ”The Doughnut for Urban Development” a tool for

“In what ways can the LCIA methodology be applied to practically assess off-site biodiversity
within the construction sector, considering its impact on planetary boundaries?”. This should
cover the LCIA methods’ scientific relation to biodiversity and biosphere integrity, enabling the
construction sector’s impact to be linked to indicators for the PB framework.
This paper compares five case studies of Danish buildings against the PB of biosphere integrity
to elucidate the diverse material impacts on off-site biodiversity.

BIOTOOL. Through these case studies, the paper aims to answer the subquestions ”What
influence does the change to mainly biogenic materials have on biodiversity?” and ”Does the
approach in the doughnut-BIOTOOL have the correct foundation?”.
1.1
Background
Due to activities from humans, changes in biodiversity have been more rapid in the past 50 years
than at any time in our history [World Resources Institute, 2005]. In the Convention of Biological
Diversity (CBD), the UN defined biodiversity on three levels: species diversity, genetic diversity,
and ecosystem diversity, stating a holistic approach is needed to conserve nature [United Nations
(UN), 1992]. The headlining trend from the Living Planet Index (LPI) report of 2022, adopted
by the CBD as an indicator of progress, revealed an average decline in species abundance by
69% between 1970 and 2016 [WWF, 2022].
According to the Global Assessment Report of
Biodiversity and Ecosystem Services issued by IPBES one million animal and plant species are
currently threatened with extinction [IPBES, 2019]. IPBES furthermore identifies five direct
drivers of biodiversity loss (presented in order of those with the highest impact): land/water
use, overexploitation of resources, climate change, pollution, and the spread of invasive species,
which results from indirect drivers such as unsustainable production and consumption [Damiani
et al., 2023]. The urgent need to address the IPBES report is responded to in The Kunming-
Montreal Global Biodiversity Framework (GBF), agreed upon at the 15th Conference of Parties
(COP15) to the UN CBD [CBD, 2022]. The GBF contains 23 action-oriented targets for 2030
and 4 outcome-oriented goals for 2050.
The construction sector accounts for 40% of all extracted materials, being a major contributor
to material extraction [Sizirici Yildiz et al., 2021]. Urban expansion has predominantly occurred
in low-lying coastal areas and floodplains, known for their rich biodiversity [G¨uneralp and Seto,
2013]. Consequently, this has led to the depletion of unique biodiversity hotspots hosting animal

and plant species scarcely found elsewhere. According to the findings of the World Economic
Forum (WEF), the infrastructure and built environment are responsible for 29% of the share
of (near-) threatened species impacted worldwide [World Economic Forum, A., 2020]. While
85% of the total biodiversity impact in the construction sector stems from off-site impacts from,
among others, the production of materials [ConTech Lab, nd]. Multiple sustainable building
certification schemes require developers to address local biodiversity in their plan of action - for
example, DGNB ENV2.4 [DGNB-DK, nd]. Whilst off-site biodiversity is still haltering, with
no current requirements neither quantitatively nor qualitatively to be found [DGNB-DK, nd;
LEED, nd; BREEAM, nd]. In 2023 the Doughnut for Urban Development suggested a four-

global lens (off-site), requiring developers to expand their scope [Birgisd´ottir et al., 2023]. This
urgency is reflected in coming conferences and pilot versions of regulations and certification
schemes with off-site biodiversity requirements being formulated [RFBB, 2024; Building Green,
nd; Birgisd´ottir et al., 2023].
In the construction sector, reducing energy-related CO2 emissions has been a focus since the
oil crisis in 1970 [Nwodo and Anumba, 2019]. Nwodo and Anumba [2019] underlines that in
recent years a more holistic focus on a building’s environmental impacts from all stages in the
building’s life cycle has been adopted through LCA. Several initiatives in the building sector to
limit emissions of CO2 are presented in SM 6.1.1. In the European standard (ES) ISO 14040,
an LCA is defined as a tool that addresses both the aspects and the potential impacts on the
environment throughout a product’s life cycle from cradle-to-grave [ISO 14040, 2008]. Through
the European standard, ISO 14040 and ISO 14044 guidelines and requirements for conducting
LCAs are described [ISO 14044, 2008].
The PB framework is not directly operational in LCA. An LCA is built on a life cycle inventory
(LCI) which contains elementary flows and to use these elementary flows in an LCA, character-
isation factors (CFs) are needed, which are not included in the PB framework. To address this
issue Ryberg et al. [2018] developed 85 CFs to use in LCIA for the PB framework. Using the
method from Ryberg et al. [2018] an absolute environmental sustainability assessment (AESA)
can be carried out when the share of the SOS (SoSOS) is calculated for the specific activity.
If an activity does not exceed its SoSOS for any PB it can according to Ryberg et al. [2018]
be considered absolutely sustainable. Important when considering the PB-LCIA framework is
that the PB-LCIA reveals how an activity aligns with the SOS in the PB framework but doesn’t
assess its potential impacts on resources and human health as an LCA using regular LCIA would
[Ryberg et al., 2018].
A new framework combining the earth system and human well-being into safe and just earth sys-
tem boundaries (ESBs) was suggested by Rockstr¨om et al. [2023]. According to the publication,
safe ESBs will safeguard future generations, but the belief is that Just ESBs are needed to ensure
no significant harm (NSH) for current generations, particularly for vulnerable populations.

1.1.1
LCIA Literature study
We conducted an initial review of the literature to understand how current LCIA methods
evaluate impacts on off-site biodiversity1. LCIA methods have been compared multiple times
in the past [Winter et al., 2017; Crenna et al., 2020; Damiani et al., 2023; Weidema, 2015]. We
chose to evaluate and compare seven different LCIA methods and models in total, four of these
were applied to the case studies whereas the last three are not operational for the purpose of
this study. Either because the method is not integrated into LCA software or because it does
not have an endpoint category for EQ.
Following Crenna et al. [2020]’s conclusion on operational LCIA methods addressing biodiversity,
the following three have been included: ReCiPe 2016 [Huijbregts et al., 2017], LC-IMPACT
[Verones et al., 2020], and IW+ [Bulle et al., 2019]. Furthermore, we compare a method from
Gal´an-Mart´ın et al. [2021] linking biosphere integrity to the PB-LCIA framework set by Ryberg
et al. [2018] which was initially missing. The PB-LCIA is a bottom-up framework, which means
that it directly links LCI through CFs to the PB framework. The contrary framework would be
a top-down framework where the unit for the PB would have to be converted to the unit of the
LCIA method. Since this differentiates from the rest of the assessments, this approach has also
been chosen for comparison.
Two of the non-operational methods include other important factors for biodiversity: Product
Biodiversity Footprint (PBF) [Asselin et al., 2020] and Biodiversity Impact Assessment (BIA+)
[Winter et al., 2018]. PBF covers all five direct drivers of biodiversity loss, by including the
impacts on invasive species and overexploitation (covered in species management) through ac-
cumulated scoring of numerous sub-indicators qualitatively [Asselin et al., 2020; IPBES, 2019].
BIA+ builds on existing models and aims at expanding the assessment of biodiversity aspects
to include: genetic, species, and ecosystems [Winter et al., 2018; United Nations (UN), 1992].
The framework applies a habitat factor and the impact on the biodiversity status in major
habitat types. The impacts are measured in quantitative impact factors, created qualitatively
by ’expert’ judgements. The two methods are not operational, as they are not integrated into
LCA software, though they cover key aspects of biodiversity that existing methods are missing.
Lastly, we have also compared EF 3.1 as it is the current method used when making an EPD,
and thereby for measuring indirect biodiversity impacts in the construction sector [EPD Inter-
national, nd]. This is not operational for this study, as it does not have an endpoint category
for EQ. The three methods are included in the literature study to highlight possible future
approaches to EQ measurements.
1The respective LCIA methods have different names for the endpoint category concerning biodiversity, e.g.
ecosystem quality or damage to ecosystems, forward these will be referred to under a common naming of ecosystem
quality (EQ). Likewise, for the midpoint category level we use aggregated terms to ease the comparison, original
categories and aggregated terms can be found in SM 6.4.1.

2.1
Linking biosphere integrity to comparable LCA endpoint indicators
To incorporate PB considerations into LCIA methods, it’s crucial to align endpoint character-
isations with the PB metric for biosphere integrity. This section will cover the different metrics
for biosphere integrity and their potential link to endpoint characterisations.
Initially, the PB framework primarily addressed genetic biodiversity, quantified by the Rate of
biodiversity loss set at 10 E/MSY (extinctions per million species years) [Rockstr¨om et al., 2009].
However, conventional LCA methodologies typically neglect genetic biodiversity, as presented in
Table 4. This is due to genetic diversity measuring extinctions of species (irreversible damage),
whereas the LCIA methods measure reversible damages [Rossberg, 2022]. Steffen et al. [2015]
updated the PB and divided the biosphere integrity into genetic and functional diversity. For
the latter, a preliminary boundary was defined to maintain a Biodiversity Intactness Index (BII)
of 90%, with an uncertainty range of 90 to 30%. This method compares the current population
abundance with the abundance of the preindustrial era to measure the change [Scholes and
Biggs, 2005].
Given the inherent challenge in directly connecting reversible damages to EQ and irreversible
extinctions, this study proposes linking the PB biosphere integrity - functional integrity to the
LCIA methods used in the study. Recognising the current absence of linkage between HANPP,
together with NCP-based control variables, and LCIA, this study will bridge this gap by utilising
the BII threshold as a reference point in linking LCIA methods to the PB. The four LCIA
methods are presented below including their indicator for EQ, an elaboration of the measuring
units can be found in SM 6.2.1:
- ReCiPe 2016, quantifying EQ in species years (species.yr).
A hierarchical perspective, as
recommended by the authors, was taken into account.
- IW+, quantifying EQ in potentially disappeared fraction of species (PDF) in a dedicated area
over a certain period of time (PDF.m2.yr). Only short-term perspective impact categories
were taken into account.
- LC-IMPACT, quantifying EQ in PDF over time (PDF.yr). Average preferences, all impacts
over a 100-year timeframe perspective have been chosen as recommended by the authors
[Verones et al., 2020] to get the full extent of potential impacts in a 100y period. The impact
categories dealing with ecotoxicity are originally measured in the unit PDF.m3.d and therefore
a unit conversion to PDF.yr has been implemented, see SM 6.5.
- PB-LCIA for functional biosphere integrity, quantifying EQ in loss of Mean Species Abundance
(1 - MSA).
As the PB-LCIA is a bottom-up framework, it is directly linked to the PBs.

considers MSA to be a proxy for BII and quantifies the threshold in loss of MSA of a maximum
of 10% [Gal´an-Mart´ın et al., 2021]. The main difference between BII and MSA lies in their
weighting approach: BII provides weighted values according to the species richness of areas,

ecotope close to the definition of BII. The author further clarifies this relationship within the
ReCiPe framework, expressing EQ in species.yr. According to ReCiPe, the total number of
registered species is estimated at 1.95 million [Goedkoop et al., 2008]. While Goedkoop et al.
[2008] acknowledges that this number represents only a fraction of the estimated total number
of species, they argue that it is sufficient since the LCIA model itself only accounts for the
disappearance of registered species.
Doka [2016] translates the threshold into a measure of
relative reversible species loss, defining the threshold as a 10% reduction from the total number
of species set in ReCiPe 2016, equating to 195,000 species.yr. The compatibility with BII has
been used in literature for addressing AESA in terms of changes in biosphere integrity, whilst
arguing that HANPP might be a more promising approach if such a method becomes available
[Wolff et al., 2017; Ryberg et al., 2021].
Importantly, BII measures species abundance, looking at population sizes [Steffen et al., 2015],
whereas PDF measures species richness, meaning species count [Huijbregts et al., 2017; Verones
et al., 2020]. Abundance is more sensitive than richness since population size can be affected
before a species disappears.
Consequently, the relationship between the two measurements
deviates from a 1:1 ratio.
In a revised version of the PBA’06 method [Doka, 2016], called
”PBA’07” by Gabor Doka (not yet published), a reworked link between PDF and BII is applied.
Changing the previously used 1 PDF/BII ratio to 0.6 PDF/BII used as a median value based
on MSA and PDF values for different land use types. The ratio is based on the MSA values for
the land use types defined in the GLOBIO model [Schipper et al., 2016] (used by Steffen et al.
[2015] to define the PB) and the corresponding PDF values [de Baan et al., 2013]. The values in
de Baan et al. [2013] are expressed in biodiversity damage potential (BDP) which is a measure
of reduction in species richness compared to a similar unexploited habitat, directly comparable
to PDF. When applying the values from the two papers a PDF/BII ratio between 0.46 – 0.825
is found for the different land use types, with the median value of 0.6 (SM 6.2.2). We utilise this
ratio of PDF/BII, based on the median, in this study to convert the PDF-based LCIA methods
EQ unit to BII, as shown for ReCiPe in EQ 1.

2.2
Allocating the share of safe operating space
To get from the PB threshold to a SoSOS for a specific project, a sharing principle (SP) needs
to be applied to the PB threshold. To calculate the SoSOS for the case studies included in this
study the SOS needs to be distributed to the Danish country level, construction sector level and
divided by m2 and year. This study divides the SoSOS by m2 and year to follow the Danish
standard unit for climate change (kg CO2-eq/m2/year) when conducting an LCA for a building
[BR18, nd].
We use the egalitarian method, equal per capita, to distribute the SOS to the
Danish country level, as this is the most widely used method [Ryberg et al., 2020]. This method
distributes the SOS equally among the population of the world. We apply different approaches
for the distribution to the sector level for each SP. In Table 2 the three SPs are presented with
their calculation steps for distribution to country level, sector level, and m2 pr. year.

results.

2.4
Updating the doughnut-BIOTOOL through case studies
We explored the potential for the industry to utilise the doughnut-BIOTOOL as a means of
measuring EQ impacts2. The tool operates with the Ecoinvent database and uses ReCiPe 2016,
hierarchical perspective, as the LCIA method, making it crucial to determine whether this
framework is suitable for industry adoption. The tool can evaluate the EQ impact of both on-
site through the transformation of land areas and off-site actions. However, this study focuses
exclusively on the off-site component. We applied the tool to the case studies in this research
to assess its accuracy, workflow, and result interpretation, comparing these aspects with those
of SimaPro. Among the LCIA methods we evaluated, only ReCiPe and IW+ are available in
the Ecoinvent database, with ReCiPe being the only method that supports both midpoint and
endpoint characterisation (see SM 6.2.8).
The tool requires manual input for the following aspects of a building LCA: lifetime, energy
consumption, and materials. We identified areas for improvement and optimised the tool in two
key ways. Firstly, we made it possible to import an LCAByg file, automatically adding all the
materials and quantities. This was done by translating LCAByg names to an Ecoinvent library
(see SM 6.2.6) through an Excel Visual Basic for Applications macro. Secondly, we created an
Excel sheet to get results interpreted on an impact category level. This was done by setting
up a similar Ecoinvent library as the original tool, but with the midpoint category data. This
feature is unique to ReCiPe due to the lack of data from other LCIA methods in Ecoinvent.

The original doughnut-BIOTOOL3 required users to manually enter all materials from an
LCAbyg file, which created a moderate barrier to entry. The updated version of the tool sim-
plifies this process by automatically importing most materials, leaving users to input only those
materials that are missing from the library. On the other hand, SimaPro inherently has a high
barrier to entry due to its complex software layout. This update significantly reduced the time
required for inventory setup, as demonstrated by tests on all our cases. Inventory setup time
refers to the average duration spent configuring the inventory in the tool. By adding an import
option, we have minimised the risk of discrepancies between the Ecoinvent processes and the
LCAbyg processes. These processes are now defined in a library, ensuring consistency across all
future cases. The updated tool includes a separate Excel sheet where practitioners can input
results from the tool. This sheet presents the results divided into all the midpoint impact cat-
egories. The midpoint results are displayed for individual processes, total results, and are also
broken down by LCA phases.
We did not add the network of processes feature due to its complexity. In SimaPro, this feature
allows practitioners to view the underlying processes for specific materials, aiding in under-
standing the causes of impacts and improving result interpretation and recommendations. An

4.1
Literature synthesis and study contribution
The impact on the PB is highlighted across four LCIA methods, which is one of the unique
contributions of this study, making LCIA methods comparable at the midpoint and endpoint
levels. PB transgression is seen although the LCAs conducted in this study do not include all five
direct drivers of biodiversity. This means that even with our limited measures of biodiversity we
are still transgressing the PB. The transgression of the PB relies heavily on the SP chosen. This
study doesn’t debate the choice of SP but highlights the impact of selecting a SP. When choosing
SP2 based on the Reduction Roadmap several cases transgress the PB by a significant factor.
Compared to SP1 and SP3, under half of the case studies transgress the PB. The results for SP1
and SP3 are similar because the allocation to the construction sector is nearly the same, despite
differences in the data for the calculation. Due to the similar allocation to the construction
sector, a correlation is seen between expenditure on services and the services’ energy use.
It’s important to recognise that none of the LCIA methods used in our case studies address all
five direct drivers of biodiversity loss. This omission could result in an underestimation of EQ
compared to the PB threshold. ReCiPe, IW+ and LC-Impact address land/water use change,
climate change and pollution, accounting for 52-60% of global biodiversity loss, depending on
terrestrial or aquatic nature IPBES [2019]. With LC-Impact being the only method showing
significant impacts on water use change. Whilst PB-LCIA only addresses land use change and
climate change, which for terrestrial nature accounts for 45%. This means that the current
practice of evaluating off-site biodiversity through LCA is missing a significant portion of con-
tributors to the issue. Across the five buildings, climate change was identified as the largest
contributor to EQ, with land use change as second, except for LC-Impact ranking ecotoxicity
as the second largest contributor.
None of the current methods addresses the issue of overexploitation, even though the driver is es-
timated to contribute 20-28% of biodiversity loss [IPBES, 2019]. The primary actors behind this
driver are the medicinal and pet trade. However, it is imperative to assess the potential impacts
of the construction industry. Activities such as land clearance and selective tree felling associ-
ated with construction projects may exacerbate biodiversity loss by depleting specific ecosystems
and species. The impact of the land use change is accounted for however the overexploitation
of the species itself is not [Crenna et al., 2018]. Similarly, invasive species remain unaddressed
within the LCIA methods, estimated to account for circa 10% of the global biodiversity loss
[IPBES, 2019]. Concerning the construction industry, this oversight could manifest in on-site
biodiversity degradation. The introduction of exotic or alien species carries consequences for
local biodiversity. For off-site biodiversity, the transportation or plantation of specific tree spe-
cies for construction purposes may further threaten biodiversity. Studies in Chile show that
non-native tree plantations impact ecosystem services [Benra et al., 2019]. Forests are experien-
cing a wide variety of invasive species which threaten the quality, applicability, and amount of
Page 21 of 67
==PAGE:29==

wood [Brashaw et al., 2012]. While the exact impact is unknown, it is crucial to evaluate and
understand it.
The LCIA methods evaluate EQ through differing ICs. When quantifying biodiversity into a
single number, the underlying impact models need to be understood. Though it had a minor in-
fluence on the total impact, ReCIPe and LC-Impact are the only methods to evaluate freshwater
affected by climate change. This conveys that the other methods are missing the representativity
of aquatic taxonomic groups in one of the largest direct drivers. ReCiPe, LC-Impact and IW+
operate on similar impact models for terrestrial acidification from Roy et al. [2014] and Roy
et al. [2012], yet their relative contribution was differing in the case studies. IW+ furthermore
include impact models on aquatic acidification, which contributed up to 32% to the acidification
category in the case studies and includes impacts on multiple aquatic species (see SM 6.4.1).
The time horizon selected for an IC also has a significant impact. For example, ecosystems might
regenerate after land abandonment, whereas CO2 emissions persist in the atmosphere for cen-
turies [Hanafiah et al., 2012]. These findings highlight the necessity of comparing impact models
across various LCIA methods to understand their coverage, providing a more solid foundation
for selecting an appropriate LCIA method for a specific purpose.
Including various taxonomic groups allows for a more holistic understanding of ecosystem health.
Failure to incorporate the diversity across taxonomic groups may lead to overlooking significant
ecological disturbances and underestimating the extent of biodiversity loss. The representation
of taxonomic groups varies between the methods, with PB-LCIA having the least coverage
whereas IW+ and LC-Impact have the largest coverage (see Table 17). While some groups
such as mammals, birds, and arthropods receive considerable attention across these methods,
others like annelids, echinoderms, and algae are only found in IW+ and LC-Impact. None of the
methods includes sharks and rays, or reef corals, which are among the most threatened species
[IUCN, 2023]. This discrepancy underscores the need for greater inclusivity in LCIA methods
to accurately reflect biodiversity dynamics. Furthermore, the taxonomic groups are weighted
”equally” across the ICs, but is this representative compared to the IUCN Redlist? LC-Impact
stands out from the other methods by applying a vulnerability factor to the ICs. This is based on
the IUCN Redlist, weighting highly threatened species higher than non-threatened species. Even
though this may introduce bias, an existing bias is present with equal weighting of taxonomic
groups. For instance, eutrophication might appear to have less impact when taxa are weighted
equally.
Conversely, this could lead to the oversight of significant threats if eutrophication
disproportionately affects the most threatened species.
These considerations show that a single number for biodiversity is insufficient for all purposes.
It can’t highlight individual species that are under threat and should be used in conjunction
with other indicators such as the IUCN Redlist [Scholes and Biggs, 2005]. Nonetheless, it is still
helpful for comparison, showing general trends and making it relatable to the PBs.

4.2
Recommendations
This paper endorses the initial use of ReCiPe but recommends adopting more spatialised LCIA
methods for the construction sector through updated European Standards, as more product-
specific data become available. The advantage of ReCiPe, as the foundation, is its accessibility,
and its connection to the PB presents fewer uncertainties than e.g. IW+. However, it also has its
limitations. LC-Impact and IW+ offer the most spatialised methods leading to more product-
specific results. When assessing the IC of land use, it’s important to note that ReCiPe and PB-
LCIA operate on global CFs. In contrast, IW+ and LC-Impact consider 867 ecoregions based
on Olson Biomes for a more detailed and region-specific analysis. Since land use significantly
impacts the results, it’s important to prioritise LCIA methods with higher spatial resolution
for impact categories that have larger effects. These categories will substantially influence the
results when spatialised LCI is available. This level of specificity is, however, not achievable
with the existing background processes in current software. The absence of product-specific
data masks significant differences between the products [Francart et al., 2024]. EPDs provide
this, however, a midpoint to endpoint conversion is necessary to convert it to impacts on EQ.
This could in theory be done through the midpoint to endpoint CFs provided within ReCiPe
however using another LCIA method’s CFs introduces new uncertainties. Several of the impact
categories in EPDs need a unit conversion before the midpoint to endpoint CFs from ReCiPe
could be used. A study attempting to make conversion factors between different LCIA methods’
midpoint categories has been done by Dong et al. [2021], but showed several uncertainties.
Furthermore, the evaluation of land use is not required to be published in the current standard.
Neglecting it is inadequate, as we found this IC to be one of the most severe ICs.
The case studies are buildings aiming for high sustainability scores through DGNB. Due to their
transgression of the PB for biosphere integrity, it is recommended that certification schemes ini-
tially incorporate specific requirements concerning off-site biodiversity, with the aim of eventually
integrating these into regulations. This study indicates that buildings with wooden load-bearing
constructions tend to exceed the planetary boundary for biosphere integrity more than build-
ings with concrete load-bearing constructions. This is primarily due to the extensive land use
required for forestry. While the building sector increasingly uses wood because of its gener-
ally lower carbon footprint compared to mineral resources, this study highlights the potentially
higher impact of biogenic materials on biodiversity compared to mineral materials.
This finding should not discourage the use of biogenic materials but rather encourage the in-
dustry to optimise forestry.
For example, clear-cutting forestry, which is common, is often
criticised for its severe impact on biodiversity. In contrast, close-to-nature or selective forestry
practices are argued to be more beneficial to biodiversity [Seedre et al., 2018]. When evaluating
the load-bearing sawnwood in Figure 4 through a network of processes, wood from Germany
had a smaller impact on EQ compared to wood from Sweden. However, when checking the ele-
mentary flows for the spatialised LCIA methods (IW+ and LC-Impact), Sweden has lower CFs
for land use compared to Germany. Implementing more spatialised elementary flows is therefore

recommended for a more correct and nuanced picture. Though only being able to assess this on
a country scale, it is still the first step towards more informed decision-making.
Lastly, this study also emphasises the positive effects of recycling or reusing materials, akin
to the reduction in CO2 footprint. Therefore it is recommended to reduce virgin materials in
construction projects, as this will reduce the impact on multiple PBs.
The introduction of the doughnut-BIOTOOL has initiated a focus on evaluating off-site biod-
iversity impacts in the construction sector. Our enhancements to the tool have significantly
reduced the required time while providing a more nuanced understanding through various ICs.
Highlighted as an improvement opportunity by the authors [Francart et al., 2024]. Utilising
LCAByg has streamlined the workflow, making it more accessible for the Danish industry,
though it remains limited for international users. For a more extensive national implementa-
tion, a material library that links LCAByg’s processes to EQ is suggested to be implemented by
the Danish building regulation. While we have implemented this on a small scale for our case
studies, suggesting a path, considerable amounts of products are still missing compared to the
inventory in LCAByg.
4.3
Future research needs
This study found that there is currently no link between LCIA and the PB framework’s bio-
sphere integrity used in the industry, and it is rarely addressed in the literature. In the PB 2023
iteration, Human Appropriation of Net Primary Production (HANPP) emerged as a more com-
prehensive metric for biosphere integrity compared to BII [Richardson et al., 2023]. However,
the incorporation of HANPP into LCA and software presents challenges, requiring modifications
to accommodate spatially explicit data [N´u˜nez et al., 2013] and has not yet been implemented in
operational LCIA. An LCIA method, developed by Taelman et al. [2016] directly links HANPP
to CFs for land use. However, the authors conclude that the spatial detail in current data-
bases, such as Ecoinvent, is insufficient to fully support their LCIA method. Allocating more
resources to improve this, through e.g. GIS data, could not only facilitate the implementation
of HANPP in LCIAs but also enhance the accuracy of current LCIA methods. Alvarenga et al.
[2015] developed an LCIA method that measures land use impacts on net primary production
but concludes that this is an impact category poorly understood in the LCIA context.
In the safe and just ESB framework from 2023 two new control variables are suggested based on
the Earth system nature’s contribution to people (NCP), one of the safe ESBs is set at ”50-60%
of global land surface covered by largely intact natural areas” (Rockstr¨om et al., 2023, p. 105)
while the other is ”20–25% diverse seminatural habitat including native species in each square
kilometre in human-modified lands” (Rockstr¨om et al., 2023, p. 106). This framework is a recent
study, and these control variables are not yet adequately described in the LCIA literature. To
implement the first control variable, LCI databases must include accurate data on land use types
and their intactness relative to a stable state. In operational LCIA, the biodiversity aspect of

ecosystems is currently not included, which we believe is essential for addressing this control
variable. The second control variable concerns diverse seminatural habitats, including native
species in human-modified lands. Implementing this variable in operational LCIA would require
the inclusion of invasive species, making it possible to differentiate between native and invasive
species in a certain area.
We use MSA in PB-LCIA as a directly comparable unit to BII, well-known MSA differs slightly
from BII as it treats all hectares in an area equally [Alkemade et al., 2009]. PB-LCIA’s impact
model for EQ lacks contributions from ICs other than climate change and land use [Gal´an-
Mart´ın et al., 2021]. This introduces significant uncertainties in the final results, especially for
processes related to minerals or chemicals, which are significantly impacted by ICs related to
pollution such as acidification. Thus leading to an underestimation of the impact. Furthermore,
BII assesses the taxa of plants, mammals, birds, reptiles and amphibians [Scholes and Biggs,
2005], to which the latter two are missing in the PB-LCIA method, unlike the rest of the assessed
methods. The direct comparability to BII eliminates uncertainties from converting units, clearly
linking it to the PBs. This is not the case for the other methods, for which a unit conversion is
needed.
ReCiPe, IW+, and LC-Impact use different units for EQ that require conversions to be com-
parable with the PB biosphere integrity. However, they all fundamentally rely on PDF as their
underlying measure and this paper suggests methods for converting these units to BII. We apply
the conversion method established by Gabor Doka in PBA’07 (draft version), which calculates
the ratio between PDF and BII. Using the median value, we overlook the specific land areas
dominating our LCI, which could introduce uncertainties compared to using the specific land
types in the case study. The land type with the lowest ratio is urban areas (0.46 PDF/BII),
whereas pasture and meadow have the highest ratio (0.825 PDF/BII) [de Baan et al., 2013;
Schipper et al., 2016]. The impact of using the median value instead of the specific ratios for
each land type in the inventory could be a significant factor to consider for future research.
ReCiPe is the only method that sets an absolute number in the unit species.years by multiplying
PDF with a species density. This species density is based on data from UNEP’s GEO 2000
[Goedkoop et al., 2008]. IUCN has since updated the total number of described species to over
2.1 million, which has not yet been reflected in ReCiPe [IUCN, 2023]. This discrepancy could
introduce uncertainties and future research could highlight the impact of new species densities.
To calculate the PB threshold for ReCiPe, we apply the same species density as in the ReCiPe
method to avoid additional uncertainties.
The conversion for IW+ requires the division of terrestrial natural land area.
The method
description of IW+ does not specify the area used, making the unit conversion based on the
same area unattainable [Bulle et al., 2019]. Therefore, we applied the terrestrial natural land
area from ReCiPe [Goedkoop et al., 2008]. This introduces an unwanted uncertainty, making
the unit conversion based on another method, however same conversion has been used in the

be interpreted with caution.
The unit conversion from PDF.m3.d to PDF.yr for ecotoxicity in LC-Impact introduces uncer-

the total impact on EQ, diverging from the other methods. Similar outcomes have been reported

water ecotoxicity. The LC-Impact report states that uncertainties arise due to metals having
relatively high CFs because of their long residence times in marine water compartments [Verones
et al., 2020]. Furthermore, the CFs for marine ecotoxicity are extrapolated from freshwater eco-

among the methods, consistently concluding that aluminium in the subcompartment groundwa-

specify subcompartment levels, which seemed to be the issue. Therefore to fix the issue, the CF
for aluminium in the water compartment was set to zero, to remove all subcompartments. This
was done to keep ecotoxicity in the assessment.
Upon assessing ReCiPe and IW+ with their long-term models, similar outcomes to the initial
LC-Impact implementation emerge. This suggests that the CFs from USEtox, the model used
for assessing ecotoxicity in LC-Impact, may be long-term (>100 years). These findings indicate
the need for further investigation, particularly due to the significant impact on the results of
LC-Impact.

5
Conclusion
This thesis has explored the complex relationship between the building sector and biodiversity,
emphasising the need for a holistic assessment of off-site biodiversity impacts. This research
compared four LCIA methods—ReCiPe 2016, Impact World+, LC-IMPACT, and PB-LCIA
against case studies of Danish buildings. Biodiversity is currently practically assessed in LCIA
methods through eight impact categories - global warming, water use, ecotoxicity, acidifica-
tion, eutrophication, land use, photochemical ozone formation, and ionising radiation. These
IC manage to asses three of the five direct drivers of biodiversity loss - land/water use, global
warming, and pollution. Climate change and land use were highlighted as major contributors,
however, other impact categories showed non-negligible impacts, suggesting the need for more
comprehensive coverage. This study furthermore demonstrated how the buildings transgress
the PB by linking the four LCIA methods’ ecosystem quality endpoint category and the PB
for biosphere integrity. The link to the PB is currently restricted to BII, as more recent bio-
sphere integrity measurements are not yet applicable. Quantifying biodiversity through a single
number is currently challenging since certain aspects are not integrated into LCIA. Additional
nonoperational LCIA methods were examined, showing potential for future methods to cover
all direct drivers of biodiversity loss, including a wider range of taxonomic groups, and assess
genetic and ecosystem diversity.
The shift towards using biogenic materials, such as wood, in the building sector, driven by their
lower carbon footprint, has an inattentive impact on biodiversity. The case studies revealed that
while wooden constructions typically have a lower climate change impact, they often result in
higher biodiversity impacts due to extensive land use. In contrast, mineral-based materials, such
as concrete, show impacts related to pollution and climate change but have a relatively lower
impact on biodiversity. LC-Impact showed a more nuanced picture, highlighting multiple ICs
contributing to biodiversity loss, concluding that a concrete construction had the highest impact,
unlike the rest of the methods concluding that a wooden construction was the most harmful.
These insights lead to the conclusion to have a balanced approach in material selection, together
with avoiding the use of virgin materials if possible and optimising the extraction of materials.
The doughnut-BIOTOOL, utilising ReCiPe, was assessed for its effectiveness in evaluating off-
site biodiversity impacts. While the tool provides a user-friendly interface and integrates well
with existing LCA software like LCAByg, it requires further development to enhance its accuracy
and usability. The improvements made during this research, such as automating data imports
and adding midpoint impact categories, have made the tool more accessible and effective. How-
ever, for a truly nuanced picture of biodiversity impact, it is crucial to employ multiple methods.
Here, product-specific data becomes the next frontier. While IW+ and LC-Impact offer more
spatially explicit data, a key factor considering biodiversity’s regional nature, current LCI can-
not fully utilise it. The ability to incorporate product-specific data would significantly improve
off-site biodiversity measurements through LCA and inform more sustainable decision-making.

6.1
Introduction
6.1.1
Initiatives to limit CO2 emissions in the buildings sector.
In 2018 the World Green Building Council (WorldGBC) launched a commitment called the
”Net Zero Carbon Buildings Commitment” to tackle operational carbon emissions and in 2021
this was updated to include embodied carbon [WorldGBC, 2021]. The commitment set several
requirements for building emissions for 2030 underlining the importance of immediate action for
reducing climate impacts. In January of 2023, BR18 introduced a requirement to conduct an
LCA for all buildings over 1000 m2 with a maximum threshold of 12 kg CO2-eq/m2/year [BR18,
nd]. With the new requirement, a voluntary low-emission class was introduced concurrently
with a maximum threshold of 8 kg CO2-eq/m2/year. As one of the latest initiatives, Reduction
Roadmap has translated the emission thresholds of buildings to the planetary boundaries (PBs)
and the Paris Agreement, setting a target emission for 2029 at 0.4 kg CO2-eq/m2/year [Reduction
Roadmap, 2023]. Reduction Roadmap highlights the need for much stricter thresholds for the
construction sector to stay within the PBs from the 2015 framework [Steffen et al., 2015]. Please
note that the PB framework was updated by [Richardson et al., 2023] in September 2023.
However, Reduction Roadmap has not yet implemented these boundaries due to the time of
the publication. These are just a few of the initiatives and regulations in the building industry
aimed at reducing carbon emissions from the sector.
6.2
Method
6.2.1
Description of Ecosystem Quality unit measurements
PDF refers to the concept of potentially disappeared fraction of species [Rossberg, 2022]. The
metric is defined as the proportion of locally extant species that disappear as a result of exposure
to a pressure. PDF is considered reversible once the pressure has ceased. In LC-Impact the EQ
is defined in PDF.yr. This refers to the fraction of species that is committed to become globally
extinct [Verones et al., 2020]. A PDF of 0.01 indicates that 1% of the global species pool might
disappear if the pressure (e.g., land use) persists. The duration of this pressure affects whether
the full impact materializes or not. Hence, the exposure duration to the pressure is integrated
into the unit of ecosystem impacts, denoted as PDF.yr. Therefore, it should not be interpreted
as an immediate global loss but rather as a risk unfolding over time. In Impact-World+ the
EQ unit is defined in PDF.m2.yr, this reads as a potentially disappeared fraction of species in a
dedicated area over a certain period of time [Bulle et al., 2019]. The unit can be interpreted in
multiple ways, for example, 10 PDF.m2.yr can be read as: 10 m2 has lost all of its species over
a year, 100 m2 has lost 10% of its species of a year, 10 m2 has lost 10% of its species over 10
years [Quanties, 2021]. In the context of the land-use impact category, this unit denotes that
damage increases with an increase in area size, an increase in occupation time or an increase in
Page 35 of 67
==PAGE:43==

restoration time for a previously converted area [Goedkoop and Spriensma, 2001]. Additional
explanatory steps are necessary for impact categories related to pollution, such as ecotoxicity,
acidification, and eutrophication. Consider a scenario with a constant flow of x kilograms per
year per m2. This flow leads to a stable concentration of y on a m2. In life cycle assessment
(LCA), we lack information about the flow itself, only possessing data about the mass, which
can be interpreted as a flow over a specific time period, denoted as t. Consequently, a mass can
only contribute to a concentration of y on a m2 during that specific time period. Since damage
can be associated with concentration, the flow can only be correlated with particular damage
within a specific area over a certain time period [Goedkoop and Spriensma, 2001].
Species.yr is a combination of absolute species loss at a local, regional, and global scale integ-
rated over time [Verones et al., 2020]. The calculation from midpoint to endpoint in ReCiPe 2016
involves PDF, but adds an additional step converting the unit to species.yr [Huijbregts et al.,
2017]. For the IC Climate change, PDF is multiplied with the total surface of (semi)-natural
areas of the world (m2) and average species density (species.m2) for terrestrial categorisation.
Likewise for freshwater, with the total water volume of different river basins (m3) and the fresh-
water species density (species.m3).
HANPP is an indicator that measures the intensity of land use [Taelman et al., 2016; Saikku
and Mattila, 2017]. HANPP measures the human appropriation of net primary production.
Net primary production is defined as the amount of solar energy converted to biomass via
photosynthesis [Zhang et al., 2010]. The human appropriation is the amount of net primary
production that is altered or harvested by humans thus not readily available for ecosystems.
HANPP is measured in the unit of C year-1 [Richardson et al., 2023; Saikku and Mattila, 2017].
MSA refers to Mean Species Abundance and ranges from 0 to 1, with 1 representing a fully
untouched species accumulation, and 0 being all original species are extinct [Rossberg, 2022].
MSA calculates on a reference point, i.e. preindustrial species abundance, and compares this to
the abundance of species under the influence of a given pressure. The reference point determines
which species are being included in the comparison. As a result, new species not present during
the reference period are not considered, and an increase in the abundance of individual species
beyond the reference point is not possible under this method. In the PB-LCIA method for
quantifying biosphere integrity, Gal´an-Mart´ın et al. [2021] build on the work of Hanafiah et al.
[2012] and quantifies the indicator as loss of MSA (1 - MSA).

6.2.4
Update of database
During this study, two versions of the Ecoinvent database were used for LCI data. Initially,
version 3.9.1 was used because version 3.10 was not yet available. After version 3.10 was released,
we updated the LCI database accordingly. While using Ecoinvent 3.9.1, we discovered an error
related to the CF for water use in the LCI when using LC-Impact, leading to negative values
for certain materials, such as bricks. The updated version, Ecoinvent 3.10, corrected most of
these errors. However, some waste processes, like waste aluminium, still show negative values
for water use with LC-Impact. This issue could result in underestimation or errors when using
the LC-Impact method.
