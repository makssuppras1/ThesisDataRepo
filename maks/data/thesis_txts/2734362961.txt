Abstract
The increased use of data demands more data processing and storage than ever
before. Therefore, IT companies are choosing to invest in data centers around the
globe to meet this growing need. Danish soil proves to be an attractive location for
constructing and operating large data centers, partly due to the politically stable
framework conditions, low electricity prices, and the high proportion of renewable
energy (RE) in the power grid. Denmark must be prepared for the rising number
of data centers to require vast amounts of energy and have a significant impact on
the Danish energy community. The combination of data centers and latent cooling
has been recognized as a promising approach to enhancing energy efficiency and
sustainability in data center operations.
This thesis explores a detailed comparison of three prototype systems using Phase
Change Material (PCM) storage with immersed heat exchangers, tested in the
COOLôÙDATA research project.
Rather than conducting new experiments, this
study analyzes existing data from laboratory tests on these prototypes. The core
focus was on investigating important thermal characteristics, such as the heat
transfer coefficient (UA value), thermal power per unit volume, and how consisôÙ
tently these factors perform over time.
The analysis reveals that Prototype 1 while exhibiting moderate performance,
didnãt have stable performance over the melting and solidification phases. ProtoôÙ
type 2, despite showing high thermal power during melting, face challenges with
prolonged solidification times and limited cycle stability of its salt hydrate PCM,
raising concerns about its longôÙterm efficiency. Prototype 3, on the other hand,
demonstrates a more stable and constant UA value and power curves, indicating
a potentially more reliable and consistent performance over time. Additionally,
the incorporation of latent heat storage, as shown in Prototype 3, significantly
enhances the stored heat capacity, offering a higher energy density and reducing
the required storage size. This aspect is particularly beneficial in applications with
limited space, such as small server rooms, highlighting the practical implications
of PCMôÙbased TES systems in realôÙworld settings.
The complexity of phase change phenomena necessitates the development of
simple models for latent storage in systems design. The study also develops sepôÙ
arate regression models for Prototypes 2 and 3 to predict temperature differences
during phase change processes, which shows great prediction capabilities.

Introduction
The Danish Energy Agency expects energy consumption in the service sector to
increase by around 56 percent from 2019 to 2035. The increase in the service
sectorãs energy consumption is mainly due to the construction and operation of
data centers [1].
Denmark is an attractive location to build and run data centers, due to low electricôÙ
ity prices and the high share of renewable energy (RE) in the electricity grid, where
46 percent of the total electricity production comes from Wind turbines [2]. One
of the main challenges imposed by high shares of renewable power production is
to design future energy systems to be able to balance high shares of fluctuating
power and to achieve efficient use of the energy available. Therefore, a great
focus on how to use energy smartly is on the agenda.
Thermal energy storage (TES) is a key technology that can improve the utilization
of energy, leading to reductions in energy costs related to the operation of building
air conditioning systems. The idea is based on storing thermal energy at nighttime
during low electric energy cost periods to later use in the buildingãs air conditioning
system during daytime when energy costs are higher [Thermal_energy_book_Ch3].
These cool thermal storage systems also find practical applications in data cenôÙ
ters.
This thesis project is an integral part of the CoolôÙData research initiative. CoolôÙ
Data brings together a collaborative team of researchers from DTU1. The CoolôÙ
Data project focuses on flexible cold storage operation as a part of heat, venôÙ
tilation, and air conditioning (HVAC) systems of smallôÙ and mediumôÙsized data
centers [3]. An important component of this flexible cold storage approach is the
utilization of Phase Change Materials (PCM) for cold storage. With the integrated
flexible solution, CoolôÙData aims to significantly reduce the energy need and cost
for cooling data centers and actively participates in minimizing the carbon footprint
of the sector [4].
1.1
Latent storage
Thermal energy storage (TES) is a technology that accumulates thermal energy
by heating or cooling a storage medium so that the stored energy can be used at
a later time for heating and cooling applications. There are three main kinds of
TES systems:
ãÂ Sensible heat storage ôÙ storing thermal energy by heating or cooling mostly
a liquid or solid storage medium (e.g. water, sand, molten salts, rocks),
ãÂ Latent heat storage (LHS) using phase change materials (PCM)

Latent heat refers to the amount of thermal energy that a phaseôÙchange material
(PCM) either absorbs or releases during a phaseôÙchange process, such as from
solid to liquid, or vice versa, at a constant temperature, known as the phaseôÙ
change temperature [5] [7].
As shown in Figure 1.1a LHS combines this energy associated with the phase
change along with sensible heat. The temperature will increase linearly until it
reaches the melting temperature, where the phase change from solid to liquid hapôÙ
pens. After the PCM is liquefied the temperature will again increase linearly, due
to sensible heat. When compared to storage that relies solely on sensible heat,
dashed line in Figure 1.1a, the inclusion of latent heat significantly increases the
stored heat in the storage. This, in turn, leads to higher energy density for PCMôÙ
based systems, reducing the required storage size, which is especially advantaôÙ
geous in applications with limited space [8][9] e.g. in small serve rooms where
space matters.
PCMs can be divided into three main according to the temperature ranges of their
phase transitions: 1) lowôÙtemperature PCMs, with transition below 15 ô¯C, typically
used in air conditioning applications and the food industryëƒ 2) MidôÙtemperature

Another approach to modeling Latent Thermal Energy Storage (LTES) systems inôÙ

errors in temperature predictions during the phase change process. Both modôÙ

designing and analyzing thermal energy storage systems.
P. Dolado et al. [24] focuses on the development of an empirical model based
on the relationship between the inlet air temperature, the air temperature differôÙ

the TES unit. P. Dolado uses the software Orgin Pro 8 to make a surface fitting.

outside the tested range. Whereas numerical models are often used instead.
Researchers often employ a combination of both, leveraging the detailed insights
of numerical models and the practical applicability of empirical models. This hybrid
approach maximizes the strengths of each modeling type, facilitating comprehenôÙ
sive analysis, design, and optimization of LTES systems [27].

During the analysis of experimental data, it was seen that the heat transferred

As the method used also captures the heat loss during the cycle, it can not be
assumed that the heat transfer at the end of the solidification/charging reaches
zero as heat losses in the system will occur both due to ambient temperature and
hysteresis losses. Thus it can not be determined which value it will end on either.

and therefore a temperature correction factor will be used. By adding this temperôÙ

that by using this method, the heat losses it neglected, and only the usable energy
is left. Itãs also important to note that this correction factor assumes the errors are
consistent and can be captured with a linear correction, which may not always
be the case. Itãs important to recognize that it simplifies the complex dynamics of
heat transfer and loss within the system.
As the heat transferred in the last measurement point is expected to be lower than
observed, the area below the measured transferred heat is particularly interesting.
It can be due to errors in the inlet and outlet temperature measurements. To
address this, a temperature correction factor (Tcorr) is introduced. The correction

Further work
Use case and better sensitivity analysis
Continuing the work of this thesis an interesting topping related to this work would
be to define a use case, as properly seen in the introduction the prototypes were
developed with the COOLôÙDATA field. The investigations of the specific energy
demands of such facilities and the potential benefits of the PCM storage protoôÙ
types would have been beneficial to better showcase the actual benefit. This also
leads to the fact, that it would have been beneficial to estimate the best operation
point for the storage. This could have been done by varying the input parameters
to the grey box model based on qualified assumptions from a use case.
Dynamic inlet temperature
One goal was to evaluate the semiôÙblack box model in this Thesis with the COOLôÙ
data Modelica model. This was unfortunately not achieved due to time restrictions.
A key aspect of this comparison involved the integration of a dynamic temperature
function as input to the model instead of a constant inlet temperature. Such inôÙ
tegration would help validate the grey box modelãs efficacy and also demonstrate
its compatibility with the COOLôÙdata PCM storage Modelica model. Future work
should aim at integrating the dynamic temperature function into the model. This
step is crucial for improving how the model responds to actual conditions and for
better testing its accuracy. By doing this, a clearer picture of how PCM storage
systems work in real data centers.
Interval Analysis for Model Simulation
As the modeling framework often doesnãt work in 1ôÙminute intervals, due to comôÙ
putational efficiency. Sometimes simulations are made over a year to determine
e.g. the savings in electricity prices, whereas the computer time increases a lot
if each minute needs to be considered. It could be beneficial to test the simple
grey box model with higher time intervals and how these intervals affect the accuôÙ
racy of transferred heat predictions. This could involve analyzing the sensitivity of
the modelãs output to changes in the time interval. Hereby determining the point
where increased temporal resolution no longer yields significant improvements in
prediction accuracy.
Energy losses
In this study, energy losses and uncertainties in storage were overlooked due to
the methodology chosen to address the assumption of systematic data errors. A
more precise approach would have involved accounting for energy losses using
the heat loss coefficient identified in previous studies [20] [18]. Additionally, invesôÙ
tigating energy loss due to the hysteresis effect might have reduced the reliance
on temperature correction factors. This oversight extends to the uncertainties in
experimental measurements, which were not thoroughly examined. Conducting
a sensitivity analysis on these measurements could have provided insights into
how variations in key parameters affect the outcomes, offering a more reliable
interpretation of the data.
