Abstract
This thesis explores the reduction of peak electromagnetic interference (EMI) in switched
capacitor DC-DC converters by employing spread spectrum Techniques in clock generation.
The research focuses on modulating phase-locked loops (PLLs) to dynamically vary the clock
frequency around a reference value, thereby distributing spectral energy and minimizing
EMI peaks. Traditional fixed frequency clocking systems often exhibit concentrated spectral
components, which lead to electromagnetic compatibility (EMC) violations.
A novel approach is introduced, utilizing a dual-VCO PLL architecture in which a secondary
voltage-controlled oscillator (VCO) is modulated outside the primary PLL feedback loop.
This strategy enhances loop stability compared to conventional modulation methods. Several
oscillator designs were analyzed, with a current-controlled relaxation oscillator identified as
the most suitable candidate for this application. The secondary VCO operates in an open-
loop configuration, modulated using a pseudo-random number generator (PRNG) based on
a linear feedback shift register (LFSR).
Simulation results demonstrate that EMI peaks were successfully reduced by up to 28 dB
with a center modulation of ±18.5% at a 1 MHz reference frequency, with output frequency
deviations within 5%. While the implementation did not meet all design specifications, it
validated the feasibility of spread-spectrum modulation for EMI reduction within practical
constraints. Future work should refine the loop filter design and explore additions to the
modulation schemes to further enhance system performance and stability.

Chapter 1
Introduction
This thesis addresses the challenge of reducing peak electromagnetic interference (EMI) in
switched-capacitor DC-DC converters through the application of spread spectrum techniques
in clock generation. The research investigates the potential of modulating phase-locked loops
(PLLs) to vary the output clock frequency around a reference value, thereby spreading the
clock signal’s spectrum and reducing EMI peaks.
The proposed solution aims to address the limitations of traditional fixed-frequency clocking
systems in switched-capacitor DC-DC converters, which tend to generate high peaks in the
frequency domain, leading to electromagnetic compatibility (EMC) violations.
The scope of the project includes evaluating potential PLL modulation methods, exploring
the impact of different modulation schemes on EMI, and analyzing and implementing these
techniques to achieve peak EMI reduction.

3.1
Switched Capacitor DC-DC Converter
A Switched Capacitor DC-DC Converter (SCC) is a type of power converter that regulates
voltage by switching capacitors between different voltage levels rather than using inductors.
It works by charging and discharging capacitors, where the capacitors are switched between
two or more states. In one state, the capacitors are placed in series, and in the other, they are
placed in parallel. When capacitors are in series, the voltage across each capacitor adds up to
a higher total voltage. When placed in parallel, the total voltage is simply the voltage across
the capacitors. This means that SCC circuits can allow currents to flow in both directions,
making it possible for the converter to function as both a buck and a boost converter.
When using Multi-layer ceramic capacitors (MLC capacitors) the energy density is higher
than inductors [2]. By utilizing this characteristic, SCCs are often smaller than their tradi-
tional inductive converter counterparts. This makes SCCs particularly useful in applications
where compactness and space efficiency are critical.
Switched capacitor converters typically use an array of switches to alternate between the
two or more states. These switches are generally MOSFETs (Metal-Oxide-Semiconductor
Field-Effect Transistors), which can rapidly switch between on and off states, offering the
necessary control for the charging and discharging cycles of the capacitors.
Switched capacitor DC-DC converters are commonly found in low-power electronics such
as mobile devices, hearing aids, and other small-form-factor electronics.
Their ability to
operate without inductors makes them attractive for integration into compact, highly efficient
systems, particularly where space is limited.
SCCs are well suited for integrated circuits, as we discuss in this thesis. [3]
3.2
Electromagnetic Interference
Electromagnetic Interference (EMI) refers to the disturbance caused by electromagnetic waves
that can affect the performance of electrical or electronic equipment.
EMI results from
changes in voltage or current over time, especially sharp voltage or current transitions can
cause spikes at many points in the frequency spectrum. These spikes can cause devices to
malfunction or even permanently damage sensitive components.
The importance of controlling EMI has led to the establishment of numerous regulations
and standards worldwide.
These regulations aim to limit the amount of electromagnetic
emissions that devices can emit and also the level of susceptibility they have to external EMI.
For example, in the European Union, the Electromagnetic Compatibility (EMC) Directive
2014/30/EU [4] mandates that all electronic equipment sold in the region must comply with
specified EMI limits to ensure that it does not interfere with other devices or communication
networks. Similarly, in the United States, the Federal Communications Commission (FCC)
enforces guidelines on electromagnetic emissions, which specifies permissible emission levels
for consumer electronics [5].
There are different types of EMI emissions, broadly categorized into two main types: con-
ducted EMI and radiated EMI. Conducted EMI refers to unwanted waves that travels along
electrical conductors (e.g., power lines or signal cables). Conducted EMI can enter a system
through power supplies or data transmission lines and is typically managed by using filters
[6]. Radiated EMI in contrast refers to electromagnetic waves emitted directly into the air
from a device. This type of EMI propagates through free space, often causing interference to
nearby electronic systems and is often managed by shielding [6], [7].
In this thesis, minimizing electromagnetic interference (EMI) is crucial. High-speed switching
with substantial current can generate significant radiated and conducted emissions. This is
6
==PAGE:19==
particularly relevant for a switched-capacitor converter (SCC), which operates by switching
current at high frequencies.
While both radiated and conducted emissions are concerns,
conducted emissions are the primary issue for SCCs, due to the lack of an inductive element.
Usually it is the magnetic components that lead to a high radiated emission, since there are
non in switched-capacitor converters, they usually don’t have much radiated EMI [3].
Furthermore, the reduction of EMI is not just a regulatory requirement but also an essential
aspect of ensuring the reliability and functionality of electronic systems. Excessive EMI can
lead to performance degradation or malfunction of nearby equipment.

3.3
Control Theory
The systems discussed in this section are assumed to be Linear Time Invariant (LTI) systems.
LTI systems exhibit the properties linearity and time-invariance, which allow them to be
analyzed using Laplace transforms and other standard control theory techniques.
3.3.1
Open Loop Control Systems
An open loop control system operates without feedback. The system processes the input
signal based on predetermined rules, but it does not adjust based on the output. Open loop
systems are often not acceptable in systems which require high accurate as any error due to
a change in temperature or humidity in the system will contribute to an error on the output.
Let the path from input to output be A(s):
A(s) = Y (s)
X(s)
(3.1)
Where X(s) is the input signal in the Laplace domain, and Y (s) is the output signal also in
the Laplace domain.
3.3.2
Closed Loop Control Systems
A closed loop control system incorporates feedback to continuously adjust the output based
on the difference between the desired and actual output. This feedback helps the system to
correct errors and maintain stability.
Let the feedback path be B(s). The open loop is considered:
Hopen(s) = A(s) · B(s)
(3.2)
The transfer function of a closed loop system is given by:
Hclosed(s) =
A(s)
1 + A(s)B(s)
(3.3)
Where A(s) is the forward path transfer function, and B(s) represents the feedback path.
Closed loop systems are more robust to disturbances and changes in the environment com-
pared to open loop systems [10].
3.3.3
Stability
Stability is a critical property of control systems. A stable system produces bounded output
for bounded input. The stability of an LTI system is determined by analyzing the location
of the poles of its transfer function in the complex plane [11].
Completely Stable System
For a system to be completely stable, the poles of the closed loop transfer function must all
lie in the left half of the complex plane, ensuring exponential decay. This corresponds to a
condition where the real part of each pole satisfies σ < 0. The system’s response decreases
over time, leading to a stable and well-behaved output [11].
Conditionally Stable System
Conditionally stable systems remain stable only under certain conditions. Stability depends
on system parameters or external factors, and small changes in these conditions can cause
the system to become unstable [12].
Marginally Stable System
A marginally stable system has poles that lie on the imaginary axis of the complex plane, with
no real component. In such systems, the output does not decay but oscillates indefinitely.
The system remains marginally stable but is sensitive to external disturbances that may drive
it to instability [11].
8
==PAGE:21==
Unstable System
In an unstable system, at least one pole of the transfer function lies in the right half of the
complex plane, with a real part σ > 0. This results in an exponential growth in the system’s
response over time, leading to unbounded output and a loss of control [11].

3.4
PLL Theory
This section introduces the driving principles behind Phase-Locked-loops (PLLs). An overview
is presented where after each block is explained. Then some concerns about PLLs design is
introduced and finally the Laplace domain PLL loop is derived, showing the importance of
the loop filter.
A Phase-Locked-Loop or PLL is a circuit which given an oscillating signal can produce another
oscillating signal which are locked in phase relative to the original reference signal. PLLs are
often used to provide a clock signal to a digital circuit. By being able to control the output
frequency by dividing or multiplying the reference signal a clock can be achieved over a wide
range of frequencies.
The simplest PLL design is introduced, this design omits the classically introduced loop filter
and divider. A high level overview of the design can be viewed in fig. 3.1. This design takes
in a reference frequency and outputs an identical frequency.

N divider by only utilizing a Feedback Divider. This however requires a more complicated
setup. By allowing the feedback divider to jump between N and (N + 1) a fractional ratio
can be achieved where the frequency is given by eq. (3.5), where k is the duty cycle, which
is value between 0 and 1 and is proportional to ratio of time spend at N, these solutions are
known as Delta Sigma PLLs [17].
f = N · k + (N + 1) · (1 −k)
(3.5)
The jumping between N and (N + 1) is often clocked and so this is equivalent to modulating
with a square wave (with arbitrary duty cycle). This produces spurious tones, which in the
time domain, can be viewed as an instantaneous phase error. If uncorrected, this error causes
large fractional spurs in fractional-N PLLs. One approach compensates by canceling the error
current in the phase detector/charge pump, but maintaining accuracy across voltage, process,
and temperature is challenging. Another method uses an analog delay to align the output
with the ideal response, though it introduces phase noise. While both techniques reduce
spurs, each has its drawbacks. [17].
Alternative methods of reducing spurs are the implemented in Delta Sigma PLLs, which aim
to reduce spurs. This often involves two other techniques, first of which are to not modulate
by a square wave, but instead by a pseudo random hop, which involves not only jumping
between N and (N + 1), but also higher differences like (N −1) and (N + 2). The constant
integer K in the range (N −K +1) to (N +K) is dubbed the Modulator Order. This method
has a fixed repeating sequence. Another technique for reducing sub-fractional spurs in delta-
sigma PLLs is dithering. Instead of applying a fixed repeating sequence, dithering allows for
a random (more likely pseudo-random) sequence for transitioning [17].

3.4.1
Type 1 Phase Detector
A Type 1 phase detector compares two input signals and produces an output, typically in
the form of pulses. The duty cycle of these pulses is generally proportional to the phase
difference between the signals. It is important to note that a phase detector compares phase,
not frequency. As a result, a PLL using a Type 1 phase detector might lock onto a signal at
a multiple of the reference frequency.
A Type 1 phase detector can be implemented using a single XOR gate.
However, this
implementation requires careful consideration in the design of the loop filter, as the Type 1
phase detector produces only positive pulses in its output [18].
3.4.2

phase and frequency. The phase detector presented here utilizes a Charge pump to store the
analog state. This type of phase detector is often referred to as a phase frequency detector

frequency” or ”decrease frequency” output.
The charge pump integrates the phase error and is used in the PFD to produce this discrete
”increase frequency” or ”decrease frequency” signal. The charge pump only integrates when
these signals are active. It consists of a capacitor and two switched current sources, which is

Frequency Jitter
Frequency jitter refers to the short-term variations in the frequency of the output signal of
a Phase-Locked Loop (PLL). These variations can occur due to several factors, including
noise in the circuit components, fluctuations in power supply, and environmental conditions.
Jitter can be classified into two main types: deterministic jitter, which is predictable and
can be caused by periodic disturbances, and random jitter, which is due to unpredictable
noise sources. It should be noticed that the loop filter design and frequency jitter are highly
correlated [16].
Frequency jitter is considered a performance metric. Minimizing frequency jitter is often an
13

advantage in the design of PLLs. Although for EMI reduction purposes it is common to try
and maximize the jitter [22].
Deadzone
Once the Voltage-Controlled Oscillator (VCO) gets close to matching the reference signal, the
feedback in the phase detector can become very fast, resulting in a condition where the output
of the phase detector is too small to effectively drive the charge pump. In this scenario, the
PLL essentially operates in an open-loop manner, as it cannot make sufficient adjustments
to maintain lock. This phenomenon is known as the dead zone [16].
The presence of a dead zone can significantly impact the performance of the PLL, specifically
frequency jitter is introduced once entering the dead zone.

3.5
Oscillator Topologies
The following sections introduce common oscillator topologies. The goal of this section is to
find a suitable oscillator for a VCO or exclude some options, limiting the number of oscillator
designs to choose from.
3.5.1
Terminology
Harmonic Oscillators
A harmonic oscillator, also referred to as a linear oscillator, is an oscillator whose output
is a sine wave. The system generates a periodic oscillation at a single frequency, where the
frequency is commonly determined by the properties of the inductive and capacitive elements
within the oscillator.
Relaxation Oscillators
Relaxation oscillators, sometimes called non-linear oscillators, generate waveforms such as
square, triangle, or sawtooth waves. These oscillators often rely on the charging and dis-
charging of a capacitor, which causes the system to switch between two states. Relaxation
oscillators are often used in PLLs which output clock signals.
3.5.2
Barkhausen Criterion
The Barkhausen criterion states that for sustained oscillations to occur in a feedback oscil-
lator, the loop gain must be equal to 1 and the total phase shift around the loop must be
360 degrees (or 0 degrees). These conditions ensure that the signal maintains its amplitude
and phase through each cycle of oscillation. The criterion is fundamental in the analysis and
design of oscillators. It should be noted that just because a system satisfies the Barkhausen
criterion does not mean it will necessarily oscillate [25].
3.5.3
Automatic Gain Control
Automatic Gain Control (AGC) is introduced as a critical building block for many oscil-
lators. It is particularly required in harmonic oscillators to stabilize the amplitude of the
oscillations. AGCs are used in harmonic oscillators to regulate the amplitude and prevent
excessive variations in waveform output. The control mechanism works by adjusting the gain
of the oscillator automatically to keep the amplitude within a desired range. [26]
3.5.4
LC Tank Oscillator
The working principle of any LC tank oscillator is to utilize the resonance frequency of an
inductive element combined with a capacitive element. The energy stored in the inductor
and capacitor alternates between electric and magnetic forms, causing the circuit to oscillate
at its natural frequency.
Usually, an amplifier is introduced in a positive feedback loop. The amplifier must observe
the resonance frequency of the LC tank and match its phase response. The closed-loop gain
needs to be greater than 1 to initiate oscillation. However, a gain above 1 would cause the
signal to continuously grow. Therefore, the gain must be limited to a level where it sustains
oscillations without causing runaway growth. This is done using AGC. [27], [28].

4.6.3
EMI Reduction Through Spread Spectrum FM Technology
The article [39] discusses considerations for Spread Spectrum Frequency Modulation (SSFM)
in power converters, focusing on oscillators such as the LTC6909, LTC6902, and LTC6908. It
highlights that periodic modulation functions, such as sine or triangle waves, can introduce
periodic ripple modulation, resulting in significant spectral components at the modulation
frequency. These artifacts may lead to audible tones or disturbances due to power supply noise
coupling or limited rejection in downstream circuitry. To mitigate such issues, pseudo-random
frequency modulation is proposed as an effective alternative to avoid periodic fluctuations.
The article notes that most switching regulators are capable of tolerating a frequency change
of approximately ±10%. Furthermore, it introduces the concept of frequency modulation
rate, often referred to as the hop rate, and emphasizes the importance of controlling dF
dt
(the rate of change of frequency). A high dF
dt may exceed the tracking capabilities of the
phase-locked loop (PLL), causing instability.
For electromagnetic interference (EMI) tracking, techniques such as peak detection, quasi-
peak detection, or average detection are commonly used. The article points out that capturing
instantaneous emissions would require unlimited bandwidth, which is unrealistic for practi-
cal systems. Instead, it asserts that if the clock frequency changes more rapidly than the
bandwidth of the receiver, significant interference reduction can be achieved.
However, the article cautions against excessive frequency hopping rates. If the hopping rate
surpasses the bandwidth of the switching regulator, it can result in output spikes at the
transition edges of the clock frequency. This effect is particularly pronounced in regulators
with lower bandwidths. To address this, the LTC6908 and LTC6909 incorporate proprietary
tracking filters to smooth frequency transitions, while the LTC6902 employs an internal 25
kHz low-pass filter. These filters are designed to optimize the transition smoothing across
various frequencies and modulation rates.
The article also notes that while filtered modulated signals may be acceptable for many logic
systems, the impact of period-to-period jitter must be carefully evaluated.
Additionally, the LTM4608A power converter is mentioned as being capable of providing a
frequency spread of up to ±30%. Other methods for reducing EMI, such as slowing down
sharp internal clock edges and implementing internal filtering, are also discussed.

Literature Study Discussion and Conclusion
The discussed VCO implementations provide valuable insights into oscillator design, high-
lighting key trade-offs and limitations. LC-based oscillators are predominantly used in GHz-
range applications due to the significant area requirements at lower frequencies. At lower
frequencies inductor-less designs offer a distinct advantage. Regarding phase noise, [28] notes
that low-phase-noise relaxation oscillators benefit from an RC operating principle. Resistor
noise, in this case, introduces a negligible flicker component, resulting in improved phase
noise performance.
Ring oscillators, while advantageous for compact designs, often suffer from performance
degradation under process, voltage, and temperature (PVT) variations [40]. The primary
sources of oscillation frequency inaccuracy include supply voltage fluctuations, resistor and
capacitor variations, timing noise, comparator offset/delay, and temperature changes. Fur-
thermore, [40] highlights the challenge of achieving a high figure of merit (FOM) in state-of-
the-art relaxation oscillators operating in the sub-100 µW range. The dominant noise sources
in these designs are attributed to comparator flicker noise and resistor thermal noise.

region 2.5V to 5V the relationship found to be quite linear.

5.5
Synthesis Discussion
When selecting an oscillator for frequency modulation applications, several trade-offs must be
considered. While harmonic oscillators (such as the Wien-bridge and phase-shift designs) offer
stable sinusoidal outputs, they are less straightforward to tune over a wide frequency range. In
addition, translating the sinusoidal output to a perfectly 50% duty-cycle square wave requires
additional circuitry that can add complexity and overhead without any benefit. Harmonic
oscillators also typically require amplitude stabilization components (e.g., automatic gain
control), which can introduce signal distortion or limit the tuning range.
Relaxation oscillators, such as the CD4046B-based design, are often more suitable for applica-
tions requiring wide-range frequency control. Rather than adjusting the resistor or capacitor
values, the oscillator’s frequency is controlled by a current that charges an internal capacitor.
This approach is inherently more flexible for creating a VCO over a broad tuning range. A
ring oscillator could also be used, but the measured simulations indicated non-ideal duty
cycles and potential non-linearity unless carefully engineered. A differential ring oscillators
might show more promising results, and seems to be a common solution for relaxation oscil-
lators [30], [31] and [32].
Regarding EMI mitigation, the choice of modulation scheme significantly affects peak power
levels at specific frequencies. Random frequency hopping yielded the highest attenuation of
peak spectral components in estimations, while also in ideal case providing a flat frequency
spectrum.
The open loop method shows promising results, being able keep the VCOs in phase.
5.6
Solution
Based on the evaluations presented, the most suitable oscillator solution for a frequency-
modulated clock generator appears to be a relaxation-based design. A VCO similar to the
CD4046B architecture provides the following advantages:
• A wide tuning range via current or voltage control.
• A self-starting relaxation mechanism that reliably reaches oscillation.
• A simpler path to generating a 50% duty-cycle.
In terms of modulation, the random frequency-hopping strategy demonstrated the greatest
suppression of the dominant spectral peaks.
However, it also introduces implementation
complexity, particularly in generating and managing pseudo-random frequency steps while
maintaining system lock. A method for managing the large modulation, which is expected
to be in the ±40%−45% region, is presented. The method is to modulate a secondary VCO,
while keeping the primary VCO unmodulated, this way the primary can be stable in feedback
without being effected by the modulation on the secondary VCO.

6.1
Implementation overview
In this project, significant EMI modulation is required. Traditionally, EMI reduction through
clock frequency modulation is achieved by modulating the VCO within the feedback loop.
This approach imposes strict demands on the loop filter, requiring additional design consider-
ations for ensuring stability. However, due to the extreme frequency range requirement with
a ratio of ±40 −45%, designing such a loop filter is theorized to be non-trivial. To overcome
this limitation, a novel approach is proposed and implemented.
The proposed method decouples the VCO in the feedback loop from the EMI modulation. The
primary objective is to relax the loop filter requirements and enhance system stability. This
is accomplished by introducing a secondary VCO that operates in an open-loop configuration
while being matched to the primary VCO in the feedback loop. EMI modulation is applied

6.3.1
Alternate Loop Filter Operation
An experiment is conducted to address some of the challenges posed by the large frequency
span, specifically regarding the loop filter’s inability to operate over such a wide frequency
span. The primary idea is to divide the frequency range into factors of 2, thereby reducing
the operating range for the PLL. For example, the PLL could operate in a region such as
500 kHz to 2 MHz. If the frequency dropped below 500 kHz, a divider circuit would scale
the output, allowing the PLL to continue functioning in the 500 kHz to 2 MHz range while
outputting frequencies below 500 kHz. This is a common technique in frequency synthesis.
However, the novel aspect here lies in using a reference frequency that dynamically sets the
target frequency.
Typically, PLLs operate at a fixed frequency, which is then divided or
multiplied in integer steps via circuits in the output or feedback path.
In this case, the
PLL needs to adapt to an input reference spanning from 20 kHz to 1 MHz, introducing the
additional complexity of detecting when to switch the divider settings.
During the experiment, the CD4046B VCO implementation was in use. To achieve the full
range of 20 kHz to 1 MHz, the VCO output is divided up or down to match the reference
signal. A Schmitt trigger is used to detect whether the signal requires division adjustments. It
is found that the Schmitt trigger in the chosen process triggers at 1.5 V and 3.5 V. Figure 6.9
illustrates the control voltage versus output frequency for the CD4046B VCO, with theoretical
operating regions marked at 400 kHz, 1.4 MHz, and 3 MHz corresponding to 1.5 V, 2.5 V,
and 3.5 V, respectively.

The classical CD4046B implementation had problems operating at lower frequencies, where
slew rate violations could cause uncontrolled high frequencies oscillations. A Schmitt trigger
is introduced to ensure that the slew rate requirements of the inverter are met. The modified
design achieves greater operating range, as its theoretical lower limit is0Hz.
The inverter chain is rearranged to include a D-flip-flop, allowing the VCO to operate with
an inverted control signal. This adjustment was found to improve linearity and was therefore
chosen for this design.
The VCO design utilizes an H-bridge configuration to charge and discharge a capacitor. The
corresponding charge and discharge waveforms are illustrated in fig. 6.13. In this configura-
tion, one side of the H-bridge is held at VDD, while the other side discharges the capacitor via
the control current. When the discharging side reaches the trigger point of the Schmitt trig-
ger, the XOR gate activates, enabling the AND gate to produce a pulse. This pulse clocks the
D-flip-flop, which flips the charging and discharging sides, resetting the cycle. This operation
results in a 50% duty cycle square wave, where the frequency is linearly proportional to the
control current. The capacitor’s value is chosen to be high enough that parasitic capacitance
has only a minor effect. Increasing the size of the capacitor also increases power consumption.
The capacitor’s value was chosen to be roughly 1pF. The H-bridge transistors’ drive strength
must be high enough so that they do not influence the current, they should act like switches.
Since they are operated digital they do not require to be especially big and finding a suitable
W/L ratio is trivial.

6.6
Dual VCO Modulation Scheme
A novel method for modulating a PLL is investigated, focusing on the use of a dual VCO
configuration. This approach explores whether an open-loop VCO, when closely matched to
a VCO in feedback, can maintain the same frequency. Such a setup allows the open-loop
VCO to be modulated significantly without impacting the stability of the closed-loop system.
A current mirror is used to modulate the secondary VCO relative to the reference VCO
Exploratory analysis revealed that, without modulation, the two matched VCOs, one in open-
loop and the other in closed-loop can maintain identical frequencies. A phase shift of 45◦
was observed between the two VCO outputs. This is likely due to shared VDD and ground
connections, which create a soft coupling between the VCOs. This coupling behaves like
a semi-closed-loop, where one VCO offsets its power draw in response to the other. This
finding suggested that the concept might also work in a modulated environment. However,
further tests revealed that this setup did not exhibit the same behavior under modulation,
though it may be feasible with additional tuning and reduced modulation levels. The primary
reason for the discrepancy between the unmodulated and modulated VCO is likely due to
mismatches in the turn-on and turn-off times of the switches during modulation.
These
mismatches cause an offset in the modulated VCO relative to the unmodulated VCO. There
is also a consideration with the PRNG, as it might increase the frequency by toggling the
signals between clock-edges.
The dual scheme is able to increase the stability in the closed-loop as seen in fig. 6.20, where
the red signal is with the modulation in the feedback path and the blue is with the dual
modulation scheme.
This current mirror ensures that the modulation on VCO2 remains proportional to VCO1,
even in regions where the control voltage-to-frequency conversion is not linear.
This is
achieved because the current, rather than the voltage, is mirrored.
The current mirror uses six control bits to modulate the current, with customized ratios that
deviate from the standard 2N. This customization reduces the modulation amplitude, which
is required for operating within the specifications. The current mirror can be observed in

6.8
Area Distribution
The majority of the chip area is occupied by the loop filter, which consists primarily of
large resistors and capacitors. These components are essential for achieving the desired filter
characteristics but impose significant constraints on the overall layout of the analog integrated
circuit.
The resistors in the loop filter are limited to a few Mega-Ohms, as increasing their size would
introduce several challenges. Larger resistances amplify susceptibility to parasitic noise and
perturbations, particularly in a dynamic operating mode.
In this mode, the capacitor is
periodically left unconnected, making it more vulnerable to external noise sources.
This
increased sensitivity can lead to instability or the generation of unwanted spurious signals.
The NMOS at the output of the charge pump is a 500nm, / 160µm long NMOS transistor.
This transistor helps reduce the current flowing into the charge pump, but a low enough
current could not be achieved.
The relative size of the loop-filter compared to the rest of the circuit is shown in fig. 6.30.
Figure 6.30: Physical layout of the components (not connected). The red area highlights the
capacitors and resistors associated with the loop filter. The white space near the lower left
is not empty—it contains the VCO current mirror, which is not visible here due to the small
size of its elements. The total chip area is 300 µm × 325 µm.
Based on this analysis, it can be concluded that the 20 kHz loop filter poses significant
challenges for analog IC design. The large size of the passive components and their sensitivity
to noise and parasitics make it a less viable solution for this application. Further optimization
of the filter design is needed to better accommodate the area and performance constraints of
the analog IC environment.

6.9
Implementation Conclusion
The implementation presented in this work has made substantial progress toward achieving
the design goals, but certain challenges remain unresolved. The dual VCO modulation scheme
was successful in modulating the signal in a manner that achieved modulation percentages
close to the theoretical target. By decoupling the EMI modulation from the feedback loop,
the dual VCO scheme improved overall system stability. However, this solution revealed the
limitations of the loop filter, which struggled to accommodate the low frequency of 20 kHz
due to the large resistor required for the filter’s operation.
The design of the loop filter
was found to be inadequate, as the large resistor significantly impacted the current source
behavior and the system’s stability across process corners.
The use of a secondary open-loop VCO for modulation introduced another complexity, as it
struggled to maintain the desired center frequency. Although the random-hop modulation
technique using a PRNG was implemented successfully, the removal of certain bit config-
urations caused the system to exhibit two or more distinct peaks in the output spectrum.
This is likely due to the fact that the values around the reference signal were not sufficiently
represented in the utilized bit configuration. It is theorized that the current mirror switches
are slower to switch off than on, offsetting the modulated center frequency upward in the
frequency domain.
Despite these challenges, the implementation provided valuable insights into the behavior
of the system and highlighted key areas for future work, particularly concerning the loop
filter design, frequency range coverage, and overall modulation reliability. The simulations
conducted with various frequency references demonstrated that while the system performed
mostly within specifications for each individual parameter, the combination of all specification
ranges led to the system’s inability to accommodate them all. Further optimization is required
to address the limitations at lower frequencies or to find a solution for the frequency offset of
the secondary VCO, which remains an obstacle in achieving the full design specifications.
In summary, while the proposed design and its components, including the dual VCO mod-
ulation scheme and the random-hop modulation approach, show promise, the system’s per-
formance requires significant improvement. Further work is needed to refine the loop filter,
improve modulation accuracy, and ensure stable operation across the entire frequency range.

7.1
Modulation
As presented in chapter 4, several prior studies have demonstrated the efficacy of modulating
phase-locked loops (PLLs) to reduce electromagnetic interference (EMI) peaks by spreading
the clock frequency out over a larger range. The modulation in the presented studies provides
a mono-directional modulation of up to 15%. However, the approach under consideration
employs a bidirectional deviation around a center frequency, resulting in higher effective
modulation percentages. A 25% modulation translates to a +66% and –40% range, while
a 40% nominal modulation yields +133% and –58% offsets. Such extreme modulation was
shown to have diminishing returns on the peak reduction, see fig. 5.22. Additionally, it was
shown that achieving a 40dB reduction is not possible given the results from table 5.1, while
limiting the modulation to ±50%.
One must wonder if the ±50% modulation is even a
reasonable result to aim for. Consider the implications of up modulating the signal by 133%.
Given that there are diminishing returns it might be a better idea to aim for something like
6% or 12.5%, which is comparable with the results presented in chapter 4, achieving a 20 dB
amplitude reduction.
Additionally, it should be noted that applying a lower modulating percentage also lessens the
effect on the filter. One might wonder if operating in open-loop is worth the extra area and
power compared to utilizing a single VCO. In the case of SCC the PLL is a minor circuit
with low power consumption compared to the rest of the chip. Note that the SCC in question
converts many hundreds of Watts [41]. The power consumption of a single VCO is likely not
problematic.
The current implementation effectively performs up modulation, but with the lower frequency
shifted downward to position the center frequency at the desired value. This approach in-
troduces challenges, as any deviation in the modulation percentage results in an inaccurate
center frequency. A more robust alternative would be to explicitly specify up modulation
in the design requirements, which would be a reasonable choice for a Switched Capacitor
Converter (SCC). In this case, the lower frequency could be a fixed ratio in the current
mirror. This way the lower frequency would likely be matched better. If the accuracy of
the modulation is important then the proposed method is likely not an optimal solution.
However if modulation is non-critical the proposed solution might be applicable, especially if
up modulation is chosen instead of center modulation, as discussed. For applications where
center modulation is required and it is critical to have the center frequency closely match the
reference input, dual loop solutions could be implemented, such as in [33].
Conventional solutions often limit the frequency modulation to maintain loop stability. Ob-
servations from the literature suggest that loop-filter characteristics pose challenges when
large frequency deviations are attempted, especially under rapid transitions [39]. Contrary
to these approaches, the present design decouples EMI mitigation concerns from loop fil-
ter stability constraints, see fig. 6.20, allowing faster modulation intervals of 32 clock cycles
(compared to 3200 cycles in many existing implementations) [38]. This capability signifi-
cantly lowers short-term EMI peaks, as the switching frequency never remains in any single
band long enough to accumulate high radiated or conducted noise levels. It is believed that
the reason for the high number of cycles in [38] is due to loop instability.
The current approach successfully integrates frequency-hopping across a wide range, by em-
ploying a current-dependent oscillator. The estimated and simulated performance does how-
ever not match. This might be due to the modulation intervals of 32 clock cycles being too
fast, or the switches being too slow or the RDSON not being low enough, further investigation
is needed in this area.

7.2
The loop filter
The loop filter posed a challenge due to the wide operating range, making it difficult to ensure
stable performance in all regions. In chapter 6 these problems are presented. The solution
to place a resistor at the output of the charge pump, as presented in fig. 6.5, was found to
be non-optimal. The large resistor successfully decreases the current to the loop filter and
enables the system to operate at low frequencies. However, it also forces the voltage drop
across the MOSFETs to be very low, as most of the drop occurs over the resistor. This makes
the system highly sensitive to variations in process parameters, such as transistor speed and
supply voltage. These variations directly affect the performance of the charge pump and,
consequently, the behavior of the loop filter.
This instability is particularly pronounced in conditions with higher supply voltages and
faster PMOS process corners. In these scenarios, the increased current flowing through the
charge pump exacerbates the effects of the resistor, disrupting the balance required for stable
operation. Consequently, the PLL is unable to reliably lock onto the reference signal in these
corners.
The 20kHz signal is not able to lock on. This was found to be an unsolved problem for
this iteration of this work. The problem lies in the fact that 20kHz to 1MHz range is too
large to be implemented in an integrated environment. The capacitor sizes and resistors had
to be in the order of mF and MΩfor the filter to behave reasonably. The other problem
with introducing the resistor is that the theoretical analysis of the filter was no longer able to
represent the effects seen in the simulation. This is due to the MOSFETs no longer operating
in saturation and therefore not behaving as current sources.
These design limitations could potentially have been mitigated by employing an alternative
filter topology, such as an active filter or a more complex approach. However, this aspect was
not explored in this study and is left for future research.
A possible solution to the low operating frequency issue would be to incorporate a frequency
divider in both the output and feedback path. This would enable the VCO to operate at
a higher internal frequency while still producing a 20, kHz output signal. However, this ap-
proach would result in increased power consumption. On the other hand, it would significantly
reduce the required size of the loop filter.

Future Work
This thesis has demonstrated the feasibility of using spread-spectrum techniques in clock
generation for Electromagnetic Interference (EMI) reduction in switched-capacitor DC-DC
converters. However, several areas remain open for further research and development. Future
work could focus on improving system stability, refining the modulation scheme, and extend-
ing the implementation towards hardware validation. Below are some proposed directions for
future investigations:
• Solve loop stability.
• Solve PVT variations.
• Investigate the discrepancy between the theorized 25% modulation and the simulated
18.5% modulation.
• Explore ways to integrate the secondary VCO into a feedback loop while maintaining
random-hop modulation.
• Provide a full implementation, including layout and post-layout analysis.
• Investigate noise contributions. While noise is deliberately introduced for EMI peak
reduction, analyzing feedback loop noise could lead to optimizations.
• Investigate alternative PLL architectures
• Further evaluation of Sigma-Delta PLLs. A more detailed analysis could determine
their suitability for spread-spectrum modulation in this application.
• Further explore power, area and performance trade-offs.
• Investigate SSFM and EMI filter combinations, it might be possible to reach the 40dB
target with both techniques.

Chapter 9
Conclusion
This project investigated how to reduce EMI by modulating the operating frequency. This is
done by utilizing a PLL. Unlike traditional designs, the PLL contains a dual-VCO configura-
tion, allowing it to modulate a secondary VCO outside the conventional PLL feedback loop.
This successfully increases loop stability compared to having the modulation in feedback.
Several oscillator designs were modeled, it was found that a current controlled relaxation os-
cillator would likely be the best choice for this project. The secondary VCO operates in open
loop, both VCOs are current controlled and matched by a current mirror, by digitally control-
ling the current mirror ratio the secondary VCO is successfully modulated. The secondary
VCO is modulated by a pseudo-random number generator (PRNG), which is implemented
using a linear feedback shift register (LFSR).
In this thesis, EMI peaks were successfully reduced by 28dB through center modulation at
±18.5% (target was ±25%), with a 1MHz reference frequency and an output frequency
following the reference within 5%, this could likely further be improved by up modulation
instead of center modulation. It is theorized that up modulation could completely eliminate
any discrepancies in the reference at lower frequency.
Various modulation schemes were
analyzed. Their performance was evaluated through simulations and theoretical modeling.
Even though many of the specifications were not met, it was shown that it is not possible
to reach the desired specs within ±50% modulation.
Overall, this work serves more as
an introduction to possible solutions and future project proposals rather than a finalized
implementation.
The work in this project indicates that the specifications could be stated slightly differently to
improve performance. Specifically, using a fixed reference frequency and employing a digital
representation to choose the output frequency, as in traditional PLL designs. This would
greatly increase loop stability, which was found to be a problem due to the large input range
of 20KHz to 1MHz.

Notes on the Fourier Transform
The Fourier Transform has several fundamental properties these properties include [42]:
• Time Shift and Phase Shift: A shift in the time domain corresponds to a phase
shift in the frequency domain.
• Parseval’s Theorem: The total energy of the signal is conserved between the time
and frequency domains.
• Convolution and Multiplication: Convolution in the time domain is equivalent to
multiplication in the frequency domain, and vice versa.
• Linearity: The Fourier Transform is linear, meaning that the transform of a sum of
signals is the sum of their transforms.
• Symmetry Properties: Real signals exhibit conjugate symmetry in the frequency
domain, while periodic signals have discrete spectra.
Notes on the Discrete Fourier Transform

similar to the continuous Fourier Transform but operates on discrete points. The DFT is a

A.2.3
Properties of the Laplace Domain
Some Laplace transform properties are introduced.
The Laplace transform is a linear operator, expressed as in eq. (A.42)
L{a · f1(t) + b · f2(t)} = a · L{f1(t)} + b · L{f2(t)}.

L{f′(t)} = s · F(s) −f(0).
(A.43)

L{f′′(t)} = s2 · F(s) + s · f(0) −f′(0).
(A.44)

L{f(t −t0)} = e−st0 · F(s).
(A.45)
