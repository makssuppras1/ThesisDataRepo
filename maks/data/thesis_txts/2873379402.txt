Abstract
This thesis explores the practical implementation and evaluation of network slicing in
5G standalone networks, focusing on the radio access and core network domains usôÙ
ing commercial Ericsson infrastructure. Network slicing enables multiple logical network
instances to coexist over shared physical infrastructure, each tailored to specific service
requirements. The project documents the configuration and deployment of multiple netôÙ
work slices, including slice definition, provisioning, and user equipment registration across
slices. Emphasis is placed on the use of radio resource partitioning to allocate radio reôÙ
sources across slices, enabling differentiated performance. A series of experimental sceôÙ
narios are conducted to assess interôÙslice resource differentiation, demonstrating that a
simple network slice configuration with radio resource partitioning can effectively influence
throughput behavior among slices. While the transport network is not part of the practiôÙ
cal implementation, its role in endôÙtoôÙend slicing is discussed conceptually. The thesis
concludes by outlining deployment challenges and practical considerations, contributing
operational insights into the feasibility and limitations of deploying 5G network slicing in
realôÙworld environments.

Introduction
Global mobile data traffic has historically seen substantial growth, which is projected to
continue, fueled by further smartphone and mobile broadband1 adoption, heightened data
consumption, and the rapid expansion of highôÙbandwidth applications like video streaming
and gaming [1, 2]. By 2030, annual global mobile data traffic is expected to exceed 5400
exabytesãequivalent to approximately 5.8 trillion gigabytes [1, 3]. This trend is illustrated
in Figure 1.1, which shows the projected growth of global mobile network data traffic.

To accomodate this traffic growth, mobile network operators (MNOs) must make signifiôÙ

generation (5G) networks [1]. Figure 1.2 shows how capital investment (in USD billions)
steadily increases from $108B in the second generation (2G) era to a projected $275B in

to consider a critical limitation: early 5G deployments rely on nonôÙstandalone (NSA) arôÙ
chitecture, which lacks the full capabilities offered by standalone (SA) architecture [5, 6].
5G SA deployments not only accommodate rising data demands but also pave the way
for new technologies, which open up additional monetization avenues for operators [7].

As cellular networks continue to evolve beyond traditional consumer broadband services,
they are increasingly expected to support a diverse and growing set of use cases across
industry, government, and society. These include a wide range of mobile applications,
missionôÙcritical services, autonomous driving, remote surgery, industrial automation and
largeôÙscale IoT deployments ranging from smart grids to environmental monitoring. To
meet the stringent and often conflicting performance requirements of these servicesã
such as ultraôÙlow latency, high reliability and massive device connectivityãthe 5G cellular
network introduces a fundamental architectural and technological shift: network slicing.
A network slice (NS) is a logical network instance that operates over a shared physical
infrastructure. For completely tailored endôÙtoôÙend network performance characteristics
such as jitter, latency and throughput, the NS must span the access network (AN), the
core network (CN), and the transport network (TN)ãwhich interconnects the two. This
technology empowers cellular networks to support diverse and concurrent use cases on
the same underlying infrastructure, each with distinct service requirements [9, 10].
Furthermore, the NS as a Service (NSaaS) model, enabled through the integration of adôÙ
vanced orchestration and exposure capabilities, allows third parties to create and manage
their own network slices. This model enables infrastructure owners to effectively lease
isolated, customized network environments to thirdôÙparty operators or service providers.
As a result, it can decouple the roles of infrastructure ownership and service delivery,
fundamentally reshaping the business and operational models for MNOs [11].
1.1
Motivation
Network slicing represents one of the most promising and transformative features introôÙ
duced in 5G. This thesis is driven by both academic curiosity and professional ambition to
develop a comprehensive understanding of its architectural and technological enablers.
The motivation stems from a desire to provide detailed, accessible insights that can assist
researchers and engineers in deepening their understanding of network slicing, while also
exploring its practical deployment in a realôÙworld environment.

RealôÙworld testing and validation are essential to ensure that network slicing works as

2
Literature Review
Network slicing is a key feature of 5G networks that allows multiple virtual networks to
run over a shared physical infrastructure. Each slice can have different characteristics
for latency, bandwidth, reliability, and security, making it suitable for applications such as
autonomous vehicles, remote surgery, industrial IoT, and smart cities [12].
2.1
State of the Art and Technological Enablers
Park et al. highlight that modern 5G network slicing is made possible by combining techôÙ
nologies such as SDN, NFV, and cloudôÙnative design. SDN enables centralized control
by decoupling the control plane from the data plane, while NFV allows network functions
to run as software on standard servers instead of dedicated hardware, increasing flexibilôÙ
ity and scalability. CloudôÙnative approaches use containers and microservices to deploy
network functions efficiently, and combined with container orchestration tools like KuberôÙ
netes allows for automating deployment, scaling, and management of CNFs. Although the
architecture and standards for slicing are wellôÙdefined, endôÙtoôÙend orchestration across
the RAN, transport, and core remains challenging. There are still gaps in monitoring, asôÙ
suring SLAs, and coordinating slices across different parts of the network, which limits
largeôÙscale commercial deployments [13].
BayatMokhtari et al. proposed a full endôÙtoôÙend slicing architecture combining 3GPP conôÙ
cepts with SDN controllers and NFV MANO, covering both RAN and core slices. However,
their work focuses on architectural proposals rather than providing detailed deployment
procedures [14].
On the RAN side, de Javel et al. analysed the implementation of network slicing within
openôÙRAN environments from an architectural perspective. They described techniques
such as configuring BWP, deploying sliceôÙspecific schedulers, and leveraging flexible reôÙ
source allocation strategies to support heterogeneous slice requirements. However, their
work remains conceptual without practical implementation or experimental validation, and
they note that current base station hardware and software limitations present significant
challenges for achieving fully dynamic slicing in practice [15].
Boutiba et al. address radio resource management challenges in 5G New Radio (NR) with
slicing. They propose a deep reinforcement learning scheduler to allocate different nuôÙ
merologies and bandwidth parts to slices to meet their performance requirements. While
effective in simulations, this has not been implemented in live systems [16].
Another important aspect of slicing is where to place virtualized NFs (VNFs) and conôÙ
tainerized NFs (CNFs) in the network. Attaoui et al. review placement algorithms and
highlight that deciding where to deploy functions affects latency, resource usage, and enôÙ
ergy consumption. However, finding the optimal placement is computationally difficult,
and many studies only use simulations without validation in real deployments [17].
Recent studies also extend slicing concepts towards 6G architectures. Alwakeel and AlôÙ
naim introduce a strategic framework for network slicing in 6G [12]. While their framework
demonstrates promising theoretical results for low latency and secure IoT connectivity, it
remains focused on future 6G systems and does not address practical deployment proôÙ
cedures on current 5G infrastructures.

2.2
Experimental deployments
Researchers have built experimental testbeds to study network slicing in practice. Liao et
al. implemented a 5GC slicing testbed using the openôÙsource free5GC platform combined
with OpenStack. Their experiments showed that running separate core network instances
for different slices improves throughput and latency compared to a shared core, but inôÙ
creases CPU usage [18]. However, it is worth noting that free5GC remains primarily an
experimental and researchôÙoriented project rather than a productionôÙgrade core network,
which may limit the direct applicability of their results to commercial deployments.
2.3
Field Deployments
Sun et al. implemented network slicing within the China Southern Power Grid, demonstratôÙ
ing that slices can meet strict latency and reliability requirements for power grid services
[19]. However, while their study provides detailed numerical results confirming slice isolaôÙ
tion and QoS performance, it does not include visual analyses such as time series plots or

3.1.2
2G: Digital CircuitôÙ and PacketôÙSwitching
The 2G cellular network emerged in the early 1990s with the introduction of Global System
for Mobile Communications (GSM), which introduced the circuitôÙswitching core supporting
digital services such as voice and SMS.
The 2G radio AN (RAN) consists of radio nodes called Base Transceiver Stations (BTSs)1

Enhanced Data rates for GSM Evolution (EDGE), also called 2.75G, which improved data
rates through a more advanced radio interface called GSM EDGE RAN (GERAN) [22].
3.1.3
3G: Improved Radio Interface
The 3G mobile network was the first to be standardized under the International Mobile
Telecommunications (IMT) framework defined by the International Telecommunication
Union (ITU). Specifically, IMTôÙ2000 set out global requirements for 3G systems, estabôÙ

higher data rates. To accommodate the requirements, 3GPP developed Universal MoôÙ

enabling significantly faster mobile internet, support for multimedia services (MMS), and
features such as video calling. This era also coincided with the emergence of smartôÙ
phones and mobile applications [22].
UMTS reôÙused much of the circuitôÙswitched and packetôÙswitched core network architecôÙ

and GGSN. However, it introduced a new RAN, the UMTS Terrestrial RAN (UTRAN), feaôÙ

radio nodes called NodeBs (NBs) replaced the 2G BTSs, and were controlled by RaôÙ

managing more complex tasks such as soft handovers, power control, and dynamic radio
resource management.
As data demand increased, the UMTS radio interface was further enhanced through the
High Speed Packet Access (HSPA) family of technologies, also called 3.5G. The introôÙ
duction of High Speed Downlink Packet Access (HSDPA) improved downlink speeds by
optimizing how data is scheduled and retransmitted, using new scheduling, modulation,
coding and error correction techniques. High Speed Uplink Packet Access (HSUPA) was
introduced to enhance uplink performance through similar mechanisms. These were folôÙ
lowed by HSPA+, also called 3.75G, which brought further improvements to modulation
schemes, multipleôÙinput multipleôÙoutput (MIMO), and dualôÙcarrier operation. 3G networks
remain operational in some regions today but are also gradually being phased out, much
like 2G networks before themãa process commonly referred to as 2G/3G sunsetting.

Transport Network
The transport network (TN), connects the RAN and CN. While the TN does not itself
create or terminate network slices, it provides the sliceôÙaware fabric needed to uphold
performance requirements such as bandwidth, latency, jitter, and isolation.
5G user plane connectivity must be mapped onto transport technologies defined by InôÙ
stitute of Electrical and Electronics Engineers (IEEE), Internet Engineering Task Force
(IETF) and other industry standards. As illustrated in fig. 3.12, different use cases place
different requirements on the TN. For example, uRLLC services require ultraôÙlow latency
and high reliability, favoring edgeôÙheavy architectures, while MIoT services prioritize scalôÙ
ability and cost efficiency, favoring centralized architectures with minimal edge resources.
eMBB sits between these extremes, requiring high bandwidth but with more relaxed laôÙ
tency constraints to public application servers (ASs).

Some key enabling transport technologies include [44]:
ãÂ Virtual LAN (VLAN) ã Uses IEEE 802.1Q tagging to create logical separation within
Ethernet networks by assigning traffic to specific VLAN identifiers.
ãÂ Virtual private network (VPN) ã Provides isolated network connectivity over shared
infrastructure. VPN services, such as multiôÙprotocol label switching (MPLS) Layer 3

routing tables for each tenant or slice, enabling overlapping IP address spaces and
strict traffic isolation.
ãÂ SR over IPv6 (SRv6) ã Allows the source node to define the forwarding path using
IP version 6 (IPv6) header segments.
ãÂ Traffic engineering (TE) ã Refers broadly to techniques for optimising network reôÙ

prioritisation, and queueing mechanisms to meet specific service requirements. In
the context of slicing, TE ensures that each slice receives its required performance
guarantees (e.g. delay, bandwidth, reliability) across the shared transport network.

The transport network can also be 5QIôÙaware, meaning that there is a mapping between
5QI values and transportôÙlayer QoS identifiers, such as the differentiated services code
point (DSCP) field in the IP header. This mapping enables each QoS flow to be handled
with a corresponding PerôÙHop Behavior (PHB)ãa defined forwarding treatment applied
at each network node (hop).
Figure 3.14 illustrates an example where a QoS flow with 5QI = 1 (voice traffic) is mapped

to DSCP 46, which corresponds to the Expedited Forwarding (EF) PHB. As packets enter
the transport network, routers use the DSCP marking to classify them into different output
queues:
ãÂ HighôÙpriority traffic (e.g., EF) is placed in a strict priority queue (SPQ), configured to
minimize delay and jitter.
ãÂ MediumôÙ and lowôÙpriority traffic is assigned to weighted fair queues (WFQs) 2 and

ãÂ Unmarked or bestôÙeffort traffic is sent to the default queue with no guarantees.

PHB ensures that QoS characteristicsãsuch as low latency or guaranteed bitrateãare

protocol (TCP) and to investigate whether protocol type might influence throughput or
slice performance. However, these UDPôÙbased tests produced inconsistent and often
counterintuitive results, even when no slicing configuration was applied.
In particular, when the downlink target bandwidth was set to approximately 100ã250 Mbps,
the effective throughput was frequently much lower than expected, sometimes falling beôÙ
low 10 Mbps, with packet loss rates regularly exceeding 90%. This occurred even unôÙ
der good radio conditions and in scenarios where TCP traffic was able to achieve full
throughput. Adjustments to UDPôÙspecific parametersãincluding target bandwidth, dataôÙ
gram size, and the number of parallel streamsãdid not yield stable or reproducible imôÙ
provements. Smaller packet sizes (e.g., 1200 bytes) appeared to reduce loss in some
cases, but did not eliminate the underlying instability.
This behavior could not be conclusively explained. No UDPôÙspecific traffic shaping or
throttling was known to be configured in the network, and the issue persisted despite
extensive retesting. Whether the observed effects were due to limitations in the UE, the
network, or the behavior of iperf3ãs UDP implementation remains unclear.
As a result, all subsequent throughput testing and slice comparison was conducted using
TCP only, due to its greater stability and consistency across runs. This outcome was
particularly unfortunate, as UDP tests in iperf3 offer additional performance metricsã
most notably jitter and packet lossãthat are not available when using TCP mode. The
lack of reliable UDP test results meant that these potentially valuable measurements could
not be incorporated into the performance evaluation.
5.2
Testing Methodology
The objective of the testing methodology is to validate the functionality of the implemented
network slices and to analyze their relative bandwidth allocation with concurrently active
traffic. The approach consists of generating controlled traffic flows across slices and colôÙ
lecting performance metrics to assess radio resource distribution.
Three computers, each with an iperf3 client installed, are used as traffic generators.
These computers are connected to the iperf3 server via 5G modems, which can be proôÙ
visioned to register on specific slices. On the server side, three separate iperf3 server inôÙ
stances are running. Each client connects to its respective server instance using iperf3ãs
TCP testing mode.
The test procedure is as follows:
1. Testing and Data collection
Execute iperf3 tests with the --json flag to export test results in JSON format, to
simplify the process of parsing metrics such as timestamps and interval throughput.
2. Data correlation and presentation
Execute a Python script to parse the iperf3 output. The script correlates the traces
based on their start timestamps and reporting intervals to enable direct comparison

across measurements within the same time window. The script generates aligned
plots illustrating throughput versus time. This visualization highlights the distribution
of bandwidth (throughput) and identifies whether configured RRP results in distinct
bandwidth allocation.
One practical challenge with this testing methodology is the reliance on timestamp alignôÙ

If implemented correctly, this methodology ensures a repeatable and systematic approach

in chapter 4.

Discussion
This chapter reflects on the work by examining three dimensions: the design decisions
taken under laboratory constraints, the practical steps and challenges encountered during
implementation, and the extent to which the resulting configuration delivered measurable
slice differentiation. A central theme emerged during the project: although the original
ambition was to explore richer network slicing behaviors, the available lab infrastructure
limited differentiation to the RAN. Within that boundary, RRP proved to be an effective
and educational parameter for producing observable, repeatable throughput differences
between soft network slices.
Design Motivation and Adaptation
The thesis set out to implement and validate network slicing in a 5G SA environment using
commercial infrastructure. Ideally, this would have included some degree of endôÙtoôÙend
variationãseparate, dedicated UPFs or perôÙslice routing contextsãto study how different
service types experience the network. In practice, the laboratory environment was fixed
and shared. The following limitations shaped the final design:
ãÂ Shared infrastructure across slices at all layers.
ãÂ No configurable transport network, meaning all traffic traversed a shared, opaque
transport segment that could not be engineered, marked, or segmented per slice.
ãÂ No selectable edge or central data center placement: control and user plane reôÙ
sources were preôÙdeployed and not relocatable within the scope of the project.
ãÂ Unable to test applicationôÙaware slicing instantiated by the UE or network.
ãÂ The number of test UEs was small, and all traffic was directed to a single internal
server.
ãÂ Measurements were TCPôÙonly.
In both commercial deployments and the lab environment used for this thesis, the transôÙ
port and core networks are typically overdimensioned relative to the RAN, which remains
the most resourceôÙconstrained and costôÙintensive part of the system. Additionally, the
transport network in this setup was opaque and not sliceôÙaware, making latency differenôÙ
tiation difficult and offering no meaningful levers for isolation. As a result, the RAN was
the most appropriate domain for implementing and observing slice differentiation.
RRPãa static scheduler input used to bias radio resource distribution across slicesã
was therefore selected as the primary mechanism for interôÙslice differentiation. Distinct
SôÙNSSAIs and DNNs were configured to create logical slices, but all meaningful perforôÙ
mance variation originated from RRP weighting in the gNB.
The shift from a broader slicing objective to a more focused study of logical RAN resource
partitioning reflects the practical limitations of the lab environment. While not directly
comparable, working within these kinds of constraintsãsuch as fixed NF placements,
shared infrastructure, and limited control over the coreãcan offer useful insights for other
realôÙworld scenarios where similar limitations apply. One example might be early 5G SA
deployments, where the network isnãt yet fully rolled out or flexible, and lightweight differôÙ
entiation at the radio layer could be a practical way to begin supporting and differentiating
multiple slices.

Implementation Process
Although the environment included an orchestration platform (ESOA), all sliceôÙrelated
configuration in this thesis was performed manually by design. The intent was to deôÙ
velop an endôÙtoôÙend understanding of the steps involved in setting up even a basic slicing
deployment, and to document that process clearly so that others can build on it as they
see fit.
Configuration spanned multiple systems:
ãÂ Core slice profiles and DNN configurations were defined via nodeôÙspecific CLIs.
ãÂ RAN slice profiles and RRP parameters were defined via ENM.
ãÂ Subscription profiles containing slice, DNN, and QoS associations were defined and
provisioned to subscribers via EDA.
The implementation process was highly iterative. Configurations were frequently revised,
reverted, or discarded when they failed to produce the intended behavior. Hours were
spent reviewing documentation, inspecting preôÙconfigured setups, and debugging issues
arising from subtle dependencies between components. A key challenge of working in
a shared environment arose, when configuration changes made by other users would
unintentionally affect the slicing setup. In particular, other users deploying configurations
via ESOA without first performing a ãDiscovery and Reconciliationã operation. This caused
manually applied configurations to be overwritten or removed entirely when orchestrated
jobs were committed. The issue was eventually resolved once the manual configuration
was discovered and reconciled into the orchestrated model. This experience revealed
an important operational lesson: while orchestration frameworks simplify complexity and
promote repeatability, they must be used with awareness of manual configurations. In
mixed or shared environments, coordination between manual and automated workflows
is essential to avoid unintended side effects.
Validation and Results
The goal of the measurement phase was straightforward: determine whether RRP weightôÙ
ing produces observable and repeatable throughput differences between slices. Due to
an unresolved issue with the iperf3 measurement in the lab, only TCPôÙbased traffic meaôÙ
surements could be used reliably.
Four scenarios were studied:
ãÂ Test Case 1 (Baseline): A single UE saturated the cell on one slice, establishing
the maximum achievable throughput and confirming that the test setup could drive
the RAN to capacity.
ãÂ Test Case 2 (No Differentiation): All three UEs shared a single slice with no QoS
bias. Throughput divided evenly, demonstrating the schedulerãs default fairness
across UEs.
ãÂ Test Case 3 (Equal RRP Weights): When traffic was split across two slices with
equal RRP weights, throughput was distributed proportionally, and the scheduler
maintained fairness across UEs in the same slice. Minor deviations were observed
at lower weights, though these may reflect the limited test scale rather than systemôÙ

Conclusion
This thesis has explored the design, implementation, and validation of 5G network slicing
with a focus on softôÙslicing and RANôÙlevel differentiation through radio resource partiôÙ
tioning. The work was carried out using commercial Ericsson 5G infrastructure in a lab
environment.
A lightweight yet scalable configuration was deployed using logical separation through SôÙ
NSSAIs, DNNs, and RRP, all aligned with standard 3GPP principles and fully supported
by Ericssonãs commercial platform. The thesis provides a detailed walkthrough of the RAN
and core network configuration steps required to deploy network slicing, highlighting the
roles of key components. This processôÙfocused documentation addresses a gap in the
literature, where handsôÙon deployment guidance is often lacking. Structured testing conôÙ
firmed that RRP alone can effectively enforce perôÙslice throughput differentiation. Results
are presented in time series plots that illustrate user plane traffic behavior under varying
traffic loads and slice configurations. The findings show that RANôÙlevel slicingãwithout
full endôÙtoôÙend orchestration or physical resource isolationãcan still enable differentiated
service offerings in a technically feasible and costôÙefficient manner.

Future Work
The work presented in this thesis can be extended in several practical ways:
ãÂ Evaluate RRP overhead: This thesis did not assess the compute or power cost of
enabling RRP.
ãÂ Combine RRP with transport and core slicing: This thesis focused on RANôÙlevel

mobility, and a range of traffic types, as well as speedtest servers placed at different
locations (e.g., edge and central sites) to evaluate latency variation across slices.
ãÂ Automate provisioning: ReôÙimplementing the setup with orchestration tools (e.g.,
ESOA) could improve repeatability and reduce manual configuration errors.
