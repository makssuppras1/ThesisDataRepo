Abstract
Energy savings is fundamental for the achievement of the ambitious EU environmental targets.
Various methodologies based on the pinch concept proved to be highly effective in identifying
and addressing energy savings possibilities in the industrial sector by means of Process Inte-
gration (PI). However, none of them was able to break through the industry, mostly because of
the large engineering effort required in their application.
The present thesis aims at a wider utilisation of PI techniques in the applications that nowadays
do not beneﬁt of their aid, such as dairy industries. This objective is addressed by means of
three goals: (1) identify strengths and weaknesses of the existing PI methods, (2) Develop a
simpliﬁed methodology able to ﬁnd more acceptance at an industrial level and (3) Assess
the importance of data accuracy on the design. Two actually operating dairy facilities were
studied.
The study shows that high savings can be achieved both in retroﬁt projects (24% consumption
reduction with a payback time of 4.1 years) and in grassroot designs (37% energy savings
with a payback time of 5.9 years). However, the applied PI methodologies result to be time
consuming, justifying their low acceptance at an industrial level. A novel retroﬁt methodology
was therefore developed, named "Speciﬁc Savings Potential method" (SSP). It introduces
three screening tools able to reduce the size of the problem before the traditional design
procedure. This method proved to be successful in decreasing the engineering effort and time
consumption while highlighting the most important savings opportunities. In light of this
result, the SSP has the potential to bring PI methodologies to a wider industrial audience.
Finally, the effect of some decisions required during the procedure was assessed (e.g. minimum
temperature difference), as well as the impact of data uncertainties. Some approach advices
were provided based on the ﬁndings, in order to reduce the risk of detrimental choices along
the design.

1 Introduction
1.1
Political framework
Climate change is a major threat of nowadays society. Drinkable water insufﬁciency, food
scarcity, biodiversity reduction and desertiﬁcation are only few of the detrimental effects
of such a process. It is common knowledge that the main driver of climate change is the
concentration of Greenhouse Gases (GHG) in the atmosphere. The chief responsible of
variations of this parameter is humanity. The total anthropogenic GHG emissions have
continued to rise after 1980, with larger and larger yearly increases despite the introduction of
climate change mitigation policies. The main sources of emissions are fossil fuels combustion
and industrial processes, accounting for 78% of the total [1]. The actual forecasts predict
a 46% increase in energy consumption by 2050 corresponding to a 25% increase in GHG
concentration in the atmosphere [2]. In the worst case the actual trend will lead to a 6◦C
increase in the earth mean surface temperature by 2100 [3], mining the global ecosystem
balance in an irreversible way.
In this context, many countries have ﬁnally decided to intervene in order to slow down, and
possibly stop, climate change. The European Union as whole, has decided to set ambitious
short and medium term targets. The EU climate mitigation framework sets a goal of 20% CO2
emissions reduction with respect to 1990’s level to be achieved by 2020, a 40% reduction by
2030 and an 80% decrease by 2050 [4]. Other countries have even higher targets. One of these
is Denmark, which aims at a green house gases emission reduction of 40% by 2020 with respect
to the 1990 level [5] and a complete independence from fossil fuels in the energy sector by
2050 [6].
Key measures to achieve these goals are increase the renewable share in the energy mix and
reduce the energy consumption. In this second ﬁeld energy efﬁciency plays a crucial role.
Looking at the industrial sector, heat is the main responsible of the ﬁnal energy demand in
many developed countries. For example, Hammond et al. [7] states that in the UK industry,
which has a composition similar to many EU countries, 70% of the ﬁnal energy consumption
is provided as heat. It is therefore clear that a reduction in its utilization would have a high

impact in the achievement of the climate targets. Furthermore the potential heat savings are
signiﬁcant. Estimates claim that all the processes requiring heat result in a heat discharge as
outlet, which is roughly one third of the demand [8]. Most of this energy can be easily saved
through process integration and waste heat recovery accepting investment payback times of
generally less than 6 years.
1.2
Motivation
Process Integration (PI) is a proved way of achieving energy savings in industrial processes.
Even though it comprehends different tools and approaches, as today the most spread and
effective one is the pinch analysis. Pinch analysis is a well-known and powerful tool when heat
recovery in industrial processes is sought. Numerous studies have proven that, by applying it,
large energy savings are achievable accepting a payback time of few years. However, despite
of its widely recognized performance, this tool has not found much usage at industrial level,
apart from large scale chemical facilities. According to Dalsgård et al.[9] this is caused by
the requirement of a high engineering effort, which makes it too expensive to perform a
complete pinch analysis on small and intermediate scale industries (such as dairy industries).
Moreover, the outcomes of such study are often too complex designs, which are not attractive
in operation.
Being pinch analysis out of question for such applications, no other proven methodologies
are available. Industries are nowadays forced to rely on the experience of consultants and
plant designers, who generally perform analysis guided by intuition more than scientiﬁc
indicators. This lack of instruments have two detrimental results: on the one hand it generates
scepticism in the plant owners, which do not trust consultant’s advices. On the other hand,
consultants are forced to apply general but not ad-hoc optimized process integration measures,
in order to reduce the risk of mistakes. All in all, the consequence is that many energy savings
opportunities are overlooked, exactly in the scale of industry that is retained to have the largest
savings potential. The development of a simpliﬁed methodology able to make pinch analysis
attractive and affordable for this category of industry would contribute to the solution of this
problem. It would in fact deliver a powerful tool able to recognize the best opportunities in
process integration, enhancing the conﬁdence of both consultants and plant owners towards
efﬁciency improvements able to cut costs and emissions of the industrial sector.
1.3
Goals
The overall aim of this thesis is the development of a methodology able to combine the effec-
tiveness of pinch analysis for Process Integration (PI) studies and the need of dairy industries
and, in general, food industries for fast and simple designs. This requires a thorough under-
standing of the major factors involved in the energy consumption, as well as the identiﬁcation
of the most sensible data needed for the successful completion of the PI project. Being data

acquisition the major obstacle on the implementation of the pinch method, particular atten-
tion needs to be put on how to handle a reduced amount of data loosing the least possible
energy savings potential. For this reason also the effect of the most inﬂuencing uncertainties
on the decision process has to be assessed. Not only the thermodynamic point of view has to
be considered, but also the economical one. In fact, the most important outcome of such a
study from an industrial perspective is its competitiveness in monetary terms.
In more details the objectives of the thesis are:
1. Identify strengths and weaknesses of the traditional pinch analysis, as well as more
advanced methodologies present in literature, for dairy industry implementation.
2. Develop a simpliﬁed methodology able to narrow down the heat recovery problem
before the application of an actual pinch analysis. This methodology should consider
both heat recovery potential and cost factors connected to its achievement.
3. Assess the inﬂuence of uncertainties in data acquisition and plant operation on the
decision process and the outcomes of the PI project.
The ﬁnal outcome should be a tool that is both attractive for industrial applications and
reliable for savings implementation.
1.4
Approach
The present work is purely numerical and does not comprehend any experimental validation.
It is however extensively based on reliable data extracted from existing and actually operating
factories. These data are provided by Viegand Maagøe ApS, which used them for conducting
an energy analysis before this thesis was written. Even though the considered processes are
in effect batch or semi-continuous, continuous operation is considered for the PI analysis.
This is done because of time constraints and because the applied methodology would be very
similar in case the actual operation would be analysed. Considering this assumption, the
study comprehends both retroﬁt and grassroot design cases, in order to broaden the scope of
the methodology as much as possible. Retroﬁt refers to projects dealing with performance
enhancement of existing facilities, while Grassroot is related to design projects started from
scratch.
Finally, it has to be noticed that this thesis is based on a heuristic approach rather than a
theoretically based methodology. This strategy is dictated by the aforementioned need of
simplicity and by the difﬁculty of ﬁnding a scientiﬁc methodology easier to be applied with
respect to the traditional one.

1.5
Thesis outline
The present thesis consists of 11 chapters:
Chapter 1 introduces to this project, presenting motivation and goals of the thesis, as well as
the adopted approach and the overall outline of the report.
Chapter 2 sets the background on which the thesis is founded, comprising a literature review
of the state-of-the-art methodologies for Process Integration and the fundamental processes
of dairy industries.
Chapter 3 outlines the general methodology followed during the thesis, comprehending the
theoretical basis, the followed strategy, the used tools and an introduction to the case studies
analysed along the project.
Chapter 4 includes a detailed description of the pinch analysis fundamentals, as well as the
retroﬁt and grassroot methodologies applied during the thesis.
Chapter 5 presents the modelling method followed in the project and the main assumptions
on which it builds. It includes the milk properties prediction, the case studies model and the
economic analysis description.
Chapter 6 shows the results of the retroﬁt design conducted on the two case studies. Three
different retroﬁt methodologies are applied and reviewed comparing their individual perfor-
mance. Moreover, the impact of the minimum temperature difference choice on the design is
investigated, both considering a general deﬁnition and an individual one for each stream.
Chapter 7 describes the novel retroﬁt methodology developed during the thesis. Moreover it
presents the results obtained by applying it to the case studies and a review of its strengths
and weaknesses.
Chapter 8 builds on the grassroot design of one of the case studies. The traditional pinch
methodology is applied under both continuous and semi-continuous process operation
assumption and the resulting designs are presented. Finally, the main challenges encountered
are discussed and possible simpliﬁed approaches are suggested.
Chapter 9 shows an evaluation of the uncertainties impact on the process integration design.
Two dimensions are considered, namely the process and the economic dimension. In each of
them the most important uncertainties are presented and discussed. Their impact is assessed
focusing on the targeting procedure and the economic feasibility analysis respectively.
Chapter 10 discusses about the main assumptions and limitations of the thesis, considering
their expected effect on the results and possible improvements.
Chapter 11 concludes the thesis, summarising the main ﬁndings and contributions, and
providing suggestions for future development of the present work.

2 Background
This chapter presents the background information related to process integration and dairy
industry. The conducted literature survey constitutes the basis of the thesis, from which the
main challenges and opportunities in the development of the project were detected. In light
of this, it gives an overview of the context in which the present work is collocated and the
possible contributions it can provide.
2.1
Process Integration
Process Integration is a term coined in the 80’s in order to describe a series of tools and
measures aiming at the achievement of energy savings in industry through enhancing the
efﬁciency of the processes involved. A widely accepted deﬁnition of Process Integration
is provided by the International Energy Agency as: "Systematic and General Methods for
Designing Integrated Production Systems, ranging from Individual Processes to Total Sites, with
special emphasis on the Efﬁcient Use of Energy and reducing Environmental Effects" [10]. Even
though this deﬁnition is completely general, nowadays many people incorrectly interpret
it as Heat Integration. This mistake is probably caused by the fact that the ﬁrst and still
most powerful tool of Process Integration is pinch analysis, which is voted to heat integration
applications. All the subsequent tools and methodologies developed in this ﬁeld, such as
mass transfer and water management speciﬁcally, are an extension and evolution of the pinch
concept. It does not have to surprise, therefore, that most of the works related to this thesis
refer to pinch analysis.
Pinch analysis techniques were ﬁrstly developed in the 70’s at the ETH Zurich and Leeds
University. However, it was only in the 1983 that Bodo Linnhoff, who is considered the father
of this technology, presented the ﬁrst release of the complete methodology [11]. In this ﬁrst
stage the method was only conceived for grass root design of continuous industrial processes.
However, it became shortly clear that it had a greater potential. It was therefore extended
ﬁrst to retroﬁt studies of continuous processes, then to semi-continuous and batch operation
studies. In 1994 pinch analysis was already including several other sub-tools, among which
7
==PAGE:30==
Chapter 2. Background
are water pinch, total site integration, pressure drop optimization and emission targeting
[12]. In the following years and up to today extensive research has been carried out on how to
effectively apply the outcomes of pinch analysis and on the development of software capable
of optimizing the process integration design.
Today a large number of tools and methodologies for process integration is available in
literature, differing for: (1) type of the problem (grassroot vs. retroﬁt, continuous vs. batch),
(2) scope of the analysis (single process vs. multiple processes), complexity (traditional tools
vs. simpliﬁed tools) and degree of automation (intuition based vs. computer based). The
following presents an overview of the most important tools and studies developed up to date.
Traditional pinch analysis
Traditional pinch analysis is referred to as the set of PI tools developed by Bodo Linnhoff and
his pool of researchers in the 80’s and ﬁrst half of the 90’s. Altogether they form a framework
for detecting energy integration potentials in a system and perform a nearly optimal design
from both a thermodynamic and an economic perspective.
One of the major strengths of pinch analysis is its capacity of detecting the most performing
process integration opportunities prior the actual system design [13]. This is achieved by
setting targets, both to the utility consumption and the cost of an optimal network. Based on
them, two methodologies for continuous processes are proposed in order to design a network
able to achieve performance close to such objectives. One tackles grassroot designs [11] and
the other retroﬁt projects [14].
The two aforementioned methodologies represent the back bone of the pinch design pro-
cedure, but still have a narrow perspective of the whole process integration study. In fact,
their main focus is on the utilities minimisation by means of heat integration. Supplementary
tools have been developed in order to reﬁne these two basic methodologies and increase
their effectiveness in detecting better performing designs. The most important are: (1) The
Plus-minus principle, which tackles process modiﬁcation opportunities [15], (2) A network
optimisation methodology able to account for the energy-capital tradeoff [16] [17], (3) Pres-
sure drop optimisation, which considers the pressure drops among the other design decision
criteria [12] [18], (4)Water minimisation [19], (5) Low temperature process design [12] and (6)
Emission targeting [12].
Advanced pinch analysis
Starting from the ’90s, many research teams worked on how to extend pinch analysis and
combine it with other existing process integration methodologies.
One ﬁeld of interest is in the design of distillation columns. Liebmann et.al. [20] presented
a new design methodology for distillation processes, combining a simpliﬁed analysis of the
8
==PAGE:31==
2.1. Process Integration
distillation column with the design of a Heat Exchangers Network (HEN) based on pinch
analysis. They stressed how a procedure which simultaneously considers distillation column
and HEN simpliﬁes the whole design when the minimum utility requirement is targeted. Such
approach was proven to be beneﬁcial over various distillation processes, such as oil distillation
[20] and ethylene hydration [21].
A wide area of research, nowadays, is in extending pinch analysis from single processes
to multiple process industrial clusters. It is referred to as Total Site Analysis (TSA). This
methodology seeks process integration possibilities looking at a whole industrial site rather
than a single process. In this way different processes can be integrated by means of steam
or water loops, increasing the overall energy recovery. Its ﬁrst formulation was provided by
Dhole and Linnhoff [22] and it was later developed by Klemeš et al. [23]. Lately, many studies
have been aiming at the enhancement of the method by using MILP programming on the one
side [24] and intuition based frameworks on the other [25]. Various analysis proved that the
holistic approach proposed by the TSA is capable of achieving higher energy savings than an
individual plant optimization does. One example is the study of Roman Hackl performed on
the industrial cluster of Stenungsund (Sweden) [26] [27] [28] [29].
A third research area is in the development of methodologies for grassroot and retroﬁt design
of semi-continuous and batch processes. The basic methodologies for PI of batch processes
are well known and dated back to the end of the ’80s. The most important are: (1) the Time
Average Model [30], (2) the Cascade analysis [31], (3) the Time Slice Model [32] and (4) the
Time Event Model [33]. However, these tools focus only on utility minimisation, overlooking
economic considerations in the targeting stage. In this way, the energy-cost tradeoff is not
considered resulting in non-optimal designs. Extensive research effort has been spent in order
to develop methodologies able to properly address the PI problem for batch processes. Due to
the complexity of the design, the majority of the methods are computer based. Examples are
provided by Zhao et al.[34] and Krummenacher [35].
Lastly, recent studies tried to extend the existing methodologies in order to include not only
energy and economic considerations, but also operability and safety constraints. This is
achieved through experience-based frameworks, as the one proposed by Chew et al. [36], or
through linear programming by means of the concepts of "Match penalty" [37] and "Forbidden
matches" [38].
Simpliﬁed pinch analysis
Pinch analysis, besides being a power tools, is also quite complicated especially in case of
retroﬁt. The major problems are represented by the necessity of extensive and reliable data
about each stream involved in the process, and by the difﬁculty of designing a heat exchangers
network which does not lead to unnecessarily complicated designs and high investment costs.
Therefore a remarkable effort has been spent in order to simplify the methodology and give to
the designer additional tools able to help him in the decision process.
9
==PAGE:32==
Chapter 2. Background
Polley and his pool of researchers focused their attention on the development of simpliﬁed
methodologies for retroﬁt projects by means of process decomposition techniques based on
the zoning concept proper of grassroot methodologies [39] [40] [41]. In this way it can be
quickly established which part of the plant offers the best opportunities for heat integration.
Based on it, a heat exchanger network is designed considering only the most promising
combinations of the decomposed portions of the process, using payback time as decision
criteria.
Anastasovski [42] presented a modiﬁed approach in order to design a general heat exchangers
network. Instead of separating the problem in two regions (below and above the pinch point)
as in the common pinch analysis, it considers numerous independent sub-problems deﬁned
by the number of break points in the cold and hot composite curves. In this way the network
design is simpliﬁed, requiring the solution of multiple small networks instead of two large
ones.
Dalsgård et al. [9] presented a simpliﬁed methodology for retroﬁt of intermediate size in-
dustries. It is based on a process simpliﬁcation dictated by both experience and an analysis
named "Limiting Match Method". This method relies on the estimation of the driving force
necessary to achieve energy savings with an investment lower than a benchmark. Based on
this, uninteresting streams are detected and disregarded, reducing the size of the problem
before the design.
Pouransari et al. [43] developed a methodology based on total site integration techniques, able
to simplify data extraction. Considering the difﬁculty of such task, a multi-level requirement
deﬁnition is introduced. It is proved that applying combination of data extraction levels
in place of an unique detailed one reduces the complexity of the analysis resulting in an
acceptable loss of potential energy efﬁciency measures.
Chew et al. [44] presented a simpliﬁcation of the Plus-minus principle for the impacts as-
sessment of possible process modiﬁcations during a Total Site Heat Integration analysis. The
proposed iterative algorithm is able to address the most promising process variations, it is
relatively fast and implementable in spreadsheets.
Harvey [45] presented a simpliﬁed retroﬁt method based on the cost evaluation of each retroﬁt
action. This methodology guides the analyst through the retroﬁt keeping him aware of the
possible network modiﬁcation and their investment cost. In this way he is facilitated in taking
informed decisions along the project.

encountered in Process Integration. No holistic approach able to address all the possible
problems at once is nowadays available, nor it is considered to be achievable in the future.
However, even if their perspective is limited, when used on the proper problems the presented

2.2
Milk processing
The dairy sector is responsible for the highest turnover in the food industry in many EU
countries [53]. In Denmark, ARLA group and the other 30 dairy companies process 4.7 billion
kg of milk on a yearly basis generating an export income of 1.8 billion EUR, equal to 20% of the
agricultural sector exports [54]. Moreover, the Danish dairy industry consumes roughly 5.8TJ
of energy per year, requiring the highest energy within the food industry and being second
only to cement manufacturing considering the whole industrial sector [55]. Moreover, the
highest share of energy is provided in thermal form in order to perform the numerous thermal
treatments required for bacterial growth control. However, despite the high consumption,
energy is responsible for only a small percentage of the ﬁnal product cost (1-3%) [53]. Such
small impact discourages the plant owners in investing in detailed PI studies, despite the high
energy savings potential expected for dairy processes.
Dairy industries apply most of the processes used in food manufacturing. According to Walstra
et al. [56] they can be grouped in eight categories:
1. Transfer of momentum: Pumping and, in general, ﬂuid handling.
2. Heat transfer: Heating and cooling.
3. Mixing: Processes such as stirring, atomization, homogenization and recombination.
4. Phase separation: Processes as skimming.
5. Molecular separation: Evaporation, drying, membrane processes and crystallization.
6. Physical transformation: Gel formation, butter-, cream- and ice-making.
7. Microbial and enzymatic transformation: Cheese manufacturing, production of fer-
mented products and ripening.
8. Stabilization: Pasteurization, sterilization, cooling and freezing.
The following brieﬂy presents the most important processes with regard to the case studies
considered in this thesis. For more details refer to Walstra et al. [56] and Roginski et al. [57].
2.2.1
Heat treatment
Heat treatments are always performed in dairy industries at various stages of the process. Their
main objective is to ensure the safety for consumers, which is achieved through elimination
12
==PAGE:35==
2.2. Milk processing
of the harmful bacteria. Other possible goals of such processes can be the increase of shelf-
life, mainly through enzymes deactivation and spores elimination, and the establishment of
speciﬁc properties required for further processing.
Special care has to be taken when performing heat treatments, in order to avoid undesired
variations in the milk properties. In particular, the most important parameters to be controlled
are the treatment temperature and duration. A poorly controlled process might lead to several
unwanted results, such as development of a cooked ﬂavour, loss of nutritional value and
browning. With this regard, strict surveillance is performed in order to ensure the correct
operation of the main heat treatments.
Processes
Four different processes fall under the category of heat treatments:
1. Thermalization. It is the heat treatment with the least intensity, being usually performed
for 20s at a temperature comprised between 60◦C and 69◦C. Its main purpose is to
kill bacteria and partially inactivate some enzymes. No other irreversible changes are
produced.
2. Low pasteurization (LP). This is a more intense heat treatment, which is performed
by heating at 63◦C for 30s or at 72◦C for 15s. It results in the elimination of almost all
the pathogens. Yeasts, molds and most of the other bacteria are eliminated as well.
This result is obtained without affecting the milk ﬂavour and its nutritional properties.
However, in the case of homogenized milk, a more intense treatment is often preferred,
heating at 75◦C for 20s. This might cause a perceptible change in ﬂavour.
3. High pasteurization (HP). This heat treatment is performed heating the product at 85◦C
for 20s. However, often the temperature is further increased, up to 100◦C. It is ﬁnalised
at the complete inactivation of most of the enzymes and all the bacteria. Spores are,
however, not killed. A characteristic cooked ﬂavour is developed after the treatment, but
no loss of nutritional values is detected, except for a little loss of vitamin C.
4. Sterilization. This is the most intensive heat treatment, performed taking the milk at
130◦C for 30s, or at 145◦C for 1s. Often the latter conﬁguration is preferred because the
shorter the process duration, the smaller the negative consequences on milk ﬂavour
and nutritional values.
Equipment
Liquids can be heated and cooled mainly in a batch process or by means of heat exchangers.
The choice of the principle and of the speciﬁc equipment is not straightforward, concerning
the evaluation of several aspects. The most important are controllability and reliability of
13

operation, cleanliness, cost of investment and operation, liquid properties and the desired
combination of time and temperature.
Currently the great majority of heat treatment equipments in dairy industries are plate heat
exchangers. Heat exchangers are preferred among the rest especially because they allow
continuous (or semi-continuous) operation and permit heat regeneration. Moreover, the
choice of plate exchangers is dictated by the low minimum temperature difference allowed,
going down to 1K [48]. This yields a higher heat recovery. The heat recovery capability of such
devices is of particular interest, indeed. In fact, after the aforementioned heat treatments, the
dairy product needs always to be cooled to low temperatures (normally below 10◦C) in order
to preserve its properties. By means of a regenerative heat exchanger this process can be done
recovering most of the heat necessary for the previous heating phase. In this way the energy
consumption is drastically reduced. The principle is presented in Figure 2.1. As it can be seen,
the product enters in the regenerative section where it is preheated. Afterwards, it is taken at

Then, the product passes through an insulated holding section, where the temperature is kept
for the necessary time, and it ﬂows back heating the incoming fresh ﬂuid. Finally, it is cooled
by means of an external utility, which is normally ice water.

2.2.2
Centrifugation
Centrifugation is performed in order to separate fat globes or other particles from milk. This
process can yield several products such as cream, skim milk, cream from whey, sweet-cream
buttermilk and it also serves the purpose of standardizing the fat content in milk. Thanks
to the introduction of centrifugal separation the separation process is continuous, quicker
(thus cleaner) and preciser than the natural creaming method. No concerns about undesired
changes during the process are present.

2.2. Milk processing
2.2.3
Homogenization
Homogenization considerably reduces the fat globules size. In this way the fat-plasma in-
terface is considerably increased. This process is performed for several reasons, such as:
counteract creaming which might not be appreciated by the users, improve stability, increase
viscosity (e.g. for yogurt production) and recombine milk products. Side effects of homog-
enization are a whiter colour of milk, a greater tendency of foaming and a lower oxidation
tendency.
The device for achieving this result is pretty simple, consisting of a high pressure pump and
a valve. By forcing the ﬂuid through the valve a high velocity and an intense turbulence are
generated, causing fat globules disruption and a temperature increase for dissipating the
kinetic energy so acquired.
2.2.4
Fermentation (Culturing)
Fermentation is a natural process in milk, caused by lactic acid bacteria, who transform lactose
in lactic acid. It is of crucial importance for production of yogurt, cultured butter and cheese.
In order to increase its velocity, starters are used. A starter is a culture of lactic acid bacteria
able to induce fermentation. It is obtained by growing lactic acid bacteria in milk and, then,
maintained by keeping it growing in fresh milk. In industries which require milk fermentation
cultures are kept in speciﬁc tanks and produced by means of a batch operation. Traditionally,
the starter is propagated by intorducing a growing bacteria colony in a small portion of high
temperature skim milk (close to 100◦C). Subsequently this milk is incubated until reaching
a threshold pH (e.g. 4.5) and then cooled down. This is the mother culture, which is used
as inoculum for the main manufacturing process and for the preparation of a new mother
culture through the aforementioned process.
2.2.5
Cheese manufacturing
Cheese is produced by milk clotting achieved adding rennet or fermentation starter to skim
milk. In the considered cheese plant the latter is utilised. Cheese manufacturing is traditionally
a batch process, which requires a milk temperature of around 30◦C and rest conditions.
However, it can be made nearly-continuous by using several cheese tanks in parallel. Once
the fermentation is induced, a gel forms. The gel is then cut into pieces, separating curd
particles and whey. Whey is subsequently exported to other processes, while the curd is
further processed through shaping and pressing devices resulting in the ﬁnal cheese.
2.2.6
Cleaning
Cleaning and disinfection is a paramount operation in dairy industries. Fouling occurs espe-
cially on heating equipments because milk residues stick on the metallic surface. Once dried,
15

these deposits are hard to be removed and cause many problems. The major problems caused
by fouling are (1) reduction of heat transfer coefﬁcient, (2) reduction of product ﬂow rate, (3)
loss of product and (4) hygiene related concerns due to bacteria growth in the residue. All
in all, fouling can result in high operating expenses. For these reasons cleaning needs to be
performed thoroughly and periodically.
Cleaning can be performed in different ways, but the most diffused one is the so called
"Cleaning In Place" (CIP), for its convenience, efﬁciency and low cost. Two different types
of cleaning procedure can be distinguished based on the equipment. For heat exchangers
cleaning occurs operating the device as normal, but using cleaning product in lieu of the
normal operating ﬂuid. This process might take long time because the ﬂuid is recirculated
multiple times and because it takes time for it to achieve the desired temperature. For vessels,
tanks and other voluminous devices, on the other hand, spraying of cleaning liquid is used. In
this case particular care has to be put on ensuring that the cleaning product reaches all the
equipment surface with the required intensity. In both the cases, duration and frequency of
cleaning depend on the type of equipment and process which it performs.

3 Methodology
This thesis deals with process integration in dairy industries. The modest energy impact on
the process costs (around 1-3%) sets harsh economic constraints on the engineering effort
affordable for a process integration design. The major challenge is therefore to ﬁnd nearly
optimum designs spending as few time as possible.
Besides traditional process integration methodologies, many studies have been conducted
aiming at developing simple, but effective, methodologies for implementing PI designs. How-
ever none of them, at the knowledge of the author, broke through in the industry. A new
methodology is therefore developed in this study, in order to make process integration more
affordable for intermediate size industries.
This chapter presents the fundamental methodology on which the thesis is built. In particular
it outlines the adopted strategy, the physical model considered, the used tools and it gives an
overall description of the case studies analysed.
3.1
Strategy
The development of a new methodology requires a quite heavy preliminary work. Full under-
standing of the challenges faced by the problem under investigation is required, as well as a
good knowledge of the actual tools used to tackle them. The present work is therefore built on
four main steps:
1. Data acquisition and Process understanding.
2. Existing tools test.
3. New methodology development.
4. Validation.
17
==PAGE:40==
Chapter 3. Methodology
3.1.1
Data acquisition and Process understanding
This step starts with data acquisition. All the necessary data for the completion of the PI
project need to be collected, either from simulation models (normally the case of grassroot
designs), from plant schemes or from on-site measurements (often the case of retroﬁt). This is
an extremely time consuming task which requires a high level of experience to be completed
correctly. Afterwards, the whole process needs to be investigated in order to detect its major
constraints. To do so, the physical model of the process has to be developed, and the operation
requirements have to be assessed. This is a crucial step which can determine whether the
ﬁnal project will be feasible or not. All in all this ﬁrst action is the most time consuming of the
whole project.
In order to develop a tool useful for industrial implementation, in this thesis real plants were
analysed so that the same challenges of a real project were faced. Data of two industrial
factories were provided by Viegand Maagøe ApS, which collected and utilised them in a design
project completed in 2014, prior this thesis was commenced. Thanks to them the time required
for data acquisition was signiﬁcantly reduced, and limited only at data handling. Also the
process study was conducted under the supervision of the same company, which ensured a
good understanding of the two factories and their main requirement in order to be operable.
3.1.2
Existing tools test
In this step the existing process integration tools are tested on the speciﬁc case study and
their performance are evaluated. It is therefore necessary to deﬁne some evaluation criteria.
For a PI method the most important values are precision ﬁnding nearly optimum solutions,
simplicity in being applied and time requirement.
Retroﬁt and grassroot designs, even dealing on the same process, are completely different
projects for approach and used tools. As aforementioned, while the ﬁrst one deals with the
performance enhancement of an existing facility, the second one aims at the design of a new
one starting form scratch. The two cases were therefore studied separately from this step on.
Three different methods for retroﬁt design were compared, namely the Traditional one [14],
the Matrix method [45] and the Limiting match method [9]. Moreover, as in many engineering
methods, in all the three of them decisions have to be taken (e.g. pinch point minimum
temperature difference) and many uncertainties undermine the outcomes of the analysis.
Their impact on the outcomes was investigated in order to assess their performance.
For what concerns grassroot design, as aforementioned few studies have been focused on real
applications. Moreover, all of them base the methodology on the traditional pinch analysis
[11]. Hence this was the only tool investigated in this thesis.

3.1.3
New methodology development
In this step the new methodology is developed, aiming at obtaining higher performance than
the existing ones. The starting point is always the analysis of strengths and weaknesses of the
existing methodologies. They constitute the basis for identifying possible pathways towards
an improved framework. However, analyst’s creativity and a thorough understanding of the
problem are fundamentals for the achievement of a good result. All in all, more than a single
step, the new methodology development is a trial-and-error procedure. In every iteration,
starting from new hypothesis, the method is modiﬁed and tested on the case studies through
the same performance parameters utilised in step 2. When a satisfactory result is obtained the
iterative procedure stops.
3.1.4
Validation
The ﬁnal step is the validation of the new methodology. At least another case study is consid-
ered and the new method is applied on it, as well as the traditional ones. If the results are still
satisfactory the new method is validated, otherwise the procedure is repeated from step 3.
3.2
Physical model
The development of a physical model is required in order to represent a process ensuring
to account for the physical relationships among it. Such a model is very helpful in process
analysis, because it allows a computational study of the system under varying conditions,
ensuring a good compliance between the simulation outcomes and the real system behaviour.
In this way experimental investigations are avoided and the analysis time, as well as its cost, is
signiﬁcantly reduced.
3.2.1
Model identiﬁcation
The ﬁrst step in developing a physical model is the deﬁnition of the model scope, i.e. the
complexity level of the analysis. According to Nguyen [58], four different approaches can be
applied in process integration, namely:
• Black-box approach. The plant is represented by the temperature levels and energy
requirements of the utilities only. No informations about the individual processes under-
going in the system is utilised. This is the roughest method, used mainly for Energy bill
analysis, but not particularly helpful for tackling process integration potential. However,
such kind of analysis is not uncommon in small industries, due to its simplicity and low
cost.
• Grey-box approach. The plant is represented at the interface between processes and
utilities. Therefore no process/process heat exchangers are considered, but only utili-
19
==PAGE:42==
Chapter 3. Methodology
ty/process ones. This approach is more detailed than the previous one, but still simple
enough to be conducted in a fast way. It is especially used for Total site analysis, where
the high number of processes requires some simpliﬁcation to be handled.
• White-box approach. The plant is represented by all its heat exchanges. All the processes
are accounted, except the mixing ones. This is the level of detail commonly adopted in
Pinch analysis, because it is the only way of completely describing a thermal process
and tackle all the heat recovery opportunities.
• Detailed approach. The plant is represented by all its heat exchanges, as well as its
processes generating heat (chemical reactions) and mixers. This approach is normally
considered too detailed for the beneﬁts that it generates, therefore it is not commonly
used.
This thesis adopts a White-box approach, because it is the most suitable for system that are
not too complex (e.g. individual plants) and for which the maximum saving potential is
sought. This is therefore the kind of approach that this thesis wants to make more attractive
for applications in intermediate size industries.
3.2.2
Process model
The process was modelled fundamentally on the basis of the First law of thermodynamics, the
Second law of thermodynamics and the Law of conservation of mass.
• First law of thermodynamics. It states that energy can be stored in a system, transformed
in different forms or transferred between systems, but it cannot be destroyed [59].
• Second law of thermodynamics. Various formulations of this law are present in literature.
The most relevant for this thesis is the one of Clausius [60] "No process is possible whose
sole result is the transfer of heat from a cooler to a hotter body" .
• Law of conservation of mass. It states that: "The time rate of accumulation of mass within
a system equals the difference between the total rates of mass ﬂow in and out across the
system boundary" [59].
They result in two equations which constitute the backbone of the entire model and of all the
methodologies applied in order to study the process.

Analysis tools
Pinch analysis
Pinch analysis is a thermodynamic technique extremely helpful for process heat integration.
The fundamental idea behind this tool is the representation of the whole process as just two
heat ﬂows with variable heat capacity (composite curves). Through their identiﬁcation the
maximum heat recovery of the system can be deﬁned. Therefore it sets some targets to the
analyst, giving him a benchmark useful to evaluate the performance of his design at an early
stage, saving in many cases a lot of time. However, the high effectiveness of this tool is paid

in complexity and data requirement. As aforementioned, the only way of exploiting its full
potential is the usage of a white-box model, which requires extensive data of all the processes
undergoing in the system. This limits its industrial application at large plants whose energy
saving potential overcomes the engineering effort required for conducting a full pinch analysis.
Most of the work of this thesis is based on this tool, either because it was utilised, or because it
was evaluated and discussed. In fact, on the one hand Pinch analysis was used for evaluating
two industrial plants and for designing possible modiﬁcations able to achieve energy savings.
On the other hand, it constitutes the starting point, and the core component, of the new
process integration methodology developed during this work.
Due to its great importance for understanding this thesis, a chapter is dedicated to the de-
scription of Pinch analysis in the following (Chapter 4).
Sensitivity analysis
Sensitivity analysis is a tool related to mathematical models investigation. It is particularly
used to assess the impact of the model parameters on its outcomes. Through this kind of
analysis, the model inputs are varied once at a time. For each case the model is run and the
outputs collected and evaluated. By looking at the percentage variation of inputs and outputs
for all the cases, the most inﬂuencing parameters can be detected.
As every model, also the physical models developed during this thesis are accompanied by
uncertainties. This tool was used particularly for detecting the biggest sources of uncertainties
among the input parameters. This indication was helpful as an indication for which data
requires a bigger effort in precision seeking.
Monte Carlo analysis
Monte Carlo analysis is a tool related to mathematical models investigation, similarly as
sensitivity analysis. It is based on repeated simulations with some random parameters in order
to assess the probability distribution of the model results. This is done in four fundamental
steps:
1. A set of model inputs to be analysed is deﬁned.
2. Random values of these inputs are generated, based on a probabilistic distribution.
3. For each set of random inputs a simulation of the model is performed.
4. The results are collected.
This tool could be considered as a type of sensitivity analysis, but in this work it was decided
to distinguish the two of them because of their different purpose. In fact, while sensitivity

aforementioned), Monte Carlo simulations were used for assessing the combined uncertainties

4 Pinch analysis
This chapter presents the fundamentals of pinch analysis, as well as the detailed procedure
to be followed during a retroﬁt and a grassroot design. In case of retroﬁt, three different
methodologies are described, as stated in the literature: (1) Traditional pinch methodology, (2)
Matrix method developed by the Chalmers University of Technology and (3) Limiting Match
Method developed by the Technical Univeristy of Denmark in cooperation with COWI A/S.
In case of grassroot design, just the traditional pinch analysis procedure is explained. These
methodologies constitute the theoretical backbone of the thesis.
4.1
Pinch analysis fundamentals
As aforementioned, nowadays pinch analysis comprehends numerous different tools and
various diverse methodologies have been formulated. However, all of them are founded on
the same, few, basic concepts presented in its ﬁrst formulation by Bodo Linnhoff in 1983 [11].
This section explains the basic principles and tools of pinch analysis as presented by Linnhoff
[11], Serth et al. [38] and Harvey [45], which are essential to understand the more advanced
methodologies applied in this thesis.
The most powerful feature of pinch analysis is its ability of setting a target and predict the
performance of the Heat Exchangers Network (HEN) able to achieve that target prior to the
actual design of the HEN. This saves a large amount of engineering effort, giving the analyst a
benchmark that helps him taking the necessary decisions during the design process. But what
is the most important target of an industrial process design project? As in all the "real-life"
projects, the focus should always be on economy. However, in the early stage of a project it is
complicated to calculate capital and operating costs. Therefore other parameters have been
traditionally used in order to get an idea of ﬁnal costs, namely number of units and utility
consumption. The ﬁrst is related to capital costs, while the latter to operating costs. The idea is
that a network able to minimize the number of units and the utility consumption at the same
time, should be at least close to the economical optimum. Based on this, the two targets of the
25

traditional pinch analysis are: (1) minimum utility requirement and (2) minimum number of
units.
4.1.1
Minimum utility requirement target
The minimum utility requirement target is easy to calculate prior to the HEN design, having
informations about supply and target temperature of each stream as well as their heat capacity
rate. Moreover, this target is independent on the speciﬁc network. The pinch theory provides
a methodology to identify the minimum utility requirement and design a HEN able to achieve
it. It is based on three major concepts: Composite Curves (CC), Pinch and Grand Composite
Curve (GCC).

difference (∆Tpp).
At this point the question about the feasibility of the heat exchange with such a minimum
temperature difference arises. Considering thermodynamics only, a positive temperature
difference between hot and cold streams ensures the feasibility of the heat exchange. However,
the lower the value, the higher the investment cost in heat exchange area. Therefore, generally
a Minimum temperature approach (∆Tmin) is set based on economic considerations. The
choice of this value is brieﬂy discussed later in this section. That being so, whenever the
temperature difference between the Composite Curves is in each point more than or equal to
∆Tmin, the above considerations apply. If this is not the case, the composite curves need to be
shifted in order to verify the minimum approach temperature constraint. Being the abscissa
of the CC diagram representing an enthalpy difference, this procedure can be done without
any harm. Considering the Cold Composite Curve, it is clear that the more it is shifted to the
right, the higher are ∆Tpp and minimum hot and cold utility requirement.

4.2
Pinch grassroot design method
The pinch design method was ﬁrstly created for providing a systematic design procedure for
grassroot projects (i.e. newly built projects), able to minimise the total cost of the plant [11].
Based on the aforementioned pinch principles this method is composed of two stages: (1)
design a heat exchanger network which achieves the Maximum Energy Recovery (MER) and
(2) reduce the number of units in a controlled way. Altogether, these two steps represent the
nowadays most diffused design method for heat exchangers networks. This section presents
step-by-step the overall procedure of which it is composed [11] [38].
4.2.1
Maximum Energy Recovery Heat Exchangers Network design
The ﬁrst step of the pinch design method is the design of a HEN able to achieve the MER
target. The fundamental concept for the MER achievement is the acknowledgement that
the most constrained area for a design is in the region around the pinch. The pinch point
is, after all, the only point in which the minimum temperature difference between hot and
cold streams is veriﬁed. Knowing the three pinch "Golden rules" and especially that no heat
has to be exchanged across the pinch, the method (1) recognizes the pinch division and (2)
starts the design at the pinch point considering two separate problems: the problem below
the pinch and the one above the pinch. Once away from the pinch region, the problem is not
so constrained and the designer has more freedom for matching streams considering not only
energy targets but also operability and safety.
Looking at the region around the pinch, it is clear that the possible streams matches are

4.2.2
Network simpliﬁcation
Commonly the aforementioned procedure results in too complex heat exchangers networks to
be considered practical. It might be more beneﬁcial to consider a higher energy consumption
in order to reduce the number of heat exchangers, thus capital cost and complexity. This
process can be achieved in a controlled and informed way by using the concept of (1) Heat
load loops and (2) Heat load paths.
• Heat load loops. Heat load loops are the only causes of an increased number of units
compared to the minimum one. A heat load loop is a loop in the network around which
heat loads can be shifted from one unit to another. In this way the streams duties are
not affected, but the load of individual heat exchangers are varied. By exploiting this
effect, units can be eliminated by shifting their entire duty to other heat exchangers. It
has to be noted, however, that this operation might lead to ∆Tmin violations. In this
case heat load paths can restore the correct driving forces.
• Heat load paths. A heat load path is a consecutive connection in the network between a
heater, heat exchangers and a cooler. As for loops, heat duties can be shifted along the
path. In this way stream duties are not varied, but temperatures along the path change
because of the variation in heat exchangers load. In this way ∆Tmin violations can be
restored at the expenses of higher utilities consumption.

4.3
Process Retroﬁt by using Pinch Analysis
Nowadays the majority of industrial design projects are retroﬁt designs [45], i.e. projects aiming
at enhancing the performance of existing processes. Pinch analysis ﬁnds wide utilisation in
this ﬁeld, proposing as a systematic tool for analysing the energy savings potential of existing
plants and suggesting possible energy saving measures.
The pinch method for process retroﬁt is more complex than the grassroot one, but it is
still based on the same fundamentals presented in Section 4.1. The greatest difﬁculties are
represented by data extraction, by the plant size (which can be considerable) and by the design
of process modiﬁcations that do not undermine the plant operability. In order to overcome
such complications, high experience is required.
During the years lot of research has been carried out in order to reduce the engineering effort
in retroﬁt projects simplifying the traditional methodology. This thesis tested two of them,
altogether with the traditional one, in order to assess their actual effectiveness in completing
the retroﬁt with the least possible effort. They are presented in the following, as they are
reported in the literature.
4.3.1
Traditional retroﬁt method
The traditional retroﬁt method based on pinch analysis is composed of 7 steps [45]:
1. Data extraction: The relevant streams to be included in the retroﬁt are deﬁned. Then,
their most important characteristics are collected, such as inlet and outlet temperatures,
mass ﬂows and ﬂow composition.
2. Targeting procedure: Based on the extracted data, the utility targets and pinch point
location are identiﬁed by means of the pinch analysis targeting procedure. The deﬁnition
of a suitable minimum temperature difference is required.
3. Savings potential identiﬁcation: The utility consumption of the existing system is identi-
ﬁed. The difference between the actual consumption and the minimum one calculated
in step 2 represents the energy savings potential of the process. This is the goal of the
retroﬁt.
4. Identiﬁcation of the "pinch violations": The existing heaters, coolers and heat exchangers
are examined in order to identify violations of the three "golden rules" of pinch analysis,
as presented in Section 4.1.1.The pinch violations are the only causes of excessive utility
consumption. Therefore their sum is equal to the previously calculated energy savings
potential.
5. Network retroﬁt measures: Possible options to eliminate the biggest pinch violations are
identiﬁed. Common measures are area enhancement of existing heat exchangers, or
36
==PAGE:59==
4.3. Process Retroﬁt by using Pinch Analysis
placement of new ones. More rarely heat exchangers are switched or process parameters
are varied. Finally, the new network is designed.
6. Economic assessment: The investment cost of the retroﬁt is estimated, as well as utility
cost savings achieved through its implementation. Then the economic performance of
the project is calculated. Commonly used parameters in industries are Payback Time
(PBT) and Net Present Value (NPV).
7. Retroﬁt optimisation: Step 2 to 7 are repeated using different ∆Tmin. The best solution
is selected either based on savings or economic performance. In most of the cases the
economic performance is preferred, but this is not a duty.
4.3.2
Matrix method
The Matrix method is a retroﬁt methodology developed by the Chalmers University of Tech-
nology (Göteborg, Sweden) [45]. It represents an attempt of simplifying the analyst job when
pinch analysis is applied to retroﬁt projects.
The basic goal of this method is to provide a systematic screening tool which allows the
engineer to have a constant control over the followed retroﬁt path during the whole design
procedure. The performance of each implemented decision is constantly monitored and
evaluated, giving the designer the possibility to identify bad options and formulate better
ones. In this way the experience required for the successful completion of a retroﬁt project is
supposed to be reduced and the time required for the analysis shortened.
This is an interactive tool, in which the analyst is allowed to set the desired savings level,
the relevant costs and the important processes to be included. Based on these parameters,
an iterative procedure allows the design of a heat exchangers network which achieves the
previously set energy targets. For doing so a computer software is required.
The method is based on eight steps:
1. Traditional targeting. The ﬁrst step consists of conducting the traditional targeting
procedure in order to identify the overall savings potential. A ∆Tmin is chosen for the
process and the utility targets are calculated. Clearly this choice has to lead to energy
savings, therefore the minimum temperature difference should be smaller than the one
of the existing process. Based on these outcomes, the pinch violations in the existing
network are identiﬁed.
2. Target choice. The second step regards the choice of the heat exchangers to be included
in the retroﬁt. Only the subsystem composed by these devices will be evaluated for
process modiﬁcations in the following steps. The decision is taken mainly based on the
pinch violations of each exchanging device, but also on operability and safety constraints.
37
==PAGE:60==
Chapter 4. Pinch analysis
By doing so, the energy targets of the ﬁnal system are set. The achieved savings are in
fact equal to the pinch violations of the heat exchangers included in the subsystem.
3. Divide the subsystem at the pinch. In this step the streams previously identiﬁed are
separated at the pinch. In such a way two different regions are created: above and below
the pinch. However, this procedure is not conducted rigorously. The existing network, in
fact, contains pinch violations and especially heat exchangers placed across the pinch
point. In order to have a clear idea of the actual retroﬁt possibilities, streams connected
by cross-pinch heat exchangers are not split exactly at the pinch point, but in a way that
allows the placement of the exchange devices in the actual position. In this way their
area is accounted for in the next steps.
4. Matrix creation. The fourth step consists in the creation of the cost matrices. For each
of the two regions a matrix is created, where rows are hot streams and columns cold
streams. For all the possible matches between them, the heat exchanger costs needed to
realize the heat exchange is calculated. This can comprise various cost voices decided
by the analyst. The most common are:
• Surface area cost
• Piping cost
• Pressure drop cost
• Maintenance cost
The existing heat exchangers are accounted as having a lower or nil capital cost, because
only costs related to surface area enhancement are accounted for, eventually. Further-
more, heat exchangers which violate the second law of thermodynamic transferring
heat from a lower to a higher temperature are marked as infeasible.
5. Choice of a heat exchanger. Hereby a heat exchanger is selected out of the ones presented
in the cost matrix. Normally, heat exchangers with low capital cost are preferred over
more expensive devices, but this decision criteria is not always the optimal one. In
fact, the selection of a cheap heat exchanger in this step might imply the need for a
much more expensive one afterwards. For this reason this whole procedure is normally
iterative, in order to achieve an optimal retroﬁt design. Moreover, besides the systematic
procedure of the Matrix method, experience is still needed in order to tackle the optimal
solution in a short time.
After implementing the chosen heat exchanger, the streams characteristics are updated
considering the performed choice.
6. Retroﬁt completion. In this step a new matrix is built, where the streams are updated
due to the previous design decisions. The procedure is then repeated until all the stream
needs are satisﬁed and they are brought to their target temperatures. At this point a heat
exchanger network with the energy consumption chosen at point 2 is built.
38
==PAGE:61==
4.3. Process Retroﬁt by using Pinch Analysis
7. Identiﬁcation of an optimal network. The seventh step regards the realization of an
almost optimal retroﬁt. The previous procedure is repeated seeking either a solution
that achieves the same energy targets with a lower capital investment, or a design that
reaches lower energy consumption. The latter means that more pinch violations are
included in the matrix procedure. It has to be noticed that this increase in savings is
normally paid with a higher increase in costs.
8. ∆Tmin optimisation. The eight, and ﬁnal, step consists in the optimisation of the ∆Tmin
chosen in the very beginning of the methodology. The whole procedure is repeated for
different values of ∆Tmin and the optimal solution is identiﬁed.
4.3.3
Limiting Match Method
The Limiting Match Method (LMM) is a simpliﬁed retroﬁt methodology developed by the
Technical University of Denmark (Copenhagen, Denmark) in cooperation with COWI A/S
[47][9]. It proposes as a simplifying tool to be used separately, simultaneously or in series with
the aforementioned traditional retroﬁt method.
This tool is founded on the recognition that in many cases it is detrimental to consider the
whole process for retroﬁt designs. In fact, for the majority of the plants just few streams give a
relevant contribution to the savings potential and are connected to economically interesting
solutions from an industrial perspective. The basic idea behind this method is therefore to
reduce the size of the problem before analysing it in a complete retroﬁt project. The goal
is to consider only the most interesting streams and disregard the uninteresting ones. This
simpliﬁcation is achieved through experience and by means of some experience-based and
analytically-based criteria which constitute the core of the methodology.
The method is divided in four steps:
1. Black-box division. This step consists in the local optimisation of the process. The
system is divided in possible sub-problems based on factors like location, temperature
and/or time. Experience is fundamental in choosing the right sub-division. Each of
them is then evaluated alone and optimised through a traditional process integration
study. As always, economic criteria are preferred over thermodynamic ones for the
deﬁnition of the optimum.
2. Stream matter simpliﬁcation. In this step a ﬁrst simpliﬁcation is performed, based on
the ﬂows nature. By experience it was noticed that industries often require high amount
of water for heating an cleaning. Therefore a ﬁrst simpliﬁcation is performed on the cold
streams, focusing on hot water production, at ﬁrst. All the other ﬂows are disregarded
(e.g. phase change ﬂows, gas).
3. Limiting Match Method. This step consists in the ﬁnal stream number reduction. The
basic idea behind the LMM is to connect the streams characteristics with their eco-

Conclusion
The present thesis builds on Process Integration methodologies based on pinch analysis. The

will be used further in thesis.

5 System modelling and assumptions
The development of models for industrial processes presents numerous challenges. Generally,
a high number of processes undergo and are integrated in the same facility, resulting in a
complex structure of the overall system. Moreover, reliable informations regarding physical
properties of the operating products are often scarce, especially when chemical reactions
or heat treatments are performed. These two are key challenges in the modelling of food
industries in general, and dairy industries in the speciﬁc case of this thesis.
While precise and detailed models are more reliable because closer to the real behaviour of the
processes, they risk to lead to unreasonably complex systems representations, whose effort in
the development is not repaid with a corresponding increase in precision. The only way of
avoiding complicated models is the introduction of system simpliﬁcations and simplifying
assumptions which are not paid by a too high reduction of reliability.
This chapter presents all the key assumptions and relationships utilised for the modelling and
analysis of the two dairy industries considered in the thesis. It comprises the assumptions on
dairy products physical properties, the case studies modelling and the methodology followed
for performing the economic analysis.
5.1
Milk properties
When modelling dairy industries, it is crucial to identify the main properties of milk and their
variations during all the processes through which the milk is driven. In fact, they have a direct
impact on fundamental parameters for a PI analysis, such as speciﬁc heat and heat transfer
coefﬁcient. This is a particularly hard task for dairy products, due to the fact that milk is a
diluted dispersion of several substances whose composition is not constant and which are not
in equilibrium [56] [57]. Freshly drawn milk shows, in fact, high variability of composition,
structure and properties. This is caused by many factors, such as the genetic characteristics of
the used breed and individual cow, the stage of lactation, the cow’s feed, illness of cows and
external contamination of oxygen, bacteria and other agents. However, the variability of milk

fed to dairy industries is reduced by the fact that it is a mixture of milk produced by a high
number of cows located in many different farms. Therefore its average composition is pretty

presence of oxygen, light, enzymes and microbes. Moreover, at the factory milk is processed,
causing high changes in its properties as it is intended to do.

Load factor
As mentioned in previous sections, the studied processes are not continuous, but downtime
is required especially for cleaning. Even though parallel lines are adopted in order to reduce
stops in production, not all the equipments work at the same time and not all of them work
at full capacity for the whole year. However, aiming at complexity reduction, the processes
were considered continuous. Moreover, no data regarding the equipments size and capacity
were available, but only yearly aggregated ones. An assumption regarding the operational time
during the year was therefore required.
In order to size the equipments and perform a complete pinch analysis for both the plants it
was assumed that the process is continuously operated for 20 hours per day (leaving 4 hours
for cleaning), 365 days per year. Start-up and Shut-down time was neglected. This leads to
7300 hours of operation per year.
The mass ﬂow rate of each stream was calculated from the yearly value based on this assump-
tion, determining the size of equipments.

regenerative pasteurisers and are composed of various sections embedded in the same unit.
However, in the model they were treated as series of different heat exchangers, each of them
representing one section of the real unit. Cost wise this is a conservative assumption, because
the investment of the resulting heat exchanger is expected to be lower in practice (for single

5.3
Economic analysis
When talking about real projects the main focus is on economy. Investment costs and eco-
nomic beneﬁt of the project need to be evaluated and proved before it can be accepted and
realised. Otherwise, plant’s owners would not consider the investment. In order to evaluate the
projects economy the Discounted Cash Flow analysis was utilised, using a private economic
approach [68]. The main strength of this method is that it considers the time value of money
and the fact that it changes over time (normally decreasing).
The goal of the economic analysis, for both retroﬁt and grassroot designs conducted in this
thesis, was to compare the economic performance of the modiﬁed plants with the ones of the
actually operating facilities. Considering this viewpoint, most of the voices in an economic
balance are neglected because they would be the same for both the cases. Among these,
operation and maintenance costs, revenues from sales and raw material costs are disregarded.
In order to perform the analysis three parameters are required: Expenditures, Revenues and
the choice of an Economic performance indicator.
5.3.1
Expenditures
The main expenditures of a plant are normally grouped in (1) Investment costs, (2) Operation
and Maintenance costs, (3) Energy costs and (4) Raw material costs. However, being the goal
of this thesis a comparison between the actual and the modiﬁed plants, they were reduced at

Conclusion
The development of models for industrial processes requires numerous assumptions in order
to limit the complexity of the study. This chapter presented the main assumptions and
methods used in order to model the dairy industries under study.
The biggest difﬁculties were presented by the estimation of dairy product properties under
varying conditions, e.g. density and speciﬁc heat capacity. Their behaviour was approximated
based on data found in literature and rule of thumb commonly used in the industry.
Another challenge was presented by the modelling of the processes under analysis, in order
to keep the analysis as simple as possible. To achieve this, the focus was kept on the heat
exchangers network and the plant operation was assumed continuous.
Finally, the economic analysis methodology followed in the thesis was presented. It is based
on the discounted cash ﬂow analysis for feasibility studies.

6 Process Retroﬁt Results
There exist multiple methodologies for PI retroﬁt designs, which differ in spotting heat recovery
opportunities, complexity in their application and time consumption. Moreover, by applying
them the designer is required to take many assumptions and decisions, which lead to almost
unlimited combinations. Awareness regarding the effect of the main choices is therefore
crucial for the engineer, in order to avoid misleading calls during the project, which can lead
to a signiﬁcant waste of time and, eventually, to detrimental designs.
One of the goals of this thesis is to analyse the existing process integration methods, in retroﬁt
cases. It covers various aspects such as the amount of data required, their complexity and their
suitability. The study was conducted based on the methodologies presented in Section 4.3.
This chapter presents the results of such investigation in case of a retroﬁt project. Both the
plants were considered for a traditional retroﬁt, while a deeper methodological analysis was
carried out only on the Cheese plant, which is the reference one. Such analysis compre-
hends the impact assessment of the global minimum temperature difference and the streams
individual minimum temperature differences on the design. Moreover, the two simpliﬁed
methodologies presented in Section 4.3 are applied on the Cheese plant and their strengths
and weaknesses discussed and compared to the traditional retroﬁt methodology.

6.4.1
Method
The method was applied by using the pure matrix procedure as described in Section 4.3.2.
According to it, not all the plant was analysed, but just a simpliﬁed sub-system composed
by the most relevant pinch violations. The same 12 streams selected during the traditional
retroﬁt were utilised, so that the two methodologies could be comparable. The so obtained

input matrices were required: (1) Hot streams data (comprised of utilities), (2) Cold streams
data (comprised of utilities), (3) Existing heat exchangers area and (4) Streams distances. For
simplicity, the existing heat recovery water loop was treated as a normal utility, assuming
that eventual energy unbalances would be supplied by additional external utilities. Once the
required informations were collected, a MATLAB® script was developed in order to calculate
the cost matrix at each step. It is presented in Appendix E. The procedure was therefore
followed as explained before.

6.4.3
Method review
The development of the MATLAB® script and the application of the matrix method on the
cheese plant offered many reasons for reﬂection regarding the beneﬁts of this methodol-
ogy. Moreover, some causes of the unsatisfactory performance and some suggestions of
improvements were spotted. They are presented below.
66
==PAGE:89==
6.4. Matrix method
Weaknesses
The poor quality of the results is caused by many factors:
• A ﬁrst weakness of the method is the poor clarity in the explanation of the match
selection criteria. A vague suggestion is made regarding the choice of inexpensive
matches ﬁrst and the usage of own experience. The two approaches utilised in this
thesis were meant to test the effectiveness of a selection based on costs. The obtained
results clearly show that the cost evaluation at each step does not lead to satisfying
designs. This is retained a big ﬂaw of the simpliﬁed methodology, because one of the
strengths presented by its developers was exactly the possibility to evaluate the retroﬁt
cost along the design procedure. However, it is true that just two strategies were analysed
and, by gaining more familiarity with the method, better results could be potentially
achieved.
• A second weakness is related to the control of the designer and the suggestions that the
methodology provides in taking decisions. This method was presented as a tool by which
"The performance of each implemented decision is constantly monitored and evaluated,
giving the designer the possibility to identify bad options and formulate better ones".
None of these features was veriﬁed during this thesis. In fact, the methodology takes the
designer along a high number of cost matrices for which, as explained in the previous
point, the cost does not give any suggestion on how to perform a good design. Moreover,
each decision affects the subsequent choices in a way that is not clearly shown to the
engineer. Therefore, it is very difﬁcult to trace back the cause of poorly performing
networks, especially when a high number of matrices (therefore decisions) are analysed.
Finally, the engineer does not have full control on the design path. In fact, for every
step the cost matrix evaluates only matches that achieve the maximum possible heat
exchange between two streams. However, sometimes it would be wiser to place a heat
exchanger able to partly integrate two streams, because this would allow better matches
later on during the design.
• The methodology does not suggest a wise usage of the existing area. Very often matches
are suggested where heat exchangers are already placed because of their nil cost. How-
ever, by using the existing heat exchanger in the recommended conﬁguration at this step,
only part of its area would be utilised. In this way the existing area is not fully exploited
and higher investment is expected to be needed afterwards. The methodology does not
warn the designer about such detrimental choices but, on the contrary, it incentivizes
their adoption.
• The HEX investment cost does not seem a relevant parameter for the choice of beneﬁcial
matches. This is caused by the fact that heat exchangers with lower duty are very likely
to need less area, thus a lower investment cost. In this way, looking at costs, investments
in small HEX are preferable over large HEX, even though they achieve a small stream
temperature variation. Due to this choice investment in many units is needed, with the
67
==PAGE:90==
Chapter 6. Process Retroﬁt Results
consequent high overall retroﬁt cost. Other parameters could be more signiﬁcant for
the engineer, for example a speciﬁc cost calculated as the ratio between investment cost
and heat exchanged by the unit.
• The methodology requires many decisions (i.e. many matrices) in order to complete the
network. Moreover, every decision results in the placement of a new heat exchanger or
the enhancement of an existing one. This means that at almost every step an investment
is made. Taking as example the ﬁrst approach studied in this thesis, 22 iterations were
needed, which resulted in 15 new units placed in the sole network above the pinch.
On the other hand, by using the traditional methodology, 7 new heat exchangers were
sufﬁcient for the whole retroﬁt. This higher number of units is likely to result in higher
investment costs and higher network complexity, both undesired outcomes of a PI
project.
Strengths
Due to the low performance spotted, few strengths were identiﬁed. The most important is that
the method allows the engineer interaction during the design path, while giving an overview
of the costs of each action. Therefore an expert designer has the chance to balance the afore-
mentioned weaknesses with his experience and, eventually, beneﬁt of the cost informations
that no other tool provides.
In conclusion, the matrix method appears to be something in between an intuition-based
methodology, in which the engineer is fully in control of the retroﬁt path, and a computer
driven process, in which a software takes decisions based on some optimisation target. By
doing so, it rejects the beneﬁts of both of them, but it presents all their deﬁcits. In fact, the
designer does not have the freedom allowed by the traditional methodology and neither the
strong mathematical aid of a MINLP software. On the other hand it is half blinded in his
choices, as it happens in a computer driven method, but at the same time he cannot really
trust the suggestions provided by the methodology. For these reasons it is concluded that
this is an incomplete methodology, which does not simplify pinch analysis, but it rather
complicates it.

Method review
The application of the Limiting Match method on the cheese plant showed some strengths
and weaknesses of this simpliﬁed methodology. Based on them, an overall review is presented
in the following.
Strengths
The methodology presents these strengths:
• A ﬁrst strength is the ability of providing an indicator able to connect the thermodynamic
and economic dimensions of the problem. In this way, the designer have a general
overview of the streams importance for the purpose of achieving a feasible Process
Integration design. Therefore he can take informed decisions on process simpliﬁcations
able to reduce the time required for the project completion.
• Another strength point is the simplicity of the method. In fact, the procedure is short
to be applied and keeps the engineer in charge of the decision making throughout the
whole process, while providing useful simpliﬁcation suggestions. No speciﬁc computer
software is required, but the calculations can be easily performed by using Microsoft
Excel® or similar programs. In this way it is accessible to everybody.

• The last strength is that the LMM recognizes the necessity of keeping the designer in
charge for all the PI project and the decision making. Moreover, it acknowledges the
usefulness of the traditional retroﬁt methodology in performing good retroﬁts under
energy, economic and operability viewpoints. Therefore, it does not propose as an
alternative tool with respect with the traditional one, but as an integrative instrument
for simplifying its usage.
Weaknesses
Despite the good results obtained, some weaknesses were spotted, which offer possible
improvements in the methodology.
• A ﬁrst weakness is presented by the fact that the main simpliﬁcation criteria considers
the streams energy requirements instead of the utility consumption that they cause.
In this way, highly integrated streams which require high heat amounts, but are not
causing signiﬁcant utility consumption are still considered for retroﬁt, even though they
are probably not relevant for possible modiﬁcations. Therefore they just increase the
complexity of the process to be analysed by the engineer.
• The second weakness is connected to the previous one and represented by the fact
that the simpliﬁcation criteria fails in distinguishing the importance of streams with
high energy duties. This concept is shown in Figure 6.3a. As it can be seen, the ∆Tlm
requirement curve is very ﬂat if the energy requirement is higher than 200 kW. This
means that the ∆Tlm threshold variation operated in the method is not effective in
distinguishing different streams if their duty is higher than 200kW. This means that
potentially no simpliﬁcations can be achieved on plants composed by only big streams,
even though some of them might not be relevant for a retroﬁt because already highly
integrated (as explained in the previous point). Moreover, looking at Figure 6.3b, it can
be seen that this problem is enhanced if higher PBT are considered, because the ∆Tlm
requirement curve is ﬂatter and ﬂatter for higher Pay Back Times.
• Probably the greatest weakness is the missing of an indicator able to tell the designer
when to stop reducing the ∆Tlm threshold and set the ﬁnal simpliﬁcation. In fact, as it
is today, the engineer does not know when it is most beneﬁcial to stop the simpliﬁca-
tion because the minimum number of streams that allow to perform a good retroﬁt is
achieved. It is much of a "shooting in the dark" procedure, in which the designer needs
to be conservative and set a high threshold in order not to miss good opportunities. This
results in the fact that in the performed calculations, the simpliﬁcation was stopped
having 36 streams, out of which just 19 are beneﬁcial for the ﬁnal retroﬁt.
• Finally, the LMM does not perform any simpliﬁcation on data collection, but just on the
retroﬁt design. Therefore, no time consumption reduction is achieved on the longest,
and less efﬁcient, task of the retroﬁt.

in investment costs, PBT and NPV. An optimisation study should be conducted in order to
choose the most proﬁtable ∆Tmin.

performance do not vary much if compared with the usage of a global value. However, such
result only shows that the assumption on the global ∆Tmin was not distant from the global

outcome is related to the importance of using individual ∆Tmin in place of a global one: by
choosing such option the precision of the retroﬁt is higher, being closer to the performance of
a real implementation. However, the choice of the individual values is complicated and rough
estimations have to be made.
Finally, a comparison of the three different retroﬁt methodologies studied in the thesis was
made. The matrix method proved to be not interesting for an industrial utilisation, being too
complex for application to big plants and achieving bad performing retroﬁts. The traditional
methodology resulted to be the most precise in spotting beneﬁcial retroﬁt path, but it was
also long to be applied. The Limiting match method showed interesting simpliﬁcations, able
to reduce the analysis time without loosing much savings potential. Moreover its ability
of connecting the streams characteristics to economic considerations gives the designer an
important indicator absent in the traditional method. However, some reﬁnement is retained to
be necessary, especially in an indicator able to tell the analyst when to stop the simpliﬁcation.

7 Speciﬁc Savings Potential method
The high precision of pinch analysis in detecting PI potentials is widely recognized, as well
as its complexity, which discourages its utilization in small and intermediate scale indus-
tries. Acknowledging such facts, this thesis has the primary goal of developing a simpliﬁed
methodology able to reduce the complexity of pinch analysis as it is nowadays used in indus-
try. The aforementioned analysis serve this purpose, by giving a deeper insight of strengths
and weaknesses of already developed Process Integration methodologies. In this way, an
overview of barriers and opportunities in the development of a better performing methodol-
ogy were detected. By exploiting their main strengths and ﬁxing their main weaknesses, a new
methodology, called Speciﬁc Savings Potential method (SSP) was developed.
This chapter presents the SSP method and provides results obtained by applying it for the
retroﬁt of both the Cheese and Milk plants already considered by using the traditional retroﬁt
methodology.
7.1
The method
The retroﬁt methodology developed during this thesis is based on the idea that the traditional
pinch method is already complete for what concerns the detection of beneﬁcial retroﬁt paths,
if an energy viewpoint is considered. What it lacks of, on the other hand, is an efﬁcient
screening tool by which uninteresting streams are disregarded, considering energy recovery
potential, operability, safety and economy. This could drastically reduce the time consumption
of the design, increasing the efﬁciency of the whole procedure. The main effort was therefore
focused on completing the traditional methodology with some simpliﬁcation criteria, able
to reduce the size of the problem by detecting and disregarding uninteresting streams for a
feasible retroﬁt.
The traditional pinch retroﬁt procedure presents two nodes that altogether require roughly
85% of the whole design time: Data collection and Network design. In order to save time, it
would be beneﬁcial to reduce the size of the problem as much as possible before applying
77
==PAGE:100==
Chapter 7. Speciﬁc Savings Potential method
these two steps. Founded on this strategy, the Speciﬁc Savings Potential method is composed
of the three following simpliﬁcation steps.
First simpliﬁcation
The ﬁrst simpliﬁcation step is performed before the data collection. At this stage no detailed
data regarding the processes is easily available, but only informations about the utility con-
sumption of each stream. This type of model is the so called "Grey-box model". However, based
on just these few data, a ﬁrst simpliﬁcation can be made grounded on an analytical criteria
and some experience.
1. Simpliﬁcation based on utility consumption. The percentage of utility consumption
caused by each stream with respect to the whole plant is calculated. A threshold is
set and all the streams with consumption lower than the so deﬁned minimum one
are disregarded. In order to stay on the safe side, the aggregated consumption of the
remaining streams should not be lower than 85% of the whole plant (which is considered
safe because higher than the 80% suggested by the Pareto’s principle [79]). Clearly,
whenever two streams are connected (through, e.g., a heat exchanger or a heat recovery
loop), it is not possible to ignore one of them and consider only the other. In this case
they are both considered in the reduced sub-network, even though one of them has an
utility consumption lower than the threshold.
2. Simpliﬁcation based on experience. The selected streams are screened another time and
another selection is made based on timing, safety concerns or other criteria derived
from experience. In case of lack of the necessary expertise this step can be skipped with
minor harm to the simpliﬁcation efﬁciency of the SSP method.
At this point no further simpliﬁcations can be made based on a gray-box model.
Second simpliﬁcation
3. Data collection. A full data collection is performed on the previously selected streams,
as done in the traditional procedure. Thanks to this the model becomes the so called
"White-box" one.

7.3
Discussion
The Speciﬁc Savings Potential method proved to be beneﬁcial in the two applications studied
during this thesis. In fact, thanks to the three simpliﬁcation steps, the size of the problem
was subsequently reduced, until spotting the smallest (or close to the smallest) sub-network
required for completing a beneﬁcial retroﬁt. It is opinion of the author that the major strength
and cause of the success of the method is precisely the wise choice of the three simpliﬁ-
cation criteria. In fact, they are able to compensate the major ﬂaws of the other studied
methodologies. More in details:
1. First simpliﬁcation. The ﬁrst simpliﬁcation has the merit of reducing the size of the
problem at the very beginning of the project, even before the detailed data acquisition.
In fact, only the utility consumption of each stream is needed for its completion, which
are easily available informations obtainable from factory set-points and the control
system. This can potentially avoid a signiﬁcant amount of time, being Data collection
the principal cause of time waste. Moreover, such tool was missing not only in the
84
==PAGE:107==
7.3. Discussion
three methodologies analysed during the thesis, but also in all the ones revised in the
literature. Looking next at the indicator used in order to perform the simpliﬁcation, a
selection based on the percentage utility consumption appears to be logical. In fact, a
small utility consumption of a stream can be caused by two possible reasons: (1) the
stream has a small duty compared to the rest (i.e. small heat requirement) or (2) the
stream is already highly integrated in the network. In either cases, it does not make
sense to consider it for a retroﬁt, because it does not cause a signiﬁcant increase in
consumption.
Finally, the possibility to perform a simpliﬁcation based on experience is a fundamental
tool for a PI methodology. In fact, despite the help that the various methodologies can
provide to the designer, he needs always to be in charge for taking decisions. There are
so many different plants in the industry and so many possibilities in operating systems
that creativity plays a vital role in a good design. Only the engineer can add this value
through his own experience.
2. Second simpliﬁcation. This is the most important step of the whole methodology. The
major merit of the used indicator is that it is able to signal to the designer when to stop
in simplifying the network based on savings potential. This feature solves one of the
major weaknesses of the Limiting Match Method, which is exactly the absence of such
signal. Looking next at the indicator nature, the Speciﬁc Savings Content appears to be
a reasonable optimisation parameter. In fact, representing the average savings potential
of the network streams, it is able to link the energy performance of the sub-network to
its complexity in performing the ﬁnal design. Moreover, as shown by the results, it is
able to spot the losses of very important streams, which are highlighted by signiﬁcant
drops of σ. Even though further validation of the method should be performed, this
feature is expected to function in every case, due to the fact that streams responsible for
high pinch violations always embed a high savings potential. Therefore, a loss of just
one of them will always cause a remarkable drop of σ.
3. Third simpliﬁcation. This last step has the merit of connecting the sub-network charac-
teristics to economic considerations. In doing so, it is quite similar to what achieved by
the Limiting Match Method, whose main strength was exactly in the usage of such kind
of criteria. However, the viewpoint adopted by the SSP method is quite different. In fact,
in this case, the size of the pinch violations is evaluated, and not the hypothetical heat
exchange for satisfying the streams needs, as in the LMM. In this way, the evaluation is
faster and more signiﬁcant. In fact, having the violation size threshold set, the simpliﬁca-
tion can be done just by looking at the pinch violations, avoiding additional calculations.
Moreover, it is more signiﬁcant because it better evaluates the possible beneﬁt of retroﬁt
actions. Once the maximum acceptable PBT and maximum acceptable ∆Tlm are set,
the minimum proﬁtable heat exchanger size threshold is univocally deﬁned. This sets a
lower limit to the proﬁtability of ﬁxing pinch violations. In fact, in order to eliminate a
violation, at least one heat exchanger with a duty equal to the violation itself is needed.
In such case, if the ∆Tlm of such unit is lower than the maximum deﬁned, the designer
85
==PAGE:108==
Chapter 7. Speciﬁc Savings Potential method
is sure that the investment is economically feasible. On the contrary, if the duty is lower
than the threshold, the engineer is certain that the investment is not proﬁtable, hence
no further analysis should be performed. In a real design, it is most likely to happen
that in order to ﬁx a pinch violations multiple units need to be modiﬁed and investment
in other equipment (e.g. piping or tanks) is necessary. In such case the minimum
feasible duty of one HEX would be higher. For this reason, the threshold suggested by
the procedure is just a lower limit. It does not ensure that all the considered violations
lead to feasible designs, but it indicates the ones that are surely infeasible. Anyway it
has the potential of reducing the engineering effort.
Despite the aforementioned beneﬁts, this methodology presents various limitations and a
high potential of improvements. First of all, it is based on heuristic considerations, rather
than analytical demonstrations. Therefore, (1) it is not generalizable at all the possible plants
and (2) it should be validated on a broader number of cases, in order to assert an almost
general validity. With this being said, it is retained that the beneﬁt of the SSP method would be
conﬁrmed not only in the dairy industry, but in general in the food industry. In fact, all these
applications show similar heating requirements, being composed mainly by liquid/liquid plate
heat exchangers for performing heat treatments. Based on this, similar Process Integration
problems are expected to be encountered. On the other hand, the method validity cannot be
claimed with the same conﬁdence in other types of industries. In fact, they perform different
processes and they do not work with only liquid ﬂuids. The SSP method should be proved on
them in order to test its validity and suggest modiﬁcations if needed. However, also in this
case, if used combined to experience, it is expected to give good suggestions to the designer.
The second main limitation of this method is that it only considers continuous processes. In
reality, most of the processes in the food industry are semi-continuous or even batch. However,
the main goal of the SSP methodology is to provide a simple tool able to tackle the main Process
Integration measures in the shortest time possible. In this meaning, a precision reduction is
accepted in order to reduce the complexity. Moreover, the suggestions provided by applying
the SSP can be useful even in case of a batch operation, because anyway it identiﬁes the most
promising matches. Based on them and on further considerations about batch operation a
faster design can be achieved.
A third limitation is constituted by the restriction of application on retroﬁt projects only. The
development of a methodology able to strike also grassroot designs would be beneﬁcial.
Finally, as aforementioned, this method is new and further optimization should be performed
on it. Applying the methodology to other real cases, a better calibration of the simpliﬁcation
criteria can be achieved, increasing precision and efﬁciency of the whole procedure. In
particular, more investigations should be performed on the variations of σ changing the
threshold, in order to test if it always shows an optimum value and if the optimum always
spots a beneﬁcial simpliﬁcation. Therefore, considering the early stage of development, a
signiﬁcant potential for improvement is expected. However, it is opinion of the author that the

general strategy behind the methodology should not be changed, because the SSP truly acts on
the most critical nodes of the traditional pinch method. Moreover, it is retained fundamental
for the methodology to always keep the designer in charge for decision making and informed
of the development of the project. The creativity contribution provided by an expert engineer
cannot be replaced by any method. The perfect PI method gives suggestions, not answers.
7.4
Conclusion
The simpliﬁed retroﬁt methodology developed during the thesis, named Speciﬁc Savings
Potential method (SSP), was described and applied to the Cheese and Milk plants. It is aimed
at completing the traditional methodology by inserting screening criteria able to reduce the
engineering effort in performing the retroﬁt. This result is achieved by three simpliﬁcation
steps, which help the designer to detect and disregard unimportant streams. The ﬁrst is
performed before data acquisition and based on streams utility consumption. The second
and the third are utilised before the ﬁnal retroﬁt. One performs a simpliﬁcation grounded on
speciﬁc savings content of each stream and the other on economic criteria.
The methodology was validated by applying it to the two plants already studied by means
of the traditional retroﬁt method. The results conﬁrmed the good performance of the SSP
procedure. In fact, it was able to shrink the PI problem down until considering only the
most important streams and, based on the size of pinch violations, it indicated if a retroﬁt
would have been feasible or not. This allowed a signiﬁcant reduction of time consumption in
performing the whole retroﬁt.
Finally, the method has the potential of becoming a general procedure mainly for food industry,
but also for other industries. However, further optimisation and validation work should be
done, in order to improve the method performance and test its limits of application.

8 Grassroot design results
On the contrary of retroﬁt, few Process Integration methodologies are available for performing
grassroot industrial designs. As today, the most diffused is still the traditional pinch analysis.
However, this methodology aims principally at energy or economic optimisation, but it does
not consider the most important requirements of a plant, which are operability and safety.
Established knowledge and experience are therefore the main drivers for a good design.
The recent literature is poor of grassroot design examples, probably for their scarce interest
from an academic point of view after the thorough investigations conducted in the ’80s and
’90s. However, such a study is retained relevant if the aim is to discover criteria for helping
the designer in performing such project. Moreover, it would be interesting to compare the
different engineering experiences in performing the retroﬁt and the grassroot designs of the
same plant. Aiming at these two goals, the grassroot design was performed on the Cheese
plant already studied during this thesis.
This chapter presents the utilised method and obtained results of this analysis. Finally, it
proposes a discussion with suggestions on how to reduce the engineering effort in performing
such task.
8.1
Method
The grassroot design of the Cheese plant was performed based on the traditional pinch
methodology presented in Section 4.2. However, in order to avoid operability issues, the design
was not executed seeking the Maximum Energy Recovery target, as the method indicates,
but approaching it as much as possible by ensuring the feasibility of operation. The design
strategy can be summarised in the following steps:
1. Divide the problem in two separate regions: above and below the pinch. The design is
started from the pinch and conducted moving away from it, ensuring the traditional
methodology feasibility criteria at the pinch. For all the matches, the individual mini-
89
==PAGE:112==

mum temperature difference of each stream is utilised, in place of a global one.
2. First consider matches between streams belonging to the same section, even though
little pinch violations have to be accepted. To do so the existing plant conﬁguration was
taken as inspiration. Streams with similar heat capacity and targets are preferred for
pairing.
3. After matching all the possible streams according to the previous criteria, consider direct
heat exchange between streams part of different departments. However, disregard small
duty heat exchangers and accept pinch violations instead.
4. When streams with different duties but similar temperature range remains, consider to
include them in a heat recovery water loop. The number of loops and loop temperatures
are set by evaluating the composite curves of the heat recovery problem. In order for the
loop to be feasible, a straight line needs to lay in between the process composite curves
without violating the minimum temperature difference of the various streams.
5. Finally, consider network simpliﬁcations.
For sake of simplicity, a conceptual version of the Cheese plant was studied, in lieu of the
actually operating process. To do so the various parallel lines were aggregated in single lines.
In this way the operation principle of the process was maintained while reducing the streams
number.
The design was performed in two steps, differing for the grade of detail. The ﬁrst one con-
sidered the plant design based on the assumption of continuous operation, as it was done
in the rest of the thesis. The second step considered the ﬁnal design accounting for the time
schedule of each stream during an operating day. It will be referred to as "Semi-continuous
operation".

8.3
Discussion
Performing the grassroot design on the cheese plant, many challenges were faced, showing
critical problems in the design development. Moreover, it was not possible to ﬁnd a simpli-
ﬁed methodology able to reduce the engineering effort in performing such task. Only few
suggestions on how to approach the problem were formulated. They are all presented in the
following.
The main challenge in performing a grassroot design, if compared to a retroﬁt one, is the lack
of a methodology able to focus the engineer on the important details. In fact, many retroﬁt
procedures provide a logical structure to be followed, which guides the engineer through the
design and advices him on the best decisions to be taken. On the contrary, the traditional
pinch methodology for grassroot design, is only able to provide targets and a detailed strategy
just in the pinch zone, while elsewhere it does not advice the engineer on how to proceed.
This lack of method is caused by the different approach in a grassroot design, than in a retroﬁt.
In fact, while in the latter the plant structure is already in place and the engineer’s freedom is
constrained by it, the former shows much less boundaries to the designer’s creativity and to
the possible design conﬁgurations. The possible set-ups can be a signiﬁcant amount, due to
the combinatorial nature of the problem. The main dilemma caused by such freedom regards
the plant operability. While for a retroﬁt the designer has the existing plant example in order
to know what is feasible, in a grassroot project it is hard to ensure the project operability. The
possible designs are so many that no methodologies, according to the author’s knowledge,
succeeded in deﬁning a more detailed approach to be used. Such scarcity of method calls
for great experience in order to complete a good design under both the economic and the
operability viewpoints.
The design process was analysed during its application, in order to ﬁnd possible simpliﬁcation
criteria. However, this attempt did not succeed. The main reason for this is that the biggest
problem is caused by the high number of streams, which increases the number on network
alternatives in an exponential way. When discussing about simpliﬁcation methods for retroﬁt,
the successful idea was to reduce the number of streams. However, this process cannot be
performed on a grassroot case, because all the streams needs have to be satisﬁed. Despite
such failure, some suggestions were made, regarding the most beneﬁcial approach to be used
while performing the design. They are listed below:
• At a ﬁrst stage, consider the conceptual operation of the plant, in order to discover
Process Integration patterns. This is composed just by the processes strictly necessary
for the production. Later, design all the parallel lines following them. In this way
the number of streams (thus network combinations) is lowered, reducing the design
complexity.
• In case of semi-continuous processes, ﬁrstly consider the design under the assumption
of continuous and contemporary streams operation. In this way the main integration
100
==PAGE:123==

opportunities can be spotted using a system reduced in complexity. Later, this assump-
tion can be eliminated and the possibility of including heat storage can be taken in
consideration.
• Prefer matches between streams belonging to the same process section. It is more likely
to happen that they can be connected by direct heat exchange when the time dimension
is considered for the design. In this way cost and complexity of the plant control system
can be reduced.
• When considering a match, favour streams with similar heat capacity rate and temper-
atures. In this way their integration is generally enhanced, because the satisfaction of
one stream thermal needs approaches the satisfaction of the other ﬂow.
• Do not consider to place heat exchangers of small duty, unless it is strictly necessary.
This is caused by the fact that their investment cost is not repaid by energy savings in
reasonable time. A duty threshold should be set based on both economic and thermo-
dynamic parameters, as shown in the Limiting Match Method (Section 6.5).
• When designing a heat recovery water loop comprised of storage tanks, favour simplicity
to savings. This will allow more freedom of operation to the plant.
The suggestions listed above are advices directly taken from the experience gained during this
thesis. As such, they should be taken in consideration as simple suggestions, indeed, and not
as a method to be rigorously applied.
8.4
Conclusion
This chapter presented the method and results of the grassroot design project conducted on
the cheese plant already studied in case of retroﬁt during this thesis. Based on the ﬁndings,
possible methodology simpliﬁcations were sought.
The design was ﬁrstly conducted under the assumption of continuous operation. Different
conﬁgurations were considered, showing that a 37.4% reduction in utility consumption can
be achieved by accepting a PBT=5.9 years if compared to the investment in the existing
plant design (Case (d)). Afterwards, the continuous process assumption was eliminated,
introducing the time dimension in the design. This required the design of heat recovery water
loops equipped with heat storage tanks. Different system conﬁgurations were considered,
showing that such operation requires tanks of reasonable dimensions and low investment
cost (up to 8.4MDKK), if compared to the network cost.
Finally, no methodological simpliﬁcation possibilities were spotted. The degrees of freedom in
performing grassroot designs were found to be so many that only great experience is retained
able to successfully tackle a good design. Despite of this, some advices were presented, in
order to help the engineer in conducting this task. They do not pretend to be part of a design

9 Data accuracy
Data collection and extraction is a crucial task in process integration projects. Results depend
largely on input data, as they constitute the whole foundation of the analysis when a real
project is performed. However this task is often overlooked, resulting in high uncertainties in
the project outcomes. One of the reasons is that most of the time dedicated to data collection
is revealed to be a waste with respect to the ﬁnal results. This would not be a problem if
this task did not require much time, but it is responsible for up to 80% of the total time of
the project [47]. Moreover, it consists of tedious measurements and extensive study of the
analysed process, as well as database scanning and frustrating researches for price collection
and for needed correlations.
Experience and the right skills are fundamental for a successful process integration study [82].
An engineer in possession of these prerequisites is able to distinguish the important data from
the unimportant ones, focusing only in the collection of the ﬁrst category. Moreover he knows
where to ﬁnd reliable correlations and prices. All in all, the work related to data collection is
reduced, diminishing the risk of misleading informations right at the beginning of the project.
However, even with the best expertise achievable, data collection is always accompanied
by quite high uncertainties, which are very difﬁcult to be reduced. This is in particular the
case of process parameters such as temperatures, mass ﬂows and heat transfer coefﬁcients,
and economic parameters such as investment costs and energy prices. It is therefore very
important to assess the extent of their inﬂuence on the project outcomes, in order to avoid
mistakes able to undermine the success of the study.
This chapter presents the method and results of the uncertainties impact investigation con-
ducted during the thesis. In particular, the inﬂuence of temperatures and mass ﬂows on the
pinch targeting procedure outcomes was investigated, in order to assess their importance re-
lated to the design decisions taken by the engineer. Moreover, the most important parameters
for the economic feasibility analysis were studied in order to determine their importance re-
lated to the design outcomes. The goal of such investigation was to detect the most inﬂuencing
parameters, in order to focus the effort in collecting preciser data regarding them.

9.1
Sources of uncertainties
Every model that aims to represent real processes is accompanied by many uncertainties.
According to the classiﬁcation presented by Kennedy and O’Hagan [83], this thesis shows four
kinds of uncertainties:
1. Parameter uncertainty. They come from input parameters to the model whose exact
value is not known because of lack of knowledge in their prediction. The most important
factors that cause such a problem in this thesis are: speciﬁc heat capacity (cp), overall
heat transfer coefﬁcient (U) and Purchased Equipment Cost.
2. Parametric variability. They come from random variations of the input parameters.
All the measurement errors are comprised within this category. The most important
uncertainties in this sense are caused by the temperature measurements on site.
3. Model inadequacy. The outcomes of the model are clearly dependent by the model
formulation. In this sense, every model simpliﬁcation that does not represent the
real process in a precise way causes uncertainties. The actual thesis is based on a
simpliﬁed representation of the real processes, which neglects important phenomena
such as chemical reactions in the various vessels, heat losses, friction losses and many
others. Such decision reduces the model precision. For example, by neglecting chemical
reactions the ﬂuid composition is wrongly estimated, by disregarding heat losses the
streams inlet temperatures are overestimated and by ignoring friction losses the working
pressures of the equipments are underestimated.
4. Residual variability. These uncertainties come from the unpredictability of the process,
which might vary some parameters still producing the same product. For example,
the various mass ﬂows and temperatures show a time variation around their set point,
mainly caused by control system inaccuracy. This produces an oscillation, which is
added to the aforementioned measurement errors. It can be argued that these are not
real uncertainties, because they are not a departure from the real value, but it is the
real value itself the one who changes. However, even if conceptually the difference is
remarkable, it does not have any for the model results. In both the cases, in fact, the
utilised input parameters for the model depart from the physical values, causing wrong
outcomes.

Heat transfer coefﬁcient, (6) Load hours, (7) Energy price, (8) Inﬂation rate, (9) Discount rate,
(10) COP and (11) Boiler’s efﬁciency are analysed.

9.2
Uncertainties modelling
The effect of uncertainties on the outcomes was investigated, in order to assess the inﬂuence
of data accuracy on the process integration study. Two different dimensions were analysed,
namely the process dimension and the economical dimension.
9.2.1
Process uncertainties
Most of the aforementioned uncertainties inﬂuence the study at the process level. This affects
the targeting procedure outcomes, i.e. the utility targets and the pinch point location. Mistakes
in such values are highly dangerous for they might be misleading and drive the analyst to non-
optimal designs. In fact an error in the utility targets results in an over- or underestimation
of the potential savings and is reﬂected by too high capital investments (which results in a
longer pay back time) or by low exploitation of savings (which results in lower proﬁt). The
wrong location of the pinch point has an even more dangerous effect, resulting in an incorrect
identiﬁcation of pinch violations and therefore in a potentially detrimental retroﬁt.
More in details, the following parameters cause high process uncertainties:
• Speciﬁc heat capacity (cp). An average cp value was calculated based on the average
stream temperature and the total solid content (Equation 5.4). However, the speciﬁc
heat capacity varies with both temperature and product composition [84], and varies
therefore at each process step. All these effects were not accounted for in this study,
mostly because of the difﬁculties to assess precisely these variations.
• Mass ﬂow ( ˙m). The considered mass ﬂows come from measurements performed in
the daily plant operation and from mass balances conducted based on them. These
parameters are therefore accompanied by three different kind of uncertainties. (1)
parametric variability related to the inaccuracies of the measurements, (2) residual
variability associated with time variations of the ﬂow rates and (3) model inadequacies
related to the assumptions of the unknown ﬂow rates. The ﬁrst two are expected to be
small because of the high precision of the control system, which is required to ensure
high product quality. The third one is particularly true for the splitters, is not related to
measurements and its effect is investigated further.
• Temperature (T ). The streams temperatures are accompanied by the same ﬁrst two
kind of uncertainties of the mass ﬂows: one caused by measurement errors and one by
time variations. Some of the data come from measurements performed on a daily basis,
which are highly precise for quality certiﬁcation. These informations are complemented
by measurements conducted by Viegand Maagøe ApS, which present lower accuracy be-
cause of the measurement instrument (1◦C accuracy), method (use of correlations), and
placement (sensor outside the pipe insulations). This is a bigger cause of uncertainties.
Due to the importance of temperature levels for the process integration study, it was

Mass ﬂow uncertainties
In order to assess the impact of mass ﬂow inaccuracy it is fundamental to notice that the
various mass ﬂows are not independent parameters, but they are interdependent. A model
able to ensure the mass balance throughout all the plant was therefore developed using
Simulink®. The block diagram and the input parameters are presented in Appendix E.
The input parameters to the model can be divided in two categories: measured mass ﬂows and
assumed splitting ratios. The ﬁrst ones are mass ﬂows measured on-site, related to plant feed
ﬂows or produced products. The second ones are assumed mass ﬂow ratios utilised in order to
model the splitting components. They come from two different sources: some were suggested
by plant operators, while the others are related to dairy products physical properties. They
present uncertainties due to experimental limitations and the impact of these inaccuracies is
investigated further.
In order to reduce the computational work, only the most signiﬁcant parameters were investi-
gated. Thus, the uncertainties assessment was conducted in two steps:
1. Identiﬁcation of the most important splitting ratios. The most important splitting ratios
were identiﬁed by means of a propagation analysis of uncertainties. Each splitting ratio
was varied alone by ±3% and, after using the energy targeting tool of pinch analysis, its
impact on energy targets and pinch point was considered. The most important splitting
ratios were then deﬁned as the ones which, in this range of variation, inﬂuence the
energy targets by more than 0.5% of their nominal value.
2. Splitting ratios uncertainties impact. The inaccuracy of splitting ratios was assumed to
be ±2%, after consultation with Viegand Maagøe ApS.

Temperature uncertainties
In order to asses the impact of temperature inaccuracy it has to be noticed that many temper-
atures are not independent parameters, but depend of each other. This is especially the case
of pasteurization heat exchangers, where after heating the streams up to the pasteurization
temperature, they are cooled before successive processes. Therefore, a model able to account
for these relationships was developed. It is based on three rules:
1. Hot streams input temperatures are equal to cold streams output temperatures when-
ever their respective heat exchangers are in series without any process vessel in between.
2. Cold streams input temperatures are independent parameters.
3. Hot streams output temperatures are independent parameters.
It has to be noticed that in reality many more relationships exist between the various tempera-
tures. In particular, the aforementioned rules do not take into account how vessels and tanks
inﬂuence and are inﬂuenced by their outlet and inlet streams respectively. Nevertheless, it
is impossible to account for such dependencies in a white-box model. Therefore they were
disregarded.
This model constitutes the basis of the uncertainties investigation. However, due to the large
number of temperatures that play a role in the analysis, it was decided to consider only the
most signiﬁcant ones. Therefore, the study was conducted in two separate steps:
1. Identiﬁcation of the most important temperatures. The most important temperatures
were identiﬁed by means of a propagation analysis of uncertainties. Each independent
temperature was varied alone by ±3◦C and, after using the energy targeting tool of
pinch analysis, its impact on energy targets and pinch point was considered. The most
important temperatures where then deﬁned as the ones which, in this range of variation,
inﬂuence the energy targets by more than 5% of their nominal value.
2. Temperature uncertainties impact. As aforementioned, there are two main sources
of uncertainty for the temperatures, one due to measurement errors and one due to
time variation. For each an inaccuracy of ±2◦C is estimated, based on discussion with
Viegand Maagøe ApS and analysis of their measurements (Figure 9.1).
In order to assess the combined inﬂuence of all the temperature uncertainties, Monte
Carlo simulations were conducted. No systematic uncertainties were identiﬁed, there-
fore both of the uncertainties were considered aleatory. They were simulated by adding
to each set point temperature an error, as described by Equation 9.2.

9.2.2
Economic uncertainties
The economic feasibility analysis performed in this thesis builds on many assumptions, which
involve the uncertainties described as follows. The quantiﬁcation of their impact is important,
as they may result in a wrong estimate of the economic proﬁtability and lead to improper
decisions.
The major sources of uncertainties for the economic analysis output are presented in the
following.
108
==PAGE:131==
9.2. Uncertainties modelling
• Cost correlations. The Purchased Equipment Cost of the various equipment was as-
sumed according to various sources, presented in Appendix B. They are correlations
linking the price to some capacity parameter, but they disregard many important factors
concurring in the cost deﬁnition. The most important among them are the market vari-
ability in price, the interpersonal relationship between costumer and seller and possible
discounts due to stock purchases. These uncertainties are accounted for, in some extent,
by the scaling factor utilised in calculating the Total Capital Investment (Equation 5.5).
All in all, an uncertainty of ±30% on the calculated TCI should be considered [86].
• Heat transfer coefﬁcient. The overall heat transfer coefﬁcient is crucial in the determina-
tion of the heat exchangers size and, in consequence, its cost. Moreover, the constant
value assumed in this thesis is accompanied by quite high uncertainties. In fact, in
reality it is dependent by many parameters such as ﬂow regime and viscosity of the ﬂuid.
Referring to the marked area in Figure 5.4, its real value could be comprised between
0.35 kW/m2K and 0.75 kW/m2K. This range is also conﬁrmed by Waltra [56].
• Load hours. The yearly load hours affect the volume of production and consumption
of the plant, inﬂuencing primarily the extent of energy savings achievable in one year.
This data can vary in relation to the production process and plant equipments (how
long downtime is required for cleaning) and by the market inﬂuence (the demand
volume). A variability between 16 hours/day and 22 hours/day could be considered,
always assuming 365 days/year of operation.
• Energy price. The energy price is crucial in the calculation of the projects revenues. How-
ever, energy prices show a high variability, which results in great uncertainties in their
deﬁnition and forecast. Normally, industries stipulate annual or biennial ﬁxed price
contracts with energy distribution companies, which guarantees a constant price for the
whole contract period. However, when they sign a new contract, the price changes ac-
cording to the market. Considering the historical price trend for gas and electricity [87],
a variability of ±15% and ±30% should be considered for gas and electricity respectively.
• Inﬂation rate. Inﬂation detects the increase in price levels throughout the years. In this
work a constant inﬂation rate of 2% was assumed for the future years. However, this
parameter is most likely varying from year to year, based on many factors such as the
economical wealth of the country. The real value could normally vary between 0.5% and
3.5%.
• Discount rate. The discount rate in a private economic analysis represents the return on
capital that an investor thinks he could get by investing in a different project than the
one under analysis [88]. Directly from this deﬁnition, it is clear that its value is subjective
and dependent mainly by the investor himself. In Denmark it is common practice to
set it to 7% [68] (as assumed in this thesis), but it could vary between 5% and 9% in real
practice, or even further in dependence on the particular investment.
109
==PAGE:132==
Chapter 9. Data accuracy
• COP. The Coefﬁcient Of Performance of a refrigeration cycle is a thermodynamic per-
formance indicator. It depends by the used technology and by its operating conditions.
No data regarding the refrigeration system of the plants were available, therefore a
COP=3.0 was assumed in this work. However, this value is likely to be different in the real
plants. A COP comprised between 2.5 and 3.5 is retained to be realistic for the studied
applications.
• Boiler’s thermal efﬁciency. As the COP for a refrigeration cycle, the thermal efﬁciency
of a boiler is a parameter expressing its thermodynamic performance. A traditional
boiler has an efﬁciency generally close to 0.83, but this parameter can vary based on
technology, purity of the fuel and wear. It might be assumed that this parameter could
vary between 0.75 and 0.9.
A sensitivity analysis was conducted in order to assess the effect of possible variations in the
aforementioned parameters on the economic analysis outcomes. This study was performed
varying each parameter of ±30% around its set point, and evaluating the percentage variation
caused on PBT and NPV. The analysis was carried out only on the traditional retroﬁt outcomes,
because the relative effect would be the same for all the other analysis conducted during the
thesis.

9.4
Conclusion
The major sources of uncertainties in the developed models were identiﬁed and their impact
was assessed. In order to do so, two different system dimensions were considered, namely the
process and the economic dimensions.
The process dimension regards all the uncertainties related to the thermodynamic outputs,
namely the outputs of the pinch targeting procedure. They might highly affect the project
outcomes because they represent the most important indicators for the engineer, in order to
take design decisions. The most important sources of uncertainties related to this dimension
are identiﬁed in (1) mass ﬂows and (2) temperatures. However while the former do not result
in high uncertainties in the outcomes, the latter can highly affect the results of the targeting
procedure. In fact, the standard deviation of the utility targets is maximum 6% evaluating the
mass ﬂow uncertainties, while its up to 36% considering temperature uncertainties. This result
is also proven looking at the pinch point location. In case of mass ﬂows it does not vary, while
it does change considering temperatures. Finally, an important lesson is learnt considering
the streams that causes higher uncertainties. Only the streams that have a high heat capacity
rate are relevant in this sense. Not all of them are important, but their majority show high
impact on uncertainties. The designer should be more careful collecting data of such ﬂows.
The economic dimension regards the feasibility analysis conducted on alternative designs.
Their importance is high for the plant owners, because feasibility analysis represents the major
judgement criteria when the decision to invest has to be made. Many parameters are shown
to be highly important in this sense, among which (1) energy prices, (2) plant load factor, (3)
utility efﬁciency and (4) investment cost correlations. A variation of ±30% in their value can
affect the feasibility performance indicators by up to 60%. Even if it is very hard to signiﬁcantly
reduce uncertainties in these parameters, a big effort should be put in this sense when a
feasibility analysis is performed. Finally, a deeper analysis on such uncertainties would be
interesting, accounting for their actual variability instead of a standard ±30%. Unfortunately
it was not possible to perform such study in this thesis due to time constraints, but it could be
considered for future works.

10 Discussion
This thesis is built on many assumptions and the obtained outcomes are subject to various
limitations. This chapter presents and discusses the most important constraints of the con-
ducted analysis, their effect on the results and the possible improvements to the study. Only
important factors not discussed in Chapter 9 are presented.
Continuous operation
Apart from the last scenario regarding grassroot design, the whole thesis was carried out
considering continuous operation of the processes for 7300 hours/year. However, dairy
industries and, in general, food industries are characterised by semi-continuous and batch
operation. In light of this, such assumption is probably the most impacting one on the study.
In fact, missing more detailed data, the streams duties as well as the equipment capacities
were determined based on such premise. In this way the resulting capacities might be quite
different than the actual ones. While such difference does not harm the thermodynamic
feasibility of the heat exchange because the energy requirement of the streams within an
operation cycle are in the same proportion as the ones used in continuous operation, it
affects the economic feasibility in multiple ways. (1) Streams matches retained economically
feasible under continuous operation might not be so if the real timing is accounted for. (2) The
estimated heat exchange area of HEX might be under- or over-estimated due to the imprecise
duty estimation.
The reason behind the ﬁrst problem is that a too large investment in heat storage and addi-
tional heat exchangers might be necessary in order to overcome the time and duty difference
between the streams. Such risk was reduced in the thesis, favouring matches within the same
lines at ﬁrst. However, despite of this, some connections between different departments were
considered. The possibility of indirect heat exchange with the assistance of heat storage was
considered only for grassroot design, while it was completely overlooked for retroﬁt projects.
Therefore, it is very likely that in such analysis the investment costs were underestimated,
undermining the project feasibility for a real implementation.
121
==PAGE:144==
Chapter 10. Discussion
Secondly, the area of heat exchangers is proportional to their duty. Therefore, if a stream
considered continuous in reality operates for half of the time, the heat exchangers size would
be twice the ones calculated under continuous operation assumption in order to exchange
the same energy amount in one operation cycle. The result would be a considerably higher in-
vestment cost for exchanging the same energy amount, undermining the economic feasibility
of placing such unit.
In conclusion, particular attention should be paid when semi-continuous and batch processes
are studied under continuous operation assumption. Such premise would be very helpful for
simplifying the analysis, but the designer should always be aware of the threats that it entails,
especially when it comes to sizing of equipments and economic feasibility analysis. Solutions
that promotes design compatibility between continuous and semi-continuous operation
should be preferred, in order to better estimate the design performance using continuous
operation assumptions.
Thesis scope
The Process Integration analysis conducted in this thesis comprises only energy integration
of processes. However, PI is much more, comprehending a variety of tools that were not
applied during this study, but that can be beneﬁcial for further performance enhancement
of the plants. Among the analysis that were overlooked, the most important are: (1) Process
modiﬁcations, (2) Modiﬁcations of the utilities (e.g. ﬂow rate, temperature and pressure levels)
and (3) Integration of heat pumps or, in general, investment in new utilities.
A study on process modiﬁcations can regard the variation of control system set-points, or
the complete revisiting of the process. Various PI studies conducted on dairy industries
proved that high savings can be achieved by placing new components in processes, such as
thermocompressors in multi-stage evaporators [48], or desiccant wheels prior to spray dryers
[89]. Moreover, also by varying streams target temperatures and pressures the process energy
needs can be reduced. This effect is widely proved on distillation columns [12], but good
results can be expected by applying it to other processes. Pinch analysis offers a useful tool for
detecting beneﬁcial process modiﬁcations, named "Plus Minus Principle" [15].
As aforementioned, the Grand Composite Curve can be used in order to detect possible utility
temperature level variations. Lowering the hot utility temperature or increasing the cold utility
one, savings of primary energy can be achieved. This kind of analysis was overlooked in the
thesis, accepting the existing utilities. However, a more critical study might be beneﬁcial for a
further enhancement of the plant performance from an energy consumption viewpoint.
A third possibility is to study the replacement of existing utilities with better performing
ones in terms of primary energy consumption, costs or emissions. The integration of heat
pumps is a widely used action in order to reduce the primary energy consumption and, in
turns, the emissions. As proof of its beneﬁt, such solution was recently implemented on the
122
==PAGE:145==
studied Cheese plant achieving a primary energy savings close to 30%. Other possible option
could be the replacement of the existing gas boiler with biomass ones, the integration of solar
heating systems or the connection to district heating networks or power plants. Again, such
kind of analysis could enlarge the scope of the thesis, achieving more beneﬁcial designs and
investment planning. The GCC can be used in order to analyse these possibilities.
A ﬁnal remark needs to be made on the reciprocal interaction of the aforementioned tools.
Even if all of them can potentially increase the plant performance, a thorough optimisation
study regarding their effect when placed altogether is necessary. This is because, when looking
at the economic feasibility of such investments, they affect each other’s proﬁtability. For
example, if the investment in a heat pump had to be considered altogether with the heat
integration study conducted in this thesis, the outcomes would be considerably different. in
fact, a heat pump is able to reduce the energy costs related to heating and cooling, reducing
the economic beneﬁts of heat integration alternatives. Strategic decisions need to be taken in
order to trace the most beneﬁcial design path to be followed.
Losses
No heat and pressure losses were accounted for in the design. However, in such a large heat
exchangers network they can play a remarkable role in the energy consumption, both for
heating and for pumping. Moreover, the HEX inlet temperatures can vary because of long
piping, reducing the amount of heat recoverable. Finally, the HEX investment costs were
calculated assuming not pressurized units, while in reality, due to pressure losses, they are
working with pressures up to 10 bar.
The overall effect of such assumption on the results is not retained to be high enough to justify
the increased complexity of the analysis due to the losses inclusion, especially in a compact
facility with short piping. However, the designer should always be aware of their existence and
consider them for a more precise design.
Operation issues
In the thesis, operation issues were always considered as afterthoughts and treated with
marginal importance. Moreover, they were not included in any design methodology, leaving
operability and safety considerations to the experience of the engineer. The problem in
including them is caused by the fact that such issues are very hard to be addressed, unlike
thermodynamic problems, which are based on well-known physical phenomena (e.g. laws of
thermodynamics). In light of this, their inclusion in the design sets a dilemma: on the one
hand if operability and safety are considered upfront, they result in too high complexity of the
problem. On the other hand, if they are left for later analysis, the design might result infeasible,
either economically or operability wise.
Issues related to operation that have to be considered are multiple: (1) The PI design should
123
==PAGE:146==
Chapter 10. Discussion
result in a process ﬂexible enough to allow Different operating scenarios, Startup and shutdown
and Variation in operating conditions. In order to ensure ﬂexibility, heat exchangers have to be
dimensioned for the worst case scenario. Furthermore startup heaters and coolers need to be
placed in parallel to the heat recovery HEX. They result in additional costs. Finally, if a match
happens between streams in different sections, the time lag between the two processes during
startup and shutdown should be accounted placing buffer heaters and coolers (in case of
direct heat exchange), or heat storage systems (in case of indirect heat exchange). (2) Process
Controllability needs to be always ensured. With this regard, simple systems are preferred to
complex ones, even if they can achieve higher heat recovery. (3) Safety is a main issue, both
related to product quality and operators. For example, milk should not be transferred through
too long piping because of cleaning related problems and because of risk of leakages that
would waste the product. This issue is even more important if hazardous ﬂuids are operated.
Indirect heat exchange by means of water loops should be considered instead. (4) A Total
Site concept is necessary when performing PI designs, especially between the process and
the utility departments. Their mutual interaction should be considered in order to avoid
designs beneﬁcial for the process but detrimental for the utilities, resulting in no performance
enhancement at a facility level. (5) Finally, Reliability, availability and maintenance should be
always ensured.
All these issues constitute a barrier in the implementation of savings measures, because plant
owners are sceptic with regards to design concepts not already proven in practice. Moreover,
if the complexity of the proposed measures is retained high, the project is not likely to be
considered even if high savings potentials are proven. All in all a broad experience is required
in order to perform a design that achieves the right compromise between energy and economic
performance on one side, and operability on the other. It would therefore be beneﬁcial to
include such considerations in a PI methodology in order to help the designer to tackle this
optimum arrangement. However, as today, this still remains an area of research and no
practical tools have been developed.

11 Conclusion
The present thesis studies Process Integration methodologies applied on dairy industries. This
topic ﬁnds relevancy in the low diffusion of precise PI design tools in this industrial sector,
despite the high energy consumption that it entails, representing the second most energy
demanding industry in Denmark. In particular, this work aims at (1) identify strengths and
weaknesses of the existing PI methods, (2) Develop a simpliﬁed methodology able to ﬁnd more
acceptance at an industrial level and (3) Assess the importance of data accuracy on the design.
Two actually operating plants were studied under continuous operation assumptions: a Cheese
factory and a Milk plant. By applying the pinch targeting procedure signiﬁcant energy savings
potential were detected for both of them. A 62.5% utility consumption reduction potential was
spotted for the Cheese plant, while a 33% decrease was identiﬁed for the Milk plant. In order
to determine the actually achievable savings in a real project, more detailed designs were
conducted. Both the plants were considered for a retroﬁt project, while a grassroot design was
performed only on the Cheese plant.
The retroﬁt design was conducted following the traditional pinch methodology. The Cheese
plant showed to be suitable for investment in energy savings. In fact an utility consumption
reduction of 24% with respect to the actual one was proved to be achievable accepting a 4.1
years payback time and an NPV=66MDKK. On the contrary, the Milk plant retroﬁt showed to
be infeasible because, even though a 21% savings can be achieved, such investment would
result in a PBT=18.6 years and a negative NPV=-0.7MDKK.
Two other retroﬁt methodologies present in the literature were applied to the Cheese plant in
order to assess their performance in identifying proﬁtable designs. The Matrix method devel-
oped by the Chalmers University of Technology proved to be uninteresting for an industrial
application, being too complex and achieving low performing designs. On the contrary, the
Limiting Match Method developed by the Technical University of Denmark showed interesting
features. In fact it demonstrated to be able to reduce the time requirement for the project
completion without loosing much savings potential. Moreover it resulted helpful in the deci-
sion process, being able to connect the streams characteristics to economic considerations.

==PAGE:148==
Chapter 11. Conclusion
However, some weaknesses and improvement possibilities were spotted, especially in the
absence of an indicator able to signal when to stop the proposed simpliﬁcation procedure and
start the traditional design.
Finally, the inﬂuence of the Minimum temperature difference choice on the Cheese plant
design outcomes was studied. Such decision proved to have a high impact on the targeting
procedure outcomes, determining a 361% increase in utility targets passing from a ∆Tmin=1K
to ∆Tmin=8K. Moreover, in the same analysis the pinch point location showed a signiﬁcant
variation, passing from 16◦C to 56◦C. However, such results do not undermine the project
outcomes, as long as the same ∆Tmin is used in the network design. On the other hand, such
choice have a high impact on the design feasibility. In fact, it does not only affect the achievable
savings, but also the investment costs, PBT and NPV. An optimisation study showed to be
required in order to choose the best performing ∆Tmin.
Based of the strengths and weaknesses spotted in the studied retroﬁt methodologies, a novel
retroﬁt methodology was developed, named "Speciﬁc Savings Potential method". It aims
at completing the traditional methodology by introducing screening criteria able to reduce
the size of the problem prior to the actual design. This result is achieved by applying three
simpliﬁcation steps, out of which the ﬁrst one is applied before the detailed data acquisition
and the others before the network design. One of them achieves a simpliﬁcation based on
the speciﬁc savings content of each stream, the other on economic criteria. This method was
validated by applying it to the two plants previously studied. It proved to be very efﬁcient
in reducing the engineering effort and the time required to the retroﬁt design. Moreover, it
showed to be capable to spot all the uninteresting streams, achieving a signiﬁcant problem
simpliﬁcation without loosing savings with regards to the previously performed designs.
The grassroot design was ﬁrstly conducted under the assumption of continuous operation. It
was proven that 37% utility savings can be achieved accepting a PBT=5.9 years if compared to
an investment in the existing plant design. Afterwards such assumption was removed, studying
the plant under semi-continuous operation. In this case the investment in heat recovery water
loops equipped with heat storage tanks was required in order to ensure the operability of
the plant. However, the investment cost in such equipment proved to be low if compared to
the network cost (8.4MDKK against 71MDKK) not undermining the project feasibility with
respect to the continuous operation assumption. Based on the challenges encountered in the
project some approach suggestions were formulated, in order to facilitate the design. The
most important are: (1) prefer matches between streams belonging to the same plant section,
(2) do not invest in heat exchangers of small duty unless strictly necessary and (3) favour
simple designs to complicated solutions, even accepting a lower energy performance. In this
way the project would encounter larger acceptance by the plant owners.
Finally, the importance of Data accuracy was analysed. Temperature resulted to be the most
important process parameter. in fact, the uncertainties in its values proved to have a high
impact on the targeting procedure outcomes: the spotted standard deviation of the utility
126
==PAGE:149==
targets was up to 36%, while the pinch point location showed to vary from 6◦C to 59◦C.
Such outcomes prove that data accuracy is very important, for uncertainties can result in
signiﬁcantly different targets and mislead the designer in the very beginning of the project.
Moreover, it was proven that only streams that have a high heat capacity rate can be source of
relevant uncertainties for the targeting procedure. Therefore, higher care should be taken in
measuring their temperatures.
Looking at the economic parameters it was proven that the factors inﬂuencing the design
the most are (1) energy prices, (2) plant load factor, (3) boiler’s efﬁciency and (4) investment
cost correlations. A variation of ±30% in their value can affect the feasibility performance
indicators by up to 60%. Such variability can highly affect the project, determining the design
feasibility. Therefore particular care should be taken in the assumption of these parameters,
even if it is quite complicated to reduce the related uncertainties.
The analysis performed in this thesis resulted in many interesting indications for reducing the
engineering effort in conducting PI designs. In particular, the developed retroﬁt methodology
is promising for obtaining a wider acceptance of pinch based tools in the industry, not only for
application to Dairy processes, but to the totality of the industrial sector. This would promote
the exploitation of a large heat savings potential that nowadays is overlooked, as demonstrated
by the savings obtained in the two plants analysed along the thesis. However, further work is
required in order to achieve such goal. More in details: (1) the method should be validated
on a wider range of processes and sectors of the industry, (2) its scope should be extended, in
order to include more types of projects, such as batch and total site analysis design and (3) it
would be beneﬁcial to include operability and safety considerations in a more explicit way
in the proposed framework. The challenges in achieving such goals while keeping the SSP a
simple methodology suitable for industrial application will be high. However, this appear to
be a necessity if such method has to obtain a large diffusion in the industrial sector. In such
perspective this thesis represents just a small step in a long journey. But, at the end of the day,
a long journey requires many small steps to be completed.

C Retroﬁt - case studies
This chapter presents the retroﬁt design conducted on the Cheese and Milk plants by using
the traditional pinch methodology. The procedure is followed step-by-step presenting the
outcomes and the decisions taken along the project. A short discussion about the choices and
the overall design is presented in the end.
C.1
Cheese plant
C.1.1
Data extraction
The streams data were provided by Viegand Maagøe ApS and presented in Appendix A. Some
adjustments were necessary, because of imprecisions in the acquired data. In particular,
the heat balance within some individual heat exchangers was not veriﬁed. This problem
was ﬁxed varying some streams intermediate temperatures in order to ensure the respect of
the ﬁrst law of thermodynamics, while keeping the streams targets constant. The necessary
modiﬁcations ranged from 0.5K to 4K in the majority of the cases. In the Salted whey section
the discrepancies were higher, needing variations of up to 43K.
Such problems are regular during Data extraction, contributing to the long time required to
perform this task. Moreover, it is quite hard to collect and handle such a great amount of
data without succumb to mistakes. Imprecisions, mistypings or slips are not uncommon and
can compromise the whole project. For example in this thesis, despite the numerous checks
performed during the project, one mistyping error and one oversight required to repeat the
calculations and the whole design twice, wasting approximately two weeks of work.

C.2.5
Design optimisation
Following the same method used for the Cheese plant, an optimisation of the design was
performed. The results are presented in Figure C.17, C.18 and C.19.
As it can be seen by looking at these Figures, Sub-network 1 and Sub-network 2 are able to
achieve the least savings and are not feasible in an economic perspective. In fact, they show
a PBT higher than 23 years and a negative NPV. Moreover, choosing a ∆Tmin higher than 5K
they are not retroﬁtted, accepting the actual conﬁguration. The best performing measure is
the one adopted on Sub-network 3. In fact it is the one able to achieve the highest savings
(Figure C.17), which are higher than 100kW. Moreover, it shows a PBT lower than 11 years and
a positive NPV. However, such outcomes are not good enough for deﬁning the such design
feasible under economic terms. In fact, the PBT is never higher than 7 years and the NPV is
quite low, reaching a maximum slightly higher than 1.6 MDKK for a ∆Tmin=9K. Due to such
low performance, the retroﬁt of this plant should not be considered feasible.
C.2.6
Practical consideration
The previous analysis proved that the retroﬁt of the Milk plant is not feasible under an eco-
nomic viewpoint. This is a mischance, because such design is retained to cause lower problems
in terms of operability, if compared to the design performed in the Cheese plant. In fact, Sub-
network 1 and Sub-network 2, do not introduce new matches, resulting in a robust design
from the operability point of view. Also Sub-network 3 is retained to introduce safe measures.
In fact, two new matches are created between the two high pasteurisers and the CIP water.
