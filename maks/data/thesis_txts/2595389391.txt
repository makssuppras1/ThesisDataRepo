Summary
The following paper is a study on the development of an experimental robotic platform
involving a 7-DOF Lightweight collaborative robot for future neurological therapeutic
research. The study focuses on human-robot interactions, where the approach taken in
designing such an application required a compliant robotic control strategy that would
allow for a patient to be integrated into a control loop and thus influence the control
objective. In order to do so, a haptics interface was developed in order to provide the
patient with visual feedback to the state of the system for complete integration into
the control loop. To ensure safety a simulation platform is devised prior to practical
application. The findings in the paper suggest that a 7-DOF kinematically redundant
collaborative robot can be used for therapeutic research. The practical implications of
this study relate to advancement of research within the field of rehabilitation robotics
and neuro-science.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
In the 21st century what once was considered science fiction is now slowly being rendered
reality. With industrial robotics being infamous for being unsafe for being around, re-
cent advancements made with collaborative robots often referred to as cobots, allow for
the cooperation between robots and humans within the same environment. With cobots
allowing for safer human-robot interactions, its potential application ranges wide. From
disaster response to serving dinner at a restaurant, cobots can take the shape a general
purpose autonomous robot such as Baxter, autonomous robots with manipulators and
even a stand-alone manipulator, innovation is plentiful. Recently, multiple scientific dis-
ciplines within the medical, health and neurological sciences have garnered the attention
of the technological sector for making strides within the field of robotics. Efforts have
been made to apply robotics in the field of neurology and rehabilitation with the aim
of contributing to the understanding of neurological disorders and rehabilitation strate-
gies, chief of among which have been related to the loss or lack of motor-coordination
[CS18]. The underlying etiology for these disorders can vary, ranging from an ischemic
stroke resulting in ischemia to different parts of the brain including cortical, subcortical
structures and/or the cerebellum, or be hereditary and sporadic disorders affecting the
aforementioned regions as well. For these victims/patients many of the acquired or in-
herited neurological disorders have no cure, with the only remaining treatment options
available involve physical therapy (PT), in an effort to promote motor learning through
neurogenesis and neuroplasticity.
With advancements made within the field of robot mediated technologies for the neru-
osciences, the field has expanded to multiple different subfields, couple among which a
attempt to integrate humans within the control architecture of its design. The disci-
plines are distinguished depending on their application, e.g. human augmentation and
rehabilitation. The latter concerns itself with therapeutic rehabilitation over assistive
and/or empowering robotics for the user. Additionally, the aim of rehabilitation robotics
is to fully rehabilitate the patient by means of neurorehabilitation, done by promoting
neurogenesis and neuroplasticity over time to encourage independence; assistive robotics
does not. This framework of neurogenesis and neuroplasticity is applied by developing
and adapting robotic devices to suit the patient’s needs and therapeutic design. This
subfield of robotics is called rehabilitation robotics [CS18]. The field employs technology
that extends itself into other subfields of mechatronics, neuroscience, neurocomputation,
biotechnology and control theory, all of which consider the safety and recovery of the
patient.

1 Introduction
1.1
Constraints in Human-Robot Interactions
With the dangers associated with non-compliant industrial robots, human-robot interac-
tions are typically avoided entirely. Depending on the application, robotic manipulators
vary can vary in size. The larger the robot, the more force is required for the actuators
to generate movement of the individual links. Furthermore, industrial robots used for
welding or spraypainting often operate with position control systems, without regard for
the external environment, which may collisions involving severe injuries or even death.
As a result robots are sometimes caged or isolated as a precaution to defer humans
from entering into the robot’s workspace. However, in contrast to industrial robots, col-
laborative robots are smaller robots that allow for shared workspaces between humans
and employ highly optimized safety features. These collaborative robots, often called
”cobots” allow for more involved applications, as the emerging technology advances. The
more involved the robot is with humans, the more this becomes a critical factor. For
rehabilitation robotics, this holds in particular true, with how the patient is a continuous
part of the robotic setup.
1.2
Motivation
With current existing clinical measures for neuromotor rehabilitaiton, although widely
accepted, the methods are often limited, subjective and nonlinear in recovery. Because
of this the demand for an alternative clinical measures gives rise. Rehabilitation robotics
provides a general framework to model the interactions between robot and patient during
robot-assisted training. By using wearable and operational end-effector robots, a patient
can perform exercises with the assistance and/or challenge of the robot, where the
challenge can be mediated through a haptic simulation. Robot mediated rehabilitation
has been shown to provide similar and potentially comparable to that of conventional
clinical measures, but with the advantage of measuring and modeling even modeling the
progression. With the literature providing evidence indicating significant improvements
on patient’s ADL for patients having undergone robot-mediated therapy, it is therefore
of interest to know whether this still holds true. To examine this, it is important that
continued research within the field of rehabilitation robotics is advanced, and to further
these goals an experimental setup allowing for this type of research is required.

1.3 Problem Description
3
1. Research and identify robotic control strategies used in robot-mediated neurore-
habilitation that employ safe human-robot interactions.
2. Based on the goals of this study, develop a control system that captures the iden-
tified requirements and assumptions.
3. Examine the kinematic and dynamic relationship of the interaction between the
7DoF robot and the patient undergoing rehabilitation in order to maximize both
safety and control during the robot-patient interaction.
4. Deploy an experimental platform that works on two fronts:
• Virtual robot platform.
• Physical robot platform.
5. Identify to which extent it is possible to maximize the efficacy of the rehabilitation
procedure through the design of the experimental setup, as well as the underlying
assumptions of the patient.
With the patient in this study being assumed to
exhibit: (1) a movement deficit expressed from the upper-limb region, and (2)
retain a sufficiently plastic neuro-motor-network that allows for motor-recovery,
this objective involves the following parts:
a) The design of a haptic interface that serves to integrate the patient into the
control loop, where the patient is provided with objectives and feedback.
b) The implementation of a Cartesian impedance controller with low-impedance
at the robot’s end-effector in task-space to allow for path deviation generated
by the patient at the end-effector.

collaborative robot manipulator that interacts with a human subject. The components
needed for achieving this goal are depicted on figure 1.1. This experimental study focuses
on the highlighted components.

CHAPTER 2
Rehabilitation Robotics
The following chapter covers some of the related works within the field of rehabilitation
robotics.
The following aims to focus on studies related to the objectives listed in
previous chapter 1, where the goal is to review and cover studies related to compliant
control systems, human robot interactions and clinical therapy,
2.0.1
Robot-Mediated Studies
A paper from [Fur+07] proposed a 6-DOF end-effector based rehabilitation robot de-
sign for upper limb physical therapy, including wrists, named the ”Robotherapist”. The
paper presents the design of the robot- the mechanism of the Robotherapist and its soft-
ware on physical therapy [Fur+07]. In [TGC08], preliminary research was conducted in
developing an erratic motion suppression control system of a 2DoF experimental haptic
assistive device. The device was aimed for impaired muscle ataxic users, and the assess-
ment was evaluated through attaching the device to a computer mouse interfacing with
a graphical user interface (GUI). The haptic device was tested on a single ataxic subject
and a healthy subject, with the device employing assistive impedance based techniques
through force sensing [TGC08]. The experiment suffered from a lack of subjects (but an
interesting idea). Multiple studies have been conducted investigating different possible
robot therapy exercises. Among which, challenge based exercises, with negative viscos-
ity and force control [HPM09]. In 2009, a review was conducted on the application
of robotic devices assisting in movement training following a neurological injury such
as stroke and spinal cord injury, reviewing control strategies and their respective imple-
mentations including impedance, counterbalance and EMG based controllers [MR09]. A
robotic assay of arm reaching movements in diverse neurologic populations was explored
in a preliminary study conducted in [Kan+15]. The goal of the study was to determine
whether robotic derived performance metrics (robotic assay) were capable of discerning
movement features unique to underlying pathologies. The neurologic diseases that were
compared include Huntington’s Didease, Cerebellar Ataxia ATX, Parkinson’s disease
PD and multiple sclerosis MS. The study was conducted on a sample size of 17 patients,
n=5 subjects without neurologic impairment, n=5 with MS, n=3 with PD, n=2 with
ATX, and the robot used for the study was the MIT-MANUS 2DoF planar robot. The
preliminary anlysis conducted by found that there are some potential robust markers
that distinguish across disease groups [Kan+15].

2 Rehabilitation Robotics
Related work on stroke survivors
For stroke survivors, a systematic review with meta analysis was conducted in 2017
reviewing 17 studies, 14 randomized controlled trials, 2 systematic reviews and 1 meta-
analysis, where the aim of the review was to asses the effectiveness of robotic devices com-
pared to any other type of intervention. The authors determined that, when compared
with conventional therapy, the robotassisted rehabilitation is more effective in improv-
ing upper limb motor function recovery, especially in chronic stroke patients [Ber+17].
Regarding the subacute phase of recovery the authors found found no significant bene-
fit of robot-assisted therapy over conventional therapy or any other intervention in the
sub-acute phase after stroke, and were unable to state the reason why patients in the
chronic phase had a better response to robotic treatment than those in the subacute
phase, when neuralplasticity is expected to be more evident and efficient. Despite this,
a recent 6 month follow-up study was conducted on 48 subacute stroke patients who per-
formed upper limb therapy using a planar robot end-effector. The authors found that
robot-assisted therapy may lead to greater reduction in moto-impairment [Fra+20].
Related work on neurological diseases
Similar to the aforementioned two studies, another study was carried out in [Mas+04],
on how patients with hereditary cerebellar degeneration [spinocerebellar ataxia (SCA)
type 6 and 8] were able to adapt to unknown external forces during goal-directed arm
movements when operating a manipulandum. The study explored whether the adap-
tation to unknown external forces relies on the integrity of the cerebellum, and found
that SCA patients were impaired in force adaptation, progression with cerebellar ataxia
significantly correlated with the impairment of learning, SCA patients were unable to
generalize learning to untrained workspace as opposed to healthy patients and finally
that SCA patients’ retention of learning was impaired. Based on these results asserting
that motor adaptation to unknown external forces relies heavily on the integrity of the
cerebellum. A decrease in adaptive ability was nearly linearly related to the extent of
the cerebellar degeneration [Mas+04]. On the same note, in [GBO14] an investigation
involving the 2 DoF planar exoskeleton (KINARM) was carried out to test the prevailing
internal model hypothesis for planning and movement, by recording multi-joint reaching
movements for cerebellar ataxic patients with the exoskeleton. Through recording the
movements, joint-torque trajectories were calculated and a gradient descent algorithm
found optimal, patient-specific pertuabtions to actual limb dynamics predicted. These
predictions improved the reaching errors by an average of 41%. Following the authors
experiments they tested for 4 hypotheses related to the cerebellum related to the opti-

3.2.5
Kinematically Redundant Manipulators
Kinematic redundancy occurs when a robotic manipulator possesses more degrees of
freedom than those strictly required of the manipulator to execute a given task. In
principle, this means that no manipulator is inherently redundant; rather, there are
certain tasks with respect to which it may become redundant. The main motivation
for introducing kinematic redundancy is to improve dexterity for a robotic manipulator.
But for kinematically redundant manipulators, the inverse kinematics problem admits an
infinite number of solutions, and thus a strategy for overcoming this problem is required.
The following subsections aim to introduce the reader to some of the existing methods
that allow for a forward and inverse differential kinematics implementation.

5.1
Introduction
In control theory, control strategies determine how a control objective is attained for
dynamical system through the use of control actions. For robotic manipulators, this
control action results in the motion of the robot and depending on the control objective,
this motion falls into different classes of control strategies. Compliant control, where the
objective is to comply with the external environment, and non-compliant control, where
the external environment is not involved in the control objective. Since the neuroreha-
bilitation involves human-robot interactions, a compliant control strategy becomes the
most natural choice. The following chapter describes the processes and considerations
involved in deriving the control strategy for a robotic control architecture.
5.2
Indirect Force Control
Patients undergoing robotic therapy are tasked with interacting with the robot end-
effector so as to reach a desired point or trajectory. The goal here is to integrate the
patient into the control loop of the system, where the patient’s interaction wtith the
end-effector influences the position of the robot’s end-effector. Multiple methods exist,
however the more general approaches fall typically into the following two categories:
Direct and Indirect force control. Direct force control pertains to controlling the robot
through force input, resulting in a trajectory. The opposite of this control method refers
to indirect force control, where the robot trajectory is is the control action with the
output resulting in force. An indirect force control strategy is desirable because
1. Patients undergoing robotic therapy are tasked with producing and following tra-
jectories through interaction tasks involving the end-effector.
2. Described in chapters 1 and 7 for patients with impeded task dexterity, the robot is
required to assist the patient along a desired trajectory depending on the assistive
protocol.
==PAGE:48==
34
5 Cartesian Impedance Control
3. The requirements of the therapy robot require flexibility for unknown or adapting
interaction tasks.
Regarding 1) and 2) generally for direct force control applications, the robot is moving
either completely stationary or moving very slowly, with the exception of hybrid force/-
motion control, where the end-effector motion and the contact forces are decoupled into
two sub-problems of controlling the motion along the unconstrained task directions and
force (and moment) along the constrained task directions. Here an argument could be
made for the application of hybrid force/motion control, however, as described in 3)
an unknown interaction task with adapting task constraints complicates its application.
On the other hand, indirect force control applications do not require explicit knowledge
of the environment with regards to an interaction task, and thus more versatile and
adaptable, making it an attractive option for spatial (3D) interaction tasks.

7.1
Problem Statement
It has been stated in chapter 1, that the interest of this study is to look into compliant
robotic control systems for safe human robot interactions, in the field of rehabilitation
robotics. To this end, the main question this paper aims to answer is:
Question 1. What are the main factors involved in developing a compliant
robotic control system that features persistent human-robot interactions as
part of the control system.
A compliant robotic control system refers to a control system that allows the robot
to comply and deviate its trajectory with respect to external forces acting upon the
it, as opposed to a non-complaint (stiff) control system. What persistent human-robot
interactions means refers to constant influence of a human affecting the trajectory of
the robot. The question implies the series of complications that arise when integrating
a human into a control architecture. The question has no single answer and can be
approached from multiple angles. This paper aims to answer this question by narrowing
down the field of study with another question.
Question 2. What application potential does a rehabilitation procedure in-
volving a compliant robotic control system have for clinical therapy?
The first thing this study seeks to investigate is whether robotic compliance is possible
to the extent of which the robotic configuration can be adjusted by the patient. To
answer this question, it is therefore important get familiar with controlling the robotic
platform using a compliant control system. Moreover, how the control system behaves
analytically as well as in a simulation environment must be determined, when given a
control objective while forces are interacting upon it. To this end a simulation platform

in order to investigate its applicability.
7.2
Assumptions
The following assumptions are made throughout the entire paper. The links of the robots
are assumed to be rigid bodies, bereft of elastic forces. For the simulation environment,
the joints of the robots are assumed to have zero backlash, zero damping and zero friction
and are perfectly backdrivable. Furthermore, the gear torques and gear ratios as well
as all other additional torques aside from the ones derived by the canonical expressions
of the dynamics are assumed to be zero or ideal. Patients undergoing rehabilitation
posses a motor deficiency at the upper extremity of the body involving inaccurate or
weak arm coordination and motion, and that the condition is treatable. The remaining
assumptions are listed below:
• The rehabilitation protocol is ethical.
• Rehabilitation is possible

7.4
Therapy Training Protocol
A study from 2008 conducted a training devised a training protocol that included in-
cluded static stretching, systematically varied levels of active-assisted arm exercise, and
goal-directed planar reaching tasks. The training involved 3 trials (sets) of 15 repetitions
with 30 seconds of rest between trials for the next 5 minutes. The exercises consisted
of figure-eight movements for 5 minutes, and then reaching in a point-to-point fashion,

Other secondary scales were also in-

7.5
Rehabilitation Scenario
Based on [Vol+08], the selected rehabilitation strategy will in the long term consist
of assistive control and a haptic simulation. The haptic simulation will consist of a
series of games/tasks on a graphical user interface. The patient will be given tasks to
fulfill, e.g. moving a curser from point-to-point games, etc. and in doing so providing
the patient feedback positional feedback on the robot’s position.
This acts to close
the loop of the therapeutic strategy, with the intention of encouraging the patient to
correct his/her movements. Repetitive failure in fulfilling the tasks will provide the robot-
control algorithm to adaptively model the patient’s deficiencies, and thus pertubate the
actuators to compensate for the movement.

8.3.2
Physics Engine Settings
As was determined in previous chapter 4 and in chapter 3, both the kinematic models
and the dynamic models are computed numerically and iteratively. Thus in order to
ensure that the control loop is capable of controlling the robot independent of the data
transmission rate and computational hardware constraints, the following options are
implemented:
• real-time factor - refers to the Gazebo’s physics engine update rate.
• simulated-time - refers to the time parameter used in Gazebo.
• (un)pause - refers to the two options of pausing and unpausing the physics engine.
To control for these options, the Gazebo node receives a ROS-servicecall from the con-
troller to perform the received instruction. Controlling for the real-time factor allows for
the analysis of dynamical effects on the robot to be observed and computed at a rate
slow enough to allow for the controller to compute the applied control input in time, and
to isolate the simulation platform from hardware constraints during the developmental
stage.
8.3.3
Controllers
The controllers operate on a publish/subscribe structure, where the control system pub-
lishes (transmits) and subscribes (requests) data to/from the joint topics. The nodes
publishing to the joint topics are managed by the controller manager and the robot state
publisher. The controller manager launches the low-level controller nodes that retrieve
data from a specific topic, interfaces with the robot joints and encodes data to the joints.
In contrast, the robot state publisher ”publishes” the joint state data to a topic, that
the control system subscribes to.

9.1
Prototyping Methodology
The development of the experimental platforms start with the development of an ana-
lytical model. The aim of the analytical model is to model the behaviour of a physical
robot as closely as possible, involving the mathematical modelling of the structure, kine-
matic motion and the dynamics of the robot. Proceeding the analytical model, simula-
tions and experimentation are conducted. The principled approach taken in developing
the robotic platform follows the iterative and incremental development (IID) [Coc08]
framework, where each cycle consists of the refinement of the control objective and the
development of a prototype. The goal of these prototypes is to study and model the
effects and behaviour of the control system prior to physical implementation. There
are two prototypes developed. The first prototype is a throwaway prototype, where the
control architecture is implemented in smaller scale robot in order to study the effects
and efficacy of the implementation. The second prototype is an evolutionary prototype
framework based on the designs kinematic and dynamic designs models of the physical
robot, where the final model is implemented to the simulation platform, an the control
system is implemented to the physical robot.
9.2
Prototype:Two-Link Planar Robot
A conceptual model developed for the purpose of allowing initial analytical experimen-
tation, to determine whether the implemented methods work on a simpler down-scaled
robot model as opposed to a robot with a additional DOF. The conception and the

the ”InMotion2” and ”MANUS”. The goal is to design a bare minimum kinematic and
dynamic model for a robot, in this case, a two link planar robot with 2DoF is designed
for simulation and experimentation, fulfilling the requirements for an end-effector based
rehabilitation robot.

CHAPTER 10
Haptics Interface
The following chapter describes the design methodology for haptics interface based on
preliminary studies on the topic of rehabilitation SOURCE REHAB HAPTICS. The
haptics interface serves the purpose of monitoring the end-effector position and feeding
the data back to the patient visually. By doing this one ”integrates” the patient into
the control loop, where the patient acts by manipulating and influencing the position
of the robot.
The approach taken to develop the haptics interface software follows
the waterfall methodology, following Royce’s original waterfall model [Roy70] with the
following phases:
• The capture of system and software requirements
• Analysis resulting in a conceptual model of the system
• Design: resulting in the software architecture
• Coding: The proving and integration of the software
• Operations: The installation of the software.
10.1
Requirements for the Haptic Interface
In order to design the haptics interface, the patient’s requirements need to be understood.
The performance of the Haptic Renderer/Interface determines the patients ability to
respond to the end-effector’s position. With the goal of the haptics interface being to
allow the patient to interpret the position of the robot’s end-effector, so the patient can
make adjustments and respond accordingly, the following software requirements need to
be met.
Hardware Requirements
• Computer hardware to run the software
• Computer monitor for displaying the software
In general, a workstation PC will be running the software application.

CHAPTER 13
Discussion
To answer the question from chapter 7, the effects of implementing a Cartesian impedance
controller was investigated through a series of experiments. From the numerical analysis
it was shown how a Cartesian impedance controller could deviate its trajectory while
an external force was applied. Furthermore, it was also shown that the robot could be
compliant enough to deviate its trajectory with an external force of only 1[N], based on
the numerical analysis of the TLP robot. Similar results were yielded from the Gazebo
experiments with the TLP robot, where when an external force was applied manually
to the robot, the robot would deviate it s trajectory.
There were complications with the implementation however, which was reflected by
the results shown from the gazebo implementation of the two link planar robot, with
the noisy trajectory shown in figure 11.7. The cause for this was later suspected to be
the result of a flaw in the TLP robot’s design in Gazebo. This can be slightly seen in
figure 9.2, where as the robot rotates the two cylinder rigid links, the links can register
collisions. Looking back at the force torque measurements on figure 11.7b, the reason
why it is suspected to work in this experiment is due to the slow trajectory it was provided
during experimentation. furthermore, when analyzing the collision and inertial meshes
of the robot, when a large enough force was applied to the link, it could be observed
that the inertial meshes would collide and when that happened, the simulation would
crash.
When experimenting with the Franka Emika Panda model for the analytical exper-
imentations, the nature of the hyperredudant manipulator was found to be quite prob-
lematic for motion control, in particular due to the nullspace motion control. When
applying resolved rate techniques for reconstructing the joint space trajectory, the robot
would almost always receive the incorrect joint motions, affecting the motions in Carte-
sian space. Occasionally, due to the difference in error margin, the controller would gain
the orientations to the point of which even with quaternionic representations kinematic
singularities would appear. The singularities appeared to be the result of extremely high
angular velocities and accelerations, where the robot would show moments of instability
and marginal stability. This problem was resolved when providing the robot desired
joint-space trajectory for the nullspace configurations qnull to be computed from, in par-
ticular for the nullspace impedance controller. This appeared to resolve all the issues
when controlling the robot in the simulation platform, and in view of Question 1, for
continuous human-robot interactions, this highlights the importance of controlling the
nullspace motions for both kinematic and representational singularity robustness.
==PAGE:106==
92
13 Discussion
During the experimentation of the robotic platform, the Cartesian impedance con-
troller developed by franka emika panda was found to have implemented impedance
controller with either unity inertia, or implemented inertia avoidance techniques [Ott08],
where the desired Λd = Λ(q). But this is difficult to verify given control law from the
source code of Franka Emika’s Cartesian impedance controller [Emi18]. Remarkably, the
Cartesian impedance controller is designed to allow movement of the robot by adjusting
the equilibrium position of the robot, where the robot updates the desired configura-
tion through the implementation of smoothing functions, that progressively update the
control parameters (in order to avoid ”jerking” the robot), by smoothly transitioning
the robot from one configuration to another. The advantage of implementing this kind
of control is it’s ability to variably adjust the stiffness of the robot’s links, without it
accidentally oscillate out of control, as mentioned in safety considerations in chapter 7.
In relation to the overall research Question 1 stated in chapter 7, the effect of imple-
menting a Cartesian impedance that features persistent human-robot was explored with
the robotics application, when gripping and moving the end-effector in order to generate
”patient generated” trajectory. What the experimentation with the robotic platform re-
vealed was that the robot was more than capable of suitable compliance for therapeutic
use, based on the rehabilitation requirements stated earlier in chapter 7 patients with
neuromotor defficiencies such as stroke survivors and or otherwise acquired upper limb
extremity of the body. Furthermore, for patients that may become weak, e.g. stroke
survivors, low stiffness is potentially the most important factor.
In chapter 10 it was shown that the haptic interface was capable of receiving data
from a simulated robot. Given its implementation methodology of requiring a single
thread for its implementation in order to run parallel to the network of nodes related
to the ROS network, it shows that it is possible to visually integrate the patient into
the control loop through a visual haptic interface, where the patient receives positional
feedback from the robotic platform, allowing the patient to respond accordingly (in
theory). At the current state of the prototype, it is only possible to provide the patient
with a 2 dimensional projection of the end-effector position. But there is application
potential to a haptic interface in combination with VR exercises, although this is outside
the scope of this study.
13.1
Qualitative Analysis
The Cartesian impedance controller would without a doubt be easier to implement onto
a less complex robotic platform, as was determined in chapter 11 with the robot con-
strained to 2 DOF on the [x, y] axes, rendering orientation control irrelevant. However,
such a device is limited in terms of manipulability, whereas the Franka Emika Panda
introduces 3 dimensional rehabilitation protocols, with the ability to switch out the end-
effector and potentially combine it with virtual reality training protocols, in essence -
more robotic applications are available to the programmer.

CHAPTER 14
Conclusion
This paper sought to investigate the related effects concerning patient safety and inter-
action, when implementing a compliant control system featuring a continuous human-
robot interactions for robotic therapy. This study has revealed that there is application
potential for a robotic control system, even for a kinematically redundant manipulator,
to be used as a therapeutic research robot.
In this paper, a prominent control strategies that often referenced in related work
on robot-mediated neuro-rehabilitation was identified. The particular compliant con-
trol system is impedance control for safe human-robot interactions, and with regards to
the nature of the rehabilitation task, the control system that captures the requirements
and assumptions is a Cartesian impedance controller that employs haptic feedback in
order to integrate the patient into the control loop. However, given the nature of ki-
netically redundant manipulators and the respective dynamic relationship, great care
must exercised in order to ensure that the null-space motions and kinematic singular-
ities do not become an issue, during the rehabilitation of the patient. To this end, a
virtual/simulated robot platform was developed alongside the physical robot platform.
A rehabilitation protocol was devised in order to maximize the efficacy of the rehabil-
itation procedure, with the underlying assumptions of the patient possessing an upper
limb neuromotor-deficiency. And in order to ensure this is attainable, the implementa-
tion of the Cartesian impedance controller must employ low impedance at the robot’s
end-effector in task-space, for the purpose of allowing the patient to deviate from the
generated trajectory.
As previously covered in the Discussion (chapter 13) to answer Question 1, the effect
of implementing a Cartesian impedance controller that features persistent human-robot
interaction interaction was explored through a series of experimentation involving the
simulation platform and the physical platform, where the efficacy was assessed. This was
then followed by the assessment of application potential involving a for clinical therapy.
Thus, through practical experimentation it became apparent that the main factors
involved in developing a compliant robotic control system that features safe human robot
interactions, is the ever present potential danger the robot is to a human. Unless the
robot is weak and compliant there are multiple potential dangers present in an uncon-
trolled and unstable robotic application. It was therefore paramount that a simulation
platform that simulated the dynamics was a direction of exploration, and later a req-
uisite avenue for development. Although this paper covered a covered a vast area of

14.1
Future Prospects
• Efficient software architecture: With the current control system running on
Python, computational issues may emerge, in particular for real time control, since
Python runs through an interpreter. It is therefore encouraged to migrate over to
a more efficient programming language, where the program is precompiled and
closer to the hardware, in terms of instructions such as C++.
• Assistive Modalities: A rehabilitation modality is needed for the application
of robotic therapy. One of the simpler, but highly relevant and flexible strategies
could pertain to a variable impedance control system that allows for a more
flexible approach to rehabilitation therapy. This can be further adapted into an
adaptive assistance modality that utilizes a variable impedance approach to han-
dling the robot end-effector.
• Clinical Trials: For experimental validity of the rehabilitation platform, once the
platform is completed research withing more advanced robotic neurorehabilitation
is possible.
