Abstract
Energy access has been identiﬁed as a crucial factor for several development goals, such as education,
health, equality or poverty reduction. However close to one billion people still lack an electricity
connection, concentrated in developing countries of sub Saharan Africa and south-east Asia. This
share is expected to grow in sub Saharan Africa, where population growth outpaces electriﬁcation.
This issue is worsened in informal settlements, or slums, which concentrate more than half of the
urban population in sub Saharan Africa, and experience rapid growth due to rural urban migrations.
In these unauthorized settlements, occupation of the land is frequently illegal. As a consequence,
utilities companies do not operate there, resulting in an deprivation from basic services. Electricity
access is lackluster, often achieved through unsafe illegal connections which plague the main grid.
This research therefore explores how electricity access in informal settlements could be improved by
relying on oﬀ-grid hybrid energy systems, to bypass the need for a sanctioned connection to the grid.
The study focuses on the case of the informal settlement of Old Fadama in Accra, Ghana. It relies
on a literature review, interviews and the mini-grid optimization tool HOMER. The structure of the
Old Fadama settlement was studied, revealing an organised community able to support cooperative
projects. A renewable energy resources assessment was then carried out using online tools, highlight-
ing interesting solar resources in the Accra region. An economically viable diesel-solar hybrid energy
system was then tailored to meet the needs of the community, suggesting stand-alone systems could
be a viable option to electrify informal settlements. Practical guidelines to implement the system in
an informal environment are presented, emphasizing the importance of relying on local cooperatives.
Finally the study provides a set of policy recommendations, to promote the development of oﬀ-grid
energy systems in Ghana through local initiatives and private investment facilitated by the state.

1. Introduction
1.1
Overview of the energy situation in Western Africa
Sub-saharan africa is the world region that suﬀers the most from unsuﬃcient energy access, with 19 of
the 20 countries with the lowest electriﬁcation rates in the world in 2018 (1). According to to Energy
Access Outlook 2017 projections, in 2030 out of 680 millions people left out of electricity access
worldwide, 90% will be citizens of sub Saharan Africa (2). Lagging behind on reliable electricity
access puts an additional strain on these countries fragile economy, as many businesses or small
communities depend on expensive, unreliable diesel power generators to compensate for poor energy
coverage. This further cripples the development of these regions, creating a cycle which is diﬃcult
to escape from (3). Furthermore, the reliance on diesel generators also generates health problems
due to the emanation of toxic fumes, which burdens further the populations and puts an additional
economic cost on the country. In developing countries, access to energy in the zones that are already
covered by the grid is often plagued by insuﬃcient capacity, which results in recurrent blackouts.
Those blackouts put a strain on the local economy, as businesses can not rely on a steady energy
supply. According to the International Renewable Energy Agency, the additional cost of individual
generators was estimated to range from 1% to 5% of GDP in most sub Saharan country’s economy
(4).
1.1.1
The situation of informal settlements
This situation of energy precarity is accentuated in informal settlements, or slums, which are a deﬁn-
ing feature of the developing world. Informal settlements are urban areas that lack planning and
suﬃcient infrastructure, home to destitute populations while government implication is often mini-
mal. They constitute a large share of urban areas in developing countries, as they are a byproduct
of rural-urban migrations and rapid urban growth (5). Informal settlements are a peculiar situation
regarding energy access in developing countries, as they are located in urban or peri-urban areas,
which boast higher electriﬁcation rates than rural ones (1). However these settlements are impover-
ished enclaves that lack formal electricity access plans. The insecurity of land tenure is key here, as
slum dwellers lack an oﬃcial claim to the land they occupy, and are not recognised as viable energy
consumers by energy providers. This is further aggravated by the fact that government development
plans often disregard the need to upgrade informal settlements structures, including energy access
(6). This stands opposed to rural areas that are included as priority in most energy access plans
in developing countries, such as the Ghana Energy Commission, that set rural electriﬁcation as one
of the country’s leading development priority. Therefore the trend is to an increase in informal
settlement dwellers due to rural exodus and high demographic growth in developing countries, fur-
ther worsening the share of people deprived of reliable energy access. Energy access in informal
settlements is not necessarily absent, but often characterized as an unreliable source, prone to power
shortages and blackouts. Connections are often made out of illegal tapping of the grid, which is then
sold to the local population by those that managed to get a hold of it (7).

1.2
Presentation of Ghana
1.2.1
Geography and demographics
Ghana is a west African country, bordered by Togo to the east, Burkina Faso to the north, the
Ivory Coast to the west and the Gulf of Guinea to the south. It spans 238 540 km², including a
3,5% water area. Its population as of 2017 was 29,6 million inhabitants, spanning various ethnic,
linguistic and religious groups. The country is tropical, enjoying a wet season that extends from
April to November, and a dry season the rest of the year. Sun irradiation is overall high, providing
a potential for solar power generation that will be explored later on in this paper (8) (9). The
capital city Accra is the largest settlement in the country, with a metropolitan area of over 1,6
million inhabitants; it is the largest employment and business area in Ghana. The country has been
aﬀected by a rural exodus, more populations leaving the countryside to larger urban center such as
Accra. In 1960, 23% of the Ghanaian population was qualiﬁed as urban, which rose to 43% in 2000
and 56% in 2018, according to the Ghana Statistical Service (2018) (10).
1.2.2
Economic situation
Ghana is a developing country with a GDP of 65,556 M$ and a growth rate of 6,3% as of 2018,
according to the World Bank’s Overview of Ghana, relying on 2018 data. Most of the workforce is
employed in the agricultural sector, which accounts for 53% in 2013. Ghana is the world’s second
cocoa producer, with over 830,000 tons in 2018. However services play a signiﬁcant part in the
country’s economy, with 28% of the workforce and 50% of the GDP in 2015. The country boasts
signiﬁcant natural resources, exporting substantial amounts of oil, gold and bauxite, the main alu-
minium ore. Overall the economy of Ghana has been growing steadily for more than a decade,
providing a model of stability for neighboring sub Saharan African countries It is however subject
to the ﬂuctuation of the oil markets, that constitute a large part of the Ghanaian exports ; as such,
it resulted in a reduction of growth over the years 2013 and 2016 due to low oil prices. Growth fell
from 7,3% in 2013 to 3,7% in 2016. Private sector credit grows stronger, supported largely by the
well-capitalized banking sector. Inﬂation has been kept in single digits since 2018 (11).
1.2.3
Energy situation
Electricity consumption is low in Ghana, with an average of 351 kWh per capita per year; as a
comparison the world’s average consumption is 3133 kWh, and a danish citizen consumes 5860 kWh
per year. This low electricity consumption is relatively low even by sub saharan african standards,
with a mean of 481 kWh in the region (2). This below average consumption can be explained by
several factors.
Ghana has followed since the independence in 1957 an intensive policy of electriﬁcation, relying
on hydro power generation. The country’s vast network of rivers and streams has allowed for the
creation of vast hydro electric projects, such as the Volta River dam, with an installed capacity of
1020 MW. The overall hydro capacity in Ghana is 1,6 MW, which accounts for 42% of the country’s
3,8 MW installed capacity (World Bank 2015).
The rest of the production is achieved through
thermal plants relying on natural gas. 85% of the population had access to electricity through the
grid in 2017, this ﬁgure being the second in sub Saharan Africa behind South Africa (2). Energy
production in Ghana has historically been a state-managed business, with hydro power being held
by the Volta River Authority (VRA), a 100% state-owned company, with more than 2,3 MW of
capacity installed, composed of hydro electric sites supplemented by thermal power plants. The
Volta River Authority used to be in charge of transmission of electricity in the country as well as
2
==PAGE:11==
production, prior to 2006. Independent Power Producers (IPPs) have been entering the market
over the last decade but they only accounted for 770 kWh of installed capacity in 2015, 20% of the
installed capacity. The transmission system is operated by the state-owned Ghana Grid Company
(GRIDCo), which was incorporated in 2006 following the separation of the transmission functions
of the VRA from its other activities. Distribution of electricity is carried out by the Electricity
Company of Ghana (ECG), with 2.6 million customers, which accounts for 90 percent of retail
power sales, and the Northern Electricity Distribution Company (NEDCo), a subsidiary of VRA,
which services the remaining 10 percent of the market. The state-run power structure in Ghana
has been characterized by a low eﬃciency over the last decade, with debt growing to 2,3 B$ as of
March 2017. The World Bank’s reports highlight the fact that The sector’s ﬁnancial diﬃculties
mainly stem from: (a) high cost of fuel used by thermal power plants; (b) gas supply shortages ; (c)
high payments for installed capacity to EPPs and IPPs; (d) high distribution losses; (e) low revenue
collections by ECG; and (f) non-payment by Government entities.(2) Losses in the grid are singled
out in this report, which states that up to 25% of the overall losses stem from transmission losses.
On the ﬁnancial level, contracts between state and the main energy producers are characterized
by "take or pay" contracts, common in the energy sector, which specify that the state agrees to
pay a minimum compensation for the energy generated by the producer, even if it is not consumed
in its entirety.
This leads to a lower risk for the producers but aggravated ﬁnancial losses for
the government. These contracts decrease the competitiveness of the energy sector by asserting
the monopoly of the existing suppliers. As such, Ghana currently suﬀers from overproduction of
energy, which has to be compensated by the state. As of 2015, the demand for energy in Ghana was
estimated to be 2,7 MW, while the installed capacity is 3,8 MW. Negotiations have been initiated by
the IPPs as of 2018, to re negotiate the take-or-pay accords between the government and the state-
owned companies. The ﬁnal aim is to challenge these monopolies and allow for more competition
within the energy sector, which would result in lower costs for the government (7). Ghana currently
plans to shift part of its supply to renewable sources other than hydraulic, to mitigate its reliance
on fossil fuels. The country indeed possesses vast solar resources potential, with a high average sun
irradiation. The assessments carried out for the Renewable Energy Master Plan record a mean of
5 kWh/sqm/day over the country, with sunshine duration of 1800 to 3000 hours per year. While
the highest opportunities for solar are located within the northern belt of the country, all regions
display a satisfying potential (9).
1.3
Rationale
1.3.1
Why Ghana ?
Developing countries have the fastest growing economy in the world, with growth rates frequently
over 6% (World Bank Data, 2018). Ghana currently presents a higher electriﬁcation rate than most
sub Saharan Africa countries but 16% of its population still remains to be connected in 2018. For
the growth of its economy to be sustainable, Ghana needs to improve its energy access rate as energy
access is a key factor to economic development (12). Furthermore, Ghana has witnessed a signiﬁcant
demographic growth over the last decade, from 19,3 millions in 2000 to 29,8 millions in 2018. This
growth is representative of the demographic growth experienced by sub-saharan Africa as a whole,
jumping from 680 millions inhabitants to 1,080 millions over the same time frame. The population
boom in turn leads to an increase in energy demand over the territory. As a combination of this
factors, energy demand is increasing rapidly in Ghana, and the current power structure has to be
developed to meet it (7). In sub Saharan Africa as a whole, there is an opportunity to redesign
the power system while it is still under development and base it on renewable energy, rather than
3
==PAGE:12==
facing new challenges like the ones developed countries currently have to tackle, such as a shift to a
decarbonized economy. Finally, Ghana boasts a relatively strong economy based on its exports, and
has been stable politically for more than thirty years, which presents a favourable environment for
the implementation a renewable energy projects. Renewable sources themselves have been integrated
to its energy mix for a long period now, with over 40% of the power being supplied by hydro power
in 2018 (9).
1.3.2
Why informal settlements ?
Informal settlements make up a large proportion of the urban population of western Africa, up to
50% in Sub-Saharan countries (13). This share is expected to grow as urbanisation drains the rural
areas, and climatic refugees pour in for sub Saharan regions. Energy access is a key factor to several
development goals, including education, poverty eradication, gender equality, as acknowledged by
the SDG N°7 (12). Sub Saharan Africa displays one of the lowest electricity access rates in the
world, alongside south east Asia, with two sections of the population being primarily left out : rural
inhabitants and informal settlements dwellers. Informal settlements pose new problems regarding
energy access. They do not suﬀer from remoteness, but their inhabitants are plagued by a lack of
integration to the urban network, with little access to energy, water and diﬃculties providing ID or
housing certiﬁcates (13) (14).
Informal settlements form a large part of Ghana’s urban population, which is true for the developing
world as a whole. They are left out of most electriﬁcation schemes which focus on electricity for
the rural areas, forming pockets of underserved populations trapped in poverty cycles (9). This
matter becomes crucial as it is on a rising trend : developing countries experience a massive rural
exodus, where rural dwellers resettle to slums as the only urban zone they can aﬀord (15). In sub
Saharan Africa, 200 millions people, or 61% of the urban population, live in informal settlements.
This proportion is expected to rise as the building of aﬀordable structures is outpaced by rural-urban
migrations (13). Electriﬁcation in these zones could prove crucial in empowering these people and
allowing them to move out of poverty (12). In comparison, rural electriﬁcation faces mostly technical
challenges such as remoteness, rugged terrain or lack of ﬁnancial incentive to complete the projects
(16). Informal settlements however also face a problem of recognition as citizens entitled to energy
access. The main grid is accessible physically, however electricity services are often not available
in those settlements. Most informal settlements are indeed characterized by the lack of security of
tenure. The population is not legally allowed to occupy the land, resulting in denial of services from
various actors. The lack of oﬃcial land tenure and of recognition by the state are obstacles that
have to be overcome, before facing the challenge of developing a physical solution (17) (18).
1.3.3
Why renewables ?
It has been argued by some sources that sub Saharan Africa’s need for development could be a
reason to put renewable energy use in the background, and focus on developing the power capacity
of the countries, using cheaper carbon sources (19). Indeed, western countries that are currently
focusing on renewable energy sources have achieved their level of development relying mainly on
fossil fuels, it could be argued that developing countries are entitled to the same opportunities.
However this rhetoric is overlooking the fact that development through carbon sources is inherently
not sustainable, and could not provide stability in the long run, as fossil fuels are bound to run out
on a human timescale. On another level, this reasoning fails to acknowledge the health cost of such
technologies, as diesel emanations and carbon cooking fuels contribute to signiﬁcant life expectancy
reduction in the developing world (20). Finally, carbon taxes are already being implemented in
4
==PAGE:13==
the western world as a way to curb CO2 emissions through abatement; their implementation in
developing countries is a possibility in the foreseeable future, which would further burden fossil fuel
technologies.
However developing countries are not compelled to follow the same path as developed ones, that
are currently undergoing costly and diﬃcult decarbonization procedures. As much of the power
structure remains to be developed, sub saharan Africa can be envisioned as a tabula rasa where
renewables can be implemented. The sub continent boasts important renewable resources, solar
irradiation being signiﬁcant due to the tropical location (21). Exploring hybrid structures such as
a solar and diesel system could prove to be an interesting compromise, as they pave the way for
further renewable penetration, while securing a reliable, cheap power-supply.
1.3.4
Why oﬀ-grid ?
Most of the electriﬁcation is achieved through grid connection in the current era. Since 2000, out of
1,2 billion people that received electriﬁcation, nearly all of them had it through a grid connection (22).
Therefore connection to the grid is often assimilated with electriﬁcation in government electriﬁcation
plans.
However oﬀgrid solutions are emerging as cost-eﬀective solutions to break the circle of
isolation that some communities experience due to diﬃcult energy access (23). They are among
other solutions being recommended by the World Bank as a way to extend energy access (2). It
reduces drastically the eﬀect of grid losses, which currently plague the sub Saharan African power
sector, and does not imply the construction of a grid structure, lowering the overall expenses (24).
According to the International Energy Agency, half of the new electricity access by 2030 will be
through oﬀ-grid means (21). In the case of urban informal settlements, the distance to the grid is
not the main challenge, but the connection is not established due to complex socio-economic factors.
The implementation of an oﬀ-grid solution possibly managed by the community would simplify the
matter, enhancing the communities autonomy while reducing the gap with formal neighbourhoods.
1.4
Research objectives
The goal of this paper would be to analyze the current framework conditions and institutional
arrangements to provide guidelines towards improving energy access in informal settlements of de-
veloping countries, based on a case study in Ghana. The aim of this project is to document the
energy access situation of an informal settlement, and study the impact an oﬀ-grid energy system
could have on this situation. A series of interviews from local stakeholders, including members from
international development associations and possibly NGOs, is meant to supplement the research
and provide additional insights. The focus would be on the possibility of developing an oﬀ-grid,
decentralized energy system that could be implemented by cooperatives in their neighbourhood.
The ideal eﬀects of such a structure would be a better integration of the neighborhood to the urban
network and a reduction of inequalities between informal settlements dwellers and regular urbans.
Local populations would reap social and economic beneﬁts from an improved energy access, creating
a virtuous circle.
The principal objective of this research is to outline the main factors necessary to achieve reliable,
cheap and sustainable electriﬁcation in informal settlements in Ghana. A case study will be carried
out in a selected informal settlement in Ghana.
Then the aim is to propose an oﬀ-grid energy
solution ﬁtting the need of the chosen settlement, based upon socioeconomic and ﬁnancial factors.
The ﬁrst sub-objective to achieve this will be to assess the situation of informal settlement dwellers
in Accra, focusing on the slum of Old Fadama, the largest in the Accra area. This will include

establishing contacts with local stakeholders, and assess the energy situation of the population :
consumption patterns, occurrence of blackouts, ﬁnancial means, legality of the connection.
In parallel, a second sub-objective will be to document the Ghanaian policy so far regarding electri-

1.5
Deﬁnition of key terms
1.5.1
Informal settlement
Informal settlements have been deﬁned during Habitat III, the United Nations Conference on Hous-
ing and Sustainable Urban Development in October 2016, as settlements following at least one of
three criteria :
• inhabitants have no security of tenure vis-à-vis the land or dwellings they inhabit, with modal-
ities ranging from squatting to informal rental housing
• the neighbourhoods usually lack, or are cut oﬀfrom, basic services and city infrastructure
• the housing may not comply with current planning and building regulations, and is often situated
in geographically and environmentally hazardous areas
In addition, informal settlement dwellers (i.e. slum dwellers) face signiﬁcant socioeconomic exclusion
as well as a spatial one. Slums are often associated with a negative image which aﬀects access to
employment, social services and overall equity of chances. This image has been identiﬁed by source
as a major challenge faced by slum dwellers to escape poverty, as informal settlements tend to trap
their inhabitants in a downward socioeconomic spiral source (25).
1.5.2
Renewable energy
The International Panel on Climate Change (IPCC) deﬁnes renewable energy as: “Any form of
energy from solar, geophysical or biological sources that is replenished by natural processes at a rate
that equals or exceeds its rate of use. Renewable energy is obtained from the continuing or repetitive
ﬂows of energy occurring in the natural environment and includes low-carbon technologies such as
solar energy, hydro power, wind, tide and waves and ocean thermal energy, as well as renewable fuels
such as biomass.” (26). Renewable energy generation has been developed extensively over the last
decades, providing cost-eﬃcient ways of producing sustainable energy under electrical, thermal or
6
==PAGE:15==
mechanical form. Some generating technologies such as solar arrays are suited for oﬀ-grid generation,
which will be considered in this study.
1.5.3
Socio-spatial exclusion
According to Habitat III (2016), socio-spatial exclusion is a concept that :
refers to the processes that contribute to the geographic marginalization of particular individuals and
groups because of where they live and who they are. It is characterized by their inability to access
or eﬀectively use a whole range of facilities and resources which improve well-being and position
people to take advantage of available opportunities. Particular groups and individuals often suﬀer
a disproportionate ‘disadvantage’ because of their identity, which is physically represented in urban
contexts by the presence of informal settlements (25)
1.5.4
Techno-economic assessment
A techno-economic assessment is a way of evaluating the feasibility of a project by analyzing the
technological appropriateness and economic viability of a system. This is usually done through a cost-
beneﬁt analysis which compares diﬀerent costs and beneﬁts associated with the project. The costs
assessment takes into account two types of costs: the investment costs (CAPEX) and operational
costs (OPEX). The beneﬁts assessment covers all the possible proﬁts from diﬀerent sources such
as the sale of electricity, the sale of a speciﬁc product, government beneﬁts, carbon beneﬁts or the
sale of green certiﬁcates. This method generally takes into account the time-value of money and
therefore brings both costs and beneﬁts to a present value (27).
1.5.5
Oﬀ-grid energy generation
An oﬀ-grid energy structure is independent from the country’s main power grid, and generates power
that will be consumed locally, usually through a local mini grid. It is a loose term, as it encompasses
house-sized appliances as well as plants large enough to fuel the demand of a community (2). It is
well suited for remote communities, or for those willing to be independent from the grid. Oﬀ-grid
reduces the risk of power outage due to a blackout or a grid disruption, making the supply more
reliable in countries where those disruptions occur frequently. As the wind and solar technologies
get cheaper, renewable oﬀ-grid solution become more aﬀordable on a small scale (4).
1.5.6
Slum upgrading
Slum upgrading encompasses the methods to improve on an informal settlement structure, taking
into account the dwellers needs. UN Habitat III deﬁnes slum upgrading is the following terms :
[slum upgrading] is a methodological approach that aims to address urban development imbalances
represented by slum dwellers’ living. It engages and puts all key urban stakeholders – all levels of gov-
ernment, community representatives, civil society, non-government organizations, academia, private
sector and, especially, slum dwellers – at the heart of the process to improve slums’ living standards.
This multi-stakeholder platform is considered more likely to promote the necessary partnerships,
governance arrangements, institutional structures and ﬁnancing options which result in inclusive
planning and sustainable outcomes. Slum dwellers, in particular, have important knowledge, skills
and capacity to contribute, direct and own the upgrading process, and an inclusive approach to-
wards the improvement of their living conditions brings fundamental socio cultural changes towards
a rights-based society. (25)
7
==PAGE:16==
Slum clearance Slum upgrading stands opposed to slum clearance which aims at replacing the slum
by a new neighbourhood, destroying the previous settlement.
While this process is sometimes
accompanied with relocation initiatives, it often disregards the needs of the dwellers and simply
drives them away through forced eviction. This policy has historically been prevalent in sub Saharan
Africa, where rapid urbanisation leads to an increase in land value, prompting governments to evict
poor populations with an unsure claim to the land. These initiatives can stem from an intention to
redevelop the urban structure, to build the image of the city according to more modern aspirations,
or from the original owner of the land asserting their ownership rights to it (28).

3. The concept of slum upgrading
A thorough understanding of the concept of slum upgrading is needed before delving into the project
itself.
Slum upgrading describes the processes aiming at improving on an informal settlement’s
infrastructure, and in a broader sense improving the quality of life of its residents. By considering
the deﬁnition of informal settlements provided by UN Habitat 2015, a more comprehensive deﬁnition
of slum upgrading can be deducted (25). It is an economic, social and environmental intervention
process which encompasses :
• developing access to services, of which the tenants are deprived. This includes sanitation, clean
running water, energy access, etc
• improving on the existing structures of the slum to be in compliance with building regulations,
i.e. comfort and safety. This can for instance include urban planning processes improving on
ﬁre safety, which is a recurrent threat in slum areas
• regarding legal framework, slum upgrading also comprises procedures to improve the tenants
existence in regard of the law.
Slum upgrading involves multiple stakeholders, including but not limited to : tenants,landlords,
citizen associations, governmental institutions, as well as exterior development partners (NGOs,
International Development Institutions, etc) (32). The reasons behind slum upgrading are varied
and dependent on context, but are usually aimed at poverty reduction, health and environmental
considerations.
3.1
Slum upgrading as opposed to slum clearance
Historically, the stance of several governments facing an informal settlement problem has been to
resort to slum clearance (33). This approach involves the demolition of the slum structures, to make
room for newer, more viable constructions. These procedures can incorporate a housing plan for
the displaced populations, however this is not always the case. Land clearance initiatives, common
to many African countries, aim not only at valuating the land but also at halting the unchecked
growth of informal settlements in the urban centres (34). Indeed the role of the government in these
matters is complex as it has to
• 1) provide basic services to every citizen, even in the most impoverished areas
• 2) stem the growth of informal settlements that is expected to increase substantially until 2050
at least (Cohen 2004: 24; UN-Habitat 2010: 1)
• 3) uphold the rule of law, and limit the encroachment of squatter settlements on the land that
does not belong to them (34),
As a whole, governments are willing to halt the growth of informal settlements in urban centers,
with 84% of less developed country governments wanting to reduce rural to urban migrations, up
from 73% in the previous round. as reported by the (11). This ﬁgure illustrates a hardening in the
stance of emerging countries governments regarding the development of informal settlements.
12
==PAGE:21==
Such actions have been undertaken, among other examples, in Lagos (Nigeria), directed at the
informal settlements of Maroko in the years nineteen-nineties (Recent slum clearance exercise in
Lagos (Nigeria): victims or beneﬁciaries). There the main objective the forceful removal of the
slum, and the demolition of the houses to achieve a better valuation of the land. This is often a
drive behind eviction of informal settlements tenants, as the governments see less economic beneﬁts
in improving on the condition of the tenants, rather than evict them and valuate the land through
real estate investments (28). The same trend is still observed in Accra (Ghana) more recently, with
slum clearing policies being carried out in 2015, in the very slum of Old Fadama, the largest informal
settlement in the capital. In the wake of massive ﬂoods that damaged the cities infrastructure (35),
authorities forcibly removed several shacks situated in ﬂood-risk areas, through the use of bulldozers
assisted by police. The initiative sparked riots and clashes between the police and the slum dwellers,
further widening the gap between tenants and authorities (34). Non-governmental stakeholders have
pointed out the AMA has been using the ﬂoods as a pretext to intervene in Old Fadama, which
was allegedly unjustiﬁed according to CORHE, the Center on Housing Rights and Evictions (36).
According to these accounts, the AMA has instrumentalized environmental concerns linked to the
ﬂoods to push its agenda regarding the Korle Restoration Project, and evict slum dwellers. It so
appears that slum removal is a sensitive matter, and that actions carried out without consultation
of the tenants is likely to breed conﬂict.
3.2
Mechanics of slum upgrading
Slum upgrading relies on improving the extant infrastructure, while taking into account the needs
of the tenants.
It is therefore extremely variable, adaptable to the particular structure of each
community. The aim on the long run is to revitalize the community by enabling the development of
small businesses, and bridge the gap with conventional neighbourhoods (37).
3.2.1
Legal framework
Part of the issue informal settlement tenants struggle with is the lack of recognition from state. Part
of the struggle faced in informal settlements is to acknowledge some commodities as basic rights for
every citizen in the country, including informal settlements dwellers (18). Those basic commodities
are energy access, sanitation, and decent housing ; they match the deﬁnition of informal settlements
by UN Habitat, which are deﬁned by the lack of one of those key factors (25). Therefore the ﬁrst
challenge for informal settlement dwellers is to get the government to acknowledge their duty to
provide those commodities to the tenants (38).
Among those basic rights violation is the inability to provide a physical address, which hampers
the social and economic integration of the tenants. As stated by Dr Joan Clos, Executive Director,
United Nations Settlements Program "At UN-Habitat, we are convinced that everyone has the right
to an address. If you do not have an address you do not oﬃcially exist. An address is the key to a
new life" (25). This lack of physical address has been identiﬁed as one key issue regarding energy
access in slum neighbourhoods (39). Energy companies are reluctant to provide services in a zone
where consumers cannot be formally identiﬁed by means of an address. Therefore the billing process
is complicated, and the accountability of the consumers cannot be properly established (40). Among
propositions to tackle this issue, the use of temporary addresses based on geolocalisation has been
put forward by UN Habitat, a project that is in turn carried out by several non-governmental organ-
isations. Addressing the Unadressed, a non-governmental organisation based in Calcutta (India),
aims for instance at providing a postal address to every slum dweller, using a unique ID based on
geolocalised position. In practical terms, the work is carried out by associating a unique number
13
==PAGE:22==
to each household, functioning as a physical address, as well mapping the settlement through geo-
localised data. Working in collaboration with local postal services ensures this structure is put to
use, and gives signiﬁcant returns according to the organisation reports. By February 2019, the NGO
had so far achieved an average addressing rate of 3000 households a week, aiming to cover all 1,4M
slum dwellers in Calcutta. The number of beneﬁciaries at the same date had reached 300000 overall
around the city (75 000 households), in less than half a year (41). If such a process was implemented
in Ghana, whose slum population is about 5,5 millions as of 2019, it could yield sizable results. The
Old Fadama slum alone, the largest in the Greater Accra area, was home to 79 684 people in 2009,
meaning 34 066 households (29). Assuming a comparable pace of addressing, this means the whole
neighbourhood could be provided with temporary addresses in less than three months, at a minimal
cost according to the Adressing the Unadressed 2019 report.
As a whole slum upgrading is in a large part a matter of legal enabling factors, which aim at
regularising the tenants’ situation in vis-à-vis the law. These actions, which require little ﬁnancial
resources compared to structural changes, can signiﬁcantly improve the tenants situation and in
turn break the circle of isolation which plagues the development of informal settlements (39) (42).
3.2.2
Security of tenure
Another key challenge in informal settlements situation is the approach taken towards land tenure
security. In Slum improvement in India: det (14). Many slums are in a grey area regarding land
tenure, with tenants having no legal claim to the land, occupying it in spite of the law (17). This
makes their habitat temporary in regard to the law, as they have no right to remain where they
currently live. This has been identiﬁed as heavily undermining other slum upgrading processes, since
the tenants do not feel comfortable investing in an area from which they might be evicted. Several
land tenure security acts have been implemented throughout the developing world to secure slum
dwellers claim to the land, with the ﬁnal objective of integrating the settlement in the social and
economic network of the country. This is done by ﬁrst regularizing the occupation through a legal
permit granted to the tenants, protecting them from eviction. In some cases, as in the Kenya national
slum upgrading program, this has then been supplemented by a legalization of land tenure, namely
the opportunity for informal settlements tenants to actively own the land through a government
welfare plan (17).
Situations where slum upgrading policies have been supplemented with land
tenure security initiatives have proved to be more sustainable in the long run than without it (43),
(39). It has also been theorised by Hernando De Soto’s The mystery of capital (2000), that security
of tenure is a key aspect for the economic viability of a settlement in the long term. Assessing the
legality of an occupation also makes the tenants taxable for the state, and can generate revenues
in its own right. It should however be considered that informal settlements dwellers are in general
poor populations that would not generate important wealth from the state’s point of view (17). To
conclude with, the importance of land tenure is an illustration of the former subsection, showing that
the legal aspect is a key aspect of informal settlements problematics. Consistent slum upgrading
policies need to incorporate that legal approach, to supplement structural actions taken in the ﬁeld,
and achieve sustainable results.
3.2.3
Energy access
Among the goals set by UN Habitat III in 2016, improving the life of more than 100 millions
informal settlement dwellers by the year 2020. This translates to an improvement in regard to all
the aforementioned basic commodities, energy among them. The satisfaction of basic energy needs
in impoverished urban areas is at the crossroad of two of the Sustainable Development Goals set
14
==PAGE:23==
in 2015 : SDG n°7 (Ensure access to aﬀordable, reliable, sustainable and modern energy for all)
and SDG n°11 (Make cities and human settlements inclusive, safe, resilient and sustainable) (25).
This research paper will in the later parts focus more speciﬁcally on Energy access in informal
settlements. Energy demand has been on the rise worldwide on an uninterrupted trend since the
1960s, and is currently augmenting at a faster pace in developing countries (19). Energy access
fuel businesses, employment, and indirectly access to education - therefore if informal settlements
are to keep up with the economic trend of their country and break socio economic exclusion, their
access to energy is a crucial factor (18). At the same time, new, increasingly cost-eﬃcient power-
generation technologies are emerging on the market, including new generation solar panels, with solar
panels dropping to 25% of their price over the last ten years for comparable capacity (22), eﬀective
research in mini-grid department (23), among other solutions. These alternative solutions to the
classic power plant - grid - consumer model have been endorsed by the United Nations Development
Program as viable solutions to bridge the energy gap in the developing world (44). This phenomenon,
paralleled with the low energy access rates and lack of power generation structures, make informal
settlements a new ground for innovation, a tabula rasa where new forms of low-cost, reliable energy
systems can be implemented, destined to a young, dynamic population which would beneﬁt from
any increase in energy supply (6). Therefore, a large share of urban upgrading projects include a
plan for amelioration of energy access, and it shall be the aim of this research to explore one of these
alternatives.
3.2.4
Sanitation
Another key pillar of slum upgrading processes aims at improving sanitation structures. This in-
cludes access to clean water ﬁt for human consumption, as well as sanitary structures for hygiene
and disposal of human waste. As of 2018, only 85,1% of the world’s urban population consumed
water from safely managed water services, an actual drop from 86,3% in 2002, according to the Wold
Bank (42). The burgeonning development of informal settlements is one of the key factors behind
that regression. Sustainable Development Goal n°6 : "Ensure access to water and sanitation for
all" consequently acknowledges the need for proper access to these commodities, and has set the
target to achieve 100% drinking water and sanitation access by 2030 (12). Sanitation is therefore
a cornerstone of slum upgrading processes. However the model for sanitation development on the
community level has remained rather unchanged for the last decades, and is mostly reliant on the
neighbourhood being plugged to a sustainably managed water network (45). While it is acknowl-
edged as a major aspect of slum upgrading, it will not be the focus of this research. However some
problematics linked to water management in the Korle Lagoon in Accra will be explored, as they
translate an aspect of the issue of slum governance by the government (35).
3.2.5
Structural improvement
A third prong of slum upgrading policies is structural improvement of the dwellings and slum struc-
tures, meant to provide more decent conditions of living for the tenants, while complying with
institutional regulations, specially in the ﬁelds of air quality, ﬁre safety and density of occupation.
This approach has historically been that of slum clearance (46), with new urban plans replacing the
informal structure. These policies will not be the main focus of this paper, however some policies of
the Ghanaian government to approach urban planning in some slum areas will be looked into.

3.3
Top down versus bottom-up approach
As it has been explained in the previous sections, slum upgrading is a process that encompasses
both legislation and regulation adjustments, as well as physical intervention by the development of
structure. M. Areﬁin "The World Bank and Slum Upgrading" (2018), underlines another distinction
between slum upgrading initiatives, diﬀerence between top-down approach and bottom-up approach
(33). They can be deﬁned as follows in the case of slum-upgrading :
• top-down approaches are usually initiated by exterior actors, and include a general plan than
will be implemented in several locations. They are more suited for governmental or development
stakeholders.
• bottom-up approaches include a participatory action from local stakeholders, such as NGOs or
tenants. They originate from one point, usually a settlement or a community, with scaling-up
being a possibility.
According to "What Is the Evidence on Top-Down and Bottom-Up Approaches in Improving Access
To Water, Sanitation and Electricity Services in Low-Income or Informal Settlements?" (Annamalai,
Thillai Rajan Devkar, Ganesh Mahalingam, Ashwin Benjamin, Solomon Rajan, Sudhir Chella, 2016),
a comprehensive study on urban upgrading policies by the UK Aid Foundation, bottom-up and top-
down approaches show various results according to the speciﬁc ﬁeld (40). It appears overall bottom-
up approaches are sometimes better suited in the case of an informal settlements, due to the need for
a certain customization of the response. Among other ﬁndings Services provided by public or private
agencies through centralised planning and implementation (top- down) appeared eﬀective in individ-
ual studies for connecting populations to water, sanitation and electricity. However, where studies
were suﬃciently similar to justify pooling ﬁndings in a statistical meta-analysis, this conclusion was
not conﬁrmed. Qualitative synthesis of contextual factors suggest a need for the customisation of
solutions to meet local needs, and better delivery of services by alternative/non-government service
providers.
(Summary, pp 3) However the study underlines the scaling-up problem met in most
bottom-up approaches. Furthermore, land tenure security is again put forward as a key factor for
the success of slum-upgrading processes: Neighbourhoods without security of tenure are rarely served
well top-down. Bottom-up approaches are also limited in this context, and also in Africa where eﬀorts
may be hampered by particularly modest levels of economic development. Public-private partnerships
show promise for top-down approaches to improving water supply. Bottom-up, NGO led initiatives
for improving water supply need the cooperation and support of the public sector. (Summary, pp3).
As it is detailed by P. Syagga (2012), state-backed initiatives are better suited to improve the land
tenure conditions in informal settlements. Such an initiative falls under the denomination of the
top-down approach deﬁned further-up. It seems to appear that a combination between top-down
and bottom-up approaches would yield better results. This same conclusion is reached in by An-
namalai (2016), emphasizing the need for a close collaboration between local stakeholders carrying
out bottom-up approaches and the government/policy makers (17) (40).
3.4
Limitations and criticism of slum upgrading
Slum upgrading is currently viewed as a sound method for improve living conditions in informal
settlements, and is promoted by major development actors such as the World Bank. The policy of
loans at low rates of interests to promote urban upgrading has been on the World Bank’s agenda
since its inception in 1944, supplemented by IMF loans favoring the country’s macro-economic
16
==PAGE:25==
development (33). It was again promoted by Cities Alliance and the World Bank through their
1999 "Cities without Slums" action plan (13), as a way to develop slums through investment and
entrepreneurship. However, this approach has also been criticized by some sources as a gateway
to minimal state commitment to the amelioration of slums, relying on non-interventionist policies
and leaving development in the hands of private actors. In "The Slum Upgrading Myth" (1999), H.
Werlin outlines the fact that for slum upgrading policies to be sustainable, they have to be backed by
the state ﬁnancially and legally. He provides examples of entrepreneur-driven initiatives in Jakarta,
Calcutta and Manilla over the course of the years 1990, that eventually failed to provide signiﬁcant
improvements despite positive early results. H. Werlin emphasizes again the role of land tenure
security in sustainability of slum upgrading policies, a recurrent theme in later publications (43)
(25)(17)(39).
3.5
Extant slum upgrading policies in Ghana
All sub Saharan countries face signiﬁcant slum issues (cf. Intro), and Ghana is no exception with
40% of its urban population living in informal settlements in 2014 (15). Ghana has experienced a
substantial population growth since the 1960s, as well as steep an urban population boom : from
6,6 millions Ghanaians in 1960 to 29,7 millions in 2018; while urban population sprung from 23%
to 56% in the same time fram. Migration from the countryside is mainly motivated by the search
for job opportunities, as documented by the 2009 study "Housing the Masses : Final Report To
People’s Dialogue on Human Settlements (29) in the informal settlement of Old Fadama (Accra),
a key document for this research paper. It appears in the study that 95% of the respondents came
from the countryside to Accra in search of a job or to do business. This rapid development has led
to various response from the Ghanaian government. On the one hand, as it has been the custom in
numerous third world countries since the 1960s, a policy of slum clearance and forced evictions has
been employed to curb the growth of unchecked informal settlements. Since the 1980s however, the
long term eﬃciency of these have been put into question, specially in the light of the slum removal
policies carried out in south-east Asia and the Indian sub-continent (39). In the recent years, the
informal settlement of Old Fadama, the largest of the 78 slums in Accra, has been under a legal
eviction notice from the Accra Metropolitan Authority since 2002, in the wake of an environmental
clean-up act of the Korle Lagoon, the main body of water which borders the settlement. However the
evictions have only been partial and limited to the borders of the slum, the authorities acknowledging
that a major displacement of population would generate additional issues. Yet the eviction notice
legally prevents public investments and service provision within the neighbourhood, placing the
settlements situation in a grey area (34). This situation has been deemed representative of that of
major informal settlements in Ghana, with slums being condemned by the law without the eviction
being implemented (46), limiting the involvement of the state in the matter and de-facto creating
a nomotropic (i.e. acting in of light of the rules, not necessarily according to them). Authors have
characterized this zone where local stakeholders negotiate with the authorities to access services such
as water supply or electricity as "nomotropic" area, existing outside of the rule of law but deﬁning
their own ensemble of rules to live as a community (47) (48).
A slum upgrading plan has indeed been implemented in Ghana since the year 1999 in partnership
with the World Bank, based upon the development of small businesses to favor the economic vitality
of informal settlements. The initiative relies on the development of basic needs infrastructure by
local contractors, using state and international development funds (37). This government stance has
however been called out by local authorities as a way for the government to limit its involvement in
the slum upgrading process, while leaving the settlements to mostly fend oﬀfor themselves (34). A
17
==PAGE:26==
particular notice was given, again, to the crucial aspect of land tenure regularisation that the govern-
ment could act upon to facilitate slum upgrading initiatives. The policy of the Ghanaian government
regarding land tenure security in informal settlements has not yet been swayed, perpetuating the
status quo of illegal-yet-tolerated land occupation (49).

4. Case study
In this part the informal settlement of Old Fadama in Accra will be studied under its social and eco-
nomic aspects. The objective is to assess its current socio-economic situation, to determine whether
it could beneﬁt from an oﬀ-grid energy system, and identify speciﬁc scenarios that a dedicated power
system could respond to.
4.1
Location
The city of Accra is the capital and the largest settlement in Ghana. It counts over 1,6 millions
inhabitants, while the larger Greater Accra Metropolitan Area is home to approximately 4 millions
people. It is the thirteenth largest agglomeration in Africa, as well as Ghana’s main hub for ed-
ucation, ﬁnance and tourism. It houses substantial ﬁshing, processing and textile industry, and
has been designated as a Gamma level world city by the Globalization and World Cities Research
Network since 2010, indicating its increased international inﬂuence (50). The informal settlement
of Old Fadama is the largest in the metropolitan region of Accra, and the largest in Ghana (29).
It is located close to the center of the city, on the banks of the Odaw river and the Korle Lagoon,
the main body of water of the agglomeration. According to satellite measurements, the slum spans
an area of 31 hectares as of 2019, or 0,31 square kilometer. The settlement is made of two distinct
areas, the older portion of Old Fadama and the so-called Newer Fadama. Older Fadama is the
northern portion of the settlement, situated around the Agbogbloshie Yam market, and displays
structures built in concrete, a better organisation of the streets, public toilets, drains and partly
legal electricity access. Newer Fadama is located on lower ground by the banks of the Odaw river
and the lagoon (49). Most of the shacks are made of composite materials, i.e. corrugated steel and
such, sanitary facilities are lackluster with population sometimes resorting to open defecation, and
electricity access comes through illegal tappings according to the interview carried out for this study.
4.2
Demographics
Most of the data regarding Old Fadama was made available using a report from 2010, organized by
the non-governmental organisation People’s Dialogue in Ghana ((29). The organisation is aﬃliated
to the international NGO Slum Dwellers International. The main goal of the survey is stated as
follows : The enumeration was undertaken to provide baseline data on the socio-economic proﬁle
of households, establish the number of persons and the various groups that live in the community
to ensure realistic provisions made in the event of a relocation and establish the possibility of the
residents relocating to other areas in the city or back to their places of origin. It proved to be an
invaluable source to assess the situation in Old Fadama. Population was established to be around
80000 souls in 2010, however more recent estimates ((34)) place it around 100000 in 2019. Conﬁned
to a reduced area of 31,3 hectares in 2019, this yields an extremely high population density of 322.000
people per square kilometer. The population of the slum stands out as young, with 76,9% of the
tenants below 35. 37,8% enter the 0-18 age category, and only 7% of the tenants above 55, reﬂecting
a young population. Genders are even with 50,01% males and 49,99% females according to the 2009
census (29). Regarding origins, most of the tenants are not natives of the Accra region, with under
10% of tenants in this situation, whereas close to 70% hailing from the northern region of Ghana,
and over 20% from the central regions of the country. This is represented in the ethnic mix present
19
==PAGE:28==
in Old Fadama, with 49% of the people being part of the northern Dagomba people, that are much
less represented in the south of Ghana. They are followed by Akan people (15%) and Konkomba
people (12%). Most of the settlers arrived in Accra for economic reasons, i.e. looking for a job or
expanding business activities, with 95% of respondents ﬁtting that criteria. Old Fadama houses very
little refugees, with only 2% of the tenants declaring they have ﬂed tribal conﬂicts in the north of
the country (29). It is crucial to note that over 90% of the people in Old Fadama consider it "their
home" and not a transit camp (34). Only 23% of the respondents would consider moving to another
city f the settlement was to be destroyed.
Regarding the economic activity of the slum, 96% of the population in Old Fadama is active,
displaying an extremely active population. Most of the respondents are involved in the informal
sector, outside of the control of the government. This includes handling and carrying, taxi driving,
recycling of materials in the nearby Agbogbloshie scrapyard, or businesses on markets such as the
Agbogbloshie Yam market, one of the main food markets in Accra ((50)). In that sub-population,
56% of the people work within the area of the slum. However 15% of the respondents declared they
were working in the formal sector, mostly as employees ((29)).
4.3
History of the settlement
The site currently occupied by Old Fadama is documented to have been settled since the 1880s, dur-
ing the transition of Accra from a ﬁshing village to a city, under colonial rule. The ensuing economic
and demographic growth have led to the site being occupied by temporary tenants, speciﬁcally from
the northern Ga people that were used as factory workers mid-century. Ownership of the land was
retained by Ga chieftains under a customary process, as opposed to the statutory tenure put into
place during colonial rule. In 1961, ownership of the land was passed on to the government. However
since the years 1980s, Old Fadama has been continuously occupied by populations from the North of
Ghana looking for economic opportunities ((49)). Additional growth was observed over the course of
the 1990s, with the decision of the AMA to relocate a large Yam Market to adjoining Agbogbloshie,
which in turn led to an economic upheaval and a demand for workforce (50).
4.4
Land tenure in Old Fadama
Old Fadama stands on land that was owned by Ga tribal chieftains until 1961 (49). Customary
land ownership has historically been a deﬁning feature of the Ghanaian land tenure issue, and
more generally a deﬁning feature of land tenure in sub-Saharan Africa. This has been identiﬁed as a
factor of conﬂict, since claim to the land can be justiﬁed by customary law, stemming from historical
occupation, whereas it is not recognised by statutory law (51). This has in turn been identiﬁed as
a lever used by the government against poorer populations to evict them from land they consider
to be theirs, which is not correct in the eyes of the statutory law. This system has been identiﬁed
as an inequitable one that favors the Ghanaian elites relying on statutory law inherited from the
colonial era (51). Even though customary institutions such as chieftaincies hold over 80% of the
land, their rights are often overlooked by governmental institutions favoring a western, modern,
statutory system based on written law (52). Old Fadama stems from such circumstances. In 1961,
the freshly independent Ghanaian state acquired these lands by the shores of the Korle Lagoon
from Ga people, for development projects, but then failed to use them and left them unchecked,
leaving the site occupied by Ga populations and later on temporarily resettling northern refugees
on the site in 1980s. The land is technically owned by the government but the tenants, that have
always lived there, consider it to be theirs, in regard to customary law. This identify dual system
20
==PAGE:29==
has been identiﬁed as a key to land tenure issues in Ghana, as the populations and the state will
alternatively take advantage of one of the two systems to justify their occupation of land, since
tenure is illegitimate only in the case of a dual violation of law (48). This dual system is at the root
of the informal settlement problem in Ghana, as it prevents deﬁnitive solutions to land claims issues
(52).
To conclude with, Old Fadama is a typical example of land tenure dispute in Ghana, as occupation
is justiﬁed in the eyes of the tenants by the very fact that they live on the land (48), whereas the
Accra Metropolitan Authority, backed by the Ghana High Court has ruled the occupation as illegal
and ﬁled an eviction order (53). This situation contributes to make the slum’s existence precarious,
in a climate of government-led eviction policies, while the population of Old Fadama is steadily
growing. Growth is stimulated by the unchecked inﬂux of low-income migrants from the north, an
issue the AMA has no direct mean of addressing (54).
4.5
Old Fadama and the Accra Metropolitan Assembly
4.5.1
An acrimonious relationship
Since the establishment of the current slum of Old Fadama during the 1980s, the relationships
between the tenants and the Accra Metropolitan Authority have been strained. In 2001, the AMA
ruled that the settlement was illegal since it is on government land, a situation shared by most of
Accra’s informal settlements ; eviction notices were served around May 2002 (28). However the
community ﬁled an appeal to the Ghana High Court of Justice, through the intervention of the
Center for Public Interest Law (CPIL) and Center on Housing Rights and Evictions (COHRE),
arguing the eviction was a violation of human rights, as deﬁned in the African Charter on Human
and Peoples’ Rights, that Ghana endorses. Nonetheless, the appeal was rejected in 2004 by the High
Court of Ghana (35). Since then, partial evictions have occurred in 2008, 2009, 2011, 2015 and 2020
with destruction of housing leading to clashes between the Ghanaian police and the community.
Due to the highly political aspect of the evictions, and the lack of possible relocation solutions, the
AMA has limited its slum clearances operations to the fringes of the settlement, namely the banks
of the Odaw River and the Korle Lagoon on the south, and the borders of the large avenue Hansen
Road to the north (50).
It should however be noted that the settlement on the banks of the Odaw River is situated on a
notorious ﬂood zone. When the Odaw overﬂows during the wet season, while all of Agbogbloshie
is threatened, the lower section of Old Fadama is systematically ravaged. In 2015, when dire ﬂoods
hit the city, 14 people lost their life in Old Fadama. It is probable that climate change worsens
the weather extremes, and that severe ﬂoods will be a more common occurrence in the following
years (29)(35). Interestingly enough, both formal and informal governing bodies have implemented
policies to try to mitigate the ﬂood risk. The OFADA has since 2011 forbidden the tenants to build
less than 50 m from the bank. The AMA, however, deemed the measure insuﬃcient, and entered
the slum in 2015 accompanied by military personnel, to carry out the destruction of every building
less than 100 meters from the shore (28).
This change of argumentation from the AMA since 2015 has led some sources to consider the AMA
is only using new arguments linked to climate change, to achieve an old goal of reclaiming the lands
of Old Fadama (34).

4.5.2
Eviction policies
These initiatives are part of larger dynamic of the Ghanaian government over the last 20 years,
with forced evictions intensifying since the 2000s. This trend was largely documented by the Centre
on Housing Rights and Evictions (COHRE), with 7000 people forcibly evicted from Lake Volta
between 2003 and 2006, or 800 people relocated from the Legion Village in the north of the country
by the Ghanaian Armed Forces (36). In a similar manner to other local authorities in Ghana, the
Accra Metropolitan Assembly (AMA) has taken “an aggressive, combative, and impulsive stance . . .
against slum dwellers and workers in the informal economy” (55) (50).
4.5.3
Unsustainable policies
These eviction policies have been characterized as unsustainable by CORHE and Slum Dwellers
International, since no reliable alternative is proposed to the evicted tenants, leading to a reformation
of the settlement later on (36). This opinion is also driven home in by A. Afenah (2012) in Urban
Issues, highlighting that while the AMA evicts poor populations, it fails to address the need for
aﬀordable housing in the greater Accra region, rendering the problem unsolvable as the tenants are
not relocated durably (50). These initiatives have been linked by the same author to the ambition
of the city of Accra to ameliorate its image as a developed city while getting rid of the slums. The
same point is developed by S. Greene (2003) in Staged Cities: Mega-events , Slum Clearance , and
Global Capital, stating that informal settlements problems in the developing world are often met
with short-term solutions such as slum clearance. While making the issue temporarily disappear
and improving the image of the city, it constitutes a violation of human rights and fails to address
the origins of informal settlements (56).
4.5.4
Korle Lagoon Environmental Restoration project
An example of that behaviour can be found in the highly controversial Korle Lagoon Environmental
Restoration Project (KLERP). This project, initiated by the AMA as early as 1961, aims at restoring
the natural wetlands at the mouth of the Odaw river, where it ﬂows in the Korle Lagoon- precisely
where Old Fadama is situated. The AMA estimated that destroying Old Fadama and relocating
the tenants was necessary to the project, thus securing an additional loan of 48 million dollars by
international development actors such as the Belgium government and the Standard Chartered Bank
of London, for a total of 89 millions dollars by 2007. The loan is conditional on the removal and
relocation of the Old Fadama settlement. This was done in spite of 2004 COHRE report stating
the restoration project did not require Old Fadama to be resettled, as it is only partly responsible
for the pollution of the water, with upstream pollution having been neglected by the AMA (35).
However by 2017 the settlement is still standing, and the KLERP has not been implemented. AMA
has interest in keeping it informal, as its presence constitutes a steady source of funding through
the KLERP project. Furthermore, the AMA saves on costs through not providing infrastructure or
services in Old Fadama, by deeming it "illegal" but not trying to improve its situation (49).
4.5.5
Denial of services
The AMA has been accused by COHRE (2004) of denying basic services in Old Fadama, excluding it
from the usual jurisdictions of postal services, police or ﬁre department (34)(36). The same state of
abandonment is described by Richmond (2019), stating that the AMA has chosen to "abandon" Old
Fadama completely to save on costs, using the illegality of the encroachment as its main rhetorical
argument, while negating the rights of the tenants to basic services (49). The lack of electricity
23
==PAGE:32==
access is one of the main points that this research will focus on, as Old Fadama is not oﬃcially
covered by the Ghana national grid, but is de facto an electriﬁed zone through the use of illegal
tappings. Most inhabitants pay an informal entrepreneur that managed to tap a main grid power
line, to receive electric power in their household. Illegal energy access constitutes a hazard, as it
is prone to overloading and starting ﬁres around the access point. Old Fadama is often subject
to ﬁre starts, which sometimes have devastating outcomes. Furthermore, illegal tappings also cost
energy companies part of their production, as they do not perceive anything from the retailer. In
Ghana transmission and distribution losses in the electricity sector have been over 25% for the last
decade, resulting from power theft and ineﬃcient metering (7). Both constitute reasons for several
development actors to address the current energy situation in Old Fadama, from both economic and
social approaches.
4.5.6
Image
The image of Old Fadama in Accra as a whole has suﬀered from years of conﬂict with the AMA
authority, being characterized as a chaotic, dangerous, low-income neighbourhood by a large share
of Ghanaian media (36). This vision was corroborated during the interviews, with the interviewee
declaring slums in Ghana were characterized in public opinion as such : "All in all, slums are
regarded by most people in the city as a den of thieves, drug dealers and social misﬁts. It is seen as
a hide out for armed robbers and a breeding ground for all sorts of diseases". On the other hand
it has become a ﬁxation point for criticism of the AMA policies, with the evictions being widely
unpopular with a fringe of the public opinion (34), (50). This was again the stance of the interviewee,
arguing that evictions are unpopular occurrences that can be instrumentalized by the opposition to
attack the government’s policy. To add to the perception of Old Fadama as disconnected from the
other neighbourhoods of Accra, Richmond (2019) invokes the role of language barrier between the
tenants and the rest of the city dwellers. "English is the oﬃcial language of Ghana, however, Accra’s
residents speak a mixture of English, Twe, Fantee, Dagbani, Ga, Hossah, and/or a subdivision of
a family language. In Old Fadama, Dagbani is widely spoken, given three-quarters of residents are
from the north"(49). Recently, the rising concern regarding ﬂood risks due to climate change has
put Old Fadama under the spotlight, as the AMA has accused Old Fadama of blocking important
drains, and of having a responsibility in the ﬂoods that damaged the city in 2015.
(35).
This
responsibility is however not asserted by any research, and could be another instance of the AMA
trying to undermine the image of Old Fadama among the public (53) (49).
4.5.7
Possible evolution
Interestingly enough, the relationships between Old Fadama and the central government show signs
of normalization, with stating that the Ministry of Education has accredited several schools in Old
Fadama and is willing to oversee exams; the Ministry of Gender stated in 2017 that community
training and education should be encouraged in Old Fadama (53). If the government’s stance on
this settlement was that it should be cleared, such signs of recognition would be incoherent. Finally
in 2015, an update on the 2005 governmental Housing Policy stated the upgrading of existing slums
was a priority, and that settlement like Old Fadama should lead community needs identiﬁcation and
provide project funding, with local government supporting funding and training access (49). This all
points at an increased support from the government to the informal slum organisations to normalize
habitat rather than clear it. It is however at odds with the discontinued eviction policy led by the
AMA.

4.6
Institutional structures in Old Fadama
The withdrawal of the AMA from the aﬀairs of old Fadama has left a vacuum with no formal power
structure to organise the daily life in the settlement (34). In particular, garbage disposal, police
presence, water and electricity supply are not available in the slum. However many of the services
expected to be performed in a formal community are indeed carried out, by a blend of informal
entrepreneurs and community associations. Regarding decision making processes, the Old Fadama
Development Association (OFADA) is prominent. Its role as an informal institution is two folds,
both inside the slum and outside of it, and has been for over twenty years. On the one hand the
AMA ensures communication between the tenants and the settlement of disputes, being composed of
chieftains from every ethnic group in the slum. There the focus of the AMA has been on improving
security and equality of treatment between the residents. It also oversees garbage disposal, road
maintenance, identifying of ﬁre hazard and so on (49). Finally, Old Fadama is home to a basic
educational system, with several primary schools run jointly by OFADA and outside NGOs, with
the recent approval of the Ministry of Education (53). On the other hand, the OFADA has been
the main representative body of Old Fadama when negotiating with the AMA, ensuring the ban on
services in not total, with social services entering the slum to provide post-natal help for instance.
The OFADA has played the role of an intermediary between customary and statutory institutions, as
well as worked together with the Center on Housing Rights and Evictions (COHRE) to help securing
tenure for the residents (34) (36). Overall Old Fadama stands out as an organised if informal society
with its own power institutions, bridging the gap between customary and statutory systems, and
burgeoning with collective initiatives (48).
4.7
Old Fadama and Agbogbloshie
Old Fadama is situated in the administrative district of Agbogbloshie. While the settlement itself
has already been described above, it is adjoining to two areas in particular that play a distinctive
role in this study.
4.7.1
The market - Northern Old Fadama
The Agbogbloshie market is a large food market that was established in 1993 within the northern
reaches of Old Fadama, following an authorisation of the AMA acknowledging the need for economic
development in the area, according to the interview carried out with an OFADA contact. It provides
enormous economic opportunity for the tenants, with many being employed within the informal
sector as porters or vendors. Additional specialised markets such as the onion market or the yam
market are set on the outskirts of the slum. On the North border of the slum, the large avenue Hansen
Road runs parallel to the railroad, deserving the nearby Navrongo station, which also provides major
employment as porters. This portion of Old Fadama is the most developed economically, with most
of the structures built in hard materials (i.e. concrete or bricks) (49). Rudimentary sanitation is in
place and most dwellings have energy access through illegal grid tappings. The interviews singled out
this area as the most favorable to the development of businesses, and possibly of a community-led
energy project.
4.7.2
The Agbogbloshie scrapyard
On the west bank of the Odaw, north of Old Fadama, lies the Agbogbloshie landﬁll. This vast stretch
of land has been devolved to the disposal of thrash for several years. Waste is accumulating without
25
==PAGE:34==
institutional supervision ; pollution contaminates the neighbouring Korle Lagoon, worsened during
the rain season (35).
The Agbogbloshie landﬁll has therefore garnered an infamous reputation
in Accra, as a symbol of land mismanagement (49).
The matter was further worsened in the
2010s by a series of western media investigations depicting the Agbogblosie landﬁll in extreme
terms, sparked by the independent documentary Welcome to Sodom. This one-sided report lacking
reliable fact-checking described Agbogbloshie as the largest electronic dump on Earth, receiving
out-of-use electrical components from the western world to be re purposed in appalling sanitary and
environmental conditions. While Agbogbloshie does receive electrical waste, it origins only from
Ghana and in no comparable proportions; indeed the site lacks the infrastructure and connections
to even accommodate large shippings from abroad. The site spans but a modest area of about 8
hectares, and most materials are transported by way of wheelbarrow; e-waste reprocessing is but
a part of a larger scrapyard recycling industry which employs about 6000 people, most of them
hailing from Old Fadama.
While inaccurate reporting makes for good printing material, it has
further worsened the image of Old Fadama both locally and internationally. This had a nefarious
eﬀect on an already singled-out population, widening the gap between Old Fadama - Agbogbloshie
residents and the rest of Accra (Geographies of forced eviction: Dispossession, violence, resistance,
2017).
These partly baseless allegations have since been debunked by local sources but haven’t
received comparable coverage. What’s more, the devilization of the situation in Agbogbloshie has
been described in Ghanaian opposition media as a mean for the government to legitimise the eviction
of the Old Fadama - Agbogbloshie dwellers. This example of instrumentation of the opinion against
an illegal settlement is not new to the rhetoric of the AMA ((28)(34). Further than the so-called
"apocalyptic landscape" Western media have called it, the Agbogbloshie scrapyard constitutes a
pool of employment and innovation, as demonstrated by the Agbogbloshie Makerspace Project
(AMP), a collaboration between local scrap dealers and entrepreneurs. This collective based around
shared knowledge through digital means, aims at improving the life of local smelters and scrap
dealers, while devising simple solutions using recycled materials. These initiatives denote a spirit of
entrepreneurship in Old Fadama / Agbogbloshie, as well as a strong sense of cooperation.
4.8
Community initiatives in Old Fadama
The speciﬁc situation of Old Fadama as an island partly disconnected from the urban network,
has sparked a strong feeling of community among its tenants. This cohesion is further developed
by the cultural barrier between northern Ghanaians and the citizens of Accra. Several coordinated
community initiatives have been documented, that constitute encouraging precedents for community
projects. Old Fadama among other examples features a functioning trash collecting system organised
by the inhabitants under the supervision of the OFADA ((34) (54), possibly indicating Old Fadama
could be a fertile ground for a communal energy project.
4.8.1
Flood protection in Old Fadama - Community Resilience
Old Fadama is highly susceptible to ﬂood damage due to its situation on the banks of the Odaw River.
Devastating ﬂoods have hit Accra over the summer of 2015, killing more than 150 and rendering
over 20000 homeless all over the city. Lower Old Fadama was severely hit due to its location near
water level (35). The OFADA was noticed since then for its reply to the destruction through joint
community eﬀorts, acknowledged by (49). Flood risk prevention are part of the tasks the OFADA
has taken on itself, with community volunteers marking at-risk sections, while maintaining drains
and digging additional channels through community labour (53). This type of informal disaster

4.8.2
Fire Reconstruction
Old Fadama came under the spotlight for its joint eﬀorts in another domain, that of ﬁre safety
and ﬁre ﬁghting. Fires are a signiﬁcant danger in slum areas, as the crowded occupation space and
ramshackle construction favor the spreading of the ﬂames. Construction do not follow safety norms,
buildings are close together while the response services, if any, lack room to maneuver and access
to running water. Furthermore, the common use of kerosene as lighting and cooking fuel provides
additional sources of ﬂammable material. Old Fadama is no exception, and was hit by signiﬁcant
ﬁre disasters since its establishment. On the 21st of May 2012, an unusually devastating ﬁre hit the
community, destroying the dwellings of over 3500 people. On a side-note, the source of the ﬁre was
later determined to be an unsafe electrical installation, casting light on the need for safer electricity
access in Old Fadama (58).
No institutional support was oﬀered to the tenants of Old Fadama, from the AMA, the Ghanaian
government or an international actor. The whole rebuilding process, taking over the summer of
2012, provides a rich example of collective action, while underlining a critical lack of support for

informal settlements populations in Accra. As most people aﬀected had lost homes and possession,
many were housed by relatives within the slum for the duration of reconstruction. This displays the
importance of social capital in such a scenario, as already crowded houses accommodated disaster
victims from the community. At the same time, some temporary plastic and wood shelters were
erected through the supervision of the OFADA, acting de facto as a governmental organism would
on such a situation in a formal neighbourhood (58) (36).
The rebuilding process was mostly funded by saving groups, a common dynamic in sub-Saharan
Africa, especially in tribal environments under statutory law. Money is pooled together and loaned
to a person of the group when the need arises, allowing a member to sustain a high expense at a given

reportedly followed by the community, with the new buildings being mostly built out of concrete
blocks. This shows a will to improve living conditions in Old Fadama from the OFADA, as well as
a desire from the victims to invest a substantial amount in their living environment. This could be
seen as an encouraging precedent for an autonomous energy project to be implemented.
4.9
Conclusion of the case
Most literature concerning Old Fadama has established that a strong community life is thriving
there.
Several factors are at play : the ethnic background of the majorly Dagomba occupants,
contributes to conserving a strong identity within the Accra landscape. The conservation of the
tribal structure, with chiefs retaining authority, is characteristic of informal settlements in Ghana,
and further strengthens community initiatives (52). This village-like structure extends to the housing
plan common in sub-Saharan African slums, where most tenants live in shared compound houses,
reproducing the rural structure of habitation (51). A strained relationship with the AMA due to
land tenure issues have progressively driven formal authority out of the slum, replacing it with a
local, informal, statutory council of chieftains acting as a governmental body, further fostering a
sentiment of unity. These factors facilitate dialogue with a uniﬁed community, allow to assess the
neighbourhood’s needs and to quantify key parameters, such as population or occupation. This was
shown by the series of community-led enumerations and censuses, that were extremely helpful in
this study : Housing the Masses To People’ s Dialogue on Human Settlements (29). This ability
of the community to organise itself and manage enumerations and surveys has even increased the
resilience of Old Fadama itself to pressure from the local governments, as it displays a high level of
coordination and joint eﬀort (59). This tacit governance should be relied on to design any energy
access project, obtaining feedback and insight from the tenants.
This level of cooperation through informal governance extends beyond surveys and community cen-
suses. As was explained above, Old Fadama has a history of community-funded projects, pooling
together money or labour to beneﬁt the entire neighbourhood. This has encompassed community
reconstruction as well as the sheltering of local refugees during the 2012 ﬁre (58), as well as recon-
struction following the 2015 ﬂood (35). The overarching initiatives such as the disposal of waste, or
the overseeing of ﬁre-safety, are other examples of community initiatives on various levels, making
Old Fadama an example of self-managed settlement. In this environment, a project involving the
28
==PAGE:37==
development of an energy system beneﬁting the community could beneﬁt from a lot of help from
the community itself, to evaluate the needs and then to implement and maintain the system. This
necessity of involving the community in every step of the project seems to be key, which is illus-
trated by most integrated energy projects this research is inspired from (16)(39). Three zones of
interest distinctly stand out of the Old Fadama / Agbogbloshie landscape. Each could beneﬁt from
a dedicated energy system, designed in cooperation with its users. The feasibility of such a system
will be explored for each zone in the next chapter.
4.9.1
Older Fadama - The Agbogbloshie Yam Market
In the oldest part of the neighbourhood, infrastructure is overall more solid, with concrete structures
and basic energy access mainly due to illegal connections. Informal economic activity is based around
the Agbogbloshie Market, one of Accra’s largest food market. The neighbourhood would beneﬁt
from small business-enabling structures such as refrigeration, while lighting would be a necessity.
Better habitat and security hints at higher wages for the denizens of this part of the settlement, an
advantage for the success of community-funded projects.
4.9.2
Newer Fadama - The Odaw river banks
This part of the settlement is more recent, and more at risk for it is at the heart of land tenure
issues. Constructions in this part have been described as less durable, which is coherent with the
frequent ﬂood damage the area is plagued with (49). An energy system in that area would be geared
towards lighting and basic home applications such as phone charging, rather than business enabling
appliances. As the income is probably lower in this zone, the ﬁnancing of a system by the community
might be more diﬃcult.
4.9.3
Agbogbloshie scrapyard
That interesting sub-area is located on the west bank of the Odaw, opposing Agbogbloshie. This
large landﬁll houses dynamic recycling and scrap-dealing businesses, which provide substantial em-
ployment to the tenants of Old Fadama. This might constitute a suitable site of implantation for
a decentralized energy system, as it presents a need for power for industrial needs (smelting, re-
processing, cable-stripping). The area is also at the heart of pressing environmental issues, as the
activity from the landﬁll pollutes the neighbouring Korle Lagoon, resulting in conﬂicts of interest
with the local ﬁshermen (35).

5. System Analysis
In this part a theoretical model for an urban energy center is proposed. The structure is meant
to be owned and operated by the local actors to provide energy access within the neighbourhood,
through the use of a micro-grid. The concept for this energy center is partly inspired by an idea
by the SELCO Foundation, a development fund operating in India to alleviate urban poverty, with
a focus on energy access (31). The energy center is developed through shared funding from the
community and development actors ; several models of ownership can be explored, from entrepreneur-
based to community owned.
The loan taken to meet the initial capital expenses is paid back
over time by the actors beneﬁting from the center. The purpose of this part is to gauge several
scenarios for the implementation of a renewable energy production center within Old Fadama and
its surroundings, to supplement the lackluster access to energy in the neighbourhood. The study will
focus on determining the optimal energy mix of the system, through the simulation tool HOMER.
A second part will determine the ﬁnancial viability of the system, and whether it would be feasible
or not. Finally, several structures for operating and owning the structure will be explored.
5.1
Operating principle
The IEC is meant to be a building within the area, featuring a solar array as a mean of producing
energy. It is connected to a local grid, to distribute the energy on a local scale, in exchange for
a ﬁxed tariﬀ. Center relies on a diesel generator to supplement the solar arrays : diesel fuel can
be bought by the community instead of individual purchases : such communal ﬁnancing has been
observed in Old Fadama, in reconstruction projects for instance with the OFADA handling the
pooling of money and the joint purchase of building materials (58). Regarding the organisation of
the structure, several schemes can be envisioned, based upon the Integrated Energy Center (IEC)
projects run by the SELCO foundation in India. In this approach, a development actor devises an
energy structure tailored for the needs of the community, and relies on trained tenants of the slum
to operate it (31).
5.1.1
Community ownership
Under this paradigm, the structure is owned by the community itself through joint savings; the
eventual loan is taken by the community through a tenants association, and paid back by all the
members.
The project then beneﬁts all members, and is operated by actors designated by the
community. This type of ownership is suited to homogeneous communities with strong ties and
dynamic development initiatives (57). The pre-existence of a development association of some kind
is helpful, as it has reconnaissance from the tenants and can eﬃciently promote the project. Relying
on local actors and community spirit is a key approach to slum upgrading, according to the World
Bank (33).
5.1.2
Entrepreneur-based ownership
In this model, the structure is owned and developed by a single entrepreneur, ideally from the
community. He takes a loan to develop the structure, then rents out its services to local customers.
This promotes the development of the neighbourhood through local businesses, another prong of
slum upgrading policies. In this scenario the entrepreneur might need help from a development
31
==PAGE:40==
actor to ﬁrst start the business, as the capital expenses are probably beyond his means. Such model
is suited for cosmopolitan areas where communities are not able or willing to purchase an energy
system (31).

5.2.2
Ruling out urban wind power
The assessment of wind resources is carried out through Global Wind Atlas, a partnership between
the Department of Wind Energy at the Technical University of Denmark (DTU Wind Energy) and
the World Bank Group (consisting of The World Bank and the International Finance Corporation,
or IFC). The aim of this tool is to assess the wind power potential of any given location using GIS
data, to perform preliminary calculations to a project. The GWA simulation for the location of
Old Fadama, Accra, yields mitigated results. The estimation was run for a height of 10 meters
above ground, as the project is limited to small-scale structures. The power density is 53 W/m²
on average in the considered area, due to an average wind speed of 3,5m/s. Winds also display a
seasonal variation from dry to wet season, with higher winds from May to October. This is an overall
low average, hinting at a low energy eﬃciency for a potential turbine. Wind power is a substantial
constituent of renewable energy production, making up around 4,8% of the global energy production
as of 2018 (World Bank). However its development has mostly been focused on oﬀshore and rural
wind power generation, as urban wind has encountered various obstacles. Evans (2011), points at the
more complex nature of urban landscape, the higher number of stakeholders with diﬀerent agendas,
as well as the impact of wind power on the quality of life (60). In the case of small community sized
structures, wind power also presents the downside of higher capital expenses compared to solar, costs
that become less impactful on large utility-sized projects. Solar costs have been decreasing faster
that those of wind power utilities, with a dip of more than 80% over the last decade according to
IRENA’s report on renewable costs (4). The use of wind-reliant power structures in slum scenarios
is not completely undocumented however. There is for instance a record of a wind-powered lighting
system in the slum of Kibera, in Kenya, dating back to 2007 (61). However it faces dire constraints
due to packed land occupation, and the lack of planned infrastructure, that roof mounted solar
panels can circumvent more easily.

5.3
Quantifying the current access to electricity
One of the issues that was met during this study was to quantify the current access to electricity in
the slum. Indeed, even though the area is under served in terms of electricity access, most tenants
obtain electricity under several forms. The ﬁrst mean is through illegal tapping of the Accra grid ;
this was acknowledged through interviews with contacts in the Old Fadama Development Association
(OFADA). For a sum of about 50 cedis a month (about 9 $), a tenant can obtain electric power
from an individual who makes a business out of tapping the grid ; these power-providers are a type
of informal entrepreneur part of the economic structure of Old Fadama. This method is usually
suﬃcient for a household to obtain light and for charging the applications they own (i.e. phones).
The illegal connection has been described as unreliable and subject to shortages, during the interview
with the OFADA contact through the NGO TU Empower Africa. The reason for the unreliability of
the connection is not known ; however it can be assumed that since the connection is not monitored
by a power distributor, too many connections can be established in regard to the capacity of the
cable. This in turn leads to the cutting of the power in the aﬀected zone. Furthermore, Old Fadama
is regularly subject to ﬁres originating from unsafe power connections, which in turn disrupts the
connection in the neighbourhood. The illegality of the connection also poses another problem, as
it is not measured by a meter, instead consisting of a ﬁxed tariﬀof 50 GHC/month. This can be
compared to the approximate price for electricity in Ghana of 0,31 Cedi/kWh for a consumption of
0 to 50 kWh price/year, while the mean tariﬀfor an average Ghanaian consumer (350 kWh/y) is
0,59 GHC/kWh (i.e. 0,01 $) (62). Therefore an average Ghanaian pays about 18 cedis per month,
assuming an identical consumption for each month.

Selection of the site of implantation
The study of the Old Fadama situation brings out three distinct sites that could beneﬁt from the
implantation of an oﬀ-grid energy structure ; each site presents deﬁning characteristics, as well as
speciﬁc energy needs. For the purpose of solar data, the sites were considered identical, as they
are all situated within one kilometer of each other in a ﬂat, featureless landscape. It was therefore
assumed that the solar exposition they received was identical.
5.6
Old Fadama
The ﬁrst of the three possible sites for implantation of an energy system is the older part of the Old
Fadama informal settlement, in the district of Agbogbloshie (ﬁg. 5.4).
Old Fadama residents have had access to energy in over 90% of the houses according to the enumer-
ation Housing the Masses (2010). The interview carried out during this research shows that most of
the connections are achieved through illegal connections to the grid. Therefore Old Fadama is not an
energy-deprived zone per say. It is partly connected to the grid through illegal means. However the
capacity of the illegal connections is limited, prone to blackouts, and they bring a high ﬁre hazard
to a structurally fragile settlement. Notable ﬁres have ravaged Old Fadama in 2012, 2015 and 2020,
ﬁres that were imputed to a saturated electrical connection (58). As of 2020, no connection to the
grid is envisioned by the authorities, as seen in part 7, as it would legitimize the occupation of the
site by the tenants. Therefore an autonomous energy system in Old Fadama would bring a steadier,
safer power supply to the most economically dynamic neighbourhood in the slum.

5.6.1
Technology needs assessment
5.6.1.1
Stakeholders identiﬁcation
In the oldest part of Old Fadama, stakeholders were identiﬁed as the wealthiest fraction of the
population in the slum, inhabiting sturdier structures while basic sanitation is available. Salaries
were estimated to be above 110 GHC/month as of 2010 for the wealthiest half of the population,
based on the 2010 census (29). Adjusting the revenue on the inﬂation of the ghanaian currency, this
would translate to incomes above 314 GHC in 2020. Small businesses, mostly informal, are present
in the area around the market of Agbogbloshie ; this district is one of the most Accra’s busy informal
business district, and provides above 80% of the employment for the slum dwellers.
5.6.1.2
Prioritize needs
The neighbourhood is already well established in regard to the rest of the area, and would beneﬁt
from in-house lighting, charging station for applications and ﬁnally business-enabling structures, like
refrigeration. Those could provide a boost to the economic activity of the neighbourhood, including
ﬁsh mongering from the neighbouring Odaw river. The ability to store fresh products has been
underlined (Feasibility study of a Solar Photo voltaic Adaptable Refrigeration Kit for remote areas
in developing countries) as a pressing issue in developing country, especially West Africa were ﬁshing
is a key business ﬁeld for small informal entrepreneurs (63). Food spoilage due to lack of refrigeration
grieves the business of these entrepreneurs and in turn impacts economic development.
This is
corroborated by the initiatives taken in India by the SELCO foundation (31). The installation of
solar-powered refrigerator in the community of Kariammana Agrahara was considered a step towards
economic empowerment for the community, as it enabled small businesses to thrive in the following
years. On the side of the refrigeration devices, the other aim of the project would be to provide light
inside the households, without relying on either illegal grid tappings or fossil fuel. Part of the power
would be devoted to external lighting, as there are close to no public lighting devices in Old Fadama,
which can contribute to a feeling of insecurity. Finally there is a need for charging portable devices
such as phones, as most internet connections in Old Fadama is realised through mobile devices.
5.6.1.3
Quantify needs
The purpose of this section is to quantify the energy needs of the target population, to come up
later with the overall load for the system. A key characteristic of the community are its dense
demographics, up to 240000 people per square kilometer : the energy needs of the whole population
can not be feasibly met on the ﬁrst iteration of the project. This same approach has been used by
SELCO foundation in its Energy for Slums project, with the ﬁrst pilot project being more akin to
a test run, for future extensions. Therefore here the system must be dimensioned without meeting
the needs of every tenant; the installed capacity must be determined based on other factors. The
ﬁrst step is to determine the basic size of a household.
According to the report "Housing the
Masses", 95% of the households in Old Fadama occupy a single room, while 62% of the households
do not share the room they are in with another household (29). The average room occupancy is
not given in the report but it is documented to be higher than 6 people in 80% of the cases. The
average household size is low, at 2,8 people, but is not to be relied on since it factors in numerous
single-person households living in common dormitories, therefore room occupancy is far higher than
average household size. For this study an basis of 10 people per sleeping room will be adopted, as
far larger ﬁgures are documented due to large families sharing the same roof. The report pointed at
traditional tribal structures, as well the role of extended Muslim families, as a main factor for large
37
==PAGE:46==
communal living structures.
The study will count one lamp per room as the template, considering one room per household
as the average in Old Fadama. Furthermore, the need for charging devices must me addressed.
Considering 96% of the population is employed with less than 2% children, counting one mobile
device per person is reasonable. The ﬁgure is probably less but the system needs to stay relevant
in the following years. If one phone needs charging every other day, for two hours, this amounts
to 20 hours of continuous charging every 48 hours. While one charging socket would technically be
enough, it would prove troublesome as it assumes an unrealistically even distribution. Two charging
sockets render the charging of mobile devices more practical. Finally, the number of fridges has to
be determined. Fridges constitute by far the largest consumption, as they need to stay powered

Optimized system

all energy is provided by the generator. All conﬁgurations of the system without the batteries were
ruled out by HOMER, as they were then less eﬃcient than the base case. Indeed the load is shaped
in a way most of the power is needed after dark, with only 30% of the power being consumed during
the day. If the system does not rely on batteries, the diesel generator will have to play a major part
in the mix. This is to be avoided as the aim is not only to achieve reliable energy access but to
reduce the dependency on expensive, polluting fossil fuels.

nighttime due to lighting consumption. A characteristic settlement load is more important in the
evening due to cooking needs; this is not the case here as cooking was not part of the needs covered
by the system (ﬁg. 5.6).

5.6.3
Financial analysis
In the ﬁnal case the capital expenditures would amount to 21,862$ with operating expenses of 7,325$
/ year, against a 10,965$/year for a full diesel model. The deﬁnitive system yields a ﬁnal LCOE of
0,467$ per kWh, and is the most aﬀordable scenario. In comparison, the cost of energy in Ghana
is about 0,1$/kWh for the average consumer (Energy price sheets, 2019). The system presents a
return on investment (ROI) of 15,2%, and an internal rate of return of 19,1% (ﬁg. 5.8).
5.6.4
Sensitivity analysis for best system
HOMER has carried out a sensitivity analysis on the best case system, i.e. the PV/battery/diesel
system detailed previously. The two sensitivity parameters are the price of fuel, set at 1$/liter in the
base scenario, and the discount rate set at 6%. Both parameters are explored from -50% to +50%
(ﬁg 5.9)
As expected, a shift upwards from the price of diesel fuel will shift the viable composition of the
system towards a higher renewable penetration. The current price for diesel fuel in Ghana is ﬂuc-
tuating around 4,35 GHC or 0,75$. However this price was constant around 5,55 GHC or 0,95$
up until the end of April. The COVID-19 pandemic and the ensuing lockdown orders might have
played a role in slowing down the economy and leading up to a surplus production of oil in Ghana,
thus driving the prices down. The simulations were run with therefore a base price of 1$ per liter of
diesel fuel (ﬁg 5.10).

5.7
New Fadama
The newest portion of Old Fadama - called New Fadama in this study for clarity reasons - comprises
the settlements in the lowlands on the bank of the Odaw River, below Old Fadama proper. It is
the most recent neighbourhood of the slum, and is characterized by a lower access to electricity,
more fragile structures and less urban planning.
The energy needs here would be more geared
towards illumination, both inside the houses and public, as well as application charging. A system
comparable to the one envisioned in Old Fadama could electrify a larger population group if no
refrigeration was considered, due to the precarious nature of the habitat and the lack of businesses
(ﬁg 5.12).
The area has been struck by more forced evictions over the last years than the rest of the slum,
and is at the heart of the Korle Lagoon restoration project. Settlement there has been described as
unsafe, due to the frequent ﬂoods that have resulted in lost of life and property. However the bank
has always been resettled by tenants once ﬂoods were over; this is coherent with Old Fadama being
one of the primary destinations of the most impoverished newcomers in Accra (49). This questions
the implementation of an eventual autonomous energy system for several reasons :
• Terrain : the ﬂood-prone nature of the terrain would jeopardize the sustainability of a system
; there is a substantial risk a structure would be damaged by one of the yearly ﬂoods occurring
during the wet season. This would in turn put additional costs on the project.
• Tenure : the issue of tenure security is the most acute in the lower part of Old Fadama. This
stems from two main conﬂicts of interest. Firstly the situation is linked to the ﬂood-prone
terrain, as the area is unsafe and the AMA conducts evictions partly to prevent people from
settling in an unsafe area. Secondly, the lower part of Old Fadama is at the core of a conﬂict
with the other users of the Korle Lagoon, namely ﬁshermen populations. The heavy pollution
of the Lagoon is partly attributed to the riverbank unregulated habitat. Waste from the lower
part of the slum stays untreated according to the AMA and contaminates the Lagoon’s ecology.
Therefore the riverbank is the location where most of the evictions were carried out by the
AMA, which questions the sustainability of any project carried out in these precincts.
• Finances : the population from lower Fadama hails from the most fragile group, economically
speaking, with wages under 314 GHC/month (54$/month) in 2020.
It might prove more
diﬃcult to partially or totally fund an energy development project in lower Fadama than in
Old Fadama proper.
• Length of stay : the lower part of Old Fadama is the one where the stays are the shortest,
according to the interview carried out with the TU Empower Africa member. The precarious
nature of the habitat pushes the tenants to move out if possible, to a better part of the
settlement or elsewhere.
This conjugation of factors makes the lowest part of Old Fadama an unsuitable ground for the im-
plementation of an energy system. The risk of natural disaster, the litigious nature of the occupation

5.8
Scrapyard
5.8.1
Stakeholders identiﬁcation
This terrain is out of the body of the slum, on the western side of the Odaw, and provides em-
ployment for as many as 6000 Old Fadama dwellers. The stakeholders would be the welders and
scrap-metal dealers, who have partaken in collective project ﬁnancing in the past. The association
Agbogbloshie Makerspace Project (AMP) aims at sharing knowledge and organising projects to
improve the situation of the workers. The area of the Agbogbloshie scrapyard is of interest as it
provides substantial economic activity to Old Fadama. The area so far is not electriﬁed, and the
dumping ground is not regulated by the AMA. Therefore, the local scrapyard entrepreneurs and
smelters cannot rely on machine power. Most of the recycling work, especially recuperation of metal
wires, is still done by hand, through burning, usually using tires as fuel. This process is quite slow
and work-intensive, taking often more than half an hour to strip the cables then collect the metal
from the sludge. Beyond ineﬃciency, it has drawn large criticism to Agbogbloshie, as it emits large
quantities of toxic fumes, which cause health problems among the workers, such as skin conditions,
respiratory ailments and cancers. Furthermore, the pollutants contaminate the nearby Korle lagoon
through the Odaw river, impacting the ecosystem of the lagoon, the ﬁshermen depending on it and
the populations using it as a water source.
Since 2014 however, the Agbogbloshie scrapyard is home to a recycling center, the Agbogbloshie Re-
cycling Center (ARC). This initiative by the Pure Earth foundation has received the approbation of
the AMA and has become a small employer scrap-metal workers. The factory has a grid connection,
and focuses on the recycling of discarded large cables employed by electric companies, shipped in
from Ghana and neighbouring countries such as Burkina Faso. The granulator (i.e. cable-stripping)
machines do not process small copper wires from car or home applications, which make up the bulk
of the recycling activity in Agbogbloshie scrapyard. So far the activity of the recycling center has
been described as parallel to that of the scrapyard, and all the recycling out of the center is done by
hand. Machines able to process small wires do exist, smaller caliber granulators that the recycling
center does not possess.
The concept explored in this study would be that of an oﬀ-grid, power generating structure which
would enable scrapyard workers to rely on the same kind of machine as the Agbogbloshie Recycling
Center. Running on solar panels, L/A batteries and a diesel generator, the center would proﬁt the
workers by making work faster and more eﬃcient. All the while, it would reduce burning practices
through Agbogbloshie, making the informal business plan of the site more sustainable, and more
eﬃcient.
5.8.2
Prioritize needs
The scrapyard workshop would function as a small factory, presenting the characteristics of an
industrial load : constant over the working hours and null out of these hours. The main need would
be steady electricity to power the cable-stripping machines. A secondary need is linked to the lack

of electricity : Agbogbloshie scrapyard does not see activity after dark. An additional beneﬁt of the
system would be to provide lighting, to allow for work more hours per day.
5.8.3
Document needs in ﬁgures
The scrapyard poses an interesting alternative, needing mostly electric power to operate small ma-
chinery, such as cable-stripping machines. The load will be modelled according to an industrial
load model, with a constant consumption due to machinery. The applications can be split into two
groups for the scrapyard :
• Granulator machines (cable-stripping) : these small industrial applications shred the cables to
obtain the metal within, without having to resort to burning the cable, which causes most of
Agbogbloshie’s environmental problems due to toxic emanations. The model considered for
this simulation, an Easton Tech EW-01 model, requires a power supply of 1200 W. The ﬁrst
simulation is run for two such machines, operating from 4am to 10 pm.
• Street lights : most of the work is done outdoors in Agbogbloshie, and sunset means the work
has to stop unless there is an illumination mean. This model takes into account two 100 W
lamps allowing two working stations to remain functional during dark hours. The lamps have
to ﬁll the gaps between the opening hours of the workshop (i.e. the timetables of the granulator
machines) and the natural light hours.

5.10
Possible grid injection
An option to explore the project further was the idea of injecting the electricity produced in the
grid, in the eventuality the neighbourhood was to be connected later on. This option of local power
production through Independent Power Producers (IPPs) is not new, and has been explored through
pilot projects in Ghana (18). The main issue in this case would be the legal situation of Old Fadama
in regard to land tenure. However, in a scenario where slum upgrading policies in Agbogbloshie
are successful, the upper part of Old Fadama could possibly become a formal neighborhood, acting
as an urban energy generation site. The Ghanaian government has opened the energy market to
independent power producers (IPPs), with 26 micro-grids being implemented in oﬀ-grid locations,
licensed to sell electricity through the micro-grid (9) (64).The 2019 Renewable Energy Masterplan
has an overall positive stance on local energy production and decentralization, aiming for a greener,
more diverse energy mix. Article 2.16 Mini-Grid Electriﬁcation Policy (2016) states the objective of

promoting the use of micro-grid electriﬁcation. The text is aimed at rural oﬀ-grid location, as the
issue of informal settlements is not addressed in the entire program. Therefore, in the eventuality of
Old Fadama being recognised as a legitimate neighbourhood, excess energy from the solar structures
pre-dating the connection could be sold back to the Accra grid. The connection would have to occur

As a

mentioned before, the LCOE of both systems are about 4 times higher than the cost of energy in

growing to utility-size goes beyond the means of the community. As the situation was considered in
this research, plugging the neighbourhood to the main grid would only result in the abandonment
of the system.
5.11
Conclusion of the study
The purpose of this part was to to study the possibility of implementing a decentralized renewable
energy system in the area of Old Fadama. This research responded to the fact that the settlement
is isolated from the main grid, and only achieves precarious energy access through illegal electricity
connections. Old Fadama is at the core of intense land tenure conﬂicts with the ruling authority
of Accra, the AMA. As the slum dwellers are not authorized to live there, they cannot obtain
legal electricity access from the Electricity Company of Ghana, which does not operate within Old
Fadama. Therefore a decentralized energy system seemed a viable option to explore. Retrospectively,
it appears both systems, in Old Fadama and in the Agbogbloshie scrapyard, would be economically
viable in the long run.

6. Discussion
This study provides a theoretical example of the implementation of a decentralized energy system
in an informal urban environment, the slum of Old Fadama in Accra. The reason for this research is
that the settlement legally cannot be connected to the main grid, as it does not have tenure security.
The research shows that an optimized diesel/solar hybrid system using batteries for storage would
provide cheaper, more reliable power than the current illegal connections currently used in the slum.
However numerous elements of this research must be critically discussed, to assess what they really
bring to light regarding electriﬁcation in informal settlements. Electriﬁcation of urban areas through
decentralized means remains fairly unexplored, as electriﬁcation has traditionally been conceived as
being plugged to the main grid. The accuracy of the data sets and the lack of ﬁrst-hand interviews
must also be discussed.
6.1
Modeling assumptions and data
The data used for the preliminary studies should be put into question. Before running the HOMER
simulations, the solar resources assessment was carried out using the online Global Solar Atlas,
whose data provided by the World Bank. This data should be confronted to ﬁeld data gathered over
a long-term period for more accurate measurement. Such ﬁeld data was unavailable for this project.
Along the same lines, the optimization software HOMER uses data from the NASA Meteorological
Database to carry out its simulation. Ideally the results should be based on recent ﬁeld measurements
carried out over a period of a year. Therefore the ﬁndings regarding the performance of the system
should not be considered entirely accurate.
The HOMER simulations were run excluding the possibilities for a wind-turbine. This was decided
as urban wind power is still a relatively undeveloped technology, it presents high capital expenses
compared to solar, no precedents were found for its use in informal settlements electriﬁcation and
the Accra region does not present an interesting wind power potential according to the Global Wind
Atlas (GWA). These choices will result in less renewable options for the ﬁnal energy mix, limiting
the range of the study. This could yield detrimental results when compared to rural electriﬁcation
schemes, where wind power is featured prominently (16)). This choice of not including wind power
could explain the need for a hybrid system relying partly on diesel fuel (see Results).
Regarding the optimization of the system, it was based upon an assumed, theoretical load. The
two distinct scenarios for energy systems were intended to represent to types of energy center that
could be implemented : one aimed at meeting a residential load, the other one aimed at satisfying
the needs of small industry. The choice of appliances to model the load in Old Fadama was based
on literature regarding previous energy center projects, to approximate the energy needs of a slum
population (31). A study of decentralized systems in rural Senegal relying on a ﬁeld study was
also used to model the shape of the loads, adapted to an urban environment. Therefore this study
should be supplemented by a ﬁeld survey to assess precisely the consumption of a household in Old
Fadama, that could then be extrapolated to other informal settlements in Ghana. In the case of the
Agbogbloshie scrapyard case, the consumption of cable-stripping machines had to be determined.
Small wires from home or car systems are currently recycled through burning, while large power
line cables are processed in the Agbogbloshie Recycling Center. No speciﬁc diameter was indicated
regarding the size of the cables. Therefore the load was modelled using the technical data of Easton
EW-01 granulator machines ﬁt for wires ranging from 3mm to 32mm in diameter. The exact size
51
==PAGE:60==
of the wires that would have to be processed should be assessed by a ﬁeld survey, to determine the
precise machine that would be used by a local entrepreneur. The load would then be adjusted to the
consumption of those machines. As a consequence, the load for the Agbogbloshie recycling structure
should be regarded as indicative.
Finally for the ﬁnancial study of the system, assumptions had to be made regarding the income of
the dwellers of Old Fadama. Most of the tenants work in the informal sector, do not declare their
income of pay taxes, therefore accessing recent data regarding the average income in the settlement
was a challenge. The assumptions were based on the 2010 Housing the Masses census, which was
carried out with the help of the community (29). A precise review of the diﬀerent levels of income
is available in this report; income was therefore extrapolated assuming that it was correlated with
the inﬂation of the Ghanaian cedi, and that the proportion of each income level did not vary. While
coherent, this assumption remains theoretical and the actual income levels in the population of Old
Fadama probably diﬀers from those used in this study.
6.2
Interviews and possible ﬁeldwork
The collection of data through interviews was one of the initial objectives of this research, to sup-
plement online and literary ﬁndings. However contact could only be established with a member of
local Non Governmental Organisation TU EmPower Africa based in Accra, that had a contact in
the Organisation for the Development of Old Fadama (OFADA). This way complementary infor-
mation regarding the situation in Old Fadama was acquired, including the price paid for an illegal
electrical connection. Direct contact attempts with the OFADA went unanswered. The tentative
to contact the Agbogbloshie Makerspace Project (AMP), the association of grassroot makers in the
Agbogbloshie Scrapyard, was unsuccessful as well. They could have provided valuable insight into
their own energy needs. It is likely that the COVID-19 pandemic played a role in the failure of
those contact attempts. Accra went on partial lock down at the end of March and the activity was
severely impeded. The NGO contact from TU EmPower acknowledged the crisis as a reason for
sparse answer from his own contacts in Old Fadama. It can only be assumed it prevented several
potential interviewees from giving attention to this research. It stands out from this study that it
would strongly beneﬁt from ﬁeldwork, including detailed surveys and interviews. Interviewing the
tenants directly instead of getting information through an OFADA member, might provide interest-
ing insight. Indeed the OFADA is a local government in its own right, and might have some interest
in providing biased answers. This point was highlighted by Richmond (2019) (49). The OFADA, de-
spite its recognition as a useful development group in Old Fadama, remains a monolithic, all-male,
northern, literate group that draws advantages from its leading position. Interviewing a broader
group of Old Fadama tenants would probably provide insights that correspond more to the needs of
the local population.
6.3
Results
The optimized system for both cases leads to a hybrid diesel/solar system relying on batteries for
storage. The objective was to meet the lowest Net Present Cost for the system over a period of 25
years. As a consequence, the renewable penetration in the system is only about 40%, with the rest
of the power being supplied through fossil fuel. More than half of the power is supplied through
unsustainable sources.
It has however been argued that aﬀordable electriﬁcation in electricity-
deprived areas should be prioritized over the imperative to develop renewable energy (1). Another
concern regarding the ﬁnal composition for both systems is the use of lead/acid batteries as a form
52
==PAGE:61==
of storage. Those batteries are an aﬀordable and eﬃcient form of electric storage, however they
pose environmental and health issues due to the exposure to lead. Discarding the batteries in the
immediate environment could have long-lasting consequences, with the Korle Lagoon being already
at the heart of environmental disputes.
Considering the ﬁnancial aspect of the system, the LCOE (Levelized Cost of Energy) is comparatively
very high compared to the grid price for a kWh. The simulation for an Old Fadama neighbourhood
yields an LCOE of 0,467$/kWh, while the scrapyard system’s LCOE was estimated at 0,426$/kWh.
In comparison, the main grid prices were set at 0,1$/kWh by the Public Utilities Regulatory Com-
mission (PURC) in 2019 for comparable private consumption (62). This striking diﬀerence should
however be put into perspective by several factors. The power sector in Ghana suﬀers from overpro-
duction and has featured notoriously low energy prices over the last decade (7). Connection to the
grid would be the preferred option if it was possible; however tenure conﬂicts make this outcome un-
likely. Additionally, it appears through the study that illegal connections result in a higher cost over
25 years for the slum’s population. This constitutes a strong argument for exploring decentralized
urban energy project. Furthermore, the implementation of an energy system would sustain income
generating activities like informal food businesses in Old Fadama or small-scale recycling industry
in the Agbogbloshie scrapyard. Those revenues would help the community bear the ﬁnancial burden
of the system, as well as develop a more extensive system should it be successful.
Regarding the practical implementation of a system, several logistic issues arise. This research was
based on several instances of community-funded slum upgrading projects, such as ﬁre-reconstruction.
However those initiatives did not require exterior expertise, and were carried out by members of the
community. In the case of a decentralized energy system, the implementation would require the
involvement of exterior contractors to implement the structure. This raises concerns as Old Fadama
is not an area where formally registered companies usually operate, and suﬀers from an infamous
reputation in Accra. As recommended in the Policy Recommendation part, this step would require
involvement from government agencies such as the Energy Commission to advise and guide an energy
project in Old Fadama.
6.4
Limitations of the concept
Beyond the limitations of the model, the concept of the research itself raises several questions. The
original assumption behind this research is that a decentralized, oﬀ-the-main-grid energy system is
preferable to no energy access, or to illegal access. However this assumption is based on an analogy
with rural electriﬁcation, where developing an oﬀ-grid system does not further the isolation of the
community. In the case of an informal settlement, having its own grid could theoretically worsen the
isolation dynamic that is so detrimental to informal settlements. In a hypothetical future where Old
Fadama, due to adequate support from development actors, designed and funded its own power grid,
the denial of services from the AMA might worsen, as there is less need for the local municipality
to be involved in Old Fadama’s matters. This project is aimed at making a marginalized area more
self-reliant, but this could normalize the lack of involvement from higher, constitutional authorities
such as the AMA or the Ghanaian Federal Government.
This project also cast light on the dissonant stances of the central government and the Accra
Metropolitan Authority regarding Old Fadama. The government has taken steps to reduce socio-
spatial exclusion of the slum dwellers, through the recognition of some of its structure. At the same
time the local authority which is the AMA does not recognise the legitimacy of the OFADA as an
association, and has carried out forceful evictions repeatedly, without providing relocation solutions.
This originates in a conﬂict of interests : the central government has made commitments to combat
53
==PAGE:62==
poverty, spatial exclusion and develop sustainable communities, while the land on which Old Fadama
is settled is valuable in the eye of the AMA, to develop real estate that would proﬁt the city. This
results in a dual stance from various government levels. Encouraging a development project in Old
Fadama could undermine the AMA’s authority and generate tensions with the central government,
the extent of which are beyond this research. Nevertheless, it appears from this situation that an
easier solution could be to focus on providing durable relocation solutions to the tenants of Old
Fadama rather than making this "urban village" a more sustainable one. This would be correlated
by the fact that the main power grid in Ghana is a more cost-eﬀective option than a decentralized
system. If the focus was shifted towards actively ﬁnding relocation solutions in formal neighbour-
hood for slum-dwellers, the issue of having to develop a decentralized system and its drawbacks
would vanish. However, the Accra region already suﬀers from a lack of aﬀordable formal housing,
which intensiﬁes due to fast rural-urban migrations (15). It might prove more cost-eﬀective for the
central government to encourage oﬀ-grid energy projects in these settlements, rather than invest to
develop aﬀordable neighbourhoods.
6.5
Additional work
This study could be supplemented by other initiatives to provide reliable conclusions. Old Fadama
has been chosen as an example as it is the largest informal settlement in Ghana and one of the
oldest; much data is available regarding its situation and it can be assumed its situation is similar
to that of other settlements. However it would be beneﬁcial to carry out research regarding several
informal settlements in Ghana, in the Accra region and others, to document energy needs and socio
economic structure. From there, reliable insights could be developed regarding decentralized energy
systems in informal settlements in Ghana. Another possibility could be to develop a pilot project
in Old Fadama, and observe its evolution over several years. In-depth data collection could increase
knowledge on the topic, and provide improvements to the structure. The project, if successful, could
then be scaled-up to other communities. This would be representative of a bottom-up approach,
where a viable system is then up scaled to other environments (57).

7. Guidelines for implementation
The aim of this part is to describe the practical process of implementing such a decentralized energy
system in the informal settlement of Old Fadama; while this is applied to a speciﬁc area, the
purpose is for the plan to be scalable, and the guidelines to be suited to other informal settlements
in Ghana. These guidelines are destined to several types of stakeholders who might be implicated
in the project : local entrepreneurs, informal settlement dwellers, external development actors, or
private companies. This study to improve energy access in Old Fadama remains theoretical, and
would be largely improved with additional feedback from the ﬁeld. Most data was obtained through
the abundant literature concerning Old Fadama, and partly from interviews of an OFADA contact
through a third party, from the NGO TU Empower Africa. However a key to the success of an
eventual project would be for it to be tailored to the needs of the community, a key success factor
in the development of integrated solutions for marginalised societies (57)).
7.1
Establishing contact with the community
The ﬁrst step towards implementing the solution would be to contact the target community. The
stakeholder willing to develop the system should identify an urban neighbourhood without formal
energy access. According to 2019 World Bank statistics this still amounts to 15% of the Ghanaian
urban population. Contact should then be established with the informal governing body of the
settlement; in the case of Old Fadama this corresponds to the OFADA. Customary institutions such
as chieftaincies are the most common form of organisation in informal settlements in Ghana, often
mirroring the rural origins of the tenants (53). As chiefs constitute the main governing body of the
settlement, their agreement to any large-scale project is essential. The purpose of this part is to
interest the chiefs in a decentralized energy system, explain the long-term ﬁnancial and environmental
beneﬁts as well as the community empowerment that could result from it. If customary institutions
give their approbation, the project could continue to the next step.
7.2
Consumption surveys
After getting the authorisation of local customary authorities, the preliminary work to the project
can begin. In this theoretical research, this part was carried out using literature and analogies with
other informal settlements. Surveys will be carried out within the community, with the help of its
members. Such self-organised surveys are not undocumented in informal settlements such has Old
Fadama (29), and the participation of community members is key to their success. The aim of the
surveys is to assess the level of energy demand and the consumption patterns of the tenants. An
evaluation of the use of electric appliances could be of interest as well; the aim of this part is to
document the needs the energy system will have to satisfy. Another goal of these surveys would be
to identify community dynamics and groups that could be favorable to the project : people willing
to take an active role in a cooperative, possible entrepreneurs, etc. As in the case of Old Fadama,
the community might be too large for its needs to be met by the ﬁrst iteration of a system. Here, the
team working with the community will determine which neighbourhood of the community should
beneﬁt from the system, and would be suited to install it. It is the needs of this sub-community
that shall be considered for the rest of the project.
55
==PAGE:64==
7.3
Measurements
Once this part is reached, a renewable energy resources assessment should be carried out. In this
research for Old Fadama, the assessment part was only carried out through online tools : while they
yield approximate results they should be supplemented by a ﬁeld study. This is a key point of the
initiative as it determines the system that can be implemented. A team will conduct a measurement
campaign of solar irradiation and wind speeds, working as close to the community as possible. Ideally
measurements would take place over a year to take into account all seasonal variations. Once the
measurements are complete, if they yield satisfying results, the team can proceed to the design of
an optimal energy system.
7.4
System analysis
At this point of the project, an energy system can be designed to meet the needs of the community.
The energy mix will be determined using the data from the previous measurements. The system’s
requirements are closely linked to the speciﬁcations of the community : this is crucial for the project
to succeed, that it it tailor made for the community. This part should take into account the speciﬁc
businesses and livelihoods of the tenants, to organise the system around this consumption. The core
point is to keep in mind the empowerment of the community through energy access during the design
phase; the system has to ﬁt their consumption patterns. In this theoretical research for instance,
one system was molded around the scrapyard activities in Agbogbloshie, while the other one was
modelled around the consumption of a residential neighbourhood with small food businesses.
7.5
Determine the best structure for the system ownership
The project for an energy center now requires a structure ﬁnance and to operate it. This research
explored two types of ownership structure, based on existing business cases. First oﬀ, the system
could be owned by a cooperative of individuals, members of the community. Cooperatives are deﬁned
by the Ghanaian Department of Cooperatives with the following wording : "Thus, a cooperative
is made up of people who are united with the purpose of mutual self-help and are organized for
other economic and social objectives with the intent to be based on equality and equity. They must
provide the people with necessary goods and services or make joint use of their available resources
top increase their income." The creation and operation of cooperatives is ruled in Ghana by the
CO-OPERATIVE SOCIETIES ACT 1968 (NLCD 252) Subject to the provisions of this Decree, any
society which has as its object the promotion of the economic interest of its members in accordance
with co-operative principles, may be registered under this Decree with or without limited liability. (65)
While originally intended for agricultural cooperatives, it does not exclude cooperative participation
in an energy structure. The 2019 Renewable Energy Masterplan (REMP) mentions cooperatives as
ways to operate renewable energy systems in rural settlements (9). In case of cooperative ownership,
the inhabitants of the neighbourhood should then create it, if possible basing themselves on existing
structures within the settlement. In the case of this theoretical study, the OFADA was considered
to be the development association that could guide the development of a cooperative. The alternate
option is to entrust the development of the system to a private entrepreneur, if possible originating
from the community. This option was explored in the case of the Agbogbloshie scrapyard, whose
users are dynamic, business-oriented individuals with a knowledge of the local communities.
56
==PAGE:65==
7.6
Financing
This step comes after the operating structure has been determined; the individual or collective
responsible for the project have to determine the ways the system will be paid for. Money must
be partly put up by the members of the cooperative themselves. "Saving groups" pooling together
money for cooperative projects are a common occurrence in sub-Saharan informal settlements (59)
and should provide a substantial part of the funding, as the system is ultimately going to belong
to the tenants. Engaging their own savings could strengthen the commitment of the people to the
sustainability of the project, a key aspect to the success of integrated solutions in slum environment
(57). Another source of ﬁnancing should come through possible donations from Non Governmental
Organisations. Such support has been obtained in the past in the case of Old Fadama, to fund
community structure such as a primary school in 2007 (53). Finally, the rest of the ﬁnancial burden
should be borrowed from a bank.
It might prove diﬃcult to secure a loan from an institution
due to the outside-of-the-rule-of-law nature of most informal settlements. However several African
and international banks support development projects. Among others, the Ghanaian Government
has developed a bank dedicated to low-interest loans to promote entrepreneurship in the renewable
sector, the ARB Apex Bank Project (9).
7.7
Obtaining a permit for energy production
Once ﬁnancing is secure, the project needs to obtain an energy production license from the Public
Utilities Regulatory Commission (PURC). Energy licenses in Ghana are regulated by the Ghanaian
Energy Commission through the RENEWABLE ENERGY ACT 2011 (ACT 832) (62). While tenure
security is not cited as a direct ground to reject the license, it poses a possible contentious. This
part would prove to be the most crucial part of the project. It is to be noted that energy licenses are
granted by the Ghanaian Energy Commission directly, and not by local authorities such as the AMA
in the case of Old Fadama. While informal settlements have usually conﬂictual relationships with
local authority due to tenure issues, the stance of the Ghanaian government has been more lenient
and geared towards slum upgrading over the last decades (59). This duality of stances from local
and governmental bodies will be explored further in the "Policy recommendation" section. After
the license has been obtained by the body responsible for the project, physical work can start and
the system can be implemented.

8. Policy Recommendation
Thus far in this research the energy access issue in informal settlements has been considered from
a bottom-up approach : small-scale replicable solutions tailored for communities. However those
methods are only eﬃcient when paired with the right regulatory framework, which enables these
local solutions to thrive. This chapter analyses the regulatory and legal framework of Ghana under
two combined aspects : the introduction of renewables in the energy sector, and the approach to
informal settlements.
First oﬀit considers the current policies that could be favourable to the
implementation of urban decentralized energy systems; then possible modiﬁcations to some texts
are put forward; ﬁnally, the duality of the role of local and national government in those initiatives
is explored.
8.1
Existing laws in favor of the initiative
8.1.1
Law regulating the renewable energy sector
The production of renewable energy in Ghana is regulated by the Renewable Energy Act from 2011
(Act 832). The objective of this law is to promote the development of renewable energy sources,
as well as regulate their production and distribution.
It is mostly geared towards utility-scale
production of renewable energy to be sent in the main grid. Therefore it requires its tariﬀs to be
ﬁxed by the Public Utilities Regulatory Commission (PURC), to be indexed on the Ghanaian prices
for renewable energy. As an incentive, the development of a renewable energy plant requires a power
purchase agreement between the national distributor and the private producer. This would not
apply to a small-scale decentralized system, as its main focus would be local consumption. The
main requirement to produce renewable energy is the reception of a license from PURC. The text
states that energy production licenses can be obtained by :
• (a) a citizen
• (b) a body corporate
• (c) a partnership registered under the Incorporated Private Partnership Act (1962)
According to the Incorporated Private Partnership Act, a cooperative would fall under the deﬁnition
of partnership as "Partnership means the association of two or more individuals carrying on business
jointly for the purpose of making proﬁts". (IPPA 1962). Therefore both operating structures for the
energy system could be eligible for the reception of an energy license. It is stated that licensing can
be refused for reasons founded on :
• (a) technical data
• (b) national security
• (c) public safety
• (d) food security
• (e) health
• (f) environmental safety
58
==PAGE:67==
The text does not mention mini-grids or production for a non-commercial use, typically that of a
community meeting its own needs through a cooperative structure. However the text recognizes the
importance of the private sector to develop renewable energy sources, and the need for the state to
facilitate private investments through incentives.
8.1.2
Mini-Grid Electriﬁcation Policy (2016)
This policy implemented in 2016, means to put mini-grid on a more mainstream level to achieve
faster electriﬁcation. They are considered public sector investments according to this policy, with the
Volta River Authority being responsible for production and the Electricity Company of Ghana tasked
with distribution. Again in this situation mini-grids are considered means to rural electriﬁcation,
aimed at serving remote part of the country (Ghana Renewable Energy Master Plan, 2019).
8.1.3
Decree on the REMP - Renewable energy Masterplan
In 2019, Ghana issued a Renewable Energy Master Plan (REMP), through the Ministry of Energy
and other energy sector actors. The main vision behind the plan is to achieve a more comprehensive,
eﬃcient, sustainable energy sector promoting local initiatives while mitigating climate change. It is
composed of several sub-objectives :
• Increase the proportion of renewable energy1 in the national energy generation mix from 42.5
MW in 2015 to 1363.63 MW (with grid connected systems totalling 1094.63 MW);
• Reduce the dependence on biomass as main fuel for thermal energy applications;
• Provide renewable energy-based decentralised electriﬁcation options in 1,000 oﬀ-grid commu-
nities;
• Promote local content and local participation in the renewable energy industry
The means to that end are an emphasis on private sector participation and the promotion of mini-
grid electriﬁcation, using capital investments and low-interest loans to renewable energy initiatives.
The REMP represents a total of 5,6 billion $, or over 460 million a year over 12 years. Most of these
measures are geared towards rural electriﬁcation, to electrify remote, oﬀ-grid localities mainly in the
Northern regions of Ghana. Informal settlements are not mentioned in the report. However informal
settlements actually ﬁt several of the qualiﬁcations for this plan, being underserved populations
with no formal access to the grid.
A local initiative to promote an oﬀ-grid system for tenure-
challenged population would fall under the deﬁnition of "promoting local content and participation
in the renewable energy industry". A focus on energy access in informal settlement would also curb
electricity theft practices, indirectly improving the reliability of the Ghanaian distribution network.
It overall appears that the rural electriﬁcation plan leaves out large populations of underserved
urban dwellers, while they could beneﬁt from relatively minor modiﬁcations in said plan. The plan
does set as one of its goals "Address institutional and market constraints that hamper increasing
electricity access to the poor", not specifying the rural poor as a target. Rural locations and urban
slums experience comparable dynamics of exclusion from the energy sector, which could be addressed
through private investment.
59
==PAGE:68==
8.1.4
Slum upgrading program
To realise this cross-analysis, this research considers both policies regarding electriﬁcation and those
relative to slum-upgrading.
Slum upgrading has been a concern for the Ghanaian government
since the 1980s, with several successful development initiatives to address growing uncontrolled
urbanization. The Ministry of Local Government and Rural Development especially implemented
in 1996 the Urban Environmental Sanitation Project (UESP) jointly with the World Bank, aimed
at tackling infrastructural issues in informal settlements.
The program was partly successful in
upgrading communities, and involving local governments (37). However it fell short in addressing
tenure issues, that have been identiﬁed as a key feature of sustainability for slum upgrading policies
(40). Local municipalities are less inclined to favour slum dwellers in land tenure conﬂict, as they
often inhabit land that could be valued otherwise (32).
This can be witnessed in the enduring
conﬂict between the AMA and Old Fadama, which is left to be handled by local authorities by
the central government.
No recent development plan focused on informal settlements has seen
the light of day in Ghana in the last ten years, despite the acceleration of rural-urban migrations
that emphasize the phenomenon. A formal plan akin to the Renewable Energy Masterplan would
formalize slum upgrading initiatives, by stating objectives and encouraging private investments in
that sector. Overall regarding informal settlement development, the sector is rather marked by the
absence of regulatory framework, which is coherent with the situation of these settlements, at the
border of Ghanaian society (28).
8.2
Possible changes in existing laws to facilitate the electriﬁcation
of informal settlements
A possible modiﬁcation of the renewable energy license regulation has already been proposed by
the World Bank in its case study for micro-grid implementation in Ghana (2). According to the
Renewable Energy Act (832), a power producer is required to obtain a license to indulge in the pro-
duction of renewable energy. However, as explained further up, these measures are targeted towards
utility-scale producers and such regulation could burden small local producers, and weigh down the
development of small-scale private power structures. This could have even heavier consequences in
informal environments such as slums, where the share of the informal sector is much larger than in
the rest of Ghanaian society, and most businesses are run informally. The study by the World Bank
suggested to exempt from asking for a license any grid with an installed capacity of less than 100
kW. This would make the development of mini-grids more ﬂexible and promote their implementation
throughout the territory, beneﬁting not only informal settlements but also rural areas targeted by
the current electriﬁcation policies.
8.3
Policy recommendation
Based on this research, several policy recommendation can be drawn to achieve the vision of a Ghana
with universal energy access, a long-term goal stated since the 1989 National Electriﬁcation Policy
(24). Special attention should be directed towards informal settlements, as their fast growth will
only worsen the issue of they are ignored.
8.3.1
Electriﬁcation policy
Ghana has developed detailed electriﬁcation policies, that are updated frequently to ﬁt the evolution
of renewable generation technologies, as well as the situation of the country. Those policies have
60
==PAGE:69==
evolved from the National Electriﬁcation Policy (1989), to the most recent Renewable Energy Master
Plan (2019), and keep dictating the stance of the government and other stakeholders regarding
electriﬁcation. However most of these policies are geared towards rural electriﬁcation. In doing so
they fail to target the other group that lacks electricity access in Ghana, the urban poor. So far
no recent comprehensive plan aims at electrifying the informal urban areas, that mostly achieve
electricity access through electricity theft.
Most of the 2019 REMP policies could actually be adaptable to an urban scenario, where tenure
issues prevent the access to the grid proper. As the tenants do not have a formal address, electricity
companies do not operate in the areas; all the while contentious with the local authority due to land
tenure disputes leads to a denial of service all together in the settlement’s area. This in turn leads
to a situation where slum dwellers resort to electricity theft to meet their needs. This unregulated
trade is proportionally very expensive for its customers, and contributes to the degradation of the
Ghanaian network, all the while marginalizing the populations.
To help the development of these neighbourhoods, the promotion of decentralized systems using
mini-grids should be formalized by a government plan. Through investments in small-scale systems
operated by locals, the neighbourhoods could bridge part of the gap that separates them from formal
areas. Development of small businesses, backed-up by development plans, could ﬁnancially empower
the communities, and contribute to their better integration to the Ghanaian society. In this regard,
informal settlements could be regarded like the villages electriﬁcation policies are usually destined
to.
They constitute isolated, low-income communities that could beneﬁt from electricity access
to develop and thrive. However their speciﬁc situation within Ghanaian society (remoteness, land
tenure conﬂicts) has led to them being marginalized and denied services available elsewhere. In
the case of remote villages, the choice is simple as isolation is only physical : electricity will be
available when the grid reaches the village. When the costs are prohibitive, stand-alone systems
and mini-grids stand out as a viable solution. However in the case of the slum, the grid is near but
unavailable for tenure reasons. While a legally accurate response would be "They should not be there,
hence they should not access the grid", it is also simplistic and not suited to the ﬂexible realities
of informal neighbourhoods. In many cases in Ghana, slums are there to stay, as eviction policies
prove unsustainable and rural-urban migrations intensify. Therefore, if the local government is still
reluctant to provide tenure and formal access to electricity, the idea to develop oﬀ-grid energy should
be explored. Many slums are dynamic environments where private entrepreneurs could develop a
business; through adequate institutional action, electriﬁcation in sub-urban areas could be achieved
through those means.
8.3.2
The role of the central and local governments
The role of the central government in the long run is linked to the pledges it has made for the future.
Ghana adopted the SDGs in 2015 along with 192 other countries, and as such set development
goals it should strive to meet. SDG n°7 : Clean, aﬀordable energy for everyone and SDG n°11 :
Sustainable cities and communities are the heart of this problematic. They represent a commitment
to reach these goals for every Ghanaian, including slum dwellers. Therefore, to comply with those
goals, the Ghanaian central government should put slum upgrading plans on the forefront to tackle
this growing issue (SDGs in Ghana, 2017). Beyond the SDGs, Ghana has also made pledges through
the Intended Nationally Determined Contributions (INDC), organised through the United Nations
Framework Convention on Climate Change (UNFCCC). These contributions, agreed upon at the
2013 United Nations Climate Change Conference held in Warsaw, Poland, in November 2013. In its
contribution, Ghana agreed to mitigate its climate impact by improving the following ﬁelds :
• Sustainable land use including food security
61
==PAGE:70==
• Climate proof infrastructure
• Equitable social development
• Sustainable mass transportation
• Sustainable energy security
• Sustainable forest management
• Alternative urban waste management.
Equitable social development and Sustainable energy security are crucial in this approach, as they
both apply to slum scenarios. Informal settlements are areas of uneven development, where energy
security is not assured and often relies on unsustainable practices. Therefore to meet its NDC,
Ghana has all interest to pursue a policy of slum upgrading, and developing renewable electricity
access in its growing informal settlements. Electricity access is of course but one of the aspects of
this matter, along with other infrastructural challenges such as sanitation and water supply. The key
to the sustainability of these policies however, is addressing the underlying tenure issues that plague
informal settlements in Ghana. In such an approach, the government could initiate a national slum
upgrading program to promote private sector decentralized energy systems in informal settlements,
while providing insurance that there will be no evictions. Tenure security has indeed been identiﬁed
to encourage locals to invest in the development of their neighbourhood (40).
In this scheme, the central government of Ghana could proceed in the same way as for the devel-
opment of renewable energy explained in the REMP. Through low-interest loans and investments
in cooperatives, the private sector could develop energy access locally, more eﬃciently than state-
structure that would struggle in this informal environment. The government could incentivize banks
to deliver those low interest loans, further integrating informal settlements to the economic fabric
of Ghana by going further than community-savings based projects. Incentives by the state, and
possible guarantees of payback if projects fail for instance, could provide a sense of security for ﬁ-
nancial institutions to engage their capital in those projects. Resources could be allocated following
a cost per square kilometer policy, a method that proved successful under the Urban Environmental
Sanitation Project (UESP). This way costs are kept in check by assuming the level of services is
proportional to the surface of the slum. Another success of this program was to engage the local
municipalities to pay for 10% of the slum upgrading initiatives, a way of involving the local authority
in the upgrading process, and favoring dialogue between local authority and customary authorities
within the slum (37). Promoting community participation is a crucial aspect of slum upgrading
policies, and local authorities such as the AMA in Accra could act as a coordinator between the
state and the customary authorities from the settlement to ensure communication goes well. In-
versely, the state could play the role of a mediator between local authority and informal settlement
regarding tenure disputes. Putting the evictions on hold through a governmental order could be a
way to make the slum more durable, thus promoting private investment to upgrade it. The central
government has shown over the last years signs of recognition of informal settlements as commu-
nities, for instance recognizing the schools installed in Old Fadama on the same level as those of
formal neighbourhoods. This shift towards community-oriented slum upgrading could be extended
to more recognition from the government to local customary structures, possibly acting as a referee
during tenure disputes with formal local authorities, such as the AMA.
Finally, expertise could be provided to local actors by the Energy commission. Among its responsi-
bility in the Renewable Energy Masterplan 2019 are to :

Those roles could extend to the development of decentralized renewable energy technologies in the
informal settlement areas of Ghanaian cities. The Energy Commission could play the role of an ad-

8.4
A point on COVID 19 and the Ghanaian informal settlements
The COVID-19 crisis of 2020 aﬀected all countries around the world to a certain extent, and Ghana
proved to be no exception. The pandemic hit in the country on the 12th of March, when the two ﬁrst
cases were detected. The government of president Nana Akufo-Addo reacted early on, promoting
social distancing measures. On March 30th, a partial lockdown of the cities of Accra and Tema took
eﬀect. The number of cases progressively soared to 2000 by the end of April, to a total of 18,000
conﬁrmed cases by the end of June. The situation remains under the control of the government. The
Ghanaian government observed an indulgent policy towards the poorest sections of the population,
acknowledging the fact that the crisis would aﬀect them the hardest. The minister of Works and
Housing, Samuel Atta Akyea, advised landlords to be lenient towards tenants that encountered
ﬁnancial trouble, and asked to refrain from evicting until the end of the COVID-19 crisis. Mr. Atta
Akyea hinted at the possibility for the state to pay the rent of those threatened with eviction, should
the ﬁnances allow it. However, the response to the coronavirus pandemic also outlined fault lines in
the Ghanaian society in regard to informal settlements. Informal settlements are deeply dependent
on the informal economy to survive, with Old Fadama residents being employed at 85% in the
informal sector. The lockdown aﬀected their livelihood as the activity in most markets of Accra was
slowed down. According to the interviewee, the situation was diﬃcult to live through for informal
workers, and some were considering going back to their hometown where they could sustain the
lockdown, to come back back once it blew over. Nevertheless, still according to the OFADA contact,
the AMA carried out evictions during the lockdown period. On the 15th of April, more than 1000
tenants from Old Fadama were forcefully evicted, over the course of a demolition exercise. The aim
of the maneuver was to prepare the banks of the Odaw for the dredging operations accompanying
the clean up of the Korle Lagoon. No notice was served prior to the eviction. This operation struck
the public opinion as no relocation solution was provided, and people were still ordered to remain
home. This recent development illustrate a double standard in the way informal settlement dwellers
are considered within Ghanaian society.

9. Conclusion
Several objectives had been established at the beginning of this research. Over the course of this
project, they have all been met. The ﬁrst objective of the research was to map out the situation
of the energy sector in Ghana, with a focus on energy access in urban informal settlements. The
Ghanaian energy sector stands out as well developed, relying on hydro power for 45% of its capacity;
it is however burdened by sub-par management and high energy losses, partly due to energy theft
(7). Government campaigns aim at electrifying the northern countryside; the use of decentralized,
oﬀ-grid renewable technologies has been promoted by the Ghanaian Energy Commission as a mean
to electrify remote locations. Nevertheless oﬀ-grid solutions are referred to as a situational option
and the preferred method is grid connection; the development of oﬀ-grid solution is not regulated
by a clearly dedicated policy, and has so far been left to be developed by the private sector (24).
The Energy Commission is indeed opening the market to Independent Power Producers (IPPs),
acknowledging the need for private actors to enter the energy generation sector. However eﬀorts
remain geared towards rural electriﬁcation, and growing informal settlements, consequences of rural-
urban migrations, remain oﬀthe main grid, with no deﬁnite plan to address that issue (66). As
56% of the Ghanaian population is urban in 2018, it appears addressing the issue of fast-growing
informal settlements is crucial (10).
The following goal was to carry out a study of an informal settlement in Ghana, map out its social
and economic structures and determine the nature of its energy connections. The largest settlement
in Accra, Old Fadama, was selected due to the good availability of data. It is also representative
of informal settlement dynamics as it has been engaged in a tenure conﬂict with the local Ac-
cra Metropolitan Authority (AMA) since its apparition; attempts at evicting the community have
proven unsuccessful up until today. It appears after investigation the slum is an organised commu-
nity existing outside of the formal framework, breaking the misconceptions of informal settlements
as disorganised, lawless areas. It should be noted most of the tenants are from rural, tribal back-
grounds and have retained those statutory structures. The Accra Metropolitan Area has withdrawn
from administrating this district; in turn, a confederation of tribal chiefs organises services such
as urban planning, garbage disposal, ﬁre safety or basic education. Moreover, there are instances
of joint projects using community savings to upgrade the neighbourhood’s infrastructure, such as
reconstruction with ﬁre-proof materials. Concerning electricity access, it is achieved through unsafe,
illegal connections to the grid, which pose a ﬁre hazard and place a proportionally high ﬁnancial
burden on households. Most of the community is composed of young working individuals (above
94% of the respondents), organised in communal households of more than ten persons. The major
part of those, around 85%, are employed in the informal sector. Old Fadama features an interesting
informal industrial site, as it borders an electric dumping site known as the Agbogbloshie landﬁll.
A scrapyard within the landﬁll provides recycling and smelting work to up to 6000 tenants of the
slum. This scrapyard business presents an energy scenario diﬀerent from that of the main slum; this
allows for a more diverse energy system study later on. The ﬁnancial means of the tenants have been
estimated relying on censuses organised by NGOs, while backed by the community. Results from this
census were then adjusted to inﬂation to estimate a median monthly income of 315 ghanaian cedis
per person in 2020 (about 54$ per month). The overall outcome of this case study depicts an or-
ganised community of job-active individuals, susceptible to invest in projects that would beneﬁt the
group. It also outlined the burden that unregulated electricity tapping weighs on the development
of the area.
The subsequent objective was to study the feasibility of an integrated energy center within the
64
==PAGE:73==
studied informal settlement.
Relying on a mix of renewable and fossil energy, it would provide
electricity to the community through a mini-grid, bypassing the need to plug the community to the
main grid, which is currently impossible due to tenure conﬂicts. A renewable energy assessment
has been carried out, outlining substantial PV potential and limited wind power potential in the
area; the model of an energy center is thus simulated using a combination of solar and diesel, us-
ing the optimization software HOMER. Three separate locations have been studied for a possible
implementation in the neighbourhood, for two of them to ﬁnally be selected, constituting two dif-
ferent energy scenarios the system would have to meet. The ﬁrst scenario is a neighbourhood of Old
Fadama constituted of ten people households, housing informal businesses. The energy needs of the
tenants were estimated factoring in basic appliances such as lamps and outside, as well as the need
for refrigeration for either households or small businesses. The ﬁnal load for a 30 households bundle
was estimated around 50 kWh/day. In the second scenario, the load is based on the appliances
used for the scrapyard activities, such as cable-stripping equipment. This scenario would mobilize
about 45 kWh/day for two cable-stripping machines and outside lights. It is interesting that the
residential and the industrial loads diﬀer greatly, broadening the scope of the study. The ﬁndings
of the research are that in both scenarios, a decentralized system relying on solar panels, diesel and
batteries for storage would indeed be more viable than the current illegal electriﬁcation. For the
residential scenario, the Net Present Cost (NPC) over 25 years amounts to 115,498$ for a 30 house-
holds bundle, whereas using illegal connections represents 236,863$ over the same time frame. The
Levelized Cost of Energy (LCOE) in this case is 0,467$/kWh. Concerning the industrial scenario,
the system would have a NPC of 99,310$ over 25 years and a LCOE of 0,426$/kWh. These ﬁgures
should however be compared those of an eventual connection to the main grid : the NPC is this
case would amount to 60,038 $ over the 25 years period for the same consumption, for an LCOE
of about 0,09$/kWh. It appears in conclusion that implementing a decentralized, oﬀ-grid system is
more viable than the current situation where tapped electricity is sold back to the customers at a
high price. The connection would also be more reliable in the case of an independent system, and ﬁre
safety would improve. However, it remains prohibitive when compared to regular grid connection.
According to these ﬁndings, an analogy could be realised between the situation in Old Fadama and
other settlements, and the implementation of a decentralized system could be studied, as it appears
viable in this scenario.
A secondary objective was to explore possible types of ownership for an energy generating structure,
based on the characteristics of the site of implementation. Two possible ways of proceeding were
explored : private or community-based ownership. In the ﬁrst case, an entrepreneur would take a
loan, operate and own the structure, running it like a business and presenting a fare for its use. In
the second option, which would be preferred for environments with strong communal ties, a group
would own and operate the structure through a cooperative. Both approaches have in common they
should rely on locals to own and operate them, empowering the community.
Beyond the case study of a speciﬁc settlement, the aim of this research was to study the scalability of a
local solution in other environments. Therefore, a series of guidelines to facilitate the implementation
of the system has been developed, based on precedents and on the deﬁning structures of Ghanaian
slums. The ﬁndings suggests to involve the community in every step of the project. Working with
the statutory authorities such as the council of chiefs is quintessential, as they will decide whether
the project should be implemented or not. Informal settlements constitute an informal environment
where data is scarce, and basic infrastructure is not always available. Therefore a stakeholder willing
to develop an stand-alone energy system should rely on local tenants to carry-out preliminary studies,
energy needs assessment, and to implement the system physically. Training of the local population
to the use of the energy system would be crucial to its success.
Finally, this project is aimed at drawing guidelines beyond the local implementation, for other
65
==PAGE:74==
stakeholders in Ghana to take part in the electriﬁcation of informal settlements. Ghana presents a
strong dynamic of rural electriﬁcation, backed by the state and international development actors.
The government acknowledges the need for independent power producers, including decentralized
systems, to electrify remote locations. On a diﬀerent level, the central government has also displayed
a will to normalize the relations with informal settlements. Therefore this research recommends
the electriﬁcation plan to be slightly altered to encompass pilot projects in informal settlements.
Studies to implement community-sized mini grids should be carried out, possibly by private actors,
with adequate government support, while cooperating with the locals. Policies to promote such
initiatives could assume several shapes :
• Normalizing the relationships between statutory power structures such as municipalities and
customary structures that organize life in informal settlements;
• Forgoing the need for an energy production license for energy systems under a certain capacity
(100 kW);
• Low interest loans or investments in local cooperatives willing to develop a stand-alone energy
system;
• Educating informal settlements associations on the beneﬁts of decentralized energy systems,
to provide an opportunity for these settlements to develop one;
• Promoting collaboration between local customary actors and state energy agencies such as the
Energy Commission.
This set of guidelines would serve as a guide to stakeholders willing to develop energy access in
informal settlements throughout Ghana. They aim at improving collaborations between the various
actors of the sector, and alleviating part of the ﬁnancial constraints that grieve the development of
oﬀ-grid systems. Adequate cooperation between the stakeholders, with government support, could
result in a safer, more reliable energy access in Ghanaian informal settlements, further empowering
those communities.
