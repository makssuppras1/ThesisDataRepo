Abstract
The contribution to global warming from landﬁlls can be reduced by application of biocover sys-
tems. Such systems can mitigate methane generated at landﬁlls by optimising microbial methane
oxidation inside the biocovers. The performance of a recently established biocover system at Au-
debo landﬁll in Denmark was investigated by means of a literature review as well as six ﬁeld mea-
surement campaigns, which took place in May-July, 2020. The literature review conﬁrmed that the
biocover system at Audebo landﬁll has been designed and installed according to state-of-the-art
guidelines and protocols. Hence, six biocovers with a total area of 1,949 m2 were in operation at
Audebo landﬁll as of August 2019. These biocovers were implemented as biowindows as well as
bioﬁlters. The bioﬁlters are fed by landﬁll gas passively led from leachate inspection and collection
wells into perforated gas pipes installed inside the gas distribution layers below the bioﬁlters. A
30 cm gas distribution layer with an 80 cm thick layer of garden compost on top have been ap-
plied for both the bioﬁlters and the biocovers. Results published for the most recent total methane
emission measurement in March 2020 showed that a methane removal efﬁciency of approximately
60% could be documented for the whole biocover system at Audebo landﬁll. In addition, results
from the six ﬁeld measurement campaigns of the present project revealed that some biocovers were
operating with very high methane oxidation efﬁciencies while others exhibited lower efﬁciencies.
More speciﬁcally, two selected biocovers were investigated by means of surface emission screen-
ings, ﬂux-chamber measurements, gas proﬁle probes, and raw gas composition sampling. Based on
these ﬁeld investigations, the methane oxidation efﬁciency of the two selected biocovers was deter-
mined by use of the carbon mass balance method as well as the gas concentration proﬁle principle.
This showed that one of the biocovers demonstrated an excellent methane oxidation efﬁciency of
99%, while the methane oxidation efﬁciency of the other biocover was only 49%. This may help
explaining why the March 2020 total emission measurement indicated an overall methane removal
efﬁciency of 60%. In the light of this, further investigation of the biocovers at Audebo landﬁll is
suggested focusing on identiﬁcation of biocover areas exhibiting low methane oxidation efﬁcien-
cies and/or heterogeneous gas loading within the gas distribution layer. In addition, the ﬁndings
of the six ﬁeld measurement campaigns revealed that the leachate collection system, which is used
to route landﬁll gas to the bioﬁlters, may have a gas leakage issue, because signiﬁcant intrusion
of atmospheric air was detected inside certain inspection wells. This means that methane leaks
related to the leachate system may also contribute to unwanted emissions. In relation to this, it is
worthwhile noting that a total methane emission measurement of the whole Audebo landﬁll site is
planned for September 2020. Hence, it is suggested to plan a new set of ﬁeld measurements based
on the outcome of this total emission measurement to clarify whether biocover and/or leachate
system optimisations should be initiated.

Sammenfatning
Bidraget fra affaldsdeponier til drivhuseffekten kan reduceres ved etablering af biomitigeringssys-
temer. Disse systemer imødeg˚ar metanemissioner fra deponierne ved hjælp af mikrobiel omsætning
af metanen, som sker i s˚akaldte biocovers baseret p˚a f.eks. komposteret haveaffald. Effektiviteten af
et biocoversystem, etableret p˚a Audebo Deponi i Danmark, er blevet undersøgt i nærværende pro-
jekt ved hjælp af litteraturstudier samt 6 m˚alekampagner i felten udført i perioden maj til juli 2020.
Litteraturstudiet bekræftede, at biocoversystemet p˚a Audebo Deponi er designet og installeret i
henhold til den danske miljøstyrelses nyeste retningslinjer og protokoller. S˚aledes var 6 biocovers
med et samlet areal p˚a 1.949 m2 færdigetablerede p˚a Audebo Deponi i august 2019. Disse bio-
covers er implementeret som s˚akaldte biovinduer og bioﬁltre, hvor sidstnævnte tilføres deponigas
p˚a passiv vis fra perkolatbrønde. Denne deponigas fordeles til bioﬁltrene ved hjælp af perforerede
gasdistributionsrør, som beﬁnder sig i det s˚akaldte gasopsamlings- og fordelingslag placeret under
kompostlaget i bioﬁlteret. Speciﬁkt gælder der for b˚ade biovinduer og bioﬁltre, at gasopsamlings-
og fordelingslaget har en tykkelse p˚a 30 cm, mens det overliggende kompostlag har en tykkelse p˚a
80 cm. Resultater fra den seneste m˚aling af den totale metanemission fra Audebo Deponi udført
i marts 2020 viste, at reduktionen i metanemissionen, som var opn˚aet med etableringen af bio-
coversystemet, var p˚a ca. 60%. Resultaterne fra feltundersøgelserne gennemført i nærværende
projekt viste desuden, at nogle biocovers opererede med meget høj metanoxidationseffektivitet,
mens andre udviste lavere effektivitet. S˚aledes blev to repræsentative biocovers undersøgt ved
brug af overﬂade metanscreeninger, ﬂuxkammerm˚alinger, poregasm˚alinger samt m˚alinger af gas-
sammensætningen i de perkolatbrønde, som er forbundet til gasfordelingslaget i de p˚agældende
biocovers. Med udgangspunkt i disse feltundersøgelser blev metanoxidationseffektiviteten bereg-
net for de to biocovers ved hjælp af kulstofsmassebalancemetoden samt det koncentrationsproﬁl-
baserede effektivitetsberegningsprincip. Resultatet af disse beregninger viste, at et biocover ud-
viste en fremragende metanoxidationseffektivitet p˚a 99%, mens det andet biocover kun havde en
metanoxidationseffektivitet p˚a 49%. Dette kan muligvis hjælpe til med at forklare, hvorfor total
metanemissionsm˚alingen i marts 2020 angav en metanimødeg˚aelseseffektivitet p˚a 60%. S˚aledes
foresl˚as det, at der udføres yderligere feltundersøgelser af biocoversystemet p˚a Audebo Deponi for
at afklare, om nogle bioﬁltre eller biovinduer evt. udviser lavere end forventede metanoxidation-
seffektiviteter, samt om dette evt. kan relateres til heterogen metanbelastning af kompostlaget i de
p˚agældende biocovers. Derudover viste de 6 feltm˚alekampagner, at perkolatopsamlingssystemet p˚a
Audebo Deponi m˚aske har utætheder, som kan føre til uønskede metanemissioner. Denne hypotese
baserer sig p˚a, at der i visse perkolatinspektionsbrønde blev m˚alt gassammensætninger, som tydede
p˚a, at større mængder af atmosfærisk luft kunne trænge ind i disse brønde. I denne sammenhæng
er det værd at fremhæve, at en ny total metanemissionsm˚aling er planlagt for Audebo Deponi i
september 2020. Derfor foresl˚as det, at et nyt sæt af feltm˚alinger planlægges i lyset af resultaterne
fra denne kommende totale emissionsm˚aling for at f˚a afklaret, hvorvidt optimering af biocovers
eller af perkolatopsamlingssystemets tæthed med fordel kan igangsættes.

Introduction
Increasing amounts of waste are being generated within the European Union and Denmark is one
of the top ten countries producing the most waste per capita among member states (EEA, 2019).
Between 2010 and 2016, the total amount of waste (excluding major mineral wastes) increased
by 6% in the European Union and according to (Schrader-King and Liu, 2018), the global waste
generation is projected to increase signiﬁcantly over the next 30 years unless urgent action is taken.
In relation to this, solid waste management is a key discipline to properly adapt to increasing waste
quantities. Within solid waste management, the different methods are ranked using the “waste
hierarchy”, and according to this waste hierarchy, landﬁlling is the least preferred waste manage-
ment method and should therefore only be used if the waste is not suitable for any other treatment
(DEFRA, 2017). However, should the waste end up being landﬁlled anyways, it is important to
handle it in an environmentally friendly way (Raga et al., 2010). Thus, deposited organic or mixed
waste will under anaerobic conditions produce landﬁll gas which is composed of primarily methane
(CH4) and carbon dioxide (CO2) (Kjeldsen and Scheutz, 2019). The methane will be released into
the atmosphere, which is unwanted since methane is a 28 times more potent greenhouse gas than
carbon dioxide (EPA, 2020b). In addition, the latest research results published by Rochester Uni-
versity show that the amount of anthropogenic methane in the atmosphere is signiﬁcantly higher
than previously estimated (Hmiel et al., 2020). Thus, the results of this new study indicate that
anthropogenic methane emissions so far have been underestimated by about 38 to 58 teragrams
CH4/year, corresponding to an increase of 25-40% compared to previous estimates. This makes
it even more important to implement effective countermeasures for a major methane gas emission
source like landﬁlled waste. One of the options for mitigation of methane from landﬁlls is to in-
stall biocovers on top of the waste after a landﬁlling cell has been occupied (Kjeldsen and Scheutz,
2016). A biocover consists of a gravel layer serving as a gas distributor with a thick layer of com-
post on top. Thus, the active part of the biocover is made of a compost material typically created
from garden waste. Bacterial processes within the compost material will under aerobic conditions
oxidise methane into the less harmful greenhouse gas carbon dioxide (Kjeldsen and Scheutz, 2016).
Due to the promising properties of the biocover technology, and its cost-effective nature, the Danish
Environmental Protection Agency launched the Biocover Initiative in 2015, which has resulted in a
set of guidelines and protocols on how to establish high performance biocover systems at landﬁlls
in Denmark, including the landﬁll site at Audebo (Kjeldsen and Scheutz, 2016). In this study, it is
investigated how the biocover system at Audebo landﬁll in Denmark works and how efﬁcient it is.
1.1
Objectives
The overall objective of this study is to determine the performance and efﬁciency of the biocover
system installed at Audebo landﬁll. Furthermore, it is the intention to provide suggestions on
how to potentially improve the performance of this biocover system. To determine the biocover
system efﬁciency, a series of ﬁeld measurements have been conducted throughout six measure-
ment campaigns focusing on two selected biocovers. The measurement approach included surface
screenings recording the methane concentration in the ambient air just above the biocover sur-
face. These qualitative surface screenings were supplemented with ﬂux-chamber measurements
to quantify the methane and carbon dioxide emissions from the biocover. The methane oxidation
trend was evaluated as well. This was done by inserting gas probes, coupled with a gas analyser,
and thereby measuring gas concentrations inside the biocovers to determine at which height the
methane oxidation zone was present. This gas proﬁle method also showed how far the atmospheric

gasses such as oxygen and nitrogen were able to diffuse into the compost layer. In addition to the
above, raw gas composition measurements were also carried out inside deep leachate inspection
and collection wells. Finally, the data retrieved from the ﬁeld measurements were processed by
using the carbon mass balance method and the soil gas proﬁle principle to determine the methane
oxidation efﬁciency of the selected biocovers (Kjeldsen and Scheutz, 2019). Based on the outcome
of these efﬁciency evaluations, informed recommendations on how to improve the performance of
the biocover system, as well as suggestions for further work, were provided.

Literature Review
In this chapter, a literature review is presented.
2.1
Landﬁlling as a waste disposal method
When waste is generated there are several possibilities for handling it. According to the waste hi-
erarchy, proposed by (DEFRA, 2017) and shown in Figure 1, the most environmentally beneﬁcial
approach is to reuse and recycle the waste. This will ensure a lower usage of virgin materials. The
waste which cannot be recycled or reused should be burned as means of reducing the volume of
waste. Current technology allows waste to be incinerated in waste-to-energy plants where elec-
tricity as well as district heating can be produced. Furthermore, waste with high organic content
such as household waste can be used as feed stock for anaerobic digestion for biogas production.
The remaining part of the waste, which cannot be burned due to either its caloriﬁc value or related
health issues from combustion, can then be landﬁlled.
Figure 1: Waste hierarchy (DEFRA, 2017).
2.1.1
Landﬁll gas generation
Landﬁlled waste containing organic material will under anaerobic conditions undergo anaerobic
microbial degradation generating landﬁll gas (LFG) typically composed of 55–60% methane (CH4)
and 40–45% carbon dioxide (CO2) (He et al., 2012). Additionally, the LFG contains numerous
tracer gases, where nitrous oxide (N2O) and volatile organic compounds (VOCs) such as halo-
genated hydrocarbons and aromatics are the most predominate (Scheutz et al., 2009a). The two
main components of LFG are both classiﬁed as greenhouse gasses. Despite methane having a short
lifetime in the atmosphere, its global warming potential (GWP) is estimated to be 28 times higher
than carbon dioxide based on a 100-year time horizon (M´enard et al., 2012). The higher GWP100
of methane is attributed to its strong ability to adsorbing inferred radiation (IPCC, 2014). Further-
more, methane emission from landﬁlls is one of the major anthropogenic sources of atmospheric
methane. More speciﬁcally, waste disposal contributes with 20% of the anthropogenic methane
4
==PAGE:16==
emission in Europe, making it the second largest methane emission source according to the Euro-
pean Environment Agency (EEA, 2016).
Figure 2 presents the different phases of landﬁll gas generation from active landﬁll (Phase I) to
closed landﬁll (Phase V). The ﬁrst three phases last several months while phase IV spans over
multiple decades. In Phase I, the degradation process begins with aerobic bacteria breaking down
organic compounds. The yield of aerobic metabolism (respiration) is primarily carbon dioxide,
water, and heat. Respiration is an exothermic reaction meaning that the temperature gradually in-
creases within the landﬁll cell due to microbial activity (Muller, 1977). Oxygen (O2) is consumed
in Phase II by continuous aerobic metabolism until it is depleted. This leads to anaerobic condi-
tions meaning that anaerobic bacteria develop and the methane production (methanogenesis) begins
(EPA, 2010).
In Phase III, anaerobic conditions are established, free nitrogen (N2) becomes depleted, and the
anaerobic bacteria release enzymes, which begin hydrolysing large complex organic compounds
into monomers; amino acids and long chain fatty acids (Hagos et al., 2017). With access to easily
degradable organic compounds, the anaerobic bacteria undergo fermentation (acidogenesis) where
volatile fatty acids (VFA) are produced as a metabolite (Angelidaki et al., 2019). Phase IV is
the methanogenic phase where methane is produced as the ﬁnal product. The produced VOC are
further metabolised (acetogenesis) into one of two pathways: Acetic acid or hydrogen and carbon
dioxide (Weiland, 2010). The last step is called methanogenesis where bacteria consume acetic acid
and produce methane and carbon dioxide. Another pathway involves bacteria which metabolite
hydrogen with carbon dioxide to form methane and water (Zheng et al., 2014). As mentioned
above, Phase IV lasts the longest and as methane concentrations deplete within the landﬁll via
diffusion into the atmosphere, via collection wells or via oxidation in biocovers, the methane- and
carbon concentrations diminish, and nitrogen begins to rediffuse back into the landﬁll. In Phase V,
the landﬁll thereby slowly transitions back into its original state after all biodegradable substrate
have been consumed. Hence, the gas compositions slowly migrate back to atmospheric conditions
(EPA, 2010).
Figure 2: Landﬁll gas composition. The red curve denotes free nitrogen, the green curve denotes
carbon dioxide, the dotted black curve denotes oxygen, the dotted blue curve denotes methane, and
the dotted green curve indicates degree of settlement (EPA, 2010).

2.1.2
Types of landﬁll
Over time, use of landﬁlling as a waste disposal method has undergone signiﬁcant changes in terms
of waste acceptance and waste management. The ﬁrst landﬁll sites were open dumps where aban-
doned gravel- or clay pits were ﬁlled up with all types of waste including industrial, toxic, and
hazardous waste. These sites had problems regarding smells, rodents, and local ﬁres (Raga et al.,
2010). In the light of this, the sanitary landﬁll emerged as an improvement to the open dump
method. The landﬁll was sealed off with fences limiting access to the site. Additionally, the fences
would limit the migration of waste from the site to the surroundings. Furthermore, the sanitary
landﬁll introduced soil covering of the disposed waste for better concealment. Despite its improve-
ments compared to open dumps, issues regarding gas emission and leachate generation were not
in focus. Thus, lack of leachate handling leads to pollution of groundwater and local bodies of
water (Raga et al., 2010). As cases of ground- and surface water pollution accumulated due to
leachate leaking from sanitary landﬁlls, a new landﬁlling technology, called the controlled landﬁll,
emerged to improve on the previous iteration. This landﬁlling technology offered both gas han-
dling and utilisation, as well as leachate treatment and collection. Introduction of liners, treatment
facilities and collection systems allowed for leachate and gas control. Gas handling was originally
done by ﬂaring of the gas to mitigate the greenhouse effect of methane. However, with time, gas
utilisation for energy purposes emerged as a better alternative, although more costly (Raga et al.,
2010). Leachate re-circulation was also introduced to enhance gas generation making utilisation
more economic. Additionally, leachate re-circulation lead to waste stabilising by increasing the
moisture content available for the microorganisms. Later, experience showed that gas collection
was often not complete, so to ensure efﬁcient gas collection, engineered landﬁll covers were added
(Raga et al., 2010).
Two different types of controlled landﬁlling technologies emerged: ”The dry tomb” and ”bioreac-
tor landﬁlls”. The concept of the dry tomb method was to completely cover and seal off the landﬁll
to prevent water intrusion into the waste, thus minimising leachate generation. In addition, full
collection of landﬁll gas was intended for the tomb. However, the lack of leachate resulted in low
degradation rates of deposited organic waste combined with low stabilisation of organic material
resulting in a low gas generation. As leaching was minimised, it also led to conservation of the
deposited waste meaning that the time perspective for this landﬁlling technology was potentially
eternal (Raga et al., 2010). An opposite approach was the bioreactor approach where gas produc-
tion was encouraged. Thus, waste with high organic content, such as municipal solid waste, was
6
==PAGE:18==
deposited in the landﬁll. In this way, organic matter would undergo anaerobic oxidation and biogas
would be produced and later harvested via gas collection systems. Furthermore, increased degrada-
tion and stabilisation of the waste were ensured by adding both liquids and air to maintain optimal
microbial growth conditions (EPA, 2020a). Thus, leachate and oxygen were added to enhance
bacterial breakdown and to enhance gas production.
Modern, controlled landﬁll sites utilise both gas extraction and a leachate collection and removal
system (LCRS). Furthermore, engineered landﬁll sites are constructed on geologically imperme-
able materials such as clay due to their ability to limit advective transport, as they represent a low
hydraulic conductivity (Edil, 2003). Additionally, landﬁll sites are constructed using composite
bottom liners, which consist of a geo-membrane (polymer) like high density polyethylene overlain
a compacted clay bottom liner (Edil, 2003). The composite bottom liner system is built to avoid
or reduce leachate intrusion into groundwater. This very low hydraulic conductivity of the bottom
liner system controls the movement of the leachate out of the landﬁll, because the leachate move-
ment is described by Fick’s law of diffusion (Raga et al., 2010). On top of the bottom liner system,
the leachate collection and removal system are installed. The LCRS consists of a drainage layer
that ensures easy horizontal leachate drainage by means of gravitational collection or pumping (Sil
and Kumar, 2017). As mentioned, leachate re-circulation helps stabilise and decompose the waste
over time, but the constituents dissolved in the leachate are toxic to the environment and should
be properly treated before being emitted back into the environment (Raga et al., 2010). Moreover,
minerals, salts and hydrolysed organic matter are dissolved in the leachate. Finally, leachate also
contains a broad spectrum of volatile organic compounds (VOCs) in addition to inorganic com-
pounds such as heavy metals and nitrogen compounds like ammonia and ammonium. The nitrogen
species make the leachate reach alkaline pH values (Edil, 2003).
Energy recovery can be beneﬁcial if a landﬁll produces sufﬁcient amounts of gas. The energy
content of the LFG (methane) can be utilised as fuel in a gas engine producing electricity (Raga
et al., 2010). This methane mitigation technique typically requires that the LFG is collected actively
via pumps coupled to gas extraction wells reaching into the waste layer. Such methods for gas
execration are discussed further in Section 2.1.4.
2.1.3
Landﬁlls in Denmark
Landﬁlls in Denmark are typically mineral waste landﬁlls, which accept primarily non-organic
materials like construction and building material, incineration ashes, metal, and other inorganic
waste (ARGO, 2020). Active landﬁlls in Denmark are controlled engineered landﬁlls with bot-
tom composite liners composed of low permeability soils like clay and high-density polyethylene.
Additionally, gas and leachate collection systems are installed to recirculate and treat leachate and
to deal with methane emissions (Raga et al., 2010). The most common mitigation technique is
landﬁll gas utilisation. If LFG utilisation is not found to be economically feasible, alternatives like
biocovers or ﬂares are typically installed to ensure proper mitigation of methane.
The ﬁrst Danish nationwide waste management plans were made in 1992 with targets deployed for
recycling, incineration, and landﬁlling. From 1992 to 2012, recycling has increased from 35% to
69%. In the same period, landﬁlling has decreased from 39% to 7% as a result of the recycling. An
underlying reason for the decreased landﬁll activity, was the ban of landﬁlling combustible waste
which came into effect in 1997 (Miljoestyrelsen, 2013). The Danish Environmental Protection
Agency (EPA) reported that landﬁlling of waste accounted for 4% in 2015 (Miljoestyrelsen, 2017b).
7
==PAGE:19==
Recently, the Danish EPA granted 178 million DKK in funding for establishing biocover solutions
at Danish landﬁlls as a methane mitigation initiative towards 2020 (Miljoestyrelsen, 2020). In 2016,
there were 14 landﬁlls with gas extraction installed in Denmark. Only nine landﬁll sites utilised
the LFG for energy purposes, whereas the remaining landﬁll sites ﬂared off the LFG. The Danish
government funding aims at providing a total of 100 biocover installations by 2020, distributed
across 40 active landﬁlls as well as closed off landﬁlls where gas emissions are still signiﬁcant
(Affaldsforening, 2016).
2.1.4
Gas extraction technology
Gas extraction is the most common methane emission mitigation technique. However, gas extrac-
tion for energy utilisation is only viable when landﬁlling large amounts of organic material, since
only organic material can be converted into methane, which is harvested. Gas extraction systems
at landﬁlls use vertical wells or horizontal collectors to collect the produced methane. The har-
vested methane can for instance be utilised as fuel in a combustion engine to produce electricity
(Rakopoulos and Kyritsis, 2001). Intensive ﬁeld studies of the methane mass balance at a French
landﬁll by (Spokas et al., 2006) showed how effective gas extraction was established using an en-
gineered landﬁll with low-permeability bottom liner and low-permeability cover. This achieved
methane recovery efﬁciencies of more than 90%, even up to 97% (Spokas et al., 2006), (Metz
et al., 2007), (Scheutz et al., 2009a). If gas extraction is not found to be economically advanta-
geous, due to low methane production at the site, ﬂaring can be an alternative for treating methane
that otherwise would escape to the atmosphere. Flaring of the LFG is a mitigation method where
the methane is thermally oxidised into carbon dioxide via a standard combustion reaction (Scheutz
et al., 2009a). However, ﬂaring of LFG poses a risk of producing toxic combustion products, which
can be emitted to the atmosphere (Scheutz et al., 2017). In the light of this, the biocover technology
is optimal when dealing with landﬁll cells containing low amounts of organic matter resulting in
relatively low methane gas production.
2.1.5
Biocover technology
Installation of a biocover system is an alternative methane mitigation option if gas collection is not
viable due to low gas production (Kjeldsen and Scheutz, 2016). In addition, biocover technology
is considered among the most efﬁcient and low-cost mitigation approaches for methane emissions
(IPCC, 2014), (Cassini et al., 2017). The core principle of the biocover technology is the microbial
oxidation process of methane (Kjeldsen and Scheutz, 2016). Thus, the produced carbon dioxide
from the oxidation process is considered to be biogenic (Kjeldsen and Scheutz, 2016). In this way,
the biocover technology can mitigate the methane emission and thereby reduce the environmental
impact from the landﬁll (Pedersen et al., 2012). As shown in Table 3, there are several different
types of biocover systems: Full surface biocover, biowindow system, bioﬁlter passive open bed,
bioﬁlter passive closed bed, bioﬁlter active open bed, bioactive intercepting trench, and systems
which combine the above types (Kjeldsen and Scheutz, 2016). Figure 3(A) shows a full surface
biocover where the cover functions as a vast bioﬁlter covering the entire landﬁll cell area. In ad-

In general, the biocover is designed to optimise the microbial methane consumption and thereby
to mitigate the methane emission from the landﬁll as much as possible. The biocover consist of
two layers as illustrated in Figure 4. The bottom layer is a so-called ”gas distribution layer” (GDL)
composed of gravel which possesses a high gas permeability (Lakhouit et al., 2014). The GDL is
placed on top of the waste as illustrated in Figure 4. The purpose of this layer is to homogenise

LFG ﬂuxes and distribute the LFG load evenly for the ”methane oxidation layer” (MOL) placed
on top of the GDL. An even gas distribution is a prerequisite to ensure an effective operation
without the formation of hot spots creating high methane emissions at the surface (Cassini et al.,
2017). The MOL is often constructed from mature compost made from garden waste, sludge, food
waste or straw (Kjeldsen and Scheutz, 2019). The methanotrophic bacteria live within the MOL
and ensure the oxidation of the methane via their metabolism, which is explained in Section 2.2.
Several features make compost a viable medium for methane oxidation. It possesses a high gas
permeability, a water retention capacity, low thermal conductivity, large available surface area and
high microbial activity (Pedersen et al., 2012). A common trait for all materials with high oxidation
capacity used in biocover systems is their porous and coarse structure, which facilitates optimal gas
transport of both methane and oxygen. Additionally, materials that are rich in organic matter tend
to have a high oxidation capacity as well (Scheutz et al., 2009a).

In 2010, (Cabral et al., 2010) compiled a list of materials used in methane oxidation experiments
from 1990 to 2006. This material list was further expanded by (Wei et al., 2016) and it includes:
Compost, paddy soil, sand, activated carbon, earthworm cast and stabilised waste. To achieve opti-
mal oxidation conditions, only mature compost should be used. Maturing the compost over a min-

material itself. Compost maturity is important as it ensures minimum competition for the available
oxygen. The compost contains bacteria which will undergo respiration as their metabolism. These
compost bacteria will compete for the available oxygen with the methane oxidising bacteria, which
could decrease the effectiveness of the microbial oxidation process (Scheutz et al., 2009a). An
immature compost can lead to anaerobic conditions within the lower part of the MOL if oxygen is
used up by the compost bacteria for respiration. This results in less methane being oxidised, as the
reaction is strictly aerobic (Pedersen et al., 2012), (Thauer and Shima, 2008).
Additionally, the use of compost-based materials proves advantageous in regions with cold climate
during wintertime. Thus, elevated temperatures, relative to ambient temperatures, are observed
in the MOL since the methane oxidation reaction is highly exothermic. More speciﬁcally, the
enthalpy of formation at standard temperature and pressure (STP) is ∆=-802.3 kJ/mol (Stutz and
Poulikakos, 2005). The heating released from the process can maintain optimal growth conditions
in terms of temperature. This results in high oxidation rates even during cold periods due to the
formation of heat (Kjeldsen and Scheutz, 2019). However, cost is one of the disadvantages of a full
surface biocover due to the large area it has to cover (Wei et al., 2016). Moreover, the compost will

up in the GDL, which disrupts the LFG distribution (Kjeldsen and Scheutz, 2016). Furthermore,

2.2
The microbial methane oxidation process
Methanotrophic bacteria (methanotrophs) are a subset of a physiological group of bacteria known
as methanotrophs. Methanotrophic bacteria have the ability to utilise methane as their carbon and
energy source (Hanson and Hanson, 1996). Methylotrophic bacteria are those aerobic bacteria that
utilise single-carbon compounds, which is a physiological trait of the methylotrophs (Megonigal
et al., 2013), (Hanson and Hanson, 1996). The enzymatic canalisation of methane to methanol us-
ing the enzyme “methane monooxygenase” (MMO) is a speciﬁc characteristic of these organisms
(Dedysh and Dunﬁeld, 2011). Furthermore, methane oxidation can happen either via assimilation
or disassimilation. The disassimilatory pathway fully oxidises methane to carbon dioxide yielding
”adenosine triphosphate” (ATP) which is cellular energy. The produced carbon dioxide is released
to the surroundings and no cellular material or biomass is produced. See Figure 6 for an illustration
of this process. On the other hand, the assimilation pathway will oxidise the carbon from formalde-
hyde and convert the carbon into biomass (Mancinelli, 1995). Methanotrophic bacteria possess the
ability to do both. However, it should be noted that in both pathways, it is implied that methane is
oxidised to methanol, which is further oxidised to formaldehyde, as shown in Figure 6.
Figure 6: The pathway for the oxidation of methane and assimilation of formaldehyde by Methan-
otrophs (Hanson and Hanson, 1996).
In addition to the above, formaldehyde can be oxidised further to formate by enzymatic catalysis.
The formaldehyde is used as a reduction power in the electron transport chain for adenosine triphos-
phate (ATP) production (Zahn et al., 2001), (Mancinelli, 1995). The ﬁnal oxidation step carried out
by the methanotrophs is the oxidation of formate to carbon dioxide. Thus, the produced carbon
dioxide can be released to the surroundings and this process is called disassimilation. Furthermore,
the carbon can otherwise be biosynthesized into biomass (Mancinelli, 1995). As illustrated in Fig-
ure 6, this constitutes the complete microbial oxidation reaction of methane into carbon dioxide,
assuming that the formaldehyde is fully oxidised instead of being assimilated. Alternatively, the
12
==PAGE:24==
bacteria can assimilate carbon from the formaldehyde into biomass. In the literature, and as shown
in Figure 6, these reaction pathways are divided into two pathways according to (Mancinelli, 1995):
Type I methanotrophs which follows the ribulose monophosphate (RuMP) pathway, and Type II
following the serine pathway. There is also a third type, which according to (Hanson and Hanson,
1996) is nominated Type X. This type of methanotrophs assimilate the formaldehyde through the
same pathway as Type I, but differs in that Type X possesses similar enzymes (ribulosebisphos-
phate carboxylase) which are present in the serine pathway in the same way as in the Type II case
(Hanson and Hanson, 1996), (Bowman, 2006). The RuMP pathway has the highest temperature
optimum of the two pathways. In the following, focus will only be on Type I and Type II including
their different modes of operation.
All methanotroph bacteria possess the enzyme MMO (Mancinelli, 1995). Both Type I and II have
the ability to create a particulate (membrane bound) form of MMO, named pMMO, when growing
in the presence of copper (Scheutz et al., 2009a). The way Type I and II start to differ is by Type
II’s ability to synthesize a soluble state of the enzyme MMO, named sMMO. This can happen
when copper is the limiting factor for bacteria growth. An advantage of Type II methanotrophs
assimilation is that the enzyme (sMMO) is less substrate speciﬁc compared to pMMO, meaning that
Type II can oxidise chlorinated hydrocarbons and aromatic hydrocarbons (Kjeldsen and Scheutz,
2016). Furthermore, only Type II is able to directly ﬁxate free nitrogen from air via enzymes
called nitrogenase. The RuMP pathway (Type I and X methanotrophs) is the most energy efﬁcient
assimilation pathway. Thus, a single ATP molecule is used in the formation of one glyceraldehyde-
3-P molecule from three formaldehyde molecules. The serine pathway (Type II methanotrophs)
uses three ATP molecules to assimilate two formaldehydes and a single carbon monoxide (CO)
molecule (Hanson and Hanson, 1996). Therefore, the Type II methanotrophs assimilation pathway
needs more cellular energy to produce biomass. In addition, the Type I methanotrophs have shown
to be dominant in the upper part of the soil layer since these prefer lower methane concentration
alongside higher concentrations of dissolved oxygen to thrive (Hanson and Hanson, 1996). The
diffusion of atmospheric oxygen, and thereby the concentration of oxygen, is higher in the top
part of the soil. In relation to this, the limiting factor for Type I methanotrophs is the accessibility
of methane. On the other hand, the Type II methanotrophs have better living conditions when
the methane concentration is high, and the dissolved oxygen concentration is lower. This is the
case for the lower layers of the soil/biocover. Here, the diffusion of atmospheric oxygen is lower,
and therefore, the methane concentration becomes higher. Moreover, a limiting factor for Type II
bacteria is the higher concentrations of oxygen, which is why they are mostly present in the bottom
of the biocover.
As shown in Figure 7, the aerobic microbial oxidation of methane occurs in the biocover wherever
methane and oxygen are present simultaneously. Such conditions arise when diffusive oxygen from
the atmosphere encounters the methane which is produced in the waste and transported upwards
through the biocover. Thus, the oxygen from the air and methane from the waste meet as counter
gradients as illustrated in Figure 7 (Kjeldsen and Scheutz, 2019).

otrophs (Scheutz et al., 2009a). The oxidation process with its intermediates have been explained
above, but the overall aerobic microbial oxidation reaction described in the literature is as given in
Equation (2) (Kjeld et al., 2016), (Fjelsted et al., 2020), (Thauer and Shima, 2008).

The majority of the methanotrophic bacteria is obligate methanotrophs and strict aerobes (Hanson
and Hanson, 1996). This dictates that the methane oxidation reaction is conﬁned to narrow hor-
izontal bands in the upper layer of the biocover, since the downwards diffusive concentration of
oxygen decreases with increased depth (Kjeldsen and Scheutz, 2016). This is illustrated in Figure
7 where the upward diffusive ﬂux of methane is within an anaerobic environment where the oxygen
concentration is below 5% vol. Thus, minimal methane oxidation takes place up until the conﬁned
horizontal oxidation zone where both oxygen and methane are in the stoichiometric amounts. The
most active part of the methane oxidation zone is typically located between 15-20 cm under the
soil surface. Thus, the methanotrophs meet the optimal conditions regarding oxygen and methane
concentrations at this depth. However, the oxidation zone can also move further down to 40 cm or
deeper (Scheutz and Kjeldsen, 2004).
As mentioned above, the two types of methanotrophs, Type I and II, have different conditions in
terms of optimal growth. The Type I methanotrophs thrives in the upper part of the biocover where
the environment has a lower methane concentration and a higher oxygen concentration. On the
other hand, Type II will be dominant in the lower part of the biocover since these prefer a higher
and constant ﬂow of methane in combination with lower oxygen concentrations (Scheutz et al.,
2009a).
Methanotrophs are obligate aerobes meaning that oxygen is required to promote methane oxidation
(Hanson and Hanson, 1996). Methanotrophs, among many other strains of bacteria, are able to
produce exopolymeric substances (EPS). EPS are high molecular weight compounds that consist
14
==PAGE:26==
primarily of polysaccharides, and their main function is to adhere to soil surfaces as an anchorage
(Scheutz et al., 2009b). Observations made by laboratory column experiments with prolonged gas
exposure resulted in accumulation of EPS (Wilshusen et al., 2004), (Scheutz et al., 2009b). This
caused an efﬁciency decline with regards to methane oxidation (M´enard et al., 2012). Thus, it
was suggested that this decline was caused by clogging of soil pores causing LFG restriction or
reduction in oxygen diffusion (Wilshusen et al., 2004). Due to EPS adsorptive characteristics, EPS
can create a bioﬁlm which acts as a barrier for ions and promote accumulation of nutrients (Fletcher,
1992). Finally, a study by (Hilger et al., 2000) suggested that EPS production could inﬂuence the
methane oxidation rate due to constrains in the oxygen diffusion through the bioﬁlm.
2.2.1
Degradation of trace gasses
As mentioned earlier, biogas produced from landﬁlls consists primarily of methane and carbon
dioxide. Apart from the two main gas components, water, hydrogen sulphide, hydrogen alongside
various non-methane organic compounds (NMOCs) are also produced. The NMOCs cover more
than 200 species which were identiﬁed in biogas from different landﬁlls (M´enard et al., 2012).
Thus, the NMOCs species were halogenated hydrocarbons and aromatics, many of which were
toxic and/or contributed to climate change by depletion of the ozone layer (Kjeldsen and Scheutz,
2019). The NMOCs were either formed as intermediates from biochemical reactions associated
with degradation processes, or from the degradation and volatilization of other organic wastes
disposed in landﬁlls (M´enard et al., 2012). Furthermore, chlorinated compounds have a lower
biodegradability in landﬁlls. Thus, some types of chlorinated compounds (TCE, VC, and HCFCs)
have been found to be inhibitors of methane oxidation by means of inhibiting the sMMO enzyme
(M´enard et al., 2012). However, laboratory column studies simulating landﬁll cover conditions
have shown that several halogenated organic compounds could be dechlorinated under anaerobic
conditions (Kjeldsen and Scheutz, 2019). The anaerobically formed degradation products could
moreover be removed in the aerobic part of the columns. This observation is depicted in Figure 7.
In relation to this, the researchers hypothesized that degradation processes within landﬁll covers,
both aerobic and anaerobic, could have a signiﬁcant effect on gaseous emissions of NMOCs from
landﬁlls (Kjeldsen and Scheutz, 2019). In addition, (Scheutz et al., 2009b) later demonstrated a
similar ability of compost materials to degrade halogenated hydrocarbons.

2.2.3
Environmental impact on methane oxidation in soil and biocovers
The factors impacting the microbial oxidation process are numerous. These factors include: Tem-
perature, pH value, relative humidity, inorganic nitrogen (ammonium and ammonia) content, methane
and oxygen concentrations, soil texture, precipitation and barometric pressure (Hanson and Han-
son, 1996), (Scheutz et al., 2009a). A selection of the most important factors is described be-
low. Temperature has an immense impact on all the biological processes since the enzymes which
catalyse the different reactions in the methane oxidation have an optimum reaction rate at a spe-
ciﬁc temperature (Shukla and Varma, 2011). Hence, (Hanson and Hanson, 1996) found that most
methanotrophs are mesophiles meaning they have optimum temperatures around 25–35 °C in terms
of methane oxidation in soil environments. Moreover, a study by (B¨orjesson et al., 2004) showed
that all bacteria in low temperature environments belong to the Type I methanotrophs. This could
indicate that temperature display a selecting effect determining which of the two main types of
methanotrophs will be predominate based on optimum growth conditions. Thus, Type I has a lower
temperature optimum compared to Type II. Furthermore, the methane oxidation might be signif-
icantly reduced or even come to a standstill if exposed to cold temperatures which drops below
5–10 °C (Scheutz et al., 2009a). On the other hand, (Christophersen and Kjeldsen, 2001) found
that the methanotrophs are still able to oxidise methane around freezing temperatures (1-2 °C). In
the light of this, heat generation inside compost-based biocovers, coming from methane oxidation
as well as aerobic respiration of the compost material, can be important sources of heat. Thus, this
added heat can create advantageous mesophilic conditions for the methanotrophs even during the
winter season (Kjeldsen and Scheutz, 2016). In the literature, Q10 describes the number of times
the oxidation rate increases when the given temperature increases 10 °C (Wang et al., 2011). Within
the temperature interval of 10 to 30 °C, the Q10 response has been observed to exhibit exponen-
tial growth according to (Christophersen and Kjeldsen, 2001), meaning a small deviation from the
16
==PAGE:28==
optimal temperature can have dire effects on the methane oxidation.
The optimum pH values for methanotrophic growth, and thus methane oxidation, in soils are gener-
ally between 5.5 and 8.5 (Semrau et al., 2010). While pure cultures of methanotrophic bacteria lie
between pH 6.6-6.8 (Hanson and Hanson, 1996), the pH-value in the landﬁll cover depends on the
material used. However, given the wide pH-range of methanotrophs and their capability to adapt to
environmental conditions, pH limitations are less likely according to (Scheutz et al., 2009a).
Relative humidity (moisture) is an essential factor for bacteria to sustain their activity since water
act as the transport medium for nutrient supply to the bacteria as well as transport medium for
removal of residual metabolic compounds (Kjeldsen and Scheutz, 2016). However, if too much
moisture is accumulating within the soil or biocover material, it will slow down the gaseous trans-
port processes in the soil since molecular diffusion in water is about 104 times slower than in the
air (Cabral et al., 2004). Additionally, if the soil’s degree of saturation reaches 85%, the air-ﬁlled
voids are no longer interconnected and the gases have to diffuse into the liquid phase (Cabral
et al., 2004). This drastically reduces the availability of methane and oxygen which is limiting the
methane oxidation. In contrast, a decrease in moisture content below 5% will reduce the oxidation
rates signiﬁcantly due to microbial water stress resulting from desiccation (Scheutz and Kjeldsen,
2004). A consequence of low moisture content, typically occurring during dry seasons, is cracks
ﬁssures in the landﬁll cover soils due to desiccation of the topsoil. Such imperfections in the cover
soil surface can lead to methane emission vents called hot spots (Scheutz et al., 2009a). The op-
posite case, where soil is saturated with water, can lead to increased emissions from lateral gas
transport. Lateral gas emissions happen adjacent to the landﬁll if the vertical permeability of the
topsoil is too saturated with water. Thus, heavy rain can occupy the pore space of the upper soil
layer thereby decreasing the vertical gas permeability (Scheutz et al., 2009a). Additionally, an at-
mospheric pressure building up can create the driving force for advective transport through the soil
(Kjeldsen and Fischer, 1995).
Methanotrophic bacteria need sufﬁcient amounts of nutrients for optimal growth. Thus, methanogens
have a high demand for nitrogen during assimilation. More speciﬁcally, for every mole of as-
similated carbon, 0.25 mole of nitrogen is required (Scheutz et al., 2009a). The bacteria utilise
inorganic nitrogen species such as ammonium (NH+
4 ) and nitrate (NO−
3 ). Inorganic nitrogen can
both stimulate and inhibit methane oxidation in soils. It all depends on the species of nitrogen
and its concentration, as well as methane concentrations, pH, and type of methanotrophs (Kjeldsen
and Scheutz, 2016). Several studies have according to (Scheutz et al., 2009a) shown that higher
ammonium concentrations in soils tend to inhibit methane oxidation, since ammonium acts as a
competitive inhibitor towards MMO enzymes. This means that ammonium binds to the active site
in the MMO enzyme as methane, occupying the spot (He et al., 2012). Furthermore, ammonia has
proven to be inhibitory through osmotic effects, but only at high concentrations that are generally
not found in landﬁll environments (Bodelier and Laanbroek, 2004). Finally, lower concentrations
of nitrogen stimulate the methanotrophs and act as a nutrient rather than as an inhibitor at higher
concentrations (Wang et al., 2011).
As illustrated in Figure 7, landﬁll gas generation creates both pressure- and concentration gra-
dients. These gradients will lead to advective- and diffusive gas transport, respectively (Gebert
and Groengroeft, 2006a), (Gebert and Groengroeft, 2006b), (Christophersen and Kjeldsen, 2001).
The pressure gradients arise from the continuously ongoing gas production within the deposited
waste passing through the biocover and into the atmosphere. According to (Christophersen and
Kjeldsen, 2001), the diffusion gradient will always be present since the concentration of methane
17
==PAGE:29==
in the atmospheric air is very low. Furthermore, (Czepiel et al., 2003) observed a signiﬁcant in-
verse relationship between LFG emissions and atmospheric pressure. This study showed that the
LFG emission is heavily inﬂuenced by the atmospheric pressure dynamics. The same tendency
was found by (Gebert and Groengroeft, 2006a), (Gebert and Groengroeft, 2006b). They concluded
that high atmospheric pressure results in a decrease of LFG emission. The higher pressure above
the biocover is forcing the atmospheric air into the landﬁll and the LFG within the biocover has a
harder time escaping against this gradient. On the other hand, the opposite is the case for decreas-
ing atmospheric pressure where the LFG can escape more easily. To sum up, LFG emissions are
sensitive with regards to changes in atmospheric pressure.
2.2.4
Methane oxidation kinetics
The methane oxidation rate, commonly quantiﬁed as g CH4/m2·d, is deﬁned as the difference in
methane ﬂux from the waste and methane ﬂux from the top of the biocover. The methane oxidation
efﬁciency is the vertical change in methane ﬂux across the biocover given as a percentage. Fur-
thermore, the oxidation efﬁciency is, according to (Chanton et al., 2011), an inverse function of the
methane emission. Their ﬁeld study showed that for lower oxidation rates, the methanotrophs con-
sume the vast majority of the methane they were exposed to. On the other hand, at higher oxidation
rates, an increased supply of methane did not increase the oxidation rate. This could indicate that
an oxidation limit was reached. This limitation could be caused by oxygen depletion according to
(Chanton et al., 2011).
The methane oxidation rate has within the literature been described with Michaelis–Menten kinet-
ics. Thus, (Scheutz et al., 2009a), (Chanton et al., 2011), (Wang et al., 2011), (Chanton et al., 2011)
showed that the methane oxidation rate behaves in a way similar to enzyme kinetics where the re-
action rate increases in a linear manner at lower substrate levels and ﬂattens out at higher substrate
(methane) concentrations. Michaelis–Menten kinetics were originally developed for bio-catalytic
(enzyme) kinetics (Chanton et al., 2011). The model can describe the kinetics of the enzymes
released by the methanotrophic bacteria.

2.2.5
Gas proﬁle method (CH4/CO2 ratio)
The study by (Gebert et al., 2011) evaluated a method to estimate the methane oxidation efﬁciency
at different depths inside landﬁll covers, biowindows or bioﬁlters, by measuring the gas concen-
tration proﬁles of methane, carbon dioxide and oxygen. The samples were all collected from a
vertical gas proﬁle in the landﬁll cover. The gas concentrations and their ratios can be used to
estimate the depth of the methane oxidation zone and qualitatively assess the oxidation efﬁciency,
i.e., the accumulated oxidation efﬁciency. This has been conﬁrmed by an independent laboratory
column experiment by (Pratt et al., 2013) as well. The method assumes that a shift in the CH4/CO2
ratio in the gas proﬁle, compared to the ratio in the raw LFG, is a direct result of the oxidation
process. This enables the calculation of the cumulative share of methane oxidised up to a particular
depth (Kjeldsen and Scheutz, 2019). The method is independent of the nature of the ﬂux, whether
its diffusive or advective. Additionally, it assumes that the pore gas (methane and carbon dioxide)
is diluted equally in the soil pore volume meaning no gradient build up. Furthermore, an equal gas
dilution means that it is also independent of ingress of atmospheric air from the surface (Gebert
et al., 2011). This assumption was validated using mass balance data from batch and column stud-
ies by both (Gebert et al., 2011) and (Pratt et al., 2013). Furthermore, this approach is traditionally
a supporting method in combination with other measurements. With the ﬁnding of (Gebert et al.,
2011) and (Pratt et al., 2013), the method is considered as a stand-alone approach for estimating
microbial oxidation efﬁciencies in landﬁll top covers. Additionally, the method is inexpensive and
simple to carry out meaning that it is an attractive approach for ﬁeld use, especially in developing
countries (Pratt et al., 2013). To calculate the CH4/CO2 ratio, (Gebert et al., 2011) assume the
following:
• The oxidation reaction follows Equation (2), meaning that 1 mole of methane is converted
into 1 mole of carbon dioxide.
• The investigated landﬁll cover is established and is stable, meaning that the population size
of the methanotrophs is stable.
• The assimilation of carbon is in equilibrium with release of carbon due to cell decay. There
is no net transfer of carbon into biomass.

the aerobic respiration of biocovers plays a role here as carbon dioxide is a product of respi-

neglected.
• Carbon dioxide is in equilibrium within the water- and gas phase.
• Precipitation of carbon dioxide is negligible, despite the fact that carbon dioxide has a higher
water solubility (Pratt et al., 2013)
• The system has steady state gas ﬂow rates.

The proposed CH4/CO2 ratio method utilises the change in carbon mass balance of the soil gas
phase to quantify methane oxidation (Gebert et al., 2011). However, the CH4/CO2 ratio method
has some limitations which should be taken into account. The landﬁll cover might not be in steady
state during the sampling time. Furthermore, gas ﬂow rates vary with changing weather conditions
as mentioned previously such as atmospheric pressure and precipitation, meaning that steady state
is rarely obtained in the ﬁeld. Also, ﬂuctuations in atmospheric pressure in particular can lead to
over- or underestimation of the efﬁciency. However, reliable results from LFG covers can be ob-
tained during periods of stable weather conditions according to (Gebert et al., 2011). Furthermore,
microbial respiration, assimilation when carbon is ﬁxated into biomass during population growth
of the methanotrophs, and difference in solubility between methane and carbon dioxide are all
factors that affect the CH4/CO2 ratio. Microbial respiration and vegetation produce carbon dioxide
from respiration and photosynthesis, respectively. Both contribute to increased carbon dioxide con-
centrations within the landﬁll cover. This is of particular concern especially for covers with high
content of organic matter (Pratt et al., 2013). However, experiments done by (Gebert et al., 2011)

showed that respiration depends on the methane oxidation rate in soil covers. Thus, the share of

same authors also reported that biocovers, such as biowindows, are expected to have high enough
methane loading and oxidation rates, so that carbon dioxide generated from methane oxidation out-
weighs other sources of carbon dioxide (Gebert et al., 2011). During precipitation, more gaseous
carbon dioxide will also be dissolved in water percolating through the landﬁll cover compared to
methane, which can offset the CH4/CO2 ratio. Finally, carbon dioxide has a higher water solubility
compared to methane.
2.2.6
Methods for determining in-situ methane oxidation
The following paragraph will describe how the methane oxidation efﬁciency within biocovers can
be evaluated based on a different approach called the carbon mass balance method. Both methods
share many of the same initial assumptions and both methods rely on mass conservation of carbon
in the biocover. The ﬁrst method applies concentration ratios of the raw landﬁll gas and surface ﬂux
measurements, while the latter method uses gas concentration ratios based on gas concentrations
measured inside the cover to estimate the oxidation efﬁciency.
2.2.7
Methane oxidation efﬁciency using a carbon mass balance
Flux measurements at the biocover surface combined with biocover gas proﬁles can be used to
calculate an estimate of the methane oxidation efﬁciency according to (Christophersen and Kjeld-
sen, 2001). The methane oxidation efﬁciency can be determined by using a carbon mass balance
approach based on surface ﬂux measurements and measurements of methane and carbon dioxide
concentrations at the base of the landﬁll cover or inside the waste body. The mass balance approach
assumes that methane and carbon dioxide are the only two constituents of the LFG. Hence, carbon
contained in VOC’s is assumed to be negligible and is therefore not incorporated (Kjeldsen and
Scheutz, 2019).
The method is based on the conservation of mass for carbon (equations (6) and (7)), which assumes
the relationship between the ﬂuxes, and the concentrations, of methane and carbon dioxide is equal
in the bottom and top of the landﬁll cover (Equation (8)). Additionally, a uniform vertical gas ﬂow
is assumed at the sampling point (Scheutz et al., 2017), (Kjeldsen and Scheutz, 2019). Analogue
to the gas proﬁle method proposed by (Gebert et al., 2011), this method also assumes that steady
state conditions have been reached for the methanotrophic bacteria. This means that the bacterial
growth rate is equal to the decay rate and that assimilation of carbon is not accounted for (Kjeldsen
and Scheutz, 2019). The dilution of gaseous carbon dioxide in percolation rainwater is likewise
assumed to be negligible. This assumption is backed up by the ﬁndings of (Christophersen and
Kjeldsen, 2001). Unlike previous iterations of the model, (Christophersen and Kjeldsen, 2001),
(Scheutz et al., 2017), the model proposed by (Kjeldsen and Scheutz, 2019) takes carbon dioxide
from respiration into account. Thus, the respiration ﬂux is experimentally determined in the ﬁeld
and is implemented as an experimental constant. The load of methane into the biocover can be
calculated by combining equations (6), (7) and (8) which yields Equation (9). Furthermore, the
methane oxidation rate through the biocover can be determined by Equation (10). The difference

From that, the oxidation efﬁciency can be calculated, as shown in Equation (11). This is the vertical
change in methane ﬂux across the biocover given as a percentage.

The in-situ mass balance approach described above faces several challenges originating from its
previously stated assumptions. Steady state is assumed, but is rarely obtained in the ﬁeld, as
variations in LFG ﬂow rates occur with changing atmospheric pressure and precipitation. Thus,
the uncertainty of the steady state assumption could lead to over- or underestimation of the efﬁ-
ciency, depending on the weather conditions much like the gas proﬁle method. As mentioned, the
JCO2,respiration can be measured by laboratory experiments and therefore not directly in the ﬁeld
or in ﬁeld areas without methane loading for reference (Kjeldsen and Scheutz, 2019). Hence, top
covers with high organic content, such as compost based biocovers, can have signiﬁcant carbon
dioxide contributions. Carbon dioxide concentrations can be inﬂuenced due to additional processes
(excluding methane oxidation) which consume and produce carbon dioxide at the subsurface and
near subsurface. These processes include respiration, oxidation of organic matter and photosynthe-
sis (Kjeldsen and Scheutz, 2019). However, if the biocover possesses a spot with a zero methane
loading, the carbon dioxide ﬂux of that speciﬁc spot can be measured and that will provide an esti-
mate of the carbon dioxide respiration ﬂux of the particular biocover. Lastly, due to carbon dioxide
having a higher water solubility compared to methane, this can lead to carbon dioxide partitioning
to the moisture found in the top cover material. This means that less gaseous carbon dioxide is
measured which can skew the CH4/CO2 mass ratio and therefore can lead to underestimations.

This technique was used to prevent water accumulation due to capillary effects. Otherwise, a
capillary barrier could form at the compost and GDL interface. The capillary barrier could occur
due to the high permeability of the GDL and this could lead to accumulation of water within the
interface, and in these cases, anaerobic conditions could develop as a result of water accumulation
(Cassini et al., 2017). Covering the GDL was the 80 cm thick methane oxidation layer. This layer
was made of 6 months mature compost made of garden waste according to the guidelines provided
in (Olesen et al., 2018). Furthermore, the compost size distribution was between 15-25 mm, which
was ensured by means of serving as recommended by (Olesen et al., 2018).

4
Method
In this section, the methods and measurement approaches applied during the ﬁeld investigations are
described.
4.1
Objective of ﬁeld measurements
The objective of the ﬁeld measurements was to investigate the performance and efﬁciency of the
biocovers at Audebo landﬁll. More speciﬁcally, focus has been on evaluation of the methane oxida-
tion efﬁciency of two selected biocovers by means of both the carbon mass balance method and the
soil gas proﬁle principle previously presented in the literature review. Evaluation of the methane
oxidation efﬁciency from biocover gas proﬁles had according to Kjeldsen and Scheutz (2019) not
yet been applied in the ﬁeld. This meant that the present project would be able to provide new
knowledge on this, since it had so far only been validated using an experimental laboratory soil
column set-up.
4.2

and surface ﬂux-chamber measurements were carried out at ﬁve different locations on three dif-
ferent biocovers as shown in Figure 27. The results of these initial measurements can be found in
Appendix 5.

4.6
Surface ﬂux-chamber measurements
The ﬂux-chamber measurements were performed by inserting the ﬂux-chamber at a ﬁxed depth
(1-2 cm) into the biocover surface. A photo of the applied ﬂux-chamber can be seen on Figure 32.
After inserting the ﬂux-chamber, the concentration of gas inside the chamber was measured by use
of the INNOVA 1512i photoacoustic gas monitor. Prior to each measurement, the sampling tube
was ﬂushed by the INNOVA 1512i to ensure no interference of previous samples. More details
on this ﬂushing feature can be found in (AdvancedEnergy, 2020b). The total measurement time
for each spot was around 5 minutes and the resolution corresponded to an interval of 1 minute,
in accordance with the procedure described in (Pedersen et al., 2012). The concentration inside
the ﬂux-chamber increased, or decreased, linearly when either a positive or negative ﬂux from the
surface was present, and based on this, the gas emission could be calculated. Negative ﬂuxes will
be explained in Section 5.

monitor performs measurements by use of an advanced photoacoustic infrared detection method

free nitrogen, and oxygen. The detection limit depends on the gas type, but it is typically in the

in compensation for variation in temperature, pressure, water vapour and for other present gasses
(AdvancedEnergy, 2020b). The performance and accuracy of this instrument are given in Table 16.

Results of surface methane emission screening
The qualitative results of the surface methane emission screenings can be observed in Figure 37 and

measured were typically at the same level as the atmospheric methane background concentration

cating that no signiﬁcant hot spots existed on the surface of this biocover. Thus, methane gas leaks
previously detected by NIRAS A/S on November 1st, 2019 with magnitudes up to 17,000 ppm
(see Appendix 9) were no longer observed. This was in line with the fact that such hot spots were
mitigated after November 1st, 2019 according to NIRAS A/S. However, in general, the measure-
ments done by NIRAS A/S showed overall higher methane concentrations (typically 10-100 ppm)

compared with the measurement results of the present project ranging from 0-15 ppm. In relation
to this, it is important to note that the atmospheric pressure can cause signiﬁcant changes in the sur-

measurements took place during stable atmospheric pressure around 1.002 atm (DMI, 2020), while
the November 1st, 2019 measurements took place during decreasing atmospheric pressure (from

an example of this, four out of the nine biocovers at the Danish landﬁll Klintholm were basically

be in areas with high methane load, otherwise this will not contribute to an increase of the overall
methane removal efﬁciency. An expansion of the existing low performance biocovers or better gas
distribution in general could be alternative improvements. Furthermore, a potential improvement
could also be to transfer more LFG to the least loaded bioﬁlters, which in turn would require
additional gas pipe installations. Finally, increasing the thickness of the compost layer at low
oxidation areas, such as Bioﬁlter S2, could potentially increase the methane removal efﬁciency.

to methane emission hot spot areas. However, this presumes that sufﬁcient oxygen will be present
in the lower compost layers ensuring that the bioﬁlter does not become anaerobic.
5.7.1
Uncertainties and sources of errors
It is important to note that the applied methane removal efﬁciency estimation methods imply a num-
ber of uncertainties and sources of errors (Kjeldsen and Scheutz, 2019). As pointed out in Section
2.2.7, the carbon mass balance principle and soil gas proﬁle approach share similar assumptions.
Both methods assume steady state, which might not have been the case in the present project. Fur-
thermore, carbon dioxide is generated and consumed by multiple processes both inside and at the
surface of the biocover, beyond the methane oxidation process itself. This includes organic mat-
ter oxidation, photosynthesis, and soil respiration. In addition, the use of simple CH4/CO2 mass
ratios are uncertain since carbon dioxide tend to partition to soil moisture, as it is more soluble
than methane (Hemmaplardh and King, 1972). In reality, the carbon dioxide concentration might
thus be higher meaning that the methane oxidation is underestimated. Moreover, the use of gas
proﬁle probe nests and ﬂux-chambers only represents the methane oxidation in very small areas
(less than 1 m2) and during short time periods of sampling (less than 15 min). This means that
the full biocover area and longer time spans cannot be covered. In addition, the soil gas proﬁle
method is mainly applicable when the carbon dioxide concentrations are not signiﬁcantly impacted
by respiration, or when methane loading and oxidation rates are sufﬁciently high to exceed other
non-oxidation sources of carbon dioxide. According to Section 2.2.5, these assumptions may not
hold true for compost based biocovers with signiﬁcant respiration activities. Hence, the estimated
carbon dioxide respiration for the present project is 59% of the measured carbon dioxide ﬂux at the
surface of Biocover I2+IIa and 25% of the measured carbon dioxide ﬂux at the surface of Bioﬁlter
S2 (see Appendix 1 and Appendix 4). This means that the respiration activities are in fact signiﬁ-
cant, which increases the uncertainty of the soil gas proﬁle based efﬁciency calculations presented
in this report. In other words, if the carbon dioxide production due to compost respiration is in
fact higher than assessed in this study, the methane oxidation is overestimated. Finally, vegetation
74
==PAGE:86==
on the biocover surfaces caused uncertainties for the ﬂux-chamber measurements. The vegetation,
like straw, weed and bushes, had in some cases grown too thick. This meant that the ﬂux-chamber
was not able to be properly inserted into the compost layer in an airtight manner, resulting in under
estimation of ﬂuxes as the LFG could escape underneath.

Conclusion and further work
The performance of the biocover system at Audebo landﬁll has been investigated with focus on the
methane oxidation efﬁciency of selected biocovers. The investigations included six ﬁeld measure-
ment campaigns spanning across three months, as well as analysis of existing results of recent ﬁeld
measurements provided by NIRAS A/S and FORCE Technology. During the six measurement
campaigns, two selected biocovers were investigated by means of surface emission screenings,
ﬂux-chamber measurements, gas concentration proﬁle probes, and raw gas composition sampling.
Overall, it was found that the methane removal efﬁciency of the whole biocover system amounted
to approximately 60%, while individual biocovers showed efﬁciencies ranging between 49% and
99%. Methane and carbon dioxide surface ﬂuxes were found to be in the range of -0.2 to 507
g CH4/m2·d and 78 to 1,172 g CO2/m2·d, respectively. Regarding compost respiration, this was
estimated to a carbon dioxide ﬂux of 78 g/m2·d, corresponding to 25-59% of the carbon dioxide
ﬂux measured at the surface of the biocovers. This respiration was observed to be stable throughout
the three months of ﬁeld measurements. In addition, the average methane loads to the individual
biocovers were estimated to be between 17 and 110 g/m2·d, and the raw landﬁll gas had a CH4/CO2
ratio between 1.8 and 2.7.
Since the original objective of the Audebo biocover project was to achieve a methane removal
efﬁciency of 80%, this suggests that optimisation of certain biocovers, as well as addition of more
biocover area, could potentially help meeting the 80% removal target. In addition, the ﬁndings of
the six ﬁeld measurement campaigns revealed that the leachate collection system at Audebo landﬁll
may have a gas leakage issue, because signiﬁcant intrusion of atmospheric air was detected inside
certain inspection wells, showing gas compositions with 70%v/v free nitrogen and 18%v/v oxygen.
This means that methane leaks related to the leachate system may also contribute to unwanted
emissions. In terms of biocover optimisation, the results point to existence of hot spot areas on
certain bioﬁlters, which may occur due to heterogeneous gas distribution and thus an uneven gas
loading to the compost layer.
This implies that further investigation of the biocovers at Audebo landﬁll could be beneﬁcial, focus-
ing on identiﬁcation of hot spot areas exhibiting low methane oxidation efﬁciencies. An example
of this was Bioﬁlter S2 which showed an estimated methane oxidation efﬁciency of 49 ± 7%, sug-
gesting that this is not an optimally functioning bioﬁlter. On the other hand, an excellent methane
oxidation efﬁciency of 99 ± 2% was demonstrated by Biocover I2+IIa, pointing to the fact that
this biocover operated as intended, also during periods with increasing methane load caused by
decreasing atmospheric pressure. All in all, this suggests that it would be relevant to investigate
the remaining four biocovers in a similar way as it has been done in the present project for two
out of the six biocovers at Audebo landﬁll. It could also be relevant to carry out a long-term ﬁeld
measurement campaign covering seasonal variance as well as changes in atmospheric pressure,
precipitation and landﬁll gas ﬂow rates.
When it comes to biocover area, the ﬁndings of this report reveal that a total area of 2,625 m2 is
theoretically required to meet the 80% methane removal goal. Thus, the current biocover area of
1,949 m2 seems to be too small in the light of the validated average methane oxidation capacity of
64 g/m2·d. However, if additional biocover area were to be added, this should be located in places
with sufﬁcient methane load in order to obtain the wanted effect. As a supplement, the thickness
of the biocovers could also be increased in cases where low methane oxidation efﬁciencies are
detected, as long as the biocovers do not become anaerobic.
76
==PAGE:88==
Furthermore, it is important to note that a range of uncertainties and sources of errors were related
to the ﬁeld measurement methods as well as the efﬁciency calculations. This calls for additional
measurements involving longer time spans as well as more biocovers and higher numbers of mea-
suring points. In relation to this, it is worthwhile noting that a total methane emission measurement
of the whole Audebo landﬁll site is planned for September 2020. Hence, it would be ideal to plan
and select a new set of ﬁeld measurements on the individual biocovers based on the outcome of
this upcoming total emission measurement. Such a new set of measurements should also include
an investigation of the leachate collection- and transport system to clarify whether air intrusion is
still detected, and to ﬁnd out whether signiﬁcant methane leaks are present in this system or not.
Finally, it could be considered whether new innovative approaches to ﬁeld measurements would be
beneﬁcial. This includes potential application of drones equipped with infrared cameras (thermal
and methane sensitive), as well as lasers capable of measuring methane. Long-term ﬁeld investi-
gations may also be possible by use of IoT (Internet of Things) enabled low-cost and low-power
infrared sensors designed to monitor the concentration of various gases (like methane) for an ex-
tended period of time.

Comparison of methane gas screening measurement results from 2015
and 2019
A methane gas measurement campaign took place on November 1st, 2019 and was carried out by
the company NIRAS A/S (NIRAS, 2019b). The purpose of this campaign was to investigate the
performance of the biocovers which were installed at Audebo landﬁll in the period from June to
August 2019. As shown in Table 12, a laser technology based methane-speciﬁc gas leak detector
called ”Laser One” was used for the measurements (Huberg, 2020). Furthermore, the screening
measurements showed a maximum methane concentration of 72,000 ppm above background level.
Thus, some of the highest concentrations were measured at the following locations: 17,000 ppm
at Biocover I2+IIa, 72,000 ppm at Bioﬁlter S4 and 12,000, 42,000 and 70,000 ppm at Biowindow
IVb+IVa. These high concentrations were typically seen at wells, fence poles and storm stiffeners.
More details on this are available in Appendix 9. In addition, one example of these measurement
results is shown in Figure 63. This ﬁgure illustrates how three signiﬁcant hot spots are detected for
Biowindow IVb+IVa as mentioned above. As can be seen from Appendix 9, a conclusion on this set
of available measurement results has not yet been written down by NIRAS A/S. However, according
to (Project manager Jens Engelbreth Larsen, NIRAS A/S, personal communication, February 25,
2020), the measurements in Appendix 9 has been applied as guidance for improvement of the
biocovers at Audebo landﬁll. More speciﬁcally, the most signiﬁcant identiﬁed methane gas leaks
have afterwards been mitigated by means of various countermeasures such as addition of compost
at the identiﬁed hot spots. Thus, according to (Project manager Jens Engelbreth Larsen, NIRAS
A/S, personal communication, February 25, 2020), it is expected that as of today, the performance
of the biocovers are improved compared to the measurement results from November 1st 2019.

As can be seen from Table 29, signiﬁcant methane concentration reductions are observed for three
out of six biocover areas, namely IVb+IVa, I1 and S4. On the other hand, unexpected increases are
seen for two biocover areas, namely I2+IIa and Va. However, both these cases can be explained,

emissions which means that according to (NIRAS, 2015), an installation of a biocover for this area

that the biocover installations at Audebo have in fact had a positive effect on the methane gas
emissions. However, as pointed out in (Kjeldsen and Scheutz, 2016), it would be beneﬁcial to carry
out additional measurements, especially ﬂux-chamber measurements and trace gas measurements
to enable a quantitative assessment of the performance of the installed biocovers.
