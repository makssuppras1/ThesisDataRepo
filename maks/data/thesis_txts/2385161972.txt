Abstract 
The aim of this project was to define the optimum conditions of forward osmosis (FO) application 
in the food industry by testing the performance of different binary and ternary draw solutions. A 
simulation program called OLI Analyzer is used to investigate the osmotic pressures generated by 
the different mixtures and the promising candidates are further evaluated by experimentally 
measuring the achieved water flux and the observed reverse solute flux.  
It was found that MgCl2 has the highest osmotic pressure compared to CaCl2, NaCl, KCl, MgSO4, 
Na2SO4,  sucrose and fructose. MgCl2 and NaCl were chosen for further study because of their 
higher osmotic pressure and lower tendency to precipitate.  Sucrose and fructose were also chosen 
as possible candidates for ternary mixtures to evaluate whether they can mitigate reverse solute 
flux due to their high molecular weight. 
Of the binary solutions selected for further study, NaCl has both the highest water flux and reverse 
solute flux Different ternary mixtures of NaCl, MgCl2 and Sucrose were further investigated, with 
the combination of MgCl2 and Sucrose showing the highest synergic factor compared to the other 
pairings.  Adding sucrose to binary solutions of NaCl or MgCl2 was shown to significantly reduce 
the reverse solute flux. The ternary solution of sucrose with MgCl2 has the lowest reverse flux and 
is the preferred choice for a good draw solution compared to the combination of sucrose with NaCl 
or of the binary solutions of NaCl and MgCl2.

1 Introduction  
The quality requirements of processed food are growing higher in the modern food industry with 
emphasis being on the preservation of natural tastes, flavors and nutrient contents. Liquid food 
products such as fruit juice which are thermally sensitive and susceptible to chemical, physical and 
microbiological changes which can all contribute to the perceived quality by the consumer.  
Alternative processing technologies such as evaporation can affect the quality of liquid food and 
denature the nutrients, while using more energy than membrane technologies such as Forward 
Osmosis [1, 2].  
Forward osmosis, also known as direct osmosis, uses low hydraulic pressure, lower processing 
temperature and produces less fouling than evaporation, and is therefore less energy demanding 
and easier to clean. The driving force in forward osmosis is the osmotic pressure difference 
between two solutions separated by a semipermeable membrane; which drives water molecules 
from the feed solution (low osmotic pressure) to a high osmotic pressure solution that is called a 
draw solution [1, 2].  
Many draw solutions have been tested since the mid-1990s for food applications and these include; 
organic solutes, inorganic solutes, polymer based macro solutes and volatile solutes. Having 
attracted a lot of attention in the food industry where high quality is required, the critical challenges 
in forward osmosis revolve around maintaining uniform production while keeping the product 
quality high. As a result, phenomena such as concentration polarization, membrane fouling, 
reverse solute diffusion and draw solute design are critical in forward osmosis (FO).  There are 
many criteria for selecting a suitable draw solution for different FO processes. The main criteria 
are high osmotic pressure, economic recovery of diluted draw solution, minimal membrane 
internal concentration polarization (ICP), non-toxicity, low reverse solute flux and high solubility 
in water.

2.1 Forward Osmosis 
2.1.1. Osmotic principles 
Osmosis is a process where water moves from a region of low solute concentration to high solute 
concentration through a semi permeable membrane. This diffusion is driven by osmotic pressure 
across the membrane that allow passage of water and reject most molecules or ions. There are 
many factors that effecting the rate of osmosis such as pressure, concentration gradient, surface 
area, membrane characteristics and difference in water potential.  
The osmotic pressure can be determined by using following equation, that considering dilute ionic 
solution[4]:

in Kelvins.in this case the ideal gas model is used for solute molecules.  
The osmotic pressure can also be defined as a function of the water activity [4]: 
𝜋= −𝑅𝑇𝑙𝑛(𝑎𝑤)/𝑉𝑚                                   (2) 
Where 𝑉𝑚 is the partial molar volume of water and 𝑎𝑤 is activity of water. This thermodynamic 
model allows to determine the osmotic pressure in concentrated electrolyte solution and even 
solution with multiple solvent. 
There are three categories of industrial osmotic processes, namely: 
- 
Reverse Osmosis  
- 
Pressure Retarded Osmosis 
- 
Forward Osmosis 
As opposed to FO, The reverse osmosis (RO) process see Fig. 1, an external hydraulic pressure 
∆P, which is greater than the opposing osmotic pressure , is used as a driving force, with the rate 
of water transport proportional to the difference [5]. 
The second industrial osmotic process is pressure-retarded osmosis (PRO)  see Fig.1,  where water 
across the membrane from low concentration (feed) to high concentration (draw) by using 
hydraulic  pressure on the draw side, where the difference in osmotic pressure is higher than the 
difference in the hydraulic pressure. [5].

solution and draw solution, separated by a semipermeable membrane. The transport of water 
molecules is from the low solute concentration feed to the high solute concentration draw solution 
across the membrane (see Fig. 1) and in this study I will focus on the FO process . [5].

2.1.2 Concentration polarization (CP) 
Concentration polarization (CP) is the main obstacle in both pressure-driven and osmotically 
driven membrane processes. In membrane processes, the concentration polarization is divided into 
two categories, namely external concentration polarization (ECP), which occurs at the surface of 
the membrane active layer and internal concentration polarization (ICP), which occurs within the 
support layer.  The ECP and ICP have two other sub-categories which are dilutive and 
concentrative. 
CP leads to a reduction in osmotic pressure difference between both sides of the membrane active 
layer, due to the movement of water and solute across the asymmetric membrane and the slow 
diffusion rate of solute in the membrane support layer (see Fig.2)[6].  
 
External concentration polarization 
Osmotic pressure can increase or decrease at the active layer depending on the membrane 
orientation. It can increase at the membrane active layer when it faces the feed or decreases at the 
active layer when it faces the draw solution (see Fig.2).  The concentrative external concentration 
polarization phenomenon (CECP) when the feed solution flows through the active layer of the 
membrane, solutes will accumulate on the surface of the active layer, causing the concentrative of 
solutes there to be higher than the bulk solution [6].  Similarly, the dilutive external CP 
phenomenon is when the draw solution is contacts with the permeate side of the membrane is being 
diluted at the interface by the permeating water.  
Both CECP and DECP reduce the osmotic driving force across the membrane resulting in lower 
water flux. ECP can be minimized by increasing the flow velocity and mixing at the membrane 
surface or controlling water flux and this will lead to a negative effect of ECP on the performance 
of FO process. Also, due to low hydraulic pressure used in FO, the effect of ECP is usually 
negligible and is not of considerable important and is not the main reason for flux decline [6, 9].

Internal concentration polarization 
ICP reduces the water flux through the membrane in the FO process. The concentrative ICP takes 
place when the feed solution is facing the membrane support layer, a polarized layer is formed 
along the inside of the dense active layer and thereby the osmotic pressure difference across the 
membrane will decreases (see Fig. 3). This phenomenon is similar to CECP. Furthermore, the 
dilutive ICP is when the active layer faces the feed solution and the draw solution within the porous 
layer of the membrane become diluted (see Fig.3). The effective osmotic pressure difference of 
the dilutive ICP is much lower than the effective osmotic pressure difference of the concentrative 
ICP. In addition the bulk osmotic pressure difference is much higher than the osmotic pressure 
difference across the membrane and the effective osmotic pressure difference is much lower than 
the bulk osmotic pressure difference and the osmotic pressure difference across the membrane[6]. 
==PAGE:12==
12 
 
 
Figure 3. Dilutive ICP and concentrative ICP across an asymmetric FO membrane[6]. 
In this study, the FO membrane operation mode for all membrane experiments was chosen as FO 
mode, i.e. the feed was facing the active layer (diluted internal concentration polarization) (Fig.3), 
expecting lower reverse flux and higher rejection then PRO mode (draw facing the active layer). 
A downside of FO mode and the associated diluted internal concentration polarization is that the 
water flux is lower compared to PRO mode (concentrative internal concentration polarization).  
Generally, the ICP, the reverse solute diffusion, membrane fouling, the membrane characteristics 
and the draw solute properties are related to each other in FO processes (see Fig.4). The ICP can 
be reduced if the membrane support layer is porous and the reverse solute diffusion decreases by 
having a higher selectivity in membrane active layer. In addition, the membrane fouling can be 
reduced by minimizing the reverse solute diffusion. ICP is caused by the low diffusion coefficient 
of the draw solute. A draw solution with higher diffusion coefficient, lower viscosities and smaller 
molecules size will minimize ICP and will have better permeate flux, but it can increase the reverse 
solute diffusion and the membrane fouling[6, 9].

2.2.1 Membrane material 
2.2.1.1 Biomimetic membrane  
The development of molecular engineering has increased significantly during the past years. The 
ability to mimic biological structures with near molecular precision has increased due to 
supramolecular chemistry together with advanced high-resolution analytical techniques. As a 
result, the research and development within the field of biomimetic membranes has improved[10, 
11]. Biomimetic membranes are a result of taking traditional membranes and integrate them with 
biological structures and thereby improve their proprieties. Selective water channel proteins are a 
discovery that has induced interest by using proteins as building blocks for a new kind of 
membranes. The aquaporin protein is illustrated in Fig.7(B) [3, 12]. An exceptional hourglass 
structure with preserved charged residues is created by the six membrane-spanning domains which 
forms an opening allowing selective water transport and solute rejection. The color of the atoms 
is as such; carbon (green), oxygen (red) and nitrogen (blue). The unique structure of the water-
selective AQPs is the reason for these separation properties, as illustrated in Fig.7 (A) below[3, 
10-13].

Biological membranes, in fact, can perform transport in some cases with exceptional flux and

2.2.2 Membrane module  
2.2.2.1 Hollow Fiber 
Hollow fibers are a long porous membranes and are packed inside a tube vessel. The filament is 
narrow in diameter and flexible. The feed solution flows inside the fibers and the draw solution 
flows counter outside the fibers, herewith first passing the active layer and then the support layer 
of the membrane (see Fig.4).  
==PAGE:15==
15 
 
 
Figure 5:Hollow Fiber Membrane[15] 
The advantages are that the hollow fiber have a very high packing density because of the small 
strand diameter and flexibility of the strands. Hollow fiber can be used in different types of 
filtration, ranging from microfiltration to RO.    
The main disadvantages are the irreversible fouling because of the flexibility of the fibers, can 
breaks under high stress (they are more likely to break when under high stress) compared to e.g. 
spiral wound or tubular. It is also expansive[16].  
The three mean reasons for hollow fibers membrane for FO processes: 
• Self-support. 
• Easier to produce than tubular modules and packing density is relatively high. 
• Liquid can flow on both sides of the membrane and a thin support layer in FO can be more 
suitable for FO because FO do not need a thick support layer like FS RO membrane. 
(thinner support layer will reduce the ICP)[5]. 
 
2.2.2.2 Plate and frame 
The plate and frame is the closed to the flat membranes used in the laboratory. The plate and frame 
membrane have a spacer between two set of membranes, where the feed and permeate flows on 
both sides (see Fig.5) below.

In larger plate and frame heat exchanger is more complicated and have a disadvantage as low

2.2.2.3 Spiral-wound  
The most used design is spiral -wound. Spiral-wound operated with one feed stream, and the 
permeate stream flows in the channel by the two glued membranes (Two sheets are glued together 
to form a leaf which is wrapped around the central tube). The spiral wound membrane cannot be 
used in FO because the draw solution cannot flow inside the channel by the two glued membranes. 
The advantages are that the spiral wound have a very high packing density and easy to clean. The 
disadvantages we can get higher fouling than tubular filtration processes and contain lower packing 
density than hollow fiber[5, 16].

2.3 Draw solution for Forward Osmosis 
The draw solution (DS) is crucial for advancing FO technology. Many draw solutions have been 
tested so far for wide range of different applications and in this section, there is a review of how 
to select criteria of suitable DS for food application and recover method.  
The selection of suitable DS includes first an initial screening and laboratory performance (1. Lab 
scale,2. Full scale) see Fig.8 below [7]. 
 
==PAGE:18==
18 
 
 
Figure 8.Flow diagram for the selection of draw solution[7].  
The selection of the draw solutions should have following characteristics[1]: 
• Low cost 
•  Non/zero toxic  
• High osmotic pressure at low concentration 
• High solubility in water 
• Low viscosity (<10 cp) 
• Easily recoverable/regeneration with a low energy input 
• Low reverse solute diffusion (occur due to the concentrations difference)  
• Not adversely affected by contamination of ions from the feed solution 
• Minimal effect on internal concentration polarization 
Other parameters that should be consider is the pH. The pH should be near natural pH, when 
selecting the draw solution. If a reverse draw solute flux occurs, the driving force decreases, the 
feed solution is contaminated and the replenishment costs of the draw solute are increased.

Many organic or inorganic compounds have been tested as draw solutions used in FO for various 
applications, e.g.  food production or sea water desalination with different recovery methods. 
 
2.3.1 Organic compounds  
Organic compounds are known as non-polar compounds, high solubility in water and generate 
high osmotic pressure. 
In 1975 Kravath and Davis investigated glucose as a draw solution and seawater as a feed solution 
by using dialysis cell. This concept was suggested to be used for emergency water supply in 
lifeboats, where glucose would create the draw solution. There was used Hollow fiber cellulose 
acetate membrane. The feed passes through the dialysis and the water passes through the 
membrane, that lead to dilution of the glucose solution. The diluted glucose solution was edible 
and did not require additional treatment. No water flux data was reported. In addition, in 1976 
Kravath and Davis, Kessler and Moody, Stache 1989 investigated nutrient solution mixture of 
glucose and fructose or glycine as a draw solution in FO with none-recovery method. This mixture 
generated a higher osmotic pressure than glucose solution, and glycerin generated higher osmotic 
pressure than fructose but glycerin can be toxic at high concentration[17-19]. The two draw 
solutions generated high osmotic pressure and high solubility. In 1992 Yaeli developed a hybrid 
system by combining FO process with low pressure RO process for the recovery of glucose. Yaeli 
reported that 2M glucose generate an osmotic pressure at 55.03 atm[17].   
 
 2.3.2 Inorganic compounds 
Several inorganic compounds are known that can produce reasonably high water fluxes and can 
be easily recovered by RO [8] or nanofiltration (NF)[20]. It can also be used directly without other 
treatment[21]. 
Achilli et al. in 2010 investigated different draw solutions where the performance analysis was in 
order of CaCl2, KHCO3, MgCl2, MgSO4 NaHCO3 ranked highly and for the cost analysis KHCO3, 
MgSO4, NaCl, NaHCO3 and Na2SO4. The draw solutions that ranked high in performance analysis 
and cost analysis were MgSO4, KHCO3 and NaHCO3. In addition, MgSO4, KHCO3, (NH4)2SO4 
and K2SO4 have the lowest RSF and KCl, NH4HCO3 and KBr have the highest reverse salt 
diffusion/RSF. The recovery process was by RO process[8]. In the same year Tan and Ng et al.  
investigated different draw solutions such as MgCl2, CaCl2, NaCl, KCl, MgSO4 and Na2SO4 by 
combining FO with NF[20]. Tan and Ng et al concluded that KCl and NaCl generate the highest

water flux of more than 25 LMH while MgCl2 and CaCl2 generate the highest osmotic pressure 
due to their high solubility in water. Furthermore, Tan and Ng et al reported that MgSO4 and 
Na2SO4 draw solution are good to produce high quality water by tested on the hybrid FO/NF

Later in 2011 Phuntsho et al.  have found that KCl as a draw solution generate an osmotic pressure

Other study reported that binary solution such as MgCl2 solution have higher osmotic pressure

sugar with binary or ternary solutions generate higher osmotic pressure but can cause fouling. In 
addition that MgCl2 and NaCl can be considered to be the best candidate in the future in a FO 
application[22]. At the same year R.W. Halloway (2015) et al reported that the reverse solute flux 
will be reduced and the water flux will increased when MgCl2 was added to NaCl[23].  
 
2.3.3 Mixed organic compounds with inorganic compounds   
In 2012 Su et al. investigated sucrose as a draw solution in wastewater treatment using double 
skinned hollow fiber membranes. Su et al. reported that sucrose was more effective than MgCl2. 
It is because that sucrose has a larger molecules size than MgCl2 and it result with a very low 
reverse solute flux. Su et al. reported that 1M sucrose has generated an osmotic pressure of 26.7 
atm and was recovered by NF method.  Additionally, Su et al. reported that MgCl2 generate higher 
osmotic pressure, higher water flux and much higher reverse salt flux than NaCl because MgCl2 
has larger solute size than NaCl[17, 24].  
Another study by Hamdan et al.  at 2015 reported that nutrient compounds such as sucrose and 
maltose generate approximately the same osmotic pressure and  by mixing a fixed sugar 
concentration with NaCl or MgCl2 will generate higher osmotic pressure than only sugar (sucrose 
or maltose)[22].

2.4 Application of forward osmosis in food processing  
Membrane processes are used in many areas such as seawater desalination, wastewater 
purification, drug industry and food industry.  FO treatment for liquid foods has been studied at 
laboratory scale and forward osmosis is a promising membrane technology to be used in food 
application. Forward osmosis has many advantages as a process for concentrating beverages and 
liquid food, and operation at low temperature and low pressure that promote high retention of 
sensory and nutritional quality, high rejection and low irreversible fouling.   
In 1996 Opper et al. were the first who investigated FO of grape juice using flat sheet cellulose 
acetate polymeric and tubular membrane modules with a RO membrane of FS cellulose acetate 
membranes, where 8 mol/L NaCl was used as a draw solution.  The grape juice was concentrated 
from 16 to 60 ºBrix with a flux of 2.5 𝑘𝑔∗𝑚−2 ∗ℎ−1. In addition, the high salt diffusion to the 
product leads to bad juice taste[25]. 
 In 1998 Petrotos et al. investigated FO in tubular membrane modules for the concentration of 
tomato juice, where 4 mol/L NaCl was used as a draw solution achieving a water flux of 3.1 𝑘𝑔∗
𝑚−2 ∗ℎ−1.  Furthermore, Petrotos reported that in FO process by increasing the temperature the 
transmembrane flux will increases too. Petrotos investigate different draw solutions such as NaCl, 
CaCl2, Ca(NO3)2, sucrose, glucose and PEG400, where NaCl has the highest osmotic media and 
the highest flux around 3.10 𝑘𝑔∗𝑚−2 ∗ℎ−1due to the low viscosity. In the pretreatment of tomato 
juice for FO application. The results show a good potential by using ultrafiltration as pretreatment 
for tomato juice before concentration the juice by FO. The thickness of a membrane is an important 
factor since its influence on the water flux, so by having a thinner membrane and low viscosity 
will lead to that the flux will increase[26]. 
Babu et al. in 2006 have evaluated FO for the concentration of pineapple juice by mixing non-
responsive combination of sucrose-sodium as the osmotic agent. This mixing of sucrose-sodium 
salt was investigated to minimize the salt content in the solution and minimize the back diffusion 
of the salt in the feed solution. The pineapple juice was concentrated around from 12 to 60 ºBrix 
at 25ºC and the water flux was 1.15 𝑘𝑔∗𝑚−2 ∗ℎ−1. Sugar solutions such as sucrose and fructose 
give lower water flux than salts at low concentrations, it will increase the viscosity and the CP will 
increase too[1, 27, 28].

4.1.3 Theoretical osmotic pressure compared with  published data 
This section describes the theoretical data that were obtained from OLI Stream Analyzer compared 
with other experimental data from published literature. Both studies have determined the osmotic 
pressure based on water activities using Equation 2. 
OLI Stream AnalyzerTM (OLI Systems, Inc,Morris Plains, NJ) was used to obtain the osmotic 
pressure as a fuction of molarity (mol/L) of binary and ternary draw solutions at 20 ºC and 1 bar.  
Binary solution 
The binary systems of MgCl2, NaCl, KCl, CaCl2, Na2SO4, MgSO4, sucrose and fructose showed 
an increasing in osmotic pressure with molarity. And within the range used, they displayed the 
following sequences of osmotic pressure at 3M: MgCl2> CaCl2> NaCl>KCl > MgSO4 >Na2SO4  
and for sugar the sucrose> fructose at 2M and sucrose approximately equal to fructose at 3M see 
figure 18 below:

at 20 ºC. 
 
Hamdan et al.described the osmotic pressure versus molality for different binary solutions such as 
MgCl2, NaCl, sucrose and maltose at 25 ºC . The binary solutions show the same trends in both 
this study and the data obtained from literature, where MgCl2 has a higher osmotic pressure than 
NaCl sucrose and maltose while  sucrose was approximatly the same as  maltose see Fig. 19 below 
[22].  
 
Figure 19. Osmotic pressure versus molality for MgCl2,NaCl, Sucrose and maltose at 25 ºC[22].

 Studies by Hamdan et. al. and Hribar et. al. suggest that the high osmotic pressure when using 
NaCl is due to the interaction between the solute and the hydrogen bond where an induced ion-
dipol occurs and breaks the hydrogen bonds of the water[22, 34].     
Another study by Wilson and Stewart reported that it is also posibile to explain  the relationsship 
between water hydration and osmotic pressure.  A solute with a greater cation charge density will 
generate higher osmotic pressure. The hydration numbers for different compound are as followed: 
Mg2+ is 13, Na+ is 3.9, Li+ is 6,6 and K+ is 1.7. Theoretically it means that Mg2+ have a higher 
osmotic pressure than Na+ and Li+ generate higher osmotic pressure than Na+[4].  
In my study, the osmotic pressure was plotted versus molarity and in the other study (Fig. 19) the 
osmotic pressure was calculated based on molality. At low solute concentrations such as those 
used in this study, the molarity and molality values are very close to each other due to the low 
contribution of the solutes mass to the weight of the entire solution[4].  
Elimination of binary solutions 
This section describ the reason for elimination of  binary solutions. The elimination of binary 
solutions was based on the osmotic pressure, molecular weight and other reasons such as 
precipitation. Figure 18. shows the osmotic pressure versus molarity. The two binary solutions of 
MgCl2 and NaCl were chosen to be investigated further as a ternary solutions due to  the highest 
osmotic pressure compared with KCl, Na2SO4, MgSO4 . The other binary solutions such  KCl, 
Na2SO4, MgSO4 and CaCl2 were eliminated. CaCl2 binary solution was eliminated because it 
precitates at 2M and can causes fouling of the membrane. The other binary solutions such  KCl, 
Na2SO4, MgSO4 and CaCl2 was eliminated. CaCl2 binary solution was eliminated because it 
precitated at 2M and it can cause scaling when using the solutions with  membranes. The other 
binary solutions such as Na2SO4, MgSO4, KCl were eliminated because it generate the lowest 
osmotic pressure compared to  MgCl2 and NaCl.  Sucrose and fructose were also investigated to 
be mixed with MgCl2 and NaCl to evaluate the best candidates as a draw solutions. 
 KCl has higher molecular weight than NaCl, but NaCl has a higher osmotic pressure than KCl. 
So theoretically KCl will have lower reverse flux than NaCl due to the high molecular weight and 
NaCl will generate higher water flux than KCl[6].

Ternary solution  
As the ternary systems, the following solutions were studied: (1) MgCl2+NaCl, (2) 
MgCl2+Fructose, (3) MgCl2+Sucrose , (4)NaCl+Fructose,(5) NaCl+sucrose. These will be 
considered separatly and compared with the behavior of the corresponding binary solutions. The 
comparison is done by taking the difference between the osmotic pressure of the ternary system 
and the sum of the osmotic pressure of binary solutions. This difference is called the synergic 
factor (SF) and can be used as a basis for the selection criteria for the candidates as draw agent[22]. 
The synergic factor can be determined as followed: 
𝑆𝐹= 𝜋𝑡𝑒𝑟𝑛𝑎𝑟𝑦−(𝜋1 + 𝜋2) 
Where 𝜋1𝑎𝑛𝑑 𝜋2 are the osmotic pressure of solute one and solute two, respectively. The SF is 
reffered to as a positive syngergy system if 𝜋𝑡𝑒𝑟𝑛𝑎𝑟𝑦> 𝜋1 + 𝜋2, while 𝜋𝑡𝑒𝑟𝑛𝑎𝑟𝑦< 𝜋1 + 𝜋2 
reffered to a negative synergy system and if  𝜋𝑡𝑒𝑟𝑛𝑎𝑟𝑦= 𝜋1 + 𝜋2 can reffered as a additive system.

From Table 18-20 the fluxes for the compounds increases with an increase in osmotic pressure, 
while it has shown that there is not a linear relationship between DS concentration and water flux. 
At high concentration, the relationship becomes logarithmic[8, 36].   Table 18 and Table 19 shows 
NaCl results at two different pressure. The two experiments were performed with different 
modules.  Table 18. shows NaCl (𝜋= 47.1 bar) have a water flux (16.33 LMH) and reverse flux 
(1.13 gMH), while Table 19 shows NaCl (𝜋= 50 bar) have a water flux (15.4 bar LMH) and reverse 
flux (3.17 gMH). There was found a difference between the results of NaCl in Table 18 and the 
results of NaCl in Table 19, where NaCl (𝜋= 47.1 bar) have higher water flux and lower reverse 
flux compared to NaCl (𝜋= 50 bar). The expectation when comparing the reverse flux results of 
NaCl was to get almost similar results even though the two experiments were performed with 
different module; but it has been shown that there is a high difference in the values. Studies by 
Petrotos et al., Nayak and Rastogi, Babu et al., McCutcheon et al, and V. Sant’ Anna et al. all 
reported that increasing in temperature will decrease the viscosity of a solution while increasing 
the diffusion coefficient, which can help in increasing water flux [1, 26, 37, 38].  
The solute leakage of NaCl changes only slightly (3.17-3.7 g/m2h) at an osmotic pressure between 
50-100 bar (Table 19). This shows that there is a slight increase in reverse solute flux due to 
concentration, however not significant or proportional to the achieved increase in water flux. This 
little change in reverse flux for NaCl with a high reverse flux may be due to that the setup was a 
little unstable. The setup was moved to another location and the remaining experiments for binary 
and ternary solutions were run with the new setup.

Table 18 and Table 19 show different reverse flux of NaCl, where 1M NaCl has a reverse flux of 
around 1.65 g/m2h, while 1.057M NaCl have higher reverse flux of around 3.17 g/m2h. This 
different in reverse flux of NaCl could be because of the different in module.   
NaCl and MgCl2 
Table 18 and Table 20 shows NaCl and MgCl2 results. A comparison of membrane fluxes between 
0.5M NaCl (𝜋= 22 bar) and 0.47 M MgCl2 (𝜋= 31bar).  There was found a different between NaCl 
and MgCl2 fluxes. Table 18 shows that 0.5M NaCl (𝜋= 22 bar) have the highest water flux and the 
highest solute leakage compared with 0.47 M MgCl2 (𝜋= 31bar), where water flux is 11.05 (LMH) 
and reverse flux is 0.23 (gMH) (Table 20). Generally, it is shown that MgCl2 leakage is much 
lower than NaCl. Similar results by Tan et al., Achilli et al., Wang et al.,Yen et al., and Phuntsho 
et al. suggested that the fact that NaCl has higher reverse solute flux than MgCl2 is due to NaCl 
having a lower molecular weight than MgCl2 [8, 20, 21, 39]. R.W Holloway et al. reported that 
NaCl has higher water flux and diffusion coefficient compared to MgCl2[23]. It means that there 
is a trend between water flux and diffusion coefficient. Generally monovalent ions have higher 
water flux as a result of higher diffusivity, however this well result higher reverse flux, where 
bivalent ions have lower reverse flux and lower water flux [40]. 
Sucrose and MgCl2  
The comparison of membrane fluxes between sucrose at 22 bar and MgCl2 at 19 bar is shown in 
Table 20 and Table 21. It shown that sucrose at (𝜋= 22 bar) have a reverse flux (0.19 gMH) and 
water flux (8.34LMH), while MgCl2 at (𝜋= 19 bar) have a reverse flux (0.20 gMH) and water flux 
(8.58LMH). No high differences could be found for reverse flux between sucrose (𝜋= 22 bar) and 
MgCl2 at (𝜋= 19 bar), while sucrose has lower water flux than MgCl2, even though the osmotic 
pressure is slightly higher. In my study sucrose and MgCl2 generate approximately the same 
reverse flux, while Su et al. reported that sucrose generates lower reverse flux than MgCl2 due to 
high molecular weight[24]. 
 In my study MgCl2 and sucrose have different concentration, even if the osmotic pressure is 
approximately the same, while Su et al. reported that 1M sucrose can produce the same water flux 
compared with 1M MgCl2[24] .  
As mentioned before sucrose at 22 bar generate a water flux around 8.34 (LMH), while another 
studies by Achilli et al. and Ge et al show that sucrose has a flux of 12.9 (LMH) at 27.01 bar. 
Based on these results of sucrose shows that increasing in osmotic pressure can affect the water 
==PAGE:51==
51 
 
flux, while compared by other study by Achilli et al. and Ge et al. reported also that increasing in 
osmotic pressure could have effect on water flux.  
NaCl and sucrose 
The comparison between sucrose and NaCl at 22 bar is shown in Table 18 and Table 21. It is 
shown that sucrose at 22 bar has a water flux of 8.34 LMH with a reverse flux at 0.19 (gMH), 
while NaCl has a water flux of 11.6 LMH and the reverse flux is 1.13 (gMH). NaCl was found to 
have higher water flux than sucrose, while sucrose has higher reverse flux than NaCl.  One of the 
reasons for this high difference in water flux may be due to a slightly higher temperature of NaCl 
solution than sucrose solution. Petrotos et al. and V. Sant’ Anna et al reported that NaCl has the 
best performance of water flux compared to glucose and sucrose at the same osmotic pressure as 
lower viscosity yielded higher process performance due to higher diffusivity. The reason for the 
difference in reverse flux may be because sucrose has higher molecular weight than NaCl and it 
means that sucrose has lower mobility than NaCl.  A study by Lobo and Quaresma reported the 
reason for the difference in reverse flux is because larger molecules diffuse more slowly and 
therefore less reverse flux trough the membrane [1, 26]. 
Tables 18 - 21 also show values for Js/Jw which is the ratio of reverse flux to water flux. A higher 
ratio implies a decrease in the selectivity of the membrane and lower efficiency of the process[41]. 
The Js/Jw parameter can be used to compare membranes with respect to the economy of the solute 
recovery process.   
It is important to consider the solubility of the solute in water when choosing draw solutions. A 
study by McCutcheon et al. reported the connection of molecular weight, solubility and water flux. 
McCutcheon et al. reported that a draw solution with a low molecular weight and high water 
solubility will generate high water flux across the membrane than a high molecular weight with 
low solubility in water[32].   
 
Conclusion 
After investigating the membrane fluxes performance for binary solutions, it can now be concluded 
that: 
- 
NaCl has higher water flux than MgCl2 and sucrose, while MgCl2 have slightly higher 
water flux compared to sucrose at the same osmotic pressures. The reason for the higher 
==PAGE:52==
52 
 
water flux may be because NaCl has higher diffusion coefficient and solubility in water 
compared to MgCl2 and sucrose; as previously explained  
- 
With respect to the reverse solute flux; NaCl has the highest reverse flux compared to 
MgCl2 and sucrose, while MgCl2 and sucrose have the same reverse flux. The reason 
for the high reverse flux of NaCl is due to low molecules weight.

Reported that using a combination of sucrose at high concentration with NaCl is preferred for fruit

increasing sucrose (40% w/w) will result in increasing the water flux and decreasing the reverse

water flux and increasing the reverse flux [1, 27].  
Looking at Table 20 and Table 23 show a comparison between the MgCl2 binary solution (𝜋= 26 
bar) and the MgCl2 with sucrose (𝜋= 27 bar) ternary system. MgCl2 binary solution generates 
higher water flux (10.36 LMH) than sucrose with MgCl2 (9.44 LMH), while sucrose with MgCl2 
ternary system has slightly lower reverse flux (0.20 gMH) than MgCl2 (0.22 gMH).  A study by 
Babu et al. reported that high molecular weight solutes such as sucrose mixed with salt can 
substantially decrease the rate of back diffusion[28]. In addition, D. Torreggiani et al. reported that 
mixing sucrose with salt will preserve fruits and vegetables[42].  
Table 23 shows sucrose with MgCl2 has lower reverse flux (0.20 gMH) at 42 bar compared to 
sucrose + NaCl. The reverse flux of sucrose + NaCl is in the range between 0.34- 0.79 (gMH) at 
the pressure between 39-57 bar (Table 22), it is therefore certain that the reverse flux cannot 
decreases lower than 0.34 (gMH) at 39 bar.  
From the reverse flux of sucrose with NaCl, or MgCl2 with sucrose results in Tables 5 and 6; it is 
probable that NaCl with sucrose or MgCl2 with sucrose is limited by other mechanisms besides 
differences in concentration for the combination of salt with sucrose draw solutions. It may be that 
there is an interaction between ions or between the ions and the membrane surface that have an 
effect on the transport of ternary solutions across the membrane and minimize the reverse flux. 
Sucrose has higher molecular weight and lower mobility than NaCl or MgCl2, therefore it can be 
that sucrose prevents the diffusion of salts[27, 43].

5 Conclusion 
In this work the behavior of binary and ternary systems of draw solutions was investigated by 
using the simulation program, OLI Analyzer, combined with a series of experiments. NaCl, MgCl2 
and sucrose were selected as the most promising candidates from the theoretical analysis and tested 
further, experimentally, as binary and ternary draw solutions for use in FO food applications. The 
water flux and reverse flux performance for the binary solutions and ternary solutions were 
experimentally evaluated and the main finding of this study are summarized:  
- 
The binary solution of MgCl2 has the highest osmotic pressure compared to CaCl2, 
NaCl, KCl, MgSO4, Na2SO4,  sucrose and fructose. 
- 
The results showed that the ternary solutions of MgCl2 with sucrose have significantly 
high positive synergy and could be a possible suitable candidate as draw solutions.  
- 
0.5M NaCl (𝜋= 22 bar) is shown to have higher water flux than 0.47 M MgCl2 (𝜋= 
31bar), while MgCl2 has lower reverse flux compared to NaCl.  
- 
MgCl2 (𝜋= 19 bar) has higher water flux compared to Sucrose (𝜋= 22 bar), while no 
high difference was found in reverse fluxes between sucrose and MgCl2: 0.19 (gMH) 
and 0.20 (gMH) respectively. In addition NaCl has higher water flux compared to 
sucrose at 22 bar.  
- 
NaCl has the highest reverse solute flux compared to sucrose at 22 bar. The reason for 
the high reverse flux of NaCl is due to low molecules weight.   
- 
Binary solutions such as NaCl and MgCl2 have higher water flux than their ternary 
mixtures with sucrose; while the ternary mixtures have lower reverse solute flux than 
the binary solutions.  
- 
Ternary solutions of MgCl2 with sucrose have lower reverse flux than than those of 
NaCl with sucrose.

6 Future work  
NaCl gives higher water flux than MgCl2 and sucrose, while sucrose and MgCl2 generate lower 
reverse solute flux than NaCl. It will be interesting in the future to investigate the fluxes achieved 
by combining NaCl, MgCl2 and sucrose. Furthermore it could be interesting to replace NaCl with 
LiCl, since LiCl generate higher osmotic pressure (10M LiCl 1782.4 bar) than NaCl (10M NaCl 
at 403.5 bar), but the only disadvantages could be reverse flux because LiCl has lower molecular 
weight than NaCl. To minimize the reverse flux of LiCl, it would be a good idea to mix LiCl with 
a second compound such as sucrose or even saccharin (183.18 g/mol), Acesulfame potassium 
(201,2 g/mol), aspartame (294.3 g/mol) and Steviol glycosides (318.5 g/mol)[44].   
It would also be interesting to investigate a combination of gluconate salts with sucrose or other 
additives, because gluconate salts have a higher molecular weight than other salts such as NaCl or 
MgCl2.
