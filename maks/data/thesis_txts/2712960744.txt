Project Description:  
This master thesis investigates how one can support and sustain the creation of infection 
preventive behaviour in complex systems. Furthermore, the thesis addresses how we can design 
for ambiguity in the context of nursing homes within the field of behavioural design. Through a 
scientific literature review, we provide the theoretical foundation and argue that research is 
needed within the field. Through the use of Research through Design as an overall approach, 
important aspects related to behavioural change in the complex system of nursing homes, were 
identified. The interactions between these aspects serve as a basis for our theoretical contribution 
to the field.

Abstract 
 
Due to elderly citizens increased risk of infections, there is a demand for high compliance with hygienic 
measures within the context of nursing homes in order to prevent infection spread. However, it remains 
challenging for nursing home employees to comply with hygienic regulations and recommendations. This 
indicates a need for behaviour change in order to create and support infection preventive behaviours 
within the context of nursing homes. However, there is limited research on, and interventions developed 
for, changing behaviours in this context. Additionally, upon reviewing prior literature on behaviour 
change, we identify multiple gaps. First, the complexity in nursing homes, especially related to its dual 
purpose of being both a workplace and a home, makes it a challenging context for behavioural change. 
Behaviour change methods typically do not tackle complex systems, thus creating a gap. Second, existing 
research often either proposes organisational policies (instructions, educations), or nudging individual 
behaviours as a means of behaviour change. This indicates a neglection of the ambiguities and complexity 
within the nursing home context, in existing research. As such, we identify a need for further research on 
these topics. This need is the basis of this thesis, the purpose of which is to identify how one can support 
and sustain the creation of infection preventive behaviour in the complex systems of nursing homes, and 
how we can address ambiguity within the field of behavioural change. 
 
Our research was conducted using a Research through Design (RtD) approach. The research included 
three RtD phases (or iterations): phase 1 – Social Implications Design (SID), phase 2 – Co-Creation, and 
phase 3 – Design Intervention. Phase 1 aimed to provide a future view of the world within which our 
research would be used, phase 2 aimed to involve nursing home employees in the creation of the 
intervention, and phase 3 aimed to evaluate the intervention. Based on these three phases, dimensions, 
themes and categories were identified through reflective practices on the design process. Three 
dimensions were identified: perceived ambiguity, professional identity, and perspectivism, each 
appertaining to a number of themes and categories. 
 
Considering the findings pertaining to each of the three dimensions, and to prior literature related to 
them, the thesis is able to make five main contributions to theory. First, the aim should not be to reduce 
perceived ambiguity, as suggested in prior literature, but rather to recognise and try to accommodate it. 
Second, we apply existing research on identity in a new context by suggesting that professional identity 
influences the role assumed by an individual, thus affecting their prioritisation of tasks. Third, we identify 
a direct link between perceived ambiguity and perspectivism, as the perceived ambiguity leads to the 
interpretations based on individual perspectives, and oppositely perspectivism can contribute to perceived 
ambiguity due to the influence of varying interpretations. Fourth, based on our findings, we argue that 
perceived ambiguity can lead to an increase in the significance of professional identity, as familiarity in 
tasks can reduce dubiety. This familiarity is based in experience, which is a core element of professional 
identity, thus establishing a link between perceive ambiguity and professional identity. Fifth, we identify a 
link between perspectivism and professional identity, by indicating that an employee’s professional 
identity affects their interpretations, which are influenced by their individual perspectives. On the other 
hand, perspectivism plays a role in defining professional identity, as it affects the internal contributors of 
this. 
 
All in all, in order to design for ambiguity in regards to infection prevention in the context of nursing 
homes, our research indicates that the designer should be attentive to perceived ambiguity, professional 
identity, and perspectivism in the system. In accordance with this, we suggest a focus on recognising the 
ambiguity in the system and facilitating the communication of information between the societal and 
individual level. In order to do this, our research points to the need for establishing a collective 
professional identity among care personnel in nursing homes, as well as a collective professional identity 
across nursing homes on the intermediate level. By aligning the interpretations made by the nursing 
homes, it helps to bridge the current gap between the societal and individual level.

General Information 
 
This thesis is a subsidiary project to the PhD project: Designing sustainable behaviour change to prevent spread of 
contagious diseases in nursing homes. The researcher of the PhD project, Carolina Falcão Duarte, has been co-
supervising this thesis. The link to the PhD has provided us with an initial frame and a general direction 
for our thesis. The two participating nursing homes, Klostergården (from this time forward referred to as 
nursing home 1) and Langgadehus (from this time forward referred to as nursing home 2), were the same 
ones used for data collection in the PhD project. Both of the two nursing homes are located within 
Copenhagen Municipality. We hope that the findings of this thesis will contribute to the PhD. 
 
Due to a general lack of human resources in the two nursing homes, only limited time could be allocated 
to different participant workshops. Thus, the number of workshops conducted has been constrained by 
this, which was both due to practical reasons expressed by the nursing home administration and out of 
ethical considerations from both us and our supervisor. Furthermore, due to the time constraint, some of 
the data used for the first stages of this thesis are interviews conducted as part of the data collection for 
the PhD.

Learning Objectives 
 
The learning objectives of the master thesis are described as the student's ability: 
1. To understand principles about behaviour design and discuss the interplay between 
psychology and design. 
2. To understand the factors that encourage or inhibit behaviours related to infection 
prevention. 
3. To collect and analyse qualitative data about a phenomenon and be able to extract insights 
from it for designing for behavioural change. 
4. To critically evaluate decisions taken in the analysis of the collected data. 
5. To critically evaluate and identify the strengths and weaknesses of behaviour change 
techniques and behavioural change methods. 
6. To develop a behavioural change intervention and report its mechanism. 
7. To report decisions taken when designing for behavioural change 
8. To report contextual determinants that can influence on the impact of a behavioural design 
intervention. 
9. To critically reflect on ethical issues when working with behaviour design and behaviour 
design interventions (related to infection prevention). 
10. To test a behavioural design intervention.

Nomenclatures 
 
Behavioural Design: A process in which knowledge on, and understanding of certain behaviours is used 
when developing practical solutions that aim to influence these behaviours (King, Thompson, & Darzi, 
2014). The goal of behavioural design is to achieve an intended behaviour, which is desired by both the 
individual as well as society (Khadilkar & Cash, 2020a). 
 
Co-Creation: The goal is to involve users throughout the design process, as far as possible. Here, the use 
of artefacts as communication and thinking tools, is common (Sanders & Stappers, 2012). Co-creation 
seeks to actively involve the people one wishes to serve, through a design process. 
 
Complex System: A complex system has the ability to self-organise and adapt its functionalities to 
accommodate the conditions within the system’s environment (Comfort, 1994).  
 
Complex Interventions: Complex interventions are defined as multicomponent, non-linear, and 
context-dependent interventions (Petticrew, et al., 2018). 
 
Context Factor:  Individually represented pieces of the future world that one is designing for and trying 
to define. They can be classified into more dynamic categories (including trends and developments) and 
more static categories (including states and principles) (Tromp & Hekkert, 2018). 
 
Context Structure: Context structures are a way to organise context factors and put them into 
connection with each other.  By doing so, valuable insights can be extracted. To create the structures, 
clustering is used. Clusters of context factors is based on interesting connections or conflicts for the 
purpose of creating clusters that each tell a story relevant to the context of the domain. 
 
Design Intervention: Design interventions are seen as a form of inquiry that can be used to investigate a 
phenomenon (Halse & Boffi, 2015). 
 
Research Through Design: “Conceptualizing research done by means of the skilful practice of design activity revealing 
research insights” (Krogh, Markussen, & Bang, 2015). RtD is highly systematic and explicitly reflective in its 
approach, and it requires detailed documentation of the design process and the logic behind it 
(Zimmerman & Forlizzi, 2014).

Chapter 1 
Introduction 
==PAGE:18==

Danish citizens live longer than ever before (Danmarks Statistik, 2017), and it is estimated that the 
number of people over the age of 80 will more than double by year 2057 (Danmarks Statistik, 2019). The 
increased life expectancy is a positive result from decreased mortality and a generally improved public 
health, but it does, however, also present some issues. 
 
Becoming older is usually followed by age-related comorbidities and a decline in functionality which leads 
some citizens to move into nursing homes in order to get more care assistance (Juthani-Mehta & 
Quagliarello, 2010). It is recognised that the elderly population is vulnerable to infections, which is partly 
due to risk factors such as a decrease of the functionality of the immune system, cognitive deficit, and 
functional impairments (Richards C. R., 2007; Montoya, Cassone, & Mody, 2016). The growing elderly 
population, their increased health complexity, and the complexity of balancing both an institutional as 
well as a homely environment in the nursing home, add to the pressure on the institutions who are 
already challenged from a lack of resources. Unfortunately, infections are rather common in long-term 
care facilities such as nursing homes, which leads to increased morbidity and mortality among the 
residents in nursing homes (Richards C. R., 2007). 
 
Human behaviour has an important role in regards to spread of infections – this is also the case for 
residents end employees of nursing homes (Funk, Salathé, & Jansen, 2010). The discipline of behavioural 
design can serve as an effective means to create behavioural change for the benefit of infection 
prevention. The need and effectiveness of behavioural change has been further brought to the public 
attention during the COVID-19 pandemic. Here, behavioural change proved to be an effective way to 
prevent the spread of infection (West, Michie, Rubin, & Amlôt, 2020). However, two issues arise when 
considering behavioural change within the context of nursing homes.  
 
First, many of the care tasks are perceived by the employees to be ambiguous. However, the risk of 
infection spread and the extent of this risk, it is not clear to the employees. The perceived ambiguity leads 
to individual and varying evaluations on how to prevent infection spread in different situations, and these 
evaluations may not comply with the societal recommendations. This leads to a gap in how we might 
address ambiguity when designing for behavioural change in nursing homes.  
 
Second, knowledge on how to design for sustainable behavioural change in complex systems such as 
nursing homes is scarce. This is despite a well-recognised need for a complex systems approach for 
developing behavioural change interventions. Behavioural change methods typically do not tackle 
complex systems. As we are considering a nursing home to be a complex system, this is an essential gap 
for our project. 
 
1.1 | Project Aim 
The aim of the project is to investigate how we can support and sustain the creation of infection 
preventive behaviour in complex systems, and how we can address ambiguity within the field of 
behavioural change. Within the context of nursing homes, we have formulated the following overall 
research question: 
 
How might one design for ambiguity in relation to avoiding the spread of infections in the context of 
nursing homes within the field of behavioural design? 
 
To further address our aim, the following sub-areas will be covered throughout the thesis: 
• 
Clarification and description of the nursing home context with a focus on the dual purpose of 
nursing homes as well as the needs and values of nursing home residents 
• 
Clarification and description of how infections spread and how to prevent this, both generally 
and in the context of nursing homes 
==PAGE:19==
 
7 
• 
Clarification and description of relevant behavioural change theory, both on a more general level 
and in relation to behavioural change within the area of complex systems 
• 
Exploration of how we might address the complex environments of nursing homes when 
designing for sustainable behavioural change 
• 
Exploration of the role of identity and individuality when designing for behavioural change 
within the context of nursing home 
• 
Explore how we by designing an intervention can support the creation of a common 
understanding and evaluation of ambiguously perceived situations occurring in the context of 
nursing homes 
 
We consider ourselves to be unbiased in regards to the project, as we do not have any relations to nursing 
homes, societal entities, or similar. However, some of the literature sources used may be biased, which we 
throughout the project are aware and considerate of, to the best of our abilities. 
 
1.2 | Report Structure 
This master thesis consists of six chapters. Chapter 1 – Introduction aims to set the stage of the project and 
present the project’s relevance and aim. Chapter 2 – Literature Review consists of our review of relevant 
literature within the scope of our thesis. The literature is three-part and aims to cover and explain relevant 
aspects of the context of nursing homes, the infection spread and prevention and behavioural design 
theory. Chapter 3 – Research Approach and Methodology covers the overall approach of the project and the 
methods used in order to conduct our research. Chapter 4 – Research Through Design (RtD) presents our 
design process and the identified findings. Three RtD iterations were done, all aiming to investigate the 
overall research question. However, through the first two iterations of RtD, the research question was 
further scoped down, thus leading to the formulation of two specified research questions. The iterations 
will be outlined in a coherent order and the conclusions of each will serve as building blocks for the 
following iteration. In Chapter 5 – Discussion we will discuss which theoretical contributions our project has 
led to. Furthermore, we will outline the implications and limitations of the thesis. Lastly, in Chapter 6 – 
Conclusion we will conclude on the project.

2.1 | Elderly Care 
There are different types of elderly care including nursing homes, home care, and elderly residences 
(Ældresagen, 2021a). These options are offered in order to suit various levels of capabilities and 
independence among elderly citizens. This project is centred around nursing homes and as such, this 
section is limited to focusing on elderly care in nursing homes. 
 
In 2.1.1 | Nursing Homes we will establish what a nursing home is with a focus on the duality of nursing

2.1.1 | Nursing Homes 
A nursing home is a residence for citizens, who are unable to live independently and are in need of care. 
The nursing home has care personnel available at all times to provide assistance when needed (Bolig- og 
Planstyrelsen, 2022). Additionally, nursing homes provide the opportunity to engage in social interactions 
through different activities arranged either by the nursing home itself or by the local community 
(Ældresagen, 2022a). 
 
The main purpose of a nursing home is to help preserve the residents’ quality of life and dignity. Thus, 
life in a nursing home should be viewed through the eyes of the residents, according to The Elderly 
Commission (Ældrekommissionen, 2012) – a council which focuses on self-determination and care of the 
elderly (Gyldendals Leksikonredaktion, 2011). 
 
When a citizen is in potential need of relocating to a nursing home, the municipality is contacted by either 
the citizen itself, its friends and relatives, its doctor, or a health care institution. Visitation is then set up by 
the municipality to determine if relocation is recommended and if so, the citizen is either relocated right 
away or placed on a waiting list – the latter being the typical outcome. However, there is a time limit of 
two months on the waiting list, within which the citizen is guaranteed to be offered a placement (Bolig- 
og Planstyrelsen, 2022). To determine if relocation to a nursing home is needed, the municipality looks 
into four aspects (Ældresagen, 2022a): 
 
• 
Assessing the citizen’s physical and psychological capabilities 
• 
Assessing the need for care as well as personal and practical assistance 
• 
Assessing potential changes in conditions in the citizen’s current residence 
• 
Assesses friends’ and relatives’ potential need for support or relief regarding their loved ones 
 
A nursing home can aid these aspects by providing full-time access to social care.

2.1.1.1.1 | Residents’ Different Levels of Capabilities 
Nursing home residents are dependent on getting assistance for different everyday tasks, the extent of 
which varies due to different levels of capabilities (Døhl, Garåsen, Kalseth, & Magnussen, 2014). A good 
elderly life in a nursing home includes utilising the residents’ individual capabilities (Døhl, Garåsen, 
Kalseth, & Magnussen, 2014). Residents in nursing homes will typically require assistance for certain 
tasks, but in order for them to have the highest influence on their own life, they should do as much as 
they are capable of doing. A challenge in relation to this is that their capabilities are individual, as each 
resident varies on which tasks they are able to perform on their own or to what extent they are able to do 
so independently (Ældrekommissionen, 2012). 
 
It is important that the residents feels that they are in a safe space in order to feel comfortable in what is 
now their home (Ældrekommissionen, 2012). Additionally, it is essential that this space is able to be 
adapted to suit residents’ individual needs, rather than requiring each resident to conform for the purpose 
of fitting into an already established system. Flexibility within the system in a nursing home is therefore a 
key factor in achieving a good elderly life for the residents (Ældrekommissionen, 2012). The variation in 
capabilities among the residents and the need for system flexibility creates complexity in the system of 
nursing homes. 
 
2.1.1.2 | Employees in Nursing Homes 
The nursing home employees’ role is vital to the well-being of the residents. It is their responsibility to 
ensure that the residents receive the best care possible by assisting with tasks such as showering, eating 
and taking medication. Simultaneously, the employees must also be attentive to those of the residents’ 
needs that do not concern health-promoting care, such as social needs (Ældrekommissionen, 2012). As 
the needs of residents require both health-related care as well as social care, both professional and 
humane qualifications are important for nursing home employees. Thus, they should not only have care 
experience and professionalism, but also show empathy, presence, and companionship 
(Ældrekommissionen, 2012). 
 
The health-related part of the employees’ job is not only to provide care for the residents by assisting 
them with tasks they are unable to perform themselves, but it is also to help the residents live their own 
dignified life (Sundhedsstyrelsen, 2016). Combined with the residents’ varying capabilities and needs, this 
results in the employees’ tasks being unpredictable and ill-defined. Another aspect making this further 
complicated is the residents’ situations, which can change rather quickly thereby creating sudden 
immediate tasks and perhaps eliminating or delaying other tasks (Ældrekommissionen, 2012). As a result, 
the assistance needed varies from resident to resident and the employees must constantly be ready for 
change and prepared to adapt to their work (Ældrekommissionen, 2012).

2.1.1.2.1 | Relationship Between Nursing Home Employees and Residents 
Relationships between employees and residents are a delicate aspect of life in nursing homes. Although, 
the 2012 SFI study indicated that the vast majority of both employees and residents feel that they have a 
good relationship between them (SFI, 2012), there is an uneven distribution of power. This is influenced 
by the residents’ dependency on employees' service in multiple aspects of their daily lives. Because of this 
uneven power distribution, The Elderly Commission suggests the need for continued development of the 
employees’ professional competencies such as communication (Sundhedsstyrelsen, 2016). 
 
Communication is an essential part of the relationship between employees and residents. The variation in 
residents’ capabilities results in communicative challenges for some residents, which in terms require the 
employees to adjust their communication to suit each resident (Ældrekommissionen, 2012). 
Communication between employees and residents impacts the residents’ well-being and as such, it is 
important that the employees are attentive to the communicative challenges and preferences of each 
resident. Employees must be capable of identifying residents’ signals while also being acknowledging, 
reflective, curious, and good listeners throughout the communication (Ældrekommissionen, 2012). 
 
Additionally, it is important for the residents’ well-being that employees both perform the needed care 
tasks, but also participate in the residents’ everyday lives. An example of this is that employees – 
according to the Elderly Commission – should eat with the residents during the meals, as this will increase 
social interactions among the residents and influence them to eat more (Sundhedsstyrelsen, 2016). By 
doing so, it will have a positive influence the quality of life experienced in nursing homes. 
 
2.1.1.3 | Dual Purpose of Being Both a Home and Work Place 
Being able to offer full-time care entails employing care personnel, thereby making the nursing home a 
workplace. However, as residents live there, it is simultaneously a home. This duality is one of the core 
elements of the complexity in nursing homes, since it is caused by, but also affects, the physical 
environment of nursing homes as well as the working conditions for the employees. The dual purpose 
creates the need for balancing the workplace and home aspects (Kjeldsen, 1997). Having a homely feeling 
is one of the ways in which the residents can maintain their quality of life despite the need for increased 
assistance in their daily life (Ældrekommissionen, 2012). 
 
2.1.1.3.1 | Physical Environment in Nursing Homes 
As mentioned, a nursing home consists of private rooms for each resident but also work facilities where 
only employees have access (such as offices) and community facilities (such as dining rooms and garden 
areas) (Ældresagen, 2022b). How these areas are formed and balanced depends on the particular nursing 
home. For the private rooms, residents are legally tenants, which makes it their private property. On the 
contrary, the common areas are a part of the institution that is the nursing home, which is managed by 
the municipality (Ældresagen, 2022b). 
 
There are certain requirements for the private rooms in nursing homes, as they must be accessible by 
wheelchair, walker, and other assistive devices. This entails that there should be wide door openings and 
no doorsteps, among other things (Ældresagen, 2022b). Even though the residents’ rooms are their 
private property, the rooms are also part of the employees’ work environment and therefore, the interior 
must abide by the Danish laws related to working conditions (Ældresagen, 2022b).

2.1.1.3.2 | Working Conditions and Resident Safety 
For nursing homes, ensuring suitable work conditions can be challenging due to two main reasons: 1) 
interplay between the work environment and resident safety and 2) ensuring proper hygiene in a non-
clinical environment. Achieving suitable working conditions for the employees and simultaneously 
ensuring a high level of resident safety is challenging, as “[p]atient safety cannot be maintained unless the 
working conditions are good and the work environment can never be acceptable if patient safety is 
compromised” (Johansson, Persson, Olander, & Erlingsdottír, 2015). In other words, working conditions 
and resident safety are mutually dependent on each other. This further increases the level of complexity in 
the system that is the nursing home. 
 
The second challenge, ensuring proper hygiene in a non-clinical environment, concerns an issue of 
complying with hygienic guidelines (Johansson, Persson, Olander, & Erlingsdottír, 2015). When providing 
care or treating a resident in their own home, the care personnel are able to take hygienic precautions 
such as performing hand hygiene and using protective equipment. However, due to the fact that nursing 
homes are not clinical environments, the surfaces may not be clean – especially when performing care in a 
resident’s private room (Johansson, Persson, Olander, & Erlingsdottír, 2015). This further emphasises the 
complexity and ambiguity of nursing homes and their dual purpose of being both a home and a 
workplace; the working environment in nursing homes cannot be fully clinical unlike other institutions 
such as hospitals, yet the residences cannot be fully controlled by the residents, unlike a typical home. 
 
2.1.1.4 | Vision for Nursing Homes in Denmark 
Ældre Sagen – an association working to improve the conditions for elderly citizens in Denmark 
(Ældresagen, 2022c) – has defined an overall vision for Danish nursing homes. It states that a nursing 
home should be a place where you want to come and you feel at home (Ældresagen, 2022d). This further 
entails that it is a place of presence, care, and companionship, thereby counteracting loneliness, boredom, 
and helplessness. The important values of nursing homes include respect and self-determination, and 
essential resources include good administrative leaders as well as professionally and empathetically 
competent employees. The purpose of these values and resources is to – as far as possible – preserve the 
residents’ quality of life (Ældresagen, 2022d). 
 
2.1.2 | Needs and Values for a Good Life in Nursing Homes 
As mentioned in 2.1.1 | Nursing Homes, one of the main purposes of nursing homes is to ensure a good 
quality of life. To do so, it is important to understand the needs and values of the individuals, since these 
can differ a lot. There are multiple different sets of defined needs and values for individuals in terms of 
having a good quality of life. Based on specified relevance pertaining explicitly to the care of elderly 
citizens, rather than human needs and values in general, this literature review will include the following 
three sets of needs and values: 
 
• 
Eight Domains Regarding Quality of Life for People in the Need of Social Care (SFI, Omsorg og 
livskvalitet i plejeboligen, 2012) 
• 
Seven Dignity Policies for Elderly Care (Copenhagen Municipality, 2019) 
• 
Five Fundamental Values for a Good Life in a Nursing Home (Ældrekommissionen, 2012)

2.1.2.3 | Comparison of Defined Sets of Needs and Values 
When comparing the three sets of defined needs and values for elderly citizens, multiple overlaps are 
identified (see Figure 7). First, quality of life is part of all three sets. It is mentioned as one of the seven 
dignity policies; it is the overall aim of the eight domains; and it is part of the foundation for the five 
fundamental values. Therefore, quality of life is considered to be an essential part of the needs and values 
of elderly citizens. 
 
Second, the concept of dignity is also part of all three sets. It is mentioned in relation to death in the five 
fundamental values and the seven dignity policies. It also makes up one of the eight domains and 
simultaneously forms the foundation of the seven dignity policies. Additionally, dignity also plays an 
essential role in the three related areas ‘Control Over Daily Life’, ‘Self-Determination’, and ‘Influence on 
Own Life’, which are spread across the three defined sets of needs and values. An essential aspect of 
these three related areas is individuality. Elderly citizens have individual needs, values, interests, and 
capabilities, which must be recognised in their daily lives. Therefore, nursing homes cannot be set up in 
such a way where all residents are expected to have the same daily routines, but instead must embrace 
their individuality and give the residents the opportunity to control and influence their life.

2.1.2.4 | Institutional Alignment with Individual Needs 
Although nursing home residents have these various needs and values, they are not always well-reflected 
on a societal level. An example of a situation where this is evident, is the residents’ shower schedules. 
Elderly recipients of care have been allocated up to one shower per week, which is a decision made on a 
societal level (Herschend, 2017). However, their needs and values indicate that they should have an 
influence on their own daily lives in order to preserve their dignity as well as their quality of life 
(Copenhagen Municipality, 2019). Some nursing home residents may find a weekly shower sufficient, 
whereas others consider it to be either too often or seldom. As such, there may be resident needs that 
cannot be met, thus suggesting a misalignment between institutional resources and individual needs. 
 
Another example of constitutional constraint can be seen in the residents’ personalisation of the space. 
Because their rooms are their own property, they have the opportunity to personalise this according to 
their own taste. However, they do not have full freedom to do so, since the residences have to adhere to 
certain restrictions given by the nursing home in order to provide suitable working conditions, as 
presented in 2.1.1.3.1 | Physical Environment in Nursing Homes.

2.1.3 | Conclusion for Elderly Care 
In this section, we identified some particularly challenging aspects of elderly care in nursing homes, which 
illustrate the complexity and ambiguity of this context. Firstly, its dual purpose of being both a home and 
a workplace is difficult to balance – particularly for the employees as they need to be considerate of both 
purposes. Secondly, the residents’ variation in capabilities along with the constant possibility of change 
causes the employees’ tasks to be difficult to define, thereby increasing ambiguity in nursing homes. 
Thirdly, the employees’ practices are made further complex and ambiguous, as the employees must 
function as both health-promoting caretakers and social caretakers. As such, they need to balance 
professionalism and empathy/companionship among other characteristics in their daily practices.  
 
Additionally, this section established that the institutional offerings in nursing homes sometimes collide 
with individual interest. There is extensive research on the needs and values of elderly citizens receiving 
care and there are societal policies in place for this. However, the connection between these two levels 
has proven to be problematic when dealing with issues such as lack of resources. The knowledge on the 
flexibility within the system is scarce and as such, we see a need for further research on the connection 
between the societal and individual level.

2.2 | Hygiene and Infections 
As made evident in 2.1.1 | Nursing Homes, nursing homes are highly complex due to factors such as its 
dual purpose. Because of this complexity and ambiguity, defining standardised and clear hygienic 
precautions for infectious diseases, is difficult. 
 
In 2.2.1 | Spread of Infections, we will briefly describe important aspects of the spread of infections. 
Here, we will outline the chain of infection with a focus on pathogenic microorganisms and different 
possible transmission routes. This will be followed by an outline of different preventive measures for 
infections spread in 2.2.2 | Preventing the Spread of Infections. Here, we will describe how to disrupt the 
chain of infection with a focus on hand hygiene measures. In addition, a description of the individual and 
societal interplay in infection prevention will be presented. Lastly, in 2.2.3 | Infections in a Nursing 
Home, we will focus on infections in nursing homes, where the most common infections in nursing 
homes and strategies used for infection control in nursing homes will be covered. This will be concluded 
with a description of different identified main challenges pertaining to infection prevention. 
 
2.2.1 | Spread of Infections 
According to the Center for Disease Control and Prevention (CDC), an infection occurs when germs 
have entered the body, increased its population, and caused a reaction in the body (Center for Disease 
Control and Prevention, 2016). Germs cover different kinds of microorganisms such as bacteria, viruses, 
and yeast, all of which can result in varying infections (Madigan, Brock, & Martinko, 2006). In order for 
an infection to spread, CDC describes three factors needed: 1) a source (places where infectious 
microorganisms can live), 2) a susceptible person with an opening for the germs to enter its body, and 3) 
a transmission (a way in which the germs can be transferred to the susceptible person) (Center for 
Disease Control and Prevention, 2022). Statens Serum Institut, Denmark, (SSI) expand this by describing 
six factors, which collectively make up the chain of infection (see Table 4). We will only elaborate further 
on microorganisms and transmission routes, as the other factors are considered out of scope for this 
project.

2.2.1.2 | Microorganisms 
As mentioned, there are many different microorganisms. Based on relevance in the case of nursing 
homes, we will only cover theory surrounding bacteria and viruses. Most bacteria and viruses do not 
cause disease in humans. The human body harbours a large collection of microorganisms such as fungi, 
bacteria and viruses. These live both inside and on the surface of the body and is often referred to as the 
human microbiome. The human microbiome has become increasingly researched and is understood to be 
crucial to our health (Turnbaugh, et al., 2007). Despite the positive associations between the human 
health and indigenous microorganisms of the body, some microbes do cause disease and are referred to as 
being pathogenic. Commonly known pathogenic bacteria include species within Streptococcus, 
Staphylococcus, E. coli and Salmonella. Viral infections span from being rather harmless such as a 
common cold, to being more severe like smallpox and HIV (Madigan, Brock, & Martinko, 2006). 
 
Many infections caused by viruses have no known curing treatment and is usually centred towards 
relieving the symptoms, although in some cases, antiviral medication can be used effectively (Gaglia, 
Cook, Kraemer, & Rothberg, 2007). Another strategy is to prevent the occurrence of viral infections, e.g., 
through a vaccine (Iwasaki & Omer, 2020). For bacterial infections, different types of antibiotics are 
normally utilised. However, there is an increasing problem with the development and spread of antibiotic 
resistance in bacteria, making infections difficult or impossible to treat (Hutchings, Truman, & Wilkinson, 
2019). On a global scale, since 2019 around 700,000 people have died annually from drug-resistant 
bacterial infections, according to the UN (United Nations, 2019). 
 
The source of an infection can be either endogenic or exogenic. For endogenic infections, the responsible 
microorganism originates from the recipient’s own microflora, whereas exogenic infections are caused by 
microorganisms from an external source. It is not always clear whether an infection is caused by 
endogenic or exogenic sources, thus making it difficult to develop relevant preventive measures 
(Johansen, et al., 2017).

2.2.1.3.1 | Contact-Related Transmission Routes 
Infections transmitted through physical contact are separated into two 
subcategories: direct contact and indirect contact. To be infected 
through direct contact (see Figure 8), microorganisms need to be 
transferred through physical contact between the carrier of the 
infection and the recipient of the infection. The carrier can either be 
someone who is sick or someone who is a non-sick carrier (Buhl, et 
al., 2019). For indirect contact-related transmission routes, the 
microorganisms will be transferred to the recipient of infections 
through an intermediate link (e.g., doorknobs). This is by far the most 
common transmission route (Johansen, et al., 2017). 
 
2.2.1.3.2 | Droplet-Related Transmission Routes 
For this transmission route, microorganisms are spread through 
different-sized droplets, which then hit the mucosa membranes in the 
mouth, nose, eyes, etc. These are usually spread through coughing, 
vomiting and sneezing (see Figure 9), but they also appear in situations 
of squirts of stool, blood or urine. Droplets cannot travel for long in 
the air (around 1-3 seconds) and therefore, the recipient of the 
infection has to be close to the carrier of the microorganism in order 
to be infected (around 1 metre) (Buhl, et al., 2019). 
 
2.2.1.3.3 | Airborne- and Dust-Borne Transmission Routes 
Airborne diseases are spread through small respiratory droplets (see Figure 
10). Due to their size, they can float in the air for several hours, creating a risk 
of people inhaling them (Buhl, et al., 2019). Although SSI allocate a separate 
category for dust-borne transmissions of infection, in some literature it is 
considered a subcategory of airborne transmission (Gao, et al., 2021). Some 
microorganisms can live a long time in dried-out dust, which can build up 
over time on surfaces and get whirled up in the air. This is mainly an issue in 
sterile surroundings, e.g., operation rooms (Buhl, et al., 2019). 
 
2.2.1.3.4 | Vehicle-Borne Transmission Routes 
The spread of microorganisms through contaminated blood, water, 
food, drinks, medications and disinfectants, is called vehicle-borne 
transmission. This category is further separated into four sub-
categories. First is inoculation/blood-borne transmission, which 
happens when contaminated blood inoculates into the recipients of 
infected tissue, skin or mucosa. Second is alimentary transmission, 
which happens when a recipient of pathogenic microorganisms 
consumes contaminated food or drinks. Third are faecal-oral 
transferred infections, which happen when microorganisms from stool 
are ingested in the mouth by faecal-contaminated hands, food, 
artefacts, doorknobs or other contact points. Fourth is insect-borne 
infections (see Figure 11), where infected insects such as flies, 
mosquitoes, rafts, tics and fleas, transfer pathogenic microorganisms 
by penetrating the skin (Buhl, et al., 2019).

2.2.2 | Preventing the Spread of Infections  
To prevent the spread of infections, the chain of infection must be disrupted (Chisari, et al., 2020). Where 
to most effectively disrupt this chain, depends highly on the context and the type of infection. Disrupting 
the chain of infection can be done on societal or individual level. 
 
2.2.2.1 | Preventive Measures on a Societal Level and Individual Level 
Minimising transmission of infectious diseases is one of the main functions of the public health law in 
Denmark (Sunheds- og Ældreministeriet, 2018). Here, different evidence-based preventive measures, such 
as national vaccination programs (Statens Serum Institut, 2018), educational campaigns (Olivarius, Worm, 
Petersen, Kroon, & Lynge, 1992), and policies/regulations (Sundhedsministeriet, 2021a), are used. 
Usually, many of these measures depend on people voluntarily participating. For example, although many 
vaccinations are offered for free to Danish citizens, everyone has the right to not get vaccinated 
(Sunheds- og Ældreministeriet, 2018). 
 
During the COVID-19 pandemic, forced preventive measures were seen both globally and nationally 
(Islam, et al., 2020). The Danish government pushed regulations onto the citizens forcing them to isolate 
themselves, get tested, and work from home (Sundhedsstyrrelsen, 2020; Uddannelse- og 
Forskningsministeriet, 2020). The Danish government additionally used hard nudging, as seen in the use 
of the corona passport, to encourage vaccination (Frost, 2021). Other countries went as far as to lawfully 
force vaccination upon citizens (Bell, 2021). As such, different strategies were used in different countries, 
which indicates a societal responsibility of implementing preventive measures. 
 
Societal preventive measures and individual measures are dependent on each other. As an example, there 
is a high focus from the Danish government on getting people to perform proper hand hygiene 
(Sundhedsministeriet, 2021b). The individual’s ability to do so, is supported by the societal level, e.g., 
through education, however, the behaviour is dependent on e.g., the individual internal motivation 
(Jumaa, 2005). This further increases the complexity of preventing infections. 
 
2.2.2.1 | Hand Hygiene 
Most microorganisms are transferred by the hands, thereby making good hand hygiene an essential 
element in order to break the chain of infection (Allegranzi & Pittet, 2009). Hand hygiene procedures are 
defined as either washing hands with soap and/or using rubbing alcohol (Holt, Ilan, & Kristensen, 2021). 
There are many different recommendations by governmental institutions on when, how, how long, and 
with what tools hand hygiene should be performed. Over time, different recommendations have been 
made in order to be applicable in everyday life (see Figure 12). Overall, it is recommended that you wash 
your hands with soap if your hands are visibly dirty, otherwise rubbing alcohol can be used (Holt, Ilan, & 
Kristensen, 2021). 
 
Often seen as being secondary to handwashing, drying the hands is highly important to avoid the spread 
of microorganisms (Gammon & Hunt, 2019). Microorganisms are more likely to transfer from wet skin 
than from dry skin (Todd,, Barry, Smith, Greig, & Bartleson, 2010). The SSI recommends that hand 
sanitising follows hand washing, however, if hands are not dry, the disinfecting effect of the rubbing 
alcohol will be drastically reduced (Holt, Ilan, & Kristensen, 2021).

2.2.2.2 | Evaluation and Quality of Hand Hygiene 
It can be a challenge to evaluate if our hand hygiene performance is effective, since the effectiveness of 
hand hygiene is dependent on multiple factors (Jensen, Danyluk, Harris, & Schaffner, 2015). As a result, 
many evaluation tools are based on duration, and the recommendations regarding this, varies. For 
example, UNICEF and CDC recommend that hand hygiene should be performed for at least 20 seconds  
(Center for Disease Control and Prevention, 2021; Unicef, 2020), WHO recommends it to be done for 
20-30 seconds (World Health Organization, 2009), and SSI describes the process as something that 
should take 50-60 seconds (including the drying process) (Holt, Ilan, & Kristensen, 2021). These hygiene 
measures do not explain why they focus on duration, but they serve as easily understandable heuristics 
(Jensen, Danyluk, Harris, & Schaffner, 2015). 
 
It is important to note that although hand hygiene is usually evaluated on its duration, other variables 
affect the quality of hand hygiene as well. What you use to wash your hands, the amount and type of 
microorganisms present, and the individual handwashing technique are likewise important for the 
reduction of microorganisms (Jumaa, 2005). 
 
2.2.3 | Infections in a Nursing Home 
There is a high prevalence of infections in nursing homes, which have consequences for the residents 
(Assab & Temime, 2016). As made evident in 2.1.1.1 | Residents in Nursing Homes, the average age of 
people in nursing homes is increasing. Generally, older age residents are predisposed to infections due to 
different risk factors such as weakened immune system and loss of integrity of the physical barriers of 
entry for microorganisms (Richards C. L., 2007; Montoya, Cassone, & Mody, 2016).

2.2.3.1 | Common Nursing Home Infections 
Some infections are more likely to occur and spread in nursing homes than others. These include urinary 
tract infections (UTI), lower respiratory tract infections (LRTI), and gastroenteritis (Montoya, Cassone, & 
Mody, 2016). 
 
UTIs can be caused both by endogenic or exogenic transmission (see 2.2.1.2 | Microorganisms). 
Endogenic transmission of UTIs usually happens for elderly using either diaper or catheter where their 
own microflora ends up creating an infection. This is typically due to lack of sterilisation or infrequent 
changing of the item (Houlind, et al., 2019). On the other hand, UTIs can also occur through exogenic 
transmissions, which can occur in intimate care situations (Omli, et al., 2010). UTIs have been a large 
issue for many years in Danish nursing homes and have resulted in a significantly higher use of antibiotics 
compared to neighbouring countries (Sjøgren, 2021). Issues such as lack of internal communication in the 
nursing homes, the complexity in residents’ health, and the bureaucracy of the health care system, can 
lead to difficulties in diagnosing the residents correctly followed by incorrect medical treatment (Sjøgren, 
2021). Simple interventions in nursing homes have shown an impact in reducing occurrences as well as 
the use of antibiotics of UTIs (Arnold, et al., 2021). 
 
Of the LRTIs, pneumonia and influenza are the most common. Pneumonia is usually either an exogenic 
infection transmitted through contact, droplets, or airborne routes, or endogenic infection caused by poor 
oral hygiene (Montoya, Cassone, & Mody, 2016). As a result, there are different focus areas for preventing 
this disease such as improved oral hygiene and general infection prevention. However, as many types of 
pneumonia are getting increasingly resistant to antimicrobials, it is highly important to improve the 
prevention of the disease (Montoya, Cassone, & Mody, 2016). Influenza is caused by exogenic factors and 
is usually present as a seasonal disease (Lofgren, Fefferman, Naumov, Gorski, & Naumova, 2007). 
Influenza can be a severe and deadly infection for elderly patients. In the US, of the 20,000 yearly deaths 
caused by influenza, 90% of them are people above the age of 65 (Richards C. L., 2007). In addition, 
influenza has been hugely present in the everyday life in both Danish nursing homes and the Danish 
society in relation to the COVID-19 pandemic (Rostgaard, 2020). 
 
For gastroenteritis, the most common (and highly infectious) diseases are norovirus, rotavirus, and 
diarrhoea. Usually, these infections are transmitted through person-to-person contact but can also occur 
as a result of contaminated foods and drinks (Morillo & Timenetsky, 2011). Outbreaks of norovirus and 
rotavirus are often a result of vulnerable individuals in a close-proximity, rather unclean environment and 
they can lead to severe dehydration and even death (Montoya, Cassone, & Mody, 2016). These diseases 
are very infectious due to their explosivity of body fluids with high attack rates once established, resulting 
in fast spread if proper hygiene measures are not implemented (Richards C. L., 2007). Studies have shown 
that improved compliance with hand hygiene measures of the care personnel can lead to significantly 
fewer occurrences of norovirus and effectively decrease the magnitude of infection spread (Assab & 
Temime, 2016). 
 
2.2.3.2 | Infection Control in Nursing Homes 
How to prevent infections and keep them under control in nursing homes, depends on various factors 
such as the type of disease, type of institution, and employees. In this section, we focus on strategies and 
useful measurements for controlling infections.

In order to have effective infection control, nursing homes need to be proactive when it comes to 
identifying infectious diseases and implementing appropriate control practices (Richards C. L., 2007). In 
order to prevent infections in nursing homes and other healthcare institutions, there is a demand for an

2.2.3.2.5 | Isolation of Residents 
An effective way of controlling an outbreak of a disease can be through isolation of the infected 
individuals. In a mathematically modelled intervention, it was shown that the most effective way of 
preventing the spread of norovirus in nursing homes was through isolation of infected residents (Assab & 
Temime, 2016). However, it was not significantly more effective than increased compliance with hand 
hygiene measures among the care personnel. In addition, the implementation of isolation measures can be 
hindered by several factors. First, it is generally not allowed to force a resident in a nursing home into 
isolation (Mikkelsen, et al., 2020), but there are some exceptions. This was for example seen during the 
COVID-19 pandemic, where isolation of residents was widely used in order to keep the outbreak to a 
minimum (Sundhedsstyrrelsen, 2022). Second, isolation can create mental issues, such as depression from 
loneliness (Su, et al., 2022). 
 
2.2.3.2.6 | Education and Training of Personnel 
In order to prevent infections and stop transmission routes, general knowledge is needed. Ongoing 
education of nursing home staff has been proven as highly effective in preventing infections (Montoya, 
Cassone, & Mody, 2016). In order to fulfil and understand guidelines, nursing home employees need 
knowledge on transmission routes, knowledge about clean and unclean procedures, risk evaluation of 
different situations in context, reflection on own behaviour, and knowledge about the relevant guidelines 
(Mikkelsen, et al., 2020). 
 
Infection prevention hygiene is a part of the general education of healthcare workers. The need for well-
educated and knowledgeable care personnel has been broadly acknowledged over the past few years, yet 
the knowledge about hygiene in relation to infection prevention has been lacking in the educational 
material (Montoya, Cassone, & Mody, 2016). The subject of infection prevention hygiene is part of the 
education for nurses, social health assistants, etc., however, a lack of priority and tangibility of hygiene in 
these educations has been identified and criticised, as they lead to gaps in the knowledge. This creates a 
need for ongoing updating and development of the competencies of care personnel (Kjeldsen, Skru op 
for hygiejnen!, 2017). This is further supported by the administrative nursing home leaders’ indication of a 
need for further development of competencies among employees in nursing homes, as presented in 
2.1.1.2 | Employees in Nursing Homes. 
 
2.2.3.3 | Challenges in the Context of Infection Prevention in Nursing Homes 
Although there are a lot of recommendations, guidelines, knowledge, etc. about the prevention of 
infections, the number of residents in nursing homes getting infected, is high (Lee, Lee, Lee, & Park, 
2019). This is partly a consequence of living in a nursing home, as this is an environment with different 
key characteristics that facilitate the spread of infections. The high number of residents getting infected is 
further affected by behaviours that result in low compliance of hand hygienic measures. 
 
First, as mentioned in 2.1.1.3 | Dual Purpose of Being Both a Home and Work Place, a nursing home is 
both a healthcare institution and a home. This can challenge the prevention of infections, as there are 
different environments in the residential and institutional areas of the nursing home, respectively. The 
limitations caused by these different environments are also reflected in government regulations for 
nursing homes, as these regulations differ from those for other institutions, such as hospitals. As an 
example, isolation of patients with highly infectious diseases are allowed in hospitals, but have not been 
an acceptable measure in nursing homes (before COVID-19) (Buhl, et al., 2019). 
 
 
 
Second, nursing homes require a balance between what is seen as an optimal environment for infection 
==PAGE:44==
 
32 
prevention and a homely environment (Pineles, et al., 2017). The homely environment is partly ensured 
by different social options, treatment options, and visitors in the nursing home. Furthermore, there are 
shared facilities, in which close contact with other possibly vulnerable or ill people, can occur. Thus, the 
risk of transmitting diseases from resident to the resident or from employee to resident, is high if hygienic 
compliance is low (Montoya, Cassone, & Mody, 2016). 
 
Third, as mentioned in 2.1.1.1.1 | Residents’ Different Levels of Capabilities, residents living in nursing 
homes need different types of assistance to preserve their quality of life. These care tasks vary, thus 
resulting in different levels of risk of infection spread (Pineles, et al., 2017). Physical distance is not seen 
as a possibility in many of these situations, thereby making hand hygiene compliance and knowledge 
highly important for the prevention of infections (Allegranzi & Pittet, 2009). This complexity in tasks 
combined with the high turnover in staff, as presented in 2.1.1.2 | Employees in Nursing Homes, can 
negatively affect compliance with hygienic measures (Montoya, Cassone, & Mody, 2016). 
 
2.2.4 | Conclusion for Spread of Infections 
An infection occurs when germs have entered the body, increased their population, and caused a reaction 
in the body. In order for an infection to spread, the different factors of the chain of infection need to be 
present. Preventing infections is achieved by breaking the chain of infections, and the most effective way 
to do this depends on the context and type of infection. Infection prevention guidelines are provided on a 
societal level, but we see a gap between the societal guidelines and the employees’ compliance with them. 
Since nursing homes are a place of dual characteristics, there is a need for a tailor-made approach to 
infection prevention. Hand hygiene is one of the most effective infection preventive measure, but 
currently the compliance is low. This can be linked to challenges with preventing infections in nursing 
homes, which include 1) limitations in regulations due to the dual purpose of nursing homes, 2) close 
contact among residents and employees due to shared spaces, and 3) variation and complexity in tasks 
due to different resident needs and capabilities.

2.3 | The Need for Behaviour Change 
The behaviour of individuals is essential to the transmission of diseases. Behaviours such as good hand 
hygiene and keeping a distance, can help disrupt the chain of infections (Allegranzi & Pittet, 2009). 
Health-promoting behaviours and their effects, are generally acknowledged, however, there is a need for 
behaviour change in order to enforce them. This became particularly evident during the COVID-19 
pandemic, where a large-scale behaviour change was needed to incapsulate the disease (West, Michie, 
Rubin, & Amlôt, 2020). In order to achieve such a change, the behaviours of multiple actors including 
governments, health and social care institutions, media outlets, and individuals, must be in alignment and 
complementary to each other. However, this network of actors is highly complex resulting in difficulties 
with creating behaviour change. During the COVID-19 pandemic, the promotion and regulation of 
behaviours such as self-isolation, social distancing, and limited physical contact, placed a significant 
burden on individuals. For this kind of behavioural change, knowledge from both social and behavioural 
science is needed when elaborating policies that aim to change behaviours (Bavel, et al., 2020). 
 
The work structure of nursing homes demands for compliance with hygiene measures, but as mentioned 
in 2.2.3.3 | Challenges in the Context of Infection Prevention in Nursing Homes, the low compliance of 
these, is a collective challenge. Furthermore, there is a disconnect between what policies prescribe and 
what happens in the daily practices in nursing homes due to the complexity and ambiguity in the system. 
An example of this is that despite hand hygiene being a basic hygienic practice and part of the employees’ 
educational backgrounds, the compliance is low (Jumaa, 2005). This shows a need for a behavioural 
change in order to increase compliance among both nursing home residents and employees (Lee, Lee, 
Lee, & Park, 2019). Supporting an ongoing behaviour change strategy is highly complex, especially due to 
the dual purpose of nursing homes. Therefore, additional research that considers the complexity of life in 
nursing homes, is needed in order to propose behavioural change interventions. 
 
2.3.1 | Ambiguity 
There is ambiguity related to the complexity caused by factors such as the dual purpose of nursing homes 
as well as the variation in residents’ needs and employee tasks, as presented in 2.1.1.2 | Employees in 
Nursing Homes. To better grasp the ambiguity of the system, we must first look into what ambiguity is. 
 
Ambiguity as a concept is quite vague and dubious, as made evident by its multiple definitions and their 
ambiguity. Merriam Webster’s Dictionary defines ambiguity as uncertainty (Merriam-Webster, 2022). 
However, the definition given by Oxford English Dictionary present a duality, as it on one hand agrees 
with Webster’s definition, but presents a second aspect in which it can be assigned multiple meanings 
(Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy, 2021). Within the area of decision theory, the definition also 
corresponds to Webster’s definition, but elaborates further on the reasonings behind the uncertainty, 
saying that it is caused by limitations in regards to the reliability, credibility, or adequacy aspects of 
information (Taber, et al., 2015). In the area of science and technology, Andy Stirling includes the 
circumstances of ambiguity in his definition, saying that a situation is ambiguous if the probabilities are 
known but the outcomes are not (Kovacic & Di Felice, 2019). He explains that this unknownness of the 
outcomes is not necessarily a result of lack of knowledge, thereby partly disagreeing with the definition 
given by decision theorists. Instead, the unknownness can be due to one not being able to predict which 
of the known outcomes will end up being realised, which is a result of what he calls contradictory certainties. 
 
As a last addition, when talking about ambiguity as a concept it can negatively affect one’s ability to 
determine the level of validity in arguments (Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy, 2021). Ambiguity 
resolution is therefore essential for one’s cognitive understanding and abilities to interpret information 
(Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy, 2021).

2.3.1 | Existing Research on Strategies to Prevent Infections in Nursing Homes 
Over time, different strategies have been used to change behaviours in order to contain the spread of 
infectious diseases. However, many of the strategies are dependent on changing the behaviour of 
individuals in order to achieve compliance with infection preventive measures, which is highly complex 
(Pittet, 2000). Some specific strategies have been successful in improving the infection prevention in 
nursing homes, such using supervision, mentoring and hierarchical structures of the workplace (Huis, et 
al., 2011). However, most of these strategies focus on either individual behaviour or educational efforts. 
 
In addition to strategies, studies implementing specific interventions aiming to prevent infections in 
nursing homes, have been conducted. The interventions proposed in these studies cover areas such as 
protocol compliance (e.g., hygiene compliance (Yeung, Tam, & Wong, 2015)) education and training 
(Baldwin, et al., 2010), and equipment used on residents (Mody, Krein, & Saint, 2015). 
 
However, there are challenges with both strategies and interventions. Generally, such attempts to change 
behaviours in long-term care facilities often ignore the ambiguities that exist in nursing homes. They 
often either propose organisational policies (instructions, educations) or nudge individual behaviours. 
Thus, there is a neglection of the complexity in the context of nursing homes, in which the intervention is 
implemented. 
 
Additionally, to our knowledge, there is limited research on, and interventions developed for, nursing 
homes compared to other institutions. For example, a large number of studies on behavioural change 
aimed to decrease infection spread, have been conducted in the context of hospitals (Iversen, et al., 2020; 
Owens, et al., 2015; Koll, et al., 2013). As mentioned in 2.2.3.2 | Infection Control in Nursing Homes, 
the context of nursing homes is rather different than that of hospitals (both in the care tasks, regulations, 
etc.). This makes it difficult to translate the interventions and research conducted in hospitals into useful 
knowledge for nursing homes. 
 
Understanding behaviours in an isolated manner through the use of behavioural models can be helpful 
for understanding how they occur. Some behavioural models, such as the Behavioural Change Wheel, 
point toward possible intervention domains (Michie, van Stralen, & West, 2011), however, they provide 
little guidance on how to design the intervention and how to integrate the complexity of the context in it. 
As previously mentioned, interventions aiming to promote behavioural change in nursing homes need to 
acknowledge the complexity of where these behaviours occur. In this sense, behavioural design can 
facilitate the designers’ process of promoting behavioural change. 
 
2.3.2 | Behavioural Design and the COM-B model, Behavioural Change Wheel, 
and Theoretical Domains Framework 
Behavioural design is a process in which knowledge on, and understanding of certain behaviours is used 
when developing practical solutions that aim to influence these behaviours (King, Thompson, & Darzi, 
2014). It uses a foundation of design, social science, and cognitive psychology, from which key insights 
can be used to develop behavioural interventions that aim to achieve the desired behavioural outcome 
(Bay Brix Nielsen, Daalhuizen, & Cash, 2021). The goal of behavioural design is thereby to achieve an 
intended behaviour, which is desired by both the individual as well as society (Khadilkar & Cash, 2020a). 
 
There are many other variation of user-centred design approaches, such as human factors design, human-
centred design, and emotional design, but where behavioural design especially differs from these is in its 
behaviour-changing purpose. Some of these other approaches can result in a change in behaviour, but 
behavioural design is the only one where human actions are changed directly and purposefully (Khadilkar 
& Cash, 2020b). In other words, the target of other design processes is typically a product, whereas the 
==PAGE:47==
 
35 
target in behavioural design is a particular behaviour or change in behaviour. 
 
Behavioural design can be supported by many behavioural models, which can help to understand 
behaviours and their determinants, including the COM-B model (Michie, van Stralen, & West, 2011), the 
Behaviour Change Wheel (Michie, van Stralen, & West, 2011), and the Theoretical Domains Framework 
(Lawton, et al., 2015), to name a few. The COM-B model aims to provide an understanding of a 
behaviour and identifying the behavioural need for change in order for an intervention to be successful 
(Michie, van Stralen, & West, 2011). The Behaviour Change Wheel also aims to provide an understanding 
of a behaviour and additionally determine which types of interventions could prove successful for 
changing the given behaviour (Michie, van Stralen, & West, 2011). Lastly, the Theoretical Domains 
Framework provides a theoretical foundation for understanding the determinants of a particular 
behaviour that can be used to design interventions aimed to change this behaviour (Lawton, et al., 2015). 
 
In other words, a wide variety of models and techniques can be used for the purpose of changing 
behaviours, but behaviour change models focus mainly on understanding the behaviour. The three 
methods mentioned above, all aim to explain the behaviour in some capacity, and the Behaviour Change 
Wheel goes one step further by pointing the designer in a direction for designing the interventions 
(Michie, van Stralen, & West, 2011). However, none of them directly touch upon how to actually design 
these interventions.  Neglecting how to design interventions is problematic, since the interventions are 
key to promote behavioural change. 
 
When looking into behavioural change methods, their approaches to designing interventions vary. The 
approaches can differ in regards to the perspective, from which they aim to intervene (Neidderer, Clune, 
& Ludden, 2018). For example, the SID method has a broader, societal perspective (as described in 
Chapter 3 – Research Approach and Methodology), whereas nudging has a more individual perspective (Fabbro, 
2021). Additionally, design methods can vary depending on what the designer is trying to achieve 
(Neidderer, Clune, & Ludden, 2018). For example, if the designer’s aim is to gain new insights on a 
situation for change, the COM-B model is useful, as it helps to provide an understanding of the 
behaviour (Michie, van Stralen, & West, 2011). On the other hand, if the designer’s aim is to use 
behavioural design methods to support or guide the development of the interventions, the Behavioural 
Change Wheel can be used, as it points the designer in a direction for designing interventions (Michie, 
van Stralen, & West, 2011). Lastly, if the designer’s aim is to provide a framework that facilitates the 
holistic process of designing for behaviour change, the Systematic Framework can be used, as it not only 
views the behaviour in a systematic context, but also focuses on evaluating the interventions (Oinas-
Kukkonen & Harjumaa, 2008). 
 
2.3.2.1 | Ethics 
Because the aim of behavioural design is to change an unwanted behaviour or achieve a desired 
behaviour, it raises some ethical concerns (Wendel, 2013; Verhulsdonck & Shalamova, 2020; OECD, 
2019). The design should help a user perform a certain action by using techniques such as persuasion, 
without forcing the user to do so. In other words, the action cannot be involuntary as the user must retain 
their freedom of choice (Wendel, 2013). 
 
Another ethical concern is regarding transparency. Behavioural design should not be kept a secret from 
the users, which the design is aiming to affect (Wendel, 2013), because doing so can be perceived as 
unethical by restricting the users’ autonomous decisions. Despite many behavioural design interventions, 
particularly nudges, being highly subtle, studies show that the transparency of nudges do not influence 
their effectiveness (Bruuns, Kantorowicz-Reznichenko, Klement, Jonsson, & Rahali, 2018). 
A subcategory to behavioural design is deceptive UX – also known as dark patterns. These occur when a 
designer is attempting to trick a user into performing an action that they do not want to perform 
==PAGE:48==
 
36 
(Verhulsdonck & Shalamova, 2020). By doing so, the user is disempowered and they are misled to act 
against their will, thus resulting in their own interest being neglected. Because of deceptive UX, 
behavioural design is considered a double-edged sword (Verhulsdonck & Shalamova, 2020). 
 
Some ways of ensuring proper ethics when working with behavioural design is by maintaining the user’s 
freedom of choice (Wendel, 2013), providing transparency regarding the design (Wendel, 2013; Bruuns, 
Kantorowicz-Reznichenko, Klement, Jonsson, & Rahali, 2018) and conducting an ethical evaluation of 
the behavioural intervention (OECD, 2019). 
 
2.3.2.2 | Behavioural Design in Complex Systems 
A complex system has the ability to self-organise and adapt its functionalities in order to accommodate 
the conditions within the system’s environment (Comfort, 1994). Despite extensive research and theories 
on both behaviour change and complex systems, behaviour change theories typically do not incorporate 
complex system principles, thus do not target complex systems (Heino, Knittle, Noone, Hasselman, & 
Hankonen, 2021). The need for a complex systems approach for developing interventions is well-
recognised, which further accentuates this gap (Heino, Knittle, Noone, Hasselman, & Hankonen, 2021). 
Additionally, this is supported by the brittleness that is often present in current solutions when seen in a 
complex context (Schmidt & Stenger, 2021). 
 
As a means to reduce brittleness and thereby increase resilience, the approach Strategic Behavioural Public 
Policy is developed (Schmidt & Stenger, 2021). This strategic approach includes: 1) expanding potential 
sources of data, 2) designing interventions with a system view, and 3) monitoring the progress towards 
the desired end outcomes (Schmidt & Stenger, 2021). Through these steps, heterogeneity within the 
group of users that the intervention will reach, is taken into account, thus aiming to accept the complexity 
of the system. When considering behavioural design in complex systems, it is relevant to introduce the 
term complex interventions, which are interventions that can possess complex system properties (Clark, 
2013). Complex interventions are defined as multicomponent, non-linear, and context-dependent 
(Petticrew, et al., 2018). Because of the increasing level of complexity in interventions, there is a growing 
interest in complex interventions (Petticrew, et al., 2018). As a form of extension of complex 
interventions, is the concept of the complexity perspective. This perspective prompts the intervention 
designer to intervene on multiple levels, from an inside-out perspective, and with a focus on the current 
situation regardless of what caused it (Nistelrooji, 2021). Doing so, will allow the designer to better 
understand the world as a whole, thus embracing the complexity (Nistelrooji, 2021). 
 
A set of eight complexity aspects identified through complex systems theory, are important to consider in 
order to operationalise the complexity perspective when developing health system interventions 
(Petticrew, et al., 2018). The eight aspects include: 1) interaction between intervention components, 2) 
interaction of interventions with context, 3) dynamism, 4) adaptivity and co-evolution, 5) emergent 
properties, 6) non-linearity and phase changes, 7) feedback loops, and 8) multiple outcomes and 
dependencies (Petticrew, et al., 2018). Although these aspects make up a foundation for understanding 
the different elements within the complexity perspective, it is not indicated how one should make 
effective use of them when developing interventions, thus revealing a gap in the literature. That being 
said, it is worth mentioning that there has been developed a step-by-step guide for the purpose of using 
these aspects effectively when developing guidelines while adopting a complexity perspective (Movsisyan, 
Rehfuess, & Norris, 2020). This guide includes the following five steps: 1) guideline scoping, 2) 
formulating questions 3) retrieving and synthesising evidence, 4) assessing the evidence, and 5) developing 
recommendations. However, the guide has not yet been used and properly tested on any guidelines 
(Movsisyan, Rehfuess, & Norris, 2020). 
 
As such, behavioural design can be useful when designing for behaviour change in nursing homes for the 
purpose of preventing infections. However, the gap in behavioural design theory when moving from 
==PAGE:49==
 
37 
understanding behaviours to changing them, creates a challenge in doing so. Additionally, the neglection 
of embracing complexity in behavioural design theory is another difficult aspect, as nursing homes are 
found to be highly complex. 
 
2.3.2.3 | Policy-Making Level and Individual Level 
When reviewing existing behavioural change and behavioural design approaches, it becomes evident that 
these typically tackle either a societal level or an individual level (King A. C., 2015). To our knowledge, 
there is scarce knowledge on how to design for the middle ground, which results in difficulties bridging 
the two levels. However, some behavioural design methods partly touch upon the bridging. This is for 
example the case for the COM-B model, which talks about the relationship between the levels, but it does 
not include the interaction between them (Michie, van Stralen, & West, 2011). 
 
Many behavioural interventions tackle the societal level, thereby neglecting the individual. This is for 
example the case with policies that impact individuals, such as isolating them (Buhl, et al., 2019). On the 
other hand, there are also many behavioural interventions that tackle the individual level such as nudging 
people to sanitise their hands through visual cues (Fabbro, 2021). Despite seeing both interventions on a 
societal level and an individual level, behavioural design interventions tackling the bridging of the two are 
very rare (King A. C., 2015). Without focusing on linking the two levels, the complexity of nursing homes 
is neglected. By adopting a perspective that spans the individual level and societal level, as well as moves 
beyond this to consider the circumstances and the complexity of these levels, the interventions will be 
multi-dimensional. This influence the interventions to be more robust, sustainable, and impactful (King 
A. C., 2015). 
 
2.3.3 | Conclusion for Behavioural Change 
This chapter establishes a need for behaviour change to improve infection prevention in nursing homes, 
partly since the elderly are at higher risk of having severe infections. This behaviour change should link 
interventions on a societal and individual level, as existing interventions mainly operate on a single level. 
The disconnect between the two levels are further supported by behavioural design approaches, which 
typically tackle either a societal level or an individual level without addressing how to bridge the two. 
 
Behavioural design as a concept can be considered complex. It is made further complicated since 
behavioural design techniques mainly focus on understanding behaviours, rather than guiding the 
designer on how to change these in order to achieve the desired behaviours. Another complicated aspect 
of behavioural design is the ethics, to which the designer must make many considerations in order to 
ensure that the user does not in any way feel forced or tricked into perform a certain action. 
 
The complexity in nursing homes, especially related to its dual purpose of being both a workplace and a 
home, makes it a challenging context for behavioural change. Behaviour change methods typically do not 
tackle complex systems, thus creating a gap. The need for a complex systems approach for developing 
interventions is well-recognised and increasing, yet still present. As we are considering nursing homes to 
be complex systems, this gap is essential for our project. There is additionally a need for balancing 
optimal infection prevention and a homely environment, which is challenged by the high level of variation 
in tasks and situations that can cause them to be ambiguous. 
 
This has led to the following research question: 
How might one design for ambiguity in regards to infection prevention in the context of nursing 
homes?

To investigate and answer the outlined research question, we have used Research through Design (RtD) 
as an overall approach for the thesis. We use the definition of RtD as “conceptualizing research done by means 
of the skilful practice of design activity revealing research insights” (Krogh, Markussen, & Bang, 2015). By using 
RtD, we have been able to employ methods, practices, and processes typical to the design practice in 
order to generate new knowledge (Zimmerman & Forlizzi, 2014). RtD provides the opportunity to use 
the strength of design as a reflective practise, where reinterpretation and reframing of problems is done 
on a continuous basis. It uses the designer's ability to continuously learn from findings and adjust the 
course of action (Krogh, Markussen, & Bang, 2015). Although RtD has many similarities to typical design 
practice, it is more systematic and explicitly reflective, and it requires more detailed documentation of the 
process and the logic behind this process (Zimmerman & Forlizzi, 2014). 
 
For this project, three design iterations, hereon forward referred to as phases, were conducted as a part of 
the RtD approach. The outline of the overall RtD process can be seen in Figure 16. Phases 1 and 2 
mainly had the same design process: research/selection, design and reflection. For phase 3 this was 
further expanded to: research/selection, design, evaluation and reflection. For each phase, different 
design tools and methods were used. These were all documented and followed by a reflection that 
considered the initial research question. The design practices chosen for the phases were outlined at the 
beginning of the project, but the specific process of each phase (including tools and methods used within 
the design practice) was not defined until the previous phase was complete. 
 
Research/select covers choosing an area of focus and improving the understanding of it. Although this 
step initiates each phase, the thesis considers it as more of a transitional step between the different 
phases. The design step is the design activity. It does not necessarily a demand the result to be a design of 
an intervention, artefact or similar, but instead focuses on using design practices to investigate the 
problem and research question. As an additional step in phase 3, an evaluation of the design intervention 
is carried out concerning the research question. Reflection is done at the end of each phase, with a focus 
on what is learned and can be drawn back to the research question (Zimmerman & Forlizzi, 2014). 
 
In the following sections, we will describe the three RtD phases and what different design frameworks, 
methods, tools, and general project activities we have used in these. Although different focus points and 
(sub)goals are described for each phase, the main goal have been to contribute to the overall RtD process 
and as such, investigate our overall research question.

3.1 | Phase 1 – Social Implications Design 
Phase 1 aimed to provide a thorough understanding of different areas relevant to the context, as well as 
identifying and understanding unwanted behaviour. For this, parts of the Social Implication Design (SID) 
approach were used as inspiration (using the stages: X, Debriefing, Anticipating the Future, and Goal 
setting), as seen in Figure 17. SID comes from the practice of social design, where it is acknowledged that 
designing for society is quite different from designing for an individual user. For this approach, people are 
considered as a part of communities. Furthermore, the relationship between human livelihoods – the day-
to-day worlds within which humans interact – and the systems supporting them is considered as well, 
which aligns well with the complexity of the project context. SID is particularly effective in settings where 
the focus is to foster the common good over the short, medium, or long term, which is the case for this 
project (Tromp & Hekkert, 2018). 
In stage X, a debriefing of the context was done in order to create an initial understanding of the context. 
This was based on early information about the case and literature research. For stage 1 the goal was to 
define a worldview for the thesis. First, the domain of the project was identified which served as a frame 
for the ongoing section of stage 1. This was followed by the development and description of the 
contextual factors, which were based on data gathered from several sources, which will be described later 
on. To get an overview of the context and the information collected, the context factors were sorted into 
different context clusters. In order to identify relations between the different context clusters, structuring 
of the clusters was done using a Venn-diagram. In stage 2, problematic behaviours regarding infection 
prevention identified through observational studies in the nursing homes, were described. The method 5 
Whys was used to try to determine the root causes for the behaviours. The behaviours were then 
considered in relation to the context clusters to see which areas are particularly problematic. Upon 
choosing an area of problematic behaviours, a brainstorm was conducted on possible desired behaviours 
within the area, which determined the goal for the behavioural change. By using laddering and 
categorisation of the desired behaviours, different context statements were combined, resulting in final a

3.2 | Phase 2 – Co-Creation 
To get a deeper understanding of the mindsets of the employees involved and to ensure that the design 
intervention in phase 3 would align with the needs of the employees, the method of co-creation was used. 
Co-creation comes from the design research approach of participatory design, which seeks to actively 
involve the people one wishes to serve, through the design process. Participatory design wishes to involve 
users throughout the design process as far as possible. Here, the use of artefacts as communication and 
thinking tools, is common (Sanders & Stappers, 2012). By having users guide the design process, 
designers have the possibility to get a more holistic view of what a future product or service may include 
(Interaction design Foundation, 2022). 
 
By taking the identified area of intervention from phase 1 as the frame for an overall goal of the co-
creation session, subgoals were formulated and used as the basis for the different parts of the co-creation 
session. Design games were conceptualised, created, and utilised in the co-creation sessions. This was 
followed by a qualitative analysis of the data collected, through which main themes and key insights were 
identified. 
 
3.3 | Phase 3 – Design Intervention 
As a close companion to RtD and Behavioural Design, design interventions are seen as a form of inquiry 
that can be used to investigate a phenomenon (Halse & Boffi, 2015). By creating a design intervention 
based on the overall research question and knowledge gained through phases 1 and 2, we investigated 
some of our core ideas and assumption that were made throughout the RtD process. First, we identified 
an initial behavioural challenge, a goal, and an overall means to achieve this goal. A three-part 
intervention was conducted with a focus on sensitising, intervening, and evaluating the intervention. 
Similar to the co-creation phase, this was followed by a qualitative analysis of the data collected, through 
which main themes and key insights were identified.

4.2.1 | Stage X – Debriefing 
The goal of the project is to investigate how to reduce the spread of infections in nursing homes in 
Copenhagen Municipality. The interest from Copenhagen Municipality and nursing homes is similar, as 
both include a desire to improve the work conditions for the employees. They also share interests related 
to health reasons, as they both care about improving the health of the residents. However, Copenhagen 
Municipality’s health interests span further, as they are additionally invested in improving the health of all 
elderly as well as the general public health. 
 
The objective of the project can be framed both in terms of social impact and the individual user. When 
considering the short-term goal of the project – that is improving hygiene in nursing homes through 
behaviour change to help prevent the spread of infections – it yields individual user benefits for the 
nursing home employees and residents. However, a long-term goal of the project is to improve the health 
of nursing home employees and residents, and eventually improve public health. When considering these 
goals, the objective of the project is therefore framed more so in terms of social impact, contrary to that 
of the short-term goal. Considering the complexity and dependencies between the societal and individual 
level related to nursing homes, it is not surprising that the objective of the project is also touching upon 
both of these framings.

resident feels vulnerable, a resident typically prefers an employee, with whom they feel comfortable and 
perhaps already know, which can clash with the high use of substitutes. This creates an additional demand

When expanding the perspective to include the connection between different levels concerning the 
nursing home system, the communicative aspect proves interesting. How information is understood relies 
on multiple factors including the amount and quality of the information, the channels it is communicated 
through, and who the receiver of the information is. Generally, the amount of information transferred in 
our society is high (Bawden & Robinson, 2020). In addition, the number of channels, through which this 
information is communicated, is growing. Since so much information is given to us constantly, it has 
created an overload of information and also added more distractions to our everyday life. Still, humans 
are limited in their cognitive capacity, meaning we can only process and understand a limited amount of 
this information. People usually tend to interpret and filter the information in a way that makes sense for

their reality. Thus, unclear communication leads to people filling in the blanks themselves, which causes 
them to make subjective interpretations. This situation is very much present in the nursing home context,

time inherent habit, creates the need for willpower to do it in all of the guideline-recommended situations. 
Willpower takes energy, and the more your mental resources are depleted, the more unconscious 
behaviour will take over resulting in people ultimately forgetting to perform hand hygiene. Having future 
risk of infections becoming more evident – as seen in the COVID-19 pandemic – performing proper 
hand hygiene is essential to decreasing risk of infections spreading.

4.2.3.4 | Contextual Paradoxes 
When considering the context statement above, we identified eight key paradoxes (see Figure 24). These 
paradoxes can be divided into three areas pertaining to communication, complexity and motivation, 
respectively. 
 
4.2.3.4.1 | Communication 
There are two main communication paradoxes: Information Overload vs. Cognitive Capacity and Communication 
Sender vs. Communication Receiver (see Figure 24), both of which represent disconnects between the societal 
and the individual level. 
 
The multiple different societal organisations providing regulations and the top-down approach to 
communication sharing in the nursing home system, as presented in 4.2.2.2 | Current System 
Surrounding Nursing Homes, result in large quantities of information being communicated to the care 
personnel through multiple different channels. These large quantities cause the care personnel to often 
experience information overload, as the human cognitive capacity is limited. The societal goal with this 
information is to try to ensure that the care personnel have all of the necessary information on hygiene 
practices needed to live up to hygiene regulations. However, the limited cognitive capacity prohibits the 
care personnel from successfully processing and understanding all of the information provided, thus 
creating a paradox. 
 
Secondly, there is a disconnect in how information is being communicated by the sender to the receiver. 
On the societal level, the information is being communicated using terms such as clean procedures and 
unclean procedures. However, the research indicated a possible lack of knowledge or familiarity with the 
terms, among the employees. If this is indeed the case, it creates a clear disconnect, as entities on the 
societal level are communicating important information in a way that is not comprehendible for the 
employees on the individual level. These individuals and their ability to understand the provided 
information, is a necessity in order for them to be able to carry out the actions called upon in the 
information. 
Figure 24: Contextual Paradoxes 
==PAGE:71==
 
58 
 
4.2.3.4.2 | Complexity 
There are four main complexity paradoxes: Institution vs. Home, Practical Care vs. Social Care, Regulation 
Expectations vs. Regulation Reality, and Static Environment vs. Dynamic Environment (see Figure 24). 
 
The dual purpose of a nursing home being both a home and an institution creates a paradox, because the 
two have conflicting qualities concerning areas such as resident comfortability, work conditions, and 
regulations, as presented in 2.1.1.3 | Dual Purpose of Being Both a Home and Work Place. As such, a 
nursing home needs to balance clinical and comfortable environmental aspects, which proves paradoxical 
due to the complexity in the system of nursing homes. 
 
Secondly, the employees have to balance practical care and social care in order to support resident 
sociality and comfortability, while simultaneously managing the health-promoting care aspect of their job. 
Additionally, the care personnel are primarily motivated by social care tasks. However, it became evident 
in our field research that this type of care is not acknowledged by the other employees in the same way as 
health-promoting care. 
 
The last two complexity-related paradoxes identified through the field research, Regulation Expectations vs. 
Regulation Reality and Static Environment vs. Dynamic Environment, both represent disconnects between the 
societal and the individual level. The regulations are formed on the societal level, but they have proved to 
be unrealistic for the care personnel to follow, thus indicating a societal neglect to consider the individual 
level. For example, hand hygiene regulations in practice result in care personnel having to perform hand 
hygiene over 100 times daily for 60 seconds each time, which is not logistically possible given the current 
resources in nursing homes. This can also affect the employees’ motivation, as the unrealistic expectations 
can refrain them from even attempting to live up to the regulations. 
 
Additionally, the information provided by the entities on the societal level is quite general. The regulations 
are rather generally defined, which suggest that the situations requiring hygiene precautions are black and 
white. However, the complexity in nursing homes result in many grey areas. As such, there is a 
disconnect, as societal level regulations are formed for static environments, whereas the environments in 
reality are highly dynamic. When dealing with circumstances that vary from the standardised performance 
of tasks, the requirements may not directly apply, thus creating ambiguity. The ambiguity results in the 
care personnel having to make subjective interpretations of the provided information. As people think 
differently, subjective interpretations lead to variation in interpretations and thereby variation in actions 
related to hygienic precaution. 
 
4.2.3.4.3 | Motivation 
There are three main paradoxes pertaining to motivation: Present vs. Future, Motivation vs. Information, and 
Self-Identity vs. Hygiene Practices (see Figure 24). 
 
Firstly, the present bias inclines people to have a greater focus on the present, rather than the future. As 
such, the care personnel's hygienic precautions are typically based on the present, which affects the 
frequency and quality of hand hygiene, as they do not fully comprehend the risks of future infections. In 
other words, there is a disconnect between present and future, as the current focus is on the present. 
However, by enhancing the focus and comprehension of the future risks, it could increase hygiene 
motivation among employees. 
 
Another disconnect between the societal and the individual level is evident in the lack of information on 
the purpose of various hygiene regulations. On an individual level, an essential part of an individual’s 
motivation is seeing the logic and a reason for carrying out a certain behaviour. However, this is not taken 
==PAGE:72==
 
59 
into consideration on the societal level, as the regulations only focus on what the individual needs to do, 
but not why this needs to be done. Therefore, the care personnel in nursing homes are only being 
informed on what the regulations are, which they need to follow, but now why each of these regulations 
are in place. As such, the care personnel may not find logic in the regulations and may therefore be less 
likely to find the motivation needed to follow them. 
 
Lastly, the employees tend to view themselves as hygienic and believe that they already take the necessary 
hygienic precautions. However, findings from the field research indicate that this is not the case. This 
results in a disconnect between the employees’ self-identity and their actions, which becomes further 
paradoxical when also considering their lack of awareness and acknowledgement of the disconnect. 
 
4.2.4 | SID Reflection 
Through the use of the SID method, we achieved a thorough understanding of the high level of 
complexity that is seen in nursing home systems. This understanding is centred around our overall 
research question: 
 
How might one design for ambiguity (in relation to avoiding spread of infections) in the context of 
nursing homes? 
 
Upon taking a step back and reflecting on our work thus far, we are able to derive three dimensions that 
are essential in relation to our research question: perceived ambiguity, professional identity, and 
subjectivity (see Table 8). 
 
Perceived ambiguity stems from two main themes: 1) managing complexity and 2) top-down 
communication. Managing complexity is formed based on the three categories: 1) balancing the dual 
purpose of nursing homes, 2) dynamic environment in nursing homes, and 3) power dynamics in nursing 
homes. These categories are the main contributors to the high level of complexity that is seen in nursing 
homes in relation to perceived ambiguity. As such, it is something that needs to be considered when 
designing for ambiguity in the context of nursing homes. Top-down communication as a theme is based 
on the three categories: 1) communicating hygiene regulations, 2) information overload, and 3) 
bureaucratic system. The categories and theme pertain to the disconnect that is seen between the societal 
level and the individual level, where information is hierarchically communicated through organisational 
entities within the system. When designing for ambiguity in the context of nursing homes, this theme 
suggests a need for considering the top-down structure of the communication as well as a facilitation of 
information between the two levels. 
 
Professional identity stems from two main themes: 1) balancing health-promoting and social care and 2) 
care personnel’s motivation. Balancing health-promoting and social care is formed based on the three 
categories: 1) prioritising health-promoting care, 2) recognising the importance of social care, and 3) 
reserve residents’ quality of life. Firstly, the care personnel’s job entails both types of care, but the context 
of social care is more complex and ill-defined, which makes it further difficult to balance the two. This 
balance is therefore vital to consider, when designing for ambiguity in the context of nursing homes. 
Secondly, care personnel’s motivation as a theme related to ambiguity, consists of three categories: 1) 
performing proper hand hygiene, 2) understanding future risks, and 3) being who you perceive. All of 
these categories are important factors that affect the care personnel’s motivation, and could 
advantageously be considered when designing for ambiguity in the context of nursing homes. Exploring 
these categories and the theme of motivation could yield interesting aspects and areas that could be 
utilised to effectively achieve behaviour change.

Lastly, perspectivism stems from two main themes: 1) diversity and 2) varying interpretations. Diversity is

4.3.3 | Co-Creation Findings 
As mentioned, the specified research question, which was the primary focus in the co-creation phase of 
the project, was: 
 
How might one address the role of individuality and identity when designing for behavioural change 
within the ambiguous environment of nursing homes? 
 
Through our research, it quickly came to our attention that there is a language barrier in nursing homes. 
Two out of the six participants struggled with speaking Danish (and English) at times, which resulted in 
us periodically having difficulties understanding them. During the observational studies, it was also seen 
that this language barrier created challenges in regards to communicating with both other employees and 
residents (see appendix B). Since the number of people of non-Danish descent is expected to continue to 
increase, as established in 2.1.1.1 | Residents in Nursing Homes, we likewise expect these communicative 
challenges to become more present. 
 
One of the main aspects that was explored in the co-creation was identity, which was investigated by 
asking the participants to assume another identity for the problem and solution brainstorm in round 3. 
Through the execution of this round, it became evident that the participants were not able to assume 
another identity. Two of the six subjects were, however, able to relate to their persona since they knew 
someone similar in their workplace. Despite this recognition, these two participants were still not able to 
think as that particular persona, but instead could think as themselves being in a situation with the person. 
Therefore, they were partly able to identify problems and potential solutions related to the persona, but 
not in the intended way by assuming the identity of the given persona. Another aspect of recognition was 
seen in regards to the employees’ different tasks. Through the co-creation session, it was discovered that 
the level of familiarity in tasks is reflected in the level of clarity. In other words, the more familiar a task 
is, the clearer it is for the employee to execute.

Additionally, Table 10 shows a difference in the level of agreement between the groups of participants in 
the two nursing homes, the participants in nursing home 1 generally being in closer agreement than those 
in nursing home 2. The participants in nursing home 1 are therefore more aligned in their subjective 
interpretations of the level of cleanliness or uncleanliness, respectively, than those in nursing home 2. 
When an individual makes subjective interpretations, there are multiple personal contributors including 
educational background, prior experience, and identity (Johnson, Cowin, Wilson, & Young, 2012). 
However, there are also external contributors affecting subjective interpretations including the 
informational input that they receive from the nursing home regarding hygiene regulations. As mentioned 
previously in 4.2.2.2 | Current System Surrounding Nursing Homes, information is being communicated 
between entities following a top-down approach. This means that regulations formed on a societal level is 
being transferred through the various entities, eventually reaching the individual level. Considering this 
communication structure, the difference in levels of agreement between the groups of participants in the 
two nursing homes could indicate that there is variation between the informational inputs received from 
the nursing homes. In other words, the information that is provided on a societal level may be translated 
in different ways by the two nursing homes, thus providing the two groups of participants with varying 
information. This could be a possible attribute to the variation in alignment within each of the two 
nursing homes.

The co-creation showed that there is a general consensus among the employees regarding the fact that 
hand hygiene should be performed before a clean procedure and after an unclean procedure. However, 
the definitions and understandings of clean and unclean procedures vary from employee to employee, 
which is indicated in their individual explanations of the two terms (see Appendix N). As these terms are 
being used on the societal level to communicate regulations, it suggests a conflict between 
communication on the societal level, and theoretical knowledge on the individual level. This further 
supports the suggested communicative disconnect between the two levels described in 4.2.3.4.1 |

4.3.4 | Co-Creation Reflection 
In phase 1 of our RtD process, we identified the three dimensions: perceived ambiguity, professional 
identity, and perspectivism. Based on the findings in the co-creation, we are able to elaborate further on 
all of the three dimensions, as seen in Table 12. 
 
Firstly, one of the identified themes under the perceived ambiguity dimension is top-down 
communication. As mentioned in 4.3.3 | Co-Creation Findings, the findings in this phase of the process 
support the suggested communicative disconnect between the terms used on the societal level and the 
definitions on an individual level. Accordingly, we add a new category under the perceived ambiguity 
dimension relating to how these societal terms yield varying individual interpretations, as seen in Table 11. 
 
Another aspect of the communicative disconnect between the societal level and the individual level is the 
informational input that the employees receive from the nursing homes. The findings indicate that this 
input may vary, thus suggesting that different nursing homes may interpret the received input from the 
societal level differently from each other. Therefore, we add a category related to nursing homes’ different 
translations of the information under the perspectivism dimension. As such, the topics of perspectivism 
and interpretations are no longer limited to the individual level but are also valid and present on the 
intermediate level that connects the societal level and the individual level. 
 
Lastly, the findings from the co-creation provide the foundation for adding care personnel’s experience as 
a theme under the professional identity dimension. This theme consists of two categories: 1) assuming a 
recognisable identity and 2) clarity in familiar tasks. The findings showed that it was not possible for the 
employees to assume another identity, but some were, however, able to relate to a recognisable identity. 
This was dependent on their experience, as recognition was the key element to their ability to relate to the 
identity. The clarity in familiar tasks is another aspect related to recognition, as a higher level of familiarity 
with a task resulted in a higher level of clarity for the participant. As familiarity stems from experience, 
this category also contributes to the theme of care personnel’s experience. 
 
Based on the reflections on the findings from the co-creation, we formulate the following specified 
research question, which will be a focal point moving into our next iteration: 
 
How might one support the creation of a common understanding and evaluation of ambiguous 
situations where infection spread may occur in the context of nursing homes?

4.4 | Phase 3 - Design Intervention 
From the co-creation workshop in phase 2, different aspects of interest in regards to answering the 
overall research question, were identified. First, as identified in phase 1 – SID, employees tend to make 
subjective interpretations of guidelines. Additionally, guidelines are communicated in a general way 
through a top-down structure. Furthermore, from phase 2 we saw that the evaluations of the different 
situations were not aligned, as the participants disagreed on whether some of them were clean or unclean. 
These findings will be further investigated through phase 3. The design intervention sessions were 
conducted as a workshop, which consisted of three rounds. 
 
We will first look into the development of the intervention in 4.4.1 | Developing the Design 
Intervention, covering each of the three rounds. This will be followed by a presentation of the results 
from the intervention sessions, presented in 4.4.2 | Results of the Design Intervention. Our findings 
from the sessions will then be presented in 4.4.3 | Design Intervention Findings, and lastly, we will cover 
a reflection on the findings in accordance with RtD in 4.4.4 | Design Intervention Reflection. 
 
4.4.1 | Developing the Design Intervention 
For the design intervention, we started out by identifying 1) a behavioural challenge that we wanted to 
tackle with the intervention, and 2) a desired outcome that was the goal for the intervention (see Figure 
31).  These were based on the different problematic behaviours that were identified through the research 
and observational studies of phase 1 – SID. The selection of the behavioural challenge was based on both 
the overall research question and the specified research question for phase 3 presented in 4.3.4 | Co-
Creation Reflection. 
 
The intervention session was conducted with the same participants as in the co-creation. Similar to the 
co-creation, we held six separate workshops, as each session was carried out with one participant. Each 
workshop consisted of three rounds. Round 1 was a sensitising exercise. Round 2 consisted of video clips 
used to present concretised risk situations within the area of infection spread. Round 3 was used as an 
evaluation of the intervention.

4.4.3 | Design Intervention Findings 
The design intervention sessions yielded four main findings. Firstly, even though it was not initially 
something that we actively wanted to investigate in the intervention session, the sensitising exercise in 
round 1 did yield interesting findings related to the participants’ knowledge. When asked to describe a 
situation related to each of the different transmission routes, the vast majority of the participants did not 
know exactly what each transmission route was and many of them also mixed the different transmission 
routes. This indicates a possible lack of theoretical knowledge among the participants. The findings in the 
literature review further supports the gap, since the nursing home employees’ level of knowledge has been 
criticised, as presented in 2.2.3.2.6 | Education and Training of Personnel. Furthermore, the lack 
challenges the understanding of theoretical terms, such as clean and unclean processes. This supports the 
previously identified conflict between societal-level information, and how it is interpreted by the 
individual. 
 
Secondly, although the lack of theoretical knowledge could be expected to serve as an issue for the 
participants when identifying different possible problematic behaviours in the videos, this was not the 
case. Some participants naturally performed better than others, but the participants identified many of the 
same problematic behaviours and gave similar reasonings for identifying them as risky. As shown in Table 
16, the participants managed to identify at least one of almost every problematic situation. Additionally, 
when viewing the identifications of behaviours for each nursing home, only one situation is not identified 
at all either one of the nursing homes, and most of the situations are identified by the vast majority of the 
participants, as seen in Table 17. This indicates that when an ambiguously perceived situation is 
concretised in regard to the context of the task, the participants are able to much better recognise the 
risks of infection related to problematic behaviours. The difference in the ability to identify risks in 
concrete and ambiguously perceived situations, suggests that there is a missing link between theoretical 
knowledge and practical knowledge when it comes to infection spread. Even though the participants may 
not be able to define the transmission routes from a theoretical perspective, they are able to identify them 
in practice. 
 
In addition, we need to note that the results regarding the identification of problematic behaviours in 
situations that are perceived as ambiguous, may be affected by the fact that some situations were shown 
more often than others, as their number of occurrences varied between 1 and 4. By showing some 
situations more times than others, the chances of the participant identifying that particular situation are 
higher than that of a situation shown fewer times – and vice versa.

Fourth, as mentioned, the evaluations of situations are different when comparing the two nursing homes. 
The main difference is that the alignment of participant evaluations from nursing home 2 is increased, 
while it is decreased in nursing home 1. This supports the finding from phase 2 that point to the fact that 
the informational input given by the nursing homes to the employees, may vary from nursing home to 
nursing home (see 4.3.3 | Co-Creation Findings). As mentioned in 4.3.3 | Co-Creation Findings, 
subjective interpretations can be affected by internal factors and external contributors. Because there are 
similarities in the interpretations within each group of participants, yet still major differences in the 
interpretations between the two groups, it indicates that their external contributors may vary. The 
participants all have similar educational backgrounds and 5+ years of experience in the field. This makes 
their place of work the major difference between the two groups of employees when considering external 
contributors. As such, we see an indication of varying informational inputs from the nursing homes to the 
employees, thus indicating a variation in translation of information from nursing home to nursing home.
Lastly, when comparing the participants’ evaluation of situations, with the problematic behaviours 
identified by them in the video clips, it lead to an interesting finding, as these did not always align (see 
Figure 35). For two specific situations (use of telephone or tablet and touching one’s own face), some 
participants explicitly disregarded their video clip identifications when making the evaluations of the 
situations in the following round. E.g., despite participant 3 continuously identifying an employee’s use of 
phone during the video clips, as something that was “full of bacteria”, the participant evaluated the 
corresponding situation as having a low risk of infection spread (see Figure 35). As such, when situations 
are concretised, it does not necessarily translate into reflection.

4.4.4 | Design Intervention Reflection 
Based on the findings from phase 1 and phase 2 of our RtD process, we have extracted and further 
explored different dimensions, themes and categories. The findings of phase 3 allow us to further 
elaborate on two of the three dimensions: 1) professional identity and 2) perspectivism (see Table 20).  
 
For the professional identity dimension, the theme of care personnel's experience was expanded to 
include the category: missing link between theoretical foundation and practical knowledge. Currently, we 
see that the care personnel have difficulties connecting concepts from theoretical knowledge regarding 
infection prevention, to practical knowledge on the same topic in the context of nursing homes. This 
missing link could stem from a lack of theoretical foundation for infection prevention. However, it could 
also stem from the theoretical knowledge being rather homogeneous and oversimplified, whereas the 
practical knowledge is acquired in the dynamic environment of the nursing home, as mentioned in 4.2.4 | 
SID Reflection. The dynamicity and complexity of the nursing home make the practical knowledge 
embrace ambiguously perceived situations to a larger extent than the theoretical knowledge. It is 
important that there is a link as well as a balance, between these two types of knowledge when evaluating 
situations that are perceived as ambiguous. Both theoretical knowledge and practical knowledge are 
important in order to make competent and desirable evaluations that consider both the risk of infection 
spread and nursing home residents’ quality of life. Currently, it appears that the employees mainly base 
their evaluations on practical knowledge, and as this type of knowledge stems from the care personnel’s 
experience, it is placed accordingly under the corresponding theme. 
 
For the perspectivism dimension, we have further elaborated the theme pertaining to varying 
interpretations. Here, the categories 1) nursing homes’ subjective translation of information, and 2) 
variation in information input of employees, have been added as an additional basis for the theme. The 
second category concerns the varying informational input that the employees at the individual level 
receive from the nursing home on the intermediate level. This leads to the first category, as it indicates 
that the nursing homes make subjective translations of the information given on the societal level. As 
mentioned in 4.2.4 | SID Reflection and elaborated on in the findings of this phase, the current 
subjective interpretations on the individual level are affected by the varying inputs given from each 
nursing home. When an individual makes subjective interpretations, multiple personal contributors add to 
their understanding. However, the interpretation is further affected by the external contributor of the 
specific nursing home context of which the user is a part. Therefore, these categories support and expand 
the subjective interpretation theme in the perspectivism dimension.

As presented in 1.1 | Project Aim, our overall research question is: 
 
How might one design for ambiguity in regards to infection prevention in the context of nursing 
homes? 
 
By using RtD as our approach, we have executed a design project and reflected upon this from a 
theoretical research perspective. This has led us to two specified research questions: 
 
1) How might one design for ambiguity (in relation to avoiding spread of infections) in the context of 
nursing homes? 
 
 
2) How might one support the creation of a common understanding and evaluation of ambiguous 
situations where infection spread may occur in the context of nursing homes? 
 
By answering these specified research questions, it will help us to add and build a foundation for 
answering our overall research question. In 5.1 | Dimensions’ Correspondence to Theory we look into 
how the three identified dimensions relate to prior literature and how our findings contribute to theory. 
The relations between the dimensions are described in 5.2 | Dimension Relations. These are further 
explored in 5.3 | Broad View on Dimension Relations, where the perspective is broadened to view the 
relations across all three dimensions in order to address our overall research question. In 5.4 | 
Implications and Further Research we will review our implications and further work and lastly, 5.5 | 
Limitations will present our limitations in regards to the design process and research. 
 
5.1 | Dimensions’ Correspondence to Theory 
As part of the RtD method, we made a theoretical reflection after each of our three phases of the project. 
The product of these reflections was summed up in Table 21, which covered the three dimensions: 
perceived ambiguity, professional identity, and perspectivism. We will now consider the findings for each 
dimension and their correspondence to theory. 
 
5.1.1 | Perceived Ambiguity 
Our research indicates that the perceived ambiguity in our context consists of two main categories: a 
complexity category and a communication category. Both of these categories align with prior research. 
The first category, complexity, is commonly agreed upon as being either part of, or completely constitutes 
the definition of ambiguity, as established in 2.3.1 | Ambiguity. The second category, communication, is 
viewed as being an essential part of ambiguity in existing literature, which is made evident through its 
recognition within the concept linguistic ambiguity (Boyarskaya, 2019; Kadlub, 2017). Prior literature 
suggests that ambiguity can affect one’s ability to determine the validity of arguments, as presented in 
2.3.1 | Ambiguity. This corresponds with our findings on regulation logic, in which logic was found to be 
essential in order for the employees to comprehend and comply the regulations, as indicated in 4.2.3.4.3 | 
Motivation. 
 
Additionally, theory suggests that problems related to disambiguation of communicated information, can 
have multiple different causes, which include the information recipient having a lack of knowledge or 
making wrong deductions (Boyarskaya, 2019). The occurrence of incorrect individual deductions can 
happen due to a variation in individual attributes. These can be within areas including cognition, 
==PAGE:105==
 
92 
professionalism, and sociality (Boyarskaya, 2019) . The two causes related to lack of knowledge and 
making wrong deductions, correspond well with our findings in regards to the communication category. 
This category concerns varying individual definitions due to terms used by the information source, as well 
as varying translation of information, thus aligning with the two causes presented in prior literature. 
However, through our research we additionally see another side to the communication category, which is 
the information overload, as presented in 4.2.3.4.1 | Communication. This contradicts the cause related 
to lack of knowledge, as our research also suggests that an overload of information can contribute to the 
perceived ambiguity. The idea that information overload can increase the level of perceived ambiguity has 
been previously suggested in theory, but it has had a much smaller focus than the lack of information 
(Menon & Kyung, 2020). 
 
In continuation of communication, we now take a look at policy-making and the role of ambiguity in this 
matter. Typically, scientific literature suggests that the aim should be to reduce ambiguity (Kovacic & Di 
Felice, 2019). However, recent theory proposes that the challenge is to recognise the ambiguity, which 
causes the attempted reduction of ambiguity to be ineffective at times (Kovacic & Di Felice, 2019). In the 
context of nursing homes, we see that there has been an attempt to reduce perceived ambiguity by 
generalising the hygiene regulations that are formulated on the societal level – an example of this is the 
use of terms such clean and unclean procedure. However, through our research it was found that the 
application of regulations on the individual level was still perceived to be ambiguous, due to the perceived 
ambiguity of the surrounding context. By making the communication more abstract and thereby 
simplifying it, the perceived ambiguity for the employees increases, which forces them to make 
evaluations in certain situations. Therefore, contradictory to what most scientific literature suggests 
(Kovacic & Di Felice, 2019), our research indicates that the formulation of regulations should not focus 
on aiming to reduce ambiguity. Instead, it should concentrate on recognising the perceived ambiguous 
context, in which it is being used and try to accommodate this as best as possible. 
 
5.1.2 | Professional Identity 
Our research indicates that professional identity in the context of nursing homes consists of three main 
categories: a category on balancing tasks, a motivation category, and an experience category. All three 
categories align with prior research. 
 
Professional identity, although difficult to define clearly (Trede, Macklin, & Bridges, 2012; Johnson, 
Cowin, Wilson, & Young, 2012), is generally described as one’s personal view of themself in their 
professional role (Hoeve, Jansen, & Roodbol, 2013). It is based on elements such as attributes, traits, 
beliefs, values and experiences (Johnson, Cowin, Wilson, & Young, 2012), thus corresponding well with 
our identified experience category of professional identity. Additionally, prior literature has identified 
three major dimensions of professional identity for health promoting practitioners: 1) behaviour, 2) 
cognition and 3) motivation (Biehl, Wieber, Abegglen, & Glässel, 2021), thereby illustrating the alignment 
of our identified motivation category with existing research. 
 
Lastly, expanding our view and looking into identity as a concept, this has been established as a key 
determinant of an individual’s prioritisation of the different roles, which they perceive themself to have 
(Janke, et al., 2021). This corresponds to our identified category on balancing tasks, as the employees are 
balancing their roles as a health-promoting care taker and a social care taker, respectively. However, 
through our findings we apply the existing research on prioritisation of work determined by identity, in a 
new context – namely professional identity. This theoretical contribution suggests that in workplaces 
where an employee assumes more than one role, the professional identity influences which role is 
assumed at which time. Therefore, as the assumed role dictates which tasks the nursing home employee 
should prioritise, we establish a link between professional identity and prioritisation of tasks. 
 
==PAGE:106==
 
93 
5.1.3 | Perspectivism 
Our research indicates that perspectivism in our context consists of two main categories: a diversity 
category and a category on varying interpretations. Both of these categories align with prior literature. 
Perspectivism is defined as a philosophical concept, in which interpretive processes uses different points 
of view (Merriam-Webster, 2022). Basing interpretations on different points of view creates variation in 
the interpretation, thus corresponding to our category on varying interpretations. As for the first category, 
diversity is defined as the state of having or consisting of elements that are different from each other 
(Merriam-Webster, u.d.). Due to perspectivism’s basis on varying views, it corresponds well with our 
identified diversity category. As such, our findings on perspectivism in the context of nursing homes do 
not yield any apparent theoretical contributions. 
 
5.2 | Dimension Relations 
In order to get a more holistic understanding of the dimensions, we now explore the relations between 
them. These relations are visualised in Figure 33. All of the three dimensions are part of the surrounding 
complex system that is the nursing homes. Within this system, the professional identity and perspectivism 
dimensions are both related to the employees’ self, and helps to form their perceived ambiguity. Thus, 
professional identity and perspectivism can be viewed as internal dimensions with the perceived 
ambiguity as an outcome, as depicted in Figure 36. 
 
5.2.1 | Perceived Ambiguity and Perspectivism 
When considering the perceived ambiguity and perspectivism dimensions, it is evident that they have 
important relations. The ambiguous situations in nursing homes in many ways create a need for making 
interpretations. These interpretations become subjective, as people have individual perspectives, which is 
the essence of perspectivism, as established in 6.1.3 | Perspectivism. Without perceived ambiguity, it 
decreases the employees’ need for making evaluations, which are the basis for the subjective 
interpretations.

further dubiety among employees, as our research has indicated. In other words, a context, within which 
variation in perspectives leading to different subjective interpretations of situations, can result in 
employees having a higher level of uncertainty related to their work. This way, perspectivism can act as a 
contributor to perceived ambiguity. 
 
These identified relations between perceived ambiguity and perspectivism has to our knowledge not been 
widely researched. In organisation theory, researchers associate complexity with multiple perspectives or 
approaches (Burrell & Morgan, 1983; Martin, 1992). Additionally, a link is established between ambiguity 
and complexity stating that “complex environments breed ambiguity” (Thiry, 2011). As such, organisation 
theory creates an indirect connection between ambiguity and perspectivism through the common element 
of complexity. Our findings however, point to the existence of a direct connection between perceived 
ambiguity and perspectivism; the perceived ambiguity leads to the interpretations based on individual 
perspectives, and oppositely perspectivism can contribute to perceived ambiguity due to the influence of 
varying interpretations. Additionally, this connection is found in the context of nursing homes, in which a 
relation – neither direct or nor indirect – to our knowledge has not been explored. 
 
5.2.2 | Perceived Ambiguity and Professional Identity 
When considering the perceived ambiguity and professional identity dimensions, we see essential relations 
between them. Situations with perceived ambiguity make the professional identity become more evident, 
as the employees have to use it more in order to make their evaluations of the situations. In these 
situations, the foundation for their actions is formed by their motivation and experience, which are some 
of the main contributors to their professional identity. As such, their professional identity plays a larger 
role in these situations, which can affect their perceived ambiguity. 
 
Prior research recognises the fact that ambiguity can affect identity, but this effect concerns identity 
development (Acunã & Sanfuentes, 2016), rather than the change in significance of professional identity 
as a result of perceived ambiguity. Furthermore, the effect is related to general identity, whereas our 
research points to effects on professional identity. Therefore, our research points to a theoretical 
contribution that links the effect of perceived ambiguity to the extent of use of professional identity, 
suggesting that a higher level of perceived ambiguity leads to a higher extent in use of professional 
identity. 
 
On the other hand, professional identity affects the perceived level of ambiguity. The main contributors 
to the professional identity, including motivation and experience, affect the quality of the care that they 
provide. For example, having a high level of experience decreases the number of unfamiliar tasks. 
Previous research related to lexical ambiguity (ambiguity in semantics) shows that individuals are only 
hindered in making evaluations, when the informational input is in direct conflict with the previously 
assigned meaning (Simpson & Kang, 1994). This partly corresponds with our findings, however, we view 
it in a different context and in a slightly different way. Our findings indicate that when an employee has 
familiarised themself with a task by having performed it multiple times, they will likely consider it to be 
less ambiguous. As such, the contributors to professional identity can reduce the perceived level of 
ambiguity in the tasks, thus resulting in less frequent need for new evaluations of situations. 
 
Here, we consider our second specified research question: 
 
How might one support the creation of a common understanding and evaluation of ambiguous 
situations where infection spread may occur in the context of nursing homes? 
==PAGE:108==
 
95 
Because professional identity can reduce the perceived level of ambiguity, this can be a useful component 
to support the creation of a common evaluation of situations that are perceived as ambiguous. By 
influencing the employees’ professional identity to be more aligned and have more shared values and 
similar contributors, it can influence them to be in closer agreement when making these evaluations. 
 
5.2.3 | Perspectivism and Professional Identity 
When considering the perspectivism and professional identity dimensions, much like the other 
dimensions, we see interesting relations between them. An employee’s professional identity affects their 
subjective interpretations, as the contributors, including motivation and experience, factor into their 
judgement and final evaluation of situations. 
 
On the other hand, perspectivism helps to define professional identity. The internal contributors, 
including personal values, interests and attitude, on which one’s perspective is based, all additionally affect 
the formation of a person’s professional identity. Therefore, one’s perspective and professional identity 
are interdependent on each other, as each one affects the other. 
 
Existing research linking perspectivism and professional identity together, is to our knowledge quite 
limited. However, research concerning the medical profession suggests that professional identity is “a 
subjective effort of lifelong learning and identification” (Olesen, 2001). Thus, prior literature 
acknowledges a relation between professional identity and subjectivity, which we argue correlates with the 
subjective interpretation aspect of perspectivism. Despite the recognition of this connection, our findings 
indicate a theoretical contribution, in which professional identity and perspectivism in the context of 
nursing homes, is related in the ways described above. 
 
Here, we consider our first specified research question: 
 
How might one design for ambiguity (in relation to avoiding spread of infections) in the context of 
nursing homes? 
 
Individuality can be thought of as a part of perspectivism, since individualism is considered a 
subjectivistic way of viewing the self (Strazzoni, 2015). The explored perspectivism and professional 
identity dimensions and the relationship between these, therefore suggest an interdependent relationship 
between the two. Individuality and professional identity therefore need to be addressed and managed as 
such, when designing for behavioural change within the ambiguous environment of nursing homes. 
 
5.3 | Broad View on Dimension Relations 
Exploring the relations between each of the three dimensions has added to the foundation required in 
order to be able to answer the overall research question: 
 
How might one design for ambiguity in regards to infection prevention in the context of nursing 
homes? 
 
All of the three dimensions have interconnections that are essential, and we now try to view them from a 
more holistic perspective, thereby raising the level to match that of the overall research question. 
 
 
 
==PAGE:109==
 
96 
As discussed above in 6.1.1 | Perceived Ambiguity, perceived ambiguity creates the need for making 
subjective evaluations of situations, where uncertainty is present. However, one’s professional identity 
affects the perceived level of ambiguity, thus rendering it possible to use professional identity as an asset 
when designing for behaviour change within an ambiguous system. Subjective interpretations are formed 
based on internal and external contributors, as mentioned in 4.3.3 | Co-Creation Findings. As the internal 
contributors are personal, these are more difficult to affect in a way that would steer the employees in the 
same direction when evaluating situations that are perceived as ambiguous.  
 
On the other hand, the external contributors, such as the informational inputs from society, are identical 
for all – e.g., the regulations communicated from the societal entities are identical prior to subjective 
interpretations. However, as made evident in 4.3.3 | Co-Creation Findings, the informational inputs from 
the societal level to the nursing homes are interpreted differently by the various nursing homes. This leads 
to the employees in one nursing home receiving one interpretation of the information and those in 
another nursing home receiving a different interpretation. Aligning the informational inputs from the 
nursing homes on the intermediate level to the employees on the individual level, requires the nursing 
homes to have similar interpretations. In other words, the nursing homes need to make aligned, and 
perhaps collective interpretations of the information received from the societal level. By aligning the 
inputs from the nursing homes on the intermediate level to the employees on the individual level, it can in 
terms help to increase level of agreement among the employees when evaluating ambiguous situations. 
Thus, a collective evaluation, rather than an individual one, is created among the employees. 
 
Taking a step back to consider the entirety of a complex system, theory defines three levels within societal 
structures: 1) micro level, which focuses on individuals and their interactions, 2) meso level, which 
focuses on interactions within networks and organisations, and 3) macro level, which focuses on large-
scale and society-wide interactions (University of Saskatchewan, u.d.). We see that the complex system 
that surrounds nursing homes has an intermediate level that is made up by the nursing homes, as 
presented in 4.2.2.2 | Current System Surrounding Nursing Homes. This intermediate level in the context 
of nursing homes connects the societal level and the individual level, thus we argue that the societal level, 
intermediate level, and individual level correspond with the macro level, meso level, and micro level, 
respectively. 
 
Prior literature related to the topic of social movement suggests that the macro level influences the meso 
level as well as the perception on the micro level (Staggenborg, 2002), thus recognising meso level, but 
not its influence on the micro level. Another area of literature looks into the macro, meso and micro level 
in regards to organisational communication, where the meso level is presented as the connecting link that 
involves interaction between macro and micro level (Barbour, 2017). This literature thereby recognises 
the overall role or purpose of the meso level, but disregards what is in fact happening on this level.  
 
Theory on complex systems takes it one step closer to our context by showing that adopting an analytical 
approach to complex systems analysis requires an increased focus on the connection between micro and 
macro level  (Liljenström & Svedin, 2005). It suggests that one focuses on the meso level in order to 
understand the interplay between the macro and micro level within a complex system. This corresponds 
well with our findings, as the nursing homes on the meso level need to make aligned or collective 
interpretations of the informational input given by the societal entities on the macro level. Our findings 
therefore support the suggestion of having an increased attention at the meso level, as this will help to 
facilitate the communication of information between the macro and micro level.

When considering the dimensions from a holistic perspective, we see that collectivity is forming as a vital 
concept for designing for behaviour change within an ambiguously perceived complex systems, as 
illustrated in Figure 37. Firstly, the information communicated by the societal level to the nursing homes 
should be aligned and collectively interpreted by the nursing homes. As a result, the employees on the 
individual level will receive similar informational inputs from the nursing homes, which then need to be 
collectively interpreted by the employees. In order to collectively interpret these, the employees’ 
professional identities are essential, as subjective interpretations lead to variation. 
 
 
Secondly, a different and perhaps more unorthodox theoretical perspective on professional identity, takes 
it from an individual level to a collective level, thus describing a collective identity of a profession 
(Andrew, 2012; Wiles, 2013). This interpretation of professional identity is based on a collectively 
represented profession, whereas the more typical theoretical interpretation is based on an individually 
established self (Wiles, 2013). Our findings therefore suggest a need for a collective professional identity 
of the sort that is presented here, where a profession is collectively represented. By influencing the 
professional identities to have a more common basis, it can create a form of collective professional 
identity for the employees in nursing homes, which can help to ensure a closer alignment when evaluating 
ambiguous situations. Therefore, collectivity is crucial when designing for behaviour change within an 
ambiguously perceived complex system in regards to infections prevention in the context of nursing 
homes. 
 
However, to our knowledge prior literature has focused on collective professional identities for groups 
consisting of professionals within the same field, whereas this is not applicable in our case. The care 
taking professional groups in nursing homes include both nurses, social health helpers, social health 
assistants, as presented in 2.1.1.2 | Employees in Nursing Homes. As such, our findings point to a need 
for further examination of the creation of collective identities for groups consisting of professionals 
across different fields.

5.4 | Implications and Further Research 
Through our research, we support the need for attention to the meso level, as suggested in prior 
literature. However, as presented in 2.3.2.3 | Policy-Making Level and Individual Level, behavioural 
design methods are typically limited to target either the micro or macro level. This indicates the need for 
further research on designing for behavioural change on the meso level and creating interventions that 
function across multiple levels. The meso level, which in our context is the nursing homes, has the role of 
connecting the macro and micro level. The connection between these levels is particularly related to 
facilitating the communication of information between the societal entities and the employees in the 
nursing homes. Our research indicates that the communication of regulations is being interpreted on both 
the meso level and the micro level, thus allowing for two points of failure in regards to aligning the 
interpretations. Therefore, our findings point to a need for establishing a collective professional identity 
among nursing homes on the meso level, as well as a collective identity among the employees in the 
nursing homes. 
 
The collective identity among nursing homes on the meso level was found in our research to be a 
prerequisite for aligning the employees’ interpretations on the micro level. By having a designer be 
attentive to the meso level and the interpretations that happen here, it will foster the creation of a 
foundation that encourages and promotes aligned individual interpretations among the employees on the 
micro level. Prior research has to our knowledge mainly explored collective professional identity among 
individuals on the micro level. Therefore, we see a need for further exploration of collective professional 
identity on a meso level between networks or organisations, rather than groups of individuals. 
 
The collective professional identity among nursing home employees was in many ways found to be 
dependent on an aligned informational input from the nursing homes. Upon receiving an aligned input, 
however, there is still a need for the employees to collectively interpret this input. A collective 
interpretation of this will help to align the employees’ subjective evaluations of situations that can be 
perceived as ambiguous. In order for them to do so, our research indicates a need for establishing a 
collective professional identity among the employees in nursing homes. By encouraging the designer to 
focus on establishing a collective professional identity among individuals on the micro level in a complex 
system, it paves the way towards being able facilitate communication of information between the three 
levels more effectively. 
 
A collective professional identity among individuals on the micro level has in prior literature presumed 
the individuals to be of the same profession. However, this is not the case in the context of nursing 
homes, thus making the establishment of a collective identity among nursing home employees a complex 
task. How a collective professional identity is formed across multiple different disciplines has to our 
knowledge not been looked into. Therefore, our research points to a potentially unidentified and 
unexplored research gap. This gap is not within the scope of our project, thereby indicating that a 
subsequent further exploration is required.

5.5 | Limitations 
Some limitations within our research have been identified. These will be described in the following 
section. For simplicity, the limitations have been separated into two main areas: 1) resource limitations 
and 2) participant limitations. Additionally, as mentioned in 1.1 | Project Aim, some sources may be 
biased. This could potentially influence the research towards a certain direction, but we did, however, stay 
as objective as possible and we considered the potential bias of each source used. 
 
5.5.1 | Resource Limitation 
The amount of time that we – as researchers – had available was limited. Time restrictions led to having 
to make prioritisations regarding what fieldwork to conduct and which different aspects of the project to 
go more in depth with, and vice versa. At the same time, the state of the nursing homes also proved to be 
a limitation in the research. As mentioned in 4.2.2.6 | World View, both human and monetary resources 
are currently limited in nursing homes – a situation which was also expressed by the administration of the 
two nursing homes. The administrations made us aware of the fact that the time we spent with employees 
would both be taken from the elderly. Additionally, it would put pressure on the other employees at 
work, who then had to perform additional care tasks in order to fill in for the participants. This led to 
ethical considerations and a realisation that activities demanding participants, needed to be quite 
restrictive in its time usage, which limited the number of participants we could include in our exercises. 
We tried to accommodate this by optimising the time usages of the exercises, but also by collecting initial 
observational data that did not demand active employee participation. We were thereby able to gather a 
sufficient amount of qualitative fieldwork data (both observational and participatory) to use for our 
analysis. 
 
In addition to the limited resources available, we only had the possibility to work with two nursing homes 
both located in Copenhagen Municipality. This decreases the representativeness of the data across 
nursing homes in general. Therefore, the thesis cannot support a sufficiently validated generalisation of 
nursing homes, but instead it can serve as an indication of interesting areas for further investigation. 
 
Lastly, COVID-19 served as an initial limitation of the project. Although we were able to visit the nursing 
homes while complying with current restrictions, we did have to make ethical considerations around the 
safety of the residents. This restricted the number of observational studies made in the two nursing 
homes, as these were conducted in the common areas of the nursing homes, where the residents were 
also present. This limitation was accommodated by minimising the number of visits to the nursing homes 
and making several observational studies of different situations on the same day. 
 
5.5.2| Participant Limitation 
The qualitative data from the co-creation session and the design intervention were rather restricted in 
number of participants and participant representativeness. As the resources were limited from the 
perspectives of the nursing homes, we were not able to request a specific type of participant for the co-
creation and design intervention. However, we were able to conduct the exercises with the same six 
participants throughout. 
 
The demographics of the participants both served as a limitation to the project and an advantage. All of 
the participant had similar educational background (between 1 and 2 years duration within the same field) 
and they all worked within the same area of the nursing home aspect (direct care of the elderly). As a 
limitation, this added to a narrowing of the perspectives in the answers of the participants, which can 
result in an inaccurate depiction of the system within the two nursing homes. Including participants of 
other educational background, such as nurses, or participants with different roles within the nursing home 
system could have presented valuable insights and broadened the perspectives in the data. However, the 
==PAGE:113==
 
100 
demographics can also be seen as an advantage. Despite limiting the variety of different employee 
backgrounds and roles, the demographics also increase the representativeness of the particular type of 
employees with whom we worked. In other words, having all of our participants be of similar educational 
and experience backgrounds may not provide us with the opportunity to look into a variety of different 
job roles in relation to our research. However, it did allow us to gain a deeper understanding of nursing 
home care takers with 1-2 years of educational backgrounds, in regards to evaluation of situations that can 
be perceived as ambiguous and have a risk of infection spread. 
 
A rather important limitation within data collection was the language barrier between us – as the 
researchers – and the participants. For two of the participants, Danish was not their native tongue, which 
led to the occurrence of multiple misunderstandings. First, both the purposes and the procedures of the 
exercises were not always clearly understood by the participants. Second, issues occurred both for the 
participants trying to understand our questions and for us when trying to understand the participants’ 
answers or explanations. We had to treat this limitation with care in order to not wrongly interpret what 
was said by the participant. To do this, we had to carefully consider how we could ask questions to clarify 
misunderstandings without priming or influencing the participants’ answers in regards to the exercises. 
However, at times the language barrier still resulted in difficulties understanding some of the explanations 
in the data.

Chapter 6 
Conclusion 
==PAGE:116==
 
103 
Elderly citizens are at a higher risk of infection, thereby creating the need for a behaviour change in order 
to improve infection prevention in nursing homes. Nursing homes are complex systems for multiple 
reasons, including their dual purpose of being both a home and an institution, as well as the residents’ 
varying needs and capabilities. This complexity is reflected in the hygienic practices among the employees, 
generally causing a low compliance with the recommended guidelines. One of the reasons for this, is that 
hygiene regulations are provided by organisations on the societal level, which do not successfully consider 
the complexity of the context on the individual level. Additionally, the communication within the system 
follows a top-down structure, meaning that the information is communicated from the societal level and 
passes through multiple entities on the intermediate level, before eventually reaching the employees in the 
individual level. This creates multiple points of failure within the system regarding the facilitation of 
information. 
 
The hygienic guidelines are communicated in a quite general way, using terms such as clean and unclean 
procedure. Due to the complexity of the context, these terms result in many situations being perceived as 
ambiguous by the employees, who therefore need to make subjective interpretations regarding hygiene 
precautions. This results in a variation in hygienic practices and combined with the complexity of the 
system, this establishes a need for a tailor-made infection prevention strategy for nursing homes. 
Improving hygienic practices requires a behaviour change. This behaviour change should link 
interventions on a societal and individual level, as existing behavioural interventions mainly operate on a 
single level. Additionally, behaviour change methods do not typically tackle complex systems, thus 
creating another challenge. 
 
Through a review of relevant literature, we were able to formulate the following overall research question: 
 
How might one design for ambiguity in regards to infection prevention in the context of nursing 
homes? 
 
By following the RtD approach, our design process yielded many interesting findings, which were 
reflected upon. The reflections granted the following two specified research questions: 
 
1) How might one design for ambiguity (in relation to avoiding spread of infections) in the context of 
nursing homes? 
 
2) How might one support the creation of a common understanding and evaluation of ambiguous 
situations where infection spread may occur in the context of nursing homes? 
 
Through the reflections, we identified three dimensions regarding our overall research questions: 1) 
perceived ambiguity, 2) professional identity, and 3) perspectivism. Overall, many of our findings within 
each of these dimensions corresponds well with prior literature. 
 
When considering perceived ambiguity, as established in prior literature, problems with disambiguation 
are typically related to either a lack of knowledge or making wrong deductions, which aligns with our 
findings. However, our research additionally indicates that information overload can contribute to 
perceived ambiguity, thus establishing a need for considering the amount of information being 
communicated, when designing for ambiguous and complex systems. Additionally, reducing ambiguity, 
which is the typically suggested aim suggested in prior literature, was found to be ineffective in the 
context of nursing homes, as simplifying societal-level policies increased the perceived ambiguity among 
employees. As such, our research indicates that aim should not be to reduce ambiguity, but rather 
recognise it and try to accommodate it.  
==PAGE:117==
 
104 
When considering professional identity, prior literature suggests that identity influences an individual’s 
prioritisation of the different roles that they perceive themselves to have. Employees in nursing homes 
have multiple roles, including health-promoting care taker and social care taker, the tasks for which they 
need to balance. Our findings indicate that professional identity influences their assumed role, thus 
affecting their prioritisation of tasks. As such, our findings build in the existing research by applying in a 
new context – namely professional identity. 
 
Perspectivism as an isolated dimension, did not yield any apparent theoretical contributions. However, 
this is not the case when considering perceived ambiguity and perspectivism together. We identify an 
indirect link established in prior literature, between perceived ambiguity and perspectivism, as they both 
pertain to complexity. This is established through the fact that the cause of complexity can be related to 
both ambiguity as well as multiple perspectives. Our findings, however, suggest a direct link between 
perceived ambiguity and perspectivism, as the perceived ambiguity leads to the interpretations based on 
individual perspectives, and oppositely perspectivism can contribute to perceived ambiguity due to the 
influence of varying interpretations. Additionally, this connection is found in the context of nursing 
homes, in which a link – neither direct or nor indirect – to our knowledge has not been explored. 
 
Additionally, when considering perceived ambiguity and professional identity together, we are able to 
provide theoretical insights based on our research. In prior literature, ambiguity’s ability to affect identity 
is recognised, however, our findings suggest a new addition to this. The findings indicate that perceived 
ambiguity can lead to an increase in the significance of professional identity, as familiarity in tasks can 
reduce dubiety. Familiarity in tasks is based on experience, which is a core element of professional 
identity. Thus, we establish a link between perceived ambiguity and professional identity, which apply the 
existing research in a new context. 
 
When considering perspectivism and professional identity, existing research linking the two together, is to 
our knowledge quite limited. However, prior literature establishes a link between professional identity and 
subjectivism, which we consider to be one of the aspects of professional identity. An employee’s 
professional identity affects their interpretations, which are influenced by their individual perspectives. 
On the other hand, perspectivism plays a role in defining professional identity, as it affects the internal 
contributors of this. These links have, to our knowledge, not been identified, thus indicating theoretical 
contributions. 
 
When adopting a broader view, we identified that perceived ambiguity creates the need for making 
subjective evaluations, which leads to varying interpretations. These varying interpretations are influenced 
by both the employees’ individual perspectives as well as their professional identities. As such, 
professional identity can be used as place to intervene when designing for ambiguity in regards to 
infection prevention in the context of nursing homes. One way in which this can be done, is to create a 
collective professional identity in order to increase alignment in the employees’ evaluations in situations 
that are perceived as ambiguous. However, a challenge here is the fact that care personnel in nursing 
homes consist of multiple different types of employees, including nurses, social health helpers, and social 
health assistants. Prior literature on the topic of professional identity is, to our knowledge, limited to 
concern a collective professional identity of only one profession. Therefore, our research implies a need 
for further research on creating a collective professional identity across multiple disciplines. 
 
Another factor that influences the variation in subjective interpretations among the employees, is the 
informational input from the nursing homes. Our findings suggest that this input differs from nursing 
home to nursing home, thus indicating that interpretations are also occurring on the intermediate level of 
the system. Therefore, a collective identity across the nursing homes could additionally help to increase 
alignment in the employees’ evaluations of situations. Creating a collective identity across entities on the 
meso level of a complex system has, to our knowledge, not yet been explored. As such, we identify a need 
for further research on the topic, as well as suggest a particular focus on this level when designing for 
==PAGE:118==
 
105 
behaviour change in the complex system of nursing homes. 
 
All in all, in order to design for ambiguity in regards to infection prevention in the context of nursing 
homes, our research indicates that the designer should be attentive to perceived ambiguity, professional 
identity, and perspectivism in the system. In accordance with this, we suggest a focus on recognising the 
ambiguity in the system and facilitating the communication of information between the societal and 
individual level. In order to do this, our research points to the need for establishing a collective 
professional identity among care personnel in nursing homes, as well as a collective professional identity 
across nursing homes on the intermediate level. By aligning the interpretations made by the nursing 
homes, it helps to bridge the current gap between the societal and individual level.
