Abstract 
 
This report focuses on an optimisation framework for wind farm layouts with the goal of 
minimising cost of energy (COE) from wind farms. This framework is heavily inspired by the 
TOPFARM project, a DTU-led research project into layout optimisation for offshore wind farms, 
however the main novelty is an improved method of computation of fatigue loading above rated 
speed, as this was identified as shortcoming of the TOPFARM approach. This improved method 
of fatigue computation utilises the Dynamic Wake Meandering (DWM) Model in combination 
with surrogate models and load case modularisation, in order to efficiently consider the wake 
effects of multiple upstream turbines on fatigue loads above rated speed. Such an approach is 
required in order to minimise computational cost, whilst still providing an accurate method of 
load evaluation for any arbitrary wind farm layout. Several test cases for this approach are 
presented, and the results evaluated. Overall, the approach shows promise in that it is able to 
provide fatigue assessments above rated speed that are closer to the true multi-wake case loads 
than other approaches, such as considering the first upstream turbine only, as was done in the 
TOPFARM project. A brief discussion of the challenges in extending the framework for use in 
onshore applications is also included.

1. Introduction 
 
1.1. 
Motivation 
 
In order to maximise wind farm efficiency and reduce the cost of energy (COE) from wind power, 
wind farm layouts are often determined during the design phase after several layout iterations. 
However, the layouts chosen are often selected primarily on the basis of the expertise of the 
involved siting engineer, due to computational expense and time limitations making quantitative 
assessment of every possible option not feasible. In addition, the process is usually handled in 
two stages. First, layouts are generally chosen to maximise Annual Energy Production (AEP), 
and then second, the structural load implications for the turbines due to such a layout are 
assessed. If the loads exceed the design capacity of the turbines, then production curtailment is 
often applied to reduce the loading to within allowable limits, however this also reduces AEP. 
Alternatively, another iteration is assessed to attempt to improve the result. Hence, what 
appeared to be an optimal layout with respect to AEP, may not actually be optimal, once loads 
or other constraints are considered. This is a symptom of an attempt to manually optimise a 
multivariate problem, by considering only one variable at a time. In addition, the time required 
per layout generally prohibits more than a few layouts from being assessed for a given project. 
Therefore, notwithstanding the expertise of the siting engineer, it is unlikely that an optimum 
layout will ever be found using this approach. Rather, the result is commonly the best option 
that was assessed in the time available.  
 
 
1.2. 
Aims 
 
This work will firstly aim to present a more comprehensive, systematic and automated approach 
to wind farm layout optimisation, which is intended to address many of the shortcomings of the 
current industry standard, resulting in more optimal layouts identified in the same or less time 
than currently required. It is expected that this will ultimately reduce COE, making both 
onshore and offshore wind more competitive with other energy sources. Within this approach, 
there are however several challenges which must first be overcome before more comprehensive 
layout optimisation will be feasible. These challenges are largely related to the quantification of 
turbine loads in arbitrary layouts, as well as wake modelling in onshore wind farms. These 
challenges will therefore be the focus of the remainder of the report. Several key problems will be 
identified and where possible, potential approaches will be presented which aim to resolve them. 
In order to provide a basis for proper understanding of these challenges, the following sections 
present the background and context, and following this, specific project goals are defined.

2. Background and Current State of the Art 
 
2.1. 
Current Site Assessment and Layout Design Process 
 
The current process for layout optimisation usually begins with a wind conditions measurement 
campaign, utilising a meteorological mast (known as a ‘met mast’) or, more recently, Light 
Detection and Ranging (LIDAR) remote sensing systems. After the measurement campaign is 
completed, this data is filtered and processed to determine the expected wind conditions at the 
site of interest. Commonly derived variables include 10-minute averaged, long-term-corrected 
wind speed and direction probability distributions, as well as turbulence intensity, shear and 
temperature statistics.  
 
This data is then combined with other site specific data, such as the surface roughness, 
orography, obstacles and existing wind farms, using industry standard software packages such as 
WAsP, WindPro, WindSim, etc. If the proposed farm is offshore, variables such as water depth, 
ocean currents, wave height and frequency, sea bed composition and others are also taken into 
account. Based on this combination of data, as well as other constraints such as noise 
restrictions, and minimum distances from dwellings or roads, the siting engineer then usually 
designs several layouts which, based on experience, should provide what they consider to be the 
best trade-off between AEP and turbine structural loading, whilst satisfying other constraints. 
Several turbine models are usually also considered in an attempt to match the turbine to the 
conditions optimally.  
 
Calculations of both AEP and structural loading are then made for each layout. This involves 
the application of flow models of varying levels of computational expense, depending on terrain 
complexity, which aim to predict the ambient wind conditions at the individual turbine locations, 
by extrapolating the measured data from the met mast position and applying modifications to it 
in order to take the local roughness and orography into account. The interactions between 
turbines in the form of wakes are then superimposed onto the ambient wind field using wake 
models, to account for wake effects on power and loads. A key point here is that this method 
only enables calculations to be made once the layout has been defined, and only for the specified 
turbine positions. The entire configuration must be re-calculated if a single turbine position or 
other characteristic is changed, which can be rather time consuming depending on the wake 
models and load assessment approach used. Also key is the fact that these wake models currently 
assume flat terrain and constant orography, which may or may not be valid depending on the 
specific site. 
 
It should also be noted that presently, different methods are used for calculation of AEP and 
loads. This is due to the different input requirements for each output, and the fact that, in order 
to maximise calculation speed, different assumptions and simplifications are made in each case. 
For power production, one of the most popular and still widely used models is the Jensen PARK 
wake model [1],[2]. This model is based on a simple momentum balance and geometric 
considerations, but is stationary and completely neglects turbulence as a factor, or its interaction 
with turbine control systems. Its popularity is justified because, despite its simplicity and very 
loose links to the underlying physics of the flow, the model actually matches the time-averaged 
power output of a wind farm quite well, and because its simplicity makes it very fast to compute. 
==PAGE:17==
 
13 
However, due to its omission of turbulence as a variable, it cannot be used to compute fatigue 
loads.  
 
For fatigue load estimation, there are currently two main approaches commonly used in industry. 
The first is the lower fidelity Frandsen model, also known as the effective turbulence model [3], 
and the second, higher fidelity option is simulation of turbine loading with wake affected inflow 
conditions, using aeroelastic codes such as the DTU-developed HAWC2 [4]. Readers are directed 
to [3] and [4] for a full explanation of these approaches – as comprehensive documentation is 
available elsewhere, such explanations will not be repeated here in detail. However, for the 
purposes of this project there are some important differences, which will be highlighted in 
subsequent sections. For now, it is sufficient to note that only the Frandsen model is routinely 
used for site assessment purposes, as aeroelastic codes are too currently computationally 
expensive, despite often being more accurate. Secondly, like the Jensen PARK model for power 
production, the Frandsen model is stationary in nature, applying a time-averaged approach to 
fatigue loading.  
 
 
2.2. 
Fatigue Load Computation 
 
As turbines spend most of their operational life under normal operational conditions, fatigue 
loads tend to be one of the most important load considerations when designing and siting 
turbines. Material fatigue damage occurs due to many cycles of repeated application and 
relaxation of external forces, something a wind turbine is particularly exposed to given it 
operates in a continually changing wind field and continually rotates. Fatigue load is usually 
assessed through estimation of the lifetime equivalent load (LEL). This is computed by first 
determining the short-term (usually ten minutes) damage-equivalent loading (푆푟,푒푞), for each 
expected set of site conditions and upstream turbine topology (this is layout and wind direction 
dependent). Then the probability-weighted sum of all different load cases is computed to arrive 
at the LEL. The weighting reflects the proportion of time each load case is expected to occur 
over the project lifetime. The term equivalent in both cases refers to the fact that the equivalent 
load is a single load amplitude applied at a constant frequency (usually 1 Hz), that will cause the 
same fatigue damage over the period of interest as the actual varying load amplitude over the 
same time. This can be determined using the Palmgren-Miner approach:

When aeroelastic simulations are used and so a load signal time series is available, the number of 
cycles at each load amplitude from the stochastic load signal are determined using a Rainflow 
counting algorithm. In the case of the Frandsen model, a time-averaged approach is taken 
avoiding the need for full time-based simulations, so Rainflow counting is not required. Instead, 
an “effective turbulence intensity” is used to quantify the level of turbulent fluctuations that is 
equivalent in terms of damage to the varying fluctuations from different wind speeds, directions 
and wake conditions throughout the turbine lifetime. The Palmgren-Miner approach is used in 
both cases (albeit slightly differently) to compute the lifetime equivalent fatigue load in the case 
of aeroelastic simulations, and the effective turbulence intensity for the Frandsen model.  
 
The simplifications made in order to enable fatigue load estimation (a fundamentally dynamics-
driven quantity) from a stationary model, and moreover to do this with sufficient computational 
efficiency, again result in a formulation only loosely based on the underlying physics, and hence 
the Frandsen model is susceptible to inaccurate load estimates, depending on the specific site 
configuration. One example of this is shown in Figure 1 below [5]. In this figure, the Frandsen 
model (solid line with crosses) is compared with both aeroelastic simulations and real-world full 
scale measurements for blade flapwise bending moment equivalent loads, and it can be seen that 
the Frandsen model is overconservative for the majority of sectors, especially those affected by 
single wakes. In the figure, the single wake cases are those that correspond to the dashed vertical 
lines labelled 3.3D. This particular example is for free wind speeds of 8-10 and 14-16 m/s, but the 
trend holds across the full range of wind speeds, upstream turbine spacings, and load channels.  
 
It should also be noted that the Frandsen model is based on the concept of a component-specific 
effective turbulence intensity. Essentially, to account for increased turbulence under wake 
conditions, the assumption is made that operation in a wake results in increased loading, and 
hence fatigue loading for a given component is based on the addition of ambient and wake- 
added turbulence to yield the “effective” turbulence, but the addition relies upon the specific 
Wöhler exponent of each component. This means that for each different material, the calculation 
must be repeated, as the effective turbulence differs for each component.

Another critical point to note for both the Frandsen model and aeroelastic simulations, is that 
both methods were originally designed for offshore wind farm applications, and therefore they do 
not consider the effects of terrain or changing surface roughness in the region upwind of each 
turbine on the development of the wake. Terrain and surface roughness are generally only 
considered in the ambient flow models that are used to model the ambient wind climate.  
 
The key point here is that layout optimisation, and in fact any site assessment that is based on 
inaccurate or overconservative models, is unlikely to yield a layout that is optimal in the real 
world, as the result of any optimisation is only as good as the inputs provided to it. In addition, 
whilst such models have served the industry well to-date, to continue to drive down the cost of 
energy into the future, it is considered necessary to target these inaccuracies and minimise them 
wherever possible. In a marketplace where wind power is competing with an increasing number of 
technologies, every possible improvement must be taken advantage of to maintain and increase 
wind power’s competitiveness.  
 
2.3. 
Need for Improved Optimisation and Associated Challenges  
 
From the above, it should be clear that prevailing site assessment process and the models used 
within it do not lend themselves well to layout optimisation. Aside from the potentially 
overconservative/inaccurate models used, many layouts need to be assessed during a 
comprehensive optimisation, resulting in many time-consuming iterations requiring manual setup. 
This increases the possibility of human error, and relies largely on human assessment of the 
result of each iteration, in order to make decisions about the next layout to try. In addition, 
when considering that the timeframe currently expected for a site assessment in the wind 
industry is usually between one week to a month or two, and that it is common for siting 
engineers to work on multiple projects simultaneously – it would seem optimistic to expect an 
optimum layout to result from such an approach. Regardless of the dedication and skill of the 
siting engineer, the design space is simply too large, and the process too inefficient and 
potentially inaccurate.  
 
Hence, several main issues require addressing to enable improved optimisation of layouts. One 
issue is the highly manual, inefficient process for assessing a given layout, and another is the need 
for a unified, accurate method for computing both power and loads for any arbitrary layout 
simultaneously, whilst somehow taking into account the effects of terrain and surface roughness 
in wake development. The reason this is especially challenging within an optimisation context is 
that this method must be computationally affordable enough that it can be applied many times 
in quick succession, thus rendering automated optimisation feasible.  
 
2.4. 
TOPFARM Project 
 
Several previous projects have been initiated with the goal of addressing these issues, and 
subsequently enabling improved layout optimisation, however very few have considered the 
effects of loads as an input to the optimisation. One of the most notable projects that did 
consider loads is the TOPFARM project. This project was a collaboration between industry and 
research institutions across multiple European countries, coordinated by the Technical University 
of Denmark (DTU). The goal of the project was to develop a holistic approach to optimised 
==PAGE:20==
 
16 
offshore wind farm design, with maximised profit the goal of the optimisation [6]. The 
TOPFARM framework consisted of the construction of a cost function that took into account all 
layout-dependent revenue and costs, such as revenue derived from power production, grid and 
foundation costs, component degradation costs due to fatigue and maintenance costs. The 
number of turbines was considered pre-defined, so the cost of turbines was not layout specific, 
and hence not included.  
 
To evaluate this function for a given layout, models of each of these cost components were 
required. Here, a multi-fidelity approach was employed, whereby simple but fast models were 
used as a “first pass”, and then more computationally intensive, but more accurate, models were 
used to refine the results from the initial optimisation. The first pass utilised stationary wake 
models to make power production computations, while the second, more detailed approach 
employed the Dynamic Wake Meandering (DWM) Model, developed by Larsen et al at 
DTU/Risø [7], to determine not only power production but wake-induced fatigue loads, for input 
into the component degradation and maintenance cost models.  
 
Whilst the use of this wake model allowed for more accurate power production and fatigue 
assessments, it was originally developed primarily for use in aeroelastic simulations, rather than 
layout optimisation. Hence, the computational effort required to assess every combination of 
wake loads for every possible layout and set of inflow conditions (a requirement in order to be 
able to assess arbitrary combinations of turbine positions, as an input to the cost function in an 
optimisation context) was prohibitive, and instead a loads database was generated, with 
interpolation used to approximate the results in between the load cases actually simulated. 
 
For TOPFARM, the input variables for this loads database were limited to wind speed, 
turbulence intensity, the upstream distance of the closest upstream turbine, and the angle 
between the line joining the two turbines and the wind direction. In particular, limiting the 
upstream turbines considered to a single turbine significantly decreased the computational 
complexity, to the point where all combinations of these input variables could be accurately 
represented via interpolation between only 7500 simulation cases [6] (using reasonable step sizes 
for each variable). Interpolation was then used to determine the loads and power output for cases 
in between the simulated combinations. Although the 7500 simulations required significant 
computational effort on a high performance computing cluster, this effort was of a one-time 
nature, and once completed, the power and loads for any arbitrary layout could be computed 
very quickly, thus enabling the cost function to be evaluated in a very short time for a given 
layout, which in turn allowed the subsequent optimisation to proceed at a much faster rate. The 
lifetime revenue and costs associated with the power production and loads were then calculated 
based on the weighted sum of each set of inflow conditions expected during the farm life, with 
the weighting determined based on the probability distributions of wind speed and direction.  
 
In an attempt to maximise speed without sacrificing quality of results, different optimisation 
algorithms were used for the lower and higher fidelity phases. The lower fidelity stage, as 
previously mentioned, used simpler stationary models for power production and did not include 
fatigue loads. For this stage, a global, genetic algorithm optimisation approach was used, to 
avoid the possibility of becoming trapped in local optima, as would have been an issue with 
gradient-based optimisation strategies over such a large modelling domain. The limitation of this 
==PAGE:21==
 
17 
approach however is that possible turbine positions must be pre-defined, hence the possible 
layouts are limited to combinations of these positions. Therefore, the results from this stage were 
then passed onto the higher fidelity phase, and refined using a gradient-based optimisation 
algorithm called the Sequential Linear Programming method, combined with more detailed 
models for both power and loads. This stage used an unstructured domain in order to enable fine 
tuning of the first stage results between the pre-defined positions of the first phase. This resulted 
in both faster convergence to an optimum, and less risk of becoming trapped in local optima.  
 
Overall, the TOPFARM project was successful in its goal of developing a framework for layout 
optimisation based on maximisation of profit, and is notable as a pioneering attempt to include 
loads in the layout optimisation process, as well as for its use of automated, systematic 
optimisation strategies. However, the approach has several limitations that currently prevent 
real-world commercial use on a wide range of projects. The first of these is the limited 
applicability to only offshore wind farms. Given that the framework was only ever intended for 
offshore farms from the outset, it overlooks a number of variables that are arguably required to 
be included if accurate evaluation of costs is to be achieved in an onshore setting. These include 
the effects of varying orography and roughness local wind conditions - for example, turbulence 
intensity, shear, inflow angle and wind direction cannot be considered constant across the entire 
farm in an onshore case. In addition, other external factors such as noise constraints and 
landowner compensation costs were not considered.  
 
The second limitation relates to the use of the DWM model. Whilst this model itself has been 
validated with promising results against measurements from several offshore wind farms (most 
notably Lillgrund in Sweden [8] and Egmond aan Zee in the Netherlands [9]), there are several 
issues with the database approach used in TOPFARM, and in order to explain these in detail, an 
overview of the DWM model itself is first required. Therefore, the model itself shall first be 
outlined, and then the issues with the TOPFARM approach shall be returned to. 
 
2.5. 
The Dynamic Wake Meandering (DWM) Model 
 
The DWM Model is based on the fundamental assumption that wakes are made up of velocity 
deficits that are advected downstream from the wake generating turbine, acting as passive 
tracers following a stochastic “meandering” path dictated by the large scale lateral turbulent 
fluctuations of the ambient wind field.  Such a turbulent wind field is constructed using three 
different components, and the wind field then acts as input to an aeroelastic simulation. The 
three components are: 
 
1. The velocity deficit behind a turbine; 
2. The large scales of the ambient wind field turbulence; 
3. The smaller scale wake-added turbulence localised to the deficits due to boundary layer 
shear at the edge of the wake deficit and shed blade-bound vortices [10] . 
 
These components are illustrated in Figure 2 below. 
 
==PAGE:22==
 
18 
 
Figure 2: Idealised schematic of wake meandering phenomenon with velocity deficits, large scale meandering and 
small scale wake added turbulence. 
The velocity deficit directly downstream of a turbine rotor is initially computed based on a Blade 
Element Momentum (BEM) approach – the classical method by which to assess the amount of 
energy extracted from the free wind, and hence the reduced wind speed profile across the rotor. 
This initial deficit is then propagated to the position of the next downstream turbine (the 
turbine where loads are being assessed) using a propagation model heavily inspired by the Ainslie 
Eddy Viscosity model [11]. This model takes into account the deficit recovery and expansion due 
to ambient turbulence, by applying an eddy viscosity closure to a 2D axisymmetric and 
incompressible approximation of the Navier-Stokes equations. Here it should be noted that 
ambient turbulence is currently considered constant, so the rate of recovery and expansion is not 
modified depending on downstream position. 
 
In the aeroelastic code HAWC2, for a wind farm scenario where there are multiple upstream 
turbines generating wakes, each deficit is treated independently, in that each upstream turbine 
initial deficit is computed based on the turbine operating in free stream wind conditions (not in 
the wake of further upstream turbines). Each deficit is then propagated downstream to the 
position of the rotor of interest for loads assessment, such that the shape and magnitude of the 
deficit at the rotor plane is known independent of the other deficits.  
 
The deficits at the rotor plane are then subjected to meandering in the horizontal and vertical 
directions (i.e. perpendicular to the streamwise direction). This meandering is determined based 
on filtering of the ambient turbulence to remove fluctuations on a scale smaller than the rotor 
diameter, and the relative spacing of the upstream turbines. Lateral turbulence scales smaller 
than the rotor diameter are assumed not to contribute to the meandering, so these are filtered 
out before the meandering path is defined. Finally, small scale turbulent fluctuations are 
superimposed on top of the ambient turbulence in the region local to each deficit, to reflect the 
additional turbulence generated by boundary layer shear at the edge of the deficit and shed 
blade-bound vortices.  
 
==PAGE:23==
 
19 
To combine wakes from multiple sources, a two-pronged approach is currently used in order to 
account for differing regimes below and above rated wind speed. Below rated wind speed, at each 
time step and each grid point covering the rotor plane, the minimum velocity from all deficits is 
used as the deficit velocity at that grid point. Above rated speed on the other hand, all deficit 
velocities are summed at each grid point and each time step. This is required due to the pitching 
of the blades making upstream turbines more ‘transparent’ to the air flow [12] above rated speed. 
The pitching of the blades reduces the relative deficit size from each upstream turbine when 
compared with the below rated case. Below rated wind speed, the first upstream turbine in a row 
slows the wind speed behind it so much that an ‘equilibrium speed’ is reached much earlier in the 
row – essentially after the first turbine. An example of this is shown for different inter-turbine 
spacings in Figure 3 below [12], where a numerical study was carried out to investigate the 
velocity deficit build-up along a row of turbines. For reference, the rated speed of the turbine in 
this case was 11.2 m/s, so the black and red lines represent below rated speed situations. Note 
that in this study, meandering was not included, so the results in Figure 3 assume that the 
deficit centres stay perfectly in line with the row of turbines as they advect downstream. In this 
figure, it can be seen that the equilibrium speed occurs when the deficit generated by a turbine 
and the deficit recovery before the next turbine are balanced, such that for subsequent turbines 
in the row, the wind speed is reduced by the same amount as it recovers before reaching the next 
turbine. Hence, below rated speed, taking the load contribution due to the deficit with the lowest 
velocity is equivalent to the load contribution from the deficit build-up along the full row of 
turbines. Put differently, below rated speed the size of the deficit at turbine 8 is almost the same 
as the size at turbine 2.

2.6. 
Under-Prediction of Loads Above Rated Wind Speed  
 
With an overview of the workings of the DWM model in hand, it is now appropriate to return to 
the issues with the TOPFARM database implementation. To recap, the TOPFARM approach 
relied on a database of power and loads calculations for a number of different input condition 
combinations, and for inflow conditions between the combinations actually computed, 
interpolation was used to estimate power and loads. However, the simulation cases only ever 
considered one upstream turbine, and this required 7500 simulations to cover the possible load 
case combinations with sufficient resolution to enable accurate interpolation. Then, when 
computing loads, the assumption was made that the closest upstream turbine would be the most 
dominant in terms of its contribution to fatigue load, and hence further upstream turbines were 
neglected. For below rated speed, this was a reasonable assumption given that for a row of 
turbines aligned with the wind direction, if the deficit from the furthest upstream turbine passes 
though subsequent upstream turbines before reaching the turbine of interest, then as mentioned 
previously, the deficit does not exactly “build up” along the row of turbines. Rather, the furthest 
upstream turbine extracts the optimum amount of energy from the flow, and the subsequent 
turbine sees a lower wind speed if the deficit passes through it. Hence, it in turn can extract less 
energy from the flow, and the wind speed decreases much less after the second turbine, to the 
point where an equilibrium velocity is reached before the deficit reaches the turbine of interest. 
When meandering is taken into account, even when the mean wind direction is parallel to a row 
of turbines, the deficit from a further upstream turbine only passes through the swept area of the 
next downstream rotor a portion of the time. However, when approximating this scenario by 
neglecting further upstream turbines and only considering the closest upstream turbine, a very 
similar load result is obtained. This is because below rated speed the deficit from a single 
upstream turbine is essentially equivalent to the aggregate deficit of several turbines in a row, 
and only very rarely does the situation occur that the deficit from a further upstream turbine 
‘misses’ the closest upstream turbine but then still ‘hits’ the turbine of interest for loads. Hence, 
==PAGE:28==
 
24 
below rated speed, this simplification captures the main driving mechanism of the fatigue well in 
the vast majority of cases.  
 
When wind speeds above rated speed are considered however, then the single turbine approach is 
no longer valid, as the single deficit of the closest turbine is now not representative of the 
aggregate deficits that build up over multiple turbines. Again, depending on the specific 
turbulence conditions and layout, this may only occur a small portion of the time. However, 
when this does occur, the amplitude of the load cycles can be large, as the free wind speed is high 
and the aggregated velocity deficit is large, so fluctuations between these two are significant.

fatigue load amplitude, raised to a Wöhler exponent of 4 (typical for steel components) is 
equivalent to just 1 cycle of a load amplitude only 3.16 times higher. For blades, assuming a 
Wöhler exponent of 10, the same fatigue damage is seen with a load amplitude only 1.58 times 
higher, as shown below.

If the same approach used in TOPFARM was extended to consider multiple upstream turbines, 
for each extra upstream turbine considered, the number of required simulations would increase 
exponentially. For example, to move from a single upstream turbine to two upstream turbines, 
and limit the possible lateral and streamwise offsets of these turbines to only six, would yield the 
following required number of simulations:

Another challenge when considering loads above rated speed for an onshore wind farm is the 
following: if it is assumed that multiple upstream turbines must be taken into account above 
rated speed, which turbines are considered upstream for a given turbine of interest and a given 
set of inflow conditions? This is because, unlike in offshore wind farms, the presence of the 
terrain causes the wind direction to change throughout the wind farm. For very flat terrain this 
effect may only be modest and possibly negligible, however, as terrain complexity increases, the 
streamlines through a wind farm will deviate progressively more from straight lines.  
 
2.8. 
Other TOPFARM Issues 
 
Within TOPFARM the conversion between the fatigue load and the degradation and 
maintenance costs was based on a probabilistic model which, while logical in nature, was not 
based on any real-world data. In principle, a wind turbine manufacturer should have access to 
data that can be used to determine exactly what cost should be attached to a given lifetime 
equivalent fatigue load, however this is quite commercially sensitive information, so was not 
possible to access in the context of TOPFARM.  
 
In addition, even if such data was available, in practice it is not straightforward to decipher the 
exact effect of an incremental increase in loads on costs, as there are many other reasons for 
maintenance and component replacement cost fluctuations, and many of these causes are in no 
way dependent on layout or fatigue levels. For example, differences in technician skill levels at 
different sites may have a significant impact on maintenance and replacement costs, and this is 
almost impossible to separate from the influence of higher fatigue loading due to site conditions 
or layout. Hence, it is reasonable to suggest that another approach to the use of loads evaluation 
may be more practical.

3. Project Goals 
 
Given the discussions of the previous section, it is fair to say that solutions to a number of 
problems must be found before more comprehensive optimisation will be a possibility, especially 
in an onshore setting. This project therefore attempts to break down the challenges into key 
areas that can be investigated somewhat independently of each other. Thus, the following were 
selected as the primary goals:  
 
1. Consideration of multiple upstream turbines in load calculations, in a way that is 
computationally affordable enough to permit use in an optimisation framework yet 
accurate enough to provide confidence that the solutions found are actually optimal. 
2. Consideration of the effects of terrain and surface roughness on whichever approach is 
determined most feasible from goal 1. 
 
It is acknowledged that there are also numerous other issues that will impact the feasibility of 
improved layout optimisation. Some of these include the selection of appropriate optimisation 
techniques, minimisation of convergence time, and selection of appropriate models for non-wind-
related but important inputs such as grid layout, and operation and maintenance strategies. 
However, it is reasoned that these points are mute without sufficient models of the most 
important inputs into the optimisation process – something that addressing the chosen goals aims 
to resolve. In addition, these goals are fundamental, physics-based issues that are relatively 
specific to wind energy, whereas broader issues of how to “optimise the optimisation” or how to 
model operation and maintenance strategies, are relevant in many disparate fields, and hence 
progress in these areas is expected to continue at a faster rate than the more “niche” physics 
issues relating primarily to the wind industry. With these points in mind, and given the technical 
background of the author, it was deemed to be more valuable to focus on advancing the 
prerequisites for optimisation – accurate models of inputs, rather than the optimisation process 
itself.

4. Objective Function for Optimisation 
 
Whilst issues have been raised with certain aspects of the TOPFARM approach, it is believed 
that the overall framework provides a sound basis for layout optimisation. Therefore, the 
approach proposed here draws strongly from the TOPFARM example. In particular, given that 
cost is in most cases the deciding factor as to whether or not a wind farm is built, framing the 
problem in terms of COE, and hence the use of a cost function to weigh the value of different 
layout options, is considered essential. Maintaining this approach then necessarily requires cost 
models for all components that contribute to the COE, and are layout dependent, or at least all 
components that make a significant difference to COE. It is here in which the proposed approach 
will differ slightly from that of TOPFARM.

Larsen in [6] and used for TOPFARM. The 퐹퐵 would be maximised through the optimisation 
process, signifying a maximal production of energy for the least cost. The 퐹퐵 is defined in terms 
of net present value at year zero over the life of the farm as:

As a final note on the cost function, it should be noted that accurate weighting for each term in 
the function is vital to ensure convergence to an actual optimum layout. Similarly, whilst other 
attempts to optimise layouts have tried to keep the cost function as simple as possible to 
minimise optimisation time, inevitably, this results in a less accurate and less widely applicable 
result, which defeats the purpose of optimisation in the first place. Therefore it is reasoned that a 
properly weighted, detailed cost function must form the basis of any comprehensive optimisation. 
 
With the cost function laid out, the next step is to determine how to evaluate each of the inputs 
for the function terms. For some terms, such as the landowner costs, grid costs, foundation costs, 
turbine costs, inflation and interest rates, evaluation is straightforward. Whilst there may be 
many options for how to evaluate these terms, none of these pose a problem in terms of 
computational expense, mathematical complexity, or physical limitations, therefore they will not 
be discussed further. The terms that do pose such problems however, are the value of power

well as ambient turbulent fluctuations, and as discussed, computation of such loading for any 
arbitrary layout is not trivial. Therefore from this point onwards, the primary task is to 
determine an accurate, computationally efficient method with which to achieve this, as per the 
first project goal.

5. Efficient, Accurate Fatigue Loads Evaluation  
 
As previously alluded to, when attempting to evaluate loads in an optimisation context, below 
rated speed the first upstream turbine only approach appears to be adequate. However, above 
rated speed when more than one upstream turbine must be considered, the main challenges are: 
 
• 
Massive number of load cases to compute, regardless of the method used 
• 
Current methods are either less accurate but more computationally efficient, or more 
accurate and less computationally efficient 
 
Given these issues, and the fact that the DWM model has the potential for improved accuracy, it 
was decided to focus on utilising the DWM model as the fatigue evaluation method of choice. 
Additional benefits of this choice include the fact that that the DWM model is physics-based, 
and is a single model unifying both power and loads evaluation. This is particularly valuable in 
an optimisation context, as switching constantly between models to evaluate power and loads 
separately is inherently inefficient. In addition, use of the DWM model in a site assessment 
context improves consistency between design loads characterisation, in which aeroelastic 
simulations are usually utilised, and site specific turbine suitability assessment.  
 
Considering the above challenges in combination with the preference to utilise the DWM model, 
there would appear to be several possible solution approaches that would render the problems 
manageable. The first would be to somehow select the “important” load cases, and then compute 
these using aeroelastic simulations with DWM. In practice this is not straightforward because 
picking the important load cases is site specific, and in general, complex – and even if this was 
achievable for a given layout, the important cases would likely change as soon as the layout was 
modified, hence this would still result in an infeasible number of computations. 
 
The second, more promising approach would be to try to break the potential cases down into 
“building blocks” that could be used to construct any arbitrary load case, thereby covering all 
possible cases without actually having to run expensive simulations for every case. This 
modularisation would keep the number of required computations within reason – however an 
open question is whether such a breakdown is possible without affecting accuracy to an 
unacceptable extent.  
 
Assuming such a breakdown is possible, it is useful as a starting point to approximate how many 
simulations might be needed in this case. This is required, because as discussed earlier, even if 
only one upstream wake-generating turbine is considered, and the user-modifiable input variables 
are limited to four (wind speed, turbulence intensity, upstream turbine distance and angle from 
wind direction), then approximately 7500 simulations are required to cover the modelling domain 
sufficiently for interpolation, assuming an “even coverage” approach. In extending the approach 
to onshore, it is reasonable to postulate that the following variables should also be included – 
shear exponent, wind direction (i.e. yaw misalignment), inflow angle, and density. Assuming the 
same approach and breaking these extra variables into 6 increments, the required number of 
simulations would then be at least:

With this in mind, inspiration was drawn from Mahulja in [14], who employed surrogate

database with interpolation method to compute loads, a surrogate model was developed to take 
the place of the simple database.  
 
In order to understand the benefit that surrogate modelling can provide, a brief overview of the 
process is now presented, after which the modularisation approach to loads evaluation shall be 
outlined. 
 
 
 
==PAGE:38==

6. The Surrogate Modelling Process 
 
Surrogate modelling is termed as such, because the core concept is to replace computationally 
expensive simulations with a representative model of the results (a surrogate), that can be 
evaluated for any arbitrary combination of input variables much more quickly that having to run 
the actual simulation in each case. A surrogate is generated as follows. First, an initial selection 
of input variable combinations (herein termed sample points) is chosen to be evaluated, using the 
expensive simulation process. Various methods are available through which these sample points 
can be selected. These range from random sampling, to even distribution throughout the sample 
space (also known as the modelling domain), to complex algorithms for specialised applications, 
such as when something may be known a priori about likely behaviour or characteristics of the 
surrogate.  
 
Once these sample points have been simulated, the resulting outputs are analysed using 
computer algorithms, and a model is automatically fitted to the outputs based on this analysis. 
The fit of the model is evaluated using various measures, and based on the quality of the fit 
further sample points are then selected for evaluation using the “expensive” simulation process. 
Once this round of samples is evaluated, they are added to the total pool of samples available for 
use when fitting the model, and a new model is generated. The subsequent sample points are 
generally chosen in areas of the modelling domain where few points have already been evaluated, 
and hence less information is available to guide the fitting process, or similarly, in areas where 
the output appears to be highly non-linear, and hence more information is needed to improve the 
fit. This process is repeated iteratively until one of several possible limits is reached – this could 
be a time limit, a maximum number of simulations limit, or when a specified measure of model 
error is reduced below a pre-set threshold, indicating the model error is acceptable. 
 
Numerous model fitting techniques are also available. These include splines, Kriging, radial basis 
functions, rational functions, least squares support vector machine algorithms, artificial neural 
networks, and many more. The details of these techniques are not the main focus at this stage – 
however it is important to note that different techniques may be more or less appropriate 
depending on the application, and this will affect the accuracy of the final model. 
  
An example of surrogate generation is shown in the plots of Figure 13 below. In this example, the 
modelling technique is Kriging (a mathematical approach named after a South African gold 
miner who pioneered the technique), and the evolution of the model through successive rounds of 
sample evaluation is illustrated. When comparing the initial model based on 24 samples with the 
final model based on 308 samples, it is clear that the addition of extra points improves the fit, as 
expected. However, such an improvement in fit relative to the number of points is possible only 
because the locations of each round of samples are based on knowledge gained from the previous 
rounds. Therefore, if all sample points were chosen at once in what is termed a “one-shot” design, 
then the model error is likely to be higher, because in all but very fortunate cases, the samples 
chosen will necessarily fall in the most advantageous positions for model fitting. As an example of 
this, Figure 11 and Figure 12 below show the progression of error reduction when sequentially 
sampling compared with a one-shot approach. Both versions have the same number of total 
samples with which to fit a model, however as can be seen in the plots, the locations of these 
samples does make a difference to the accuracy of the final model. In this case, the one-shot

design results in 3.27% root relative square error (RRSE), whereas the sequential design results 
in 1.46% RRSE. This is a reduction in error of just over a factor of 2 – significant considering the 
relative simplicity of the relationship being modelled. For more complex relationships with more 
input variables, and hence a higher dimension modelling domain and more non-linear behaviour, 
this error reduction can be higher still.

This illustrates the key benefits of the surrogate modelling process over simpler approaches such 
as database interpolation. Iterative sampling allows samples to be selected in the most critical 
areas with regard to model fitting, hence deriving the most information possible about the 
relationship between the inputs and the outputs with the least number of simulations. Combined 
with appropriate error measures and modelling methods, the result is maximal accuracy with 
minimal error, using a fraction of the samples and hence taking a fraction of the time that a 
simple database approach would take, in order to achieve similar levels of accuracy.  
 
It is thus expected that for load cases involving a single wake-generating turbine, surrogate 
modelling will deliver the required reduction in computation time to enable industrial use. In 
addition, the effort required to generate the surrogate is of a one-time nature for a given turbine 
model, and once this is completed, the resulting model can then be used repeatedly in an 
optimisation context, with almost instant load evaluation for a given set of input variables. It 
==PAGE:41==

should be noted however, that such a surrogate model is turbine model-specific, so if the turbine 
model is revised, such as through control updates to modify power or thrust curves or dynamic 
response, the surrogate model will need to be regenerated. It is expected however, that limiting 
surrogate model generation times to a day or two should be acceptable in order to enable 
surrogate regeneration every few months (given turbine manufacturers currently tend to release 
turbine updates not more than a few times a year).

8. Surrogate Model Development 
 
In order to efficiently generate a surrogate model, automation is a requirement. In this case, the 
process of model generation involves the setup, execution and post-processing of numerous 
aeroelastic simulations. These are set up based on a variety of input variable combinations, and 
are executed using the DTU-developed HAWC2 aeroelastic code. This is followed by post-
processing to derive 1-Hz, 10-minute equivalent fatigue loads, power and other supplementary 
outputs. In order to build the surrogate model, an analysis of these results is then performed, and 
a model is generated which attempts to approximate the results with minimal error. Based on 
the quality of the model, as well as criteria chosen to determine where subsequent samples should 
be evaluated, selection of the next round of input variable combinations is made. This loop 
continues until the model generated meets specified error criteria, depending on the level of 
accuracy required and the measures deemed appropriate for assessing the error. Hence, given that 
several tens of thousands of simulations and corresponding post-processing and analysis must be 
performed, the task cannot be achieved unless it is automated to a large extent.  
 
The main components to enable this automation were as follows: 
1. DTU Risø high performance computing clusters with Torque job scheduling system 
2. MatLab Software Package (Distributed Computing Server not required)  
3. MatLab functions and scripts 
4. PBS/bash scripts 
5. SUMO Surrogate Modelling Toolbox for MatLab 
 
Most high performance computing clusters usually allocate each user a home directory, which is 
the base folder where files can be uploaded, stored and written to during computations or after 
computations have been executed. The cluster is made up of computing “nodes”, with each node 
composed of a number of “cores” or CPUs. Usually, one of the nodes is reserved solely as the 
“login node”, which is the one that users first log into when they access the cluster. From here, 
computation jobs can be submitted to a job scheduler, either directly or using PBS/bash 
scripting. In this case, the scheduler used is the Torque scheduler. This scheduler assesses the 
requested computing resources required for the job, and then submits the job to the job queue 
accordingly, so that cluster resources are managed most efficiently. 
Each user’s home directory is accessible from all nodes, however large amounts of data transfer 
between the home directory and each node lead to very slow computations due to bottlenecks 
associated with read/write speeds across the network. Hence, it is common that each node has a 
“scratch disk”, where files required for the computations are transferred at the beginning of each 
job, to enable fast read/write speeds during the job. The required outputs can then be copied 
back to the home directory upon completion. As part of the job completion, files written to the 
scratch disk during the job are then deleted to make room for the next job.  
It is also important to note, that depending on the cluster setup, it may be the case that not all 
nodes can be used to submit jobs to the job queue. So, if one running job is required to submit 
more “child” jobs to the queue, the login node must first be accessed from the compute node, 
before this can occur. In addition, depending on the software used and the cluster setup, it may 
or may not be possible for one job to utilise more than one node worth of cores for a single job, 
as by default, one node does not have access to the others or their scratch disks.

node are submitted to the queue. It should be noted that this is a very flexible and scalable 
way of handling large numbers of simulations, because it can be adapted for different clusters 
with different hardware, and different job sizes – any number of simulations can be set up to 
run this way, and they will all be run eventually – how long it takes is only a function of 
cluster availability and hardware limitations, rather than programming bottlenecks or 
==PAGE:46==
 
42 
restrictions. Once the jobs are submitted, ‘htc_define_inputs_cluster.m’ then changes 
directories and monitors the results directory for simulation results to appear. 
5. Each ‘.sh’ file is a bash script which submits the simulations to be done on each node to the 
queue. Once the job commences, the ‘top_level_HAWC2_turbine_model’ directory is 
replicated on the scratch disk of the allocated node (once per simulation in subfolders), and then 
‘exec_hawc2.sh’ is called.  
6. ‘exec_hawc2.sh’ is a bash script that reads the ‘.txt’ file for the node, and then executes the 
various HAWC2 simulations (one per node CPU) using the htc files specified in the ‘.txt’ file. 
Once the simulations are complete the result files are moved from the node scratch disk back 
to the home directory ‘res’ folder, where ‘htc_define_inputs_cluster.m’ is monitoring for 
their arrival. 
7. As ‘htc_define_inputs_cluster.m’ registers the arrival of results files, it gradually copies the 
files to the scratch disk of the node it is running on, to avoid slow network access when 
reading the files during upcoming post-processing. This is done progressively to minimise 
time spent waiting for files to copy. Once all the results are copied onto the scratch disk ‘res’ 
directory, ‘htc_define_inputs_cluster.m’ then commences post-processing, which requires the 
various functions shown in the ‘res’ directory above. The output is the 1-Hz, 10-minute 
equivalent fatigue loads for each case (averaged over the number of seeds), for the load 
channels specified in the ‘.inp’ files generated earlier, as well as the average power output. 
The power calculation requires inputs from the ‘.sta’ files, also generated earlier. The 
simulation input variables and outputs are then combined into a single matrix, and returned 
as the output of ‘htc_define_inputs_cluster.m’. No longer required files are then cleaned 
from the various directories. 
8. The sumo toolbox then reads the outputs, and, if the batch size (number of samples specified 
to be passed to ‘htc_define_inputs_cluster.m’ simultaneously) was less than the total 
requested number of samples for this loop through the modelling iteration process, then the 
remaining sample input variable combinations are sent to ‘htc_define_inputs_cluster.m’, 
repeating the process from step 4 until all requested samples have been simulated and post-
processed. 
9. Once all requested samples are available, surrogate model generation commences according to 
the settings specified in ‘default.xml’. The quality of the model is evaluated using the 
measures and error functions specified in ‘default.xml’, and if the error is above the specified 
level, new sample points are then selected for the next round of simulations. These points are 
also selected according to the choices specified in the ‘default.xml’ file, and are generally a 
mixture of areas where the error is high, and unexplored areas in the modelling domain.  
10. When the error drops to below the specified level, or another constraint such as maximum 
run time or maximum samples is reached, the best surrogate model is saved for subsequent 
use.

accuracy, air density was omitted as an input variable and was instead fixed at 1.225 kg/m3. 
This was also justified by the fact that all outputs were expected to scale relatively simply with 
density, so the outputs from the fixed density surrogate could be scaled afterwards to account for 
different densities. Results were not expected to scale so simply when inflow angle and wind 
direction were varied, so ideally these would have been included as input variables. However, it 
became clear that there was no point in attempting this at this stage, because the current version 
of HAWC2 does not consider the effect of wind direction on the development of wake deficits as 
they are advected downstream. Regardless of the wind direction and hence the yaw

misalignment, the wake simply progresses downstream as though there is zero yaw error. Given 
this would not represent effects of wakes correctly, it was deemed to be of little value to include 
this as an input.  
 
Similarly, for inflow angle, although arbitrary inflow angles can be selected in HAWC2, no 
consideration is given to the difference in wake propagation due to inclines or declines in terrain, 
and when wake generating turbines are included in a simulation, the only way that this can even 
loosely represent reality is if the inflow angle is consistent with elevation differences between the 
wake-generating turbines and the wake-affected turbine. This is illustrated in Figure 15 below. In 
the top example, the terrain incline is constant for all turbines, so the wakes will loosely follow 
the same incline, and the inflow angle at the wake-affected turbine can be set to match this 
angle, however this still doesn’t include the effect of non-zero incline on the wake propagation 
itself. In the bottom example, the terrain is undulating along the ‘row’ of turbines, so the wake 
propagation and interaction is very different than in the top example, yet in this case, in 
HAWC2 the inflow angle of the wake-affected turbine is set identically to the top example, in 
which the physics of the wake propagation is rather different.

Within the context of surrogate model generation, there are a wide range of factors to consider, 
that will affect the speed of convergence and the accuracy of the final model. These include: 
 
• 
How large or small should the modelling domain be? Would better results be obtained by 
splitting the modelling domain into sub domains, each considered using a separate 
surrogate? Are there expected to be discontinuities in within the modelling domain which 
would warrant this approach? 
• 
Are all input variables actually required, or can the modelling domain (and hence 
computational expense) be reduced by parameterising some variables in terms of others, 
or simply scaling the results? 
• 
Which method of selecting samples is most appropriate for the specific case? 
• 
Which modelling technique should be used for each output? Can the same technique be 
used for all outputs or are different techniques more appropriate? 
• 
Which model quality measures and corresponding error functions are appropriate for the 
given problem? Should different measures be used for each output or the same measure 
for all outputs? 
• 
For each of these measures, what level of error is acceptable in the context of the 
problem? 
 
A full investigation of all of these questions is a significant undertaking, and is not the primary 
focus of this project, as the use of surrogate modelling is only one part of the proposed solution 
to one of the challenges of layout optimisation. This being said, some thought was put into what 
a reasonable starting point would be, from which to refine the surrogate generation process 
further in the future, and the following decisions were made. With regard to the size of the 
modelling domain, the view was taken that it would be best to first attempt a higher dimensional 
domain including more variables, in order to understand based on the results, whether any of 
these dimensions could be removed in subsequent versions, due the results being able to be 
replicated by simple scaling or parameterisation in terms of other variables. In addition, as 
previous uses of surrogate modelling in this context have already shown that lower dimensional 
domains can be modelled with sufficient accuracy in reasonable timeframes [14], it was deemed to 
be of more value to go beyond these attempts, to quantify the corresponding increase in 
computation time, and any change in model accuracy as a result.  
 
==PAGE:50==
 
46 
With respect to the method for selecting samples, for the initial set of samples, a hybrid 
approach using both a Latin Hypercube and a full factorial method was used. The Latin 
Hypercube method alone breaks down the modelling domain into sub domains, and then chooses 
points within each sub-domain randomly, which is a good way of covering most of the domain, 
whilst ensuring that any correlation between equally spaced variable increments is avoided. The 
inclusion of the full factorial method (with factor 2) as well simply also ensures that the very 
extremities of the domain are evaluated in the initial batch of samples.  
 
For the subsequent sets of samples, a hybrid LOLA-Voronoi and error-based approach was used. 
The LOLA-Voronoi algorithm is itself a kind of hybrid, as it allows a balance between on the one 
hand selecting samples in places where the local relationship between inputs and output is highly 
non-linear, and on the other hand selecting samples in regions where no other samples have been 
evaluated, in order to investigate the relationship in unknown areas. This was combined with an 
error-based approach, where the model is evaluated on a coarse grid, and then samples are 
chosen in places where the model error is estimated to be the largest [16]. For the present 
application, this was considered to be a logical choice given that for most load channels, testing 
showed that the outputs varied smoothly in most of the domain, and the most non-linear 
response tended to arise for samples where the upstream turbine was located only a small 
distance from the wake-affected turbine. Essentially, only a small portion of the overall model 
domain was expected to be relatively non-linear compared to other areas, so to minimise the 
number of samples required to capture the non-linearity, clustering of samples around these areas 
seemed reasonable. Similarly, in more smoothly varying regions, less samples would be needed to 
capture the relationship accurately.  
 
Which modelling technique should be used for each output is a large and complex topic, and full 
consideration of this would require far more time than available for the present project, so model 
selection was in this case based on the recommendations of the authors of the SUMO Toolbox 
[17], as well as the toolbox itself. This was possible as the toolbox provides the functionality to 
use multiple modelling techniques within the one surrogate generation process. This way, when 
each batch of sample results is available, multiple techniques are applied, their resulting models 
assessed based on the specified error criteria, and the model scoring the highest is chosen 
automatically. The chosen technique may change in subsequent rounds of model fitting when 
more points are available, resulting in a “survival of the fittest” approach to modelling technique 
selection. This has the potential to slow the surrogate generation process down considerably, 
depending on how many modelling techniques are included and the requirements of each, so is 
not recommended for every time a revised surrogate is to be built, however as a starting point to 
quantitatively identify which modelling technique performs the best for each output, it is quite a 
robust solution. Then, for new versions of the surrogate, the modelling technique can simply be 
set to the one known to have performed best previously, saving computation time. 
 
With regard to which model assessment criteria (model quality measures, error functions and 
target values) are most appropriate for the present application, choices were again made based 
primarily on recommendations from the authors of the SUMO toolbox [17]. The two main model 
quality measures considered were cross-validation and auto-generated validation sets. These 
methods are somewhat similar in that they both use some of the computed samples to train the 
model, and some of the samples to assess the quality. Cross–validation essentially partitions the 
==PAGE:51==
 
47 
samples into groups in different ways, fits models to all samples except those singled out to assess 
the quality, and then uses the singled-out samples to assess the fit. This is done a number of 
times (5 “folds” is common, but this is dependent on the modelling domain size and number of 
dimensions), and the scores for each fold are combined in the final score. This way, if the model 
fits well across the domain, the score should be high, however, if the model only fits well in parts 
of the domain, the score will only be high for some folds but low for others, so the overall score 
will be lower.   
 
Validation sets simply split the total number of samples up into a training set and validation set. 
Then, the model is fitted using the training set, and finally the model prediction is compared 
with the samples in the validation set. This is can be faster than cross-validation, as the model 
only has to be fit once to the training set, as opposed to each fold. However, depending on how 
the validation set is chosen, the time savings may be offset somewhat, as the overhead to ensure 
that the validation set is spread evenly over the domain can also be significant for high 
dimensional domains. The downside of validation sets is that they “waste” some simulations 
because some samples are not used in actually training the model, whereas cross-validation uses 
all simulations in at least one fold [17].  Hence, in this case, 5-fold cross validation was chosen, in 
the hope that the model fitting would be improved by the ability to make use of all samples.  
 
With regard to error functions and target error thresholds, the following reasoning was employed. 
Firstly, it was obviously considered important that some measure of average error was small, 
however this could still leave open the possibility of large errors in some parts of the model 
domain being offset by small errors in others. Hence, the combination of both a limit to average 
error and maximum error was desired. A maximum error on its own was tempting, but this could 
allow the average error to be close to or even equal to the maximum error, which, unless the 
maximum error was set quite low, would lead to a model with a relatively large error. On the 
other hand, using only a maximum error with a low target value could discount a model which 
has even zero error for all but one output sample – in this context, such a model would be 
considered quite acceptable given the size of the modelling domain. So, in order not to exclude 
such possibilities, the average and maximum combination was used.  
 
The other consideration was whether a relative or absolute error measure should be used. As the 
magnitude of each load channel and the average power output would vary considerably over the 
domain, an absolute error threshold did not make much sense, as this would allow much larger 
relative errors when the true value was small, and much smaller errors when the true value was 
large. In this context, the larger the true value, the less important a given absolute error was 
considered to be, so a relative error measure appeared to be a more appropriate choice.  
 
A combination of maximum relative error and average relative error measures was therefore 
chosen, and the penultimate step was then to consider which error function should be used for 
each of these. A full discussion of all possible options is again not the primary focus of this paper 
– readers are directed to [17] for an insightful summary of the main error functions and their pros 
and cons. To summarise the decisions made, Average Relative Error (ARE) and the Maximum

9.  Modularisation of Load Cases 
 
With generation of single-upstream-turbine surrogate models in hand, the task is then to extend 
this to consider multi-wake load cases. Given that the expected computation time to generate a 
single wake-generating turbine surrogate is in the order of 1-2 days, it is clear that any 
modularisation attempt of multi-wake load cases will need to be based on the combination of 
single-wake cases, as surrogates based on any more than a single upstream turbine will quickly 
become too computationally expensive, especially given that for input into optimisation any 
arbitrary number of upstream turbines must be considered. With this in mind, there are several 
options for possible combination approaches. These include but are not limited to combinations 
of: 
 
• 
Fatigue equivalent loads 
• 
Accumulated fatigue damage 
• 
Load cycles at each load amplitude 
• 
Wake deficits 
 
Each of these poses its own set of challenges, however the fundamental problem is that each load 
case cannot be considered independently from the others, because in reality for a multi-wake 
case, all of the single wake cases can be considered to be occurring simultaneously, and hence 
interacting with each other in some way, depending on a variety of parameters and the particular 
upstream turbine topology. Hence, it is instructive to first consider the simplest possible case and 
increase complexity incrementally. The simplest case would be that due to relative turbine 
spacing and the prevailing wind conditions, the wakes do not interact at all (as shown in Figure 
16), and hence the load signal for a given load sensor resulting from combined inflow field would 
be equal to the linear summation of the accumulated fatigue damage from each single wake case, 
or equivalently, the linear summation of the number of cycles in each load amplitude range. The 
corresponding equivalent load is then trivial to compute using the Palmgren-Miner approach.  
 
In practice however, this situation will generally only occur when the lateral spacing of the 
upstream turbines is so large, and/or the ambient turbulence intensity so small, that the 
meandering paths of the individual deficits never crosses paths, meaning in general that the 
turbine of interest will only feel the effects of one of the wakes. Given the designs of modern wind 
farms, and especially farms with multiple turbine rows, this is not a very common occurrence.  
 
Figure 16: Idealised representation of multiple, non-interacting wakes. 
==PAGE:54==
 
50 
Hence, the question of how to account for wake interaction is of critical importance if the 
modularisation approach is to be successful. This could be framed in several ways, such as how 
often do the deficits overlap and to what extent, or how correlated are the load signals from 
single wake cases? If the deficit overlap perspective is taken, this quickly becomes rather 
complicated, because for a large wind farm of say 100 turbines, then there will be times when far 
over 50 turbines could be considered upstream, and the probability of deficit overlap for each 
combination of each of these 50+ turbines needs to be computed. This alone becomes a rather 
computationally intense task, with effort increasing exponentially with the number of turbines. 
Even once this is complete, then the subsequent effect on the combined load case is not 
straightforward to compute, as this does not address the difference in partial to full wake loading 
for example. Hence, this approach would not appear to provide much potential for a decrease in 
computational effort, or an easy-to-implement solution. 
 
The second perspective of how correlated the load signals are, combined with the summation of 
accumulated fatigue equivalent loads, appears to be more promising. With this approach, 
provided that the same seed is used for the ambient, meandering and wake-added turbulence 
boxes, then the ambient field is identical, and the only difference in loading between the cases is 
due to the differences in meandering and wake-added turbulence, which in turn arise due to 
differences in upstream spacing and deficit recovery. The assumption is then made that the load 
signal fluctuations for each single-wake case can be considered a zero-mean, Gaussian-distributed 
random variable. This is justified by the fact that the ambient turbulent fluctuations and the 
large scale lateral meandering fluctuations driving the load signal fluctuations can be 
approximated as Gaussian (for non-extreme fluctuations). Under this assumption, the fatigue 
equivalent load for each single-wake case is hence taken to represent the square root of the 
variance (i.e. the standard deviation) of the load signal.  
 
In order to compute the summation of the accumulated fatigue loads from each process, the 
statistical properties of the Gaussian distribution are utilised, and a key conjecture is that the 
variance of the true multi-wake load case signal can be approximated by the variance of the sum 
of the single wake load case signal fluctuations (henceforth referred to as load processes). This is 
based on the assumption that the loads at any given point in time for a set of single wake cases 
can be added linearly to arrive at the corresponding multiple wake case instantaneous load 
magnitude. As per the statistical property of the Gaussian distribution, for multiple, non-
correlated processes, the variance of the sum of the process is equal to the sum of the variances 
of the individual processes. However, as alluded to earlier, in most cases the load processes are at 
least partially correlated, so the relation extends to: the variance of the sum of the processes is 
equal to the sum of all terms in the covariance matrix, as shown below for an example of three

compute the moments for the even integers above and below the odd exponent of interest, and 
then take the average of the equivalent loads determined for each case, analogous to linear 
interpolation between points of a curved function.  
 
Especially for even Wöhler exponents, in theory this appears to be quite a robust and flexible 
approach to computing the loading in a multi-wake case, using only single wake case equivalent 
loads. Notably, it only requires correlation between pairs of load processes, or in other words, 
correlation between pairs of single wake cases, and it would potentially enable the use of different 
turbine models or power curves (for example, for differing noise modes) within a wind farm, as 
the wake-generating turbines would not need to be identical to the wake-affected turbine for the 
approach to work. This would however require some minor modifications to HAWC2 as this 
particular aeroelastic code does not currently support setting up different turbines as the wake-
generating and wake affected units, and additionally, for each additional power curve or turbine 
model, a dimension would be added to the surrogate model, so this would increase computation 
time. It also remains to be seen whether this would be required, or if results from a single power 
curve could be scaled to represent additional power curves in place of adding surrogate 
dimensions.  
 
To recap, if a surrogate model can be produced for the power and loads generated in single wake 
cases, then for below-rated speed cases these can be used as-is to represent the loading, based on 
an approximation of the maximum deficit approach employed in HAWC2. For above rated 
speed, multiple upstream turbines must be considered as the deficit from a single turbine is not 
representative of the deficit that builds up over a row of upstream turbines. The above method 
==PAGE:58==
 
54 
thus enables the outputs of the single wake case surrogate model (i.e. the average power and 
equivalent loads) to be used as building blocks to compute the equivalent load in a multi-wake 
case above rated speed, based on summation of the accumulated fatigue damage from each single 
wake case, and taking into account the correlation between the single wake case load processes.  
 
However, several questions still remain. The first is how to determine the correlation between the 
load cases. The second is how to ensure that contributions to the equivalent load from load 
driving forces such as the ambient fluctuations, rotational sampling of shear, and gravity loads, 
which are present in all single wake cases, are not “double counted” when performing the 
summation of accumulated fatigue damage using these cases. In the true multi-wake case, these 
elements are present only once, so any load modularisation approach needs to take this into 
account. The questions of correlation and double counting are also interrelated, because the 
choice of how to avoid double counting then affects how to account for correlation.  
 
The approach decided upon to avoid double counting was to use not one surrogate model but 
several. The initial thinking was that this would work in the following way. Firstly, a single wake 
case surrogate model (SM1) for loads and power would be generated, with the full complement of 
driving factors for the overall load included. That is, each equivalent load and power output 
would include the effect of the following: 
 
• 
Ambient turbulent fluctuations 
• 
Periodic rotational sampling of vertical wind shear profile 
• 
Periodic loading due to the effect of gravity combined with blade rotation 
• 
Periodic rotational sampling of tower shadow effects 
• 
Periodic loading due to blade azimuth (e.g. two blades pointing up at 60 and 300 degrees 
and the third pointing down at 180 degrees causes imbalance in aerodynamic thrust 
across the hub, because of the difference in blade angle of attack due to nacelle tilt, 
resulting in a periodic tilting moment at the nacelle) 
• 
Wake meandering  
• 
Wake-added turbulence 
 
Next, a second surrogate model (SM2) would be generated that would include the effects of: 
 
• 
Periodic rotational sampling of vertical wind shear profile 
• 
Periodic loading due to the effect of gravity combined with blade rotation 
• 
Periodic rotational sampling of tower shadow effects 
• 
Periodic loading due to blade azimuth  
• 
Wake meandering  
• 
Wake-added turbulence 
 
For this surrogate model, the ambient turbulence would be set to zero, however the wake 
meandering would be still be driven by large scales of ambient turbulent fluctuations in the 
lateral directions (both horizontal and vertical). A third surrogate model (SM3) would then be 
generated that would include only the effects of: 
 
• 
Periodic rotational sampling of vertical wind shear profile

55 
• 
Periodic loading due to the effect of gravity combined with blade rotation 
• 
Periodic rotational sampling of tower shadow effects 
• 
Periodic loading due to blade azimuth  
 
For this model, the ambient turbulence would be set to zero, and no wake-generating turbines 
would be included.  
 
The combination of single wake cases would then work in the following manner. The load 
contribution to the multi-wake case from the first upstream turbine would be taken from SM1 – 
that is, it would include not only wake meandering and wake-added turbulence, but also, the 
effect of shear, gravity and ambient turbulent fluctuations. For all subsequent upstream turbines, 
the load contribution would be taken from SM2, with the result from SM3 subtracted from it, 
utilising the Isserlis’ Theorem approach and assuming full correlation between the load processes 
corresponding to the SM2 and SM3 results to facilitate the subtraction. This would ensure that 
the impact of ambient fluctuations on the multi-wake case equivalent load were only counted 
once – in the contribution from the first upstream turbine. Similarly, the impact of rotational 
sampling of the vertical shear profile and tower shadow, the effect of gravity and blade azimuth 
would only be counted once – also in the contribution from the closest upstream turbine. These 
effects would be “removed” from the second to 푛-th upstream turbine via the subtraction, 
leaving only the effect of the wake meandering and wake-added turbulence for all upstream 
turbines except the first.  
 
The assumption of full correlation between the load processes implied by SM2 and SM3 was 
based on the thinking that above rated speed, even when the wake-affected turbine is under full 
wake, the wind speed is likely to be above the rated speed for all except wind speeds very close 
to rated speed. Hence, the rotation speed of the wake-affected turbine will not change 
significantly as the turbine controller will act to keep the rotation speed as constant as possible 
whether wakes are reducing the rotor averaged velocity experienced by the downstream turbine 
or not. This being the case, the periodic loading due to sampling of shear and gravity that 
completely defines the equivalent fatigue load for SM3 should be fully correlated with the portion 
of the equivalent load of SM2 that is due to the same loading effects, despite the additional 
presence of wake effects in SM2. In reality this is not exactly the case, however some assumption 
of the correlation was required, and this was thought to be a reasonable approximation. 
 
For the correlation between the single wake case loads taken from SM1 and SM2, the initial 
approach used was to link the correlation to the relative spacing between the turbines, via the 
adoption of a limiting length/time scale based on correlations in the upstream and lateral 
directions of the velocity field. This approach was motivated by the consideration that the 
meandering deficits from multiple upstream turbines will be correlated if the turbines are within 
a certain separation distance in the streamwise and lateral directions, so therefore the correlation 
of the resultant load signals due to these meandering deficits should also depend heavily on the 
relative spacing.  
 
This “velocity-based” limiting length scale was evaluated using the meandering-scale filtered 
Mann turbulence box, and the autocorrelation in the streamwise direction was computed for an 
ensemble of streamwise velocities at grid point rows through the box in the streamwise direction.

by the time it reaches the wake-affected turbine. Thus, the load processes from a single wake 
case with an upstream turbine located close to the wake-affected turbine, and a single wake case 
with an upstream turbine located far upstream, are less correlated at higher turbulence 
intensities. At extremely low turbulence intensities however, the correlation effect on loading is 
somewhat counteracted by the fact that wake recovery is very low, but the meandering itself is 
also very minimal, so the correlation is high, but the actual loading is low. This is because the

This complex interplay of competing effects again highlights that it is not trivial to apply a 
simple analytical relationship to estimate the correlation, and subsequently the loading, for any 
arbitrary combination of load cases. Therefore it was determined that a different approach was 
needed. The investigation into correlations using a direct computation of the coefficients from the 
load signals themselves provided a potential solution, however this would technically require the 
actual load signals for all cases, which would not be available if a surrogate model was used to 
generate the equivalent loads for all single wake cases. However, it was reasoned that the 
application of surrogates could be extended to the coefficients themselves, removing the 
requirement for all raw load signals to be available. This would work as follows – first, the single 
wake case surrogates (SM1, SM2, and SM3) would be generated, and the output load signals 
from all of the simulations actually performed during their generation would be saved. Then, the 
correlation coefficients between all of these cases would be computed. Finally, a model would 
then be fitted to the correlation coefficients, in order to provide an estimate of the correlation 
between cases which were not explicitly simulated. Implementing this approach would both be 
relatively time/computationally efficient, as no further simulations would be required in excess of 
those already completed for the single wake case surrogates, and hopefully relatively accurate, as 
the simulations would be performed in regions of the modelling domain with the most non-linear 
behaviour – the same regions in which the correlation would be expected to vary the most due to 
this non-linear behaviour. Hence, even if the surrogate for the pairwise coefficients was generated 
in such a way that the previous simulation results were not used as inputs, and new simulations 
were run, the input variable combinations chosen by the sample selection algorithm would likely 
choose very similar points to evaluate anyway, so minimal value would be gained by starting 
from scratch in this way and iteratively selecting batches of input variable combinations.  
 
It was also recognised that a different approach to the load case modularisation was required, in 
order to minimise the potential for error due to double counting. The previous approach of 
splitting the load contributions from the ambient turbulence, meandering and wake-added 
turbulence, shear, and gravity loads into the three surrogate model components, meant that 
there was still higher than necessary potential for inaccuracies. This was due to the need to 
subtract the double counting of rotational sampling of shear and gravity effects in the load 
contributions of the second to 푛-th upstream turbines, by subtracting this portion of the load 
(the result from SM3) from the wake load contribution (the result from SM2). Instead of this, it 
was decided that a better approach would simply be to “switch off” the shear and gravity in the 
simulations used for SM2, and in addition to switch off the tower shadow effect in both SM2 and 
SM3. This would result in the following surrogate model setup.  
 
SM1 would be unchanged, and would include the effects of: 
 
• 
Ambient turbulent fluctuations 
• 
Periodic rotational sampling of vertical wind shear profile 
• 
Periodic loading due to the effect of gravity combined with blade rotation 
• 
Periodic rotational sampling of tower shadow effects 
• 
Periodic loading due to blade azimuth  
• 
Wake meandering  
• 
Wake-added turbulence

SM2 would include only the effects of: 
 
• 
Periodic loading due to blade azimuth 
• 
Wake meandering  
• 
Wake-added turbulence

• 
Periodic loading due to blade azimuth 
 
Using this modularisation approach, the only load contribution with the potential for being 
double counted due to an inaccurate estimation of the correlation coefficient, is the periodic 
loading due to blade azimuth, as this is the only load contribution that is present in both SM1 
and SM2. The benefit of this is that the load contribution from periodic loading due to blade 
azimuth is also much smaller on its own for all load channels than when combined with the 
effects of shear, gravity and tower shadow, so even if the correlation coefficient between SM3 and 
SM1 and SM2 is approximated poorly, the subsequent increase in error for the overall result is 
minimised. It should also be mentioned however, that the equivalent loads from SM3 will not be 
exactly equal to the portions of the SM1 and SM2 equivalent loads that are due to blade azimuth 
position, because this loading is also heavily dependent on the rotor speed, which is varying in 
SM1 and SM2 due to wake and ambient wind speed fluctuations, but is not varying in SM3, so 
some error is unavoidable when trying to subtract this portion of the load from all the SM2 
results. Again, this is however minimised with the proposed modularisation approach when 
compared with the initial approach.   
 
Similarly, when adding the load contributions from SM1 and SM2, the rotor speed throughout 
the simulations will be different in each case, due to the different wake effects. Ideally, the 
contributions could be added on the basis of the same rotor speed and blade pitch, so that the 
correlations would be more accurate, however this is not possible, so there is again some error 
introduced into the correlation coefficients, due to differences in loading caused by the difference 
in rotor speed, meaning that rotational sampling of the incoming wind field is different in each 
simulation. Unfortunately, this error will also increase the more correlated the individual velocity 
deficits are, because the difference in rotor speed between the single wake cases and the true 
multi-wake cases is largest when the deficits are aggregated.

cases, chosen specifically to identify possible limits for the approach applicability, and understand 
more about what drives such limitations.

12. Summary and Discussion of Test Case Results 
 
Based on the above results, the summation approach appears to perform the best when 
turbulence intensity is higher, and/or when a lateral offset exists between the upstream turbines. 
It is important to note that even a slight lateral offset is sufficient to noticeably improve the 
performance, which is significant given that in any layout, the amount of time that the wind 
direction is directly parallel with a row of turbines is generally very small relative to the amount 
of time that there is some offset between the wind direction and the row. Similarly, for many 
onshore wind farms and some offshore ones, rows are avoided altogether, either due to necessity 
or by design. Additionally, when the wind direction is parallel to a turbine row, if the turbulence 
intensity exceeds approximately 10-12%, the performance of the current approach is still 
relatively good.  
 
Another important point is that the approach appears to be superior to the first upstream 
turbine only approach for the majority of cases, and in the cases where it is not, there is only a 
minor difference between the two approaches. In contrast, in cases where the summation 
approach is superior, there is often a larger difference between the two approaches. Essentially, 
then the summation approach is better, it is often significantly better, whereas when it is worse, 
it is generally not significantly worse.  
 
The main conditions for which the approach appears to perform more poorly are wind speeds 
between rated wind speed (in this case 11.4 m/s) to around 14 m/s. Obviously this is turbine 
model-specific, as rated speed will vary from model to model, and hence the range in which the 
performance deteriorates will also vary. The channel that appeared to deteriorate the most at 14 
m/s was the tower fore-aft moment, so this was the first channel that was investigated further, 
as outlined below. 
 
12.1. Tower Fore-Aft Moment 
 
In test case 7 (14 m/s free wind speed), the tower fore-aft loads in particular were significantly 
different to the true multi-wake cases. In an attempt to isolate the cause of these large 
deviations, each of the components used to arrive at the summation approximation were 
investigated and compared with test case 8, in which the only difference was a 2 m/s increase in 
wind speed, yet here the summation approach yielded results much closer to the true multi-wake 
cases. Firstly the correlation coefficients were compared. As can be seen in Figure 25 below, the 
correlations are almost identical for the tower fore-aft moment, and the standard deviations are 
also relatively low.  
==PAGE:79==
 
75 
 
 
 
Figure 25: Comparison of correlation coefficients between single wake load signals - left plot is test case 7 (14 
m/s free wind speed), right plot is test case 8 (16 m/s free wind speed). 
Next, the single wake case equivalent loads used to arrive at the summation approximation were 
compared. As can be seen in Figure 26 below, the loads from the single wake cases used to 
represent the first upstream turbine (first portion of the light blue bars with only a single wake-
generating turbine) in each test case are relatively similar in magnitude for both test cases, with 
a slight increase in loading for test case 7 (14 m/s free wind speed). This could perhaps be 
expected given the lower wind speed, and hence the lower pitch angle of the upstream turbine 
blades causing a larger deficit behind the turbine relative to the free wind speed.  
 
The difference in the magnitude of the single wake cases used to represent the loads from the 
second and third upstream turbines (yellow bars) was substantially larger from test case 7 to test 
case 8. In addition, for test case 7 (14 m/s) the relative difference between the loads from these 
upstream turbines when ambient turbulence, gravity, shear and tower shadow were switched off 
(yellow bars) and the corresponding cases with all these effects switched on (blue bars) were 
larger than for test case 8 (16 m/s). 
 
In summary, for the tower fore-aft moment the coefficients were relatively unchanged for the two 
cases, while the magnitudes of the single wake case loads used to make up the multi-wake case 
loads were larger for test case 7, even though the true multi-wake case loads for test case 7 were 
smaller. On first glance this appears to indicate that for case 7, either the single wake case loads 
were too large, the correlation coefficients were too high, or possibly a combination of both. 
These two factors are also related because, in this case, the presence of ambient turbulence, 
gravity, shear and tower shadow appears to dampen the load signal fluctuations compared to the 
same simulation with these load contributions switched off. Therefore, in order for the correct 
level of loading to be arrived at using the summation approach, the correlation coefficients must 
compensate for this larger loading in the individual load cases by being smaller or even negative.  
 
 
==PAGE:80==
 
76 
 
       
 
 
Figure 26: Comparison of single and multi-wake case raw loads between single wake load signals - left plot is test 
case 7 (14 m/s free wind speed), right plot is test case 8 (16 m/s free wind speed). X-axis represents upstream 
turbine streamwise and lateral offsets, e.g. ‘[3,0];[6,0];[0,0]’ represents one turbine 3D upstream, one turbine 6D 
upstream, and no third upstream turbine, and no lateral offsets. Y-axis represents the 10-minute, 1-Hz equivalent 
load. 
Another explanation is as follows. An important factor to consider is the effect of the control 
system on turbine loads when regulating torque, pitch angle, and ultimately power output. Below 
rated speed power output is maximised through regulation of the generator torque to maintain 
an optimal tip speed ratio, and the blade pitch angle is constant at zero. Once rated wind speed 
is reached, the rotor torque and speed are held constant by pitching the blades progressively out 
of the wind, along with regulating generator torque to maintain a constant value. This is shown 
in Figure 27 below.  
 
However, the ability of the controller to maintain these set points in the face of continually 
changing wind conditions is a key driver of fatigue loads. For example, if a controller has poor 
damping or high sensitivity to high frequency changes in wind speed, then the rotor speed or 
pitch can be changed violently, resulting in both significantly reduced power output and 
significantly increased fatigue loads. This is because the turbine is continually trying to maintain 
a set point that it either continually overshoots due to poor damping, or is continually perturbed 
away from by high frequency wind speed changes.  
 
In test case 7 (14 m/s), when considering only the wake effects from the closest upstream 
turbine, the wind speed actually experienced by the wake-affected rotor is already hovering 
around rated wind speed the majority of the time. Due to the small streamwise spacing to the 
closest upstream turbine, the deficit centre from this turbine is never transported far from the 
centre of the wake-affected rotor by meandering, so the wind speed almost never returns to the 
true free wind speed of 14 m/s. Then, when the velocity deficits from the further upstream 
turbines overlap with the deficit from the closest upstream turbine, the wind speed is reduced to 
below rated speed, primarily because the lower blade pitch angle means that each turbine 
generates a larger deficit relative to the free wind speed, and wake recovery is reduced due to 
reduced turbulent fluctuations in comparison with the 16 m/s test case. This means that 
generator torque is the primary mechanism being used to control the turbine speed, and hence 
maximise power output.  
 
==PAGE:81==
 
77 
 
Figure 27: NREL 5MW reference turbine performance curves [15]. 
 
In contrast, in test case 8 (16 m/s), when considering only the wake effects from the closest 
upstream turbine, the wind speed actually experienced by the wake-affected rotor is still above 
rated wind speed for the majority of the time, because the free wind speed starts 2 m/s higher 
than test case 7, the deficit is smaller relative to the free wind speed due to higher blade pitch, 
and the wake recovery is improved due to increased turbulent fluctuations. Again, due to the 
small streamwise spacing to the closest upstream turbine, the deficit centre from this turbine is 
never transported far from the centre of the wake-affected rotor by meandering, so the wind 
speed almost never returns to the true free wind speed of 16 m/s. However, even when the 
velocity deficits from the further upstream turbines overlap with the deficit from the closest 
upstream turbine, in this case the wind speed is rarely reduced to below rated speed, due to the 
same reasons as outlined above. This means that blade pitch is the primary mechanism being 
used to control the turbine speed, and hence regulate power output.  
 
==PAGE:82==
 
78 
The key point is that the response of the turbine to wind speed fluctuations differs below and 
above rated speed, due to the different mechanisms used for control in these separate speed 
regions. For the 16 m/s case, the wind speed in the single wake cases used to derive the multi-
wake case load estimates rarely crosses into the below rated speed regime. This is desirable 
because in the true multi-wake case when aggregation of deficits occurs, the wind speed still 
rarely crosses into the below rated speed region, so the same turbine control mechanism is being 
used in the single and multi-wake cases. In contrast, the wind speed in the single wake cases used 
to derive the multi-wake case load estimates for the 14 m/s case also rarely crosses far into the 
below rated speed region, but the true 14 m/s multi-wake case wind speed rarely leaves the 
below rated speed region.  
 
In the above rated speed region, and particularly just above rated speed, it appears that changes 
to the thrust occur more suddenly due to pitch variation, whilst in the below rated speed region, 
changes appear to occur more smoothly with less overshoot due to generator torque variation. 
The changes to pitch are also most aggressive just above rated speed due the variable gain 
scheduling in the blade pitch controller. This is believed to be the reason why there is less 
deviation from the true multi-wake case loads in test case 8 (16 m/s), than in test case 7 (14 
m/s) – because in test case 7, the single wake case loads used to derive the multi-wake case 
loads, and the true multi-wake case loads are not generated based on the same turbine control 
mechanism. Hence, the single wake case loads are over-estimated, and therefore so too is the 
multi-wake case load estimation based on them. This can be seen in Figure 28 below, where the 
blue, red and black lines are the thrust from the single wake cases used to make up the multi-
wake case approximation, and the purple line is the thrust from the true multi-wake case 
including all three upstream turbines. It is clear that the amplitude of the thrust fluctuations in 
the single wake cases is similar in magnitude to the true multi-wake case here, whilst in Figure 
29, which is the corresponding plot from test case 8 (16 m/s), the amplitude of the true multi-
wake case is much larger than any of the single wake cases.  
 
It stands to reason for test case 7, that if the amplitudes of fluctuations for some of the 
individual single wake cases are comparable to the true-multi-wake case, then summing them will 
yield an overestimation of the multi-wake case load, even taking into account correlation and 
applying the Isserlis summation approach.  
 
It should hopefully be clear that this is a somewhat isolated issue where at 14 m/s, the specific 
combination of conditions results in the single wake case wind speeds being just above rated 
speed the majority of the time, while the multi-wake case wind speed mostly stays below rated 
speed. However, it is expected this will also be a problem for wind speeds such as 13 m/s, 
because the individual single wake case wind speeds are not aggregated, and so the wind speed 
still stays above rated speed the majority of the time, when in reality it should drop below it.

It is therefore desirable to formulate a version of the summation approach which does not suffer 
from the same limitations. To this effect, the following modification is proposed for cases when 
turbines are in a row parallel to the wind direction. In such a case, the wind speed used to 
evaluate the load contribution from each single wake case should be computed progressively 
down the row of turbines, instead of simply using the free wind speed for all turbines. This could 
be implemented as follows.  
 
1. The load contribution from furthest upstream turbine in the row is evaluated from 
surrogate model 2 (SM2) at the actual free wind speed. 
2. The inter-turbine spacing in the streamwise direction is computed between the first and 
second turbine in the row. 
3. The mean inflow velocity experienced by the second turbine this distance downstream 
from the first is evaluated using SM2 and the actual free wind speed. 
4. The load contribution from second turbine in the row is evaluated from SM2, using the 
mean inflow velocity computed in (3) as the “free wind speed” for this turbine. 
5. The inter-turbine spacing in the streamwise direction is computed between the second 
and third turbine in the row. 
6. The mean inflow velocity experienced by the third turbine this distance downstream of 
the second is evaluated using SM2 and the free wind speed computed in (3). 
7. The load contribution from the third turbine in the row is evaluated from SM2, using the 
mean inflow velocity computed in (6) as the “free wind speed” for this turbine. 
8. Steps 5-7 are repeated for all subsequent turbines in the row, except the last turbine in 
the row before the turbine of interest for loads (i.e. the closest upstream turbine when 
looking upstream). For this turbine, the load contribution is evaluated using SM1, to 
include the effects of ambient turbulence, shear, gravity and tower shadow. 
 
Using this procedure, the velocity deficit build-up along the row can be taken into account, and 
no extra surrogate modelling or other computationally intense calculations need to be made. The 
only difference is that the load contribution from each single wake case is computed at a different 
“free wind speed” rather than assuming all upstream turbines are exposed to the same free wind 
speed. This should result in a reduction in loading consistent with the true multi-wake case in 
the situation where turbines are in a row parallel with the wind, and using “free wind speeds” 
evaluated from SM2 also enables the effect of differing turbulence intensity to be captured. For 
example, at higher turbulence intensities, wake recovery will be improved and meandering will be 
more pronounced, and so the overall mean wind speed experienced by a wake-affected turbine in 
a ten-minute period will be higher, as the turbine will be “out of the wake” more often, and the 
deficit won’t be as large when “in the wake”. Similarly, for lower turbulence intensities, the wake 
recovery and meandering will decrease, and hence the mean wind speed experienced by the 
downstream turbine will decrease.  
 
In theory this approach could also be used for upstream turbine topologies where a lateral offset 
exists between the upstream turbines, however with even a small lateral offset this would 
degenerate into the current summation approach where all upstream turbines are assumed to 
operate under the actual free wind speed, so such an approach is not expected to be necessary in 
these cases. 
 
==PAGE:85==
 
81 
Another nice aspect of this approach is that it should dovetail smoothly into the below rated 
speed regime, as when the free wind speed is below the rated wind speed, the deficit generated by 
the furthest upstream turbine will be large (likely almost reaching the equilibrium speed), and 
hence the “free wind speed” used for the second wind turbine will be much smaller, so the deficit 
will be smaller, and the deficit will reach a steady state as it is progressively calculated along the 
row.  
 
12.2. Tower Side-Side Moment 
 
The tower side-side moment is primarily driven by torque fluctuations due to different 
aerodynamic loads on different parts of the rotor and at different points in time. Especially in 
cases where turbines are in a row, this channel appears to be under-predicted for close upstream 
turbine spacings, and gradually transitions to being over-predicted for larger upstream turbine 
spacings. These trends are not universal however, so it is particularly difficult to pinpoint the 
cause of the deviation between the summation approach and the true multi-wake cases.  
 
When comparing test case 7 (14 m/s free wind speed) and test case 8 (16 m/s free wind speed), 
the tower side-side moment error is larger in test case 7 for most of the spacings, and is an over-
prediction rather than an under-prediction. Therefore, it also appears to be affected to some 
extent by the same over-prediction error due to differing control mechanisms in the single wake 
cases versus the true multi-wake cases, as the tower fore-aft moment. This would appear to make 
sense, given that the root of the issue is different control mechanisms causing torque and thrust 
variations to be more aggressive above rated speed than below, and the main driver of side-side 
load is torque fluctuations. Therefore, the same solution proposed in Section 12.1 is expected to 
resolve this issue for the side-side moment as well. 
 
However, apart from this issue, variation in the prediction quality across the test cases and even 
within test cases for different spacings seems to be more variable for this channel than most 
others. One possible reason for this is that the variance in load across the ten different seeds used 
for each load case was the highest for this channel out of all the channels, and this was consistent 
across all load cases. It is hypothesised that this is due to the very low aerodynamic damping in 
the side-side direction, meaning small changes in torque can lead to large variations in side-side 
moment. Taking into account this high variability, overall the summation approach still appears 
to provide a reasonable approximation of the side-side moment in multi-wake conditions. 
 
12.3. Tower Top Roll Moment 
 
The tower top roll moment at the yaw bearing is due to the transfer of torque from the shaft to 
the nacelle through the generator. As the NREL 5MW turbine includes a gearbox rather than a 
direct drive transmission, this transfer of torque occurs on the high speed shaft side of the 
gearbox, so torque is significantly reduced from the low speed shaft side. As for the tower side-
side moment, the torque fluctuations are due to the different aerodynamic loads on different 
parts of the rotor and at different points in time, from shear, ambient wind fluctuations and 
wake meandering, and so on.  
 
==PAGE:86==
 
82 
In general, this channel was predicted very well, however a slight increase in deviation was seen 
in test case 10 (18 m/s free wind speed). Despite this, significant time was not spent on analysis 
of this channel due to the generally very good agreement with the true multi-wake cases and the 
summation approach, as well as the low magnitude of equivalent load meaning this channel is 
unlikely to be a design driving load. 
 
12.4. Tower Top Yaw and Tilt Moments 
 
Again comparing test case 7 and test case 8, it is interesting to note that some channels were 
more affected by the different control mechanisms issue discussed above than others – that is, 
some channels had a similar magnitude of deviation from the true multi-wake cases in test case 7 
as in test case 8. This illustrates that some channels are more heavily influenced by changes in 
wind speed than others. For example, the magnitude of deviations for the tower top tilt and yaw 
moments were only marginally different when comparing the two test cases. In fact, the 
differences between the true multi-wake loads themselves for the two cases was also very small 
relative to the difference between the tower fore-aft moments. Inspecting the correlation 
coefficients for these two channels in Appendix A, it is clear that they are very similar, just as 
the tower fore-aft coefficients were. However in this case the single wake case raw load 
magnitudes were also very similar for all component sing wake cases, in contrast to the tower 
fore-aft moment.  
 
The tower top yaws and tilts primarily in response to a difference in velocity from the left to 
right and top to bottom of the rotor respectively. This is transferred to the tower top via the 
horizontal and vertical components of the blade flapwise moments, so the resultant yaw and tilt 
moments are hence very sensitive to small changes in the vector sum of the individual flapwise 
moments [19]. Another important factor is the position of the blades at a given point in time 
creating a periodic imbalance in force on one side of the rotor to the other, due to variations in 
blade angle of attack, which are amplified by dynamic stall and stall hysteresis. Because the 
differential velocity from one side of the rotor to the other is what drives the loading, rather than 
the ‘rotor averaged’ absolute velocity in the case of the tower fore-aft moment, the resulting 
loads are less influenced by the absolute velocity. In addition, changes in the rotor speed or blade 
pitch affect the entire rotor rather than just one side, so the differential force on the rotor that 
causes the yaw or tilt is less affected by this, and hence the deviation from the true multi-wake 
cases is also less affected. 
 
The reason for the general over-prediction of loads for the tower top tilt and yaw moments, not 
just in test case 7 and 8 but most test cases was initially thought to be as follows. A portion of 
the single wake case fatigue loads is caused by the periodic variation of blade angle of attack, 
which is a result of numerous factors including the nacelle tilt, vertical wind shear and turbulent 
fluctuations, cross winds and so on. These variations result in yaw and tilt moments that are 
then amplified by dynamic stall and stall hysteresis, which are in turn sensitive to the rate of 
change of angle of attack. In the true multi-wake case these loads are obviously only accounted 
for once. However, using the summation approach, this is accounted for in every single wake case 
equivalent load. In order try to isolate only the effects of the wake meandering and wake added 
turbulence in the load contributions from the second to n-th upstream turbine, so as not to 
double count this load contribution, a subtraction of n-1 multiples of the load due to only the 
==PAGE:87==
 
83 
blade azimuth position is employed within the Isserlis summation, as discussed earlier. However, 
as the simulations used to derive this load use a completely uniform inflow field, the periodic 
change in angle of attack only occurs due to the nacelle tilt, and the rate of change of the angle 
of attack is much lower and smoother throughout the rotation, so the amplifying effect of 
dynamic stall is largely mitigated. This means that the loads are much smaller than those they 
are trying to cancel out, because the dynamic stall effect is much larger when operating in a 
turbulent and wake affected wind field due to the increased rate of change in angle of attack.  
 
It was initially hypothesised that one way to resolve this would be to change the separation of 
the component load cases so that all component load cases, including the SM3 load cases, would 
include the effects of ambient fluctuation, thus minimising the difference in the load magnitudes 
when using subtraction to cancel the portions of the load that were present in all single wake 
cases. However, when tested this did not have the desired effect. Further investigation is 
therefore required into this channel. Another hypothesis is that the difference is simply due to 
different distributions of force on the blades in the true multi-wake case than in the single wake 
cases. This may have led to some offsetting of the moment fluctuations in the true multi-wake 
cases that was not present in the single wake cases and was not able to be captured by the 
correlation coefficients, thus explaining the over-prediction by the summation approach. 
However, further investigation is required to confirm or reject this hypothesis. 
 
12.5. Blade Flapwise and Edgewise Moment 
 
The blade flapwise moment is systematically under-predicted by the summation approach, for all 
but the 14 m/s test case. The edgewise moment is similarly slightly under-predicted, even in the 
14 m/s test case. The deviation is however much less for the edgewise moment.  
 
To further understand why these channels are under-predicted, it is instructive to revisit the 
original premise upon which the summation approach is based. Essentially, each single wake case 
produces multiple time series of power and loads fluctuations. These are then combined using 
summation of accumulated fatigue damage, to arrive at the multi-wake case power and loads. 
Partial correlation between the various single wake cases and their respective time series’ is taken 
into account by assuming each time series is a Gaussian-distributed stochastic process, and hence 
the summation is of all terms in the covariance matrix raised to a power half that of the Wöhler 
exponent of the component of interest.  
 
Strictly, this approach is only valid if the time series (load processes) involved are actually 
Gaussian-distributed. With the exception of the edgewise blade moment, analysis of the 
individual load processes showed this to be an imperfect but not unreasonable assumption. For 
the edgewise blade moment however, the loading is primarily periodic due to gravity, hence this 
would appear to be an issue for the application of the approach for the edgewise moment.  
 
A second requirement for the approach to be strictly valid is that, when computing correlations 
between two individual load processes, the forces driving the loading in each case should not 
affect the fundamental dynamics of the structure itself. For wind turbines, this would require the 
turbine rotor to be either locked in place or set to rotate at a fixed speed with fixed pitch 
regardless of the incoming wind field, however obviously in this case this would change the entire 
==PAGE:88==
 
84 
simulation, and would not be at all representative of the situation attempting to be modelled. 
Hence, there is a fundamental issue with the approach, in that the rotation speed is coupled to 
the wind speed, which then causes the blades, and subsequently the entire structure, to be in 
different positions at a given point in time from one single wake case to the next. Similarly, as 
the turbine is rotating at slightly different and varying speeds in each simulation, above rated 
speed the pitch is also changing in combination with the rotation speed.  
 
An example of this is shown in Figure 30 and Figure 31 below. These plots are an example from 
test case 8, with mean wind speed at 16 m/s, TI at 6%, and upstream turbines at 3D, 12D and 
21D upstream, and no lateral offsets. The blue line in Figure 30 is the rotor speed from the single 
wake case with a wake-generating turbine at 3D upstream and no lateral offset, and ambient 
turbulence, shear, gravity and tower shadow effects included. Again, this is the same as the setup 
used for the first surrogate model (SM1). The red line shows the rotor speed from the single wake 
case with a wake-generating turbine at 12D upstream and no lateral offset, and ambient 
turbulence, shear, gravity and tower shadow effects switched off. Similarly, the black line 
represents the rotor speed from the single wake case with a wake-generating turbine at 21D 
upstream and no lateral offset, and ambient turbulence, shear, gravity and tower shadow effects 
switched off. The red and black lines therefore use the same simulation setup as the second 
surrogate model (SM2).

The net outcome of this is that error is introduced into the computed correlation coefficients 
between a pair of load processes, because the wind speed, and hence the rotor speed and 
potentially the pitch is varying differently in each single wake case simulation, and therefore the 
turbine is not in the same position at each time step across all simulations. To further illustrate 
this point, Figure 32 shows a magnified view of the blade azimuth positions at each time step for 
the same test case example as Figure 30 and Figure 31 above. Here it can be seen that at some

time steps, there is over a 25-degree difference in blade azimuth position between the black and

between the blue and black single wake cases.  Given the blade length of 61.5 m, and neglecting 
other geometric details such as hub radius, pre-cone angle and so on, a rough estimate of the 
subsequent distance between the tip of the same blade at the same time step in each simulation

simulations. Also important to note is that this is not constant throughout a 10-min simulation 
period, so a constant offset cannot simply be applied to “synchronise” the rotor rotation and 
eliminate the error.  
 
In reality and in the true multi-wake case simulations, a given blade tip can only be in one 
position at once, so therefore there will always be some level of deviation between these 
simulations and the summation approach. The key questions are how this affects each load 
channel, and whether the deviations are significant enough to warrant concern.

The effect of wake meandering is very small in the case of the edgewise moment, as the number 
of cycles and cycle amplitude is almost completely determined by the rotor speed and the mass of 
the blades themselves, accelerated by gravity each rotation. Generally speaking, this means that 
the deviation in load prediction due to the error involved in the correlation is quite small. This is 
because the gravity driven portion of the overall loading is captured in the contribution from the 
first upstream turbine only, when gravity is switched on. Although there is a slight difference in 
the number of revolutions in the ten-minute period when the true multi-wake case is compared 
with the single wake case from the first upstream turbine, this is almost negligible. In the true 
multi-wake case however, there is some slight increase in loading relative to the first upstream 
turbine single wake case, due to the larger torque fluctuations under multiple wake conditions. 
==PAGE:91==
 
87 
This is captured in the load cases from the second to n-th upstream turbine, because gravity is 
switched off in these cases, so the loading is mainly due to torque fluctuations. Ideally these load 
contributions would be fully correlated amongst all single wake cases, as the rotor would be 
exposed to the same changes in wind speed at the same point in time, however for reasons 
discussed above, this is not the case. Therefore, the correlation is lower than 1, which causes the 
loads to be under-predicted using the summation approach. Fortunately, in the case of the 
edgewise moment, this can be easily remedied by assuming full correlation between all the single 
wake cases when computing the combined multi-wake equivalent load, because the full 
correlation is implied by the heavily periodic nature of the loading. As can be seen in each of the 
comparison plots in Appendix A, the summation assuming full correlation (orange bars in each 
plot) results in an extremely good agreement between the true multi-wake loads and the 
summation approach in all cases. 
 
Unfortunately, for the flapwise moment, the same solution cannot be used, because the loading in 
this case is partially periodic and partially stochastic. The periodic load contributors are 
rotational sampling of shear, nacelle tilt, tower shadow, and so on, and the stochastic load 
contributors are the velocity fluctuations due to ambient turbulence and wake meandering. As 
the flapwise moment is in the rotating frame of reference that travels with the blade, the 
stochastic velocity fluctuations are also sampled periodically by the blade, so the behaviour of 
the flapwise moment is highly complex. On the one hand, if only the periodic load contributions 
were considered, then the correlations should be fully correlated. However, as can be seen in most 
of the comparison plots in Appendix A, summation assuming full correlation (orange bars in each 
plot) results in an over-prediction of the loads. This is because on the other hand, the stochastic 
load contributions in each of the single wake load cases are only partially correlated, depending 
primarily on the distance between each of the wake-generating turbines.  
 
In addition, the fact that the summation approach underestimates the load in all but test case 7 
highlights that the same issue that affects the tower fore-aft moment also affects the flapwise 
moment. As discussed in more detail earlier in Section 12.1, when the free wind speed is closer to 
the rated wind speed, the actual wind speed experienced by the wake-affected rotor in the true 
multi-wake case is below rated speed the majority of the time, so the power is maximised via 
regulation of generator torque. However, in each of the single wake cases, the wind speed 
experienced by the wake-affected rotor tends to stay just above rated wind speed most of the 
time. Hence, the power is regulated through blade pitch variation rather than generator torque 
regulation. The two control mechanisms have different response characteristics, and blade pitch 
seems to change more aggressively which cause higher loads than the smoother torque regulation. 
For the flapwise moment, in test case 8 this actually results in an improved load prediction by 
the summation approach, because it offsets some of the underestimation caused by the 
underestimated correlation coefficient.  
 
On the positive side, the revised approach to the summation when turbines are in a row, as 
described in Section 12.1, is expected to minimise the deviation due to the different control 
mechanism responses. However, this still leaves the issue of inaccurate correlation coefficients to 
deal with. One possible solution may be to apply a low pass filter or moving average to the load 
signals prior to computing the correlations. This is proposed because the purpose of the 
correlation is to quantify how much the wakes are interacting, and it is known that wake 
==PAGE:92==
 
88 
meandering is driven by large scale, low frequency turbulent structures. The periodic loading due 
to rotor rotation on the other hand occurs at somewhat higher frequency ranges. Hence, a low 
pass filter or moving average could be employed to filter out the parts of the load signals that are 
responsible for the inaccurate correlation, leaving only the parts of the signal relevant for 
correlation of wake effects. Further investigation is however required to confirm whether this will 
result in the desired improvement in deviation. 
 
12.6. Shaft Yaw and Tilt Moments 
 
In general, shaft yaw and tilt were the most well predicted of all the channels (with the exception 
of the blade edgewise moment), across the test cases. It should also be noted that both the yaw 
and tilt moments were computed in the rotating frame of reference, so these two channels are 
almost identical – the only difference is the phase shift in the loading seen, which does not affect 
the equivalent loads. There was however a slight under-prediction of loads for most test cases 
using the summation approach, so this was explored further to understand the cause.  
 
The main driver of shaft yaw and tilt in the rotating frame of reference is generally the gravity 
load due the weight of the rotor cantilevered over the front bearing [20]. For a given nacelle tilt 
angle, the rotor speed is the main factor which determines the number of fatigue cycles that will 
be experienced in each ten-minute period, however the influence on the equivalent load of the 
variation in the total number of cycles experienced due to changes in rotor speed is very small. 
Hence wake meandering has a negligible effect on this.  
 
Another driver of shaft loading is the differential aerodynamic loads which arise due to wind 
shear, tower shadow, nacelle tilt, variation of blade angle of attack, turbulent fluctuations and so 
on. These differential loads are secondary to the gravity loads, however the larger the differential 
between the blades, the larger amplitude of the moment. Therefore, wake meandering does have 
an effect on this portion of the load.  
 
As the shaft yaw and tilt moments are computed in the rotating frame of reference, the loading 
is largely periodic, and hence the issue of inaccurate correlation coefficients due to different 
rotation speeds in each single wake case, as discussed in Section 12.5 for the blade moments, is 
believed to be one of the reasons for the errors observed. Therefore, the same proposed solution is 
suggested – application of a low pass filter or moving average to the load signals prior to 
computation of the correlation coefficients, in order to filter out the portion of the signals causing 
the lower than desired correlation.

13. Surrogate Modelling Results 
 
The test case results and discussion in the previous section attempted to evaluate the validity of 
the summation approach for combining single wake load cases to arrive at the power and loads 
for multi-wake cases. However, accurate estimation of loads from each of the single wake cases is 
also important for the overall multi-wake loads approximation to also be accurate. Hence, an 
assessment of how well the single upstream turbine surrogate models are able to predict loading 
is also required. To address this, a summary of results from the surrogate modelling effort are 
presented below.  
 
As discussed in previous sections, three different surrogate models are proposed: 
 
1. Surrogate Model 1 (SM1) – including the effects of ambient turbulent fluctuations, shear, 
gravity, tower shadow, blade azimuth position, wake meandering and wake added 
turbulence. 
2. Surrogate Model 2 (SM2) – including the effects of blade azimuth position, wake 
meandering and wake added turbulence, with ambient turbulent fluctuations, shear, 
gravity, and tower shadow switched off. 
3. Surrogate Model 3 (SM3) - including the effects of blade azimuth position only, with 
wake meandering, wake added turbulence, ambient turbulent fluctuations, shear, gravity 
and tower shadow switched off. 
 
The results of each of these surrogates will be discussed separately in the following subsections. 
However, an important note for all surrogates is that HPC cluster availability and the maximum 
time limit of three days for jobs submitted to the cluster at DTU limited the maximum number 
of samples able to be evaluated during the surrogate generation process. The net result of this 
was that the resulting models were not as accurate as desired, because a large proportion of the 
three available days for a job was spent with simulations queued, waiting to be executed. If 
cluster availability or maximum time limits were not such an issue, it is expected that a much 
higher number of samples could have been evaluated in the same timeframe, improving model 
convergence, so this is more an issue with the particular cluster implementation at DTU rather 
than a fundamental issue with the ability of the surrogate model to provide accurate results.  
 
13.1. Surrogate Model 1 (SM1) 
 
For this surrogate, the total number of samples able to be simulated, and hence the total number 
of samples available to fit the model to was 3041. However, the maximum time limit was reached 
before all outputs were able to be modelled with this many samples, so the best models for some 
channels were based on all but the last batch of samples. This meant that 2603 samples were 
used for these channels instead of 3041. For each sample, ten different turbulence seeds were 
used, so the outputs for a given sample are the average of the 10 simulations corresponding to 
the 10 seeds. Five-fold cross-validation was used as the error measure in all cases, and the error 
functions used were mean relative error and maximum relative error. 
 
Table 3 below summarises the model performance for each output using these metrics. As can be 
seen from these results, more samples are required to improve the model quality before usage in

combination with the summation approach will have a chance of yielding accurate results for 
multi-wake cases. However, given the relatively small number of samples, it is encouraging that 
the errors are not much larger than those obtained. For three out of the eleven outputs, the

relative error was below 5%. The maximum relative error target of 10% was only met for the

13.2. Surrogate Model 2 (SM2) 
 
For surrogates 2 and 3, it quickly became apparent that the use of the mean relative error and 
maximum relative error was not appropriate. This was because, unlike in surrogate model 1, the 
effects of ambient turbulence, gravity, tower shadow, and shear were switched off, so for some 
samples, the resultant equivalent loads became very small. This being the case, although the 
absolute errors were not large in the context of the range of loads across the modelling domain, 
relative to the actual values they were. This meant that the maximum relative error was 
massive, and the relatively small number of samples with extremely large relative errors then also 
dominated the mean relative error. The end result of this was that the model generation was 
guided using flawed data, and so the model quality was poor.  
 
Instead of mean relative error and maximum relative error, root relative square error (RRSE) 
was therefore used. This error function is less intuitive, but it avoids the issue of tending towards 
infinity if the actual sample outputs are very close to zero. This is because it is a measure of the 
proportion of error in the generated model relative to the simplest possible model – the mean of 
all results. For example, if a model has a 1% RRSE this means that the model has only one 
percent of the error level of simply taking the mean of all results.  
 
In some cases, this can be a poor choice of error function, because if the mean of all results 
actually fits the data relatively well (for example if the data has very low variance and is of 
similar magnitude throughout the modelling domain), then the RRSE can remain quite large, 
because it is difficult to significantly improve on an already good fit. However, in this case there 
was expected to be enough variance and variation in magnitude in the output that this would 
not be an issue.  
 
On its own, the RRSE does not provide an intuitive feel for how much error still exists in the 
model, so in this case the mean absolute error and maximum absolute error were still computed 
for reference, but not used to guide model generation. These can be compared with the minimum 
and maximum evaluated values to get an idea of how large the error is relative to the output 
range. A summary of these model metrics is provided in Table 4 below, and again histogram 
plots of the error distributions are provided in Figure 34, however this time the absolute error is 
shown instead of the relative error, for the same reasons as described above. All outputs apart 
from the blade edgewise moment were in this case modelled based on the same number of 
samples – 2777. As the maximum modelling time limit was reached before the blade edgewise 
moment model could be updated with the last batch of samples, this model was based on 2391 
samples.  
 
Lastly, 10 turbulence seeds were also used for each sample, and the average was again taken to 
give the final result for each sample. This was still required because although for this surrogate 
ambient turbulence was switched off, the meandering was still driven by large scale turbulent 
fluctuations according to the meandering turbulence box, so variability was still observed 
between each seed. 
 
As with the previous surrogate model, it is clear that both more samples are needed, and in this 
case also a revised modelling strategy would likely improve the model error, however

14. Extension to Onshore Wind Farms  
 
The second primary goal of this project was to investigate the challenges involved when trying to 
extend the optimisation framework to an onshore environment. However, due to time constraints, 
a proper investigation of this area was unfortunately not possible. Instead, this section aims to 
provide a very brief introduction to some of the main barriers that currently prevent improved 
optimisation onshore, in the hope that it will spur further research into their resolution.  
 
Extending the previously outlined optimisation framework for use with onshore wind farms is not 
trivial, however the optimisation process itself is not the main obstacle, but rather the inputs to 
this process. As with offshore farms, the main complication is flow modelling throughout the 
farm in order to predict power and loads in a computationally affordable way to enable fast 
assessment, which in turn makes optimisation feasible. However, the main challenge in an 
offshore farm is the wake interaction, whereas in an onshore farm, wake interaction is only one of 
several important and interrelated issues that must be taken into account in order to accurately 
predict power and loads with sufficient speed. 
 
For onshore wind farms, the main factors affecting flow modelling are surface roughness, 
orography and stability, as well as wake interaction. As all of these factors interact, making 
accurate modelling, and in turn accurate prediction of power and loads rather complex. The 
current state of the art in flow modelling generally involves some form of computational fluid 
dynamics (CFD), or linearised or mass consistent flow models. The main advantage of CFD is 
the ability to model the interactions between the various flow influencing factors in a physically 
consistent way, as opposed to engineering models which are generally much faster, but tend to 
have more limited ranges where their application is valid, and may lack the accuracy of CFD, 
depending on the level of convergence CFD is able to attain for a given site.  
 
Whilst many steady CFD simulations of whole wind farms have now been performed for both 
resource assessment and applications such as wind farm control, and such simulations are 
becoming more common, the steady nature and resolution of such simulations means they are 
unable to natively provide an assessment of turbine loads.  
 
The computational expense of unsteady simulations of whole wind farms at a resolution that 
would enable loads assessment is unfortunately still prohibitive. Even a steady simulation of a 
single layout, taking into account different wind speeds and directions, takes on the order of 
hours to days, depending on the resolution and accuracy required. Because of this high 
computational cost, even when CFD is utilised to characterise the ambient flow field and 
possibly time-averaged wake effects - for example to make a siting assessment in complex terrain 
- engineering models such as the Frandsen model or aeroelastic simulations with DWM are then 
relied upon to quantify loads, based on inputs from the CFD simulations.   
 
In order to break down the challenges in an onshore environment, it is useful to first consider the 
separate effects of surface roughness, orography, and stability on the wind flow.  
 
 
==PAGE:103==
 
99 
14.1. Surface Roughness 
 
Surface roughness is a way of quantifying the effect of the local ground cover, such as forestry, 
buildings, open farmland, and so on, on the wind conditions. The higher the roughness, the more 
the wind is slowed by the ground cover as the distance above the ground decreases, due to 
friction effects. The net result of this is a sheared vertical velocity profile, as well as increased 
turbulence intensity.  
 
Assuming the surface roughness is constant and the ground is flat (in terms of orography, so no 
elevation changes), for a given wind speed and direction, a steady velocity profile is maintained 
and a steady turbulence intensity exists for a given height. However, when the surface roughness 
changes, the effect of this on the flow is gradually transported further up the velocity profile due 
to vertical momentum exchange, and assuming another surface roughness change does not occur 
further downstream, then some distance downstream the old velocity profile and turbulence 
intensity will be completely replaced with a new profile and turbulence intensity. Continual 
surface roughness changes however will lead to a continually evolving velocity profile and 
turbulence intensity. This is important when considering the effect that both turbulence intensity 
and shear have on the evolution and propagation of the wake deficit.  
 
It should be mentioned that surface roughness is also a factor in the development of wakes 
offshore, given that surface roughness tends to increase with wind speed in offshore farms due to 
wave generation, however this tends to affect the entire wind farm in much the same way, rather 
than varying locally within the wind farm. One exception to this however is when offshore farms 
are built close to shore, and so still “feel” the effects of surface roughness changes when wind 
blows from the land towards the wind farm. In this case, the velocity profile and turbulence 
intensity are still adjusting throughout the wind farm, resulting in local variation.  
 
14.2. Orography 
 
Orography, or the ruggedness and elevation of the terrain, primarily affects the wind flow by 
causing local increases (speed-ups) or decreases (slow-downs) in wind speed, as well as changes in 
direction, due to the presence of features such as hills which perturb the flow. If the changes in 
terrain gradient are steep enough, this can cause flow separation and regions of recirculation, 
which lead to increased turbulence intensity. In addition, the terrain can also have an effect on 
the structure of the turbulence, for example through the modification of the degree of isotropy. 
This is again important for the propagation and evolution of the wake deficit, especially in the 
case when the wind direction is modified, as wake deficits that may have “hit” a downstream 
turbine in flat terrain may have a minimal effect on the same downstream turbine if the terrain 
is able to cause a significant enough change in wind direction between the two turbines, that the 
downstream turbine is “missed” by the wake. 
 
14.3. Stability 
 
Stability refers to the thermal stratification of the atmospheric boundary layer, which occurs due 
to the difference in temperature between the land surface and the air above it. During the day, 
==PAGE:104==
 
100
solar radiation tends to warm the Earth surface, making it warmer than the air directly above 
the surface. Hence, heat flux occurs from the surface upwards into the atmosphere, adding to 
mechanical turbulent mixing due to shear, and resulting in what is termed unstable conditions. 
At night, when the Earth is cooler than the air above it, an opposite heat flux occurs from the 
air to the ground. This suppresses the mechanical turbulent mixing, and results in a more 
stratified, stable boundary layer. Neutral stability occurs when mechanical turbulent mixing is 
neither hindered nor enhanced by thermal effects – this usually occurs on cloudy or very windy 
days, and also diurnally near sunrise and sunset when the Earth surface is not significantly 
hotter or colder than the air above it.  
 
Stability affects wake propagation through its influence on the turbulence intensity, isotropy and 
the shear profile, as well as through its combined effect with orography. For example, in highly 
stable conditions, air will not flow over a hill but around it – an important effect when 
attempting to model wake meandering. 
 
It is important to note that stability also affects offshore wind farms, but due to the much larger 
heat capacity of the ocean, a diurnal cycle is less commonly observed. Rather, the sea tends to 
stay warmer than the air above it even at night, and whether conditions are stable, unstable or 
neutral tends to depend more on the advection of colder or warmer air from different latitudes, 
as well as annual weather variations. In spring and summer, when the atmosphere warms 
rapidly, stable conditions tend to prevail, while in autumn and winter when the atmosphere is 
much cooler than the water, the conditions are usually more unstable.  
 
14.4. Turbine Noise  
 
Another consideration onshore is the fact that many prospective wind farm sites are subject to 
noise constraints, which, like loads, can restrict turbine placement, compromising power 
production. Constraints such as these can be built into the optimisation process relatively easily, 
in several different ways. For example, an extra dimension can be added to the surrogate models 
to take into account different power curves for individual turbines corresponding to different 
noise modes. Then, when initialising the optimisation, zones where required noise levels are 
needed can be set up based on the location of dwellings or other noise sensitive areas. As the 
noise contribution from individual turbines is added linearly [21], the optimisation can be 
constrained based on the sum of noise contributions at the noise sensitive areas, and if the limits 
are exceeded, this layout is penalised with a certain cost that renders it non-optimal. The 
optimisation process then has two levers with which to improve upon this layout – to change 
turbine locations or change noise modes – these can be weighed against each other using the cost 
function and the best option determined.  
 
14.5. Wake Interaction with Onshore Factors 
 
Stability, orography and surface roughness all individually influence the development of wakes, 
and when combined, the effect can be even more significant. For example, the path a wake 
deficit follows when encountering a hill may be quite different depending on stability as well as 
surface roughness, if roughness changes also cause a significant difference in the wind speed 
==PAGE:105==
 
101
profile. In addition, the turbulence intensity and structure of turbulence may also be significantly 
modified, affecting wake recovery as well as the shape and extent of the deficit.  
 
A model which is able to accurately account for all of these inter-related effects in terrain of any 
arbitrary complexity, whilst including dynamic changes so that loads may be computed directly, 
is not feasible with today’s computing resources. The current most common approach to dealing 
with this is therefore to first use steady CFD models of varying expense depending on the terrain 
complexity, to approximate the ambient flow field. This approximation is expressed in terms of 
perturbations to the mean wind speed and direction, as well as representative turbulence 
intensity estimates, usually linked to local turbulent kinetic energy levels.  
 
This characterisation of the ambient wind climate is then combined with an engineering wake 
model, such as the Frandsen model or DWM model. A key point here is that these models 
assume flat terrain and constant surface roughness – which makes sense given that both models 
were originally developed primarily for offshore applications. Stability can be considered to some 
extent through modification of the turbulence intensity and shear profile, however for different 
conditions the calculations must be repeated. So although the effects of terrain and surface 
roughness are considered when defining the ambient wind climate and flow field, this information 
is not used in the computation of wake effects, other than to determine the wind speed, ambient 
turbulence intensity and shear. The paths of wakes are assumed to be unaffected by terrain, and 
local variation of wake recovery due to local turbulence intensity variation is neglected.  
 
It is acknowledged that the validity of these assumptions is dependent on the specific site. For 
example, in a site which is completely characterised by open farmland, and is essentially flat, 
these assumptions may be reasonable.  At the other end of the spectrum where terrain is highly 
complex and the site includes numerous surface roughness changes, these assumptions are 
questionable and likely not valid. However, the impact of wakes on power production and loads 
at such a site is likely secondary to the effect of the ambient conditions anyway. This is because 
in such conditions, turbulence intensity is likely to be high, enhancing wake recovery and 
reducing the downstream distance where deficit magnitude has a meaningful effect on power and 
loads. Also, high site complexity usually precludes layouts of turbines in grids or rows, so 
multiple wake cases are likely to be more rare. In these cases, the effects of wakes on power and 
loads are more easily handled by engineering wake models, because the computations must 
consider less upstream turbines (in many cases only one or none) and so the relevant history of 
upstream perturbations to the flow by terrain or surface roughness is shorter. Therefore, the 
validity of the assumptions made in application of the engineering wake models is improved.  
 
In complex terrain, it is also reasonable to expect that in some cases, wake effects are negligible 
relative to the large effects of the terrain on the wind flow, and hence power and loads, so 
therefore the need for wake models in the first place is considerably diminished. This is because 
in complex terrain the wake from one turbine might only very rarely have an effect on a nearby 
turbine, as the combination of layout and topology simply results in the wake from upstream 
turbines never interacting with other turbines. An example of this is when there are very large 
changes in elevation between turbine positions, such as when turbines are placed on two adjacent 
parallel ridges of different elevations and the wind direction is perpendicular to the ridges. 
 
==PAGE:106==
 
102
Essentially, at the two extremes of terrain and surface roughness variation, the use of engineering 
wake models appears to be either reasonably valid, or barely required, respectively. However, in 
an optimisation context, any arbitrary level of variation in between must be able to be handled, 
or at least an upper limit on terrain complexity and surface roughness variation should be given, 
above which applicability does not hold. 
 
Given their importance offshore, a substantial body of work exists on the validation of the 
engineering wake models for loads in an offshore context, however to-date there has not been as 
much effort put into validating the use of the current versions of the Frandsen model or the 
DWM model on land, particularly in undulating or moderately complex terrain, or sites involving 
multiple surface roughness changes. Some attempts have been made to consider additional 
influences on the models due to terrain and roughness, for example in the Windfarm Assessment 
Tool (WAT), part of the WAsP software suite [22]. In WAT, only the Frandsen model is 
utilised, and the wake centre angles are modified by half the angle between the flow direction at 
the upwind and downwind turbine, as computed from the ambient flow model. This is similar to 
assuming wake centrelines follow circular segments as they curve due to terrain. In addition, 
turbulence intensity variation due to terrain and surface roughness is modelled using the Mann 
model, based on upwind flow deformation by wind shear and terrain effects and gradual adaption 
to new equilibrium turbulence conditions after changes in surface roughness. This is not used to 
actually vary the turbulence intensity along the propagation path of the deficit however. While 
these assumptions would appear quite logical and potentially better than completely ignoring 
terrain and surface roughness effects, their validity is still an open question. In this context, it is 
a reasonable statement that the validation of engineering models for computing fatigue loads 
onshore is very much a work in progress.  
 
 
14.6. Proposed Initial Approach for Extension of Optimisation Framework Onshore 
 
Pending further research and validation, a reasonable first approach to extension of the 
optimisation framework outlined in this report, including the use of the DWM model, surrogate 
models and load case modularisation, is as follows. Firstly, an upper limit should be set on the 
terrain complexity, for which the approach is reasonably expected to be capable of providing 
accurate results. Based on various observations that linearised flow models are unable to make 
accurate predictions of flow fields due to flow separation when terrain gradients exceed 0.25-0.3 
[20][21], it would seem reasonable to set an upper gradient limit significantly lower than this, at 
0.1, to be reasonably confident that streamlines, and hence mean wake deficit paths do not 
deviate significantly from straight lines. It is acknowledged that this is a very loose guideline, 
however given the sensitivity of the DWM model to upstream turbine lateral offsets, it is 
reasonable to assume that significant differences in fatigue loads will be seen for the same turbine 
layout if the terrain causes anything other than very minor deviations from nominally straight 
streamlines. This recommendation should also preclude significant differences from occurring due 
to interactions between stability and terrain, because the terrain effect should not be large 
enough that stability conditions actually alter flow paths.  
 
As for handling of surface roughness variation, a tempting initial recommendation is to simply 
limit the amount of surface roughness variation permitted within an area defined by the distance 
==PAGE:107==
 
103
over which wake effects are observed. This is quite difficult to quantify however, because even 
assuming flat terrain, this is largely dependent on stability, wind speed, layout, and the 
definition of roughness variation itself. For example, it is unclear whether a single but significant 
change in surface roughness should be permitted, but multiple smaller changes should not, and so 
on. Hence, no specific recommendation on this is provided at this point in time. Instead, it is 
hoped that further research will provide insights into how the current versions of engineering 
wake models should be modified to eliminate these constraints, and allow wider applicability. 
 
14.7. Ambient Flow Models as Inputs 
 
As a final note, if and when further research is able to shed more light on the best guidelines for 
onshore applications of the optimisation framework, it is clear that flow models will most likely 
be required as inputs for ambient conditions. Hence, the ability of the chosen flow model to 
provide accurate results in a reasonable time frame is critical. For example, most linearised flow 
models are currently based on neutral stability conditions only, so if a given site is shown to 
deviate significantly from this (which many do) a large proportion of the time, then a CFD 
model is most likely to provide a better-quality input. In addition, in an optimisation context, 
the conditions at any arbitrary position and for numerous sets of boundary and initial conditions 
are needed, so the flow models used must be able to provide data throughout the domain, not 
simply for a few selected turbine locations, and must also be fast enough to enable simulation of 
all relevant combinations of inflow and boundary conditions, not only a selection. This is 
stretching the limits of current CFD implementations, so some effort will undoubtedly be 
required to develop such models to a point where this is feasible.

15. Conclusions and Future Work 
 
This project was initially intended to investigate the current state-of-the-art in automated layout 
optimisation, identify key limitations preventing wider adoption of such methods in the wind 
industry, and where possible propose ways to overcome these limitations. Through research into 
the current state of wind farm layout optimisation, it became apparent that the key limitations 
did not reside with optimisation techniques, but rather with the current methods for computing 
some of the inputs to the optimisation process - in particular, power production and structural 
loads. 
 
It was considered important that the optimisation should be framed in terms of COE, as this is 
the real goal for most layout optimisations in the wind industry. This is because for wind farm 
developers a lower COE means a more profitable project, for manufacturers and consultants it 
means a more attractive offering for their clients, and for society it means wind as an energy 
source is more affordable and competitive with other sources. An optimisation of COE requires 
cost models for all layout-dependent costs and revenue, most of which are not especially 
challenging to develop. However, power production and structural loads – two inputs which have 
a significant influence on the overall COE – are difficult to model due to the complicated physics 
involved. Power production obviously has a direct effect on COE, however structural loads have 
a more indirect effect – they not only increase maintenance costs and reduce turbine life, but can 
also preclude turbines from being placed in locations which might otherwise provide increased 
power production, if the expected loads exceed design loads.  
 
Because of the complicated physics involved, methods for loads evaluation in particular tend to 
be either highly simplified but less computationally intense, or more detailed but more 
computationally intense. A fast but detailed (and hence potentially more accurate and widely 
applicable) method would be ideal especially for optimisation, so the decision was made to utilise 
a more detailed method in the form of the DWM model, and attempt to maintain its accuracy 
whilst overcoming the issue of computational cost. 
 
In the process of research into the DWM model, it was found that the model under-predicted 
loads above rated speed if wake effects from only a single upstream turbine were considered – 
this was the approach used in TOPFARM, the project from which inspiration for the present 
work was drawn. Hence, an already computationally intense problem was made more so by the 
need to consider wake effects from multiple upstream turbines above rated speed. The main body 
of this work therefore focused on resolving this issue, as this was perceived to be the most 
immediate issue requiring attention. The extension of the DWM model to consider varying 
terrain and surface roughness for onshore applications was also identified as a key challenge, 
however time constraints permitted only a brief introductory exploration of this. As such, this is 
highlighted as an issue of importance for future research. 
 
With respect to the issue of under-prediction of loads at high speeds, and the generally high 
computational effort required, surrogate modelling and load case optimisation were proposed as 
solutions. The combination of these two concepts enabled the massive number of combinations of 
inflow conditions and upstream turbine layouts to be handled in a computationally efficient 
manner, and also improved load prediction at high speeds. Whilst refinement is required in the 
==PAGE:109==
 
105
generation of the surrogates themselves, no critical issues emerged during the development of 
surrogates that would appear to discount this approach from being feasible. Similarly, load case 
modularisation, or estimating turbine loading in a multi-wake situation from the results of single 
wake cases, also shows promise as a way to further reduce the number of simulations required, 
whilst overcoming under-prediction of loads. However, further testing and refinement is required.  
 
Assuming these remaining issues with surrogate generation and the load case modularisation 
approach above rated speed can be resolved, then the ability to accurately utilise the DWM 
model as an input to a cost function which is then minimised within an optimisation framework 
is within reach. Even if optimisation is not required, and an assessment of loads and power for a 
single layout is required, this approach provides a promising potential solution to one of the 
barriers currently precluding the use of the DWM model in a site assessment context. The ability 
to use the DWM model for site suitability studies is a step forward relative to the incumbent 
Frandsen model, as it not only provides the potential for more accurate calculations of fatigue 
loading in its current form, but it also enables users to take advantage of future improvements in 
the model as they are built-in. The fact that the DWM model is more closely linked to the 
underlying physics than the Frandsen model means that implementation of improvements as new 
knowledge is gained on wake interaction, turbulent structures and so on, is considerably more 
likely and simpler.  
 
The immediate next steps are further testing and refinement of the summation approach, as well 
as refinement of the surrogate generation process, as mentioned. For example, further 
investigation is needed to understand the causes of the deviations for the tower top yaw and tilt 
moments when applying the summation approach, as the attempted remedy to the deviations 
seen did not provide the desired results. The surrogate generation process needs to be further 
refined, through more comprehensive investigation into the best modelling algorithms, guiding 
error functions and settings for model training to use for the present application. Once this is 
complete, the proposed formulation for when turbines are in a row should be tested to assess 
whether the expected improvements in error are seen, when compared with the true multi-wake 
cases. In addition, the quality of the surrogate generated for correlation coefficients should be 
assessed, and the effect of low pass filtering or applying a moving average to the load signals 
prior to computing the correlation should be evaluated. 
 
In the longer term, the adaption of the DWM model to include the influence of onshore factors 
such as variations in orography and surface roughness is a high priority, however this will require 
considerable research and validation effort. Another priority is the development of flow models 
for ambient conditions that can provide all required input data at any arbitrary point on a site of 
interest, and for all relevant combinations of boundary and initial conditions, in a reasonable 
timeframe.

17. Appendix A 
 
This appendix contains the full results of the test case simulations. For each test case as listed in 
Table 2, three figures are provided.  
 
The first is a summary of the raw equivalent loads taken directly from the post-processed 
HAWC2 results, for average power and each load channel, and averaged over 10 seeds. The bar 
plots for each channel are split into four colours – dark blue, light blue, green and yellow. The 
dark blue bar represents the no wake case simulated with the full complement of load driving 
factors – ambient turbulence, shear, gravity, tower shadow, and effect of blade azimuth position. 
This is a primarily a reference case. The light blue bars represent the equivalent load from each 
of the single and multi-wake cases, again simulated with the full complement of load driving 
factors. The multi-wake cases here are the “true” multi-wake cases that the summation approach 
is attempting to approximate. The single wake cases are the cases used to quantify the 
constituent equivalent load from the first upstream turbine, as they include all load driving 
factors. The green bar represents the no wake case simulated with ambient turbulence, shear, 
gravity, and tower shadow all switched off. The loading here is solely due to the blade azimuth 
position, and as such it is the load subtracted during the summation approach 푛−1 times for 푛 
upstream turbines. Lastly, the yellow bars are the single wake cases simulated with ambient 
turbulence, shear, gravity, and tower shadow all switched off, and hence they include only the 
effects of meandering, wake added turbulence and blade azimuth position. These single wake 
cases are used to quantify the equivalent load from the second to furthest upstream turbine, so 
that the load driving factors that are switched off are only counted in the constituent equivalent 
load from the first upstream turbine. On the x-axis of each plot is the specific upstream turbine 
layout. For example, ‘[3,0];[6,0.2];[0,0]’ represents an upstream turbine topology with one wake-
generating turbine 3D upstream and 0D laterally offset, one 6D upstream and 0.2D laterally 
offset, and no third upstream turbine.  
 
The second figure for each test case shows all of the pairwise correlation coefficients between the 
load signals from the single wake cases involved in each multi-wake test case. For example, for 
three upstream turbines, there will be a correlation computed between the single wake case load 
signals from turbine 1 and turbine 2, turbine 1 and 3, and turbine 2 and turbine 3. In addition, 
the correlation between each single wake case and the no wake case with ambient turbulence, 
shear, gravity, and tower shadow all switched off, is also computed, however as these correlations 
have a minimal contribution to the overall results from the summation, these have been omitted 
from the correlation coefficient plots for clarity.  
 
The final figure for each test case is the comparison between the “true” multi-wake case 
equivalent loads and the summation approach approximation of these loads. Each plot shows a 
set of five bars per upstream turbine topology. The first, dark blue bar represents the true multi-
wake case that we are trying to match with the summation, and the second, light blue bar 
represents the result of the summation approach using the computed correlation coefficients. The 
green, orange and yellow bars are for reference and represent the summation assuming zero 
correlation between any of the single wake case load signals, summation assuming full 
correlation, and lastly, the loading when considering only the first upstream turbine. This last 
bar represents the approach taken in TOPFARM both below and above rated speed. For clarity,
