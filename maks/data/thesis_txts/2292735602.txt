Abstract
This thesis explores theoretical hydrogen-deuterium(HD) exchange catalysis on sub-
nano Pt8 clusters on α-quartz(0001) supports.
This study has unravelled some
information about the support’s role in stability and catalytic activity of the catalyst.
The results have also contributed to explaining experimental results obtained at
DTU Physics, where it was found that oxidation of clusters increased the catalytic
activity.
Pt8 cluster geometries were found from some qualitatively diﬀerent initial shapes.
I found that the clusters can be ripped apart by the support and prefer to be ﬂat,
round and pyramid shaped.
The support charges the clusters, and the Pt-Support binding was ionic to covalent.
The clusters were more stable on the support than as atoms in fcc, thereby they are
protected from sintering.
Barriers for H2 dissociation were found to be zero for some sites, thus desorption
could be the rate-limiting step for HD-exchange.
Hydrogen’s binding on Pt8 clusters was found to be preferably ontop, bridge and
hollow respectively. Comparing my energies with existent literature showed that the
support enhances the activity by weakening the H-Pt bonds.
The catalytic activities were estimated with a simple Langmuir model and
Harmonic Transition State Theory. The activity was found to increase of 5% for 1
ML H due to oxidation and 50 % at 1/8 ML H coverage. However the number of
catalytic sites that I sampled were insuﬃcient to draw any strong conclusions. Also
the model was very sensitive.
The main result of the project is that I found strong indications that oxygen acts
like a promoter for the HD-exchange activity on small silica supported sub-nano
platinum clusters.
This happens since the dissiociation of H2 is barrierless and
oxygen lowers the H-Pt bonds.

Resume
Denne afhandling handler om teoretisk hydrogen-deuterium exchange katalyse på
sub-nano Pt8 klynger understøttet på α-kvarts(0001). Dette har givet viden om silica
supportets virkning på stabiliteten og den katalytiske aktivitet af katalysatoren.
Resultaterne har også bidraget til forklaringen af nogle eksperimentelle resultater
fra DTU Fysik, hvor oxidering af klyngerne øgede den katalytiske aktivitet.
Jeg fandt klynge-geometrier fra kvalitativt forskellige startbud.
Klyngerne
kunne blive revet over af supportet, og de foretrak at være ﬂade, runde eller
pyramideformede.
Supportet lader klyngerne, og Pt-support bindingerne er af ionisk til kovalent
karakter. Klyngerne var mere stabile på supportet end atomer i en uendelig krystal,
derfor er de beskyttede mod sintring.
Brints binding og de katalytiske aktiviteter grundet oxidering undersøgt. Barrierer
for brint dissociering blev fundet til nul for nogle sites.
Brints binding på Pt8 klynger var foretrukkent ontop, bridge og hollow respektivt.
En sammenligning af disse energier med literaturen viste at supportet øger
aktiviteten ved at svække H-Pt bindingerne.
De katalytiske aktiviteter blev estimeret med en simpel Langmuir model og
Harmonisk Transition State Teori.
Jeg så at aktiviteten steg med 50% for 1/8
ML dækningsgrad og 5% 1 ML dækningsgrad. Dog blev for få sites regnet på til at
drage nogle stærke konklusioner, og modellen var meget følsom.
Hovedresultatet i dette projekt er at ilt ser ud til at være en promoter for HD-
exchange aktiviteten på små silica undersøttede sub-nano platin klynger. Dette sker
siden H2 spaltning er barriereløs og oxidering svækker H-Pt bindingerne.

Chapter 1
Introduction
During this project I have investigated the shapes and catalytic hydrogen-deuterium
exchange activities of sub-nanometer sized Pt8 particles supported by silica. I was
given the task to explain some interesting experimental results obtained by PhD
student Jakob Nordheim Riedel who measured the HD-exchange activity in a micro-
reactor for these small silica supported mass-selected Pt particles.
The goal with this thesis is to present my theoretical investigations of the structural
and catalytic aspects of small Pt8 clusters on a SiO2 support. Part of this project was
also about investigating the support’s contribution to the stabilities and catalytic
activities of sub-nano particles.
First I will give a brief description of the need for catalysis in a societal context,
then I will explain Riedel’s experiments that I have based this project on. After
that I will present the scope of this project and last I will describe how this thesis
is structured.
1.1
Heterogeneous Catalysis in Modern Society
A catalyst is a substance that provides an alternative pathway for a chemical reaction
to follow without being used up itself. This pathway should enable the conversion
from reactants to products to run much faster.
The quality of a catalyst for a
given reaction is typically measured in terms of selectivity towards a certain species,
catalytic activity, stability opposing sintering mechanisms, availability and price.
Catalysis is of vital importance to many central aspects of modern society. This
includes cleaning exhaust gas, producing fuel for the transportation sector and
manufacturing various chemical products.
Enzymes in biological matter do also
act as catalysts for various reactions. Chorkendorﬀand Niemantsverdriet[13] write
that 85-90% of the products of the chemical industry are manufactured using one or
more catalysts. Thus catalysis is of vital importance to the chemical industry and
life.
Some very important reactions that are made scaleable, feasible or even possible,
only by use of catalysis are:
Methanol synthesis, ammonia synthesis, syngas
production via steam reforming and last but certainly not least the cleaning of
automotive exhaust.
Another interesting ﬁeld where catalysis will play a major role is in the harvesting
of energy from the sun, which shines more on the earth in one hour than we consume
in one year(425×1018 J) as formulated by Lewis and Nocera[27]. The water splitting
technology by photocatalysis and development of solar fuels is currently a hot topic
discussed by many authors.

CHAPTER 1. INTRODUCTION
Rostrup-Nielsen[39] explains that to meet the ongoing needs for balancing energy
security and sustainable growth one simply needs to use catalysts in the complex
network of energy creation, carrying and conversion.
There is a need for development and research in catalysts considering the ongoing
increasing demand for energy while protecting our environment.
This need is
described thoroughly by many authors, e.g. Lewis and Nocera[27] who in one section
give a review of the continuously growing energy demand. They argue that catalysts
are key for harvesting energy from sunlight and also in conversion and storage of
this energy.
The materials that we need for producing the large amounts of energy in the
terra-watt scale are scarce. The platinum metals are often being used as catalysts,
and they are especially expensive and scarce. This is put into further perspective
by Vesborg and Jaramillo[45] who argue that one must consider how mineable an
element is in addition to considering only crustal abundance. Platinum is not that
abundant and is only produced in a few countries of which South Africa is the main
producer[45].
I see a need to study small sub-nano clusters because catalyst materials can be
scarce. Thereby one needs to maximize the number of atoms participating in a
given reaction. Also it is important to bridge the gap between sub-nano, nano, and
extended surfaces to better understand materials on multiple scales with all the
resulting similarities and diﬀerences.
A benchmark process of determining the quality of a catalyst is how well it
dissociates a given molecule such as H2, and the ﬁrst step in every catalytic reaction
is the adsorption of a molecule. The Hydrogen-Deuterium(HD) exchange reaction
is an appropriate model reaction since it can be measured relatively easily on
experimentally due to the isotope diﬀerence between H and D. The HD-exchange
reaction can be written as follows:
H2 + D2 ⇋2HD
(1.1)
Typical nanoparticle catalysts are supported by a high surface area support
material that seems to play a very important role for the catalytic activity and
stability.
In this project I have investigated the structure of supported sub-nano Pt8 clusters,
the adsorption on these and the catalytic H-D exchange activity on a low energy
cluster. The clusters were supported on SiO2 since the support plays an important
role for catalysis and cluster structure.
I would like to mention a book chapter by Alexandrova and Bouchard[1] that also
coins the term sub-nano clusters as a word for clusters of a few atoms. They write
that these systems might have nothing in common with extended surfaces or even
nanoparticles and essentially are to be considered molecules. Thus here is a void in
the research that can be ﬁlled. This thesis should ﬁll part of this void since these
supported sub-nano clusters are currently not very well investigated.
The structures of sub-nano clusters are also not very well-studied since even when
using modern electron microscopy it can be diﬃcult visualizing and ﬁnding such

1.2
HD-Exchange Experiments
This project was partly motivated by experimental ﬁndings of PhD student Jakob
Nordheim Riedel at the Center for Individual Nanoparticle Functionality(CINF).
Riedel measured the Hydrogen-Deuterium exchange catalytic activity on small
Platinum clusters supported on SiO2. This he did for particles of sizes 7-13 atoms.
The experiments were performed using a micro-reactor setup. A micro-reactor is
basically a reactor fabricated on the micron scale. The working principle and design
of the micro-reactors used in the experiments are thoroughly described by Henriksen
et al.[20], and another paper by Vesborg et al.[46] describes how the reactor is closed
without destroying the catalyst by heat damaging.
Henriksen et al.[20] write that micro-reactors give the advantages that they have
low temperature gradients across the reactor and provide safety when using explosive
gas mixtures due to low reactant concentrations. Another key advantage is that the
reactor can measure on low product concentrations, i.e. testing of new non-optimized
catalysts are possible and relatively cheap.
The catalytic activity are measured by the masses in the gas chamber by a
Quadropole Mass Spectrometer(QMS). A QMS identiﬁes elements by their mass
to charge ratio by a voltage applied over its 4 metal rods. Thus it can separate H2
and D2.
The clusters in the range 7-13 atoms were found to exhibit strange reproducible
behaviors that could not easily be explained. More speciﬁcally the activity seemed
to increase systematically; ﬁrst at 1 hour it rises, and then after 2 hours the activity
increases drastically again.
On ﬁgure 1.1 one can see such an experiment that was conducted on a Pt9 cluster
with a temperature of 80 ◦C in a micro-reactor setup.
The hypothesis of this project is that the changing activities seen on ﬁgure 1.1 are
due to diﬀerent oxidation steps of the clusters since the partial pressure of oxygen
slowly decreased in the reactor. That is there was an oxygen line, which was cleaned
too slowly in the experiments thereby oxygen was available to the system during
the full experiments.
These ﬁndings of Riedel suggested strongly that if I was to reproduce the
experimental ﬁndings at for example ﬁgure 1.1, I should look at the hydrogen
dissociative adsorption with and without oxygen on small Pt clusters and estimate
the catalytic activity using a simple model.
It is also important to rule out the possibility that water is hindering the reaction,

1.3
Project Scope
The scope of this project is to calculate the catalytic activity of small Pt clusters
on silica with and without oxygen adsorped on the catalyst. This is done in two
steps: First the structure of supported nanoparticles is investigated, and second the
binding properties and catalytic activities of the clusters are calculated.
The cluster size I work with is 8 atoms since the reproducible catalytic activity
oscillations were observed for all the particle sizes.
This project is also meant to investigate the oxide-support’s eﬀect on the catalytic
activity and geometries of small Pt clusters.

1.4
Thesis Structure
This structure of this thesis is made in a speciﬁc way to help the reader better focus
on the diﬀerent aspects of this project. That is theory is introduced ﬁrst where it is
needed to enhance focus.
This thesis can be thought of as divided into two parts, a part where I describe
the the conﬁgurational aspects of the small supported Pt clusters and a catalytic
activity part that investigates the binding and activity of the clusters.
The ﬁrst part consists of chapter 2 and 3. The second part is chapter 4 and 5.
• Chapter 2 - Density Functional Theory:
Is a theoretical chapter
that deals with the theory and practical discussion of Density Functional
Theory(DFT) that is a method for calculating energies of a system of atoms.
• Chapter 3 - Cluster Conﬁgurations:
Is about the cluster conﬁgurations.
I describe how to ﬁnd locally low energy particle shapes. Then I present my
convergence tests of the DFT calculations. Further I discuss the support and
the structures I found. Also it is discussed how oxygen binds to the cluster
and I investigate the charging of the systems by a Bader Charge analysis.
• Chapter 4 - Modeling the Catalytic Activity of Pt: Is a theory chapter.
I brieﬂy discuss Transition State Theory, which is a theory that deals with
calculating rate constants, a vital property of catalytic reactions.
Then I
present the Nudged Elastic Band method that is a method for calculating the

2.1.1
Born-Oppenheimer Approximation
The Born-Oppenheimer approximation is an approximation that helps separate the
electronic degrees of freedom from the nuclear ones, and it argues why the electronic
properties are really hard(and interesting) due to its classical treatment of the nuclei.

The Born-Oppenheimer approximation is composed of two sub-approximations: The
Adiabatic approximation and the classical nuclei approximation.
These I shall
discuss now.
Adiabatic Approximation
The ﬁrst approximation of the Born-Oppenheimer approximation is the so called
adiabatic-approximation, that can be summarized as: Electrons follow the nuclei
and stay in the same state of the electronic Hamiltonian.[24, Chap. 1]. Speaking of
the electronic Hamiltonian implies that the Hamiltonian can be split into a nuclei
and electron part; this is the essence of the adiabatic approximation.
The adiabatic approximation holds if the largest frequency of rotation of the
wavefunction times the electronic mass is small compared to the nuclei mass times
the energy gaps.
Since the mass of an electron divided by the nuclei mass always fulﬁlls m/M <
5×10−4 the adiabatic approximation holds for most systems except these where the
energy gaps are too small.
Metals have no energy gaps, and another argument must be applied to justify
the adiabatic approximation for these. Namely that typical temperatures from say
[300, 2000] K are much lower than typical Fermi-Temperatures, thus the zero-energy
excitations, that are possible without a band gap, are conﬁned to a small region
about the Fermi-Surface.
Most properties are not aﬀected by the few electrons
creating such a phenomenon, and the adiabatic approximation holds also for most
metals.

suggests. I now deﬁne the thermal wavelength λT =

2.2
Density Functional Theory
The main idea behind Density Functional Theory is that the ground state energy of
a system is determined by its electronic density. This was formulated by Hohenberg
and Kohn[21] in 1964 as the famous two Hohenberg-Kohn Theorems.
The 1st
theorem can be formulated as follows(Kohanoﬀ[24, Chap. 4])
Hohenberg-Kohn Theorem I: The external potential ˆVext is uniquely determined
by the ground state electronic density ρ0(r). Since the external potential is uniquely
determined, the ground state energy is also determined because it is a functional of
ˆVext.

2.2.5
Including Dispersive Interactions
Dispersive interactions, such as van der Waals interactions, between two non-
bonded species are important for some systems. The van der Waals interactions
are generated from the coupling of the electric ﬁeld of one molecule to another
molecule set up by electronic ﬂuctuations. The nature of these forces are dynamical
and cannot be captured by ordinary static DFT.
The van der Waals interaction must go like A(r, r′)|r −r′|−6 where A is an eﬀective
kernel. This kernel really determines the quality of the approximation. This implies
a double integral over all space and is computationally expensive to include.

2.3
Practical DFT
The recipe for performing a DFT calculation probably seems simple in spite of the
many ingredients. This is however far from the truth.
In his project I have used the Grid-Based Projector Augmented Wave(GPAW)
computer code published by Mortensen et al.[31] and reviewed by Enkovaara et
al.[15]. This code is found and documented at the webpage wiki.fysik.dtu.dk/gpaw.
The GPAW version used was 0.10.0.11364.
This I have done together with the Atomistic Simulation Environment software
package found and documented at the webpage wiki.fysik.dtu.dk/ase and published
by Bahn and Jacobsen[6].
Doing DFT on interesting systems in practice demands ﬁrst and foremost that
one represents the wavefunctions on the computer. In this project the systems were
large, hence I used the Finite Diﬀerence mode where w.f.s are represented by values
on a grid.
Other basis-sets are possible such as plane waves, which is a popular choice for
solid-state systems. The grid was chosen since the grid-mode paralellizes well over
real space as diﬀerent processors can be assigned to diﬀerent regions.
This is
important when having large systems.
I mentioned that one must solve the Kohn-Sham equations self-consistently, and in
practice this is done by giving an initial ansatz of the density, calculating the Kohn-
Sham potential from this density, solving the Kohn-Sham equations, constructing a
new density and continue this procedure until the input density equals the output.
Often eﬀective algorithms mixes a small number of previous output densities into
the new ansatz to converge better. This is true since this mixing smooths out the
changes between each iteration.
A central concept in solid-state physics and this project is periodic systems. To
represent these on a computer correctly one must demand that Bloch’s Theorem is
fulﬁlled. This gives an arbitrary phase factor from a plane-wave component of the
wave-functions. The wave-vector of this arbitrary plane-wave factor give rise to the
so-called k-points in reciprocal space.
The energy bands must be found and in practice this can be done by sampling the
k-points in the special 1st Brillouin Zone(BZ) of reciprocal space. For an inﬁnite
crystal the k-points lie inﬁnitely close and one must do an integral over BZ. This is

practically impossible and one chooses to estimate this integral as a sum over special
k-points, which has received the name Brillouin Zone sampling.
The Brillouin

Actually this is just an unbiased uniform sampling of BZ considering special points

Symmetries in BZ are often used to save some calculation time and actually time-
reversal symmetry of the Hamiltonian ensures that when calculating about the Γ-
point(k=0) only positive k’s need to be considered. When all symmetries are utilized
the remaining part one only has to consider is called the irreducible part of the BZ
where special points contribute with some weight.
So in reality one has to solve a set of coupled Kohm-Sham equations; one for
each k-point in the irreducible part of the BZ. The coupling being that the density
depends on the wavefunctions.
A larger cell means that the BZ is smaller due the deﬁnition of reciprocal space,
thus the number of k-points used to ﬁt the bands times the cell size in a periodic
direction is a number that has to be converged. This number is a material speciﬁc
quantity.
Another problem for convergence of these systems is that the Fermi-Dirac
distribution function becomes a step-function at zero temperature nF(ϵ) = θ(ϵF −ϵ).
Metals often have complicated Fermi-Surfaces and this means that at one iteration
one band could contribute and in the next not leading to convergence issues, thus
one could replace the step-function by another more smooth occupation function,
e.g. the Fermi-Dirac distribution at a higher temperature. Then after a calculation
naturally the result must be extrapolated to zero temperature.
Now I will discuss how to represent the states close to the nuclei that are rapidly
oscillating in real space. This is done by the Projector Augmented Wave(PAW)
method. Without PAW they would be impossible to store on a computer due to the
rapid oscillations.
2.3.1
Projector Augmented Waves
The Projector Augmented Wave(PAW) method is motivated by the fact that the
wavefunctions are strongly oscillating in regions close to the nuclei due to the
attractive force on the electrons, and behave rather smoothly in interstitial regions.
Therefore it is much more computationally expensive to represent the wave-functions
inside such a so-called augmentation region where a larger basis set or denser grid
is needed to represent the wave-functions with adequate precision.
It is useful to distinguish between three types of electrons: Core, semi-core and
valence electrons. Core electrons are chemically frozen and do not participate in
bonding, semi-core electrons feel the environment and polarize but do not participate
directly in bonding either and valence electrons are the chemically active ones.
An all-electron calculation is deﬁned as a calculation where electrons are classiﬁed
as either core or valence electrons and space is divided into regions near atoms
called augmentation spheres and the interstitial-regions between the atoms. Then

Parallelization Options
To run parallel calculations with many CPUs raises a question on how to use the
computer resources most eﬃciently. I will now give a brief discussion on the options
for performing parallel calculations in GPAW.
First and most obvious is the parallelization over regions in real-space. Here real-
space is decomposed into domains where diﬀerent CPUs can perform calculations
and communicate over the network.
Another option that is usually the most eﬃcient for DFT calculations is the
parallelization over k-points, i.e. over points in reciprocal space. Since this type
of parallelization is most eﬃcient for many systems GPAW does maximize the
parallelization over k-points per default.
It is also possible to perform calculations on the bands in parallel. This approach
is however only feasible when a large number of bands is needed in the calculation.
I have parallelized the calculations in this project in the order k-points, real-space
domain decomposition and band-parallelization to utilize the computer resources
most eﬃciently. Band paralellization was never necessary though.

3.1.1
The BFGS Algorithm
BFGS(LineSearch) is a Quasi-Newton Method. A Quasi-Newton method stems from
Newton’s principe that makes a quadratic approximation to the multidimensional
total energy function E(x) where the 3N-dimensional position vector is x =
(x1, x2, · · · , xN) for N particles. Newton’s method requires explicit calculation of
the Hessian that is an expensive operation involving solving a system of equations.
The Hessian is a square-matrix containing the second order partial derivatives in its
elements. The eigenvalues of the Hessian for a critical point x0 provides information
about the type of critical point; maximum, minimum or saddle-point.
Quasi-Newton methods approximates and updates the Hessian at each iteration.
Actually the inverse Hessian is approximated to save computational resources.
Speciﬁcally the BFGS algorithm updates the inverse Hessian matrix at iteration
k, W k as follows[12]:

A general Quasi-Newton Algorithm consists of four steps.
Before starting one
initializes the positions, chooses a convergence criterion ϵ, initialize the inverse

Then initially the forces are also computed. The algorithm is as follows[12]:
Step 1: is to see if the maximal force is small enough that it is converged: max |F| ≤
ϵ. If not go to step 2.
Step 2: is to compute the step direction d from W: d = −WF.
Step 3: Compute, e.g.
by Line-Search the step size Quasi-Newton Algorithm
starting from the full step size corresponding to t = 1 in xk+1 = xk + td. If the
energy is not close to quadratic this means that the algorithm could climb up in
energy if t is too large. Thus Line Search algorithms solves this problem of ﬁnding
t.
Step 4: Compute new inverse Hessian W and go to step 1.
The Line Search algorithm chooses an optimal step size when the direction is
calculated from the inverse Hessian. It therefore roughly said minimizes E(xk + td)
by optimizing t while keeping t as large as possible to ensure a faster convergence.
The BFGSLineSearch algorithm uses the so-called Wolfe conditions(see Bonnanas
et al.[12, Chap 3.]) to achieve this balancing of the step-size.

The FIRE Algorithm
For the energy barrier calculation with Nudged Elastic Band I have used the FIRE
algorithm that was invented by Bitzek et al. [10]. FIRE was used since it performs
well in Nudged Elastic Band Calculations.
It is a molecular dynamics(MD) method, thus one integrates Newton’s 2nd Law

under the inﬂuence of some forces. Let in the following F, v and x mean the 3Natoms
dimensional force, velocity and position vectors for the system respectively.
In FIRE one integrates Newton’s 2nd Law adjusting the trajectory by two rules.
The ﬁrst rule is that upon uphill motion in the energy landscape, determined by
the power P = v · F ≤0, the velocities are set to zero instantaneously.
The
second rule is mixing the force-vector and velocity v by a mixing parameter α:
v →(1 −α)v + α ˆF|v|. This rule comes from the need for a ﬁnite time-step ∆t.
The relatively fast convergence to the minimal positions must all be attributed to
the increment of the time step, which is done when the power has been positive for
some steps. In this way FIRE uses the system inertia to minimize the energy.

Modeling the Silica Support
The silica support used in the experiments of Jakob Riedel was amorphous. This
section is devoted to describing how I chose to represent the support on a computer.
As it was probable that the Pt clusters bind to defects on the support, I will
also present my investigations of the Pt cluster binding to support defects. The
parameters used in these calculations were presented on table 3.3.
First and foremost I must mention that the modeling of these systems was based
on a review article of experiments concerning metals on oxides by Fu and Wagner[16]
that in one section discuss metals on SiO2 surfaces.
In this section Fu and Wagner writes that α-Quartz is the most stable conﬁguration
of SiO2 and the (0001) surface can be considered a model surface. Thus I have
chosen to look at the (0001) surface of α-Quartz. The experiments were also run at
temperatures and pressures where α-quartz is the most stable polymorph.
Fu and Wagner also write that metals bind weakly to regular sites, but strongly
to vacancies.

non-bridging oxygen(≡Si −O·) followed by the Silicon dangling bond(≡Si·). The
non-bridging oxygen defect actually corresponds to bonding the Pt clusters on the
oxygen-terminated side of the Quartz(0001) surface, i.e. without any vacancy. Thus
I will refer to the non-bridging oxygen systems as having no vacancies.
So I chose to investigate the Pt8 binding energies on 3 diﬀerent support systems,
one without any vacancies and two with two distinct Si-dangling bonds. These Si-

7 Pt atoms were placed in a hexagonal shape stemming from the fcc(111) surface,

guesses for the geometry.
Another argument for the initial guess is that it stems from a fcc(111) surface since
Pt to some degree prefers this structure.
This approach gives 6 unique cluster conﬁgurations for a given Pt-cluster position
in the quartz-surface plane.
Thus these 6 initial clusters were also relaxed over
diﬀerent positions on the Quartz-surface. Also I have experimented with placing
the 8th Pt atom in the same plane as the 7 ﬁrst this also gives 6 possibilities for
each (x,y) coordinates of the initial 7 atoms.

Binding Energies
Looking at the binding energies of the Pt8 clusters with respect to bulk on ﬁgure
3.6 one can see that atoms in the most stable clusters actually are more stable when
in clusters than when in bulk fcc crystals. This indicates in fact that sintering is
unfavorable.
When a catalyst sinters it loses some activity due to the nanoparticles assembling.
This is true since catalysis happens on the surface of the catalyst. Avoiding sintering
is of vital importance for a catalyst that ideally must be stable for years in various
applications, i.e. it is expensive to shut down a large plant for example to change
the catalyst.
The relative error bars on the cluster binding energies are small, and the low energy
cluster I can conclude is signiﬁcantly lower in energy than the others.
Zyubina et al. also found that the support binds the clusters with on average
2.13 eV per atom for the supported Pt6 cluster.
This is in excellent agreement
with my minimum energy cluster where the binding energy is 18.11/8 eV = 2.26 eV,
i.e. very close. However my and their reference vacuum clusters are not the global
minimum energy cluster, and our reference clusters mismatch. The energy orders of
magnitudes are comparable and this is really what makes sense to compare due to
many uncertainties.

4.1
Transition State Theory
In this section I shall describe how to approximate rate constants since they are
essential for modeling catalytic activities. My summary is based on the work of
Mills et al.[29].
Transition State Theory(TST) is a theory and a practical method for calculating
chemical rates.
Usual discussions of TST are very complex and try to be generally applicable. I
would like to discuss TST in a manner that is speciﬁc to the purpose in this project.
Namely that I use NEB to ﬁnd the MEP and then calculate the reaction rate using
TST.
In the following it is assumed that the reactants, consisting of N atoms, move
around in reaction coordinate space of dimension 3N.
TST is based of the division of reaction coordinate space into reactant and product
regions. This division is done by ﬁnding a reasonable dividing surface called the
transition state. The surface is of dimension 3N −1.
There are some important assumptions involved in TST, the most important being
that the reactants are Boltzmann distributed, thus also the rate of transition needs
to be suﬃciently slow that this distribution is maintained. The word slow means
relative to the time-scale of vibrations. Also here one should note that it is assumed
that most hydrogen molecules that reach the barrier in reaction coordinate space are
reﬂected. Then one can write the reaction, e.g. hydrogen dissociation, as a reaction
with the transition state as an intermediate state in equilibrium with the reactant
state:

5.7
Summary
I have investigated the chemisorption on Pt(111) and supported Pt8 clusters at 1/8
ML and 1 ML H coverage.
First I presented the zero point energies that were
consistent with existent literature. The Pt(111) hydrogen adsorption site was found
to be fcc-hollow.
At 1/8 ML hydrogen coverage the preferred binding site was found to be both
ontop and bridge depending on the geometry. Hollow binding was never observed.
The minimum energy path for H2 dissociation was found to be barrierless both with
and without oxygen.
At 1 ML coverage the binding was more clearly ontop, likely due to H-H repulsions.
The oxygen presence lowers the bond strength of the hydrogen, and since desorption
is likely the rate limiting step the catalytic activities increase. There were found
barriers for splitting H2 in some cases, but I did not have time to make a systematic
study.
The catalytic activities were found to increase a bit due to oxidation for the 1 ML
covered cluster. They increase more when using the numbers for binding at 1/8 ML
coverage. This suggested that the model is too sensitive to small energy changes,
thus probably also changes in Pt8 cluster shapes.
The oxide(OH-group) was found to be stable on the cluster for some time due to
the ﬁnite barrier for removing OH as H2O. This is pleasing since it makes this whole
explanation that oxide is a promoter for HD-exchange possible.
All in all I have found indications that oxidation of the Pt8 cluster is promoting
the catalytic HD-exchange activity but the model is too sensitive and the sample
size is too small to make any ultimate conclusions.

Chapter 6
Conclusion & Outlook
I have presented Density Functional Theory calculations at the Generalized Gradient
level to investigate the structure and catalytic HD-exchange activity of Pt8 clusters
on silica supports. The eﬀect of oxygen on the catalytic activity was investigated.
6.1
Conclusion
My most important conclusion is that I found strong indications that oxidizing Pt
sub-nano clusters on silica supports lowers the hydrogen chemisorption energy, and
since I found H2 dissociation to be barrierless the catalytic activity increases due to
oxidation.
To get to this result I ﬁrst and foremost I investigated what type of defect in the
silica support surface would bind the Pt strongest. I found that no oxygen vacancy
resulted in the strongest binding on average.
Then I have found the local energetic minimum shapes of six qualitatively diﬀerent
nanoparticles. These small clusters prefer nothing like fcc structures and must be
considered as molecules. The clusters were found to be round, ﬂat and pyramid
shaped on the support. The Pt-Support interaction were to strong that some clusters
were ripped apart.
The clusters were very much alive, but despite their liveliness they were very stable
compared to a Pt8 cluster in vacuum. Also 3 clusters were more stable than as 8 Pt
atoms belonging to a bulk fcc crystal. The fact that they are more stable than when
in bulk suggests that sintering would not be deactivating the clusters as catalysts.
The binding energies of the Pt8 clusters were found to be in the order of 1.7-2.3
eV/atom with respect to a vacuum cluster.
The stabilization of the Pt8 clusters are partly due to the interactions with the
support through charge transfer, and oxidation of the cluster charged it additionally.
Oxidizing the cluster revealed quickly that the bridge site was preferred as binding
site, consistent with literature. This O is further stabilized by 0.48 eV by adsorbing
a H making it an OH group. The oxygen binding energy was found to be on the
order of 1-2 eV using water as a reference.
I also found a barrier for removing OH as water, thus the oxygen is stable for some
time on the cluster even though gaseous water is a more stable system. This adds
to my belief in the initial hypothesis that oxidation enhances the activity.
The structural parameters are hard to measure experimentally on such small
systems, however comparing my results with XAFS measurements of nearest
neighbor distances and numbers one could see that my structures were very plausible.

CHAPTER 6. CONCLUSION & OUTLOOK
In the second part of this thesis I presented the results about binding and catalytic
HD-exchange activity calculations on these systems.
I started investigating H binding on the Pt(111) surface and found the fcc site as
the preferred site for H binding. The zero point energy correction shifts the preferred
site from ontop to fcc and must by all means be included.
The preferred site for hydrogen binding on the nanoparticles were found to be ontop
and bridges. For 1/8 ML H coverage bridge and ontop were equally preferable, but
at 1 ML the preferred site was clearly ontop likely due to H-H repulsion. This is
consistent with literature.
The binding energies were signiﬁcantly lowered by the support, thus the catalytic
activity is enhanced by the support. This result is based on comparison with Zhou
et al.[50] that did a theoretical study of sub-nano clusters in vacuum.
I found that the binding energies of H on the Pt cluster decreased on the oxidized
clusters for some sites while other sites were unaﬀected. This was likely partly due
to charging and partly due to reconﬁguration of the cluster.
The H2 dissociation happens without any barrier for some sites both at 1/8 ML and
1 ML H coverage and for the oxidized cluster. At larger 1 ML coverage there is a non-
negligible barrier at some sites. With no barrier for H2 dissociation, desorption is the
rate-limiting step. That is assuming that diﬀusion barriers are small as suggested
by Zuybina et al.[51].
I also observed that molecular adsorption was favorable in one case. This molecular
adsorption were realized by ripping at Pt apart from the cluster that essentially
became a PtH2 molecule. Hence the clusters are very much alive although they are
stable.
The catalytic activities were estimated using a simple model. Only a very small
increase in total activity of 5 % due to oxidation were observed at 1 ML H coverage.
A sensitivity analysis of the model showed that at 1/8 ML H coverage the total
activity of the cluster increased by 50% due to oxidation. This showed that my
model was way too sensitive to small changes.
I have also a way too small sample size: Both the number of sites sampled was too
small and one should also sample multiple clusters to get something like an ensemble
average.
Although I cannot draw any sharp conclusions I think that when putting together
all the pieces of this puzzle the picture points towards oxygen being a promoter for
HD-exchange activity on silica supported sub-nano Pt clusters. So all in all I can
conclude that my results support the initial hypothesis.
6.2
Outlook
This project has supported the experimental investigations that oxygen is a promoter
for HD-exchange on sub-nano Pt clusters.
Since oxygen might be too dangerous to use in large scale applications one could
look for other promoters, maybe someone that are stable on the clusters for much
longer times.

since these small clusters are very diﬀerent from larger particles.
Given a little more time I would have liked to investigate what coverage the oxygen

This thesis has ﬁlled a small gap in the understanding of the behavior of
small supported clusters for catalysis and hopefully one day the accumulated
understanding from several studies will be beneﬁcial to the ﬁeld of catalysis.
