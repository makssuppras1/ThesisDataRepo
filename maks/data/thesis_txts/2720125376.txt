Abstract
The service and residual life of gas turbines are essential parameters for turbine operators
to have access to. Turbine blades are often the components that determine the lifetime of
a gas turbine. Non-destructive (NDT) and destructive techniques are available to exam-
ine the condition of a blade exposed to service. The lifetime of the blade coating is usually
shorter than that of the material it covers. Its degradation is easily repaired by removing
the coating and applying a new one. Conversely, if the coated material’s microstructure
is damaged, it becomes unusable, causing significant material and economic losses.
The lifetime assessment and rejuvenation of the Siemens GT-200 gas turbine first-stage
conventionally cast blade made of MAR-M247 is presented. Four blades from different
gas turbines, but the same model, were provided by FORCE Technology for microstruc-
tural study and characterization. These blades experienced external cracks in different
airfoil and root sections assisted by coating and base alloy degradation due to operation
at high temperatures. After visual and X-ray examination, the blade with the least appar-
ent external and internal distortion was chosen for study.
A detailed analysis of all elements which influenced the blade lifetime during service was
carried out, namely: coating degradation due to oxidation and diffusion; decrease of alloy
mechanical properties due to the precipitation of detrimental phases and carbides in grain
boundaries; the influence of the gamma (γ) and gamma prime (γ′) phase distribution and
its impact in the overall alloy behavior during service due to its morphological changes
(nucleation, growth, and coarsening).
Through thermodynamic calculations and microstructural LOM and SEM analysis focused
on the γ and γ′ phase evolution, considering mainly the blade core rather than the outer
edges, a solution and aging heat treatments were found to optimally rejuvenate the mor-
phology, distribution, and homogeneity of the γ′ phase in the γ matrix after service.

Preamble
The present research, which is of a confidential nature, was carried out in collaboration
with FORCE Technology and the Technical University of Denmark (DTU). Therefore, some
of the experiments and investigations were carried out at FORCE Technology’s premises
in Brøndby, metallographic preparations, SEM, and EDS analyses. While CT scanning,
blade sections, and heat treatment were performed at DTU facilities.
Due to time constraints and unforeseen events, some comparisons between the mi-
crostructures were less accurate because of disruptions with the equipment used. For
example, the SEM equipment used for the first analyses became inoperative, and an al-
ternative model had to be used as a substitute for the later studies. This resulted in the
image resolutions obtained in the rejuvenated section being slightly outside the range of
the other two sections.
Outline
The first two chapters present the theoretical behavior of MAR-M247 superalloys in the
context of the first-stage turbine blades of the industrial gas turbine object of study. Chap-
ter three describes the theories of nucleation, growth, and coarsening, where special
attention is given to Ostwald ripening. Chapter four describes the surface and internal
characteristics of the blades through visual examination and X-rays, with different soft-
ware (Avizo and ORS Dragonfly). Chapters five to seven describe the metallographic
preparation of the samples and the microstructural analysis of the coating via LOM, SEM-
EDS, and a more in-depth study of the core areas, which is of greater importance for the
project, with the software (ImageJ). Chapter eight describes the thermodynamic proce-
dure developed for the determination of the heat treatment, with the software (Thermo-
calc and Python), and chapter nine its microstructural analysis. Finally, chapters ten and
eleven contain the conclusions and future work, respectively.

1
Introduction
Nowadays, in modern gas turbines (GT), more and more emphasis is placed on improving
efficiency and reducing NOx emissions. The selection of hot gas path component materi-
als has an essential role in achieving these goals through higher operating temperatures
and reduced cooling. As turbine inlet temperatures approach 1.200◦C or higher, alloys
with greater creep rupture strength, increased fatigue strength, and better environmental
resistance are required. Hot gas path components in modern gas turbines experience
cyclic stress or strain conditions under gas turbine operation. Cyclic stresses can be
caused by vibrations or just by cyclic mechanical loads. Cyclic strains can arise due to
temperature changes during operation or due to start-ups and shutdowns. Thus, fatigue
stresses and strains are an operational requirement for components in the hot gas path
[1].
1.1
Industrial gas turbines
A gas turbine is a power plant that produces a large amount of energy for its size and
weight ratio. In the last 40 years, their use has only increased, especially in the power
generation industry, ship propulsion, and aeronautics. Nowadays, fossil fuels, mainly oil
and gas, are the significant sources of heat for conventional gas turbines. The calorific
value (Hf) is one of the most important factors to consider when choosing a fuel, which
is why gas turbines traditionally operate with fuels of high calorific value, such as natural
gas (Hf = 42-55 MJ/kg) and diesel fuel oil (Hf = 42-46 MJ/kg) [2].
Over the past three decades, gas turbine technology has undergone intensive develop-
ment and has successfully increased the amount of energy produced by power plants.
This is due to the rise in operating temperatures through advances in materials tech-
nology, coatings, and cooling systems [3]. These factors, together with the compressor
pressure ratio increase, have improved the gas turbine’s thermal efficiency from around
15% to more than 45% at elevated temperatures exceeding 1.600◦C [4].
1.1.1
Gas turbine general components
The gas turbine engine is an energy conversion device that transforms chemical energy
stored in the fuel into mechanical energy through rotational power. The basic operating
principles involve the introduction of atmospheric air into the engine, where it is com-
pressed, mixed with fuel, and combusted. The hot gas generated is transported at high
speed through a series of blades that transfer the energy created by combustion to rotate
an output shaft. During the process, the residual thermal energy from the exhaust gases
can be used for different industrial processes. The principal components of the industrial
gas turbine SGT-200 are illustrated in Figure 1.1. Each of these components is subdi-
vided into multiple elements. The present work focuses on the first-stage turbine blades,
the most critical components of gas turbines.
1.1.2
Operating principle
The thermal cycle represented by gas turbines is the Brayton cycle, depicted in Figure
1.2. In particular, SGT-200 operation follows an open cycle as the fluid passing through is
continuously renewed. Air is drawn in from the atmosphere, compressed, and then flows
into the combustion chamber, where it mixes with the fuel, and ignition takes place. The
high-temperature gases from combustion flow through the turbine, expanding and moving
the shaft, which drives the turbine compressor and the alternator.

First stage turbine blades
First-stage blades are located downstream of the combustion chamber. Their root at-
taches them to a turbine disc and, in some cases, as in the present work, exhibit a casing
at their tip, which forms a ring when all blades are assembled, Figure 1.3a.
Operating conditions are such that the high temperature of the gas flow and the stress to
which they are exposed give rise to various degradation mechanisms making them among
the most critical components of power plant gas turbines. Materials with improved thermo-
mechanical properties can provide only a certain heat resistance capability. Therefore,
blade cooling technology is essential; they may feature two cooling types: passive pro-
tection and air-cooling methods, which have been singularly successful systems for the
past 50 years [12].
1.3.1
Passive protection
According to the literature, high-temperature hot corrosion (HTHC) in Nickel-base super-
alloys results from accelerated oxidation at temperatures typically above 700◦C, whereas
low-temperature hot corrosion (LTHC) occurs at lower temperatures. During service at
these temperatures, materials are exposed to oxygen and other environmental compo-
nents in the form of gases such as CO2 and SO2, molten salts, and solid particulates.
Some constituents are exhaust products of the industrial process, while others arise as
fuel impurities. The solid particles may remain in particulate form or melt during their
transit through the system.
Coating and temperature interactions
The interaction of these environmental components with the blades causes corrosion and
erosion. Corrosion consists of parasitic chemical reactions, while erosion consists of me-
chanical impact and associated material loss. However, the latter can accelerate envi-
ronmental interactions by removing protective oxide scaling [13].

1.3.3
Failure mechanisms
The service life of the blades can be reduced when degradation mechanisms are activated
over time. Studies reveal that industrial turbine blades’ most critical failure mechanisms
are creep damage, fatigue, and environmental attack (oxidation, hot corrosion, erosion,
and foreign object impact damage) [18]. As a protocol, the power plant is shut down in
the event of blade failure, leading to prolonged stoppages resulting in severe economic
losses. When this happens, it is necessary to carry out a detailed analysis of the failures
to understand the problem and improve the reliability of the turbine system.
Structural changes in a gas turbine blade as a result of high temperature and
stress
When a gas turbine is in operation, the start-up and shut-down phases cause the blades
to be subjected to non-stationary mechanical and thermal loads. This exposure signif-
icantly influences their reliability. Overheating of the superalloy contributes significantly
to decreasing the creep resistance at high temperatures, especially in the presence of
tensile stresses [19].
The maximum thermo-mechanical stresses and strains are not evenly distributed along
the blade. This depends on the conditions of the operating fluid as well as on the proper-
ties and geometry of the blade. Generally speaking, it can be stated that the mechanical
stresses are concentrated on the lower edges of the blade, specifically in the root, since
it is fixed to the rotor axis. This fact justifies that the blade root is thicker than the blade
tip since it withstands higher centrifugal forces. On the other hand, the blade deformation
reaches larger displacements at the tip than at the base of the blade. The blade thickness
becomes thinner and thinner as the blade height increases in order to reduce the centrifu-
gal force it has to withstand. Consequently, the blade tip undergoes greater deformations
[20, 21].
The airfoils feature convex and concave sides, with the rotor turning towards the con-
cave side, Figure 1.3b. On the concave side, the pressure side (PS), the pressure flow
has negative angles of incidence, while on the convex side, the suction side (SS), the
pressure flow has positive angles of incidence [22]. Taking the analysis further in steady-
state, the leading edge (LE) undergoes maximum temperatures decreasing towards the
trailing edge (TE) and blade root. This results in a temperature fall from the LE to the
TE as heat is transferred from the region of high temperature to the region of lower tem-
perature [23]. Hence the maximum heat flux occurs at the TE, which will therefore be
where the maximum total deformations occur. Thus, the blade tip on the TE has the most
significant overall deformation.
For the current project, understanding this thermo-mechanical behavior is crucial to select
the areas of the blade on which to focus the study.

Nickel-base superalloys
There are two main classes of superalloys depending on the element taken as a base,
namely Ni or Co base superalloys [24]. These superalloys have characteristics that dif-
ferentiate them from other metals or alloys. The first is their capacity to operate at a
temperature close to their melting temperature. The second is their mechanical stability
to long working cycles.
In particular, Nickel-base superalloys are the most widely used as they exhibit high me-
chanical strength at elevated temperatures, high creep resistance, thermal fatigue, ero-
sion, and excellent resistance to oxidation [25]. These thermo-mechanical properties
are mainly influenced by its microstructural characteristics consisting of a matrix phase
gamma (γ) and the precipitated phase gamma prime (γ′).
Lattice misfit and volume fraction of γ′
The lattice misfit parameter and the volume fraction of the γ′ phase in the γ matrix are
among the most critical microstructural features, which strongly affect the dislocations
density and network evolution rate and, consequently, the creep life of superalloys.
The lattices of the matrix γ and phase γ′ are crystallographically coherent. The crystal
structure L12 of γ′ is a face-centered cubic crystal structure (FCC) with nickel (Ni) atoms
at the centers of the unit cell faces and aluminum (Al) or titanium (Ti) atoms at the corners,
Ni3(Al, Ti), Figure 2.1b, with a lattice parameter similar to that of the Ni-rich FCC structure
of γ Figure 2.1a. The γ′ precipitates with an ordered L12 crystal structure are coherently
embedded in a γ nickel solid solution. Depending on the alloy composition, both phases
often have slightly different lattice parameters [26]. This γ/γ′ lattice misfit δ is defined

Given the coherence between the two phases and the atomically ordered γ′ phase, it is
difficult for the dislocations of the γ to penetrate the γ′ phase. The order interferes with
the movement of the dislocations and thus strengthens the alloy [29]. These factors im-
prove the creep behavior of Ni-base superalloys, making them functional at temperatures
equivalent to more than 70% of their melting temperature (Tm). Few metallic materials
possess excellent creep resistance at such high temperatures [30]. The exceptional yield
strength evolution with the temperature of Ni-base superalloys under service conditions

Classification
There are three main classifications of Ni-base superalloys, according to their primary
reinforcement mechanism [32, 33], namely:
• Solid solution strengthened superalloys:
Solution strengthened are superalloys that get their strength primarily from the formation
of γ phase. The strengthening results from lattice distortions caused by solute atoms.
This distortion in the structure generates local stress fields that interact with the stress
field associated with the dislocations and act by hindering dislocation motion, leading to
an increase in strength.
The way in which alloying elements are introduced into the structure is predominantly
determined by their size. If the alloying element is large enough to directly replace an
atom in the lattice of the pure metal, this will result in a substitutional alloy. While, if the
alloying element is small enough, usually up to 57% of the radius of the principal atom, it
will fit into the interstitial sites between the atoms of the pure metal [34, 33].

• Precipitation strengthened superalloys:
Precipitation strengthened are superalloys that get their strength primarily from the pre-
cipitation of γ′. In these superalloys, coherent precipitates restrict dislocation movement.
When precipitate sizes are small, strengthening occurs by dislocation shearing of the pre-
cipitates, while at larger precipitate sizes, strengthening occurs through Orowan looping
[32, 33].
• Oxide dispersion strengthened superalloys (ODS):
Alloys strengthened by oxide dispersion are manufactured by mechanical alloying and
contain fine incoherent oxide particles that are harder than the matrix phase and inhibit
dislocation movement via Orowan looping. Dislocations can only overcome these parti-
cles by climbing; this scaling is less energetically favorable (it occurs at high temperatures)
than simple shearing and therefore stops dislocation movement more efficiently [32, 35].

Investment Casting
Investment casting is a metallurgical processing technique consisting of coating a wax
pattern, Figure 2.6a, with a refractory ceramic slurry, Figure 2.6b. The wax in the mould
is then melted to form a cavity which is fired to obtain a sintered mould with sufficient
mechanical strength, where the molten alloy is poured into the void left by the wax. The
precise control of the cooling process gives the material its high performance thermome-
chanical quality. Once solidified, the mould and internal core (which gives rise to the cool-
ing channel in the blade) are subjected to a demoulding process, during which it is sep-
arated from the casting by mechanical or chemical processes. Lastly, a dissolution heat
treatment is applied to the product to mitigate shrinkage porosity, homogenize composi-
tional segregation and dissolve interdendritic precipitates and microstructure, followed by
an aging treatment, thus conferring the desired mechanical properties and morphology
[39].

Influence of the manufacturing process on the material properties
Metallurgical factors, such as grain size, affect the mechanical properties of the alloy,
being a critical parameter for Equiaxed (EQ) alloys. Larger grain sizes reduce fatigue
life, yield strength, and toughness [58]. In EQ alloys, the grain boundaries are oriented
in arbitrary directions, which increases the overall grain boundary area, thus, making the
material more susceptible to transverse loading. Compared to directionally solidified (DS)
or single crystal (SX) alloys, EQ alloys are more prone to grain boundary slippage and
creep porosities. An increase in grain size decreases ductility and leads to increased seg-
regation of melting point depressing elements at the grain boundary. The grain boundary
phases cannot completely dissolve into the surrounding matrix, and their partial dissolu-
tion leads to the formation of low melting point eutectics [59]. The formation of these γ/γ′
eutectics is deleterious and leads to crack nucleation [60].

3
Nucleation, growth and coarsening
Solidification and melting are transformations between crystallographic and non crystal-
lographic states of a metal or alloy. Thus, material particle and grain size distributions
are essential for characterizing superalloys microstructures and their time evolution un-
der temperature exposure. As a matter of principle, it is assumed that not only the mean
particle or grain size but also its stochastic counterpart influences many microstructure
properties relationships of Ni-base superalloys [63].
When a single-phase system transitions to a two-phase metastable state, for instance,
by high-temperature cooling or pressure change, a second-phase nucleates, grows, and
coarsens. This second-phase nucleation takes place as the energy of the single-phase
system can be reduced by the formation of second-phase regions (precipitates). Nuclei
growth then continues due to heat diffusion from the nuclei to the matrix or mass diffusion
from the matrix to the nuclei. The result is a dispersion of second-phase particles into
the matrix. Then, the second-phase coarsening, also called Ostwald ripening, occurs, in
which larger particles grow at the expense of smaller ones through a diffusion-controlled
process [64, 65]. All three processes (nucleation, growth, and coarsening) do not occur
in a strict sequence but overlap in time.
For this reason, nucleation, growth, and coarsening play an essential role in the mi-
crostructural evolution during solution and aging heat treatments.
3.1
Nucleation and growth principles
Nucleation is typically treated as a stochastic process, whereas the subsequent growth
and coarsening follow more deterministic mechanisms.
In solid-state transformations, such as precipitation hardening from a supersaturated solu-
tion, the material is rapidly cooled from a single-phase field to a low temperature and then
annealed at higher temperatures. The transformation process is slow because solid-state
diffusion through the crystal lattice is comparatively to liquid diffusion, and it is, therefore,
possible for metastable phases to nucleate and grow with time and temperature. At equi-
librium, the system is in the two-phase region. Figure 3.1 is a schematic representation
of a theoretical phase diagram showing a precipitation process between a binary mixture
of A and B atoms. For an initial alloy composition equal to X0

temperature is usually too slow to trigger nucleation and growth. As shown in the figure,
a heat treatment Tanneal is necessary to trigger diffusion. The supersaturation and the
average matrix concentration change continuously during this heat treatment [65].

at the original value. This gives rise to a concentration gradient resulting in a mass flow
of B atoms toward the growing particle. The flux of B atoms into the particle limits this
particle’s growth, which depends on the concentration gradient.
3.2
Coarsening principles
The microstructure of a two-phase alloy is unstable if the total interfacial free energy is
not minimal. A dispersion of solid or liquid particles in a matrix increases the overall en-
ergy due to interfacial energy associated with the interfacial area. The interfacial area
decreases with time as the system tries to minimize the energy. However, many small
particles have more interfacial area than large particles with the same volume. Thus, as

the number of particles in the dispersion increases, the energy contained in the interfacial
area of dispersion increases. Decreasing the number of particles reduces the excess en-
ergy associated with the interfaces. Therefore, a dispersion coarsens. This phenomenon
is referred to as Ostwald ripening [69, 70, 65, 68].

The coarsening of γ′ precipitates in superalloys involves multiple factors and impacts the
performance of mechanical properties, such as strength and creep resistance.
In practice, the particle coarsening rate may not follow a linear relationship, as described

strength depends on the dispersion of fine precipitates should have a low value of this
ripening coefficient.

Solidification process
As the casting is poured, the liquid in contact with the mold wall is cooled in a controlled
process below the liquidus temperature. Solid nuclei then form on the mold wall and begin
to grow in the liquid. Thus, resembling an originally spherical solid particle, the nuclei will
develop arms in many directions, as shown in Figure 3.5.
As the primary arms elongate, their surfaces become unstable and break into secondary
and even tertiary arms, Figure 3.5a. This growth, which starts parallel to the direction
of heat flow, depends on the interaction between the temperature gradient, the dendrite
protrusion shape, and solute segregation, Figure 3.5b. Accompanying the change in mor-
phology there is a change in the direction of the primary arms, which move away from
the direction of heat flow and toward the crystallographically preferred directions. By this
mechanism, once the growth of individual crystals in the liquid has been completed during
cooling, the resulting structure is a randomly oriented equiaxed grain arrangement.

Predictive models for temperature and coarsening
Blade geometry and service conditions make the temperature of a turbine blade in service
challenging to measure. Accurate service data, such as temperature, is necessary to pre-
dict service life, as it influences degradation. Advances in numerical modeling in the past
years have made it possible to develop models capable of estimating service tempera-
tures based on the microstructure. This approach can then be used to estimate in-service
blade temperatures of similar materials and a more accurate prediction of residual life.
Among these models for estimating metal temperatures of a first-stage GT blade exposed
to service is the one developed by K.V. Dahl & J. Hald [72], in which the interdiffusion at
the interface between a MCrAlY coating and the underlying IN738 superalloy was stud-
ied in a large matrix of isothermally treated specimens, establishing a parabolic growth
model to estimate the metal temperature near the coating/substrate interface based on
the growth kinetics of the precipitate-free zone according to the Arrhenius equation.

Sample preparation
The metallographic preparation of samples to determine microstructural characteristics
requires the following steps in sequence: sectioning, mounting, grinding, polishing, and
etching. The equipmeent used is summarized in Appendix A.
5.2.1
Sample extraction
In general terms, nickel-base superalloys have characteristics that make them difficult to
cut. This is due to having a high-temperature resistance, high hardness, high strength,
high degree of work hardening, and poor thermal conductivity [76]. Being face-centered
austenitic (FCC) alloys, machinability is poorer than that of steels and is more challenging
to section than most steels when they have a high gamma prime γ′ content [77]. Many
variables affect the machining operation, the cutting tool being one of the most significant.
Some requirements for any cutting tool material used for the machining of Ni-base super-
alloys include good wear resistance, high strength and toughness, good thermal shock
properties, high hardness, and adequate chemical stability at elevated temperatures [76].

5.2.2
Mounting
The principal purpose of mounting the metallographic samples was to conveniently handle
specimens of complex shapes or sizes during the subsequent steps of metallographic
preparation and examination. In addition, they help to protect and preserve extreme edges
or surface defects [78].
Cold mounting
Cold mounting, also known as mouldable mounting, was carried out for this particular
case. The samples were first cleaned with ethanol and independently introduced into sil-
icone rubber molds, then a liquid mounting medium was used to enclose the samples,
i.e., epoxy resin. After enclosing the samples, a catalyst was used to cure and harden the
material. Subsequently, they were introduced into a vacuum chamber at 0,15bar. The
curing process was slow and lasted eight hours.
It should be noted that mold material selection was essential to avoid shrinkage effects
during curing.

Coating and surface analysis

6.1
Coating analysis
The Ni-base superalloy under study exhibited the typical features of a diffusion coating
based on a mixture of pure silicon and aluminum powders enriched with platinum incor-
porated onto the substrate surface. This combined mixture, by means of a heat treatment
process in an inert gas atmosphere, gave rise to a diffusion coating of silicon-platinum
modified aluminide coating [12, 80, 81]. Given the lack of information provided by exter-
nal sources to the current project, the available data is not sufficient to determine the pro-
cess used to incorporate this diffusion protection coating on the substrate, among others,
according to literature and the observations in Figure 6.1 and Table 6.1, both discussed
below. It is estimated that the coating could have been added by slurry silico-aluminizing
of different thicknesses of platinum-electroplated MAR-M247 [80, 82] complemented by a
final aluminized coating Al2O3 incorporated by thermal spray [81, 83]. FORCE Technology
already corroborated the presence of this latter aluminized coating.
6.1.1
Coating cross section characteristics
In order to determine the characteristics of the coating and its influence on the superalloy,
an area in the trailing edge (TE) close to the suction side (SS) of Sample 1 was subjected to
SEM and EDS examination. This area was chosen among others as it is less susceptible
to high temperatures due to the thermo-mechanical nature of the GT blades and would
present a comparatively less degraded coating than, for example, the leading edge, which
is exposed to higher temperatures in service, as explained in chapter 1.

Coating evolution and lifetime
As indicated in Figure 6.1 with the interdiffusion zone, due to concentration gradients at
high temperatures, the elements diffuse inwards into the substrate from the coating, which
currently acts as a reservoir. Conversely, the alloying elements, such as Cr, Ni, and Al,
from the substrate tend to diffuse outwards [82]. These diffusion processes change the
composition near the surface by enrichment with elements from the diffusion reservoir.
Depending on which process is faster, the inward or outward diffusion process is more
pronounced, as was observed in different locations of Sample 1. Layer degradation is di-
rectly connected with the diffusion result [32]. As other studies on GT blades demonstrate
[72], the width of this interdiffusion layer increases with temperature. It is a critical param-
eter for the blade lifetime. SEM and LOM details of the coating in the different Sample 1
locations are given in Appendix B, where among other things, it can be noticed that the
level of degradation of the coating varies depending on the area of the blade, being in the
leading edge (LE) where the coating is most affected, as it appears practically consumed,
showing cracks that progress toward the inner material, Figure B.1f.
In addition to what has been previously mentioned, H.M. Tawancy, N.M. Abbas and T.N.
Rhys-Jones report in their investigation that Pt prevents the diffusion of refractory ele-
ments toward the outer zone of the coating [86]. For this case, as the results demonstrate,

EDS elemental mapping would be necessary to support it. Long-term service or heat
treatment may cause the decomposition of the MC carbides toward the grain boundaries
into more stable, lower-carbon M23C6 and M6C [90], according to the reactions in equa-
tions 2.3 and 2.4 described previously in chapter 2. In contrast, the borides predicted by
Thermocalc were not observable, probably due to their small quantity and size.
On the other hand, the dendritic structure is well defined by the formation of secondary
arms, Figure 7.3b. The appreciable secondary dendrites, which solidify after the primary
ones, result from capillary instabilities at the dendrite’s tip. Such disturbances are minor
at first and then grow at the expense of the smaller arms through Ostwald ripening. The
driving force for this coarsening is the resulting interfacial energy at the tips of the smaller
arms due to the greater curvature that exists there [91]. Measurements were made of the
arm spacing between primary dendrites. Although the SEM images were not accurate
enough for this, the distance between two parallel lines crossing two dendrites at their
midpoint was averaged between various primary dendrites in the same region. Values
of arm spacing close to 80µm were measured. Moreover, in the interdendritic spaces,
the γ´ precipitates are characterized by much greater variability in their distribution and
morphology, Figure 7.3e, and are more susceptible to TCP phase proliferation.
In addition to all this, it is remarkable in all the images the presence and heterogeneous
distribution of bright particles, ”whiter tones,” that can be associated with etching effects.

Heat Treatment
The elimination of micro-segregation by homogenization after casting plays a key role in
the thermomechanical behavior of Ni-base superalloys under high-temperature and high-
load applications.
For this particular case, the information on the homogenization heat treatment after the
blade manufacturing process is proprietary to Siemens. In order to evaluate if a homog-
enization heat treatment (HT) was performed on the blade, the SEM images obtained in
the root, Sample 3, were studied since it is the region that has been less influenced during
service and would have the most similar characteristics to those of the material manufac-
tured and heat treated before service. In Figure 8.1, some microstructural heterogeneity
can be observed. This gives indications that if a homogenization heat treatment was
performed, it had certain discontinuities along the blade.

The typical rejuvenation heat treatment of Ni-base superalloys consists of solution treat-
ment followed by aging. The objectives of this heat treatment are the complete dissolution
of γ′ phase in the γ matrix and minimizing the segregation from non-equilibrium solidifi-
cation in preparation for the aging heat treatment where the γ′ is precipitated [92].
Well aware of the typical rejuvenation treatments mentioned above, to be more precise in
the previous hypothesis, if the image at 8.000x magnification set is analyzed, it reveals a
series of dark contrasted γ′ phase particles. This γ′ phase is not uniformly precipitated in
the core because the turbine blade was probably cast, and no subsequent processes of
solution and aging heat treatments were applied, or these treatments failed during their
application. The correct application of these treatments usually results in a microstructure
composed of uniformly precipitated γ′ and γ-fine channels. It should be emphasized that
similar micrographs were obtained throughout the core region, which means that the γ′
phase in the core did not develop well during casting [93].

8.1
Heat treatment determination
Two studies were carried out to determine the most suitable heat treatment for the MAR-
M247 superalloy under study. The first was a thermodynamic study based on equilibrium,
Scheil-Gulliver solidification, and volume fraction calculations using Thermocalc (dataset:
TCNI5: Ni-Alloys v5.1), followed by a second study using Python in which the equivalent
γ/γ′ volume fraction is computed on Sample 3 to simulate conditions prior to service.
8.1.1
Thermodynamic modelling
Given the complexity of Ni-base superalloys containing a large number of alloying ele-
ments, a Ni-Al phase diagram was modeled with Thermocalc by taking the Al content of
MAR-M247, resulting in a binary alloy that will be used as a reference alloy system. The
Al-Ni phase diagram is shown in Figure 8.2 and will be used to consider the heat treatment
of the superalloy.
As reported in other studies, Juraj Belan [32], in the present project, the Ni-rich region
of the binary Al-Ni alloy system will be used as the reference alloy system.
Figure 8.2: Al-Ni Phase diagram, Thermocalc
Theoretically, as described by Semiatin´s analysis [94], solution heat treatment of Ni-
based superalloys should take place between the γ′ solvus and solidus temperatures.
In Figure 8.2, the solvent γ′ separates the regions (γ + γ′) and γ′. In practice, segregation
caused by non-equilibrium solidification limits the actual range of treatment due to incipi-
ent melting below the solidus temperature of the alloy. This range is usually between 100
and 200◦C [95]. Then, by thermal aging treatment, it is possible to achieve a controlled
re-precipitation of the γ′ phase, resulting in a uniform and isotropic material. This aging

occurs at a temperature lower than the solvus and allows homogeneous nucleation, coars-
ening, and growth of γ′. The temperature and duration of the aging treatment are selected
to optimize the alignment, morphology, and size distribution of this γ′ precipitates [95, 32].
The resulting microstructure, besides depending on the heat treatment parameters, also
relies on the physical properties of the alloy, such as lattice mismatch, the volume frac-
tion of γ′, coherent energy of the γ′ interface, and the elastic properties of the matrix and
precipitate [32]. Although the nucleation is mainly homogeneous, heterogeneous nucle-
ation at grain boundaries and dislocations is expected to be observed in the subsequent
metallographic analysis.
Scheil-Gulliver and equilibrium solidification simulation
To comprehend the equilibrium and non-equilibrium nature of solidification of the fusion
zone for the MAR-M247 superalloy after solidification and before any subsequent heat
treatment, thermodynamic simulations were carried out in Thermocalc software. To esti-
mate the solidification range of MAR-M247 superalloy after solidification and before any
subsequent heat treatment, a Scheil-Gulliver and equilibrium solidification simulation was
performed, where the mole fraction with respect to temperature for both graphs is plotted
on the same chart, Figure 8.3.
Thermocalc calculations concerning equilibrium assume complete diffusion in the liquid
and solid states. However, this approximation is only partially accurate. In practice, solid-
ification often occurs under non-equilibrium conditions, i.e., the cooling rate is too high to
allow time for a complete redistribution of the alloying elements according to equilibrium.
This means that equilibrium calculations do not always provide a reliable representation
of the material state after solidification. In contrast, Scheil solidification simulations offer
a better alternative for the investigation by assuming an infinitely fast diffusion in the liquid
phase and null or limited diffusion in the solid phases [96]. Additionally, it also assumes
little dendritic tip undercooling and maintenance of equilibrium at the solid-liquid interface
[97]. This, therefore, provides a better estimate of the actual state of the material following
solidification.

the Scheil-Gulliver equation 8.2 and its derivatives.

9.1
Pre-heat treatment microstructure considerations
The resulting microstructure of Sample 1 is characterized by thicker and spherical γ′ pre-
cipitates than those of Sample 3. And, in addition to this, a lower volume fraction of γ′ in
γ′ matrix.This increased coarsening was gradually developed during service.
According to the literature, while a finely dispersed homogeneous distribution of γ′ precip-
itates improves high-temperature strength and creep behavior by delaying crack growth,
relatively large γ′ precipitates negatively influence the ultimate high-temperature tensile
strength [42]. Therefore, solution treatment is considered necessary to improve the me-
chanical properties and thus rejuvenate the microstructure.
9.2
Heat treatment effects on the microstructure
Material background together with Thermocalc heat treatment analysis
Suppose the results of the SEM analysis on Samples 1 and 3 are put into the theoretical
context of the heat treatment background studied in previous chapters. In that case, an
estimate can be made of the results to be expected once the heat treatment has been
carried out.
In general terms, heat treatment mitigates elemental segregation, refines the dispersion
of γ′ and carbides in the γ matrix, decreases the amount of γ/γ′ eutectic, and optimizes
the morphology and distribution of carbides at the grain boundary [99].
The eutectic γ/γ′ islands observed are an unwanted component in superalloys, but their
complete dissolution in the matrix during solution treatment is not possible. However, the
high solution temperature may change the thermodynamic conditions and lead to partial
dissolution, which consequently influences the differentiation of the sizes of the γ′ precip-
itates around the γ/γ′ eutectics. The grain boundary pinning by the presence of carbides,
seen in Samples 1 and 3, is expected to prevent grain growth during the heat treatment.

Conclusion
A detailed study of the characteristics determining the service life of gas turbine blades
and a detailed study of the microstructural changes associated with a rejuvenation treat-
ment (dissolution and aging) of the polycrystalline Ni-base superalloy MAR-M247 are pre-
sented.
Although the least damaged blade was selected, the surface characteristics exhibited
degradation of the protective diffusion layer and surface cracks in the root zone. The
degradation of this layer was directly related to the result of elemental diffusion between
the substrate and the coating, where the Si and Pt elements of the coating, and the Cr
and Al elements of the substrate, play a crucial role. The most deteriorated geometrical
areas are those most exposed to temperature (LE and TE) and pressure (root). In addi-
tion, uncoated regions, such as the cooling system, revealed significant oxidation.
After LOM and EDS analysis, microstructural features in areas far from the outer and
inner surface of the non-heat-treated samples reveal a microstructure characterized by
the heterogeneous presence of MC carbides, γ /γ′ eutectic, TCP phases, and porosities,
precipitated mainly in grain boundaries and interdendritic regions. While the airfoil shows
a heterogeneous distribution of globular geometry and coalesced γ′ phase in the γ ma-
trix, the root shows homogeneous distributions with smaller γ′ precipitates and cuboidal
geometry comprising a volume fraction of 57%, very close to the ideal value reported by
literature for this superalloy.
Regarding the heat treatment, the LOM and SEM analyses demonstrated good agree-
ment with the Thermocalc simulations. The precise effect of small temperature changes
on dissolution kinetics was discussed. Thermodynamic calculations indicated that the
minimum dissolution temperature was about 1.224◦C and the maximum 1.272◦C, oc-
curring in the three-phase field γ + MC + MB2. These calculations made it possible to
determine an optimal condition for the solution treatment at 1.240◦C for 4 hours and, sub-
sequently, by means of image analysis based on calculations of γ/γ′ volume fractions in
SEM images on the sample with the most similar microstructure to the noble one, the
aging treatment was determined at 870◦C for 20 hours. The results of the microstructural
analysis show a rejuvenated microstructure with fine carbides at the grain boundaries and
a regular γ′ geometry with homogeneous distributions reaching volume fractions of 53%
in the γ matrix. The uniform distribution characteristics of the strengthening phase show
similarity to that obtained in the root. However, the residual segregation after the heat
treatment did not disappear after the thermal aging treatment, retaining large γ′ particles
in some regions of the material.

butions influence on the material’s mechanical properties, e.g., by subjecting the samples
to tensile and creep tests and comparing the results with those that the material might
perform under novel conditions.
Further studies can be developed to improve the evaluated aging heat treatment. Its per-
formance did not significantly influence the previous microstructure after the solution treat-
ment, leading to remaining segregation. It would be meaningful to carry out treatments at
temperatures slightly higher than 870◦C. At the same time, it would be worth considering
the possibility of taking the study further by incorporating a hot isostatic pressing (HIP)
process to eliminate defects such as porosities arising after casting.

D
Carbides Morphology
This Appendix schematically identifies the morphological evolution of the carbides in MAR-
M247 at different growth rates.
At slow growth rates, carbide growing in layers maintains the octahedron shape at equilib-
rium Figure D.1(A). As the growth rate increases, carbide mounds grow on the octahedron
surface Figure D.1(B). If the growth rate continues to increase, some matrices γ are incor-
porated into the carbide, and the carbide mounds overlap with the master carbide Figures
D.1(C),(D). If the growth rate rises again, carbide arms start to develop from the apex of
the octahedron Figure D.1(E). At a further increase in growth rate, the carbide arms grow
upwards and downwards to form the carbide containing holes Figures D.1(F),(G). With
keeping increasing growth rate, the thin, long carbide arms extend rapidly from the octa-
hedrons pinnacle, and the carbide takes on an irregular dendrite shape Figures D.1(H),(I).
At extremely fast growth rates, as in the cooling process, thin carbides are formed in the
form of needles, flakes, or dendrites Figure D.1(J) [49, 48] .

The carbide growth rate gradually increases from A to J. Figure from [49]
