Abstract
Radars cannot be placed at will, as the RF domain is a highly sought commodity and emis-
sions may be restricted for certain applications. Passive radar can circumvent restrictions
by utilising illuminators of opportunity to record signals reflected by targets of interest.
This study aims to build and test a passive bistatic radar with two Software Defined Radio
(SDR) as the IF to digital link.
Terrestrial Digital Video Broadcasting (DVB-T) is chosen as the reference signal, and
hardware is built specifically for the isolation of a single DVB-T channel for passive radar
application. Two for this purpose specialised Radio Frequency (RF) receive channels
are designed and fabricated with as low phase differences and frequency response dif-
ferences as possible. A primary challenge in double-channel signal reception is the syn-
chronisation of the channels, which is tackled here by coupling a common reference signal
slightly out of band from the DVB-T channel into the receiver and using it so align the re-
ceived spectra. MATLAB scripts are written for the time-synchronous reception of the two
data-channels and post-processing reference-signal alignment and Range-Doppler pro-
cessing of received data. The build radar is tested in two field-tests on driving cars on an
interchange in Gladsaxe, Denmark.
The two designed receiver channels are measured to have as low a phase difference
as 7.3◦, which is accounted for in post-processing, and S21 difference of less than 1dB
at the centre frequency. Time synchronisation of at least 1ms is achieved through well-
timed reception calls and synchronisation to the reference signal. Finally, signatures on
a range-Doppler map from received data are correlated with a car moving in a reference
video.
Problem Deﬁnition
To localise a target, passive radars utilise two separate time-synchronised receiver chan-
nels. One for measuring the reference signal from the emitter of opportunity, and one to
receive the scattered reflection from a target. In this project, these two receiving channels
are going to be mimicked by two ADALM Pluto Software-Defined Radios (SDR) boards.
The first step is to investigate to which degree two SDR can perform this task by them-
selves. Then analogue electronics are going to be designed to enhance the operational
quality of the radar. The necessary microwave-electronics (Filter, LNA, coupler) are going
to be defined, designed, built, and tested to make the passive radar work.
Signal processing on received data is going to allow for the detection of a target finally.
This includes techniques such as matched filtering and reference-signal envelop detec-
tion, it requires insight in ambiguity diagrams and it takes advantage of parallel processing
for synchronous channel-operation. Such techniques would also be implemented, along
with the conduct of field tests. The Gladsaxe DVB-T transmitter is used as a reference
transmitter during this project to try to identify a target in a field test. The primary goal is
to investigate the capabilities of passive radar technology for Terma A/S and DTU Space
to gain insight into the field and to determine the difficulty associated with Passive Radars
and deployment thereof.

CHAPTER1
Introduction
Radars can not be placed at will, as the RF domain is a highly sought commodity and emis-
sions may be restricted for certain applications. With more and more frequency bands
being overtaken by the mobile communications sector, this problem will only increase.
Passive radar can circumvent these restrictions by utilising illuminators of opportunity to
record signals reflected by targets of interest, instead of transmitting signals themselves.
This also makes it impossible to detect passive radar, which obviously can be of tactical
significance.
What is Passive Radar
The term ’Passive Radar’ can sound counterintuitive as traditionally the passive version of
radar is a radiometer. Passive radars, however, have little to do with traditional radiome-
ters, as the measured quantity is an entirely different one. To locate a target, passive
radars utilise two separate time-synchronised receiver channels - one for measuring a
reference signal from a chosen Emitter Of Opportunity (EOO) such as a terrestrial TV sig-
nal transmitter, and one for receiving scattered reflections of said signal. The Reference
(ref.) channels output can then be used just like the transmitted signal in a traditional
radar. Correlating it with the received signal from the Measurement (Surveillance) (mes.)
channel through matched filtering then reveals range contours corresponding to distances
to targets, and applying a frequency compression even allows for Doppler-shift determi-
nation.
The proposed radar system is a combination of the well-known bistatic radar and the prac-
tical advantages the radio-silent passive radar utilised together to form an undetectable
remote sensing system. Both passive and bistatic radar have been proposed as alterna-
tives to the traditional monostatic radar since WW II, where German engineers built the
first passive radar utilising British radars as illuminators which were thereby able to detect
British bombers as they flew on their missions ([1], p.9). The requirement to process two
radar channels simultaneously alongside the requirements on digital signal processing
set by passive radars, however, had them shelved again until very recently. With the in-
creased processing efficiency of modern computers and the ever-increasing demand for
more bandwidth for data transmission, the subject has gotten drive again. Constant high
power broadband illuminators bring opportunities for extended integration time on high
bandwidth signals, and thus high resolution, without the power-requirements and legal
boundaries of transmitting this kind of signal.
Traditional monostatic radars have their transmitter and receiver colocated. This can also
be achieved for passive radars when setting up the receiver very close to an EOO, which
limits deployment and influence regions severely. In most cases, transmitter and receiver
are not colocated for a passive radar, which means the geometry of bistatic radars applies.
This geometry, alongside the requirement of absolute time-synchronicity of the receive
and measurement channels, are the expected challenges for the implementation of such
a radar. One of the main goals for the project is thus to identify if these can be overcome
through hardware implementation on pre-build receivers such as SDR, and to identify the
challenges associated with building and deploying a passive radar.

Why PASSIVE radar?
Apart from the immediate gain in stealth and deployment-ease and the accompanying
tactical advantages, the underlying idea of using signals otherwise restricted for radar ap-
plication can have a high impact on the probability of detection. Stealth aeroplanes are
never totally invisible over the entire electromagnetic spectrum. Instead, they are shaped
and coated strategically to target specific frequencies at which the aircraft will prevent di-
rect backscattering of hitting signals. The advantage here is that new parts of the spectrum
now can be accessed and used for tracking which might have been chosen as unlikely
surveillance bands by the producer of the aircraft, which means the target is not stealth
in these bands, as they not typically are used for detection. Another advantage is that
due to the cheap deployment of Passive radar; multiple units at different frequencies can
be deployed simultaneously. This way, one can use the large range of FM radio as an
early warning system and then switch to other, more broadband, signals like DVB-T for
close-range detection with better range and Doppler resolution [2]. Passive radar should
not be seen as a replacement for active radar. The inherent advantage of an active radar
of being able to choose a transmitted signal with the exact Doppler and range character-
istics for a given target to be detected can not be matched by passive radar. However,
the stealthy passive radars can be used for rough, first estimates that supplement active
radars to gain strategical advantages by keeping the active radar silent until a potential
target is in range. During times of active war, the reliance on civilian infrastructure is,

Scope of the Project
In this project, the hardware for two receiving channels for such a passive radar is devel-
oped such that they are compatible with the Analog Devices ADALM-PLUTO Software
Defined Radio (SDR) boards. The boards, which from now on are referred to as Pluto
boards, have a dynamic frequency range from 70MHz to 3.6GHz. This range means that
most of the relevant frequency spectrum is covered, and EOO can be chosen freely. Two
of these boards are used for the IQ-demodulation and analogue-to-digital interface of the
radar.
The first step in the process of building this passive radar is to determine the require-
ments for any receiver hardware that is to be designed. This is done by going through
the theory of bistatic passive radars and the practical limitations of the Pluto boards. Af-
terwards, the circuits for all of the needed components are designed and fabricated, and
their performance is characterised. This step is followed by designing interfacing soft-
ware in MATLAB for the operation of the Pluto boards to act like two time-synchronous
IQ-demodulating receivers. Lastly, the entire system is tested in practical field tests and
resulting range-Doppler maps are evaluated. For proof of concept, the goal is to detect a
driving car in the vicinity of the radar on a range-Doppler map.

2.2
Bistatic Passive Radar
The difference between a traditional monostatic radar and a bistatic passive radar is that
passive radar no not transmit any signals. Instead, the passive radar relies on other
transmitters in the area, Emitters of Opportunity (EOO), to illuminate the targets. The
echos of objects in the area are then received by a set of receiver antennas which make
out the passive radar. The challenge is, in order to be able to make out the distance to
a target, the transmitted signal must be known such that it becomes possible to tell how
far the waves have travelled. A passive radar does this by having two separate receive
channels where one is pointed directly at the EOO and thereby measures the transmitted
signal, called the reference (ref), and the other channel is pointed towards areas of interest
in order to receive echos from targets, called the measurement channel (mes). Such a
setup that utilises a transmitter in one location and receivers in another location is called
a Bistatic Radar.
In the following, the geometrical differences are described, and their consequences ex-
plained. The monostatic theory from the previous chapter is applied to the new scenario,
and the governing equations of a bistatic radar are shown.

2.3
Emitters of Opportunity
Since the reference signal is not self-generated and transmitted, an overview of different
kinds of available on-air signals is necessary.
2.3.1
Types of on-air signals
From a few kHz up to multiple GHz of the electromagnetic spectrum varying amounts of
the spectrum are used for communication purposes, and it must be determined which
one is most suitable as the reference signal for a passive radar. RF waves are used for
GPS from satellites; long FM waves are used for FM radio, TV signals are broadcast over
terrestrial and satellite-borne channels and not least mobile internet through GSM or other
modulations are examples for possible candidates.
FM Radio
In Denmark, FM radio is limited to the band 87.5 MHz to 108 MHz where each
channel has an allocated bandwidth in the tens of kHz region. As the signal is built upon a
single carrier frequency modulated with the sound information, the bandwidth of FM radio
can vary widely depending on content. By looking at different types of channels, it has
been found that speech radio has an effective bandwidth around 9kHz, rock radio around
26kHz and reggae radio close to 80kHz [7]. Referring these values to the range resolution
equation for a radar (2.2), it is clear that the reggae radio with a range resolution of approx.
1.8km is the best of the three due to having the widest bandwidth. A considerable problem
with radio is that the bandwidth is not consistent as it is content dependant. At times radio
stations will transmit music with high bandwidth, and at times there will be speech with
poor range resolution. In Denmark, some of the highest power transmitters are used for
FM radio [8] with up to 100kW, which for the radar equation means good maximal range
for constant min. SNR, but the poor range resolution means short-range target tracking
is impossible. With these characteristics, FM radio would be an excellent choice for early
warning systems, but not for close-range target detection.
GPS
According to the standard for Global Positioning System (GPS) [9], all satellites
transmit at 1575.42 MHz, and the bandwidth is relatively constant for GPS due to no
significant changes in contained information. The signals are ”transmitted with enough
power to ensure the minimum received signal power level of -158.5 dBW” [9], which means
the carrier of the GPS signals are transmitted such that at least -158.5dBW are available
for receiver antennas to pick up. Using such low transmitter power in the bistatic radar
equation would require close to unity minimum SNR for adequate maximal ranges. That
being said, the predictivity of GPS signals makes them a worthwhile consideration, and it
has even already been shown that using it as the reference is possible [10].
UMTS
- Universal Mobile Telecommunications System is the overarching name for high-
frequency mobile communication standards including 3G and 4G. For 3G, frequencies
above 2.1GHz with bandwidth around 5MHz [11] with good correlations characteristics
make the signal theoretically an excellent choice for passive radar. In practice, however,
UMTS signals are not reliable for surveillance since they are only driven on full bandwidth
while connected to a client. When no client is connected, the transmitter defaults to pulsed
transmission to save power, which is useless for the desired long integration times. It has
even been shown to be possible, when transmitting a UMTS signal for consistency, to use
the signal for Passive Radar application [12].

The modulation used for DVB-T signals is Orthogonal Frequency-Division Multiplexing

This structure means the 8MHz wide channels are made up of multiple sub-carriers with

The more straightforward approach to frequency multiplexing is using FDM (Frequency
division multiplexing), where too, multiple sub-carriers encoded with information are trans-
mitted at slightly different frequencies inside of a channel. With FDM however, small fre-
quency spacing between the sub-carriers is necessary (called guard bands) such that they
do not mix. With Orthogonal Frequency division multiplexing, however, the sub-carriers
are intentionally modulated in frequency such that the sidelobes of the sub-carriers (which
are sinc-functions in the frequency spectrum) cancel each other. This modulation allows
for much better use of the frequency band but requires Guard Interval (GI) for synchroni-
sation in time to prevent multi-path propagation from interfering with reception. Figure 2.6
is an illustration of an OFDM signal transversing in frequency and time. Every sub-carrier
holds a part of the total transmitted information in the form of symbols, which change with
time as the TV image changes. The GI shown are not typically placed after each sym-
bol, but rather in intervals 1/4, 1/8, 1/16, or 1/32, depending on the specific protocol that
is used. More GI mean higher stability against multipathing, but it also takes from the
transmission efficiency.

modulation, and its sensitivity to frequency shifts due to the close sub-carrier spacing [17].

Software-Deﬁned Radio
Nailing down the definition of what a software-defined radio (SDR) is, can be tough. In
most general terms it is described as a device that to some degree utilises software for a
part of the operation as a radio. This definition does not pin down exactly which devices
are SDR and which are not, as it allows the reader to interpret it such that any digital
radio is an SDR, which most certainly is not the case. The term ”software-defined” spec-
ifies that the operation of the radio changes depending on software commands. Thus,
an SDR is a device that is implemented with a part that can change through the use of
the software. An SDR typically has an RF front end that is hard-implemented, followed by
some Analog to Digital Converter (ADC) or Digital to Analog Converter (DAC) which leads
into an Field Programmable Gate Array (FPGA) or microprocessor and sent to be inter-
preted by another device. This FPGA or microprocessor is then the part that can change
through software, interfacing the transceiver hardware, where it can influence parame-
ters like gain, Local Oscillator (LO) frequencies, and sometimes which set of transceiver
components is used. Not all SDR will, therefore, be able to do signal demodulation by
themselves but would rely on an external processor to fulfil this task.

2.5.4
Band-pass ﬁlters using passive components
The four primary forms of filters are the low-, high- and band-pass, and band-stop filters.
To design a band-stop or band-pass filter two or more low-pass and or high-pass filters
are combined into one circuit such that their behaviour complements each other. Here
the approach of first designing a low-pass filter and then converting it to a band-pass filter
is described. The simplest kind of low-pass filter is simply an inductor in series. As its
impedance increases in accordance to Z = jωL, where L is its inductance in Henries, the
amount of current passing through the component is limited as the frequency increases.
A single inductor is always going to have the lowest impedance at DC, while the goal of
low-pass filters is to be able to let specific frequencies pass, and not attenuate them all.
So by adding a capacitor in shunt, whose impedance decreases with higher frequencies,
Z = 1/jωC, the two components can complement each other, and an equation for the
cut-off frequency can be determined. Continuously adding more components in this way
increases what is known as the order of the filter, which generally increases the roll-off
rate. Appendix C gives an introduction to constant k-type filter design.
After designing a low-pass filter with the desired characteristics, it can be converted into a
pass-band or stop-band filter. In Figure 2.14, transformations between normalised low- or
high-pass filters to band-pass or -stop filters are shown. Normalised means the compo-
nent values are independent of the characteristic impedance, so they are simply divided
by it.

2 and the Elliptic filters. They can all be realised using lumped components in the manner

An RF coupler is a device which splits its input signal into a number of output channels.
If this split is 50/50, i.e. half the power goes to either output, the component is called an
equal splitter. Combiners are the opposite of RF couplers and splitters in that they take
two inputs and add them together into one output. Splitters and combiners are the same
circuits, they are just connected in opposite ways.
Generally the idea of a splitter or a coupler is to have a transmission line terminate into
two (or more) lines which have impedance such that the input power of one line is smaller
than or equal the output the other line(s). A simple three-way equal split can be achieved
by arranging resistances such that the impedance of all ports is equal. Such a splitter is

3.1
Design Considerations
First, the most general top-level considerations are made in the form of a link budget

3.1.1
Frequency of operation
The first decision that has to be made is the choice of signal type. Using FM radio is due
to is poor range resolution not chosen, and UMTS (or any other mobile communication
signal-type) is not chosen due to their unreliable performance. Both GPS and DVB-T
are possible reference signals [4][10]. However, the low signal power of GPS signals
is seen as an unnecessary constraint to put on the project. Due to these reasons and
the advantages stated in section 2.3.2, terrestrial television signals are chosen as the
reference signal.
Last year, the Danish government decided to go over to the new DVB-T2 standard, which
moves the channels down in frequency and changes the modulation considerably. As this
change is set to be rolled out during the project timeline, it is decided to have the design
be modular, so a channel filter can be changed at a later time if necessary. Fortunately,
not all frequencies are set to be changed - according to [19] are channels 31 and 23
continually operated from the Gladsaxe transmitter throughout the entire process and will
also remain active after DVB-T2 is established. For that reason, these two are chosen
as possible centre frequencies for the passive radar. Later on, due to the Corona crisis,
the standard change is delayed to after the project deadline by the ministry of culture
[20]. Chronologically this is made public after some of the hardware already had been
fabricated, so the above decisions could not be changed.
As stated in section 2.3.2, a DVB-T transmitter is located in Gladsaxe, which is chosen
to be the reference for this project. It transmits at 10kW from a horizontally polarised
antenna at 210m height [33].

3.2
RF Hardware Design
The basic versions of the circuit used in the following have been discussed in the Theory
chapter. They are now modified to fit the systems needs and evaluated through simula-
tion in Keysight Advanced Design System (EM simulation software) (ADS). The process
of these simulations is the following: First, the components are added to a new schematic
with ideal components to verify the theory. Then the components are swapped for those
standard values available in the lab, such that the results show if the small changes in
values have a significant influence. S-Parameters are simulated and thus used for deter-
mining characteristics of the circuits. After the circuits are verified through circuit simula-
tions, PCB are laid out, and Electromagnetic (EM) co-simulations are conducted with the
layouts. These give insight into how much the metal around the components influences
the behaviour of the circuits. In ADS it is possible to create an EM model of a print board
layout such that it can be imported into a schematic and used as a component. When the
desired layout is either created in or imported into the program, connection pads can be
marks with pins and an EM simulation creates S-Parameters between each pin. When
a component then is created, it can now be set to mimic the characteristics of the print
inside a schematic simulation. This process is called co-simulation in ADS and is useful
for determining the cross-talk between different parts of the board and thus the boards
influence on the circuit.

3.2.3
Band-Pass Filter Design
A band-pass filter with high roll-off and relatively small bandwidth must be designed, as
the requirements for the application are too specific to find this component on the market.
8MHz bandwidth at 554MHz corresponds to a Fractional Bandwidth FBW = 8/5.44 =
1.44%, making it an ultra-low bandwidth filter, which is not easy to fabricate. Therefore
some compromises are made along the way. Both channels 31 and 23 are valid candi-
dates to use as reference channels. However, the slightly higher frequency of channel
31 means it lies more comfortably in the operating region of the ADL5523 LNA, which
is defined for the above 400MHz application. Therefore the filter for the receiver-chains
is designed with centre frequency 554MHz. The adjacent channels transmitted from the
Gladsaxe transmitter, channel 33 and 23 at centre frequencies 570MHz and 490MHz re-
spectively, are thus the main targets that need to be suppressed. With a gap of only 8MHz
between channel 33 and 31, one of the compromises will be that channel 33 is not as at-
tenuated as the other channels. The next higher frequency channel 40 at 622-630MHz
can be suppressed reliably. A constraint to keep in mind is that slightly outside the chan-
nel a reference-chirp will be introduced into the spectrum. Ideally, the filter attenuates any
signals coming at that frequency significantly beforehand.
The first iteration of filter that is designed is a Chebyshev type 2 filter. It is chosen due to its
desirable flat pass-band characteristics, high roll-off rate and because it allows the use of
higher value components than most other types of filters at such a small bandwidth. The
inverse filter is chosen since ripple in the stop-band does not affect the desired channel,
where if the standard Chebyshev filter were chosen, a ripple in the pass-band would be
inconvenient during post-processing.

CHAPTER5
Software Implementation
With the hardware for the passive radars receives chains in place, the Pluto boards must
be enabled actually to capture the radar data. Synchronous data capture and the subse-
quent data processing is thus tackled in this chapter. The MATLAB scripts produced for
these purpose of reading RX data synchronously, and performing post-processing on out-
put data are explained, and stage-wise output is shown. All described code is enclosed
as an appendix .zip file and will be described abstractly here.
The difference between the two needed scripts is that one has to operate in real-time and
capture data, while the processing script can be deployed offline. The requirements for
the data capture script, referred to from now as Start radar script, which can be seen in
the flowchart in Figure 5.1, are:
• Control two ADALM PLUTO boards time-synchronously.
• Transmit a chirp from one of the boards for reference.
• Define reception parameters including centre frequency, bandwidth and integration
time.
• Read IQ data from the two ADALM PLUTO boards at the same time.
• Save received data alongside time references in external files for later processing.
The Post-processing script, for which the flowchart is shown in Figure 5.2 then has to:
• Load data
• Re-form the frequency spectrum with reasonable resolutions
• Synchronise the two data packages using out-of-band chirp, then remove the phase-
shift introduced by the hardware.
• Correlate reference and measurement signal using Matched filtering
• Create range-Doppler map of receiver data and corresponding ambiguity diagrams.
Each of these tasks is described in the following.

the two channels, a waveform with distinct reference points is needed. For this purpose,

Starting Parallel Pool and initialising
The first thing the Start radar script does is start two parallel processes using MATLAB
Parallel Processing Toolbox and reading the chirp-file into their workspaces. A practical
limitation of the parallel workers is that their workspaces cannot be accessed thought
MATLABs IDE, making debugging them individually a challenge.
The two boards must start capturing data at the same time, so theoretically, if both parallel
workers start initialising the boards at the same time, they should capture synchronously
too. However this is not the case as differences in USB communication introduce accu-
mulative errors for each initialisation command sent to the boards, plus one board must
actually perform more than the other, as it must generate the reference signal. So to
ensure the boards take about the same time to get ready before the reception, they are
both initialised for both transmission and reception, where one worker sets its TX cen-
tre frequency to 3GHz and the other sets it to be 4.5MHz above the centre frequency
of the receiver channels. This way the first boards’ transmission won’t interfere with the
performance of the radar, while making sure the power available for the receiver chan-
nels is equal as both need to use the same amount on transmission, thereby evening the
playing field. During initialisation sample rate, frame size, gain and centre frequency for
the receivers and transmitters are defined. The hard-implemented IF filter in the AD3963
transceivers do not have a perfect passband, which becomes particularly pronounced
when he reception bandwidth (same rate) is set low. Therefore, instead of only sampling
at 8MHz + the 1MHz where the chirp is positioned, a band of 12.5MHz is captured as it
is the outer 1/6 of either side of the band that are attenuated by the IF filter. The centre
frequency is set to that of the designed receive chain, i.e. either 554MHz or 490MHz.
Gain and sample-size are determined by a given test-setup.
Referring to the flowchart in Figure 5.1, the above is displayed as the yellow boxes. From
this point on the reference-chirp is being generated continuously.
Synchronous RX reading
Absolute synchronisation between the boards would be easy if the parallel workers would
allow interference with their workspaces. For example, both channels could enter a wait-
ing loop when they are done initialising the boards, ending this loop synchronously when
the user presses a button. This button press, however, would only be registered by one
worker at a time, which again would introduce a tiny offset between two presses. Global
variables are not passed into the parallel workers, so setting a flag there is not possible
either. One reliable way to set this flag, however, is by setting a timer for it. The way this
is implemented, shown in the blue boxes of Figure 5.1, is that the current time is captured
before entering the parallel processing, and 12 seconds are added, and this value is saved
as a stat-time variable. It takes the boards between 7-10 seconds to initialise, so giving
them 12 leaves a bit of head-space. When they are initialised, they enter said pause-loop
for a time equal to the difference between the current time and the set start-time.
Looping reception for the number of iterations
When the current time for both workers is the same, they call the ”read from the Pluto
boards” command in parallel. In practice this means the boards start capturing data with
the set sample rate until the given frame size is filled up, this takes, at 12.5MSpS and the
max. frame size of 224, around 1.34 seconds, and then this data is transferred to the com-
puter. 1.67MS · 4bytes/S · IQ = 134MB is the amount of data generated by both boards,
so it also takes some time to transfer this data from the boards to the PC. However, the
exact file size is highly dependant on the signal content, which means the measurement

its file before the reference channel. This means synchronisation between every iteration

5.2
Post Processing Script
This script is build up around generating accurate range-Doppler maps for all iterations of
a given measurement setup. The first thing this requires is loading the received Reference
(Ref) and Measurement (Mes) IQ data files stored by the Start radar script. Values such
as frame size and sample rate for the captured data are included in the saved files and
extracted at the beginning. Further, since the script allows for plotting the current state of
the data at multiple stages thought the processing, it is defined here at which points these
are to be plotted. A flowchart of the full script is shown in Figure 5.2, and the above part
is shown in the blue boxes up top.
Spectrum recreation
The output data from the Pluto boards is a complex vector, including in-phase (I) and
quadrature (Q) information for each sample that originate from the IQ demodulation at the
transceiver. This means if the boards’ frame size is set to 224, the vector has a length of
1.67million points. With the sample rate set to 12.5MSpS, it is known that the received
data points correspond to a spectrum of 12.5MHz bandwidth around the chosen centre
frequency. Thus if the received data vector is reshaped to a matrix of size ncarrier times
nsymbol, where their product equals the frame size, the Fourier transform of the data can
be used to reproduce the spectrum with time resolution corresponding to 1/nsymbol and
frequency resolution 1/ncarrier. Doing a fftshift operation is now necessary to fold the
spectrum into a recognisable form with axis corresponding to slow-time samples and fre-
quency samples centred around the carrier frequency. The dark blue ’Reshape’ box in
the flowchart Figure 5.2 shows these steps. An example of the spectrum at this point of
the processing is displayed in Figure 5.3. nsymbol is 2048 and ncarrier is thus 8192. The

Reference Envelop Detection
Envelope detection is a standard tool in image analysis, which means converting the
spectrum matrix into an image allows for uncomplicated recognition of the chirp. The
matrix is rescaled to a grayscale image where each pixel has a value between 0 and 1,
where 0 is black, and 1 is white. Following this, the image is cut to only include the out-
of-band chirp, such that a threshold detection can make use of the relatively high signal
level of the chirp versus noise around it. The image is converted to a binary image where
only the values above 0.8 are set to white and the others to black. Next the MATLAB
bwboundaries() function is utilized. It takes a binary image as input and outputs vectors
that are the outside boundary of any shape in the image. This is used as the envelope
detector of the chirp. From the boundary-vectors, the positions of the peaks of the chirps in
the reference and measurement channels are determined and aligned. In the flowchart of

CHAPTER7
Results and Discussion
The overlying goal for the project has been to identify challenges and capabilities of
passive radar with simple implementations like the ADALM PLUTO SDR learning mod-
ule. Insight into these subjects has been gained through system-design, quantification of
board capabilities and implementation of needed improvements in the form of extra hard-
ware. The build microwave receiver hardware, with a combined 32MHz 3dB-bandwidth
of 32MHz and measured Noise Figure of LNA below 1.8dB, is theoretically able to bring
the SNR of car targets at 300m distance to +17.7dB, while introduced phase-differences
between the reference and measurement channels are limited to 3.4◦, which can be ac-
counted for in post-processing. A MATLAB script has been written to make two software-
defined radios synchronously sample received signals from the two channels of the radar
while having one board generate a reference signal for synchronisation. The built radar
has been taken on two separate field-tests where it has been set to capture the direct
signal from the chosen EOO and reflections of the same signal from driving cars. Signal
post-processing has been applied to the received data, and appropriate Range-Doppler
maps with correspond ambiguity diagrams have been generated. The goal of this chapter
is to elaborate on some of the less prominent choices made during the project and to give
a discussion of the generated Range-Doppler maps from the field test.
7.1
Field-Test Result Discussion
Simply looking at the range-Doppler maps displayed in the previous chapter is not enough
to determine whether targets can be discerned and if not, why that might be. Therefore
these results are discussed here.
7.1.1
Range-Doppler map post-processing
All the shown Range-Doppler maps have been altered slightly to make reading them more
apparent. The first challenge met is that the highest correlation point on the range-Doppler
maps is not positioned at 0 range and 0 Doppler. The shown maps in the previous chapter
have been aligned to those coordinates, but the on-aligned maps are shown in appendix
D. The spectra used to generate range-Doppler maps have been created with resolutions
corresponding to 1kHz (n_carrier = 12500) and as close to 1ms as possible (n_symbol =
⌊SampleRate/12500⌋). The range-Doppler maps pixel resolution is c/12.5MHz = 27m
times IntegrationTime/(n_symbol −n_alignmentpixels) ≈1Hz1. With the targets moving
maximally 60km/h in the test-scenes, they advance up to 22.3m while integrating, which
means they can take up 2 pixels in the final map.
Objects right beside the radar should be present at 0 bistatic range. An offset from 0 range
means there is a time delay between the reference and measurement channel, which is
the case with many of the test-runs as the best alignment accuracy is one pixel through
the chirp alignment. A different possibility to align the reference with the measurement
channel is first to compute the Doppler-cut ambiguity function of their correlation, which
shows at which time delay they correlate.

This is most definitely the case, as has also been tested. The LO of the two Pluto boards
are not synchronous, and the shown spectra in Figure 6.16 suggest that they aren’t even
set to the same frequencies despite having the same input parameters. If they were, a
clear line should be visible at the two transmitting frequencies, which there is not. The
test shows however, that the drift is constant over a given integration run, and can thus
not explain the drift seen in the range-compressed data seen in 5.5.
Lastly, thresholding on the noise floor at -45dB is performed such that the background
of the figures no longer appears full of clutter and the main-responses are more clearly
visible. The thresholding value is chosen to be 5dB lower than the average noise in the
maps. No targets should be erased n this way as they are expected to lie 17.7dB above
the noise, this is only removing clutter.

7.2
General Discussion
The design of the microwave hardware is generally considered a success. The designed
LNA and coupler we fabricated without problems, and their measured characteristics fol-
lowed those set in design relatively closely. The designed filters characteristics are not
quite achieved, but none the less have working filters been fabricated through tuning after
assembly. During the design of the filters, more attention could have been given to their
phases. Having them align almost perfectly is a consequence of them being made up of
the same components. Looking at the phase-response shown in Figure 4.8 however, it is
seen that even the smallest frequency deviations have an impact on the phase and thus,
the phase difference. Minor flaws can , as shown, be corrected in post-processing, but
not having to do it would have been even better.
Development of Passive Radar
93
==PAGE:108==
Some time could have been saved at the beginning of the project if it was not assumed
that the electrical length of the component is so small that impedance matching would
be irrelevant. A more proven approach to solving the problem would have been to use
a different dielectric in the PCB from the beginning and do the impedance matching cor-
rectly. It is possible that this could improve the losses through the filters, but the achieved
characteristics were deemed sufficient for the purposes.
Using three capacitors in series is not a reliable way of achieving 0.3pF capacitance.
This is common knowledge since the parasitic capacitance in solder paste is known to
influence design. Going through multiple iterations should, however, be seen as a good
thing as it has given insight into the fabrication limitations for these kinds of RF filters.
Buying an LNA instead of designing one from scratch turned out to be sound because the
extra time it would have taken, might have meant that the corona-virus related lock-down
could have influenced the hardware fabrication. The fabrication of the second iteration
filter ”Rev2” was finished on the day before lock-down.
The fact that not all components ended up being equal was mitigated by aligning them
optimally in the receive chains. The resulting gain differences had of no apparent sig-
nificance for range-Doppler map quality, and the phase-difference could be removed in
post-processing.
According to the datasheet of the used LNA chip ADL5523, it should have a noise figure
of 0.8dB while operated at the here present biasing and frequency. Only measuring an
NF of above 1.3dB for both LNA can have two reasons. The first being that some slight
miss-matching is occurring, but this is deemed unlikely at the input and output reflection
coefficients are close to the stated values of the datasheet. The other reason for the
difference could be that the noise floor during measurement has been too high, and a
noise calibration of the network analyser would be needed as described in [26]. This
would most likely only yields a minor difference, as a noise figure of 0.8dB is, even with
better noise-floor levels, hard to determine accurately.
In hindsight, the Pluto boards oscillators should have probably been aligned in some way.
This was a known issue from the beginning, but the choice was made not to damage the
Pluto boards. Having their sampling aligned could possibly have removed an error source
form the results. A simple approach stated in [40] would be to remove a specific capacitor
and the oscillator from one board, and simply connect the other boards’ oscillators to
correct pins on the first board. This could potentially minimise frequency drift and thereby
align sampling for better focusing. It might be the case that this alignment could reduce the
LO drift between boards and mark stationary targets near 0-Doppler on the range-Doppler
map.
It has been tried to analyse the LO drift by setting the boards to transmit a single tone signal
and receiving it on both simultaneously. The results from this test are indecisive at best,
as they were conducted in sub-optimal conditions (especially regarding multipathing), but
it was consistently seen that the tone was received on different frequencies over several
tries. It is, therefore, still deemed probable that the offset from 0-Doppler on the Range-
Doppler maps is due to LO differences on the two Pluto boards.
It is found that it is indeed essential to align the readout of the Pluto boards performed by
the computer, as drift from the USB interfacing between every iteration otherwise accu-
mulates. This has been determined by the results from the first field tests where almost
no iterations of a given run would correlate due to time offsets. The approach of setting
a timer for the parallel workers has made alignment of results, from the second field test,
possible on all iterations. A minor practical limitation with the implementation of this timer

is that whenever a full minute passes, both workers enter a full minutes break. They are
still be aligned, but there is simply a one-minute time-gap to the previous iteration. This
can be solved with an error handler, but the problem was only detected during the last
field test and could thus not be implemented. It has no influence on the accuracy of the
produced range-Doppler maps.
There is no reason to reduce the time between measurements by sacrificing integration
time, no benefits during the first proof of concept surveillance is found. It has also been
determined that for continuous surveillance, a different approach is needed than using
the ADALM PLUTO boards, due to their limited integration times.
No improvements are seen between the results with and without the used primitive clutter-
shielding behind the mes. antenna during field test 2. It was used to reduce both clutter
and the influence of the direct signal on the mes. antenna but none of two could be shown

where the mes. antenna is isolated more from the reference antenna should be used.
Without bandpass filtering, echoes and even ambiguities, vanish in noise. This has been
shown by doing test-runs without the hardware connected to the receiver (the antenna
was connected directly to the combiner stage) as shown in Figure 6.10. A second trial
without filer and LNA should, however, be made with the longer integration times of the
second test-runs. It is however unlikely from the seen outcome of the other trials that
this would improve the result to such a degree that the microwave electronics would be
deemed useless.
The ambiguity calculations are computationally heavy for the data sets of 16.7M samples
while aligning to the chirp is can be done in no time. Calling the ’chirp align’ function
consistently takes 1.8 seconds while the ambiguity calculation typically takes more than 5
minutes on the same machine. Cross-correlating every single sample with all others takes
orders of magnitude more time to compute than to find the position of the binary chirp-
pixels with only tiny improvements to the position on the range axis, which are shown to
also be achievable by manually aligning the axis.
Using channel 31 was in hindsight maybe not the best choice, 490 has fewer adjacent
channels that can drive up the noise, and the attenuation outside the main band of the
receiver sets 3 and 4 are significantly higher than the used ones. As already stated in
the results section of the relevant experiment, the shown results with anomalies in the
channel 23 band could not be reproduced, but the decision had already been made to
go with channel 31. A new filed-test using the 490MHz filter should be undertaken to
determine if it improves the radar. This could, at best, improve the signal to noise ratio
lightly.
If the exact structure of the DVB-T signals transmitted from Gladsaxe was known, it would
have been possible to remove the ambiguities arising from the modulation of OFDM in-
stead of having to compare every range-Doppler map with a corresponding ambiguity
diagram. Which sub-category is used is, however, not public knowledge.

CHAPTER8
Conclusion
The goal of the project has been to build a passive radar using Software-Defined radios
and to determine its capabilities. To improve the signal to noise ratio of the ADALM-
PLUTO SDR boards used as back end analogue-to-digital interface of the passive radar,
two identical receiver channels have been built. They are compromised of a band-bass
filter with 32MHz 3dB-bandwidth around 554MHz, which corresponds to the DVB-T chan-
nel 31 of the Gladsaxe DVB-T transmitter, an LNA with Noise figure measured below 2dB
and gain above 20dB and a splitter/combiner stage for time alignment between the chan-
nels to a given reference signal. By aligning the components most optimally, two receive
channels have been produced with a combined phase difference of 3.4◦after alignment
to the reference chirp, and a loss-difference of 1dB between the two is achieved.
The built radar has been taken on two field tests where two MATLAB scripts written to
make the ADALM PLUTO boards work as passive radar receivers were used. The first
script utilising the MATLAB parallel processing toolbox for maximal synchronisation be-
tween read-out calls on the Pluto boards has been used to capture data. The second
script intended for post-processing and time alignment achieves time synchronisation by
referencing the envelope of the reference chirp with at least 1ms accuracy and is capa-
ble of producing the range-Doppler maps and corresponding ambiguity diagrams of the
captured data. Both scripts have no dependency on the internal structures of the DVB-T
modulation and neither does the microwave-hardware, so the radar will be operational
even after the transition to DVB-T2 for Danish TV broadcasting. LO drift between the
used ADALM-PLUTO boards has been detected which makes them non-ideal for the use
as receiver modules while no modifications are made to their oscillator hardware.
Range-Doppler maps resulting from the field-tests have been analysed and related to
video footage of the test-runs. No clear, unambiguous targets could be determined in the
measurement channel, but a car passing behind the radar has been captured. Comparing
the results to other studies reveals they achieve similar results from erroneous test-setups
which suggests a better set up field-test would improve target detection, suggesting that
passive-radar deployment is not as versatile as expected. It was possible to show that
the range-Doppler map quality is improved when using the built RF receiver hardware.

8.1
Future Work
As the era of passive radar is only just about to begin, not all possibilities of passive radar
could be investigated in this study. Work on the project should continue, and the following
are suggestions for areas to investigate next.
• New test runs with improved antenna positioning such as proposed at the end of
the discussion chapter should be conducted. Placing the measurement antenna
such that it is shadowed from direct the EOO illumination, effectively reducing the
direct signal in the receive channel, could make echoes more visible. Placing the
reference antennas such that no passing cars are in its direct path would yield much
better ambiguity responses.
• Modifications to the ADALM-PLUTO boards, including oscillator alignment by fabri-
cating a quartz-based reference-oscillator and using it as the clock for both boards
could be a possible approach for solving the LO drift problem.
• New trials using the channel 23 receiver hardware should be conducted to determine
if they have better characteristics than the used 554 MHZ filters. This could improve
SNR slightly.
• Guard-Interval blanking and direct signal mitigation on the received data can im-
prove ambiguities in the range-Doppler maps. This requires first the determination
of the Danish DVB-T structure. Since the DVB-T format is set to change to DVB-T2,
the investigation should be based on that standard. A possible way of determining
said structure is to investigate ambiguity diagrams from clean reference measure-
ments. The repeating ambiguities in range and Doppler give indications of cyclically
repeating characteristics of the signal.

Radars cannot be placed at will, as the RF domain is a highly sought commodity and emissions
may be restricted for certain applications. Passive radar can circumvent restrictions by utilising
illuminators of opportunity to record signals reflected by targets of interest. This study aims to build
and test a passive bistatic radar with two Software Defined Radio (SDR) as the IF to digital link.
Terrestrial Digital Video Broadcasting (DVB-T) is chosen as the reference signal, and hardware is
built specifically for the isolation of a single DVB-T channel for passive radar application. Two for this
purpose specialised Radio Frequency (RF) receive channels are designed and fabricated with as low
phase differences and frequency response differences as possible. A primary challenge in double-
channel signal reception is the synchronisation of the channels, which is tackled here by coupling a
common reference signal slightly out of band from the DVB-T channel into the receiver and using it
so align the received spectra. MATLAB scripts are written for the time-synchronous reception of the
two data-channels and post-processing reference-signal alignment and Range-Doppler processing
of received data. The build radar is tested in two field-tests on driving cars on an interchange in
Gladsaxe, Denmark.
The two designed receiver channels are measured to have as low a phase difference as 7.3◦, which is
accounted for in post-processing, and S21 difference of less than 1dB at the centre frequency. Time
synchronisation of at least 1ms is achieved through well-timed reception calls and synchronisation to
the reference signal. Finally, signatures on a range-Doppler map from received data are correlated
with a car moving in a reference video.
