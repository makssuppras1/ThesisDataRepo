Abstract 
 
In 2016, the United Nations introduced the 17 Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs), which 
emphasize the interconnectedness of environmental, social, and economic aspects of 
sustainable development. By 2050, the global population is expected to reach nearly 10 billion, 
posing a signiﬁcant challenge in ensuring the production and distribution of sufficient healthy 
food for everyone. A predominantly plant-based diet rich in vegetables, fruits, berries, pulses, 
potatoes, and whole grains is essential, with protein intake primarily derived from plant sources. 
The Nordic Nutrition Recommendations advocate for the consumption of pulses and legumes 
due to their high protein content. However, potential hazards associated with consuming large 
amounts of high-protein plants must be considered. Trace metals, such as cadmium, present a 
possible risk, but the impact of cadmium is not well understood. To accurately assess the risk of 
cadmium exposure from black-eyed beans and sweet potatoes, more knowledge about its 
bioavailability through preparation and digestion is needed. This study aimed to introduce the in 
vitro model INFOGEST 2.0, which simulates the gastrointestinal tract, to our lab by testing the 
bioavailability of cadmium in black-eyed beans and sweet potatoes. Cadmium levels were 
quantiﬁed using ICP-MS/MS, and a risk assessment was conducted.

Introduction 
In 2016, the United Nations (UN) introduced 17 Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs). 
These goals came into effect, with the aim of setting a direction towards a more 
sustainable development for both people and the earth. All the UN's 193 member states 
have committed to work with SDGs, which includes a goal to sustain responsible 
consumption and production (SDG 12) and take action to combat climate change (SDG 
13, ﬁg.1), reduce the global waste at retail and consumer level per population by year 
2030, as well as reducing food loss in production and supply chains (FN, 2023). To 
achieve the goal, it is necessary to decrease meat production to bring down the level of 
carbon dioxide.  
 
Fig. 1. UN Sustainable Development Goals, SDG icons for the SDG and for the two action goals, 12 and 13 respectively. 
 
Knowledge about nutrition and dietary habits is important to promote new habits and at 
the same time prevent diet-related diseases. This includes risk beneﬁt assessments that 
assess the balance between the positive and negative effects of a food in order to 
determine whether it has an overall health beneﬁt or whether it poses a risk. Risk-beneﬁt 
assessments can also be used to assess the impact of fortiﬁcation, new production 
forms, dietary recommendations and changes in diet. 
The last decade there has been an increasing understanding of the complex composition 
of the human gut and the importance of the health of the microbiota. The status of gut 
microbiota has an effect on the risk of developing diseases such as type 2 diabetes, 
cardiovascular diseases and allergies (Licht and Bahl, 2019). Besides the direct effect of 
foodborne pollutants on the composition of the gut microbiota, we now know that gut 
microbiota has an effect on the conversion of some of the chemical components from 
food and the absorption of chemicals in the body. 
With changing the nutritional recommendation – to eat more plant-based food – higher 
intake of Potentially Toxic Elements (PTEs), as arsenic and cadmium (Cd) are at risk. So, 
==PAGE:5==
Page 3 of 43 
 
consuming a plant-based diet includes eating a larger amount of rice, pulses and 
legumes and we need to consider the possible hazards in the consumption in larger 
amounts. We know trace metals are a possible hazard in these kinds of foods, and we 
understand the issues of arsenic, but the knowledge about the impact of cadmium is not 
understood very well. To make an accurate risk assessment of the cadmium exposure 
from the Nordic Nutritional Recommendations (Blomhoff et al., 2023), we need more 
knowledge about the bioavailability of cadmium through preparation and digestion to 
draw a line of the health impact of cadmium exposure.  
Aim and scope 
The aim of the thesis is to give an insight into the amount of the potential toxic element 
cadmium and its bioavailability in different food matrices and to indicate to which extent it is 
absorbed in the gastrointestinal tract (GI). 
We will investigate the bioavailability of cadmium by exploring two different food matrices, 
black-eyed beans and sweet potato, by implementing the in vitro method INFOGEST to simulate 
the digestion of the upper intestinal tract and test the release of Cd into the gut microbiome by 
simulation of the lower intestinal tract by in vitro fermentation. We will evaluate the laboratory 
results to look into the effect of risk assessment on pulses and legumes. The analysis will be 
performed by using ICP-MS/MS. 
Cadmium 
Cadmium belongs to the group of transition metals and was ﬁrst identiﬁed in 1817 by Frederic 
Stromeyer a German Chemist. 
It is a soft blue shining toxic metal and is naturally found in the environment. The normal 
oxidation number is +2. Naturally occurring zinc minerals always contain Cd, as Cd is 
geologically closely linked to zinc and is therefore extracted by reﬁning zinc ores (MST.dk 27/2-
25; Nordberg et al., 2009; Montes-Bayon et al., 2018). Cd is commercially used in Cd-Ni 
batteries, solar cells and accumulators, for surfacing for special resilience of metal surfaces 
such as traffic signs. It is used as pigments in paint and glace, as stabilizers in plastic, as alloys 
etc. It also comes as a byproduct in coal, oil products, cement and tobacco and as emissions 
by air (Rouyu Wang et al., 2022). Cd is found in soil and water and is absorbed by crops and 
aquatic systems. This affects the Cd concentration of crops, and the concentration of Cd may 
increase with acidiﬁcation of Cd- containing soils (WHO, 2019; Claus et al., 2016; Hale et al., 
2001; Hamon et al., 1998). Cd is a non-essential mineral for lifeforms and therefore appears as 
a toxic metal and has severe adverse effects on plants and human health.

Pathway 
The natural occurrence of Cd in the environment is very low. In non-contaminated soil, it varies 
from 0.01 to 5 mg/kg Cd that primarily enters the environment through mining and 
contamination of water and soil, however inappropriate disposal of Cd containing waste has 
increased the emission in populated aeras. The metal has a propensity to accumulate within 
various organs by displacing the essential divalent cation Ca2+, which plays a crucial role in the 
physiology of lifeforms (Peralta-Videa et al., 2009). 
Phytochelatins 
In plants, Cd is taken up by the electrochemical potential gradient of the plasma membrane in 
the roots. The absorbed Cd is stored in root cells by phytochelatins, vacuoles in the cytoplasm 
or in the plasma membrane, in the cell walls or transported to the shoots and grains of plants. 
Phytochelatins (PCs) are small, cysteine-rich peptides that play a crucial role in the 
detoxiﬁcation of heavy metals in plants. PCs have a high affinity for binding Cd and other heavy 
metals and consist of three amino acids: glutamine (Glu), cysteine (Cys), and glycine (Gly). The 
low affinity cation transporter (LCT1) pathway that is responsible for the calcium transport 
seems to also be a route for the Cd to be transported within plants. Five groups of PCs have 
been classiﬁed - PC2 – 6 - and they have been identiﬁed in a wide variety of plant species and in 
some microorganisms (Peralta-Videa et al., 2009; Lui, 2013; Nordberg et al., 2009). 
Metallothionein 
Cd is also found in metallothionein’s (MTs). MTs are found in various mammalian and plant 
tissues, in the cytoplasm of cells. MTs are heat-stable, low molecular (6-7 kDa), single strain 
polypeptides, containing 20 cysteines and no aromatic amino acid and histidine residues. MT 
has been shown to be an effective scavenger of oxygen radicals after radiation-induced 
oxidative stress (Prozialeck et al., 2012; Sabolic et al., 2010; Wang et al.,2023) by binding metal 
ions through their thiolate clusters and this enables them to play the important role in 
detoxiﬁcation, see ﬁg 2.

The speciﬁc sequences are structurally arranged in two unique metal domains, α and β 
clusters, each having different metal binding capacity. Because of the numerous cysteines with 
thiol (SH) groups, MTs shows high-affinity/high-capacity binding properties for various reactive 
metal ions, so that one molecule of MT can bind 7–9 Cd ions.  The reactive thiol groups promote 
spontaneous oligomerization of the thionine molecules, and an aggregate can bind more than 7 
g atom Cd/mol MT (Sabolić et al., 2010).   
Human physiology 
Plants absorb Cd and distribute it in different parts, after which Cd is entering the food chain 
with a risk to human health.  When humans ingest Cd-contaminated foodstuff or drink water of 
high Cd-concentrations, vomiting occurs immediately after the ingestion. The interval between 
ingestion and the onset of symptoms is short and there is a possibility of Cd poisoning. A 
deﬁnitive diagnosis is made when a high concentration of Cd is detected in the food or water. 
Treatment is symptomatic, with recovery usually rapid with no apparent complications. When 
plants are exposed to high Cd concentrations, it results in stunted growth and low yields. 
Prevention is based on avoiding the use of high Cd-contaminated areas for agriculture, 
collection of contaminated drinking water or materials containing Cd (Groten and Bladeren, 
1994; Soni et al., 2024).  
Long-term high exposure of Cd from food, due to environmental pollution or atypic dietary 
habits, leads to renal tubular dysfunction, disturbance of calcium metabolism, osteoporosis 
and osteomalacia. Cd is classiﬁed as a human carcinogen (Group 1) by the International 
Agency for Research on Cancer (Nordberg, Chapter 32, 2009). 
The critical renal Cd concentration is the amount of free Cd that, after long-term renal Cd 
accumulation, (that is not bound to metallothionein) is high enough to cause cytotoxicity 
(Groten and Bladeren, 1994). 
When digesting food, a large proportion of ingested Cd is unabsorbed (Nordberg, Chapter 32, 
2009). Animal studies have demonstrated that the true excretion of Cd in feces is dose 
dependent. True fecal excretion is partly proportional to the body burden, particularly at low 
doses. Fecal excretion mainly arises from the intestinal mucosa and only a smaller portion 
originates from the bile and pancreatic ﬂuid (Nordberg et al., 1985). 
Immediately after Cd uptake from the gastrointestinal tract, the Cd is bound to albumin and 
other larger proteins e.g. glutathione GSH, in blood plasma. Cd bound to albumin is taken up by 
the liver. Cd induces the synthesis of MT in the liver cells and gradually an increasing proportion 
of liver Cd is bound to MT. In the early phase after administration of Cd, plasma Cd is mainly 
bound to albumin, and Cd uptake by the kidneys is limited (Nordberg, Chapter 32, 2009).

Any differences in Cd availability from different foodstuffs will have a great impact on the risk 
evaluation of dietary Cd and on the estimation of tolerable Cd levels in different types of food 
products. 
The dietary uptake of Cd can either be uptake from the intestinal lumen into the mucosa or 
passage across the basolateral membrane into the serosal ﬂuid and blood stream. The rate of 
passage through the basolateral membrane is probably much lower than the rate of uptake from 
==PAGE:9==
Page 7 of 43 
 
the intestinal lumen, and that the uptake from the intestinal lumen may have a regulatory role in 
the absorption of Cd. The difference between the two pathways will result in an accumulation of 
Cd in the intestinal tissue. Cd accumulation in the intestine is, however, low due to the 
continuous renewal of the intestinal epithelium and migration of the old mucosa (Groten and 
Bladeren, 1994). This makes it interesting to look into the bioavailability of Cd in foodstuffs. 
Risk assessment 
Metal-induced carcinogenesis is becoming increasingly important, as modern biomarkers are 
capable of detecting early cellular effects of low exposures to toxic metals. The cellular 
exposure of toxic metals produces early effects, such as the formation of reactive oxygen 
species (ROS), modiﬁcation of apoptosis, and methylation of DNA. For a limited number of 
metals there is adequate information to make reliable dose-response assessments for humans. 
Cd exposure through food usually constitutes the major exposure route for the general 
population, though smoking is an additional important source, giving rise to considerable 
increases in blood Cd and may act as a confounding factor in the assessment from food intake, 
especially when food consumption is low. Cd exposure can be estimated by determining the 
consumption and metal concentration in foods with the amount of metal in feces. The 
measurement of Cd in feces provides a reasonably good estimate of the daily intake, because 
90-95% of ingested Cd usually remains unabsorbed. 
In European and North American countries, the average amount of Cd ingested is 10-20 μg/day 
and the corresponding average urinary excretion is 0.5-1.0 μg/day. Human studies, with 
volunteers, have shown a Cd uptake of 4.7-7% with a higher uptake in women than in men 
(Nordberg, Chapter 32, 2009).  
The EFSA (2009) recommends a tolerable weekly intake (TWI) of 2.5 μg/kg bw (corresponding to 
0.36 μg/kg bw per day). The mean dietary exposures in European countries are close to or 
slightly exceed the TWI (2.04-3.66 µg/kg bw per week). Subgroups such as vegetarians, children, 
smokers, and people living in contaminated areas may exceed the TWI by about twofold, so 
exposure to Cd at the population level should be reduced (EFSA, 2009). 
The Nordic Nutrition Recommendations recommend a protein intake of 0.66g/kg/day for both 
male and female (Blomhoff et al., 2023). EFSA has calculated that 26g of 100g meat is protein 
and 21g of 100 g pulses is protein. For a 70 kg vegetarian person to get the recommended 
amount of daily protein from our Black-eyed beans, they would have to eat 220 g/day. This 
would give a cadmium intake of 22.4 mg/day and with an estimation of 90% passing through the 
intestines the daily intake of Cd would be 2240 µg/day which corresponds to 32 µg/kg bw pr day.

INFOGEST 2.0 
Numerous intervention trials have tried to correlate human diet-related diseases with 
nutrition/foods, trying to understand the physiological response to speciﬁc foods in detail. 
The New Nordic diet Recommendations (NNR) recommend substituting meat proteins with 
plant proteins (Blomhoff et al., 2023), and in order to acquire deeper understanding of the 
bioavailability of Cd in high protein foods, we need to investigate the different compartments of 
the GI tract. 
To do so, it becomes necessary to follow the digestive processes within the different 
compartments of the gastrointestinal tract (GI) in larger detail. These are very invasive methods 
and not simple procedures to perform on humans. Animal models are widely used, but animal 
physiology and gene regulation are not necessarily comparable with human and usually 
involves the death of the animals in question. Many types of in vitro digestion methods have 
been developed, though many are very expensive to set up and maintain and are often not 
suitable for food digestion. Therefore, a network of experts, the COST INFOGEST network, were 
selected to develop a procedure for in vitro simulation of upper gastrointestinal tract food 
digestion. The COST INFOGEST network published a harmonized method in 2014 and in 2019 
they published the 2.0 version (Brodkorb et al., 2019; Mimekus et al., 2014). 
To study the bioavailability of Cd in food throughout the entire GI tract, we choose to examine a 
low and a high protein containing food, with the INFOGEST 2.0 protocol for simulating the upper 
GI tract, and perform a bacterial fermentation, to simulate the large intestines. 
The procedure for the investigation of bioavailability of Cd in the experiment was followed to 
examine the bioavailability of Cd in low and high protein containing foods, in the upper GI tract. 
For a summary of the protocol, see ﬁg 4.  
The procedure can be divided into three phases. Preparation, digestion and sample treatment. 
Preparation is the part where determination of enzyme activities and bile concentration are 
done. These tests were done by using the recommended assays for amylase, pepsin (both RGE- 
and porcine pepsin), lipase (gastric and pancreatic) and trypsin from the INFOGEST 2.0 
protocol. Data can be found in Appendix A. Sweet potato and black-eyed beans are high in 
carbohydrates (Cardini et al., in print), and amylases are needed for breakdown, therefore we 
added salivary amylase in the SSF for all samples. We chose to do the experiment with Rabbit 
Gastric Extract (RGE) as recommended by the INFOGEST team, because of the close 
resemblance to human gastric juices. The second part is the digestion phase. Here the food is

exposed to three successive digestive phases; oral, gastric and Intestinal. Explained in detail for 
this investigation below. 
The last step of the digestion procedure involves sampling, sample treatment, storage and

Methods preparations 
Prior to running the experiment, all enzymes were tested for their activity according to the 
recommendations from the INFOGEST article (Brodkorb et al., 2019). Salivary amylase, for 
digesting the starch of the matrix, was used in the oral phase. RGE and porcine pepsin were 
used for the lipid and protein digestion of the gastric phase and pancreatin was used for the 
ﬁnal digestion in the intestinal compartment. Data and results can be found in Appendix A. 
Oral phase 
5 g of the freeze-dried material of sweet potato and black-eyed beans was weighed into 50 ml 
sample tubes and dissolved in 15 ml and 4 ml of prewarmed, 37°C, 1.25X SSF electrolyte stock 
solution respectively, to imitate a swallowable bolus. The SSF electrolyte stock solution 
consisted of 15.0 mM KCl (Merck, Supelco, Bellefonte, Pennsylvania, United States), 3.74 mM 
KH2PO4 (Merck, Darmstadt, Germany), 13.63 mM NaHCO3 (Merck, Supelco, Bellefonte, 
Pennsylvania, United States), 0.15mM MgCl2(H2O)6 (Merck, Darmstadt, Germany), 0.06 mM 
(NH4)2CO3 (Merck, Sigma-Aldrich, St. Louis, Missouri, Unites States), 1.1 mM HCl (Merck, 
Supelco, Bellefonte, Pennsylvania, United States) adjusted to pH 7 (SevenCompactS220 Mettler 
Toledo, Greifensee, Switzerland). 93.8 µl and 5 µl of 0.3M CaCl2 (Merck, Sigma-Aldrich, St. 
Louis, Missouri, Unites States) were added to the solutions to reach a ﬁnal concentration of 1.5 
mM, respectively.  2.0 M NaOH (Merck, Darmstadt, Germany) were added to reach the pH 7 
value for the oral phase (100 µl and 200 µl respectively). 475 µl and 593.8 µl MilliQ® (Merck 
Millipore, Darmstadt, Germany) were added to equilibrate the volume of the samples. Human 
salivary amylase (Merck, Sigma-Aldrich, St. Louis, Missouri, Unites States) was then added (15 
ml and 4 ml respectively) to a ﬁnal concentration in the samples of 75U/ml. To the enzyme blank 
samples, no salivary amylase was added, but 15 ml and 4 ml prewarmed 1.25x SSF electrolyte 
stock solution and 980 µl and 593.8 µl MilliQ water were added, respectively, to reach equal 
volumes to the enzyme containing samples. Reaction negatives were 5 ml water samples, with 
no food, but salivary amylase (500 µl) added. To both food negative samples, 4 ml prewarmed 
1.25X SSF electrolyte stock solution was added. One negative contained only MilliQ water, and 
one negative contained MilliQ water and Cd standard (SCP Science, PlasmaCAL, Baie D’Urfé, 
Quebec, Canada) added to a concentration of 250 ppb. All samples were incubated for 2 min 
with agitation (K532, Heidolph Instruments GmbH, Schwabach, Germany), at 37°C (Dryline UV-
1800 Shimadzu, Kyoto, Japan).

Gastric phase 
After incubation, the oral solutions were taken out of the incubator and all samples were added 
to 20 ml prewarmed 1.25x SGF electrolyte stock solution. The 1.25 x SGF solution contained 6.9 
mM KCl, 0.9 mM KH2PO4, 25 mM NaHCO3, 47.2 mM NaCl (Merck, Supelco, Bellefonte, 
Pennsylvania, United States), 0.12 mM MgCl2(H2O)6, 0.5 mM (NH4)2CO3, 15.3 mM HCl adjusted 
to pH 3.  
12.5 µl 0.3M CaCl2 was added to the sweet potato and 5 µl to the black-eyed bean solutions, 
for a ﬁnal concentration of 0.15 mM. Water was added to the sweet potato (1.75 ml) and to the 
black-eyed beans (1.0 ml) solutions to reach the intended volume. pH was adjusted to pH 3 
with 1 M HCl (3.6 ml and 2.35 ml, respectively) to mimic the gastric phase of pH 7. Pepsin was 
then added to a ﬁnal concentration of 20 U/ml by adding a combination of Rabbit Gastric 
Extract (RGE), RGE20-100mg (Lipolytech, Marseille, France) and porcine pepsin (Merck, Sigma-
Aldrich, St. Louis, Missouri, Unites States -Aldrich). For description on enzyme activity, see 
appendix A. For the black-eyes beans, 500 µl of each solution was added and for the sweet 
potato, 500 µl RGE and 1250 µl porcine pepsin solution were added. In the enzyme negative 
samples, MilliQ water was added to reach the ﬁnal reaction volume, instead of enzyme. To the 
food negatives, 5 µl 0.3M CaCl2, 0.3 µl 1M HCl, 500 µl RGE and 500 µl porcine pepsin (ﬁnal 
concentration of 20 U/ml) was added. The samples were then incubated in a shaking incubator 
at 37°C for 2 hours.

Fermentation 
The digesta pellets were freeze-dried (LyoQuest 55PLUS Telstar, Barcelona, Spain) for 24 hours 
to remove the remaining water. After freeze-drying the pellets where grinded for 5 seconds in a 
KN 295 Knifetec grinder (FOSS analytical A/S Hillerød Denmark) to equalize the particle size. 0.5 
g of the freeze-dried and grinded digesta pellet were mixed with 1.2 ml of 10% fecal slurry 
(anonymous person delivery) and 10.8 ml of minimal media in a Whitley A95, (Don Whitley 
Scientiﬁc Limited, Bingley West Yorkshire UK), anaerobic bench, Appendix B. All samples were 
incubated for 24 hours, continuously agitation (Tube Revolver 88881002 Thermo Scientiﬁc 
Waltham, Massachusetts United States) under anaerobic conditions. After 24 hours, 2 ml 
samples were collected, and all samples were centrifugated at 4000 rpm for 5 min. (5424R 
Eppendorf Enﬁeld Massachusetts United States). Supernatant and pellets were separated. A 
summary of experiment data can be found in Appendix B. 
Preparation for analysis 
The pellets were freeze-dried (Sentry VIRTIS Company INC., Gardiner, New York, United States) 
for 24 hours, to remove the reminding water and all samples, supernatants and freeze-dried 
pellets, and were stored at -20°C until further testing. All samples were then processed using 
ICP-MS and QTOF-MS analysis to measure the total amount of Cd in the fragments. The method 
used is a newly developed method by Julian Cardini from the Research Group for Analytical 
Food Chemistry Analyze at DTU Food, A Novel Approach for the Identiﬁcation of Cadmium-
Chelating Compounds in Plant-Based Foods Using SEC-ICP-MS/MS and SEC-QTOF-MS (Cardini 
et al., in print).  
Acid digestion and total Cd measurement with ICP-MS/MS 
In brief, 0.3 g of each sample was weighed into 18 ml quartz tubes. Following this, 1 ml of 
ultrapure water and 4 ml of concentrated HNO3 (67-69 %) were added to the tubes. The samples 
were then subjected to a digestion program of a 10-minute ramp to 250˚C, holding this 
temperature for 20 minutes before a 30-minute cool down period, using a Multiwave 7000 
==PAGE:15==

microwave oven (Anton Paar GmbH, Graz, Austria). After digestion, the digested samples were 
transferred into 50 ml disposable polypropylene tubes. Ultrapure water was added to bring the 
total volume up to 25 ml. The samples were then diluted 10 times in 2 % HNO3 in preparation for 
analysis. Measurements were performed on the Agilent 8900 ICP-MS/MS instrument using an 
SPS 4 autosampler in single quadrupole mode measuring 114Cd using either no cell gas or cell 
gas. All data is collected in appendix B and C. 
Results 
After the INFOGEST procedure, simulating stomach and small intestine digestion and a 
fermentation step on feces, to simulate the large intestines, we measured the amount of 
bioavailable cadmium released from the chosen food matrix. This was done by acid 
digestion and identiﬁcation of Cd chelating compounds on an ICP–MS/MS. 
The raw data is available in Appendix C. 
Water samples 
Data was processed with Excel (Microsoft® Excel® for Microsoft 365 MSO (Version 2502 
Build 16.0.18526.20144) 64-bit) and a plot, ﬁg. 5, of the water samples shows that the 
INFOGEST water_ref had no measurable Cd and that remained throughout the INFOGEST 
procedure. The same is true for the water_ref sample in the fermentation. These water 
samples are MilliQ water, and it was expected to not contain any Cd. The water_Cd 
samples were water spiked with a Cd standard to a concentration of 250 ppb. 
For the water_Cd sample in the INFOGEST part it is clear that only part of the Cd is released 
into the supernatant. As the digest pellets are continued into the fermentation, we do not

step, new water samples were needed as there was no pellet from the INFOGEST 
procedure to continue with. New Water_ref, no Cd, and Water_Cd containing 250 ppb Cd 
were prepared. Results of these show a substantial absorption in the pellet. The total Cd 
concentration in the fermentation pellet shows a higher Cd concentration than the spiked, 
which can only be explained by the feces already containing Cd.

Discussion 
Unfortunately, the results of the experiment cannot tell us much about the bioavailability of Cd 
in the chosen food matrixes and the reason for this is due to freeze-drying the pellets after 
INFOGEST digestion, in glass bulbs. Cd has a very high affinity to glass and large amounts were 
clearly absorbed during the freeze-drying step. Despite the low amounts of Cd left in the food 
digesta, we still see a difference in the concentrations of matrix and matrix_ref. This is clear in 
both the gastric and intestinal compartment samples, indicating that enzymatic digestion helps 
with the release of Cd from the food matrixes. Thus, it would be relevant to retest the food 
matrixes. Not having any results also means a risk assessment is not applicable.

However, the water samples clearly show that the Cd, to a large extent, is excreted with the 
feces. This is also what literature states. 90-95 % of Cd remains unabsorbed (Blomberg et al., 
Chapter 32, 2009, Omprakash et al, 2024). Fecal Cd determination has also been used to study 
Cd intake by ingestion in human volunteers with results showing absorption rates that varies 
from 4.7 to 7.0%, with higher uptakes in women than in men (Nordberg Chapter 32, 2009).  
Fecal determination has also been used in studies of in contaminated areas in Japan, where 
intakes have been found in the range of 150-250 μg Cd/day 
Animal studies have demonstrated that the true excretion of Cd in feces is dose-dependent and 
true fecal excretion is partly proportional to the body burden, particularly at low doses. In 
rodents, the rate is lower and in these animal studies, it was also seen that the composition of 
the diet was important. The marginal bioavailability of Cd in the small intestine implies that 
most dietary Cd passes through the upper gastrointestinal tract to interact with gut bacteria in 
the colon and this is also what our investigation suggests. 
Diets with high ﬁber content reduced the uptake of Cd and the same was seen when sufficient 
zinc (Zn) and iron (Fe) were available (Nordberg et al., 1985). Diets low in proteins, minerals and 
certain vitamins (A, C, B6 and B1) have been reported to increase Cd absorption and toxicity. In 
contrast, diets rich in ﬁber have the reverse effect (Nordberg et al., 1985).  
The INFOGEST method is a relevant procedure for simulating the upper gastrointestinal tract. It 
has been reﬁned and is an excellent alternative to animal trials, though no method is a hundred 
percent perfect. A fall back in the case of INFOGEST2.0 would be that all testing is done under 
aerobic conditions, which is not optimal as a gastrointestinal test procedure. 
As mentioned earlier, concentrations of cadmium in the digested food matrixes are very 
low and the study cannot, in any satisfactory way, able to enlighten us in in the sense of to 
what extent Cd is bioavailable. It can, though, be a way to get a better understanding of the 
physiological responses of different food matrixes, in a non-invasive way. 
Cd is ever present in our environment and there is a need to ﬁnd ways of mitigating 
exposure to reduce human exposure. Different intervention models are already 
developed. An example is a Heptaglucan product to remove “trace elements” in plant 
products for food consumption already commercially available 
(https://www.agriton.co.uk/heptaglucans, 14. March 2025, 7 pm). Other mitigating 
applications for decreasing Cd accumulation in the environment are under development. 
Nanoparticles for absorption of metals to avoid heavy metal stress to plants (Soni et al., 
2024) and wheat straw biochar to bind bioavailable Cd from contaminated water and soil 
(Cui et al., 2019) are just a few examples.

Conclusion   
With the aim of creating a powerful and healthy link between people and the planet, to protect 
and promote both, the Nordic Nutrition Recommendations (2023) recommends, amongst other 
things, people to exchange their protein intake from meat products to high protein plant 
products. To express it in a trendier way “eat less meat and more pulses”. Is this really a good 
advice, though?  
The purpose of this project was to implement the INFOGEST 2.0 method to our laboratory and 
calculate the amount of bioavailable Cd in the gastrointestinal compartments, in two food 
matrixes. One food matrix containing a high amount of protein, black-eyed beans > 5g/100g, 
and one food matrix containing a low amount of protein, sweet potato <2g/100g.  
Unfortunately, we are not able to give a quantitively reliable answer to this question, although 
this was our aim.  
INFOGEST 2.0 has successfully been implemented in the laboratory. It has not been possible to 
give an estimation of bioavailability of Cd in black-eyed beans and sweet potatoes. The reason 
for this is as earlier explained a mistake of freeze-drying the food matrix pellets in glass bulbs, 
not knowing the high affinity to glass and thereby the massive loss of Cd. Having the total Cd 
count of the food matrixes before digestion led us to make a theoretical estimation of available 
Cd in the food products. Black-eyed beans contain 102 µg/kg Cd and sweet potatoes contain 
128 µg/kg. This would give a cadmium intake of 22.4 mg/day and with an estimation of 90% 
passing through the intestines the daily intake of Cd would be 2240 µg/day which corresponds 
to 32 µg/kg bw pr day. This suggest that the bioavailability of Cd is very important. Hence it 
would be interesting to redo the experiment and also to look into the diversity of the gut 
microbiome and its possible change. And even though, the environmental impacts are among 
the lowest in food products, supporting pulses as part of a plant-based healthy diet, the Cd 
concentrations suggest not to change the daily protein intake entirely to one of the plant 
products “examined” in the project.
