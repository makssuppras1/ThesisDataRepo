Abstract
The primary goal of this project is to propose a solution that allows a quadrotor to
navigate in an orchard, create a 3D map of the orchard, and detect fruits on the trees.
The 2D occupancy map was created using a top view photo of the orchard, and a
multiple goal path planning was implemented with a combination of A* and TSP integer
programming. The 3D map is created by means of RTAB-MAp while the quadrotor is
navigating along the predetermined path. Finally, the fruits are detected and located
using a color-based filter and RANSAC model fitting segmentation.
The developed method, after testing, can essentially perform the functions of 3D map
building and fruit positioning. However, in terms of orchard size, number of fruits, etc,
both simulation and experimental environments are overly idealistic when compared to
real agricultural harvest environments.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
The world is facing the severe challenge of unsustainable food production, owing to
factors such as population growth, urbanization, climate change, etc. Around 800 million
people, or almost 11 percent of the world’s population, are still hungry (FAO, IFAD
and WFP, 2015b).
Furthermore, the world’s roughly 500 million smallholder farmers, who contribute 80
percent of our food, are amongst the poorest and most malnourished groups. In fact,
climate change could force over 100 million people into extreme poverty by 2030, mostly
through impacts on agriculture and food security.
Hence, improving the performance of the food system is critical if we are to sustainably
feed nearly 10 billion people by 2050 while raising farmer incomes, protecting them from
climate change and helping them to thrive.
Figure 1.1: Growth in Total Food Consumption by 2030, Relative to 2015
Introducing robots to agriculture has great potential to improve agricultural produc-
tion eﬀiciency. Nowadays, agricultural robots can replace humans in certain activities,

including seeds sowing, weeding, crop-dusting and harvest. Yet two factors limit the util-
ity of agriculture robots: intelligence and cost. For starters, the degree of agricultural
automation is insuﬀicient. In most cases, robots requires manual operation, sometimes
even professionals’. Besides, most robots work for specific tasks. For instance, if the
harvest robot can only harvest one kind of seasonal fruit, limited work time will make
the cost of robots higher than that of manual harvesting.
DTU participates in The Mohamed Bin Zayed International Robotics Challenge, one
challenge of which requires UAVs and UGVs to perform a set of agriculture-related tasks
autonomously, in the hopes of inspiring future agriculture robots. This project aims to
contribute to the competition by planning a energy-saving path and build a 3D map of
the farmland using UAV.
1.1
Research Objectives
In this section, the objectives of the project are presented. The objectives are chosen in
such a way that if they are fulfilled, the quadrotor should be capable of navigating in an
orchard of specified size, mapping the orchard, and detecting and localizing the fruits
on the trees.
The quadrotor should be able to:
• Avoid static obstacles in the orchard
• 3D Mapping a certain number of trees of known location with the shortest path,
assuming suﬀicient battery capacity
• Extract and localize the fruits from the built point cloud map.
It should be noted that neither optimization nor innovation of mapping algorithm or path
planning algorithm is the scope of this project. The goal of this project is to integrate
existing algorithms to achieve new outcome: 3D mapping an orchard autonomously.

CHAPTER 2

2.1
Related Work
Navigation can be defined as the combination of the three competences [11], including
localization, path planning and map-building. The ability to navigate in an orchard or
a farmland is extremely important for a agricultural robot. In general, the fundamental
requirement of navigation is to avoid collisions. The higher level requirement is to make
the robot get to a desired position, even getting there in the optimal path.
Classify navigation in terms of sensor, there are GPS navigation, visual navigation, LI-
DAR navigation, etc.
GPS navigation system can provide the absolute position of the robot and has been
widely used for localization of outdoor robots. While in an orchard, canopies may block
satellite signal, it is not reliable to navigate by GPS [5].
Due to low prices of cameras and the rapid development of computer vision, visual
navigation tends to be a good option for robots.
Torres-Sespdra presented a visual
navigation method based on a neural network classifier [13]. They classified the image
into sky, land/soil, orange crown, orange trunk via a multi-layer feedforward neural net-
work. Then detect borders and generate path using edge detector and Hough transform.
Andrew English proposed a vision based navigation approach, which generates an over-
head view of crop rows by processing the image with the attitude of robot [2]. Then
estimate the vehicle’s heading relative to the crop rows by estimating the direction
of the dominant parallel texture in the overhead image. Stereo vision could be used
for navigation as well. Rovira [10] proposed a method to create 3D terrain maps by
combining the information captured with a stereo camera, a localization sensor, and
an IMU(inertial measurement unit). By means of information from IMU and GPS, the
point cloud acquired by the stereo camera is transformed from image coordinates to
ground coordinates and then stitched to a global map.

2.2
Problem Analysis
Inspired by the literature cited in section 2.1, we use a 2D occupancy grid map to assist
the quadrotor navigation to build the 3D map of the orchard. This method is more
eﬀicient and simple than autonomous exploration [15]. The 2D occupancy grid map
could be transformed from an RGB image of the top view of the orchard, acquired from
a down-looking camera mounted on the quadrotor. With the 2D occupancy map, we
are able to plan obstacle-avoidance paths for the quadrotor. As for 3D mapping, open
source visual SLAM algorithms could be implemented to acquire the point cloud of the
trees in the orchard. Finally, to detect and localize the fruits on the tree, the point cloud
segmentation algorithm can be employed based on the color and shape characteristics
of the fruit.

Drone Modeling and
Control
A quadrotor is a kind a multi-copters that generates lift with four rotors. In general,
the four rotors are of the same size and distributed symmetrically, two of which spinning
clockwise and the other two counterclockwise. This configuration enables the quadrotor
to keep hovering by canceling the torque from each motor. Pitch, roll and yaw of the
quadrotor could be controlled by independent variation of the speed of each rotor.

3 Drone Modeling and Control
As shown in Figure 3.1(a), rotors 1 and 3 spin in one direction, while rotors 2 and
4 spin in the opposite direction, yielding opposing torques for control. The quadrotor in
(a) hovers or adjusts its altitude by applying same speed to all propeller and hence zero
total torque. In (b), by increasing the speed of rotors rotating in one direction, resulting
in the total torque of the motors to be non-zero, the quadrotor adjusts its yaw angle. In
(c), total torque equals to zero, while more thrust to rotor 2 and less thrust to rotor 4
causes pitch adjustment. By the same token, the quadrotor adjusts its roll angle.

3.2
Controller of a quadrotor
Adopt cascaded controller to control the quadrotor [14]: the inner control loop is re-
sponsible for attitude control, the feedback states are the angular velocities measured
by onboard accelerometers and gyros and the reference is the output of the position
controller; the outer control loop is responsible for position control, it calculates the
error between estimated and desired position and velocity and outputs desired thrust
and roll pitch angles.

5.3
Path Search Algorithm
In robotics, the most basic and commonly used graph search algorithms are Breadth
First Search, Dijkstra’s Algorithm, Greedy Best First Search and A*, etc.
Breadth First Search explores evenly in all directions, starting from the starting point
and expanding the search scope called frontier, until reaching the goal. All the child
nodes are added to a queque(first in first out), nodes whose neighbors have not yet been
checked are placed in a container called open, and nodes that have been checked are
placed in a container called closed.
Instead of searching evenly in all directions, Dijkstra’s Algorithm considers the cost of
movements and keeps track of the total movement cost from the start location. It uses a
priority queque to store the nodes so that the frontier node with the lowest cost will be
processed first. As we may end up visiting a location multiple times, add a location to
the frontier if the new path to the location is better than the best previous path rather
than the location has never been reached.
In Dijkstra’s Algorithm, the total cost from the start is used for the priority queue
ordering. While, in Greedy Best First Search, we use the cost to the goal(heuristic func-
tion) for the priority queue ordering instead.
==PAGE:46==

5 Mapping
Dijkstra’s Algorithm is able to get the shortest path, while wasting time on outrageous
directions. Greedy Best First Search explores in promising directions but it may not
find the shortest path when there is large obstacles between starting point and the goal.
The A* algorithm synthesizes Dijkstra’s Algorithm and Greedy Best First Search, using
the sum of the distance from the start point and the estimated distance to the goal point
as the cost for the priority queue ordering, which promises to find the optimal path as
long as the heuristic does not overestimate distances.

is, the further the tree is from the origin, the greater the error of the center coordinates.
See in table 5.1, the comparison between the real position and the extracted coordinates
of the trees in first column is shown. The reason for the error is : (1)deviation between
the center of the trunk and the center of the canopy contour; (2)inaccuracy of the scale
factor that transforms pixel coordinates to world coordinates; (3) the quadrotor has to
fly high enough to capture the full top view of the orchard, resulting in a large scale
factor between world coordinates and pixel size.
The minimal unit of image is one
pixel and the difference between extracted pixel coordinates and real pixel coordinates
is magnified by the scale factor.
To sum up, the method presented in this project is only suitable for small orchards.

CHAPTER 7
Conclusion & Future Work
7.1
Conclusion
The objective of this project is to plan an optimal path to map an orchard and detect
fruits autonomously with a quadrotor equipped with an RGB-D camera. For this, a full
coverage path circling around the tree for reconstruction is generated. The reconstruc-
tion is based on RTAB-Map, which is known for its high performance on large-scale and
long-term online operations. On account of energy eﬀiciency, a multiple goal path plan-
ning for orchard mapping is proposed, which is based on a combination of A∗algorithm
and TSP integer programming. As for fruit detection module, a color-based filter is used
in conjunction with RANSAC sphere segmentation. The algorithms are tested in both
simulation and physical environment.
Because of the lack of texture features in the trees and their similar appearance in the
simulation environment, visual odometry and loop closure are disabled in the simula-
tion.Instead, the point cloud is stitched together using ground truth odometry. The
point cloud map of the tree is dense and clear, thanks to the precision of ground truth
odometry. The extraction and localization of the fruits are very precise. Furthermore,
the multi-goal path planning algorithm allows the quadrotor to map a predetermined
number of trees using the shortest path.
It is possible to generate a point cloud for a model in physical world. However, the
point cloud contains noise and distortion, resulting in deviation or even failure to de-
tect the apples. Possible explanations include: (1) insuﬀicient ladder features; (2) the
quadrotor’s fast movement and large ascent distance, resulting in a low overlapping ratio
between frames; and (3) the string tying apples is thin and long, causing apples to shake
violently when the quadrotor creates strong airflow.

7 Conclusion & Future Work
7.2
Future Work
According to the simulation and physical experiment results, meanwhile comparing the
simulation and physical experiment condition with the actual harvest scenario, the future
work is expected as follows:
1. In this project, the 2D occupancy grid map is constructed from the aerial image
of the orchard. Consider the irregularity of the crowns, the position of the trees
obtained from the center of the crowns will be significantly different from the truth
after error accumulation of edge detection and center extraction, particularly for
those trees located far from the reference origin. This could lead to collisions. A
simple solution is to set some location marks in the orchard and calibrate the scale
factor based on these marks.
2. From chapter 6, it can be seen that there are noises and distortion in the reconstruc-
tion of the ”tree”. One possible explanation is that there aren’t enough features
detected in each frame. It is required to do similar reconstructions for real trees to
verify the explanation. Furthermore, combining other sensors such as GPS, IMU,
and LIDAR should be attempted to see if the odometry accuracy improves.
3. The apples are displaced optimally in both the simulation and the physical exper-
iment. In reality, the situations are far more complicated. For example, the leaves
cover the apples, the apples overlap, the immature apples are greenish red, and so
on. As a result, the color-based filter threshold and parameters for fitting apples
as spheres should be tuned for robustness.
