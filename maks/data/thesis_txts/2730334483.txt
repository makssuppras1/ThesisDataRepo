Abstract
Energy poverty, a persistent global challenge, signifies households’ inability to afford essential energy
services. This project explored its complex nature, using Greece as a case study- where approximately
one in ten households faced severe energy poverty, especially amid its financial crisis.
Beginning with a thorough literature review, the study examined the concept of energy poverty,
exploring its various definitions and the key metrics used to measure its extent. The research underlined
socioeconomic factors such as age, education, ethnicity, gender, and health that were instrumental in
influencing energy poverty. To provide a comprehensive context, a global perspective of energy poverty
was presented, further narrowing down to its manifestation in Greece.
Various methods- including Correlation analysis, Descriptive statistics, and the Vector Autoregres-
sion model- were employed to reveal underlying patterns and relationships. Techniques such as Impulse
Response Function and Variance Decomposition Analysis further illustrated how changes in one variable
potentially impacted another over time. Significant insights emerged, highlighting the relationship and
inner dynamics among certain socio-economic conditions and the ”inability to keep home adequately
warm”, a key indicator of energy poverty. A tangible connection was observed: households with di-
minished work opportunities tended to have reduced energy usage over basic needs, underscoring the
interplay between employment conditions and energy accessibility. Moreover, the arrears on utility bills
indicated the financial challenges often accompanying energy poverty. Variance decomposition analysis
revealed the interconnections among employment, energy consumption patterns, financial stability, and
overall living conditions.
Despite the profound insights, the research encountered challenges, particularly in data consistency.
Yet, its findings hold potential implications for energy sector policymakers and stakeholders. The study
concludes by emphasizing the need for a better understanding of this multifaceted problem to enable
well-rounded strategies against energy poverty. Finally, the study advocates for future research explor-
ing wider socio-economic and environmental indicators, underlining the criticality of addressing energy
poverty in the context of sustainable development goals.

Introduction
Energy poverty is a critical global issue, particularly pronounced when households struggle to afford
basic energy services essential for decent living conditions. The repercussions go beyond mere discomfort,
impacting health, educational attainment, and overall quality of life. The challenge of addressing energy
poverty is further complicated by the broader climate change context, where the necessity to reduce
greenhouse gas emissions intersects with the need to ensure energy accessibility for all.
Within the European Union, the pursuit of sustainable development and the well-being of its citizens
holds central importance.
However, the transition towards a greener environment poses challenges,
especially for economically vulnerable households. The EU acknowledges the issue of energy poverty,
emphasizing the importance of ensuring all citizens can afford essential energy services. The European
Energy Poverty Index (EEPI) underscores the disparity in domestic energy poverty challenges among
member states, particularly those with a GDP per capita below the EU average.
Greece, amidst its financial crisis, presents a unique case to to examine the challenges of energy
poverty. Approximately one out of ten households in Greece faces severe energy poverty, a struggle
intensified by the economic downturn since 2007. The evolving energy consumption patterns, policy
responses, and the socioeconomic factors influencing energy poverty in Greece necessitate a thorough
investigation to inform more sustainable solutions.
This study aims to unravel the relationship among socioeconomic factors and energy poverty in
Greece. Through a comprehensive review of existing literature and data collection from reputable sources,
this research aims to bridge the understanding gap regarding the interplay between socioeconomic factors
and energy poverty in Greece. The data, divided into objective indicators and socioeconomic factors,
serves as the foundation for exploring the dimensions of energy poverty. For this study, the Energy
Poverty indicator was selected to ensure a pronounced correlation relationship, while the socioeconomic
factors were chosen to illuminate the various societal dimensions interacting with energy poverty. The
process of data cleaning and validation ensured the integrity and relevance of the data, serving as the
backbone of this research.
The study utilizes the Vector Autoregression (VAR) model, to capture the interdependencies among
the chosen variables. Through the VAR model, the goal is to better understand how disturbances to
socioeconomic factors impact energy poverty and vice versa.
This model’s implementation includes
the assessment of lag length, Impulse Response Functions and Variance Decomposition Analysis to
understand the variables’ dynamic relationships and the factors level of contribution to energy poverty.
With the insights derived from these methodologies and analyses, the study aims to offer a comprehensive
understanding of the relationship between socioeconomic factors and energy poverty in Greece, which
may influence future strategies and policies.

3.1
Introduction to Energy Poverty
3.1.1
What is Energy Poverty
Energy poverty is a multifaceted issue that impacts households across the world. While it typically refers
to a situation where households are unable to access or afford the essential energy services required for
a decent standard of living, it is worth noting that there is not an undisputed definition for the term.
The concept encompasses a variety of dimensions, including adequate warmth, cooling, lighting, and
the energy needed to power appliances. It can result from high energy expenditure as a proportion of
household income, low income levels, poor energy efficiency, or inadequate access to energy services.
Each of these aspects can be influenced by various factors such as energy prices, market conditions,
income distribution, and the energy performance of buildings.
Understanding and addressing energy poverty requires recognizing its multidimensional nature and
considering the broader context of energy services rather than merely focusing on energy consumption.
This shift in perspective emphasizes the importance of capability and utility, which can help identify
households that are struggling to meet their basic energy needs.
Energy poverty is also interconnected with social and financial poverty, energy vulnerability, and
health concerns. Households experiencing energy poverty may face additional challenges, such as an
increased risk of illness due to inadequate heating or cooling, social isolation, and financial stress due
to high energy bills. Given the diverse and complex nature of energy poverty, it is essential to develop
comprehensive and targeted policies to tackle its various dimensions effectively. This includes considering
the specific needs and challenges faced by different households, as well as addressing the broader structural
factors that contribute to energy poverty. By acknowledging and addressing the nature of energy poverty,
policymakers and stakeholders can work together to develop more effective and inclusive solutions for
improving access to essential energy services and promoting a better quality of life for all.
3.1.2
Energy Poverty Definitions
Despite its significance, a universally accepted definition remains elusive, as different researchers and
policymakers approach the problem from various perspectives. Throughout the literature, several def-
initions have emerged, each attempting to encapsulate the essence of energy poverty while accounting
for its diverse dimensions. These definitions often revolve around factors such as: inadequate access to
energy, affordability, expenditure on energy services, and the impact on household well-being. In order
to gain a comprehensive understanding of energy poverty, it is crucial to explore these definitions and
consider the unique aspects they bring to light, acknowledging the intricate nature of the issue and the
challenges it poses to individuals and communities alike.
The definitions of energy poverty, presented in Tables 1 and 2, reveal the complexity of the phe-
nomenon.
Table 1 provides definitions from different countries, while Table 2 cites various authors’
perspectives on the issue. These definitions touch upon issues of affordability, access, and adequacy of
energy services, with some taking a narrow focus on heating or fuel consumption, while others adopt a
broader perspective. Key differences among the definitions include the emphasis on the percentage of
income spent on energy or the inability to access essential energy services. It is important to recognize
that national and regional contexts influence the prevalence and manifestation of energy poverty, and
that current trends in research and policy making may shift the understanding and mitigation of the
issue.

Understanding Energy Poverty
3.2.1
Main Approaches for Measuring Energy Poverty
Diverse methods of measurement offer their unique perspectives and insights, enabling to create targeted
strategies to address energy poverty. Each approach, brings a different view to the issue, broadening our
comprehension and shaping the development of effective interventions.
Expenditure-based Approach:
The expenditure-based metrics focus on a household’s energy ex-
penditures in relation to its income. Energy poverty is typically defined as occurring when a household
spends more than a certain percentage of its income on energy-related expenses. This approach provides
a straightforward and quantifiable way to assess energy poverty, as it relies on readily available data such
as household income and energy costs. However, it may not capture the full range of factors contributing
to energy poverty, such as the quality of energy services or the energy efficiency of a household’s dwelling.
Consensual-based Approach:
The consensual metrics, in contrast, are based on perceived depriva-
tion, identifying households that report facing difficulties in meeting their basic energy service needs.
This approach takes into account the subjective experiences of households and acknowledges the impor-
tance of societal values and cultural context in determining energy poverty. By incorporating public
input and focusing on social consensus regarding minimum standards, consensual-based metrics offer a
more comprehensive understanding of energy poverty. However, this approach may be more susceptible
to biases and variations in individual perceptions.

Each approach offers its own unique perspective on the issue, with the former providing a quantitative
assessment based on income and energy expenditures, and the latter incorporating subjective experi-
ences and social consensus. By understanding and applying these approaches, we can develop a more
comprehensive understanding of energy poverty and devise effective strategies to address it. In addition
to the Expenditure-based and Consensual-based approaches, there are various other methods used to
measure energy poverty. Most notably, the Temperature-based and the Outcome-based approach, which
focuses on the outcomes associated with energy poverty, such as disconnections, arrears, and cold-related
mortality. While these metrics provide valuable insights into the consequences of energy poverty, their
use may be limited due to a lack of consistent national statistics and limited access to utility-based data.

3.2.2
Key Energy Poverty Metrics
It is worth noting that there are different categories of metrics such as single, compound, and notably,
composite metrics. Composite metrics endeavor to encapsulate the various aspects of energy poverty
within one comprehensive figure. In the following section, we will delve into an examination of three
specific composite metrics: the Energy Development Index (EDI), the Multidimensional Energy Poverty
Index (MEPI), and the Energy Poverty Index(EPI).[15]
Energy Development Index (EDI):
Developed by the International Energy Agency, employs an
evenly-weighted average of three normalized components: per capita commercial energy consumption,
the share of commercial energy in final energy use, and electrification rate. Despite its utility in tracking
the maturation of national energy systems, critics suggest it falls short in depicting energy deprivation
at the household level. Specifically, it tends to emphasize the population’s access to an electric network,
thus not thoroughly covering all aspects of household energy services.[16][15]
Multidimensional Energy Poverty Index (MEPI):
Conceptualizes energy poverty as a form of
energy deprivation. It incorporates various components such as cooking, lighting, and services delivered
by household appliances, including entertainment, education, and communication.
Deprivation rules
are set for each component, contributing to an aggregate that, if exceeding a predetermined threshold,
categorizes a household as energy poor. Notably, a weighted average of the population that is poor in
each category is calculated. However, while it offers a robust understanding of domestic energy poverty,
it neglects to account energy used for productive purposes and beyond the household.[16]
Energy Poverty Index(EPI):
Developed by Mirza and Szirmai, the Energy Poverty Index (EPI)
constitutes of the average of energy shortfall, a percentage of the minimum basic level of energy con-
sumption and the inconvenience of energy. This inconvenience measure encompasses the frequency of
sourcing energy, the distance travelled from the household, the transport used, involvement of household
members in energy acquisition, the time spent on energy collection weekly, household health, and the
involvement of children in energy collection.[17]
European Energy Poverty Index (EEPI):
The European Energy Poverty Index (EEPI) offers a
comprehensive measure of EU Member States’ progress in mitigating both domestic and transport energy
poverty, as well as their interconnectness. This index is divided into two sub-indexes: the European
Domestic Energy Poverty sub-Index (EDEPI) and the European Transport Energy Poverty sub-index
(ETEPI). The EEPI score, derived for the population belonging to the first income quintile (lowest income
level), is the geometric mean of the EDEPI and ETEPI scores. The EDEPI specifically evaluates domestic
energy poverty by considering factors such as the proportion of energy expenses in total expenditures,
the inability of the lowest income citizens to maintain optimal home temperatures, and the prevalence
of substandard housing conditions, like dampness or rot. Conversely, the ETEPI focuses on transport
energy poverty, examining metrics like transport energy costs for car owners, the affordability of public
transportation for the least affluent, and their access to such transport services. A higher score on the
EEPI signifies better country performance in addressing energy poverty.

3.2.3
Relevant Terms
Energy Insecurity
Households encounter what is known as energy insecurity when they lack sufficient
financial means to afford electricity or other energy utilities. Chen, Greig, Nelson, and Li categorize
energy insecurity into three key aspects[19]:
1. The energy burden, referring to the proportion of household income spent on utility bills, thus
depicting economic constraints;
2. Energy hardships, accounting for deficiencies in physical dwelling, structural aspects of building
materials, and the availability of energy resources or appliances that assist in satisfying basic needs.
3. Energy behavior, which encapsulates strategies employed to lessen the health, economic, and phys-
ical consequences of energy insecurity.
Energy insecurity isn’t solely dependent on financial difficulty, but is also affected by the physical state
of housing that may escalate costs, as well as the behavioral strategies implemented to compensate for
the necessity of electricity. Considering the fundamental role of energy in satisfying basic human needs,
it’s viewed as a significant determinant of social and public health. Consequently, energy insecurity can
have detrimental effects on both physical and mental health, even to the extent of escalating the risks of
mortality.[20]
Energy Democracy
Unraveling the concept of energy democracy from a vast body of literature, it
emerges as both an ideal and a process[21]. The ideal envisions a future characterized by a low-carbon,
sustainably growing world where individuals and communities participate in and own energy systems.
This ideal positions energy democracy as an embodiment of a future energy paradigm. As a process,
energy democracy focuses on the reinforcement and actualization of the individual and collective right
to participate in energy policy decisions. This is pursued in the hope of achieving more equitable and
sustainable energy outcomes.Inherent in the concept of energy democracy is the emphasis on participa-
tion. Hence, energy democracy becomes a lens through which to reinterpret participation rights, while
also spotlighting local communities as important actors.
It should be pointed out that, there is a conflict within the literature regarding the full definition of
energy democracy, revealing limitations in our current understanding. For example, within the Euro-
pean Union, energy democracy is seen as a socially desirable policy objective [22]. Recognizing these
limitations is essential to reassess the concept and expand its scope to provide a more comprehensive
and inclusive definition.

Energy Justice
Energy Justice, rooted in the ethos of environmental and climate justice, addresses
the disproportionate adverse human health or environmental effects experienced by specific groups[23].
It involves distributional, procedural, and recognition justice, respecting individuals’ rights and demands
concerning their energy and environment. Furthermore, energy justice includes the energy democracy of
countries, lying within broader governance, institutional, and democratic frameworks.
As a complex concept, energy justice promotes equitable treatment across diverse populations and
social groups, particularly concerning energy infrastructure and production.
Substantive and formal
equality form a key part of the energy justice, advocating for policies that account for inequalities while
ensuring similar cases are treated alike. [24]

3.3
Socioeconomic Factors Influencing Energy Poverty
3.3.1
Age
Older individuals, particularly those living alone, are significantly more susceptible to energy poverty.
This susceptibility is often related to factors such as lower household income, energy inefficiency of homes,
and relatively high energy costs. Furthermore, severe material deprivation rates, which include the ability
to afford basic goods and services, have been observed to be more frequent among older women than
older men.
The situation across the EU Member States varies, as depicted in Figure above. Across all EU-27
households in 2019, 7.6% reported inability to maintain adequate warmth, indicating energy poverty.
However, this issue was particularly acute for single older adults, where the rate rose to 10.7%. Con-
versely, households composed of two adults, at least one aged 65 years or more, reported a relatively
lower incidence at 6.7%. Looking closer at Greece, the figures are notably higher. For ”All households”,
”Households with two adults, at least one aged ≥65 years”, and ”Households with one adult aged ≥65
years”, the respective rates are approximately 17.5%, 18%, and 26%. These figures demonstrate the
heightened vulnerability to energy poverty among older people, particularly those living alone.

3.3.2
Education
Energy poverty has been recognized as a significant hindrance to educational opportunities, especially in
rural areas. Lack of access to energy impacts learning outcomes by limiting study times, particularly at
night, which subsequently hinders academic performance and future prospects. This inequality is most
pronounced between the urban centers and rural-poor, where the incidence of energy poverty is high.
Studies over a span of nearly three decades across 50 developing countries have shown a strong positive
correlation between energy development and improved health and education outcomes. These countries
also showed that those with the lowest performance in health and education are the same ones that
rank low on the energy development index. Additional indirect effects of energy poverty on education
include higher rates of school absenteeism and illness, further underscoring the need for energy access as
a priority in strategies aimed at poverty reduction. This synthesis of findings underscores the vital role
of energy in both promoting equal access to education and improving health outcomes. Therefore, efforts

to address energy poverty are integral to any comprehensive poverty alleviation and social development
strategy.

3.3.3
Health
Energy poverty constitutes a significant public health concern with both direct and indirect impacts
on health outcomes. The energy poor, frequently depend on harmful energy sources such as biomass-
generated energy for basic needs like cooking and heating. The smoke produced from such substances
is linked to respiratory infections causing premature deaths, especially among women and children, with
over 2 million people affected each year. Additionally, due to energy poverty, less water is boiled for
consumption and hygiene purposes, thereby increasing the risk of waterborne diseases [27].
On top of that, it should be pointed out that there is a significant impact on mental health, par-
ticularly in the face of job losses and lower investments due to a pandemic. Such conditions render it
difficult for individuals to meet basic survival needs, including electricity and other forms of energy [28].
Furthermore, individuals in energy-poor households experience compromised physical health, including
higher susceptibility to respiratory diseases, general illnesses, and the amplified effects of cold and damp
housing conditions on existing health issues.
Interestingly, energy consumption in the residential and commercial sectors positively impacts health
outcomes. It indirectly improves health through facilitating clean cooking fuels, safe drinking water,
better communication about health services, and mitigating respiratory and cardiovascular diseases from
exposure to indoor air pollution. Therefore, reducing energy poverty, by making energy more affordable,
might contribute to controlling increasing public healthcare expenditure. It’s crucial to note that the
association between energy and infant mortality rate is contingent on affordable healthcare services and
more accessible better quality cooking fuels.

Ethnicity and Race
The racial difference in energy poverty is significant, largely encouraged by societal structures that pro-
mote discrimination and inequality. Research suggests that addressing this inequality could potentially
reduce the frequency of energy poverty among these communities by about 17% [29]. These differences
in energy poverty are mostly tied to income, but they also mirror a deeply set societal hierarchy that
encourages discrimination.
For instant, in US, low-income African-American and Latin households, whether they rent or own
their homes, carry an unfair amount of energy burdens.
These burdens are heavier for Black and
Hispanic households with young children, those requiring electronic medical devices, or those living
in poor conditions, making them especially susceptible to energy insecurity and utility disconnection.
Systemic factors like income inequality, wealth gaps, and racial segregation act as barriers, preventing
these communities from escaping this cycle. However, the research suggests that improving income and
health conditions could potentially reduce the role of race in energy poverty, offering viable ways for
intervention [30]. Such interventions could significantly lessen these disparities and drastically improve
the living conditions.

3.3.5
Gender
Gender inequality significantly influences energy poverty across different socioeconomic contexts. Women,
in particular, face a unique set of challenges, largely as a result of societal norms and roles. For instance,
in energy-poor households, the ability of women to gain salaried employment or pursue career devel-
opment can be severely compromised. This disadvantage can further exacerbate gender inequalities in
income and power dynamics, making it even more difficult for these women to escape the cycle of energy
poverty[31].
Women in energy-poor households can also experience a degradation of their health and human
capital, further constraining their opportunities for advancement. As such, energy poverty does not just
represent a lack of access to basic resources; it also reinforces existing gender disparities and can hinder
the achievement of equality in other spheres, such as health, education, and socio-political-economic

In addressing these issues, it is crucial to consider gender-specific strategies.
Policymakers must
account for the different roles and responsibilities that men and women carry within their societies, and
design interventions that are sensitive to these differences. For instance, promoting renewable energy
projects, providing education about renewable energy options, and enhancing economic opportunities
can all help to alleviate energy poverty, particularly for low-income female-headed households [32].
However, such measures will only be effective if they are accompanied by broader efforts to combat
gender inequality. This might include actions to dismantle harmful gender norms, improve women’s access
to education and income sources, and ensure equitable distribution of resources within households. By
taking a comprehensive and gender-inclusive approach, it will be possible to address the root causes of
energy poverty, and thereby contribute significantly to gender equality.
3.3.6
Economic conditions
Economic conditions, both on a macro and micro scale, serve as key determinants in the context of
energy poverty. Specifically, in less affluent nations, primarily in Asia and Africa, the accessibility to
clean energy emerges as a paramount challenge. In these regions, the reliance on traditional fuels for
cooking results in substantial health implications due to the associated indoor air pollution. Contrarily,
in wealthier nations, the financial burden of maintaining domestic indoor warmth can lead to an array
of health complications, ranging from respiratory illnesses to mental health issues, exacerbated by the
stress of financial instability.
The relationship between income and energy poverty changes with the country’s GDP. In lower
GDP nations, variations in relative income significantly impact the objective accessibility of energy,
while affordability remains less affected.
However, as a nation’s GDP ascends, the focus transitions
from energy accessibility to its affordability.
In economically disadvantaged nations, specific electric
appliances, such as washing machines, air conditioners, and personal computers, are not widely available.
Consequently, household income becomes a critical determinant of how many households can afford
these amenities. Inversely, in richer countries, these appliances are relatively accessible to all households,
though expenditure on them fluctuates based on the country’s relative income levels.
In essence, a country’s level of economic development and income inequality impact household en-
ergy poverty across three dimensions—accessibility, reliability, and affordability. Concerning accessibility
and reliability, energy poverty is more prevalent among households residing in less economically devel-
oped countries. In terms of affordability, households in countries characterized by mid-level economic
development and higher income inequality are more susceptible to energy poverty.[33]

3.4
Energy Poverty in a Global Context
3.4.1
The Twin Challenges of the Global Energy Landscape
The global discourse on energy has been increasingly dominated by the pressing issue of climate change.
This climate crisis not only threatens the natural environment and peoples’ wellbeing but also casts a
shadow over future generations. Energy production is accountable for a staggering 87% of global green-
house gas emissions, with the most significant contributors being the world’s wealthiest nations. With
the continual production of greenhouse gases, their concentration in the atmosphere steadily increases.
This escalating scenario compels a strategic response aimed at diminishing emissions towards net-zero.
Achieving this reduction is crucial for stabilizing the levels of these gases in the atmosphere, a key step
towards mitigating the advance of climate change[34].
However, the global energy issue is far from one-dimensional. In fact, it presents a two-fold challenge:
energy poverty and excessive carbon emissions. On one side, the world faces the issue of energy poverty.
Many people around the globe don’t have enough access to modern energy sources, leading to poorer
living conditions. This is the first aspect of the energy challenge. On the other side, those who do have
energy access, mostly through fossil fuels, are producing excessive greenhouse gas emissions. This leads
to the second part of the problem: balancing the need for energy to lead a good life and the crucial task
of reducing carbon emissions. In essence, the global challenge extends beyond merely reducing emissions
towards net-zero but also ensuring everyone has access to energy without exacerbating climate change.
3.4.2
Global Disparities in Energy Access
Undeniably, access to electricity and clean fuels has profound implications on quality of life and environ-
mental sustainability. In this context, it’s generally recognized that ”access to electricity” encapsulates
the provision of electricity, the availability of safe cooking facilities, and a minimum threshold of con-
sumption. Over the past few decades, the global percentage of people with such access has seen a gradual
but encouraging increase, from 71% in 1990 to 87% in 2016. The number of individuals lacking access
has also dropped below one billion for the first time in decades, or perhaps ever in the history of elec-
tricity production. It fell from over 1.5 billion in 1990 to 760.89 million in 2019, a reduction that is truly
significant.

normal distribution. The insights drawn from this analysis played a crucial role in guiding the indicator
selection process based on their inter-relationships.

The data collection process for this study primarily revolved around sourcing accurate and pertinent

5.1.2
Variable Selection: Reasoning and Criteria
When addressing the topic of variable selection, it’s essential to understand that there isn’t a single,
universally accepted methodology to measure energy poverty.
The objective is not to define energy
poverty in its entirety or identify a definitive solution. Instead, the study aims to shed light on the
interactions between socio-economic factors and everyday life aspects and their implications on energy

5.2
VAR Model Implementation
5.2.1
Introduction to VAR Model
The Vector Autoregression (VAR) model, a multivariate time series model, captures the linear interde-
pendencies among multiple time series. The VAR model, by considering lagged values of all variables
in the system, provides a robust framework to analyze how exogenous shocks to socioeconomic factors
propagate through the system and influence energy poverty, as represented by the chosen indicator. With
the stationary dataset in hand, the study constructed a Vector Autoregression (VAR) model to elucidate
the dynamic interrelationships between the chosen dependent variable (indicator) and the three selected
independent variables (socioeconomic factors). The general representation of a VAR model of order p
(VAR(p)) is:
Yt = A1Yt−1 + A2Yt−2 + · · · + ApYt−p + ϵt
Where Yt is a k-vector of endogenous variables, Ai are k × k coefficient matrices, and ϵt is a k-vector of
white noise error terms.
5.2.2
Lag Length Selection
In the context of time series analysis, ”lag length” refers to the number of past time points that are
used to predict the current time point in a model. In a VAR model, lag length determines how many
previous observations of each variable in the system are included in the model. The selection of lag
length in a VAR model is extremely important, as The lag length can influence the model’s accuracy and
explanatory power. An inappropriate lag length might lead to omitted variable bias, autocorrelation, or
overfitting. Therefore, it’s crucial to rely on scientific criteria to determine the optimal lag length. For
the purpose of this project, the following criteria were considered:
1. LR(Likelihood Ratio): This test assesses the likelihood ratio, comparing the fit of the model with
lag ”p” against the fit with lag ”p-1”. A higher LR indicates a better fit. Tests are often conducted
at the 5% significance level.
2. FPE(Final prediction error): Measures the one-step ahead prediction error. A lower FPE suggests
that the model with the corresponding lag length has better predictive accuracy.
3. AIC(Akaike information criterion): AIC balances the fit of the model against its complexity. A
model with a lower AIC is generally preferred as it indicates a good trade-off between model fit
and complexity.
4. SC(Schwarz information criterion): Similarly, SC also balances model fit and complexity. However,
it penalizes complexity more heavily. A model with a lower SC is desirable.
5. HQ(Hannan-Quinn information criterion): This is another criterion that strikes a balance between
the fit and the number of parameters in the model. A model with a lower HQ is often preferred.
As presented in Table 3, multiple criteria suggest a lag order of 2 as optimal for this dataset. An
asterisk (*) next to a value denotes the lag order recommended by the respective selection criterion,
indicating its optimal performance for the dataset in question. Thus, based on the collective insights
from these criteria, a lag of 2 will be adopted for further analysis. This decision aligns with the primary
objective of achieving a balance between model accuracy and simplicity. By selecting a lag of 2, the
Vector Autoregression (VAR) model aims to effectively capture the dynamics among the variables.

5.2.3
Impulse Response Function
Impulse Response Functions (IRFs) are a fundamental tool in the analysis of VAR models. They depict
the reaction of one variable in response to a one-time shock to another variable, assuming all other shochs
are held constant. By introducing such shocks to each variable in the VAR model, the study was able to
observe and analyze the reactions of all other variables. This analysis illuminated both the immediate
and long-term effects of unexpected changes in each variable on the entire system.
Generalized Impulse Response Function
The Generalized Impulse Response Function (GIRF), pioneered by Pesaran and Shin [46], offers a way
to compute IRFs without being constrained by a specific variable ordering. While traditional methods
dictate which variables can instantly respond to shocks based on this ordering, GIRF promotes ordering
independence. This ensures an unbiased analysis and a true reflection of dynamic relationships. Unlike
conventional techniques that use orthogonalized shocks, GIRF maintains the simultaneous correlation of
shocks. This flexibility becomes essential when the interrelationships between variables are ambiguous or
secondary to the primary focus of the research. With its impartial methodology, the GIRF strengthens
the analysis, ensuring its robustness. Because of these unique features, GIRFs have been widely adopted
in fields like macroeconomics and finance.
Mathematical Representation
The GIRF evaluates the effect of a shock on the expected values of all system variables without a
set variable order. Mathematically represented, the Generalized Impulse Response for a shock to the jth
variable at time t on the expected value of another variable at time t + n is:
GIRF(n, ξj, t, Ωt−1) = E[yt+n|ξj,t, Ωt−1] −E[yt+n|Ωt−1]
Where:
• E[yt+n|ξj,t, Ωt−1] is the expected value of the variable y at time t + n given a shock ξj,t to the jth
variable and the information set Ωt−1.
• E[yt+n|Ωt−1] indicates the expected value of y at time t + n in the absence of the shock but
considering the same information set.
5.2.4
Variance Decomposition Analysis
Variance Decomposition, also known as forecast error variance decomposition, provides a breakdown of
the variance of the forecast error for each variable into components attributable to shocks from each
variable in the system. This analysis offers a quantitative perspective on the relative importance of each
shock in explaining the forecast error variance of the variables. Variance Decomposition Analysis was
performed in order to reveal the dominant contributors on energy poverty indicator and examine the
connection among the variables. This method also helped in understanding how much forecast changes
might be due to unexpected shifts in the other variables.
A more recent advancement in this domain is the Generalized Forecast Error Variance Decompo-
sition (GFEVD). Introduced by Pesaran and Shin [46], GFEVD offers an alternative approach to the
conventional variance decomposition. It ensures that the decompositions are invariant to the ordering
of variables in the VAR, thus eliminating potential biases. This tool has proven valuable in econometric
analyses, offering a more robust examination of inter-variable dynamics.

In essence, the decomposition paints a picture of interconnections among: employment conditions, en-

energy poverty and work intensity in households, emphasize the need for holistic policy interventions that
address these issues to achieve improvements in household well-being and tackle the phenomenon of En-
ergy Poverty.

7
Conclusion
The subject of energy poverty has emerged as a critical issue between sustainable development and
socio-economic policy. This study delved into Greece’s unique circumstances and examined the pivotal
relationships among the selected variables in order to comprehend their impact on energy poverty. Based
on the extensive research and methodologies presented, this concluding section highlights key insights,
addresses the study’s limitations, and outlines potential implications and future pathways for addressing
energy poverty more effectively.
7.1
Key Insights
• Interactions Over Time: The Impulse Response Function analysis highlights the interplay be-
tween energy consumption, household warmth, utility bill arrears, and work intensity in a household
setting. A significant observation is that during a shock in the ability to keep their homes ade-
quately warm, there is an initial positive impact for all variables. This immediate positive response
indicates a potential short-term resilience or coping mechanism within households.
• Energy Consumption Dynamics: A distinct relationship between energy poverty and household
work intensity was observed. This relationship underscores the interrelation between employment
conditions and maintaining household warmth.
• Financial Challenges and Socio-economic Conditions: There’s an obvious impact of the
inability to maintain home warmth on utility bill arrears, representing the immediate financial
ramifications of energy poverty. This reflects the conflict between households’ financial capabilities
and their essential needs.
• Decomposition Analysis Insights: The decomposition analysis examines further the complex
web of influences, revealing how economic struggles can quickly manifest as energy poverty. For
many of the cases, the dynamics observed between the 10th and 15th year horizons suggest a trend
towards stabilization. Another important finding is the bidirectional influence, especially between
energy poverty and work intensity in households.
• Holistic Policy Approach: The combined findings from both the IRF and decomposition analy-
ses emphasize that tackling energy poverty requires an understanding of the broader socio-economic
context. It’s not just about addressing energy needs, but also about understanding the broader
ramifications on employment conditions, household finances and overall well-being.
7.2
Research Limitations
One undeniable challenge faced during this research was data accessibility and consistency. Disparities in
data, including missing values, varied timelines, and inconsistencies, posed considerable hurdles. These
limitations did not affect the Vector Autoregression (VAR) model, as the chosen variables were consistent,
but there were limited choices among the variables that could be examined.
7.3
Implications and Future Directions
The insights derived from this study could potentially be valuable for policymakers in the energy sector.
Recognizing the multifaceted nature of energy poverty can guide more precise and targeted interventions.
Moreover, the bidirectional influence between energy poverty and consumption stresses that solutions
should be more comprehensive than merely ensuring energy access.
The methodology employed, despite its challenges, could be used on different contexts or for a wider
range of socio-economic indicators. Future endeavors can benefit from more expansive datasets, exploring
a broader range of socio-economic and environmental indicators, and delving into localized studies to
uncover similar relationships at community or municipal levels.
In conclusion, as the world confronts the complexities of sustainable development, addressing energy
poverty remains at the forefront. The ambition of this research, with its insights and the acknowledged
limitations, is to set the groundwork for more comprehensive and informed strategies.
