Abstract 
In this research report the energy-saving potential of personalized ventilation is investigated 
through means of proposing a new ventilation system design for the existing office building in 
Malmö, Sweden occupying by the technical consulting firm Tyréns AB. The main components of 
this study represent a literature review within the fields of ventilation system design, indoor 
environment and energy consumption (including previously made energy-related studies based on 
simulation works for personalized ventilation). An occupant survey consisting of 36 questions 
aimed towards occupants seated at floor plan 5 in the existing office building. Comprehension of 
the existing ventilation system design in the office building and proposal of a new ventilation 
system design incorporating the principles of personalized ventilation. An interview conducted 
with two HVAC experts at Tyréns AB discussing the proposals made and agreeing on the most 
optimal ventilation system design. Energy simulations in IDA ICE evaluating the energy-saving 
potential of the proposed design (incorporating the principles of personalized ventilation) and air 
distribution simulations in FloVent evaluating the indoor environment as well as air distribution of 
the proposed design. This research report concludes by stating that the potential of reducing the 
yearly energy consumption by using ventilation system design based on the principles of 
personalized ventilation are much greater than on the principles of mixed ventilation. It also 
concludes that the indoor environment quality (thermal comfort and indoor air quality) are at the 
same level and sometimes partly higher in some respects than mixed ventilation. 
 
KEYWORDS: Personalized ventilation, energy optimization, indoor environment, ventilation 
system design, energy simulations, IDA ICE, air distribution simulations, FloVent

Chapter 1: Introduction 
1.1 Background 
The world is developing towards a more globalized and urbanized place to live in and the effect of 
these forces are being felt by many people, especially those living in developed and modern 
societies. According to Statista (2016) the percentage of the population living in urban areas in 
2016 by continent ranges from 70-81% for all continents, except for Asia and Africa with 48% and 
41% degree of urbanization respectively. This translates into higher levels of outdoor air pollution 
in the urbanized areas in comparison with rural areas, not at least in Europe and Sweden (European 
Environment Agency [EEA], 2014), as people, buildings, cars and other modern technical 
inventions are being used more intensively in compact areas of space. Hence, it appears to be no 
surprise that authors/scholars such as Melikov (2016) and Awbi (2003) are calling for the research 
community and practitioners to put greater emphasis on the development of the indoor 
environment and ventilation system design in buildings. 
 
Awbi (2003) states that the ventilation philosophy has undergone major developments during the 
last three decades starting with emphasis on the mechanisms of air infiltration and introduction of 
the term “sick building syndrome SBS” (air quality problems in buildings) in relation to reduced air 
infiltration. Followed by emphasis on understanding the underlining causes of the sick building 
syndrome by introducing new ventilation concepts such as new air quality units, age of air and need 
for higher supply airflow rates. Ending with the most recent emphasis on the reduction of energy 
consumption and increased levels of environmental awareness which (Awbi, 2003, p. ix): 
“has focused the minds of researchers and designers alike on the potential of natural 
ventilation and user control of the local environment. As a result of these changes, 
new ventilation standards and guidelines have been written to reflect the importance 
of ventilation on the quality of indoor environment.” 
 
That is, the concepts of indoor environment, ventilation system design and energy consumption 
in buildings are interrelated with changes in one area affecting the others. This realization has made 
some authors/scholars propose alternative methods to the potential of natural ventilation in 
occupied spaces. By means of focusing on the individual user-control of the local-
/microenvironment, through the emphasis and introduction of the design concept of advanced air 
distribution (supply of clean air where, when and as much as needed or simply stated design of 
non-uniform indoor environment) for succeeding with the goal of achieving high-quality indoor 
environments with low energy consumption (Melikov, 2016). 
==PAGE:16==
16 
 
Two key methods for implementing an advanced air distribution (AAD) design in occupied spaces 
are personalized ventilation and personal ventilation. The former refers to attaching units to local 
building parts or furniture with the potential of being stationary or transportable, whereas the latter 
refers to attaching units to the occupant’s wearable surroundings such as clothing, headset or 
personal computer (Melikov, 2016). 
 
The general development of the AAD concept and call for a paradigm shift from the design of 
collective indoor environments (solely total volume air distribution TVAD) to individually 
controlled local-/microenvironments in occupied spaces (solely AAD) has lead the research 
community to develop further AAD methods and some of these have even reached the 
practitioners and been implemented in practice. In many cases the AAD methods are 
complemented by TVAD for the areas outside of the occupied space (also known as dual 
ventilation systems), but it appears to be possible to develop fully functional AAD in some specific 
cases as well (Melikov, 2016).  
 
It comes as no surprise that the implementation of AAD in practice is going slowly forward with 
regards to the fact that the current indoor climate standards are developed for TVAD and there is 
a lack of design guidelines for AAD. Not to mention the higher initial investment costs as well as 
implementation and maintenance costs of potentially having dual ventilation systems (Melikov, 
2016). Nevertheless, this is not to say that the benefits of AAD in a dual ventilation system context 
such as better occupant health (fewer sick leave days) and increased occupant performance (higher 
firm revenues) does not outweigh the economic costs. Rather that the connections between the 
benefits of AAD and consequently increased revenues are clearly intangible and indirect in their 
nature. 
 
Consequently, the focus should be on increasing the current knowledge base in society through 
further research studies concerning the implications of advanced air distribution methods on 
different types of buildings. By doing so hopefully all major stakeholders will be enlightened to 
push for better indoor environments in our future buildings without compromising the need for 
lower energy consumption in buildings. 
 
1.2 Scope of work 
This research report (MSc thesis) sets out to contribute to this overarching research agenda, 
implicitly, of increasing the current knowledge base in society and hopefully contribute in pushing

the paradigm shift a few steps forward in the field of indoor environment and ventilation system 
design from solely TVAD to AAD or dual systems. Hence, the title “design of indoor environment: 
energy optimization with personalized ventilation”. 
 
There are literally speaking more ways than one can even imagine contributing to this overarching 
research agenda implicitly. However, explicitly there are fewer ways and since this research report 
is part of the completion of an advanced engineering degree in civil engineering specialized in 
building services representing 30 ECTS, it has been decided to limit the work to a practical case 
study: an office building located in Malmö (Sweden) occupied by the technical consulting firm 
Tyréns AB. 
 
The explicitly research objectives, hypothesis reflecting the knowledge gap and detailed study 
design are presented at the end of the literature review (chapter 2). The literature review focus on 
presenting recent studies on personalized ventilation from an energy analysis perspective and the 
concepts of the three interrelated areas. That is, the concepts of indoor environment, ventilation 
system design and energy consumption in buildings. Further details about the office building (case 
study) are presented under the reference case (chapter 3). In this chapter, information relevant for 
the general design and technical design of the whole office building is presented along with further 
information about the chosen office space to be simulated in upcoming chapters. 
 
In the forthcoming chapter (4) the technical design of the office space chosen to be simulated is 
presented in detail with great emphasis on the energy concept, indoor environment and ventilation 
system design. This is followed by presentation of the results from a questionnaire survey 
conducted with the occupants at the chosen office space. Aiming to give better understanding of 
the current indoor environment conditions, familiarity and attitude toward personalized ventilation 
and provide information about factors such as occupant behaviour, density and profile. 
 
Based on the existing technical design (especially ventilation system design) and preference of the 
occupants. New ventilation system designs are presented to the HVAC experts at Tyréns AB 
incorporating the principles of AAD with personalized ventilation as the key method for the 
occupied space. These new ventilation system design proposals are discussed in detail with the 
HVAC experts by means of an informal open-ended interview ranging for approximately 1 hour.

Chapter 2: Literature review 
2.1 Energy consumption 
The great emphasis, that has been growing the last decades, on energy consumption in buildings 
has its roots in the fact that they consume around 40% and 36% of the total energy usage in Europe 
and Sweden respectively (European Commission [EC], 2017; Swedish Energy Agency, 2015). In 
Sweden, the energy usage of the residential and service sector (households and non-residential 
buildings represent 90% of the energy consumption in this sector) has decreased steadily during 
the 2000s and was recorded to be 147 TWh (around 132 TWh for the households and non-
residential buildings) in 2013 (Swedish Energy Agency, 2015). 
 
During the same year, in 2013, the energy usage for heating and hot water in households and non-
residential buildings totalled 80 TWh (55% of the total energy usage in the sector) with the non-
residential buildings representing 28% of this value: 22.4 TWh. This shows that the energy usage 
for heating and hot water in buildings has steadily decreased the last decade due to measures such 
as replacement of primary energy sources from oil to electricity and district heating, increased 
number of heat pumps, additional isolation in buildings, replacement of windows in old buildings 
and other energy-saving measures, whereas the electricity usage has remained relatively steady 
during the same period (Swedish Energy Agency, 2015). 
 
In non-residential buildings, the business electricity (electricity used for fixed installations in the 
building as well as for business activities conducted in the building) has increased from 8 to 30 
TWh between the period of 2004-2009 but recently, in 2013, returned to the levels of 2004. As 
explained in the Swedish Energy Agency (2015) the statistics are uncertain to the reasons for this 
behaviour and no real conclusions have been drawn. However, according to the study Stil2 
(Swedish Energy Agency, 2015, as cited in Swedish Energy Agency, ER2007:34, 2007; ER2007:11, 
2007; ER2008:09, 2008; ER2009:10, 2009; ER2010:17, 2010; ER2011:11, 2011) focusing on the 
electric usage in non-residential buildings, conducted between the period of 2005-2011, it was 
concluded that lighting and fans represent the largest amount of electric usage in non-residential 
buildings. 
 
Recognizing that district heating (23 TWh in 2013, Sweden) is the most commonly used form of 
energy for heating and hot water in non-residential buildings, followed by electricity accounting for 
3.3 TWh in 2013, Sweden (Swedish Energy Agency, 2015). It should be noted that energy-saving 
==PAGE:20==
20 
 
measures are progressively becoming reality in the built environment in Sweden and the 
introduction of new technical design solutions should not compromise this agenda if ever to be 
successfully implemented in practice. Not to mention all the policies and legislation that are 
constantly being introduced, updated and implemented in Europe and Sweden supporting this 
agenda of lowering the energy consumption in buildings (Melikov, 2004; Swedish Energy Agency, 
2015). Most notably, in Sweden, the general requirement for all buildings to be “near-zero energy 
buildings” by 2019 and 2021 for government and privately-owned buildings respectively (Boverket, 
2015). 
 
2.2 Indoor environment 
The indoor environment in a building context is a fascinating and comprehensive field of study. 
Boiled down to its core root, it mainly concerns the comfort, health and performance of human-
beings occupying the building (Melikov, 2016). Nevertheless, this is not to say that the building 
itself is not affected by the indoor environment as a medium of spreading heat, cold, pollution 
(chemical and sensory) and relative humidity (RH) to different building parts. All these factors are, 
indeed, recognized for their importance. But for the purposes of this research report the focus will 
be on the effect on human-beings occupying the building (particularly thermal environment (TE) 
and indoor air quality IAQ) inside the occupant zone at each workstation local environment rather 
than the building itself. 
 
The key factors affecting the human experience of the indoor environment in buildings are divided 
into two main categories: physical environment and individual-specific. The former refers to the 
thermal environment, indoor air quality and technical-related aspects (acoustics, lighting and 
electrical environment), whereas the latter refers to physiological, psychological, genetical and social 
aspects (Nilsson, 2000). Generally, the physical environment factors are easier to adjust for whereas 
the individual-specific ones are not for obvious reasons. That is, human-beings by nature are very 
different in their individual preferences, not at least when it comes to the indoor environment in 
buildings where large portions of their lives are spent. Melikov (2004) states that these differences 
in preference are always present to a certain degree and continues to highlight the “physiological 
and psychological response, clothing insulation, activity, air temperature and air movement” 
(Melikov, 2004, p. 1) as key areas in this light. 
 
The current standards used in Sweden and elsewhere in Europe are based on the concept of 
standardisation. The main standards are EN 15251:2007 and EN-ISO 7730:2005 where the former 
==PAGE:21==
21 
 
has its focus on energy performance addressed by IAQ, TE, lighting and acoustics and the latter 
has its focus on the thermal comfort using calculation methods of predicted mean vote (PMV), 
predicted percentage dissatisfied (PPD) and local thermal discomfort (Swedish Standards Institute 
[SIS], 2007; Swedish Standards Institute [SIS], 2006). These standards are good to have initially for 
reference purposes when evaluating tolerance limits for different variables in the background and 
local environments. However, they cannot readily account for all the individual changes among 
different human-beings’ due to primarily being based on average values for large group of 
occupants and uniform indoor environments (Melikov, 2004; Melikov, 2016). 
 
2.3 Ventilation system design 
The general purpose of the ventilation system in a building context is to create an acceptable indoor 
environment for the human-beings occupying the building especially with regards to the thermal 
environment and indoor air quality regardless of whether the building is air-conditioned. Or as 
Awbi (2003, p. xi) explains it: 
”air-conditioning is generally understood to mean the heating, cooling and control of 
moisture in buildings. Consequently, this involves heating and cooling load calculations 
in addition to the design of plant components, ductwork and control systems. 
Ventilation, on the other hand, refers to the provision of sufficient quantities of outside 
air in the building for the occupants to breathe and to dilute the concentration of 
pollution generated by people, equipment and materials inside the building. This is 
necessary for both air-conditioned and non-air-conditioned buildings.” 
 
In this light, several methods have been developed for designing the ventilation system in buildings 
mainly depending on the requirements for indoor environment in the specific building as well as 
the chosen energy concept (heating and cooling) and ventilation concept (air distribution pattern). 
Natural ventilation systems, mechanical ventilation systems and hybrid ventilation systems 
(combination of natural and mechanical) are the primarily used methods. 
 
The most commonly used method, in office buildings, for a long-period has been mechanical 
ventilation systems with mixed air distribution although displacement air distribution has started 
to be used more frequently lately (Awbi, 2003; Melikov, 2004). Both methods are total volume air 
distribution methods and the key difference between them can be found in how the pollutions are 
dealt with inside the building. Mixing air distribution focus on evenly distributing the air in the 
space with the purpose of decreasing the concentration levels of different pollutants in any given 
==PAGE:22==
22 
 
point. Displacement air distribution, on the other hand, focus on creating two layers: the bottom 
layer (inside the occupant zone) being the clean zone and the upper layer (outside the occupant 
zone) being the pollution intensive zone (Müller, Kandzia, Kosonen, Melikov, & Nielsen, 2013; 
Skistad, Mundt, Nielsen, Hagström, & Railio, 2007). 
 
2.4 Personalized ventilation 
The main idea of personalized ventilation is to allow individual occupants to adjust the air settings 
(air temperature, air velocity/flow rate and/or direction of the supply airflow) around themselves 
based on their personal preferences as well as the provision of clean, cool and dry air close to each 
occupant breathing zone (Melikov, 2004; Schiavon & Melikov, 2009). The velocity and turbulence 
levels are usually low when provided to the breathing zone. Personalized ventilation is further 
characterized by attaching units (supply air terminal devices and sometimes also exhaust air terminal 
devices) to local building parts or furniture with the potential of being stationary or transportable 
as well as user-controlled or self-responding (Melikov, 2016; Melikov, 2004). In this way, the clean, 
cold and dry air entering the building passes straight or close to the occupants breathing zone and 
pollutions are extracted at their source (assuming local exhaust ATD are used). In comparison to 
total volume air distribution methods focused on the complete occupant zone, personalized 
ventilation is an advanced air distribution method focused on the occupants’ local environment. 
 
Personalized ventilation has been available in the marketplace since the 1980s but has not been 
widely used in practice due to the current perception that it leads to higher system and installation 
costs (Dieckmann, Cooperman, & Brodrick, 2010). Several arguments have been put forward for 
its usage such as higher energy-savings, improved thermal comfort, improved indoor air quality 
(especially inhaled air quality), decreased SBS symptoms and enhanced protection against cross-
infections consequently leading to better comfort, health and performance for the human-beings 
occupying the building (Dieckmann, et al., 2010; Melikov, 2004; Schiavon & Melikov, 2009). These 
benefits (particularly the energy-saving potential) are believed to be prevailing whenever the 
following strategies are implemented: reducing the outdoor airflow rate (due to higher ventilation 
effectiveness), expand room temperature comfort limits (due to controlled local environments) and 
supply of personalized ventilation air only when the occupants are present at their desks (Schiavon 
& Melikov, 2009). 
 
Interestingly, Melikov (2004) states that compliance with the current thermal environment 
requirements in the standards is not a guarantee that occupants will feel satisfied with the indoor 
==PAGE:23==
23 
 
environment as the building is taken into operation as many studies has shown. In fact, research 
reports have shown, that among different occupants, there exist differences in the preferred air 
temperature of up to 10°C, differences in the preferred air movement (air velocity/flow rate) of up 
to four times and large differences in the perceived indoor air quality (Melikov, 2004, as cited in 
Grivel & Candas, 1991; Melikov, Arakelian, Halkjaer, & Fanger, 1994; Summer, 1971). These 
results are not surprising considering that the comprehensive standardization report (CEN-CR 
1752:1998) for expressing the quality of the indoor environment for ventilation system design in 
Europe is based on average values and does not include detailed information about the local 
environment, beyond that individual control of the local environment for each occupant might be 
needed to achieve the highest category A (European Committee for Standardization [CEN], 1998; 
Melikov, 2004). This goes in line with the other key indoor environment standards in Europe (EN 
15251:2007 and EN-ISO 7730:2005), which are also based on average values for large group of 
occupants. 
 
2.5 Interrelation between concepts 
The dynamic interaction between the concepts of energy consumption, indoor environment and 
ventilation system design (including personalized ventilation) is a complex field that is very climate 
(location in the world) and building specific (European Commission [EC] - Join Research Centre 
[JRC], 1996). Nevertheless, it needs to be understood to a certain degree for the purposes of this 
research report at least in general terms and not necessarily for specific situations. 
 
In a nutshell, the ventilation system design (assuming heating and cooling is done through 
ventilation) is where most of the energy consumption is found for the purposes of creating a good 
thermal environment and indoor air quality. Simple stated, this is the main relationship between 
these concepts. In case the ventilation system is used for solely ventilation purposes (pollution 
removal and provision of clean air or simply stated to ascertain acceptable IAQ), then the energy 
consumption represents the part of the business electricity used for running the fans (as previously 
discussed) regardless of which air distribution pattern is used (personalized ventilation or not). The 
rest and major part of the energy consumption goes for the heating and cooling devices whether it 
be radiators (primarily radiation but also convection), active chilled beams (primarily convection 
but also radiation) or any other technical devices with the aim of providing good thermal 
environment for the occupants. 
 
==PAGE:24==
24 
 
Nevertheless, it should be noticed that due to the natural physical laws (buoyant forces) taking 
place inside the occupant zone (including the local environment) changes in these heating/cooling 
devices and their placement will also affect the movement of pollution as it follows the air 
movements (from cold to warm air) in the space. The number of occupants and their distribution 
in the building in question will also affect this interplay as human-beings provide pollution to the 
space through body released bio-effluents, exhaled air and thermal loads through their warm 
body/head temperatures by means of conduction (indirectly to the air through heated floor areas), 
convection and radiation. Adding the ventilation velocity and turbulence levels to the mix makes 
the interaction in the indoor environment even more dynamic, not to mention the additional 
dynamic interface (assuming personalized ventilation in a local environment context) going on 
between the personalized ventilation flow (from the supply ATD), free convection flow 
(convective boundary layer and thermal plume generated by the human body) and respiration flow 
(exhalation through the breathing process of human-beings). 
 
In case the ventilation system design is based on combining the purpose of ventilation and air-
conditioning (heating and/or cooling) then obviously, the dynamics of the interplay just described 
changes drastically. Nevertheless, in both cases control systems are usually implemented for 
providing sufficient levels of clean air (including heating and/or cooling if air-conditioned) either 
at constant air volume (CAV) or variable air volume (VAV) depending on factors such as occupant 
profiles (time during the day spent at workstation in specific room) or any other activity-based 
measure. 
 
The major problems that usually occur in this dynamic interaction are between the aims of wanting 
to implement energy-saving strategies and simultaneously achieve high indoor environments 
(especially indoor air quality) which can contradict each other. Increased air exchange rates usually 
lead to improved IAQ but this strategy also increases the energy consumption and vice versa. If 
air-conditioning is used through means of the ventilation system, then this relationship becomes 
even more complex and sensitive to changes. The dynamic relationship between these two 
parameters strongly depends on the placement of the supply ATD, directions of the ventilation 
flow rates, type of building (including its design), occupants (including their activities), location and 
type of heat sources and configuration of the technical systems. Suitable materials (low pollutions), 
no allowance for smoking and air-tight buildings contribute to making it smoother to deal with 
(European Commission [EC] - Join Research Centre [JRC], 1996). 
 
==PAGE:25==
25 
 
The thermal environment (heat exchange between the human body/head and the local 
environment of the occupant) is also affected by the placement of the supply ATD, direction of 
the ventilation flow and increased levels of air exchange rates through higher risks for draught 
discomfort due to higher velocity and turbulence levels (Melikov, 2004). Basically, airflow control 
is crucial for both energy consumption as well as thermal comfort and indoor air quality (especially 
inhaled air quality) and this has been highlighted a lot in the literature not at least when it comes to 
the occupants’ immediate local environment (Melikov, 2015). 
 
2.6 Energy-related studies 
The number of energy-related studies when using personalized ventilation appears to be limited 
mainly focused on full-scale measurements in chambers, field studies and simulations (Lyubenova, 
Holsøe, & Melikov, 2011; Schiavon, Melikov, & Sekhar, 2010). In this section, some of the most 
recent energy studies with regards to personalized ventilation and energy simulations will be 
presented. Note, that the focus is on the energy-saving potential as the main driving force rather 
than the improvement of health, comfort and performance of the occupants when presenting these 
studies although the consequences on these parameters from implementing different energy-saving 
strategies are clearly indicated. 
 
Looking at the first study its focus is on exploring the potential energy-savings of using 
personalized ventilation coupled with passive chilled beams utilizing the simulation tool BSim2008. 
The simulations are done on a yearly basis in a cold and dry climate (Herlev, Copenhagen, 
Denmark) in an open plan office space (149.4m2 floor area and 2.8m height) in a building occupied 
by the Danish consulting firm HaCaFrø A/S. More precisely, the simulations are based on a 
comparison between three different settings with desk mounted personalized ventilation ATD: 
solely mixed ventilation, PV coupled with mixed ventilation and PV coupled with passive chilled 
beams without mixing ventilation. Several results and conclusion are drawn. First, the concept of 
personalized ventilation has higher potential for energy-savings in warm and humid climates in 
comparison to cold climates. However, the potential for energy-savings in cold climates is still 
prevailing preferably combining the PV system with another background ventilation system for 
heating and cooling (Lyubenova, et al., 2011). 
 
This study is further characterized by having an air supply temperature for the personalized 
ventilation ATD between 18-20°C without the ability for occupants to individually control it. 
However, the airflow is individually controllable within the range of 1-10 l/s. For the background 
==PAGE:26==
26 
 
ATD, these are ceiling mounted and controlled by CO2 or temperature levels. Same air handling 
unit for supplying the air for both the personalized ventilation and background system are used 
with constant pressure regulation. Regarding the occupant behaviour, the key things that seem to 
be important are occupant profile during the working hours and occupant density per m2 floor area 
with the main purpose of reducing the PV flow rates when not needed and thereby decreasing the 
energy consumption of the PV system. 
 
Two key energy-saving strategies are highlighted as necessary for succeeding with the goal of 
achieving desirable results. That is, going beyond the maximum allowed room temperature with 1-
2°C as stated in the Danish Standard DS474 (standard used for the thermal comfort compliance 
throughout the study) which is argued to be acceptable when having individually controlled air 
movements according to the European indoor environment standards EN 15251 and EN-ISO 
7730 (Lyubenova, et al., 2011). In a nutshell, the following is concluded (Lyubenova, et al., 2011, 
p. 7): 
“the use of PV coupled with background mixing ventilation may lead to 20% reduction 
in the airflow rate supplied per person in comparison with mixing ventilation alone. 
The reduction may increase to 30% when room air temperature is kept 1°C above the 
recommended standards and to 40% when the increase is by 2°C. This may lead to 
energy saving. PV combined with passive chilled beams may lead to 80% reduction in 
the supplied air per person in comparison with mixing ventilation alone. The 
occupancy density seems to be important factor which affects the supply flow rate per 
person and thus may affect the energy saving.” 
 
The next study from Schiavon & Melikov (2009) also focus on energy-saving strategies in a fictive 
open-space office (120m2 floor area and 3m height) in cold climates (Copenhagen, Denmark) using 
personalized ventilation (including heating/cooling) and energy simulations in IDA ICE. The 
European standard EN 15251 is used for compliance of the thermal comfort conditions and 
ventilation specifications. The focus is on the supply air temperature as the means of reducing the 
energy consumption. Basically, it is concluded that the control strategy of the supply air 
temperature has large impact on the energy consumption and that the energy consumption of the 
personalized ventilation system might increase a lot (61-278%) compared to solely mixed 
ventilation systems if not using appropriate control strategies. Some very interesting findings are 
further highlighted (Schiavon & Melikov, 2009, p. 543): 
==PAGE:27==
27 
 
“the results show that the best supply air temperature control strategy is to provide air 
constantly at 20°C. The most effective way of saving energy with personalized 
ventilation is to extend the upper room operative temperature limit (saving up to 60% 
compared to the reference case). However, this energy-saving strategy can be 
recommended only in a working environment where the occupants spend most of 
their time at their workstation. Reducing the airflow rate does not always imply a 
reduction of energy consumption. Supplying the personalized air only when the 
occupant is at the desk is not an effective energy-saving strategy.” 
 
Next study on the list from Schiavon, et al. (2010) focus on a fictive open-space office building 
(120m2 floor area and 2.7m height) in a hot and humid climate in Singapore by using the simulation 
tool (IDA ICE) and comparing a personalized ventilation system (including separate air handling 
unit and excluding mechanical exhaust ATD) coupled with an independent ventilation system 
(adjustment of airflow rate or supply air temperature not applicable and absence of heating system) 
for keeping the background space outside of the occupant zone in desirable conditions. These 
settings are compared to a conventional mixing ventilation system with constant air volume and an 
overhead fan coil unit. In this study, the following is documented: the control strategy of the supply 
air temperature and its relationship to the outdoor climate does not have large effects on the energy 
consumption in hot and humid climates and vice versa in cold climates. This leads the authors to 
suggest that it could possibly become a problem for implementing personalized ventilation in cold 
climates when simultaneously using the same ventilation system for heating and cooling purposes 
due to the risk of them working towards each other (Schiavon, et al., 2010). 
 
In line with the other studies, it concludes that energy-savings can be made with up to 51% in 
comparison to solely mixing ventilation when applying appropriate control strategies for outdoor 
airflow rate reduction (due to higher ventilation effectiveness), increased maximum room air 
temperature limits allowance and decreased outdoor airflow supply (only provided when the 
occupants are at their desks). Also, it is highlighted that the personalized ventilation system should 
be used in settings with high occupant densities (people spend most of their time at their desks 
during the day). Otherwise, the risk of low indoor air quality is more prevailing due to the potential 
lack of sufficiently supplied fresh and clean air or as Schiavon, et al. (2010, p. 706) explain it “for 
total volume ventilation the outdoor airflow is controlled by the number of people in the ventilated 
space; in personalized ventilation, the outdoor airflow is controlled by the number of people seated 
at the desks”. 
==PAGE:28==
28 
 
The fourth study from Yang, Sekhar, & Melikov (2010) utilizes advanced energy calculations (no 
simulation tools are stated) focusing on personalized ventilation in a fictive office space (84m2 floor 
area and 2.7m height) in a hot and humid climate (Singapore) with ceiling mounted personalized 
ventilation (separate ATD conditioned through primary AHU) coupled with mixing ventilation for 
the areas outside of the occupant zone (separate ATD conditioned through secondary AHU). In 
comparison, to solely mixing ventilation and mixing ventilation including individually controlled 
desk fans (ability to generate additional air movement, should not be confused with personalized 
ventilation). The control strategies used in this study are very focused on trying out different types 
of ATDs for personalized ventilation and especially supply airflow rates.  
 
Conclusions are drawn supporting the usage of personalized ventilation with the total energy usage 
decreasing (although energy for transport increases) for the ceiling mounted PV coupled with 
mixing ventilation case in comparison to the solely mixing ventilation case. In comparison to the 
mixing ventilation including individually controlled desk fans case similar conclusions are drawn 
that the ceiling mounted PV system coupled with mixing ventilation has better ability to realize 
cooling effects and decrease the total energy consumption. 
 
2.7 Knowledge gap and hypothesis 
The trend that can be seen, based on the literature review done in this research report, is that the 
interest for studying energy-related aspects of implementing personalized ventilation through 
means of energy simulations is increasing. Nonetheless, the number of studies are very limited and 
focused on office-space in buildings in Denmark and Singapore. It also appears that different 
standards are used for comparing the simulated results in the different studies not to mention that 
different simulation tools are also used. This makes sense, since buildings by nature are very specific 
in their configurations hence different standards and/or simulation tools might be more applicable 
for different configurations. Nevertheless, it also creates a barrier for comparing the studies among 
each other on the same basis due to their fundamental differences. This is not to say that fruitful 
insights are not added to the knowledge base through the reviewed studies but rather to highlight 
that each study contributes with a slightly different angle for whether personalized ventilation 
should be supported from an energy-saving potential perspective. 
 
Looking at the aspects that the studies have in common several similarities are also found. Many 
of the studies appear to combine personalized ventilation with another background ventilation 
system (often mixing ventilation) and compare this with solely mixing ventilation or personalized 
==PAGE:29==
29 
 
ventilation coupled with specific heating/cooling devices. Although, having slightly different focus 
area such as supply air temperature, supply airflow rate and/or placement of ATD throughout the 
comparison between different ventilation system designs. The concluding results appear to be 
rather similar in the sense that personalized ventilation has the potential to lead to energy-savings. 
That is, if appropriate control strategies are applied and the system is implemented in an office-
space where occupants utilize their desks often during the day. 
 
For the purposes of checking the air distribution pattern (mixing ventilation or personalized 
ventilation) actual behaviour and its implications on the thermal comfort and indoor air quality, 
advanced calculations appear to be the mostly used means of solving this matter. The usage of 
FloVent or similar computational fluid dynamics (CFD) tools for this purpose does not appear to 
be apparent at least not in combination with energy simulations in IDA ICE. Finally, as far as the 
author is aware no studies have been done in Sweden focusing on the energy-saving potential of 
personalized ventilation through means of the simulation tools (IDA ICE and FloVent) in an actual 
or fictive office-space building for that matter. The reasons for this could probably be that the 
Swedish higher education curriculum for civil engineers and the construction industry as whole 
have a tradition of neglecting the importance of the indoor environment, whereas in Denmark the 
opposite has been the case for both the higher education curriculum and construction industry. 
 
There is clearly a necessity for further studies investigating the energy-saving potential of 
personalized ventilation through means of simulation tools not at least in Sweden. That said, the 
general hypothesis of this research report that the research objectives are going to investigate more 
explicitly is as follows: 
Implementing new ventilation system design with personalized ventilation in 
office buildings with mixed air distribution pattern should lead to greater 
energy-savings and the same or higher indoor environment (thermal comfort 
and IAQ)

The overarching aim of this research report is to investigate the energy-saving potential of 
personalized ventilation through means of proposing and simulating a new ventilation system 
design with different configurations to the current office-space building in Malmö occupied by the 
technical consulting firm Tyréns AB. This is going to be done through solving the following 
objectives:

 Investigate different ventilation system design configurations with personalized ventilation

2.9 Study design 
The scope of this research report has already been stated by highlighting the key focus areas 
through the presentation of its structure. Hence, this section will focus on presenting more detailed 
matters relevant for conducting the study such as investigation approach (complete building, floor 
and/or room level), standards for thermal comfort and indoor air quality as well as the interrelation 
between the two simulation tools. 
 
The investigation approach in this research report follows the thinking/procedure of integrated 
design presented at Technical University of Denmark (DTU) by the authors Petersen & Svendsen 
(2008). The focus is on component (floor/room) level analysis for achieving low energy consuming 
buildings with high-quality indoor environment, with the argument that this leads to complete 
buildings operating at the same level of energy consumption and indoor environment when the 
different components are placed together. Due to the limited time and scope of this study not all 
floors and rooms are investigated by means of simulations. Instead, the most representative and 
utilized floor and rooms are chosen to represent the whole building. 
 
For the purposes of choosing standards to be used throughout the simulation works of this study 
the following two have been chosen: EN 15251:2007 and EN-ISO 7730:2005 to represent the 
major requirements, formulas and categories to look at when evaluating different indoor 
environment related matters. The reason for this is rather simple. Both are mostly applicable in 
Sweden when looking at the available standards in the SIS (Swedish Standards Institute) database, 
both are translated into Swedish and both are presented as being currently valid on their website 
(Swedish Standards Institute [SIS], 2017). However, for the indoor environment related parameters 
==PAGE:31==
31 
 
of ventilation effectiveness and turbulence intensity the requirements and formulas in the standard 
CEN-CR 1752:1998 are used. Finally, for the energy-related performance requirements the latest 
and currently prevailing national building requirements are used: Boverkets Building Regulations 
(BBR). 
 
The major driving force for the simulation-related work is IDA ICE. FloVent is considered a 
secondary application for the purposes of investigating the air distribution pattern and determine 
whether the current and proposed conditions satisfy the indoor environment requirements 
especially for thermal comfort and IAQ. This is not to say that more advanced and fruitful insights 
cannot be generated in FloVent, but rather that the focus of this study is on investigating the 
energy-saving potential of personalized ventilation with the mindset that the indoor environment 
is fine, as long as, it is kept at the same level (or higher) as for the current conditions. 
 
Based on the insights gained throughout the literature review of previously made energy-related 
studies this study will also focus on investigating the proposed ventilation system design 
configuration. By means of different cases with changing parameters (described more in detail later) 
such as supply outdoor airflow rates, supply outdoor air temperature, maximum allowed room air 
temperature, occupant profile and occupant density.

3.1 General design 
The office building in Malmö has been occupied by the technical consulting firm Tyréns since the 
year of 2002 as the building was finalized. The building has 6 storeys (excluding the underground 
basement). The first and sixth storey are slightly different from storey 2-5 in the sense that these 
accommodate the entrance/reception (including some office-/meeting space) and canteen/roof 
terrace respectively. Storey 2-5, on the other hand, are rather similar in size, design and technical 
configuration and mainly accommodate office and meeting space for the technical consultants. An 
atrium space named “Ljusgården” is also available passing through storey 1-6. The total floor area 
of the existing building (including the underground basement) is approximately 4520m2 (gross) and 
4242m2 (net). The height of the building (excluding the underground basement) is approximately 
23.465m (gross) and 22.865m (net), and the height of the underground basement is about 4.8m 
(gross) and 3m (net). 
 
An extension building of 2600m2 (floor area, net) and 18.875m (height, net) with five storeys (floor 
plan 2, 3 and 4 are expected to be occupied by Tyréns) is currently being built and supposed to be 
finished in 2018 (winter season). The extension building will be fully integrated with the existing 
building through the atrium space on the southside façade. The existing building part (excluding 
the extension building part) is expected to be completely renovated to modern standards by 
September 2019 except for the underground basement. Storeys 2-5 are expected to be rather similar 
to the present settings with regards to design layout. Storey 1 and 6, on the other hand are expected 
to be slightly more altered. Storey 1 which currently house the entrance/reception as well as office-
/meeting space will be identical to the present case apart from the exception that all office space 
will be turned into seminar-/meeting space. Storey 6 which currently house the canteen and large 
seminar room will also be identical to the present case apart from the exception that the large 
seminar room will be turned into dining space and be integrated with the existing canteen. 
 
In this research report the extension building part is overlooked. Instead, the existing building part 
is the focus with the aim of proposing new HVAC solutions incorporating personalized ventilation 
for the forthcoming renovation in 2019. Rather than considering all storeys it has been decided to 
only consider storey 5 which is perceived to be representative of storey 2-5 due to their similarities. 
This storey accommodates several departments (including the HVAC department) and is the most 
heavily occupied with regards to number of seated occupants.

3.2 Technical design 
The overall building has a rather complex system design for ventilation, heating and cooling. The 
ventilation (provision of clean and fresh air) is made through two separate air handling units sharing 
common inlets on the façade for outdoor air intake. The first air handling unit serves storey 1-5 
(average constant airflow 4500 l/s) whereas the second only serves storey 6 (average constant flow 
2100 l/s). For handling the exhaust air throughout the building there are 7 fan systems. Three of 
these are directly relevant for the occupied space on the different storeys. The first system mainly 
serves the toilet, pantry and postal space of storey 1-6 (average constant flow 1310 l/s) whereas the 
second system only serves the remaining space of storey 6 (average variable flow 500-2100 l/s). 
The third system serves the atrium space which passes through storey 1-5 with an average constant 
flow of 3200 l/s. Except for the summer season when it switches to average variable flow of 3200-
6000 l/s to account for additional loads in the atrium space. In this regard, it functions as the main 
exhaust fan system for extracting the polluted air from the office and meeting space on storey 1-5 
somewhat complemented by the first exhaust fan system for the toilet, pantry and postal space. 
 
The heating/cooling system is rather similar for storey 1-5 and slightly different for storey 6 and 
the atrium space. Nevertheless, the shared primary energy source for heating and cooling are district 
heating and district cooling respectively. The differences are mostly found on the secondary energy 
source level where the office and meeting space on storey 1-5 are heated by means of wall attached 
radiators (mainly radiation but also convection) placed below the windows and cooled through 
suspended ceiling mounted active chilled beams (mainly convection but also radiation). These 
active chilled beams are integrated with the supply air terminal devices in the sense that incoming 
supply air is cooled down by them whenever necessary. 
 
The control and regulation for ventilation, heating, cooling and lighting are based on two systems 
AS1 (plant room) and AS2 (fans room) which are placed in the underground basement and plan 6 
respectively. AS1 has 4 units that are used to serves objects on plan 0-6 including inlets and 
distribution of hot and cold water, time-constraints for the lighting, control of both the air handling 
units (relevant for the office and meeting space on plan 2-5) and fans for extracting the polluted 
air (not relevant for the office and meeting space on plan 2-5). AS2 has 2 units that are used to 
serves objects on plan 6 including room regulation (three in total) for plan 6 and fans for extracting 
the polluted air (three in total) with two of these fans being relevant for plan 1-5. The first one 
handles the extraction of air for the office and meeting space through the atrium for storey 1-5 and 
==PAGE:34==
34 
 
the second one handles the extraction of air through exhaust ATD placed in the toilet, pantry and 
postal area on storey 1-6. 
 
In general, most of the flows (whether containing water or air) and their respective distribution 
channels are constant and regulated through measuring devices and PI-regulators controlling 
different valves. The air handling unit for the provision of clean and fresh air to storey 1-5 works 
based on time-constraint schedules (as well as the related fans for extraction of the polluted air). 
There are individual external timers for each storey level opening and closing the provided air based 
upon two schedules of normal hours and night hours (night cooling). The air that is provided is 
always constant (CAV) regulated by PI-regulators that control valves of cooling, heat-recovery and 
heating in sequence so that the desired setpoints are achieved. The pressure of the air that is 
provided is also kept at constant pressure by PI-regulators that control frequency converters 
making sure that the desired setpoints are achieved. For the different pumps, there is a complex 
control system based upon being constant with PI-regulators controlling different valves and 
making sure that the free cold, heat recovery and heat circulation all function well together. 
 
The valves for heating and cooling on storey 2-5 are controlled in a similar fashion. If the floor 
plan is not occupied the valve for cooling is blocked for that specific storey. However, the air flow 
is still provided for ventilation purposes to that specific storey. The default mode for controlling 
the air temperature on each storey is rather straightforward divided into two types both controlled 
by P-regulators controlling the valves of heating (for the radiators) and cooling (for the active 
chilled beams) in sequence. For the room air temperature in specific rooms the default is the case 
if no adjustments are made. However, the occupants have the possibility to adjust both the heating 
and cooling (still in sequence) manually if wanted to do so. For the landscape space (and other 
remaining areas) the average value of these areas is used to provide the desired air temperature. 
That is, for storey 2-5 air temperature is the only measurement used to control the valves for 
heating and cooling through P-regulators in sequence. 
 
Nevertheless, the vertical distribution that is connected to water based radiators horizontally on 
storey 1-5 (each with separate heat valves) is kept constant and controlled by PI-regulators so that 
desired setpoints are reached. For the cooling of the air that is located inside the supply ATD 
horizontally through means of water-based active chilled beams the vertical distribution is kept 
constant and controlled by PI-regulators that control two cold valves in sequence (the first from 
circulating free cooling and the second from the primary cooling) for storey 1-5 so that desired 
==PAGE:35==
35 
 
setpoints are reached. However, it should be stated that for the cooling of the active chilled beams 
there is an additional adjustment that can be made based upon a measurement that is placed in the 
distribution duct for extract polluted air through the fan for handling toilet, pantry and postal areas 
on storey 1-6. It is an absolute humidifier that can shift the setpoint (0-5°C) depending on the 
extracting airs humidity/temperature content. 
 
Regarding the lighting it is rather straightforward. In the morning, it is manually turned on by the 
occupants and during the night it closes automatically at 19.30. For the windows, there are internal 
blinds that need to be manually closed to decrease the level of sunlight entering the building. All 
windows are non-openable except for the ones in the single office rooms on each storey which can 
be partly opened on the upper side of the window. Finally, there are also passive chilled beams in 
the suspended ceiling of the passage space, on each storey, near the atrium being automatically 
controlled with the aim of sustaining an average air temperature level by means of cooling the rising 
warm air due to buoyancy forces. 
 
3.3 Storey five 
The important areas to highlight on storey level 5 are the gross floor area and net floor area which 
are 731.8m2 and 694.4m2 respectively. The net room height is 2.8m and the gross room height 
(including suspended ceiling and floor deck) is 3.66m. The suspended ceiling is 0.52m and the floor

4.1.4 Energy and ventilation concepts 
The energy and ventilation concepts for storey level 5 are rather straightforward to grasp. The key 
idea of the energy concept is that the radiators should act as the main source of heating (by means 
of radiation primarily and convection secondarily) and the active and passive chilled beams as the 
main source of cooling (by means of convection primarily and radiation secondarily). All systems 
for heating and cooling are water based and connected to primary energy sources of district heating 
and district cooling respectively. The pipework for both heating and cooling of floor plan 5 passes 
through the suspended ceiling and for the radiators (placed under the windows near the different 
facades) the pipework continues vertically through the internal walls nearest the façade areas. All 
windows are non-openable except for the ones in the single office space areas which can be 
manually opened on the upper side with approximately 15% of the glass area. All windows have 
manually adjustable internal blinds and the placement of the radiators under the windows make 
sure that internal cold convection is avoided near the window space areas during the winter season. 
Finally, the daylight factor has obviously been considered by placing as much office space as 
possible near the windows and having rather large windows. 
 
The ventilation concept is designed in such way that one can with great certainty state that it is 
based on the principals of mixed ventilation (or mixed air distribution pattern) at least in the office 
space areas. That is, the supply ventilation airflow (colder than existing room air) and the direction 
of its flow (evenly distributed on the long sides of the active chilled beams towards the suspended 
ceiling) ensures that the incoming ventilation airflow gradually falls around the occupant’s 
workstation (or local environment) and continues to rise from the feet level passing the head level 
and onwards to the suspended roof level. That is, thermal plumbs are created due to temperature 
differences between the bottom and top levels and the released heat from the occupants 
themselves. This behaviour is applicable for both cases of supplied ventilation air without cooling 
==PAGE:43==
43 
 
as well as supplied ventilation air with cooling since the incoming stream of air will always be lower 
temperature wise than the existing room air. 
 
The idea appears to be that the mixed ventilation air from the office space areas should continue 
its path through the different passages (on all height levels but mostly nearest the suspended ceiling) 
and exit the floor plan through the atrium space. For the meeting space areas, the situation is slightly 
different since the incoming stream of supply air can be cooled as well as heated. For the cooled 
case, it would create similar behaviour as for the office space areas. Nevertheless, for the heated 
case, it can be assumed that the incoming warm air is forced down to lower levels through higher 
airflow levels appropriately directed, which is possible due to the variable air volume capability of 
the air terminal devices in the meeting rooms. The existing room air in the meeting rooms is 
supposed to find its way to the atrium space for extraction by means of passing the passage areas 
through transfer units placed on the suspended ceiling level in both the meeting and passage space 
areas.

4.4.1 Existing building 
The interviewees started by recognizing that the principal dilemma currently prevailing is that the 
existing office building is not working (or functioning) as it was initially thought with regards to 
parameters such as the design layout, technical systems, control systems and similar. They 
continued by expressing the need for a completely different solution for the existing office building. 
They gave the single office rooms as examples stating that the current settings with the radiators 
near the windows, thermal plumb (created by the occupant) and placement of the active chilled 
beams in the suspended ceiling creates a flow that makes it very warm in these office spaces due to 
the cooling never reaching the occupant zone properly.  
 
Ironically, one of the interviewees stated that he would ideally prefer to start over from scratch and 
design the ventilation system accordingly but simultaneously recognized that this may not be the 
most economically feasible way forward. In a nutshell, the existing design layout of having open 
landscape office space areas connected to a ventilation system design originally created for single 
office space areas is not appropriate and does not work properly. 
 
==PAGE:61==

4.4.2 Personalized ventilation 
In general, both interviewees appeared to be curious about the innovative solutions that 
personalized ventilation can bring to the table. However, they were still very clear in their 
expressing of their standpoint towards implementing personalized ventilation in practice. That is, 
a standpoint leaning more towards the sceptical spectrum due to several practical and psychological 
issues related to personalized ventilation. Mostly, the parameters contributing to this sceptical 
standpoint can be found in the technical challenges of integrating personalized ventilation into the 
office environment in functional, appealing and flexible ways. One of the interviewees explained 
that he had experienced rather similar attempts in practice that have ended up not working properly 
(not working 100%) when taking them into operation. 
 
The interviewees continued by highlighting the flexibility aspects. They explained that the latest 
trend in office buildings in some parts of Sweden is going towards having flexible workstations in 
open landscape office spaces. That is, most employees do not have fixed workstations and instead 
take a seat on whatever workstation they find available and un-occupied. The discussion continued 
by relating this trend to the existing office building in Malmö occupied by Tyréns. The interviewees 
stated that currently they have also followed the trend by going from single office room space to 
landscape office space with many occupants seated together in an open environment. Nevertheless, 
they believed that this trend will turn for them within the nearest future. They also stated that when 
the extension building part is finalized in 2018 they will not be sitting as many people on floor plan 
5 as they are doing today. 
 
The whole point of this discussion is to highlight the need for flexibility when considering the 
ventilation system design and if personalized ventilation is ever to be successfully implemented it 
needs to be design in such a way to be able to accommodate these potential changes. For instance, 
adding/removing workstations and/or adding/removing internal walls. Or as Daniel Sörensson 
explains it:  
“when we are designing office space areas for clients, the flexibility matter consume 
large portions of the dialogues. For instance, enabling flexibility by creating office 
room space in small modules with specific fixed dimensions or active chilled beams 
with specific fixed dimensions.” 
 
When asked about whether the importance of flexibility is limited to changes in the existing office 
environment (single space to landscape space and vice versa) or changes for the whole building 
==PAGE:62==
62 
 
complex such as turning an office building into a retail building. The interviewees explained that 
the former is often the case and matters related to changing an existing office building to some 
other type of building are of much less importance. Rather, if parts of the existing office building 
need to be rented out to another organisation. Then it is common to make changes in the design 
layout by adding or removing internal walls, stairwells and similar and this needs to be accounted 
for when considering the ventilation system design. 
 
Another major issue highlighted as very important for the ventilation system design including 
personalized ventilation is the psychological aspect. That is, both interviewees agreed that 
ventilation always has a big role to play in the work environment. Everyone has their own opinion 
toward (or perception of) the indoor environment and existing ventilation system design. And this 
is only natural and something to expect since human-beings by nature are different. Or as Daniel 
Sörensson explains it:  
“I can be seated on a particular workstation and find the indoor environment to be 
nearly perfect, whereas if Magnus was seated there he would probably experience 
draught and find it too cold and vice versa. There is so much thinking and feeling and 
this makes it all harder to cope with during the design of ventilation systems.” 
 
The dialogue continues with Magnus Wilén giving an example concerning the decision-makers: 
“some property owners and/or clients can have many opinions and feelings that need 
to be taken into consideration. Such as, clearly expressing that they do not want chilled 
beams in the suspended ceiling due to the potential risk of creating rains of cold. In 
other words, sometimes thinking and taste govern the decision-making process rather 
than pure facts about flows and temperatures.” 
 
The interviewees further agreed that the belief of having certain devices or options available can 
sometimes be enough without the occupants actually utilizing these devices or options in practice. 
This was considered to shed some further light on the psychological aspect (beyond the individual 
preferences) towards different indoor environments and similar. The dialogue continued towards 
open landscape office space (without the possibility of controlling the local environment) with the 
interviewees stating that in these landscape offices you can typically expect around half the 
occupants to find it too cold and the other half to find it too warm. Here, it would probably be 
beneficial to have personalized ventilation in the sense that the psychological needs (among 
different occupants) would more likely lean towards being satisfied. However, both interviewees 
==PAGE:63==
63 
 
acknowledged their limited knowledge in this particular area, stating that people may change their 
opinion and preference with time (as personalized ventilation has been operating for a while) and 
this could potentially alter the psychological aspect (such as perception) of the occupants toward 
personalized ventilation for better or worse. 
 
After the rather fruitful dialogue about personalized ventilation at individual, group and building 
levels the dialogue continued with the interviewer asking questions aimed at understanding the 
viewpoint of the Swedish construction industry towards innovative solutions such as personalized 
ventilation. Both interviewees appeared to agree that the general viewpoint in Sweden is rather 
sceptical towards technical solutions such as personalized ventilation. The reasons for this common 
belief seem to be diverse in nature. Beyond the already stated concern about matters such as 
flexibility and psychological aspects, both interviewees think that many people may be sceptical 
toward having technical devices (or tools) located next to them. It may look strange or even feel 
strange. Then, there is also the fact that there have not been enough practical cases in Sweden 
implementing innovative solutions such as personalized ventilation and this could probably 
contribute to the existing perception of personalized ventilation being somewhat unnecessary. 
 
The viewpoint of the main contractors (turnkey contracts being superior) in the Swedish 
construction industry should also be highlighted according to the interviewees. That is, these actors 
usually aim to provide construction projects as cheap as possible not to mention the ambitions of 
the property owners. Or as Daniel Sörensson explains it: 
“we have an industry that is rather conservative in this light. As a property owner, you 
do not build with the purpose of owning the property for 25 years. Instead, you build 
with the purpose of selling it within a 5-year period. This makes it more important that 
everything is working properly for 5 years (until the due date for selling) rather than 
for 25 years. This could also be a contributing factor for not having built properties 
with personalized ventilation and similar innovative solutions. The focus is rather on 
satisfying the basic requirements stated in the Swedish regulation for construction 
projects (Boverket Building Regulations BBR) with regards to airflows and similar to 
find the lowest (or cheapest) common denominator. Hence, the general hesitation 
towards personalized ventilation. Partly due to the common belief that it leads to more 
expensive buildings and partly due to the common belief that it leads to larger 
aggregates, additional flows and similar.” 
==PAGE:64==
64 
 
The interviewer continued by giving a hypothetical case asking whether proving that personalized 
ventilation leads to lower energy costs, satisfied occupants with the indoor environment, 
functioning regulation of the local environment and airflow interaction between personalized and 
background airflows, would lead to greater interest for it in the Swedish construction industry. The 
short response from the interviewees is that some would be interested but far from all. This 
probably due to the previously mentioned issue of main contractors wanting to build at the 
cheapest price possible. 
 
On the other hand, if one could integrate personalized ventilation or some of its aspects into well-
established environmental certification systems for buildings such as BREEAM or the Swedish 
certification system “Miljöbyggnad”. Then, it may receive more attention from the key actors in 
the construction industry due to being part of a recognized system for assessing buildings and the 
associated formal certificate that comes with it. In this way, personalized ventilation could act as a 
parameter for receiving higher scores in the different environmental classification systems rather 
than being a requirement. This connection is much more likely to lead to interest for the energy-
saving potential of personalized ventilation than purely showing its energy-saving potential on 
stand-alone basis. It would probably also facilitate its entrance to the marketplace the interviewees 
believed. 
 
4.4.3 Proposed case 
The discussion started with the interviewees expressing their opinion of the choice of floor plan to 
simulate. In their opinion, it would be better to have chosen floor plan 3 or 4 instead of floor plan 
5. The reasoning behind this has its ground in the psychological aspect of preconceptions. That is, 
most of the current occupants on storey level 5 are technical consultants working with ventilation 
and indoor environment which makes Daniel Sörensson believe that:  
“this is not a good reference group. Around half the occupants will probably be 
negative towards personalized ventilation. The other half will probably be curious and 
some few ones will probably never acknowledge the concept of personalized 
ventilation regardless of whatever you do. That is, since they are already working with 
it daily in the construction industry they are probably biased. It would make more sense 
to have floor plan 4 as a reference group, for instance, where structural engineers, 
project managers and similar are seated. Do they experience that the ventilation gets 
better or not? If you try it on floor plan 5 some may say it did not get any better even 
though it actually did improve.”

It goes without saying that at this stage, it is not possible to accommodate the suggested advice 
above from the interviewees. Hence, the dialogue continued onwards focusing on the proposed 
cases outline previously sent to the HVAC experts. The distinction between aggregate and 
distribution in part 1 and 2 respectively was very well received (see table 1 on page 57 or appendix 
B). In this way, part 1 is mostly focused on producing satisfying airflows and fulfilling different 
requirements making it more convenient to go with a traditional (or standard) solution for these 3 
choices. Part 2, on the other hand, is the one that requires more innovative (or complex) solutions 
for the different distribution parameters (choices available in part 2) such as cooling, heating, 
airflows and air distribution patterns depending on how people are seated. Hence, it makes more 
sense to look at what is going to be decided in part 2 and thereafter choose the corresponding 
settings for part 1. 
 
For part 2, the first major decision to be made is whether to have single or double (coupled) air 
distribution methods. The interviewees appeared to agree that double (coupled) air distribution 
method is the preferred choice. That is, having two separate distribution ductworks on storey level 
5 for the personalized ventilation and background ventilation respectively. This choice was made 
with the purpose of enhancing the control of the complete air circulation in the building rather 
than only for some local parts. This was partly explained by stating that you still have pollutions 
from building materials and equipment that need to be dealt with that may not be in space areas 
constantly occupied by people. 
 
The remaining choice that had to be done was between 4 different cases under the category of air 
distribution methods (double or coupled). The first parameter to be discussed was whether to have 
ventilation, heating and/or cooling for the supplied personalized ventilation airflow. This was a 
rather easy choice for the interviewees stating that ventilation and cooling should be possible 
through the supplied personalized ventilation airflow whereas heating was rather unnecessary. 
Instead, they reasoned that the existing radiators below the windows should be the major source 
for heating the complete space areas (local and background environments) by means of radiation 
(including partly convection) due to the unavoidable requirement of needing to deal with internal 
cold convection near the window space areas during the winter season. 
 
The dialogue continued to focus on whether to have ventilation, heating and/or cooling for the 
supplied background ventilation airflow. In this light, it was rather clear that the preferred choice 
was to have similar settings as for the supplied personalized ventilation airflow. That is, that the 
==PAGE:66==
66 
 
background ventilation should be used for ventilation and cooling purposes as well. The reasoning 
behind this is to facilitate the interaction between the background and local airflows and resulting 
air circulation in the space areas without creating to big extremes in temperature differences. 
Another major reason for supporting this setting for the supplied background ventilation airflow 
is the desire to be able to cool down the complete space areas (local and background environments) 
of the building whenever deemed necessary. At this stage, it is rather obvious that case 2 is the 
preferred choice under the category air distribution methods (double or coupled) as can be seen in 
table 1 on page 57 or appendix B. 
 
For the corresponding three choices, in part 1, the preferred method for placement of the air 
terminal devices (or supply diffusers) is to keep the current settings of having the ductwork 
horizontally passing the suspended ceiling and then vertically for each zone area continue down (in 
a visible fashion) to distribute supplied personalized ventilation airflows to several workstations. 
The other two options did not make any sense for the office building in question in their option. 
To change the pipework (waterflows) and ductwork (airflows) from the suspended ceiling to the 
floor deck would not only be unnecessarily expensive with regards to the current placement of 
having these inside the suspended ceiling. But it would also complicate future changes in the floor 
deck which is a rather common amendment to expect in office buildings. 
 
Moreover, the direction of airflows from the middle of active chilled beams has been partly tested 
in practice and sometimes leads to complications such as draught. Simply stated, they believe that 
visible ductworks vertically showing people that the personalized ventilation existed in symbolic 
terms also could contribute in enhancing the psychological aspects in a positive manner. Or as 
Magnus Wilén explains it: “sometimes the belief that you can change something or knowledge of 
it can be more than enough to satisfy some people”. Both interviewees recognized that the design 
aspect may be affected in a negative manner when having ductworks vertically inside the occupant 
zone for the purposes of supplying personalized ventilation airflows. Nevertheless, they still 
believed the positive manners from the psychological aspects would outweigh the design issue to 
a certain extent. Magnus Wilén also mentioned an interesting solution applicable for other office 
buildings than the one in question: 
“we are not doing it here, but at some companies they have several pipes for electricity 
and computer wires coming down from the suspended ceiling in the middle area of 
the zone for a number of workstations. Here, a potential solution could be to integrate 
the personalized ventilation ductworks with these already existing pipes.”

For the next choice in line the focus was on whether to complement the exhaust fan systems 
with additional local exhaust air terminal devices for each occupant, several occupants in a 
zone area or keep it as it is without any local exhaust devices. The preferred choice here was 
rather straightforward. That is, to keep the settings as they are now without any additional 
local exhaust devices. In case, the air circulation and passage from the office space areas to 
the atrium space show a need for local exhaust devices. Then, the solution with local exhaust 
devices (placed at the suspended ceiling level) for zones with several occupants would be the 
preferred choice. This potential dilemma with changes in the air circulation and passage 
between different space areas was also highlighted as a reason for creating concerns and 
sceptical views toward additional personalized ventilation flows among professionals in the 
Swedish HVAC industry. 
 
For the final choice of having one or two separate air handling units (or aggregates) for the 
personalized ventilation and background ventilation, the choice was rather straightforward. That 
is, having two separate air handling units (both located in the basement level) with one for the 
personalized ventilation and one for the background ventilation was the preferred choice. The 
reasoning behind this was that it makes it easier to control different parameters related to the 
respective airflows for personalized ventilation and background ventilation on each floor plan and 
thereby creates more flexibility for the overall ventilation system design. 
 
Moreover, on each storey level it was preferred to have variable air volumes for the personalized 
air flows and constant air volumes (as the current settings) for the background ventilation airflows. 
In case, it does not work well with constant air volumes for the background ventilation airflows, 
variable air volumes were suggested as an alternative method for creating more harmonic 
interactions between the different airflows and resulting air circulation. Finally, the direction of the 
supplied personalized ventilation airflow was also considered to be very important mainly due to 
the psychological aspect.

 Reducing the supply outdoor airflow leads to greater energy-savings 
 Reducing the supply outdoor air temperature leads to greater energy-savings 
 Decreasing the occupant density leads to greater energy-savings 
 Decreasing the occupant profile leads to greater energy-savings 
 Allowing higher maximum room air temperature in the background environment leads to 
greater energy-savings

4.7 Simulation: Boundary conditions 
For the investigations in both simulation tools boundary conditions exist. That is, limitations to 
how the different investigations are carried out. For IDA ICE, simply stated, there exist only 2 
models: one model representing the existing conditions with the current ventilation system design 
and its corresponding settings. Another model representing the newly proposed ventilation system 
design and its corresponding settings. The yearly energy consumption per m2 floor area is compared 
for these two models by means of changing several parameters. Nevertheless, deeper analysis of 
the reasons for getting different yearly energy consumption whenever changes are made in the two 
models are not going to be pursued or presented for that matter from the IDA ICE output results 
(such as variable comparisons among zones). Instead, the focus is on stating the observations made 
in the output results from making changes to the different simulation models. Finally, for the 
indoor environment related aspects operative temperature (Top), PPD, CO2 and RH levels are 
presented from the IDA ICE output results whereas aspects related to daylight, lighting, acoustics 
and similar are overlooked. 
 
For the air distribution pattern simulation works done in FloVent there also exist several boundary 
conditions. Major one being that steady-state conditions are used with summer design criteria as 
the primary means of investigation. Four models are created in FloVent, two representing the single 
office space lettered B and two representing the landscape office space lettered F, as shown in 
figure 1 on page 35. These locations have been chosen in dialogue with the HVAC experts at 
Tyréns during the interview stage and found to be the most interesting ones for comparison 
between the existing and proposed ventilation system designs. For the indoor environment related 
aspect, air jets, speed, operative temperature, turbulence intensity, draught rate, PMV, PPD and 
ventilation effectiveness are presented from the calculations made based upon the FloVent output 
results. Notice, that the indoor environment aspects are looked at twice from different angles. That 
is, once in IDA ICE and once in FloVent. However, the output results in FloVent are considered 
more valid particularly for the thermal comfort (PMV and PPD) and IAQ (ventilation 
==PAGE:71==

effectiveness) due to inherited limits in IDA ICE for accurately investigating air distribution 
pattern. 
 
4.8 Simulation: Methodology 
The simulation works done in the different simulation tools are perceived independently from one 
another. Simulations are first carried out in IDA ICE (version 4.7.1) primarily for energy purposes 
followed by simulations in FloVent (version 11.3) primarily for air distribution purposes. As 
previously mentioned in section “2.9 Study design” FloVent is approached as a complementary 
tool due to the core research focus of this report of evaluating the energy usage in office buildings 
for different ventilation system designs. Nonetheless, for both models the same input values are 
used (as much as possible) such as heat loads from occupants, equipment and lighting as well as 
pollution levels in the room air. 
 
In general, many assumptions have been made whenever information is not available with regards 
to fulfilment of the current requirements in Boverkets Building Regulations (BBR) for amendments 
in existing office buildings. For some space areas, key values such as U-values and precise material 
information has been available and used accordingly. For the creation of the IDA ICE models an 
Autodesk Revit 2011 model has been provided from Tyréns with details regarding different 
geometry sizes. This Revit model from 2011 was updated to function in Autodesk Revit 2017 and 
thereafter extracted to the Industry Foundation Classes (IFC) format following the guidelines 
stated in EQUA Simulation (2017). This IFC file was further processed in SimpleBim 6 using the 
add-on tool for IDA ICE and the guidelines stated in Datacubist (2017). Finally, the processed IFC 
file was imported to IDA ICE 4.7.1 using the guidelines stated in EQUA Simulation2 (2017). 
Regarding the models in FloVent 11.3, these were created from scratch using geometry sizes and 
other relevant details from the Autodesk Revit 2017 model and provided drawings from Tyréns. 
 
Moreover, the two models for the existing and proposed cases created in IDA ICE are identical in 
all aspects except for the ones being tested such as placement of air terminal devices, airflow levels 
and number of air handling units. This will be further elaborated under the relevant section (5.2 
Settings for inputs). The same applies for the FloVent models. That is, the two single office room 
models for the existing and proposed cases as well as the two landscape office space models are 
identical to each other except for the placement of air terminal devices, airflow levels and similar. 
This will be further elaborated under the relevant section (6.2 Settings for inputs). 
 
==PAGE:72==
72 
 
Finally, the investigation approach for IDA ICE simulations is threefold and rather straightforward. 
That is, investigate the parameters directly linked to the 5 specific hypotheses for IDA ICE (first 
agenda), parameter variation of several other parameters (second agenda) and optimize the model 
to fulfil the requirements in BBR and EN 15251 (Class 2) if possible (third agenda). For the FloVent 
simulations, the investigation approach is onefold and rather straightforward. That is, investigate 
several indoor environment related parameters including the ones directly linked to the 2 specific 
hypotheses for FloVent (only agenda). Notice, that for all simulations in IDA ICE and FloVent 
the agendas are applied for both models (existing and proposed case) and later compared among 
each other. Except for the energy optimization part (third agenda) which only apply for the 
proposed case and only shows yearly energy consumption.

5.2 Settings for inputs 
The initial idea was to simulate storey level 5 as shown in figure 17 on page 67. However, this 
proved to be nearly impossible and extremely time consuming due to several complex details that 
IDA ICE needed to account for with regards to the indoor environment. 
 
That is, instead of using zones that are completely leaking through their internal walls (which would 
more realistically account for air distribution mixtures) and having supply air terminal devices solely 
in the office and meeting space areas with total constant volume flow 885 l/s on average. As well 
as, exhaust air terminal devices solely in the toilet, pantry, postal and passage space areas (total 
constant volume flow 240 l/s) and extraction through the south façade walls near the atrium space 
(total constant volume flow 885 l/s). It was decided to completely ignore both configurations by 
keeping the internal walls for each separate zone and assuming that supply and exhaust ATD are 
placed locally in each specific zone. 
 
Other important assumptions made are to treat the existing space areas for toilet, pantry, postal 
and meeting rooms as passage space represented by four “passage” zones. In which, each one of 
these zones has total supply and exhaust airflow of 50.25 l/s respectively (representing the 
previously mentioned supply airflow of the meeting rooms 205 l/s). For the “occupied” office 
zones the supply airflow is kept at 20 l/s and the exhaust airflow is assumed to be 20 l/s for each 
zone, making a total of 680 l/s for the supply and exhaust airflows respectively. Additionally, the 
partly openable external windows (mostly found in the single space areas) are assumed to be closed 
always. 
 
In total, two models have been created in IDA ICE: one for the existing design configuration and 
one for the proposed design configuration. Both models have identical input settings as will be 
shown shortly. However, they differ in a few aspects which are the number of zones, control system 
for the indoor environment, allocated internal loads for each specific zone with regards to 
occupancy, equipment and lighting as well as number of air handling units (the existing design 
layout has one AHU for all space areas whereas the proposed design layout has two separate AHU 
for the office and passage spaces respectively). 
 
Moreover, the common settings for the simulations in IDA ICE are introduced below (as well as 
in appendix D) followed by the specific settings for each of the two models: one for the existing 
design layout (see appendix E for further details) and one for the proposed design layout (see

appendix F for further details). The common settings are mostly focused around general settings

5.3 Measuring points 
The measuring points (or parameter variations) are several during the IDA ICE simulations. For 
each parameter variation, it has been applied on both models on equal terms to see how it affects 
each model separately and create basis for comparisons. The goal is to find the most optimized 
case from an energy perspective. That is, the yearly energy consumption of the building (kWh/m2 
floor area). This is the primary deciding parameter for the optimized case whereas all other 
parameters are considered sufficient if they pass the minimum requirements for class 2. 
 
In tables 5-7, on the upcoming pages, the parameter variations are shown in rectangles representing 
columns and their corresponding rows. Each column (for instance case 1) and row (for instance 
variation A) combination represents an independent and separate simulation (for instance case 1 
with variation A). Whenever more than one parameter has been change during the running of 
energy simulations this will be further illustrated by having larger rectangles with two or more 
columns including their corresponding rows. The parameter values that are stated under the 
column “reference case” are referring to the initial values of the first two models created using class 
2 setpoints for the existing and proposed case respectively. Notice, that the information in the three 
tables (5-7) corresponds to the parameter variations for the three agendas. That is, table 5 shows 
parameter variations for investigating the five specific hypotheses (both models), table 6 show 
parameter variations for several other parameters (both models) and table 7 shows parameter 
variations (iterations) for the optimization of the proposed case model. This table (7) only presents 
yearly energy consumption and has different principles regarding its methodology. That is, 13 
iterations are done in total starting with 1 and ending with 13. In this process decisions are made. 
Whenever it is written No it means that the previous iteration has been neglected and whenever it 
is written Yes it means that the results of the previous iteration are built upon for the upcoming 
iteration. 
 
Finally, the output results from the energy simulations are not further processed since IDA ICE 
has built-in options of generating indoor environment results with calculated values for important 
factors such as operative temperature (Top), number of hours above certain operative temperatures, 
PPD, CO2 and RH levels based on the calculation principles presented in the European standard 
EN 15251:2007 (Swedish Standards Institute [SIS], 2007).

5.5 Optimized design for energy performance  
Based on the output results from the IDA ICE simulations the models are compared in this section 
in general terms starting with the five hypotheses, followed by the other parameters and energy 
optimization of the proposed design. This section ends with stating whether the proposed design 
with personalized ventilation, overall, has better energy-savings potential than the existing design. 
As well as, highlighting the possible impact of some few and small errors made. 
 
From the first results showing the comparison between the existing and proposed design it clearly 
shows that the proposed design outperforms the existing one with 10.1 kWh/m2 floor area and 
year. Hence, supporting the proposed design but only with small differences. The first hypotheses 
stated that reducing the supply outdoor airflow leads to greater energy-savings. For both the 
existing and proposed cases this hypothesis is clearly not supported. The highest increase in yearly 
energy consumption for the two variations (A and C) reducing the airflows in different spaces are 
9.4 kWh for the existing design and 7.5 kWh for the proposed design. For the highest increase in 
yearly energy consumption for the other two variations (B and D) for increasing the airflows in the 
different spaces. The values are 69.1 kWh for the existing design and 17.9 kWh for the proposed 
design. That is, the existing design is more sensitive to changes in the supply outdoor airflow than 
the proposed design. Only support for the existing design is variation A which should a small 
decrease of 0.6 kWh for the existing design. However, this could be a statistical error with regards 
to the values of all the other simulations (8 in total) done for this hypothesis. 
 
The second hypotheses stated that reducing the supply outdoor temperature leads to greater 
energy-savings. For both the existing and proposed cases this hypothesis is clearly not supported. 
The highest increase in yearly energy consumption for the two variations (E and G) reducing the 
air temperature in different spaces are 14.1 kWh for the existing design and 7 kWh for the proposed 
design. For the highest increase in yearly energy consumption for the last variation (F) for 
increasing the air temperature in the different spaces. The values are 3.2 kWh for the existing design 
and 2.7 kWh for the proposed design. Strangely enough, even an increase of the supply outdoor 
temperature leads to higher yearly energy consumption. However, since the results values for the 
decreasing of the air temperature are higher this hypothesis still stays unsupported. Finally, the 
existing design is more sensitive to changes in the supply outdoor temperature than the proposed 
design based on the results from these simulations (6 in total). 
 
==PAGE:107==

The third hypotheses stated that decreasing the occupant density leads to greater energy-savings. 
For both the existing and proposed cases this hypothesis is clearly not supported. The highest 
increase in yearly energy consumption for the variation (H) for decreasing the occupant density is 
1.9 kWh for the existing design and 0.2 kWh for the proposed design. This shows that the existing 
design is more sensitive to changes in the occupant density than the proposed design based on the 
results from these simulations (2 in total). 
 
The fourth hypotheses stated that decreasing the occupant profile leads to greater energy-savings. 
For both the existing and proposed cases this hypothesis is partly supported. For the decrease in

the proposed design stayed the same at 109.6 kWh. This shows that the existing design is more 
sensitive to changes in the occupant profile than the proposed design based on the results from 
these simulations (4 in total). 
 
The fifth hypotheses stated that allowing higher maximum room air temperature in the background 
environment leads to greater energy-savings. For both the existing and proposed cases this 
hypothesis is not supported. As shown in the results from variation K the yearly energy 
consumption increased for the existing and proposed designs with 3.7 kWh and 7 kWh respectively. 
This shows that the proposed design is more sensitive to changes in the air temperature of the 
background environment based on the results from these simulations (2 in total). 
 
For the other parameters detailed analysis are not going to be presented. Instead, two things are 
looked at. Which of the two models had the most number of increase/decrease based on the results 
from variables AA-EE (case 1-4, 10 simulations in total) and what was the single highest value for 
the increase/decrease from the results from all variables. This should in theory show which one of 
the two models simulated is more sensitive to changes in different parameters. For the existing 
design, the number of increase are 3 (highest increase 7.2 kWh) and the number of decrease are 2 
(highest decrease 15.6 kWh). For the proposed design, the number of increase are 2 (highest 
increase 45 kWh) and the number of decrease are 3 (highest decrease 15 kWh).  
 
This shows that the existing design is more leaning towards increasing in kWh whereas the opposite 
is true for the proposed design. However, it also shows that the changes are more extreme for the

makes it hard to establish which design is more sensitive to changes than stating the obvious that

6.3 Measuring points 
The measuring (monitoring) points for the FloVent simulations are based on the definition of the 
occupant zone made in the national regulations for constructing buildings in Sweden: Boverkets 
Byggregler [BBR] (2016) and the European Committee for Standardization [CEN] (1998). That is, 
the occupant zone is defined as being everything located inside the space area limited by certain 
horizontal and vertical planes. The first horizontal plane is 0.1m above the floor and the second is 
2m above the floor and everything between these two planes is part of the occupant zone. For the 
vertical planes, the following are excluded from the occupant zone: space areas 0.6m from internal 
walls and external walls (without openings) as well as 1m from windows and external walls (with 
openings such as windows or doors). 
 
Monitoring points are placed in all corners of the occupant zone, some middle areas of the 
occupant zone and next to occupants. The measures are made at five heights for each monitoring 
==PAGE:121==
121 
 
point: 0.1m, 0.6m, 1.1m, 1.7m and 2m. Vertical air temperature differences are usually compared 
between ankles and head. For a seated person, these are represented by the 0.1m and 1.1m heights 
respectively. For a standing person, the 0.6m and 1.7m are often used to represent the ankles and 
head respectively. The main parameters measured in the FloVent simulations are the air 
temperature and speed/velocity at all five heights and containment/pollution at two heights (1.1m 
and 1.7m). For the exact placement of the monitoring points for each model see figure 32-33. 
 
Finally, the results from the CFD simulations are used to calculate temperature differences in 
height, speed differences in height, turbulence intensity, draught rate, PMV, PPD and ventilation 
effectiveness using the calculation methods in DTU Byg2 (2016) and European Committee for 
Standardization [CEN] (1998). See appendix H for an overview of the calculations.

6.5 Optimized design for indoor environment 
Based on the output results from the FloVent simulations including the corresponding calculations 
the models are compared in this section on equal basis. That is, the single office models for the 
existing case (called EC onwards) and proposed case (called PC onwards) are compared with each 
other and the landscape office models for the existing and proposed cases are compared with each 
other. This section ends with stating whether the two hypotheses made for the CFD simulations 
are supported. 
 
For the speeds (including air jets) in the single office it appears that both EC and PC passes the 
maximum requirement in BBR at 0.25 m/s as well as the requirements for class 2 in the standard 
at 0.19 m/s. For the landscape office, it appears that that the both landscape offices EC and PC 
pass the requirements in BBR and class (B). The landscape PC even passes the requirements in 
class 1 (A) in the standard at 0.12 m/s. However, there are some indication for higher speeds than 
0.19 m/s but lower than 0.25 m/s for both the single PC and landscape PC when looking at these 
form an air jets perspective. One should not forget that these speeds can be lowered in the PC 
scenario which is the whole purpose of personalized ventilation so in theory this should not be a 
major concern. However, the consequences based on further investigations in FloVent by means 
of sensitivity analysis has obviously not been made to support this statement in a quantitative 
manner. 
 
For the temperatures in the single office it appears that both EC and PC pass the requirements for 
vertical temperature differences between head (1.1m) and ankles (0.1m) which should be less than 
3°C. For the standard criteria, it appears that EC performs slightly better in this light with all 
measured points passing the criteria for class 2 (maximum 26°C) except for one point. PC, on the 
other hand, only passes the class 2 criteria at four measured points. However, the remaining 
measured points in PC pass the criteria for class 3 (maximum 27°C). For the landscape office both 
EC and PC pass the requirements for vertical temperature differences. For the standard criteria, 
PC performs much better with most of the measured points at the seated level (0.1-1.1m) pass the 
requirement for criteria 2 and the remaining points above 1.1m passing criteria 3. EC, on the other 
hand does not pass criteria 2 at any measured point but passes the criteria 3 for all measured points. 
This gives mixing signals since the temperature performance is better for the PC in the landscape 
office space and vice versa in the single office room. Further investigations are needed to figure 
out the reasons for this observed behaviour. 
 
==PAGE:144==
144 
 
For the turbulence intensity in the single office it appears that EC has reasonable values with 
regards to the recommended level for mixing ventilation at 40% (range between 30-60%) and the 
same applies for the PC with regards to having values below those for mixing ventilation. However, 
the PC values are ranging from 19-42% with the average around 28% (preferred value for 
displacement ventilation is 20%). For the landscape office, it appears that EC also has reasonable 
values with regards to the recommended level for mixing ventilation. However, for the landscape

7.1 Literature review, occupant survey and experts interview 
In the literature review it was stated that personalized ventilation has been around since the 1980s 
and that it had not been widely used in practice due to several reasons such as perception that it 
leads to higher costs and similar. From looking at the combined results from the occupant survey 
and experts interview, this clearly seems to be the case. That is, 61.8% were not familiar with the 
concept of PV, 11.8% answered maybe and 26.5% answered yes. 
 
The attitude among the occupants based on whether they believed it should be implemented at 
their workspace seems to be rather positive and negative at the same time. That is, almost half of 
the occupant (that choose to answer this optional question) answered that they had a positive 
attitude towards implementing PV at their current workplace stating things such as “could be 
interesting to try out! Innovation pushes the boundaries” and “POSITIVE” in capital letters. The 
other half that was rather sceptical stated things such as “I think it sounds unnecessary and 
expensive. The thermal environment could be fixed with regulated adjustment” and “my attitude 
is thanks, but no thanks. Better solutions are available”. This is rather interesting since both the 
HVAC experts during the interview stage mentioned something similar. That the psychological 
aspect has a large impact on how people approach new concepts such as PV and in fact went so 
far as to say that around half of them will probably be positive and the other half negative which

also turned out to be the case (notice that the interviewees have not seen the results from the 
occupant survey).  
 
Another important aspect mentioned in the literature review which also seems to be supported in 
this particular office building is that indoor environment problems are real, present and we as 
human-beings should put greater attention to them. That is, the literature review shows many times 
that several studies have been conducted all around the world showing that people are not satisfying 
with the overall thermal environment and indoor air quality. Well, this research study supports this 
claim. From the occupant survey, it was shown that only 20.6% responded that the indoor air 
quality was “clearly acceptable” whereas 44.1% responded that it was “just unacceptable” and 
35.3% responded that it was “just acceptable”. For the same question but for the thermal

7.2 Literature review and simulation works 
The first major point to highlight mentioned in the literature review and clearly experience during 
the simulation works is the very fact that the current European standards (the ones used in this 
report as performance requirements) clearly are not accounting for personalized ventilation. That 
is, the criteria that they have for the different categories/classes and way of evaluating the indoor 
environment and air distribution pattern are often based on mixing ventilation. Some statements 
are found focusing on displacement ventilation but very few ones. For the purposes of this research 
report displacement ventilation was considered to be the closest thing to PV and hence assumed 
to be that when looking at the different standards. However, the literature review is simply stated 
RIGHT. There is critical need to develop standards that account for personalized ventilation and 
not just state criteria and methods for evaluating the complete indoor environment as if it was one 
big space used all the times by all occupants. The definition of the occupant zone is great but maybe 
further flexible definitions are needed for this zone than the current ones. 
 
In the literature review it was often shown that PV has the potential to lead to energy-savings but 
with great emphasis on having controlling and regulation systems that fit accordingly to its specific 
settings. Also, it was mentioned that PV is mostly applicable for office buildings that are often 
occupied. That is, occupants seated at their workstations most parts of the day. In this practical 
case study of this research report the office building should be considered an ideal case for the 
purposes of trying out PV. That is, occupants are available at their desk between 07-18 with most 
of them being at least between 08-17 (excluding the lunch hour between 12-13). However, from 
the viewpoint of having an extremely over-occupied case this floor plan probably was not the best 
to simulate to give a fair view of the PV system. 
 
==PAGE:150==
150 
 
Nevertheless, most of the hypotheses made for the energy simulations in IDA ICE proved not to 
be supported. Actually, the only one partly being supported was to decrease the occupant profile 
and that this would lead to greater energy-savings. This is very interesting since most of the 
hypotheses for the energy simulations were made based on the authors understanding of previously 
made studies within the same or similar fields based on the literature review. However, one should 
not forget that the number of energy simulations done in this research report (37 energy simulations 
in total) focusing on several parameters rather than specific ones in depth can be considered partly 
insufficient to give proper answers to the reasons why most of the hypotheses do not support 
claims made in the literature. On the other hand, looking at the comparison between the existing 
and proposed cases respectively based on changes in the yearly energy consumption it was clearly 
shown than in almost all cases the proposed case acted in a more stable fashion. That is, the existing 
case was more sensitive to changes and particularly changes leading to incensements in the yearly 
energy consumption. This does, indeed, indicate that PV can lead to certain energy-savings and if 
not that at least more stable working environments from an energy perspective.  
 
Interestingly enough, flexibility was stated as being a major concern for property developers and 
clients as discussed during the interview stage. However, the flexibility these actors refer to often 
has to do with design layout and non-technical issues. Well, this research report shows that the 
concept of flexibility among these actors should be broaden. Partly, due to the fact that PV shows 
greater flexibility to diverse range of changes from an energy perspective. Partly due to the fact that 
all buildings according to the current ambitions of Boverket (provider of the Swedish national 
regulations for buildings) should be near-zero energy buildings by 2019 and 2021 for government 
and privately-owned buildings respectively. And finally, partly due to the very fact that energy 
optimization has been carried out in several areas of the built environment whereas for the 
ventilation system design (including heating and cooling) there is still much to do and energy to be 
saved. 
 
Regarding the output results from the FloVent simulations focusing on air distribution pattern 
some insights are shown in this research report. The major one being that PV slightly outperforms 
mixing ventilation looked at from an aggregated perspective and especially with regards to thermal 
environment (PMV and PPD) and indoor air quality (ventilation effectiveness). It should also be 
stated, however, that the purpose of the CFD simulations was never to investigate the airflow levels 
such as supply airflow and placement of different ATD. But rather to get a good picture of the 
flow distribution in different cases by comparing mixing ventilation and personalized ventilation. 
==PAGE:151==
151 
 
That is, more simulations are needed to really understand how the supply airflow levels and 
placement of ATD affect the room space conditions and flow distribution rather than the ideal 
values set in this research report based on different recommendations. Not to mention the usage 
of steady-state conditions instead of transient ones and simplifying the 4 different simulation 
models in several areas. 
 
Finally, regarding the optimization of energy in terms of reaching the minimum total energy 
consumption per year. The proposed design did not reach this criterion of yearly total energy 
consumption of 65 kWh/m
2 floor area. However, it was possible to decrease the yearly energy consumption 
a lot based on only 13 iterations (parameter variations) with small changes in the different parameters. More 
precisely, from 109.6 kWh to 80.5 kWh (total decrease of 29.1 kWh). That is, if anything this research report 
shows that it probably would be possible to reach minimum total energy consumption per year if more 
energy simulations had been done. 
 
7.3 Knowledge gap and hypothesis 
In the starting sections of this research report it was stated that there is a need for further studies 
investigating the energy-saving potential of personalized ventilation and other innovative solutions. 
After having conducted this research study this claim could never be truer than ever. Hopefully 
this report has contributed in pushing the paradigm shift from the design of collective indoor 
environments to individually controlled local environments in occupied spaces. Or at least created 
more interest and dialogue among different professionals in Sweden for the fascinating concepts 
of personalized ventilation, indoor environment and general ventilation system design. As well as, 
hopefully brought about more fruitful insights to the knowledge base of our society on how to 
work for a healthier and greener future. 
 
The explicit general hypothesis of this research report was the following: 
Implementing new ventilation system design with personalized ventilation in 
office buildings with mixed air distribution pattern should lead to greater 
energy-savings and the same or higher indoor environment (thermal comfort 
and IAQ)   
 
At this stage, it should be obvious that the general hypothesis of this research report is 
supported. The simulations in FloVent clearly showed that the indoor environment (thermal 
comfort and IAQ) is at least the same if not better for PV than for mixing ventilation in the 
specific office building looked at. And the energy simulations in IDA ICE clearly showed 
==PAGE:152==
152 
 
that PV overall consumes less energy and hence has greater potential of leading to energy-
savings and flexibility for the ventilation system design of office buildings that are currently 
design according to mixed ventilation principles. 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
==PAGE:153==
153 
 
Chapter 8: Concluding remarks 
This research report shows that the potential of reducing the yearly energy consumption by using 
ventilation system design based on the principles of personalized ventilation are much greater than 
on the principles of mixed ventilation. It also shows that the indoor environment quality (thermal 
comfort and indoor air quality) are at the same level and sometimes partly higher in some respects 
than mixed ventilation. 
 
At least as important this research report shows that many of the claims made in the literature 
among different scholars are supported. That is, that occupants are generally dissatisfied with the 
current indoor environment conditions and ability to influence their local environments. That there 
is a need for change within the fields of ventilation system design to more flexible and individually 
adjustable systems such as personalized ventilation. That the current European standards are not 
ideal for personalized ventilation and that more tailored or updated versions of these standards are 
needed. 
 
Finally, this research report shows that there are organisations interested in learning more about 
the innovative knowledge found in the academic work and hopefully bring this to practice. That is, 
the recommendations for Tyréns are rather straightforward. They should further investigate the 
possibility of implementing personalized ventilation for the upcoming renovation in 2019 due to 
the several limitations of this research report. This might not only lead to greater energy-savings 
and better indoor environments for their future workplace. It might even lead to them leading the 
way in Sweden within the fields of ventilation system design, indoor environment and energy-
savings in office buildings. By combining the experience and knowledge of indoor environment 
from the other side of the Oresund Region (Denmark) with their local understanding of the 
Swedish marketplace.

Chapter 9: Future research 
For the purposes of future research studies several proposals can be made. However, the focus will 
be on highlighting the three most important ones. The first being that someone should continue 
where this research study stopped by doing more energy simulations and parameter variations to 
better understand the relationship between changes in these parameters and yearly energy 
consumption. The second proposal for future research would be to investigate the air distribution 
pattern and airflow levels more in detail using different computational fluid dynamic tools such as 
FloVent to better understand how different changes might affect the overall air distribution. The 
final proposal for future research study is mostly suited for someone that understands regulations 
and technology. That is, to investigate the possibility to either tailor or update the current European 
standards to better fit the innovations brought about by the academic world such as the concept 
of personalized ventilation.
