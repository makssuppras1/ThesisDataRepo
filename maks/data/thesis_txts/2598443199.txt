Abstract
The microbiota composition markedly varies along the GI tract, but most studies focus
on the fecal microbiota, leaving other parts of the GI tract unexplored, particularly the
small intestine[22]. The main reason for little exploration on small intestine is its limited
accessibility. This thesis studies the feasibility of a hydrogel-based microdevices for small
intestine sampling.
The first part of this thesis described the physical properties, by the aids of microscopy.
Besides, the hydrogel formation inside the microdevices has also been visualized.
The second part of the study investigated the capturing ability of microdevices in bacterial
suspension. Green fluorescent protein(GFP) tagged bacteria was incorperated in the
visualization of sampling outcome. The microscopy images shown that microdevices were
able to trap the bacteria inside its hydrogel structure.
The third part of the thesis evaluated the sampling bias toward different bacterial species.
This bias can be reflected on bacterial composition difference from microdevices and
suspension mixture. qPCR assay result shown that there is no significant different in
the relative bacterial composition between the microdevices sampling outcome and the
original suspension content.
The last part of this thesis evaluated the replacement and contamination possibility when
the microdevices went from one sampling site to the other sampling site. This is possible
when the microdevices need to move along side the human GI tract before they are col-
lected from feces. qPCR assay are applied to exam this replacement and contamination
level.
This is the first study to investigate the feasibility of this novel hydrogel-based microde-
vices on small intestine sampling. The sampling function of this microdevices has been
validated in bacterial suspension in many aspects. This study provides foundation for
further validation on the sampling functions of this microdevices in vitro and/or in vivo.
Our study results can also promote the optimization of this microdevices as an effective
sampling device.

Background
1.1
Small intestine
There is an increasing awareness of Small Intestine(SI) in gut microbiome research.
1.1.1
Feces-based gut microbiome researches
Fecal sample is an important object used in gut microbial study, as the collection of feces
is convenient and non-invasive. However, a recent study[14] have shown that the associ-
ation between gut microbiome (fecal sample-based) and human disease is complex, and
the controversy of studies results has always been an issue.
Obesity has been linked with gut microbiome for a long time. However, there are incon-
sistent reports of the abundance change of specific bacterial species. While some stud-
ies[13] found that there is no connection between the gut microbiota species and obesity,
many other studies[7] reported a certain abundance change in the gut microbiota of obese
study participants. Additionally, another large study[6] also found a decrease in the bio-
logical diversity of the gut microbiota of the obese patients compared to the non-obese
subjects.
1.1.2
Geography of human GI tract
The microbiota is biogeographically varied within the gut on different site. In the small in-
testine,Lactobacillaceae and Enterobacteriaceae dominate, whereas the colon is charac-
terized by the presence of Bacteroidaceae, Prevotellaceae, Rikenellaceae, Lachnospiraceae
and Ruminococcaceae[3]. Meanwhile, the small intestine was found to exhibit lower bac-
terial diversity than the colon[3]. In the duodenum and jejunum, the bacterial concentration
is relatively low, between 103 and 106 /ml, much less than that of stools that can reach
1012 /ml[14]. Study[4] also found that the anaerobic/aerobic bacteria ratio in the feces is
much higher than it is in the small intestine.
The biogeographical difference suggests that researches might result in different conclu-
sions while using different sampling sites[20] [9] [16]. A research[21] that studied the
effect of transplanted mice supports this assumption. While no statistically significant dif-
ferences were detected between the faecal microbiotas from obese and control human
donor groups, the ileal and caecal (but not colonic) microbiotas differed between the trans-
planted obese and control mice. These results increased the awareness of using small
intestine sample in gut microbiome research. Differences may have existed in the upper
gut microbiotas between different subjects group, which were not reflected in faeces.
1.1.3
Human small intestinal microbiota
The small intestine is a harsh environment for microbial life owing to short transit time,
the existence of digestive enzymes and bile, and food substrate delivery [4]. As a result,
the bacterial populations in this region of the intestinal tract are less diverse, but are more
dynamic, given the need to respond to rapidly changing luminal conditions[4].
Bacterial genera commonly found in the small intestine include Lactobacillus, Clostridium,
Staphylococcus, Streptococcus, and Bacteroides[3] [4], among others.
Contrary to the more stable fecal microbiota, the SI microbiota displays pronounced com-
positional fluctuations[1]. The temporal variation in the small intestinal microbiota differs
the relatively stable composition in the colon [4], which most likely reflect the subject’s
variations.

1.1.4
Functions of the human small intestinal microbiota
Carbohydrate fermentation is a core function of the gut microbiota. A study[22] shows that
the small intestinal metagenome is significantly more enriched with genes related to car-
bohydrate metabolism, compared with the fecal metagenome. These functions were pre-
dominantly assigned to Streptococcus. Moreover, other nutrients have also been linked
to the small intestinal microbiota[4], such as fat digestion, and vitamin metabolism. How-
ever, the exact response of small intestinal microbiota to these activity composition are
still unknown.
Small intestine microbiota is also found to drive the immune response of host. The ter-
minal ileum is associated with the largest mass of lymphoid tissue in the human body[1],
such as the gut-associated lymphoid tissue (GALT) and Peyer’s patches. Disruption of
intestinal host-microbe homeostasis can drive pro-inflammatory cascades[1].Thus, small
intestinal microbiota are essential for maintenance of host immune homeostasis. How-
ever, Further insight in the molecular interactions between the microbiota and the (SI)
mucosal immune system is still needed.
1.2
Sampling technology
1.2.1
Animal study
Due to the inconvenience of small intestine sampling from human body, animal models
are often used to study the small intestine content. For animal study, animals were often
killed and dissected[20] [9] [16]. Luminal contents from the duodenum, jejunum, ileum,
caecum and colon were collected immediately after dissection. All samples were then
stored at −80°C until further analyses.
1.2.2
Current practice on human study
The SI is poorly accessible in healthy individuals. The regular way to get human SI content
is to collect luminal samples during routine gastroduo-denoscopy and colonoscopy prac-
tices [18]. However, volunteers are fasted before placing such catheters, what’s more,
inspecting may require luminal flushing, which may disturb the ’normal’ SI microbiota in
humans. Moreover, this procedure is incompatible with repeated sample collection over
time, limiting the measurements to single time-points, and hindering the study of micro-
biota dynamics and diet responses.
In order to analyze the relationship between SI microbiota dynamics and time, or diet,
ileostomy subjects are often recruited in SI microbiota research[18]. Ileostomy subjects
underwent colectomy and have their terminal ileum connected to an abdominal stoma,
which makes non-invasive and repetitive sampling of the small intestinal lumen possible.
Besides, the sample will be obtained without interfere the physicochemical and ecological
characteristics of the microbiome.
1.2.3
Advance technology for human study
Recently many remote-control drug delivery capsules have been adjusted to non-invasive
sampling devices for the human SI[18]. However, the high cost of these devices may
obstruct their application in larger scale trials in human. Their small sample-size is also a
technological challenge for the further analysis. Moreover, in order to obtain unchanged
samples from the SI, these devices requires a design of reaction quenching in the sample
during the remaining transit time through the rest of the intestinal tract.
For examples, osmosis-controlled released systems have used in oral drug delivery sys-
tem for a long time, using an osmotic pump to deliver drugs. Recently, this noninvasive
technology has been adjusted to sample the intestinal lumen in vivo. An ingestible os-

1.3
Microdevices
The microdevices that have been used in this study were invented by IDUN(The center
for Intelligent Drug delivery and sensing Using microcontainers and Nanomechanics) from
DTU. One of their research focus is on drug delivery micro-container design. As more and
more drug delivery systems have been modified to human intestine sampling devices[18],
the hydrogel-based microdevices from IDUN seem promising candidates. The design
of microdevices is based on TRAP bots[17], a real-time in situ hydrogel polymerization
system. In order to serve as a sampling device, the reagents in microdevices are slightly
differerent from TRAP bots (Unpublished research).
The microdevices need to be developed in separate steps: (1) Micro-wells fabrication, (2)
Reagents loading, (3) Deposition of lid.
1.3.1
Micro-wells fabrication
The cylindrical containers have an outer diameter of 300 μm and a cavity depth of 200 μm.
The fabrication of microcontainers are performed on silicon chips. Each squared silicon
chip contains 625 microwells, aligned into a 25×25 matrix.
The microwells was fabricated by using SU-8 material. SU-8 is a negative photoresist[17]
which is commonly used in micro and nanotechnology for fabrication of microstructures.
Photolithography is used for the definition of the microwells. The overall fabrication pro-
cess has been illustrated step by step in the previous study[17].
1.3.2
Reagents loading
There are many techniques for microcontainer reagents filling and loading, such brush
loading method, doctor blading technique[17] and inkjet printing technology[8]. All these
approaches result in spreading reagents in microcontainers layer by layer, or in a mixture.
Here, a modified brush loading method was used to fabricate the microdevices for in
this study[17]. The reagents for hydrogel formation was loaded into the microdevices.
The reaction will be initiated by H2O when the microdevices were put into an aqueous
environment.
The microdevices need to be kept in anhydrous conditions prior to use. Therefore, a
water-resistant mask was loaded on the top of the microwells. The mask was peeled-off
before being used.
1.3.3
Polymeric hydrogel
The rapid hydrogel formation proceeds via a polymerization mechanism, while an aque-
ous environment is presented. This real time polymerization system enable a hydrogel
structure for in situ target capturing. The target gets entrapped inside the hydrogel during
polymerization process.
The hydrogel maintains its structure due to chemical or physical cross-linking of individual
polymer chains[11]. This hydrophilic polymers network can swell in water and hold a large
amount of water inside its structure. Much of like anything made of water, hydrogel will
eventually dry out lossing their flexibility, unless there is continuous supply of water.

microscope, with six laser lines and three detectors.
Microscope was used followed instruction manual. The LSM main system and laser sys-
tem need to be warmed up firstly. Then, started the ZEN 2009 to process the images.
The light path and detected channels were set according to the experiment design. After
the scanning image process was finished, adjust the imaging setup and image display
tool to optimize the image.

GFP-tagged E.coli culture preparation
A GFP-tagged E.coli strain(MS 22404) was used in this study, which was preserved in our

the following experiment. For the washing step, centrifugation was used to separate the
washing liquid and the bacteria, with the speed of 4 000 rpm for 10 mins.

4.1
Methods and materials
4.1.1
Growth of the bacteria
The following bacteria strains are using in this study: Escherichia coli (DSM 18039), En-
terococcus faecium (DSM 20477), Lactobacillus rhamnosus (DSM 20021), which were
obtained directly from Deutsche Sammlung von Mikroorganismen und Zellkulturen gmbH
(DSM, Germany), and kept in the lab bacterial repository. These microbes were grown
on Macconkey agar, Blood Agar, and MRS agar respectively, and incubated overnight at
37°C. One colony from each plate was then suspended in Gifu Anaerobic Medium(GAM)
broth individually and incubated overnight at 37°C.
4.1.2
Bacterial mixture suspension
A bateria culture mixture was made by mixing these three pure bacterial broth together.
In order to ensure these three bacteria were mixed at the same cell density, the OD600
value (Optical density under 600nm) was measured, using NovaSpec III Visible Spec-
trophotometer (GE Healthcare, USA). Pure Gifu Anaerobic Medium(GAM) broth is used
as standard in OD600 measurement. All these three bacterial medium were then mixed
with approximately the same cell density. The cell density of pure bacterial broth was
calculated by the equation: cell density = OD600 * volume.
Bacterial culture mixture was then centrifuged under 4000rpm, 5mins. The pellet was
washed by distilled water twice and suspended in distill water to obtain the bacterial mix-
ture suspension.
4.1.3
Microdevices sampling
Microdevices sampling was performed by immersing the microdevices chip into the bacte-
rial suspension in a 10ml tube. To simulate the microdevices sampling scenario in human
body (move along side the human GI tract with a certain speed), the tube was placed in
a shaker at 180 rpm, 37°C, for 20 mins. Microdevices chips were then washed by dis-
tilled water, twice. Next, microdevices were removed from the chips by tweezers, and
suspended in distill water for the following use.
4.1.4
DNA extraction
DNA was extracted from the microdevices and bacterial suspension respectively, using
the DNeasy PowerLyzer PowerSoil Kit (QIAGEN,Germany), according to the manufac-
turer’s protocol with slight modifications.
In the beads beating step, suspension was firstly transferred to a 2ml tube that fits the
TissueLyser (QIAGEN,Germany). The supernatant needs to be discarded before adding
beads and powerbeads solution. Thus, centrifugation was performed at 10 000 rpm for 1
mins, leaving only bacterial pellet or microdevices pellet for beads beating. The TissueL-
yser was set at its highest speed, for 10 mins, twice.
In order to increase the DNA extraction efficiency, the volume of extracted solutions have
also been adjusted. Specifically, 850 μl Powerbeads solution (Kit protocol: 750 μl), 120 μl
Solution C1 (Kit protocol: 120 μl), and DNA 60 μl Solution C6 (Kit protocol: 100 μl) were
used.

4.1.5
DNA quality assessment
Extracted DNA was quantified by Qubit 2.0 Fluorometer (Thermo Fisher Scientific, USA).
Qubit working solution was prepared by adding 1 μl Qubit dsDNA HS (High sensitivity)
reagent into 199 μl Qubit buffer. 190μl Qubit working solution was mixed with 10 μl Qubit
standards to obtain standard sample. Here, Qubit standards 1 and Qubit standards 2
were used for standard correction. 10 μl DNA sample was then added to 190 μl Qubit
working solution, and mixed by vortex, before measured on Qubit 2.0 Fluorometer.
4.1.6
Real-time qPCR conditions
Quantitative real-time PCR was performed on a LightCycler 480 Instrument II (Roche,
Switzerland). All amplification reactions were carried out in transparent LightCycler 480
Multiwell Plate 384 (Roche, Switzerland) reaction plate and sealed with LightCycler 480
Sealing Foil (Roche, Switzerland) in a total volume of 11 μl. The reaction mixture contains
5.5 μl LightCycer 480 SYBR Green I Master (Roche, Switzerland), 0.22 μl of each primer
(10pmol/ul), 2 μl template DNA , and 3.06 μl nuclease-free water (QIAGEN, Germany).
The amplification program was identical for all amplifications and consisted of one cycle
of 95 °C for 5 min; 45 cycles of 95 °C for 15 s, 60 °C for 15 s and 72 °C for 45 s ; and
finally dissociation curve analysis (95 °C for 5 s, 60 °C for 1 mins, then increasing to 95
°C at 0.11 °C /s ramp rate).

4.3.3
Relative amount deviation
The primer P134, P84 and P32 were used to detect the relative amount of different bacte-
ria species within the bacterial suspension. However, the relative amount differed signifi-
cantly amongst these three bacteria species, where it is expected that the amount portion
of each bacteria species is approximately equal, with a percentage of around 30%, and a
total of 100%.
Figure 4.22: Relative amount calculation results
(1) Cell density estimation approach
The possible causes for this deviations could be the cell density estimation approach. The
bacterial suspension was made by mixing different bacteria species in approximately the
same cell density. The cell density was estimated by the equation: Cell density = OD600
* volume.
Figure 4.23: Bacterial mixture was made based on OD600 value
Normally, a more accurate estimation from OD600 to cells number is based on calibration
curve. Both cell size and shape would also effect the OD600 value. But we only take into
consideration of density of the cell suspension in this study, assuming that the suspension
density is directly proportional to cells number, as shown in figure 4.23. Additionally, the
linear range of OD600 needs to be taken into consideration as well, when estimating the
cell number basing on OD600.
(2) Copy number of 16s rRNA
The copy number of 16s rRNA varies among different bacteria species[5]. In this study, the
relative amount of 16s rRNA gene was correlated with the bacteria number. However, the
estimation of 16s rRNA copy number in common bacteria also varies among surveys[19].
If the variation of 16S rRNA copy numbers among bacteria is considered, a more accurate
relative bacteria amount can be estimated.

(3) qPCR efficiency correction
The relative amount quantification is usually based on the relative presence of a target
gene versus a reference gene. In this study, the relative amount calculation derived from
classic double delta Ct method. The relative amount of a bacteria is obtained using the
formula 2-ΔCt, which is basically the percentage of bacteria in the sample. However, this
approach didn’t consider the qPCR efficiency. The qPCR efficiencies for reference and
target gene are different, which might effect the quantification results. Thus, a mathemati-
cal model that normalizes the qPCR data with efficiency, as such Pfaffl method[12], might
be able to provide a more accurate quantification result.

5.3
Discussion
The calculated loss level of this microdevices sampling content is high. Thus, optimiza-
tions on enclosing the microdevices is needed, once upon the sampling process finish.
An additional sealing design would be able to reduce the loss of sampling content and
avoid the bacteria replacement while going pass the lower site of GI tract.
However, the high loss level might result from the contaminations in Bacterial suspen-
sion 2. No matter how many original content are lost, the relative amount of all the
bacteria species should add up as 1, since the relative amount represents the percent-
age. It is possible that the Bacterial suspension 2 was contaminated by other bacteria,
which consists of the loss part in total loss level. Meanwhile, the lack of technique
replicates make it hard to conclude from current result. More repeats samples and a
larger scale further study are needed.

6
Summary
Studies of intestinal microbiota are often based on faecal samples, due to the challenge
of sampling from sites higher up in the gut. The invention of hydrogel-based microde-
vices opens the opportunity for in vivo sampling of the gut microbiome upstream of the
colon.
This feasibility study of hydrogel-based microdevices on small intestine sampling shows
that the microdevices are able to capture bacteria inside its hydrogel structure. There
is no obvious sampling bias towards different bacteria species. The sampling outcome
resembles the original content, to a large extent. However, it is also shown that the
sampling content was largely lost after immersing in other suspension, which indicates
that the sampling content from sampling site would be easily lost during the remaining
transit time through the rest of the intestinal tract. Additionally, there is also possibility
that the sampling content would be replaced by other microbiome community in the
lower part of the intestinal tract.
However, appropriate research methods would be a great challenge for such novel
microdevices. The current research methods need a great effort to optimize for this
study, for example, microscopy and DNA extraction approach, as described in session
2.3.1 and session 4.3.1. The optimization process have been discussed previously.
Besides, larger scale of study design is needed in order to make a conclusion on the
feasibility of this microdevices on small intestine sampling. Due to the shut-down of
laboratory during the covid-19 situation, only limited lab works can be done for this
study. The lack of proper technique replicates makes it hard for the data analysis
process.
To optimize this microdevices for small intestine sampling purpose, more design on
microdevices is needed. For example, enclose design the microdevices during the
remaining transit time through the rest of the intestinal tract, immobilization design that
nnable the microdevices to sample more time from a targeted region, and collection
design for better collect the microdevices after sampling. As the awareness of small
intestine in gut microbiome research is increasing, there are needs for a noninvasively
sampling devices. This hydrogel-based microdevices looks promising in such a area.
