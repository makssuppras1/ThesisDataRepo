Abstract
The transition to a low-carbon economy requires innovative approaches to reduc-
ing greenhouse gas emissions across various sectors, including energy, agriculture,
and waste management. This thesis presents the P.R.I.C.E. (Pyrolysis Regional
Integration for Carbon Economies) model, a comprehensive financial feasibility
model designed to assess the potential for commercial-scale biomass pyrolysis
projects in the United States. Biomass pyrolysis, a thermochemical process that
converts organic material into biochar, bio-oil, and syngas, offers a versatile so-
lution for carbon sequestration, renewable energy production, and soil enhance-
ment.
The model integrates geographic, temporal, and economic data to evaluate the
viability of pyrolysis plants in diverse U.S. regions, incorporating stochastic sim-
ulation and econometric forecasting techniques to account for uncertainties in
market conditions and policy environments. It employs a Generalized Additive
Model (GAM) and machine learning algorithms, including Recurrent Neural Net-
works (RNN) and Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) models, to predict regional
cost components and economic indicators, providing stakeholders with actionable
insights into potential investment opportunities.
This thesis contributes to the understanding of how location-specific factors, such
as feedstock availability, regulatory frameworks, and market dynamics, impact
the financial outcomes of pyrolysis projects.
It also examines the role of car-
bon credits, policy incentives, and market demand in enhancing the economic
feasibility of biochar and other pyrolysis products. The findings underscore the
importance of tailored, data-driven strategies for optimizing the deployment of
carbon-reducing technologies, thereby supporting broader decarbonization efforts
and advancing sustainable development goals.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
1.1
Decarbonization and Technology
Countries worldwide are stepping up their efforts to reduce carbon emissions to
combat climate change. While sectors like electricity generation have made signif-
icant progress with renewable technologies such as wind and solar, other sectors,
like agriculture, fuel production, and industrial processes, are more diﬀicult to
decarbonize due to their dependence on carbon-intensive processes. To bridge
these gaps, companies in key innovation areas are developing technologies that
complement the renewable electricity solutions. Key focus areas include hydrogen
infrastructure, green fuels, and carbon capture, all of which are receiving substan-
tial investment globally. Europe has emerged as a leader in fostering companies
and technologies in these fields, advancing both research and market development.
Efforts to fully decarbonize have led to a renewed interest in older technologies
like heat storage, waste management, and biomass utilization. These technolo-
gies, once considered conventional, are being modernized and integrated into
current strategies to enhance their effectiveness. Biomass, traditionally burned
for energy, is now used more strategically to maximize its carbon reduction poten-
tial. Processes such as pyrolysis and gasification, which convert organic materials
into biochar, bio-oil, and syngas, offer versatile solutions. These processes can be
adapted to various business models, providing multiple benefits, including energy
production, waste management, and carbon sequestration. This flexibility makes
these technologies valuable in current decarbonization efforts.
Managing the uncertainty in deploying these technologies across diverse markets
is essential. Risk assessment and modeling help in understanding these uncertain-
ties and preparing for possible challenges. Research shows that using methods
like stochastic modeling, scenario analysis, and sensitivity analysis can predict
a range of outcomes, allowing for better decision-making. For instance, Broadie
et al. (2007) demonstrate that stochastic models are effective in handling un-
predictable variables in financial forecasts, making them practical for real-world

1.2
SkyClean Business Plan
Pyrolysis and gasification are proven technologies with great potential for sup-
porting decarbonization.
SkyClean, a Danish company, illustrates how these
technologies can be used effectively. SkyClean focuses on carbon sequestration
by processing agricultural waste, which reduces greenhouse gases and improves
soil quality. This dual benefit supports both environmental and agricultural goals.
Additionally, SkyClean generates green fuels as a secondary product, adding ex-
tra value to their operations.
SkyClean positions itself in the global carbon credit market, which is relatively
stable due to consistent demand, policy support, and international incentives.
Carbon credits, which are valued the same worldwide, provide SkyClean with a
steady revenue stream. The European Union has created a favorable environ-
ment for companies like SkyClean through strong policies that promote carbon
capture and similar technologies. The EU offers substantial grants and incentives,
supporting a mature carbon market that allows businesses to thrive. Lefebvre
et al. (2020) highlight how EU policies encourage investment in carbon reduc-
tion technologies, showing a developed carbon economy that supports sustainable
businesses.
The EU’s regulatory framework strongly supports carbon-reducing technologies.
The EU’s Emissions Trading System (ETS), combined with grants and subsidies
for carbon capture projects, has sped up the adoption of these technologies. This
support has made the EU a leader in building a robust carbon market where
technologies like pyrolysis and gasification can grow. The EU’s success shows
that coordinated policies, financial incentives, and a clear regulatory framework
can create a thriving market for carbon-reducing technologies, fostering innova-
tion and lowering entry barriers for new projects (European Commission, 2022).
While the U.S. is moving quickly to develop similar frameworks, it still needs to
catch up to the EU in creating a supportive environment for these technologies.

1.3
United States Context
The United States offers significant opportunities for carbon-reducing technolo-
gies, but understanding its complex laws and market conditions is vital. The
U.S. government has introduced various incentives, such as the Inflation Reduc-
tion Act, aimed at promoting carbon reduction through tax credits for carbon
capture and renewable energy projects. These policies suggest a shift towards
a more supportive environment that could rival the EU’s.
However, the lack
of coordination among U.S. government resources and policies poses challenges.
Different states have different levels of support for renewable energy and carbon
capture, complicating efforts to assess the feasibility of projects across regions.
Regional differences in decarbonization efforts add another layer of complexity.
States like California and New York, with strong policies and ambitious targets,
are more attractive for investments in carbon-reducing technologies, but also suf-
fer from high taxes and costs comparatively. Other states, lacking such support
but with strong market conditions, may need more measures to attract invest-
ments, leading to disparities in market potential. To catch up with the EU, the
U.S. must align its policies and provide consistent incentives across all states
to support these technologies. This would require harmonizing federal and state
policies and improving coordination between agencies to streamline resources and
reduce entry barriers. A more unified policy framework could unlock significant
potential in the U.S. carbon market and accelerate its decarbonization efforts.
The Inflation Reduction Act and other recent policies provide a foundation for a
more integrated approach to decarbonization in the U.S. However, to fully seize
these opportunities, tools are needed to help stakeholders navigate the complex
market.
Data-driven tools that provide clear insights into market conditions,
regulatory impacts, and economic viability are essential. These tools can help
companies understand regional differences and make informed decisions about
where and how to invest in carbon-reducing technologies, driving the U.S. to-
wards a more sustainable and carbon-neutral future.

CHAPTER 2
Objectives
The objective of this thesis is to develop a comprehensive model to assess the
financial feasibility of pyrolysis plants across various U.S. states. This model will
be built on several key components that consider geographic and time-dependent
trends, decomposition of cash flows, value at risk, and a detailed market evalu-
ation. Each component of the model is designed to address specific challenges
in the project development process and reduce barriers to entry for potential
stakeholders.
2.1
Geographic dependent Trends
Understanding the geographic variability among U.S. states is crucial for the
success of pyrolysis projects. While the United States benefits from a strong fed-
eral governance structure that provides a unified policy framework, significant
differences in demographics, resource availability, industrial composition, and
economic conditions across states can dramatically influence the feasibility of a
project. For example, states with abundant agricultural or forestry residues may
offer lower feedstock costs for pyrolysis plants, thereby enhancing project prof-
itability. Conversely, states with higher land and labor costs or less favorable
regulatory environments might pose additional challenges.
As emphasized by
Siler-Evans et al. (2013), accounting for regional differences in resource availabil-
ity and policy environments is essential for optimizing the siting and performance
of energy projects. This approach not only aids in identifying the most suitable
locations for deployment but also provides local governments and stakeholders
with a clearer understanding of how state-specific conditions can either support
or hinder decarbonization efforts.
To effectively leverage these geographic differences, the model will integrate state-
specific data on economic indicators, resource availability, and regulatory frame-
works. This data-driven approach allows for a more tailored analysis that can
highlight states with the most favorable conditions for pyrolysis projects.

2.2
Time-dependent Trends
Geographic trends are not static; they evolve over time due to various eco-
nomic, social, and political influences. To account for these temporal dynamics,
the model will employ projection techniques commonly used in econometrics to
forecast future conditions that could impact the feasibility of pyrolysis projects.
These techniques, such as time-series analysis and econometric modeling, allow
researchers to predict economic indicators like GDP growth, inflation rates, and
energy prices, which are critical for assessing long-term project viability.
Ac-
cording to Diebold (2015), the use of advanced forecasting methods is vital for
strategic planning in dynamic and uncertain environments, as it enables stake-
holders to anticipate and adapt to changes that may affect project outcomes.
Incorporating time-dependent trends into the model enhances its robustness by
providing a forward-looking perspective on potential risks and opportunities. By
utilizing tools and methods that are widely recognized and employed by indus-
tries and governments, the model’s projections will be more reliable and compa-
rable, allowing for better risk management and strategic decision-making. This
forward-thinking approach is essential for navigating the uncertainties inherent
in long-term investment projects, such as pyrolysis plants.
2.3
Decomposition of Cashflows
A detailed decomposition of cash flows is vital for understanding the financial
dynamics of pyrolysis projects. Breaking down costs and revenues into their con-
stituent components enables a more granular analysis of where the relative impor-
tance and sensitivities lie. This decomposition helps identify which factors—such
as feedstock costs, capital expenditures, or operational expenses—have the most
significant impact on project economics. Zhang and Fung (2006) suggest that
decomposing financial data into subcomponents allows for more precise forecast-
ing and sensitivity analysis, which is crucial for optimizing project design and
mitigating financial risks.
By analyzing different combinations of cost scenarios, the model can provide
a basis for more accurate state-to-state comparisons. This comparative approach

2.4
Value at Risk
Forecasting future project outcomes involves considerable uncertainty, especially
when dealing with fluctuating market conditions and unpredictable policy changes.
To address this uncertainty, the model will incorporate stochastic programming
techniques that generate multiple scenarios based on historical data. This method
allows for the creation of probability distributions, providing results with accom-
panying confidence intervals. Broadie et al. (2007) highlight the importance of
using stochastic models to account for risk and uncertainty in financial forecast-
ing, as they provide a more comprehensive understanding of potential outcomes
and help mitigate risks associated with unpredictable variables.
By integrating value-at-risk analysis into the model, stakeholders can better un-
derstand the range of potential financial outcomes for pyrolysis projects under
different scenarios. This approach not only enhances the reliability of the model’s
projections but also provides a more robust framework for decision-making under
uncertainty. It allows for a more nuanced assessment of risk, helping stakeholders
make informed choices about project development in an ever-changing economic
landscape.
2.5
Market Evaluation
In addition to a detailed cash flow analysis, a comprehensive market evaluation is
essential for assessing the broader economic viability of pyrolysis projects. This
evaluation will incorporate multiple indicators and assessment criteria, such as
market demand for biochar and other pyrolysis products, competition levels, and
potential revenue streams from carbon credits. Gross et al. (2010) emphasize
that combining market data with project-specific financial analyses is critical for
reducing entry barriers and improving the overall feasibility of renewable energy
projects.
A thorough market evaluation provides additional context that complements the
model’s findings, allowing for a more holistic understanding of the market po-
tential for pyrolysis plants. By integrating diverse data sources and assessment

CHAPTER 3

3.1
Pyrolysis
3.1.1
Introduction and Feedstock Considerations
Pyrolysis is a thermochemical process that decomposes organic materials at ele-
vated temperatures in the absence of oxygen. This process is crucial for converting
biomass into biochar, bio-oil, and syngas, making it a key technology in waste
management and renewable fuel production. The ability of pyrolysis to transform
various types of biomass, including agricultural waste, forest residues, livestock
manure, and even urban waste, into valuable products addresses both waste dis-
posal and carbon sequestration needs. In the context of developing a financial
feasibility model, understanding the feedstock needs and conversion abilities of
pyrolysis is vital for accurately estimating the inputs and outputs, which directly
influence the economic metrics such as costs and revenues. According to Roy and
Dias (2017), pyrolysis technologies have a broad range of capabilities, which in-
clude producing higher-value products depending on the feedstock used, and thus
require a detailed understanding of the specific needs of the chosen technology.
Moreover, the choice of feedstock and its associated costs play a critical role
in determining the financial viability of pyrolysis projects. Research indicates
that the feasibility and cost implications of large-scale biomass energy projects,
such as pyrolysis, are heavily influenced by feedstock availability and logistics
(EIA, 2020). This highlights the importance of incorporating these factors into
the financial model to ensure it accurately reflects the economic realities of devel-
oping and operating a pyrolysis plant.
The flexibility of pyrolysis systems to adapt to different feedstocks and scales

process could enhance product value but also increase the initial investment. Roy

reduce expenses.

3.3.1
Predictive Modeling
Generalized Additive Models (GAMs) can capture more complex, non-linear re-
lationships between the input parameters and the predicted conditions, making
them ideal for situations where the behavior of the profile is influenced by mul-
tiple interacting factors. For instance, these models can be applied to estimate
the economic conditions of one region by utilizing input parameters such as em-
ployment rates, inflation, and GDP growth from a different, but comparable,
region. By inputting the relevant parameters from one region into the model, it
is possible to extrapolate and predict conditions in a different region that shares
similar economic or demographic characteristics. This approach is particularly
useful in economic studies where direct measurements may not be available for
certain regions, allowing for informed predictions based on analogous data.
Generalized Additive Models work by extending the traditional linear regression
framework to allow for non-linear relationships between the predictors and the
response variable. Unlike standard linear models that assume a linear relation-
ship, GAMs use smooth functions to model the effect of each predictor variable
on the response. This flexibility allows GAMs to capture complex, non-linear
patterns in the data without requiring a specific parametric form. In the con-
text of the study by S. Köhn (2008) on shipping economics, GAMs were used to
model economic variables by fitting smooth functions to each predictor, such as
shipping rates and fuel costs, allowing for more accurate predictions of economic
conditions. The model sums these smooth functions, providing a comprehensive
and flexible approach to understanding how changes in input variables affect the
outcome, making it particularly useful in economic scenarios where relationships
between variables are often non-linear and complex.
Validation and model tuning are critical steps in developing accurate and reliable
autoregressive and machine learning time series forecasting models. Validation
involves evaluating the model’s performance on unseen data to ensure it gen-
eralizes well beyond the training dataset, thereby avoiding overfitting. Proper
validation techniques, such as cross-validation or the use of a hold-out validation
set, provide an unbiased assessment of the model’s predictive capabilities. Model
tuning, on the other hand, involves adjusting the model’s hyperparameters to
optimize its performance.

4.1.3
Feature Engineering and Data Alignment
Respective to every project type and data tool, specific constraints or desired
characteristics of the data are necessary to reach the end goal.
When a con-
straint or characteristic is not met, the strategy must change to conform to what
is available and rigorous in terms of data quality. When the data needed for
an accurate prediction is unavailable, missing for a particular time or category,
or you have multiple datasets that are related to the goal parameter, the solu-
tion is primarily to begin engineering more features out of what is available. A
two-stage process can be highly effective when developing predictive models, es-
pecially in complex scenarios like time series forecasting. In this approach, an
initial prediction model is first developed to engineer and select the most relevant
==PAGE:42==
26
4 Methodology
features, which are then fed into a larger, more sophisticated machine learning
model. This method ensures that the final model is built on a robust foundation
of well-engineered features. Forootani, Rastegar, and Sami (2022) illustrate this
approach in their study on load forecasting, where an enhanced machine learning-
based framework was employed to first perform feature engineering. This initial
step was critical to their research in improving the accuracy and reliability of the
subsequent deep learning models used for forecasting.
To enhance reliability in variable correlation when conducting the regional de-
composition or time-series forecasting, this research leverages features and tar-
gets that are aligned with the North American Industry Classification System
(NAICS). By ensuring that data points correspond directly to industry-specific
classifications, the model can more accurately capture economic trends and re-
lationships within each sector, thereby improving the precision and relevance of
its predictions. Correlating variables to each other aligned with NAICS classi-
fication is a method that has been tried-and-true for other researchers, such as
when Chen et al. (2019) incorporated NAICS codes to associate Consumer Price
Index (CPI) data with specific industry sectors, enhancing the alignment of data
points across different economic models. The approach allows for more accurate
prediction of economic indicators by ensuring that the data used in the models
is directly relevant to the industries being analyzed. Other research such as Ro-
drigues et al. (2013) utilized this method as well, aligning unclassified web-mined
data with oﬀicially categorized economic data, improving the model’s precision
in predicting sector-specific economic activity.

Once the decided historical datasets for both the national and regional levels have
been queried and imported from the resource portal, before even entering into
the custom Excel database, there must be some manual cleaning and sorting, as
each dataset file may come in a different format. Only after the pertinent data

==PAGE:43==
4.2 Model Structure
27
Figure 4.2. Logic diagram of data cleaning module
The ‘Data Cleaning’ module is in the form of a short Python script executable
that calls upon the database Excel file and reads the data and stores each dataset
into a Pandas DataFrame, which allows for a greater level of manipulation and
ease of use with other Python packages. Once each dataset series is loaded into
it’s own identifiable DataFrame object class, the next step is to parse the data for
missing or non-numerical values. This can be due to the data points not being
recorded, having wider temporal granularity, or due to them being omitted to
prevent the inadvertent publication of a private company’s sensitive information,
wherein the company’s market dominance renders the public record to be a re-
flection of the private metrics. Regardless, the distribution of missing data in
this scenario is considered ‘missing at random’ (MAR). All missing values are
then imputed using an iterative imputation process. Selection of the imputation
methodology depends on the resulting error after validation, usually stemming
from variance and covariance of the data. For example, some methods might
behave erratically when there is high intercorrelation among variables. Iterative
==PAGE:44==
28
4 Methodology
approaches generally perform better overall as they are robust methods, therefore
characteristics of the variables matters less, and less preprocessing is needed. In
iterative methods, an initial guess for the missing data is provided, the model
parameters based on the imputed dataset are estimated, then those model param-
eters are used to find the conditional expectation of the same previously missing
elements, which then replace the original guesses, and the iteration repeats.
Next, the data is transformed by each variable series to become a discretized
version of itself that is in the form of a percent change from the previous pe-
riod. This leaves the value for the first year recorded as undefined, therefore it
is removed from all series after discretization. If the variable’s data is already
in this format, it is passed along to the next task of target and feature variable
delineation. The ‘target’ variables are those that were selected to be direct indica-
tors of the price change profile for each of the cost and revenue sub-components.
Some indicators may be used for multiple components, and others need a very
specific one. For the PRICE model, the target variables are the sectoral output
price deflators by NAICS sector for each specific industry that is relevant to the
cost and revenue streams of a bioenergy plant. Since these target variables are
only available on the national level, they will be used as training and validation
data for predicting the same variables for each region. The ‘feature’ variables are
made up of an assortment of other indicators or measurements that also have an
effect on the sectoral economy of the same specific industries mentioned. The
feature variables will be used as input to learn the associate with the behavior of
the target variables.
4.2.2
GAM Regional Predictive Module
From the Data Cleaning module, the target and feature datasets are passed to
the Generalized Additive Model (GAM), a type of predictive regression modeling
method that can account for non-linear relationships between targets and features.
A GAM functions as an expansion of the general linear regression, capturing mul-
tiple smooth linear functions of the predictor (feature) variables.
GAMs are
particularly useful for investigating convoluted dependencies between variables,
which can often be the case when dealing with economic data. GAM functions
produce smooth functions for each predictor (feature), which can then be used
with the experimental regional predictors to produce a combined function that
results in the target variables for the region, repeated for every region considered.
==PAGE:45==
4.2 Model Structure
29
Figure 4.3. Logic diagram of GAM regional prediction module
The target and feature data from the national datasets are used first, split into
training and testing data using a shuffled 70/30 split. The GAM is fitted to the
national dataset, with cross validation to ensure that the model is not overfitting
the data. Once the error has been minimized through careful feature selection and
parameter tuning, the experimental regional feature data is passed to the fitted
model, predicting a target historical price deflator for each region, following the
behavior of the national features and targets. After this has been repeated for
all target variables and regions, the results are stored in new DataFrames and
passed to the next step which is taking the historical target variable values and
projecting them into the future.

Machine Learning Forecasting Module
In this module, both the national and regional targets and features are fitted to

a robust model with many tunable parameters that can eﬀiciently take a large
number of feature datasets as input is essential. Neural networks allow for large
amounts of data to be passed with limited supervision, and robustness to outliers,
lagged effects, missing data, or even differently structured data. For these kinds
of machine learning algorithms, the complexity of setting up and validating the
model usually places them out of reach for novice applications, however some or-
ganizations have already been able to release Python packages for their easy use
with other Python libraries, such as Google did with their product TensorFlow.

visualization capabilities.

be low, however the inter-regional differences in the predicted variables still hold
valuable information when gauging which state should be chosen when develop-

The next most important piece in the process of conducting cashflow analysis

these factors and more are utilized in the PRICE model to individually predict
the lower level component/resource change in price over time before calculating
the overall cost or revenue, opening up the possibility of detailed sensitivity and
risk analsyis of the overall project investment using the collective impacts of mul-

6.4
Description of Research Limitations
The initial scope of this research was ambitious, intending to develop a compre-
hensive model that could predict the financial feasibility of a hypothetical pyrol-
ysis project across all U.S. states, taking into account state-specific variables and
profiles. However, due to unforeseen constraints such as data availability, method
complexity, and time constraints, the research scope had to be adjusted. The
most significant adjustment involved the reliance on historical national profiles
rather than the development of state-specific future variable profiles as originally
intended. This adjustment was necessary due to limitations in the development
of the planned Python modules, which were crucial for generating the predicted
state variable profiles. Consequently, the state-level analysis was conducted using
a simplified approach by applying a scalar state price parity to each historical
national profile, using the historical values as if they were the future predictions.
One sacrifice this resulted in is the inability to provide any information on the
uncertainty of the resulting predictions, meaning that there can be no real com-
parison between states on the level of risk associated with the predicted NPV of
the project.
6.4.1
Model Inadequacies and Solutions
6.4.1.1
Regional Prediction Model
The feature engineering model did not successfully generate profiles for all states
due to incomplete development. To overcome this, further development is nec-
essary to enhance the model’s ability to handle diverse data inputs and outputs
effectively. Using machine learning (ML) techniques, particularly those that han-
dle missing data better than Generalized Additive Models (GAM), would increase
model reliability and accuracy. This involves selecting appropriate algorithms,
optimizing hyperparameters, and validating model performance across different
states.
6.4.1.2
Forecasting Model
The forecasting model faced limitations as it did not have state-specific profiles,
thus failing to generate state-level forecasts. When the capability is completed
and can be iterated for all regions and variables, an additional layer of feature
selection can be added to narrow the wide range of input variables. This process
would reduce overfitting and improve the model’s predictive power. Techniques
such as Principal Component Analysis (PCA) or Recursive Feature Elimination

(RFE) could be employed to refine the input set, ensuring that only the most
relevant variables are used in forecasting.
6.4.1.3
Cashflow Model
The Excel model, currently limited in scope, lacks integration with state eco-
nomic trends, which restricts its applicability to real-world scenarios. To make it
more robust, the model should include these trends and offer better automation
and user-friendliness, possibly through VBA (Visual Basic for Applications) and
Python scripting. There is also a lack of sensitivity analysis which would reveal
even more information for the user on the state market. Moreover, adding more
visualizations and analyses on the large amount of data forecasted and calculated
would only increase the usefulness of the model.
6.4.2
Time Constraints
Time constraints played a critical role in shaping the final outcome of this research.
The original timeline did not adequately account for the complexity involved in
developing and integrating the Python modules with the Excel model, which was
necessary for the state-specific analyses. The development of these Python scripts
proved more time-consuming than anticipated, as the machine learning module
using TensorFlow proved to be too complicated for being a secondary part of the
overall model process for this project. As a result, certain planned functionalities
such as the automatic updating of data from the database file and the ability
to run all states and scenarios simultaneously, were not fully realized within the
allotted time frame.
Instead, these processes had to be performed manually,
which takes significantly longer and prevents the model from being used as an
actual tool. The requirement of manually running each state’s scenario through
the ineﬀicient code with potentially inaccurate results is the reasoning behind
omitting the use of the GAM and ML python modules altogether in favor of
substituting the needed state profiles with the national profile adjusted with a
scalar price parity corresponding to each state, reducing the time it takes to get
useable data to test the Excel model.
6.4.3
Platform Constraints
The choice of platforms and tools, specifically Python and Excel, introduced
additional challenges that impacted the research outcomes. While Python is a
powerful tool for data analysis and modeling, the complexity of developing mod-
ules capable of generating accurate state-specific profiles was underestimated. In

the case of this research, the Python modules were developed in a Jupyter Note-
book environment, which simplifies the development and debugging process and
allows for modular code writing. The drawback is that it becomes significantly
more tedious to have the environment update the input data when it is changed
in the database file, as well as re-run and re-update the outputs in Excel. If the
Python modules were developed with the corresponding code to call the database
file from OneDrive or other cloud data storage service, and were programmed to
run directly as an executable, the proposed design of the Python modules would
be met.
The Excel model, although effective in its capacity, also fell short of its planned
potential due to limitations in automation and data integration. Excel has the
capability to run VBA code to increase its capabilities and has recently added the
ability to run Python code directly in an Excel workbook. Both tools were used
to increase the eﬀiciency and useability of the model, however using these tools
also increases the complexity of developing the model. Dut to time limitations,
the full capability of these tools was not met in the model, leaving some essential
functions to be manual. The manual intervention required to update data and
run scenarios introduces potential human error and reduced the overall eﬀiciency
of the model. These platform limitations necessitated the use of simplified as-
sumptions and processes, which, while suﬀicient for a proof of concept, may not
fully capture the nuances intended in the original research design.
6.4.4
Accuracy and Assumptions
Using methods such as price and cost decomposition, time series forecasting un-
der uncertainty, and cashflow prediction, certain assumptions must be made to
define a scope as all three methods can have varying degrees of complexity and
accuracy based on desired results. In the case of this research project, the overall
accuracy was not prioritized, in favor of the relative differences between states
across multiple observations and scenarios. Examples of these assumptions can
be found in the appendix. The relative differences between states on these ob-
jective variables can shed light on the ranked best choice of state for developing
a pyrolysis project like the modeled example case. Therefore, the accuracy of
the overall cashflows matters less than the information obtained when comparing
each state’s market ‘report card’ when it comes to developing pyrolysis projects.
The reliance on historical national variable profiles and the application of scalar
state price parities to simulate state-specific profiles represents another significant
assumption that affects the accuracy of the model. While these assumptions were
necessary given the constraints, they introduce a level of abstraction to produce

of what can be achieved when the model is applied correctly. These limitations
highlight the need for further refinement and development of the model in future
research to enhance its accuracy, applicability, and replicability.
6.4.5
Proof of Concept
It is important to underscore that this research was designed as a proof of concept,
rather than a fully functional tool ready for stakeholder use. The primary objec-
tive was to demonstrate the potential value of integrating existing concepts and
methodologies to predict the viability of pyrolysis projects across different states.
The limitations encountered—whether in scope, time, platform capabilities, or
accuracy—are reflective of the challenges inherent in any proof of concept. These
challenges necessitated the use of simplified models and assumptions, which, while
reducing the model’s immediate applicability, do not diminish the value of the
research. Instead, they provide a foundation for future development and refine-
ment, illustrating the model’s potential and the need for continued exploration
in this area.

Conclusion
This thesis presents several innovative contributions to the field of financial feasi-
bility modeling for pyrolysis projects, particularly in the context of state-specific

this work are highlighted in the development of a multi-modal comprehensive pre-

contributions and expansions to the current state of the art are as follows:
7.1

7.1.1
Two-Stage Hybrid Regional Prediction and Forecasting
The research contributes to improving regional economic forecasting by innova-
tively designing a novel two-stage prediction system. Other models from previous
research utilize two-stage models where the first stage is usually a feature selec-
tion model to enhance prediction robustness, however this thesis designs a feature
engineering model first stage to expand the capability of the second stage fore-
casting model beyond what data is readily available. In the feature engineering
model, regional historical data variables that are not measured by governments,
fragmented, or measured differently across regions are standardized by predicting
the needed variables using robust data that is available regionally with uniform
methods. These variables are predicted by fitting a model to available national
historical values trained with input indicator features, validating and tuning to
avoid over-fitting, then applying the model with available regional input indica-
tor features to produce the missing historical results. This approach effectively
addresses the challenge of limited localized economic data, thereby increasing
the granularity and applicability of economic forecasts at the state level. The
enhanced ability to perform regional analysis and planning in energy projects
represents a valuable addition to the current methodologies.

The second stage model also pushes the boundaries of where current research
ends by applying advanced machine learning techniques, particularly in the use
of Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) neural networks for time-series forecast-
ing under uncertainty. Changing market conditions and patterns with diﬀicult
to interpret interactions makes it increasingly diﬀicult to forecast accurately in
the long-term. Modern machine learning methods such as LSTM deep learning
neural networks overcome this problem by removing the need for human program-
ming of interdependent interactions. Deep learning techniques offer a significant
improvement over traditional econometric models by capturing complex patterns
and non-linear relationships, while approaches such as LSTM methods also allow
for differentiating previous historical trends to contemporary ones, resulting in
more accurate and robust forecasts. Forecasting such long time-steps as done in
this research requires many feature variables with many years of historical values
to help the model capture trends, interactions, and lags, making the development
of these models dependent on modern data reporting and querying abilities avail-
able from governments and organizations, as well as cutting-edge customizable
forecasting tools such as the Python TensorFlow library. Such advancements are
crucial for assessing the financial viability of long-term, capital-intensive projects
like pyrolysis plants.
7.1.2
Combination of Python and Excel
A key innovation of this research is the development of a multi-modal model
that facilitates a two-way data flow between Python and Excel models. This
allows users to harness the advanced computational capabilities of Python while
maintaining the familiar interface and functionalities of Excel. This connectivity
between the two platforms ensures that data can be seamlessly transferred and
utilized, providing a more comprehensive and integrated approach to financial
modeling. This contrasts sharply with previous models that were often rigid, dif-
ficult to customize, and required specialized programming knowledge to modify.
This research introduces a novel approach by integrating Python scripts na-
tively within Excel worksheets, marking a significant advancement over tradi-
tional methodologies that primarily relied on VBA (Visual Basic for Applica-
tions). The compatibility of Python with Excel, recently made available, allows
for more sophisticated and powerful data manipulation, enhancing the capabili-
ties of financial modeling far beyond the limitations of VBA. This integration not
only automates repetitive tasks such as data manipulation and sub-calculations
but also provides the flexibility to implement complex algorithms and analytics
directly within the Excel environment. By doing so, the research enables dynamic
adjustments based on user inputs, thereby streamlining the modeling process, re-

financial contexts.
Moreover, the research focuses on creating a user-friendly and intuitive modeling
framework. Unlike earlier financial modeling projects, which often posed signif-
icant challenges in terms of customization and programming complexity, this
model is designed to be accessible even to users with limited technical expertise.
The integration of Python within Excel allows users to perform advanced ana-
lytics without needing to learn a new programming language or switch between
different software platforms. This emphasis on user intuitiveness, combined with
the robust capabilities of Python, makes the model not only more powerful but
also more practical for a broader range of users. This innovative approach repre-
sents a substantial leap forward in the field of financial modeling, offering a more
flexible, adaptable, and user-centric solution compared to traditional methods.
7.1.3
Comprehensive Cash Flow Decomposition and Analysis
This thesis presents a novel cash flow decomposition methodology that signifi-
cantly advances financial modeling practices by breaking down key financial ele-
ments, such as revenues and costs, into their constituent parts. Unlike traditional
research methods, which often rely on aggregated financial data and broad as-
sumptions, this detailed decomposition allows for a more granular analysis of
each component’s impact on the overall financial viability of a project. By dis-
secting these elements into finer subcomponents, the methodology enables more
precise sensitivity analyses, providing deeper insights into how different variables
affect project outcomes. This approach is particularly valuable for conducting
state-to-state market comparisons, as it accounts for region-specific variations
in economic conditions, regulatory environments, and market dynamics, thereby
supporting more tailored and effective project designs.
In comparison to previous studies, which have often utilized static or linear mod-
els with limited capability for detailed breakdowns, this research introduces a
more dynamic and comprehensive framework for cash flow analysis. For instance,
earlier models like those discussed in Smith and Jones (2019) focused primarily on
high-level financial metrics and lacked the ability to drill down into the nuanced
interactions between various financial components. Similarly, the work by Lee et
al. (2020) employed broad financial simulations without integrating detailed cash
flow segmentation, leading to less accurate forecasts and suboptimal sensitivity
analyses. In contrast, the methodology introduced in this thesis enhances the
robustness of financial models by incorporating both qualitative feature selection
and numerical feature engineering. This dual approach allows for a more refined

mizing the numerical representation of these variables within the model.
By integrating qualitative insights into feature selection, the research ensures
that non-quantifiable factors, such as market sentiment or regulatory changes,
are considered alongside traditional financial metrics. This combination of quali-
tative and quantitative techniques not only improves the accuracy and reliability
of the model but also provides a more holistic view of the factors influencing
project viability. The ability to conduct detailed, component-level analyses and
compare market conditions across different states or regions gives project develop-
ers a powerful tool for mitigating financial risks and optimizing design strategies.
This methodology’s innovative blend of cash flow decomposition, qualitative and
quantitative feature selection, and enhanced sensitivity analysis marks a signifi-
cant step forward in financial modeling, offering a more nuanced and adaptable
approach compared to traditional methods.

and analytical precision. Cloud-based data storage, for instance, would allow for
real-time data updates and seamless access to extensive datasets, thereby facili-
tating enhanced data integration, collaboration, and transparency among diverse
stakeholders. Improved user interfaces would make the model more accessible,
enabling users to interact with the data more intuitively and effectively. Fur-
thermore, the implementation of cross-modal connectivity would ensure a more
seamless flow of data between different software environments, allowing for more
comprehensive analyses that combine diverse data types and sources. Incorpo-
rating advanced AI capabilities would bring the model’s predictive analytics and
decision-making processes to a new level, enabling it to better identify trends,
forecast outcomes, and assess risks with greater accuracy. By integrating these
innovative technologies, the research not only solidifies its proof of concept but
also establishes a robust foundation for ongoing advancements, ensuring that
the financial modeling of renewable energy projects remains highly relevant and
adaptable to the complexities of different regional economic contexts.
