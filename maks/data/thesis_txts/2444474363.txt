Abstract
This study is part of the RiBuild research project and aims to create a webtool for sup-
porting decision-making in energy retroﬁt projects of historic buildings. The webtool
provides a quantitative sustainability evaluation of hygro-thermally optimized insulation
solutions through a climate burden indicator. Additionally, the study presents a liter-
ature review on life-cycle assessment of insulation systems. This is achieved through a
life-cycle impact assessment (LCIA) of the global warming potential of ﬁve insulation
systems for installation in a historic building in 160 potential locations across seven
countries, including several choices of heating systems. The functional unit is 1 m2 in-
sulation system with the equivalent U-value installed as a part of an energy renovation
of a historic building with the lifespan of 30 years from 2020 to 2050 and CML-IA base-
line 3.05 is used as LCIA-method. The Heating Degree Days method has been used to
determine the saved energy consumption from renovation. The literature review found
that CML was the most used LCIA-method in the reviewed studies. Of the life-cycle
stages, the production stage was the most widely covered, and was found to be most en-
vironmentally burdening in most studies. The combination possibilities in the webtool
generated 213,180 results. For both induced and net impacts, the ranking of the in-
sulation system changed among the countries. In most application scenarios, the net
impacts were negative. 2 % of all net impacts were positive. This situation was most
pronounced for Italy. The case studies found that the net impacts of the insulation
systems were negative for all heating systems with both static and dynamic energy grids
for Copenhagen, Denmark. For Pantelleria, Italy, the only scenario resulting in negative
net impacts was with electricity as heating system. Across all analyses, the insulation
system including mineral wool was the most sustainable solution. This study illumi-
nates that the sustainability of internal insulation retroﬁts was dependent on both the
geographical location and the heating system of the building. To further improve the
sustainability performance of energy retroﬁts in historic buildings, the implementation
of recycling solutions and measures for reducing impacts from the production stage are
needed.

1 Introduction
The potential consequences of climate change and the concomitant rise in global mean
temperatures has received global attention resulting in the Paris Agreement in 2015 [1].
In the Paris Agreement, 184 parties to the United Nations Framework Convention on
Climate Change (UNFCCC) agreed on goals aiming at reducing greenhouse gas (GHG)
emissions and restricting the rise in global mean temperature to less than 2 ◦C. In a
European context, the European Council [2] has set a target of 40 % reduction in GHG
emissions in 2030 compared to 1990 levels. As the existing building stock accounts for
40 % of the energy use and 35 % of the GHG emissions, energy retroﬁts for improvement
of the energy eﬃciency of the existing building stock oﬀer a great potential to reduce
the European GHG emission levels [3, 4].
Historic buildings are part of the existing building stock and represent architectural,
as well as historical and cultural values, that needs to be preserved. Consequently, energy
retroﬁts of historic buildings pose diﬀerent constraints and challenges than traditional
insulation retroﬁts, as their visual character and building fabric must be taken into
account [5, 6]. External insulation retroﬁt measures impact the aesthetic appearance of
a building, why these solutions are not always a possible option when renovating historic
buildings. In this light diﬀerent approaches, such as internal insulation retroﬁts, must be
considered. However, these solution options are less favorable from a hygro-thermal point
of view, as the temperature of the external wall is decreased when applying insulation
internally. The subsequent lowered dew point and increase in relative humidity between
insulation and the external wall aggravate the risk of condensation and mold growth [7].
1.1
The RiBuild research project
The European Union funded Horizon 2020 project ”Robust Internal Thermal Insulation
of Historic Buildings” (RiBuild) is a research project which aims to develop comprehen-
sive guidelines for improving energy performance of historic buildings while maintaining
a minimal risk of mold growth through internal insulation retroﬁts. RiBuild is a collab-
oration between research departments across seven European countries: Belgium, Den-
mark, Germany, Italy, Latvia, Sweden, and Switzerland. This project is part of RiBuild
and will serve as a deliverable to Work Package 6 (WP6) which aims at developing a
web tool where insulation solutions for internal insulation energy retroﬁts of historic
buildings are optimized on their hygro-thermal performance. In the RiBuild project,

historic buildings are deﬁned as all protected and non-protected buildings constructed
before 1945 [7].
1.2
Objective
This project aims at creating a sustainability module for the RiBuild web tool. With this,
an additional angle to the hygro-thermal optimization is provided to support decision-
making of the most appropriate internal insulation solution in a renovation scenario.
With the sustainability module, this project aims to provide a quantitative life cycle as-
sessment (LCA) of hygro-thermally optimized insulation solutions for historic buildings.
In addition, the objective of this project comprises a literature review on sustain-
ability assessment of thermal insulation materials for buildings.
1.3
Scope
This project will provide a climate burden indicator in the form of GHG emissions
associated with project tailored insulation systems. In practice, the project will provide
a range of unit climate burdens for a range of unit operations on induced impacts (i.e.
from production, transport, installation, and disposal of the insulation system) and
avoided impacts (i.e. from saved energy consumption), which through the user interface
of the web tool can be combined with a multitude of application scenarios (geographic
locations, heating systems, and wall types in the existing building).
The project will perform a quantitative assessment of ﬁve insulation systems of
varying thickness (with the insulation materials calcium silicate, polyurethane, phe-
nolic foam, mineral foam, and mineral wool) for installation in 160 locations across the
seven participating countries with a lifespan of 30 years from 2020-2050. The project in-
cludes the choice of ﬁve diﬀerent existing wall types and three heating systems for each
country, generating 213,180 possible application solutions.
Additionally, an in-depth
analysis of two case studies will be performed. The locations of the case studies have
been chosen from the web tool: Copenhagen, Denmark and Pantelleria, Italy. In the
case studies, the insulation thickness of the insulation systems is selected to comply with
the Danish Building Regulation’s (BR18) requirements for thermal transmittance values
in renovation scenarios [8].

2 Method and materials
This section presents the chosen methods for carrying out the literature review as well
as the LCA of the insulation systems in the web tool and the case studies. Furthermore,
the life cycle inventory modeling of the assessed insulation systems and the functionality
of the RiBuild web tool are presented.
2.1
Literature review
The literature review aims to produce a detailed overview of literature on sustainability
assessment of insulation materials used in buildings. The review focuses on identifying
how the environmental life cycle impacts of insulation systems in buildings are assessed.
In practice, the review quantiﬁes the life cycle impact assessment (LCIA) methods em-
ployed, which life cycle stages are covered and which life cycle stages are found to
contribute the most to the overall environmental impact.
The review follows the criteria for systematic literature reviews (SLR) as presented
by Okoli & Shabram [9]. This approach was chosen to ensure transparency in how the
review was conducted. An SLR is composed of four stages with an underlying eight step
guideline. The four stages are planning (where the purpose is identiﬁed, and the search
protocol is set up); selection (where the literature search and the practical screening are
carried out); extraction (including quality appraisal and data extraction), and execution
(data analysis and writing). In the planning stage, the research question for the literature
review was deﬁned as
How are life cycle assessments of insulation systems in buildings per-
formed?
For this research question, several keywords have been identiﬁed such as ”life cycle assess-
ment”, ”life cycle inventory assessment”, ”life cycle impact assessment”, ”environmental
impact analysis”, ”thermal insulation”, ”insulation material”, ”insulation system”, and
”building envelope”. For the protocol search strings, search strategy, and selection cri-
teria were deﬁned. In the selection stage, the literature search was conducted using two
research databases: DTU Findit and Scopus. The following search strings derived from
the identiﬁed key words were used for the literature search:

These three search strings were chosen to ensure that all relevant LCA studies con-

on the two databases returned 480 results. The scope was further limited to reduce
the number of studies by introducing two selection criteria. The ﬁrst selection criterion
was introduced to ensure topic relevance; the criterion entailed exclusively identifying
studies considering LCA of insulation materials used for thermal insulation of building
envelopes, or building envelope sections including a thermal insulation system. A time
limitation (2010-2018) was introduced as the second selection criterion to ensure focus
on the most recent literature on the subject. The incorporation of these selection cri-
teria reduced the number of articles to 57 after all 480 initially identiﬁed articles were
screened (ﬁrst the titles and then the abstracts). The subsequent quality appraisal fur-
ther rejected seven articles as they did not include a life cycle impact assessment. This
step yielded 50 articles which form the basis of this literature review.
2.2
Life cycle assessment
The LCA of the insulation systems in this study follows the ISO14044 and ISO14040
standards [10, 11], and covers the following steps: goal and scope deﬁnition, life cycle
inventory analysis, life cycle impact assessment, and interpretation.
The intended application of this study is a comparative LCA of ﬁve project tailored
insulation systems selected on their hygro-thermal performance for an internal insula-
tion retroﬁt of historic buildings. This study provides a climate burden indicator for
these insulation systems, which supports decision-making relating the choice of the most
appropriate solution in renovation projects. This study considers the insulation systems’
related GHG emissions from induced and avoided impacts through the impact category
Global Warming Potential (GWP).
The system boundaries of the insulation systems covered in this study are in ac-
cordance with EN15804:2013 [12]: the production stage (A1-A3), the assembly stage
(A4-A5), the operation energy use stage (B6), and the end-of-life stage (EoL) (C1-C4),
thus, making this a ’cradle-to-grave’ simpliﬁed LCA. The impacts from the life cycle
stages A1-A5 and C1-C4 are considered induced impacts, whereas the impacts from the
==PAGE:15==
2.2. Life cycle assessment

life cycle stage B6 is considered avoided impacts via saved energy consumption for space
heating. Maintenance (B2) and replacement (B4) have been excluded from this LCA,
as the projected service lives of the materials for the insulation systems are longer than
the life cycle deﬁned in this assessment [13–17].
The study seeks to model product-speciﬁc systems by using country and manufacturer-
speciﬁc data through environmental product declarations (EPD) an data sheets when
possible. Background data and additional data has been derived from the ecoinvent 3.4
database. The life cycle inventories (LCI) are modeled as attributional with allocation
at the point of substitution, as this assessment is a comparison of speciﬁc goods which
is in accordance with the ILCD guidelines [18].
The sustainability assessment of the insulation systems is divided into two: The ﬁrst
part is an overall analysis of all possible combinations comprised in the web tool with
the functional unit (F.U.) in alignment with the intended application of the study:
1 m2 insulation system with the equivalent U-value installed as a part of
an energy retroﬁt of a historic building with the lifespan of 30 years from
2020 to 2050.
The second part is an in-depth analysis of two the two case studies with the F.U.:
1 m2 insulation system with the U-value 0.18 W m−2 K installed as a part
of an energy renovation of a historic building with the lifespan of 30 years
from 2020 to 2050.
The chosen U-value for the in-depth analysis is in alignment with the current Danish
Building Regulations for renovations projects [12].
The LCIA method used is CML-IA baseline 3.05 with normalization factor EU25+
2000 [19]. All results will be reported as characterized.
2.2.1
Life cycle inventory
LCIs in this LCA consists of ﬁve insulation systems of varying thickness with 160 possible
installation locations across the seven countries (See Appendix A for locations). These
locations provide coordinates for determining transportation distances in the transporta-
tion scenarios (A4) as well as Heating Degree Days (HDD) proﬁles for determining the
saved energy consumption for avoided impacts (B6). There are three choices of heat-
ing systems per country for assessment of the avoided impact from saved energy con-
sumption. LCIs have been modeled using the SimaPro 8.5.2.0 software and date from
ecoinvent 3.4.

Saved energy consumption
Installation of an insulation system in a historic building aﬀects the building’s energy
performance. Due to reduced transmission loss through the wall, the energy consumption
for providing space heating will be reduced as well. The saved energy consumption is then

The RiBuild web tool
The sustainability module of the RiBuild web tool provides a climate burden indicator
to assist selection of the most appropriate insulation system for retroﬁts of historic
building. In the user interface part, the user is requested to characterize the historic
building for renovation. First, the user is required to deﬁne the renovation location by
choosing a country. Based on the selected country, the user is presented with the choice
of a more precise location in the form of a city. Secondly, the user is required to deﬁne
the heating system of the building. Thirdly, the user has to identify the stone type
and thickness of the existing wall in the building (ﬁgure 2.1). Based on these choices,
the web tool provides a climate burden indicator for the insulation systems in terms of
induced impacts from 1 m2 insulation systems and avoided impacts via the saved energy
consumption for 1 m2 wall section.

3.1.1
Life cycle impact assessment methods
Life cycle assessment is a ﬁeld of study that allows for various methodological approaches
when assessing sustainability performance and environmental impacts through LCIA.
This review found that eight diﬀerent LCIA methods were used in total among all
reviewed studies (ﬁgure 3.2).

3.3.2
Global warming potential as an indicator of sustainability
This study has chosen GWP as the single indicator of the insulation systems’ sustain-
ability performance. This approach might overlook other potentially detrimental aspects

3.3.3
Dynamic development of the energy supply for space heating
The European Council [2] has set goals to increase the use of renewable energy sources
and reduce the GHG emissions towards 2030. In this light, this study examines the
inﬂuence of the use of more sustainable energy sources on the sustainability performance
of the insulation systems. This analysis simulates a dynamic development of the energy
grid which supplies space heating in the period 2020-2050. As the internal insulation
retroﬁt generates a saving in energy consumption, the dynamic development aﬀects the
avoided impacts. A linear regression simulates the dynamic development with the current
energy grid as starting point and 100 % of the energy grid consisting of a sustainable
energy source as the endpoint (See appendix I). The avoided impact from the entire
period is then accumulated and is referred to as dynamic development accumulated
(DDA) analysis.
Two heating systems from each case study have been chosen as examples: District
heating and heat pump for Copenhagen, and electricity and heat pump for Pantelleria.
The current energy grids are referred to as static scenarios. As future energy supplies
for the DDA, second generation biomass has been assumed for district heating and wind
power has been assumed for electricity and heat pump.

4 Discussion
This section discusses aspects of the results of the literature review, the web tool, and
the case studies. In addition, this section addresses study limitations and future work
potential.
4.1
Literature review
This literature review on LCAs of thermal insulation materials in buildings comprised 50
publications. Compared to other reviews on similar topics, it is argued that the number
of publications included is suﬃcient to provide a sound review on how LCA of insulation
materials is carried out.
As most of the studies included in this review were published after 2013, this shows
an increase in interest in LCA of insulation solutions over the latter half of the studied
period from 2010-2018.
8 diﬀerent LCIA methods were used in the reviewed studies. 38 % of the studies
even used more than one LCIA method within the same study, as Monteiro & Freire
[44] who used a case study of insulation materials to assess the robustness of three LCIA
methods. In addition, the review found diﬀerences in LCI modeling framework between
the studies as well. Varying system boundaries such as ’cradle-to-gate’, ’cradle-to-gate’
including operational energy use, ’cradle-to-grave’, and ’cradle-to-grave’ excluding the
use stage were identiﬁed in the studies. The subdivision of life cycle stage varied as well,
e.g. the use stage was both treated as a pay-back period of the induced impact from
material production and as an inducing life cycle stage in itself.
The number of comparative studies and studies that oﬀer no conclusions on the most
impacting life cycle stage shows a greater focus on product comparison of several insu-
lation materials than the sustainability performance of the individual life cycle stages.
This literature review has quantiﬁed the accumulated scientiﬁc knowledge from 50
research articles on how LCA is performed on insulation materials for buildings. The re-
view showed variations in both methodological approaches and LCI modeling framework
between the reviewed studies. This lack of streamlining poses challenges for comparisons
of ﬁndings between studies. This is supported by Schiavoni et al. and Pargana et al. [33,
79], who likewise ﬁnd that variation in system boundaries and diﬀerent methodological
framework complicates comparisons.

4.2
Web tool
In the web tool LCAs of project tailored insulation systems for energy retroﬁts of historic
buildings were carried out to assess their sustainability performance. When considering
the induced impact, the average induced impact showed, that the second, third, and
fourth most appropriate insulation system for energy retroﬁts depended on the country
in which the system is installed. The transportation consequently had a noticeable inﬂu-
ence on the performance of the insulation system. Additionally, the ranking showed, that
decisions regarding the second, third, and fourth most appropriate insulation systems are
diﬀerent from country to country, indicating that geographical location and the associ-
ated transportation distances aﬀect the interpretation of the sustainability performance
of the insulation systems. As most of the materials comprising the insulation systems
where produced in Germany, the ranking of the insulation systems were identical for the
two scenarios. Similarly, the ranking of insulation systems where the same for Belgium,
Denmark, Italy, and Switzerland. These countries are all but Italy neighboring coun-
tries to Germany therefore resulting in smaller average transportation distances than for
Sweden and Latvia. The ranking of the insulation systems were identical for Sweden and
Latvia. This indicates that longer transportation distances inﬂuence the sustainability
performance of the insulation systems diﬀerently than shorter transportation distances.
The avoided impact from saved energy consumption consists of the transmission loss
through the wall section with and without a retroﬁt, the climatic proﬁle of the location
and the GHG intensity of the heating system. A large avoided impact was associated
with either a cold climate, a GHG intensive heating system or a combination of the
two. This shows that the energy grid supplying heating has a considerable inﬂuence on
the sustainability of a retroﬁt in a renovation scenario. This ﬁnding is supported by
Ghose et al. [36] who concluded that maximum beneﬁts only occur if the post-retroﬁt
energy grid is based on high coal generation. As the distribution loss on the energy
grids supplying heating was both an uncertain and sensitive parameter, this showed
that changes in distribution losses would further aﬀect the sustainability performance
of the insulation solutions. Changes in sustainability performance inﬂuence decisions
regarding the energy retroﬁts of historic building, thus, emphasizing the importance of
determining correct distribution losses.
When considering the net impact results, the ranking of the insulation systems
changed between countries and heating systems. However, two insulation systems were
consistent in their ranking. S5 (mineral wool) as the most sustainable solution, and S1
(calcium silicate board) as the least sustainable solution. The diﬀerence in the ranking

4.3
Case studies
The case study considers insulation levels corresponding to BR18 renovation standards
[12]. For Pantelleria, net impacts were positive in the majority of application scenarios
(with the exception of S5 for heat pump and natural gas boiler and all insulation systems
for electricity).
Energy retroﬁts were not a sustainable solution in these application
scenarios. This ﬁnding was supported by Sohn et al. [81] who conclude that high levels
of insulation materials in a single family house is most likely to be environmentally
detrimental if constructed today.
This study uses GWP impact as single indicator for the sustainability performance
of the insulation systems. The choice of GWP as the only impact category can po-
tentially overlook other detrimental aspects addressed by other impact categories. The
ranking of the insulation solution for each impact category included in the used LCIA
method demonstrated this issue. For instance, the ranking of S3 (phenolic foam) was
==PAGE:48==
38
4 Discussion
not robust. S3 ranked the second most sustainable solution with GWP and the third
with the average ranking approach for both case locations. With the impact categories
Fossil Abiotic Depletion (ADP-F) potential and Photo-Chemical Ozone Creation Poten-
tial (POCP), S3 was ranked the least sustainable solution. For Acidiﬁcation Potential
(AP) and Human Toxicity Potential (HT), S3 was ranked fourth (See Appendix H).
These diﬀerences in ranking between impact categories show that the use of GWP as
single indicator indeed might overlook other detrimental aspects. However, using the
GWP impact category allows for greater robustness in the impact data as GWP data is
well documented in the assessment methods [85]. Using an average ranking based on all
impact categories might overlook other detrimental aspects as well, and would not be an
optimum solution either. Inclusion of all impact categories on the other hand ampliﬁes
the complexity of interpretation. Using a single indicator simpliﬁes interpretation and
makes communication of the results easier. Thus, the commissioner of the LCA must
weigh the assessment options and thoroughly consider his or her audience and the con-
text of the LCA to make a qualiﬁed decision on which indicators of sustainability are to
be included and why.
The DDA analysis showed that a development towards a more sustainable energy
grid decreases the advantage of the retroﬁt over time. With the development towards
more sustainable and less GHG intensive energy grids the avoided impacts are diminished
and the induced impacts become more inﬂuential of the overall performance. However,
a decreased avoided impact implies a less GHG intensive energy grid for heating which
in itself is a more sustainable scenario. Sohn et al. [86] emphasized that integrating a
dynamic energy mix in building life cycle assessments is preferable to a static energy
mix, as future projections are available and this provides an indication of the building’s
overall environmental performance against possible future energy mix scenarios. In light
of the reduction of avoided impacts when introducing DDA of energy grids, measures
towards reducing induced impacts from production are needed to improve the overall
sustainability performance of the insulation systems. This approach is supported by
ﬁndings of Ylm´en et al. [73] and Sohn et al. [81]. Another approach would be intro-
ducing recycling solutions, either through the use of recycled materials or improve the
recycling potential when carrying out energy retroﬁts. Ingrao et al. [74] highlighted
the improvement of insulation systems’ environmental performance from using recycled
insulation materials compared to conventional insulation materials.

4.4
Study limitations and future work
This section addresses limitations of this study and there through the future work po-
tential. The limitations have been divided into three themes: methodological framework,
LCI modelling, and calculation setups which will be discussed in that order.
Methodological framework
Within the methodological framework, there are several matters to address. First is the
length of the life-cycle, which is 30 years spanning from 2020-2050. It can easily be
argued that a lifespan of a historic building after a retroﬁt would be prolonged more
than 30 years.
A longer life cycle would aﬀect the net impact through the avoided
impacts as the accumulated energy savings would increase. The length of the life-cycle
in this study has been chosen as a result of a limitation in the procured weather data
set: The weather data adjusted for climate change eﬀects only span from 2020-2050.
The weighing of whether to extend the life cycle or include climate change projection
in estimation of energy savings potential fell out in favor of the latter. In future work
however, an extension of the life cycle would be of interest.
The second matter is the F.U. of 1 m2 wall section. This F.U. does not take into
account other factors contributing to the heating demand of the building, i.e. other parts
of the building envelope or the HVAC systems, which potentially obscures the actual
energy savings from a retroﬁt. However, with the choice of 1 m2 as F.U., it is argued
that the energy savings exclusively due to the new insulation system are isolated, which
is within the scope of this LCA. In addition, building and craftsmanship traditions have
national diﬀerences between the assessed countries which makes 1 m2 a more universally
applicable F.U. for comparisons than including the whole building. This sustainability
assessment is also supposed to be applicable for a large variety of historic buildings
The third is the choice of a single impact category as indicator for sustainability.
This has already been addressed in section 3.3.2 and 4.3.
LCI modelling
The modeling of the inventories of the insulation system materials, heating systems, and
mean of transportation posed several challenges.
For modeling of the insulation system materials, the biggest challenge was the lack
of material data available. Only some of the products had EPDs, and even these were
varying in their level of information detail. Thus, assumptions for the production stage
==PAGE:50==
40
4 Discussion
were made, e.g. no information on waste percentage in the production stage, which
was therefore excluded. The same was the case for waste handling in the EoL stage
why land-ﬁlling was assumed for all materials. In the transportation scenario, trans-
portation by freight truck was assumed for all materials, for the sake of simpliﬁcation.
However, phenolic foam, which is produced in the UK by Kingspan would more likely
be transported some of the way by either train or barge.
Finding exact distribution losses on the energy grids was a challenge too, as this
information is not readily available. In addition to this uncertainty, the distribution
losses are a sensitive parameter as well. Therefore, in potential future elaboration of this
study, focus should be on determining more exact distribution losses.
Calculation setups
For the calculation setups, there are several matters to address.
The ﬁrst is static
calculations as opposed to dynamic simulations. Speciﬁc transmission losses for the wall
section has been calculated statically as U-values with thermal properties derived from
material data sheets. In addition, the U-values have been calculated as homogeneous,
meaning that both mortar and in-homogeneous layers in the wall have not been taken into
account. Dynamic simulations of transmission losses would likely enhance the quality of
the results.
For the calculation of energy savings, the HDD method was applied. However static,
this method oﬀers transparency in the calculation steps opposed to a dynamic building
simulation. A dynamic building simulation would, on the other hand, provide more dy-
namic energy saving results, and it would be recommended to incorporate this approach
in future elaboration of the study. However, a dynamic building simulation would require
a reference building model and, as mentioned previously, building and craftsmanship tra-
ditions have national diﬀerences between the assessed countries. This should be carefully
considered. In addition, the balance point temperature in the HDD calculations was set
to 15 ◦C, which in hindsight probably was too low, as the last 3 ◦C contributed internal
gains of the room would only result in an indoor temperature of 18 ◦C. Using 18 ◦C as
balance point temperature would have been more appropriate.
The last static calculation setup is the static energy mixes for the heating systems.
This has been addressed in section 3.3.3 and 4.3.
The transportation distances have been calculated based on coordinates as the dis-
tance between two points on a sphere projected to a plane. First, this method provides
a beeline distance between two points which in itself results in inaccuracies in the calcu-

lated distances. Second, radius in these calculations has been assumed to be the radius
of Earth at equator which is inaccurate as well since all installation locations are located
considerably north of the equator. However, as exact transportation routes in the spe-

pose uncertainties.
This study did not consider cooling demand for the buildings. However, it is still
relevant, as a number of the potential renovation sites where located in warmer climate
zones. The cooling demand would aﬀect the energy savings, therefore it would be rec-

This study provides no statistical veriﬁcation of the conclusions due to time limi-

5 Conclusion
This study aimed to provide a detailed overview of literature on LCA of insulation mate-
rials and a quantitative sustainability evaluation of hygro-thermally optimized insulation
systems to support decision-making of the most appropriate solution for energy retroﬁts
of historic buildings.
A literature review on 50 publications was carried out to quantify LCAs of thermal
insulation materials used in buildings. The literature review found an increase in the
interest in LCA of insulation systems within the reviewed period, as 86 % of the studies
were published in the latter half of the period of 2010-2018. In addition, the review
showed diﬀerences in both methodological approaches and LCI modelling frameworks
among the studies. These diﬀerences makes comparisons of ﬁndings between studies
diﬃcult. This leads to the conclusion that streamlining of LCA on insulation systems
should be prioritized to enable exploitation of the potential that an increased research
volume oﬀer.
An LCA of project tailored insulation systems was carried out to assess the sustain-
ability performance of insulation solutions for energy retroﬁts of historic buildings. The
analysis of the 213,210 results derived from the web tool showed variations in the sus-
tainability performance of the insulation systems between diﬀerent assessment scenarios.
When including the transportation scenario in the induced impact proﬁle, the choice of
the most appropriate insulation system depended on the country of installation. This
indicated that longer transportation distances inﬂuence the sustainability performance
of the insulation systems diﬀerently than shorter transportation distances. Analysis of
the avoided impacts showed that the combination of a large saved energy consumption
and low impacting heating system resulted in a small avoided impact, whereas the com-
bination of small saved energy consumption and high impacting heating system resulted
in a large avoided impact.
When considering the net impact results, the ranking of
the insulation systems changed between countries and heating systems. However, two
insulation systems were consistently the most sustainable (S5, mineral wool) and least
sustainable solution (S1, calcium silicate board). In some scenarios, insulation retroﬁt
measures were not a sustainable solution as the induced impacts exceeded the avoided
impacts. This situation was most pronounced for Italy, followed by Sweden, and showed
that in general, energy retroﬁts are most sustainable for colder climates and GHG in-
tensive heating systems.
In the case studies, all insulation systems were assessed with BR18 insulation levels.
==PAGE:54==
44
5 Conclusion
For the Danish case, all insulation systems were sustainable solutions for energy retroﬁts.
However, in the Italian case, some solutions had positive net impacts. For both cases, the
most substantial beneﬁt was found for high impacting heating systems such as natural
gas boiler (DK) and electricity (IT). The DDA analysis supported this and showed
that the sustainability of the insulation solutions was diminished when the energy grid
supplying heating developed towards more sustainable and less GHG intensive energy
carriers.
When using GWP as single indicator for sustainability performance, conclusions on
the least and most sustainable solutions were robust. For the remaining solutions this was
not the case. However, the inclusion of more impact categories in an assessment ampliﬁes
the complexity of interpretation. For the decision-makers and commissioners of the LCA,
these options of easy to communicate conclusions and more complex interpretations
should be balanced against his or her audience and the context of the conclusions.
Across all analyses, S5 (mineral wool, vapor barrier, and gypsum board) was the
least GHG intensive and thereby the most sustainable solution.
Conclusions on the
remaining insulation systems depended on which life-cycle stages were incorporated in
the assessment, e.g. whether the transportation and the avoided impacts were included.
For decision-makers who commission the LCA, these methodological choices must be
carefully considered as they will aﬀect recommendations on the most appropriate solu-
tion for renovation of historic buildings. Both the overall analysis and the case study
analyses with static energy mixes, showed that internal insulation retroﬁts were not a
sustainable solution in all scenarios. This ﬁnding was emphasized by the DDA analysis:
A development towards a more sustainable energy grid decreases the advantage of the
retroﬁt over time. With the development towards more sustainable and less GHG inten-
sive energy grids the avoided impacts are diminished and the induced impacts become
more inﬂuential of the overall performance. This elucidates that the environmental jus-
tiﬁcation of internal insulation depends on the geographical location of installation and
the type of heating system supplying the building being retroﬁtted. As the inﬂuence
of induced impacts on the overall sustainability of the insulation systems increases, the
implementation of recycling solutions and measures for reducing production impacts are
needed to further improve the sustainability performance of energy retroﬁts in historic
buildings.

6 Recommendations
The following recommendations concerns the web tool and go beyond the scope of this
project. The RiBuild research project is thought to be the recipient of these recommen-
dations. Amelioration potential within the scope of this project has been addressed in
section 4.4.
Expansion of the web tool
The web tool developed for this project comprised ﬁve insulation systems, three heat-
ing system options for each country and ﬁve choices of existing wall types. Thus, the
ﬁrst recommendation is an expansion of the web tool to increase its functionality and
adaptability. This expansion would entail:
⋄more insulation systems to choose from
⋄more heating systems to choose from for each country (preferably with dynamic
simulation of the energy grid)
⋄more choices of existing wall types and thicknesses, perhaps oﬀering the user the
possibility of deﬁning the thickness manually
⋄dynamic simulation of savings in energy consumption instead of static calculations
⋄extension of the lifespan beyond 2020-2050
⋄inclusion of a price parameter (both as price for the insulation system and monetary
savings due to saved energy consumption)
Multi-criteria indicator
When venturing into a renovation project of a historic building, other factors than the
sustainability performance of the insulation systems plays a role. One of them, which is
the raison d’ˆetre for this project, is hygro-thermal optimization because of the increased
mold growth risk in these types of renovation projects. Therefore, a multi-criteria indica-
tor for choosing the most appropriate insulation solution is proposed. Five optimization
categories to be comprised in the indicator have been identiﬁed:

⋄space optimization
⋄hygro-thermal optimization
⋄sustainability optimization
⋄price optimization

The space optimization criterion should be included as a retroﬁt conserving the
largest percentage of the original ﬂoor area is preferred. This criterion would then be
based on the thickness of the insulation system.
The hygro-thermal optimization criterion should be included, as mentioned previ-
ously, to ensure the smallest risk of mold growth. This criterion would then be based on
the most hygro-thermally safe insulation system.
The sustainability optimization criterion should be included to ensure a sustainable
retroﬁt. This criterion has been elaborated throughout this project.
The price optimization criterion should be included as inexpensive retroﬁts might be
preferred by the commissioner of the renovation project. This criterion would then be
based on the price of the insulation system.
The energy consumption optimization criterion should be included as large reductions
in space heating demand, and there through monetary savings, might be preferred by
commissioner of the renovation project. This criterion would then be based on the saved
energy consumption.
These criteria should have diﬀerent priority and weight in the indicator. Either this
should be deﬁned by the RiBuild research team or the commissioner could be oﬀered
the possibility of deﬁning the priority him or herself. A proposed prioritization would be
hygro-thermal optimization, sustainability optimization, energy consumption optimiza-
tion, space optimization, and price optimization in that order.
