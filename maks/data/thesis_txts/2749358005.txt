Abstract
A result of the green energy transition is an increasing share of renewable energy sources in the energy
systems adding more uncertainty and complexity to the modelling of the energy system. To combat
this, a decision-based forecasting model, Smart, ”Predict, then Optimize” is implemented for both
the stakeholder and the system’s case to improve the decision-making under uncertainty. For the
system, the ”Economic Dispatch Problem” has been investigated in both a copperplate and nodal
formulation, while for the stakeholder, the producer’s problem for a hybrid power plant has been
investigated. For the SPO framework to be applicable, the problems are transformed using either
duality or reformulations. SPO is compared with several conventional regression models, including
linear regression, linear regression with either ridge or lasso regularisation and random forest regression.
Firstly, it is shown that the SPO model outperforms conventional regression models when only an
uncertain cost is considered. Secondly, it is demonstrated that it is possible to use the SPO method
on problems where uncertainty is located in the constraints while still outperforming some of the
conventional regression methods. Lastly, a joint approach of SPO and a conventional regression
model is proposed for cases with uncertainty in both the objective function and the constraints. These
demonstrations show that decision-based prediction can be used in energy system modelling and may
be a part of the solution for the increased complexity related to the new production patterns.

1.
Introduction
It has been clear to scientists for a long time that something has to be done to stop the emerging
climate crisis. To avoid the worst irreversible damages caused by climate change, global greenhouse
gases need to be reduced [1], and this aﬀects various sectors. One sector that is widely aﬀected is the
energy sector, for which 81% of the total global energy supply came from fossil fuels in 2022 resulting
in a greenhouse gas emission of almost 35,000 MtCO2eq [2]. To decrease these emissions, countries
worldwide have increased their focus on renewable energy sources and international agreements have
been made. The European Commission aims to reach net zero emissions in the energy sector by 2050
and states that the member states ”have to transform their energy systems into a fully integrated,
digitalised and competitive EU energy market that is based largely on renewable sources” [3]. Of
the total electricity generation in the EU, wind and solar production reached a share of 30% in the
ﬁrst half of 2024 [4]. This is great news for the green transition, but there are also some challenges
linked to it. A higher share of wind and solar energy in the energy system results in less stable
power generation and a higher degree of uncertainty in the energy system since they depend highly
on weather conditions. Furthermore, parts of the transport sectors are being electriﬁed, and at the
same time it seems that people are becoming more aware of how the energy price is aﬀected by
consumption, especially after the recent energy crisis which drove the prices up. These circumstances
lead to additional uncertainty added to the energy system with the demand also being uncertain.
In electricity markets, such as the day-ahead market, optimisation problems can be used for decision-
making from both a market operator’s and a producer’s viewpoint. For a market operator, the goal is
to operate the electricity system at the lowest possible cost, and the major uncertainties aﬀecting
the problem are demand and renewable energy production. For a producer, the goal is to maximise
the proﬁt from generation making the price of electricity the main uncertainty. If the producer has
renewable generation units, the generation level can also be a major uncertainty. Each stakeholder
has to make decisions under uncertainty, which aﬀects their objectives. Until now, the reliability of
conventional generators has kept the scheduling relatively simple but with the increasing uncertainty
in renewables and demand, the scheduling gets more complex.
Conventionally, there are diﬀerent ways of dealing with uncertainty in energy systems. Stochastic
optimisation uses scenarios to describe the uncertain parameter and can be used to make informed
decisions. The downside of stochastic optimisation is, however, the large number of scenarios needed
to properly describe the distribution of uncertainty, and this results in large and complex problems,
which are computationally hard to solve. Instead of solving a stochastic problem, point predictions of
the uncertain parameters can also be used in a deterministic version of the problem. Using point
predictions, the problem is easily solved, but then the scheduling is very dependent on the prediction
accuracy.
Machine learning (ML) models can be utilised to create point predictions of uncertain parameters.
Here ML models leverage contextual information to minimise the prediction error of the uncertain
parameter. The prediction and optimisation are often solved separately in a predict-then-optimise
(PO) approach. The ML model does thereby not consider the downstream optimisation problem,
which handles the core of the problem, namely the decision-making. Using the PO approach it is
assumed more accurate predictions will result in more accurate decision-making. The downside of this
approach is that a small prediction error can lead to suboptimal decisions, which can result in high
costs compared to the optimal decisions in the case of full information of the uncertain parameter.
Instead of focusing on minimising prediction error, a decision-based approach can be taken, and
this has opened up a new research area where ML models focusing on the task at hand instead of
the prediction accuracy are developed. The ML models are thereby created with the downstream
optimisation problem in mind, and the objective is no longer to reduce prediction error but instead to

reduce the ﬁnal decision error and the resulting cost. In this study one of these models, an approach
named Smart ”Predict, then Optimise” (SPO) is used. SPO combines forecasting and contextual
optimisation into one decision-based framework as a further development of the PO approach and
can be used on linear optimisation problems with uncertainty in the objective function [5]. In this
report, it is explored if SPO can be used on energy systems from diﬀerent stakeholder perspectives,
and since there can be several uncertainties included and the uncertain parameters can appear in the
constraints of these problems, diﬀerent methods are used to reformulate the problems to ﬁt to the
SPO-framework. section 1.1 explains more about the motivation for using decision-based predictions,
section 1.2 gives a short introduction to electricity markets, and section 1.3 gives a summary of some
of the diﬀerent decision-based prediction methods that have been developed emphasising why it is
chosen to focus on SPO in this report.
1.1
Motivation
In many real-world occasions where decisions must be taken in complex systems with uncertainty,
the standard approach is the so-called ”Predict-then-Optimise” (PO) approach. Here, the prediction
of the uncertain parameter and the optimisation based on the prediction are dealt with separately
due to the possible complex nature of both problems. The prediction of the uncertain parameter is
conventionally based on ML models focused on minimising the prediction error, while the optimisation
deterministically handles the decision-making. This two-step approach has few complications if the
prediction is good. However, a small error in the predictions can result in even bigger errors in the
decisions of the optimisation problem.
The idea of decision-based prediction is to take the optimisation problem into account in the forecasting
model and thereby combine the two steps of the PO-approach. The objective of the forecasting
model is then to minimise the decision error instead of the prediction error. One can ask what the
diﬀerence is between the conventional PO approach and the new decision-based approach? In this
section, examples using illustrations from the article ”Smart Predict, then Optimise” [5] are presented
to indicate the main diﬀerences.
Suppose an uncertain cost vector ˆc produces an optimal decision vector w∗(ˆc) considering a polyhedral
feasible region S. The left side of ﬁgure 1.1 illustrates two predictions ˆcA and ˆcB, of the cost vector c.
Here the dotted circle illustrates their prediction error (least squares). The example highlights how the
two predictions result in two diﬀerent decision vectors w∗(ˆcA) and w∗(ˆcB), although their prediction
error is the same. One of the predictions (A) leads to the optimal decision, which would be the
result of any prediction in the grey area, while the other (B) ends up in another corner point of the
feasible region (outside of the grey area). This simple example illustrates the point that minimising
the decision error is not always equivalent to minimising the prediction error.

accurate, but focuses on getting the predictions to point in the right directions leading to an accuracy
in the decisions.
1.2
Electricity markets
This section brieﬂy introduces electricity markets, market participants and the assumptions made in
this investigation.
Electricity is traded like any other commodity, based on supply and demand. The electricity market
involves three main participants: Consumers, producers, and the market operator. Consumers include
large industrial entities and retailers who supply private households. The electricity demand is assumed
inﬂexible for entities such as critical industries or households, meaning they require the needed
electricity regardless of price.
A producer is a market entity which owns one or several generation facilities. These could be
conventional, e.g. coal or gas-ﬁred combined heat and power plants or renewable like solar or wind
farms. The objective of an economically rational producer is to maximise their proﬁt.
Market operators are non-proﬁt entities responsible for collecting bids and ensuring market eﬃciency.
In most electricity markets, the merit order principle is used to determine the supply of electricity and
the market clearing price of electricity. The merit order ranks generation bids by increasing price. The
intersection between supply and demand sets the market equilibrium, determining the cleared volume
of electricity and the corresponding electricity price. Until the delivery of power at day d, several
energy markets (futures, day-ahead, intra-day and balancing) are available to ensure that production
and demand are equal. This study focuses primarily on the day-ahead (DA) market.
The DA market, as the name suggests, is cleared one day before the actual delivery of electricity and
is responsible for most of the scheduled delivery. The oﬀers and bids are submitted by producers and
consumers on an hourly basis, meaning the market is cleared using the merit order principle for each
hour of day d. Here uniform pricing is implemented, meaning all producers are compensated with the
same price. The oﬀers, specifying quantities and prices, must be submitted before the gate closes on
day d −1. Under perfect competition, these oﬀers are based on the marginal cost of production. For
renewable energy producers, the marginal cost of production is assumed to be zero.
The DA schedule outlines the amount of power a producer has committed to generate for each hour of
the day, as well as the prices they are compensated with. Due to the merit order principle, renewable
producers are assumed to have their oﬀers accepted. The intermittent nature of renewable energy
forces producers to make decisions under uncertainty, often based on forecasts. This is the main
problem investigated in the producer’s problem. The market operator, e.g., Nordpool for the Nordic
and Baltic synchronous zones, is responsible for the market clearing. With increasing renewable
penetration, the market operator also has to make decisions under uncertainty. The market clearing
is investigated both as a simpliﬁed copperplate model and using nodal representation.

Related literature
As mentioned, a lot of new research has been published in the ﬁeld of decision-based forecasting. In
the preparation of the investigation in this report, diﬀerent decision-based prediction approaches have
been taken into account, although only SPO is used for testing. This section highlights SPO and 2

other methods that have shown potential and these are brieﬂy described together with their main
advantages and drawbacks.
The article ”From predictive to prescriptive analysis” [6] was published in 2014 and seems to be one
of the pioneer articles in the ﬁeld of decision-based forecasting. This article is focused on conditional
stochastic optimisation problems, and they combine ideas from machine learning and operations
research to propose a framework where the aim is to ﬁnd ”predictive prescriptions” that are as close
to the full-information optimal decisions as possible. The predictive prescriptions can be found in
diﬀerent ways, but the main idea is to use weight functions that are derived from data and inspired by
diﬀerent machine-learning techniques in the construction process. This method shows good results
for a large number of samples, and applies to a range of diﬀerent decision problems, meaning that it
could probably also be used for energy system modelling. However, one concern, if it were to be used
on energy systems, is that the whole problem, including the prediction part has to be run each time a
prediction is made. For energy systems, this could be a possible drawback, since the schedules are
needed daily, and it would require time and computational power to run the prediction model every
day.
Another article that provides a promising approach is ”Task-based End-to-end Model Learning in
Stochastic Optimization” [7] from 2017. This article is in the same way focused on the task error, but
here, a straightforward stochastic gradient descent approach is used. Since the problem is that the
distribution of the uncertain parameter given the data is unknown, an algorithm is designed where the
decisions from one distribution are found, and the cost from these decisions are then compared to the
true cost. The model can then be updated accordingly and the process can be repeated until the cost
is minimised. This method is used on diﬀerent problems including energy system models that have
to do with scheduling and storage, and it could thereby be interesting to investigate. However, one
drawback of using this method is that it diﬀerentiates through the arg min-operator and is therefore
only applicable to problems with strongly convex objective functions and constraints. It would thereby
be diﬃcult to use this method on the optimisation problems that are tested in this report., without
changing the problems signiﬁcantly.
In relation to the two methods mentioned here, the SPO method that is presented in the article
”Smart Predict, then Optimize” [5] uses empirical risk minimisation, and is thereby based on the
same framework as the conventional PO-approach. The diﬀerence is that a new loss function is
proposed focusing on the task error. Contrary to the other methods mentioned, it can be used on
linear optimisation models and the model does not need to be trained each time a prediction is made.
It is required for SPO that the optimisation model tested is on a speciﬁc form with uncertainty in the
objective, but in the investigation in this report, this requirement is challenged.
Several other decision-based approaches than these three have been proposed by diﬀerent researchers
in recent years and literature reviews are made constructing an overview of these where they are
categorised into diﬀerent ML and optimisation ﬁelds [8].

introduce slack variables. Either of these methods creates problems with SPO. A quadratic penalty
would mean the objective function is no longer linear, violating one of the main assumptions behind
SPO. The use of slack variables does not solve the problem either, as the slack variables are added
to both the objective function and the constraint in question - thereby still having the uncertain
parameter in the constraints.

4.
Method
In this chapter, the data utilised for the investigation is presented, and the general treatment of data
and any assumptions made are also stated. Further, the experimental machine learning approach is
presented. This includes a quick introduction to the feature selection methods and the modelling
workﬂow that leads to a base model for each problem. Lastly, the tests that the base model is used
for are explained, and the models used for comparison are introduced.
4.1
Data
As for any method based on contextual information, the foundation of a good model is a good dataset,
comprised of features and targets. The same applies to the SPO method and this investigation.
The features utilised in this investigation can be divided into three main categories: Production and
consumption, power prices and climate.
Production and consumption
The historical settlement data for production and consumption in Denmark is accessed from Energi
Data Service [11]. The historical production for central power plants, solar farms, and on- and oﬀshore
wind farms includes consumption, transmission and grid losses. Transmission to neighbouring bidding
zones is also included. Historical forecasts for the production of wind and solar power [12] and total
scheduled generation [13] are gathered. The forecasts for day d, are published on day d −1, though
after gate closure. For the sake of this investigation, this is ignored and they are assumed available.
Power prices
Power prices can be diﬀerentiated between spot prices for the day-ahead (DA) market and real-time
(RT) prices for the balancing market. Spot prices are obtained for Denmark’s bidding zones, DK1
and DK2, along with neighbouring bidding zones in Germany, Sweden and Norway [14]. Balancing
prices are collected for Denmark and each bidding zone in the Nordic synchronous zone, i.e., Sweden,
Norway and Finland [15]. The features that have been mentioned until now are used as lagged
features. This means that for a prediction on day d, the available features would be from the day
before i.e., d −1. The reason for doing this is that some features are highly correlated with the target
e.g. spot prices in DK1 and DK2. Further, in a realistic setting, it would not be possible to utilise
data from day d.
Climate
Historical climate data for Copenhagen and Aarhus are used as proxies for DK1 and DK2 and are
provided by DMI [16]. Climate data is used for day d, as it is assumed that an accurate weather
forecast could be utilised instead. Climate data consists of, among others, temperature, wind speed
and precipitation. Climate data is included as both renewable energy and demand are inﬂuenced by
the weather.
To ensure continuity across diﬀerent data sources, all data is collected in an hourly resolution and
deﬁned by a timestamp in UTC. In the case of missing data, linear interpolation is used if the gap is
less than 4 hours, otherwise, the sample is removed. Similarly, if a sample does not contain the full
24 hours, it is removed. In addition to the mentioned features, the temporal aspect of the data is
expanded. As electricity markets have some natural temporal and seasonal tendencies it could prove
beneﬁcial to the model. Therefore, features such as season, month, day and hour are added.
All features are scaled to a 0-1 range by min-max scaling. The minimum and maximum value of each
feature is found from the training set to avoid data leakage between the training and testing sets. In
total, there are 126 features available. The ﬁnal dataset contains 988 samples of 24 hours, spanning

4.2
Machine Learning Approach
A crucial part of any machine learning process is ﬁnding an eﬀective and well-performing model. This
section presents the approach used in this investigation. The approach can broadly be split into three
categories: Feature selection, feature transformation (or dimensional reduction) and hyperparameter
tuning. To make well-informed decisions for these categories a strategy is needed to assess the
modelling performance. Here, cross-validation is used to assess the general performance.

==PAGE:40==
Cross-validation
To assess the modelling performance of each modelling choice a 10-fold time-series cross-validation is
performed. A rolling time series cross-validation is used to emulate the model’s performance over
time, as more data becomes available. For this, the training set is split into 10 folds. Figure 4.3
visualises how the training set is gradually extended for each fold. The validation set is always the
next fold, emulating the most recently available data.
Figure 4.3: Rolling time series cross-validation for training set
This ensures that the natural temporal order of the data is preserved. The expected SPO loss is found
across all folds. The cross-validation therefore showcases how a given model generalises to the data.
Any choice regarding the model setup is made based on cross-validation.
Feature Selection
Features are the basis of any ML model, but they need to be carefully selected. Having many features,
there is a high chance that some features are not signiﬁcant for the performance of the model. Having
too many features will increase the computational time and possibly reduce the performance. A
feature selection process is therefore needed to determine the best subset of features for the given
model. In the case of SPO, this is a necessity, given the size of the coeﬃcient matrix B ∈Rd×p, with
p being the number of features for each hour and d being the number of the decision variables for
each hour. As the SPO model ﬁts the relationship between each input feature p to each decision d in
each hour, the number of coeﬃcients grows substantially by the inclusion of an extra feature. As the
number of decisions d and hours are based on the problem, it is only possible to change the number
of features p.
As the hypothesis class H(x) is linear, a linear correlation between features and targets is expected
to prove beneﬁcial. However, in the article ”Smart Predict, the Optimize” [5], SPO is also shown
to work well with non-linear data. For the feature selection in this investigation, a set of feature
selection techniques are assessed, and these techniques are shortly explained in the following.
Feature - target correlation
Pearson’s correlation coeﬃcient is calculated for each combination of target(s) and features. The
objective is to remove features which do not have a linear relationship with the target. If the absolute
linear correlation between a feature and the target is below 0.75, then the feature is disregarded. In
the case of multiple targets, either a feature-target correlation of 0.75, or a mean correlation of 0.6 is
needed, otherwise the feature is disregarded.
Pairwise correlation
Since most features are present from several bidding zones, there are naturally some highly correlated
features, e.g. mean temperature or spot prices in DK1 and DK2. If two features are highly correlated,
keeping just one of them is in many cases enough, as they provide related information. The objective
30
Decision-based Prediction in Day-Ahead Electricity Markets
==PAGE:41==
of this method is to remove redundant features. Pearson’s correlation coeﬃcient is calculated between
all combinations of features. If two features are strongly correlated, i.e. have an absolute correlation
coeﬃcient over 0.6, then one of the features is removed. The feature with the lowest variance is
removed. Variance is used as a metric for how much information, the feature holds.
Mutual information
Mutual information (MI) measures the dependence between two variables. Contrary to Pearson’s
correlation, it is not limited to a linear dependence but can measure non-linear relationships as well.
Mutual information is implemented using scikit-learn [19]. Again the objective for this method is
to remove features which do not provide information for the target. Through experimentation, an
MI-score of above 0.6 between a feature-target set was found appropriate as a threshold. In the case
of multiple targets, an MI-score of 0.4 is used as the mean threshold.
Shapley values
Shapley values or SHAP [20], is a game theory-based approach to decompose a particular prediction
into the contributions from each feature used. Given a set of predictions, the average importance
of a feature can be determined by the aggregated and normalised contributions. To access the
contributions an SPO model is trained on a subset of the train set. The features which cumulatively
have attributed with at least 90% of the importance are chosen.
Greedy algorithm
Since multiple methods are available to determine the best feature set, a greedy algorithm is designed
to ﬁnd the most optimal feature set. Each feature selection method is utilised separately to produce
a reduced feature set. The performance of the feature set is assessed, as mentioned earlier, by
cross-validation using the SPO loss of the SPO model as an error metric. The feature selection
method and reduced feature set with the lowest expected SPO loss are chosen as the new baseline.
The remaining feature selection methods are then used on the reduced feature set and the expected
SPO loss is again found by cross-validation. The algorithm iterates until the expected SPO loss does
not decrease. Then the feature selection method(s) and ﬁnal reduced feature set are determined.
Dimensionality Reduction
In addition to the feature selection process, a feature transformation is also investigated. Here
Principal Component Analysis (PCA) is utilised to reduce the dimensions of the feature set. The idea
of PCA is to transform the data into a lower dimensional space consisting of uncorrelated variables
named principal components (PCs). The PCs are the eigenvectors of the covariance matrix of the
data, and their corresponding eigenvalues are indications of how much of the variance is explained.
PCs are thereby linear combinations of the initial features and one can get as many PCs as there are
features. The ﬁrst PC contains the most variance or information from the initial features, the second
PC contains as much as possible of the information that is left and so on. Thereby, the PCs retain as
much variance as possible. Note that the PCs are not very interpretable, but are just a way to lower
the dimensions while keeping as much of the information from the initial features as possible[21]. For
the investigation in this project, PCA can make sense in the same way as the feature selection since
the model carries a lot of complexity and lowering the dimensions can reduce the computational time
and may also help the model performance, for instance, if it tends to overﬁt. However, PCA can also
simplify the information in the dataset too much and lower the model performance. In the process of
ﬁnding a good base model, PCA is tested on the model where 5 diﬀerent thresholds for the required
variance explanations are set. Cross-validation is then used to evaluate the model in the same way as
for the feature selection but here for each PCA threshold. Scikit learn is used to implement the PCA
in Python[22].

4.3.1
Regression models for comparison
One of the main motivations for decision-based forecasting is the increased performance when
considering the downstream optimisation problem. To compare the performance of the SPO model,
conventional regression models are needed. The conventional approach is a sequential framework
consisting of a regression model and an optimisation problem. Each target is predicted separately and
used as input to the optimisation models.

As H(x) is assumed linear for SPO, a linear regression (LR) model is the closest and most important
comparison. The linear regression model is extended with Ridge and Lasso regularisation.
In the article ”Smart Predict, then Optimize” [5], SPO is compared to a random forest (RF) regression
model, where it was shown that SPO in some circumstances can perform as well, if not better, than
RF. Random forest regression is, alongside decision trees (DT) another tree-based regression model,
used as a comparison model.
The regression models will as mentioned predict each uncertainty separately. For OPF this entails
predicting the demand at each node, while it for the producer’s problem entails predicting the wind
production and electricity price. To be comparable to SPO, the comparison models will also be

To evaluate and compare the performance of the SPO model and the other regression models, the
SPO loss is used as a metric.
4.3.2
Stochastic models for comparison
In chapter 1, stochastic optimisation was mentioned as one of the main and widely used techniques
for solving optimisation problems under uncertainty. A comparison between stochastic optimisation
and SPO could therefore be insightful. As all problems are based on the DA market only, it can be
problematic to set up stochastic formulations of the problems. This is caused by the fact that a
one-stage stochastic formulation cannot be found since the equality constraints, i.e. power balances,
cannot be satisﬁed across multiple scenarios.
Instead, simple two-stage stochastic problems introducing balancing variables are formulated for
both problems as presented in section 3.2.5 and section 3.3.5. To be comparable to the original
problem formulations, the objective of the two-stage problem needs to be focused on the DA market.
Therefore ancillary services are not modelled, but instead, the second stage will only focus on the
diﬀerence between the DA schedule and the realised scenario. The training set is used for in-sample
scenarios, where each sample is a scenario. The stochastic problem returns a single DA schedule
to use across all test sample, while SPO and conventional regression models create a DA schedule
to each test sample. To compare the models an out-of-sample performance is found by solving the
balancing part of the stochastic problem formulation for each model.
4.4
Robustness test
Testing the base model and comparing it to the other models can show the model performance in
”normal” circumstances, but to analyse how the model performs in unexpected circumstances and to
see how stable the model performance is, robustness tests are performed on the model. These are also
performed on the conventional regression models so that the stability of the models can be compared.
One of the main ﬁndings in the article ”Smart Predict, then Optimise” [5] was the performance
of SPO in a series of experiments and the robustness tests are inspired by these experiments. The
diﬀerent tests are explained in the following.
Number of training samples
In the ﬁrst test, the number of training samples is gradually increased to see how the amount of
information aﬀects the model’s performance. Here the model’s need for historical data is investigated.
From the knowledge of the uncertainty distributions shown in section 4.1, it could be expected
that the models perform better with more training data for EDP and OPF, as the train and test
distributions are very similar for the net demand.
For this robustness test, the test samples are kept at the last 198 samples. A gap of 50 samples is
used, so there is time between the train and test set. This is done so any local trends in samples
close to each other do not give any advantage to a small training set. The training set is expanded
backwards in time i.e. the larger the training set, the more historical data is used and the train sizes
used are 100, 300, 500 and 740 samples.

generated from a Gaussian distribution, N(0, 1) and scaled by the standard deviation of the variable.

The 8 features from MI + Feature Target that were presented in section 5.1.1 are used to test the
SPO model using Lasso regularisation with γ = 0.1. The ﬁrst observation that is made is related to
the shape of c. Using the SPO method explained in chapter 2 uncritically with c, as it was explained
in section 3.2.3, as the uncertainty yields a fairly good solution when the SPO loss is used as metric.
However, the SPO loss is calculated using w that includes the dual decision variables λ and µG for
EDP and additionally µF and σ1 for OPF. These are multiplied by P D and P G (and P F for OPF)
in the objective function. Since these are all predicted in ˆc, the model can choose to try to get µG
values up by producing to the max capacity of the generators by predicting a lower P G. For OPF the
same thing can happen with µF and P F .
Since the capacities of the generators and the transmission lines are ﬁxed in real life, predicting these
does not make sense. Two diﬀerent approaches are tested to investigate how this can be solved. The
ﬁrst is to ﬁx the capacities after ˆc is found but before the SPO loss is found. For EDP this means
ﬁxing P G. This is done to see how wrong the predictions for the capacities are and how much it
aﬀects the SPO loss. The other more complicated approach is to set extra constraints into the ERM
for SPO (2.9) to ﬁx the part of ˆc that should be constant. This way, the capacities are restricted
when the prediction is made, and the model can set the variables in w(ˆc) accordingly. The SPO loss
distribution for these 3 variations of the SPO loss is shown in ﬁgure 5.1. In the box plot here and all
box plots from here on, outliers are removed. This is done to better show the main distribution of
the loss, since most models have some high outliers.

after the prediction and where they are ﬁxed in the ERM constraints

6.
Discussion
The previous sections have investigated the implementation of the SPO framework in energy systems,
with a speciﬁc focus on the DA market. Here both stakeholder perspectives have been considered.
The system is considered in the market clearing problem, for a copperplate version (EDP) and a
nodal version (OPF), while the producer has been considered in the producer’s problem. The main
learnings from these investigations are summarised and discussed here.
6.1
EDP and OPF
Generally, the results presented for EDP show very promising model performances for the SPO
framework in terms of SPO loss. SPO obtains a lower expected loss than both LR and RF, but in
most instances, the SPO framework has a slightly higher loss than the linear regression with Ridge
regularisation. However, when noise is added to the feature data, the SPO model performance is
more stable across the diﬀerent levels of noise than it is for most conventional models.
For OPF, the optimisation model is more complex and this makes the ﬁtting of SPO more complex.
Since the capacities are ﬁxed in the constraints for both EDP and OPF, the main diﬀerence in c is
that the demand is split into ﬁve nodes instead of one. The SPO model for OPF seems to prefer fewer
features than for EDP since pairwise correlation is added as a feature selection technique, resulting
in only 2 features. The low number of features is beneﬁcial for the conventional linear regression
model, as it consequently outperforms SPO in the OPF case. This is further backed by the learnings
from testing polynomial features on EDP. Here the same two features were used as the base and the
performance of LR was improved. The expected SPO loss for OPF is 33,808 DKK which corresponds
to 1.38% of the expected total cost, and this is a bit higher than for EDP where the expected loss of
the SPO model corresponds to 0.75% of the expected total cost. Both losses are low compared to
the expected total cost, showing that the model performance in general is good. Since there are some
big ﬂuctuations in the net demand at the end of the test period, it can be shown that the losses are
very much higher for this period for all models, thereby reducing the test period by removing the last
sample results in even lower losses for both problems. The relative diﬀerence between the diﬀerent
model performances, however, seems to be the same, but this shows how the model performances are
quite dependent on the stability of the test set.
The robustness tests for both EDP and OPF show that the implemented SPO model is quite robust
and can handle diﬀerent circumstances. The noise experiment showed that the SPO model is much
more stable than the linear regression alternatives for both cases, while the training set size and
the polynomial features experiments show that SPO has stable performance comparable to the
conventional linear and RF models. Although both models show promising results, the results for
EDP are more convincing, and this is probably a result of the less complex c. One aspect that could
have been interesting is to test combinations of noisy data, polynomial feature data and smaller
training set sizes as done in ”Smart Predict, then Optimize” [5]. Here the SPO method is tested on
simpler problems in the form of the shortest path problem and portfolio optimisation. There, the SPO
model seems to show signiﬁcantly better performance than the comparison models when the training
set size is low and there is a high polynomial degree on the feature data. From the results shown
in this report, one could imagine that some of the same conclusions could be drawn when testing
combinations of diﬀerent circumstances. Generally, it seems that for OPF it is harder to beat other
models such as LR, Ridge and RF, and here it could also be interesting to test the robustness further.

6.3
Reformulation of problems
As it was explained in chapter 2, the SPO method is developed for linear optimisation problems with
uncertainty in the objective function. The initial idea of the SPO method is to use it on problems
that have an objective of cT w in their primal form, where c is the uncertain cost vector. Testing
the SPO framework on more complex optimisation models such as EDP/OPF and the producer’s
problem, that are initially not on the required form was expected to be a bit diﬃcult, ﬁrstly because
of the high dimensions but also because reformulations are required to get them on the form that
is needed for SPO. There are therefore some issues that should be considered when using the SPO
framework on the energy system problems.
One drawback with using the SPO framework on EDP and OPF is that the dual formulation of the
objective function makes the interpretation of the results harder compared to the primal form. For
example, as discussed in 5.1 and 5.2 sequences of c need to be ﬁxed in constraints added to the
ERM problem for SPO. Otherwise, the model can predict in a way where dual variables µG and µF
can be higher than 0, suggesting congestion, although the generators and the lines are in reality
not congested. It is thereby important to ensure that if there are known parameters in c, they are
constrained so that the SPO model does not create unrealistic scenarios to gain a better objective
value.
Furthermore, for OPF, a negative demand was in some instances predicted, and here it can be
discussed if it should be restricted to be positive in the ERM or not. In the same way as for the
capacities, negative demand can create unrealistic scenarios, because the negative demand then acts
as an extra generation unit in the power balance. Since the decisions are the main output of the
SPO model, it is probably more correct to ensure that the demand is not negative. Looking at the
predictions for the diﬀerent regression models, the demand is always predicted as zero in the nodes
where the true demand is zero, while it is quite similar to the real demand in the nodes where the
demand is higher than zero. It can thereby be discussed if the demand should be further restricted to
zero in the nodes, where demand is in reality not possible. This could be interesting to investigate as
a further development.
The discussion of when to restrict c touches the nature of the SPO model. The idea is namely, that
the decisions should come as close to the optimal decisions as possible using the SPO loss function,
and the predictions in c should be designed to induce good decisions. However, in cases such as EDP
and OPF, the dual variables µG and µF are dependent on the capacities P G and P F and should not
be able to get a value from a predicted capacity and then be multiplied by the original capacity value,
when the SPO loss is calculated.

For the producer’s problem, one approach is tried, where constraints restrict parts of c. As opposed

closer to the desired c but the decisions are not similar to the true optimal decisions. The restrictions

increasing the complexity of the restriction. Generally, from the results in this report, the added

simple.
The complications that create the need for extra constraints in the SPO ERM formulation come
from the added complexity of using problems that are not initially in the format required for SPO.
Problems such as the shortest path problem where the decisions only depend on the uncertain cost
are more interpretable in the SPO form since the uncertainty is not a restriction in the problem and
only aﬀects the decisions. This would correspond to using SPO on an economic dispatch problem
where the production cost is uncertain, and the decisions would be which generator to produce with
the demand known. For the producer’s problem, it corresponds to the case where only the DA price
is uncertain.
Including the balancing market when analysing the results for the DA schedule makes the results
easier to interpret for some cases. For EDP and OPF, testing the production values pG and the ﬂows
θ in the balancing market are more comparable to the original optimisation problem, than only using
the SPO loss, as it is based on the dual variables. Comparing the models in terms of balancing costs
thereby gives another angle to assess the performance of the models. For the producer’s problem,
it also helps get a better understanding of the decisions, especially since there are cases where the
scheduled DA production is higher than the realised wind production. Only considering DA-market
there is no penalty for these cases, as there would be in a realistic setting. It could be interesting to
include the balancing market in the original problem formulation, so the SPO model would consider
both markets. However, this would add more uncertainty to the model in the form of the balancing
price, potentially increasing the complex.
6.3.1
General discussion
To conclude, the tests on EDP and OPF show that the SPO method can be used on optimisation
problems with uncertainty in constraints, when duality is used to reformulate the problems, as long as
it is ensured that the restrictions from the original problems are still kept. The SPO model performs
at the same level or better than most of the conventional PO methods. For the Producer’s problem,
SPO performs great in the case of only the DA price being uncertain as working with two uncertainties
makes it more complex. Relaxing the problem makes it behave in ways that the original problem
could not because the feasible region is changed, but generally, SPO works in the way that could be
expected, getting the decisions as close as possible to the true optimal decisions. It is also shown
that combinations of conventional predictions and SPO predictions can give promising results when a
problem has several uncertain parameters.
Generally, the SPO model uses quite a lot of computational power to ﬁt which results in a high
computational time and this is a drawback in relation to some of the conventional methods. However,
since the model performance seems to be quite stable, it does not need to be re-ﬁtted often. The
B-matrix could thereby be found once, with a long computational time and then for a prolonged
period. This ability was one of the reasons why SPO has been chosen as a focus for this report from
the start instead of some of the other decision-based prediction methods that were mentioned in
section 1.3.
It should be kept in mind that the investigations in this report are based on choice based upon the
SPO loss of the SPO model. The comparison models could have chosen other methods as their

7.
Conclusion and Further Work
In this report, the decision-based prediction method ”Smart Predict then Optimise” (SPO) has been
tested in the energy system, from both the system’s and the stakeholder’s perspective. SPO is a
new prediction method developed for linear models with uncertainty in the objective function and
has shown great potential for some simple optimisation problems. The energy system models that
are tested are ﬁrstly the economic dispatch problem with one node (EDP) and with 5 nodes (OPF),
where the demand is uncertain and located in the constraints, and secondly, the producer’s problem
that has both an uncertain DA price in the objective function and an uncertain wind production in
the constraints. The optimisation models constructed only consider the DA market, and the decisions
that are predicted are thereby the DA schedules. The same data is used for both problem types and
using feature selection and hyperparameter tuning, a base model is found for each problem. The
SPO models are compared to conventional regression models using SPO loss as the error metric.
A two-stage stochastic optimisation problem is further used for comparison. Here the focus is on
potential balancing costs from the predicted DA decisions. Both variants of the economic dispatch
problem are reformulated using strong duality ensuring that the uncertainty is placed in the objective
function. The SPO model is constructed by empirical risk minimisation (ERM) of the decision-based
SPO loss function. To ensure that the SPO model does not predict unrealistic scenarios, extra
constraints are added to the ERM.
The SPO model for the copperplate version of EDP utilises 8 features with Lasso regularisation at
γ = 0.1. It achieves an expected SPO loss of 18,185 DKK which corresponds to 0.75% of the expected
total cost. This is lower than most of the conventional regression models and when the robustness
is tested by diﬀerent training sizes, noise levels and polynomial feature data, the performance is
proven to be stable. The SPO model for the nodal problem (OPF) utilises 2 features using Ridge
regularisation at γ = 0.5. This results in an expected SPO loss of 33,808 DKK which is 1.38% of the
expected total cost. The performance of the SPO model on the OPF problem is on the same level as
the conventional regression model, but here, the simple LR model achieves a slightly lower expected
SPO loss. However, when noise is added to the feature data tested, the SPO model performs much
better than most other conventional models. In terms of balancing costs, the performance of the
SPO model is around the same level as the conventional regression models for both EDP and OPF.
The balance cost for SPO is in all cases lower than the stochastic model.
For the Producer’s problem, diﬀerent methods are used to tackle the two uncertainties. Firstly,
a reformulation of the problem was tried and showed promising results. A consequence of the
reformulation is the loss of a decision variable pDA, thereby it is not possible to fulﬁl the DA schedule.
This method is disregarded in its original form as it can’t be implemented for the producer’s problem.
To solve this an experiment of adding extra constraints to reverse the reformulation of the problem was
done. The constrained reformulation resulted in the desired prediction, but was deemed unsuccessful
as the performance was not very good. As the SPO framework was designed for optimisation problems
with only uncertainty in the objective function, it was not surprising. Simulating a case where the
wind is known, and only the DA price is uncertain shows that the SPO framework works very well.
Here the SPO model has a loss of 18,589 DKK, being 10% lower than the loss of RF, compared to an
expected proﬁt of 873,052 DKK. This leads to the investigation of combining SPO with a conventional
regression model for the Producer’s problem. This joint approach, where Ridge regression is used to
predict the wind production, and SPO is used to predict the DA price and handle decision-making
shows promising results, performing signiﬁcantly better than using conventional prediction methods
for both uncertainties.
To conclude, the investigation in this report shows that the SPO framework can be successfully used
in the energy system setting, even when the uncertainty is not in the objective function, with a
64

performance comparable to conventional PO methods. However, dealing with uncertainty in both the
objective function and the constraints is diﬃcult. This is expected since the SPO framework is not
designed to handle uncertain feasible regions. Instead, the joint approach described above is proposed.
Being able to extend the SPO method to also be applicable on complex optimisation problems, where
the uncertainties are not only in a cost vector in the objective function, is an important ﬁnding,
and getting results that are comparable to conventional linear, non-linear and stochastic models is
promising for the further development of the method and for the ﬁeld of decision-based prediction
methods in general.
7.0.1
Future work
Several aspects of the investigation in this report are based on ﬁrst-time experiments, where the work
could be continued. As mentioned in chapter 6 some more robustness tests could be set up for the
SPO model, maybe by combining some of the tests from this report. For the producer’s problem,
there are many diﬀerent directions which could be investigated more, related to working with two
uncertainties. In the preparation of this investigation, both Lagrangian relaxation and duality have
brieﬂy been examined as solutions to the double uncertainty. These are directions that could be
explored further. Some experiments were made using clustering on the uncertain data, to ﬁt several
smaller SPO models. A more thorough investigation into this area, to reveal if the performance
can be improved could be of interest. The stochastic gradient method for solving the SPO ERM
could be investigated as an alternative approach to the one taken here, and the eﬀect of considering
other hypothesis classes than linear for H could also be interesting. Lastly, the methods used in
this project could be explored further and extended to more complex problems, for instance by also
including the balancing market in the investigated optimisation problem. With this said, there are
also other decision-based methods developed that could be investigated as an approach for energy
system optimisation.
