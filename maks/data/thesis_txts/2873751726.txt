Abstract
District heating networks are a fundamental component of the decarbonation strategy for the
residential and tertiary sectors. To enhance their efficiency and incorporate a greater share of
low-carbon energy sources, the reduction of their operating temperature is being investigated.
However, as existing district heating networks were not originally designed for this mode of
operation, issues like hydraulic congestion appear.
One promising yet relatively unexplored
approach in the literature involves the strategic deployment of distributed thermal energy storage
(TES) units within the network. To address this, a custom genetic algorithm based on the NSGA-
II framework has been developed to systematically identify the optimal location and design of
TES on the network. The network’s operation is simulated using the DistrictLab-H Simulation
Studio software. The proposed method is benchmarked against other metaheuristics and a Mixed
Integer Linear Programming (MILP) formulation on a small test case. Results indicate that the
developed algorithm outperforms other metaheuristics by converging faster and more effectively
avoiding local optima. The solutions obtained are comparable in quality to those from the MILP
approach. However, the reliance on simulation leads to significantly higher computation times.
A key finding is that the deployment of distributed TES units not only helps mitigate hydraulic
congestion under low-temperature operation, but also offers additional benefits such as peak
shaving.
keywords: district heating networks, distributed thermal energy storage, design optimization,
metaheuristics, genetic algorithm, hydraulic congestion, peak-shaving

I
Introduction
==PAGE:11==
Thermal Storage Optimization Using Metaheuristics for District Heating
August 2025
District heating networks supply heat to residential neighborhoods, office buildings and industrial
facilities through a network of pipes carrying hot water produced by centralized or decentralized
generation units. For several decades, efforts have been made to reduce the temperature of the
circulating water. This approach offers multiple benefits, including a reduction in thermal losses
and the ability to integrate low-temperature renewable energy sources. Such improvements can
significantly decrease both energy consumption and the carbon intensity of heat production,
which is often dominated by boiler-based systems.
Lowering the supply temperature, however, requires upgrading existing networks that were not
originally designed for such operating conditions. In particular, a reduction in temperature leads
to an increase in both flow rate and pressure in the pipelines, causing hydraulic congestion when
the existing pipe diameters are insufficient to meet the demand. To avoid this phenomenon,
several solutions exist. The approach studied in this work involves the implementation of dis-
tributed thermal storage systems along the network. The specific technology under consideration
is tank energy storage. This raises the question of determining the optimal number, location and
volume of such storage units on the network in order to resolve congestion issues and perform
peak shaving as effectively as possible.
The problem is formulated as a bi-objective optimization task with the aims of minimizing both
the operating temperature and the combined installation costs of storage systems and operational
costs of the network. It is addressed using a metaheuristic algorithm inspired by the NSGA-II
(Non-Dominated Sorting Genetic Algorithm), modified to meet the specific requirements of the
case study. The method is tested on a reference network originally designed to operate at a
temperature of 120°C, which we aim to retrofit by integrating storage units to enable a reduction
in production temperature.
The network consists of three centralized production units and
four consumers. The DistrictLab-H Simulation Studio [1] software is used to simulate network
behavior, providing the necessary information to verify operational constraints and calculate
production costs.
The first part of the work is dedicated to defining the problem. It begins with a presentation of
the operation and components of a district heating network, followed by an explanation of the
benefits and challenges associated with lowering the operating temperature. A general description
of metaheuristics is then provided, followed by a detailed presentation of the functioning of several
selected metaheuristic algorithms.
Next, a review of the state of the art on the use of metaheuristics for district heating network
optimization is presented. Special attention is given to research on the metaheuristic optimization
of distributed storage integration in such networks.
The general formulation of the problem is then presented, followed by the case-specific formu-
lation for the study network. The proposed algorithm for solving the problem is described in
detail.
Finally, the results obtained for the case study are presented, analyzed and compared with
those from a MILP (Mixed-Integer Linear Programming) formulation developed by Lemelle A.G.
[2], as detailed in Appendix B. The performance of the proposed metaheuristic in terms of
convergence and optimality is further examined through a sensitivity analysis of its parameters
and is compared to other classical metaheuristic algorithms.

II.1
District Heating Networks
II.1.1
General description
District heating networks are large-scale infrastructures built at the neighborhood or city level.
They are designed to meet the heating and domestic hot water needs of multiple consumers from
a shared heat production system. Figure II.1 shows a generic schematic of a district heating
network.
In the upper left, a heat production unit provides the network’s heat supply.
A
network may include several boiler plants distributed across one or more production sites. The
red and blue lines represent the supply and return pipes of the primary network. The supply
pipes deliver hot fluid to consumers, while the return pipes bring the cooled fluid back to the
production site to be reheated. Along the way, the fluid transfers its heat to consumers at the
substations (orange cubes in the figure). On the consumer side, the network can supply single-
family houses and residential buildings as well as public service facilities and office buildings.
In some cases, industrial facilities are also connected to the network. Finally, the pipes linking
consumers to the substations make up the secondary network.

II.1.2.1
Production Units
The heat required by the network can be generated from a variety of energy sources and tech-
nologies:
— Boilers: Common and flexible heat sources, often used for base load or peak demand
coverage. The fuel may be natural gas, fuel oil or biomass (wood chips, pellets or other
organic residues).
— Geothermal: Recovers heat from the ground using underground water pipes.
— Solar thermal plants: Capture solar radiation via thermal panels through which a heat-
transfer fluid circulates and is heated.
— Waste-to-energy plants: Recover heat from the incineration of municipal solid waste.
— Waste heat recovery: Captures and reuses heat lost during an energy process.
— Heat pumps: Transfer heat from low-temperature sources (air, water or ground) to the
network, often powered by electricity.
— Combined Heat and Power (CHP) plants: Simultaneously generate electricity and recover
heat from the process, increasing overall energy efficiency.
— Nuclear heat: Recovers heat from the tertiary cooling circuit of a nuclear power plant.
According to the International Energy Agency (IEA), the total amount of heat produced for
district heating networks worldwide in 2022 was approximately 17 EJ. China, Russia, and Europe
accounted for more than 90% of global district heat production. Nearly 90% of this heat was
generated from fossil fuels: predominantly coal (over 48%), especially in China; natural gas
(about 38%), in particular in Russia; and oil (around 3%). These figures, illustrated in Figure
II.2, highlight the strong dependence of district heating on fossil fuels and underscore the need
to accelerate its decarbonization.
Figure II.2: Annual global energy supplies to district heating networks in the Net Zero
Scenario, 2010-2030 [4] (light blue: coal, dark blue: natural gas)
“Others” includes heat recovery, non-renewable waste, hydrogen and nuclear. Direct use of
renewables is excluding ambient heat harnessed by heat pumps, which adds to other renewables.

II.1.2.2
Consumers
District heating supplies multiple sectors:
— Residential and tertiary buildings: Providing space heating and domestic hot water.
— Industrial facilities: Supplying process heat for manufacturing or other thermal applica-

II.1.2.3
Distribution Network
Two main structural configurations of distribution networks can be distinguished (Figure II.3):
— Tree structure: A centralized production site forms the trunk of the network, with con-
sumers located at the extremities.
— Grid structure: A meshed network where production units can be either centralized or
distributed, allowing for greater redundancy and operational flexibility.
In these graphs, nodes represent substations, consumers, production units or junctions between
pipes, while branches represent the pipes.

II.1.2.4
Substations
Substations are the interface between the primary network and the consumers. They contain
heat exchangers that transfer thermal energy from the primary side (network) to the secondary
side (consumers) without mixing the two fluids. Control systems within the substation regulate
the heat supply according to the consumer’s demand profile.
II.1.3
Thermal storage
Thermal storage systems are designed to store heat and release it when required. Heat can be
stored through three main mechanisms: sensible storage, which recovers the heat absorbed by a
solid or liquid material without a phase change (e.g., water, soil), latent storage, which recovers
the heat absorbed during a solid–liquid phase change of a material and thermochemical storage,
6
==PAGE:16==
Thermal Storage Optimization Using Metaheuristics for District Heating
August 2025
which releases heat through a reversible chemical reaction. Currently, sensible heat storage is
the most widely used technology in district heating networks.
Several configurations of sensible storage exist. Borehole Thermal Energy Storage (BTES) stores
heat in the ground through geothermal probes, preferably in highly rocky or water-saturated
soils without significant flow. Aquifer Thermal Energy Storage (ATES) uses at least two wells,
charging by heating cold water extracted from the cold well, injecting it into the hot well and
discharging by reversing the process to recover heat via a heat exchanger or similar device. Pit
Thermal Energy Storage (PTES) involves heating water or mineral material contained in a pit,
insulated and sealed with an impermeable membrane to protect against weather and minimize
heat loss. Tank Thermal Energy Storage (TTES) stores hot water in an insulated tank, either
above or below ground. When the tank is relatively small, it is referred to as a thermocline tank,
relying on thermal stratification, with hot water at the top and cold water at the bottom. In
such systems, hot water is typically injected or extracted from the upper part of the tank, while
the return line of the network injects or extracts cold water from the bottom (see Figure II.4).

Thermal storage offers three main benefits for district heating networks:
— Peak shaving and economic optimization: When demand is high, boilers operating at a
higher cost and often with greater carbon intensity are started to meet the additional load.
Thermal storage can avoid the need to start such units by supplying the required energy
directly from stored heat.

II.3
Metaheuristics
II.3.1
Definition
Metaheuristics are approximate optimization methods used to solve complex problems. For a
given problem, the solution obtained by the same metaheuristic algorithm may differ between
runs and may not necessarily correspond to the global optimum. Such problems often feature
a very large search space with numerous local optima, making the identification of a globally
optimal solution challenging.
According to Gendreau and Potvin (2019) [8],
Metaheuristics, in their original definition, are solution methods that orchestrate
an interaction between local improvement procedures and higher level strategies to
create a process capable of escaping from local optima and performing a robust search
of a solution space.
Local improvement procedures aim to refine solutions within their neighborhood, whereas higher-
level strategies are generic methods that can be adapted to a wide range of contexts.
Breaking down the term metaheuristic:
— Heuristic (from Greek “to find”): An approximate method designed to solve a specific
problem.
— Metaheuristic (“meta” = beyond): A general-purpose heuristic applicable to a broad class
of problems.
II.3.2
Classification
These strategies are often inspired by natural or conceptual phenomena. They can be grouped
into several families according to their source of inspiration [9]:
— Inspired by evolutionary theory: Genetic Algorithms (GA), Differential Evolution (DE),
Evolution Strategy, Genetic Programming.
— Inspired by collective behavior of living organisms: Particle Swarm Optimization (PSO),
Ant Colony Optimization, Firefly Optimization, Grey Wolf Optimization.
— Inspired by physical or chemical processes: Simulated Annealing, Harmony Search Algo-
rithm, Artificial Chemical Reaction Algorithm.
— Inspired by human behavior: Tabu Search.
Other classifications also exist, such as the one shown in Figure II.10.

II.3.3
Examples
In the following section, three metaheuristic algorithms used in the continuation of this study are
presented: the Genetic Algorithm (GA) and its variant NSGA-II, Differential Evolution (DE)
and Particle Swarm Optimization (PSO).
In order to illustrate the principles of the algorithms, we consider a problem whose objective
function, called the fitness, may be either one-dimensional or multi-dimensional. Such problems
are referred to as single-objective or multi-objective problems, respectively.
II.3.3.1
Genetic Algorithm (GA) and NSGA-II
The Genetic Algorithm (GA) belongs to the family of population-based metaheuristics. The
goal of such metaheuristics is to explore the search space through candidate solutions that make
up the population. This population evolves over the iterations of the algorithm by modifying
its candidate solutions, combining exploration of promising regions with randomness in order to
converge towards the optimal solution.
In a genetic algorithm, a candidate solution is referred to as an individual or chromosome. Each
chromosome is composed of a set of genes, each gene corresponding to a different decision variable
15
==PAGE:25==
Thermal Storage Optimization Using Metaheuristics for District Heating
August 2025
of the problem, which can take a value within its predefined bounds. A population is therefore
a set of such individuals.
The algorithm proceeds as follows. First, the population is initialized by assigning a random value
to each gene of each individual. Each individual is then evaluated by computing its objective
function value, referred to as its fitness. A new population is then generated by applying crossover
and mutation operators to individuals from the previous population, followed by evaluation of
the resulting offspring. The best individuals (those with the highest fitness in a maximization
problem or the lowest in a minimization problem) from both the parent and offspring populations
are retained to form the next generation during a selection process. These steps are repeated
iteratively, with each new population undergoing the same reproduction and selection steps until
the termination criterion is met.
A well-known variant of the genetic algorithm is the Non-dominated Sorting Genetic Algorithm
II (NSGA-II), introduced by Deb (2002) [11]. Two main modifications distinguish NSGA-II from
the standard GA. First, only a subset of individuals is chosen to undergo crossover and mutation.
This subset is selected using tournament selection, a process that favors the selection of the best
individuals while maintaining diversity. Second, the selection step at the end of each generation
is replaced by a procedure based on non-dominated sorting and crowding distance.
In non-dominated sorting, individuals are grouped into fronts according to the concept of Pareto
dominance: an individual is said to dominate another if it is no worse in all objectives and
strictly better in at least one.
The first front contains all non-dominated individuals in the
population; the second front contains individuals dominated only by those in the first front, and
so on. Within a given front, the crowding distance is used to rank individuals based on their
relative isolation in the objective space. This metric encourages the preservation of diversity by
favoring individuals that are far from others in the objective space, thus avoiding an excessive
concentration of solutions in a specific region of the Pareto front.
II.3.3.2
Particle Swarm Optimization (PSO)
The PSO and DE algorithms implemented in this study are directly inspired by the work of
Ikeda (2015) [12].
Particle Swarm Optimization (PSO) is a population-based optimization method inspired by the
social behavior of birds flocking or fish schooling.
In PSO, each candidate solution is called a particle and moves through the search space by
adjusting its position and velocity according to its own best-known position and the best-known
position among all particles in the swarm.
At iteration t, the velocity associated to variable d of particle i is updated according to:
vi,d(t + 1) = ω vi,d(t) + c1r1 (pbesti,d −xi,d(t)) + c2r2 (gbestd −xi,d(t)) ,
where:
• xi,d(t) is the current position (value) of particle i in dimension (variable) d
• vi,d(t) is its velocity
• pbesti,d is the best position found so far by particle i
• gbestd is the best position found so far by the swarm
• ω is the inertia weight controlling the influence of the previous velocity

• c1 and c2 are acceleration coefficients (cognitive and social components)
• r1 and r2 are random numbers uniformly distributed in [0, 1].
The position is then updated as:
xi,d(t + 1) = xi,d(t) + vi,d(t + 1).
(II.1)
In this study, we use the m-PSO, an enhanced version of PSO proposed by Ikeda (2015) [12]. This
variant introduces a mutation mechanism: with a small probability, the particle’s position for a
variable d is randomized; otherwise, it is updated according to Equation II.1. The probability is
fixed at 0.1 in our case.
II.3.3.3
Differential Evolution (DE)
Differential Evolution (DE) is another population-based optimization algorithm, which evolves
candidate solutions through mutation, crossover, and selection operations.
DE relies on the differences between randomly selected individuals to explore the search space.
For each target vector xi(t) in the current population, three distinct vectors xa(t), xb(t), and
xc(t) are randomly chosen. A mutant vector is generated as:
vi(t) = xa(t) + F · [xb(t) −xc(t)]
where F ∈(0, 2] is the mutation factor controlling the amplification of the differential variation.
A trial vector ui(t) is then created by recombining vi(t) with xi(t) according to a crossover
probability CR ∈[0, 1]:

Finally, the trial vector replaces the target vector in the next generation if it yields a better

modeling approaches for operation found in the literature.
— Defined strategies
Razani (2016) [24] assumes that the power delivered by the production unit is constant.
By comparing the yearly heat load duration curve including network losses with the base

— Logical rules

— Studies that also optimize operation
For each selected configuration or design, the operation is also optimized. This is done
through two-level methods as in Wang (2023) [26].
Indeed, the three levels of system
optimization (configuration, design, and operation) are rarely optimized simultaneously
using a metaheuristic, due to the complexity arising from their strong interdependence.
In fact, for a given configuration, multiple designs are possible, and for a given design,
numerous control strategies can be considered. As a result, the performance of an operation
strategy strongly depends on the underlying design, which is itself closely linked to the
chosen configuration. Consequently, the optimal operation for one configuration may differ
significantly from that of another. This nested structure of the problem leads to many
local optima, making the search for a global optimum particularly difficult.

constraints. To do this, three methods are used:
— Included in a MI(N)LP/LP formulation.
— Calculated using simulation software.
— Calculated using a model and mathematical equations.

mainly for optimizing system operation, such as Alghamdi (2023) [31] and Wozniak (2018) [32].
Finally, some studies focus on the direct comparison of different metaheuristics applied to district
heating network operation. Ikeda (2015) [12] evaluates several approaches (notably PSO, GA,
and other bio-inspired methods) to simultaneously optimize the operation of production and
storage.
III.2.5.3
Hybrid methods
In the literature, metaheuristics are frequently combined with other optimization approaches to
improve convergence and reduce the problem’s resolution time.
First, studies that optimize both the configuration or design and the operation of the network
often decouple the optimization of these two aspects. This separation is due to the interdepen-
dence issue mentioned earlier and allows for more efficient search and better convergence. A
first metaheuristic algorithm optimizes the variables related to sizing and configuration of the
network. For each set of these variables (i.e., each configuration and sizing choice), a second
algorithm optimizes the variables related to network operation. However, this separation is not
always strict and may vary depending on the objectives and the structure of the problem being
studied.

A common approach involves coupling a metaheuristic with a deterministic method. For example,
Fazlollahi (2014) [20] proposes an approach in which certain variables are optimized using a multi-
objective evolutionary algorithm (EA), while others, particularly those related to operation, are
handled using deterministic algorithm under a MILP formulation. A similar strategy is adopted
by Elsido (2017) [16] and Casisi (2019) [33], where configuration and design are managed by a
Genetic Algorithm (GA), while operation is optimized using a deterministic approach. Capone
(2021) [34] adopts a multi-stage approach, in which demand is optimized with a GA, followed by
distribution simulation and linear optimization of production. Takada (2018) [35], for his part,
combines PSO for system design with MILP formulation for operation.
In other studies, several metaheuristics are used complementarily. For instance, Hu (2024) [36]
employs two separate GAs for the sizing of district heating networks and sub-stations, explicitly
accounting for their interaction with the electricity system. Chen (2022) [37] and Wang (2023)
[26] adopt a similar strategy, using respectively two instances of DE and GA for system design
and operation.
Hybrid approaches involving other optimization techniques have also been explored. Urbanucci
(2019) [38], for example, combines GA for design with exhaustive search and greedy methods for
operation, modeled as a series of independant static problems at each time step.
Some studies focusing exclusively on the configuration or design phase also use a metaheuristic
in combination with another method. Falke [18] and Allen [39] use minimum spanning trees
(MST) to efficiently connect nodes, then apply metaheuristics for optimizing the rest of the
system. Conversely, other works focus exclusively on system operation. Eladl [40] and Zheng
[41] combine PSO with Sequential Quadratic Programming (SQP) to improve local solution
accuracy. PSO-based Model Predictive Control (MPC) variants are also proposed by Ma [42]
and Wei [43].
Finally, some studies use complementary methods to refine the solutions produced by meta-
heuristics. Gopalakrishnan (2015) [44], for example, proposes a strategy combining GA with
gradient-based search to improve convergence.

The genetic algorithm is preferred for solving the problem because:

— It is practical for multi-objective optimization, allowing efficient handling of multiple ob-

— Genetic algorithms are robust when dealing with discrete and mixed-variable problems,
which often arise in system design and configuration.
However, Particle Swarm Optimization (PSO) and Differential Evolution (DE) will also be com-

combine the metaheuristic approach with a deterministic method, opting instead to rely solely
on metaheuristics for the optimization process.

4. Mass flow profiles
We initially define ˙ms,d,k, s ∈S,d ∈D,k ∈[1, nt], denoting the mass flow rate of water

However, defining a variable for the mass flow at each time step significantly increases the
number of variables in the problem. For instance, if the considered network has 4 possible
storage locations (nS = 4) with a simulation made over 3 periods (nD = 3) of 24 time steps
each (nt = 24), this results in a total of 4 × 3 × 24 = 288 mass flow variables.
A large number of variables slows down the convergence of the algorithm. Indeed, the
metaheuristic works by exploring the search space. The larger the dimensionality of this
space, the longer and more difficult the exploration becomes, increasing the likelihood that
the algorithm will get trapped in suboptimal regions. In particular, the metaheuristic may
struggle to find a coherent mass flow profile. If the algorithm is left completely free, it
is likely to explore solutions which make no operational sense. Therefore, we will reduce
the number of variables in the algorithm by imposing a predefined pattern for the mass
flow profile of the storage. This will help the algorithm more easily find realistic mass flow
profiles, thereby enhancing its convergence speed and reducing the risk of getting stuck in
local optima.
Firstly, we choose to define each period d ∈D as a period during which we know the stor-
age mass flow profile consists of a charging phase followed by a discharging phase. We then
choose to define the flow rate for each phase (charging and discharging) by a single vari-
able. One might consider using constant mass flow rates for both charging and discharging
periods. However, this approach introduces a constant flow into the network, which simply
shifts the velocity profile vertically without altering its shape. On the other hand, if the
mass flow profile is made proportional to the thermal power demand, the resulting velocity
curve in the pipeline flattens, which helps satisfy the velocity constraint more effectively.
Indeed, P = ρAcp∆T v where A is the area and v is the velocity. Assuming a constant
∆T and a constant specific heat capacity cp, the approximation of proportionality between
power P and velocity v is valid.

However, it is necessary to reformulate these constraints so they can be properly handled by the
metaheuristic algorithm. Indeed, some candidate solutions generated by the metaheuristics may
end up violating these constraints because nothing can predict in advance whether a candidate
solution will violate them.
There are several ways to handle such cases. One option is to discard any candidate solution
that violates a constraint, effectively removing it from the population. However, when feasible
(i.e., non-penalized) solutions that satisfy the constraints are difficult to find, the algorithm may
struggle to make progress or converge. Another, more commonly used approach is to penalize
the objective function of any solution that violates a constraint, for example by adding a large
penalty value. The second method is thus preferred, as it allows these solutions to remain in the
population. This helps the algorithm explore and gradually move toward solutions that better
satisfy the constraints by minimizing penalties.

V.1
General description
As explained in Section III.4, a genetic algorithm has been chosen to solve the problem. While
other metaheuristics will also be tested, this section focuses on describing the functioning of
NSGA-II and the adaptation of its components to the problem.
In the genetic algorithm, the problem is represented as follows. Genes correspond to decision
variables. An individual (or chromosome) is a set of variables (genes). When each variable is
assigned a value, the individual represents a candidate solution to the problem. A population is
a collection of individuals. The construction of an individual and the population is illustrated
in Figure V.1. In each individual, variables related to a specific storage unit StorX are grouped

The general procedure of the modified NSGA-II is illustrated in Figure V.2 and follows the
classical structure of a genetic algorithm. The number of individuals in a population is called
the population size and is denoted by npop.
— Initialization: Each gene of every individual is randomly assigned a value within its
defined bounds. The resulting initial population of size npop is denoted by Pt, t = 0.
— Evaluation: Each individual is evaluated by computing its objective value based on its
gene values and through simulation using DistrictLab-H Simulation Studio.
— Reproduction: A new population of individuals, denoted Ot, is generated via crossover
and mutation applied to individuals from the current population Pt, then evaluated simi-
larly.
— Selection: The populations Pt and Ot are merged and the best individuals are selected to
form the next generation population Pt+1 of size npop.
Then these steps are iterated until the stopping criteria are met.

V.3
Evaluation
Evaluating individuals consists of computing their objective values. As a reminder, the problem
is bi-objective: it involves minimizing the supply temperature Tsupply and minimizing the total
cost, which includes the storage capital expenditure (CAPEX), network operating expenditure
(OPEX) over a 20-year period and potential penalties for constraint violations.
The supply temperature is a decision variable in the model and can therefore be obtained directly.
Similarly, the storage CAPEX can be calculated analytically based on the individual’s encoded
values. However, computing the OPEX and verifying constraint satisfaction requires simula-
tion using the software DistrictLab-H Simulation Studio, which models the network’s dynamic
behavior.

All the mutation and crossover operators described below are specifically designed to enhance
the diversity of the population, particularly in terms of the choice of storage locations explored
by the algorithm. Without such mechanisms, the algorithm may rapidly converge to a specific

optimization. As a result, other promising storage locations might be overlooked. By promoting
diversity, these custom operators enhance the exploration of the search space and reduce the risk
of premature convergence.

Multi-objective
The same genetic algorithm is run multiple times with different supply temperatures. The tem-

VI.1. We prefer launching multiple single-objective optimizations (with the supply tempera-
ture as a fixed parameter) rather than a single multi-objective optimization (where the supply
temperature would be both a variable and an objective) for several reasons. After many tests
with multiple objectives, the results do not converge very well. Besides, because the best tem-
perature and cost combinations do not create a perfectly increasing or decreasing Pareto front,
some solutions might be left out as non-optimal, but we want to keep solutions at different
temperatures.
Figure VI.8b shows the storage solution selected for each supply temperature.
The storage
location remains the same (Stor1) and the storage volume increases as the temperature decreases.
This is logical, since lower temperatures lead to more congestion and, consequently, a larger
amount of energy must be stored to avoid this issue.
However, the costs in Figure VI.8a are not strictly increasing or decreasing with temperature.
As the differences in cost are very small (less than 3,000 € between the solution with the highest
fitness and the one with the lowest), one possible explanation is that the metaheuristic did not
find the global optimum for each supply temperature.

VII
Conclusion
==PAGE:96==
Thermal Storage Optimization Using Metaheuristics for District Heating
August 2025
The results demonstrate the effectiveness of distributed storage in addressing congestion issues
when lowering the network supply temperature. While centralized storage alone is insufficient to
resolve congestion, it appears to be the most effective configuration for performing peak shaving.
Nevertheless, distributed storage can also be used to both mitigate congestion and perform peak
shaving when required.
The total storage volume required to simultaneously address both problems depends on the one
that demands the greatest energy capacity. In the one-day case study, the energy required to
perform peak shaving on the third production unit exceeds that needed to maintain the velocity
limit of 2 m/s.
Moreover, the storage volume obtained from optimization based on a single
critical day may be oversized, as such extreme conditions rarely occur over the course of a year.
Therefore, performing the optimization over multiple representative days helps to mitigate the
risk of oversizing the storage.
Results are found to be sensitive to several factors, including the selected cost parameters, the ini-
tialization method for storage units in the DistrictLab-H Simulation Studio software, constraints
on the storage mass flow profile and the tolerance allowed for demand fulfillment.
A comparison between the MILP formulation and the metaheuristic approach reveals similar
cost performance but differences in the selected storage volumes and locations. Unlike the meta-
heuristic, the MILP is formulated as an energy–power problem. Therefore its solution may not
satisfy the velocity constraint. On the other hand, the MILP produces smoother peak shaving
results, as the metaheuristic’s search space is restricted by the operational constraints imposed
on storage units.
The modified NSGA-II algorithm developed in this study converges more frequently to high-
quality solutions compared to the classical NSGA-II and the m-PSO (Particle Swarm Opti-
mization with mutation strategy), thanks to its mechanisms for preserving diversity in storage
configurations within the population. However, the Differential Evolution (DE) algorithm yields
results comparable to those of the modified NSGA-II.
The modified NSGA-II also shows good convergence and stability for the one-day problem, but
it encounters greater difficulty when extended to the three-day problem due to the increased
number of decision variables. The primary challenge lies in the simultaneous optimization of
storage configuration, design and operation, which makes the search space highly complex. A
potential improvement would be to decouple the optimization of configuration and design from
that of operational strategy, as proposed in some studies that combine metaheuristics with MILP
formulations in hybrid approaches to address similar problems.
For these reasons, along with the high computational cost of the metaheuristic combined with
network simulations, the proposed method is not suitable for industrial applications or for scaling
to significantly larger networks. An alternative could be to develop an optimization algorithm
based on a MINLP (Mixed-Integer Non-Linear Problem) formulation, which would more accu-
rately capture system behavior while remaining computationally feasible.

MILP Formulation
The following MILP formulation of the problem has been developped by Lemelle A.G. [2].
B.1
Parameters and variables
The decision variables of the problem include both the state variables of the operating district
heating network and the configuration variables (location), sizing variables (height and diameter
or volume) and control variables of the storage units (charging/discharging flow rate).
The position of a storage unit at a network node is represented by a binary variable Y (Y = 1
when a storage unit is present), and its operation at each time step is represented by a binary
variable X(t) (X(t) = 1 when the storage unit is charging). The introduction of such binary
variables leads to a Mixed Integer (MI) problem. It is possible to relax these binary variables by
replacing them with continuous functions, such as polynomial or sigmoid functions. However,
this relaxation may introduce numerical instability issues.
The other variables are continuous. In this linear formulation of the optimization problem, the
continuous variables correspond to the power and energy characteristics of the different elements
of the system.
