Abstract 
 
As financial systems have been growing in complexity, consumers with low financial literacy have been 
increasingly left behind. This thesis aims to study how product configuration theory can be utilized to 
help these individuals improve their financial literacy and support their financial decisions. To do this, 
research is first conducted on relevant product configuration and financial theory, in addition to 
existing financial IT systems. Though much research exists within financial literacy, and the many 
benefits of improving it, there was little practical research on systems to improve it. Existing 
commercials options also proved limited in their scope and availability.  
Through utilizing a human-centric design thinking approach, a prototype of a financial configurator 
was developed for testing and interviews. The interviewed individuals ranged from domain experts in 
various fields to simple end-users with little relevant experience. In close collaboration with these 
interviewees, the configurator was further expanded, and the concepts behind the ideal financial 
configurator started to emerge. Product configuration ultimately proved to be an apt method for 
solving this problem, with the ability to develop a system with high levels of feasibility and usability.  
During the process it was realized that the initial scope of the project proved too large, requiring 
additional development of the prototype to achieve the various features requested by the interviews. 
It is then recommended to expand the pool of interviewees to get a higher variability and target the 
most vulnerable consumers.

constraints/interdependencies. Type of expert system that uses weak AI to make suitable selections

monetary products, services and concepts.

In the wake of increasingly complex financial systems, many consumers that do not have the financial 
literacy to understand their own life-cycle economy have been left behind. This was highlighted by an 
OECD report published in 2016 that concluded only 60% of adults had a household budget, and only 
50% set long term financial goals (OECD, 2016). However, research has shown that education and 
awareness are effective at improving the understanding of market volatility, and attitudes towards 
investment (Rodrigues et al., 2019). This thesis is conducted to provide a practical tool for consumers 
to create an awareness of their economy and improve their financial literacy, through inputting the 
key parameters related to their financial life cycles. 
Since much of current financial regulation has failed to protect consumers from increasingly complex 
financial products, the research field of financial literacy has grown tremendously in recent years. This 
field is concerned with the current level of financial understanding individuals and the potential 
factors to improve this understanding. Recent studies have shown that individuals can improve their 
financial literacy (Rodrigues et al., 2019). Moreover, it was previously found that financial literacy has 
positive effects both on consumers savings decisions (van Rooij et al., 2011), and wealth over time 
(Lusardi & Mitchell, 2007). Hence, it is possible and effective to educate the consumers and help them 
achieve their financial goals. 
To the best of the author’s knowledge, most of the research within financial literacy has been heavily 
theoretical or has relied on methods that are designed to fit a large sample size of the population 
(Rodrigues et al., 2019). Furthermore, studies have investigated the consumers educations about 
financial theory, such as risk diversification or the theory of complex financial products (van Rooij et 
al., 2011). Such terminology might be useful, but the consumers first need a rudimentary 
understanding of their own financial life cycle. Hence, they need to be presented with the impact of 
very basic financial decisions they could take through a simple user-friendly interface. After 
implementing a human-centric personalized configuration system, it might be useful to educate the 
consumers on more complex terminologies.  
As consumption has grown, production companies also faced increasing demand for customization 
while maintaining a high level of quality and short delivery time (Shafiee et al., 2014). Hence, product 
configuration systems are introduced as one of the most popular solutions. Product configuration 
systems allow users to customize orders, and automatically receive outputs on the cost breakdown 
and delivery time. Such systems can include constraints between product parts, and provide 
automatic documentation and quotes, saving vital time for users (Hvam et al., 2008).  However, to the 
best of author’s knowledge, configuration systems have primarily been used for production and 
engineering companies, with smaller attempts at service projects – but it has never been tried to 
develop a human-centric financial tool.  
This thesis will explore the interface between financial literacy and product configuration, attempting 
to use the practical approach of production configuration theory to the topic of financial literacy. 
Meanwhile, the author will use methods such as design thinking to explore the customers’ 
requirement and deliver a sustainable and responsible configurator. This topic has a growing interest, 
not only within research, but also for the consumers – yet little practical tools have been developed 
for people of low financial literacy to improve their situations.

The objective of this research project is to support or contradict the following hypothesis: 
Building a financial configuration tool for consumers based on personalized life-cycle information, 
with the aim to improve financial literacy, will be both feasible and usable.

users need to be identified. In this research, we face two groups. The first group of users are domain 
experts, both within the financial category who support this study with the required base knowledge 
and IT experts who help with the design of the IT system. The second group are the non-expert users 
who will be the end-users of the system.

1.1.2 Research validation 
In validating the research questions, frameworks by Hvam et al. (2008) and Shafiee et al. (2018) are 
utilized.  The first framework by Hvam et al. (2008) will be used to build the configurator, and will thus 
help determine the configurator feasibility, answering relevant research questions. The other 
framework by Shafiee et al. (2018), however is concerned with scoping the configurators and 
managing the knowledge they require. Alongside these frameworks, a design thinking approach for 
configurator design developed by Shafiee et al. (2020) will be utilized. Hence, this framework helped 
to answer the usability questions which are mainly relevant to domain experts. The hypothesis will be 
validated through building the configuration system model and answering each of the RQs. 
Furthermore, the configuration system will be tested on variety of users, ranging from domain experts 
within finance and IT to regular consumers for which the system is being designed.

Subsequently, the following processes were performed sequentially: 
 
1. 
Development of interview questions. The structure of the questions for the semi-structured 
interview was based on the feasibility and usability of implementing configurators. In accordance with 
the research objective and questions, the interviews were designed to systematically compare the 
benefits gained from a configurator with currently available IT systems for financial purposes. The 
semi-structured interview process involved experienced researchers, experienced product 
configuration system (PCS) project specialists/stakeholders and finance experts’ representatives. 
 
2. Pilot interviews. The identified questions based on the literature were tested in pilot 
interviews with one relevant domain expert and one researcher with sufficient experience to assess 
the clarity of the questions and their relevance to the multistage solution. The pilot interviews were 
conducted over multiple rounds and concurrently with the development of the prototype.  
 
3. Refinement of interviews. Based on the pilot interviews, the number of questions was 
reduced, and the interview questions were categorized. The purpose for this categorization was to ask 
relevant questions from end-users and domain experts. Specific questions were removed when 
interviewing end-users if they could not answer them sufficiently based on their current knowledge. 
 
4. Conducting the interviews. The feasibility related interviews were conducted separately 
with 8 domain experts and 21 end-users. Moreover, this round of interview included all the questions 
both related to feasibility and can be interviewed with both end-users and domain experts. However, 
only domain experts answered usability questions as these questions were both irrelevant and out of 
the domain knowledge of end-users. So, the usability interviews were conducted separately with 8 
domain experts. They all completed the questionnaire and participated in a short conversation about 
the questions and the results. The final goal was to discuss the results and agree on the final 
conclusions. 
 
5. Confirmation of results. The results were compiled, and selected answers were presented 
to selected interviewees to confirm the results and elaborate on potential discussion and propose the 
potential solutions. 
 
6. Presentation of final results. Based on the final remarks of the interviewees, the results 
were checked, discussed, edited, and improved for the last time before being presented in this paper.

This research has been conducted as part of this new area of study, occurring at the interface between 
personal financial theory and product configuration. The project idea arose from an older scientific 
article by (Markowitz, 1991). The article outlines game-of-life simulation as an alternative to the 
standard Markowitz portfolio theory for modelling individual financial management. Markowitz also 
outlined various complexities that were currently limiting such an approach from being implemented, 
mainly the variability in goals and decision-making existing across individuals (Markowitz, 1991). As 
the complexity of global financial systems has grown, financial investing and planning have only shifted 
further from individuals to more institutionalized organizations (Cumming & Groh, 2018). Sufficient 
research or progress within this area could aid in bringing more control back to the users over their 
own financial planning. 
Engineering-to-order (ETO) companies have faced comparable challenges in attempting to deliver 
highly customized products/services to customers with varying needs. In responding to this, some 
companies have been turning to product configuration theory (Crazzolara & Milicia, 2003). Through 
cataloguing their products and services in a configuration system - including features, sub-parts, 
constraints and relationships between parts - ETO companies have been able to customize specific 
products/services and automatically receive quotations or product/service specifications (Hvam et al., 
2008). To define the project, the four-step framework to scope configuration projects by (Shafiee et 
al., 2018) will be used, while a seven-step framework will be considered in building the configuration 
system (Hvam et al., 2008) and a design thinking method will be considered to create a user-centric 
configurator (Shafiee et al., 2020). 
This thesis will attempt to apply recent configuration theory, based on relevant frameworks, to the 
problem of individual personal life-cycle planning. It therefore becomes vital to develop the financial 
configuration model in conjunction with domain experts within finance, to ensure it is not only 
accurate but also useful for the end-users  (Forza & Salvador, 2002). Currently little research exists on 
configuration theory within service systems (Heiskala et al., 2007), while there is no research 
specifically on financial service systems. 
The theory section is organized into three subsections, as outlined below: 
1. Product configuration systems: Introduces the fundamental literature of product configuration 
theory used for the proposed system, and the frameworks used to build the configurator. 
2. Growing need for financial services: Explains the need for financial configuration systems for 
consumers, and closely aligned existing literature and the research gap on topics. 
3. Customization of current financial service systems: Consists of a comparative analysis of currently 
available tools, what they do well, and where they may be improved for a new system.

2.1 Product configuration systems 
Throughout the past century, industrial markets have undergone significant changes from scattered 
local markets, to mass markets, and in recent times towards further niches and mass customization 
again, as outlined in Figure 2-1 (Davis, 1989). Advances in mass markets were largely driven by 
industrialists such as Henry Ford, who famously exclaimed: “You can have any car color as long as it’s 
black”, summarizing the characteristics of mass production; the ability to mass produce products with 
little variance. However, as buyer markets dominate, companies were increasingly forced to consider 
the individual needs of their customers, trending towards further market niches and greater 
customization. While adopting these principles, companies still had to compete with the efficiency of 
production that mass production allowed (Felfernig et al., 2014).  
In responding to this, many ETO companies have been turning towards mass customization, the 
concept of “customer-individual production of highly variant products near mass production pricing 
conditions” (Felfernig et al., 2014). Configuration technologies have proven themselves as the leading 
technology to support the trend of mass customization, providing customers with the ability to 
customize their products and services to a very high degree (Hvam et al., 2008). Designing configurable 
products for mass-customized markets involves specifying certain pre-designed elements, and the 
rules that define how these elements can be combined to meet the customer requirements (Salvador 
& Forza, 2004). Defining these specifications in an error-free way for customer-centric products can 
sometimes provide difficult due to specific interdependencies or incompatibilities across the product 
parts creating high complexities. However, product configurators have proved themselves apt to deal 
with such complexity in most scenarios (Forza & Salvador, 2002). Product configurators are 
information systems that encompass knowledge on the product parts, and related interdependencies 
and constraints, and using artificial intelligence supports error-free selection of product specifications 
(Hvam et al., 2008; Sabin & Weigel, 1998). 
2.1.1 Product specifications and configuration stakeholders 
Throughout its lifecycle, products are privy to various specifications and processes that end up 
defining the final product – as outlined in Figure 2-2. Designing a configurable product specifies the 
set of pre-designed specifications and rules on how the specification processes can be performed 
(Heiskala et al., 2007). Various stakeholders support the definition of these specifications and 
processes (Felfernig et al., 2014). 
Specifications: Specifications are some of the features that create the targeted product or service, 
allowing the requirements and application of it to be communicated between groups. For ETO 
companies this will include several categories of knowledge such as product drawings, lists of 
parts, assembly instructions or similar. For a service, it could be the customer requirements 
or instructions on how to perform the specific service (Hvam et al., 2008). Essentially all the

required knowledge that allows anyone to perform the necessary service is integrated into 
the product configuration system for it to be utilized automatically. 
Specification processes: This refers to the business processes that are reliant on the details of the

Stakeholders of a configuration project: Knowledge experts, using their knowledge of configuration 
systems, are responsible for developing and maintaining the configurator application. 
Throughout the development, they work closely with domain experts, who provide the main 
technical, marketing or sales knowledge. This knowledge is transferred between these 
stakeholders through a process known as knowledge acquisition, consisting of various 
communication interactions between the knowledge and domain experts. The last group of 
stakeholders are the end-users, who will utilize the configurator to simplify certain processes 
in their daily work or personal life. The knowledge experts will ultimately test the final 
configurator on this group (Felfernig et al., 2014).

2.1.2 Customization in product service systems 
While the theory of mass customization and product configurators have been utilized consistently for 
customizable goods, many researchers have argued that the current body of research on

2.1.4 Interface theory and design thinking 
DT is a human-centered approach to the development of IT systems, where the knowledge experts 
consistently work to include the perspective of both domain experts and end-users (Shafiee et al., 
2020). DT is thus an iterative interplay between the diverging exploration of the problem space and 
solution space, occurring simultaneously with the converging processes of synthesizing and selecting 
the viable alternatives (Lindberg et al., 2011). The problem space is concerned with the current 
working situation, and the issues that are currently faced by the end-users, while the solution space 
includes the set of possible solutions that exist within the imposed constraints (Forza & Salvador, 2008; 
Shafiee et al., 2020).  DT has three key characteristics (Lindberg et al., 2011), as outlined in Figure 2-7: 
1) Exploring the problem space: An intuitive understanding is developed through observing current 
use cases and synthesizing the knowledge of various stakeholders. 
2) Exploring the solution space: Simultaneously, the possible solutions are considered, sketched and 
prototyped within the necessary constraints to obtain tangible alternatives. 
3) Iteratively aligning both spaces: The tangible alternatives serve as a means of communicating the 
understanding of the problem between the knowledge experts, end-users and domain 
experts. This helps to refine and revise the final solutions. 
The iterative process in close alignment with stakeholders is vital, so that functionalities and user 
interfaces are not incomprehensible from the end-user’s point of view. Otherwise, the knowledge 
expert could fall into an innovation trap of spending continuous time developing technically novel 
solutions that are too complex, involving features that are of no value to the users (Lindberg et al., 
2011). This same logic can also be used to illustrate configuration projects, as outlined in Figure 2-8. 
The knowledge expert starts with an empty configurator shell, which is essentially the product 
configuration tool without any product knowledge. In this shell, the developers will define relevant 
parts, models, attributes and the constraints between them, and slowly construct the solution space. 
As the configurator is then used by domain experts or end-users, product choices are made within the 
Figure 2-7: Problem and solution space in design thinking (Lindberg et al., 2011)

solution space, until the customized product is done, arriving at a particular product instance within 
this space (Shafiee et al., 2020).

2.2 Growing need for financial services 
The research topic of financial literacy has grown consistently in recent years, as it has been discovered 
several consumers have been adversely affected by the increasingly complexity in our financial 
systems. Individuals with high levels of financial literacy have consistently been shown to have higher 
returns on investments, and generally make smarter financial decisions. Financial product 
configurators have a lot of potential to aid in increasing the levels of financial literacy throughout 
society through providing tailored solutions related to the individuals own financial situations. 
2.2.1 What is financial literacy? 
Recognizing the growing demand for greater financial education, the President’s Advisory Council on 
Financial Literacy (PACFL) was founded in 2008 with the goal to: “Promote more awareness among 
Americans of the state of financial literacy in the United States and of their own financial literacy and 
dedicate more resources toward educating Americans on how to improve financial literacy”. Their 
initial definition of financial literacy was quite broad, being concerned with one’s attitude and ability 
to deal with individual financial resources, for the purpose of long-term financial well-being (Schwab 
et al., 2008). Later definitions have included more specific understanding of the underlying economic 
and monetary concepts along with the skills to use this information in handling one’s financial 
resources (Rodrigues et al., 2019). In a survey by Standard & Poor’s, the Scandinavian countries of 
Norway, Denmark and Sweden all ranked 1st on financial literacy (Rodrigues et al., 2019) 
Recognizing the tendency of many individuals to possess low financial literacy and lack knowledge of 
even basic economic principles, certain studies have looked to educate consumers on financial literacy 
(Lusardi & Mitchell, 2007; van Rooij et al., 2011). All these studies have concluded that, through a 
concentrated effort, individuals are able to improve their financial literacy. However, the methods 
used have often been generic, e.g., standard quizzes, instead of providing personalized solutions. 
Furthermore the definition of financial literacy has been quite high level, focusing on things such as 
risk diversification or the theory behind complex financial products such as derivatives (Rodrigues et 
al., 2019). While it may be important for consumers to understand these terms, they also require the 
individuals to understand certain basic financial concepts first. The individuals most in need of support 
for financial decisions may often not have this basic understanding. Even if certain individuals come 
to understand these concepts, they could still struggle to relate it to their own financial situation (E-
F3). The best solution would be tailored to everyone’s financial situation, making it easier for them to 
understand any new concepts in relation to their own financial life cycle (Interview E-F4). 
The author would therefore argue that the ideal financial configuration system should build on very 
simple concepts. And there should be a strong focus on constantly displaying individualized inputs to 
the users, that explain how that specific financial concept could impact their own situation (Interview 
E-F5, E-F6). 
2.2.2 Growing need for financial literacy 
As the complexity of our financial systems have increased, consumers have increasingly been expected 
to manage their own financial training. However, not all consumers have been able to hold their 
financial literacy at the level required in today’s economy. The OECD, whose 37 member countries 
account for about 50% of the worlds GDP, outlined in a 2016 global report how only 56% of adults 
achieved a score above 5/7 – the minimum target score on financial knowledge (OECD, 2016). The 
level of financial literacy tested went from very basic concepts such as inflation and interest to more 
advanced topics within compounding interest and financial risk. This need for financial literacy has 
only grown and will likely continue growing due to two major trends that are also related: the growing 
complexity of our financial systems, and the increasing expectations on consumers financial literacy.

Increasingly complex financial systems 
Global finance systems have always consisted of a high level of complexity due to the highly 
interconnected systems and institutions involved in an economy. There are numerous trends and 
events, intended or natural, that can cause shocks in these systems. However, due to globalization 
and advances in financial innovation these systems have become increasingly complex (Blundell-
Wignall, 2017). Due to globalization, the world markets have become much more intertwined, as 
supply chains can often span the whole globe. For individuals this means more products are now 
available, which is not inherently bad, but it requires further research to distinguish between them. 
And similar logic has occurred within the financial products available to consumers, as innovation has 
led to many new complex financial products such as collateralized debt obligations (CDOs) or other 
complex derivatives (OECD, 2016). 
The responsibility to understand these products often ends up falling on the consumer, as the 
predatory practices of financial industries are often underregulated (Fraser, 2017). As globalization 
has made more investments available to individuals, it has increased the burden of researching and 
selecting the right investments. This is something that is inherently easier for individuals with high 
levels of financial literacy (Lusardi & Mitchell, 2007), and thus disproportionally affects individuals that 
are not. Individuals lacking the necessary level of financial literacy therefore have a harder time with 
financial planning and asset management decisions. 
When financial crashes then occur, people with low financial literacy are therefore disproportionately 
affected, as they often do not save or plan for bad scenarios. They are generally also more prone to 
predatory practices by credit or mortgage brokers, as they do not understand the underlying 
terminology (Crotty, 2009; Norvilitis et al., 2003) 
Societal expectations of consumer literacy 
As the complexity of available financial systems have grown, it has inherently placed a greater demand 
on individuals’ financial literacy. This has happened concurrently with an attitude of financial 
deregulation based in classical economic theory that also relies on consumers possessing a sufficient 
understanding of the economic systems (Attanasio & Weber, 2010). These trends together paint a 
picture of a society that increasingly expects individuals to maintain a certain level of consumer 
literacy. 
Just as advances in information technology have increased the complexity of various financial systems, 
it has also provided consumers with greater control of and access to their investments through e-
banking or brokerage applications. This trend is causing individuals to demand further responsibility 
for an increasing number of financial decisions (OECD, 2016). However, such as shift from advisory 
assistance on investments to self-analysis requires greater knowledge and information about the 
economic systems and the underlying assets (Marsden et al., 2011). To have full control over their 
own investments, individuals should ideally have a basic understanding of inflation, compounding 
interest, risk diversification and more (OECD, 2016) – ideally presented in a personalized manner so 
the individual can understand the impact of these concepts on their own financial life-cycle.  
The argumentation for removing financial regulation is often rooted in classical economic theory 
(Attanasio & Weber, 2010), that relies on the concepts of rational choice and intertemporal choice 
(Walker, 1946). These concepts are distinct, but also inherently related. Rational choice theory states 
that individuals, when carrying out any tasks or making financial decisions, will perform a personal 
cost and benefit analysis to determine the best possible outcome (Pizzorno, 2007). Intertemporal 
choice is concerned with how individuals make these decisions at various points in time. Each decision 
to save or consume at various points is done in order to maximize their expected utility – calculated 
==PAGE:27==
Chapter 2 - Theoretical foundations 
19 | P a g e  
 
 
using the expected utility hypothesis (Berns et al., 2007). This means our regulation is relying on 
rational and educated consumers that can understand all the complexities of our financial system and 
come to the most profitable decisions themselves. Essentially, all consumers must be able to predict 
and understand the impact of economics decisions around savings, loans and interest rates – they 
must be financially literate (Attanasio & Weber, 2010). 
However, growing research within behavioral economics and financial literacy has disproven the myth 
of the rational financially literate consumer. Prospect theory originated as a critique of the expected 
utility theory that relied on rational agents, outlining the irrational way in which people view financial 
decisions. One aspect of this was loss aversion, which is the tendency of people to prefer avoiding 
losses to acquiring gains – the sadness of losing 100 DKK is greater than the satisfaction of gaining 100 
DKK (Kahneman & Tversky, 1979). This explains why some individuals could be hesitant to invest in 
financial assets, even when returns have shown to trend positive in the long term. Companies have 
long understood the basic irrationality of the consumers and implemented it within their marketing 
techniques (Elliott, 1997). This encompasses not only consumer goods, but also predatory mortgage 
and credit companies. And as the complexity of our financial systems grow, it is becoming increasingly 
hard to be a rational consumer (Interview E-F1, Interview E-F3). 
2.2.3 Personalized financial configuration system 
Providing users with a personalized financial configuration system could entice them to increase their 
financial literacy through focusing on how the related concepts could affect their own financial 
situation. This would be useful in dealing with the increasing complexity of financial systems and allow 
users to become more proficient at various financial practices such as cash flow management, credit 
management, savings and investments (van Rooij et al., 2011). People with lower scores in financial 
literacy have consistently been shown to make poor investment decisions (Lusardi & Mitchell, 2007). 
The ideal financial configuration system would include various topics such as incomes, expenses, 
pensions and assets such as investments and real estate. It would combine these various areas to give 
the users a detailed overview of their economy, allowing them to make informed financial decisions, 
understanding the impact such decisions would have on their portfolios (Interview E-F3, Interview E-
F5).

to outsource them to FinTech firms as well (Interview E-F3). Nonetheless, an analysis of these tools

2.3.3 The ideal financial configuration tool 
The outlined tools exhibit very useful features for solving the problems discussed in earlier sections, 
however there are still limitations. The most developed tools are targeted to individuals with high 
levels of capital and are furthermore not focused on increasing the financial literacy of its users. As 
outlined in section 2.2, the individuals most in need of financial literacy are from lower socioeconomic 
backgrounds and are likely in need of understanding different financial concepts and terminology than 
people with high incomes. The accessibility of these services is also limited, as they are sold as a 
service, which further disincentivizes the companies from providing teaching opportunities for their 
clients. The final limitation is due to geographical reasons – as previously mentioned, financial 
regulation varies significantly across countries, and is also continuously changing. Therefore, the ideal 
configuration tool should either be a global tool taking this into consideration, or there needs to be a 
financial configuration tool individually tailored to each country. 
2.4 Summary of literature review 
In responding to increasingly mass-customized markets, many companies have been turning to 
product configuration theories, and their underlying frameworks. For various ETO companies this has 
proven tremendously successful in meeting varying customer specifications. As the complexity of 
financial systems have grown, many consumers are arguably facing similar problems as ETO 
companies, though few options exist to aid them in improving their financial literacy and taking 
informed decisions. The few options that do exist are generally limited in their features, accessibility, 
and are not tailored towards the individuals that need them most. This creates a clear need to develop 
financial configuration systems with the consumers and their best interests in mind.

3.1 Project scope 
For configuration systems it is of vital importance to develop a project scope early, and continuously 
revise it as new information appears. If the project scope is too wide, it may not be feasible to 
complete the configurator within the project deadline, while a too narrow scope could mean vital 
features are not implemented. While other frameworks were used for the development of the 
configurator, a framework by Shafiee was used (Shafiee et al., 2014) in scoping the configurator, that 
considered five key areas – the fourth area of products and product features is not applicable for 
financial configurators, and therefore not included in the scoping below: 
1. Aims & purpose for the configuration system 
2. Identification of stakeholders and their requirements 
3. Definition of the IT architecture 
4. Products and product features to include in the configuration system 
5. Project plan including resources, timetable and modelling approach 
3.1.1 Aim & purpose 
As outlined in section 2.2, it is becoming increasingly difficult for consumers to manage their economic 
portfolios within our increasingly complex financial systems. At the same time, the expectations on 
the level of financial literacy of consumers has also increased, without sufficient resources to educate 
them about these topics. While some systems exist, as outlined in section 2.3, these are generally not 
as straight forward and all-encompassing as they could be. Most of them were for single purposes, for 
example taking a mortgage loan, and did not develop an economic profile of the consumers over time 
or provide sufficient advice on considerations that the consumers should make. 
The aim of this configuration system is therefore to provide a simpler, and more general interface that 
would allow users to get a complete overview of their economy. Due to time constraints, the scope 
did have to be narrowed from a general overview to considering only pension schemes, though it was 
still attempted to set-up the architecture in a way that it could be further developed. 
3.1.2 Identification of stakeholders and requirements 
As was discussed in section 2.2, all consumers could generally benefit from the increased financial 
literacy that a financial configuration tool could provide – however the configurator is primarily aimed 
at people with limited financial education. The tool is furthermore also aimed at inhabitants of 
Scandinavia, or even more specifically Denmark, as there are certain local laws and regulations that 
need to be taken into consideration. However, many of the principles discussed are still applicable on 
a global scale. As the project scope was later narrowed towards pension schemes, the tool was further 
aimed at middle-aged people nearing pension age. However, it could also be argued that it would be 
best to reach younger people and teach them about the importance of saving for their pension early 
on, when smaller economic decisions will have a greater long-term impact. 
All the potential users are expected to have the same requirements of increasing their financial 
literacy, though the extent of their current financial understanding could vary greatly – as was seen in 
the various interviews (refer to Table 1-4). This is where the potential of product configuration really

3.1.3 IT architecture 
The IT architecture encompasses the design of the configurator itself, the various inputs and outputs, 
the functionalities of the system, and the decision flows. As the scope was narrowed down throughout 
the project, not everything included in the IT architecture has been implemented in the final model. 
This section therefore serves more as a view of what a final financial configurator could look like or 
include. 
Inputs 
The inputs include any information that is relevant to the user’s financial situation. At the most basic 
level this would include things such as their age, current salary, current savings and current 
expenditures. For a more advanced analysis however the expenditures could be segmented, in 
addition to the investments (pension savings vs other investments), the risk profiles of these 
investments, value in real estate, and any potential loans or mortgages. Additional inputs could be the 
financial goals that the user has, such as saving for real estate, or looking to retire by a certain age. 
To engage the user, the configurator is ideally designed in a way that outputs are provided and shown 
to change consistently as new inputs are provided. For example, the first output might occur after only 
one or two input variables have been defined. This picture would then become more accurate and 
detailed as the additional inputs are added. 
Outputs 
The output will first be an overview of the user’s current financial situations, in addition to a prediction 
of how their financial situation would change over time. These outputs should be highly customizable, 
so that the users are able to edit various variables, to determine the impact current financial decisions 
would have on their future portfolios. 
Additional outputs could include suggestions on how the users might optimize their financial choices 
to achieve the financial goals that they have inputted. 
Decision flow 
As mentioned, the configurator should ideally start with basic inputs, such as name, age and initial 
financial information such as salaries and predicted expenditures. Then the user would be provided 
the option to choose additional areas that they want to focus on – for example they could choose to 
segment their expenses, input more detailed pension data, or add information on their real estate 
investments. The idea is to let the user choose which areas they want to focus on, and the level of 
detail they are comfortable inputting to the model. 
Integration with other systems 
Ideally the configuration system can interact with other systems to provide outputs in the most 
convenient manner for the user, potentially offering outputs in multiple formats and systems with 
varying levels of complexity. Furthermore, it would be useful if the configurator is able to integrate 
well with existing financial systems, such as banking systems, NemID (Scandinavia-specific), and local 
pension systems.

4.1 Interview variability 
Though many interviews were conducted, and there was a generally high level of variability both 
within the ages and fields of these individuals, it could be argued there are several limitations to the 
interview results. As was outlined in the literature review, most of the individuals in need of financial 
configuration tools are people with low levels of financial literacy. While these are decidedly harder 
to find within Denmark, a country with some of the highest levels of financial literacy, all interviews 
conducted were still with people of high educational and financial status. There was not much testing 
conducted on people with lower socioeconomic backgrounds for whom the financial configurator 
would be most relevant. This resulted in the development of the configurator becoming tailored 
towards these individuals, and their financial goals. As such, the level of complexity that was aimed 
for within the configurator may be too high. Further suggestions for continued work is also based on 
these datapoints.  
Though it was attempted to test on people of varying age groups, most interviews were conducted on 
people that are closer to, or nearing, retirement age. As has been outlined earlier, there could be 
multiple benefits to targeting configurators towards younger individuals, as this is when their financial 
decisions could have a greater impact on their financial life cycle. It was attempted to mitigate this 
through asking all individuals for the financial goals and considerations they had during their earlier 
years as well. There could however also be different levels of understanding, and varying goals 
between generations, so it would be useful to further interview more young individuals. 
4.2 Financial configuration tool 
Though two different prototypes were developed for this project, the research serves more as an 
introduction for further projects on financial configuration theory. An initial understanding of financial 
configuration tools was developed, in addition to an analysis of what such a system should include 
and how it could best be developed. 
4.2.1 Feasibility  
If such a tool could be developed successfully, it has been shown both through scientific research and 
various interviews that the benefits to users would be numerous. Ranging from improved investment 
opportunities, increased budgeting and decreased financial stress throughout the user’s life cycle. 
Through implementing configuration theory, even users of high financial literacy could improve their 
understanding of new concepts. Through displaying users with customized visualizations specific to 
their own situations, they could also become more encouraged to take the small steps and 
modifications necessary to reach their goals – or for some individuals to even set financial goals in the 
first place. When developing such a tool it is therefore of paramount importance to consider the ideal 
interface that would work to encourage the users to get further engaged through small nudges. 
4.2.2 Usability 
The answers to the basic usability questions in table 3-6 were generally very positive, indicating the 
prototype was easy to learn, and would be quite useful for the users in terms of saving time and 
providing additional understanding. This is a good reason for applying design thinking and involving

Users were found to have many varying goals, ranging from different levels of comfort in retirement,

calculations, while other users need lots of graphical visualization to enable them to understand the 
financial concepts. This number of varying interfaces would require significant work and is likely best 
achievable using dedicated product configuration software.  
4.3 Financial configuration theory 
The current tool developed in businesslogic was shown to be fairly limited in a lot of these respects, 
as it does not include any product configuration theory. All users are presented with the same view, 
which was shown to break down at high incomes. As mentioned earlier in the discussions, most 
interviews and tests were conducted on people in higher income groups, for which the tool was also 
shown to break down. Many users questioned the lack of their other financial assets, such as real 
estate or investments such as stocks or bonds. It was attempted to implement some of these features 
in the underlying Excel engine, though this often resulted in circular logic. In the authors view, these 
challenges could best be solved by utilizing dedicated product configuration software. The importance 
of including these aspects for a thorough life cycle analysis should not be understated however, as all 
the areas are generally intertwined. Any excess money that does not go towards expenditures or 
pensions will still be saved by the individuals, either as a free asset, or going towards investments such 
as stocks or bonds. 
Through the interviews, the importance of continued maintenance of such systems was also 
highlighted, as they rely on fundamental laws imposed by the government – e.g., tax law as outlined 
in section 3.3. It would be easier to automate such maintenance through utilizing software that easily 
integrates with currently established cyber security software.

Chapter 5 - Conclusions & perspectives 
50 | P a g e  
 
 
 
It has been shown throughout the report that there is a growing demand for improved financial tools, 
imposed by increasingly complex financial systems. This demand has only grown through financial 
crises, as the most vulnerable individuals have been further left behind. Configuration theory seems 
to be an apt way of dealing with this financial problem, though considerations need to be made in 
ensuring people with varying levels of financial literacy can be enticed to use the tools. This can be 
achieved through utilizing a human-centered approach around design thinking, ensuring that users of 
all levels are engaged through sufficient interaction with the interface.  
Though several financial tools already exist, and some of them seem to be applying certain product 
configuration aspects, there is still very little scientific research on such tools. Furthermore, most of 
these tools have very low levels of accessibility and are not necessarily reaching the individuals that 
need them the most. Meticulously applying configuration theory to these tools could certainly 
improve them, while further public funding might be necessary to expand the access to disadvantaged 
groups. None of these tools seem to vary their content based on financial literacy either, rather being 
targeted towards individuals of high incomes that are likely already aware of most financial aspects, 
and currently manage their own investment portfolios.  
It has been proven that it is possible to build a financial product configurator with high levels of 
feasibility and usability through utilizing product configuration theory with a strong focus on human-
centered development around design thinking. The interviews highlighted the need for high levels of 
variability, but also indicated the many benefits that users might experience from such a tool. Such 
benefits ranged from increased financial returns to greater financial literacy and less worry about their 
financial situation. The interviewees also indicated many areas of improvement for the current 
configurator, though it would require significantly more work than was allocated to this project to 
design and build the ideal financial product configurator. 
Additional work would be required to expand the financial analysis within various areas, such as 
incomes, housing assets, investments in the market and more, to ensure the accuracy of the life cycle 
analysis. These areas would likely need to be designed and developed concurrently to ensure the 
interrelationships are considered thoroughly. More work would also need to be put into the interface, 
ensuring high levels of visual and graphical representation, while still allowing the users to edit the 
various features and see the impact on their life cycle. Additional interviews should be conducted with 
the aim of targeting people of lower education levels and socioeconomic status, as this was a large 
gap in the current interviewee pool. Though exceptions exist, this should also correlate to more 
individuals with lower financial literacy - the general target group of the ideal financial configuration 
tool. Another interesting point could be to conduct additional research on younger individuals, as 
these are most prone to change their financial habits, and also have the most potential to still impact 
their financial life cycle. Such research could start with university students but could even be expanded 
to high schools or middle schools – though the complexity of the content would need to be adjusted 
accordingly.
