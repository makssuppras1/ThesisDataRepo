ABSTRACT 
Sustainability assessments of buildings have in recent years gained greater attention in 
the construction industry. Methods like life cycle assessment (LCA) and life cycle cost 
(LCC) are used today in the design and planning of almost all new buildings and larger 
building renovations. A building is built from many different materials and components, 
all with their own service life, the numbers used today often give the product's technical 
service life, but what happens if aspects such as economy and aesthetics are taken into 
consideration. In this study three methods are used to estimate the service life of fibre-
cement boards, Factor method, a deterministic approach, the Step-by-Step method, a 
stochastic approach of the factor method and a simplified method for assessment of ac-
tual average service life, based on a product’s technical, functional, economic and aes-
thetic service life. Based on the three methods used to estimate the service life, the Step-
by-Step had the highest estimated service life, 52 % higher than the actual service life. 
The simplified method for assessment of actual average service life was - 9 % lower 
than the actual service life. The estimated service life based on the Factor method was 
closest to the actual service life with a difference of - 6 %, however the Factor method is 
very sensitive to how the factor range is set.  
 
Keywords:  
Service Life estimation, ISO 15 686 Factor method, Step-by-Step, Simplified method 
for assessment of actual average service life of building components, Life Cycle As-
sessment.

1 INTRODUCTION  
Sustainability assessments of buildings have in recent years gained greater attention in 
the construction industry. Methods like life cycle assessment (LCA) and life cycle cost 
(LCC) are used today in the design and planning of almost all new buildings and larger 
building renovations. Sustainability certifications like Green Building Council Denmark 
(DGNB) has likewise received increasing attention since the Danish version was 
launched in 2012. 
 
Methods like LCA is used to assess a building’s environmental impact throughout its 
service life; from the extraction of raw materials, the production processes, time of use 
and end of life. While information like the input of raw materials, energy use for pro-
duction and the handling of end life is often known by the manufactures, the situation is 
different when it comes the use stage. The use stage of a building it is often difficult to 
predict, due to its long expected service life. Where information about energy for heat-
ing and electricity is known based on building codes and energy calculations, infor-
mation about maintenance and replacement is often based on the manufacturer's guide-
lines and the building component or materials’ technical service life. A building is built 
from many different materials and components, all with their own service life, the num-
bers used today often give the product's technical service life, but what happens if as-
pects such as economy and aesthetics are taken into consideration? The service life of 
building materials and components will often be influenced by a number of factors; 
composition of materials, design, environment and maintenance e.g. an inaccurate esti-
mation of the service life may lead to incorrect calculation of the building’s environ-
mental impacts. 
 
During a renovation on 15 4-storey blockhouses in 2003 to 2005, all facades claddings 
facing towards north were dismantled, façade cladding consisted of fibre-cement 
boards. The main reason for the dismantling was that the fibre-cement boards were dis-
coloured due to algae growth and did therefore not meet the aesthetic requirements that 
were desired. Technically there was nothing wrong with the fibre-cement boards. 
 
Today, it is expected that residential buildings will have a functional average service life 
of 120 years (SBi 2013) or at least a service life equivalent to the primary building 
components’ technical service life, e.g. loadbearing steel structures or loadbearing con-
crete elements. Replacement of these is often not possible or economically worthwhile. 
Complementary building components and surface materials will often have a service 
life that is significantly shorter than the primary building components, and they will 
often be replaced several times during a building’s service life. Figure 1-1 illustrates the 
service life of the different layers of a building.

In the past, estimations of the service life of building components and materials was 
often based on experts' assessments. The experts were often influenced by knowledge of 
specific cases, which led to estimations of relatively short service life. In (Arbejdsrap-
port fra Miljøstyrelsen – Bygningers og byggematerialers levetid 1997) the service life 
of buildings and building materials were evaluated based on statistical registration on a 
large number of buildings. Internationally, there has been extensive research on service 
life estimation methods. Since 1999, the commission for International Council for 
Building (CIB) has been working on three different methods to estimate the service life 
of building materials and components. (Hovde, P. J, 2005) 
 
In 2000, the Factor method was presented in ISO 15 686 - Building and Construction 
Assets - Service Life Planning-part 1. The Factor method is a simple deterministic 
method used to estimate the service life based on a reference service life and seven 
modifying factors. 
 
Nationally, here in Denmark, Danish Building Research Institute (SBi) has published 
the report ”LEVETIDER AF BYGNINGER VED VURDERING AF BÆREDYGTIGHED 
OG TOTALØKONOMI” (SBi 2013). This report provides a simplified method for actu-
al average service life calculations, based on the building material or component’s tech-
nical, functional, economic and aesthetic service life.  
  
At present, there is no national or international standard of values for actual service life 
of building components or materials. (SBi 2013) The EU Commission’s Joint Research 
Centre (JRC) is working on the development of a system and register, which will even-
tually contain data on actual service life. (Aagaard, N.-J., Møller, E. B., Hansen, E. J. 
P, 2014).

1.1 Intended Application of the Study 
 
The aim of this Master Thesis is to estimate the service life of fibre-cement boards, 
based on a renovation case in “Brøndby Strand Parken” housing area. Three methods 
are used for estimating the service life of fibre-cement boards, ISO 15 686 Factor meth-
od, a deterministic approach, the Step-by-step principle, a stochastic approach of the 
factor method and a simplified method for assessment of actual average service life, 
based on the fibre-cement’s technical, functional, economic and aesthetic service life.  
 
A simplified Life Cycle Assessment of fibre-cement board’s environmental impact is 
used to demonstrate the importance service life has in relations to the sustainability as-
sessments. The calculations are based on five scenarios of service life. 
 
• Actual service life of fibre-cement boards. 
• Manufacturer’s expected service life of fibre-cement boards.  
• Retrospective service life estimation of the fibre-cement boards, based on Factor 
method (Deterministic approach) 
• Service life estimation of fibre-cement boards, based on Step-by-Step principle. 
(Stochastic approach) 
• A simplified method for assessment of actual average service life.

BACKGROUND 
The service life of buildings are often seen as infinite, and this recital is often due to the 
many historic buildings still standing all around the world. However, when looking at 
the individual buildings, the situation is very different. In Denmark, the average age for 
a residential building is 63 years (Sbi 2013). As a part in the increasing development of 
evaluation buildings environmental impact, there is a need for determine the buildings 
service life, where sustainability certifications system like DGNB use a fix number of 
50 years for all buildings (DGNB, 2013). Resent study has shown the actual service life 
of buildings are 70 years, this number is based on 20999 Buildings that were demol-
ished between 2009 and 2015, the study also showed that factors such as Region, Re-
furbishment, type of usage and wall material has an impact on a building’s service life,

ing’s age and demolition rate for different usage types conducted at SBi, a building’s 
functional average service life is determined based on its usage, see Table 2-1. Where the 
functional average service life offices are 80 years, functional average service life is 120

2.1 Service lives definition 
 
A building material or a component’s service life is defined as the time from which it is 
built into a building to when it is finally dismantled. The service life is affected by three 
main factors, (Miljøstyrelsen 1997).  
 
• Sociological development 
• Technological development 
• Mechanical attrition 
 
A building consists of many different building materials and components, all of which 
have a different service life. This means that through the building's service life, many of 
these will be replaced, often more than once. The length of a building material or com-
ponent’s service life is often based on its 
 
• Technical service life 
• Functional service life 
• Economic service life 
• Aesthetic service life 
 
The technical service life is defined as the period starting when a material or compo-
nent is installed in the building, to the time where the material or component is no long-
er technically able to fulfil its original function. The material or component must have a 
certain resistance against impacts to comply with the technical function, and when it no 
longer has this resilience, its technical service life is achieved/has come to an end. Some 
of the key factors that influence the technical service life are material quality, design, 
implementation and impacts from indoor and outdoor environment, usage conditions, 
and maintenance. (SBi 2013) 
 
The functional service life is defined as the period starting when a material or compo-
nent is installed in the building, to the time where there is no longer need for its func-
tion. Some of the key factors that influence functional service life are social changes, 
technological developments, changing life patterns, and urbanization. (SBi 2013)  
 
Economic service life is defined as the period starting when a material or component is 
installed in the building, to the time where it is no longer economically justifiable to 
maintain and replace parts of the building component. The economic service life is de-
termined by price and interest rate conditions at the given moment. The economic ser-
vice life will often be shorter than the technical service lifetime (SBi 2013) 
 
Aesthetic service life is defined as the period starting when a material or component is 
installed in the building, to the time where the aesthetic standards can no longer be 
maintained. The main factors of aesthetic service life are lifestyle and societal change in 
the perception of the aesthetic expression. (SBi 2013)

Where the technical, functional service life can be applicable to all building parts of the 
building, the economic service life is both relevant for the complementary building part 
and installations, but the architectural service life is only essential for complementary 
building components and surface materials e.g. the surface treatment of exterior walls 
etc. (SBi 2013) 
2.1.1 Actual service life 
 
The actual service life is the actual period of time in which a building material or com-
ponent is built into the building before it’s dismantled. The actual service life will often 
be dominated by the lowest of the competing four service lives or of a combination.  As 
it can be seen in the example in Figure 2-1 from (SBi 2013) the distribution of the four 
service lives and actual service life, the economic service life is the lowest of them all 
and will therefore influence the actual service life on a given building material or com-
ponent. Generally, the actual life cannot be determined from a statistical knowledge of 
the individual service lives distribution, but by empirical data from a large number of 
building components or materials, after which the average actual service life can be de-
termined. There is no such empirical data in Denmark yet as describing how old the 
individual building components actually are and what determines the service life. (SBi 
2013)

2.2 ISO 15 686 Factor method  
 
Service life planning is today an important process when it comes to the design and 
planning of almost all new buildings and larger building renovations, service life plan-
ning is used to ensure that the service life of a building or building components obtains 
its design life. In Table 2-1 the minimum design life of buildings and building compo-
nents are listed, a building component design life depends on if it is replaceable or not. 
(ISO 15 686-1, 2011) Estimating service life is a basic problem in the early stages of 
project planning, due to the lack of relevant information. The concept of service life 
planning can be used to assess the need of maintenance, in order to ensure that the ser-
vice life for a given building component or material is as long as possible, this can in the 
end reduced early replacement. 
 
“Service life planning is a design process that seeks to ensure that service life of a 
building or other constructed asset will equal or exceed its design life. If required, ser-
vice life planning can take into account the life-cycle cost(s) of the building and its life-
cycle environment impact(s).”  
(ISO 15 686-1, 2011 p. V)

techniques can be applied, reliability and flexibility of use of the building can be in-

The Factor method was first presented in 2000, in the “ISO 15 686 - Building and Con-
struction Assets - Service Life Planning-part 1”. ISO 15 686 - Building and Construc-
tion Assets - Service Life Planning-part 1-11. The Factor method is a deterministic 
method used to estimate the service life of a particular building component or material 
under specific conditions. The method was based on a similar method developed in Ja-
pan a few decades earlier. 
 
The estimated service life of a building component or material is based on the reference 
service life and a series of modifying factors A to G, the factors are determined by di-
viding the object-specific in-use condition with the reference in-use condition. (ISO 15 
686 -8, 2008a). The reference in-use condition, is based on reference service life data, 
this includes reference service life and qualitative or quantitative data describing the 
validity of the reference service life. The object-specific in-use condition is based on the 
in-use condition meaning any circumstance that can impact the serive life of the build-
ing, building component or material under normal use. (ISO 15 686 -8, 2008a)

Factors A, B and C describe the building material or component’s performance and re-
silience. Factors D, E, F and G describe the impact the building material or component 
is exposed to during its service life. Equation (1) is an expression of the estimated ser-
vice life (ESL). Estimated service life is based on a reference service life (RSL) multi-
plied with the above-mentioned factors.  
 
A - Quality of component: The quality of material that the component is composed of; 
this covers both the actual material as well as the quality of the surface treatment, pro-
tecting the component against environment impacts such as moisture or frost. (ISO 15 
686 -8, 2008a)  
 
B - Design level, how well is the component designed, it is designed so it is protected 
against exposures like weather, water or drying out which all can reduce the service life. 
Is the design complex, e.g. consisting of many joints, does it consist of several different 
materials? (ISO 15 686 -8, 2008a)  
 
C - Work execution, how is work on the construction site executed. Has the component 
been exposed to shock or moisture during the execution? Proven executions will often 
be equal to a longer service life. (ISO 15 686 -8, 2008a)  
 
D - Indoor environment, this factor is often only applied to interior building compo-
nents. A poor indoor climate will often affect the inside window frame as condensation 
could lead to growth of mold, which in the end can results in reduced service life. It is 
important that the building is equipped with the correct air exchange.  
Detoxification from chemicals or changing usage patterns can also reduce the service 
life. (ISO 15 686 -8, 2008a)  
==PAGE:16==
17
 
E - Outdoor environment; which environmental impacts is the component exposed to, it 
is often necessary to select different sets of factors such as light, wind and UV rays 
which will have a different impact on the component. This includes the building's loca-
tion and orientation. Air pollution may also have an impact on the service life of the 
building components. In Denmark, the location is divided into two climate zones, by 
coastal or coastal remoteness. The same applies for vegetation near the building enve-
lope; at worst this could reduce the service life due to algae growth. (ISO 15 686 -8, 
2008a)  
 
F - In-use conditions, refers to the use of the building component, e.g. mechanical wear 
and tear. If a window is opened inexpedient it could result in that the window will be 
faster worn down, which in turn reduces the service life. The same applies to the floor-
ing; inexpedient tear could reduce the service life. On the other hand, frequent use can 
prevent degradation mechanisms such as corrosion or moisture-dependent degradation.  
(ISO 15 686 -8 2008a) 
 
G – Maintenance, regular maintenance can extend the service life of various materials 
and components. E.g. by ensuring that gutters are not filled with leaves you can prevent 
water for running back down the building envelope. By painting exterior woodwork, 
you can prevent the woodwork from attrition due to weather condition. (ISO 15 686 -8, 
2008a)  
 
2.2.1 Application of Factor method  
 
Even though the ISO 15 686 Factor method has existed since 2000, where “ISO 15 686 
- Building and Construction Assets - Service Life Planning-part 1” was published, there 
are a limited number of research papers or reports describing the usage of the method. 
One reason for the limited practical use of the method can be due to lack of knowledge 
of the method among practitioners (e.g. architects, consultants and building owners and 
managers) and the lack of common set of values for the seven factors. (Hovde, P.J, 
2004). Some examples of application of factor methods are presented in this section.  
 
“Much of the discussion and criticism of the Factor Method has been related to the fact 
that it is necessary to select a specific value for each of the factors, and that the multi-
plication of all the factor values makes the value of the estimated service life (ESL) very 
sensitive to slight variations of each factor value.” (Hovde, P. J, 2004 p. 5) 
 
In Hovde, P.J, (2005) paper “The Factor Method – a simple tool to service life estima-
tion” outlines some of the issues there is with the Factor method and how it could be 
used in practice, he also proposes a way to assess the key factors. One of his main con-
cerns is the missing guideline on how to set the factor values and the issues that can 
make the estimated service life uncertain due to even a small change in each factor val-
ue that can have an influence on the calculated estimated service life. 
 
“Increasing application of the Factor Method over the last years has shown that there 
is an urgent need for input data, both for the reference service life as well as rules for 
selection of the factor values.” (Hovde, P. J, 2005 p. 1)

2.3 Step-by-Step, stochastic approach of Factor method 
 
In Aarseth, L.I. and Hovde, P.J. (1999) the principle Step-by-Step is introduced, the 
Step-by-Step method is a stochastic approach of the Factor method. The Step-by-Step is 
based on the “successivprincippet” and was developed at NTNU in Norway. The prin-
ciple Step-by-Step is used as a tool for improving the quality of the basis for decisions 
in project planning under uncertainty. 
 
The advantages of using the principle Step-by-Step to estimate the service life, is that 
for each factor the uncertainty will be estimated and all conditions that influence the 
service life is identified in a systematic way. (Aarseth, L.I. and Hovde, P.J. 1999) 
 
“The statistical treatment in the “step-by-step” principle is based on a structure where 
each cost element or factor can be assumed independent. In the ISO factor method, 
there normally will be strong interactions between the factors.” 
(Aarseth, L.I. and Hovde, P.J. 1999 p.7) 
 
”The estimating process can be improved by changing the factors into elements

2.4 A simplified method for assessment of actual service life 
Since the report “Arbejdsrapport fra Miljøstyrelsen – Bygningers og byggematerialers 
levetid” was published in 1997, there has been further development in the assessment of 
service life in Denmark.  Based on a bill on house inspection schemes from 2010, Dan-
ish Building Research Institute published the report “Levetider for bygningsdele om-
fattet af ejerskifteforsikring og huseftersynsordningen” in 2012. The main purpose of 
the report was to contribute with service life tables for well-maintained building com-
ponents and materials to determine the compensation in the case of structural damage. 
 
The method used to determine the service life of well-maintained building components 
and materials is based on the Factor method presented in (ISO 15686-1 2011). For the 
estimated service life, the factors A to F are set to be for Average conditions and the 
factor G- maintenance is set to be higher than normal. See Section 2.2 for a description 
of the seven factors categories. In contrast to the ISO 15686 Factor method, the method 
used in (SBi 20129 includes the standard deviation in the assessment. The basic of this 
lies in the fact that the estimated service life depends on many factors, which in the end 
can result in uncertainties. 
 
“When the experientially is extremely difficult to determine the service life for building 
components, it is because the service life will depend on of so many of the mentioned 
factors, that the spread of the service life will be very large, unless the circumstances 
can be determined very carefully” Quote translated into English (SBi 2012 p. 10) 
 
The standard deviation of the average service life is indicated as low (L), medium (M) 
or high (H), as an expression of how much factors A to F will influence the service life. 
(SBi 2012) This means that some building components or materials will be more affect-
ed by the factors than others. The following example is given in (SBi 2012 p. 15)  
 
” An example of differences on the service life factor’s importance are the roofing 
of thatched roof and natural slate, where the service life of a thatched roof as opposed 
to natural slate is highly dependent on the orientation of the roof and the building's lo-
cation. The spread on the service life of the thatched roof has therefore been given the 
termed H, while natural slate has been termed L” Quote translated into English. 
 
In 2013 Danish Building Research Institute published the report “LEVETIDER AF 
BYGNINGSDELE VED VURDERING AF BÆREDYGTIGHED OG TO-
TALØKONOMI” as part of the growing trend in relation to buildings’ sustainability 
assessments. The report is based on further development of the method used and pre-
sented in (SBi 2012). The report presents two different approaches to how the actual 
service life can be determined. One based on the standard deviation of all four service 
lives, and one based on the technical and functional service life. The following rules are 
applied in both approaches. The service life is stated as a specific number of years, 
rounded to the nearest 10 for service lives over 30. (SBi 2013)

2.5 Use of service life in LCA 
 
LCA is a method used to quantify the potential environmental impact and resource con-
sumption associated with a product or service. LCA is based on life cycle thinking, 
which means that the entire product/service’s life cycle from cradle to grave is consid-
ered in the environmental assessment. The industry has used LCA since the late 80s and 
it has today become an industry in itself. Many larger companies today have an in-house 
expert in this field. Today LCA is used as an important decision-making tool when it 
comes to improving existing technologies and in the development of new. ILCD (2012). 
  
For quite some time LCA has played an important role in the construction industry, 
from the building material/component to the building as whole. A building’s life cycle 
assessment covers the time starting from the extraction of raw materials through to the 
disposal at the end of life see Figure 2-4. The functional average service life for residen-
tial buildings is set to be 120 years (SBi 2013, Table 3), which means the use stage is 
very long. This means that the service life of building materials and components play an 
important role when it comes to the assessment of a building’s environmental impact. 
Quite often, the building materials and components have a shorter service life than the 
actual building’s life, which means that some parts will be replaced throughout the con-
sidered period. On this basis, it is important to know the actual service life or to estimate 
it as accurately as possible, to form a realistic image of the building’s environmental 
impact.

Service life is not only used in LCA. Service life is also an important part of Life Cycle 
Cost analysis (LCC). An LCC analysis calculates the total building economy from the 
purchase of land, the design, craftsman costs, energy costs, cleanliness and maintenance 
cost, to the cost of demolition and disposal. The time horizon for a LCC is often set to 
be 50 years DGNB (2013). During these 50 years, some building materials or compo-
nents will be replaced. It is therefore important to know their actual service life.

the buildings, was a town dominated by grey concrete. But unlike other previously in-

and types of facades. BRØNDBY STRAND PARKERNE (2013)

Brøndby Strand Parken is today one of Denmark's largest residential areas consisting of 
about 300.000 m2 of housing spread over approximately 2,880 household residences.

The buildings constructed in Brøndby Strand Parken were prefabricated Building, also 
called industrialized construction, the name of a new building practice that was intro-
duced in Denmark during the post-war period. Some of the most famous industrialized 
buildings in Denmark are the high-rise buildings in Bellahøj in Copenhagen that was 
built in the period 1950-1960, making it some of the earliest industrialized buildings in 
Denmark. The industrialization of construction began in Europe during the interwar 
period. The inspiration for industrialized construction was, among others, Le Corbu-
sier's DOM-INO system. His vision was to create a system consisting of a pillar / plate 
without bearing walls or facades. This was supposed to make it easy and inexpensive to 
build new homes as replacements for those that was lost during World War 1, and even 
though the system was never used in practice, it has formed the basis for the further 
development of industrialized construction. In 1928 a group of architects formed 
CIAM, the Association of Modern Architecture. Industrialization of construction was 
one of their key points, they considered the traditional building practices such as brick 
buildings to be relics of the Middle-Ages. The first actual industrialized buildings with 
prefabricates parts were terraced houses in the Törten built in the1920s. [6] 
 
“The modernist architects saw it as an opportunity to exploit industrialization and the 
new ways of production to mass produce good and efficient homes that would be af-
fordable to all”

Industrialized construction became a reality in Denmark during the post-war period of 
the 50s and 70s due to the growing population and the increasing economic prosperity 
in Denmark. The buildings were built along a crane track that made it possible to build 
in large units, and buildings with up to several thousand m2 residences at the same time. 
In average 10-12 million m2 of residences per year was built during this period.

2.6.2 Fibre-cement 
Facades that were renovated and dismantled were made of fibre-cement, also called 
“Eternit” in Danish, with the reference to the English word “eternity”, was invented by 
Austrian Ludwig Hatschek in the late 19th Century. At that time the fibre-cement con-
sisted of 90% cement and 10 % asbestos, due to the content of asbestos, the fibre-
cement was of high durability, this mixture was used in Denmark until 1987 when as-
bestos was made illegal due to health-related problems concerning the material. In solid 
form, asbestos is not dangerous; the problem is with the dust from asbestos-containing 
materials – like when a plate is being cropped. Today, the fibre-cement consist of ce-
ment, mineral filler, fibres of polyvinyl alcohol (PVA), cellulosic fibres, water, air, and 
water borne coatings. [7]

The main benefits of using fibre-cement boards for façade cladding are: 
• The material in fibre-cement has a high durability.  
• The materials are open to diffusion, which means resistant to rot and fungus.   
• The fibre-cement boards are easy to assemble and install, since they are relative-
ly thin compared to other facade solutions.  
• They are non-flammable. 
• Resistant to wind and weather. 
 
The main disadvantages of using fibre-cement boards for façade cladding are: 
• The boards are not repairable if cracks occur; this means that the whole boards 
must be replaced. This can result in a deterioration of the aesthetic expression, 
due to colour difference between the existing boards and the new board.  
• The material does not gain patina with age, like wood. 
 
Facades consisting of fibre-cement boards are almost maintenance-free, as it is only 
recommended to perform an annual inspection of boards to ensure that there are no 
loose screws or boards, and that the ventilated cavity is not clogged with dirt. As men-
tioned, the fibre-cement boards are resistant to wind and weather, however air pollution 
could change the colour of the façade. Like other facades with a northern orientation, 
algae formation can occur. This can be removed by regular cleaning. It is also recom-
mended that cobweb and vegetation is removed regularly. [3] 
 
“A Cembrit cladding need almost no maintenance to preserve its strength, properties 
and function” Quote translated into English [8]

5 DISCUSSION 
5.1 Actual service life 
 
The actual service life is determined based on the renovation case of Brøndby Strand 
Parken, where the fibre-cement boards were dismantled due to their aesthetic appear-
ance and not due to any technical or functional problems. At present, there are no meth-
ods or data on how to assess the aesthetic or economic service life (SBi 2013). The 
Brøndby Strand Parken case is an excellent example of how the aesthetic service life 
can affect the actual service life. If the aesthetic and economic service life’s impact on 
the actual service life should be assessed, it would require empirical data, for each 
building component, that documented the actual service life and the reason for disman-
tling. 
5.2 Manufacturer’s expected service life 
 
Scenario 2 is based on Cembrit’s expected service life at 50 years, this number is based 
on the products technical and functional service life. Cembrit offers a warranty of 15 
years on their products, if their guidelines relative to the installation and maintenance 
are met. The aesthetic appearance of the fibre-cement boards depends on which type of 
fibre-cement boards are being used. Cembrit Cover is a facade solution that has a sur-
face of strong water-based acrylic paints, whereas Cembrit Patina is a facade solution, 
which has a natural, untreated surface that will eventually get patina. [10] When looking 
at the aesthetic aspect, they only offer a 3 years’ warranty. See quote translated into 
English [2] 
 
” Colour changes and colour degradation may occur, and colour changes and colour 
attrition appearing after more than 3 years after commissioning considered as a conse-
quence of exposure to weather influences and therefore releases no claim against Cem-
brit A/S. Salt efflorescence / lime precipitates and similar phenomena in cement-based 
products are not considered a defect in the product.”  
 
In appendix 4 are listed some comments from customers of Cembrit façade solutions. 
As it can be seen in the two quotes from the comments, this indicate there is some prob-
lem with the aesthetic in relation to the products and that the aesthetic service life of 
fibre-cement is significantly shorter than the technical and functional service life.

5.3 Factor method 
 
The case used in this study is based up on fibre-cement boards installed in the late 60s/ 
early 70s, this period was known for its testing of new materials and new construction 
technologies, which was not tested under Danish conditions (Bech-Danielsen, C. 2011). 
The estimation of the reference in-use condition is based on Cembrit’s guidelines, 
which applies to products used today. If the reference in-use condition for factor catego-
ry A and G is changed from being average to poor the estimated service life is 43-years, 
which is an increase of 39 % in relation to the calculated service life in scenario 3. 
 
The fibre-cement boards from the case were dismantled due to their aesthetic appear-
ance (Aesthetic service life). If the estimated service life only was assess based on a 
lack of maintenance level the calculated service life would be 40 years.  
 
Common recital for those who worked with  Factor method is the lack of guidelines on 
how the factors range is set. By changing the factor range to [0.9-1.1] in scenario 3, the 
estimated service life increased with 29 % compared to the estimated service life with 
the factor range [0.8-1.2]. This means that the choice of factor range has a major impact 
on the estimated service life. This only emphasizes the importance of setting a common 
set of rules for the evaluation of factor range. 
 
“Much of the discussion and criticism of the Factor Method has been related to the fact 
that it is necessary to select a specific value for each of the factors, and that the multi-
plication of all the factor values makes the value of the estimated service life (ESL) very 
sensitive to slight variations of each factor value.” (Hovde, P. J, 2004 p. 5)

5.6 General discussion

reference service life being used. A lower reference service life would result in lower 
estimated service life for all three methods. This study only focus on the estimate ser-

or materials is not known, a similar study can show whether there is a connection be-
tween the choice of method and the estimated service life. This would require 
knowledge of the actual service life and what was the reason for the building component 
or material was then dismantled, such data are currently not available in Denmark.

5.7 Life cycle assessment 
 
The calculations of fibre-cement board’s environmental impact shows that there is a 
difference of 29 % between the manufacturers expected service life and the actual ser-
vice life. In this study, only the façade cladding of fibre-cement was considered. A 
building consists of many different building components and materials, all having their 
own service life, if the actual service life is much shorter than expected, the environ-
ment impact of the building is much higher. This only emphasizes the importance in 
evaluating service life as correctly as possible in order for realistic sustainability as-
sessments. As (Strand, S. M. and Hovde, P. J. 1999) describes both methods deal with 
the same problem, where information such as maintenance can extend a building com-
ponent or material’s service life, it can simultaneously also have an impact on the envi-
ronmental impact e.g. paint or products used for clean, this is one of the reason why 
service life planning is an important part of the design process. 
“The service life of a specific part will have a great influence on the outcome of an LCA 
of the complete object. Selection of alternative parts that have different service lives or 
where the service life varies depending on alternative maintenance procedures, may 
also have a great influence on the overall outcome of the LCA. The introduction of LCA 
into the building and construction sector will therefore increase the need for service life 
prediction of construction products.” Hovde, P.J. (2004) p. 19

CONCLUSION 
In recent years sustainability assessments of buildings has gained greater attention in the 
construction industry. In order  to assess the environmental impact and life cycle costs of 
buildings, an accurate estimation of building components and materials’ service life is 
essential. In this study, different methods for estimation service life of fibre-cement 
boards were evaluated. The study is based on a renovation case at Brøndby Strand Parken, 
where fibre-cement boards were dismantled due to their aesthetic appearance.  
 
The study shows that the expected service life of 50 years stated by the manufacturer was 
52 % higher than the actual service life of 33 years. Based from the three methods used 
to estimate the service life of the fibre-cement boards, the Step-by-Step principle had the 
highest estimated service life with a difference of 52 %. The method developed by the 
Danish Building Research Institute (SBi) simplified method for assessment of actual 
average service life was 9 % lower than the actual service life and the Factor method 
was 6 % lower the actual life of the fibre-cement boards.  
 
A simplified LCA analysis were used to illustrate the importance of service life in rela-
tion to sustainability assessment. The study showed a difference of 29 % between the 
actual service of the fibre-cement boards and the manufacturers expected service life.  
 
The estimated service life based on the Factor method was closest to the actual service life, 
however the Factor method is very sensitive to how the factor range is set, the study 
showed that by changing the factor range from [0.8-1.2] to [0.9-1.1], the estimate ser-
vice life increased by 29 %. This emphasizes only the need for forming some guidelines 
for setting factor range or a specific set factor range 
 
Generally, the service life of building components and materials are based on their tech-
nical and functional service life, the study shows how the aesthetic service life can af-
fect the actual life

RECOMMENDATIONS 
The service life tables presented in the SBi (2013) is based on the building component 
or materials technical and functional service life, without taking the economic and aes-
thetic conditions into account. Economic and aesthetic service lives influence on the 
actual service life should be investigated further, to create more realistic service life 
tables. Service life information is an important part when it comes to sustainability as-
sessments like LCA and LCC, if service life is used today is to high compared to the 
actual service life there will be a high degree of uncertainty when it comes to buildings 
overall sustainability assessment. Further studies should consider whether it is more 
realistic to see more conservatively at service life table used for sustainability assess-
ment, as the service life tables used to determine the compensation in the case of struc-
tural damage. (SBi 2012)
