1
Introduction
Microscale robotic systems are increasingly being designed to operate autonomously in
complex environments by leveraging environmental stimuli for control and functionality.
The integration of responsive materials and advanced fabrication methods has enabled
significant advancements in this field. Below, we outline several key aspects that underpin
the development of environmentally responsive microrobots, with a focus on fabrication
techniques, material selection, and actuation strategies.
• Environmentally responsive microrobots: The development of microscale robots ca-
pable of responding to environmental stimuli and enabling external control has attracted
increasing attention. These systems hold promise for applications such as targeted drug
delivery, cell manipulation, and dynamic microfluidic control [ref:microrobots_review].
• Role of Two-Photon Polymerization (2PP): Precise microscale fabrication is a prereq-
uisite for building functional microrobots. Among the available techniques, two-photon
polymerization (2PP) is a lithography-based additive manufacturing method that en-
ables the direct fabrication of three-dimensional micro- and nanostructures with submi-
cron resolution. Specifically, 2PP enables 4D printing by integrating stimuli-responsive
materials (e.g., pH-sensitive hydrogels) with rigid scaffolds in a single fabrication pro-
cess, forming “structural-programmed smart devices” capable of gripping, locomotion,
and sensing [ref:4D_2PP_microrobots]. Compared to other methods (e.g., soft lithog-
raphy, SLS), 2PP offers superior resolution (100–300 nm), design flexibility, and multi-
material capability, making it indispensable despite its lower throughput [ref:2PP_vs_softlithography].
• Significance of pH-responsive materials: pH-responsiveness enables materials to
react to localized acid–base changes, making them vital in drug delivery, biosensing,
and tumor-targeted therapies. In microrobotics, these materials respond autonomously
to physiological pH gradients in biological fluids. They are also used in microfluidic
and environmental platforms as actuators or sensors, and in soft robotics to enable
pH-triggered shape change, locomotion, or gripping.
• Swelling as a strategy for responsive actuation: Swelling is a highly effective method
of translating chemical stimuli (e.g., pH or ionic strength) into mechanical deformation.
Swelling-capable materials enable actuation without external power, producing motions
like bending or shape reconfiguration. High swelling ratios also enhance functionality
for drug delivery or molecular capture at the microscale.
• Need for precise quantification of swelling: Reliable swelling measurement is essen-
tial to evaluate microrobot performance, compare behavior under different conditions,
and guide optimization. Quantifying deformation ensures reproducibility and supports
effective design of responsive systems.
1.1
Project Aim
This project aims to design and characterize a pH-responsive microrobotic component
fabricated via two-photon polymerization (2PP), with emphasis on enabling localized actu-
ation and chemical sensing in microscale environments. Building on a stimuli-responsive
hydrogel developed within our research group, the work is structured around the following
two core objectives:

• Development of a reliable and automated swelling quantification method: To as-
sess the functionality of responsive structures, it is essential to measure geometric
changes resulting from pH-induced deformation. This project establishes a consistent,
automated image-based workflow to extract structural dimensions—particularly the top-
view diameter—from optical microscopy images. The approach is validated through
comparison with manual measurements to ensure accuracy, reproducibility, and ro-

• Investigation and optimization of design and fabrication parameters to enhance

– A focused parameter sweep to refine and determine the optimal fabrication conditions

Both aims contribute to the overarching goal of enabling predictable and programmable
actuation within pH-sensitive microrobotic devices. As a final step, this work also eval-
uates whether the optimized responsive element maintains its swelling capability when
integrated into a functional base structure designed for optical trapping.
1.2
Workflow and Methodology
This project adopts a structured, multi-stage workflow to achieve its two central objectives:
(1) the development of a robust automated method for quantifying the pH-responsive
swelling behavior of 2PP-fabricated hydrogel structures, and (2) the optimization of design
and fabrication parameters to maximize the swelling response. The workflow consists of
the following key stages:
• Design and fabrication of responsive structures: A cylindrical hydrogel geometry
was selected as the responsive unit due to its pH-dependent swelling capability and
compatibility with 2PP fabrication. The cylindrical shape offers geometric simplicity, ro-
tational symmetry, and ease of characterization through circular approximation. Struc-
tures were fabricated using a commercial 2PP system, enabling precise control over
geometry and process parameters.
• Microscopic imaging and automated measurement: Top-view optical microscopy
was used to image the printed structures. To avoid the subjectivity and inefficiency
of manual measurements, an automated pipeline was developed to detect the top-
edge boundary and extract diameter values. The pipeline follows a logical flow from
pH-induced deformation to image-based geometric analysis using contrast-based edge
detection. These diameter measurements form the basis for swelling ratio calculations
across different pH environments.
• Quantification of swelling ratio under pH actuation: The printed hydrogel structures
were immersed in buffered solutions of varying pH levels. Swelling was quantified by
comparing the diameters before and after immersion. The swelling ratio, defined as
the relative change in diameter, served as the primary quantitative metric for evaluating
responsiveness.
• Parameter screening and optimization: An initial screening was conducted using a
Taguchi L9 orthogonal design to evaluate four parameters: cylinder diameter, height,

Theoretical Background for Fabrication
2.1
Two-Photon Polymerization Printing
2.1.1
Two-Photon Absorption
The phenomenon of multi-photon absorption refers to a process in which multiple photons
interact with a molecule or atom in a single quantum event. Among these, two-photon
absorption (TPA) is a nonlinear optical process where a molecule simultaneously absorbs
two photons to reach an excited electronic state. This process contrasts with one-photon
absorption (OPA), a linear mechanism in which a single photon promotes the molecule to
an excited state. OPA typically occurs under low-intensity UV or visible light and has a
relatively large absorption cross section.
In contrast, TPA becomes significant only under extremely high photon densities, such as
those provided by tightly focused femtosecond-pulsed lasers. Because TPA depends on
the square of the light intensity , absorption is confined to a small focal volume, enabling
precise 3D spatial resolution—an advantage not achievable with OPA.
2.1.2
Two-Photon Polymerization Process
While two-photon absorption (TPA) promotes molecules to an excited state, this pro-
cess alone does not lead to polymerization. To achieve polymerization, TPA must oc-
cur in a specially designed photoresist, typically containing photoinitiators and polymer-
izable monomers. The excited photoinitiators generate reactive species—such as free
radicals—which then trigger a chain reaction to form solid polymer structures.
Because TPA only occurs at the focal point of a high-intensity laser beam, the resulting
polymerization is highly localized, often confined to sub-micron dimensions. This spatial
confinement enables three-dimensional micro- and nanoscale fabrication, making two-
photon polymerization a powerful tool for applications requiring high precision, such as
micro-optics, bioengineering, and photonic structures.
2.1.3
Two-Photon Polymerization System: Nanoscribe Photonic
Professional GT2
All 3D microstructures in this study were fabricated using the Nanoscribe Photonic Pro-
fessional GT2, a commercial two-photon polymerization (2PP) system that enables direct
laser writing with submicron precision. The system employs a femtosecond pulsed in-
frared laser (typically 780–800 nm), focused through a high-numerical-aperture objective
to achieve nonlinear absorption and highly localized polymerization at the focal volume.
Key features of the GT2 system include:
• Galvo and piezo scanning modes: Offering a trade-off between speed and accu-
racy for different writing strategies.
• High-resolution objectives: Enabling voxel sizes as small as 200 nm laterally and
500 nm axially.
• Multi-material capability: Supporting material exchange through solvent develop-
ment steps.
• Software integration: Structures were designed and sliced using DeScribe and
printed via Nanoscribe’s proprietary control software (NanoWrite).

In this work, 2PP was used to print multimaterial microrobotic elements, combining a rigid
photopolymer scaffold with a pH-responsive hydrogel component. Laser power, scan
speed, and hatching parameters were varied to control local crosslinking density and
mechanical properties.
IP-L
IP-L (Nanoscribe GmbH) is a negative-tone photoresist commonly used for printing me-
chanically stable, rigid microstructures. It consists of an acrylate-based monomer system
optimized for two-photon absorption and high-resolution 3D fabrication.
In this study, IP-L served as the structural backbone material due to its:
• Excellent printability and low shrinkage during development.
• High stiffness after polymerization, allowing it to support flexible components.
• Compatibility with aqueous post-processing, enabling integration with hydrogels.
Structures printed in IP-L retained their geometry under swelling conditions and provided
a stable base for integrating responsive elements.
AA Hydrogel
The pH-responsive material used for actuation in this study was a hydrogel formulated
from acrylic acid (AA). When polymerized via 2PP, the resulting network exhibits signif-
icant volume changes in response to environmental pH.
Key features include:
• Swelling behavior: At low pH (e.g., pH 2.3), the carboxyl groups of AA remain
protonated, and the hydrogel remains contracted. At higher pH (e.g., pH 7.4), de-
protonation leads to electrostatic repulsion, resulting in substantial swelling.
• Biocompatibility: AA-based hydrogels are frequently used in biomedical applica-
tions such as sensors and drug delivery.
• Printability: When appropriately formulated with photoinitiators and solvents, AA
hydrogels can be printed via 2PP, though with lower stiffness and higher shrinkage
than IP-L.
In this work, AA hydrogel structures were printed directly adjacent to or on top of rigid IP-L
base features. Their reversible swelling under pH change enabled quantitative measure-
ment of actuation performance as a function of fabrication parameters.

Image Analysis and Automated
Measurement
To enable practical and consistent quantification of pH-responsive deformation, this study
abstracts the complex physical response of the hydrogel microstructures into an image-
based computational task. Rather than attempting to reconstruct the full 3D geometry—
which is limited by optical constraints and unnecessary for relative comparison—we focus
on extracting consistent geometric metrics from visually observable contours.
This work does not aim to advance edge detection theory, but rather applies and adapts
existing methods to develop a repeatable and scalable pipeline for measuring the geom-
etry of 3D-printed hydrogel microstructures. The goal is to enable accurate and repro-
ducible swelling ratio quantification under varying fabrication conditions.
In other words, a complex material response problem is transformed into a repeatable,
computational image analysis problem—enabled by the fact that swelling leads to geo-
metric expansion, which becomes observable as structural movement in microscopy im-
ages. This abstraction follows a multi-step logical path, which in this study branches into
two complementary routes: a contrast-based approach relying on edge detection, and a
segmentation-based approach relying on region classification.
1. Swelling →Geometry Hydrogel structures swell in response to environmental pH due
to osmotic water uptake driven by ionization of functional groups. This swelling is in-
herently a geometric transformation—it causes an increase in the physical dimensions
of the printed structure, such as diameter, height, or volume.
2. Geometry →Image-Observable Feature In microscopy images—especially under
top-down observation—the most accessible representation of geometric change is the
position of the outer edge or region boundary. As the structure expands, this contour
shifts outward. Capturing that shift allows us to infer geometric deformation.
At this point, two strategies diverge:
• A. Edge-Based Path: Edge →Contrast →Measurement In brightfield optical mi-
croscopy, edges are not measured directly but are inferred from variations in image
intensity. The boundary between the hydrogel and surrounding medium appears as
a contrast gradient due to light scattering, refractive index mismatch, and optical path
accumulation. This contrast enables manual or automated edge detection—through
visual fitting or gradient-based algorithms—yielding geometric parameters such as
diameter.
While multiple geometric metrics could be used—such as area or perimeter—diameter
was chosen for its simplicity, visual clarity, and historical use in manual characteri-
zation. Diameter is also less sensitive to segmentation artifacts than area, making it
more robust for consistent comparison.
• B. Segmentation-Based Path: Geometry →Region →Measurement An alter-
native route is to apply trained segmentation models (e.g., Omnipose) to identify the
structure as a foreground region. These models generate masks directly from pixel-
wise features, enabling boundary extraction through region geometry rather than
Thesis title
7
==PAGE:16==
local gradients. Once segmented, geometric fitting (e.g., minimum enclosing circle)
is applied to the region mask to derive the diameter.
This approach benefits from spatial consistency, scalability across structure sizes,
and relative immunity to subtle image contrast variations. Adjusting the reference
boundary (e.g., outer vs. inner edge) requires only a single post-processing step,
independent of absolute structure dimensions.
Each link in the chain represents a modeling decision with alternative possibilities:
• Swelling could be measured through volume change, surface curvature, or out-of-plane
deformation. However, top-view lateral expansion (diameter) was chosen due to ease
of acquisition and relevance to functional displacement.
• Geometry need not be inferred from the edge alone; techniques like full-profile recon-
struction (e.g., confocal z-stacking) exist, but are time-consuming and require assump-
tions about symmetry and opacity.
• Feature extraction could be edge-based or region-based; both are implemented here
and compared.
• Measurement is ultimately constrained by how clearly the structure can be separated
from the background—through contrast gradients or regional masks—making edge vis-
ibility and segmentation fidelity key limiting factors.
This decision pathway reflects a balance between accuracy, reproducibility, throughput,
and implementation complexity—guiding the construction of the automated measurement
pipeline used in this study.
3.1
Overview and Imaging Setup
Despite the optical transparency of the hydrogel structures and their immersion in a liq-
uid of similar refractive index, the boundary between the structures and the background
remains distinguishable under brightfield microscopy. The edge detection task in this
context presents several challenges arising from both material properties and imaging
conditions:
1. Multi-layer contribution due to transparency: Although the top surface is theoreti-
cally the most swollen layer, it does not obscure the layers below due to the material’s
transparency. As a result, images taken with different focal depths across the structure
height still display contributions from multiple axial layers, making it difficult to identify
the exact top boundary.
2. Enhanced edge contrast from layer accumulation: Interestingly, the stacking of
multiple partially visible layers often enhances the contrast at the outer edge, which
makes the boundary visually more distinct. Cylinders with greater height tend to exhibit
sharper-appearing edges, whereas structures with minimal height appear more diffuse
and difficult to detect.
3. Trade-offs in magnification and imaging fidelity: Lower magnification provides
greater depth of field and easier focus stability, but offers limited resolution due to
fewer pixels across the structure. Higher magnification provides better spatial reso-
lution but leads to a shallower depth of field, stronger sensitivity to focal shifts, and
increased image noise—especially problematic with transparent structures.
4. Selection of consistent measurement reference: Since the true boundary of the top
surface is neither directly observable nor practically relevant in a comparative study,

we define the analysis boundary as the most visually distinct outer edge. All images
were acquired at 500× magnification to balance clarity and resolution.
3.2
Circular Fitting
Although the intended design of the structures is cylindrical, the 2PP fabrication process
is inherently point-by-point or line-by-line, which can introduce anisotropies in the final
structure. In this study, we recognize three main sources of such deviations:
1. Z–XY anisotropy: The resolution and material response in the z-direction are gener-
ally worse than in the xy-plane. However, this study focuses exclusively on in-plane
(xy) swelling behavior, and we have also found that any anisotropy in the z-direction
does not noticeably impact our measurements or conclusions. Thus, only the swelling
in XY is considered from now on.
2. Scan-direction effects: We employed a staggered scan strategy to mitigate direc-
tional accumulation of error during layer-by-layer writing. While this approach cannot
eliminate all anisotropy, it significantly reduces its practical influence on the overall
symmetry of the structure.
3. Thermal accumulation and light-material interaction: These are known to induce
deformation or asymmetry in polymerized microstructures. For single hydrogel mi-
crostructures with dimensions on the order of tens of micrometers, the effect of ther-
mal accumulation under standard two-photon polymerization (2PP) conditions can be
considered negligible based on empirical observations.
However, the precise impact of these effects is difficult to assess in our case. The struc-
tures are small, printed in transparent material, and imaged via optical microscopy—
meaning that the top-view edges are never perfectly sharp or unambiguous. Under these
imaging constraints, even if a measurement standard is defined, there remains no reliable
way to determine whether subtle deviations (e.g., in roundness or eccentricity) arise from
the structure itself or from detection artifacts.
This study does not aim to evaluate the geometric perfection or isotropy of the printed
structures, but rather to quantify their swelling response under different pH conditions.
The measurement is based on the top-view geometry, and the primary goal is to establish
a consistent and reproducible method for estimating swelling. This choice also aligns with
empirical observations.
In this context, we found that fitting a perfect circle to the top-view edge—both manually
and automatically—provided visually satisfactory results. Manual measurements used
the circular selection tool in ImageJ, and visual inspection indicated little to no mismatch
between the selection and the actual boundary. The automated pipeline was developed
to replicate this choice. As a result, circular fitting serves not only as a practical approx-
imation but also as a unified geometric model that supports comparability across mea-
surement modes.
3.3
Manual Edge Measurement
In all the fields, the distinction between ”manual” and ”automated” methods is always
inherently relative. Manual workflows typically involve active human decision-making, of-
ten aided by different kinds of tools, while automated processes rely more on mechanical
or algorithmic routines. In this study, the manual measurement procedure was imple-
mented using ImageJ, an open-source image analysis platform. Although the workflow
Thesis title
9
==PAGE:18==
required software interaction, the core decisions—such as shape selection and boundary
interpretation—were made visually and manually by the user.
The manual procedure, based on practical experience, visual intuition, and operational
convenience, follows the logic chain discussed previously—centered on contrast-based
edge detection. The central detection mechanism in this manual context is human vision.
As such, the role of ImageJ is to facilitate, but not replace, edge-based interpretation
through contrast.
For the transition from edge detection to the actual characterization and measurement of
the edge, several possible strategies were considered:
• Drawing a straight line by hand across the diameter based on visual intuition;
• Clicking multiple boundary points and fitting a circle to those coordinates;
• Using the Oval Selection tool in ImageJ (note: despite its name, this tool supports cir-
cular fitting).
Considering both mathematical consistency and practical efficiency, the third approach
was adopted. Each visually distinct top-view structure was framed using a perfect circle
via the Oval Selection tool, assuming approximate circularity as justified earlier. For each
image, the number of selected structures varied, and each was measured individually.
Upon completing the selections, the “List Elements” function in ImageJ was used to extract
the coordinates and diameter of each circular region.
All measurements were initially recorded in pixels, as this is the unit used by both the im-
age and ImageJ’s internal outputs. To convert the diameters into physical units (microm-
eters), a known pixel-to-micron calibration factor was applied, derived from the optical
magnification (e.g., 500×) used during imaging, as specified in the microscope documen-
tation.
3.4
Automated Edge Measurement
To implement automated geometry measurement, two distinct approaches were developed—
one based on classical edge detection and the other on deep-learning-based segmenta-
tion. The first method follows a traditional edge-based pipeline. It begins with Canny
filtering, which identifies all strong local intensity gradients across the image, producing
a binary edge map that includes both object contours and potential noise. The result is
then passed to a Hough Circle Transform, which attempts to identify circular structures
by matching subsets of edge pixels to circular templates within a specified radius range.
This approach is intuitive and computationally efficient, though highly dependent on local
edge characteristics. The second method employs a segmentation-based approach us-
ing the Omnipose model, which generates pixel-wise masks for objects based on learned
texture and shape priors. These binary masks are post-processed to extract geometric
features, such as the diameter, through circle fitting. In later study, the Omnipose pipeline
was adapted to the specific visual properties of printed hydrogel structures.
These methods were compared across multiple imaging scenarios. The following obser-
vations summarize their respective behaviors:
1. Performance under varying image clarity On well-focused, low-magnification (500×)
images, both methods yielded accurate and consistent results. However, with high-
magnification (1500×) or lower-quality images, differences in robustness became pro-
nounced:
10
Thesis title
==PAGE:19==
• The Omnipose-based method tended to miss some structures entirely under poor
contrast or noisy backgrounds. It operates conservatively, only segmenting regions
that confidently match learned object features. As such, it reduces false positives
but risks underdetection, particularly when boundaries are indistinct or occluded by
texture.
• The Hough-based method, on the other hand, is more aggressive and often attempts
to fulfill the target number of detections regardless of edge quality. In noisy or low-
contrast conditions, this leads to frequent false positives, such as partial arcs, internal
rings, or fitting circular shapes to background textures. It may even ”hallucinate”
circles in noisy regions to meet its output quota.
These behaviors are directly influenced by how each method interprets edge sharp-
ness, width, and intensity gradient:
• Low magnification images typically exhibit sharp edge transitions—thin boundary
regions with high pixel-level contrast—allowing both methods to perform well.
• High magnification images, by contrast, exhibit: Wider boundary zones (due to trans-
parency and light scattering), Lower peak gradients, and Broader, slower contrast
transitions, which visually appear as blurring.
Such subtle contrast cues degrade edge reliability for Hough and region confidence
for Omnipose, though their error modes differ.
2. Edge positioning and parameter adjustability In circular structures—regardless of
edge clarity—Hough-based detection cannot reliably distinguish between inner and
outer contours unless the radius range is tightly constrained. Without precisely tuned
minRadius and maxRadius values, it may match to an inner ring, middle arc, or the
outer boundary, depending on the local gradient distribution. Moreover, these radius
parameters are tightly coupled to the expected structure size, requiring reconfiguration
for each magnification level or structure batch.
By contrast, the Omnipose-based method always segments the full structure, and geo-
metric measurement is extracted from the entire mask region. To select the outermost
boundary or adjust measurement depth (e.g., inner vs. outer edge), only a single di-
lation or erosion operation is needed—regardless of structure size or magnification.
This makes Omnipose more scalable and less sensitive to parameter tuning. Once
reasonable defaults are established, the same model and parameters can be applied
consistently with little or no adjustment.
3. Theoretical extensibility Although not utilized in this project, the segmentation-based
pipeline offers greater extensibility in downstream analysis. Since the full mask of each
structure is preserved, additional shape descriptors—such as area, roundness, aspect
ratio, or curvature—can be extracted with minimal additional processing. In contrast,
the Hough transform is limited to circular templates and does not generalize to non-
circular or irregular geometries, making it less suitable for studies involving diverse
structural morphologies.
In summary, the segmentation-based method using Omnipose was selected for all sub-
sequent automated measurements due to two primary advantages:
• Improved robustness under real-world imaging conditions, particularly when edge con-
trast is degraded;

• Low parameter sensitivity, enabling consistent use across datasets with minimal recon-
figuration.

3.4.1
Automated Detection via Segmentation: Adaptation of the
Omnipose Pipeline
To implement a robust and generalizable method for automated structure detection, a
segmentation-based approach was adopted using the Omnipose model. Omnipose is
an extension of the Cellpose framework, designed specifically for high-precision instance
segmentation of cell-like and blob-like objects. Compared with conventional threshold-
based methods, Omnipose combines deep learning with spatial flow reconstruction to
produce high-quality segmentation masks, even under noisy or low-contrast imaging con-
ditions.
Omnipose outputs a binary mask for each detected object, delineating its full contour
regardless of edge sharpness or shape irregularities. Unlike classical edge detection
methods, it does not rely on local gradient peaks but learns global object features, making
it well suited for the ambiguous and blurry boundaries typical of hydrogel structures under
optical microscopy.
In this study, the pretrained Omnipose model (model_type='cyto') was used as a starting
point. To tailor the segmentation pipeline to our specific imaging and geometry conditions,
the following modifications were introduced:
• Preprocessing Standardization: Since the original Omnipose pipeline expects im-
age dimensions and intensity scaling optimized for fluorescence cell images, we im-
plemented a custom normalization step using normalize99(), followed by grayscale
conversion for our brightfield images. This ensured contrast enhancement and con-
sistent dynamic range across image batches.
• Parameter Tuning: Several segmentation parameters were adjusted for better per-
formance on hydrogel structures:
– mask_threshold was increased to 1.0 to avoid over-segmentation of faint pe-
ripheral artifacts.
– flow_threshold was reduced to 0 to accommodate weak flow fields at blurred
boundaries.

4.1
Justification for Using a Cylindrical Structure
The responsive component in this study was designed as a cylinder, based on both prac-
tical considerations for automated analysis and broader relevance to functional micro-
robotic design.
From a technical standpoint, this project involved developing a custom automated pipeline
for detecting and measuring microscale structures. Cylinders offer a geometrically simple
and symmetric form, which facilitates fabrication, optical imaging, and algorithm devel-
opment. In particular, circular shapes are well supported by classical image processing
techniques—such as edge detection combined with Hough transforms or segmentation
followed by circular fitting. These methods are more robust and computationally efficient
for circles than for irregular or anisotropic shapes. Furthermore, the top-view projection
of a cylinder aligns naturally with brightfield microscopy, where the swelling-induced de-
formation appears primarily as lateral expansion of the circular contour.
In addition to algorithmic convenience, cylindrical geometries are themselves widely used
in microscale device applications due to their symmetry, isotropy, and ease of fabrication
via two-photon polymerization (2PP). Their uniform response under environmental stimuli
simplifies mechanical modeling and improves reliability in actuation or sensing tasks.
Taken together, these considerations make cylinders a natural and effective choice for
both the development of automated analysis methods and the design of functional pH-
responsive microrobotic components.
4.2
Selection of Key Design Parameters
Although our parameter design was intended for hydrogel structures, the experiments
involving fillet, contour count, and height were conducted using IP-L. This was because
our initial focus was solely on printability, and in this regard, the two materials do not
exhibit significant differences. At that stage, the potential effects of material differences
on swelling behavior were not yet considered. Moreover, the observations were qualitative
rather than quantitative.
Based on these observations, it was found that differences in fillet and contour count did
not have a noticeable impact on the print quality of the cylinders. Therefore, in subsequent
designs, a configuration with no fillet and a contour count of zero was adopted.
As for height, all tested values at the current diameter were successfully printed without
collapse or structural failure. However, due to the transparency of the material, cylinders
with heights less than 10 μm exhibited poorly defined or indistinct edges. Thus, a minimum
height of 10 μm was required for all subsequent experiments.
4.3
Taguchi L9 Orthogonal Design: Initial Multi-Parameter
Screening
The swelling performance of hydrogel-based cylindrical structures is influenced by mul-
tiple geometric and fabrication parameters. In the early stages of this study, four factors
were selected for investigation based on theoretical relevance and fabrication feasibility:
Thesis title
17
==PAGE:26==
• Diameter (D): Larger structures may experience reduced swelling near the center
due to limited solvent penetration, leading to non-uniform expansion.
• Height (H): Since the base of the cylinder is rigidly attached to the substrate, swelling
is constrained near the bottom. Taller cylinders may allow greater freedom for the
upper layers to expand, resulting in higher overall swelling.
• Scan Speed (SS) and Laser Power (LP): These jointly determine the exposure
dose during two-photon polymerization. Higher exposure results in denser crosslink-
ing and stiffer networks, which restrict swelling. Conversely, lower exposure may
improve deformability but risks under-polymerization and structural collapse.
In addition to their individual effects, potential interactions between these factors may
also play a significant role. However, many of these dependencies are difficult to model
or predict purely from theory. Hence, a data-driven experimental approach was adopted
to systematically investigate their combined influence.
To establish a feasible parameter space for subsequent optimization, a broad multi-parameter
screening was performed. Based on prior experience within the research group, each fac-
tor was assigned three discrete levels:
• Height (H): 10 µm, 20 µm, 30 µm
• Diameter (D): 10 µm, 20 µm, 30 µm
• Scan Speed (SS): 6000 µm/s, 8000 µm/s, 10000 µm/s
• Laser Power (LP): 75 mW, 80 mW, 85 mW
Testing all 81 combinations (34) would be impractical at this stage. Therefore, a fractional
factorial design was employed using a Taguchi L9 orthogonal array, which reduces the
total number of experiments to 9 while still allowing main effects to be estimated efficiently.
The nine parameter combinations are shown in Table ??, and the corresponding printing
array and results are illustrated in Figure ??.
Based on the results of the initial screening, it was observed that although scan speed (SS)
and laser power (LP) jointly determine the exposure dose and are thus highly correlated,
their individual effects on print success are distinct. In our experiments, a high LP was the
primary cause of overexposure and printing failure due to localized burning. In contrast,
even the lowest SS tested (6000 µm/s), which also leads to a high exposure dose, did not
result in print failure.
Consequently, LP was reduced to mitigate printing defects, while SS was retained at its
lower bound (6000 µm/s) to ensure sufficient exposure. To avoid under-polymerization,
the maximum SS level was also reduced in the refined parameter space.
Regarding geometry, height was expected to have a strong effect on swelling, but using
tall structures with small diameters may introduce structural instability (e.g., toppling or
collapse during swelling). To mitigate this and maintain structural integrity, the diameter
was fixed at 20 µm for subsequent experiments.
Combining these considerations, the refined factor levels used for further parameter op-
timization were as follows:
• Diameter (D): fixed at 20 µm
• Height (H): 10 µm, 20 µm, 30 µm

4.4
Parameter Sweep of Height, Laser Power, and Scan
Speed
A full factorial design was employed to investigate the effects of key fabrication parame-
ters on the swelling behavior of the 3D-printed hydrogel structures. Three factors were
selected: laser power, scan speed, and cylinder height, each tested at three distinct lev-
els. This resulted in a total of 3 × 3 × 3 = 27 unique combinations.
• For each parameter combination, four identical cylindrical structures were printed.
• To ensure statistical reliability, the entire set of 27 conditions was repeated in three
independent experimental runs, resulting in 81 total measurements.
• This design allowed for the evaluation of both main effects and two-way interactions
among the three fabrication parameters.
Although a full factorial design with 27 unique parameter combinations was implemented,
the printing was not performed in a fully randomized sequence. Instead, samples with the
same height were printed consecutively, primarily to simplify the experimental workflow
due to equipment constraints. While this deviates from strict randomization, we consider
the potential impact of this ordering to be negligible under the controlled conditions of
our cleanroom environment and the stability of the 2PP printing system. In future stud-
ies, randomized printing sequences may be implemented to further reduce any potential
confounding effects.
For the statistical analysis, we adopted a batch-level aggregation approach. That is, the
average swelling rate was first computed for each batch using the four cylinders printed
under identical conditions. These three batch-level averages were then used to compute
the overall mean swelling rate and the associated standard deviation for the corresponding
parameter combination.
• This method captures the between-batch variability, which reflects the reproducibility
of the printing and swelling behavior under repeated fabrication. The calculated stan-
dard deviation is therefore based on the three batch-level means, yielding a degrees of
freedom of two.
• This approach is particularly appropriate for assessing whether the fabrication process
produces consistent results under nominally identical conditions.
• However, a limitation of this method is that it does not incorporate within-batch variability
(i.e., variation among the four cylinders in the same batch), which may also contribute
to the total experimental uncertainty.
Nevertheless, since the primary goal of this analysis is to evaluate the stability of the
process across independent repetitions (rather than quantify every source of noise), the
current method was deemed suitable.

Conclusion
This project investigated the design, fabrication, and quantitative characterization of pH-
responsive hydrogel microrobotic structures using two-photon polymerization (2PP). A
cylindrical geometry was selected for its geometric simplicity and robustness in automated
image analysis. Using both manual and automated approaches, the top-view expansion
of hydrogel structures was measured under pH actuation. A segmentation-based detec-
tion pipeline, adapted from Omnipose, was implemented and validated, enabling repro-
ducible swelling quantification based on structural diameter.
To identify optimal design and fabrication conditions, a two-stage parameter exploration
was conducted: an initial Taguchi L9 screening and a subsequent parameter sweep fo-
cused on height, laser power, and scan speed. The swelling rate was used as the per-
formance metric to evaluate each structure’s responsiveness, and the best-performing
parameter combination was subsequently integrated into an optical trapping-compatible
base structure to test its retained functionality.
The results demonstrate that (i) swelling rate can be consistently quantified using top-
view geometry, (ii) segmentation-based measurement pipelines can operate reliably un-
der varying image quality, and (iii) process parameters significantly influence the respon-
sive behavior of 2PP-fabricated hydrogel devices. This work establishes a foundational
workflow for integrating automated measurement with design optimization in microscale
stimuli-responsive systems.
Future Work and Limitations
1. Swelling rate definition: Although the swelling rate was ultimately defined in terms
of surface area, all measurements were conducted via diameter. This choice was
made for convenience and robustness, but further analysis could explore whether
alternative geometric descriptors (e.g., height, curvature) yield more meaningful or
multidimensional definitions of swelling—particularly if additional imaging modalities
(e.g., confocal or side-view microscopy) become available.
2. Robustness to blur: Both image processing pipelines (edge-based and segmentation-
based) can be further improved for performance on low-contrast or blurred images.
Future work could explore advanced denoising, attention-guided segmentation, or
synthetic training augmentation.
3. Quantification of roundness: While this study used circular fitting as a practical
approximation, a more rigorous evaluation of circularity—especially post-swelling—
could provide insight into mechanical anisotropy or structural instabilities.
4. Effect of magnification: Image quality differences across magnification levels were
only qualitatively assessed. A future study could use manually measured results
as a baseline to compare whether swelling measurements are consistent across
magnifications.
5. Shape generalization: The current detection and fitting pipeline is specific to circu-
lar structures. Adapting the segmentation and measurement methods to accommo-
date other shapes (e.g., ellipses, crosses, custom stimuli-responsive geometries) is
a natural next step.

6. Design material inconsistency: Although the final experiments focus on hydrogel
responsiveness, early design studies (e.g., fillet radius, contour count, and height
effects) were performed using IP-L due to ease of handling and printability.
At
that stage, swelling was not yet considered, and only qualitative observations were
made. It was observed that fillet and contour count had negligible impact on print
fidelity for small cylinders, and that cylinders below 10 µm in height became visually

7. Error metrics: The method comparison relied on absolute difference in diameters.
