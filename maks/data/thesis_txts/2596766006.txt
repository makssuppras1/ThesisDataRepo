Abstract
Biochar is a substance that can be used to solve many problems currently being faced
on our planet, including mitigating climate change, improving food security, providing a
clean alternative to fossil fuels and reducing the load on waste management systems. It is
a charcoal-like by-product produced by the thermal conversion of biomass under a limited
supply of oxygen and at relatively low temperatures. Its unique physical properties of
having an increased surface area and porosity make it very attractive to be used for the
purpose of sequestration of CO2 in soils.
This report aims to study the properties of
biochar and propose a strategy to increase biochar implementation and then perform a
techno-economic analysis on a biochar production plant.
The key parameters for improving the yield of biochar are studied and an implementation
strategy in the form of a decision support tool is proposed based on a collection of results
from the literature.
This tool has the potential to increase awareness on the benefit
of biochar by making it easier for biochar producers and consumers to understand how
biochar can be used as a soil amendment. A techno-economic analysis of two production
processes of biochar was feasible by studying it’s profitability based on its net present value.
A sensitivity analysis was also done to study the robustness of the system and analyse the
effects of market and engineering variables on the system’s profitability. Following this, a
case study on MASH Energy’s current production process was undertaken to determine
the impact of practical variables such as raw material price, biochar selling price and the
price of CO2 being traded on the global market.
Based on the above studies, a recommendation is made to MASH Energy on how to proceed
with the production and implementation of biochar to have an increased probability of
achieving positive results related to benefiting society and the environment by amending
targeted problem soils with biochar, while also maintaining profitability.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Motivation and Problem Statement
The motivation for this report is to theoretically investigate the potential for the produc-
tion and implementation of a system to produce biochar for MASH Energy. There is a lack
of information connecting the agricultural science knowledge of biochar, the engineering
knowledge on biochar production and the commercial aspect of biochar. This report aims
to converge these three different fields to get a better overall perspective on biochar to
help support the goal of having a more sustainable future.
Climate change is one of the most critical challenges that the world is facing today, causing
global surface temperature to rise, warming of oceans, more droughts and heatwaves,
shrinking ice sheets, decreased snow cover, rising sea levels, ocean acidification and other
extreme events like increased rainfall and intense winds. This is caused by gases in the
atmosphere like carbon dioxide (CO2), Methane (CH4), water vapour (H2O) and Nitrous
oxides (NOx) that block the heat from escaping. The urgency to address these issues
creates a demand for finding viable solutions to reduce the effects of climate change that
can be implemented immediately or in the near future.
The use of biochar as a soil amendment could prove to be an effective measure to help
tackle climate change. Biochar is known to have unique properties that allow it to sequester
atmospheric carbon dioxide in soils for the long term, thereby reducing greenhouse gas
emissions and improving soil health and quality, thereby improving crop productivity.
However, there have been very few cases of large scale implementation of biochar as a
crop amendment worldwide. This is possibly due to the lack of knowledge in this field
as it is relatively new and there is still a lot of research taking place about the effects of
biochar.
The biochar approach provides a uniquely powerful solution: it allows us to address food
security, the fuel crisis and the climate problem, and all in an immensely practical manner.
Among the most valuable outcomes of the application of the biochar technologies are
greatly increased economic efficiency in agriculture, enhanced crop yields, and slowing the
return to the atmosphere of carbon captured by plants. The result is diverse and clean
energy supplies, more food per unit of input, and climate security. [1]

reproducible data. Further, studying current biochar production techniques, identifying
key engineering problems and then solving these problems efficiently would lead to the

An implementation strategy in the form of a decision support tool can help connect agri-

compared by varying parameters to identify the conditions at which an optimum solution
for maximising profitability of the venture is obtained. This will facilitate certainty in
making a final decision on whether to invest in such a venture or not.
A case study of the current manufacturing process of bio-oil by pyrolysis of cashew shells
is undertaken to identify the potential of selling biochar and explore it’s financial benefits.
The case study compares the present situation and a future where biochar is sold. This
would give MASH Energy more information about biochar and work as a guideline for
future expansion and decision making.
1.3
Methodology
This report studies different aspects of the topic of biochar:
• The study on what biochar is and how it is beneficial
• How to produce biochar
• Where biochar can be used as a soil amendment
• The economics behind the production of biochar
A comprehensive literature study was conducted to study what exactly biochar is and to
determine the reason why it has been gaining in popularity as a possible tool to help fight
climate change. This was done by reviewing articles and papers using search terms such
as ”biochar”, ”pyrolysis”, ”biokol”, ”gasification”, ”black carbon”, ”carbon sequestration”,
”carbon capture”, ”carbon storage”, ”carbon stability”, ”terra preta”, ”soil amendment”,
”bio energy” and ”climate change” on online databases like Web of Science, DTU Findit
and google scholar.
This resulted in obtaining a broad perspective into the world of
biochar.
From the literature review, it was observed that there exists a lack of uniformity related
to the information provided in articles and research papers on the production conditions
for the manufacture of biochar.
To address this issue, researchers and experts in the
field of biochar were contacted for interviews.
Interviews were conducted in the form
of email questionnaires, telephonic conversations, video calls and meetings in person. A
total of 89 researchers and 22 companies working with biochar were contacted during the
course of this report. Questions were asked to ascertain the technology readiness level

of biochar production and to gain perspectives on the biochar market based on their
experiences. The key parameters affecting crop growth, case studies where biochar has
been implemented successfully, identifying reproducible results, reliability of data and
finding out what information is lacking was discussed. This information provided new
perspectives on the possible applications of biochar and gave insights on the problems
currently being faced in commercializing biochar and scaling up it’s implementation.
Based on the results from the interviews of the researchers and experts in the field of
biochar, it was decided that there was a lack of knowledge on what type of biochar could be
used on a particular soil type and how that biochar is to be manufactured. To address this
issue, it was decided to create a decision support tool to help guide biochar manufacturers
and prospective buyers of biochar to create and purchase an appropriate biochar to amend
problem soils.
The current manufacturing processes being used by MASH Energy were studied based on
discussions and information provided by Krishna Chakravarty, Head of R & D, MASH
Energy. Using the collected information, mass and energy balance calculations for the two
systems were made and were used as the basis for the techno-economic analysis of the
two systems being used by MASH Energy. The capital expenses and operating expenses
were determined based on the costing and project evaluation calculations from Coulson and
Richardson’s Chemical Engineering Design. The profitability of the system was calculated
based on the net present value of the system over a period of five years. A sensitivity

2.1
What is biochar?
Biochar can be defined as a charcoal like by-product produced during the thermal con-
version of biomass such as wood, manure or leaves under limited supply of Oxygen and
at relatively low temperatures, usually below 700°C).[1] It features small, black, light and
highly porous fragments just like charcoal. It is mostly composed of carbon and ash, how-
ever the overall composition depends on various factors such as the type of biomass used
as the feedstock and the production conditions such as temperature, pressure, residence
time and heating rate.[3] Biochar is applied to soil in a deliberate manner with the intent
to improve soil properties. This is a fundamental difference between biochar and other
forms of carbon such as charcoal, which can be used as a source of energy and activated
carbon, which can be used for filtration applications. It shows promise in mitigating cli-
mate change, improving soil quality as well as reducing waste and producing energy as a
by-product. Thus, it has been identified as a promising technology for reducing carbon
emissions by the International Panel on Climate Change. [4]
2.2
History
The history of biochar is thought to have originated over two thousand years ago in the
Amazon Basin of South America. Archaeological studies have indicated that extensive
regions of dark, highly fertile soil known as terra preta (Portuguese for black earth) that
was used by native Amazonian’s to amend nutrient-poor tropical soils in order to prosper
in agrarian civilizations. It is still believed to cover 10% of the Amazon Basin. A map
showing the distribution of known terra preta sites is shown in Figure 2.1. Upon analysis
of this terra preta soil, high concentrations of charcoal and organic matter such as plant
and animal remains were discovered.[5] The key ingredient in these terra preta soils was
found to be carbon. These soils contain up to 9 percent carbon, compared to surrounding
soils which contain only up to 0.5 percent carbon and can hold up to 250 tonnes of carbon
per hectare compared to a maximum of 100 tonnes per hectare of carbon in surrounding
soils. The extra carbon is not just in the char, but also in the organic carbon and en-
hanced bacterial biomass that the char sustains. High-carbon char changes the chemical
behaviour of the soil, facilitating its retention of nutrients, thereby improving growing
conditions. These terra preta soils have been found to retain soil fertility for centuries and
produce a yield of up to four times greater than crops grown on regular soil.[6] A graphical
representation of terra preta compared to regular soil is shown in Figure 2.2.
The reason behind the dark black colouring of the terra preta soil is the extra carbon
contained in the terra preta soil. As living organisms cannot consume these thermally

for thousands of years, leading to an increase in nutrients gathered, water retained and
providing a home to different types of micro-organisms. This leads to an increase in crop

Typically, jungle soils are not very fertile. It is theorized that the natives of the Amazon
Basin used a ’slash and char’ method to develop a fertile soil. In this method, large deep
earthen pits were dug and filled with food scraps, agricultural waste and other organically
decomposable residues.[8] This waste was then set on fire and covered with soil, ensuring
high heat and low oxygen conditions. The carbon created from the burning of this waste
was then mixed as biochar into the topsoil. Most of the carbon in the vegetation was
isolated, creating a particularly hospitable amendment, which in turn nurtured beneficial
micro-organisms that transformed the degraded soil to extremely rich and stable humus.[9]
The soil was believed to be able to retain vital organic matter, plant nutrients and moisture
essential for plant growth. This practice allowed annual cultivation of the same farmlands,
rather than slash and burn practices necessary to grow crops on new land every few years.
Due to a stable location for agriculture and soils made fertile by terra preta, steady food
supply was possible, thus allowing for the establishment of large communities.[10]
The first reference to biochar’s discovery was by Swedish geologist Carl W. Scheele in
1870, noting areas in South America with peculiarly productive soils and high absorptive
qualities. Modern-day research into terra preta soils began with the work of Wim Som-
broek in the 1960s. Sombroek lived through the time of the Dutch famine in 1944 when
his father improved the fertility by adding ash and cinders from their home. Sombroek
later came across terra preta of the amazon and found it similar to what was done by his
father, which made him more interested in this topic, finally culminating in his 1966 book
- Amazon soils, which was the first scientific study on terra preta.

2.3
Properties of biochar
Since biochar is a generic term used to refer to the high carbon solid formed due to thermal
transformation of organic matter, the material could have originated from a diverse range
of biomass products. The physical and chemical properties of biochar are determined by
the feedstock and the pyrolysis operating conditions. The combined heterogeneity of the
feedstock and the full range of chemical reactions which occur during processing gives rise
to a biochar product with a unique set of physical and chemical characteristics.
Biochar’s physical and chemical nature gives it the unique ability to attract and hold
moisture, nutrients and agro-chemicals. Biochar also has the ability to hold gases such as
Co2 and N2O, which reduces the emissions of these gases into the atmosphere. Biochar’s
large surface area and complex pore structure also provide a secure habitat for micro-
organisms and fungi, which can help improve the nutrient-uptake capacity of plants. [12]
2.3.1
Structure
Thermal degradation of cellulose between 250°C and 350°C results in a loss of mass in
the form of volatiles, leaving behind a rigid amorphous carbon matrix. As the pyrolysis
temperature increases, so thus the proportion of aromatic carbon in the biochar, due to
the relative increase in the loss of volatile matter and the conversion of alkyl C to aryl C.
Around 330°C, polyaromatic graphene sheets begin to grow laterally, at the expense of the
amorphous C phase and eventually come together. Above 600°C, carbonization becomes
the dominant process and is marked by the removal of most remaining non-C atoms and
consequent relative increase of the C content, which can be up to 90% (by weight) in
biochars from woody feedstocks.[13]

2.3.2
Physical Properties
Physical properties of biochar can have both direct and indirect effects on soil systems.
Soils have their distinct physical properties depending upon the nature of mineral and
organic matter present in them, their relative amounts and ways in which these minerals
and organic matter are related. When biochar is present in these soil mixtures, it can influ-
ence physical factors of the soil such as depth, texture, structure, porosity and consistency
through changing the bulk surface area, pore size distribution, particle size distribution,
density and packing. These can then have a direct impact on plant growth as this depends
on the depth of penetration and availability of water in the soil. In addition, indirectly,
many chemical and biological aspects of soil fertility can be inferred from physical prop-
erties, such as the physical presence of sites for chemical reactions and the provision of
protective habitats for soil microbes. [15]
Wood-based feedstocks produce coarser and more resistant biochar on pyrolysis, while
manure crop residues such as rye and maize produce finer and less robust biochar. Fine
biochar has high ash content and tends to be nutrient-rich and therefore, more readily
degradable by microorganisms compared to woody feedstocks. Though biochar may be
produced from a variety of different feedstocks and under different pyrolysis conditions,
it’s high carbon content, and aromatic structure are constant features. [13]
2.3.3
Chemical Properties
The exact chemical composition of biochar is a function of the conditions during com-
bustion, such as temperature and moisture content of the fuel. Biochar composition is
highly heterogeneous, containing both stable and labile components. Carbon, volatile mat-
ter, mineral matter (ash) and moisture are generally regarded as its major constituents,
and the relative proportion of these components determines the physical and chemical
behaviour of biochar, which in turn determines it’s suitability for application for different
soil types. [1]

2.4.1
Soil improvement
One of the most widespread applications of biochar is as a soil amendment. Many regions
across the world have very poor soils which are weathered and nutrient-depleted. These
conditions are not conducive for crop growth, and the productivity of these crops is very
low as a result. This results in a lack of food security and has a negative effect on the socio-
economic development of the region. This is especially common in sub-Saharan Africa and
South Asia, where malnutrition is a critical issue. [18]
Improving agricultural practices by the introduction of new technologies such as irrigation,
improving crop variety, and the use of pesticides and fertilizers are being introduced in
these regions. However, it is not enough for sustainable agricultural growth. Fertilizers

charge and charge density, therefore offering the possibility to improve crop yield. [11]

2.4.2
Mitigation of climate change
The dramatic increase of CO2 emissions to satisfy the global carbon demand for chemi-
cals, goods, and fuels has been regarded as one of the main contributors triggering climate
change. In order to counter this, there is a need to capture and sequester CO2 wherever
possible. However, the current processes for carbon capture and storage are not economi-
cally feasible, and this remains an obstacle for practical applications to sequester carbon.
Thus, it is desirable to develop efficient CO2 sorptive materials that are economically feasi-
ble. One such material is biochar. It is recalcitrant to biodegradation and is known to last
for thousands of years in the soil, making it an excellent option for carbon sequestration.
[23]

Biochar’s environmental stability is a crucial property towards achieving its potential to
mitigate climate change through sequestering atmospheric CO2 for long periods while also
reducing GHG emissions. [22] The C sequestration potential of biochar could be divided
into two measures of C: stored carbon and emitted carbon. The most significant chemical
difference between biochar and other organic material is the higher proportion of aromatic
C and fused aromatic C structures. This fused aromatic structure can have varying forms
at high temperatures which dominates at lower pyrolysis temperatures. The high stability
of biochar is due to the nature of these C structures. [11]

atmosphere. Under normal circumstances, green plants remove CO2 from the atmosphere

2.4.3
Energy production
A practical biochar system captures energy during biochar production to be used as a fuel
while using the biochar generated during pyrolysis as a soil amendment with the benefit of
reducing emissions to increase the value proposition of the overall system. Using biochar
as a fuel is a more energy-efficient method to utilise pyrolysis products compared to using
biochar as a soil amendment. However, the emissions reduced by adding biochar to the soil
is greater than the fossil fuel offset in it’s use as a fuel. [11] Biochar does not have a very
high calorific value of 20 MJ/kg compared to bio-oils which usually have a calorific value
of 40 MJ/kg, making it not the most lucrative source of energy. Hence, it is advocated to
use biochar primarily for environmental solutions rather than only as a source of energy.
Bioenergy can contribute and be part of the goal to achieve a future supply of green energy.
However, it is unlikely to be able to solve our current energy crisis and satisfy the rising
global demand for energy on it’s own. An assessment of potential global bioenergy from
forestry yielded a theoretical supply of 71 EJ in 2050. In comparison, worldwide energy
consumption in 2005 was estimated to be 489 EJ. While this may not be encouraging,
it should be noted that even if bio-energy contributes to a fraction of the future energy
requirements, it will significantly reduce the dependence on fossil fuels and is ultimately
benefiting the planet. [1]
In regions that require biomass as a source of energy, such as rural Africa and parts of
South Asia and South America, pyrolysis based bio-energy can provide an opportunity for
a cleaner and more efficient source of energy compared to traditional sources of energy like
burning wood as a fuel. This also increases the scope for the number of biomass feedstocks
options that can be used for pyrolysis as there are less constraints than when biochar is
being produced as a soil amendment. There is no longer a need to match the type of
feedstock with the soil problem and the crop to be grown. [1]
Another valuable benefit could be the use of biochar as a source of clean heat, which
can be used to develop cost-effective cooking technology with lower indoor pollution. It
is estimated that there are more than two billion people who still cook and heat their
food with primitive stoves or open fires by burning wood, animal faeces and coal. These
inefficient technologies emit air pollution that can cause respiratory problems for the local
pollution while also accelerating deforestation due to the increased demand for wood. New
stove technologies can produce both heat for cooking and biochar for carbon sequestration.
[25]

Other uses

Biochar can be used in animal farming for feeding, litter or in slurry treatment.
It’s

Biochar’s properties of extremely low thermal conductivity and high water absorption

2.5
Drawbacks
The list of potential benefits from applying biochar to soils that are reported in the liter-
ature is almost endless; however, unfortunately there are drawbacks to these arguments.
One of the biggest drawback to the application of biochar to soils is that there is no ”one
size fits all” approach to using biochar.
Different biochars behave differently in differ-
ent soil types. Since biochar can be produced from all kinds of biomass and at varying
temperatures and heating rates, there is huge variation in the substance and hence it’s
characteristics. Finding an appropriate biochar to use for soil amendment involves years
of research to study the effects of different types of biochar for the selected soil type.
It should also be noted that biochar might not affect all soil types. Based on a meta-
analysis study of 109 independent studies on biochar’s effects on crop yield, it was found
that biochar had little to no effect on crop yield in temperate regions. This could be
14
Techno-economic feasibility study for implementation of biochar-based carbon capture and storage
==PAGE:25==
because these soils are already having moderate pH and receive high fertilizer inputs,
leaving little room for additional benefits. There are also cases when the application of
biochar has had a negative effect on crop yield. [22] Yields may decline due to sorption
of water and nutrients by the biochar, which reduces the availability of these resources
to the plant. Certain types of biochar can also be a source of contaminants like heavy
metals, volatile organic carbons and Polycyclic Aromatic Hydrocarbons (PAH’s), which
are harmful to plant growth. [3]
There is also the issue related to the economics of biochar as a soil amendment. The use
of biochar is still a new and emerging practice around the world, and it’s feasibility is
constrained by it’s high price and the low price of carbon being traded in the market. [30]
This makes it potentially inaccessible and a luxury for the people that actually require
biochar to help amend weathered soils in developing parts of the world. The high cost
of setting up a pyrolysis machine and the scaling up of the pyrolysis process is another
economic hurdle when it comes to large scale biochar production. Currently, there is a
lack of knowledge and awareness of carbon credits and the carbon market and how to
access it and evaluate the costs and benefits of using biochar. A framework is required to
certify the carbon sequestered in biochar as a trade-able commodity and to quantify the
amount of carbon being sequestered in biochar. [31]
To promote the use of biochar, more consistent and uniform products are required, which
requires a universal set of guidelines to be followed. Research is needed to identify the ideal
production conditions for desirable results for different feedstocks and blends of feedstocks.
The current guidelines given by the International Biochar Initiative [32] and the European
Biochar Certificate [27] are only voluntary and are not standardised, leading to the sale of
unverified and potentially harmful sale of biochar in the market. Having a set of guidelines
to follow could also educate consumers on the quality of the biochar being purchased and
the effects that a particular type of biochar can have on various soils.
Since this is a relatively new field, there is still a lack of knowledge on how biochar actually
affects soil properties and the reasons for it’s behaviour in the soil. There are very few
studies on the long-term effects of biochar on soil productivity, which would indicate how
the properties of biochar and soil would change over time. There is also a lack of any large-
scale biochar implementation projects to use as references or case studies for future work.
This leads to the problem of a lack of knowledge on how to scale up biochar production
and on whether it is feasible or not.
Lastly, care should be taken while reviewing literature regarding the characteristics of
biochar as experimental conditions are highly variable and these are not always reported
in sufficient detail. This applies to the conditions under which the samples have been
prepared and the conditions under which they have bee analysed. The lack of data related
to this can cause a lot of confusion and can lead to wrong co-relations to be made related
to the various effects of using biochar. [1]

process is called thermal decomposition usually from pyrolysis, gasification or combustion.
These methods can produce clean energy in the form of gas or oil along with the biochar.
This energy may be recoverable for another use, or it may simply be burned and released
as heat. It’s one of the few technologies that is relatively inexpensive, widely applicable
and quickly scalable. [35]

The products of cellulose decomposition can vary markedly depending upon reaction condi-

2.6.1
Traditional Production Techniques
The earliest known methods of biochar production focused on simplicity and low cost.
This was done in charcoal kins consisting of temporary pits or mounds, operating in batch
mode and requiring the periodic charging and discharging of the kiln.
Pit kilns use the most straightforward strategy for controlling the air input and reducing
heat loss during carbonization by burying the stack of smouldering wood in the ground.
A small fire is started in the pit and additional wood is added to make it stronger. At this
point, a canopy of branches and leaves is added as a support layer. Carbonization may
proceed for up to two days before the pit is uncovered and the charcoal is allowed to cool
before unloading.
A mound kiln is an above-ground version of a pit kiln, where the earth is mounded up
over a stack of wood for the purpose of controlling air filtration and heat loss due to
carbonization.
Long pieces of fuel wood are stacked vertically against a central post,
while shorter logs are placed vertically towards the edges. It is covered with straw or dry
leaves and then a layer of loamy soil to seal the mound. About six to ten vents at the base
of the mound allow for control of air filtration during carbonization.

2.6.3
Gasification
Gasification can be defined as a thermochemical process which transfers heating value
from carbonaceous materials into syngas (i.e., a mixture of H2 and CO), tars and biochar
at high temperature (>500°C) and oxygen-deficient conditions. The gasification process
generally involves four consecutive steps, i.e., drying, pyrolysis (i.e., thermally-induced
fragmentation via bond dissociation and dehydrogenation), partial oxidation and reduc-
tion.
Usually, the gases produced are hydrogen (H2), carbon monoxide (CO), carbon
dioxide (CO2), but nitrogen (N2) and methane (CH4) can also be present. Gasifiers can
be categorized into three categories based on their gas-solid contacting mode: fixed bed,
moving bed and entrained flow gasifiers. However, choosing the correct type of gasifier is
contingent on the type of biomass and the desired final product. [38]
In general, biochar yield from the gasification process is less than other thermochemical
processes such as pyrolysis, which can be explicable by the conversion of carbon into
carbon monoxide (CO) due to its partial oxidation conditions. [38]
2.6.4
Pyrolysis
Pyrolysis, from the Greek word ‘pyro’ meaning fire and ‘lysis’ meaning decomposition,
is the decomposition of organic material at elevated temperatures in the complete or
partial absence of oxygen. The definition of pyrolysis has changed over time with old
literature referring to pyrolysis as the carbonization of biomass to produce a primary solid
char product, while in today’s literature the term can often describe a process where any
one of liquid, gas or char products are preferred. [36] The pyrolysis process is mainly
characterised by solid fuel thermal degradation, which involves the rupture of carbon-
carbon bonds and the formation of carbon-oxygen bonds. Pyrolysis requires temperatures
of up to 400–550°C, although it can be done at temperatures even higher as shown in
Figure 2.9. [39]

1. Transfer of heat from a heat source, to increase the temperature inside the fuel

Fast Pyrolysis
Fast pyrolysis is the primary process used for bio-oil production. The process involves
faster heating rates and much shorter residence times (seconds) than slow pyrolysis. A
good homogenization of the feedstock, with a particle size of around 2 mm, and drying
to less than 10% moisture is required to reach high yields. Under the right conditions,
the liquid yield can increase by as much as 75%. Despite its particular importance for
bio-oil, fast pyrolysis produces 10 to 15% of solids, which can be used as biochar, raw or
pelleted charcoal. Since fast pyrolysis is usually performed at high temperatures (above
500°C), the aromaticity of the resulting biochar is generally higher and O/C atomic ratio
compared to slow pyrolysis for the same residence time. [34] [39]
Flash Pyrolysis
Flash pyrolysis has as its main characteristics, very high heating rates and very low resi-
dence time of the biomass in the reactor. These characteristics favour the production of
vapours, and make the process very similar to gasification. Due to the high heating rate,
where biomass residence times are only a few seconds, reactors are needed to meet these
heating needs. The fine particles of feedstock are burned by flash fires. This process is
used mainly to produce bio-oil. Temperatures ranging from 500 to 950°C promote rapid
depolymerization and feedstock cracking, resulting in the production of bio-oil.
Unlike traditional processes, this is an advanced process with carefully controlled param-
eters to obtain high liquid yields. In order to carry out this process, the following must
be observed: subjecting the biomass particles to an optimum temperature so that they re-
act, and minimizing their exposure to low intermediate temperatures that stimulate coke
formation. [34] [39]
2.7
Biochar and soil
Soil is the loose surface material consisting of inorganic particles and organic matter that
covers most of the land surface. Soil provides the structural support and the source of
water and nutrients for plants used in agriculture.
[41] It is vitally important to the
sustainability of an ecosystem because it serves as a natural medium for the growth of
vegetation. Soil is made up of five main ingredients - minerals, soil organic matter, living
organisms, water and gas.
The minerals present in the soil can be further divided into three categories: sand, silt and
clay. Sand particles are essentially small rock fragments and have little or no ability to
grow crops and they range from 0.05 mm to 2 mm in diameter. Sandy soils provide good
drainage in the soil but have very poor water holding and nutrient holding capabilities.
The particles classified as silt are intermediate in size with physical properties between
sand and clay. Silt particles have a limited ability to retain plant nutrients and hold water.
They are considered to be relatively fertile, and the particles range from 0.002 mm to 0.05
mm in diameter. Clay particles are the smallest and finest particles with a size of less
than 0.002 mm. They are responsible for the retention of many of the plant nutrients and
water. However, due to their fine particle size, clay holds more water than is required and
has very poor drainage. The relative proportion of sand, silt and clay particles determines
the physical properties of soil including the texture. A soil triangle as shown in Figure
2.10 can be used to determine the type and texture of a particular soil type based on its
mineral composition.

Sandy soils
Sandy soils are those that are generally coarse-textured until 50 cm depth and consequently

Saline soils
Saline soils are those soils that have a very high amount of salt dissolved in the soil solution,
that affects the growth and productivity of crops. Soils are considered to be saline if the
electrical conductivity of a soil solution is higher than 2 dS/m or 4 Ohms/cm. Saline soils
are defined by a thin film of white salt (usually sodium, potassium and magnesium salts)
on the surface, especially in the dry season as seen in Figure 2.12. It gets covered by rain
every monsoon, but due to it’s poor drainage, the water remains on the surface until it is
evaporated, thereby leaving a fresh crust of white salt. [48] [49]

Saline soils are predominantly found in regions with an arid and semi-arid climate, with
enough rainfall to leach salts through the soil. In desert areas, the irrigation water by
itself could be high in salt content. They are also common near the oceans, where strong
winds carry salts and deposit them on nearby land, creating problems for roots and foliage.
They can also be present in the soils adjacent to walkways and roads in countries where
salt is used to prevent icing during winters. [48]
An excess of soluble salts in soil can harm plant growth by causing the plant to suffer
from a lack of water and an excessive amount of elements like sodium and chloride that
are constituents of salt that are accumulated in the soil. The presence of these salts also
affects the plant uptake of nutrients and the microbiological activity in the soil. This leads
to crop failure, a reduction in yield and low quality produce. [44]
Soil salinity can be reduced by improving the internal drainage system, allowing salts to
be washed out of the soil. Treating these soils with gypsum and other amendments like
biochar can help with drainage, leading to a reduction in soil salinity. Following agro-
climatic guidelines and developing and growing saline resistant crop varieties by genetic
engineering can also help improve these soils. [49]

2.7.4
Steep lands
Slopes that are steep are challenging for growing crops and are often difficult to amend,
fertilize and water as they are susceptible to erosion. Slopes can also cause problems such
as low fertility, the formation of shallow soils and low soil fertility based on their gradient.
[51] In the tropical regions, most of the steep lands are inhabited by small-scale farmers
whose livelihoods can be endangered by land degradation and loss of productivity, making
the management of these steep lands important. [18]
Steep lands usually have shallow soils. They are characterised by thin topsoil being the
only layer lying between the ground surface and the sub-surface rock layers. Soils are
considered shallow if they extend less than 10 inches below the surface before hitting an
impervious layer. Trees and shrubs that grow in shallow soils have less support for the
root layers as they cannot penetrate deep into the soil. This makes nutrient and water
uptake of the plant poor. Watering shallow soils is difficult as the water tends to run off
instead of sinking into the soil. [52]
In order to make these soils utilisable, measures should be taken to prevent soil erosion.
This can be done by planting the hillside with ground cover. Mulch or biochar can be
spread on these soils, and they can be held in place by erosion netting and crops should
be grown through the holes in the netting. The netting prevents erosion until the ground
cover is established. Terrace gardening is also a good solution for breaking up the gradient
of a steep slope. This, however, is a more expensive approach. [51] The other issue faced
by shallow soils is that of drainage. This can be solved by laying drain lines on top of
the bedrock to carry water away or by using drip irrigation systems. Another way to deal
with shallow soils is to seek those plants known to have shallow root systems and promote
their presence in these soils. [52]

Carbon Capture and Storage
Biochar as a climate change mitigation tool is believed to have a large potential and has
been identified as a promising negative emission technology by the International Panel on
Climate Change. Based on the literature, there is robust evidence and high agreement
that biochar soil carbon sequestration is a feasible CO2 removal method.[4] Anthropogenic
carbon sustainable biochar implementation could offset 12% of anthropogenic Green House
Gas emissions on an annual basis. This amounts to a total of about 130 pentagrams of
CO2 equivalents over a period of 100 years. [53]
Businesses generate unavoidable greenhouse gas emissions as part of their operations. They
can take steps to reduce the size of their carbon footprint by switching to renewable sources
of energy, promoting the use of public transport amongst employees and by supporting
similar climate-friendly practices whenever possible.
For the emissions that businesses
can’t reduce, they can compensate for their unavoidable emissions by financing emission
reduction projects around the world. This is called carbon offsetting. [54]
A formal system for carbon offsetting is facilitated through the system of trading carbon
credits. A carbon credit is a permit that allows a company that holds it to emit a certain
amount of carbon dioxide or other greenhouse gases.
One carbon credit permits the
emission of a mass equal to one ton of carbon dioxide. This limit is reduced periodically
to incentivize a reduction in greenhouse gases by the company as they will be fined if they
exceed their permitted amount of emissions. Companies that reduce their greenhouse gas
emissions to a point where they no longer need their permitted amount of carbon credits
can sell these credits to other companies that might need them. This acts as another
source of revenue and promotes a reduction in greenhouse gases being emitted. [55]

Concepts & Theories
In a techno-economic analysis, the process is examined simultaneously from a technical
and economic point of view – and by extension from an environmental point of view. This
analysis is done based on desktop research, but also through intensive communication
with external experts working on biochar research and from the industry. The goal is to
formulate answers, in close collaboration with technology-developers, to these questions
and looks for the most promising value chains with the use of our techno-economic analysis.
The key concepts being used to perform the techno-economic analysis are discussed in this
chapter.
3.1
Material Balance
The first step for performing a techno-economic analysis of a process is to study and design
the process. This can be done by making a material or mass balance for the process. A
material balance is the application of the law of conservation of mass - ”Matter is neither
created nor destroyed”. [56] Material balances are used to account for material entering
and leaving the system and are widely used in industries for the calculation of mass flow
rates of different streams entering and leaving the system in a chemical process.
The general material balance is given by the equation:
input + generation = output + accumulation
(3.1)
The rates of generation and accumulation involve changes occurring inside the system.
The rate of input and output concern flows crossing the boundaries of the system. In the
cases being analyzed in this study, there is no accumulation nor generation taking place,
and thus, the material balance consists of only the input and output streams.
3.2
Energy Balance
Once the mass flow rates in and out of the system have been determined, the next step is
to determine the flow of energy in the system. An energy balance is the application of the
law of conservation of energy - ”Energy can neither be created nor be destroyed; energy
can only be transferred from one form to another”. [56] These calculations are useful in
calculating the amount of energy being generated by the thermal degradation of biomass
and thus, how much energy is required by the system for normal operations.
Energy can be in various forms like kinetic energy, potential energy and internal energy.
This study does not analyse kinetic and potential energy as it is negligible for this process,

Operating Expenditure
The operating expenditure is the expense a business incurs through normal day-to-day
operations and is often abbreviated as OPEX. Operating expenses usually include rent,
equipment running costs, inventory cost, marketing, payroll, insurance, taxes, licensing
fees and funds allocated for research and development. [58]
The operating expenses in focus in this study are the expenses due to heating and the

The goal of a company should be to maximise output relative to OPEX. Thus, OPEX
represents a core measurement of a company’s efficiency over time. [59]

based on the heating rate - slow pyrolysis and fast pyrolysis.
Lower heating rates provide milder conditions for pyrolysis reactions and no thermal crack-
ing of biomass takes place; thus, the conditions for char formation are suitable, leading
to an increase in biochar yield as seen in Figure 4.2. Fast pyrolysis involves heating the

4.3
Residence time
Residence time is another criterion separating fast and slow pyrolysis processes and affects
both the carbonization rate as well as the yield of biochar. A longer residence time usually
results in greater biochar carbonization, leading to lower labile carbon content. Longer
residence times also provide a greater chance for repolymerization of the constituents of
biomass, thereby enhancing the yield of biochar. [68] [67] Another reason for the increase
in biochar yield with an increase in residence time is due to the gradual removal of volatiles
from the reactor with slow heating. This permits a secondary reaction to occur between
char particles and volatiles, leading to secondary char formation. [65]
In a continuous pyrolysis process, fresh biomass enters the reactor and its vapours encoun-
ters the old, hot charcoal that has already been pyrolysed. This hot charcoal absorbs these
vapours and forms heavy biochar particles. Thus, high temperatures (650 °C - 750 °C)
combined with longer residence times results in a higher biochar yield. Shorter residence
times results in repolymerization of biomass not being completed, leading to higher liquid
and gas yields. [71]

Residence time also affects the physical properties of biochar. Longer residence time results
in the enhanced formation of both micro and macro pores , thus increasing the specific
surface area of the resultant biochar. [66] Residence time was however not seen to have
any significant impact on the pH of biochar. [72]
It should be noted that there is insufficient information provided in literature about what
residence time in particular is being referred to as the residence time for biochar, vapours
and gases are different. This makes it difficult to draw a straightforward conclusion on
the effect of residence time on biochar properties. [66] Further, the effect of residence time
is often dominated by temperature and heating rate, making it challenging to decipher
the role that residence time alone plays on the production of biochar. [68] There are also
conflicting results about the role residence time plays on biochar yield as greater residence
time could result in more mass being volatilized during longer pyrolysis conditions. [72]
Therefore more research is required to study the effects of residence time on biochar yield.
4.4
Pressure
The pressure inside the reactor is another parameter that impacts the yield of pyrolysis
products. Biochar yield increases when pyrolysis takes place under the influence of pres-
sure higher than ambient pressure. This is due to the fact that an increase in pressure
leads to an increase in residence time. This results in volatile matter being added to the
carbonaceous material, forming secondary carbon and thus increasing biochar production.
[67]
If volatile substances that lead to the formation of tar are present in the biomass, then
the biochar yield can be enhanced by increasing the pressure or by decreasing the heating
rate. [73] High pressure inside the reactor also affects the carbon content in the biochar.
The concentration of carbon increases when biomass is pyrolysed under increased pressure
conditions, resulting in an increase in the energy density of biochar, thus forming a more
valuable product with a higher market value. [68] An increase in density of biochar would
however result in a decrease in porosity. Thus, Biochar produced at high pressure suits
applications as an energy source rather than as a soil amendment.
4.5
Particle size
The particle size of biomass being pyrolysed is an important factor to consider before start-
ing the pyrolysis process as it can impact the distribution of the final products. Particle
size can control the rate at which heat is transferred to the input biomass. As biomass
is a poor conductor of heat, it creates difficulties in the transfer of heat during pyrolysis.
[68] [67]
On increasing the particle size, the distance between the surface of the biomass and it’s
core increases, thus retarding the flow of heat from the hot to the cold end. This reduces
the formation of liquid and gaseous products and favours biochar formation. Figure 4.3
shows the variation of biochar yield for different feedstocks with an increase of particle size
from 0.5 mm to 2.3 mm. Further, on increasing the particle size, the vapour formed during
thermal cracking of biomass has a larger distance to cover through the char layer, thus
giving the opportunity for secondary reactions to occur, increasing the biochar yield. [68]
Fine biomass particles offer less resistance to the escape of condensable gases, which there-
fore escape relatively quickly to the surroundings before undergoing secondary cracking,
leading to an increase in liquid yield. [65]

4.6
Biochar feedstock
The yield and properties of biochar do not depend only on the pyrolysis operating con-
ditions. The type of biomass being used as the feedstock and it’s composition also plays
an important role in understanding the relationships between these parameters and thus
selection of an appropriate feedstock is critical if biochar is to be produced for specific ap-
plications. Conventional feedstocks usually used for pyrolysis include wood, crop residues,
animal waste and sewage sludge.
Biomass is associated with some amount of water or moisture content. This water inside
biomass can exist as water vapour, chemically bound water or free liquid water. High
water content in the biomass is not desirable during pyrolysis as large amounts of heat
is required to remove water. This in turn affects the heating rate, increasing the time
required to reach the optimum pyrolysis temperature and thus increasing the amount of
heat required for the process and the economics of the process. Biomass with a moisture
content greater than 30% is not suitable for pyrolysis and if the feedstock has a high
moisture content, pre-treatment in the form of drying is required before pyrolysis.
The three major components of ligno-cellulosic biomass have their preferred temperature
range of decomposition as shown below: [65]
• Hemicellulose: 150 - 350°C
• Cellulose: 275 - 350°C
• Lignin: 250 - 500°C
Different biomass feedstocks have varying compositions of cellulose, hemicellulose and
lignin and these individual components of the biomass undergo pyrolysis differently, lead-
ing to varying contributions to product composition and yield. It should however be noted
that the same type of feedstock can produce very different biochars based on the pyrolysis
parameters. The effects of the five most common types of biomass used for pyrolysis is
studied further.
4.6.1
Wood
Woody biochars have a high content of cellulose, hemicellulose and lignin and the biochar
produced is known to vary based on the type of wood species used. Hardwood derived
biochars have higher alkalinity, cation exchange capacity and micropore volume compared
to softwood derived biochar.
[76] Wood-based biochars are also known to have high
specific surface area and low ash content. [77] Additionally, hardwood derived biochar has
higher aromacity, indicating a higher stability. This is due to the higher lignin content in
hardwood compared to softwood. Therefore, the type of woody biomass should be chosen
based on the intended application of the biomass.
4.6.2
Crop residues
While the body of a plant or tree mainly consists of lignocellulosic biomass, crop residues
are a source of carbohydrates, starch and sugar and are commonly used as a feedstock for
biochar production. Some of the popular crops on which experiments have been conducted
in order to study it’s potential for biochar production are corn cobs, rice hulls, wheat straw,
sorghum stems and cotton stems.
Crop derived biochars are appropriate for application for the purpose of soil amendment.
They have been used as a replacement for conventional Silica fertilizer an also used in
combination with fertilizer to show positive effects on soil properties as monocotyledons
(rice, wheat, corn, barley and sugarcane) accumulate Silica in plant tissues and are thus
good sources of Silica. [78]

Grass can also be an interesting feedstock that can be used for biochar production. This is
especially true for species of grass such as monocot C4 perennial grass and elephant grass,

Biochar derived from crop residue biomass are high in carbon content and contain a range

4.6.3
Animal waste
Biochar produced from animal waste such as poultry and swine manure possesses distinct
physical and chemical properties compared to plant-based biochar because of the very
different nature of the feedstock. Manure biochar has a high Nitrogen and Sulfur content as
a result of the high Nitrogen and Sulfur content of the feedstock. If the aim is to introduce
N-containing groups or S-containing groups onto the biochar surface, then animal waste
based feedstocks should be used. [66]
The advantage of using animal wastes to produce biochar is that it also helps reduce
the stress on the waste management system on farms. Excess manure poses a problem
related to disposal and if this can be used to produce a valuable soil amendment, it has
an increased value to the farm ecosystem. [1]
4.6.4
Sewage sludge
Similar to animal wastes, sewage sludge also poses a problem related to proper disposal
and is a burden on the waste management system. Conventional disposal methods such
as incineration, landfilling and anaerobic digestion have raised safety concerns as sewage
sludge may contain heavy metals, organic contaminants and microbial contaminants that
pose risks to human beings and the environment. Pyrolysis can be an environmentally
friendly and economical method to reduce the solid waste, remove certain harmful con-
taminants and produce value added products of bio-oil and biochar. [66]
However care must be taken before using biochar derived from sewage sludge as some
heavy metals might not be completely removed by pyrolysis and it can still have a toxic
effect on soil. Therefore it is not recommended to use this type of biochar directly as a
soil amendment. To reduce the risks related to heavy metals and toxic compounds in the
biochar, sewage sludge can be co-pyrolysed with other feedstocks such as crop residues or
wood. [66]

5.1.1
Feeding System
Before biomass can be fed into the two-stage gasifier, the system should be at it’s ideal
operating temperature. This temperature varies based on the composition of the biomass
being used and the desired properties and compositions of the products. For this study,
the system is assumed to be at 600 °C and is operated 24 hours a day.
The biomass is fed to the system using a screw conveyor. A lock hopper system which
uses a double flap valve ensures that the system is airtight and no gas escapes the system.
The biomass is then fed into a box from where another screw conveyor feeds the fuel to the
pyrolysis unit. The rotational speed of the screw controls the feeding rate of the biomass.
This can be varied to alter the composition of the products. Increased residence time of
biomass in the system leads to a decrease in bio-methanol yield and an increase in the
yield of solid biochar. As the primary desired product of this operation is bio-methanol,
the rotational speed of the screw is set to 0.5 rpm.
The second screw conveyor consists of a jacket with the supply of hot syngas from the
reaction chamber, this is where the first stage of heating the biomass takes place. These
gases have passed through a cyclone separator, where solid particles such as ash are sepa-
rated from the gas. The jacket has a diameter of 20 mm, ensuring very good heat transfer.
These hot gases are at a temperature of 800 °C and pass through the jacket in a helical
manner to heat the biomass being fed to the pyrolysis unit. This ensures that uniformly
heated biomass enters the system, while also making use of the heat being produced by
the system. The outlet syngas is then fed to tube-side inlet of the first heat exchanger.
5.1.2
Reaction Chamber
The reaction chamber is where the gasification of the biomass takes place. Temperatures
for gasification are very high and are usually between 900 to 1000°C. This is the second
stage of heating in the two-stage gasifier. The reaction chamber consists of a burner, radar,
an inlet for hot air being recycled from the heat exchanger and a rocker grill. The energy
required by the system to reach it’s initial operating temperature is provided by a burner
which burns 48 kg of LPG over a period of 6 hours.
The heated biomass is fed to the reaction chamber by the screw conveyor at a temperature
of 600 °C and it falls onto the rocker grill. The biomass is heated by a combination of
heated air entering the system from the shell-side of the first heat exchanger and the LPG
burner. The metal grill is in direct contact with the biomass and it provides an air tight
reactor wall to keep the gas from bypassing the char bed. The biomass produces gases
containing volatile components such as long chain carbon molecules. Under limited Oxygen
conditions, these volatiles are broken down into smaller molecules such as Hydrogen and
Methane.
The tar being produced by the gasification process is further burnt by the
addition of Oxygen into the system and is also converted into Hydrogen and Methane.
This is a major advantage of this system as it avoids clogging of char particles. These
hot gases move in a downward direction due to the work being done by a suction pump
and passes through the biochar formed and into the cyclone separator. This heats the
biochar and produces additional gaseous products to such an extent that there are no
more volatiles that can be produced.
In this system, 89% of the heat that is required for a continuous pyrolysis process is
provided by the gases being produced on heating the biomass. The remaining 11% of
the heat requirement is provided by burning biomass. The heat transfer efficiency in the
system is assumed to be 50%.
There is a radar system installed at the top of the reaction chamber to detect the height of

5.1.3
Heat Exchanger
There are three shell-and-tube heat exchangers being used in this process. The purpose of
the heat exchangers is to cool down the hot gases being used to heat the biomass in the
screw conveyor.
The first heat exchanger has a suction pump at the shell-side inlet being used to input air
into the system. This air cools the hot syngas entering from the tube-side and exits as hot
air which is recycled back into the reaction chamber at a temperature of 600 °C to heat
the biomass. The syngas is cooled to a temperature of 300 °C at the tube-side outlet and
is then passed to the next heat exchanger where it is further cooled from 300 °C to 200 °C.
The gases then pass through a filtration system to remove any solid particles that may still
be present in the gas. The filter being used is a baghouse filter, which uses long cylindrical
bags made out of fabric as a filter medium. The temperature is maintained above 100 °C
to ensure no water formation. The cleaned gas is then cooled from a temperature of 150
°C to room temperature in the third heat exchanger.
There is scope for improving the efficiency of the system. This can be done by creating
a system to utilise the heat from the second and third heat exchanger, which is currently
being wasted. This heat can be used for the purpose of district heating if the two-stage
gasifier unit is set up in a region which has a district heating system.

6.1
Acidic soil
The problems faced by acidic soil are usually due to the presence of heavy metal toxins
like Aluminium, Manganese and Molybdenum. These toxins affect the water quality in
the soil and thus the crop growth. Another factor hindering crop growth is the availability
of nutrients at a low pH. Plant nutrients leach from the soil faster at a pH range of 5.5 to
7 as this is the range in which most nitrogen releasing bacteria operate most efficiently.
The goal of biochar addition to acidic soils is threefold:
1. Increase soil pH
2. Remove harmful heavy metals present in soil
3. Add nutrients which are currently deficient in soil
Figure 6.2: Flow diagram for acidic soil
Biochar is known to retain nutrients in soil directly through the negative charge that
develops on it’s surface, and this negative charge can buffer acidity in soils. [81] Therefore
using high pH biochar can increase the pH of the soil. The pH of biochar is found to
increase with an increase in temperature and is also dependant on the biomass feedstock
used to produce the biochar. Animal based feedstock like manure and poultry litter are
found to have a higher pH compared to woody or grassy biomass feedstocks. [1] Thus, using
an animal based feedstock which has undergone pyrolysis at a high temperature would
produce biochar that can increase soil pH. However, it must be noted that in practice,
plant based biomass and woody biomass is also used after being mixed with fertilizers.
Biochar can also be used in the remediation of contaminated soils. Harmful elements and
compounds present in acidic soils need to be removed or reduced in concentration so that
the crop obtains the required amount of non-contaminated water for growth. Due to the
high surface area and porosity of biochar, these harmful substances can be adsorbed onto
the surface of the biochar particles, thus rectifying the soil. Biochar with high surface
area and porosity is obtained by pyrolysis at temperatures between 500°C and 600°C. [1]
Adsorption capability of biochar can be enhanced by activating it with steam. [82]
As mentioned earlier in Chapter 2.3, biochar has the ability to hold nutrients on it’s
highly porous surface. Useful nutrients which can enhance crop productivity like Nitrogen,
Phosphorous and Potassium are present in some biochars based on the biomass feedstock
being used. The nutrients in soil can be increased in two ways, by increasing the cation

Sandy soil
Sandy soils are amongst the most difficult soils to grow crops on. This is mainly due to the
fact that these soils contain very coarse sand particles. These sand particles contain large
pores and allow water to flow through it quickly, thereby absorbing a very small quantity
of water. These soils are also prone to erosion as sandy soil particles bind together rather
loosely. This is a problem in upland and undulating regions. Furthermore, sandy soils
have low contents of organic matter, potassium and phosphorous, which are essential for
plant growth. The cation exchange capacity of these soils is also very low, making the
soil’s response to fertilizer poor. [44]
Biochar can enhance sandy soils in the following ways:
1. Improve water retention in soil
2. Increase soil cation exchange capacity
3. Reduce the impact of erosion
44
Techno-economic feasibility study for implementation of biochar-based carbon capture and storage
==PAGE:55==
Figure 6.3: Flow diagram for sandy soil
Biochar has the ability to improve the quality of soil by helping in the retention of water.
This is beneficial in sandy soils as water drains through these soils extremely quickly. The
increased water holding capacity is likely to be associated with the large surface area and
porosity of the biochar particles introduced in the soil. The surface area of biochar particles
tends to increase with an increase in pyrolysis temperatures as mentioned in Chapter 4.1.
The surface area and porosity of biochar can be further enhanced by activating it with
steam for the best results. [94] [95]
Sand has a very low capacity to exchange cations because it has no electrical charge. This
results in a poor potential of the soil to hold nutrients such as K+, Ca2+, Mg2+, Na+ and
NH+
4 , which are essential for crop growth. Biochar has a large surface area and the ability
to develop a negative charge on its surface [17] and thus the addition of biochar to sandy
soil improves its ability to hold positively charged nutrients. Cation exchange capacity
of biochar is found to decrease with an increase in pyrolysis temperature. [96] Thus a
pyrolysis temperature should be selected to compromise between the high temperature
required for an increased surface area and a lower temperature for an increase in cation
exchange capacity based on the requirements of the soil. A pyrolysis temperature of 450°C
is suggested for optimal results. [26]
Sandy soils contain coarse textured particles that do not stick together.
This makes
it susceptible to erosion by wind and water, especially when there are no crops being
harvested on these soils. [44] The large sand particles also hinder the penetration of roots
deep into the soil, making it difficult for roots to access nutrients and water. The addition
of biochar reduces soil erosion by increasing the size of the soil aggregates as well as
stabilizing these aggregates. Fine biochar particles can fill in the gaps available between
the larger sand particles and can help bind these particles together. This improves the
soils physical properties and makes a good environment for soil microbes to thrive. The
addition of organic matter forms stabilized soil aggregates and reduces the potential of soil
erosion. [97] The addition of fine biochar particles to the soil can fill in the gaps between
the larger sand particles and allow roots to penetrate deeper into the soil to extract more
water and nutrients, improving crop productivity.

6.3
Saline soil
Most crops do not grow well on soils that contain an excess of salts. Saline soil usually
contains several types of salts, and one of them is sodium salt. The presence of salts in
soil causes a reduction in the rate and amount of water that the plant roots can take up
from the soil and the presence of some salts in high concentrations can also be toxic to
the soil. Soils with an excess concentration of sodium are known as sodic soils and are
characterized by poor soil structure, low infiltration rate and are poorly aerated, leading
to difficulty in cultivation. [18] Sodic soils do not allow water to pass through it easily and
as a result of the lack of drainage, water is collected on the surface of the soil and gets
evaporated, causing the soluble compounds to remain in the soil. [50]
The addition of biochar can help amend saline soils in the following ways:
1. Reduce the presence of excess salts
2. Improve drainage in the soil
3. Adding nutrients to the depleted soil
46
Techno-economic feasibility study for implementation of biochar-based carbon capture and storage
==PAGE:57==
Figure 6.4: Flow diagram for saline soil
Biochar application on saline soils has been found to be beneficial to plant growth. Biochar’s
high surface area and negative charge enhances the removal of sodium salts from the soil.
This makes salt leaching quicker and reduces the time required for reducing salt concen-
tration to an acceptable level for growing plants. A high cation exchange capacity can
help replace sodium from exchange sites with calcium ions, which are beneficial to plant
growth. A reduction in salt concentration will lead to an increase in water and nutrient
uptake capabilities of the soil as well as improving the microbial activity of the soil. [105]
High surface area biochar is produced at higher temperatures. Saline soils are alkaline and
thus care should be taken to find an appropriate pyrolysis temperature as the biochar’s pH
also increases with the increase in temperature. Cation exchange capacity of the biochar
also increases with an increase in pyrolysis temperature, [1] thus a moderate pyrolysis
temperature should be used.
To solve the problem of poor drainage in saline soils, biochar particles of a relatively
larger size should be added to the soil. These coarse particles can facilitate better water
drainage, allowing the water that was previously collected on the surface of the soil to now
pass through it and reach the roots of the plants being grown. This will lead to a more
robust and long-term yield increase of the crops.
Saline soils are also deficient in nutrients and the addition of biochar can be immensely
beneficial to these soils. As mentioned in Chapter 2.3, biochar has the ability to hold
nutrients. However biochar made from animal based feedstocks cannot be used as they
are of high pH values, which would be counter-productive in saline soils. Thus, other
lignocellulosic biomass feedstocks such as wheat straw, plant residues and woody biomasses
have to be used. These are not rich in nutrients, hence the addition of nutrients is done
by mixing the biochar produced with fertilizers or compost so that the nutrients present
in them get adsorbed into the biochar and can then be transferred to the soil. [106]

Shallow soils in mountainous regions are very prone to erosion due to the presence of only

Discussion
8.1
Key parameter survey
The key parameter survey was undertaken to obtain a more in-depth knowledge of the
production conditions of biochar by pyrolysis. It should be noted that the perfect or ideal
production conditions for biochar does not exist. What exists is a range, within which
biochar production can be optimised. This survey studies the trends on increasing and
decreasing the process parameters and based on the results, a hypothesis is made on the
effect of that particular parameter on the production of biochar. The parameter being
varied in the study is isolated and does not take into account other variations like that
of feedstock. It is often also the case that a particular physical property of biochar is
obtained by varying multiple process parameters, for example, the surface area of biochar
is increased when there is a high pyrolysis temperature coupled with a low heating rate
and a longer residence time. [66] [68] [67] [72] This has the potential to reduce the accuracy
of the findings.
Another aspect to be considered is that of the lack of consistent data available on the
production conditions of the biochar as different authors have published studies on different
types of biochar from varying biomass feedstocks. The biomass feedstock being used is
the most important parameter to be considered and is the determining factor on the final
properties of the biochar being produced. [65] It was observed that most studies focus on
woody biomass feedstocks and crop residues such as maize. A wider variety of feedstocks
should be analysed to obtain a better understanding on all the possibilities to produce
biochar. There is a lack of uniformity and reproducible results in literature, casting a
shadow of doubt on the results.
8.2
Implementation strategy
The implementation strategy in the form of a decision support tool is a novel method to
assist in the growth of biochar as a tool for soil amendment. This is done by bridging
the gap in knowledge between the agricultural benefits of biochar to improve soil quality
and the engineering behind making different types of biochar. The merits of the decision
support tool lies in its ease of use, its flexibility, and in the fact that it targets both the
biochar producers and consumers.
The decision support tool uses a simple set of instructions represented in the form of
a flow diagram to guide the reader towards progressively finding a solution for biochar
implementation. The tool does not give one particular result but gives different options
Techno-economic feasibility study for implementation of biochar-based carbon capture and storage
69
==PAGE:80==
to the user as all biomass feedstocks are not readily available or economically viable to
use in different parts of the world.
However, there are limitations to the decision support tool being produced and there is
scope for improvement. It currently only addresses four different problem soil types, when
there are other problem soils such as calcareous soils, gypsiferous soils and rocky soils
which could be amended by adding biochar. It also targets only six positive effects of
biochar on soil properties. Properties like carbon sequestration potential and reduction
in NO2 emissions are important, but have not been addressed by the tool in it’s current
form. The number of process parameters being varied can also be increased to give a more
comprehensive result on how to make different bespoke biochars.
The data collected for the basis of the decision support tool had some key points worth
mentioning. Most of the data collected is from tropical regions, as that is where most re-
search is currently taking place on biochar applications in soil. There is also a bias towards
growing maize as it is a crop that is commonly grown in these regions. Many research
papers focus on the improvement of soil physical, chemical and biological properties and
ignore the effect biochar has on crop yield. These results could not be used in the decision
support tool as the basis for successful implementation was chosen to be an improvement
in crop yield. The results for positive crop yield were hand-picked and show only the best
case scenario in each study. Further, the results are based on the percentage increase in
crop yield. This can be a deceptive metric to use as the yield increase is magnified when
the original value of yield is very low, for example, high yield increases are noticed in soils
where crop productivity is near zero before the application of biochar. This can lead to
unrealistic expectations for yield increases, which might not be the case. However, it was
considered to be the most appropriate metric to use due to the availability of such informa-
tion. Data on biochar application rate, number of cropping seasons and definition of yield
lack uniformity as this information is not always provided. Focusing on research papers
for information also leads to the issue of publication bias, where only positive results are
published, leaving a gap in knowledge regarding the potential use of different biochars on
problem soils.
In summary, the decision support tool proposed in this report is a good starting point
to work on something more significant, to help more people get access to information
regarding the use of biochar for soil amendment more easily. There is a lack of information
on this aspect of biochar, which has been slowing down its potential growth, and the need
for such a decision support tool has been expressed by both researchers and companies
producing biochar.
8.3
Techno-economic analysis
The techno-economic analysis on the two production processes currently being used by
MASH Energy yielded positive results and gave an indication that the production of
biochar alongside the bio-methanol and bio-oil products was economically feasible and prof-
itable. This is an encouraging sign for producers of value added products from biomass as
it is good for the environment, helps improve food security and it is financially sustainable.
The study focused on the net present value to determine the profitability of the system
as it takes into account the time value of money, inflation and risk while giving a realistic
value on the profitability of the system. Most cases in the sensitivity analysis were shown
to have positive NPV values, inferring that the two systems are relatively robust. However,
this is the case only if all the products being produced are sold and there is no wastage of
the product.

Thus, the system should be configured to favour bio-methanol production as
much as possible, but the biochar being produced as a by-product should not be ignored.

8.5
Suggestions for further research
Biochar is a relatively new topic and research in this field is still in the nascent stage.
There is a lot of potential to use biochar as a solution to tackle a myriad of problems
including climate change, food security, dependence on fossil fuels as a source of energy
and reducing the stress on waste management systems. With the increasing interest in
climate friendly initiatives, different governments and world organisations can play an
important role in facilitating the process of increasing the research and development being
done on biochar.
There is a lack of information related to the long term effects of biochar application on
soil fertility apart from the historical perspective of terra preta being used in the Amazon
region thousands of years ago.
There are very few scientific trials documenting these
effects on different types of soils, using different types of biochar and in different regions.
The complexity and large number of variables involved to perform these long term studies
make it challenging. Another observation on the present research on biochar is that the
scope of most research articles is very microscopic and focuses only on a particular aspect
of biochar’s effect on the soil. A more holistic approach on biochar’s impact would be
beneficial to get a better overall understanding of the system.
Large scale implementation of biochar is scarce as most projects involving biochar are on a
very localised level. Due to this, there are no case studies to be analysed to learn about the
advantages and disadvantages of a large scale implementation of biochar or the potential
difficulties that could be faced by scaling up the various processes. A theoretical study
by modelling such a plant would be beneficial in this regard. Another possible strategy
would be a design of experiments to be set up to systematically determine the relationship
between factors affecting the process and its output.

Chapter 9
Conclusion
This report aimed to investigate the feasibility and potential for the production and im-
plementation of a system to produce biochar for MASH Energy. It also aimed to identify
and develop the most effective implementation strategy for the use of the biochar being
produced by MASH Energy, in order for it to have the most significant economic and
social impact.
The key parameter survey conducted based on the literature review and interviews with
researchers and experts in the field of biochar gave insights on the optimum production
conditions to maximise the yield of biochar from pyrolysis. It was found that maximum
biochar was produced at low temperatures (<400°C), slow heating rate (<10°C/min),
long residence time (>10 minutes), pressure greater than atmospheric pressure and larger
biomass particle size (>2 mm). However, producing biochar at low temperature’s leads
to the complication of the presence of harmful polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons (PAH’s)
to be present in the biochar, which should then be removed before it is used.
An implementation strategy in the form of a decision support tool was created to help
determine likely scenarios for successful applications of biochar as a soil amendment in
problem soils. It was determined that biochar for acidic soils should be high in pH and
surface area. This can be produced at a high temperature (>550°C) and the feedstock can
be selected based on the nutrient requirements of the soil. If the soil requires nutrients,
animal based biomass feedstocks are preferred. Sandy soils require biochar for the improve-
ment of water retention capacity, improved root penetration in the soil and to reduce the
effect of erosion. This can be achieved by producing biochar with high porosity and fine
particle size at a moderate temperature between 400 and 500°C. The feedstock is chosen
based on whether the soil is lacking in nutrients or not. Plant based feedstocks like maize
showed promising results. Biochar benefits saline soil by reducing the amount of excess
salts in the soil, improving soil drainage and adding nutrients to the soil. Biochar for saline
soils should have high surface area and porosity, large particle sizes and should be rich
in nutrients. This is achieved by pyrolysis at a moderate temperature between 400 and
500°C and with biomass feedstocks mixed with fertilizer or compost for additional nutri-
ent content. Steep lands require biochar with a high porosity, fine particles and nutrients.
This can be produced at high temperatures (>550°C) and with a biomass feedstock rich
in nutrients. It should be noted that all types of biochar are known to sequester carbon,
irrespective of how it is produced.
Based on the techno-economic analysis conducted in Chapter 7, it was determined that
both the Gasification and the Pyrolysis processes studied in this report are economically

the gasification process, calculated over five future cash flows and at a discount rate of
20% was found to be $ 567,645.43 and the Net Present Value for the pyrolysis process,
calculated over five future cash flows and at a discount rate of 20% was found to be $

A Case Study on MASH Energy’s pyrolysis production plant in Gujarat, India was con-

probability for increased crop productivity in these soils. These highest increase in crop
productivity was observed in acidic and sandy soils found in tropical regions. Therefore
