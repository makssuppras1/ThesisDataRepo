Abstract
The main goal of this MSc thesis is to simulate the Tohoku Tsunami 2011 using
MIKE 21 provided by DHI. Initial test showed that a sharp initial disturbance
fast would generate a dispersive tail having many dispersive waves, while a
blunt initial disturbance would generate a small tail consisting of fewer and
less dispersive waves.
The initial disturbance caused by an earthquake was
considered as being blunt and generated long waves. Dispersion and refraction
was here found to be important when investigating long distances of tsunami
propagation and less important close to the tsunami origin. The waves generated
by the initial sharp and blunt disturbances such as the tsunami showed little
non-linear during oceanic propagation.

Introduction
Tsunamis are a fascinating and quite well-known phenomenon currently being
studied for many scientic reasons.
Due to the complex and rapidly chang-
ing nature of tsunamis, they are also feared by near-coastal communities and
exposed populations.
Being able to predict the propagation of a tsunami is
therefore important in order to provide sucient tsunami warnings to those ar-
eas aected. The need for real-time tsunami forecasting tools is here essential,
and keeping the computation time down is therefore an important criterion.
Multiple studies have been made in order to investigate the propagation and
origin of tsunamis in the ocean, for example landslides, earthquakes and aster-
oid impacts [Glimsdal et al., 2007]. Most of the studies have considered earth-
quake generated tsunamis, for example the 2011 Tohoku Tsunami, which have
been modeled by [Løvholt et al., 2012], [Tang et al., 2012], [Grilli et al., 2013],
[Ren et al., 2013] and [Madsen et al., 2016] using both dispersive and nondis-
persive models.
Other studies are concerned with the investigation of tsunami propagation.
Some of these have found that tsunamis caused by earthquakes in the deep
ocean will tend to generate extremely long waves having a small steepness
[Madsen et al., 2008]. and that dispersion will become noticeable in the case
of transoceanic propagation [Glimsdal et al., 2013]. Furthermore, it has been
found that the eect of dispersion increases with a decrease of the source width
and that the eect of including the Coriolis force increases with an increase
of the source width [Kirby et al., 2013].
On the contrary, tsunamis caused
by submarine landslides will tend to generate tsunamis having shorter wave
lengths, from which dispersion eects becomes notable even in the near-eld
[Tappin et al., 2014].

In this MSc project the 2011 Tohoku Tsunami will be studied, assuming that the
waves originating from the earthquake will evolve as a so-called transient wave
train and that the leading wave approximately will be travelling with the shallow
water celerity, the non-linear eect will here be relatively small for the tsunami
propagation in the ocean. However when considering a tsunami propagation at
shoaling water the dispersive eects become lesser important as the length-to-
depth ratio increases. In that case non-linear eects may become important as
the amplitude increases. [Glimsdal et al., 2013] [p. 1510]
The aim of this study will be rst to perform basic tests of wave propagation in 1-
D and 2-D in order to get an understanding of the wave propagation originating
from dierent initial disturbances and determine the importance of dispersion.
In that context the variation of the wave number, the wave length and the wave
celerity along the transient wave train will be investigated and the kh-numbers
which are produced by the dierent initial disturbances will be determined. In
addition to that, the importance of dispersion in the tail of the wave train will
be further investigated by comparing the tails obtained with MIKE 21 Boussi-
nesq Wave with similar waves obtained from the more dispersive Convolution
model. Lastly, the eects to be observed when changing the bathymetry will be
determined.
After the basic tests the 2011 Tohoku Tsunami, which occurred due to an evo-
lution of an earthquake just outside the coast of Japan, will be tried simulated
using the hydrodynamic MIKE 21 Flow Model.
The goal of this will be to
simulate the tsunami propagation through the Pacic Ocean and compare the
obtained results with real time obtained surface elevations. The sensitivity due
to grid resolution will be investigated and the best obtained result will be com-
pared to similar studies made by [Madsen et al., 2016] who uses the dispersive
Convolution model and [Ren et al., 2013] who uses GeoClaw, a software based
on non-linear shallow water equations. The importance of refraction will be
studied and the assumption of the non-linearity in tsunamis being small veri-
ed.
For this study it was not only important to investigate and understand the
propagation of the 2011 Tohoku Tsunami, but also to understand the general
propagation of possible future events. For that reason, a nal goal of this MSc
project will be to change the bathymetry of the Pacic Ocean and investigate
the propagation of the resulting tsunami investigated.

Linear Wave Theory
Linear wave theory is used for waves having a small ratio of wave height to water
depth (H/d) for shallow water and a small ratio of wave height to wave length
(H/L) in deep water.
Figure 1.1 shows the propagation of a simple surface wave with the wave length
(L) and wave height (H). The x-axis is located at the Still Water Level (SWL),
with z-axis being positive in the vertical upward direction from the still water
level. The surface elevation varying from the SWL is represented by (η) and the
depth representing the location of the SWL by (h). The actual water depth (d)
will then be the sum of the still water depth (h) and surface elevation (η).
The uid domain will be bound by the free water surface located at z = η[x, t]
and the horizontal sea bed located at z = −h.

The gravitational acceleration (g) will in reality depend on the location con-

following sections as well.

Shallow Water Waves
Waves are considered as being in shallow water when the water depth is lesser
than half of the wave length. This means that waves having very long wave
lengths (i.e. tsunamis) will be in interaction with the seabed all the time from
which their crest will undergo refraction or bending around higher seabed to-
pography [Bryant, 2014]. When the wave length is relatively bigger than the

wave increases, which leads to an increase of the wave height.

Chapter 2
Basic Tests
This chapter will look on the propagation of two simple surface elevations prop-
agating in Cartesian coordinate systems. The tests will be performed using the
MIKE 21 Boussinesq Wave model (BW) and MIKE 21 Flow Model (FM).
The models are based on the assumption that an instant seabed displacement
results in an instant elevation of the sea surface. This leads to the model having
an initial surface elevation at the rst time step, from which waves will propagate
when the simulation starts.
Normally these waves would propagate in all direction surrounding the initial
source. However since the propagation of a symmetric source will be symmetric,
only a corner of the source and the resulting propagation will be considered.
The two dierent surface elevations will be shaped as a square box and a blunt
Gaussian disturbance. The propagation of the two elevation will be investigated
when travelling in 1-D and 2-D at constant or varying depth.
The results will be validated by comparing similar results obtained with the
Convolution model. Furthermore they will evaluated for the purposes of nding
the eect of dispersion, refraction and shoaling.

2.1
Bathymetry Description
This section will describe the bathymetry of the 1-D Model and 2-D Model. The
bathymetries in this chapter will in all cases consist of a Cartesian coordinate
system with notations as described in Section 1.1. The Cartesian coordinate
system, which neglect the Coriolis eect, should be sucient for regional scale
tsunami simulations, however, global tsunami propagation, sphericity and Cori-
olis eects can be relevant for the simulation of a tsunami [Kirby et al., 2013][p.
39].
MIKE Zero Grid Series is used to model the bathymetry since this tool gives
the possibility of producing grids having a xed grid spacing. As described in
Appendix A J refers to the number of columns and K to the number of rows.
These expressions will be used in this chapter.
2.1.1
1-D Model
The 1-D model was created in order to investigate the propagation of waves
over larger distances in one direction. This meant that the simulation domain
had to consist of a suciently large number of grid elements in the J-direction
when compared to the observation points, in order to prevent noise from waves
returning from the edge of the domain. Here 10,000 grid elements was chosen in
the J-direction. However the number of grid elements in the K-direction would
have to be determined by a grid number sensitivity analysis.
2.1.1.1
Grid Number Sensitivity
Multiple grid series were constructed for the 1-D model, all having 10,000 grid
point in the J-direction, but with a varying number of grid point in the K-
direction. The goal was to determine how sensitive the models in MIKE 21 were
to changes of number of grid points in the opposite direction of the propagation
of the wave. The still water depth was (h = 4 km) and did not change over

2.7
Summary of Chapter 2
During the ten dierent MIKE Boussinesq Wave (BW) and the four dierent
MIKE 21 Flow model (FM) simulations multiple eects were determined. It
could in general be concluded that the intensity of the time varying surface
elevation diminishes with increasing distance from the source as the energy
would be spread out.
The Gaussian disturbance would in all cases generate a leading wave having the
biggest amplitude and longest wave length. The waves generated by MIKE 21
FM and MIKE 21 BW lie in good agreement in the beginning of the propagation.
However as the waves propagates the dispersive eects in the Boussinesq model
have had time to develop a dispersive tail and the two models become less
comparable. The waves generated by the Convolution model are however still
in very good agreement with waves generated by MIKE 21 BW.
The initial box disturbance generates very dierent waves depending on whether
it is simulated by MIKE 21 FM or MIKE 21 BW. The waves modeled with MIKE
21 FM have multiple peaks in the leading wave followed by several small wave
groups. The peaks in the leading wave is evened out as the wave propagates.
The waves modeled with MIKE 21 BW would at 600 km propagation have
generated a leading wave followed by a strong tail with highly dispersive waves.
Again the waves generated with MIKE 21 BW and the Convolution model are
in good agreement when considering the leading wave. However this is only the
case for wave numbers up to kh = 0.6 with the dispersion factor B = 0 and for
wave numbers up to kh = 3.2 with B = 1/15.
Common for all waves is the increase in wave number kh with increasing distance
to the leading wave together with a decrease of the individual wave lengths. The
Gaussian disturbance is only able to generate kh−numbers up to 0.6 while no
such limit have yet been found for the square box disturbance. The a−factor
would also decrease with increasing distance to the leading wave which itself
would have a →1. The leading wave would be propagating approximately with
the shallow water celerity from which the wave celerity would decrease with
increasing distance to the leading wave.
The eect which was present for the 1-D model was also present when con-
sidering the 2-D model. However since the propagation area was larger in the
2-D model, the wave energy was able to be spread out on a bigger area leav-
ing the wave amplitudes in general to be lower. Furthermore when changing
the bathymetry from a constant depth to a slowly changing depth the eect
of shoaling starts to appear in the 1-D model and the waves arrives later at
the observation points. In the case of a depth changing 2-D model, shoaling

Convolution model has also become bigger when changing the depth. For a
depth-changing bottom the Convolution model multiply the wave amplitude
with Green's law and uses the shallow water celerity for all waves in the tran-
sient wave train. Good similarities were obtained when comparing the leading
wave obtained from the 1-D model with MIKE 21 BW and the Convolution
model. A phase shift will, however, again appear in the tail because the waves
in the dispersive tail will be travelling to fast for the Convolution model. The
variation between the Convolution model and MIKE 21 BW will become even
bigger in the 2-D model since the convolution model does not include refraction.

3.2
Program Selections for MIKE 21 Flow Model
- Flexible Mesh
MIKE 21 Flow Model - Flexible Mesh (FFM) were used to model the tsunami
propagation. The ow model with the exible mesh was chosen in order to make
the model regime capable of handling locations given in longitude latitude, which
makes it impossible to use grid cells with a xed grid size over the entire domain.
All the program selections can be seen in Appendix C.3 and some of the more
specic choices will be further discussed in this section. The computation time
for the main simulations which was performed during this study can be seen in
Appendix C.4 with corresponding domain description.
3.2.1
Time Step
The time step for the hydrodynamic calculations will be determined by the pro-
gram in order to satisfy the stability criteria. However the program still requires
a specied maximum and minimum time step given in seconds. The maximum
time step will have to be equal to the discrete output time step which was chosen
to be t = tmax = 30 sec, since this would provide a reasonable resolution of the
discrete time series obtained at the DART stations. The minimum time step
would then have to be suciently small in order for the program to adjust and
nd the time step to be used in order not to exceed the critical Courant (CFL)
number. It was chosen to use tmin = 0.01 sec.
The output result is given every 30 sec which gives a sucient resolution of the
surface elevation in order to make acceptable comparison between the real time
obtained surface elevations and those obtained with MIKE 21 FFM.
3.2.2
Bed Resistance
Previous studies have shown no dierence between simulations of tsunamis with
and without bottom friction [Ren et al., 2013][586]. There will for this reason
not be any further investigation of the bottom friction and a constant Manning
number on 32 [m1/3/s] will be chosen for all simulations regarding the Tohoku
Tsunami 2011. It should however be noted that the Manning coecient could
play an essential role in simulations regarding tsunami wave run-up and inun-
dation [Ren et al., 2013] [p 586]. However this will not be studied in this thesis.

Chapter 4
Conclusion
A simulation of the Japan Tohoku 2011 tsunami was successfully carried out
with a grid resolution of 4 arc min covering an area from (120 E◦,60 N◦) to
(-100 E◦,-15 N◦) and validated using eld measurements obtained from eleven
dierent DART stations.
An initial work with fourteen tests were successfully carried out, using MIKE
21 FM and MIKE 21 BW. During these tests it was found that the leading wave
of a transient wave train was travelling with approximately the shallow water
celerity and that the celerity together with the wave length was decreasing for
the individual waves with increasing distance to the leading wave. These waves
would be considered as being linear and satised the dispersion relation. It was
also found that in general the leading wave from a given initial source and a
given distance of propagation will gradually generate a dispersive tail following
the leading wave. The magnitude and needed evolution time of this dispersive
tail will, however, depend on the shape of the initial disturbance.
A blunt
initial disturbances such as the Gaussian disturbance will generate a small tail
consisting of fewer and less dispersive waves and will only be able to produce kh-
numbers up to 0.6. A very sharp initial disturbance such as the box disturbance
will on the other hand generate a dispersive tail faster, having a higher range
of wave numbers being more dispersive. Here, no limit in the generation of the
kh-numbers was found.
==PAGE:107==
94
Conclusion
As determined from the basic tests, dispersive eects will for even a blunt initial
disturbance have become notable after a long time of wave propagation. This
was also found during the simulation of the 2011 Tohoku Tsunami. Here, a
high degree of consistency was obtained between MIKE 21 FMM and real time
measured surface elevations at six DART stations located in within 3200 km
of the earthquake origin. A worse resemblance was obtained between the wave
generated by MIKE 21 FFM and the real time surface elevations obtained at
ve DART stations located within 6000 to 11000 km from the earthquake origin.
The combination of these results indicates that, for the generation of very long
waves travelling in deep ocean dispersion eect can be negligible in the near-
eld, whereas, after a long time of propagation dispersion becomes notable. In
order to obtain better results at the DART stations located in the far-eld a
Boussinesq model may be used.
The basic tests showed that shallow water waves travelling in 2-D are aected by
shoaling and refraction. The leading wave of the tohoku tsunami was estimated
to be in the range of 100 - 200 kilo meters and would denitely be considered as
a shallow water wave and it would undergo shoaling and refraction when travel-
ling along a varying ocean bottom. It was also observed that the tsunami front
was refracted around islands (e.g Hawaii) from which it underwent diraction.
Simulations in which the bathymetry was changed quantied the waves depen-
dency on the bathymetry and it is concluded that refraction must be considered
in the far-eld tsunami propagation. Non-linearity was found to be extremely
small in the near- and far-eld as the relative wave height was very small.
Comparison between the waves simulated with MIKE 21 FFM and the Convo-
lution model showed larger variations in arrival time and wave amplitude. It
was here found that the convolution model generally generated bigger waves
arriving earlier than the ones found with MIKE 21 FFM in the simulation us-
ing the original bathymetry. However, when changing the bathymetry into a
les varying ocean bottom, better resemblance would at some DART stations be
obtained as the waves would arrive at the same time. The wave amplitude was
still overestimated by the Convolution model though.
To conclude, the overall project aims of simulating the leading wave produced
by the 2011 Tohoku earthquake and investigating the eects of dispersion and
refraction were satised.
