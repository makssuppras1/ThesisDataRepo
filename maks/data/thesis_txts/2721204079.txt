Abstract 
Drastically increasing prices for fossil fuels and a growing urge to decrease carbon emissions 
have led to a rise of renewable energy sources in the electricity generation. Due to the 
dependency on natural influences, these are, however, very volatile. Their influence on the 
development of the market is therefore of high interest. Additionally, the overall price level has 
increased significantly over the last year due to external shocks, like the Covid pandemic and 
the war in Ukraine. Electricity is therefore a major cost factor for a majority of industrial 
organisations. Both cost reduction and emission reduction are important goals for companies.  
This thesis aims to investigate the impact of renewable energy sources and other factors on the 
electricity price. To achieve this goal, a linear regression model was set up. Understanding the 
influence of solar and wind energy was the main objective. The results show clearly that both 
have a negative impact and are drivers of the price decrease. The gas price was identified as 
the main positive contributor. 
Additionally, the regression model also serves as an electricity price forecast. It can be used in 
a practical context to make operational and processual decisions. It creates value especially as 
a tool for demand side management purposes in order to save costs for industrial organisations. 
Based on this forecast, an index was developed to include the carbon emissions from the energy 
mix into the decision-making. The so-called Price-Emission-Index allows companies to 
optimise their operations based on the two objectives of reducing the carbon footprint and 
improve the financial situation.   
Both forecasting models are capable of predicting the respective values on a short-term 
horizon. They can therefore be used in an actual practical setting. An improvement in accuracy, 
as well as confirming the tools in a use case are potential areas for future research based on this 
report’s findings.

1. Introduction  
1.1 Importance of a stable energy supply 
Access to electricity has become vital in today’s world. It is not only important for one’s 
pleasure and leisure, but also critical for most communication, as well as a provider of news 
and information of any kind via the internet. In addition, people rely on it for heating, and other 
basic household functions (International Energy Agency, 2023a). It is therefore an absolute 
necessity to always have stable and constant access to power. The importance is so high, that 
Löfquist (2020) even considers the question if access to electricity can be considered a human 
right.  
However, the relevancy stretches further than the personal use. Energy market security is also 
a fundamental factor in economic stability and growth (Almutairi, Medlock and Pierru, 2020). 
Since almost all industrial production and transportation relies heavily on stable access to 
energy (Timme, Kocarev and Witthaut, 2015), it has become the basis for economic prosperity. 
Ferguson, Wilkinson and Hill already showed in 2000, that globally, “economic development 
occurred ‘hand in hand' with electricity consumption” (p.934). Güler, Haykır and Öz (2022) 
have recently updated and confirmed this causal relationship. It is therefore only logical that 
industrial processes are one of the main power consumers. In 2019, 41,9% of all consumed 
electricity was used within the industrial sector, with the basic metal industry as the most 
energy-intensive user (International Energy Agency, 2023b).  
This trend of growing demand seems to continue. While in 1973 the global power consumption 
was 194 Exo Joule (EJ), it skyrocketed to 418 EJ in 2019 (International Energy Agency, 
2023b). During this span, the consumption in Europe and the United States has seen only a 
moderate growth, developing nations – especially China – however, have multiplied their 
power use over the past decades (International Energy Agency, 2023c). According to different  
scenarios by the World Energy Council, this trend is set to continue into the future (World 
Energy Council, 2019). Therefore, the reliance on a functioning electricity market to supply 
electricity will increase further (International Energy Agency, 2023a).  
1.2 Energy Mix 
Electricity generation typically consists of different energy sources. These can be divided into 
fossil fuels, nuclear energy, renewable energy sources (Pfenninger and Keirstead, 2015).

Historically, fossil fuels have been the leading energy source and still are a major factor within 
the energy mix (International Energy Agency, 2022b). Especially in Europe, however, the 
industry is changing with a  continuously growing demand for more sustainable electricity 
(Rapier, 2022). 
In 1990 the dominant source of power generation in Europe was coal with a share of more than 
38%. Overall, fossil fuels accounted for more than half of the total production (International 
Energy Agency, 2023b). Meanwhile, renewable energy was responsible for less than one fifth 
and almost exclusively represented by hydro power (International Energy Agency, 2023b). By 
2020, the share of renewable energy had more than doubled with wind and solar energy 
increasing their share drastically within these three decades (International Energy Agency, 
2023b). In that timespan, fossil fuels dropped from over 55% to less than 40% signalling a 
clear trend away from carbon-intensive sources. A contributing factor to this is the decrease of 
total production of coal-powered electricity by more than half (International Energy Agency, 
2022c). The global picture, however, is telling another story. Fossil fuels account for roughly 
80% of the total global production. The rest is divided into nuclear power (5%) and different 
renewable energy sources (RES) (International Energy Agency, 2023b).  
1.3 Exogenous influences  
Over the last few years, different external factors have seriously influenced the energy market. 
Firstly, the Covid-19 pandemic has presented a unique – but temporary - decline in energy 
demand (International Energy Forum, 2023). Due to lockdowns and the resulting decrease in 
economic activities, the demand dropped heavily. Severely affected nations reported an 
average collapse of 25% during those times (Amamou and Bargaoui, 2022). Subsequently, the 
price for energy fell drastically, too (Amamou and Bargaoui, 2022). However, not all sectors 
were influenced equally. While the consumption dopped especially in the industrial and 
transport areas, the residential share increased due to new consumer behaviour and more time 
spent at home forced by lockdowns (Zhang et al., 2021). This led to a high volatility and 
fluctuating overall demand numbers, which consequently resulted in lower investments in the 
sector. Additionally, the global supply chains were disrupted, which complicated operations 
throughout the world (Zakeri et al., 2022). Overall, the pandemic caused high uncertainty 
within the market and led to adaptations in both political and industrial strategies (PWC, 2023; 
Szczygielski et al., 2022).

Another external disturbance took place in early 2022 when Russia invaded Ukraine and caused 
a state of geopolitical shock. Due to Russia’s extraordinary role as (one of) the biggest exporter 
of natural gas and oil to the European Union, the war had a severe impact on the energy market 
(Umar, Riaz and Yousaf, 2022). As a result of the aggression, the EU lost trust in the Russian 
Federation and implied heavy sanctions (European Council, 2022). Overall, the amount of gas 
exports from Russia to the EU have dropped significantly since the start of the war. Following 
this, the gas price jumped from 20€ in early 202 to 80€ per MWh in February 2022 with 
significantly higher peaks (Osička and Černoch, 2022). This, again, led to uncertainty and 
instability within the market (World Economic Forum, 2022).  
Apart from these singular events that took place unexpectedly, climate change as another 
external factor has been influencing the energy market. In recent years global warming has 
played an increasingly bigger role in power generation (United Nations, 2023). In 2020, the 
energy market created more than two thirds of all global greenhouse gas emissions and is 
therefore a major factor in global warming (Walton, 2020). This creates a need for more 
sustainable energy production and less carbon emissions. Several nations have therefore set 
targets to increase their share of renewable energy generation. The European Union has 
declared a plan to achieve a total share of 45% renewable energy generation in the year 2030 
(European Commission, 2020). Others, like the United States, have also committed to building 
additional capacities in the next years and decades (U.S. Department of the Interior, 2023).  
Overall, these external factors lead to a growing demand for independence in the energy sector 
(European Commission, 2022b). Governments want to be independent on other, possibly 
hostile, states in order to not be susceptible to political pressure through energy imports 
(European Commission, 2022b). The EU countries have been hit hard by the lack of gas from 
the Russian Federation as a consequence of the war in Ukraine resulting in higher prices for 
energy and electricity (Adolfsen et al., 2022). Furthermore, there is a growing demand for 
independence from fossil fuels (European Commission, 2022b). To fulfil sustainability goals, 
the energy sector needs to significantly decrease their carbon emissions in almost all countries 
(UNFCCC, 2021).  
One way to achieve both of those goals is the strengthening and expansion of renewable energy 
generation. Since they do not rely on the consumption of finite resources, they can be fully 
produced within a country’s own boarders and therefore reduce dependences on foreign nations 
and fossil fuels (European Commission, 2022b).

1.4 Industry perspective  
Industrial organisations have been hit particularly hard by these price developments and spikes 
(Stratmann, 2023). Especially energy-intensive industries and producing companies often 
spend high percentages of their total cost on energy. In the aluminium sector for example, the 
electricity costs usually account for 30-50% of all production costs (Grave et al., 2015). 
Therefore, price increases hurt these organisations exceptionally hard. Especially smaller 
companies might be struggling to overcome them or even survive (European Commission, 
2022a). In several European countries, like Germany, Norway, or the Netherlands, 
organisations had to decrease or stop their production lines due to the high energy costs 
(Alderman, 2022; Tagesschau, 2022). While political efforts to stop the increase in costs, like 
the energy price brakes in Germany (German Federal Government, 2022b), are made, the 
impact often takes time to come fully into effect. In the short term, however, the companies 
must secure their own well-being and adapt to the circumstances. High energy prices are also 
an international disadvantage in attracting and keeping companies and therefore capital. 
Research has shown that electricity costs are a major factor in companies’ relocation decision 
(Panhans, Lavric and Hanley, 2017) and Germany is already losing high-tech companies due 
to the high price level (Hofer, Hoppe and Stratmann, 2021). Therefore, reaction strategies must 
be developed on how to optimize operations regarding energy costs. Ideally, these reaction 
strategies simultaneously should take the emissions within the energy mix into account to 
minimize the carbon footprint. These strategies not only help in times of crises but can also 
boost profits during other periods.   
1.5 Goals, added value and research questions 
In the context of these developments, this thesis focuses on the relationship between the 
electricity market and renewable energy in Germany. The first goal of is to do a scientific 
literature review and explain the relevant mechanisms of the complex electricity market in 
detail, as well as its characteristics and regulations. Afterwards, the relationship between the 
renewable energy supply and the electricity costs will be modelled. The research question here 
is “How does renewable energy generation impact the electricity price?” The influence will 
be analysed through constructing a multiple linear regression model. The model will also serve 
as a forecast of the electricity price based on several variables including renewable energy 
generation. Additionally, a comparison with historic, pre-pandemic and pre-energy crisis data 
will be made to highlight differences and ensure robustness. The required data – namely

German Federal Network Agency for Electricity, Gas, Telecommunications, Post and Railway

Furthermore, an index is developed as a decision-making basis for companies and

The final result of the thesis will consist of a model for price and CO2-intensity of the electricity

and practical implications. Firstly, it contributes to the scientific analysis of the electricity 
market and its irregularities and specifics. Secondly, it creates an practical approach for 
organizations on how to deal with these uncertainties in order to decrease costs while using 
renewable and therefore sustainable energy.  
 
2. The electricity market in Germany  
2.1 Historic development  
The electricity market in Germany today is a complex construct with various actors and 
mechanisms. However, it has only been the case for slightly more than two decades. A big 
change in the late 1990s led to the current liberalised market (Heddenhausen, 2007). 
In the 20th century, the market was mostly monopolised. Based on the National Energy Act 
(German: Energiewirtschaftsgesetz, EnWG) introduced in 1935, the country was divided into 
zones in which organisations– usually publicly owned municipal utilities – had a right to 
exclusively sell their products to the customers (Heuterkes and Janssen, 2008). These 
organisations could either generate the electricity themselves or buy it directly from other 
producers. The consumers were therefore reliant on their municipal utility (Bundesnetzagentur, 
2017).  
==PAGE:14==

In 1998, an EU directive was translated into German national law in the form of an updated 
version of the EnWG. The goal of this directive was threefold: Secure the supply of electricity, 
decrease CO2 emissions and become more sustainable, and to reduce costs and make energy 
affordable (Agora Energiewende, 2019). This led to the liberalisation and privatisation of the 
entire sector. The monopolies were destroyed, and consumers were given the possibility to 
choose their preferred supplier (Heddenhausen, 2007). The suppliers themselves could also 
expand and offer their services to other regions of the country (Bundesnetzagentur, 2017).    
As intended by the new law, in the following years, the electricity price dropped heavily. In 
2000, the industrial price was only 65% of the 1998 level (Burger, 2014). However, since then, 
a sharp increase can be observed (Burger, 2014; Federal Ministry for Economic Affairs and 
Climate Action, 2019). Since 2000, the electricity costs for private consumers have almost 
doubled (Federal Ministry for Economic Affairs and Climate Action, 2019). In 2018, an 
average price of more than 15ct/kWh made Germany one of the most expensive energy markets 
in Europe (Federal Ministry for Economic Affairs and Climate Action, 2019).  
The electricity consumption in Germany, however, has been relatively steady during that time. 
Fuelled by increases in energy efficiency and several governmental efforts, the overall 
consumption peaked in 2007 (German Environment Agency, 2022b). Since then, a small 
decline can be observed with an additional dip in 2020 due to the Covid pandemic. In the future, 
a reversal of this trend is to be expected. In order to tackle climate change, areas like 
transportation or heating will focus on electricity more than in the past and therefore increase 
the overall demand (German Environment Agency, 2022b).  
Today, two trends are shaping the German electricity market. Firstly, the need for a more 
environmentally friendly energy mix (European Commission, 2020). The share of production 
from renewable energy sources has increased continuously. While RES accounted for less than 
5% of all consumed electricity in 1990, it rose to more than 40% in 2021 and is likely to further 
increase (German Environment Agency, 2022b). During the same period, the percentage of 
conventional power, like natural gas, coal and nuclear power dropped from over 80% to less 
than 40% (German Environment Agency, 2022a). Secondly, high gas prices – mostly as a result 
of the Russian invasion in Ukraine – led to higher electricity prices for consumers and 
industries. A 500% increase within one year was the result (Gillespie and Mathis, 2022). The 
high energy prices are also an economic problem on a national and international level. They

are a major driver of the skyrocketing inflation, both in Germany and the entire European Union 
(Mayr, 2022).  
Overall, the electricity market in Germany is still highly dependent on fossil fuel generation 
and therefore subject to high price increases, especially since early 2022. The share of 
renewable energy within the sector is growing rapidly but still below 50% (German 
Environment Agency, 2022a).   
 
2.2 Basic Market Model  
The electricity market itself is a complex structure with several different actors and 
characteristics. For the purpose of this thesis, only the economic level is of importance. The 
physical mechanisms will therefore not be explained in detail.    
The basic concept of the market is very common. Supply and demand determine the price of 
the goods. However, while traditional goods can be easily traded, electricity is harder to store 
and therefore not as available  (Bundesnetzagentur, 2023h; Ellenrieder, 2022). The demand 
fluctuates significantly depending on the needs of private and industrial customers (Federal 
Ministry for Economic Affairs and Climate Action, 2020). Because of these fluctuations, grid 
stability is a critical issue. It needs to be ensured through a balancing mechanism to enable a 
functioning network (The European Power Exchange, 2023).  
The objectives of the market are also relatively simple. The core principle is a stable supply of 
electricity for all customers. In addition, efficiency within the market – both long- and short 
term – is to be achieved (Ashour Novirdoust et al., 2021). Other goals, like transparency, 
fairness and simplicity should also be considered and implemented (Cramton, 2017).  
Germany is part of the European Energy market (Federal Ministry for Economic Affairs and 
Climate Action, 2023b). The structure of the market itself features a variety of different 
participants. The energy generation happens in power plants of different shapes and forms. 
Those can range from small photovoltaic units, over on- or offshore wind parks, to huge coal 
plants or nuclear reactors. These are the initial suppliers of the electricity, that feed their product 
into the network (Graebig et al., 2020). The demand side is represented by individual 
consumers or larger entities like organisations and companies that need the electricity for their 
own purposes, as well as the Power-to-X sector that transform surplus electricity into other 
energy forms for storage (Graebig et al., 2020). There are, however, several other actors in

between the end-user and the supplier. Firstly, the transmission system operators (TSO) and 
distribution system operators (DSO) are ensuring that the electricity can successfully be 
delivered to the customers. They therefore act as a supplier of the necessary infrastructure 
within the market, namely the high-voltage power grid (Federal Ministry for Economic Affairs 
and Climate Action, 2023a; Graebig et al., 2020). Additionally, there are wholesalers and direct 
marketers. These entities are trading at the electricity exchange market and try to acquire the 
needed amount. They usually sell it to utility companies that own and operate the low-voltage 
network within their region and can deliver the power directly to the end-users (en:former, 
2022; Graebig et al., 2020).  
Since Germany is a member of the European Energy market, exports and imports of electricity 
are a common practice that ensures stability for all participants (Federal Ministry for Economic 
Affairs and Climate Action, 2023b). Overall, there are nine neighbouring countries that 
Germany is trading with directly. In 2018, a net export of 51,2 Terawatt-hours (TWh) was 
generated (Federal Ministry for Economic Affairs and Climate Action, 2019).  
From production to distribution, there are several steps in the electricity market. Firstly, the 
power is produced at the plants and sold to wholesalers. The electricity is then auctioned off at 
the energy exchange and sold to utilities. These, again, are responsible for the distribution to 
their consumers (Graebig et al., 2020). The physical distribution is carried out via the 
transmission and distribution network. While this is the logical sequence, it does not necessarily 
reflect the actual temporal order. In fact, the power is traded at the exchange before it is actually 
produced. The demand is determined first and will then be met by the respective power plants 
(Bundesnetzagentur, 2023h; Federal Ministry for Economic Affairs and Climate Action, 2020). 
The mechanism will be explained in more detail in section 2.4.

2.3.1 Power Generators 
The actual production of electricity happens within power plants. These take different sizes 
and rely on various energy sources. Therefore, small photovoltaic systems are included, as well 
as wind parks, nuclear and fossil fuel power plants (Graebig et al., 2020). In general, every 
system that generates electricity and feeds it into the network is part of this group. These 
amount to a total capacity of 214.2 gigawatts in Germany, over 50% of which are from 
renewable energy plants (Federal Ministry for Economic Affairs and Climate Action, 2019). 
Overall, there are more than 2 million of these electricity generating plants in Germany, mostly 
smaller ones based on RES (Graebig et al., 2020). After producing electricity, the power 
generators sell their product on the exchange market or to wholesalers or virtual power plants. 
They also interact with the transmission system operators to feed the electricity into the 
network (Erbach, 2016; Graebig et al., 2020).    
2.3.2 Direct Marketers 
Today, the power plant operators rarely sell the produced power themselves. Instead, they rely 
on direct marketers. These act as an intermediary between the producers and the electricity 
exchange. For a commission fee they sell the electricity on the exchange market. This model

2.3.3 Energy Exchange  
While a small fraction of the electricity is traded privately over the counter (OTC), the large 
majority is sold via the exchange markets, where transparency and stability can be ensured 
(Bundesnetzagentur, 2023h; European Commission, 2023a). Different forms of energy 
exchanges exist based on the timeframe of the supplied energy. Firstly, the futures market – in 
Europe namely the European Energy Exchange – provides customers with long-term electricity 
contracts. Electricity for the next months to years can be bought on the futures market 
(Bundesnetzagentur, 2023h; The European Power Exchange, 2023). The spot market – EPEX 
Spot in Europe – is for securing short-term energy. It is split into the day-ahead market, where 
contracts for every hour of the next day are traded, and the intra-day market, which supplies 
electricity for the next hours within the day (Bundesnetzagentur, 2023h). Through these 
different market types, long guarantees can be secured, as well as spontaneous reactions 
(Bundesnetzagentur, 2023i; The European Power Exchange, 2023). The price for the electricity 
is determined by the exchange itself through a market clearing mechanism and is equal for all 
participants (Europex – Association of European Energy Exchanges, 2023).  
2.3.4 Transmission Network Operators/Distribution Network Operators 
Networks are a major factor in a functioning electricity market. They ensure the power gets 
safely transmitted from the power plants to the end-users (Bundesnetzagentur, 2023i). The 
transmission network covers the whole country, contains the highest voltage and is made to 
deliver the power from the plants to the distribution networks (Bundesnetzagentur, 2023i). 
According to German law its goal is "to operate and maintain a safe, reliable and efficient 
energy supply network and to optimise, reinforce and expand the network in line with 
requirements in a non-discriminatory manner" (section 11(1) of EnWG) (Bundesnetzagentur, 
2023i). Germany’s transmission network is divided into four zones which are each operated by 
a different company  (Bundesnetzagentur, 2023i). The distribution network is designed to 
deliver the power to the consumers (Oberschmidt, Klobasa and Genoese, 2013). It is connected 
to the transmission network and usually operated by communal utilities. It is divided into high, 
medium, and low voltage circuits (Federal Ministry for Economic Affairs and Climate Action, 
2023a).

2.3.5 Power Suppliers/Utility Companies 
The utility companies are responsible for supplying the consumers with electricity. In 
Germany, these are often public utilities owned by the municipalities (en:former, 2022). Since 
the liberalisation of the market, the customers can choose their preferred company freely 
(European Commission, 2023a). While their main goal is to ensure the economic connection 
of customers to the market in form of electricity contracts, many also operate the distribution 
networks and are therefore also responsible for the physical transport and delivery (en:former, 
2022).  
2.3.6 Consumers 
The consumer group includes everyone that receives power from the market. That can be 
individual households, bigger entities like organisations and companies, as well as sector 
coupling systems (Graebig et al., 2020). While smaller consumers usually get their electricity 
through contracts with utility companies, larger industrial customers often buy it directly from 
the exchange market (Graebig et al., 2020). In the sector coupling electricity is used as a

2.3.7 Prosumers 
Prosumers are both producers and consumers. The term refers to households or organisations 
that produce their own energy, e.g. through solar panels (Graebig et al., 2020). As long as the 
production exceeds their own demand, power gets fed into the network, if the production does 
not meet the required amount, the prosumers act like a normal consumer and receive electricity 
from the grid. (Graebig et al., 2020; Parag and Sovacool, 2016)  
 
2.4 Pricing mechanisms 
Electricity prices are determined in different ways. In the over-the-counter trading, the prices 
can be negotiated freely between the involved parties (Graebig et al., 2020). They usually relate 
to the prices on the energy exchanges, which are the more relevant to look at due to the higher 
volumes being traded (Bundesnetzagentur, 2023h; Ellenrieder, 2022). As mentioned before, 
there are different types of energy exchanges: The Futures Market and the Spot Market, that 
includes day-ahead and intraday trading (Ellenrieder, 2022). Pricing on the futures market is 
determined solely by demand and supply. Long-term contracts are traded to secure risk-free 
electricity and be protected against price spikes. For this security, the customers usually pay a 
higher price (Bundesnetzagentur, 2023h). The spot market, however, has a different 
mechanism to set the price. Since the timeframe is much shorter, it can react to short-term 
changes in supply, demand, and other influencing variables (Ellenrieder, 2022). 
The pricing works as follows: All interested parties from both the demand and the supply side 
submit their offer to the exchange (Graebig et al., 2020). Power plants therefore signal if they 
can supply electricity for the respective period and how much capacity they will be able to 
generate. Additionally, they must disclose their marginal cost to produce one unit of electricity 
(MWh) (Forschungsgesellschaft für Energiewirtschaft, 2022). Utility companies and large 
consumers on the other hand submit their request for a specific amount of power they would 
like to buy for the period (Graebig et al., 2020). Therefore, the total demand for the specific 
==PAGE:21==
On the organizational response to fluctuations in renewable energy supply 
 
 
 
 
- 13 -  
 
 
time can be calculated. This demand is then met by the submitted plants (EPEX Spot, 2018). 
At the energy exchange, this process is completed via a certain method known as the merit 
order (Ellenrieder, 2022). It ensures the fulfilment of all submitted demand by through adding 
plants until the required amount is met. The power generators are then added in the order of 
their marginal costs starting with the cheapest one (Kittel, Roth and Schill, 2022).  
The most expensive still required one is setting the price for the whole market, the so-called 
market clearing price (MCP) (Ellenrieder, 2022). Therefore, the last plant has its marginal costs 
met while all cheaper producers are rewarded with higher margins. Higher priced energy is not 
needed and therefore not sold at the exchange (Graebig et al., 2020). As a result, the cheapest 
energy producers are more likely to generate profit. However, in the merit order only marginal 
costs are displayed, the fixed costs, such as initial investments, are not included. It is therefore 
possible that even plants with a positive margin in the merit order are overall not profitable and 
lose money (Ellenrieder, 2022).   
Due to the lack of required resources, the renewable energy producers usually have the lowest 
marginal costs. This means, that RES are added first within the merit order and typically are 
used when they are available (Dean et al., 2017; Roldan-Fernandez et al., 2016). Afterwards, 
nuclear energy is often the cheapest option before the demand is fully met by adding fossil 
fuels. Consequently, power produced by fossil fuel often set the price for the entire market 
(Antweiler and Muesgens, 2021; Forschungsgesellschaft für Energiewirtschaft, 2022). In times 
where resources, such as gas, experience heavy price increases, the power market feels these 
implications heavily (Weibelzahl, 2022).

While the system functions generally, several points of criticism have been voiced. Firstly, the 
marginal costs do not realistically represent the overall costs. Especially for renewable sources 
and nuclear energy there are high investments and upfront costs that are not considered 
(Ellenrieder, 2022). Secondly, RES are often subsidised by governmental programs. Even 
though electricity prices are usually decreasing with a growing share of RES (merit-order-
effect), this is partly paid for through these subsidiaries (Figueiredo and Da Silva, 2019; 
Roldan-Fernandez et al., 2016). Lastly, a growing share of cheap electricity, namely from RES, 
leads to a lower MCP. As a result, the profit margins for the power producers are becoming 
smaller. This leads to a cannibalisation effect: The more electricity from RES is generated, the 
less revenue and profit there is to exploit for the companies (Graebig et al., 2020). Especially 
wind and solar power, which are highly time-dependent, appear in spikes and results in high 
shares of cheap energy. Therefore, the companies are decreasing their profits (per unit) by 
adding more cheap energy into the mix (Benhmad and Percebois, 2017). This can lead to a lack 
of investment in new power plants to not further lower the profit margins (Graebig et al., 2020).    
2.5 Negative Prices 
The merit order principle can also lead to extraordinary situations in which the market model 
seems to be counterintuitive. One such phenomenon is the case of negative electricity prices. 
These usually occur when renewable energy production is high and the overall demand for 
power is low (Bundesnetzagentur, 2023d). Since the production from renewable sources is 
extremely inflexible, the generated electricity needs to be used or stored. Storage, however, is 
difficult and expensive and producers are often not able to store their own produced power  
(Atănăsoae, Pentiuc and Hopulele, 2020). Additionally, many power plants have high ramp up 
costs and cannot be shut down easily (Genoese, Genoese and Wietschel, 2010). As a result, the 
producing companies need to dispose of this electricity. While conventional plants, like coal 
or nuclear power, can be shut down and stop generating, solar and wind energy cannot. 
Therefore, the supply of renewables sometimes exceeds the demand on the market (Fanone, 
Gamba and Prokopczuk, 2013; Prokhorov and Dreisbach, 2022). This leads to negative prices, 
so buyers take the electricity and get paid to do so. While these occurrences are relatively rare, 
they become more frequent as the sustainable energy share increases. Negative prices can be 
considered a risk in the market, especially for optimisation and forecasting models (Fanone, 
Gamba and Prokopczuk, 2013).

2.6 Market Balancing & Redispatch 
The important basis for market balancing is that energy generation and consumption must 
always be equal. This ensures a constant frequency (of 50 Hz) within the grid and therefore a 
stable power distribution (Bundesnetzagentur, 2023g; Graebig et al., 2020). Negative prices 
are one economic outcome if demand and supply differ initially. But there are also other 
scenarios in the electricity market where a gap between generation and consumption arises 
(Bundesnetzagentur, 2023h).  
One common challenge appears when energy providers cannot fulfil the supply they sold on 
the day-ahead market, e.g. through plant outages or differing weather conditions. Since they 
are selling electricity based on a forecast, the actual production volume might differ. As a result, 
a gap between demand and supply emerges (Bundesnetzagentur, 2023b). This gap needs to be 
addressed in order to ensure the stability of the market (Ellenrieder, 2022). In the following the 
basic German market balancing mechanisms will be explained.  
Ensuring the functionality of the network is the responsibility of the system operators. 
Therefore, the frequency of the distribution grid needs to be steady at 50Hz (Lopes, 2021). To 
balance the market and stabilise the grid, producers and consumers can offer so called control 
power, which makes them Balancing Service Providers (Graebig et al., 2020). The system 
operators then acquire the needed amount for their grid through an auction process. Through 
this mechanism, deviations within the market can be offset (Ellenrieder, 2022). The providers 
are then compensated for their services by the grid operators (Graebig et al., 2020). The control 
power is positive in case additional electricity needs to be provided or consumption is lowered 
(Müsgens, Ockenfels and Peek, 2014). It is negative if less power needs to be fed into the grid 
or consumption is increased (Ellenrieder, 2022).   
There are three levels of balancing: Firstly, the primary control power or frequency 
containment reserves (FCR) is automatically activated when the frequency is deviating by more 
than 10Hz (Bundesnetzagentur, 2023b). It needs to be activated immediately after requested 
and must be available the entire day. It is provided directly by the power plants (Graebig et al., 
2020). If the state of deviation takes longer than 30 seconds, the secondary control power or 
frequency restoration reserves with automatic activation (aFRR) is centrally activated. This 
level needs to be at peak capacity within 5 minutes (Bundesnetzagentur, 2023b). The minutes 
reserve or frequency restoration reserves with manual activation (mFRR) is the last level, 
which is used to release the secondary power control and must reach its capacity maximum 
==PAGE:24==
On the organizational response to fluctuations in renewable energy supply 
 
 
 
 
- 16 -  
 
 
within 15 minutes (Bundesnetzagentur, 2023b). The first level needs to be available the entire 
day and must be both positive and negative. The secondary and minutes reserves are offered in 
4 hour-intervals and have separate auctions for positive and negative balancing (Graebig et al., 
2020). If the control power is needed and applied to the system, the Balancing Service Provider 
will get monetary compensation from the system operators (Lopes, 2021). These will then 
collect a network fee from its customers which will offset their balancing costs. Therefore, the 
consumers will eventually pay the price for increasing imbalances in the market (Ellenrieder, 
2022; Graebig et al., 2020).  
There are various approaches to improve the power markets in order to achieve a better-
balanced system (van der Veen and Hakvoort, 2016). However, with increasing renewable 
energy shares, these imbalances will likely occur more frequently. It also opens up new revenue 
possibilities for consumers by offering balancing services on the market. (Kath et al., 2020; 
Schleicher-Tappeser, 2012) 
 
Redispatch is another mechanism created to stabilise the power grid, specifically to solve 
network congestion problems (Ellenrieder, 2022). While control power is trying to mitigate the 
effects of a global over- or underload, redispatch is a more closely related to the physical limits 
of the grid. Network congestions appear when peak voltage or currents are exceeded in a certain

therefore need to be absolutely avoided. It is a common phenomenon when the electricity 
generation is not geographically equally divided within the grid (Bundesnetzagentur, 2023e). 
The main principle behind redispatch is the adjustment of the power plants’ schedules to avoid 
or resolve these congestions. In practice, this means reducing the load on one side of the 
congestion while increasing it on the other, thus, resulting in an unchanged overall capacity 
and relieved networks (Bundesnetzagentur, 2023f). The adjustments are handled by the 
network operators which forecast the loads and try to predict potential congestion dangers 
(Graebig et al., 2020). Since the operating plants are the cheapest ones available, any 
adjustment of the schedules will lead to an increase of costs. Like the control power costs, this 
will be paid by the consumers eventually through an increase in network charges (Graebig et 
al., 2020). The frequency and volume of redispatch measures has increased in the last years 
due to the reduction of stable nuclear energy and the growing share of fluctuating renewable 
energy (Bundesnetzagentur, 2023f; Kunz and Zerrahn, 2015). It can be reduced by an 
expansion and improvement of the network (Graebig et al., 2020).

3. Industrial demand flexibility  
3.1 Importance  
Energy prices are one of the major cost factors in the current industrial environment. Especially 
energy-intensive companies are highly dependent on affordable electricity for their processes. 
They are often accounting for a large share of all production costs and therefore a major driver 
of total expenditures (Grave et al., 2015). Due to various reasons, electricity prices have been 
rising and consequently have become challenging for large parts of the economy (Cooban, 
2022). As seen in Figure 6, the industrial prices for electricity have more than doubled since 
2015. While being relatively stable until 2021, they have skyrocketed since (Statistisches 
Bundesamt, 2023).  
For some organisations, this resulted in an increase in production costs of almost 50% in only 
one year (Crispeels et al., 2022).  In a 2022 Deloitte survey among CFOs of German companies, 
the highest rated risk for the next year was “rising energy prices”. While the outlook for the 
upcoming year seems to be more positive, a general uncertainty regarding the price 
development is still lingering (Favas, 2022; Weber and Look, 2023).  
Additionally, the emissions from the energy sector are still extremely high. In the European 
Union, the energy production accounted for more than one quarter of all carbon emissions 
(Eurostat, 2023). Electricity production from renewable energy sources is reducing those 
emissions but is simultaneously increasing the volatility in the energy mix. The dependency on 
natural influences, like wind and sun, leads to a more fluctuating supply of electricity that needs 
to be compensated (Brunner et al., 2020; European Environment Agency, 2021). While the 
balancing mechanisms explained in chapter 2.6 are reducing the fluctuation on the supply side, 
the demand party can also take measures to stabilize the market (Ellenrieder, 2022). Therefore,

organisations need to develop strategies to adapt to these circumstances (Brunner et al., 2020). 
Through adjustments of internal processes and operations, not only can the market be stabilized 
but costs can be saved and emissions reduced (Sousa and Soares, 2023; Valdes et al., 2019). 
3.2 Definition 
Golmohamadi (2022) defines demand flexibility as “the ability of electrical consumption to 
change/shift/adjust/curtail the electricity consumption in response to an external request, e.g., 
electricity price, financial incentives, and technical requirements.” (p. 3). It refers to a reaction 
in electricity demand from the consumer side through undefined measures in order to achieve 
a certain goal (Golmohamadi, 2022). Two terms that are often used when talking about 
adjustments on the demand side are demand side management (DSM) and demand (side) 
response (DR or DSR). While there are no unambiguous definitions and the concepts are often 
used interchangeably, they need to be explained in more detail. Firstly, demand side 
management is the overall adaptation of a company’s electricity consumption. This includes 
energy efficiency (EE), as well as demand response activities (Behrangrad, 2015; Ford, Gillich 
and Mirzania, 2020; Stanelyte, Radziukyniene and Radziukynas, 2022). While energy 
efficiency is certainly an important aspect in a company’s efforts to tackle its electricity costs 
and carbon footprint it is relatively independent on the market developments (Khan, 2019; 
Usman et al., 2022). Therefore, the focus of this thesis will be on the demand response 
strategies. Demand response refers to the initially cited definition and means a change in 
consumption based on external signals (Golmohamadi, 2022; Haupt et al., 2020; Müller and 
Möst, 2018). It sometimes is also referred to as Load Management (Haupt et al., 2020) and 
requires the potential for flexibility from consumers and their internal processes (Müller and 
Möst, 2018). 
The advantages of DR implementation include both the demand and the supply side 
(Golmohamadi, 2022). Firstly, the market is benefiting by having a balancing mechanism. The 
increasing share of renewable energy generation leads to a need for more flexible demand due 
to high fluctuations and low flexibility on the supply side (Haupt et al., 2020). One goal of the 
concept is, therefore, to balance the demand and supply of energy in the market (Golmohamadi, 
2022) by increasing consumption in times of high supply and decreasing it for lower supply 
(Stanelyte, Radziukyniene and Radziukynas, 2022). On the demand side, companies can 
benefit either from lower electricity prices or incentives to reduce the load (Golmohamadi, 
2022). The International Energy Agency (2022a) differentiates between two DR mechanisms:

Implicit demand response, which is rewarding consumers through lower prices and explicit 
demand response in which the customers get paid directly for adjusting their demand. 
Additionally, demand response strategies can also lead to carbon emission reductions (Sousa 
and Soares, 2023). Hence, it is possible to address both economic and environmental goals 
simultaneously.  
3.3 Strategies  
The adjustment of consumption can be achieved through various strategies. Incentive-based or 
explicit techniques are trying to market flexibility in demand to secure the market and grid 
stability (International Energy Agency, 2022a). Companies are then rewarded directly for 
providing these adjustments by the grid operators. The time-based or implicit approach tries to 
minimise the own electricity costs by adapting processes and production (International Energy 
Agency, 2022a; Khan, 2019). Shifting the load into less costly times will consequently reduce 
the total costs (Khan, 2019; Stanelyte, Radziukyniene and Radziukynas, 2022). The focus of 
this thesis lies on time-based, rather than incentive-based demand response techniques. 
According to de Christo, Perron et al (2019), there are five main methods of load management:  
• Peak Shaving/Clipping 
Peak Shaving is the practice of reducing the demand during peak demand times to lower 
the maxima. It is helpful if costs are particularly high during these peak hours. (Christo et 
al., 2019; Johra et al., 2019)  
• Valley Filling 
In this technique, the lower demand hours are “filled up”, which means that the demand is 
increased during the off-peak times. The main goal is to take advantage of low prices in the 
low-demand times. (Christo et al., 2019; Johra et al., 2019)  
• Load Shifting 
Through load shifting, the curve is adjusted, and demand is relocated into other periods. 
(Christo et al., 2019; Johra et al., 2019) 
• Load Building 
Load building works similar to valley filling in a smart grid context. (Christo et al., 2019) 
• Flexible Load  
The concept describes an adjustable demand that can be adapted on the basis of incentives. 
(Christo et al., 2019)

4. Methodology 
4.1 Literature Review 
Based on all current developments within the energy sector, it can be observed that the 
importance of renewable energy production is becoming increasingly higher (International 
Energy Agency, 2021). Not only does it lead to independence from fossil fuels and therefore 
political interests, but it is also cheap in production due to the lack of required resources 
(Masterson, 2021). While some of its forms can generate power relatively steady, like hydro 
power plants, others are subject to larger fluctuations and higher variability. This applies 
especially to solar power, which is dependent on sunlight, and wind energy, both on- and off-
shore (Brunner et al., 2020; Zhu, 2019). It brings new challenges and raises the question how 
the market reacts to these fluctuations and which effect these have on electricity prices. Being 
able to link the renewable energy production to the electricity price can provide organisations 
with useful information on when to buy and use power to decrease costs, as well as emissions 
within the energy mix (Maciejowska, 2020). Therefore, one goal of this thesis is to set up a 
forecast model which uses renewable energy generation as input.  
Electricity price forecasting has been well established in the scientific discussion (Ziel and 
Steinert, 2018). Different approaches have been used to analyse the market and predict these

also differ heavily in their scope and complexity (Weron, 2014).  
To identify the right approach for this thesis, the objectives and required characteristics were 
examined. Additionally, existing literature was used as a basis to discover advantages and 
disadvantages of specific models. The goal for the desired model is threefold: Firstly, it should 
be able to reliably predict electricity prices with a reasonable accuracy. The forecasting horizon 
is short-term. The forecast is supposed to work as a pragmatic tool for industrial organisations.   
It should be precise enough to make organisational decisions based on it, e.g., for load 
management purposes but low in complexity to decrease organisational hurdles in the 
implementation. Secondly, it should be able to link renewable energy production to the 
electricity price. The goal is therefore to measure the impact RES have on the price 
development. Additionally, other external factors and their influence should be covered to 
describe other exogenous effects.

specific approaches with hybrid models also being possible.

can lead to better results but increase time but work effort significantly. Other newly 
constructed methods rely heavily on machine learning principles, like the Kernel-based

4.2 Multiple Linear Regression 
The linear regression is a statistical model that aims to explain the behaviour of a response 
variable (Brown, 2014; Stoetzer, 2020). It is therefore a suitable model for predictions and 
forecasting of future developments of the respective values (Rubinfeld, 2011). It also shows a 
linear relationship between the independent variables and the dependent response variable 
(Brown, 2014; Rubinfeld, 2011).

The usual sequence is to first identify the desired response variable. Afterwards, the response 
variable is chosen which is relevant to the research question. Lastly, other independent 
variables are added to improve the model’s fit and prediction ability (Rubinfeld, 2011). 
Contrary to the simple linear regression, the Multiple linear regression tries to show the 
dependency of the response variable to several predictor variables. The idea of the MLR is, 
therefore, to add independent variables to explain parts of the response variable that have not 
yet been explained (Stoetzer, 2020).  
Apart from having more than one independent variables, a multiple regression model has 
several other requirements that must be met in order to achieve the best possible fit and 
reliability (Stoetzer, 2020; Urban and Mayerl, 2008): 
1. Linearity  
The linearity assumption describes that an MLR requires linear relationships between the 
predictor and response variables. Therefore, if other relationships, e.g. quadratic, are visible, 
the regression must be adapted or the variable cannot be used.  
2. Homoscedasticity 
The assumption states that residuals should be equally distributed. If this assumption is correct 
and no dependence of the residuals on the variables is detected, it is called homoscedasticity. 
Otherwise, heteroscedasticity will lead to less reliable results for the affected parts of the 
regression. The results are, however, still valid. 
3. Autocorrelation  
The term describes a trend in the residuals, meaning that either positive residuals mostly follow 
positive residuals (positive autocorrelation) or positive and negative residuals alternate 
(negative autocorrelation). It is a problem which often occurs in time-series models.  
4. Normally distributed residuals  
The residuals should also be normally distributed. It is based on the fact that the error term 
should display the sum of random external influences which are independent of each other. 
Non-normally distributed residuals can lead to distorted results when looking at smaller 
samples of the data. The mean of the residuals should also be close to zero.

5. Multicollinearity 
This assumption states that the independent variables should not be correlated with each other. 
Perfect multicollinearity means that two or more predictor variables have perfect correlation 
and therefore their individual influences on the response variable cannot be analysed.  
6. Outliers 
Outliers are a problem for a linear regression because they have a high influence on the overall 
model. This means that outliers can “falsify” the results. Especially in smaller samples the 
sensitivity to outliers is usually high. If detected, outliers need to be further evaluated.   
An additional assumption is that no important predictor is missing. If the regression model is 
lacking relevant independent variables, the results and relationships between the other 
predictors and the response variable can be distorted. Again, the results are still valid if a 
relevant variable is missing but should be analysed carefully (Urban and Mayerl, 2008).  
The regression model’s fit can be determined by the R2 and adjusted R2 values. It measures the 
linear relationship and how much of the data can be explained through the respective regression 
model (Stoetzer, 2020). 
4.3 Data basis  
For the regression model, the data sample needs to be chosen, as well as the dependent and 
independent variables. The goals of the study – price forecasting and impact of the predictors 
– always need to be considered in this process. Additionally, the analysed samples also need to 
be selected. The model is set up initially with real-life data from April 2022 until the end of 
September 2022. As a result, it deliberately includes the effects of the Covid pandemic, as well 
as the war in Ukraine. Another sample of the same months in 2018 is also modelled to show 
the results of these exogen factors as well as current trends and developments and differences 
in the variables’ coefficients. For transparency it needs to be stated that shortly before the 
pandemic, the market regions were adjusted. While it currently only contains Germany and 
Luxemburg, it previously also included Austria (Bundesnetzagentur, 2018). As a result, the 
absolute numbers from 2018 are often higher. Hence, comparisons between relative values are 
often more meaningful and informative than absolute numbers.

4.3.1 Response Variables  
Electricity Price  
While the first response variable seems to be clear, it needs to be further defined. Since the 
electricity price is to be forecasted, it must be the response variable. However, there are 
different prices for electricity on different markets (Ellenrieder, 2022). For this model, the day-
ahead prices on the spot market are chosen. This has two major reasons: Firstly, the day-ahead 
prices react faster and more strongly to changes in energy production or other developments in 
the surrounding. Long-term prices on the futures market are steadier and therefore do not show 
adjustments as well (Ellenrieder, 2022). Secondly, the day-ahead electricity market gives 
companies and other organisations the possibility to react to changes in the energy mix and 
price developments (Würzburg, Labandeira and Linares, 2013). Contracts on the futures 
market are often long-term and not suitable as a reaction mechanism to short-term 
development, while intraday electricity trading only contains a smaller volume and is therefore 
not as impactful (Würzburg, Labandeira and Linares, 2013). At the day-ahead auction 
electricity for every hour of the following day is traded. The volumes and prices are determined 
at noon, meaning that all bids from suppliers and buyers must be submitted by then 
(Ellenrieder, 2022). Based on these market mechanisms, the response variable is a time-series 
variable with an hourly scale.  
Price-Emission-Index  
In the latter part of the regression, another response variable is introduced for a second 
forecasting model. The main idea is to develop and introduce a new metric which includes the 
electricity price, as well as the carbon emission from the energy mix at the respective time. It 
therefore uses the forecasted price (the first response variable) as a basis and adds a monetary 
carbon price factor. It should be viewed as a decision-making index that not only includes cost 
optimisation but also the second target level of emission reduction. The exact formula and other 
details will be displayed in section 5.5.

Based on these studies, several essential predictor variables have been identified:  
• Total load  
• Renewable energy production (especially wind and solar based generation)  
• Fossil fuel prices (natural gas price) 
• Trade/Net exports 
These will be the independent variables of the regression model. The carbon price within the 
EU and the price for coal would also have been included, however no reliable datasets could 
be found. Implementing these additional variables could improve the model further. All used 
variables are metric time-series data. They are either forecasted/predicted values (total load, 
solar production, wind production, and net exports) or lagged data from the day before (gas 
price). Additionally, dummy variables are utilized to represent seasonality and other time-
series-related effects.  
Renewable energy generation  
The predictor variable which is of most importance for this thesis is the production of 
renewable energy. It is essential to answer the research question. Especially the generation of 
wind- and solar-powered energy is subject of high fluctuations. In this context, renewable 
energy therefore only refers to these two production sources. Since renewable energy has very 
low marginal costs, it is typically added first in the merit order (Graebig et al., 2020). Based 
on previous research, it can be suggested that these have a negative impact on the electricity 
price (Cludius et al., 2014; Würzburg, Labandeira and Linares, 2013). However, this 
relationship should be proven and updated in this model. The generation of wind energy and 
solar energy are consequently chosen as independent variables. The production can be either 
used as absolute volumes or as a share of the total electricity production. Both approaches have

been used and analysed. The data is taken from the Bundesnetzagentur which is monitoring all 
energy production within the German market, as well as trading prices (Bundesnetzagentur, 
2023c).  
Total energy production load  
Another important factor is the total electricity production in the market. Through the merit 
order mechanism this is highly dependent on the demand of electricity in Germany. It is also 
influenced by the trade with other markets inside the EU. Since the highest still needed power 
plant is determining the overall market clearing price, a higher demand will typically lead to 
higher prices (Würzburg, Labandeira and Linares, 2013). Since it is, however, also dependent 
on the energy mix, it needs further analysis. The production load is measured in MWh. The 
data source is again the Bundesnetzagentur (2023c).  
Net exports  
As mentioned above, the total demand does not automatically equal the production load. This 
is due to the fact, that inside the European Energy Market, electricity gets traded between 
countries and markets (Federal Ministry for Economic Affairs and Climate Action, 2019). If a 
country’s exports exceed the imports, the variable is positive. It is also taken from the 
Bundesnetzagentur (2023c).  
Natural gas price  
The natural gas price is a highly influential variable within the energy market. Since the Russian 
invasion of Ukraine, it plays an even more important role since it is the basis for one of the 
most expensive power plants (Alvarez and Molnar, 2021; Eichhammer and Scheuer, 2022). 
Especially in 2022 gas plants often determine the market clearing price and therefore are a 
major driver of the electricity price (Eichhammer and Scheuer, 2022). The difference between 
2018 and 2022 is of special interest and can be used as a basis for industrial, as well as political 
decisions. Due to lack of reliable datasets, the natural gas price is the only variable in this model 
that is only available on a daily, not hourly, basis (Bundesnetzagentur, 2023a). 
Dummy variables   
To reduce seasonal factors and other errors in the model, three different dummy variables are 
integrated. Firstly, a monthly variable is set up to decrease the seasonal influence. Secondly, 
the weekdays are modelled to display the influences within the week and especially for the 
weekends. Lastly, an hourly variable is implemented to show variations within the days and 
between night and day.

5.3 Influence of renewable energy generation on day-ahead prices  
The analysis clearly shows the influence of renewable energy generation on the electricity 
price. As seen in model 1.1, 1.2, and 1.3, both solar energy and wind energy production have 
a negative correlation with the day-ahead price. This means that an increase in the production 
of these renewable energy sources leads to a decrease in prices. Interesting observations can 
also be made regarding the comparison of both renewable energy sources. The coefficient 
levels are very similar, meaning that they influence the electricity price almost equally. This is 
in line with results of previous studies (Würzburg, Labandeira and Linares, 2013). A 
production increase of 1 MWh by RES leads to a price decrease of 0,01 €/MWh or 10€ per 
produced GWh. The absolute impact of solar energy slightly exceeds the wind energy’s as long 
as the total load is also included in the model. One possible explanation is that high photovoltaic 
production usually occurs during peak load times when prices are typically higher. The wind 
energy share, however, has a bigger impact than the solar energy share. Hence, increasing the 
share of wind energy leads to a higher price deduction. It is, however, clear that both not only 
have a positive effect on carbon reduction and therefore the fight against global warming, but 
also on energy costs for industrial consumers, as well as individuals. This conclusion is 
intuitive because of the merit order effect. Since renewable energy has low marginal costs, it 
is prioritised in the pricing mechanism and therefore leads to a lower market clearing price. 
However, the exact numerical impact is of value for political and industrial decision-making.

5.5 Price-Emission-Index 
While the electricity price forecast of the regression model is working and can be used for 
organisational decisions, it is a monetary-only value. In times with a growing need for 
sustainability and emission reduction to tackle climate change (Whelan and Kronthal-Sacco, 
2019), however, this might not be satisfying for everyone. One solution here could be an index 
that takes the electricity costs, as well as carbon emissions into account. In the European Union, 
the electricity price is already influenced by carbon emissions through the so-called Emissions 
Trading System (ETS) (European Commission, 2023b). The international system makes sure 
that companies that emit CO2 have to pay a certain amount of money for every tonne. The 
certificates can be bought through the energy exchange market and are therefore volatile in 
their value (European Commission, 2023b). In the energy market this affects mostly fossil fuel 
power plants since these are the main producers of carbon emissions. Renewable energy 
sources and nuclear energy on the other hand can be viewed as carbon neutral energy 
production (Erbach, 2016). Therefore, the electricity prices for fossil fuel plants are already 
increased through the ETS. This is a mandatory price everyone is obliged to pay. However, the 
stakeholders’ pressure for sustainable operations is increasing for many companies (Wang, Li 
and Qi, 2020). Customers and businesses demand greener products and services and increased 
efforts (Close, 2021). For organisations that want to reduce their carbon footprint further, a 
numerical basis for operation research decisions is set up in the following.  
The main idea is to use the electricity price as a basis for the index. While the emissions should 
play a role, the costs are still the main factor for companies. Since it is supposed to be used as 
a decision basis, a forecasted price is taken. In this case, the forecast from the previously set

advantages for the organisation: Firstly, the ability to calculate the own electricity costs and 
subsequently make educated economic decisions, e.g. about investments. Secondly, based on 
these, adjustments can be made, and load management techniques can be used to optimise the 
consumption and procurement. This is not necessarily a new finding, but an updated model 
provides new values for the current market situation. As seen in chapter 5.3, the coefficients 
differ from 2018 to 2022 and need regular adjustments.  
Also, the relationship between volatile renewable energy generation and the electricity price 
becomes clear. The results show that both solar and wind energy have a negative influence on 
the price development. The same can be said for their shares of the entire load. Practically, this 
means forecasts for solar and wind energy production can be used to model electricity price 
curves. Additionally, it becomes clear that an increase in renewable energy generation benefits 
both cost and emission reduction.  
The main takeaway for companies is, however, the Price-Emission-Index. It creates a new basis 
for load management and demand response strategies which takes the duality of goals into 
account. It is a unique tool to optimise processes with the objective to reduce costs but also 
including the carbon emissions from the energy mix. Especially in times of growing demand 
for sustainable products and sustainable supply chains, the pressure of reducing carbon 
emissions is high (Wang, Li and Qi, 2020). Through the index costs can be kept low while 
simultaneously decreasing the carbon footprint. The production itself can then be considered 
more environmentally friendly. This leads to better marketing possibilities which are based on 
facts and calculations. Ideally, it is combined with not only load management but additional 
measures like own electricity production through solar panels, as well as energy storage. This 
ensures flexibility in times of high costs and high emissions. Therefore, peak shaving 
techniques can be applied without shifting the load to other times but instead use self-produced 
electricity which is emission-free and cheaper. It does, however, require initial investment and 
is not always feasible. Cost-benefit calculations based on the PEI must be carried out before 
such investments.  
Overall, this thesis gives companies pragmatic tools for their load management proceedings. 
Either cost-optimising decision can be derived on the basis of the electricity price forecast or

cost and emission-decreasing optimisations can be run through the PEI. The relatively low 
complexity allows for an easy integration into existing infrastructure and processes.  
6.1.2 Theoretical Implications  
On a scientific level, the thesis provides an update of previous electricity price forecasts in a 
time with highly volatile and quickly changing surrounding factors. The comparison between 
2022 and 2018 highlights the differences and developments in the German electricity market. 
Especially in the light of several external influences – namely the Covid pandemic and the war 
in Ukraine – these newly tested values provide insight into a dynamic market. The Price-
Emission-Index also provides an additional measurement which can be further analysed and 
studied. While there are several studies about load management processes, there is a lack for a 
sustainable basis. Its effects on the electricity market could also be observed, if applied in an 
industrial context. 
6.1.3 Political Implications  
The model clearly shows the negative relationship between renewable energy generation and 
the electricity price. Therefore, a clear signal for political decision-makers to invest in and 
subsidise these technologies can be derived. Not only does it help governments to reach their 
climate goals (German Federal Government, 2022a), but it can ease the financial burden for 
the industry and individual households that are suffering from higher prices. Especially in 
Germany, where renewable sources still account for a relatively small part of the overall 
required production compared to other European countries, like Denmark, Norway, or Portugal 
(Statista, 2022), it is essential to accelerate the expansion of solar and wind energy. The 
(delayed) complete shutdown of all nuclear power plants in 2023 only increases the need for 
more sustainable energy generation to reduce the dependency on expensive and carbon-
intensive fossil fuels (Connolly, 2022). As a result, the expansion of renewable energy 
generation can not only benefit industrial organisations in two ways but also entire economies 
and nations (Bhuiyan et al., 2022).

6.2 Comparison to other markets  
The findings only relate the German electricity market. It might therefore make sense to 
compare them to other countries. Since Germany wants to decrease carbon emission further 
(German Federal Government, 2022a), markets with high renewable energy shares are of 
special interest. Denmark’s share of renewable energy sources in the overall power generation 
was at over 60% in 2021 (Statista, 2022). Based on the results of the regression model, a 
significantly lower electricity price than in Germany is to be expected. However, the price in 
2022 shows a similar level and development to the German market with a large increase in the 
late summer (Statista, 2023).  
 
It shows that the results of the analysis cannot automatically be used in other European 
electricity markets. While renewable energy has a decreasing impact in Denmark too 
(Rintamäki, Siddiqui and Salo, 2017) and the share is significantly higher, the price levels are 
almost identical. This highlights the need for further inter-market analysis to identify the 
differences and discover the underlying reasons for this phenomenon. Especially discrepancies 
within the European electricity market are interesting for subsequent studies.

6.3 Limitations & Outlook 
The study has limitations that need to be addressed to ensure an appropriate analysis. Firstly, 
the forecasting model’s accuracy can be improved. The inclusion of more predictor variables 
could increase the regression fit and therefore the precision of predicting prices. Namely, the 
carbon emission certificate price and the coal price are variables that should be implemented 
to check their significance. While not automatically improving the model, they should be 
analysed regarding their influence. They were not included in this study due to the lack of 
reliable data. Additionally, a combination of different methodologies, like used in several state-
of-the-art studies could be beneficial for the precision of predicting prices and therefore make 
the model more reliable in its results.  
Secondly, the practical usefulness of the PEI should be further analysed. It is introduced as a 
theoretical concept without validation from the industry. Hence, it is not sure whether the index 
has a use case in a practical environment. A next step would be to discuss this with industrial 
representatives and implement potential changes or adjustments. While it is supposed to be a 
pragmatic tool for decision-making, organisational hurdles might limit its use cases. A case 
study which observes the implementation and consequences of the index in an industrial setting 
could therefore be of interest.  
 
6.4 Conclusion  
 
Overall, this thesis explains the electricity market with its relevant mechanisms. Through the 
regression model, a relevant negative relationship between renewable energy generation and 
the electricity price can be concluded. In contrast, the gas price and total load have a positive 
effect on the price development. It is also noticeable that the market has become significantly 
more volatile with higher price spikes and also higher coefficients of the independent variables.  
A forecast is possible with these predictor variables along with seasonal and timely factors. 
The forecasting model certainly has potential for improvement but does capture the overall 
price development with a reasonable accuracy. 
 The developed Price-Emission-Index is a basis for decision-making that not only includes the 
electricity price but also the carbon emissions from the energy mix to optimise the costs as well 
as the carbon footprint. It can be used for load shifting purposes or other organisational and

they are producing through a less carbon-intensive energy mix.  
The thesis also has impliucations for policy-makers. The proven negative effect of renewable 
energy sources provides a reason to further invest in their expansion. Both cost reduction and 
emission reduction can be achieved by it.

F. Learning Objectives  
In the process of conducting this thesis almost all previously set objectives were reached. 
Firstly, the electricity market was covered in depth. The relevant mechanisms, as well as 
characteristics were explained, and current development displayed. These also relate closely to 
newly arising challenges that the market has to face, namely high prices and the growing 
demand for sustainability.  
To address these challenges, a literature review revealed potential approaches to tackle both 
economic issues, as well as sustainability concerns. Demand response strategies were identified 
as a tool to help companies in reaching both goals. Therefore, state-of-the-art scientific studies 
was identified and analysed. 
Subsequently, a regression analysis was chosen after careful consideration as a tool to create 
new quantitative findings in the field. By examining the impact of external factors on the 
electricity price, a contribution to the field of energy market research has been made. Especially 
the influence of renewable energy generation was inspected in detail and several conclusions 
could be drawn.  
Additionally, the findings have a practical application, too. The regression model is able to 
forecast the electricity price and therefore provide a tool for industrial decision-making. The 
transfer of the results from theory to practical context is obvious. However, an additional 
measurement was introduced to include carbon emissions from the energy mix into the 
decision-making process. The Price-Emission-Index is a newly innovated tool that allows for 
both sustainable and cost-efficient choices. Due to a heavier focus on the theoretical model, the 
actual usefulness for organisations could not be investigated.  
Lastly, the basis was established for further research. Either through the application of different 
methodological approaches in conducting the forecasting model or a more practical-focused 
investigation of the PEI, new insights can be gained. Also, comparing to results to other 
markets, either within the European Union or in other regions, might prove to be interesting 
and of value.
