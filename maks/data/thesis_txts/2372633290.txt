Summary
Often in structural optimization the bottleneck of efficiency is the repeated solution of
the linear system, which arises from the discretized PDE constraint. In this thesis we
investigate how the computational effort in Topology Optimization can be reduced,
by performing the optimization using an approximation of the solution to the linear
system.
We demonstrate that you can obtain accurate results from the optimization when
using an approximated solution of the analysis equations, generated by an early ter-
mination of an iterative solver. By implementing an approximation of the dual norm
of the residual, we show that the residual is dominated by the discretization error
after only a few iterations of the iterative solver.
To efficiently decrease this discretization error, we devise a strategy for adaptively
refining the mesh during optimization. Using this strategy we are able to reduce the
error per degrees of freedoms, but it still dominates the residual after few iterations
of the iterative solver.
Finally we implement a strategy for approximating the solution of the linear sys-
tem, by introducing an alternative stopping criterion to the iterative solver, based on
the dual norm of the residual. Using this strategy we demonstrate that it is possi-
ble save 99% of the iterations necessary to reach convergence of the iterative solver,
while still getting accurate results from the optimization. However the method we
propose requires inverting a large mass matrix in each iteration, still it indicates that
it is possible to reduce the computational effort significantly if one has an efficient
method to compute the dual norm of the residual.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
An important question in mechanics and engineering is finding structures that achieves
optimal performance with respect to certain criteria. Structural optimization is there-
fore a very active field of research. Topology optimization deals with the problem of
distributing a given amount of material inside a design domain in order to achieve
optimal performance and it is a popular method in the field of structural optimization.
It is especially useful in initial stages of a design process, since the design produced
with topology optimization can attain any shape in the given design domain. A classic
example is to distribute a fixed amount of material in order to minimize the compli-
ance of the design, corresponding to maximizing the stiffness, subject to a given set
of loads and boundary conditions.
The objective function in structural optimization often depends on the solution to
an underlying PDE constraint, which is also the case when minimizing the compliance.
In topology optimization the optimization is often performed on the design variables
only, treating the solution of the discretized PDE as a function call which requires
solving the large linear system.
Ku = f.
(1.1)
This approach is called the nested approach and in such an approach solving the
linear system is the bottleneck of efficiency, especially when dealing with large scale
problems, such that problems in 3d, which are of great interest in practical applica-
tions.
Recent studies have shown that one can decrease the computational effort, by
using an approximation to the solution of (1.1) during the optimization, while still
obtaining accurate results from the optimization. Common in these studies is that
the approximation is obtained by early termination of an iterative solver applied to
(1.1).
The authors of [ASS10] propose an alternative stopping criteria to the Precondi-
tioned Conjugate Gradient method (PCG) used for solution of (1.1). They terminate
the iterative procedure when the relative change in the object function is less that a
certain tolerance, possible before one has reached convergence in the sense that the
norm of the residual is small.
In [AAL14] a multigrid-CG method coupled with an alternative stopping criterion
is investigated. Here they relate the iterative method to the progress of the optimiza-
tion process, by terminating the iterative method when the residual is smaller that
the error in the Karush–Kuhn–Tucker (KKT) conditions.

Finally in the brief note [AS11] the authors consider using a fixed maximum num-
ber of PCG iterations in the optimization process, for example at most min{nx, ny, nz}
iterations, with nx the discretization size in the x-direction etc. They find that the
method still yields accurate results while the computational effort is reduced signifi-
cantly. It is even indicated that the arbitrary number of PCG iterations chosen here
could have been even smaller.
The aim in this project is to investigate how an approximate approach can be
used to improve the efficiency in topology optimization and try to devise a strategy
for such an approach.
The structure of the report is as follows.
In Chapter 2 we will introduce the
mathematical model, going through the problem definition and parametrization, first
order necessary optimality conditions and finally the optimization algorithm that we
will use. In Chapter 3 we will present the discretization of the problem and elaborate
on implementation aspects. In Chapter 4 we will replicate some of the results from
[AS11] and investigate the behavior of different measures of the residual, to asses the
quality of the approximations we use during the optimization. In Chapter 5 we will
develop an Adaptive method for topology optimization, to decrease the discretization
error. Finally in Chapter 6 we will devise a strategy for an approximate approach in
topology optimization based on the findings in the previous chapters.
We will in the beginning of each chapter present the relevant theory and the
numerical method we use, before we present the results of our numerical experiments.
The implementation is written Python.
The optimization algorithms is imple-
mented from scratch using the FEM library FEniCS [LMW12] to handle the FEM
analysis. The full code implementing the strategy from Chapter 6 can be seen in
Appendix B.1. A snippet of the code from Chapter 5 can be seen in B.2.

2.4
Filtering
When considering material distribution methods, as for instant the SIMP interpola-
tion scheme, two complications arise.
First of all the problem is mesh dependent. This means that if we refine the mesh
we get a qualitatively new design rather than a refinement of the design. In fact
the original problem (2.2) lacks existence of solutions [BS03, Chapter 1.3]. It can be
shown that by introducing smaller and smaller holes to the structure while keeping the
volume fixed, one can find a sequence of designs with decreasing compliance. From a
material point of view we have in the limit introduced micro structures that are not
isotropic, hence the limit is not a feasible design.
The second complication is the occurrence of the so called checkerboard pattern.
These patterns are regions with alternating solid and void elements in a checkerboard
fashion when using certain elements of low order. These patterns occur due to an
artificial stiffness of such designs coming from numerical errors when using the Finite
Element Method for discretization.
To give an example of these complications we here show an example of the final
design, generated using the 88-line Matlab from [And+11]. The example is shown
in Figure 2.1. We can here see the effects of both complications. First of all the
checkerboard patterns are clearly visible and also there are qualitative differences
when refining from a 45x15 to a 90x30 grid, which indicates mesh dependence. When
using triangular elements we get patterns similar to the checkerboard patterns as can
be seen in Figure 2.2 generated using our own code.
Many strategies to solve these problems have been investigated in the literature.
One of the strategies is perimeter control [BS03] where one puts a constraint on the
perimeter of the design. The perimeter is given by the sum of the lengths of all inner
and outer boundaries and a constraint on this will make a solution with to many
holes infeasible. Another strategy is the introduction of a minimum length scale to
the problem, for example as in [KY00]. One of the most popular strategies is the
density filtering method where one only consider densities that have been filtered

We saw in Chapters 4 and 5 that after less than 10 PCG iterations, the residual is

6.3
Discussion
As seen in the previous section we can save many PCG iterations, by early termination
when the change in |||R||| h
2 is small. Infact for the examples we have considered we
saved 99% or more of the PCG iterations necessary to meet a classic stopping criterion.
But the method presented here is quite expensive both memory and time wise. We
have to calculate the solution with the mass matrix MH1 in each PCG iteration and
since we need a refined mesh when calculating the dual norm we have MH1 ∈R4n×4n.
The matrix MH1 is not changes during the optimization, so the factorization can
be reused during the optimization. But in practice iterative methods like PCG is
interesting when a direct factorization is infeasible, due to the size of the problem.
This would make saving factorization MH1 even more infeasible.
The PCG algorithm with the classic stopping criterion, seen in Algorithm 3, re-
quires one matrix-vector multiplication and on solution of the preconditioner K0 ∈
Rn×n which can be factorized beforehand.
But if one could get the residual a more efficient manner, then the method pre-
sented here could be very effective. There are several ways one could do that.

Firstly one could estimate the residual differently. There has been done a lot of
work in the field of a posteriori error estimation for FEM, see for instant [AO97].
Some of the error estimators presented is gradient recovery methods, the element
residual method and the equilibrated residual method, to list a few. Gradient recovery
estimators is as the name suggest based of recovering the gradient of the solution uh.
For FEM with piecewise linear functions an example of such an estimator is the
Babuska and Rheinboldt estimator. Here one computes a continuous approximation
to the gradient of uh and estimate the error by the computing difference between this
approximation and, the piecewise constant, gradient of uh. I tested the use of such
an estimator during this project and I found that the estimator performed well when
considering an exact, to machine precision, solution to the equation KEu = f. But
since the error estimator only use information from the solution uh, and not from the
actual problem, it did not provide good estimates during the PCG algorithm.
The element residual and equilibrated residual methods are more similar to the
approach used here. The idea is to use that the error e satisfy
aE(e, v) = aE(uh, v) −ℓ(v)
∀v ∈V.
But instead of solving a problem like this on a fine mesh, ad we have done here,
you split the problem into a sequence of subproblems which can be solved effectively
and possibly in parallel. The idea is to consider the local problems on each element.
But inorder to solve these subproblems one has to supplement the local problems
with boundary conditions, which is the main difficulty of this type of estimators, but
severals strategies for doing this is reviewed in [AO97]. I did not have time to test
these estimators in this project.
Another way to get the residual efficiently is to use discretization schemes where
the residual is easily obtained. One example of such a method is the Discontinuous
Petrov-Galerkin method [BMS02].
In this method one solves for the Riesz repre-
sentation of the residual simultaneously when calculating the approximation of the
approximated solution to the PDE. Also other Least-Squares Finite Element methods
exists [BG06] where one can get the residual efficiently.

CHAPTER 7
Conclusion
7.1
Conclusion
We have in this project demonstrated how an approximate approach for treating the
analysis equation can reduce the computational effort in topology optimization, by us-
ing an approximation found by an early termination of the Preconditioned Conjugate
Gradient method. We found that, when minimizing the compliance, accurate results
from the optimization can be achieved when performing as few as 3 PCG iterations
to obtain an approximation of the solution, to use in the optimization algorithm, in
accordance with the results in [AS11]. Investigation of the residual yielded that the
dual norm, approximated on a fine space to capture some of the discretization error,
was after a few PCG iterations dominated by the discretization error.
To decrease this discretization error we investigated an adaptive strategy for topol-
ogy optimization. We saw that it was possible to decrease the error per degrees of
freedoms, when using the adaptive strategy. But even though the discretization error
was decreased, it still dominated the residual after less that 10 PCG iterations.
Finally we devised a strategy for an approximate approach in topology optimiza-
tion. By introducing an alternative stopping criteria to the PCG algorithm, that
terminates the algorithm when the discretization error dominates the residual, we
saved 99% of the PCG iterations and still got accurate results from the optimization.
The method was tested for both minimum compliance examples and a more compli-
cated compliant mechanism example. The method requires computation of the dual
norm of the residual during the PCG algorithm and are therefore not very efficient.
But the result indicates that if one has an efficient method of finding the residual, for
example by using least-square FEM methodologies, where the residual are obtained
easily, then the computational effort in topology optimization might be decreased
significantly.
7.2
Further Research
This work have raised some new questions to consider. Why is the relative dual norm
of the residual in some cases larger than 0.5? And are the final designs useful when
the residual is so large?
The estimation of the residual we have used here requires solving a large linear
==PAGE:68==

system so it is not very efficient. So it would be very interesting to investigate whether
we could use a more efficient error estimator, so that we reduce the computational
effort further. It could also be worthwhile to investigate whether the same approach
can be used together with a least-square type of FEM method where the residual can
be obtained without extra computational effort.
The numerical examples presented here have all been computed on a single laptop,
which is why we have not considered large scale problems. But it would indeed be
interesting to see how the stopping criteria from Chapter 6 would perform on large
scale problems. This could be 2d problems with high resolution or 3d problems.
We have here only worked with examples coming from linear elasticity, but it
would also be interesting to investigate whether the approximate approach carries
over to other areas of application, for instant heat transfer or flow problems.
Finally we have used the factorized stiffness matrix for a solid design as a precon-
ditioner. This requires storing the LU factorization, so one looses the advantage that
PCG is memory efficient. An interesting question to investigate is whether we can
use the same method, with a more memory efficient preconditioner.
