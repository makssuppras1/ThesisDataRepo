Abstract
The Aspergillus niger genome encodes ﬁve pectin lyase genes (AnPL1, AnPL2, AnPL3,
AnPL4 and AnPL5).
In this study the kinetic properties of these pectin lyases were
investigated to postulate why A. niger express multiple genes encoding the same enzyme
activity.
Three of the A. niger pectin lyases (AnPL1, AnPL2, and AnPL3) were
successfully characterized, along with a homologous pectin lyase from Aspergillus aculeatus
(AaPL1). These did all belong to the same phylogenetic cluster. AnPL4 and AnPL5
did, on the hand, not cluster with any of pectin lyases characterized in this study. An
assessment of the three-dimensional structure, estimated by homology modelling, revealed
that the substrate binding cleft of AnPL4 and AnPL5 had a more open conformation.
Among the A. niger pectin lyases AnPL1 was the most thermostable, having t½ values of
333±84 and 14±1 min at 50 and 60 ◦C, respectively. Response surface methodology was
used to predict the combined temperature and pH optima. This revealed that predicted
the pH optimum of the enzymes was lower than previously reported optima. The predicted
optima were pH 4.9 for AnPL1 and AnPL2, and pH 6.8 for AnPL3, all measured on
69 ± 3.1% DM Apple pectin. The decrease in pH optima was a consequence of decreased
thermostability due to the optima being measured at higher temperatures, compared to
the previous studies. Additionally, it was observed that the activity of AaPL1 was heavily
dependent on the presence of the four tested cations; Ca2+, Mg2+, Mn2+, and Na+. The
activity of AnPL1 and AnPL3 were only found to be slightly activated by the two cations;
Mg2+, Mn2+. Based on observed diﬀerences in the kinetic properties of the pectin lyases,
it was postulated, that the multiple pectin lyases in the A. niger genome has been evolved
to aid the fungus with a more robust pectinolytic enzyme arsenal.

Introduction 1
Pectinases are enzymes which are capable of catalyzing the degradation and modiﬁcation
of pectin.
Pectin is a highly diverse bio-polymer,
which consists of a linear
homogalacturonan (HG), backbone, as well two branched rhamnogalacturonan (RG)
regions RGI and RGII. Pectin is a structural bio-polymer found within the cell walls
of plant cells. Thus, pectinases a have a broad range of applications within industries
dealing with the processing of fruit and vegetables. Here pectinases may be used during
the extraction and clariﬁcation of fruit juices (e.g. apple juice). Other applications of
pectinases include the lowering of the viscosity of concentrated fruit purees, such as papaya
and mango puree. Pectinases can, furthermore, be applied in bioreﬁneries, where they
are used in the liquefaction and sacchariﬁcation of biomass [Kashyap et al., 2001].
Enzymes are bio catalytic proteins and hence, their catalytic capacity is dependent on
the conformation of the molecule.
The active conformation is found within a certain
temperature and pH range, which means that the catalytic capacity of the enzyme is
dependent on speciﬁc conditions. It is, therefore, important to characterize the kinetic
properties of a speciﬁc enzyme, to ensure that these ﬁt the conditions of the industrial
application, in which they are intended [Cherry and Fidantsef, 2003].
Enzymes are
moreover very speciﬁc, and are only capable of catalyzing one type of reaction. Hence,
multiple types of enzymes are often required to eﬀectively degrade a biopolymer, such as
pectin, into smaller fractions [Benoit et al., 2012].
In nature, microorganisms such as ﬁlamentous fungi, are specialized in secreting a broad
spectrum of diﬀerent enzymes in order to eﬀectively degrade and utilize the surrounding
biomaterials. Additionally, it has been shown that microorganisms may contain multiple
genes, encoding enzymes with the same activity [Pel et al., 2007]. Characterization of
the individual genes can be used to eﬀectively select enzymes for a commercial enzymatic
cocktail and to optimize the degradation of a substrate under a speciﬁc set of reaction
conditions [Benoit et al., 2015]. Moreover, the kinetic properties of similar enzymes may
provide valuable information, which can be used when engineering an existing enzyme via
rational design, to improve its kinetic properties [Lutz, 2010].
The ﬁlamentous fungi Aspergillus niger expresses ﬁve diﬀerent pectin lyases. Hence, the
aim of this study was to investigate the kinetically properties of these ﬁve pectin lyases.
The kinetic characterization will then provide the basis of a postulation on why, A. niger
encodes multiple genes for isoenzymes with the same activity. It was hypothesized, that
the multiple gene copies of the same isoenzyme is a consequence of evolution and a given

1.1
Pectin
Pectin is a hetero-polysaccharide which is present in the cell wall of plants. It is believed
to be among the most complex macromolecules in nature, since it may consist of up to
17 diﬀerent monosaccharides and more than 20 diﬀerent linkages [Voragen et al., 2009].
Pectin is composed of three major structural domains, namely HG and RGI and RGII,
which are linked together via covalent bonds. A schematic representation of pectin is
shown in ﬁgure 1.1.

1.1.1

HG is the most abundant structural domain within the pectin molecule. It is comprised
by galacturonic acid (GalA) moieties, which are linearly linked by α-(1-4) bonds. The
GalA moieties may, furthermore, be methyl esteriﬁed on C-6 and/or acetylated on O-2
or O-3 [Voragen et al., 2009]. A schematic illustration of the HG backbone is shown in
Figure 1.2. The substitutions of the HG backbone are controlled enzymatically by pectin

1.1.2
Rhamnogalacturonan
The backbone of pectin, furthermore contains some heavily substituted domains, known
as the "hairy regions", which includes RGI and RGII. The most abundant is RGI, which
is made of a repeating backbone consisting of [→2)α-Ł-Rhap-(1 →4)-α-d-GalpA-(1 →]
residues. The rhamnosyl moieties of the backbone may then be substituted at the O-4 with
neutral sugar side chains which includes Arabinan, Galactan and arabinogalactan[Caﬀall
and Mohnen, 2009][Voragen et al., 2009]. The lesser abundant, but more complex, RGII
has a backbone similar to HG. However, in contrast to HG, the backbone of RGII is
heavily substituted with side chains, which in total consists of 13 diﬀerent sugar residues.
Despite being extremely complex, the structure of RGII is highly conserved among pectins
[Ndeh et al., 2017]. Distant RGII domains may additionally be cross-linked, through their
side chains. These are cross-linked by two apiosyl residues via the formation of a borate
diol ester linkages [O’Neill et al., 2004].
1.1.3
Pectinolytic enzymes
Given the high complexity and multiple glycosidic linkages within the pectin molecule,
multiple enzyme activities are needed for the complete degradation of pectin. The HG
backbone may be hydrolyzed by polygalacturonase (PG) (EC 3.2.1.15), which catalyzes
the hydrolysis of the α-1-4 bond between non-methylated GalA residues. These bonds
can also be cleaved by pectate lyases (PaL) (EC 4.2.2.2), which breaks the bonds via β-
elimination. The methylations may be hydrolyzed by pectin methylesterase (PME) (EC
3.1.1.11), and thereby resulting in an increase of catalytic sites for both PG and PaL. In
addition, the HG backbone may be deacetylated by pectin acetylesterase (PAE). Figure
1.2 shows a schematic illustration of the HG backbone, along with the catalytic cleavage
sites of the HG active pectinases. There are multiple enzymes that are active on the other
structural domains of pectin. However, these are not described in this thesis.

1.2.1
CAZy classiﬁcation of pectin lyases
The enzyme name and its corresponding Enzyme Commission number (EC) is limited to
the speciﬁc reaction, the enzyme catalyzes. Therefore, all Carbonhydrate-active Enzymes
(CAZy) are classiﬁed according to their amino acid sequence. Given that this classiﬁcation
is only sequence dependent, enzymes with diﬀerent activities may be classiﬁed in the same
families. [Lombard et al., 2014]. Currently all pectin lyases are classiﬁed as polysaccharide
lyase family 1 (PL1) enzymes according to the CAZy. This is a relatively small family,
which except from pectin lyases, only contains pectate lyases [Lombard et al., 2010].
Pectin lyases does, unlike pectate lyases, not require the binding of a Ca2+-ion in order
to catalyze its reaction [Vitali et al., 1998].

1.2.2
Industrial application
Pectin lyases can be used industrially for the depolymerization of esteriﬁed pectin and
thus, it can be used for processes which contain pectic biomass. One of these processes
are the clariﬁcation of juices, which contain highly esteriﬁed pectin.
Here the haze,
which is caused by insoluble pectin, is removed by depolymerizing the pectin.
The
depolymerization of pectin results in the formation of pectic oligos, which are solubilized
and hence clarifying the juice. In this process, pectin lyase has an advantage compared
to hydrolysis using PG. Since PG only catalyzes the hydrolysis of non-esteriﬁed GalA
residues, PME is often required for the de-esteriﬁcation of the methyl substituted GalA
residues. Resulting in the removal of an ester group, which contributes to the aroma of the
juice. Furthermore, this removal also results in the release of the toxic product methanol
[Mantovani et al., 2005]. Though the methanol may be removed during the concentration
of juice, this is not possible in other industrial processes, such as processes were pectinases
are used to lower the viscosity, or in juices, which are not made from concentrates [Yadav
et al., 2009].
1.3
Filamentous fungi
Most commercial pectinases are currently produced by ﬁlamentous fungi, since these
microorganisms naturally secrete a large variety of pectinolytic enzymes [Benoit et al.,
2012].
Filamentous fungi are ubiquitous within all terrestrial ecosystems, where they function
as a sink, which are capable of degrading, and thus releasing and ﬁxating organic
material.[Moore et al., 2000]. Several of these fungi are specialized in degrading plant
material, which consist of diﬀerent bio-polymers, mainly polysaccharides and lignin. The
polysaccharides are mainly composed of cellulose, hemicellulose, and pectin which makes
up to 70 % of the biomass [Van Den Brink and De Vries, 2011]. These are broken down
into smaller compounds, which then can be taken up by the fungi. The polysaccharides
are degraded by a wide mixture of enzymes, which are secreted by the fungi - each with
speciﬁcity towards a given bond within a bio-polymer [Benoit et al., 2012].
Though
ﬁlamentous fungi are specialized in degrading biomass, the type of biomass, which the
fungus can utilize, and the mode in which nutrients are made available for the fungus is
highly diverse. The latter can be divided into three diﬀerent modes: Saprotrophic (living
on dead organic material), necrotrophic (invades and kills the host cells before it feeds on
the dead organic material) and biotrophic (invades the host without killing it, and feeds
on nutrients hereof) [Moore et al., 2000]. These diﬀerent natures of the ﬁlamentous fungi
have resulted in the evolution of diﬀerent sets of enzyme, depending on the nature of the
fungus. A comparative analysis of the CAZymes of 103 diﬀerent fungal genomes showed
that the amount and types of the CAZymes encoded by the diﬀerent species was also
highly diverse. Here it was shown that Saprotrophic organisms encoded fewer enzymes
than biotrophic and necrotrophic fungi. In addition, pathogenic fungi towards dicots,

1.4
Aspergillus niger
A. niger is a mesophilic organism, which is most widespread in warmer climates, although
it can grow at temperatures ranging from 6 to 47°C with optimum at 35 to 37°C.
Furthermore, it can tolerate an acidic environment, and are thus capable of growing
at acidic conditions down to pH 2 [Hocking, 2006]. A. niger has been used industrially
as a cell factory since 1919, where it was used for the production of citric acid [Cairns
et al., 2018]. In addition, A. niger is, as mentioned above, also used for the production
of enzymes. The fungi within the Aspergillus genus are among the most common fungal
species involved in food spoilage. Hereof, A. niger is the most common species, which
is responsible for decay of fresh fruit [Perrone et al., 2007]. Given the complexity and
diversity of the bio polymers located in cell walls in fruit, A. niger is a natural producer
of a large set of enzymes. Moreover, A. niger has a highly eﬃcient secretion mechanism,
which along with the large pool of enzymatic genes has made A. niger very attractive for
the production of commercial enzymes [Pel et al., 2007].
The sequencing of the A. niger genome revealed that the gene pool of A. niger contained
14,165 protein coding genes, in which 927 contains 100 or more amino acids. These genes
include 131 diﬀerent genes coding for enzymes, which are active on polysaccharides and
that may be secreted to the surroundings by the fungi [Pel et al., 2007].

(PelA, PelB, PelC, PelD, and PelF) [Pel et al., 2007]. Expression studies have shown that
these genes are active at diﬀerent stages during the degradation of pectin. PelA and PelD
were predominately expressed after a short incubation time, thus indicating that these

2.2
Monosaccharide characterization of substrates

Quantiﬁcation of DM and DAc

saponiﬁcation. 30 mg of pectin was suspended in a 1 mL in a mixture of water and iso-

index and quantiﬁed using acetate and methanol as external standards in the range of
0.007-1 mg/mL and 0.04-5 mg/mL respectively.

2.3
Enzyme kinetics
2.3.1
Initial reaction rate
The initial reaction rate was estimated based on the change in the absorbance at 253 nm
(A235). This was then converted into change in product formation using the extinction
coeﬃcient. The extinction coeﬃcient is 5500 M-1 cm-1 for the unsaturated product released
by pectin lyases [Kester and Visser, 1994].
2.3.1.1
Continuously measurements
The enzymatic reactions assayed at 40 ◦C or below were conducted directly in a 96
well microtiter plate.
The reaction was incubated and continuously measured using
an Inﬁnite® 200PRO plate reader (TECAN, Männedorf, Switzerland).
Prior to the
addition of enzymes, the substrate and microtiter plate was preheated to the desired
assay temperature. The ﬁnal reaction volume was 200 µL, consisting of 180 µL substrate
and 20µL enzyme dilution. The initial reaction rate was measured based on the rate of
the change in A235, in initial period, where the observed A235 had a linear increase over
time.
2.3.1.2
Stop assays
The plate reader used in this study could not incubate samples at temperatures above
40 ◦C. Hence, the initial reaction rate of the enzymatic reactions assayed above 40 ◦C
were estimated based on stop assays. The enzymatic reaction was performed in a 1.5 mL
eppendorf tube incubated in a thermomixer (Eppendorf, Hamburg, Germany) set to the
desired reaction temperature and 1000 RPM. The substrate in solution was preheated
to the reaction temperature prior to the addition of enzyme. The enzymatic reaction
was incubated for 5 min, and afterwards inactivated at 95 ◦C 1000 RPM in another
thermomixer.
Subsequently the samples were centrifuged at 14000 ×g for 5 min, to
remove the insoluble fraction and redissolve the condensate from the lids of the eppendorf
tubes. 200µL sample was then transferred to a 96-well microtiter plated and A235 was
measured using a TECAN plate reader. The change in A235 was calculated by subtracting
the A235 from a background, which was handled as the samples, but the addition of water
instead of enzyme.
2.3.2
Substrate screening
The initial reaction rates of the ﬁve A. niger pectin lyases were tested on ﬁve
diﬀerent pectin substrates: AP, CP, SBP, 93 % DE CP, and Poly-GalA. The substrate
concentration was 0.1 %(w/V) in 0.1 M sodium acetate buﬀer pH 5. The enzymes were
assayed at room temperature for 10 min. Enzyme concentrations was 0.4 %(w/w) enzyme
to substrate (E/S) for AnPL1, and AnPL3, 0.625 %(w/w) E/S for AnPL2, and 1,25
%(w/w) E/S for AnPL4 and AnPL5. The initial reaction rates was calculated according
to section 2.3.1.1.

A phylogenetic tree, consisting of all the reviewed pectin lyases from UniProt, was

To assess whether the ﬁve A. niger pectin lyases were active on the same substrates
the speciﬁc activities were initially screened on four diﬀerent types of pectin as well as

An interaction study was made to determine if some of the enzymes had speciﬁcity towards
the products from the other pectin lyases. In the interaction study, all enzymes were added
in combination with each other, and the product formation was measured after 16 h. If
an enzyme was capable of degrading the product of another enzyme, it would be expected

combined product formation of both of the reactions which only contained the individual
enzymes. This was especially interesting for AnPL4 and AnPL5 which did not show any
activity on pure substrate (ﬁgure 3.5) and which also had structural diﬀerences within
the substrate binding cleft (ﬁgure 3.4). An additive increase in product formation would,
on the other hand, indicate that the enzymes would be capable of degrading diﬀerent
substrates, initially present in the pectin. Hence, they would not be capable of degrading
each others products.

3.2
Thermostability
The native conformation of an enzyme is ﬂexible.
The ﬂexibility of the active site
allows the enzyme to eﬀectively bind to the substrate, and subsequently catalyze the
reaction. However, the ﬂexibility may eventually result in an irreversible unfolding of the
enzyme, thus resulting in an inactivation of the enzyme. The ﬂexibility is induced by the
temperature, hence the rate of inactivation is dependent on the temperature[Kamerzell
and Middaugh, 2008]. It is therefore important to determine the thermostability of an
enzyme to ensure that the enzyme is stable under the reaction conditions, in which they
are intended.

3.2.1
Thermal inactivation
At constant reaction conditions, thermal inactivation of an enzyme may be approximated
by a ﬁrst order decay reaction as shown in equation 3.1 [Godfrey et al., 1996].
Nt = N0 · e−kd cot t
(3.1)

ln(At) = ln(A0) −kd · t
(3.2)

time it takes for an enzyme to loose half of its activity, hence t1/2 can be used as a
quantitative measure of the thermal stability at a given set of reaction conditions. t1/2
can be calculated according to equation 3.3 [Godfrey et al., 1996].
t1/2 = ln(2)

3.3
Optimal conditions
Whether enzymes are used in nature or in an industrial process such as juice
manufacturing, the catalytic activity of these are heavily dependent on the physiological
conditions of their surroundings.
The most signiﬁcant physiological factors aﬀecting
enzymatic activity are the temperature and pH [Godfrey et al., 1996]. Hence, the optimal
conditions of these factors will be investigated in this section.
3.3.1
pH optimum
The amino acid residues, which make up an enzyme, may include acidic or basic groups
in their side chains. The ionization of these groups aﬀects both the structure and stability
of the enzyme as well as contributing to the catalytic mechanism. Hence, the ionization
state of the amino acid side chains are important for the activity of an enzyme [Godfrey
et al., 1996].
The initial reaction rate was measured at pH values ranging from 2.5 to 10.5 at room
temperature, in order to neglect the thermal stability, which as seen in section 3.2.2,
was highly aﬀected by the pH. As with the determination of the thermal inactivation
constants, pH was only investigated for the enzymes with the highest speciﬁc activity,
namely AnPL1, AnPL3, and AaPL1.

3.3.2
Combined temperature and pH optimum
As seen in section 3.2, enzymes are also aﬀected by temperature. This eﬀect is, however,
not only limited to thermal inactivation. The temperature does also aﬀect the catalytic
rate constant, kcat (also known as the turnover number), of an enzyme. The correlation
between the temperature and the kcat of an enzymatic reaction is described by the
Arrhenius equation (equation 3.4).
kcat = A · e
−Ea
R · T
(3.4)
Here R is the ideal gas constant, T is temperature, Ea the required activation energy,
and A is the Arrhenius constant, which is also dependent on the temperature.
The
temperature optimum is therefore a function of the increase in the kcat described in
equation 3.4, as well as the thermal inactivation, described in equation 3.1. However,
as seen in section 3.2.2, the thermostability of an enzyme is highly dependent on the
pH. Thus, the pH may also contribute to the temperature optimum.
Furthermore,
temperature also aﬀects pH of a solution.
Hence, the optimal conditions should be
measured under a two dimensional experimental design, to encounter for the described
interactions between the temperature and pH.

observed pH optima on AP could therefore indicate that the charge of the substrate is
important for the enzymatic activity, since AP would require a higher pH to achieve the
same net charge as CP and SBP.

Another signiﬁcant contribution to the enzyme activity is the substrate concentration.
The relationship between the substrate concentration and the reaction rate of an enzyme
may often be described by the Michaelis-Menten equation (equation 3.6)
v = [S] · Vmax
[S] + KM
(3.6)

(3.7)
Here, the maximum reaction rate, Vmax, is a measure of the maximal potential of an
enzyme on a given substrate, while the Michelis-constant, KM, deﬁned as the substrate
concentration at ½Vmax, is a measure of the substrate aﬃnity of the enzyme. Here it is seen
that the reaction rate, v, at substrate concentration, [S], approaches a zero order reaction
at which v ≈Vmax when [S] ≫KM. Hence, the reaction rate is not signiﬁcantly aﬀected
by S under these conditions. However, at lower substrate concentrations ([S] ≪KM), the
reaction rate approaches a ﬁrst order reaction, and is therefore more sensitive to changes
in the substrate concentration.
As seen in equation 3.7, Vmax is dependent on the enzyme concentration [E]. Hence, the
turnover number, kcat, which is independent of [E], can be used to compare the catalytic
potential between diﬀerent enzymes, which catalyzes the same reaction.
The relationship between initial reaction rate of AnPL1, AnPL2, AnPL3, and AaPL1 and

3.7
Summary
Pectin is present in almost all plant cell walls. The function of pectin, along with other
polysaccharides in the cell wall, is to provide physical strength and protect the cell against
the outside environment, such as degradation by fungi [Scheller et al., 2007]. From the
characterization of pectin from apple, citrus, and sugar beet (table 3.1), it was seen
that the composition of pectin diﬀered between the diﬀerent plant species. Based on
the diverse nature of pectin, it was hypothesized that the ﬁve pectin lyases produced by
A. niger have been evolved to speciﬁcally suit diﬀerent physiological conditions and the
structure of speciﬁc pectins.
From the characterization presented in this study, it was seen that the ﬁve A. niger
pectin lyases exhibited diﬀerent kinetic properties. Among these were the predicted pH
optimum at optimal conditions. AnPL1 had a pH optima between 6.4 and 6.8 depending
on the substrate. This was signiﬁcantly higher than AnPL1 and AnPL2 which both had
pH optima between 4.3 and 4.9. This could indicate that A. niger has evolved multiple
pectin lyase genes in order to be robust against the physiological surroundings of the
fungus, since it was seen that these enzymes catalyzed the same reaction. The enzymes did
also diﬀered in terms of thermostability. Among the enzymes, which were characterized
kinetically it was seen that AnPL3 was least stable at optimal conditions. The kcat-value
of AnPL3 was, however, signiﬁcantly higher on both AP and CP, compared the other
enzymes. This observation is common within enzyme kinetics, since a high activity is
often caused by a high ﬂexibility which leads to a lower stability [Siddiqui, 2017]. The
TM-values of the ﬁve pectin lyases from A. niger were, however, in general more than 20 ◦C
higher than the temperature optimum of A. niger. Which as mentioned in section 1.4 is
between 35 to 37 ◦C. Hence, it would be expected that all of the enzymes would be stable
during growth of A. niger. It could therefore be argued that the observed diﬀerences
in the thermostability would not have a evolutionary advantage of A. niger.
AnPL1
and AnPL2 were more or less identical in terms of thermostability and temperature/pH
optima. However, it was seen that these two enzymes diﬀered signiﬁcantly in terms of the
kcat-values. The kcat-value of AnPL1 was approximately 10 times higher on AP, CP, and
SBP compared to AnPL2. The two enzymes likewise diﬀered in terms of their KM-values.
Since pectin from diﬀerent plant sources are highly diverse, the observed diﬀerences in
speciﬁcity could indicate that AnPL1 and AnPL2 have been evolved to target diﬀerent
pectins. The observed diﬀerences in the size distribution of the oligos released by AnPL1
and AnPL2, after enzymatically degradation of AP (ﬁgure 3.7) could also suggest that the
cleavage site on the HG backbone diﬀers between AnPL1 and AnPL2. It could therefore
be speculated if AnPL2 would have a higher speciﬁcity towards pectin from other sources,
which are structural diﬀerent than apple, citrus, and sugar beet.

Conclusion 4
A kinetic characterization of ﬁve A. niger pectin lyases (AnPL1, AnPL2, AnPL3, AnPL4,
and AnPL5) was conducted in this project.
The characterizations were carried out
to investigate whether the multiple ﬁve pectin lyases were a evolutionary strategy of
A. niger to obtain robustness towards degradation of the highly divers plant cell wall
polymer, pectin. A homologous pectin lyase from A. aculeatus (AaPL1), which share
76% sequence similarity with AnPL1 was included in the study to provide more knowledge
on the kinetic mechanism of fungal pectin lyases. Prior to the kinetic characterization
the phylogenetics of all reviewed pectin lyase entries from UNIPROT revealed that fungal
pectin lyases were divided into three clusters (FC1, FC2, and FC3). The A. niger genome
included pectin lyases from each of these clusters. A comparison of the three-dimensional
structure, generated by homology modelling, revealed that the substrate binding cleft of
the pectin lyases from FC2 and FC3 (AnPl4 and ANPL5, respectively), had a more open
conformation. From the characterization it was not possible to detect pectin lyase activity
from AnPL4. Hence, the kinetics of this enzyme could not be characterized. AnPL5 was
not successfully characterized, due to a low speciﬁc activity. The kinetic characterization
presented in this thesis, does therefore only include pectin lyases from FC1 (AnPL1,
AnPL2, AnPL3, and AaPL1). An assessment of the thermostability of these enzymes
showed that they diﬀered. The maximal TM-values were observed at pH 4 for AnPL1
(73 ◦C) and AnPL2 (71 ◦C), while the maximal observed TM-values for AnPL3 (65 ◦C)
and AaPL1 (75 ◦C) were found at pH 5. The temperature and pH optima was predicted
on pectin from apple, citrus, and sugar beet using RSM methodology.
This revealed
that all four characterized pectin lyases had the highest pH optimum on apple pectin
which was 4.9, 4.9, 6.4, and 6.8 for AnPL1, AnPL2, AnPL3, and AaPL1, respectively.
Apple pectin, as substrate, resulted in a signiﬁcantly higher turnover number compared
to the other substrates. The turnover numbers were highest for AnPL3 on all substrates,
indicating that this enzyme had the highest catalytic potential, whereas AnPL2 had the
lowest. Lastly, an assessment of the cation dependency of AnPL1, AnPL3, and AaPL1
showed that AaPL1 was heavily dependant on the presence of Ca2+-, Mg2+-, Mn2+-, and
Na+-ions. AnPL1 and AnPL3 were only slightly activated by Mg2+, Mn2+-ions.
In overall conclusion it was seen that the A. niger pectin lyases diﬀered in terms of
phylogenetics as well as kinetic properties.
Hence, it could be postulated that these
kinetic diﬀerences were most likely found to be a consequence of evolution, which would
aid A. niger with a more robust pectinolytic enzyme arsenal. However, further studies
are needed to fully conﬁrm this theory.

Future perspectives 5
Only three of the ﬁve pectin lyases from A. niger were successfully characterized.
The homology model did however indicate that AnPL4 and AnPL5, which were not
successfully characterized, had structural diﬀerences within the substrate binding cleft. It
would therefore be interesting to determine the crystal structures of these two enzymes.
The speciﬁcity of the characterized enzymes were only determined using heterologous
pectin from natural sources. A kinetic study using homologous GalA oligos with diﬀerent
DM and DAc could therefore provide a better understanding on the speciﬁcity of the
diﬀerent pectin lyases.
5.1
Application of characterized pectin lyases
Among the substrates used in this study it was seen that all the pectinases had the
highest turnover number on apple pectin. Hence the use of pectin lyases in processes
dealing with this type of pectin would be suggested. These includes both the extraction
and clariﬁcation of apple juice.
The speciﬁcity constant on AP, was not signiﬁcantly
diﬀerent between AnPL1 and AnPL3. However, due to the lower pH optima, and higher
thermostability, AnPL1 are better suited for the reaction conditions used for the enzyme
clariﬁcation, which is pH 3-4.5 and 45-60 ◦C for 2-3 hours [Godfrey et al., 1996].
AnPL3, on the other hand, would be preferable at industrial processes, which unlike the
apple juice clariﬁcation, can be operated at higher pH values. Such processes include the
retting of plant ﬁbers such as; ﬂax, remmie and hemp, to remove pectin from the ﬁbres
[Liu et al., 2016].
Additionally pectin lyases could be used for the extraction of the RGI, and RGI derived
oligos, has shown prebiotic and anti-cancer activities [Prandi et al., 2018] [Maxwell et al.,
2015]. Given that SBP is rich in RGI 3.1, this would be an ideal substrate for an enzymatic
extraction of RGI using pectin lyase. Since the substrate speciﬁcity of AnPL3 was highest
on SBP, this would be an ideal candidate for the extraction of RGI from SBP. The used
of pectin lyases fro the extraction of RGI has previously been descried by [Holck et al.,
2011].
