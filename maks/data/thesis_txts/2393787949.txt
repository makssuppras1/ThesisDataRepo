Preface
This dissertation is written in partial fulﬁllment of the degree of Master of Science in
Engineering in the ﬁeld of Civil engineering with specialization in Building structures
at the Technical University of Denmark (DTU). The project is carried out under
the expert guidance and supervision of Mr. Arne Egerup, associate professor at the
Department of Civil Engineering at DTU. The project work constitutes 35 ECTS
points. The work period of this project is from the 1st of February 2017 to the 31st
of October 2017. It involves an individual contribution of the student Tejas Deepak
Kulkarni.
The aim of this project is the structural analysis and design of a six-storied
building at the campus of Technical University of Denmark. The work comprises
of static analysis and design of the structure with the use of ﬁnite element mod-
elling and conventional calculation methods. The purpose of this Master’s project
is to analyse and design the existing building structure with the original building
components, and a proposal for an alternative design with the use of some new and
advanced structural components to those used in the original building. This leads
to an alternative design solution for the same building.

Abstract
Analysis of a high-rise indeterminate building structure involves the determination of
individual loads and external actions that the building is subject to, and a design of
its individual elements and their connections in order to sustain the loads or actions
with an acceptable level of safety. Structural analysis of Building 220 is carried out
primarily with the use of ﬁnite element modelling software ’Strusoft FEM design 16’
which involves the static analysis of the structure with consideration of individual
load cases and their critical load combinations. Analysis outputs like deﬂection,
shear, bending and buckling of individual building components within limits imply
that the building as a whole is structurally safe and stable.
Structural design of the most critical individual building elements including slabs,
beams, diaphragm walls, columns and foundations is carried out by conventional
manual calculation methods. Computer based tools like Mathcad and MS Excel
have been used for the purpose. A static check of the FEM results is presented
at the end which validates the analysis of the software. A comparison of analysis
results obtained in both the methods discussed is carried out to check the variation
in results, and to propose a design solution with acceptable level of accuracy.
Structural elements like hollow core slab panels proposed by Consolis Spaencom,
composite steel beams proposed by Peikko group have been used in the building
project as new and advanced structural solutions. New solutions also involve more
eﬀective beam-column and column-column construction joints which ensure struc-
tural rigidity of the connection, and an eﬃcient transfer of structural loads.
==PAGE:7==
Scope of work
The project covers the static analysis and structural design of a six storied building
at DTU with the use of ﬁnite element modelling software ’Strusoft FEM design’,
and conventional manual calculation methods which include computer based tools
like Mathcad and MS Excel. Modelling and analysis of the entire building structure
is carried out in Strusoft, however structural design by conventional method of only
the most critical of each structural component is presented in detail. A comparison
of the analysis results from the two methods considered is presented at the end.
Norms, guidelines and provisions of the national codes of reference, namely Eu-
rocodes and their corresponding Danish national annexes are used throughout the
design. Other references include standard design guidebooks, provisions of standard
element manufacturing companies and internet websites which are mentioned as and
when used.
The project comprises of static analysis of Building 220 with the consideration of
static structural loads and wind action. Dynamic analysis including seismic action,
non-linear analysis and second order eﬀects are beyond the scope of the project.
Temperature eﬀects in the building, creep and fatigue stresses and expansion of
concrete are not accounted for in the analysis. Accidental impacts on the building
are not considered in design, however a brief robustness assessment is presented.
Building 220 consists of a soil retaining wall surrounding the basement of the
structure. Exterior columns of the building are supported by pile foundations. De-
sign of these geotechnical structures is not considered anywhere in the project. Main
supports of the building structure consist of isolated footings and wall foundations.
Analysis and design of these elements is purely structural, and geotechnical eﬀects
of ground uplift and settlement of the structure are not considered.

2.2.1
Foundation system
Building foundation comprises of continuous wall foundation system that supports
the diaphragm walls, and isolated squared or pad footings or pad footings to support
square columns. The foundation system provides a ﬁxed support at the basement
level of the building, and thus ensures safe transfer of lateral loads to the soil below.
Wind loads on diaphragm walls result in a bending moment at the foundation (due
to the rigid connection), which is safely taken by the wall foundations (discussed in

4.10
Robustness of structure
Building 220 has an irregular and unsymmetrical geometry. Columns and diaphragm
walls are the vertical load carrying members of the building structure. Failure of
one or more of these members in an event of a natural hazard or accident or due to
constructional errors would pose a major risk to the stability of the entire building.
As a result, assessment of robustness of the building is necessary to check if the
structure can withstand the failure of one or two of its members without collapsing.
Assessment of robustness of the building is done in accordance with DS/EN 1990
(Annex-E, Ref. [8]). Based on its usage category, the building belongs to a high
consequence class CC3. This requires a documentation of limited risk to structural
members to unintended eﬀects and defects (Annex-E, (6)) and loss of member in
acceptable level of collapse. The latter speciﬁes a maximum limit of damage of 15%
of the ﬂoor area or up to 240 sq.m. and not extending more than two ﬂoors in an
8-storied building. Typical ﬂoor plan of the building clearly shows that diaphragm
walls in the ﬂoor plan (in X and Y directions) are rigidly connected, and failure of
November 1, 2017
==PAGE:158==
4.10. Robustness of structure
140
one of the short walls due to faulty construction like misalignment or in an event of
earthquake would not compromise the building stability, as the neighbouring walls
located close to the one failed would compensate for the loss of resistance oﬀered
by the wall. This is applicable in case of the short walls at the interior building
staircase location, and short core walls or elevator walls.
Building columns (in the interior) are spaced far apart with a maximum spacing
of 9 m center to center. Considering the column designed in one of the previous
sections, the distance from supports on each side is 9 m and 6 m (longitudinally),
and 5.6 m and 7.6 m (laterally), where neighbouring columns and shear walls are
located. Loss of this column in consequences like ﬁre or explosion would aﬀect the
neighbouring area (more than 100 sq. m.), which is still within the limit prescribed
above. However, considering the high load reaction oﬀered by the column (at the
basement level), its failure would lead to a major hazard which should be avoided.
Risk of columns to ﬁre is prevented with the provision of additional ﬁre proof coating
in the form of intumescent paint (Ref. [25]) which would reduce spalling in concrete,
and result in higher endurance. However, the building would sustain the loss of a
column only when the neighbouring columns or vertical supports are closer. This is
practically not possible for the discussed column system.

Chapter 6
Conclusion
Building 220 is a statically indeterminate high-rise structure with unsymmetrical
geometry. Structural analysis of the building is carried out by modelling it in Stru-
soft FEM design.
Detailed analysis and structural design of individual building
components is carried out with the use of conventional manual calculation methods.
A static check of the model outputs with hand calculation results shows a match
with minor deviations. This concludes that static inputs assigned to the model are
correct, and that the modelling and obtained results are accurate and reliable.
Comparison of structural analysis outputs for individual building components by
Strusoft and conventional design methods has shown an acceptable level of match.
Thus it is concluded that static analysis of Building 220 by both the methods con-
sidered has lead to an accurate and reliable design of the building.
The original (existing) structure of Building 220 involves slight changes with the
use of new and advanced structural solutions. Floor elements of the building involve
hollow core slab panels proposed by Consolis Spaencom. Composite beam sections
proposed by Peikko group are used in place of hollow cross-section plain steel beams
originally used in the building. This has lead to an economical solution with higher
load and moment capacities. In addition to this, new structural joint connections
like beam-column joint are introduced in the project, which would be an alternative
way of establishing an eﬀective beam-column connection (between composite steel
beam and prefab concrete column). The use of column-column longitudinal joint
proposed by Tectonica is not only a cost eﬀective solution, but also establishes a
149
==PAGE:168==
Chapter 6. Conclusion
150
rigid connection between columns and an eﬀective transfer of bending moments to
the foundation. With the use of these components in the structure of Building 220,
a new design solution is thus proposed.
The scope of current project was limited to static analysis only. However con-
sideration of seismic and dynamic analysis of the structure is possible with the use
of the developed Strusoft model. The model can further be simulated to study the
behaviour of the building for various load combinations, second order and inelastic
eﬀects. Further discretization of the model can be considered in order to obtain anal-
ysis results with an even higher level of accurracy. Structural design of the building
was considered by manual calculation method only. However, the developed model
can further be used to design reinforced and prestressed concrete sections in a similar
way as considered in manual design.
All in all, the work carried out in the project including the development of the 3D
structural model in FEM serves as a stepping stone in the development of modern
buildings with the use of new and advanced technological solutions.
