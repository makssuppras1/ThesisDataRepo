ii
Abstract
Antibodies are highly versatile molecules and indispensable for the immune system. A
key feature is their ability to target almost any intruding antigens the body encounters in a
speciﬁc manner. This feature makes antibodies a very interesting and important tool regard-
ing development of drugs and research tools. Developing monoclonal Antibodies (mAbs)
by immunization or display methods can be time consuming, expensive and encounters
drawbacks such as polyspeciﬁcity or low stability and expressibility, thus developing and
utilizing in silico methods for antibody engineering and design are highly prioritized.
Unknown molecular mechanisms still need to be elucidated about the properties and
functions of the Insulin Receptor (IR) and Insulin-like Growth Factor-I Receptor (IGF-1R). In
addition, the biological function of the IR/IGF-1R hybrid receptor, which is found whenever
IR and IGF-1R are co-expressed, is unknown. Thus, antibodies able to distinguish between
the IR and the IGF-1R could unravel a new research ﬁeld and potentially impact knowledge
about major diseases such as type 2 diabetes and cancer. For that reason, the overall aim of
this project was to develop mAbs able to distinguish between the active IR and the active
IGF-1 receptor.
In this project, an antibody already known to bind to the phosphorylated IGF-1R was
subject to engineering in order to enhance the speciﬁcity. Some of the challenges in this
project has been the lack of structural knowledge of the antigen, hence some of the already
developed in silico methods were not applicable. The sequence similarity of the IR and the
IGF-1R can be up to 84% in certain areas, adding to the complexity of developing speciﬁc
antibodies for the two receptors. A mAb cloned from a rabbit B cell (0023) was shown pre-
viously to bind to the phosphorylated IGF-1R in a speciﬁc manner but not sufﬁciently to
be used further as a robust assay antibody. Therefore, it was of interest to evaluate the im-
portance of the somatic hypermutations generated in the 0023 B cell during immunization.
A small library of backmutations to the corresponding germline genes was generated and
these mutants were tested in several ELISA assays and compared to the 0023 mAb. Some of
the mutated residues were found to be essential for the phospho-speciﬁcity and others were
not, making them a perfect target for alteration in the potential design cycle. An interesting
result was found in a western blotting analysis of the 0023 mAb and some of the mutated
mAbs. The 0023 was shown to be speciﬁc for the phosphorylated IR with no cross-reactivity
to the IGF-1R, which is contradictory to the ﬁndings made previously at NN. Another inter-
esting result was the switch of speciﬁcity towards the phosphorylated IGF-1R in two mAbs
with different single mutations. With these results in mind the design process could be even
more complex if only one residue is able to change the speciﬁcity from one receptor to the
other. However, these contradictory results also need further clariﬁcation in order to make
any ﬁnal conclusions.
Many aspects still need to be considered in order to generate highly speciﬁc and robust
assay mAbs against both receptors but a foundation is made and with this knowledge the
design process can begin.

Resume
Antistoffer er meget alsidige molekyler og uundværlige for immunforsvaret. En essen-
tial karakter er deres evne til at genkende nærmest ethvert antigen der trænger ind i krop-
pen. Denne evne gør dem til et perfekt og interessant værktøj i forhold til udviklingen af
lægemidler og forskningsværktøjer. Udviklingen af antistoffer via immunisering af model
organismer eller display metoder kan være tidskrævende og dyrt. Samtidig kan der være
ulemper såsom polyspeciﬁcitet, lav stabilitet og titer. Derfor er det en høj prioritet at udvikle
og udnytte in silico metoder for antistof design.
Molekylære mekanismer mangler stadig at blive belyst omkring egenskaberne og funk-
tionerne af Insulin Receptoren (IR) og Insulin like Growth Factor-1 Receptoren (IGF-1R).
Samtidig kender man ikke den biologiske funktion af hybrid receptoren mellem IR og IGF-
1R, som ﬁndes i alle væv hvor IR og IGF-1R udtrykkes simultant. Derfor kan udviklingen
af speciﬁckke antistoffer, som kan skelne mellem IR og IGF-1R være med til at belyse et helt
nyt forskningsfelt og potentielt påvirke den viden, der ﬁndes omkring type 2 diabetes og
kræft. Af denne årsag er det overordnede mål med denne opgave at udvikle antistoffer, der
kan skelne mellem den aktive IR og den aktive IGF-1R.
I dette projekt skal speciﬁciteten af et antistof, man allerede ved binder til IGF-1R, højnes.
Nogle af de største udfordringer er manglen på struktur af antigenet, hvilket betyder at
nogle af de allerede udviklede in silico metoder ikke er brugbare. Sekvenserne på de to
receptorer er meget ens og i nogle områder deler de op til 84 % af aminosyrerne. Dette
tilfører yderligere til kompleksiteten i udviklingen af disse speciﬁkke antistoffer. Et anti-
stof klonet fra en kanin B celle (0023) viste sig at være speciﬁk for den aktive IGF-1R, men
ikke tilstrækkelig til at kunne bruges i assays. Derfor var det interessant at evaluere be-
tydningen af de somatiske hyper mutationer, som blev genereret i denne B celle ved im-
munisering. Et lille bibliotek af tilbage mutationer til de respektive germline gener blev
lavet og disse mutanter blev analyseret i forskellige ELISA assays og sammenlignet med
0023. Nogle muterede aminosyrer viste sig at være essentielle for forsforspeciﬁciteten og
andre ikke, hvilket gør dem til det perfekte udgangspunkt i en design process. I et west-
ern blot blev det fundet at 0023 i langt højere grad binder til den aktive IR end den aktive
IGF-1R. Dette resultat står i stor kontrast til hvad der tidligere blev fundet på Novo Nordisk
omkring speciﬁciteten af 0023. Et andet interessant resultat var e skifte mellem IR og IGF-
1R speciﬁcitet udelukkende ved en enkel mutation. Disse resultater vidner om en komplex
design process når en enkeltmutation kan skifte speciﬁciteten så drastisk. Samtidig skal der
yderligere information til for at kunne konkludere på disse inkonsistente resultater.
Mange faktorer skal stadig vurderes for at kunne generere robuste antistoffer, der har høj
speciﬁcitet for enten den ene eller den anden receptor, men et bredt fundament er etableret
og med denne viden kan design processen sættes i gang.

iv
Preface
The Insulin Receptor and the Insulin like Growth Factor-1 Receptor are able to form the
IR/IGF-1R hybrid receptor, whenever both receptors are expressed. An increased num-
ber of these receptors is found in certain tissues in patients suffering from cancer or type
2 diabetes. Two complex diseases that touches the lives of almost everyone worldwide.
Approximately 9.6 million people died of cancer in 2018 and 18 million new cases were
observed (WHO, 2019). Furthermore, 380 million people are living with type 2 diabetes
globally (NovoNordisk, 2019).
Knowledge that can aid in the diagnosis and treatments of these major diseases is there-
fore highly valuable. Today the knowledge about the molecular mechanisms of the hybrid
receptor and what part it plays in these diseases or in healthy people are limited.
The development of antibodies able to target these hybrid receptors, the subject of this
project, would help increase the knowledge within this ﬁeld and potentially lead to the
discovery of a new drug target.

Chapter 1
Background
1.1
The Antibodies
Antibodies, also known as Immunoglobulins (Ig) are highly versatile molecules and impor-
tant players in the adaptive immune system. The most characteristic feature of antibodies is
their ability to target almost any antigen that the body encounters. This feature makes them
ideal candidates in the development of drugs and research tools. As of May 2018, 80 ther-
apeutic monoclonal antibodies were approved by the Food & Drug Administration (FDA),
more than 100 are in clinical trials and orders of magnitude more is used as research tools
(Cai, 2018).
1.1.1
The Structure of Antibodies
The structure of antibodies is conserved among higher vertebrates with some exceptions.
In human, rodents and rabbits the most common antibody found in serum is Y-shaped and
consists of four polypeptide chains, two heavy chains (HCs) and two light chains (LCs).
Each chain is divided into a variable domain and one or more constant domains. The
LC consists of a variable light (VL) domain and a constant light (CL) domain. The HC
consists of a variable heavy (VH) domain and 3 constant heavy (CH) domains, CH1-CH3.
The CH1 and CH2 domain are separated by a ﬂexible hinge region, Figure 1.1. The variable
domains interact with the antigen and the CH domain interacts with effector molecules and
cells. Antibodies are very similar to the B cell receptor (BCR), however the BCR contains a
hydrophobic sequence in the C-terminal, which anchors the BCR to the B cell membrane.
The complete antibody consists of 12 discretely folded regions, called Ig domains, where
each LC contains two domains and each HC contains four domains. The 3D structure of a
complete antibody is presented in Figure 1.2a. Within each of these Ig domains the structure
consists of two anti parallel β-sheets folded onto each other like a sandwich, held together
by a disulphide bond. The variable and constant domains contain different number of β-
strands, but both regions contain the β-sandwich. This recognizable fold is known as the
immunoglobulin fold.
The entire antibody is held together by disulphide bridges between the HCs in the so-
called hinge region and between the HC and LC, Figure 1.1. The antibody consists of a
Fragment crystallizeable (Fc) domain which corresponds to Hinge-CH2-CH3, and a Frag-
ment antigen binding (Fab) domain that corresponds to the LC, VH and CH1.
The class or isotype of the antibody is determined by its type of HC. There are ﬁve differ-
ent isotypes; IgG, IgM, IgD, IgA and IgE. The different isotypes are distributed throughout
the body corresponding to the needs at the speciﬁc sites. The most common Ig is the IgG. In
contrast to the HC, only two types of LC’s exist, κ and λ, where the ratio between the two
differs between species with κ as the most abundant in most species. However, for cow the
λ : κ ratio is 95:5.

FIGURE 1.1: Components of IgG. A schematic representation of the structure
of an antibody with the different components annotated in the legend. The
ﬁgure is adapted from Thermo Fisher (Fisher, 2019).
The antibody binds speciﬁc antigens in the hypervariable domain. This domain is di-
vided into three loops called Complementarity Determining Regions (CDRs), which are in-
terconnected by framework regions (FR). The CDRs are denoted L1, L2 and L3 for the VL
and H1, H2 and H3 for the VH, Figure 1.2b. The FRs are denoted accordingly; FR1, FR2,
FR3 and FR4. The FR segments are highly conserved in structure and to some extend in
sequence between species.
The binding region of the antibody is called the paratope and the antigen region recog-
nized by the antibody is called the epitope. Antibodies and BCRs are able to recognize
discontinuous epitopes.
This is in contrast to the T-cell receptor (TCR) that recognizes
linear peptides in the form of degraded antigens presented on Major Histocompatibility
Complex (MHC) class I molecules on the surface of all nucleated cells and MHC class II
molecules on the surface of antigen presenting cells (APC) such as B cells, dendritic cells
and macrophages.
1.1.2
Development of Antibodies
A huge repertoire of antibodies are developed by the adaptive immune system in the body
when an antigen recognized as non-self is encountered. Antibodies are produced and se-
creted by plasma cells which arise from hematopoietic stem cells present in the bone mar-
row, Figure 1.3. During development the antibody germline genes are rearranged and hyper
mutated in order to produce the great sequence variability necessary to recognize and com-
bat intruding pathogens.
HC and LC sequences arise from germline genes that undergo a recombination event
called V(D)J recombination in HC and VJ recombination in LC, due to the lack of D genes
in the LC locus. The HC locus consists of multiple V, D and J segments and it is the ran-
dom combination of these segments that contribute to the great variability in the VH. The
combination of one D and one J segment occur prior to the combination of one V and the
DJ segment. The last segment of the locus consists of the constant domain of the HC. The
recombined DNA is transcribed and RNA splicing removes the introns between the variable
segments and the constant segments. The overall process is similar for the LC. The κ and λ
LCs arise from two different loci, where the single HC locus contains the genes for all the
HC isotypes.

The activated B cell enters the dark zone of the germinal center (GC) where it under-
goes somatic hypermutation (SHM) to enhance the speciﬁcity for the presented antigen and
travels to the light zone in the germinal center where follicular dendritic cells (FDC) present
antigens to the afﬁnity matured B cells, Figure 1.4. If the B cell has lost its afﬁnity to the
antigen the survival signals from T follicular helper cells are not given which result in apop-
tosis of the B cell. Contrary, if the B cell has gained high afﬁnity for the antigen T follicular
helper cells transmit a signal for the B cell to undergo class switching and proliferation. The
selected B cell differentiate into either a memory B cell or a long-lived plasma cell, that can
either migrate to the site of infection or to the bone marrow where it secretes antibodies
against the intruding pathogen (Heesters, Myers, and Carroll, 2014). High afﬁnity B cells
can also reenter the dark zone in the GC and undergo additional SHMs to increase the afﬁn-
ity even further. Every plasma cell secretes a distinct antibody, referred to as a monoclonal
antibody (mAb). MAbs only recognizes a single site on an antigen the so-called epitope.
This complex system is utilized in antibody production where an immunogen is intro-

serum or the spleen. An immunization often consist of multiple introductions of antigens

selections. Not all molecules are immunogenic and therefore carrier proteins are conjugated
to the antigens to ensure an immune response. This makes immunization with synthetic
peptides possible despite their low molecular weight that would otherwise not stimulate
antibody development. The serum contains many antibodies speciﬁc for different epitopes
on the same antigen. This mixture of antibodies are referred to as polyclonal antibodies

Characteristic Features of Rabbit Antibodies
Compared to mice, rabbits have many advantages in the production of antibodies. They
are bigger and naturally contain more B cells in the spleen and bone marrow that can be
extracted. At the same time, the immune system of the rabbit is further developed than the
immune system of the mice. It is observed in many immuno histo chemistry studies that
the antibodies developed in rabbits are more speciﬁc than in mice (Chinellato et al., 2005),
(Weber, Peng, and Rader, 2017).
However some characteristic features should be accounted for in choosing the rabbit as
animal model for antibody production. Especially the intrachain disulphide bridge formed
between Cys 80 in VL and Cys 171 in CL, which is not observed in human and mouse anti-
bodies (Weber, Peng, and Rader, 2017). This disulphide bridge is suggested to be important
for expression of the antibodies (McCartney-Francis et al., 1984).
In rabbits the most dominant V gene is the VH1, which is observed in 80-90% of the HC
in matured antibodies. This lack of diversity in the utility of gene segments is compensated
by N-nucleotide addition at the junctions between the segments in the recombination, gen-
erating on average longer and more diverse H3, but also longer L3 than found in mouse and
human antibodies. The longer L3 is also suggested to arise from the more frequent somatic
gene conversion although there is some discrepancy in the literature on this matter (Becker
and Knight, 1990), (Lavinder et al., 2014).

1.1.3
Interaction Between the Paratope and the Epitope
The paratope on the antibody is complementary in structure to the epitope on the antigen.
Thus, the surface structure of paratopes on different antibodies are very diverse. Small
antigens such as peptides typically bind in a small pocket or groove between the VH and
VL. Antibodies often adopt a ﬂat structure if the antigen is a large protein and typically
requires all six CDRs to maintain the interaction (Ramaraj et al., 2012).
Many different forces contributes to the antibody:antigen interaction, such as electro
static interactions between charged residues, hydrophobic interactions where hydrophobic
surfaces exclude water while approaching each other, hydrogen bonds between electroneg-
ative atoms that share a positive H-atom and Van der Walls interactions, where weak forces
between atoms effect each other’s polarization.
Some of the amino acids commonly found in the paratope of the antibody are aromatic
amino acids, more speciﬁcally tyrosine and tryptophan (Krawczyk et al., 2013). In general,
these residues participate in hydrophobic and Van der Waals interactions, but are also capa-
ble of making hydrogen bonds and the strong cation-π interaction due to the aromatic ring
(Dougherty, 2007). These amphipathic properties of tryptophan and tyrosine are proposed
to be important for the change from hydrophilic to hydrophobic environment upon antigen
binding (Mian, Bradwell, and Olson, 1991). Another paper also mentions glycine and ser-
ine as favorable amino acids in the paratope of rabbit antibodies. This is not observed as a
general pattern in all other vertebrate species. However, the general idea that the center of
the paratope is slightly hydrophilic is conserved across evolution (Lavinder et al., 2014).
Disordered epitopes, such as peptides or regions in proteins that lack a three dimen-
sional structure, make up a smaller binding surface between epitope and paratope regard-
ing the number of residues and interacting surface area, compared to ordered epitopes. It
is suggested that the surface on the antibody is concave and that shape complementarity
is greater for disordered epitopes, hence disordered epitopes are buried to a higher extend
than ordered epitopes. It is also suggested that polar contacts are enriched in disordered
epitopes. (MacRaild et al., 2016)
Even though the residues directly involved in the interaction between paratope and epi-
tope are important, residues in the framework can also have an impact on speciﬁcity and
afﬁnity (Koenig et al., 2017). Likewise, the residues involved in the association between VH
and VL and residues interacting with the constant domain and thus far away from the bind-
ing site, is shown to be important for paratope and epitope interaction (Chailyan, Marcatili,
and Tramontano, 2011), (Wang et al., 2009).
Speciﬁcity versus Afﬁnity
Speciﬁcity is the ability of an antibody to recognize a single epitope. By developing highly
speciﬁc antibodies it is possible to distinguish between very similar antigens.
Afﬁnity is a measure of the strength in binding between two molecules, in the case of an-
tibodies, the strength between a single epitope and a corresponding paratope. The strength
is determined from the forces between the two molecules.

1.1.4
The Status of Computational Design of Antibodies
Developing mAbs by immunization of animals or display methods is time consuming, ex-
pensive and encounters some drawbacks such as polyspeciﬁcity, low stability and express-
ibility (Baran et al., 2017). Thus developing and utilizing in silico methods for antibody
engineering and design is of great interest.
Computational methods in protein design are based on experimental data, such as crys-
tal structures and nuclear magnetic resonance (NMR) analysis. The ability to produce 3D
structure models only from a query sequence has revolutionized the ﬁeld of structural bi-
ology and today protein folding can be predicted with high accuracy, owing especially to
homology modeling. The computer aided design of antibodies is highly dependent on reli-
able structure models and therefore improvements in this ﬁeld has happened at a fast pace.
Antibodies that contains six variable loops of which the H3 and sometimes L3 are very long,
wouldn’t be ideal targets for protein structure prediction. Antibodies also consists of multi-
ple chains that needs to be packed together (Abhinandan and Martin, 2010). Nevertheless,
it has been observed that antibody hypervariable loops structure can be predicted to a very
high accuracy thanks to the so-called ıcanonical structure paradigm (Al-Lazikani, Lesk, and
Chothia, 1997), that has been used extensively for developing antibody and TCR modeling
software.
The increase in data and computer power has unraveled the ﬁeld of molecular dynamics
(MD) and protein-protein docking that can aid in the prediction of antibody:antigen com-
plex formation and to calculate the binding free energy in these systems. It is however
important to highlight that both tools require high quality models of the antibody and the
antigen structure, respectively.
In line, paratope and epitope prediction tools exist that can apply prior knowledge to a
potential docking or MD simulation (Soria-Guerra et al., 2015), (Veliˇckovi´c et al., 2018).
Even though these tools have advanced the engineering of antibodies, the complete de-
sign of antibodies using only computational tools continue to be challenging.
1.1.5
Homology modeling
The increasing amount of structural protein data makes homology modeling possible with
accuracies almost comparable to experimental results. Homology modeling is based on the
idea that proteins through evolution are more conserved in structure than sequence as made
apparent by the stagnation in the number of unique folds added to the Protein Data Bank
(PDB) as deﬁned by CATH Protein Structure Classiﬁcation database(v4.0.0) (PDB, 2019).
Homology modeling has revolutionized protein engineering making in silico practices the
ﬁrst step in e.g. drug design in order to save money and time.
Overall homology modeling can be divided into seven steps (Krieger, Nabuurs, and Vriend,
2003):
1. Template recognition and initial alignment
2. Alignment correction
==PAGE:19==

8
3. Backbone generation
4. Loop modeling
5. Side-chain modeling
6. Model optimization
7. Model validation
Modeling of antibodies contain additional steps as modeling of regular proteins such as
CDR grafting and VL and VH packing (Abhinandan and Martin, 2010). The immunoglob-
ulin fold is conserved in many higher vertebrates and the structure between antibodies are
therefore similar to a high degree, even when the sequence similarity is low.
The CDR regions are more variable in structure compared to the framework regions
though Chothia and coworkers discovered that the very diverse CDR regions obtain a lim-
ited number of conformations named canonical structures, which is determined based on
the length of the loop and key residues (Al-Lazikani, Lesk, and Chothia, 1997). Later this
subject has been revisited and reﬁned by many researchers (North, Lehmann, and Dun-
brack, 2011).
This information aids in the antibody loop structure prediction and with a high qual-
ity template it is possible to obtain a structure model with high quality. Although H3 and
sometimes L3 given their lengths and variability are still challenging the antibody loop pre-
diction. In lack of template the H3 is often modeled Ab initio, where prediction is based on
primary sequence and follows physical principles of protein folding.
The LYmphocyte Receptor Automated Modeling webserver (LYRA) is optimized for
modeling antibodies and TCRs with high accuracy (Klausen et al., 2015). LYRA incorpo-
rates the information about the canonical structures of the query and the template. Due to
antibodies modular conformation LYRA is able to choose different templates for the differ-
ent domains and piece them together. CDR regions different from the FR template can also
be chosen and is subsequently grafted on the model.
When the ﬁnal model is generated it is important to validate the quality to ensure that no
clashes and unfavorable dihedral angles occur. Another indication of the model quality is
the R values obtained in the template structure. In crystallography the structure is obtained
based on a diffraction pattern or electron density map. The atoms in the molecule are ﬁtted
to the electron density map. The higher the resolution the easier it is to ﬁt more atoms into
the electron density map. At certain resolution values it is even possible to ﬁt the H-atoms.
A high resolution structure has a resolution value <1.8 Å. The Rwork is a measure of how
good the calculated structure ﬁts the original diffraction pattern. The Rwork should have a
value around 1/10 of the resolution. If this value is low it can also mean that a bad struture
is over-reﬁned to make it ﬁt to experimental data, hence a third R value, the Rfree, needs to
be calculated. This is done by randomly excluding 10 % of the data in the reﬁnement and
then use these data to calculate the Rfree in a similar way as Rwork. The Rfree value should be
5 % of Rwork. (Williamson, 2012)
1.1.6
Molecular Dynamics
Molecular dynamics originates from theoretical physics and was used mainly in material
sciences. It gained its popularity in biophysics and biochemistry and is highly used within
these ﬁelds today. It is developed as a tool for studying interactions and motions of atoms
and molecules and is based on Newtons laws of motion (De Vivo et al., 2016). It is used for
protein folding, protein-protein interactions, protein-ligand interactions and the emerging
ﬁeld of computational drug discovery among others. The old static lock-and-key paradigm
==PAGE:20==
Chapter 1. Background
9
for protein-ligand interactions has been overtaken by the theory that everything is in motion
at all time and conformational rearrangement occurs upon interactions between molecules.
GROMACS is a MD simulation program that is widely used. During a GROMACS MD
simulation, positions and velocities of particles are speciﬁed over time and gathered in a
trajectory ﬁle (.trr). Several other ﬁles are generated before the actual MD simulation. A
structure ﬁle (.gro), which as the name implies contains information about the structure
of the molecule and the solvent, a topology ﬁle (.top), that contains information about the
bonds, charges and angles, a post-processed structure ﬁle (.itp), that apply a position re-
straining force on the heavy atoms in the protein. A parameter ﬁle (.tpr) is generated for
all the atoms in the system based on a parameter input ﬁle (.mdp), that dictates the number
of steps in the run, the output control, the neighbor searching cut-off scheme, that deter-
mines the neighbors of all particles and thereby the interaction forces. It is also in the mdp
ﬁle that the thermostat and barostat are deﬁned together with the equilibration temperature
and pressure. (Berendsen, 2014)
Force ﬁeld
A force ﬁeld (FF) in molecular dynamics is a mathematical description of the potential en-
ergy within a given system. The potential energy function cover bonded and non-bonded
interactions of all atoms in the system. A set of parameters are assigned to every type of
atom within that FF. The parameters describe bond stretching, angel bending, protonation
states, van der Waals radii etc., and act as a model describing the forces between atoms.
Most FFs used in MD contain information of proteins, lipids and carbohydrates and has
a wide range of applications. The parameters are often obtained empirically, though some
FFs, such as CHARMM36, are a mixture of quantum mechanics calculations and ﬁtting from
structural data, such as NMR and X-ray (Nerenberg and Head-Gordon, 2018).

1.1.7
Parapred
Parapred, developed by Edgar Liberis et al., is a tool for predicting the paratope of the
antibody, without any knowledge of the antigen. It is a sequence-based probabilistic deep-
learning algorithm and only needs the hypervariable loops with 4 adjacent residues as input
to make a prediction. It is based on 277 crystal structures of antibody:antigen complexes re-
trieved from the Structural Antibody Database (Veliˇckovi´c et al., 2018). If a PDB ﬁle is given
as input Parapred calculates a probability score to each residue, where 100 corresponds to
100 % probability that the given residue is in the paratope and 0 corresponds to 0 % proba-
bility.

The Antigen
The antigen in this project corresponds to the active Insulin like Growth Factor I Recep-
tor (IGF-1R), more speciﬁcally the region surrounding the phosphorylated tyrosine 1316
(pTyr1316). The speciﬁcity towards the IGF-1R are compared to the corresponding phos-
phorylated tyrosine 1334 (pTyr1334) region on the active Insulin Receptor (IR).
Even though extensive research has contributed to establishing the properties and func-
tions of the IGF-1R and IR, unknown molecular mechanisms still needs clariﬁcation in this
complex ﬁeld (Malaguarnera et al., 2017), (Denduluri et al., 2015).
The two receptors are both members of the receptor tyrosine kinase family(RTK). Com-
mon features within this family are the transmembrane spanning domain of the monomers
and auto phosphorylation of tyrosine, serine and threonine residues upon binding of lig-
and. A presentation of the IR and IGF-1R monomer and the tyrosine phosphorylation sites
are presented in Figure 1.5. Receptors within RTK dimerize upon ligand binding, where
IR and IGF-1R have covalent dimeric α2 β2 structures even in the absence of a ligand. The
extracellular α domain consists of the ligand binding site and is bound to the intracellular β
domain by a disulphide bridge. A disulphide bridge is also formed to keep the two α do-
mains together. The β domain contains the transmembrane part of the receptor and the part
with kinase activity, from where signal cascades are transduced upon receptor activation.
Both receptors have high afﬁnity to their corresponding ligands and a 100-fold lower
afﬁnity to their non-cognate ligand (Blakesley et al., 1996). The preferred ligand for the IR
is insulin and for the IGF-1R the preferred ligand is IGF-1 although IGF-2 also activates
the receptors. The preferred ligands and the possible receptor formations are presented in
Figure 1.6.
The ﬁndings of the exact molecular mechanisms of the downstream signaling pathways
of the two receptors are contradictory. The different effects are observed not only as a con-
sequence of different response patterns between the receptors in a given cell but also as
varying patterns between different cell types (Ursoe et al., 1999). Thus, varying end re-
sponses are observed in different tissues. Boucher et al. points towards similar gene ex-
pression patterns, but different modulation of amplitude of the signals between the two
receptors, whereas Dupont et al. shows distinct expression patterns between the two re-
ceptors (Boucher, Tseng, and Kahn, 2010), (Dupont et al., 2001). Both studies uses cDNA
microarray but in different cell lines; ﬁbroblasts and preadipocytes, respectively. Hence,
the exact molecular mechanisms of the pathways continues to be discussed in the ﬁeld, but
the general function of the pathways and the receptors has been elucidated. The IGF-1R
plays a key role in cellular growth and tissue homeostasis. This is demonstrated in humans

1.4
Aim and Hypothesis
The overall aim of this project is to develop phospho-speciﬁc antibodies against the IR and
the IGF-1R, to be used in receptor speciﬁc immunoassays not yet developed. The receptors
are in the tyrosine kinase family and are auto-phosphorylated upon ligand stimulation. The
phosphorylation are on serine, threonin or tyrosine. In this work the focus is on tyrosine
phosphorylation, more speciﬁcally on the pTyr1316 in the IGF-1R, even though antibodies
against both receptors are desirable. The antibodies should support in further analysis of
the biological role of the hybrid receptor, that is formed between IR and IGF-1R.
It is hypothesized that it is possible to evaluate the role of somatic hyper mutations in an
antibody from a library containing single and multiple germline back mutations.
It is assumed that the 0023 WT will show binding to phosphorylated IGF-1R in a speciﬁc
manner as previously established.
Finally it is hypothesized that it is possible to enhance the speciﬁcity with computational
procedures.

Methods
This chapter describes the methods and procedures used in the project.
2.1
Computational Methods and Procedures
2.1.1
Homology Modeling of 0023 WT
LYRA (Klausen et al., 2015) was used to create 3D structure models of the 0023 WT antibody
of known sequence. To properly model the antibody, the LYRA database was updated with
rabbit antibodies based on sequence similarity to 0023 identiﬁed by a plastp search.
The generated model was imported into Maestro (2018-4, 2018) and the side chains were
added by the prediction program Prime that uses an all-atom force ﬁeld and a generalized
Born model of solvation (Jacobson et al., 2002). Restrained minimization, using force ﬁeld
OPLS3 and convergence of heavy atoms to an RMSD of 0.5Å, was performed for reﬁnement
of the model.
The advanced homology modeling feature in Maestro was used to make knowledge
based models of the L3 of 0023. The following properties were checked when the modeling
was run; preserve the residue numbers of the template, optimize side chains, minimize
residues not derived from templates.
HC and LC clashes were removed by choosing the residue rotamers with less steric hin-
drance in Pymol and subsequently another round of restrained minimization in Maestro.
2.1.2
Paratope Prediction of 0023 WT
The Chothia numbered PDB ﬁle of the generated 3D structure model of the 0023 WT was
given as input to the Parapred program. The new PDB ﬁle with changed b-factor values
was imported into Pymol and colored according to the prediction values. The color scheme
red_white_blue was chosen and residues with a prediction score >90 was highlighted as
sticks. Residues with a low probability were colored blue, intermediate were colored white
and high probability were colored red.
2.1.3
Simulation of Peptide Binding
Molecular dynamics simulations were done in GROMACS (version 4.5.6). The 3D structure
model of the 0023 WT variable domain was used together with a generated peptide of 7
aa of either the IR or IGF-1R, respectively, corresponding to the tyrosine phosphorylation
site of the two receptors. The peptides were generated in Pymol by the build sequence
script, build_seq_phi_psi.py, adapted from Queens University. NetsurfP 2.0 was run on the
IR and IGF-1R to give an educated guess of the dihedral angels in the peptide chain. MD
simulations were made in triplicates for the two different peptides. When the PDB ﬁle was
converted into a GROMACS format ﬁle the CHARMM36 was chosen as FF. The complex

was centered in a dodecahedron box 2 nm from the box edge. The deﬁned box was solvated
and Tip3p was used as water model to describe the water molecules.
25285 water molecules were added to the IGF-1R complex and 25691 were added to the
IR complex. Ions were added to neutralize the system and subsequently to reach a salt

prevent them from rotating. The simulations were visualized in VMD. RMSD and RMSF

Western blot is used to separate and detect proteins in a sample e.g. cell lysates, where a

and a reducing agent is added to remove disulphide bridges.

done by an electrical current that forces the negative charged proteins to migrate into the
membrane.
After transfer the membrane is blocked to prevent unspeciﬁc binding of antibodies to
the membrane. The detection antibody is then added. Incubation time of membrane and
antibodies varies between procedures. After incubation the membrane is washed in TBS
with detergent to remove unbound antibodies. The secondary antibody is added and incu-
bated for a proper amount of time. The secondary antibody is conjugated to e.g. horseradish
peroxidase and a chemiluminescent substrate is added to enable detection.

reproduced between the two experiments. If the p-value < 0.01 and the direction of the
signals were consistent, the difference was considered to be signiﬁcantly higher than the
difference observed within the negative controls.

tested. 2 antibodies were tested in two independent ELISA experiments and with the as-
sumption that no difference was observed within the signals for the three treatments a
t-distribution was assumed for the entire test set. For that reason, the mean from that t-
distribution + 2 standard deviations (µ + 2σ) was set as threshold for binding.

4.1
How Good is the Model?
In general the quality of the models produced by LYRA for human and mice antibodies is
comparable to manually curated and optimized models. LYRA failed to produce a model
of a rabbit antibody tested during launch when comparing with similar methods due to the
short FR3 in the HC in general (Klausen et al., 2015). This short FR3 is also present in the
antibody developed in this project. To circumvent this, solved structures of rabbit antibodies
with similar short FR3 are added to the template database of LYRA.
Besides the FR3 and the long loops in L3 and H3, the rabbit antibodies does not differ
much from human and mice antibodies in structure. Therefore it is assumed that the frame-
work and the short CDR regions resembles the structure of the natural 0023 antibody to a
high extend.
The CDR3 regions in 0023 of both chains are very long and no templates are found for
either loops. It is therefore questionable how reliable these parts of the model are. These are
unfortunately some of the most important parts of the variable domains due to the fact that
these CDR3 regions are an essential part of the antibody:antigen interaction in general (Xu
and Davis, 2000). It was considered to make a new HMM alignment for rabbit antibodies
in order to utilize a rabbit antibody light chain as template and perhaps ﬁnd a better model
for the long L3. Since predicting the structure of long loops is a general problem in protein
modeling, it is uncertain if this would generate something more useful. Therefore a human
template is used and the L3 loop is modeled in Maestro by the advanced modeling feature
subsequently.
The model is based on templates with high resolutions (<2Å ). However the R-work and
R-free values are a bit high for the 4HBC, which could be an indication of over-reﬁnement
of the structure. The 3NAC structure has the following R-values; Resolution: 1.8Å, R-free:
0.184 and R-work: 0.212. These R-values are an indication of a good quality structure and
a perfect template for homology modeling. The generated model is minimized to remove
any steric clashes and a ramachandran plot is made to evaluate the dihedral angles in the
molecule. When either clashes between atoms nor residues with unfavorable angles are
present, the model is validated to be in good enough quality to proceed with. Unfortunately
it is not possible to run the model in ProQ or another model validation program due to lack
of licenses at Novo Nordisk.
The predicted paratope from Parapred can be used to direct the 7 aa peptide position
in relation to the 0023 model in a MD simulation. Parapred is sequence based and with-
out knowledge of the antigen it will only predict amino acids that are commonly found in
antibody:antigen sites and could therefore not tell anything about the speciﬁc residues im-
portant for binding to IR or IGF-1R. Accurately it predicts a lot of aromatic sidechains to be
in the paratope, and most residues are within the H3 which is the most important region
in the VH domain. This point in the direction that H3 may have a high inﬂuence on the
speciﬁcity.

The APBS plugin in Pymol is used for calculating the surface potentials of the generated
model. It is assumed that the phosphorylated tyrosines are part of a disordered region,
since no structural data is available for that region. This assumption is backed up by the
fact that the antibodies are able to recognize the denatured receptor in the western blot and
the natural occurring receptor in the ELISA, hence the denatured receptor must resemble
the natural receptor in this region. Disordered peptides normally binds in grooves and gets
buried on the antibody. From the solvent accessible surface representation it does not seem
as if there is a groove present in the model between the VH and VL that could contain a
peptide.
The MD simulations for the unstimulated IGF-1R peptide are successfully generated and

the 100 ns long MD simulation where the IGF-1R peptide binds to the antibody almost after
10 ns. It seems that the structure is more open between the VH and VL, which give some
space for the peptide. The simulation gives a interesting insight into what could be the
binding site of the unstimulated IGF-1R peptide but in order to make ﬁnal conclusions from
the simulation, further analysis needs to be made. At the same time it is important to run the
stimulated peptides as well. The next natural step is to analyze antibody:antigen complex
with the IR peptides and set up MD simulations with the antibody:antigen complex with
the phosphorylated peptides.
4.2
Experimental Data is Necessary
Because there is no structural information of the C-terminal tail of either IR or IGF-1R and
the antigen binding site is difﬁcult to predict it was prioritized to create some data experi-
mentally.
The receptor activation ELISA is an assay that is designed to detect the hybrid recep-
tors only. This has been shown by Slaaby, R. 2015, but is only possible with one of the
setups where IGF1R monomer is captured and IR monomer is detected by a speciﬁc anti-
body against pTYR1334 on the IR. Hence, the pTyr of the IR monomer is detected. The other
way where IR is captured and IGF-1R pTyr is detected is not possible because of lack of
speciﬁc antibodies.
From the results in the receptor activation ELISA in this project it is clear that the an-
tibodies are not speciﬁc enough to be used in the assay for the correct purpose. What can
be extracted from the assay is the observation that some antibodies loose their phospho-
speciﬁcity even when a single mutation are reverted back to germline. Thus, these muta-
tions must be essential for the antibody to recognize the active receptors.
As would be expected there is a smaller signal when the IGF-1R is captured and the
cells are stimulated with HI compared to IGF-1 stimulation, which could be explained by
the hybrid receptor’s higher afﬁnity for IGF-1. A higher signal is observed when the IR
is captured and the cells are stimulated with IGF-1 compared to unstimulated. This could
resemble the hybrid receptor, although the difference in signal is not signiﬁcant and the
assay is not receptor speciﬁc, so this can not be concluded.
It is also observed that the speciﬁcity is gone when the CDR regions are reverted back to
germline. Since the CDR regions are the most important in the variable domains it is also
expected that they have the highest impact on the speciﬁcity. It is observed that some of the
mutants only show a difference in OD when either one or the other receptor is captured.
Two mutated antibodies, Y-N in CDR3 and R-S in CDR1 in the VL, resulted in a higher OD
for stimulated receptor when IR is captured.

antibodies that lost the response to the stimulated receptors are plotted and mapped to the
3D structure model. A common feature for the negative mutations are their difference in
residue size between WT and mutant. In all of the mutations either a Ala, Gly, Ser or a
deletion is involved and exchanged by a large residue, such as phenylalanine or tyrosine.
These mutations could create steric clashes which is observed for the Ala - Glu or unfavor-
able buried pockets, which could be the case for the deletion of the phenylalanine. These
large aromatic residues can also be important for aromatic interactions, contributing to the
stability of the molecule. Regarding the phospho-speciﬁcity none of the WT residues that
are mutated are positive amino acids, so the loss in signal is not directly explained by e.g.
a loss of electrostatic interactions between two charged atoms. However, mutated residues
in one site of a molecule can effect another site because everything is coupled and all the
atoms effect each other indirectly. Some of the mutations did not have an effect on the signal
to the stimulated receptors, hence these residues cannot be essential for the speciﬁcity. This
suggest that these sites could be a target for further improvement regarding speciﬁcity but
also afﬁnity and stability. Unfortunately the common mutations within these germlines are
not investigated, but these could also give an insight into which residues that are inﬂuential.
In an ELISA assay it is important to minimize the signal to noise ratio. If the blocking
is not sufﬁcient a high background is observed caused by unspeciﬁc binding directly to the
ELISA plate due to its high afﬁnity for proteins in general. This can be accomplished by us-
ing the correct blocking and washing buffer. The signals of the negative controls, that come
from either Expi293 media or an empty expression vector, are low. But when unspeciﬁc an-
tibodies are used as negative controls a much higher signal is observed. Therefore it would
be necessary to test if these antibodies truly does not bind to anything that is added in the
ELISA steps and subsequently investigate the blocking and washing procedure. In order to
obtain quantitative data from an ELISA assay a saturation curve needs to be performed to
establish the antibody concentration that result in low level of detection in the ELISA and
the concentration of the antibody that leads to saturation of the antigen, the so-called dy-
namic range. From this saturation curve the dissociation constant KD can be obtained. It is
necessary to work with antibody concentrations within the dynamic range to evaluate the
antibody afﬁnity, otherwise if all the antigens are saturated with antibodies the OD values
are only a measure of the antigen concentration. As this is a screening of an antibody library,
semi-quantitative data for the antibodies are sufﬁcient. Hence antigen and antibody concen-
trations are not measured quantitatively. Due to the difference in signal between stimulated
and unstimulated receptors it is assumed that the concentrations of the antibodies are below
the saturation level.
In the western blotting analysis it is found that a single mutation is able to switch the
speciﬁcity between the two receptors with no cross-reactivity to the other receptor. The data
from this experiment is not consistent with the data from the ELISA. It is not possible either
to see in the ELISA that the WT should only be speciﬁc for the stimulated IR and not IGF-
1R as is observed in the western blot. This inconsistency points towards further analysis.
Speculations can be made of the structure of the two receptors in the western blot compared
to the ELISA.
In order to gain more information of the speciﬁcity, the different antibodies should be
puriﬁed and the concentrations determined so the same amount is added to each well in
the ELISA. Then it would be possible to compare the antibodies between each other. All the
ELISA plates contain either IR-D2 or IGF-1R-0226 capture antibody, so the results between
the IR and IGF-1R assays are also affected by factors such as different exposure times for
different ELISA plates.
The experimental results give some insight into what determines the phospho-speciﬁcity.
Much more information could be retrieved in a more complete mutational library of the vari-
able region such as Koenig and colleagues has made (Koenig et al., 2017). Even though the

Chapter 5
Conclusion and Future Perspectives
5.1
Conclusion
The overall aim of this project was to enhance the speciﬁcity of monoclonal antibodies al-
ready known to bind to a speciﬁc antigen. The main focus was on the 0023 antibody from
Novo Nordisk, which was found to bind the phosphorylated IGF-1R in a speciﬁc manner.
It was investigated what the SHMs of the 0023 B cell meant for the speciﬁcity against
the phosphorylated IGF-1R. Interestingly, in a western blotting analysis it was found that
the 0023 antibody binds to the phosphorylated IR, with only minor cross-reactivity to the
IGF-1R. This discovery was in great contrast to the discoveries made previously at Novo
Nordisk.
The small library of single and multiple mutations in the 0023 was analyzed in several
ELISA assays. The majority of the SHMs did not seem to be essential for the phospho-
speciﬁcity, although the SHMs of H3 was not tested, making them a perfect target for alter-
ation in a design process. Two mutations was found to switch the speciﬁcity from the phos-
phorylated IR to the phosphorylated IGF-1R. It is difﬁcult to conclude how these residues
interact with the receptors based on the current knowledge of the antibody and the antigen.
In addition, the structure of 0023 was modeled by homology modeling. This created a
visualization tool and supported in the explanation of the effects of the SHMs. Furthermore,
prediction of the paratope and MD simulations was estimated to help in the understanding
of the antibody:antigen complex. Further analysis needs to be made on this matter.
Even though these SHMs give information that can be used for the engineering of more
speciﬁc antibodies against the IR and IGF-1R, more knowledge is necessary to progress with
this task.
When enhancing the speciﬁcity of an antibody it is important to keep in mind that afﬁn-
ity, stability and solubility are all factors that contribute in an evaluation of a leading anti-
body candidate regarding if the goal is the design of a research tool or a therapeutic drug.

5.2
Future Perspectives
In the short term it is desirable to conﬁrm the location of the binding site of the 0023 WT
antibody. It is believed that the most valuable suggestion of a binding site would come
from molecular dynamics simulations and therefore effort should be made to complete this
task. Mutations made in the 0023 WT could be run subsequently and the binding energy
for the different complexes calculated to see if a mutation could result in a more favorable
interaction energy between antibody and antigen.
A numerous in silico methods are developed regarding the ﬁeld of design and engineer-
ing of antibodies. An interesting tool is the optMAVEn 2.0 which is designing variable anti-
body regions targeting antigen epitopes de novo (Chowdhury, Frederick Allan, and Maranas,
2018). It would be fascinating to see how much this tool could accomplish regarding the de-
sign of antibodies against the phosphorylated tyrosines on IR and IGF-1R.
In the long term where the speciﬁc antibodies are developed they can be used in a vari-
ety of immunoassays, such as ELISA, Western blot, immunocytochemistry, ﬂow cytometry
and immunoprecipitation to shed light on the biological role of the IR/IGF-1R hybrid recep-
tor. Although the target of these antibodies are intracellular many procedures exists on the
interactions between antibodies and intracellular antigens. It requires a permeable cell wall
of the cells being investigated. Recombinant expression of single-chain variable fragment
(scFv) inside the cells, referred to as intrabodies, could also ensure an intracellular antigen
detection (Heng and Cao, 2005). In that sense there exits almost no limit for the assays that
could be developed with these antibodies.
