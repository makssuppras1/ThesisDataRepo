Abstract
The new generation of mobile networks envisions to venture into areas which have never
before been associated with this particular field of engineering. At the same time it seeks
to deliver never before experienced levels of service to the users, both in terms of the
offered datarates and the conditions under which the services are provided. This imposes
very stringent requirements on the technologies that will be used to build these networks.
The standards developed with the fifth generation of mobile networks in mind have to
face these high expectations. One of the areas to which particular attention was paid is
the availability of services regardless of location. As the users should be provided with
the high bitrates even when the signal conditions are poor, it was necessary to address
the issues arising in the areas notorious for the high interference: cell edges. One of the
ways that are currently being investigated in terms of usefulness to the ultimate goal of
perfect accessibility is machine learning. The advancements in artificial intelligence are
changing the world at an increasingly high rate. It is therefore not unreasonable to try
and apply these techniques to mobile networks. The focus of this thesis was to investi-
gate established machine learning algorithms in the context of applying them to reduce
interference by controlling beams of telecommunication antennae. The study involved
theoretical analysis of which algorithms may be used for this purpose and simulations
of their operation. Three different implementations of machine learning techniques were
measured against a simple heuristic algorithm with no learning capabilities. A number
of use-cases with increasing complexity were evaluated. While one of the approaches did
not manage to produce satisfactory results even in the simplest scenarios, the other two
algorithms performed reasonably well, increasing the overall quality of the signal expe-
rienced by the users. However, the outcomes of the simple heuristic were comparable,
which put some doubt in the applicability of machine learning techniques in these cases.
The conclusions drawn from the results point to the technology being useful in pico and
femto-cell applications.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
As the world grows more and more connected, mobile networks are becoming an in-
creasingly important part of the daily lives of most people in the developed countries.
They have already revolutionised the way content is consumed. The users now take it
for granted that they are able to look up some important information online, reach out
to a friend or even watch a high resolution video to help kill boredom during the long
morning commute to work, regardless of when and where they might feel the need to
do so. Even today, consumers in most countries spend about twice as much time on
mobile devices as on desktops.[1] This trend is expected to continue, with equipment
prices falling and developing countries catching up very quickly in terms of market pene-
tration of such devices. The next generation of networks is expected to provide services
to over 20 billion human-interactive terminals.[2] What is more, the rapidly developing
idea of the Internet of Things (IoT) will introduce new types of traffic - pieces of home
equipment, sensor networks and other autonomously working machines exchanging data
in small bundles. Over 1 trillion such devices is predicted to be connected to the mobile
network by the year 2020.[2]
Figure 1.1: Mobile traffic increase in recent years[3].
==PAGE:15==
1 Introduction
2
As a result, the technology of the next generation of mobile networks will face many
challenges to keep up with the overwhelming growth of the demand. One of the issues
that comes up is the performance at the edges of the cell. Mobile networks are inherently
non-uniform - as the medium the data gets transmitted on is the electromagnetic field
the conditions change dramatically with the distance from the transmitter and under
the influence of nearby transmissions. Furthermore, due to the cellular nature of the
mobile communication system, the edge of the covered area is the place where not only
is the signal from the serving tower the weakest, but also the interfering influence of the
nearby towers the highest.
Figure 1.2: Signal quality in cells - darker colour represents lower quality.
The users should not experience any connectivity issues, such as drops in transfer speeds,
simply because they happen to find themselves in an area where several cells overlap.
With the mobile network cells getting ever smaller and more densely packed, the problem
with inter-cell interference becomes even more problematic. Since the issue has been
taken into account for a long time, solutions have been developed to mitigate these
problems. As the interference is caused by signals not intended for the user, a simple
way to get rid of it is to try and only send the transmission to the location of the receiver.
The apparent simplicity is deceptive though, as many issues arise in the implementation
phase.
The solutions need to adapt constantly to the changing conditions and act in real time,
in order to bring any measurable change to the user experience. Due to the fact, that the
next generation will be heavily based on flexible technologies, such as Software Defined
Networking (SDN) and Network Function Virtualisation (NVF)[2], a natural extension
would be to introduce software into the configuration process of the networks. This
is realised in the notion of Self Organising Networks (SONs). Here is where machine
learning can bring a true revolution. A clever enough algorithm can use the feedback it
gets from the network (such as signal quality measurements) to figure out how exactly
different network parameters affect the transmission quality.
Thanks to this, it can
control the settings - for example of the antenna beam to focus it on a selected user,
thereby reducing the interference for all others.

1.1 Scope of the project
3
1.1
Scope of the project
These considerations led to formulation of a scope for this project. On a general level,
the goals might be described as follows.
1. Finding a way to represent and model signal quality for a user. A way of describing
to an algorithm how the parameter changes affect the users is indispensable in a
project like this. The model needs to allow to change the shape of the antenna
beam (in a simpler case just the downtilt parameter) and the resulting metric needs
to describe the quality of signal a user experiences.
2. Proper machine learning techniques need to be chosen from the cornucopia of
methods available in the literature right now. The available algorithms need to
be assessed in terms of usefulness to the task of controlling an antenna in a mo-
bile network environment. Furthermore, the techniques should be compared with
an engineered approach - an algorithm that does not take advantage of machine
learning.

Mobile networks

2.1
3G
As the newest solutions in the modern mobile network industry build heavily upon their
predecessors it is useful to discuss the architecture and capabilities of the older versions.
After both the first - Global System for Mobile Communications (GSM) - and the second
- General Packet Radio Service (GPRS) - generations of mobile networks proved to be
extremely successful, efforts were made to improve the capabilities of mobile devices.
With this purpose in mind the Universal Mobile Telecommunications System (UMTS)
was created. Since full backwards compatibility was required to reduce deployment costs,
the design relied on circuit switched connections for voice transmissions, whereas data
was transferred in a packet-switched manner. While the core network relied on ideas
from the previous generations a real novelty was the radio access network in the form of
Universal Terrestrial Radio Access Network (UTRAN) which was a true revolution in the
telecommunications world. The communication utilised a new air interface method called
Wideband Code Division Multiple Access (WCDMA) - because unlike in GSM the users
were separated by using unique assigned codes. Furthermore, a much wider bandwidth
- 5MHz - allowed to achieve much higher transfer speeds - up to 384kbps, compared to
20kbps for GPRS (without using the Enhanced Data rates for GSM Evolution (EDGE)
technology). [4] It consists of:
• User Equipment (UE) - any UMTS capable device used to communicate through
the network; used to be called Mobile Station (MS) in previous standards.
• Node B (NB) - communicates directly with the UEs; performs modulation and
coding; corresponds to Base Transceiver Station (BTS) in GSM.
• Radio Network Controller (RNC) - supervises NBs connected to it; performs
encryption, resource management and in some cases facilitates mobility manage-
ment; controls NBs’ power.
In the core of the network, the standard mimics that of GSM/GPRS with small enhance-
ments. In this way it was extraordinarily easy to deploy UMTS by simply connecting the
new Radio Network Subsystem (RNS) - RNCs and NBs - to the existing infrastructure.
The changes involved new protocols and interfaces between the devices.

2.2
4G
As far as cellular telephony is concerned, the most advanced technology available to date
is the Long-Term Evolution (LTE), which constitutes the fourth generation of mobile
networks. It is a natural extension of the UMTS that was the basis of the third generation.
Its principles were laid out in 3GPP release 8 and amended in the ones that followed.
To address the limitations of previous technology, it required a completely new physical
architecture as it put an end to circuit switched connections, relying solely on packet
switching. Voice traffic is either handled using Voice over Long-Term Evolution (VoLTE)
protocol, or simply falls back to the older generation architecture, which is usually left
in place when LTE is deployed by the service provider. It brings the simplicity of the IP
based communication to all the interfaces in the core network. Furthermore, a completely
new air interface design was implemented for this generation, which allowed to better
utilise the spectrum, resulting in higher transfer speeds. The coding scheme is chosen
based on the radio conditions from among QPSK, 16-QAM and 64-QAM. The radio
==PAGE:19==
2.2 4G

transmission relies on the Orthogonal Frequency Division Multiplexing (OFDM) which
spreads the transmission across many 15kHz sub-carriers (summing up to maximally
20MHz), that is sending the data-stream simultaneously on many frequencies within the
band. The fact that all the cells use the entire frequency band means that previously
existing methods of avoiding inter-cell interference are not applicable. This is because
they relied on spreading the available frequencies between different cells. Obviously, new
techniques, such as Inter-Cell Interference Coordination (ICIC) needed to be developed
and the details will be elaborated on in section 2.3.2. Another important feature of
LTE is the concept of the Multiple-Input Multiple-Output (MIMO) transmission, which
involves sending numerous data streams to a single user at the same time. This is done
by using multiple antennae for both the transmitter and the receiver.[4]
2.2.1
Network architecture
Units that the UE connects to over the air interface are called Evolved Nodes B (eNB).
They are the most complex devices in the network and in comparison to their 3G equiv-
alents perform many more functions. In addition to, conspicuously, dealing with trans-
mission over the radio interface, they also handle mobility management, load balancing
and ensure quality of service (QoS) and perform interference management. A special
interface between neighbouring eNBs, called X2, allows for faster information exchange
e.g. in the case of handovers. Together, all the eNBs, UEs and the air interfaces com-
prise the Evolved Universal Terrestrial Radio Access Network (E-UTRAN).
The core network of LTE, also known as Evolved Packet Core (EPC), is comprised of

2.2.2
Radio access
The unique feature of the LTE technology is its air interface which utilises OFDM
principles. As mentioned before, there are many simultaneous data streams per user
transferred over the same medium. In this way, the datarate the user is served can be
high, despite each separate stream having much lower speed. This is done in order to
avoid the issues connected with the multipath transmission that occur at higher transfer
rates. The signal gets reflected from various surfaces present in the environment and
therefore each path reaches the destination at a different moment.
That introduces
additional interference, especially problematic in the case of high speed transmissions.
The way in which this split into these separate sub-carriers is by means of Inverse Fast
Fourier Transform (IFFT). Each sub-carrier is orthogonal to all others. The output of the
IFFT is a time domain signal, which can be converted again into frequency domain by a
Fast Fourier Transform at the receiving end. In this way, the signal can be reconstructed
back from the sub-carriers into a single stream. The process is depicted in figure 2.3.

• Carrier aggregation - combining multiple bands in order to achieve higher band-

• More complex MIMO - in addition to the MIMO schemes defined previously,
more advanced scenarios are possible (up to 8x8 in downlink and 4x4 in uplink).
• Coordinated Multipoint (CoMP) - a more advanced way to mitigate inter-cell

2.3.2
Cell-edge interference reduction methods
As mentioned before, LTE and its extensions usually have the neighbouring cells use
the same frequency band. Aware of this issue, the authors of the standards addressed
them by introducing a series of progressively advanced methods to increase cell-edge
performance. The standards have been specified, yet not many systems actually include
their implementations, especially for the newest techniques.[4]
• Inter-Cell Interference Coordination (ICIC) The first 3GPP release for LTE
(Release 8) already contained the specification for ICIC as it was anticipated that
cell-edge performance would be an issue.
In this scheme the eNBs coordinate to choose which resource blocks will have
higher power in which cell. Only users far from the centre would require higher
transmission power. Therefore, if the neighbouring eNBs agreed to use different
RBs for the cell-edge users interference could be avoided, since all other RBs’ range
would actually be smaller and the cells would overlap less. Effectively, this creates
a two tiered cell with some RB designated for users near the centre and others
for the ones further away. This scheme is also called Fractional Frequency Reuse
(FFR).
• Enhanced Inter-Cell Interference Coordination (eICIC) LTE-Advanced
brought by the idea of Heterogenous Networks (HetNets). As the area covered
by a micro-cell fully overlaps with that of a neighbouring macro-cell the regular
ICIC scheme would be rendered useless. Hence, Release 10 introduced the eICIC
idea. Instead of coordinating just the power on a particular RB, in this case some
time frames would be left completely empty in the time domain by the macro cell.
They can be used by the micro-cells within, effectively taking away some of the
transmission capacity of the larger cell to provide a chance for the smaller ones to
operate.
==PAGE:26==
2.3 5G
13
• Coordinated Multipoint (CoMP) The most advanced set of methods to avoid
interference for now is Release 11’s CoMP. It provides three different ways to
address the issue of interference and takes into account the special traits of both
homogeneous and heterogeneous networks.
The common point for all of them
is the fact that they need to introduce a centralised decision-taking, which goes
against the architectural principles of LTE where eNBs are mostly autonomous.
However, with ideas such as Cloud Radio Access Network (C-RAN) these kinds of
implementations become possible.
– Joint Transmission (JT) The same signal can be transmitted by multiple
Remote Radioheads (RRHs) on the same RB. In this way the signals can
actually interference constructively and improve the signal quality. This is
the most advanced method among the three.
– Dynamic Point Selection (DPS) This method addresses mostly moving
users. The data for transmission is made available to several RRHs, as in
the case of JT, but is only transmitted by one at a time.
However, very
quick handovers are possible, so that the signal is always transmitted from
the source that would provide the best quality of transmission.[10]
– Coordinated Scheduling/Coordinated Beamforming (CS/CB) The
last possibility utilises also the newest advancements in the antennae tech-
nology. Since the RRHs are now often equipped with antenna arrays, it is
possible to change how the signal is supplied to each element in the array
and therefore change the shape of the beam. This idea relates to the con-
cept of MIMO and will be further elaborated on in section 2.3.3. When the
beam-forming and scheduling are coordinated by the neighbouring cells, it is
possible to reduce the interference a particular user experiences.
2.3.3
Multiple-Input Multiple-Output
One of the ways the transmission of the air medium can be improved is MIMO com-
munication, in which there are both multiple transmit and receive antennae. This can
mitigate some of the common issues in wireless communication. For example, assuming
the signals coming from different antennae are uncorrelated, such a transmission would
be immune to Rayleigh fading, since the signal would have to fade on all paths it is
transmitted, which is much less likely than in the Single-Input Single-Output (SISO)
case. What is more, since the received signals across multiple antennae can be combined
using signal processing techniques, the correct information can be retrieved even from a
lower quality signal. An example of such algorithm Maximal-ratio Combining (MRC).
MIMO is particularly relevant in the case of mmWave communication, as the antennae
used in this frequency range are very small and hence, can be easily arranged in arrays
of many elements.
Several cases of MIMO can be considered.[11]
==PAGE:27==
2.4 Network simulations
14
• Spatial Multiplexing This is the scheme that is most commonly associated with
MIMO. It involves transmitting multiple symbols with the same power and on
the same carrier, effectively increasing the spectral efficiency of the spectrum. It
assumes that the propagation environment is rich enough for the paths the sig-
nals take to be uncorrelated and therefore the signals to be distinguishable. This
assumption is now a subject of research.[11]
• Spatial Diversity In this scheme the same signals is send on different paths in or-
der to combat small-scale fading (such as Rayleigh fading). The signal, received on
numerous antennae, can be reconstructed more efficiently using signal processing.
It is important to note, that effects such as delay spread can be taken advantage of
in this scheme. Delay spread is the phenomenon caused by the fact that the paths
have different lengths and therefore the same signal can arrive at the receiving
antenna at different times.
• Beam-forming This is the MIMO scheme particularly important for the CoMP
technique. The same signal is transmitted through numerous antennae, but with
different amplitudes and phases. It results in the antenna array becoming similar
to a single antenna, but with a controllable beam shape due to constructive and
destructive interference. This can be done both at the transmitting as well as the
receiving end, and allows to improve the propagation conditions.
• Hybrid Precoding It is a combination of spatial multiplexing with beam-forming.
The signal goes through a complicated process of precoding using advanced signal
processing techniques. The result is a very flexible transmission system. Again, due
to the ease of producing large antenna arrays this scheme is particularly valuable
in the case of mmWave communication and therefore also 5G.
2.4
Network simulations
In order to be able to properly assess viability of various solutions in a mobile network it
is indispensable to conduct simulations. The cost of hardware and skilled labour makes
it not feasible to deploy networks without prior analysis, that usually involves numerical
computations. What is more, whenever a new type of technology is discussed it can
be very difficult to assess the influence a particular change might have on the network
overall. Hence, all layers of the communication system need to be modelled in one way
or another before deploying. In particular, a physical channel model is necessary in order
to measure the effect of the changes of various transmission parameters on the interfer-
ence experienced by a user. There are many models available, with different levels of
complexity and capabilities. Depending on which signal metrics need to be assessed the
simulation can go into various level of detail. Ideally, a model that describes reality the
most closely should be used. However, as resources are limited it can happen that only
a much simpler version can be used to achieve the results in a reasonable time.

Fading
The pathloss model describes the changes in the mean signal strength with the increase
of distance. There are other factors which influence the strength of the received signal.
Simulations are usually done to see exactly what happens at any specific instance of time.
Therefore, it is also necessary to add a randomised fading term to equation 2.4. Such
a term, called shadow fading is connected with objects obstructing the propagation.[19]
The fact that it is modelled as a log-normal distribution with the mean specified by the
pathloss equation helps account for the complex and changing nature of the communi-
cation environment. Due to the fact that this kind of fading occurs on a much larger
time-scale than the changes in phase and amplitude related to modulation, it is referred
to as slow fading.
Fast fading on the other hand happens on comparable time-scales. It can be modelled
using the so-called Ricean fading.[19] It is caused by the fact that the signal received by
the user could have taken many different paths to get to that particular point (because
of scattering and reflection from obstacles). The signal then interferes with itself leading
to fading. A special case of this kind of fading, in which there is no LoS is referred to as
Rayleigh fading. Due to their complex nature they are usually modelled stochastically
as well.
Modelling noise
Among many sources of noise and interference in a wireless communication system the
thermal noise is the one that is relatively the easiest to model. After all, an electromag-
netic wave propagating through the air channel is nothing more than electrons travelling

Self Organising Networks
In order to be able to provide the necessary level of flexibility to adapt to the requirements
imposed on a network at any particular moment new ideas needed to be introduced and
the general trend toward virtualisation in networking came up with just the right ones.
• Network Function Virtualisation (NFV) Instead of using dedicated hardware
to perform specific functions in the network (such as firewall, switch, QoS assur-
ance) it is now possible to deploy a Virtual Machine (VM) that would take care
of this particular function. As a result, is it possible to very quickly change con-
figurations or add more computing power dedicated to a particular purpose. New
VMs can be very quickly deployed and brought down allowing for much greater
flexibility than ever before.
• Software Defined Networking In order to allow for easier and faster reconfigu-
ration of networks the control and data plane can be separated. In this way, one or
more controllers decide on particular routing policies based on various parameters
of the dataflows, allowing to change the behaviour on the fly. The enactment of
these policies is left to SDN applications (corresponding to switches in the tradi-
tional networking architecture), which can be fully virtualised.
The future core network of 5G systems is expected to be embracing these technologies
to a very large degree. This trend towards flexibility culminates in the idea of Self Or-
ganising Networks (SONs). Moving all the functionality to software enables not only
very fast adaptation, but also opens the door for automation. It is possible to design
algorithms which would ensure that the network itself chooses the most optimal policy
based on current requirements and state of the network.[23] What is more, Artificial

Machine learning in Self Organising Networks
As the industry pushes to incorporate the benefits of machine learning in virtually all
its branches, the mobile networks do not stay far behind. A great deal of effort is being
put into equipping SONs with the newest AI tools available. [24]
There are three main areas of focus for ML in SONs.
1. Self-Configuration When deploying a mobile network, many parameters of the
devices need to be properly set up. An experienced network operator has to deter-
mine the best configuration for the requirements of a particular deployment (such
as IP and radio configuration or global network policies), taking into account all
the factors that might affect performance. The most common use case is deploying
a new eNB. In order to facilitate this process, the base station can perform all, or
at least some of the configurations on its own.
2. Self-Optimisation The next step after deploying a new cell with the right con-
figuration is to keep the desired level of performance. The environment constantly
changes and the demands on the network are ever more challenging, so there is little
room for suboptimal operation. Therefore, the configuration needs to adapt to the
current conditions. This involves for example antenna’s beam-forming coefficients
(as mentioned in section 2.3.3) or handover parameters for the cell.
3. Self-Healing Finally, an unavoidable fact is that systems, no matter how well de-
signed and maintained, will break. Therefore, it is necessary to ensure that a single
failure will not affect the rest of the network and the impact on users’ experience
will be minimal. This can be achieved in a number of ways, including monitoring
the performance closely to detect failures before they happen and implementing
policies to allow to recover as smoothly as possible. However, usually the diag-
nosis and recovery is a complex task for a qualified engineer. The techniques for
self-healing provide a number of ways to automate this process saving time and
allowing for faster recovery.

Machine learning
This chapter describes the Machine Learning (ML) principles and techniques that can be
used in order to optimise performance of mobile networks. It starts with an introduction
of the general concepts in ML. The techniques used for the project are described in more
detail with particular attention paid to reinforcement learning, which is the main area
of focus of this thesis.
3.1
Introductory concepts
ML is a rapidly evolving field, which attracts the best and brightest researchers and
engineers. It is a sub-area of artificial intelligence (AI) which deals with algorithms that
can improve their performance based either on experience or data from the past. It
has already disrupted many areas of business - companies such as Facebook and Google
gain immense profits from their highly personalised advertisement campaigns, which
are mostly based on analysing the huge amounts of data available on each user. What
is more, the techniques start to be used in many fields and will indubitably continue
to push the boundaries of what is possible in the future. Even though the particular
algorithms become more and more advanced, the general principles have hardly changed
since the very first ideas were developed. The methods can be divided into groups based
on what exactly is used to achieve improved performance over time - learning. The best
way to introduce the differences is to describe the types of tasks each group is best suited
for.[25] The areas of focus are also analysed in terms of usefulness to achieving the goals
of the project, as described in section 1.1.
3.1.1
Supervised learning
If the task involves predicting an outcome quantity based on several other quantities,
with both input and output data available, it would fall within the class of supervised
learning problems. The supervision here means, that examples of correct outputs are
accessible and the machine can learn by replicating the existing pattern. There are two
distinct types of problems addressed by these algorithms:
• Classification In this class of problems the data consists of properties of some
objects - those can be the pixel values of an image, physical qualities such as
dimensions and colours or any other possible feature that can be quantified in
a machine readable format as a discrete value. Furthermore, each object in the
dataset is assigned to a class which is known at the point when the classifier is
==PAGE:35==
3.1 Introductory concepts
22
trained (usually by a human with specific domain knowledge). The algorithms can
then infer which input quantities influence the classification and, given enough ex-
amples of correct allotment, achieve proficiency at the classification task. Common
applications would include e.g.:
– finding out what kind of an animal is depicted in a picture,
– given patients’ medical records, determining if they suffer from a particular
illness,
– in a mobile network knowing information about users’ recent movements,
select those who will change the cell they are connected to (each user is
classified either as likely to move or not).
Not only can the application of supervised learning save valuable work hours of
qualified specialists (as computers can take over the classification), but also it can
be improved if the machines find patterns in the data that were not obvious before.
• Regression While classification dealt with discrete outputs - in the extreme case
even binary (something will/will not happen), regression problems are those, in
which an continuous output function needs to be modelled.
Examples of such
problems are:
– predicting the value of stocks of a particular company based on information
about the state of the economy and previous stock values,
– given data about a patient, forecast how many months they are likely to
survive with a particular type of cancer,
– knowing information about users in a mobile network, predict how much
capacity will be required from a particular cell at a specific time.
The common factor in these two kinds of problems is the fact, that historical data is
available and can be used to make predictions for new data in the future.
Regression is the technique that can be utilised to create a channel model to fulfil the
first goal of the project. When given enough data, a regression algorithm can provide
an accurate model of how changes of some parameters influence the signal quality a user
experiences.
3.1.2
Unsupervised learning
Unsupervised learning can be contrasted with its supervised counterpart in that there
is no previous correct output examples to learn from. In this case, the algorithms are
tasked with finding patterns in the data themselves.
This area of ML is introduced
for completeness, but will not be further considered in this thesis due to its limited
usefulness for any of the goals.
The best way to explain the algorithms is to write about them in terms of tasks they
are designed to perform.
They can include:
==PAGE:36==
3.1 Introductory concepts
23
• Clustering Given some input data, groups of similar objects can be found. It can
seem comparable to classification, but in this case there is no clear definition of a
class. Therefore, an algorithm needs to determine ways in which the input data
is grouped into clusters with similar features. An example in the area of mobile
networks might be trying to group users to offer them specific services, with the
criteria for similarity including data usage, type of content accessed, frequency of
voice calls etc.
• Density estimation It is to clustering what regression is to classification.
It
aims to model an underlying probability density function, out of which the data
was sampled (with noise). It therefore gives a continuous output of how the data
is spread in the attribute space. In mobile networks it can be used to find the
locations where the users are likely to gather in greater numbers.
• Anomaly detection This class of algorithms is designed to identify data-points
that in some way differ from all others. Therefore, they establish an idea of what
normal behaviour looks like and search for all deviations from the norm. A classi-
cal example is fraud detection for credit card transactions, where the bank tries to
determine if a given transaction is not significantly different from a user’s normal
shopping habits and notify them in case their credit card was stolen. In telecommu-
nication these algorithms may be used to detect e.g. failing hardware or malicious
traffic patterns such as Denial-of-Service (DoS) attacks.
• Association mining Another important area of interest is finding dependencies
in data. For example, recommendation engines (e.g. in the popular streaming
platform Netflix) find films a user might be interested in, based on those he or she
liked in the past.
• Dimensionality reduction As the interest in ML grows ever higher companies
tend to gather all kinds of data, thinking of possible uses for it. However, the huge
data-banks require enormous amount of expensive computing power to process.
Therefore, it is advantageous to try and determine which particular parameters
influence the outcome the most, and only consider them on further stages of pro-
cessing. This is very useful for example in lossy compression, where the audiovisual
data can be stored using less memory, while still retaining reasonably good quality.
3.1.3
Reinforcement learning
The last class of methods, Reinforcement Learning (RL), can be by some considered to
be within the domain of supervised learning. The premise is that the algorithm chooses
an action which results in either a reward or punishment. Based on the feedback received
from the environment the machine can determine the best action to take in a particular
situation, given enough tries to learn.
However, the goals (achieving which leads to
a reward) are usually very complex, so multiple actions need to be taken in a correct
sequence.
For example, a robot navigating a labyrinth needs to determine the right
==PAGE:37==
3.2 Techniques
24
sequence of turns to reach the exit, where it will be rewarded. This complexity means
that the algorithms need to be much more advanced and work very differently from
those used in the case of supervised learning. A more detailed explanation of how this
works will be given in section 3.2.4. Examples of using the technique might include
computers learning to play games - the famous Alpha Go algorithm, which won in the
Chinese game of ”Go” with the world champion Lee Sedol of South Korea, is based on
RL principles.[26]
This class of algorithms provides exactly the kind of capability needed to control the
shape of the beam. Based on what an RL controller learns about the environment it
can chose the best actions to change the parameters of the antennae to provide the best
possible quality of transmission.

3.2.2
Overfitting and model selection
When training a model, regardless of what particular tool is used, the goal is to match
the dataset as closely as possible. However, every dataset would be influenced by the
noise in a different way. Therefore, there is always a risk that when the training process
is finished, the model will align itself to some of the noise in the data in addition to the
underlying function that it is trying to match. Hence, when used to predict outcomes
for a new set of incomes, the results might be unsatisfactory. This phenomenon is called
==PAGE:41==
3.2 Techniques
28
over-fitting and finding out ways to prevent it is a major area of research in ML.
When trying to evaluate the performance of a given model, problems often arise from
the fact that the datasets are either too small or too noisy. Ideally, one part of the
dataset would be used to train the model and another, completely separate one, to test
whether the predictions are plausible and to assess how much the resulting model is
affected by over-fitting. Unfortunately, often there is no way to obtain more data or
improve its quality. Splitting the dataset into training and testing sets would result in
too few data points for the model to learn properly. Even though it is advantageous to
train the model on as large a dataset as possible (the whole dataset), it is not wise to
use a model whose performance has not been tested. Hence, validation techniques were
developed. Instead of training the model just once, it can be trained on different subsets
of the dataset. The remaining data-points would be used for validation. By averaging
the obtained errors, a much more accurate estimate of the real error of the model can
be obtained. There are two main variants of these techniques, so-called cross-validation
(CV) techniques: K-fold cross-validation and Leave-one-out cross-validation (LOO-CV).

when it comes to providing an accurate measure of model’s performance.

performance. This method gives the most accurate approximation of the real error,
but also takes much longer to complete. therefore, it is only advisable to use it in
the case when the dataset is extremely small.

of the error function.

Backpropagation
Due to the huge number of iterations a NN has to go through to learn properly, it
is indispensable that the gradient calculation is as efficient as possible. This is where
the notion of error backpropagation comes to play. At the output layer, the gradient
can be computed using the error function. However, the influence of this change on the
neurons in the preceding layers is inconspicuous. Hence, the calculated gradients are sent
back through the network, with new values being computed at each layer and propagated
further back. The process terminates when the input layer is reached. Therefore, a single
iteration of training involves two passes through the network. First, the feed-forward -
to compute the prediction of the model. This is used to calculate the error, which in
turn is backpropagated, so that the gradient for each weight can be computed. The step
ends with an update of the values of the weights and the process can continue.[27]
Stopping criteria
In theory the learning process could continue forever, so it is necessary to provide proper
stopping criteria for the algorithm. Usually a desired threshold for the error is chosen,
so that the process can terminate when the performance of the network is satisfactory
(keeping in mind that the error should be measured on a separate test dataset). However,
for practical reasons a limit on the number of iterations is also chosen, as it is difficult
to determine how well a network will perform at a given task without empirical tests. It
is also worth noting, that due to the fact, that a dataset of limited size is used, training
for too many iterations may cause over-fitting, if the same data-points are used many
times.
3.2.4
Implementations of Reinforcement Learning
Q-learning is an important idea in the study of reinforcement learning. The standard
formulation of an RL problem involves an actor interacting with an environment.[28]
The actor chooses an action a from a discrete set A. The chosen ouput changes the
state s of the environment to one of the members of a discrete set S. This is commu-
nicated to the actor as some input signal i and a reinforcement signal r, also known as
the reward. The goal of the reinforcement learning process is to explore the changes in
the environment based on actions taken when in a specific state. Then it is possible to
adjust the behaviour of the actor in such a way as to obtain a maximal possible reward.
The mapping of states to action is known as the actor’s policy. The obvious advantage
of this approach over the similar concept of supervised learning is the fact, that the
training occurs while performing the action. In a way, it is similar to hands-on training
courses, which are so effective for human learners.
==PAGE:46==
3.2 Techniques

Figure 3.4: Standard Reinforcement Learning model.
Markov Decision Process(MDP)
The general formulation of the RL problem leads to the idea of a delayed reward. Since
the next state of the environment depends on the previous one, the policy can be thought
of as a series of actions that the actor takes. The reward will rarely be awarded immedi-
ately on taking the correct action in a given state. For example, if the task is to solve a
labyrinth puzzle, the actor needs to move in the correct order and will only be informed
about the success when it reaches the exit. All the previous actions were necessary to
get there, but only the final one results in a reward. Such a problem is modelled using
the so called Markov Decision Processes (MDPs).
The elements of the MDP are:
• a set of states S,
• a set of actions A,
• a reward function R : S × A →R,
• a state transition function T : S × A →Π(S),
where each member of Π(S) is a probability distribution over S. T(s, a, s′) specifies the
probability of transitioning to a particular state s′ after taking the action a, while the
environment is in state s. The assumption of MDPs is that the state transition function
does not depend on previous states or actions, only on the current ones.[28] This is called
the Markov property.
Q-learning
In most cases the exact shape of the transition function T is not known to the model.
Therefore, two possible approaches are available:

Exploration vs. Exploitation dilemma
The issue that immediately comes to mind when examining equation 3.17 is the fact that

actions truly be examined. This issue is closely related to avoiding local minima in a
regular optimisation problem.

taking advantage of the acquired knowledge cannot be completely abandoned in favour

4.1
Machine learning workflow
Every machine learning problem can be examined using different techniques and ap-
proaches. However, the basic workflow for most problems stays the same, regardless of
the complexity and task it is supposed to carry out. It is depicted in figure 4.1.
Figure 4.1: Basic machine learning workflow.
All the steps are equally important for the successful completion of the task.
• Data preparation In order to train the model a large enough amount of quality
data is required. Therefore, this step often includes gathering the information in a
proper way. Preparing the attributes is another important task - it is necessary to
choose which aspects of the modelled phenomenon to include and to find the best
possible way to present it to the algorithm. Furthermore, advanced techniques,
such as Principal Component Analysis (PCA) and other compression mechanisms,
can be used to reduce the dimensionality of the data, so that the same information
can be conveyed in a smaller number of attributes. The intention is to create less
complex models, which should be easier to train and often perform better.
• Data modelling Every task requires a different ML technique to be used. A wide
range of algorithms offered by ML, described in detail in section 3.1, ensures that
different problems can be tackled in a similar way. It is for the engineer to decide
which approach best suits a particular situation. Very often coming up with a new

way to apply a known algorithm is the ingenious step resulting in pushing the field
forward. Obviously, there is usually more than one way to approach a problem
and therefore trial and error is sometimes necessary to obtain the best results.
• Evaluation The only way to ensure that the obtained model can actually be used
in practice is to thoroughly evaluate it. This can be accomplished for example by
testing the solution in a real world scenario. That, however, is very often infeasible,
so evaluation techniques such as Cross Validation (CV) - mentioned in section 3.2.2
- can be used. Furthermore, this step can also include a proper visualisation of the
results as it can be sometimes difficult to assess the performance. The evaluation
phase can help choose the best models, as in the case of CV technique or to assess
whether a particular approach can be used to tackle a given problem. On many
occasions, lack of promising results can end up in getting back to the drawing
board and starting the whole process from scratch.

Goal 1

3. Perform several rounds of linear regression on the dataset, using different basis

only allows to measure SINR values. The advantage is the simplicity it provides, as it
adds no computational overhead to the already time consuming process of training a
Reinforcement Learning Algorithm.

4.4.1
Simple Q-learning
The first attempt realised the idea of Q-learning without paying attention to the fact,
that in the case of a mobile network both the state space and the output were continuous.
The implementation follows the reasoning from section 3.2.4. A table for all the Q-values
is initialised with zeros. Then, based on the reward obtained from the environment the
Q value at the [st, at] is updated. The update rule (equation 3.17) also takes into account
the Q-value of the state the controller ended in, after performing the action. This is to
account for the future rewards.
The exploration vs. exploitation dilemma was solved by taking a random action with
a small probability to encourage the algorithm to explore the action space more. The
probability decreases with the number of episodes, so that the previously learned be-
haviours can be taken advantage of and the solution can converge.
The parameters of the Q-learning algorithm have been defined as follows:
• State space: the state space has been defined as the current tilt value. In other
words, the algorithm only knows at what angle the antenna is transmitting. The
tilt of the antenna is only allowed to be set to a specific value from the set: [6, 9,
12, 15, 18]
• Action space: there are only three possible actions to be taken: stay at the same
angle, go one step up or go one step down. Obviously, it is impossible to decrease
the tilt when it is already at the lowest, nor increase it in the highest state.
• Reward function: in order to be able to optimise the cell-edge performance, but
not decrease the quality for the users in other locations the reward function is
defined as follows.
r = mean(SINR) + 2 SINRedge
(4.6)
The SINR for the 5 percentile corresponds roughly to the users at the cell edge.
Using the mean of the SINR for all users assures that everyone is taken care of.
The factor 2 in the formula is there to make both values comparable and was
chosen empirically. In order to be able to compare them better the SINR values
are converted back from dB to a dimensionless value.
Such a definition of the state and action space is the minimal acceptable solution which
would have any chance of working.
That is why it is interesting to compare how it
measures against more powerful counterparts.
Analysis
The fact that the current SINR value is not part of the state space means that the
algorithm is effectively blind to the state of the channel. Therefore, it can be said to be
based on the assumption that the environment never changes (or changes very slowly).
If there is a direct correlation between just the tilt value and the performance it should
still perform reasonably well. The biggest advantage is the fact that the Q-table is very

small. With just 5 states and 3 possible actions it is an array of just only 15 Q-values.
This means it is easy to explore the entire space and find a good approximation of the
actual rewards through the Q-values.

4.4.4
Simple non-ML controller
The usefulness of the ML approach has to be evaluated against the standard way of
developing intelligent algorithms. That is why it is particularly interesting to develop a
simple algorithm based on common mobile networks knowledge against which to compare
the previous solutions.
The algorithm is a simple state machine, as depicted in figure 4.16. It uses the same
reward function as the other algorithms as its input. In every step 10 measurements are
performed and the mean value is used to decide which action to take. This allows for
greater stability in a volatile environment.
The algorithm works as follows:
Figure 4.16: State machine showing the operation of the algorithm.
1. Start by measuring the reward. If it is within a reasonable margin, that means
the tilt still has the optimal value and therefore no action is taken. Conspicuously,
that need not be the case, as it is possible that the changes in the environment
would allow for some optimisation, without affecting the SINR for the current
==PAGE:71==
4.4 Desiging an ML-based controller
58
setting. That, however, is highly unlikely and therefore can be ignored in this
simple algorithm.
Should a noticeable change be detected, the optimisation process can start.
2. The first action is to increase the tilt and check how that influences the SINR. The
value keeps being increased as long as the reward increases as well.
3. The next step is to try to decrease the tilt. The lowering of the beam continues
until the SINR value starts to drop. Then, the state returns to the previous positon
(the one that had the highest SINR up to this point) and the machine goes back
to checking if any significant change occurs.
Additionally, as a kind of educated guess as to where the algorithm should look for the
solution, every time the tilt gets outside the range [5◦; 25◦] the tilt is changed to 12◦.
This ensures that the value stays within reasonable range and also speeds up finding the
optimal solution.
The change in tilt is done in steps of 0.5◦.
The margin value when looking for the
optimum is slightly lower than the margin for the stable state. This results in a kind of
hysteresis, where the algorithm does not start to readjust as soon as any minor change in
the environment occurs, while allowing the searching steps to get as close to the actual
solution as possible.
Verification
The algorithm was verified under the same conditions as the other ones. In can be clearly
seen in the figure 4.17 the algorithm finds exactly the right angle both times. Further-
more, as depicted in figure 4.18 it achieves the correct setting fairly quickly and keeps
it throughout the episode. Every episode looks exactly the same (as can be seen in the
figures in appendix A). This is due to the fact that the algorithm is fully deterministic
and, since the environment does not change, it does act in exactly the same way each
time.

5.1
Self-Configuration
The aim in this case is for a newly deployed tower to automatically find the most optimal
setting, without any input from a human operator. The goal is to achieve ”broadband
access everywhere” as defined in 5G use cases - provide users with good quality of service
even in difficult settings. In all simulations the tilt was initially set to an unreasonably
high value of 35◦. This corresponds to an extremely small cell, so the controllers were
expected to start raising the tilt, thus increasing the cell size until the interference with
neighbouring cells would become too high. The ideal value was expected to be around
15◦which is also what all other cells were set to. The SINR was measured for the whole
cluster in order for the algorithm to also have the information about the users in the
neighbouring cells, who might experience increased interference, should the tilt value get
too low. The system is very interconnected and it is important to also take into account
the users in the neighbouring cells. Otherwise, the tilt would just increase to provide
better quality for the users connected to the cell, but placed near the edge, while the
users just across ”cell border” would be ignored.
As per recommendations[17], ten users per cell were placed inside the cluster. In or-
der to ensure that there are enough measurements in the ML-controlled cell, ten users
moved only within it, while all others were free to roam around the entire cluster. Both
the starting positions and the directions of movement were set randomly. Users follow
straight paths, travelling at 30 km/h as per ITU recommendations. When a user reaches
the edge of the cluster (or the middle cell in the case of those confined to it) they turn
back and travel the same route in the opposite direction. The positions are reset at the
beginning of each episode. Simulation step is 0.1s and a single episode is comprised of
1000 steps, called snapshots.
The ultimate goal of this scenario is to reach a reasonable tilt value despite the changing
conditions. Since the users are constantly moving it may be hard for the algorithms
to converge, as the reported SINR values are expected to fluctuate. However, since the
users are distributed randomly, the overall conditions should stay roughly the same. This
is a very real scenario, as users normally rarely stay in one place for long periods. There-
fore, it is necessary that a good control algorithm is able to perform well in such a setting.

5.1.2
Discussion
This use case relates to 5G networks’ ability to work without human input. Despite
some instability related to the changing environment all three investigated algorithms
managed to provide satisfactory level of performance, with FQL achieving slightly better
results than the other two. This is natural, as the fuzzy approach takes into account
many more factors when making decisions about the changes in the beam: the state space
includes the current tilt but also information about both mean and cell-edge SINR. The
machine learning approach was shown to be a good choice for this application. However,
it is also important to note, that the non-ML solution, while being much simpler to
implement and deploy, performed comparably well. This highlights the question if it
is really necessary to resort to the complex techniques of ML. Common sense dictates
that the simpler solutions usually are better in the long run. In this case, relying on
very simple algorithm seems to result on similarly good outcomes, while offering ease of
implementation and, if necessary, debugging. It should be pointed out, however, that
this is still quite a simple scenario as far as mobile networks are concerned. Very often
coming up with a decent non-ML heuristic would be an extremely difficult task. In
those cases, ML would be a much better solution, as it can infer patterns which are not
obvious even to experts in the area. What is more, the results show that the benefits
of beam-forming are limited in macro-cell scenarios. The improvement was visible and
can be expected to bring some changes in what quality of service is experienced by the
users, but was not revolutionary. It can be argued, that the impact of this technology
can be far greater for pico and femto-cells. For a macro cell a large number of users, all
moving in different directions does not really allow to make a full use of the technology.
Instead, the tilt adjusts to the average best position. In a much smaller cell, an antenna
could, in a way, track a single user. These considerations will be put to test in the next
scenario.

5.2.2
Discussion
The purpose of this use case is to check the ability of the network to adapt to changes
in the environment. Specifically, it deals with the ”Higher user mobility” use case from
section 2.3.1, as the moving group of users can be seen as, for example, a parade in a
city. It is therefore very close to the real world applications of the techniques. All the
algorithms achieved a comparable level of performance. What is more, the better results
were obtained when adjusting to the moving group of users than when correcting the
tilt initially. This can be interpreted as a sign that indeed adjusting the shape of the
beam (in this case simply the down-tilt) is more suited for applications in which single
users (or groups of users in close vicinity of each other) can be tracked by the antenna.
After examining both use cases, it can be argued that the benefits in a macro-cell,
though visible, are not game changing. However, in a smaller area - a pico or femto-cell
- the smaller number of users can be provided with almost personalised settings. The
movement of the receivers in many different directions limits the ability of the antenna
greatly. However, this simulation has proven that it is possible to use Q-learning and
its variations, or even a much simpler heuristic solution, to adjust to a shift in position.
This can be treated as a sort of proof of concept solution, should efforts to adopt this
technique in a femto-cell setting be undertaken.
Regarding the comparison between ML and non-ML solutions the advantage of using
the more advanced techniques is questionable. Nevertheless, it is important to keep in
mind that the heuristic was designed with just the down-tilt in mind. Should a more
general beam-forming application be considered - one in which the algorithm is given
control over the full 3D shape of the antenna propagation pattern - coming up with a
decent way to control the transmitter might be very challenging. What is more, when
the physical effects related to controlling real antennas (e.g. the emergence of side lobes
when tinkering with the propagation pattern) would complicate the matter even further.
The ML algorithms were able to take correct actions without any prior knowledge of the
system and can therefore be expected to be able to face such higher demands as well.
5.3
Summary
The results of simulations in real-world scenarios have been described in detail. All the
algorithms have been shown to achieve performance boosts in the network. However,
the optimisation process was sometimes connected with drops in quality in other regions,
which can be expected in scenarios with many users moving in different directions. The
simple heuristic was able to reach similar level of performance.

CHAPTER 6
Conclusions
The fifth generation of mobile networks is a massive project with highly ambitious goals
and a set of technical challenges to be overcome. They span all aspects of the services.
Numerous users having access to extremely high datarates with perfect accessibility is
the vision of the future of wireless communications. In addition to human-generated traf-
fic machines will start using mobile networks for communication which would increase
the total amount of data flowing through the air even more. Users, however, are not
concerned with where they are in relation to the layout of the network and should be
provided with decent quality of service regardless of their location. That is why mitigat-
ing cell-edge interference issues is an important goal for mobile networks in general and
5G in particular. Delivering the minimum downlink data rate of 50 Mbps, as required by
the specifications, means that the signal quality has to be kept at a reasonably good level.
Most advanced interference avoidance scheme, Coordinated Multipoint, offers a solution
called Coordinated Beamforming which involves adjusting the shape of an antenna’s
propagation pattern to mitigate issues with overlapping signals. In its simplest form the
beamforming can mean just changing the tilt of a regular antenna in order to change
the area covered by the cell. Machine learning offers a set of tools which can be used to
find out the best way to choose the most optimal tilt value. Reinforcement learning can
find out how the environment (in this case the conditions of the radio channel) reacts to
the actions of the RL controller and in this way perform the optimisation.
Three different variants of the RL algorithm were developed and tested using a channel
model based on pathloss equations which was able to return SINR values for different
UE positions and included the influence of the tilt. After verification it turned out that
only two of these algorithms perform as expected, so they were tested in two different
scenarios which were designed to present the RL algorithms with real-world challenges
typical for mobile networks. ML techniques were compared with a simple heuristic ap-
proach. All algorithms, including the non-ML one, performed well in a self-optimisation
scenario where a new cell got deployed in a cluster. During a self-configuration trial, in
which the antennae adjusted to changing user distribution, it turned out that all the
implemented solutions were good at tracking the spot of high user concentration, but
at the same time they could not provide cell-edge users with any improvements in sig-
nal quality. On the contrary, cell-edge performance dropped, despite the overall signal
quality averaged over all users being better.
Two main conclusions can be drawn, when it comes to applying ML techniques to mit-
igate interference.
In simple scenarios it might be beneficial to focus on algorithms
designed by network experts, as domain knowledge can be easily utilised to obtain good
results. ML approaches are much more suited to situations where it can be really hard to
==PAGE:90==
6.1 Future work
77
come up with a decent solution otherwise. Second, large number of users in macro-cells
makes it very difficult to achieve large gains just by changing the shape of the beam.
The algorithm needs to adjust to the mean distribution and there is not much place for
improvements. However, if the cell was smaller and an antenna could focus more on a
selected area, the benefits can be much greater.
6.1
Future work
The project could be continued and expanded in a number of ways. Firstly, the failed
attempt at using more complex models which also include fading can be remade with
a fully 3D capable model, such as QuaDRiGa[35].
This would allow to investigate
other signal metrics than just SINR. What is more, the NN implementation of the FQL
algorithm could be made to work with some parameter tweaking and by applying more
advanced techniques from the field of NN.
In terms of use cases, the ML approach could be evaluated in other propagation scenarios,
in particular, for pico and femto-cells. As mentioned before, the results suggest that this
might be the area in which these algorithms would truly shine.
