Master’s Thesis in Innovative Sustainable Energy Engineering
Abstract
Agrivoltaics is a system of co-locating solar photovoltaic panels with
agricultural crops, where the panels are mounted on a structure above the
crops or are integrated into the crops’ canopy. This system addresses the
challenge of competing land use between energy generation from solar ra-
diation and agricultural activities (i.e., dual use of the land), as well as
the challenge of efficient land use in the face of climate change and rising
populations. Agrivoltaic systems can enhance resource-use efficiency by
improving the utilization of natural resources, land, water, and renewable
energy sources such as solar radiation. This study presents an Agrivoltaic
system aimed at mapping the microclimate parameters (e.g., temperature,
humidity, wind) nearby and between panels to understand the impacts of
the installation of the PV panels on local environmental conditions, which
can eventually be used to understand how these conditions affect the plant
growth. The project will involve the installation of vertical mounted PV
panels over a small-scale agricultural field at Skjetlein High School, and
monitoring instruments will be deployed to measure the microclimatic vari-
ables. The data collected will be analyzed to determine the effects of the
panels on microclimate and plant growth, as well as validating predic-
tive models of the local microclimate.
The Envi-met software used for
urban microclimate modelling will be used here to create predictions on
how the local microclimate will react in the presence of the PV panels.
Photosynthetically Active Radiation and temperature are considered the
growth defining factors for the crop, and are thus important to measure
in the physical system. Additionally, factors such as precipitation, evap-
oration (humidity and wind), and soil properties will also be measured
as the water balance within the crops can be considered the most im-
portant growth limiting factors. The project’s long-term objective is to
investigate the potential of Agrivoltaic systems to improve crop yields and
resource-use efficiency. The mapping of microclimatic variables is essen-
tial to determine the optimal configurations of the Agrivoltaic system to
maximize plant growth and minimize the panels’ negative impacts on the
crop. The results of this project will provide valuable insights into the
design and management of Agrivoltaic systems at high latitude locations,
contributing to the development of sustainable and efficient systems that
can contribute to the energy transition and sustainable agriculture.

Introduction
With the ongoing threat of climate change, Photovoltaic (PV) technology is
rapidly becoming an important renewable technology aiding in the transition to
a sustainable society. PV technology, put simply, converts sunlight directly into
electricity using solar cells. In 2021, the global installed PV capacity was 940
gigawatts (GW), with about 168 GW of new PV capacity installed in the very
same year[21]. However, average annual generation growth of 25% is required by
2030 in order to reach net zero global energy-related carbon dioxide emissions
by 2050[22]. This is in line with a predicted boom in research and installation of
PV systems across the world over the next few decades[22].
1.1
Photovoltaics in Norway
PV technology has had a boom in poularity over recent years. China was re-
sponsible for about 38% of PV generation growth in 2021, followed by the United
States with 17% of the total growth, and the European Union (EU) with 10%
of the total growth[23]. PV technology in Norway specifically has had an inter-
esting evolution. Unlike some other European nations, Norway does not have
intense amounts of fossil fuels that need to be replaced by renewable electricity
production. Norwegian hydropower is relatively cheap, and on most occasions
power companies do not consider it economically attractive to invest in solar
power plants in Norway. However, that has begun to change recently. Compa-
nies have increased the selling and leasing of solar energy systems to both public
and private parties[24].
In 2016, four times as much capacity was installed in Norway when compared
to the year before. Most of these new solar energy systems were installed on
large buildings or private homes as opposed to solar farms. Because Norway
is the fourth largest silicon producing nation in the world[25], it is also active
in the production of crude silicon and silicon wafers for the solar cell industry.
Additionally, places like Oslo, Norway’s capital, can receive about 1000 kilowatt-
hours (kWh) of energy per square meter annually, which is not too different to
many parts of Germany, a country where solar power is rather popular[24].
On top of the natural resources, Norway is sure to see an increase in solar powered
systems due to economics. Whereas designing and constructing hydroelectric,
gas or coal power plants require large capital investments, solar power is much
more flexible and involve less capital. These systems can be installed as solar
farms in open fields, but can also take the form of small units on rooftops or
integrated into buildings themselves. This allows consumers to contribute to the
development, further accelerating national trends[24].

Agriculture in Norway
Norway is a small and unique country that presents several challenges for the
agricultural sector. The country has a population of approximately 5.3 million
people, with a land area of 385,000 km², of which only 3% is suitable for agri-
culture due to the country’s mountainous terrain, short growing season, and
unpredictable weather patterns. Despite these challenges, the agricultural sector
in Norway has managed to produce high-quality products that are highly valued
in the domestic and international markets.
The agricultural sector in Norway has a long history dating back to the Viking
Age, when farming was the main occupation of the population. Over the cen-
turies, farming practices in Norway have evolved to adapt to changing social,
economic, and environmental conditions. Today, the agricultural sector is diver-
sified and includes a wide range of activities such as crop production, livestock
farming, forestry, and fisheries. The sector is characterized by its focus on pro-

Norway has high purchasing power on the international market, global crises

this issue of low food security by enacting "Jordlova", translated in English to

1.3
Agrivoltaic Systems
As previously mentioned, one potential downside when it comes to solar energy
is the large footprint these systems require.
This may be at the expense of
agricultural land used to grow crops. The trade-off becomes doubly important
considering the relationship between food security issues and climate change.
Projected increases in temperatures, variations in precipitation patterns, varia-
tions in extreme weather events, and reductions in water levels may all result in
reduced agricultural productivity[29].
Therefore, it becomes necessary to minimize any externality linked with the
installation of solar energy systems. This is why the field of Agrivoltaic Systems
(AVS) has become of huge interest. AVS, put simply, is the concept of using
PV installations to create renewable energy while still providing space for local
agriculture or native habitats. The main idea behind AVS is to cultivate crops
in the space below the PV module and/or between the rows of the PV module,
which usually also allows space for farming equipment and livestock.
Ideally, AVS creates a symbiotic relationship where both the solar panels and the
crops benefit because they help each other perform better. The solar panels helps
protect the crops from extreme weather, like hail, high winds, and drought[30],
and plants have been shown to keep solar panels cooler, making them more
efficient.[31]. Along with the benefit of dual land use, the generated electricity
can be used to power electric tractors and farming equipment, while any surplus
energy can be stored in battery banks or sent to the grid for consumer use. The
current agrivoltaics market value was estimated to be USD 3.17 billion in 2021
and is predicted to grow to around USD 8.9 billion by 2030, creating a strong
case for the topic’s research and implementation[32]. An example of different
configurations for an AVS can be seen in Figure 1.2.
Despite these benefits, AVS also present several challenges. One of the main
challenges is the cost of installation, which can be higher than traditional so-
lar installations due to the additional infrastructure required. Additionally, the
shading provided by the solar panels can reduce the amount of sunlight reach-
ing the crops, which can have negative effects on some crops and require careful
selection of suitable crop varieties[33]. Furthermore, AVS require careful man-
agement and maintenance to ensure optimal performance of both the crops and
solar panels.

Theory
In order to fully understand the AVS and its affects on the surrounding crop
growth, it is first important to understand the theory behind the individual
components of the AVS and microclimate. The following is an introductory level
explanation of aspects involved in the AVS, as well as relevant microclimate
parameters.
3.1
Solar Cells
At their core, solar cells are semi-conductor devices which convert sunlight into
electricity. In this sense, these cells are an environmentally friendly and renew-
able form of energy. Solar cells are usually made primarily of silicon, and are put
together to create modules (also called panels), which are then strung together to
create arrays. Solar panels are manufactured by slicing silicon ingots, into circu-
lar disks, called wafers. Once cleaned and doped with other elements to enhance
electrical properties, the wafers are connected together to form a module.
3.2
Sunlight
Sunlight is a necessary part of the process for solar cells to produce energy. There
are a few vital aspects of solar energy which are necessary in determining how
effective the solar cell will be in converting light into electricity. First, the spec-
tral content of the sunlight is important, as it determines which wavelengths the
light consists of, and its associated energy, as shown in Figure 3.1.
Figure 3.1: Relationship between light’s wavelength, frequency and energy[3]
The radiant power density is also vital, which is given in W/m2, and is the
measure of power per unit area of the light that is received by the cell.
It
is important to have prolonged measurements in order to understand how the
radiant power density changes over the course of a month or year, to provide
accurate estimations of electrical production. Finally, the angle the sunlight hits
the solar cell is important to determine how much energy the solar cell is actually
absorbing.

The orientation of the module and sun can help determine the angle of the in-
cident sunlight, and consists of the declination, azimuth, and tilt angles. The
declination angle, given as δ , can be described as the angle between the sunlight
and the equator of the earth. It will vary throughout the year between +23.45°
and -23.45°. The azimuth angle is measured as the angle between the true North
and the incoming sunlight, assuming the modules are on an imaginary horizontal
plane. Finally, The tilt angle is measured between the plane of the PV surface
and an imaginary horizontal surface, given as β. All three angles can be shown

3.3
Bifacial Solar Cells
Bifacial solar cells are different to regular, or monofacial solar cells, in that they
generate electricity using both sides of the solar panel as opposed to only the top
side, as shown in Figure 3.4. This is significant since the backside of a bifacial
solar cell can often absorb energy from diffuse and rebounded sunlight from the
ground.
9
==PAGE:25==
Master’s Thesis in Innovative Sustainable Energy Engineering
Figure 3.4: Schematic view of bifacial (left) and monofacial (right) solar panels[6].
Solar cells in bifacial solar panels are similar to their monofacial counterparts.
The primary difference rests in how the panel is manufactured. Monofacial pan-
els usually have an opaque backsheet, while bifacial solar panels have a reflective
back or dual panes of glass holding the solar cells in place. As a result, bifacial
solar panels usually have a higher efficiency, as they are capable of capturing
sunlight on both sides. Consequentially, bifacial panels perform best when in-
stalled near reflective surfaces, which increases the amount of sunlight captured
by the panels[35]. Efficiency of bifacial solar cells, defined as the ratio of incident
power to generated electrical power, is measured independently for the front and
rear surfaces.
Two important factors with respect to bifacial solar panels are the bifaciality
(BF) and bifacial gain (BG). The BF is measured as a percent, and is the ratio
of the efficiency of the rear surface in relation to the efficiency of the front surface.
BG can be defined as the extra energy yield a bifacial panel produces over that of
a monofacial panel. High BF and BG values are therefore desired if possible[35].
3.4
Testing of Solar Panels
Testing solar panels is a crucial process to ensure their safety, durability, and
performance under various conditions. The testing process involves various tests
and procedures to evaluate the electrical, mechanical, and environmental proper-
ties of the panels. One important standard for testing solar panels is IEC 61215,
which specifies the requirements for design qualification and type approval of
crystalline silicon terrestrial PV modules[36].
IEC 61215 is important to the testing process because it provides a set of stan-
dardized test methods and procedures for evaluating the electrical, mechanical,
and environmental performance of PV modules. The standard includes various
tests, such as thermal cycling, humidity freeze, damp heat, hail impact, and static
load tests, among others. These tests are designed to simulate the various envi-

One of the tests included in IEC 61215 is the static load test. This test involves
subjecting the module to a mechanical load, such as a weight or force, to evaluate
its mechanical strength and resistance to deformation. The test is performed by
applying a force of 2400 Pa to the module’s surface for a period of 1 minute.
The module should not exhibit any permanent deformation or damage after the
test[37].
Another test included in IEC 61215 is the thermal cycling test. This test involves

resistance to thermal stress and its ability to withstand temperature changes.
The test is performed by exposing the module to temperatures of -40°C and

3.5
Electroluminescence Testing
One additional test that is not included in IEC 61215 but is commonly used in the
industry is the electroluminescence (EL) test. The EL test is a non-destructive
method for evaluating the quality and performance of solar panels. The test can
detect various types of defects, such as cracks, breaks, hot spots, and shunts,
which can affect the module’s performance and efficiency[39]. During the test, a
voltage is applied to the solar panel, which causes it to emit light. The emitted
light can then be analyzed to detect defects or abnormalities that may affect the
panel’s efficiency.
EL imaging is a widely used tool for quality control and defect detection in the
solar industry. The imaging system consists of a charge-coupled device camera,
a high-power LED light source, and a power supply.
The camera is used to
capture the EL images of the solar panels, and the LED light source provides
the excitation for the EL process. The power supply is used to apply the voltage
to the solar panel[39].
One of the main defects that can be detected during an EL test is micro-cracks.
Micro-cracks are small cracks that occur in the solar cell during the manufactur-
ing process or during the operation of the solar panel. These cracks can reduce
the efficiency of the solar panel by creating localized shunts or hotspots, which
can cause the panel to generate less power or even fail completely[40]. Micro-
cracks are often not visible to the naked eye, and the EL test is one of the most
effective ways to detect them.
Another defect that can be detected during the EL test is cell fractures. Cell
fractures are caused by mechanical stress or thermal expansion and contraction,

and they can also reduce the efficiency of the solar panel[41]. The EL test can
detect cell fractures by showing areas of reduced or absent light emission in the
EL images. An example of the resulting image from an EL test is shown in
Figure 3.7.

In addition to micro-cracks and cell fractures, the EL test can also detect other
defects such as delamination, diode failures, and broken or disconnected busbars.
By detecting these defects early, manufacturers can identify and correct problems
before they affect the panel’s performance or lifespan.
3.6
Sensors
Sensors can play a critical role in measuring the microclimate around PV panels.
The microclimate refers to the local atmospheric and environmental conditions
that affect the performance and efficiency of PV panels. Various aspects of the
microclimate, such as air and soil temperature, wind speed and direction, soil
moisture, and relative humidity, can be measured using different types of sensors.
Air and soil temperature sensors can be used to measure the temperature of the
air and soil around PV panels. The temperature of the air affects the efficiency
of the panels, while the soil temperature affects the growth and health of the
vegetation surrounding the panels. There are several types of sensors that can
measure air and soil temperature, including thermocouples, resistance tempera-
ture detectors, and thermistors[42]. An example of a soil temperature sensor is
shown in Figure 3.8.
Wind speed and direction sensors can be used to measure the speed and direction
of the wind around PV panels. The wind affects the cooling of the panels, which
can impact their performance and efficiency[43]. There are various types of sensors

anemometers, and wind vanes.
Soil moisture sensors can be used to measure the moisture content of the soil
around PV panels. The moisture content affects the growth and health of the
vegetation surrounding the panels, which in turn can impact the performance of
the panels. There are different types of sensors that can measure soil moisture,
including soil moisture probes, tensiometers, and capacitance sensors[44].
Relative humidity sensors can be used to measure the amount of moisture in the
air around PV panels. The humidity affects the efficiency of the panels, as well
as the growth and health of the vegetation surrounding the panels. There are
different types of sensors that can measure relative humidity, including capacitive
and resistive sensors.

Mounting
The mounting method of PV panels is critical for their efficiency, longevity, and
safety. Proper mounting ensures that the panels are securely attached to their
support structure, that they are angled correctly for optimal performance, and
that they can withstand weather conditions and other environmental factors[45].
The mounting method also affects the maintenance requirements and the overall
cost of the system.
There are several types of mounting materials used for PV panels, including
aluminum, stainless steel, and galvanized steel. Aluminum is lightweight and
corrosion-resistant, making it a popular choice for residential and commercial
installations. Stainless steel is more durable and can withstand harsher weather

==PAGE:30==

conditions, but it is also more expensive.
Galvanized steel is the most cost-
effective option, but it is heavier and more prone to corrosion.
Considerations for the mounting method include the type of roof or support
structure, the angle and orientation of the panels, and the local weather con-
ditions. For example, if the PV panels are installed on a flat roof, a ballasted
mounting system may be used, which involves placing weights on the panels to
keep them in place. If the panels are installed on a sloped roof, a roof-mount
system may be used, which involves attaching the panels to brackets that are
anchored to the roof.
The angle and orientation of the panels are also important considerations for the
mounting method. The panels should be angled correctly to receive maximum
sunlight and produce maximum energy. In the northern hemisphere, PV panels
are typically angled towards the south, while in the southern hemisphere, they are
typically angled towards the north. The optimal angle depends on the latitude
of the location and the season of the year[46].
Weather conditions may be another driving factor affecting the mounting method.
The mounting system should be designed to withstand high winds, heavy snow
loads, and other weather events that may occur in the area.
3.8
Albedo
A concept that is important, especially in the context of bifacial solar panels,
is that of albedo. As mentioned in the previous section, bifacial panels perform
best when installed near reflective surfaces because they are able to absorb light
from both the front and back surfaces. Albedo is the ability of a surface to reflect
light (and it’s associated heat)[47]. Albedo is measured on a scale of 0 to 1. An
albedo value of 0 means a surface absorbs all incident light and radiation. An
albedo value of 1 eans just the opposite, that a surface reflects all incident light
and radiation. Scientists estimate the average albedo value for Earth is around
0.30, but different surfaces have differing values, as shown by Table 3.1.
Given that surfaces with higher albedo values reflect more light, it is preferred
to have bifacial panels near surfaces with high albedo panels. This is sometimes
difficult, however, as albedo does not stay constant. Varying weather patterns,
like rain and humidity, can affect the physical properties of a surface, in turn
affecting the albedo value. The importance of higher albedo values can be shown
in Figure 3.9, conveying the relative contributions of different components of light
to the total annual irradiance received by the rear surface of a bifacial panel.

3.11
Growth Defining and Limiting Factors
Growth Defining and Growth Limiting Factors can be simply described as a
series of factors that affect levels of crop production. Growth Defining Factors
17
==PAGE:33==
Master’s Thesis in Innovative Sustainable Energy Engineering
are multiplicative, and help determine the maximum amount of production a
species of plant can achieve in a given environment. Specifically, Growth Defining
Factors can include Radiation Use Efficiency (RUE), Photosynthetically Active
Radiation (PAR), and average temperature[49].
On the other hand, Growth Limiting Factors consist of water-related parameters
that determine water levels in a given environment. Growth Limiting Factors can
consist of evapotranspiration, relative humidity, and volumetric water content[50].
Careful management can control these water-related parameters and can help
increase production of species in a given environment[50].
In order to understand how an AVS affects the surrounding microclimate, and
thus the eventual crop yield, it becomes necessary to investigate how the presence
of solar panels affect the Growth Defining and Limiting Factors. This can be
done by using a sensor system to measure real life data or using microclimate
simulation software, as mentioned in more depth later in Sections 4.1.6 and
4.2, respectively. Once the sensor system and/or microclimate simulations are
complete and provide spatial variation in the Growth Defining and Limiting
Factors, they can be inputted into crop yield models to determine the overall
impact of the presence of an AVS on yield.
3.11.1
Photosynthetically Active Radiation (PAR)
PAR refers to the spectral range of solar radiation that is essential for photosyn-
thesis in plants. It consists of wavelengths between 400 to 700 nanometers (nm)
and corresponds to the visible light spectrum[51]. PAR is measured in units of
micromoles per square meter per second (µmol/m²/s). PAR is crucial for the
growth and development of plants because it is the source of energy that drives
photosynthesis, the process by which plants convert light energy into chemical
energy in the form of organic compounds such as glucose[51].
PAR is the most important factor determining plant growth and yield, as plants
can only utilize a specific range of light wavelengths for photosynthesis. The
specific wavelengths of light that are most efficient for photosynthesis depend on
the type of plant and the environmental conditions. For example, blue light (400-
500 nm) is essential for the development of leaves, while red light (600-700 nm) is
important for flowering and fruiting. Therefore, a balanced light spectrum that
contains all necessary wavelengths of light is essential for healthy plant growth.

3.11.2
Radiation Use Efficiency (RUE)
RUE is a measure of how efficiently plants convert absorbed radiation into
biomass production[52]. It is calculated by dividing the amount of biomass pro-
duced by the total amount of solar radiation intercepted by the plant. RUE is
an important determinant of plant productivity and is influenced by a range of
factors, including plant physiology, environmental conditions, and management
practices.
One of the key determinants of RUE is the efficiency of photosynthesis, which
is the process by which plants convert light energy into chemical energy. The
efficiency of photosynthesis can be influenced by a range of factors, including
leaf structure, pigment composition, and the availability of carbon dioxide and
water[53]. In addition, environmental factors such as light intensity, temperature,
and water availability can also influence photosynthetic efficiency[54].
Another important factor that affects RUE is the allocation of resources within
the plant. For example, plants may allocate resources to different organs, such as
leaves, stems, and roots, in response to changing environmental conditions. The
allocation of resources can affect the efficiency with which plants use absorbed
radiation for biomass production.
3.11.3
Leaf Area Index (LAI)
LAI is a measure of the total area of leaves per unit ground area in a plant canopy.
It is a critical parameter for characterizing plant growth and productivity, as it
determines the amount of PAR intercepted by the plant canopy, which in turn
affects the rate of photosynthesis and ultimately plant growth[55]. LAI can be
measured using various techniques, such as destructive leaf sampling, optical
sensors, and remote sensing methods.

The LAI of a plant canopy is directly related to the amount of PAR that is
intercepted by the leaves. As LAI increases, more PAR is absorbed and utilized
by the leaves for photosynthesis, which leads to increased plant growth and
productivity. However, there is a threshold beyond which further increases in LAI
may have limited benefits or even be detrimental to plant growth, as the lower
leaves may become shaded and receive insufficient light for photosynthesis[56].

LAI also plays an important role in regulating the exchange of carbon dioxide and
water vapor between the plant canopy and the atmosphere. A higher LAI leads
to higher rates of photosynthesis and transpiration, which can affect the water
use efficiency of the plant and the local microclimate. Therefore, understanding
the LAI of a plant canopy is important for predicting its carbon and water fluxes
and for managing agricultural systems.
LAI can be used as a diagnostic tool for assessing plant health and productivity.
A decrease in LAI can indicate stress or damage to the plant canopy, such as
nutrient deficiencies, disease, or drought. Monitoring LAI over time can provide
insights into the growth dynamics of the plant and the effectiveness of manage-
ment practices such as irrigation and fertilization[57]. Furthermore, LAI can be
used to estimate biomass production and yield, which are important factors for
optimizing agricultural productivity.

3.11.4
Leaf Temperature
Leaf temperature is an important factor that influences plant growth and physiol-
ogy. Leaf temperature is influenced by a range of environmental factors, including
air temperature, relative humidity, solar radiation, wind speed, and soil moisture.
Leaf temperature can affect a range of plant processes, including photosynthesis,
respiration, and transpiration[58].
One important aspect of leaf temperature is its influence on photosynthesis. Pho-
tosynthesis is a temperature-dependent process, and optimal leaf temperature is
critical for maximizing photosynthetic rates[58]. When leaf temperatures are too
high, the rate of photosynthesis can be reduced due to increased photorespira-
tion and decreased enzyme activity. Conversely, when leaf temperatures are too
low, photosynthetic rates can also be reduced due to decreased enzyme activ-
ity. The relationship between leaf temperature and photosynthetic rate is shown
graphically in Figure 3.12[14].
Figure 3.12: The relationship between leaf temperature and photosynthetic rate for different
carbon fixation plants[14].
Leaf temperature also affects transpiration rates. When leaf temperatures are
high, transpiration rates can be increased due to increased vapor pressure deficits
between the leaf and the atmosphere. This can lead to water stress in plants,
particularly in areas with low rainfall or high temperatures. Conversely, when
leaf temperatures are low, transpiration rates can be reduced, which can be
beneficial in arid environments where water conservation is critical.
Leaf temperature can also affect plant metabolism and growth. High leaf temper-
atures can lead to increased rates of respiration, which can reduce plant growth
and yield. In addition, high leaf temperatures can lead to increased production

3.11.5
Soil Temperature
Soil temperature is an important factor that affects plant growth and develop-
ment. It refers to the temperature of the soil at a certain depth, usually measured
in degrees Celsius or Fahrenheit. The soil temperature is influenced by various
factors such as air temperature, solar radiation, soil moisture content, and soil
type, among others. Understanding the theory of soil temperature and its effects
on plant growth is essential in determining the optimal growing conditions for
different plant species.
The temperature of the soil affects plant growth in several ways.
First, soil
temperature influences the rate of seed germination, as different plant species
have different optimal temperature requirements for seed germination[60]. High
soil temperatures can accelerate seed germination, but extremely high temper-
atures can be detrimental to seed viability. Secondly, soil temperature affects
root growth and development. Plant roots grow and absorb nutrients more effi-
ciently when the soil temperature is within their optimal range[61]. Suitable soil
temperatures for the germination of selected vegetable seeds are shown in Figure

The effects of soil temperature on plant growth are not only limited to individual
plants but also have significant implications for crop production. For example,

temperature influences the length of the growing season, as it determines the
rate of plant development and the timing of maturity.
3.11.6
Evapotranspiration (ET)
ET is the process by which water is transferred from the Earth’s surface to
the atmosphere through the combined processes of evaporation from soil and
water surfaces, and transpiration from plant leaves. A simple schematic for the
process of evapotranspiration is shown in Figure 3.14. ET plays a crucial role in
plant growth and productivity, as it determines the amount of water available
to plants for growth, and the rate of water loss from the plant. ET is influenced
by various factors, including temperature, humidity, wind speed, solar radiation,
soil moisture, and plant characteristics such as leaf area index and stomatal
conductance[62].
Figure 3.14: Schematic conveying the process of evapotranspiration[16].
Soil evaporation is influenced by the temperature and humidity of the surround-
ing air, as well as the moisture content of the soil. Transpiration is the process by
which water is lost from the leaves of plants through tiny pores called stomata.
This process is regulated by plant hormones and is influenced by environmental
factors such as temperature, humidity, wind speed, and light intensity.
ET is essential for plant growth, as it supplies the plant with the necessary water
for metabolic processes and structural support[63].
Plants require a constant

supply of water to maintain turgor pressure, which keeps their stems and leaves
rigid. In addition, water is essential for photosynthesis, which is the process by
which plants produce carbohydrates for growth and reproduction.
Soil moisture is an important factor that affects ET and plant growth. Water
is absorbed by plant roots from the soil and is transported to the leaves for
transpiration. If the soil moisture content is too low, plants may not be able to
extract enough water to sustain growth, and may wilt or die. Conversely, if the
soil is too wet, the oxygen supply to the roots may be limited, which can lead to
root rot and other problems.
3.11.7
Relative and Absolute Humidity
Relative and absolute humidity are two important concepts in atmospheric sci-
ence that play a crucial role in plant growth. Absolute humidity is a measure
of the amount of water vapor present in the air, typically measured in g/m3 of
water vapor in air. In contrast, relative humidity is a measure of the amount of
water vapor present in the air compared to the maximum amount of water vapor
that the air could hold at a given temperature. Relative humidity is expressed
as a percentage.
The water requirements of plants are influenced by both relative and absolute
humidity[64]. Plants lose water through transpiration, the process by which water
moves from the plant’s roots to the leaves and then evaporates into the atmo-
sphere[65]. When the air surrounding the plant is dry, water evaporates more
quickly from the leaves, increasing the rate of transpiration. This can lead to
water stress in the plant, particularly in areas with low rainfall or high temper-
atures.
Relative humidity plays an important role in determining the rate of transpiration
in plants. When the relative humidity is low, the rate of transpiration is higher
because the air is not saturated with water vapor, creating a larger gradient
between the leaf and the atmosphere[66]. This can lead to water stress in plants,
particularly in arid environments where the relative humidity is low.
In contrast, high relative humidity can limit the rate of transpiration in plants
by reducing the gradient between the leaf and the atmosphere.
This can be
beneficial to plants in areas with high temperatures or low rainfall, as it helps to
conserve water.
3.11.8
Volumetric Water Content (VWC) in Soil
VWC is an important parameter that describes the amount of water held in
the soil relative to the total volume of soil. It is expressed as the ratio of the
volume of water to the total volume of soil, typically measured in percent or as
a fraction. Understanding the theory of VWC and its effects on plant growth
is critical for optimizing irrigation practices and ensuring optimal plant growth
and productivity.

==PAGE:40==
Master’s Thesis in Innovative Sustainable Energy Engineering
The VWC is a critical factor in determining plant water availability.
Plants
absorb water from the soil through their roots, and the rate of water uptake
is proportional to the VWC of the soil.
When the VWC is low, plants can
experience water stress, which can lead to reduced growth and yield. Secondly,
VWC influences soil aeration, which is critical for root respiration and nutrient
uptake. When the soil is saturated with water, the air spaces in the soil are filled,
reducing oxygen availability to the roots and limiting their ability to absorb
nutrients[67].
Finally, VWC affects soil temperature, with wet soils having a
higher heat capacity than dry soils, resulting in lower soil temperatures[68].
The effects of VWC on plant growth also have significant implications for crop
production.
Optimal VWC levels are necessary for achieving maximum crop
yield and quality. Over-irrigation can result in waterlogging and reduced crop
yields, while under-irrigation can lead to water stress and reduced crop growth.
3.11.9
Water Vapor Flux
Water vapor flux is a measure of the exchange of water vapor between the Earth’s
surface and the atmosphere. The flux of water vapor is driven by a gradient in
water vapor concentration between a surface and the atmosphere. This gradient
is typically quantified using the concept of vapor pressure deficit (VPD), which
is the difference between the saturation vapor pressure and the actual vapor
pressure[69]. The higher the VPD, the greater the driving force for water vapor
flux. The VPD is influenced by a range of factors, including air temperature,
humidity, and solar radiation.
Water vapor flux is also influenced by the resistance to water vapor transfer
within the plant and soil system.
This resistance can be modeled using the
Penman-Monteith equation, which takes into account factors such as leaf area
index, soil moisture, and stomatal conductance[70]. Stomatal conductance is a
key factor controlling the flux of water vapor from the plant to the atmosphere,
and is influenced by a range of factors including light, CO2 concentration, and
soil moisture.
The soil moisture content is an important factor influencing the flux of water
vapor from the soil to the atmosphere.
In general, higher soil moisture con-
tent leads to higher rates of evapotranspiration, as there is more water available
for uptake by the plant and subsequent release into the atmosphere. The soil
moisture content can also vary with depth, with the topsoil typically being more
moist than deeper layers. This can lead to a stratification of the water vapor
flux, with higher rates of evapotranspiration from the surface layer.

4.1.6
Sensor System
An important part of this project is to better understand how the introduction
of the AVS to the agricultural setting affects the microclimate of the surrounding
area. In order to accurately understand how this happens, sensors will need to
be installed, along with the AVS itself. The question then becomes what exact
parameters do we want to capture with the sensors?
To answer this question, measurable data has been split into two categories:
Crop Growth Defining Factors and Crop Growth Limiting Factors, as shown in
Figure 4.6. Because the changes in microclimate will affect the final crop yield, it
becomes relevant to understand what microclimate factors help the crops achieve
maximum production in a given physical environment, and which microclimate
factors inhibit the crops growth. The specific crop of interest in this situation
is the Timothy grass crop, which is grown in the field at Skjetlein High School
where this AVS will be installed.

4.2
Microclimate modelling
A microclimate refers to the climatic conditions that prevail in a localized area
and differ from the surrounding area’s climate. It is affected by various physical
features, such as topography, vegetation, and water bodies. Microclimates can
range from small areas, such as a garden or a park, to large regions, such as a
city or a mountain range. For this project, the microclimate will refer to the area
in between two sets of panels installed for the AVS. As such, the microclimate
will encompass a length of 10 meters, the length from one set of panels to the
other.
The variation in climatic conditions can have a significant impact on the environ-
ment and the living organisms within it. Physical features, such as topography,
vegetation, and water bodies, can significantly affect the microclimate of an area.
Topography refers to the physical features of a land area, such as elevation, slope,
and aspect. These factors can affect the amount of solar radiation that an area
receives, the temperature, and the moisture content of the air. For example,
areas with steep slopes or high elevation are likely to receive more solar radia-
tion and experience colder temperatures than areas with low elevation or gentle
slopes.
Vegetation also plays a significant role in shaping the microclimate of an area.
Plants can affect the temperature and moisture content of the air through the
process of transpiration, where water is released into the atmosphere from the
leaves of the plants. Vegetation can also influence the wind speed and direction,
which can affect the temperature and moisture content of the air. For example,
areas with dense vegetation are likely to have higher humidity levels than areas
with little vegetation, as the plants release moisture into the atmosphere.
Most interestingly for this project, solar panels can cause the microclimate
around them to change through several mechanisms. One of the primary ways in
which solar panels can affect the microclimate is through shading. Solar panels
installed on raised structures above the ground can cast a shadow on the sur-
rounding area, reducing the amount of solar radiation that reaches the ground.
This can result in a localized cooling effect, which can reduce the temperature
34
==PAGE:50==
Master’s Thesis in Innovative Sustainable Energy Engineering
in the area.
Additionally, solar panels can affect the wind speed and direction around them.
The turbulence created by the solar panels can result in changes in the wind
speed and direction, which can affect the temperature and moisture content of
the air. Solar panels can also affect the soil moisture content by reducing the
amount of water that reaches the soil. This can have a significant impact on the
growth and health of vegetation in the area.
Understanding how solar panels can affect the microclimate is essential for de-
signing and implementing effective agrivoltaic systems. Research and environ-
mental measurements from sensors from the physical system are needed to iden-
tify the optimal design and management practices to maximize the benefits of
both solar energy production and crop cultivation. However, since the the real-
time measurements will not be ready by the time this thesis is completed, the
project aims to predict the affects of the microclimate due to the presence of the
panels.

Description of tool
Envi-met is a microclimate simulation software that models the complex inter-
actions between the built environment and the natural environment. It is widely
used by architects, urban planners, and environmental scientists to simulate and
optimize the microclimate conditions in urban and rural areas. The software is
based on a 3D model that integrates various physical and biological processes,
including radiation, heat transfer, airflow, and vegetation growth. An example
of the results for a simulation Envi-met can output is shown in Figure 4.7.
The Envi-met modelling software was developed and progressed since 1994 by the
German geographer and professor, Michael Bruse. Twenty years later, Michael
and Daniela Bruse founded the Envi-met GmbH company. The software was
designed to simulate the microclimate conditions in urban and rural areas and to
help urban planners and architects design sustainable buildings and cities. Since
then, the software has evolved into a comprehensive tool that can simulate a wide
range of environmental processes, including radiation, heat transfer, airflow, and
vegetation growth. For this project, the software will be used to measure the
microclimactic affects caused by the presence of an AVS at Skjetlein High School.
One of the key features of Envi-met is its ability to model the complex interac-
tions between the built environment and the natural environment. The software
can simulate the interactions between buildings, roads, and other built structures
(in our case, solar panels) with the surrounding vegetation, soil, and water bod-
ies. This enables the software to accurately simulate the microclimate conditions
in a particular area, including the temperature, humidity, wind speed, and solar
radiation levels.

4.4
Limitations of the software
Like any modelling software, Envi-met has limitations that should be considered
when interpreting its results.
Envi-met’s sensitivity to input parameters is one potential weakness. The accu-
racy of the simulation results depends on the accuracy of the input data, such
as the geometry of the buildings, the location of the vegetation, and the weather
data. If the input data is inaccurate, the simulation results may not accurately
reflect the actual microclimate conditions. For example, if the geometry of a
building, or for this case, a solar panel, is not accurately represented, the simula-
tion results may overestimate or underestimate outputs like ambient temperature
and solar radiation levels[71]. This means that the geometry of our solar panel
must be carefully, outlined, from physical dimension, to material traits like co-
efficient of thermal expansion, to other traits like albedo.
Another limitation of Envi-met is its potential inability to simulate certain pro-
cesses, such as the effects of large bodies of water or complex terrain on the mi-
croclimate conditions. Envi-met assumes a flat terrain, unless otherwise noted,
and does not account for the effects of nearby hills, valleys, or large bodies of
water on the microclimate conditions. For example, Envi-met may not accu-
rately simulate the microclimate conditions in coastal areas where sea breezes
and ocean currents can affect the temperature and humidity levels. However, it
must be said that Envi-met can take aspects like hills and nearby coastal areas if
they are modelled in the Spaces Tool, or by inputting terrain information in the
form of raster or point cloud data, otherwise known as digital elevation models.
Envi-met also has limitations in its ability to model the interactions between the
41
==PAGE:57==
Master’s Thesis in Innovative Sustainable Energy Engineering
built environment and the natural environment. The software assumes that the
buildings and vegetation are static and does not simulate the effects of human
activities, such as pedestrian traffic or changes in land use. This can affect the
accuracy of the simulation results in areas with high pedestrian traffic or frequent
changes in land use. For example, Envi-met may not accurately simulate the
microclimate conditions in a park that is used for various events throughout the
year.
Finally, Envi-met’s computational requirements van also be seen as a limitation.
Envi-met requires a high-performance computing system to run large simulations.
This can be a barrier for some users who do not have access to such systems.
Furthermore, the simulation time can be long, especially for large and complex
models. This can limit the ability of users to test different scenarios and make
real-time decisions based on the simulation results.

5.2
Sensor System
When designing the sensor system for the AVS at Skjetlein High School, one
of the main requirements was being able to determine the spatial variation in
the microclimate factors being measured by the sensors. As such, a concept for
the sensor system was constructed and can be seen in Figure 5.6. The concept
includes a novel rail-motor-wagon system. A rail will sit between and run the
length of distance from one row of panels to the second row of panels. The rail
will stand on several legs which are dug into the ground beneath the rail. At each
of these legs, soil moisture and temperature sensors will be placed to measure
the soil properties. On top of the rail, a wagon will be installed which will house
multiple sensors such as the PAR sensor, weather station, and albedometer, all
useful or necessary sensors for the relevant microclimate factors. Whereas most
sensors systems for AVS in the past have been stationary, the wagon on the rail
will be connected to a motor which will provide enough power for the motor to
move from one side of the rail to the other. This periodic motion between one
row of panels to the other will allow the sensors to measure data at multiple
locations with flexible resolution. The resolution of the sensors can be calibrated
to measure data at different time stamps, and the motor can be calibrated at
different power to allow the wagon’s speed to be controlled.
The motor and
sensors have the added benefit of being able to be customized depending on the
needs of the user. However, the biggest benefit of this sensor system is that
the motor allows the wagon to move laterally between the panels, providing a
more detailed look at the microclimate factors and an opportunity to analyze
the spatial variation of the microclimate between the panels.

Validation Using Alpha Centauri Lab
As mentioned in Section 4.5.1, real life weather data from the Alpha Centauri lab
at Sentralbygget was used as a simple method to provide validation to the Envi-
met outputs and predictions. The three microclimate variables compared were
ambient temperature, relative humidity, and wind speed. The weather station
at the Alpha Centuari lab provided readings every 10 seconds, whereas Envi-met
provided outputs at an hourly resolution. Therefore, the Alpha Centauri lab data
was parsed at an hourly timestamp to be accurately compared to the Envi-met
outputs. Figure 5.8 shows the predicted Envi-met values and measured Alpha
Centauri values for ambient temperature. Envi-met’s values are generally higher
than the observed values from 03:00 to 11:00, with an average difference of 0.91
◦C. However, during the hours of 14:00 to 23:00, Envi-met’s values are lower than
the observed data, with an average difference of 0.92◦C. As such, the predicted
and observed values show a good correlation, with an index of agreement value
of 0.947 and Pearson’s correlation coefficient of 0.930. Similar strong index of
agreements and correlation coefficient were found by Ayyad and Sharples 2019
paper validating Envi-met using field measurements[72].

As mentioned in Section 4.1.6, on of the main Crop Growth Defining Factors
is air temperature. The air temperature was modelled for a scenario with the
AVS, and a scenario without the AVS. The reasoning is to understand how the
presence of the panels affect the air temperature in the surrounding area. Figure
5.11 shows a Leonardo Map for air temperature with an AVS at a height of 0.2m,
while 5.12 conveys the air temperature at 0.2m height without the AVS.
We see that the area between the two panels in Figure 5.11 experiences lower
temperatures than the equivalent area in Figure 5.12 without the AVS. There
are probably multiple reasons for this affect. First, the Sun at 11:00 June 21 is
still a few hours away from solar noon in Trondheim, meaning sunlight is coming
in from the East. The shadowing due to the East-coming sun caused by the row
of panels at x = 24m lower the air temperature near the ground, especially at
a height of 0.2m. Additionally, the heat from the solar panels would have little
affect on increasing the air temperature at 0.2m height due to the distance from
the vertical slice of air at 0.2m and the panels themselves. Solar panels tend
to have a greater affect on increasing the air temperature around them when
there are a high number of modules to compound the heating affect, as shown
in Armstrong et al[73]. There will be a total of 17 modules split between two
rows, at a distance of 10 meters apart. This relatively low number of panels and
distance between rows would most likely make any increase in air temperature
between the panels smaller than expected, and the results in Figure 5.11 seem
to confirm that. Still, the difference in temperatures between the AVS scenario

Wind Speed and Direction
One of the Crop Growth Limiting Factors is Evaporation, of which wind speed
plays an important part. It is this important to study the behavior of wind speed
with the introduction of AVS as well.
The wind speed for the scenario without the AVS at 11:00 on June 21 was 0.92
- 0.95 m/s throughout the whole 40m x 40m area as outputted by Envi-met.
Due to the uniformity of the wind speed, the Leonardo Map for this scenario
is not of interest and will be omitted.
However, the wind speed does show
significant behavior changes in the scenario including the AVS, especially when
compared to the 0.92 - 0.95 m/s speed for the non-AVS scenario. Figure 5.15
shows the results for the wind speed at 0.2m height on June 21 at 11:00 for
the AVS scenario.
The affect of the panels on the wind speed is clear, with
significantly decreased speeds around and all between the two rows of panels and
a fairly symmetrical distribution of wind speed. The panels of the AVS act as a
physical barrier that slows down the speed of the wind as it moves around the
panels. This is akin to the windbreak affect that can be used in agricultural
practices with the introduction of trees or shrubs and in turn modifies a nearby
microclimate. Windbreaks are most times intentional tools used in agriculture
to control erosion and improve plant survival in winter conditions[74], and can
improve overall farming conditions. In this sense, the lower speed in between the
rows of panels of the AVS can be a great benefit if designed with the end goal in
mind.

speed and radiation. High levels of both wind speed and and radiation would

5.4
Limitations of the Study
The following have been identified as limitations of this study:
• The primary limitation of this study is the validation method of the Envi-
met results with real life data. The initial method of validation for the
Envi-met results were meant to be the data measured by the sensor system
installed at Skjetlein. However, due to the delays in hardware, the Alpha-
Centauri lab was used as validation. Although the characteristics of the
Alpha-Centauri lab were captured in the Envi-met model, it is inherently
different than the ground-based AVS that is planned to be installed at
Skjetlein.
• When simulating the microclimate with Envi-met, TMY data was used for
the Trøndelag region and not specifically for Skjetlein high school.
• The only crop included in the microclimate simulations was 25cm Timothy
grass, meaning the simulations would need to be re-run for different plant
and soil profiles.
• The microclimate simulations were only conducted for a vertical bifacial
system, meaning simulations would need to be re-run to be accurate for
different configurations.
• The computing power associated with Envi-met simulations defined the
output results as a period of over 24 to 48 hours. To have a greater un-
derstanding of the microclimate behavior over longer periods of time, more
computationally intense simulations will need to be conducted.
• It took several weeks and months to understand the appropriate uses of
Envi-met, and even then, only a beginner level understanding of the soft-
ware has been achieved. A deeper understanding of the software over a
longer period of time would benefit this study.

Conclusion and Future Work
6.1
Conclusion
This study presents an Agrivoltaic system aimed at mapping the microclimate
parameters (e.g., temperature, humidity, wind) nearby and between panels to un-
derstand the impacts of the installation of the PV panels on local environmental
conditions, which can eventually be used to understand how these conditions
affect the plant growth.
This project is based on the installation of vertical
mounted PV panels over a small-scale agricultural field at Skjetlein High School.
A monitoring system using sensors was designed in order to measure relevant mi-
croclimactic factors. The Envi-met software was chosen to conduct simulations
to predict the microclimate changes due to the presence of the vertical bifacial
panels. Real life measurements from the monitoring system will help validate
Envi-met’s use for predicting microclimate in an AVS scenario.
Below are the main highlights of this work:
• EL images of the panels to be installed at Skjetlein were taken using Lu-
miSolarProfessional hardware. The purpose of this is to compare the start-
ing condition of the panels with EL images at a later date to better under-
stand damage to solar panels in an agricultural setting, of which there is
currently not much published research.
• A novel rail-motor-wagon sensor system was conceptualized for the AVS at
Skjetlein. A wagon runs on a rail powered by a motor between two rows
of panels. Sensors are attached to the wagon and rail, as well as ground
and rack mounted sensors. The advantage of such a system is that it is not
stationary as many systems in published research are. This provides the
ability to understand spatial variation in microclimate.
• Envi-met, a computational fluid dynamics software primarily meant for ur-
ban microclimate modelling, was chosen to simulate the influence of vertical
bifacial panels on the surrounding microclimate. The software allows the
customization of soil and vegetation parameters, offering more flexibility
in this manner than most competing softwares.
• Envi-met’s outputs were validated against weather data at the Alpha Cen-
tauri lab at NTNU. Among comparisons for ambient temperature, relative
humidity, and wind speed, the lowest index of agreement was 0.748 for wind
speed (a highly fluctuating parameter), and the highest index of agreement
was 0.947 for ambient temperature.
• Relative humidity had the least change in the AVS versus non-AVS scenar-
ios as predicted by Envi-met.
• Wind speeds are significantly lower and symmetric for the AVS scenario
versus the non-AVS scenario over the course of a 24 hour period as pre-
dicted by Envi-met. The drop in wind speed in the AVS scenario can be

Motivation
Agrivoltaics is a system of co-locating solar photovoltaic
panels with agricultural crops, where the panels are mounted
on a structure above the crops or are integrated into the
crops'
canopy.
Agrivoltaics
addresses
the
challenge
of
competing land use between energy generation from solar
radiation and food production (i.e., dual use of the land), as
well as the challenge of efficient land use in the face of climate
change and rising populations.
This poster presents methodology aimed at mapping the
microclimate parameters (e.g., solar radiation, temperature,
humidity, wind) nearby and between panels to understand the
impacts
of
the
installation
of
the
PV
panels
on
local
environmental conditions and how these conditions affect the
plant growth.
The methodology includes both the use of a commercial
software (Envi-met), and design of a system for collecting data
with sensors. This combined methodology will be used in a
project that involves the installation of vertical bifacial PV
panels over a small-scale agricultural field at Skjetlein High
School. The data collected will be analysed to determine the
effects of the panels on microclimate and plant growth, as well
as validating predictive models of the local microclimate.
Methodology
Microclimate Modelling with Envi-met
Summary
Simulations by Envi-met indicates a significant spatial variation in 
microclimate between the solar panels.
A sensor system is designed for measuring spatial variation in 
microclimate and evaluating the Envi-met simulations. This system will 
be implemented at an experimental field with vertically mounted PV 
panels.
