Abstract
The current project is part of a wider scheme that aims for the energy performance mod­
eling of a Solar Streetlight Stand­alone Product (SPLV). Two prototypes were installed at
Risø DTU Campus in 2019 to undertake the energy performance assessment of SPLV.
The present project is centered on deploying detailed energy performance models for the
storage side suitable to reproduce different operational conditions. Moreover, the models
need to be developed in such a way that the integration with the models of the rest of the
sub­components of the streetlight is guaranteed, to simulate system­level performance
on a further stage.
The first phase stands on a broad literature review analysis about the several options of
storage performance models. The selection of appropriate models is done including differ­
ent criteria i.e experimental characterization effort, electro­chemistry required knowledge,
and computational cost while sufficient accuracy.
The experimental characterization phase is developed at DTU Risø Laboratories through
battery cycling of two different batteries at different current rates and temperatures, under
controlled conditions.
The aim is to develop an extensive experimental data­set able to represent diverse oper­
ational conditions and evaluating battery performance in different situations.
The energy performance of the battery is simulated by two different models. A first model,
named as General Model, with a stronger empirical character and based on the experi­
mental dataset, and a second approach, denominated Shepherd Model, based on a sim­
ple electrochemical relation but also supported by laboratory testing. Both models are
trained with experimental data and validated afterward with field measurements provided
by the Living Lab at DTU, Risø. It is concluded that Shepherd Model is a more robust
and accurate model although it involves higher complexity in terms of understanding and
computation.
Although the integration with the rest of the sub­models remains out of the scope finally
due to time constraints, the insights gained on battery characterization and modeling will
be transferred to future projects where the integration of the sub­models into the SPLV
product is accomplished.

Introduction
1.1
Context and Motivations
Nowadays the effects of climate change are raising concern worldwide. Vital signs of
climate disasters are being reported by international institutions e.g NASA, such as loss of
sea ice, accelerated sea­level rise and longer, more intense heat waves [1]. The alteration
of climate patterns has been induced for the accumulation of carbon dioxide emissions
which concentration is being raised by human activities [1]. In this context, mitigation and
adaptation measures are urgent to avoid the acceleration of climate disasters. Efforts
to replace fossil fuels with renewable resources need to be intensified. Hereby appears
the first motivation for solar streetlight deployment, as a potential solution for sustainable
urban and rural lighting.
The solar streetlight conception as a stand­alone product implies that grid connection is
not required for the functioning and the system is suitable to operate in rural or inacces­
sible areas. This characteristic gives their common name Stand­alone PV Street Lamps
(SPLV). This fact is determinant for developing societies where grid access is limited or
nonexistent. According to the last report of the International Energy Agency (IEA) there
were still 770 million people in the world without electricity access in 2019, and 75% in
Sub­Saharan Africa [2], as it can be seen in Figure 1.1. However, it can be understood
from Figure1.2, where the global horizontal irradiation is represented, that usually, these
regions are presenting high solar potentials. Therefore, the fact that the solar resource is
plentiful in the areas where the application is needed should be a motivation for its further
development.
Moreover, the population with no electricity access increased during 2020 due to COVID
pandemic [2]. Global mobilization towards rural areas was another consequence of COVID
which has motivated as well the development of stand­alone products.

An additional motivation for the development of the SPLV is the declining cost and en­
hancement of the state of the art of individual technologies involved. The processes of
solar energy conversion, energy storage and lighting have been in continuous improve­
ment during the last decade. The cost of solar photovoltaic (PV) technology during the
last decade has decreased by 82% according to [4]. According to the chart at [5] around
3% efficiency increase was reached over the last decade for Mono­Si Best Research­Cell
Efficiency.
Second, Li­ion batteries are presenting high energy performance and declining costs. It
was reported a decrease of 88 % on the price for Li­ion batteries in transport applications
during the last decade [6]. In Figure 1.3 the learning curves of PV and Li­ion technologies
have been represented and it can be extracted that a decade ago it was not feasible
combining both technologies in an application. However, the current prices drop makes
the competition of SPLV as streetlights in the lighting market possible.
Third, Light­Emitting Diodes (LEDs) have been reported as the most efficient lighting
technology available on the market, considerably surpassing traditional streetlights per­
formance, typically sodium vapor, metal halide, incandescent, and fluorescent [7]. The
theoretical maximum luminous efficacy is dependent on the spectral distribution of the
emission of light [8]. The efficiency of the luminaries was assessed by [8] and LED are
leading the ranking with 48.7% energy conversion efficiency. Moreover, LEDs are pre­
senting a superior number of working hours than the other technologies. The LED lights
have 80,000 working hours while the later ones only run for 10 to 15 thousand hours with
more failure rates. [9]

Last, power electronics converters are evolving in such a way that the conversion of en­
ergy from the source to the load is optimized for the diverse operational conditions (low
and high irradiation), for both high scale and smaller demand generation, such as stand­
alone applications.
The development of efficient, sustainable and low­cost streetlights is particularly impor­
tant for developing countries due to the poor economies and absence of network access.
Improvements and optimization of streetlights design suitable for the context can be ob­
tained only with the development of robust energy models. The product implementation in
these countries should be facilitated by knowledge transfer and performance assessment
studies such as the one conducted by the present project.
1.2
Description of the SPLV Project
An SPLV integrates solar PV technology, a battery energy storage system, a LED lumi­
naire, and a converter that regulates the energy flows in the system. For the present case,
the solar cells cover the pole in five different orientations: North, South, West, East, and
Top. The characteristics and role of each of the components are explained in Section 2.
All the subcomponents (except PV panels) are located inside the pole with the idea of
minimizing vandalism, as can be observed in the sketch made in Figure 1.4.
The fundamental principle of an SPLV is that the solar energy is transformed to electricity
by photovoltaic cells and then accumulated during the day by a storage system to power
the LED luminaire during the night. The operation of the subsystems is regulated by power
electronics i.e. the Energy Conversion Unit (ECU). Normally the battery is designed to
provide certain days of autonomy and therefore the SPLV can provide electricity without
requiring grid connection, being defined then as a stand­alone product.

The idea of modeling the SPLV relies on obtaining a robust energy model which evaluates
the performance of the system on a seasonal and yearly basis, and in different locations.
The final purpose is to be used as a tool for the installation and optimization of the SPLV
product, facilitating its implementation on the market and contributing to its deployment.
For this purpose, development, and validation of subcomponents performance models
are required first.
The design of the SPVL is prone to constant improvements and changes, especially on
the converter and Battery Management System (BMS) side, as battery and converter
technologies are continuously improving in terms of performance and costs. Because of
that, robust, accurate, and easy to parametrize models are necessary for the subcompo­
nents. The overall project comprises the stages displayed in Figure 1.5 and is generally
explained in the paragraphs below. However, as it is a broad scheme it has been di­
vided into different sub­projects focused on particular goals. The specific objectives of
the present project are introduced in the next Section 1.3. During the project, the Msc
Project undertook by the associated student at [11] is often mentioned. The previous is
considered as a synergy of the present project with the same final target i.e energy perfor­
mance modeling of the SPVL product but different specific objectives. A joint collaboration
has been carried out regularly throughout the entire development of the projects.

The scheme starts with the characterization of each of the subcomponents. The charac­
terization of the battery is deployed in collaboration with the PV Team at DTU Photonik
Risø laboratories. The rest of the subcomponents are characterized in parallel by the PV
Team.
The second stage is the development of energy performance models for the individual
subcomponents and it is here where the efforts of the PV team have been centered.
The idea is to obtain robust models suitable to be implemented in different stand­alone
applications, being able to emulate individual behavior of the sub­components first and
the complete system functioning later.
The primary objective and focus of the present project is to develop a robust and accurate
battery performance model suitable to reproduce different operational conditions and to
be extended to further applications. The developed battery models will be used and built
upon in future projects where the Energy Management (EMS) and other submodels are
integrated into a single system.
After careful selection, development, and parametrization of individual models, validation
is needed to evaluate accuracy and robustness. The validation of the individual sub­
models is developed in a first stage based on laboratory measurements under controlled
conditions. Later the models are validated with field operation measurements, thanks to
the data provided by the database at the campus.
1.3
Project Objectives
As it was mentioned before, the present project is centered on developing an energy per­
formance model of the storage system suitable to be integrated into the SPVL application.
The following intermediate steps are set to overcome the goal.

Project Limitations
Certain objectives were not undertaken due to external constraints, especially affecting
the battery characterization and validation of the model.
First of all, characterization of DTU pole battery at different temperatures was not pos­
sible since the dimensions of the battery overtake the chamber size. To account for the
temperature effect on performance, it was attempted to scale the charge and discharge
curves of the High Star cell to the DTU­Pole size. However, due to time limitations and
measurements delay the idea could not be developed.
One difficulty which extended the testing period was that frequent interruptions along the
sequences of charge/discharge occurred when developing the battery cycling. After these
events, the complete cycling needed to be re­started, implying the loss of several working
days. The cause of these anomalies was not found until the end of the timeline and it
resulted to be discontinuities on the DC power supply which were finally overcome by
setting a stop condition delay for the charge in the testing interface.
As a wide characterization wanted to be done, the experimental phase took place along
the six months. Additionally, one cycling at the low current needs almost an entire week.
This caused a significant delay in the extraction of parameters and consequently models
completion, which could not be accomplished until June.
The battery named NSS remained out of any testing and characterization due to its late
arrival at the end of April. At this time, the thermal characterization of the High Star was
not finalized yet and it was decided to prioritize the complete thermal assessment rather
than initiating a new characterization. However, its characterization is promising since the
interface offers the possibility of measuring the SOC of the individual cells and understand
the voltage variation with the number of active cells, which is an innovation in comparison
with the previous systems.
The last objective consisting of the integration of the sub­models was not achieved before
the deadline due to a delay in the finalization of the sub­models, which was affected in turn
for the rest of the limitations. Therefore the optimization of the Streetlight design remains
out of the scope of the present project as well, which opens the gate for a potential future
research scheme in which the created models can be integrated.

3.1
Fundamentals of Electrochemical Theory
The main principle of electrochemical storage is that electric power is accumulated in
terms of chemical energy inside certain materials. The presence of two materials with
different potentials acts as the driving force of charge transfer. Although several tech­
nologies are found as electrochemical storages, which are later commented on Section
3.2 all of them rely on the fundamentals which are introduced below.
The chemical reactions take place inside the electrodes, which are materials presenting
a reduction potential difference. Due to this fact, one substance will be prone to oxidation
and the other to reduction if they are coupled. As a consequence, the compounds are
changing their oxidation state. The cathode or positive electrode suffers reduction during
discharge and the anode or negative electrode releases the electrons, therefore it gets
oxidized [14].
The oxidation and reduction of the materials generate an external flow of electrons which
is electrically compensated with an internal flow of ions. For this process to occur suc­
cessfully, it is required the existence of the electrolyte, which separates electrodes while
allows the ionic transfer from one electrode to the other one. This material needs to
presents high ionic conductivity to facilitate ions transfer while electronic insulation forces
the electrons to drive in the external circuit. As a result, the electrons transfer takes place
externally, being collected on the metallic plates that surround the electrodes. Therefore
electrons are accepted from a source during the charging or released to a load during
discharging [14].
Figure 3.1: Sketch showing the parts of a typical lithium­ion battery and sketch showing
the electrochemical principles driving the functioning of the battery. Source: [15]
A battery cell is therefore the combination of both electrodes, electrolyte, and current col­
lectors. There exist a wide variety of batteries but the project is centered on Li­ion tech­
nology. More specifically, the negative electrode is made from carbon particles grouped
by graphite layers (C6) whereas the positive electrode contains LFP (LiFePO4) as the
active material.
The reversibility of the chemical reactions inside the electrodes determine the possibil­
ity of recharge­ability. On the one hand, there exist primary batteries which experience
irreversible chemical reactions and cannot be reused once the active material has been
depleted. On the other hand, recharge­ability is possible for secondary cells due to the re­
versibility of the chemical reactions involved. Applied to the project, both electrodes have

The maximum achievable potential difference between the electrodes is named the Open­
Circuit Voltage (OCV), which is experienced when no current flows. However, the charge
transfer and internal reactions make the effective cell potential to be different from the
OCV. When the cell is connected to a certain load or source, charge transfer takes place
and certain losses affect the process. On one hand, electrochemical reactions and charge
concentration gradient incur overpotentials. This fact makes the voltage of the cell to
be dependent on its State Of Charge (SOC). On the other hand, the internal resistance
intrinsic in the electrolyte, current collectors, and electrodes has to be overcome by both
ionic and electronic charges. Typically the addition of the losses is called polarization [16].
As a consequence, the terminal voltage of the cell is different from the OCV, decreasing
for discharge and increasing for the charge as it is expressed with Equations (3.1) and
(3.2), where Vop+ and Vop−are the overpotentials at the positive and negative electrodes,
respectively, and Rpol stands for the polarization resistance.
The identification and modeling of both polarization effects is an essential factor for the
founding of battery models.

3.2
Characterization of Energy Storage Systems
In the following section the characteristics of the storage systems are compared. The
comparison of power and energy density is typically done by terms of a Ragone plot
(Figure 3.3), which allows comparing the properties of different technologies. Two different
categories can be observed: technologies able to provide small amounts of energies
in extremely short periods (low specific energy and high specific power, situated more
towards the left of the plot) versus technologies that can provide a higher amount of energy
at a low pace (right side of the plot). On the former group super­capacitors are the extreme
providing the highest power and on the latter Li­ion technology can provide the highest
energy. Moreover, Li­ion technology covers a wide interval of high power and high energy
applications. It presents high energy density 100­265 Wh/kg [21] and high power density
(up to 10,000 W/kg) [22]. The wide range found for each specific material technology
arises from the fact that the applied rate of charge/discharge determines the power and
energy extracted.
Apart from the batteries technologies showed in Figure 3.3 there exists other types of
electro­chemical storage, such as redox flow batteries and fuel cells [23]. The former is
used for accumulating substantially high amounts of energy during long periods with a low
self­discharge. The de­coupling of energy density and power density is possible which
makes them valid for a wide range of applications [24]. They are promising in stationary
storage of energy, for instance, grid balancing. The latter could be implemented in stand­
alone applications but entails the instantaneous injection of the reactants, which is not
always possible for certain applications.
It can be extracted from here that the choice of the storage system is directly related to the
expected requirements of the system which are set by the purpose of the product under
study.

Electrochemical Models
The models which represent with more detail the charge transfer process and kinetics
inside the cells are referred to as electrochemical or multi­physics battery models. They
are very diverse depending on the process addressed, but all of them include several
physical phenomena by terms of the implementation of their correspondent laws. As a
consequence, parameter identification is expensive but at the same time they describe
the systems with complete physical meaning.
The Newman model is one of the most popular electro­chemical models for Li­ion since
it represents diffusion dynamics. It can predict current/voltage with high accuracy [34].
Other traditional models are the pseudo­2D model and the Single Particle Model (SPM).
The former treats the active material as a collection of equal particles whereas the latter
is a simplification that considers both electrodes as single particles [35].
The models are based on non­linear partial differential forms and numerical methods are
needed to solve the equations. Due to the elevated computational complexity, the mod­
els have already been implemented at various software such as COMSOL Multiphysics
software or the package Battery Mathematical Modelling from Python (PyBaMM). The last
one can solve physics­based electrochemical models within reduced periods. [36].
As these models cannot be applied to real­time simulations due to the elevated compu­
tational cost, therefore simpler models are required to analyze the battery operation and
performance over longer time periods.
Mathematical Models
Mathematical battery models simplify the chemical processes to estimate running time,
efficiency, and capacities with short simulation times. The drawbacks are accuracy and
limited applications; typically the model is valid for a certain case or operating conditions.
Originally, the mathematical battery models have been inspired by the Shepherd relation
although the Unnewehr universal model and Nernst model are also available 3.1. The
three methods are voltage­current models where different parameters describe the over­
potentials explained in Section 3.1. A comparison between the three battery modelling
approaches is offered at [35] and concludes that Shepherd Model leads to the lowest error
for stationary applications, whereas the Nernst model predicts better dynamic voltage.
The modelling errors of mathematical models are typically in the range 5­20% [37]. The
addition of extra terms to the initial relations is typically done to improve accuracy, although
the characterization effort increases as well. An example is a Modified Model which is
widely described in Section 4.3.2.
ECM Models
A different approach for battery modeling is represented by the electric circuit models
(ECM) which are typically used in engineering to co­design and simulate integrated sys­
tems.There exist a wide range of ECM modeling approaches depending on the degree of
accuracy and computational effort involved.

The internal resistance is a key aspect in the performance modeling of batteries since
it defines the power capability [38]. It can be calculated by applying current steps, a
procedure named as Hybrid Pulse Power Characterization Test [38]. A certain current is
applied while the voltage difference experienced in the cell is measured. Afterwards, the
Ohm’s law leads to the resistance value by relating the voltage drop and rate involved in
the steps.
The other possibility for doing the impedance characterization is by terms of impedance
models (represented in Figure 3.6b), which utilize Electrochemical Impedance Spectroscopy
(EIS) methods to obtain a complete characterization of the dynamic equivalent circuit com­
ponents. An extra feature is that diffusion impedances are represented with the addition
of a Warburg impedance. The EIS method involves applying a known AC potential to
the cell and measuring the current response for a wide range of frequencies. The total
impedance of the cell, Z, can be expressed as a sum of real and imaginary contributions,
which is typically represented on Nyquist plots, where the real part is plotted in the x­axis
and the imaginary part in the y­axis of the diagram. Afterwards, EIS data is fitted to an
equivalent electric circuit model [40].
The ECM is considered as a trade­off between mathematical models and complex elec­
trochemical models since they can offer considerably higher accuracy without exacer­
bated complexity. However, a more detailed battery characterization is required, which
increases development time and costs. Moreover, the repetition of tests for several SOC
and temperatures considerably prolongs the characterization stage. This is the reason
why this option has been discarded for the current project.
Last, several relations can be identified between mathematical and ECM approaches.
First, the internal resistance voltage loss is added in both models with an ohmic resistance
multiplying the operational current. Second, the combined effect of all the RC networks in
the ECM simulates a low­pass filter which is represented by a filtered current in the math­
ematical models. The voltage drop in the network is then equivalent to the polarization
ohmic drop term associated with the filtered current [16].
Data­driven Models
Finally, there exist models based on measurements that disregard the internal processes
and are not based on relations or electrochemical laws. On this line, there are first em­
pirical models, which are based on experiments and fitting of parameters. Several exper­
iments reproducing operational conditions are developed and then data is collected and
fitted with Least Square Methods [41].
Second, data­driven models estimate the variables in the battery according to historic data
measured over a time period. The models are deployed by machine learning algorithms
[35]. A relation is obtained by machine learning between the inputs and the output. As
a consequence, the resultant fitted parameters are lacking physic meaning. The dataset
is divided in two groups in such a way that the model is trained with certain data and
validated with the rest.

An important aspect to consider when modelling battery performance, especially for en­
ergy storage systems deployed in environments without temperature regulation, is the
impact of temperature on battery capacity and performance. The admissible temperature
range is considered as −20 °C
60 °C at [15] while the optimal interval is contemplated
15 °C –35 °C. Degradation and safety issues are found when the operational conditions
overtake the recommended limits. Materials properties and the chemical reactions in­
volved are sensible to temperature affecting battery capacity and power. On this line, the
impact of temperature on Li­ion can be divided as low temperature and high temperature
effects. As multiple processes are referred to in the paragraphs below, a summary of the
situations has been included in Figure 3.7.
Low temperature effects on battery performance
Low­temperature effects are typically occurring in climates and applications where the
battery temperatures goes below 0°C . There are several processes compromising per­
formance simultaneously. First, the viscosity of the electrolyte increases at low tempera­
tures, with the ion conductivity reduction as an immediate consequence [42]. The diffusion
in the electrodes is also affected. This difficulty upon charge displacement is represented
by an increase in internal resistance. Moreover, high internal resistance leads to high heat
generation and less uniformity in temperatures and voltages inside the cell [42]. Improve­
ments are being done on low­freezing points electrolytes and additives [42]. Second, the
transfer resistance increases in the electrodes, which means that the performance on the
kinetics in the electrodes is affected [15]. The velocity of the chemical reactions changes
according to Arrhenius Equation.
Both effects lead to a reduction of capacity and power. The decrease of capacity is related
to the loss of effective active material driven by the insufficient performance of chemical
reactions while a decrease of power is associated with the increase of the internal resis­
tance. Third, the potential of the Li­ion plating has been considered the most severe fading
29
==PAGE:44==
process in the LIB at low­temperature [15]. This takes place because of low temperature
polarizing the anode and lithium ions start depositing on the surface of the electrodes,
blocking the surfaces and therefore causing a reduction of battery capacities.
High temperature effects on battery performance
On the other side, there are high­temperature effects that are not necessarily driven by
high ambient temperature. Heat generation inside the cell takes place because of the
chemical reactions and Joule effect when high operational currents are applied.
Degradation of a LIB battery from 25 to 55°C is studied at [43]. It can be understood
how an increase in the temperature increases the capacity of the battery but at the same
time, the rate of degradation of capacity during cycling is accelerated. This acceleration
of degradation is explained by phase change and surface modifications due to high tem­
peratures[43]. Therefore, the short­term effect of temperature is an increase of effective
capacity because of internal changes, which will affect calendar and cycle aging.
Moreover, there is an impact on the materials constituting the cell layout at high tempera­
tures. Separator and electrolyte breakdown and binder displacement can ocurred at high
temperatures Furthermore, the Solid Electrolyte Interphase (SEI) which is a passivation
layer required for the correct battery performance [44], increases at high temperatures
because of the decomposition of the substances, increasing the impedance of the whole
battery.
Finally, an extremely high temperature could turn into thermal runaway and self­ignition.
For this reason, temperature monitoring of the cells is, therefore, an important factor to
take into account in the operation of the batteries. Different Solutions are presented at
[15] which are divided as contact and contactless monitoring. Thermal runaway could be
reached if the previous factors are disregarded.

4.2
Determination of battery roundtrip efficiency
Battery Roundtrip Efficiency RTE is defined as the energy that can be retrieved after a
charging [47]. In electrochemistry, is better known as Energy Efficiency EE. It provides
information regarding how does the battery behave according to capacity and voltages si­
multaneously; Because of that, it is the efficiency definition more used from the application
point of view [46].
In electrochemistry theory, EE is defined as the product of Coulombic Efficiency CE and
Voltage Efficiency V E [48].
EE = CE · V E
(4.1)
First, CE compares the amount of charge which can be extracted and injected during a
complete battery cycle. Ideally, it would be equal; however, as side reactions are hap­
pening simultaneously, the amounts of Ah extracted at both processes might be slightly
different.
Second, V E accounts for the difference between the average voltage during charging
and during discharging. The highest the difference means the higher the overpotentials
in both processes. The overpotential is depending on the SOC; The higher the SOC
normally the higher the overpotential obtained. As a consequence, materials with a plain
voltage evolution vs SOC present therefore better voltage efficiencies. This is usually
happening with two­phase materials, such as LFP. Moreover, voltage efficiency presents
a linear relation with the applied rate. High current rates and low temperature cause high
overvoltages and therefore reduce V E [46].
In this project, energy efficiency can be obtained either with the theoretical relation at
Equation (4.1) and by introducing experimental capacities and voltages, or directly by the
measured experimental energies, i.e the ratio of discharged and charged energies for a
full cycle, as represented with Equation (4.2).

The efficiency of the battery can be extracted by charge and discharge cycles. However,

it is considered a low complex model in terms of computation since it is based on lin­

charge and discharge curves that need to be measured at certain rates and tempera­
tures. A higher set of experimental curves would decrease the errors incurred with the
interpolation but higher time and cost would be associated with the experimental phase.

a black­box. On the one hand, this makes the model accessible since pre­knowledge is
not needed; on the other hand, no physical information about the processes inside the
battery can be extracted. The main limitation is that robustness can be questioned since
the model accuracy is very sensitive to the initial SOC when a small sample of days is
simulated, as it is explained in Section 5.2.2. Additionally, model parameters need to be
adjusted by trial and error to reflect seasonality which decreases robustness.

4.3.2
Modified Shepherd Model
The Modified Shepherd model is a simplified electrochemical model, based on a current­
voltage modeling relation. It has been considered one of the most successful models to
predict Li­ion behavior, according to [50]. Moreover, it can also describe different types
of technologies, such as the NIMH and Lead­acid, which increase the value of the model
[37]. In comparison to the Shepherd Model, the Modified Shepherd Model is an improve­
ment since present extra terms to model more precisely certain internal processes. Mod­
ified Shepherd Model has been previously applied at [37] and the errors obtained were
around 5% for SOC in between 20% and 100 % .
The clear advantage of the model is that the parameters can be extracted from the cell
manufacturer data­sheet and in principle, no experimental tests are required to apply
the model. This fact reduces the characterization effort required for model applicability,
in comparison with other models which require a more extensive characterization effort.
In comparison with the General Model that requires both charge and discharge curves,
Shepherd Model can estimate discharge and charge voltages with parameters extracted
only from a discharge curve, e.g discharge curve at nominal conditions sometimes given
in the datasheet.
However, when the parameters are defined just for the nominal conditions and assumed to
be constant for the rest of the operational setpoints, the errors incurred in the model could
be high. Therefore, the contribution of the project is an attempt to improve the dynamic
application of the Modified Shepherd Model by a extending parameters characterization to
several operational conditions. This characterization is possible due to the measurements
collection obtained in the experimental phase.
The model describes the electrochemical behavior of the cell in terms of terminal volt­
age, open­circuit voltage, internal resistance, applied current, and SOC for both charge
and discharge processes. The terminal voltage is estimated considering the open­circuit
voltage and accounting for the ohmic and polarization losses, which were expressed with
Equations 3.1 and 3.2 in the previous Section 3.1.
Different expressions of Shepherd equations are found in literature review at [35], [37],
[51]. Finally, Shepherd Equations (4.7), (4.8), (4.9) implemented in [52] are chosen, as
they provided the best modelling results after trying the different options.

during the winter period. The contribution of the study is to extend the validity of the model
by trying to solve each of the latter limitations, by extracting parameters for each current
and temperature and therefore encompass a wider range of operational conditions.

4.3.4
Optimization of parameters
The optimization function uses the non­linear least squares solver from Matlab. The op­
timizer receives as first input an initial set of parameters, the actual current and voltage
curves that want to be reproduced. Given the initial set of parameters, Shepherd Model
equations are applied with those, and the voltage is estimated and compared with the
actual one; if the difference is small enough, the process stops, if not, another iteration
starts, where a different set of parameters is introduced, until a valid solution is found.
Upper and lower boundaries are applied to the initial set of parameters to limit the com­
putational effort. Moreover, the number of iterations and the tolerance of the error can be
adjusted. An important issue is that discharge and charge curves are fitted together to
minimize errors.
Once it was demonstrated the validity of the Shepherd Equations and the optimization
function, the process was applied to the experimental curves. The initial guess of pa­
rameters is obtained by visual inspection as explained before in Section 4.3.3. In Figure
4.7 can be compared the curves when the parameters are extracted by visual inspection
(Figure 4.7a) and after the fitting (Figure 4.7b), for an experimental curve. It can be un­
derstood that the visual inspection method leads to a considerably high error, especially

5.2.1
SOC estimation
Before proceeding with the voltage estimation, the battery SOC needs to be estimated
since it is one of the required inputs for the general model. One­year simulation is stud­
ied for the sake of identifying different operational conditions. The period under analysis
corresponds to June 2019­ May 2020. For calculating the SOCfield, the power measured
on the battery has been used and it is represented in orange in Figure 5.8. This could be
compared with the SOC of the ideal case i.e provided 100% roundtrip efficiency, which di­
rectly reflects the difference between PPV , and PLED integrated over time; This case has
been denominated as SOCRaw, in purple. It can be deduced that the difference between
SOCfield and SOCRaw correspond to losses generated in the process of charging and
discharging the battery. General Model takes into account RTE and therefore SOCRaw
would be decreased according to the considered efficiency; A roundtrip efficiency of 81%
has been considered (corresponding to 90% discharge and charge efficiencies) and rep­
resented in yellow. Finally, the SOC resultant with the application of SM by the imple­
mentation of Equation at (4.13) is also represented in blue. It can be understood that the
trend of this case is similar to SOCfield pattern since it was obtained based on the filtered
field current, already measured in the battery, and not with a scaling of the power inputs.
After SOC analysis it can be extracted that one potential factor that constrains battery
performance is operational temperature, as was explained in Section 3.4. For that rea­
son, correlation with temperature has been included in Figure 5.8 and it can be noticed
how the SOC starts differing when temperature decreases below 10 °C. This indicates
lower performance and supports the decision of obtaining a battery characterization at
low temperatures, i.e 5 °C and 10°C.

least two experimental tests are required for the interpolation. It was obtained with the
two models that the highest incurred errors are associated with battery SOC maximum
and minimum thresholds. This is because close to SOC limits, battery voltage dynamics
behave differently and voltage prediction is more complex. Furthermore, for Shepherd
Model, it was shown that lower operational currents lead to voltage prediction less accu­
rate. For General Model, however, the relation of model accuracy and input power was
not conclusive. Finally, it was demonstrated that the adaptation of RTE is required for
reproducing seasonality when General Model is implemented. However, as it saw the
performance of the converter also gets considerably reduced at low irradiances period.
It is not possible to conclude from the analysis if the low temperature is the main reason
for battery efficiency decrease during winter. The low­temperature effect could be asso­
ciated with higher errors on the winter at Shepherd Model since this model takes as an
input current measured in the battery, therefore the efficiency of the converter is already
accounted for. However, as it was explained in Section 5.2.3 lower currents are leading
to the highest errors; Therefore a direct association with low­temperature effect is not
possible neither.

Conclusions
Regarding laboratory testing, it was learned that the cell is subjected to higher stress
under charging than under discharging, especially at higher rates, and the capacity that
can be provided inside the safety range of voltage decreases. This is accelerated at lower
temperatures. Because of that, it is learned that consecutive charge­discharge cycling is
not the proper way of deploying the tests since the loss of capacity of a charging process
influences the consecutive test, up to a 20% of capacity fade, as was obtained with the
analysis of the experimental measurements. The addition of the CV stage after CC does
not reproduce load conditions
From the model selection, it was learned that there exists a trade­off between experimen­
tal characterization effort and error incurred in the models. An extended experimental
dataset would lead to a more accurate model but with higher characterization effort. This
is reflected with the Shepherd Model when accuracy increases when optimal parameters
are extracted at several rates. The same applies to the General Model, where a higher
number of experimental curves entered in the lookup table improves interpolation.
Another finding is that the models should be designed to reproduce better the voltage
associated with the most probable operational current required by the application. This
implies that before the experimental phase is developed, a careful analysis of the opera­
tional conditions is needed to identify both ordinary operational conditions and anomalies.
From the implemented models, it is concluded that Shepherd Model is a more robust and
accurate model since it can predict voltage with yearly average errors of 0.3% and yearly
maximum errors lower than 6%. Although low experimental characterization is needed,
it involves certain computational effort, since the fitting of the parameters is required pre­
vious to the model implementation. On the other side, General Model, based on simple
interpolation methods, performs with yearly average errors of 1.3% and yearly maximum
errors lower than 9%. General Model is considered as a not very robust model since RTE
needs to be changed during the year for reproducing field measurements trends.
Finally, the effect of low and high temperature on battery performance was assessed by
analyzing the experimental discharge and charge curves under controlled conditions in
the laboratory; however, a clear temperature effect on the battery operation in the field
cannot be concluded with the findings obtained in the project.

Improvements and Further work
The characterization stage of the energy storage system could be extended by testing
the NSS battery. With that, the temperature testing would be directly done in the modeled
battery since it fits in the environmental chamber of DTU Risø laboratory. Experimental
characterization can be done as in the present project and afterwards measured curves
dataset can be parametrized to be used in the two models.
The second potential learning driven by NSS battery is based on a smart BMS and power
electronics, which selects the operative cells according to their SOC and load voltage
requirements. Broad knowledge can be acquired from this system, acquiring knowledge
about BMS laws and power electronics algorithms. In addition, the understanding of volt­
age thresholds of the individual cells and the number of cells operating simultaneously
can derive learning about the proper operational conditions to extend the battery lifetime.
Regarding the modeling options, the Electric Circuit Model (ECM) technique was dis­
carded for the project due to a more complex experimental characterization involved.
However, according to the literature review, ECM is an excellent trade­off between com­
putational complexity and accuracy [35]. It was discovered in the last week of the project
timeline that the Battery Analyzer presented in the laboratories can determine cell capac­
itance and inductance, apart from internal resistance. Therefore a performance model
based on an ECM circuit could be attempted and compared with the models developed
in the present project.
Regarding the future application of the deployed energy models, extension to different bat­
tery technologies may be considered, such as NiCd, and consequently to a wider diversity
of stand­alone products. For the General Model, the discharge and charge experimental
curves of any technology could be entered as inputs. For Shepherd Model, the Equations
need to be slightly adapted as it is explained at [37]. Therefore, the acquired knowledge
in terms of characterization and modeling is transferable to different cases.
Regarding the validation stage, the models could be validated on the new streetlights that
will be installed in Risø in the summer of 2021. These poles are upgraded versions in
terms of converter and battery and also include irradiance sensors. Therefore, field mea­
surements would be extended and the deployed models could increase their robustness.
Lastly, as it was mentioned several times along the course of the project, the integra­
tion of the battery models with the rest of the subcomponents models into a system that
reproduces the SPVL behavior remains as future labor.
