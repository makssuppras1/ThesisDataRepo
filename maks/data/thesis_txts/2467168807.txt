Abstract
Electriﬁcation is one of the most important elements in transitioning away from fossil fuels in the energy system - a transition
needed to reach the climate goals of the Paris Agreement. Industry plays a signiﬁcant role in energy use and related emissions, in a
European context accounting for 25% of energy consumption and directly emitting more than 9% of greenhouse gas emissions in
2015. Using the fundamental energy system model Balmorel, the eﬀect of increasing industrial electriﬁcation to levels beyond the
COMBO scenario from the European Commission Long-term Strategy is analyzed. In order to adequately reﬂect the characteristics
of the industry sector, the model has been improved with an industrial heat demand proﬁle and heat demands based on end-use
temperature ranges and fuel type currently used. Using a maximum heat capacity policy, the model is used to endogenously achieve
a direct electriﬁcation degree of 70% in industry by 2050. Through a scenario analysis the eﬀects of demand-side ﬂexibility in this
high electriﬁcation settings are examined. Retaining a 50% share of the fuel-based boilers as backup show some potential for cost
savings at high electriﬁcation degrees, but at the expense of less overall electriﬁcation. Demand-shift, through the use of a heat
storage with capacity equal to 4 hours of average demand, shows promising results considering both cost, industrial electriﬁcation
rates, GHG emissions and electricity prices for both the overall system and industry speciﬁcally. Further work is needed to evaluate
the costs of ﬂexibility not included in Balmorel to calculate real world beneﬁts of further electriﬁcation and associated demand-side
ﬂexibility.
Keywords: Energy Modelling, Balmorel, Electriﬁcation, Demand-side ﬂexibility, Future Energy Systems, Industry
1. Introduction
In a world of climate change mitigation, many and drastic ap-
proaches must be applied simultaneously to achieve the rapid
transition towards the Paris Agreement goal of holding the in-
crease in the global average temperature to well below 2°C
above pre-industrial levels and pursuing eﬀorts to limit the tem-
perature increase to 1.5°C (UN, 2015).
One instrument in this is the deployment of renewable en-
ergy (RE) technology, such as wind turbines, hydropower and
biomass, to curb the greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions from the
energy sector. Recent years have shown a massive decrease in
RE prices and corresponding increases in installed RE capacity,
which globally reached 2179 GW by the end of 2017 (IRENA,
2018). However, the RE production accounts only for around
10% of global energy consumption, leaving much room for the
energy transition to pick up speed (REN21 Secretariat, 2018).
Looking into the Danish context paints a somewhat greener
picture with 31% of ﬁnal energy consumption (FEC) delivered
by RE sources, and a world-record 54% of ﬁnal electricity con-
sumption (Energistyrelsen, b). In 2018, a political agreement,
Energiaftalen, was made to ensure 55% RE in FEC in Denmark
in 2030 and net zero carbon emissions in 2050 (Danish Ministry
of Energy Utilities and Climate, 2018).
With many RE sources being inherently variable (VRE), se-
curity of supply (SoS) is an increasing concern as RE penetra-
tion continues to climb. Denmark boasts an impressive SoS of
more than 99,99% in electricity with the current levels of RE
(Danish Ministry of Energy Utilities and Climate).
Denmark relies to a large extent on biomass to deliver the
bulk of this RE production, currently accounting for more than
two thirds of production (Energistyrelsen, a). This reliance can
be a threat to the energy transition outlined by Energiaftalen, as
concerns are growing over sustainable biomass resource avail-
ability and the actual GHG emissions from biomass. To support
the goals of 55% RE in 2030 and general GHG emission reduc-
tions a range of other tools must be brought in play.
According to Dansk Energi (2019) electriﬁcation will be the
most important element in the phase out of fossil fuels in the
energy system. This paper examines the eﬀect of demand side
ﬂexibility within industry in an increasingly electriﬁed energy
system. Electriﬁcation is here deﬁned as the increased use of
electrical energy, either directly1 or indirectly2. Electriﬁcation

1.1. Industry - Energy, Electricity and Emissions
According to International Energy Agency (2018) industry3 ac-
counted for roughly 25% of FEC in EU and 21% in Denmark
in 2015. More than 9% of GHG emissions in the European
Union (EU28) originated from industrial processes and prod-
uct use in 2015, not including indirect emissions from the ap-
proximately 1.000 TWh of electricity consumed in the sector
(Eurostat, 2019).
In Denmark and EU28, electricity accounted for 21% of FEC
in 2015 with respectively 29% and 34% of industrial FEC de-
livered by electricity according to International Energy Agency
(2018). This demand is almost exclusively direct electriﬁcation,
as indirect electriﬁcation technologies are not yet signiﬁcantly
mature or widely applied. As both direct and indirect electriﬁ-
cation technologies are developed, the degree of electriﬁcation
is expected to rise drastically towards 2050. Vast amounts of
VRE are required to satisfy this demand to allow for a decrease
in fossil fuel reliance and associated GHG emissions from in-
dustry.
1.1.1. Future of Electriﬁcation
As a basis for evaluation of electriﬁcation initiatives, the EU
long-term strategy (LTS) scenario COMBO is replicated in Bal-
morel. European Commission (2018) outlines eight scenarios
varying in ambition between GHG reduction of 80% to net-
zero in 2050. COMBO represents a middle ground aiming to
achieve 90% GHG emission reduction compared to 1990 levels,
but falls short of the Paris Agreement goal of well below 1,5°C.
COMBO draws upon the cost-eﬀective aspects of the various
80% reduction scenarios, namely electriﬁcation, power-to-X4,
hydrogen and energy eﬃciency scenarios.
The COMBO scenario is reproduced in terms of FEC and fuel
share for transportation, buildings and industry. Direct electri-
ﬁcation levels are respectively 21% for transportation, 74% for
buildings, 52% for industry and 51% of total FEC. From this
basis the eﬀect of further electriﬁcation of the industry sector
is examined with varying degrees of ﬂexibility. To perform this
analysis the energy system model Balmorel is used. However,

1.2. Research Objective
This research aims at improving the modelling of industry and
related demand-side ﬂexibility in the Balmorel model.
The
COMBO scenario is then constructed in Balmorel, as well as
a more ambitious scenario for industrial electriﬁcation, called
Industry. Three further scenarios are used to evaluate the ef-
fects of demand-side ﬂexibility on the electriﬁcation potential
for industry, focusing on electriﬁcation of the Danish industry
sector with a European outlook.
The project is carried out in collaboration with Ea Energy
Analyses alongside the Ea-driven project Roadmap for Elektri-
ﬁcering i Danmark5.
2. Methodology
The following section highlights the methodological approach
applied in this research including a brief description of the Bal-
morel model, the improvements to the model and the data pro-
cessing.
2.1. The Balmorel Model
Balmorel is a partial-equilibrium, bottom-up optimization
model focused on technical and policy analysis of combined
heat and power systems. The model code is open source, but is
written in the model language GAMS, requiring a commercial
licence to run (Gatautis and Ravn, 2005; Wiese et al., 2018a). A
thorough description of the original model is given in Balmorel
Project (2001). To improve the model, proprietary add-ons have
been developed to allow for detailed analysis of parts of the
energy system. Balmorel is a suitable model for investigating
the eﬀects of demand-side ﬂexibility, as it allows for optimiza-
tion on both the longer investment horizon and detailed hour-
by-hour dispatch. The eﬀects considered are largely related to
the power system, which is well represented in Balmorel. Fur-
thermore, Balmorel largely meets the conditions of being open
source, collaboratively developed and has the structural proper-
ties required to be considered suitable for scientiﬁc work as set
out by Wiese et al. (2018b).
Geographically, the model can be applied to any country, but
is most developed for the Baltic region and Europe. In this
research the model area consists of Denmark, Sweden, Nor-
way, Finland, Germany, Estonia, Latvia, Lithuania, Poland,
Czech, Austria, Switzerland, France, Belgium, Netherlands,
Great Britain, Italy and Luxembourg. In Balmorel, countries
consist of one or more regions. Each region then contains a
number of areas.

2.1.1. Limitations
Balmorel, as any other model, has its limitations. As the model
is partial-equilibrium it does not take into eﬀect general soci-
etal eﬀects beyond those reﬂected in the input data. Therefore,
precautions must be made when concluding on the results as a
proxy for the energy system as a whole. Previous studies have
shown an eﬀect of models to underestimate uncertainties and
radical changes in general society(Craig et al., 2002). How-
ever this is less relevant when considering the relative eﬀects of
demand-side ﬂexibility more so than the overall results.
Being a full foresight model, the use of storage is artiﬁcially
eﬃcient. This also includes the heat storage reﬂecting demand
response. A detailed analysis of the use of the demand response
is carried out using a BB3 simulation to evaluate how realistic
the operation is.
Balmorel uses a simpliﬁed transmission system with only in-
terregional constraints. Intraregional constraints are increas-
ingly likely to aﬀect the energy system with increasing elec-
tricity demands. Furthermore the hourly resolution does not
account for short spikes in transmission demand. Balmorel can
be soft-linked with other models (e.g. SisyfosR) to further in-
vestigate the SoS levels of the future system.
Lastly the investments done by the model is optimized on a
one year basis (the current modelling year). Thus the invest-
ments might not be economically sensible over a longer time
period.
2.2. Industry in Balmorel
This research proposes an improvement to how the industry
sector is modelled in Balmorel. Industrial demand was rep-
resented with current electricity demand as part of the general
electricity demand and with only limited representation of the
heat demand of industry.
The model contained the option of investments in fuel
switching (FSW) based on the assumption of 25% of indus-
trial heat demand available for electriﬁcation by 2025, 50% by
2030 and 100% by 2050, linearly in between. Non-electric de-
mand was covered by fuel-based boilers with capacities based
on capacity shares given by 2015 useful energy demand shares.
Electriﬁcation was possible by investment in electric boilers.
Industrial FSW had no temporal variation in the demand pro-
ﬁle.
2.2.1. Industrial Demand Modelling
Sørensen et al. (2015) mapped the energy use in Danish indus-
tries as of 2012. Their work divided the energy use into cat-
egories based on 22 end-uses, 20 fuel types and 57 industrial
branches. For this research, the data is grouped into ﬁve end-
uses and ﬁve fuel types. End-uses are process and space heat at
low temperature (LT), medium temperature (MT) and high tem-
perature (HT), internal transport (INTT) and other electricity
usage. Fuel groups considered are ’electricity’, ’natural gas’,
’oil and petroleum products’, ’solid fossil fuels’ and ’renew-
ables and waste’. As current industrial electricity demand is al-
ready included in the general electricity demand, only the fuel-
based demands are focused upon here.
Wiese et al. (2018a) identiﬁes the decomposition of demands
as a key future development of the Balmorel model. This is
achieved by using the above data to expand the model repre-
sentation of industrial heat demand to include 12 demand areas
based on end-use temperature and type of fuel currently used
to supply the heat. Furthermore the industrial internal transport
was included as a new electricity demand at a regional level.

2.2.2. Heat Generation Technologies
Each demand area is supplied by a fuel-based boiler and possi-
bly one or more electricity-based technologies. Electric boilers
are available for all demand areas as well as the temperature-
corresponding heat pump (HP). All technologies available for
delivering industrial heat demand are shown in Table 37. Based
on Zühlsdorf et al. (2019) and an expectation of the develop-
ments in the ﬁeld of heat pumps to continue, heat pumps are
assumed to be economically feasible at a signiﬁcant scale for
the medium temperature range from 2030 onwards, but is in-
cluded in the modelling for all years.
As the industrial heat demand covers a wide range of pro-
cesses and applications, the full demand will not be supplied
by actual heat pumps, but it is assumed that it will be covered
by a technology with similar eﬃciencies and costs on average.
Furthermore, it is beyond the scope of this study to give any
detail on the speciﬁc heat supply for the heat pumps. Use of ex-
cess heat through heat cascading is likely to play a signiﬁcant
role in the implementation of heat pumps, especially at higher
temperatures.
2.2.3. Industrial Heat Demand Proﬁle
In order to accurately evaluate the eﬀects of ﬂexibility, the
varying demand in industry is important. Wiese and Baldini
(2017, 2018) shows clear temporal variations in demand in the
Danish industry . For space heating the eﬀect is mainly sea-
sonal, whereas process heating is largely varying on a daily and
weekly basis. For this research, the space and process heat pro-
ﬁles for single-, double- and triple-shift industries is combined
into a single industrial proﬁle, with respective weightings of
7,1%, 44,4% and 48,5%. Space heating was weighted as 37,9%
of total heat demand and process heating as 62,1%. The result-
ing proﬁle is shown in Figure 1 and is applied to industrial heat
demand for all model countries.

2.2.4. Industrial Electriﬁcation Policies
In order to control the degree of electriﬁcation, diﬀerent poli-
cies and capacities are developed. Based on the intended use of
the model the following policies may be activated or not.
Maximum Heat Capacity Policy
Policy deﬁning the maximum heat capacity for electricity-
based technologies. Used to mimic the socio-technical chal-
lenges of the industrial transition towards electricity-based
technologies. Uses the MAXHCAP policy type, thus allow-
ing the model to freely chose between heat pumps and electric
boilers to supply the electricity-based heating capacity.
Capacity constraining policies assume electricity-based tech-
nologies to be cheaper than fuel-based technologies to approx-
imate actual electriﬁcation degree. If fuel-based technologies
are cheaper for some hours, the actual electriﬁcation degree
will be lower than anticipated, as electricity-based sources will
not be fully utilized. Partly to compensate for this, the max-
imum capacity of electricity-based heating is set correspond-
ing to electriﬁcation degrees higher than what is estimated in
the scenario inputs. Speciﬁcally +5% electriﬁcation in 2030,
+10% in 2040 and +15% in 2050 for all demand areas. These
values are also chosen to reﬂect the increasing uncertainty of
estimating adoption rates of technology at longer time hori-
zons. Thus the model is allowed further investments, should
it be cost-eﬀective according to input.
As Balmorel does not contain a MINHCAP policy type, and
under the assumption that electricity-based heating has lower
operational costs, the choice was made to not undertake the
work of implementing a minimum heat capacity policy and ac-
companying policy type.
Heat Production Policies
Policies deﬁning the minimum or maximum heat production
from fuel-based boilers, i.e. the remaining heat after electriﬁca-
tion. Uses the MINPRODHEAT and MAXPRODHEAT policy
types. Allows for exact deﬁnition of electriﬁcation degree, but
is very computationally heavy. This policy is implemented in
the model, but is not used in the scenario analysis due to com-
putational times8.

2.2.5. Industrial Flexibility Options
Balmorel contained no options for demand-side ﬂexibility for
industrial heat demand. To this extent two ﬂexibility options are
implemented in the model. Firstly, a heat storage is introduced
to allow for demand shifting. The storage is modelled with a
95% eﬃciency to simulate the expected losses related to shift-
ing production in industry. The storage is modelled with a ca-
pacity of four hours of average demand. Both the eﬃciency and
storage capacity is subjected to a sensitivy analysis, as shown
in Section 4.7.
Another ﬂexibility option is the allowance of fuel based boil-
ers to remain as backup capacity when introducing a higher de-
gree of electricity-based heating. This is controlled through a
exogenous capacity deﬁnition using a scaling factor deﬁning
the ratio between new electric capacity and decommissioned
fuel based capacity. Currently the input allows for one scaling
factor for all areas, but could easily be expanded to cover each
area individually to account for diﬀerences between fuel types
and process temperature.
3. Data & Scenarios
Balmorel requires many data inputs to adequately describe the
energy system. The following section highlights the central in-
put data used to create each scenario.
3.1. Scenario Description
Five scenarios are created based on varying degrees of elec-
triﬁcation and ﬂexibility measures. Serving as a reference sce-
nario is the COMBO scenario. From this basis, an ambitious in-
crease in direct electriﬁcation forms the Industry scenario. The
model run with capacity policy only.
aforementioned ﬂexibility measures are then individually in-
troduced in IndustryFlexBackup and IndustryFlexStorage, and
lastly combined in the most ﬂexible scenario IndustryFlex.
Note that all demand values in the following section refers to
EU28 demands in contrast to values in Section 4, where aggre-
gates refer to the 19 modelled countries.
3.1.1. COMBO Scenario
As mentioned in Section 1.1.1, the EU LTS COMBO scenario is
replicated in Balmorel. This is done sector-by-sector by match-
ing total demands and fuel shares, as shown for 2050 in Figure
2.
Only a subset of the demands are represented in Balmorel.
Electricity and E-fuel demands are included for transport. Cur-
rent electricity demand from transport is included in the clas-
sic demand, corresponding to rail electricity usage. Additional
electricity and E-fuel consumption is expected to arise from
road transportation and is modelled as electric vehicles. The
resulting electricity demand of 47 TWh in 2020, 142 TWh in
2030, 304 TWh in 2040 and 466 TWh in 2050 are split amongst
the EU28 countries proportionate to the amount of cars in 2017,
using data from Eurostat.
For buildings the current electricity demand is also included
in the classic demand.
Based on available data from Euro-
pean Commission (2018) the electricity demand for individual
heating is estimated at 239 TWh in 2015, developing to 334
TWh in 2030, 431 TWh in 2040 and 482 TWh in 2050. To-
tal demand is split into national demands based on the number
of heat pumps estimated in the Global Climate Action (GCA)
2040 scenario from ENTSO-E’s Ten Year Network Develop-
ment Plan, TYNDP (ENTSO-E, 2018).
E-fuels demand is included in the model as hydrogen pro-
duced by SOEC with an eﬃciency of 70%. The demand for
E-fuels are added across sectors and split nationally similar to
road transport, as the main usage of E-fuels is in this sector.

3.1.2. Industry Scenario
The COMBO scenario is then further electriﬁed in the industry
sector to achieve the Industry scenario. Fuel demand for in-
dustry in 2015 and 2050 in the two main scenarios are shown in
Figure 3. Compared to COMBO the Industry scenario has a sig-
niﬁcant limitation on biomass usage, in line with the concerns
presented in Section 1. Natural gas and E-fuels are retained
at similar levels (90 TWh and 372 TWh respectively). Some
biomass is kept for HT applications (107 TWh), to some extent
in the form of biogas. District heating is retained at COMBO
levels.
Energistyrelsen (2014) estimates 88% of natural gas con-
sumption in Danish industry can be electriﬁed.
Remaining
use of natural gas is used for certain process heat applications,
mostly where a ﬂame is needed. When considering temperature
ranges, data acquired from Bühler et al. (2019a) indicates tech-
nical feasibility for complete electriﬁcation of low and medium
temperature applications.
High temperature demand is esti-
mated to be 61% electriﬁable. An overall electriﬁcation of in-
dustrial space and process heating of 83% is deemed technically
feasible. For the Industry scenario, a total heat electriﬁcation
degree of 73,5% is achieved for EU28 and 82% for Denmark,
thus utilizing almost all of the technically feasible options.
Industrial electricity demand in 2050 amounts to 1730 TWh,
an increase of 690 TWh from 2015 levels and 346 TWh more
than the COMBO scenario. Furthermore, 531 TWh of electric-
ity is required to produce the E-fuels required by the scenarios.
Internal industrial transport accounts for 105 TWh of the above
demand, fueling a 100% transition to electriﬁed transport. Elec-
trical transport is assumed to deliver twice the energy service
per fuel consumed compared to oil-based transport.
Resulting from the above measures, is a direct electriﬁca-
tion degree of 70% for European industry. Including indirect
electriﬁcation the degree of electriﬁcation reaches 85%. This
compares to 52% direct and 66% indirect electriﬁcation in the
COMBO scenario.

3.1.3. IndustryFlexBackup Scenario
This scenario is similar to Industry, but with the addition of
additional backup capacity of fuel-based boilers. 50% of boiler
capacity that would have been decomissioned due to electriﬁca-
tion are retained in the system, decreasing the exposure to elec-
tricity price peaks, but limiting the usage of electricity-based
capacity.
3.1.4. IndustryFlexStorage Scenario
This scenario is similar to Industry, but with the addition of
a heat storage with a capacity corresponding to four hours of
average demand. The storage has an eﬃciency of 95% and a
load/unload speed of four hours.Thus, the load/unload capac-
ity is one hour of average demand per hour - corresponding to
37% of peak demand. The introduction of the storage allows
for shifting demand to hours with cheaper electricity prices, re-
sulting in a higher utilization potential for installed electricity-
based capacity.
3.1.5. IndustryFlex Scenario
This scenario is similar to Industry, but with the addition of
both additional backup capacity of fuel-based boilers and a heat
storage with a capacity of four hours of average demand.
3.2. Fuel and CO2 prices
Across all scenarios the same CO2 price is used, given in
DKK/tCO2: 194 in 2020, 377 in 2030, 1056 in 2040 and 2640
in 2050. This is in line with the value of 350 EUR/tCO2 in
2050 presented by European Commission (2018) for scenarios
achieving net-zero GHG emissions in 2050. For Great Britain
a further 165 DKK/tCO2 is added in line with national policy.
This is however removed for 2040 and onwards, as CO2 tax
prices then have increased beyond the levels intended to be af-
fected by the British policy. Fuel prices are also kept constant
across scenarios. Prices from the Sustainable Development tra-
jectory in ENTSO-E (2018) are used. Prices are shown for the
fuels directly relevant to industrial demand in Figure 4.

4. Findings
In the following section, main ﬁndings from the study are pre-
sented. For additional detail, please refer to Appendix B. Note
that aggregated demands are shown for the 19 model countries,
and as such is not directly comparable to values shown in Sec-
tion 3.
4.1. Electricity Demand and Industrial Electriﬁcation
Based on the COMBO scenario, total electricity demand is
increasing strongly from 2015 to 2050 for both Denmark
(+155%) and the whole model area (+104%). The new indus-
trial electricity demand is 265 TWh for the model area and 4,9
TWh for Denmark in 2050 for the COMBO scenario. In the In-
dustry and ﬂex scenarios, the demand rise to between 513 and
609 TWh for the model area and between 9,0 and 9,6 TWh for
Denmark in 2050. The lowest demand is as expected seen for
IndustryFlexBackup, where ﬂexibility comes from substituting
electricity with other fuels in high electricity price hours. In-
dustryFlexStorage requires the most electricity, as the demand
shift oﬀers increased utilization of low electricity price hours.
These results are generally acceptable compared to the ex-
pected degree of electriﬁcation.
As shown in Figure 5, the
achieved electriﬁcation rates for Industry match decently with
the expected value for 2050, but is lower for 2040. IndustryFlex
shows a deviation from that trend, slightly increasing electriﬁ-
cation at moderate levels in 2040, but decreasing electriﬁcation
in 2050.
Focusing on Denmark shows similar trends. In 2030 electri-
ﬁcation rates of 17,9-18,5% are achieved compared to 13,6%
expected. Expected electriﬁcation in 2040 is 43,8% with the
less ﬂexible scenario performing at 43,4% and the ﬂexible at
46,2%. For 2050 the scenarios reverse with Industry achieving
84,8% electriﬁcation compared to just 72,1% for IndustryFlex.
The expected level for 2050 is 82%.

The additional electricity demand is in an European context met
by RE sources and to a small extent increased nuclear produc-
tion, as shown in Figure 6. In the Industry scenario in 2050

4.3. Electricity Price
Electricity prices rise for all scenarios from 2015 levels of 273
DKK/MWh for the model area and 223 DKK/MWh for Den-
mark to prices between 445 and 477 DKK/MWh in 2050. Price
developments are shown in Figure 7. The increase in price is ex-
pected from the vast increase in overall demand, and is well in
line with the increase shown by European Commission (2018).
For all years after 2020, the main driver of electricity prices is
increased overall demand. IndustryFlex shows a clear reduction
in electricity prices compared to the non-ﬂexible scenario at
moderate electriﬁcation levels, indicating a demand shift from
the more expensive hours to cheaper, VRE-dominated hours -
bringing the average price close to COMBO levels. At high
electriﬁcation levels, i.e. 2050, the price eﬀect is very small,
yet still showing a reduction.
4.3.1. Industrial Electricity Price
More signiﬁcant eﬀects are seen considering industrial elec-
tricity prices.
As shown on Figure 8, electricity prices are
strongly related to degree of electriﬁcation and ﬂexibility mea-
sures. Due to the shape of the industrial demand proﬁle, as
shown in Figure 1, the industry sector has signiﬁcant overca-
pacity for most hours, rendering the eﬀect of additional backup
capacity very small before 2050. The eﬀect of overcapacity
is seen for both Industry and IndustryFlexBackup at moderate
electriﬁcation levels in 2040, as relatively more demand is cov-
ered by electricity in low-priced hours and substituted by fuel-
based boilers in high price hours. This is also clear from the
strong price increase for Industry in 2050, when very high elec-
triﬁcation degrees leads to limited ﬂexibility in fuel choice for
most hours for LT and MT applications.
Demand shift, here modelled as a heat storage, shows a sig-
niﬁcant eﬀect on price for the years 2030-2050. IndustryFlex
shows the overall lowest prices for industrial electricity con-
sumption, especially in 2050 where the combination of backup
capacity and demand shift allows for signiﬁcantly reduced aver-
age prices - a 35% reduction compared to Industry. Looking at
the model area, the ﬁndings are largely the same with ﬂexibil-
ity measures having considerable impact on average industrial
electricity prices.

4.4. GHG Emission Intensity
Increased direct electriﬁcation from the COMBO scenario will
eﬀectively substitute fuel-based boilers with RE-based electric-
ity, as shown in Figure 6. The resulting GHG emissions de-
crease signiﬁcantly for industrial heat. For Denmark in 2050,
the emission intensity is reduced by 89-92% relative to 2015-
intensity, as shown in Figure 9. Already in 2030 a reduction of
38-39% is found for Industry scenarios. Note that the intensity
for 2040 and 2050 does not include the share of heat demand
covered by P2X.
In 2030 and 2040 the emissions from the electricity sector
are increasing with the additional electricity demand relative
to COMBO, but a positive feedback eﬀect is seen for 2050,
where the emissions from the electricity sector itself is slightly
reduced, as RE covers more than the increased demand.
4.5. Cost
Achieving this electriﬁcation requires investments and transi-
tions of the industrial sector. Although Balmorel only covers
certain cost aspects, Figure 10 suggest a savings potential rela-
tive to COMBO when considering the system level, except for
Industry in 2050. Any conclusions based on these cost levels
are highly sensitive to cost factors for diﬀerent technologies and
fuels. Thus, care should be taken to conclude on actual levels.

4.8. Findings Summary
COMBO shows a strong increase in electricity demand towards
2050. The energy system undergoes a radical transformation
towards RE and especially VRE sources - VRE accounts for
74% of generation in 2050. The electriﬁcation of industrial heat
demand and internal transport increases demand by roughly 300
TWh relative to COMBO, depending on level of ﬂexibility. The
new demand is covered by solar, biomass and wind generation.
The new modelling performs reasonably well in relation to
electriﬁcation degrees for the Industry scenario. In 2040 the
electriﬁcation rates are somewhat lower than expected for Eu-
rope, but on par for Denmark.
2030 electriﬁcation rates in
Denmark are higher than expected, indicating positive circum-
stances for early industrial electriﬁcation in Denmark.
Average electricity prices decrease with increasing electriﬁ-
cation, especially when coupled with ﬂexibility measures. The
eﬀective electricity price for industrial heat demand areas are
strongly dependent on ﬂexibility measures with a 35% reduc-
tion from Industry to IndustryFlex in 2050. Electrifying more
of industrial heat demand can also lower prices per industrial
heat produced, especially in combination with demand shift-
ing. Similarly, total system costs are signiﬁcantly reduced by
introducing ﬂexibility.
Dispatch analysis shows in detail the eﬀects of both backup
capacity and the heat storage. The heat storage is used both for
strong daily ﬂuctuations, but also for minor price changes, es-
pecially around parity with fuel-based boilers. The use of the
storage was in some cases possibly too eﬃcient and with a ques-
tionable storage time of several days, but generally functioned
as expected.
5. Discussion
With the new additions to the Balmorel model, the industry is
represented in more detail. This allows for new analysis ap-
proaches as shown in Section 4. Depending on the desired anal-
ysis, the way of representing the industry might diﬀer from the
approach presented here - a good example being the work of
Wiese and Baldini (2018) with more detail for Danish indus-
try, but requiring substantial work to apply across other model
countries.

==PAGE:16==
The COMBO scenario serves well as a reference scenario,
considering the ambitions set forward by both Danish and Euro-
pean politicians. However, the ambitions presented in COMBO
regarding the period leading up to 2030 are low. Building upon
the recent plans by Dansk Industri (2019) and most Danish
politicians to reduce industrial GHG emissions by 70% in 2030
relative to 1990 levels, action similar to the levels presented
through the Industry scenarios are required. The medium term
ambitions are also likely to be raised in an European context, as
put forward by European Parliament (2019).
5.1. Modelling of Industrial Demand
Additions to the Balmorel model allows for more accuracy
in determining the industrial demand of each country, includ-
ing a decent estimation of electriﬁcation potentials, dependent
on end-use temperature and fuels currently used. More work
would be beneﬁcial in calibrating the demand shares for the rest
of Europe, if possible similarly to Sørensen et al. (2015). The
method applied here is uncertain due to the peculiarities of the
composition of the Danish industry sector. Especially the low
prevailance of heavy industries and nothing in the non-ferrous
branch. However, it is the belief of the author, that the method
is a step in the right direction for the model accuracy.
Splitting demand into temperature and fuel speciﬁc areas al-
low for easier and more realistic control of electriﬁcation. Hav-
ing individual areas allow for capacity constraining policies to
be eﬀective and accurate. The scenario analysis is also im-
proved by the ability to examine industrial heat demand based
on several parameters. End-use temperature ranges could be
adjusted, but the overall demand shares would not change sig-
niﬁcantly within reasonable ranges. Technology options should
be continuously assessed for all temperature ranges and possi-
bly expanded within the model. The role of biogas and district
heating in the industrial heat demand should also be considered.
District heating also in the context of energy cascading oppor-
tunities.
The modelling of industrial internal transport could be im-
proved in future work. Both in terms of electriﬁcation possi-
bilities, ﬂexibility and demand proﬁle. Optimally the industry
demand would be split completely from classic demand, simi-
larly to the ideas presented by Wiese and Baldini (2018).
5.2. Flexibility Measures
Introduced ﬂexibility measures generally display the desired ef-
fects - with especially the demand shift in form of a heat storage
improving the system. Backup capacity shows a positive eﬀect
only for 2050, as existing peak capacity already acts as backup
capacity at low-moderate electriﬁcation levels.
The inclusion of the heat storage shown in hourly runs is pos-
sibly too eﬃcient, as the model can freely distribute the stored
heat over a full week - which is likely too long a time horizon
for industries expectedly acting within the 36-hour horizon of
the day-ahead market. With increasing incentives for industries
to become autoproducers, this might change. The expected de-
velopment within automation and the increasing cost share of
electricity is also likely to foster a more ﬂexible demand from
many industrial branches.
Backup capacity modelling could be reﬁned by limiting it to
high electriﬁcation levels. The heat storage would beneﬁt from
further research into the socio-technical conditions surrounding
demand shift, leading to a more accurate cost and eﬃciency
representation.
5.3. Limitiations of the Model
A central limitation in the modelling is the inability to geo-
graphically shift industrial demand over longer time periods,
as industry would be progressively more willing to relocate to-
wards cheaper electricity prices as electricity becomes a big-
ger cost share. This is also the case for P2X modelling, where
the model is currently limited to production within the model
region of use. This might force Balmorel to invest in subop-
timal VRE placement, instead of relocating the demand to the
cheaper production sites. Being a partial equilibrium model,
this is one of the drawbacks.
In relation to the scenario analysis, the partiality of the model
also excludes important cost aspects of ﬂexibility, such as ex-
penditures of ﬂexible production, e.g. additional wages. The
conclusions regarding cost are also very sensitive to the cost as-
sumptions made for the boilers and heat pumps. With the indus-
trial representation being rather simple, a lot of important cost
details are surely lost along the way. Further work is needed to
reﬁne the cost analysis.
6. Conclusion
The industrial representation within Balmorel is improved by
introducing heat demand areas based on end-use temperature
range and the currently used fuel. Based on this, the electriﬁ-
cation rates can be relatively well controlled using a maximum
heat capacity policy, as electricity-based heating is generally
the cheapest option. The capacity policy is much less computa-
tionally intensive than a generation based policy. Industrial heat
demand is also reﬁned by introducing a demand proﬁle based
on Wiese and Baldini (2017, 2018). Flexibility is introduced by
retaining fuel-based capacity as backup capacity while electri-
fying, and through demand-shift in form of a heat storage.
With data from Sørensen et al. (2015) and the Odyssee
database, demands for all European countries are calculated.
The COMBO scenario from EU LTS is recreated in Balmorel
as a reference scenario from which direct industrial electriﬁca-
tion is raised from 52% in COMBO to 70% in Industry. The
ﬂexible scenarios show that the new demand can be increas-
ingly covered by VRE, is cheaper for both industry and the sys-
tem and for demand-shifting shows lower GHG emissions with
increasing ﬂexibility. Especially the eﬀect of demand-shifting
is valuable in early electriﬁcation of the Danish industry sec-
tor, yielding higher electriﬁcation potentials at reduced costs.
Further research is needed to evaluate the beneﬁts of this ﬂexi-
bility against the expected industrial expenditures outside of the
model.
The work presented here, gives some insight into the value of
demand-side ﬂexibility in future highly electriﬁed energy sys-
tems, but equally provides an oﬀset for decomposition of indus-
trial demands and their implementation in Balmorel.

Appendix A. The Balmorel Model
Balmorel is a fundamental energy system model for the partial
equilibrium of combined heat and power systems. Balmorel is
applied for both technical and policy analysis at both national
and international levels. The model has a bottom-up approach,
i.e. all technologies and units are modelled independently and
then grouped to form the overall system. Balmorel aims at min-
imizing the annualized costs for the whole energy system, while
fulﬁlling given conditions and constraints.
Balmorel can assist in the analysis and decision making for
many diﬀerent energy related issues. With geographical ap-
plications ranging from small island systems with the possibil-
ity of very detailed modelling to larger, interconnected regions
such as most of Europe. The model has no technical limitations
beyond the computational power required to solve the systems,
data availability and the ingenuity of the model operator.
The question posed to the model can range from the techni-
cal speciﬁc, over overall consumption targeted and policiy anal-
ysis to the addition of new system features, such as increased
demand-side ﬂexibility. Being that ﬂexible allows Balmorel to
be applied to many high level planning issues with a need for
optimization and evaluation of system eﬀects.
Soft-linking can be used to supplement the analysis carried
out in Balmorel. Upstream links with models focused on all
sectors such as TIMES, STREAM or LEAP can provide a
broader overview. Downstream linking with PSS/E, SisyfosR
or similar can evaluate the feasibility of the Balmorel results in
relation to grid operations.
Balmorel is in principle an open source model, but requires
a commercial GAMS license to run. Furthermore many oper-
ators have proprietary add-ons to increase model accuracy and
quality. As the main code of the model is open source, it shows
a high degree of transparency.
Appendix A.1. Functionalities and Input
The following section highlights key functionalities in the Bal-
morel model, including the objective function, various model
modes, important constraints and representations.
A visual
overview is also given in Figure A.16.
Appendix A.1.1. Objective Function
As mentioned Balmorel aims at minimizing annual costs for the
whole energy system. This is done by minimizing the objective
function shown in (A.1) under given constraints, such as fulﬁll-
ing demand.
Minimize
Annualized System Costs = Fuel costs
+ Taxes
+ O&M costs (variable and ﬁxed)
+ Capital costs
+ Transmission costs
+ Change in consumer utility
(A.1)
Fuel costs are deﬁned exogenously for the model horizon
based in this case on the Sustainable Development scenario
from TYNDP (ENTSO-E, 2018). Taxes include both fuel, en-
ergy and emission taxes, which are also deﬁned exogenously
and can vary from country to country. A general CO2 tax is es-
tablished uniformly for the whole model area, except for Great
Britain, which has introduced a national surcharge.
Investment costs and O&M costs are deﬁned on a per tech-
nology basis. Within the given bounds of exogenously deﬁned
units and endogenously invested units, e.g. Table 3, the model
calculates the system costs related to all units.
Change in consumer utility is relevant to the extent demand
is made elastic, which is not the case for this research.
Appendix A.1.2. Constraints
System feasibility is then evaluated given constraints simulating
the real world energy system limitations. Constraints are re-
lated to capacity, generation and operational characteristics of
production units, transmission lines, energy consumption and
storage facilities. Electricity, heat and other types of energy
carriers can be included in the constraints - often with other fu-
els represented through a "heat" demand. Constraints also limit
the use of certain fuels and emissions from deﬁned parts or the
whole system. Furthermore basic balancing constraints are in-
cluded, ensuring that generation meets demand. Constraints are
included both as fundamental constraints, e.g. operation of a
backpressure unit, and as policies deﬁning various limitations
to the system, such as reaching targets for RE-shares in gen-
eration or total GHG emissions. Slack variables are included
to ensure a complete model run even for technically infeasible
solutions.
Appendix A.1.3. Model Modes
Balmorel is a deterministic model with full foresight over the
modelling horizon. Any variability is set using capacity factors
and proﬁles on both production and demand side. As the model
is bottom-up each technology is modelled independently. The
model has two distinct modes run in cooperation: economic
dispatch and capacity expansion.
Economic dispatch operates units on a least-cost economic
optimization basis according to market conditions, i.e. merit
order curve based on short run marginal cost. This is calculated
for each time step and for each model region. Regions closely
resemble price regions in the Nordpool market.
Capacity expansion is based on the initial dispatch data for a
full model year. Balmorel will then evaluate whether the whole
system cost is lower when investing in additional capacity - or
when decommissioning old plants. When the new capacity op-
timum has been obtained, the model iterates back to economic
dispatch.
This co-optimization of dispatch and investments proves a
strong resemblance to the real world systems. The iterations
allow for a dynamic evaluation of the energy system year on
year, including the change in cost factors for old plants under
the changing regimes. Another strength of the model is the abil-
ity to optimize transmission and production capacity simultane-
ously.

Practically, there are three model variations available in the
core model.
BB1 Economic dispatch for one year
BB2 Economic dispatch and capacity expansion for one year
(Called investment run)
BB3 Economic dispatch for each season iteratively (Called

The choice of variation depends on the focus of the analysis.
Often an iteration between modes are required to achieve the
most accurate solution. Certain aspects in the model requires
full year runs to reasonably be represented, such as hydro power
with reservoirs, capacity investments and annual fuel use re-
strictions. It could be argued that investments ought to be made
with more than a yearly perspective. To this end a BB4 mode is
being developed by researchers at DTU allowing for simultane-
ous investment and dispatch optimization over all model years.
A series of practical approximations, assumptions and aggre-
gations are made due to the extensive computational demands
arising from modelling a large energy system bottom-up with
hourly time steps, possibly over more than 30 years. Depend-
ing on the energy system being analysed, the requirements for
simpliﬁcations vary. In this project large parts of Europe are
considered and the time horizon stretches to 2050.
Appendix A.1.4. Geographical Representation
Three geographical layers are represented in Balmorel. Coun-
tries are modelled (CCC set), and are subsequently divided
into regions (RRR set) resembling each price area in Nordpool.
Each regions holds areas (AAA set), which each represents a
speciﬁc part of the energy system. Countries in Balmorel are
shown in Figure A.17.
Power is transferred between regions with no intraregional
transmission limitations considered. Regions are also the basis
for the balance of production and demand. Areas are used to
deﬁne speciﬁc capacity distributions, such as wind farms with
a certain production proﬁle or a district heating demand. There
are no limits as to how many areas can be within each region.
In Ea’s version of the Balmorel model, Denmark is mod-
elled in great detail with almost individual plant representation.
Other European countries are also modelled fairly closely, but
with a higher degree of aggregation of production capacity. The
grouping is usually done based on fuel type and eﬃciency of
units.
Any subset of modelled countries can be chosen to suit the
analysis undertaken. For this research the choice of countries
reﬂect a focus on Denmark, but with the understanding that the
Nordics and Central Europe is of great importance in the opera-

Appendix A.1.5. Temporal Representation
Temporal dimensions in Balmorel are years (YYY set), which
are divided into seasons (SSS set). Each season consists of a set
amount of time steps (TTT set). A Balmorel year is constituted
by 52 seasons (in this case weeks) of each 168 time steps (in
this case hours). This totals 8734 hours per Balmorel year, 24
hours short of a standard year.
Figure A.18: Comparison of chosen model area (left) and EU28 countries
(right).
Any selection of years can be chosen as the overall time hori-
zon, but each model run will optimize for a period depending on
the chosen model mode. In BB1 and BB2 the optimization hori-
zon is one year, whereas it is one season (often meaning week)
in BB3. Without loosing signiﬁcant validity, the temporal res-
olution of the model can be compressed, e.g. 12 representative
seasons simulating the seasonal patterns throughout a year. The
time steps can also be grouped, especially for investment runs.
Lastly not each year has to be modelled. Instead a subset of the
modelling horizon is selected to give the wanted level of detail.
For this research, time aggregation is applied for the invest-
ment runs to 12 seasons, 11 time steps and 4 modelled years
out of the 31 years in 2020-2050. This reduces the number of
timesteps to solve from 270.816 to just 528 - less than 0,2%
of the non-aggregated time steps. This achieves computational
times of approximately 2 - 2,5 hours for investment runs. For
the hourly runs the resolution was full hourly, i.e. 52 seasons
of 168 time steps. For this reason the choice was made to limit
the run to two years: 2030 and 2050. Thus the resulting time
steps to solve are 17472 or 6,5% of time steps between 2020
and 2050. As hourly runs only consider operational constraints
- and no investments - the computational times were limited to
3-4 hours.
Appendix A.1.6. Demand
Balmorel considers demand for both electricity and heat, and
is given exogenously as a nominal, annual consumption at re-
gion or area level. For electricity demands are deﬁned at re-
gional level, whereas heat demands are given at area level.
Demands are given annually and then given an hourly proﬁle,
which is then applied to achieve the demand for each time step
to be solved. Demand elasticity can be included to express the
willingness to deviate from the nominal demands calculated as
aforementioned. For this research there is no demand elasticity
included.
For this research, the granularity of industrial demand areas
have been improved. Thus industrial heat demand is now in-
cluded as 12 areas for each region. Industrial internal transport
electricity demand is included at a regional level.
Appendix A.1.7. Energy Generation and Transmission
Energy generation is handled based on the nature of the gen-
eration. For solar, a capacity is given per area with an asso-
ciated generation proﬁle. Wind is handled through a regional
wind speed proﬁle and an area speciﬁc power curve. This al-
lows for inclusion of diﬀerent generation potentials with vary-
ing characteristics, e.g. one wind zone with low expected full
load hours and another with a higher amount. Furthermore, en-
ergy is generated on dispatchable units. Initially the energy sys-
tem is deﬁned by exogenously given units, but with continuous
model investments the generation mix is increasingly endoge-
nously controlled. To this end, a wide range of technologies
are deﬁned in the model, from which the model can choose in-
vestments. Similarly, transmission capacity is initially deﬁned
exogenously, but with endogenous build-out options.

Appendix A.2. Flexibility
Balmorel has several ﬂexibility measures to ensure the fulﬁl-
ment of the given constraints and optimal operation of the en-
ergy system. Below is a list of the ﬂexibilities present in the
Balmorel energy system model.
• Pumped hydro
• Short-term heat and electricity storage
• Integration of heat sector
• Integration of transport sector
• Transmission
• Demand response
Pumped hydro is included in the model in a way that allows it
to act as a long-term storage. Diﬀering from other storages, the
inﬂow is given exogenously. Both heat and electricity storages
are included in the model. Depending on speciﬁcations of the
technology, these can be used as very-short term storages with
quick ramping possible or for example as seasonal heat storages
with slow ramping times, but generally higher capacities. The
implementation of both heat and electricity storages allow for
ﬂexibility through the integration of the heat sector, as demands
can be shifted in time in one sector dependent on the status in
the other.
The transport sector is to a limited extent included in the
model. Balmorel includes road transport in the form of EVs.
These are represented through a regional electricity demand
with associated demand proﬁle. EVs act as a source for ﬂexibil-
ity through a share of the EVs being able to shift the charging
schedule. At high-price hours the EVs will refrain from charg-
ing, at low price hours more EVs than planned will be charging.
A central source of ﬂexibility, is the interregional transmis-
sion. Especially with increasing penetration of VRE, the im-
portance of a well-developed transmission network is set to
increase. Thus the temporal variance in VRE generation can
to some extent be mitigated through geographical smoothing.
However, this is generally an expensive form of ﬂexibility, as
the capital expenditures related to transmission capacity are
high.
Therefore demand response is an important ﬂexibility mea-
sure. Demand response is included in Balmorel in diﬀerent
ways. One option is elasticity of demand, as mentioned in Sec-
tion Appendix A.1.1. This way the actual demand will shift
based on deﬁned price levels. Another option is the inclusion
of virtual storages, similarly to the work presented in this pa-
per. This is implemented for various demand types, including
classic demand and individual heating - now also for industrial
heat demand. This is a very simple implementation, which is
vulnerable to the technology parameters for the virtual storage.
It also has a limited representation of the varying steps with
which demand side response is likely to happen.
Appendix A.3. Limitations
Balmorel, like any other model, is an approximation of real-
ity. Therefore it also has its shortcomings. As mentioned, the
model is partial equilibrium and as such only considers the sup-
ply and demand balance under the given circumstances. When
considering a horizon of 2050 the eﬀects of societal changes are
likely to be immense, but not included in the model. Thus, the
conclusions drawn from Balmorel simulations only hold under
given societal structures. One case in point is the 1.5TECH and
1.5LIFE scenarios from European Commission (2018), where
the fundmental modelling of the two scenarios does not diﬀer
signiﬁcantly. The diﬀerence lies in the societal changes brought
on by circular economy. In Balmorel this is implicitly included
in the demand levels.
Perfect competition in the energy market is also assumed in
Balmorel, which is generally a decent approximation with a
widely accepted use. However, imbalances in market design
could lead to scenario outcomes very diﬀerent to the ones found
by Balmorel. In line with the argument on partial-equilibrium
there is a tendency of the model to underestimate, by actually
excluding, uncertainties in the future society.
Being a deterministic model, Balmorel operates with full
foresight. This is crucial in evaluating the increase in both stor-
age technologies and VRE sources, as uncertainties and fore-
cast errors play a major role in both investments and operations
of these units.
Transmission is also simpliﬁed in Balmorel, as no intrare-
gional transmission limitations are considered. As modelling
is usually done on a hourly, or broader, time resolution, the
transmission capacity is likely to be overestimated, as short-
term peaks are not considered. It is possible to use to model
to evaluate transmission feasibility at ﬁner time resolutions, al-
though this might prove computationally demanding.
Lastly, a limitation of the current model version is the inclu-
sion of E-fuels. As E-fuel demands are currently expressed as
heat demands, the demand must be fulﬁlled within the area in
which it is placed. It is easily imaginable that E-fuels could
be produced in one region and then transported to another, as
a share of it would be in either compressed gaseous or liquid
form. This way the production of E-fuels would shift towards
regions with cheaper, readily available VRE sources reducing
the demand for transmission capacity.

Electricity price developments have been presented in the

The curve for Denmark shows notable jumps as certain sub-
system thresholds are reached, and as such a new technology or
ﬂexibility option must be employed to meet demand. Starting
from the low price region, the tail is fairly long, i.e. many hours
with full VRE capacity, for both BB2 and BB3 simulations.
Especially at the ﬁner temporal resolution, BB3, the eﬀect of
ﬂexibility is seen, as IndustryFlex retains a visibly lower price
roughly for hours 3300-5500 and in general is cheaper. The

prices increase beyond approximately 650 DKK/MWh.

simulation. For between 235 and 255 hours the price in Den-

rough averages and as such a single country reaching the cap

the price duration curve for the whole model area is generally
