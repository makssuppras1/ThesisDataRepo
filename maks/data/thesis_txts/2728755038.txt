ABSTRACT 
As antimicrobial resistance is emerging issue nowadays, Bdellovibrio bacteriovorus, a predatory 
against gram-negative bacterium is gaining interest as alternative therapeutic strategies against 
pathogens. However, it is significantly difficult to study and genetically modify B. bacteriovorus, since 
they are endobiotic bacteria most part of their life circle. One approach is to generate a photo-
sensing tool for genetical modification of Bdellovibrio bacteriovorus and consequently a variety of 
‘dark’ (non-photo-sensing) bacteria. The plasmid will consist of a photoreceptor, a chromophore, a 
response regulator and a compatible promoter, to regulate the transcription of gene of interest. 
Furthermore, the identification of alternative pathways which will include separate triggering 
mechanisms are advantageous, as different environment stimulate different responses. For that 
reason, extensive research in Pseudomonas aeruginosa deemed necessary, as it is major contributor 
of nosocomical acquired infections and a master in fine tuning its responses in a variety of stimuli. 
Those cues include nutrient availability, autoinducers, and a range of light spectrum. In this study we 
investigate CbrA/CbrB two component system in relation with Crc, an RNA binding protein which 
regulates carbon catabolite repression in order to decipher its impact in biofilm formation and 
potentially identify putative components for a synthetic gene regulation platform. 
In this study we showed that both absence of quorum sensing gene rhlR and nutrient sensing gene 
cbrA is promoting biofilm formation. Through the analysis of randomly generated suppressors in 
rhlR cbrA we uncovered that Crc as a common factor in both nutrient and quorum sensing. 
Additionally, it is promoting biofilm formation, by activation of additional important biofilm factors, 
such as pel, cupB and cupC fimbriae. These findings, incorporated in a synthetic transcriptional 
module will potentially lead to alternative therapeutic strategies against multi-drug resistant 
pathogens.

1. BACKGROUND: 
1.1 Model System – Pseudomonas aeruginosa: 
Pseudomonas aeruginosa is a gram-negative opportunistic bacterium which has gathered attention 
due to its ability to inflict severe infections, with a high mortality rate (Reynolds et al., 2021). Its 
prevalence in nosocomial settings and its multi-drug resilience have secured Pseudomonas 
aeruginosa a spot in the ESKAPE group, among other highly virulent pathogens, against which new 
antimicrobial candidates are pressingly needed (Mulani et al., 2019, Tacconelli et al., 2018). One of 
the major reasons of Pseudomonas aeruginosa resilience in pharmaceutical compounds is its ability 
to form biofilms, a multilayer interaction of cells adhered in a matrix (Hall-Stoodley et al., 2004). The 
matrix consists of extracellular polymeric substances (EPS) such as exopolysaccharides, lipids, 
proteins, and nucleic acids (Flemming et al., 2016; Karygianni et al., 2020) which provides P. 
aeruginosa incredible resistance against conventional therapeutical approaches. Deciphering the 
mechanisms behind biofilm formation is crucial in designing new strategies to combat this threat.  
Pseudomonas aeruginosa is a well versatile bacterium which inhabits in a variety of niches, from 
water and soil to burn wounds and the lungs of cystic fibrosis patients (Green et al., 1974; Pendleton 
et al., 2013; Markou et al., 2014; Mena et al., 2009). To survive in these diverse environments, P. 
aeruginosa has developed a plethora of two component systems (TCS) to respond to different 
stimuli including nutrient abundance, population density and presence of light (Monteagudo-
Cascales et al., 2022; Rutherford et al., 2012; Mukherjee et al., 2019).  
1.2 Nutrient Sensing: 
Pseudomonas aeruginosa has a distinct carbon catabolite repression as its preferable carbon source 
deviates away from glucose (McGill et al., 2021). Unconventional carbon substrates like succinate 
and pyruvate are preferred (Collier et al., 1996; Valentini et al., 2014), which led to the term reverse 
carbon catabolite repression. Some of the proteins involved in rCCR is the two component system 
CbrA/CbrB, a regulatory system involved in the effective response in the presence of various carbon 
sources (Sonnleitner et al., 2009; Yeung et al., 2011; Park et al., 2020). CbrA is a non-canonical 
histidine kinase, while CbrB acts as its partner response regulator (Monteagudo-Cascales et al., 
2022). CbrB, upon its phosphorylation will activate the expression of small RNA CrcZ, which 
subsequently regulates the carbon catabolic repression by binding to catabolite repression protein 
Crc and Hfq and preventing their complex formation (Kambara et al., 2018, Sonnleitner et al. 2018).  
The segregation of Crc-Hfq from CrcZ will eventually promote the transcription of alternative carbon 
metabolization genes20.  
1.3 Quorum Sensing: 
Pseudomonads have another sensing mechanism which relies on the extracellular chemical cues 
triggered by cell density and are getting recognized using their cognate receptors (Rutherford et al., 
2012; Miller et al., 2001; Mukherjee et al., 2019). This allows the bacteria to respond accordingly in 
population fluctuations by alternating their gene expression patterns. However, quorum system is 
outside of the scope of this project.   
1.4 Light Sensing: 
Light is an abundant environmental cue that extends its influence far beyond the realm of 
photosynthetic bacteria. Photo-sensing receptors are crucial elements in conveying light signals 
within the cell and subsequently affecting gene transcription with a proper response regulator 
module (van der Horst et al., 2007). Photoreceptors are divided into a total of 7 families based on 
the chromophore structure they interact with, which eventually stipulates the wavelength at which

the receptor is activated (Kottke et al., 2018; Shcherbakova et al., 2015; Cheng et al., 2016, 
Yamamoto et al., 2018; Gomelsky et al., 2011; Gourinchas et al., 2019; Beattie et al., 2018). Most 
bacteria possess phytochromes (Gomelsky et al., 2011). Phytochromes consist of a chromophore-
binding domain on their amino side and a histidine kinase domain on the carboxy side (Gourinchas 
et al., 2019). Several chromophores have been identified in different bacterial species, with 
biliverdin being the main in Pseudomonas aeruginosa (Tasler et al., 2005). However, the pathway is 
incomplete without an effective response regulator to convey the message.  
Gene analysis has shown that only a small number of species possess a response regulator in 
proximity of the bacteriophytochrome gene (Beattie et al., 2018), which raises the question of 
whether the photo-sensing system is functional in those species or if they utilize alternative 
response regulators. Recent studies in Pseudomonas aeruginosa have proven that the photo-sensing 
system is actually functional and regulates both virulence and biofilm formation (Mukherjee et al., 
2019). Pseudomonas aeruginosa encodes for BphP, a far-red light-sensing bacteriophytochrome that 
interacts with biliverdin. Upon light induction, photoisomerization of biliverdin occurs, which is 
covalently attached to BphP and subsequently activates the phytochrome. This leads to 
autophosphorylation of the complex, which in turn phosphorylates AlgB, a well-preserved response 
regulator in pseudomonads. AlgB is a transcriptional regulator of the NtrC family which interacts 
with 
54 transcriptional factor to initiate transcription (Wozniak et al., 1991) . In case of 
phosphorylation through BphP, AlgB gets activated and promotes suppression of biofilms, with a 
mechanism not yet well described (Mukherjee et al., 2019).  
 
2. Application System – Bdellovibrio Bacteriovorus: 
Predation is a term not commonly associated with microorganisms, however predatory bacteria 
exist and target a variety of Gram-negative bacteria. They can be classified as obligate predatory or 
facultative, depending in their ability to survive host independently (Jurkevitch, 2007). As 
antimicrobial resistance is emerging as an issue nowadays, predatory bacteria are gaining attention, 
as potential use as live antibiotics and water clean-up agents (Yu et al, 2017). This study will focus on 
Bdellovibrio bacteriovorus, a host dependent predatory bacterium which is considered a model 
organism for bacteria predation.  Bdellovibrio bacteriovorus life cycle consists of an attack phase 
(AP), and an intraperiplasmic growth phase (GP) as well as an intermediate phase, between AP and 
GP (Rotem et al., 2015). Briefly, freely swimming bacteriovorus will adhere on Gram-negative 
bacteria, cross the peptidoglycan layer and generating a pore to penetrate into the periplasm (Tudor 
et al., 1990). Successful anchoring to prey will lead to the transitional phase, where B. bacteriovorus 
invade prey and generate bdelloplast (Sánchez et al., 1990). Bdelloplast is essential for B. 
bacteriovorus survival as it provides protection from external factors, phages and secondary 
metabolites (Markelova, 2002). As a variety of modifications are happening in the peptidoglycan of a 
usually rod-shaped bacterium, the stage of bdelloplast is quite distinct, as a circularization of the cell 
is occurred (Bratanis et al, 2020). Eventually, exhaustion of nutrients will initiate B. bacteriovorus 
division by septation and trigger cell lysis as well as progeny release. 
B. bacteriovorus might be considered the most well described predatory bacterium among all the 
bacteriovorus like Bdellovibrio and Like Organisms (BALO), however; a lot is still unknown, 
specifically for their bdelloplast phase. As bdelloplast is impermeable to chemical induction, 
conventional transformation in BALO is not effective. Alternative methods to investigate their 
genomic profile during that phase are necessary, such as generating an optogenetic or nutrient 
dependent tool to regulate transcription.

Two B. bacteriovorous enzymes have been identified as responsible of remodelling target’s 
peptidoglycan. Within 30 minutes of invasion, the predator will express bd0816 and bd3459 genes, 
which show high homology with dacB genes responsible for PBP4 DD-endo/carboxy peptidases 
(Lerner et al., 2012). Both bd0816 and bd3459 are modified as such B. bacteriovorus is not targeting 
its own peptidoglycan.  Those genes can be great candidates as live reporters in generation of a 
transcription regulatory plasmid, either via nutrient or optogenetic stimulation.  
 
3. Current photo-sensing vectors: 
The idea of gene regulation by the presence of light was born not long after the first photoreceptor 
was discovered. However, studies mainly focused on eucaryotic systems and neuroscience (Fenno et 
al, 2019).  Photo-sensing system, even though derived mostly from bacterial species, has fallen 
behind in procaryotic systems with few options available in the market. One of the limitation factors 
is the photoreceptors. As mentioned earlier, there is a total of 7 photoreceptor families, each one 
sensitive in a specific light range. Starting with rhodopsin, as it was the first one discovered, it is a G-
protein coupled receptor in the rod cells of the retina (Palczewski et al., 2000, Athanasiou et al., 
2018). As their peak absorbance is at ∼ 500 nm, they recognise visible light. Xanthopsins is a second 
photoreceptor family, found in phototrophic purple bacteria and absorb at 446 nm which is within 
the blue spectrum range (Kort et al., 1996). Cobalamin (Vitamin 12) binding receptors are stimulated 
in early ultraviolet A of the spectrum, while the most used photoreceptors in optogenetics until now 
are cryptochromes, Light-oxygen-voltage (LOV) domain phototropins, and Blue light sensing using 
flavin (BLUF)-domain protein, all sensitive to blue light. Lastly, bacteriophytochromes are activated 
in the red/far-red spectrum of light, due to their heme derivatives chromophores (Bhoo et al., 2001).  
Due to the only recent discovery of Pseudomonas aeruginosa photo-sensing cascade, optogenetic 
tools so far have been focusing on blue light sensors. However, shorter wavelengths are of high 
energy, which allows only limited range of stimulation (Chernov et al., 2017).  Furthermore, previous 
studies have shown that higher wavelengths exhibit better tissue penetration than shorter 
wavelengths as they emit within the tissue transparency window (Chernov et al., 2017). Attempt to 
utilize far red light have been made since it was discovered that Deinoccocus radiodurans has a 
functional bphP phytochrome (Multamäki et al., 2021). Even though it is the most well described 
non photosynthetic bacteria with photo-sensing cascade, no partner regulator has been identified so 
far. This forces researchers to fuse DrbphP with additional two component system, as Multamäki et

5. Materials and Methods: 
-Strain Growth Conditions: 
 E. coli Dh5alpha strains containing pREDusk plasmid were grown in Lysogeny broth (LB) in presence 
of 50 µg/mL. LB plates were solidified with 1.5% Bacto agar. 
 P. aeruginosa UCBPP-PA14 and derivative mutants were grown in either LB, in 1% Tryptone broth 
(TB) or M9 minimal media. Occasionally, appropriate antibiotics were used, in the following 
concentrations: 50 µg/mL gentamycin, 100 μg/mL irgasan. 
-Plasmid Construction and strain construction: 
All the plasmids were firstly designed in situ with SnapGene® software. For the generation of the 
optogenetic plasmids, genes and additional regions of interest were amplified using Q5 High Fidelity 
DNA polymerase (New England Biolabs). The primers were designed to containing a complementary 
20-base pair sequence of the plasmid’s region of interest upstream and downstream of insertion 
gene. Gibson assembly was performed using Phusion Polymerase (New England Biolabs) and 
successfully ligated plasmids were transformed in chemically competent E. coli DH5a cells. The 
constructs were evaluated with colony PCR and correct plasmids were isolated for further 
modifications. For the construction of in-frame deletions in Pseudomonas aeruginosa, pEXG2 
plasmids were used, which contained 700-1000 bp upstream and downstream region of gene of 
interest. Plasmids were transformed into SM10 pir which were then conjugated with Pseudomonas 
aeruginosa strains with biparental mating.    
-Beta-galactosidase Assay: 
Strains were generated including lacZ gene fused to the desired promoter. Briefly, the procedure 
includes overnight cultures diluted in Tryptone Broth to a final OD600 of 0.01. These cultures were 
then grown up to OD600 1.3. Pellets of 1 mL cultures were collected and redissolved in 1 mL Z-buffer 
with the addition of 200 g lysozyme for permeabilization. Samples were then incubated in 30 oC for 
15 min and diluted as needed in a total volume of 500 L. 4 mg ONPG solution was added in the 
samples and time was recorded. The samples were incubating in 30 oC until sufficient colour change 
was observed. The reaction was quenched by 250 L 1M Na2CO3. Standard activity was calculated in 
Miller units using the equation below:

-Pellicle formation and CV staining: 
Pseudomonas aeruginosa was cultured overnight, at 37 oC, under shaking conditions. OD600 was 
measured and appropriate amount of culture was added in 3 mL 1 % Tryptone broth to a total of 
0.01 OD600. Pellicle biofilms were developed for 72 hours in stationary 16x100 mm glass test tubes at 
room temperature. After 72 h, the cultures were vortexed to disrupt the pellicles. The samples were 
washed vigorously with tap water and were left to dry for an hour. The biofilms were then stained 
with 4 mL 0.1 % crystal violet solution for half hour. The excess solution was discarded, the samples 
were washed thrice with tap water and let to dry overnight. For elution, 4 mL of 33% glacial acetic 
acid solution was used in each tube and samples were left to rest for an hour.  The crystal violet stain 
was quantified at OD500 using a spectrophotometer (Thermo Scientific Genesys 20).

-Growth Curve: 
Single colonies were inoculated in 96 well plates containing either 200 L of Tryptone 1% broth, M9 
medium supplemented with 50 mM of succinate, M9 medium supplemented with 20mM glutamate 
or M9 medium supplemented with 20 mM glucose. Plate was incubated in shaking conditions at 37 
oC and OD600 measurements were taken every 30 minutes. Experiment was monitored with BioTek 
Synergy Neo2 microplate reader. 
-Biofilms:  
One microliter of over the day Pseudomonas aeruginosa cultures were grown at 37 oC in 1% 
tryptone broth for 6 hours and was spotted onto 60x15 mm Petri plates. The plates contained 10 mL 
1% Tryptone medium, 1% agar, 40 mg/L Congo red and 20 mg/L Coomassie brilliant dyes. Colonies 
were grown at room temperature, in the dark and images were acquired after 120 h using a Zeiss 
AxioZoom v16 microscope. Cultures with lacZ reporter were spotted in plates containing 10 mL M9 
medium, 1% agar and 20 mg/L X-gal diluted in dimethylformamide (DMF) - in the presence of 20 mM 
succinate or 20 mM glucose (data not shown). 
-Dilution Assay 
Over the day cultures were incubated at 37 oC under shaking conditions, for a total of 6 hours or 
until OD600 has reached 0.5. The cultures were then diluted 100-fold in a 96 well plate as starting 
dilution. Five 10-fold serial dilutions were performed and 5 L of samples were spotted on an LB 
agar plate in presence of 50 g/mL streptomycin. The plates were left to dry and subsequently grow 
overnight at 37 oC in light and dark conditions. The following day, the plates were removed from the 
incubators, and their colony forming units were counted. The luminescence was measured at 535nm 
both at 24 h and 48 h after spotting, in 00.00.10 exposure time on Amersham ImageQuant 800 
chemiluminescence imager. The experiment was performed with 3 biological replicants.

6.5. PREDusk is not a suitable vector for our optogenetic approach. 
In parallel with evaluating alternative components for synthetic gene regulation, pREDusk vector 
was used to incorporate our optogenetic module. However, cloning was challenging as little to no 
colonies were growing after transformation. Potential positive plasmids were sent to sequencing, 
alongside the initial plasmid for reference. Results indicated that ligation of our promoter of interest, 
PalgD, with pREDusk backbone was the limitation point. It is possible that there is a compatibility 
between the overhands of the backbone sufficient enough to promote ligation of plasmid without 
the insert.  
Increased concentration of insert was introduced to Gibson mixture (6:1), however; it was still 
unable to result in positive colonies. Switching to PkatE did not generate any positive result either.  
While waiting for sequencing results, we tested the fluorescence production of empty vector and a 
vector with replacement of PFixK2 promoter with dtomato insertion. The results revealed that even 
an empty vector is generating significant amount of fluorescence in the dark as shown in Figure 10. It 
appears to generate false positive results in absence of light so we have decided to switch our focus 
on alternative vectors. Additionally, sequencing revealed a deletion of 60 base pairs in total of the 
empty vector, which deems it unreliable for further use.

7.Discussion and Future directions: 
Pseudomonas aeruginosa is responsible of high morbidity and mortality rates, with its main victims 
being cystic fibrosis patients and immunodeficient individuals, by colonizing the lung mucus (Palmer 
et al, 2005).  Our findings suggest that presence of specific nutrient could shift Pseudomonas 
aeruginosa from motile to its sessile form through the CbrA/CbrB/Crc pathway. As mucus in the 
lungs is rich in glucose (La Rosa et al., 2019), we suspect that cupB and cupC operon contribute in the 
colonization of P. aeruginosa in the lung. Moreover, our results suggest that crc has also post-
transcriptional effect in biofilm formation, as overexpression of PelA failed to recover the crc 
phenotype. 
Several future directions are uncovering ahead of us, since the project is still at early stages. Initial 
attempts with the optogenetic module were not fruitful; however, optogenetic vectors are 
important tools for genetic research. Since pREDusk vector deemed incompatible with our 
components, following steps include introducing our module to alternative plasmids. A great 
candidate for Bdellovibrio bacteriovorus is pK18mobsacB which has the advantage of being a suitable 
vector for direct transformation.  
Moreover, it is not only Bdellovibrio bacteriovorus that requires an optogenetic vector for research 
purposes.  There have been identified, several pathogenic non-photosynthetic bacteria which 
encodes for phytochrome BphP such as Achromobacter xyloxidans, Stenotrophomonas maltophilia 
(Mukherjee et al., 2019), which triggers the question if the pathway is functional in these species. 
Purifying  and testing all proteins from all the potential photo-sensing bacteria is a highly laborious 
task, so a functional optogenetic plasmid which will incorporate the phytochrome(s) from different 
species and heterologously express it in E.coli will set an answer to our questions. 
Lastly, post transcriptional regulation is a powerful mechanism which allows bacteria to adjust 
rapidly in stimuli. Taking advantage of this mechanisms by utilizing the RNA binding protein Crc, in 
correlation with a compatible promoter such as of cupB operon, will produce an even more powerful 
gene regulatory system. Furthermore, additional research in deciphering all the potential elements 
interacting in the pathway is necessary for effective designing of a nutrient regulating plasmid.
