Abstract
This thesis explores how a digital tool can support chemical risk assessment (RA) in Dan-
ish university laboratories by aligning regulatory compliance with user-centered design.
While existing systems and regulations provide a formal framework for RA, they often
fail to match the practical workflows and needs of laboratory users. Through qualitative
research at four Danish universities, the project applies human-centered design methods
to understand how RA is currently conducted and where existing systems fall short. Key
challenges identified include unclear responsibilities, low motivation, and fragmented doc-
umentation practices.
Building on these insights, the thesis presents an interactive prototype integrated with
the Kemibrug chemical database. The tool automates parts of the risk assessment process
by generating draft assessments based on selected chemicals, processes, and equipment.
It guides users through a structured flow using the STOP principle (Substitution, Tech-
nical measures, Organizational measures, Personal protection) and adapts to different
user roles. Additional prototypes explore alternative formats for fast or process-oriented
assessments. User testing confirmed the tool’s usability and its potential to improve con-
sistency, compliance, and safety culture in university laboratories.
This thesis contributes practical design strategies and validated concepts for digital RA
systems in academic environments and highlights the importance of tailoring safety tools
to real-world user needs.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Background and Motivation
There is currently no standardized framework for assessing safety in chemical work. Legal
requirements, such as EU Directive 98/24/EC and Arbejdstilsynet’s seven-step model,
provide only abstract guidance, leaving organisations to interpret and implement risk
assessments independently [1, 2]. This lack of specificity often results in inconsistent eval-
uations and gaps in compliance, which can compromise safety [3]. Studies on laboratory
safety practices show that many individuals in academic settings perceive risk assessment
as unnecessary, relying on their own expertise instead of formalized processes [4]. How-
ever, this approach increases the likelihood of accidents and non-compliance, highlighting
the need for structured and user-friendly risk assessment systems [5].
Kemibrug, a collaboration between DTU, SDU, KU, and Aalborg University, seeks to
address these issues by providing a centralized chemical inventory and safety management
platform. With the development of a second version, Kemibrug aims to introduce a digital
risk assessment tool to streamline the process, reduce user burden, and improve safety
outcomes. While UK universities have implemented various digital solutions for COSHH
compliance, no research was found on how digital risk assessment tools have been applied
in Danish academic laboratories [6]. This study investigates how a digital risk assessment
tool can be designed to support laboratory practitioners while ensuring compliance with
regulatory frameworks.
1.1.1
Scope
This project focuses on the Danish academic context, specifically within the Kemibrug col-
laboration. The aim is to support diverse user groups, including students, lab assistants,
and administrators, in conducting effective and consistent chemical risk assessments. Ex-
1
==PAGE:7==
isting research on digital risk assessment tools in universities has primarily focused on
UK and international institutions [6, 7], but no studies were found examining Danish
universities specifically.
Preliminary discussions within the Kemibrug collaboration indicate strong interest in a
streamlined digital solution. The need for a structured yet adaptable tool is emphasized
by findings that show risk assessments often become a bureaucratic formality rather than
a proactive safety measure [4]. By integrating compliance requirements with structured
digital workflows, this project aims to develop a tool that improves efficiency, usability,
and safety culture in university laboratories.
1.2
Problem Statement
Chemical risk assessment in academic laboratories is currently hindered by a lack of stan-
dardized frameworks and user-friendly digital tools. Existing legal requirements provide
only abstract guidance, leaving institutions to develop their own assessment methods,
often resulting in inconsistent evaluations and compliance gaps [3]. Many users perceive
risk assessment as a bureaucratic burden rather than an essential safety measure, leading
to reduced compliance and potential safety risks [5].
Kemibrug aims to address these challenges by developing a digital risk assessment tool
that integrates legal requirements with an intuitive user experience. However, there is
limited research on how such a tool can be effectively designed to balance compliance
with usability in academic settings. This thesis seeks to bridge this gap by identifying
best practices in digital risk assessment and proposing a structured framework that aligns
with compliance requirements while improving user adoption.
1.3
Research Questions
1. What are the practical challenges and user experiences associated with chemical
risk assessment in Danish university laboratories, and how are these shaped by
institutional practices and compliance requirements?
2. How can a digital risk assessment tool be designed to align with laboratory work-
flows, support regulatory compliance, and address users’ needs for simplicity, guid-
ance, and functionality?

1.3.1
Motivation and Contribution
This study aims to address the gaps in current chemical risk assessment practices within
academic institutions.
By understanding user challenges and preferences, the project
will identify barriers to effective risk assessment and provide insights that inform the
development of a user-friendly digital tool.
The tool will integrate regulatory compliance with practical usability, reducing cogni-
tive load, and encouraging thorough and consistent risk assessments. Studies show that
risk assessments are most effective when they are integrated seamlessly into laboratory
workflows rather than perceived as an administrative hurdle [8, 6].
This approach contributes to safer working environments and supports the broader digi-
talization of laboratory safety processes. Additionally, this study extends existing research
by exploring the application of digital tools in risk assessment and offering recommenda-
tions for future advancements. By leveraging insights from regulatory frameworks and
university implementations, this research will help develop a structured, practical, and
regulation-compliant digital tool for laboratory risk assessment.

Chapter 2
Review of Literature and Related Work
This chapter reviews existing literature on chemical risk assessment and the emerging
need for digital tools in university laboratories. Sources were identified through academic
databases and structured search engine queries. British universities were chosen for case
examples, as they were the only institutions with publicly available, detailed documen-
tation on laboratory risk assessment practices.
To contextualize these cases, relevant
COSHH regulations and implementation frameworks were also examined. The goal of the
review is to map the current state of risk assessment and highlight gaps in practice and
research, particularly in Danish academic contexts.
The chapter is organized into four parts.
• Section 2.1 examines challenges in current risk assessment practices and relevant
regulatory frameworks.
• Section 2.2 reviews institutional approaches to chemical safety and the role of
digital solutions.
• Section 2.3 presents case studies from UK universities with more structured digital
systems.
• Section 2.4 synthesizes these insights, identifying knowledge gaps and outlining
design principles for a digital tool tailored to Danish university laboratories.
By reviewing existing frameworks and tools, it is possible to build an understanding of
what works well and what could be improved while highlighting key factors to consider
when designing a digital risk assessment tool. This sets the stage for the next part of the
thesis, which focuses on developing a solution to address these challenges.

2.1
The need for digital risk assessment tools in uni-
versity laboratories
Risk assessment practices in university laboratories vary widely, shaped by local regu-
lations and implementation strategies. In Denmark, all workplaces handling hazardous
chemicals, including university laboratories, are legally required by Arbejdstilsynet (Dan-
ish Working Environment Authority) to conduct chemical risk assessments to ensure the
safety of employees and students [2]. These assessments are legally mandated and must
systematically identify and manage chemical hazards before any work begins. Despite
these requirements, compliance remains a challenge due to the complexity of risk estima-
tion, variability in laboratory practices, and the absence of standardized assessment tools.
University laboratories lack structured safety oversight
A survey-based study conducted at Northern Illinois University on risk perception, train-
ing adequacy, and compliance among university students and researchers revealed a com-
mon misconception: that university laboratories are inherently safer than industrial envi-
ronments [4]. The study, which surveyed faculty, staff, and students of biological sciences,
chemistry, and medical laboratory courses, found that this belief is primarily due to dif-
ferences in risk communication and reporting practices.
The study also found that academic chemical labs often maintain lower safety standards
than industrial settings. This is largely due to weaker regulatory enforcement and inconsis-
tent safety training. While industrial workplaces adhere to strict regulatory frameworks,
universities typically rely on internal safety policies, which can vary significantly in effec-
tiveness and enforcement. As a result, informal safety reporting and overlooked risks in
academic labs may increase the likelihood of accidents [4].
A mixed-methods study conducted across 17 European countries on operational and man-
agement staff using surveys, interviews, and workplace safety report analysis with partic-
ipants including employees from both industrial and academic organizations found that
structured, mandatory safety training is linked to lower incident rates, whereas voluntary
or informal training results in weaker compliance [5]. In the Brazilian context, despite
existing regulations, accidents continue to occur in university settings due to weak safety
culture, insufficient oversight and non-compliance with procedures [7].

Regulations fail due to the lack of standardized implementation
Ensuring effective chemical risk assessment is essential to preventing hazardous exposure
and maintaining a safe working environment.
European legislation, such as Directive
98/24/EC [1], mandates that employers systematically evaluate chemical risks, consid-
ering substance properties, exposure duration, and technological processes [1]. However,
universities often face challenges in applying these regulations effectively, largely due to
the dynamic and decentralized nature of academic laboratory work.
A study conducted by the Central Institute for Labour Protection – National Research
Institute in Poland analyzed workplace safety guidelines and expert opinions on risk man-
agement in non-industrial environments, such as universities and research labs. The study
examined official reports and government regulations to identify gaps in risk assessment
implementation in university laboratories across Poland and other EU countries [3].
The study found that universities face difficulties implementing occupational safety regu-
lations because their laboratory environments differ significantly from industrial settings.
Unlike industrial workplaces, university labs deal with a constantly changing set of chem-
icals and experimental setups, making it difficult to apply rigid safety protocols.
The study reviewed existing chemical risk assessment tools used across Europe and in-
ternationally, including manuals, guidelines, checklists, and digital solutions. It analyzed
institutional policies to identify how different organizations apply risk assessment method-
ologies. It found that the assessment of chemical risks varies widely between institutions
due to:
• The vast range of substances encountered in academic laboratories.
• Constantly changing experimental setups, which make it difficult to maintain con-
sistent safety assessments.
• A lack of a standardized methodology—each university tends to develop its own
approach, leading to inconsistencies in risk assessment quality.
Furthermore, the study examined interactive digital tools designed to support chemical
risk assessments, such as OiRA [9] (a generic European digital risk assessment platform)
and National safety management platforms from various EU member states, and also
compared the effectiveness of digital tools versus traditional manual assessment methods.
It was found that digital risk assessment tools offer significant advantages, such as
6
==PAGE:12==
• Automating risk evaluations based on chemical databases.
• Providing real-time monitoring and compliance tracking.
• Standardizing assessment procedures across different institutions.
Despite these advantages, many universities have not fully adopted digital solutions, due
to limited awareness of these tools, lack of integration into university policies, and fi-
nancial constraints, as implementing digital solutions requires investment in software and
training. The research concluded that without efficient and adaptable assessment tools,
universities struggle to maintain compliance, reinforcing the need for digital solutions to
enhance risk evaluation and safety monitoring.
Digitalization can improve risk assessment consistency
Digital tools have the potential to make risk assessments in university laboratories more
efficient by improving hazard identification, ensuring compliance, and creating more con-
sistent safety procedures.
Technologies like artificial intelligence (AI), the Internet of
Things (IoT), and robotics can help automate routine safety tasks, reducing human error
and making risk assessments more reliable. A survey of companies in the Czech Republic
and Slovakia found that over 52% were already using digital tools for workplace safety
[6].
Through structured interviews and a SWOT analysis, the study showed that automated
hazard detection and predictive risk modeling could help organizations follow safety reg-
ulations more effectively by providing real-time insights [6]. The study also included a
qualitative risk assessment using the What-If method and Risk Matrix, which further
demonstrated how digital tools can identify potential hazards before they escalate [6].
However, while these technologies are becoming common in industrial workplaces, they
are not yet widely used in laboratory settings. More research is needed to understand
how digital tools can be adapted to university environments while ensuring they remain
practical and compliant with safety regulations.
Traditional risk assessments create compliance gaps
While regulations offer a structured framework for chemical risk assessment, their ef-
fectiveness in university laboratories depends heavily on how they are implemented and
supported by practical tools. Traditional manual assessments are often inconsistent, time-
consuming, and difficult to enforce, leading to gaps in safety compliance. These limitations

have driven the development of digital solutions aimed at improving efficiency, engage-
ment, and standardization in risk evaluation.
These challenges set the stage for a closer look at how existing assessment methodologies
and digital tools are currently applied.
The next section evaluates these tools, their
benefits and limitations, and their relevance for improving chemical safety in university

2.2
Existing frameworks and tools for chemical risk as-
sessment
To ensure the safe handling of hazardous chemicals, various regulatory frameworks and
tools have been established both at national and international levels. While their shared
goal is to minimize harmful exposure, the tools and implementation strategies differ sig-
nificantly. This is seen particularly in academic laboratory settings, where changing ex-
periments and informal workflows challenge standard procedures.
In the Danish context, the European Union provides the legal foundation at the highest
level for chemical risk assessment through Directive 98/24/EC [1], which outlines em-
ployer responsibilities in managing workplace chemical hazards.
Beyond EU-level directives, national authorities implement country-specific risk assess-
ment frameworks. In Denmark, Arbejdstilsynet [2] (Danish Working Environment Au-
thority) mandates a structured approach and specific guidelines for risk evaluation.
In the United Kingdom, the Control of Substances Hazardous to Health (COSHH) [10]
regulations by the Health and Safety Executive (HSE) provide a national framework for
managing chemical risks. British universities are used as case examples in this section
because they were the only institutions with publicly available, detailed descriptions of risk
assessment practices. To contextualize these cases, the COSHH framework was examined
to understand how it shapes institutional processes. Through these examples, the role of
digital tools in supporting risk management and improving compliance is also explored.
By examining these frameworks and tools, this section aims to provide a comparative anal-
ysis of risk assessment strategies, evaluating their effectiveness and relevance to university
laboratories. The discussion will highlight key methodologies and explore the potential
for digital solutions to enhance chemical safety management.

2.2.1
EU Directive 98/24/EC – The Chemical Agents Directive
The European Union introduced the Chemical Agents Directive (98/24/EC) to set min-
imum requirements for assessing and controlling workplace chemical risks. It mandates
that employers evaluate hazards, implement preventive measures and regularly review
risk assessments to ensure worker safety [1].
Framework and step-by-step approach
The directive applies to all workplaces handling chemicals and follows a structured risk
management approach:
1. Hazard identification: Identify hazardous chemicals, assess exposure risks (in-
halation, skin contact, ingestion), and compare levels with legal limits.
2. Risk control measures: Follow the hierarchy of control:
• Elimination – Remove hazardous substances where possible.
• Substitution – Use safer alternatives.
• Engineering controls – Apply fume hoods, ventilation, or containment.
• Administrative Controls – Establish safety procedures and training.
• PPE - Personal Protective Equipment – Provide gloves, goggles, and lab
coats as a last resort.
3. Exposure monitoring and compliance: Regularly measure exposure levels, con-
duct health checks, and update risk assessments as workplace conditions change.
4. Documentation and review: Maintain risk assessment records and revise safety
measures to ensure ongoing compliance.
2.2.2
The Danish Working Environment Authority’s requirements
for chemical risk assessment
The Danish Working Environment Authority (Arbejdstilsynet) [2] mandates that all work-
places handling hazardous chemicals conduct a structured risk assessment. This aligns
with the EU’s Chemical Agents Directive (98/24/EC) but further specifies national im-
plementation requirements to ensure compliance with Danish occupational safety laws.
9
==PAGE:15==
Key elements and workflow
The Danish Working Environment Authority follows the general risk assessment structure
outlined by EU regulations but emphasizes seven key elements, that must be explicitly
considered in Danish workplaces:
• Identifying hazardous substances and their properties.
• Assessing exposure routes and workplace conditions.
• Evaluating the effectiveness of existing control measures.
• Considering findings from occupational health research and past incidents.
• Ensuring compliance with Danish occupational exposure limits.
• Using supplier-provided safety data (SDS) to guide risk management.
• Documenting and regularly reviewing the assessment.
The risk assessment must be integrated into the company’s general workplace assess-
ment (APV) and is required to be updated when new substances, processes or risks are
introduced.
Implementation of control measures
In line with EU requirements, the Danish Working Environment Authority mandates that
preventive measures follow a control hierarchy, prioritizing elimination and substitution
before relying on protective equipment. Danish regulations enforce this through the STOP
principles, which guides risk reduction strategies:
• Substitution – Organizations must actively evaluate alternatives for hazardous chem-
icals.
• Technical Controls – Engineering measures like fume hoods and automation should
be prioritized.
• Organizational Controls – Work procedures must minimize exposure with docu-
mented training programs ensuring safe handling.
• PPE as a Last Resort – Protective gear should only be used when other measures
are insufficient.
10
==PAGE:16==
The Danish Working Environment Authority places particular emphasis on documenting
the rationale behind control decisions. If substitution or technical controls are not feasible,
companies must justify their reasoning and outline alternative risk mitigation strategies.
By integrating these structured risk assessment processes into daily operations, organiza-
tions can ensure compliance with Danish safety regulations while proactively managing
chemical hazards [11].
2.2.3
Description and analysis of the Danish Working Environ-
ment Authority’s digital tool for chemical risk assessment
in cleaning
The Danish Working Environment Authority provides a digital tool designed to support
workplaces in conducting chemical risk assessments for cleaning agents [12]. The tool
guides users through a structured input process and produces a draft risk assessment
with general recommendations. These recommendations are intended as a starting point
and must be refined based on local workplace conditions.
Structure and functionality of the tool
The tool follows a structured decision tree, where responses dynamically influence sub-
sequent questions. Hazard symbols, H-statements and handling procedures determine,
which recommendations appear in the final document. For example, eye protection is
only prompted if an H-statement indicates a risk to eyes.
Users provide input in four main areas:
• Chemical Identification and Classification: Users select the cleaning products
and specify their hazard pictograms and H-statements.
• Exposure and Handling Information: The tool records whether products are
applied manually, diluted or dispensed automatically.
• Protective Measures: Users input existing controls, such as ventilation, gloves,
masks and training programs.
• Recommendations and Customization: Based on the responses, the tool gen-
erates risk-reduction measures and compliance guidelines.

Processing of user input and required further work
Although the tool simplifies the initial assessment, the resulting document must be sup-
plemented to meet regulatory requirements. Users are expected to:
• Justify deviations from recommended protective measures.
• Document risk minimization strategies for exposure control.
• Evaluate potential substitutions for hazardous chemicals.
These refinements ensure the assessment is tailored to workplace-specific conditions and
compliant with the Danish Working Environment Authority’s regulatory requirements.
Integration of the STOP principles
The tool prioritizes risk reduction strategies following the STOP hierarchy. If a product
presents high health risks, substitution prompts appear first. Engineering and organi-
zational controls, such as ventilation or automated dispensing, are recommended before
PPE. If these controls are insufficient, PPE is suggested as a last resort.
With this regulatory foundation in place, the next section explores how universities trans-
late these frameworks into everyday laboratory practices. Case studies from York, Cam-
bridge, and Reading offer insight into how COSHH requirements are operationalized and
how digital tools support or hinder this implementation.
2.3
Case Study of COSHH Implementation in UK Uni-
versities
The Control of Substances Hazardous to Health (COSHH) regulations provide the legal
framework for chemical risk assessment in the United Kingdom. HSE require laborato-
ries to assess hazards, evaluate exposure risks, and implement control measures before
working with hazardous substances. While COSHH provides clear guidelines, universities
apply them through different workflows and tools.
This section examines how the University of York, the University of Cambridge, and
the University of Reading apply COSHH principles in their laboratory environments.
These universities were chosen based on publicly available documentation that clearly
explained their risk assessment workflows. They were found through structured search
13
==PAGE:19==
engine queries.
2.3.1
The COSHH Framework
COSHH establishes a structured approach to risk management similar to EU and Arbejd-
stilsynet regulations but tailored to UK workplace safety policies. The framework consists
of five key steps:
1. Identifying hazardous substances: Determining which chemicals pose risks based on
their classification, toxicology, and physical properties.
2. Assessing exposure risks: Evaluating potential exposure routes (inhalation, skin
contact, ingestion) and estimating duration and intensity.
3. Implementing control measures: Applying the hierarchy of controls, prioritizing
elimination, substitution, engineering controls, administrative controls, and PPE.
4. Monitoring and reviewing risk assessments: Ensuring risk assessments are regularly
updated and remain effective.
5. Providing training and documentation: Ensuring laboratory users are informed
about risks and safety measures.
While this structured approach appears consistent, the specific workflows, methods, and
tools used to implement COSHH vary. The following sections outline how three universi-
ties integrate COSHH into their laboratory environments.
2.3.2
Overview and comparison of implementation Approaches
Each of the three universities follow the same core COSHH steps: identifying hazards,
assessing risks, applying controls, and reviewing assessments. However their approaches
differ in how they manage documentation, approvals, and digital tools.
University of York
At York, the risk assessment process is designed to be simple and user-driven. Researchers
complete digital templates provided through Google Docs, which they adapt to their
specific experiments. For teaching laboratories, staff pre-fill assessments for student use.
Once completed, the assessments are approved by a supervisor and stored in a shared
document system. This system offers accessibility and quick updates but depends on
individual initiative, which may lead to inconsistent quality without sufficient oversight.
14
==PAGE:20==
University of Cambridge
Cambridge adopts a more formal and hierarchical workflow. Risk assessments must be
completed using standardized digital templates and approved by a competent assessor
before any work begins. The process includes structured evaluation of chemical hazards,
classification using hazard bands, and the analysis of potential reaction risks. All as-
sessments are stored in a central database and reviewed regularly. While this ensures
regulatory compliance and thoroughness, it introduces delays that may hinder rapid ex-
perimentation.
University of Reading
Reading focuses on standardization and clear procedural guidance. It provides researchers
with detailed risk assessment templates that include prompts for chemical classification,
exposure estimation, and control measures. These assessments must follow COSHH prin-
ciples closely and are stored in a centralized system, where safety staff periodically review
them. The institution also emphasizes written guidance and training to ensure consistency
across departments. This setup supports quality and traceability but can be administra-
tively demanding.

These case studies demonstrate that while COSHH regulations provide a common frame-

processes in different ways, from streamlining approvals to improving hazard detection
and documentation. The next section synthesizes these findings and defines the scope of
this research.
2.4
Synthesis and scope of research
The literature review shows that universities differ widely in how they implement chemical
risk assessments, even when following the same regulatory frameworks. Some prioritize
thorough documentation and structured approvals, while others aim for flexibility and
accessibility. Digital tools support these different approaches, but their effectiveness de-
pends on how well they are integrated into institutional workflows.
Importantly, no studies were found examining the use of digital risk assessment tools in
Danish university laboratories. Although Arbejdstilsynet offers a digital tool for assessing
cleaning agents, it is limited in scope and not suited to complex laboratory environments.
This gap highlights the need to explore how a digital solution can be designed specifically
for Danish university contexts—balancing regulatory compliance, practical workflows, and
user needs.
2.4.1
The need for a digital risk assessment tool
Traditional risk assessment methods such as paper forms or scattered digital files create
inefficiencies, inconsistencies, and weak documentation. As shown in both Danish and
international studies, this can lead to missed hazards, superficial compliance, and a lack
of follow-up when conditions change. Risk assessments are often treated as checkboxes
rather than active safety tools.
A digital risk assessment tool can address these challenges by automating key steps, pro-
viding structured guidance, and organizing documentation in a central system. Features
like real-time updates and digital approvals would help ensure that assessments remain
current, consistent, and easier to manage, ultimately improving both compliance and
safety practices.
17
==PAGE:23==
2.4.2
Regulatory frameworks and university approaches
Chemical risk assessment frameworks, such as EU Directive 98/24/EC, the Danish Ar-
bejdstilsynet guidelines, and the UK’s COSHH regulations, share common structures:
identifying hazards, assessing exposure, implementing controls, and maintaining docu-
mentation. However, how these frameworks are applied in practice varies across institu-
tions.
The UK case studies showed three different approaches: York emphasizes flexibility and
user autonomy; Cambridge enforces structured approval and documentation; Reading
prioritizes consistency and procedural guidance. These examples demonstrate that im-
plementation strategies reflect institutional values and practical constraints.
This variation suggests that a digital risk assessment tool must be flexible enough to
support different workflows, while still ensuring regulatory compliance.
Adaptability,
clarity, and ease of use are essential to encourage both engagement and accurate risk
evaluation.
2.4.3
Key considerations for a digital solution
Based on the challenges and needs identified in the literature, a digital risk assessment
tool should include:
• Automatic hazard identification and risk classification based on SDS and regulatory
databases.
• A guided, step-by-step workflow covering planning, hazard assessment, safety mea-
sures, and documentation.
• Built-in support for compliance with EU Directive 98/24/EC and Arbejdstilsynet’s
seven-step framework.
• A centralized system for documentation and approvals, enabling real-time updates
and structured reviews.
• A simple, user-friendly interface that supports participation from students, re-
searchers, and supervisors.
Digital tools can fulfill different roles depending on institutional priorities:
1. Compliance tools – ensuring assessments meet legal requirements.
18
==PAGE:24==
2. Efficiency tools – reducing time spent on documentation and administrative tasks.
3. Safety culture tools – making risk assessments a meaningful and integrated part
of lab practice.
This research will explore how a digital risk assessment tool can balance these roles while
supporting the specific needs of Danish university laboratories. The study will examine
both design considerations and practical challenges, aiming to develop a solution that is
usable, adaptable, and aligned with regulatory and institutional contexts.

Chapter 3
Methodology and Methods
This project focuses on designing a digital risk assessment tool to support users within
the Kemibrug collaboration. The platform currently provides general safety information,
but lacks a dedicated feature for conducting chemical risk assessments, leaving users to
interpret and perform assessments independently.
The tool aims to support both those who actively conduct risk assessments, such as
students and lab assistants, and those responsible for compliance, such as administrators,
by promoting consistency and alignment with regulatory requirements.
The research
includes concept development, workflow design, and user validation, but does not cover
technical implementation. The outcome will be an interactive prototype of the digital
tool, evaluated with end-users to ensure usability and compliance.
3.1
Double Diamond
The project followed the Double Diamond design framework [13];[14], divided into Dis-
cover, Define, Develop, and Deliver phases. These were supported by Design Thinking
principles [15], ensuring a user-centered and iterative design process.
3.2
Design Thinking
Design Thinking is a human-centered problem-solving approach that emphasizes empathy,
ideation, and iterative prototyping [15]. It aligns closely with the Double Diamond model
and ensures that solutions are based on real user needs by embedding iterative cycles of
understanding, ideation, and testing.

Discover & Define

State-of-the-Art Literature Review
A comprehensive literature review was conducted to investigate existing tools, regula-

This provided a theoretical grounding for the study and helped identify gaps where design

categorized and visualized based on their proximity to Kemibrug and their role in the
risk assessment process. The final mapping captured operational, administrative, and
strategic stakeholders and was used to identify user roles for targeted recruitment in the

As-Is User Journey Mapping
To visualize how users currently conduct chemical risk assessments, user journeys were
constructed using data from interviews, the workshop, and real life documentation tem-
plates. The mapping technique followed principles from service and experience design
[19], capturing key actions, decision points, and pain points.
Two flows were identified: one representing a formal, template based process and another
representing an informal undocumented practice. These maps provided a structured lens
through which system gaps and user frustrations could be analyzed.
Affinity diagram
To synthesize qualitative data from all prior research activities, an affinity diagram [20]
was created. This involved extracting approximately 30 concise statements from inter-
views, workshop notes, and templates, each representing a user observation or concern.
Statements were clustered manually into themes that reflected recurring patterns in be-
havior, needs, and pain points. High level groupings were then formed to reveal cross-
cutting challenges. The process supported a transition from raw data to actionable insight.
As an end to the affinity diagram analysis, a set of insight statements were formulated
to capture key understandings across clusters.
The final statements synthesized user
frustrations and unmet needs into core principles that would guide design decisions.
Point-of-View (POV) Madlib
To frame design opportunities in user centric terms, a set of Point-of-View (POV) state-
ments were created. Using the madlib format [21], each POV captured a user type, their
need, and a deeper insight: [USER] needs to [USER NEED] because [INSIGHT].
This format allowed each statement to serve as a lens through which ideation could be
focused, tying user context directly to potential solutions.
How Might We (HMW) Questions
To support the transition from user understanding to ideation, each POV statement was
translated into one or more HMW questions, helping reframe user challenges into open-
ended prompts for design. These questions guided brainstorming and helped open the

==PAGE:33==
design space without prematurely narrowing in on a single solution[21].
Design Requirements
From the collected insights and structured themes, a set of concrete design requirements
was formulated.
Each requirement specifies a function the tool must support and is
grouped by thematic focus. Supporting criteria were added where relevant to clarify ex-
pectations for implementation. These requirements provided a benchmark for developing
and evaluating prototypes[17].
3.4
Develop & Deliver
In the Develop and Deliver phases, initial design directions were developed and tested
through iterative prototyping. A combination of structured mapping, prioritization, and
stakeholder feedback helped shape the final output.
User Storymapping
As part of the development of the digital prototype, a user storymap was made. This
method was used to structure the envisioned user journey of the future tool by identify-
ing high level activities, associated user tasks, and sub-activities that users would perform.
To envision the user flow of the future tool, a story map was created [22]. User activities
were broken down into tasks and sub-tasks and laid out horizontally to represent the full
risk assessment process. Two variations were developed: one for users requiring supervisor
approval (e.g., students) and one for users who can approve their own assessments (e.g.,
employees).
This visualization clarified user pathways and served as a foundation for designing content
and system interactions.
Usability prioritization
To support the development of the prototype, twelve usability heuristics for user inter-
face design [19] were used as a guide to understand what matters most to users. Each
heuristic was compared with the feedback collected from the contextual interviews and
user workshop.

==PAGE:34==
By comparing reported challenges to these principles, a prioritization was made of which
heuristics were most relevant to users. This informed the structuring of the prototype to
ensure alignment with real-world expectations.
Prototyping
Throughout the project, several prototypes were developed to translate user insights into
tangible design solutions. These ranged from conceptual sketches to high-fidelity inter-
active prototypes created in Figma. The initial prototypes focused on exploring different
user flows and content structures to clarify system functionality and support ideation,
rather than final visual design [19]. The final version was designed to simulate a complete
and interactive risk assessment journey in greater detail, incorporating realistic user flows,
content automation, and differentiated paths based on user roles.
The first set of prototypes included a main version with three supporting alternative con-
cepts that were used to compare design directions, validate assumptions, and gather early
feedback.
Building on the outcomes of this initial testing, a final interactive Figma prototype was
created to represent the intended system in more detail. Unlike earlier versions, this pro-
totype allowed users to complete a full risk assessment process based on real user flows. It
included interactive steps, automated content generation, role-specific completion logic,
and customizable input.
All prototypes were developed in close dialogue with Kemibrug users and Risk Assessment
stakeholders in interactive sessions. This iterative approach ensured that design decisions
stayed aligned with institutional needs and everyday practices.
Testing
To evaluate the concepts, a workshop was conducted with the Kemibrug reference group,
aligning with participatory evaluation methods including elements of co-discovery and
controlled experiments [19].
During the session, the main prototype and the three alternatives were presented and ex-
plained one by one as a walkthrough. Participants were encouraged to ask questions and
give feedback throughout. For each prototype, structured prompts were used to gather
reflections on what worked well, what could be improved, and what concerns they had.

4.2
Develop and Deliver
The second half of the Double Diamond model builds on the insights gathered in the

This stage began by mapping user journeys to structure the system logic, followed by
prioritizing usability principles, prototyping solutions, and finally testing and refining
them through stakeholder feedback.

Testing
To evaluate the first iterations, the prototypes were presented to the Kemibrug reference-
group, consisting of representatives from all four universities. The session focused on gath-
ering input on usability, workflow support, and clarity of content. The main prototype
was evaluated alongside the three alternative concepts to stimulate broader reflections.
Across the prototypes, users consistently highlighted the value of automation, structure,
and role-sensitive guidance. Prototype 2, which simulated a process-centered flow based
on selected chemicals and procedures, was favored overall for its inclusion of experiment
procedures. However, several critical points were also raised concerning the need for bet-
ter visualization, clearer differentiation between local and central content, and minimizing
redundant interaction steps.

The prototype guides users through the process of conducting a chemical risk assessment
in a way that is efficient, structured, and compliant. Instead of starting from a blank
form, users select chemicals, processes, and equipment from a central database. Based
on this, Kemibrug generates a tailored draft that includes potential risks, recommended
precautions, and relevant procedural information.
Users are then guided through a review process structured by the STOP principle (Sub-
stitution, Technical, Organizational, Personal), prompting them to reflect, adjust, and
add local details as needed. The system aims to support both new and experienced users
through built-in help, flexible input, and streamlined steps.
The prototype aims to support diverse user roles and varying levels of experience. Fea-
tures such as built-in help texts and auto-generated content help onboard less experienced
users, while flexible input options and streamlined flows cater to more advanced users or
recurring tasks.
Key interactions and features of the final prototype include:
1. Input selection: Users search for and select chemicals, procedures, and equip-
ment from Kemibrug’s database, which includes structured metadata and hazard
information.
2. Automated content generation: The system uses user inputs to automatically
fill a draft risk assessment.
3. STOP-based review: The process is divided into structured steps based on the
STOP framework, ensuring that risk control strategies are considered in a priori-
tized, logical order.
4. User reflection and customization: Users can supplement system-generated
content with personal notes, local precautions, or deviations specific to their lab
context.
5. Role-specific completion: Depending on user type, the assessment is either sub-
mitted for supervisor review (student version) or directly finalized (employee ver-
sion).
79
==PAGE:85==
6. Final output: The completed assessment can be exported, stored, or shared in a
readable format, including summaries, visual indicators (e.g. pictograms), and key
safety information.
The final prototype combines safety requirements with practical lab workflows. It guides
users through each step and makes it easier to create risk assessments without adding
extra work. Designed with a user-centered approach, it forms the main design outcome
of the project, serving as a foundation for future implementation.

Chapter 5
Conclusion and Research Outcomes
This thesis set out to investigate how a digital tool could support chemical risk assess-
ment (RA) in Danish university laboratories by balancing regulatory compliance with
user-centered design. The work was grounded in user research across four Danish uni-
versities and focused on developing a concept that would integrate with the chemical
database Kemibrug, while also responding to the practical realities of laboratory work.
From the beginning, it was clear that while legal frameworks and digital systems exist
to support risk assessment, they often fail to align with the daily practices, knowledge
levels, and motivations of users.
A review of current research and literature revealed
that although there is broad research on digital safety tools and compliance systems, few
studies bridge the gap between formal regulation and everyday user behavior. This thesis
addresses that gap by applying human-centered design methods to understand how chem-
ical RA is actually performed, and by developing a system that supports both compliance
and ease of use.
The outcome of the project is an interactive prototype that integrates regulatory require-
ments with intuitive, role-based workflows tailored to students, lab personnel, and super-
visors. It illustrates how RA can be generated automatically using data from Kemibrug,
where users select chemicals, processes, and equipment from structured lists to generate
a draft assessment. They then follow a guided flow based on the STOP principle: Substi-
tution, Technical measures, Organizational measures, and Personal protection, reviewing
and adjusting the content as needed.
The system supports editable content, role-sensitive input, and exportable outputs for
documentation and approval.
Additional concepts explored alternative formats, such
as a process-oriented view and a simplified version for quick assessments. User testing
81
==PAGE:87==
throughout confirmed the prototype’s potential to improve compliance and safety prac-
tices in Danish university laboratories.
5.1
Contribution
The project contributes to the cross-section of design, user experience, and laboratory
safety in several ways.
1. It introduces a user centered framework for designing digital RA tools grounded in
both compliance and usability.
2. It offers empirical insights into how risk assessments are currently carried out in
university labs which has often been underrepresented in both research and system
development.
3. the project delivers a validated prototype developed in close collaboration with key
stakeholders, showing how a digital system can support compliance without placing
undue burden on users.
Beyond the practical tool, the thesis also contributes to the broader conversation about
how regulatory systems can become more effective by being more human. Rather than
treating compliance as a checklist to be enforced, the approach taken here shows how
systems can encourage meaningful reflection and participation, fostering a stronger safety
culture rather than simply enforcing rules.
5.2
Limitations
While the project has led to useful insights and outcomes, there are several limitations
that are important to consider:
• Participants were mainly recruited through Kemibrug: Most of the users
involved in testing were experienced and already quite engaged in safety work. This
means their input might not fully reflect how more casual or less experienced users
see and use risk assessment tools.
• Geographic challenge of users and institutions: Since the four universities
are located across the country and their labs are spread out within each institution,
it was difficult to involve a wider range of users.
82
==PAGE:88==
• Technical implementation was out of scope: The prototype focused on system
design, layout, and flow, but no technical implementation was done. As a result, it
is unknown how it would work in real lab settings and how users would respond to
it over time.
• Kemibrug is under re-development: Since the future system architecture for
Kemibrug is still being developed, it remains uncertain which features or workflows
from the prototype can realistically be implemented, and under what technical con-
straints.
• The project had a design focus: Since this thesis was done by a design student,
detailed software development and backend considerations were beyond the project’s
scope and expertise.
These limitations don’t take away from the value of the work, but they do show where
more research, testing, and collaboration will be needed in the future to move from concept
to working system.
5.3
Future Work
This thesis has resulted in a prototype that demonstrates how chemical risk assessment can
be made more intuitive, compliant, and user-centered through digital support. However,
further work is needed to move from concept to implementation and to ensure the tool’s
relevance and robustness across diverse contexts.
The following areas are suggested for future development:
• Broader user testing: Conduct usability evaluations with a wider and more varied
group of users, including students, international staff, and casual laboratory users.
• Technical feasibility: Investigate how the system design can be realistically im-
plemented within the technical constraints and architecture of the future Kemibrug
platform.
• Pilot development: Build and test a functional pilot version using real laboratory
data and workflows to evaluate performance, usability, and compliance support
under real conditions.
• Scalability and adaptability: Explore how the tool could be extended beyond
academic institutions to also support risk assessment in industrial or other profes-
sional laboratory settings.
83
==PAGE:89==
These next steps will help transform the prototype into a usable and sustainable tool that
can support safer chemical practices across a wide range of users and institutions. The
work presented here provides a foundation for continued development, and can serve as
a stepping stone toward more effective, user-centered digital safety systems in laboratory
environments.
