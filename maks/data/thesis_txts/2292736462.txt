Abstract
Water and gas breakthrough in long horizontal wells is a common issue in the
petroleum industry. To counteract this problem, Inow Control Devices (ICDs) can
be placed along the well to delay the water and gas breakthrough. As an oil reser-
voir is a very harsh environment and commonly has a lifetime of over 20 years, the
reliability of the ICDs is vital.
In this thesis, a nozzle-based ICD is experimentally tested and used as a benchmark
for numerical modelling. Using standard turbulence models and comparing the pres-
sure dierence and mass ow rate, the numerical results show good agreement with
the experiment.
To simplify the domain required to accurately model the ICD, the ICD is modelled
in a reservoir governed by the Darcy equation. To relate to the Danish petroleum
industry, properties from the South Arne eld are used. The results show that only
modelling a small portion of the reservoir is required.
The ICD is then modelled for a vast range of scenarios using the simplied domain.
This ultimately leads to a characterization of the ICD in terms of the Reynolds
number, discharge coecient and geometrical variations. Here it is shown that in
particular the nozzle llets have an impact on the ICD characteristics.
To ensure reliability of the ICD, a Lagrangian real-particle approach is used to model
erosion. The erosion rate is analysed by injecting 1.5 million sand particles into the
system for varying scenarios and geometries.
An absolute worst-case scenario is also analysed. It is assumed that all nozzles except
one are plugged and that slug ow has occurred. This scenario is analysed using a
two phase Volume of Fluid model for oil/gas ow. The goal is to analyse the erosion
eects of only one nozzle being active.
Based on the characteristics and erosion rates for varying geometries, an optimised
design is proposed.
Keywords: CFD, STAR-CCM+, ICD, Nozzle, Erosion, VOF, Two-phase.

Chapter 1
Introduction
In the petroleum industry, it is common to use long horizontal wells to increase reser-
voir contact in order to optimize hydrocarbon recovery. As horizontal wells generally
have a higher production at the heel than at the toe of well due to the pressure
dierence in the long pipe, premature water or gas breakthrough is a common issue.
Due to water having a lower viscosity than oil, coning occurs, severely decreasing
hydrocarbon production at the toe of the completion. In order to delay coning, In-
ow Control Devices (ICDs) are placed along the completion to introduce a passively
controlled pressure drop, giving a uniform inux along the completion. Figure 1.1
illustrates how ICDs can improve reservoir lifetime compared to standard open hole
completions.
Figure 1.1: ICDs delaying the water/gas breakthrough, extending reservoir lifetime.
The two upper images show the coning eect occurring when using open
hole completion and the bottom two images show the eect of even oil
inux obtained using ICDs.
As a hydrocarbon reservoir is typically in production for 5 to >20 years, the long

time reliability of the completion is of importance. Most ICDs are passive and are
therefore set to introduce the correct pressure drop before entering the well. This
means that it is of great importance that the ICDs function as expected, as repairing
or interchanging components in a well often is not a viable option.
The combination of sand particles and high ow rates makes a reservoir a harsh
domain. Therefore, maintaining the planned inux can be dicult due to factors
such as plugging and erosion. Heterogeneity of the reservoir also plays a role, as
there can be changes in the oil viscosity and sand concentration and sand type along
the reservoir.[6]
The main concern is that the properties of an ICD will vary over time depending
on the production rate and erosion. Therefore, the ICD properties have to precisely
be known for varying ow rates, uid properties and eroded geometries.
1.1
The Welltec Inow Control Device
To provide predictable hydrocarbon inux from a reservoir, Welltec is developing the
Welltec Inow Control Device (WICD), which is a nozzle-based ICD that will be
thoroughly analysed in this thesis. The main dierence between the developed ICD
and the currently available ICDs, is that the Welltec ICD has a binary shutdown
mechanism that can be activated in case of coning and that the nozzles have a 30◦
inclined angle. Figure 1.2 shows the WICD in its open and closed state. To activate
the closing mechanism, a tractor provided by Welltec enters the well and slides the
closing mechanism into place.
In order to meet every need, the customer can customize the number of nozzles
and the nozzle diameter. As a default for this thesis, four Ø6mm nozzles will be
used unless stated otherwise. As the ICD is being developed for a reservoir without
signicant sand particles, no sand screen will be applied.
1.2
Thesis Motivation and Purpose
This thesis is a part of the OPTION (Optimizing Oil Production by Novel Technol-
ogy Integration) project. The OPTION project is a collaboration between Lloyd's
Register Consulting, LR Synergy, Welltec, Technical University of Denmark and the
University of Copenhagen.
The goal is to gain understanding and develop tech-
nologies for predicting and controlling the ow between the well and reservoir. The
project is divided into four work packages and this thesis is a part of work package
1, where the focus is on near-well ow modelling.
The motivation is that ICDs play a signicant role in the well ow modelling
as they control the inow and pressure dierence between the reservoir and well.
Furthermore are the length scales for an ICD in the millimetre-range, whereas in an
oil reservoir the length scales typically are in metres, giving diculties performing
numerical simulations for both length scales simultaneously.

2.2
Literature Review on Available Passive ICDs
Passive ICDs can generally be divided into four types: nozzle-based, helical channel,
tube-type and hybrid channel. To generate a pressure drop, the nozzle-based ICD
uses a restrictive mechanism, the helical channel ICD uses a friction mechanism whilst
the tube-type and hybrid channel ICDs use both mechanisms. The hybrid design can
vary a lot, as it still is relatively new design.
The description the ICDs will mostly be based on the work of Zeng et al. [20].
Their work is a comparative study of the four types of passive inow control devices,
where they study the eect of dierent viscosities, densities and ow rates using
numerical simulations.
2.2.1
Nozzle-based ICDs
The nozzle-based ICDs generate a instant pressure drop by forcing the uid though
small nozzles, often only a few millimetres in diameter. The pressure drop character-
istic can be described as
∆pr ∝ρv2
(2.1)
where ∆pr is the pressure drop due to restriction, ρ is the density of the uid and v
is the uid velocity.
As the nozzle diameter is very small the ICD is very susceptible to plugging.
Also does the large local pressure drop result in very high velocity magnitudes and
gradients in and around the nozzle, giving a high risk of erosion.
The advantages of a nozzle-based ICD is that it is in theory insensitive to changes
in viscosity.
The simple geometry also means that some providers have made it
possible to easily adjust the ICD on-site, only minutes before entering the well. [9]
2.2.2
Helical channel ICDs
The helical-channel ICD (gure 2.2) is based on using wall friction to generate a
continuous pressure drop by forcing the uid though a long helical channel. The
pressure drop characteristic is described by
∆pf ∝µv
(2.2)
where ∆pf is the pressure drop due to friction and µ is the dynamic viscosity.

The helical channels allow a large minimum cross sectional area. This results in
very low ow velocities and is the reason that helical-channel ICDs are described
as having laminar ow. The laminar ow means that the ICD is not very prone to
erosion. Furthermore do the large cross sectional areas mean that there is a very low
possibility of plugging.
The disadvantage with the helical channel ICD is that it is very sensitive to
changes in viscosity. This means that once water or gas breakthrough occurs, the
inux at that specic ICD will increase, thus limiting the inux of the remaining oil
producing ICDs.

Chapter 9
Discussion
In this chapter will the assumptions, inaccuracies and results will be discussed. Based
on the results, suggestions to optimizing the WICD will be proposed.
The experiment showed good agreement with the CFD software, but as the ow
meter had to be recalibrated after the test, there are certain uncertainties associated
with the results. To correlate for the poor calibration, a factor of 1.6009 had to be
multiplied on the measured ow rate. The issue is that this means that a linear
calibration is assumed which, even though being plausible, can not be guaranteed.
Another uncertainty is that only three parameters were measured, the pressure at the
inlet and outlet and the volumetric ow rate. More measuring points should be used,
especially close to the jet, as nding a model for accurately capturing its behaviour in
the nozzle will have an impact on the erosion modelling. The temperature also showed
to have a signicant impact, as using physical properties for water that is 15◦C cooler
gave results that were 0.45% closer to the experimental results. Therefore should the
experiment be redone with more sensors and the temperature being measured.
For these reasons, the experimental analysis should be seen as indicative and not
as absolute. Yet, if the experiment had been done at an earlier phase in the project,
the SST k-ω model might had been used, as it showed best agreement with the given
data. Further numerical testing could also had been made using other models, such
as the Large Eddy Simulation (LES) or the Reynolds Stress Model (RSM), but this
was not done to save time and resources.
To ensure that the assumptions used for the modelling the WICD are valid, the
WICD was simulated in a porous media domain. Here two simple scenarios were
analysed using data from the Danish South Arne eld. The assumptions of having a
smooth interface between the reservoir and well bore can lead to some inaccuracies,
as the interface must be expected to have some topology from the drilling. As a very
large domain was modelled, the WICD had to be coarsely meshed in order to save
cells, but as these simulations should only serve as a basis for the domain around the
WICD, the mesh should be sucient.
The velocity prole gave indications of the reservoir acting as a wall. Ideally, as
sharper decay of the velocity should be seen, but as the velocity was near zero at
the interface between the reservoir and the wellbore, the interface can be replaced
by a no-slip wall. As the pressure was close to constant for the entire wellbore, the
reservoir domain could be shortened. The porous media analysis has to be used with
==PAGE:103==
92
Discussion
care, as it is only valid for hard reservoirs like the Danish elds. If the WICD is
to be produced for elds with signicantly higher permeability, the analysis should
be redone to ensure that the assumptions hold true. The main concern is that the
particle trajectory can vary depending on the permeability of the reservoir, resulting
in a dierent erosion rate and pattern.
The WICD was investigated in terms of nozzle angle, diameter and llet radii.
Here it was shown that in particular the llets were of importance. Therefore, the
WICD was characterized in terms of the discharge coecient, Reynolds number and
llet radii. Also was an equation was proposed for tting the data. The characteriza-
tion curves show that the sharp edged nozzle is insensitive to changes in viscosity. On
the other hand, the lleted nozzles are sensitive to changes in viscosity for Re < 4·105,
equal to a pressure dierence of ∆p < 20bar. Therefore, should the basic assump-
tions found in the literature regarding nozzle-based ICDs being insensitive to changes
in viscosity, be used with caution. In addition, this shows the need for testing an
ICD, either numerically or experimentally, over a vast range of Reynolds numbers to
ensure accurate characterization.
As a simple comparison of a sharp edged nozzle and 0.5mm lleted nozzle showed
a 35% increase in mass ow rate, erosion modelling of the ICD becomes critical
to ensure predictable performance over the reservoir lifetime. To further improve
predictability, the mounting direction of the WICD should be specied, as it was
shown that the direction of the ow in the production well does have a minor impact
on the mass ow rate through the nozzles. Even if the change in mass ow rate is
only in the range 0.13% −0.60%, this error can easily be eliminated by specifying an
ideal mounting direction.
Erosion modelling showed that using steel nozzles would result in very heavy
erosion. By using tungsten inserts, a factor 30 reduction can be achieved. Altering
turbulence models showed to have some eect on the erosion rate and erosion pattern,
with the realizable k-ε model having the highest erosion rate. Due to the lack of
experimental data, the issue of nding the best suitable turbulence model could
not be resolved, meaning that it has to be assumed that the at plate experiment
validated by Boye [2] is valid for nozzle ows. Here the realizable k-ε model was used.
Using tungsten inserts and the realizable k-ε model, variations of the nozzle ge-
ometry were analysed. The results showed that altering the angle or applying llets
can greatly reduce the mean erosion rate in the nozzles. Here, the erosion rate was
reduced to Em < 0.001 mm
year compared to the original Em,tungten = 0.161 mm
year when
simulating at ∆p = 10bar. In terms of optimization, either one of these, or a combi-
nation of the two adjustments have to be applied. If the customer is satised with
using higher inclined angle, then ideally the nozzle angle should be set to 90◦as it
showed to have no erosion in the nozzle when using 250µm particles. To deal with
the signicant erosion on the nozzle edges, llets should be simulated, to ensure that
they have the same eect on a 90◦and a 30◦nozzle. If the customer on the other
hand requires a 30◦inclined nozzle, then only applying a llet should be sucient.
For both cases the rf = 0.50mm llet can be recommended, as it showed to have
around the same eect as the rf = 1.00mm llet regarding erosion. The advantage of
==PAGE:104==
Design Proposal
93
using a rf = 0.50mm llet over a rf = 1.00mm llet, is that the discharge coecient
is less volatile when using the rf = 0.50mm llet, meaning the WICD becomes less
sensitive to changes in viscosity.
In order to investigate a worst case scenario, a two-phase simulation was analysed.
As no mesh convergence was performed, the results have to be discussed in terms of
usability and not in terms of exact values. It can be observed that the erosion rate on
the sliding sleeve might become signicant. As both slug ow in the well and plugging
of three nozzles at the same time is a very unlikely scenario, further simulations should
be made where the amount of active nozzles is varied. Due to time constraints this
was not done. Considering that the particles tend to leave the jet soon after leaving
the nozzle, there might be a similar erosion when all four nozzles are active. This was
not investigated at it would require all four jets to be well rened, resulting a very
long simulation time. In addition, the accuracy of the simulations can be questioned,
as it can not be expected that all particles will leave the jet before it impinges the
sliding sleeve. Therefore should the possibility of adding extra particle models be
investigated.
9.1
Design Proposal
As erosion was investigated for a limited set of scenarios, the recommendation can
change depending on what sand size and pressure dierence can be expected for the
specic reservoir. Yet, the performed analysis should serve as a good generic basis
for proposing optimizations. Based on the current analysis and discussion, it can
be recommend to use tungsten inserts with at least a rf = 0.5mm llet. Ideally,
the nozzle angle should be made steeper, but this might not always be possible due
to customer specications. Regarding the sleeve, some ceramic coating should be
applied for safety. If only lleted tungsten inserts are applied, the mean erosion rate
can be expected to be reduced with over a factor 2600 in comparison to steel nozzles
without any llets. Comparing with a sharp edged tungsten nozzle, the rf = 0.5mm
llet reduces the mean erosion rate by a factor 89.

Chapter 10
Conclusion
This thesis has shown that an ICD can be thoroughly analysed and optimized based
on CFD results.
The literature study showed what the requirements are for an ICD over the reser-
voir lifetime. It was also shown which types of ICDs currently are used in the industry
and how they function. In order to get insight into how the experimental work re-
garding ICDs is performed, articles were found where the ICDs are tested in terms
of characteristics and erosion. The literature study also showed that little work had
been done regarding erosion modelling of ICDs and that most researchers analyse
erosion by only drawing logical conclusions. To obtain relevant reservoir data, data
from the Danish South Arne eld was used. As this thesis is a part of the OPTION
project, available OPTION material regarding erosion was accounted for.
Based on the experimental results obtained at Welltec, the CFD software could be
validated. The results showed good agreement between the experiment and numerical
model. Even though the k-ω SST model showed the best agreement, the realizable
k-ε model was used throughout this work as most of the simulations had to be started
prior to the experimental analysis.
When characterizing the WICD it was shown that the main parameter for poten-
tial inaccuracies in predicting the mass ow rate through the nozzles was the llets
on the upper edges of the nozzles. The minor inaccuracies were the nozzle angle and
ow direction in the production well. The nozzle diameter proved to have no eect
on the characteristics. Therefore was the WICD characterized in terms Reynolds
number, discharge coecient and llet radii. The characterization showed the larger
the llet, the more volatile the WICD is to changes in viscosity.
For predicting erosion, particles were modelled using Lagrangian modelling sup-
plemented with the Oka correlation using coecients from previous OPTION work.
Here it was shown that using tungsten inserts greatly improves the durability of the
nozzles. The durability can also be further increased by altering the nozzle angle
and applying llets. The analysis was supplemented by simulating dierent particle
sizes, pressure dierence across the nozzle and modelling turbulent dispersion. Here,
especially increasing the pressure dierence proved to have a large impact on the
erosion rate in the nozzles.
To analyse a worst case scenario, a two-phase Volume of Fluid simulation was
performed with particles injected into the system after the simulation had stabilized.
==PAGE:106==
Suggestions for Future Work
95
Here it could be seen, that under absolute worst conditions, erosion on the inside
of the production well can become an issue. Therefore, it is recommend to apply
ceramic coating to protect the exposed parts.
By discussing the available results an optimized geometry is proposed. The con-
clusion is that the nozzle angle should preferably be increased to at least 60◦and at
least 0.5mm llets should be applied on the nozzle edges to prevent erosion.
10.1
Suggestions for Future Work
The investigation and analysis of the Welltec ICD is an endless task as there are
always more variations that can be tested. Based on this thesis, currently the most
relevant paths for further investigation are:
 Experiment: The experiment should be redone with additional sensors posi-
tioned closer to the jet. The possibilities of accelerated erosion testing should
also be investigated, as it can contribute to validation of both the turbulence
model and the erosion modelling.
 Erosion modelling: The sand size and sand type for relevant oil elds should
be found and implemented as a normal distribution. In addition, extensive work
should be done in testing the erosion resistance of materials with respect to these
sand types. Furthermore, combinations of the simulated variations should be
investigated, in particular the combination of varying the nozzle angle and llet
radii.
 Deformable mesh: To investigate how the nozzle geometry will alter over
time, the erosion simulations should be performed with a mesh that deforms
depending on erosion. This can provide more insight into how erosion will aect
the performance of an ICD over the reservoir lifetime.
 VOF modelling: A convergence analysis for the VOF simulations should
conducted and scenarios that are more likely to occur should be investigated.
Furthermore, a model should be developed for the particle suspension so that
the particles cannot freely leave the jet.
