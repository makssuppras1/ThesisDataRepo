Abstract
This thesis presents a programming toolchain for digital microfluidic biochips
(DMFB), consisting of a domain-specific language, compiler, execution engine,
and communication protocol.
The main purpose of the toolchain is to pro-
vide an intuitive and user-friendly interface for creating and executing proto-
cols through digital microfluidics, targeting life science practitioners, that might
not have extensive existing code knowledge. This work introduces a new way
of handling contamination, by complete contamination-avoidance. Through a
contamination-aware routing algorithm, droplet paths are calculated to mini-
mize contamination, while avoiding collisions. Dedicated communication pro-
tocols are employed to allow the toolchain to execute commands on various
DMFB hardware. With configurable files, the toolchain allows for modularity
and multi-platform support. Extensive testing and real-life protocol case studies
have shown promising results for the toolchain, and showcase that complex pro-
tocols can be expressed by the domain-specific language and that the toolchain
can execute them precisely on the DMFB.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
The DMF platform
Digital Microfluidics (DMF) represents a new approach to performing biochem-
ical processes on a small scale. This technology operates by precisely manip-
ulating small droplets of liquid across a grid of electrodes through a process
called electrowetting on dielectric [1]. By activating specific electrodes, droplets
can be moved, merged, split, or subjected to various operations that replicate
normal lab tasks. The DMF platform can be fitted with components, such as
actuators and sensors, to further increase the potential operations that can be
performed on the platform.
DMF platforms eliminate the need for big and
expensive equipment and manual labour, since pipetting between test tubes is
replaced by droplet movement, and equipment such as thermocyclers can be
built into the platform.
DMF offers a compact and efficient alternative to common lab processes. It
provides practicality and scalability through the automation of complex bio-
chemical procedures. DMF, also commonly known as “lab-on-a-chip” devices
use fewer materials, speed up experiments, and make it possible to carry out
advanced protocols in smaller portable systems. Whether for diagnostic testing,
drug research, or other life science applications, DMF transforms how scientists
work by simplifying and miniaturizing traditional laboratory workflows.

2
Introduction
1.2
Motivation
Digital Microfluidic Biochips enable small-scale experiments with minimal man-
ual intervention, representing a significant leap forward in laboratory technol-
ogy. However, programming these devices remains a challenge for many users.
The current programming language BioAssembly, introduced in Section 2.2.1,
for the DMF developed by DTU Compute, closely resembles low-level assembly
code, making it difficult to learn and use, especially for laboratory assistants
or biologists who may not have experience with programming at the assembly
level. Furthermore, working with multiple substances on the same biochip in-
troduces the risk of contamination. When droplets move across electrodes, they
can leave behind residual substances, creating a risk of cross-contamination with
other substances.
Our project seeks to address this issue by introducing a high-level programming
language and a compilation toolchain made for the DMF. The language should
enable users to create protocols with ease, abstracting away the complexity of
low-level operations.
By building a domain-specific language, compiler, exe-
cution engine, and communication protocol, we will address critical challenges
such as managing droplet contamination. By automating droplet procedures
and processes, the compiler not only streamlines workflow for the user but also
ensures greater reliability and consistency in experiments. This approach trans-
forms the way users interact with DMF biochips, making the technology more
accessible and effective for scientific research.
1.3
Presentation
This thesis presents the design and implementation of a programming toolchain
for digital microfluidic biochips.
The toolchain consists of a domain-specific
programming language, a just-in-time (JIT) compiler, an execution engine, and
a communication protocol, enabling high-level programs to be converted into
low-level instructions, that can be executed on the digital microfluidic biochip
(DMFB). The purpose of the toolchain is to provide a high-level abstraction
to the end-user, allowing them to define protocols in an easily understandable
and convenient way, without having to consider complexities, such as contami-
nation. The Toolchain is able to provide the user with syntax errors, both from
static type checking of the program through the compilation, and runtime if any
violation arises during execution. It is able to generate routes for the droplets
efficiently managing contamination and avoiding conflicts. The Toolchain can
connect to DMF platforms using communication protocols, and be able to de-

1.4
Thesis-Specific Terminology
This thesis introduces three key terms: Solution, Toolchain, and Compiler. It
is important for the reader to understand the meaning behind these terms since
they will be used throughout this thesis. In Figure 1.1 the terms are visualised
using the different components of the Solution.
Figure 1.1: Overview of the key parts of the solution, grouped by the thesis-
specific terminologies presented in this thesis.
The Solution encapsulates the domain-specific language, the user interface, com-
pilation, execution, and communication, an alias for the Solution is Program-
ming Toolchain. The Toolchain is a subset of the Solution and contains the user
interface, compilation, execution, and communication. The Compiler is a subset
of the Toolchain and consists of the user interface, compilation, and execution.

1.5
Contributions
This thesis contributes with a new language and tool, within the topic of lan-
guage design and compilation for digital microfluidic biochips (DMFB). The
contributions of this thesis can be summarized as follows.
• Language: Through this thesis we present a domain-specific language de-
sign for the DMFB. The language encapsulates all domain-specific droplet
operations and supports hardware-specific interactions with the DMFB.
Furthermore, the language is designed to be user-friendly, facilitating ease
of use for users without extensive coding knowledge.
• Compilation Toolchain: With this thesis we provide a full-package so-
lution that bridges the gap between protocols and platforms. By creating
a Programming Toolchain we let the user focus on the protocol, without
having to consider every detail of the digital microfluidic domain.
• Contamination avoidance: By introducing user-defined contamination
rules, that describe the contamination relation between specified sub-
stances, we are effectively able to move droplets in a configuration that
causes minimal contamination, while avoiding residual substances, that
would otherwise cause contamination.
• Routing algorithm: In order to support the characteristics of a just-
in-time (JIT) compiler, we have created an efficient contamination-aware
priority-based routing algorithm. It factors in contamination potential and
contamination relations in its calculations, while adhering to the domain-
specific requirements. The algorithm is able to find routes within a small
time window, which is essential when sending time-critical commands to
an external system.
• Template-based abstraction: We have introduced templates as a high-
level abstraction of the low-level electrode activations, allowing platform
designers to change the fundamental actions of the droplets on a granular
level. Templates also enable fast and efficient testing of new action designs,
such as merging and splitting in specific patterns.
• Multi-platform support: Through the configuration, we have intro-
duced a way for platform designers to modify and adjust the inner work-
ings of the Toolchain to align with a specific platform.
Together with
the modular communication protocol, the Toolchain can be extended to
support various physical and simulated platforms.
• Device interaction: By introducing a feedback system we are able to
query sensor readings and activate actuators dynamically in run-time.

the operations available to the DMFB. This chapter also presents related work,

2.1
Digital Microfludic Biochips
Microfluidics is the study of manipulating minuscule liquid volumes, often mea-
sured in microlitres. The liquid manipulation is achieved through methods, such
as micropumps, electrowetting, among others. One of the hardware that per-
form microfluidic operations is called a Digital Microfluidic Biochip (DMFB).
DFMBs can be used to automate laboratory procedures, and are often referred
to as lab-on-a-chip. They are designed to use a configuration of electrodes that
use electrowetting on dielectric (EWOD) [2] to move small droplets on a planar
surface. EWOD works by applying voltages to electrodes in order to alter the
surface tension of droplets, enabling precise movement by creating or collapsing
localized electric fields, thus moving the droplets on top of the electrode.

Related Works at DTU
Here we will present related work originating from within DTU, these related
works all work closely with the specific type of DFMB we used for testing and

Bioassembly
BioAssembly [5] is an instruction set architecture (ISA) inspired by traditional
ISAs and was developed by DTU Compute. It introduces a comprehensive set
of low-level instructions that can be executed on Digital Microfluidic Biochips
(DMFs). BioAssembly provides an independent programming interface for DMFs,
enabling users to construct protocols that can be executed directly on these
biochips.
The BioAssembly instruction set consists of fundamental commands that users
can combine to create complex functionalities. These instructions can be catego-
rized into three main types: electrode control, task management, and synchro-
nization instructions. The electrode control instructions include SETEL, CLREL,
SETELI, and CLRELI. The SET instructions activate an electrode, while the CLR
instructions deactivate it. By correctly combining these SET and CLR instruc-
tions, users can effectively control the movement of droplets on the DMF biochip.
For our solution, the electrode control instructions are particularly useful as
these can be used to communicate directly with the biochip. The task man-
agement and synchronization instructions are out of scope as our compiler only
compiles the input program into electrode control instructions.
Programming Biochemistry on Digital Microfluidics Biochips
The project Programming Biochemistry on Digital Microfluidics Biochips [6],
implemented by Wenjie Fan, introduces a programming-language-based method
for expressing and executing biochemistry protocols on Digital Microfluidic
(DMF) Biochips. The project is composed of three main components: a domain-
specific language (DSL) called BioGo, a compiler, and an executor.
==PAGE:25==

BioGo serves as a high-level textual programming language designed specifically
for DMFs, allowing researchers to express biochemical operations or protocols.
The compiler translates BioGo programs into an internal representation, which
the executor then uses to apply routing capabilities, such as determining suitable
droplet paths using the A* algorithm, while avoiding collisions. This internal
representation can be executed on both physical and simulated DMF platforms.
A novel feature of this project is its support for both asynchronous and syn-
chronous execution modes. In asynchronous mode, the BioGo protocol is com-
piled into an intermediate representation known as BioAssembly, which can
be executed on either the physical or simulated DMF. The synchronous mode
leverages a vision-based real-time detection system, using camera technology to
accurately track droplet positions on the DMF biochip. This enables error han-
dling by comparing real-time droplet positions against the expected positions
generated by the compiler, ensuring high-precision execution on the physical
DMF.
The BioGo language presented a well-structured approach with a coordinate-
based system to manipulate droplets. When developing our solution, we can
draw inspiration from the structure and coordinate-based system, as they pro-
vide a solid foundation when creating a domain-specific language for digital
microfluidic biochips.
A specialized toolchain for programming digital microfluidic biochips
Helene Ørberg Moesgaard and Tore Sebastian Bre¨uner developed a specialized
toolchain for programming digital microfluidic biochips [7].
Their toolchain
introduces a domain-specific language that enables the design and execution
of protocols for microfluidic biochips, providing nine core commands to control
chip operations. The commands consist of: Input, Output, Move, Mix, Merge,
Split, Temp(change temperature), Sense and Wait.
The toolchain models droplets as individual agents, using threads to manage
their concurrent behaviours.
Protocols are parsed and translated into com-
mands that these agents execute on the biochip. A built-in simulator represents
the chip’s layout, where agents interact with the board’s environment. To han-
dle multi-agent pathfinding issues, such as potential conflicts and deadlocks, the
toolchain applies an A* algorithm to resolve them. Notably, the system sup-
ports the movement of both amorphous and snake-like droplets, accommodating
different droplet shapes and sizes. During execution, the toolchain outputs the
droplet movements from the simulator and converts them into BioAssembly

The language presented in the project implements many features of the digital

During idea generation for our solution, these movement types were areas we

A programming interface and toolchain for digital microfluidic biochips
The project A Programming Interface and Toolchain for Digital Microfluidic
Biochips [8] developed by Oliver Tobias Siggaard and Oliver Grønborg Chris-
tensen, introduces a visual programming interface, a compiler, and a domain-
specific language made for digital microfluidic biochips. The main focus of the
project was to create an intuitive interface for designing and executing protocols
on these biochips. Their language includes seven core commands that allow for
the manipulation of fluid flow on the chip. The toolchain features a compilation
and execution engine that translates these commands into BioAssembly instruc-
tions. Additionally, the compiler uses a scheduler that attempts to parallelize
operations whenever possible, maximizing the efficiency of protocol execution.
To prevent deadlocks during execution, the project implements a deadlock res-
olution mechanism that resolves conflicts by repeating random moves until the
issue is cleared.
The visual programming language introduced in this related work, shows an
interesting idea of how to abstract protocols into visual program blocks. The
overall idea behind a visual programming language is good, as it provides an
understandable interface for users without programming knowledge. However,
as mentioned in the thesis, when the protocols becomes too complex the man-
agement of the visual blocks become difficult, and the visual language becomes
hard to understand and work with. We believe that although the visual language
provides a user-friendly interface, the downsides of scalability with complexity
outweighs the user-friendliness.

BioCoder
The BioCoder language presented in “Biocoder: A programming language for
standardizing and automating biology protocols” [10], is developed to provide a
language for formalization of bio-chemical protocols. By allowing users to write
precise, stepwise instructions for experiments, BioCoder ensures that protocols
are both machine-readable and human-readable. When a program is manually
defined in BioCoder it can made into human-readable steps and a graph of the
protocol can be generated.
The BioCoder language focuses on the formalization of biochemical processes
such that they can be read and understood, a principle that we can use dur-
ing our development to ensure clarity and compatibility of our domain-specific
language.
An Open-Source Compiler and PCB Synthesis Tool
The paper “An Open-Source Compiler and PCB Synthesis Tool for Digital Mi-
crofluidic Biochips” [11] focuses on the development of a comprehensive soft-

BioScript
BioScript as detailed in “BioScript: Programming Safe Chemistry on Laboratories-
on-a-Chip” [12] introduces a domain-specific language (DSL) aimed at simpli-
fying the programming of digital microfluidic biochips (DMFBs) by providing
a type-safe and human-readable syntax. Apart from the language the article
also outlines the creation of a compiler, and execution engine for the BioScript
language. To ensure safety and avoid hazardous chemical interactions, the Bio-
Script enforces contamination safety, this is done by ensuring type safety and
creating an internal interference graph. BioScript then divides the board into
blocks, and places the droplets in accordance with the interference graph to
ensure droplet safety.
This paper introduces a block-based approach to enforce contamination-safety,
and underlines the issue and complexity that contamination brings to the do-
main. During our development contamination is an issue that we should con-
sider, and we should consider contamination-safety in our solution.

Contamination-aware routing
The article “Unified Contamination-Aware Routing Method Considering Realis-
tic Washing Capacity Constraint in Digital Microfluidic Biochips” [13] proposes
a method for routing droplets, and cleaning electrodes on a Digital microfluidic
biochip. The goal of the proposed method is to route droplet with minimal
contamination, this is done by introducing a routing framework that calculates
droplet paths. The droplet paths may pass through contaminated electrodes,
so to prevent contamination the method uses washing droplets that clear elec-
trodes of contamination. The washing droplets are scheduled to wash electrodes
that need to have multiple droplets pass through them.
This article outlines the concept of contamination and a possible approach to
routing, concepts that are important when developing a compilation toolchain
and should also be considered while developing our proposed solution.

Gained Knowledge
Through the study of related works, we gained valuable insight that we can
use in the development of our Solution. The different languages in the related
works [6][7][8][10][12] provide a good foundation for what works and what needs
additional refinement. The language proposed by BioGo is simple and easy to
understand but requires some additional functionality. When working with dif-

on the DTU biochip. Another separates the biochip into blocks/areas to sep-

non-contaminating droplets, we would like to use an approach where droplets
can move all over the biochip. The related work [8] explores the concept of a
visual programming language, but as some protocols can become quite large,
we believe that a visual programming approach can become incomprehensible,
and limit the user in the complexity of protocols that can be created.

Domain-Specific Language and Toolchain
In the study of related works
and the unique challenges presented by DMFBs, it was discovered that the
Solution should target users with great domain-specific knowledge but limited
programming experience. To make programming DMFBs more accessible the
Solution should therefore include a domain-specific language made for the spe-
cific needs of the domain.
The programming language should be high-level,
user-friendly, and designed to simplify droplet manipulation and protocol exe-
cution on a DMFB. The project should also include a compiler to translate the
language to low-level electrode actions, an execution engine to execute low-level
electrode actions, and a communication protocol to support communication with
the DMFB.
Just-In-Time Compilation
To support protocols that rely on specific sub-
stance properties, sensors provide the capabilities to actively measure such prop-
erties. This can be achieved with static or dynamic compilation. With static
compilation, where the whole program is evaluated prior to execution on the
DMFB, every possible branch has to be pre-computed, such that all possible
cases are covered in the static program. This process is not feasible for complex
protocols, as it would require massive computational capabilities. To put this
into perspective, a while loop would require computations of every possible exit,
since the conditional property might not be able to be inferred statically.
To prevent this massive computational overhead, we build our system as a dy-
namic compilation process, just-in-time (JIT) compilational process, such that
we can dynamically identify dependencies in the program, and only compute
steps that can be resolved immediately. This reduces the overhead significantly,
as we do not need to evaluate every branch, but can rely on evaluating the condi-
tional first, and then only compute the corresponding branch. The nature of JIT
also enables the support of sensors and actuators, as we can dynamically wait
for the measurements, before we continue with the execution, without having
to pre-compute every possible outcome. However, JIT compilation also comes
with a trade-off, since we dynamically compute, resolve, and send the program,
a path that is thought to be optimal during the compilation of one dependency,
could be sub-optimal or destructive when the subsequent dependencies are re-
solved.

Chapter 4
Language
One of the major contributions of this thesis is the domain-specific language
(DSL). The DSL is designed to encapsulate all domain-specific behaviour, in-
cluding droplet actions and device interaction while being user-friendly and
convenient to use. The goal of this language is to provide an abstraction for
users that is expressive and understandable. Enabling users to express their
microfluidic protocols in the DSL, and others can gain an understanding of the
protocol logic by reading them. This chapter outlines the DSL describing the
key features and providing a complete overview of the supported commands.
4.1
Design Principles
The DSL should be able to express simple to complex protocols and to achieve
this it should include all the domain-specific operations showcased in Figure
2.2. Together move, merge, mix, sense, split, dispense, dispose, and temperature
control (actuate) form the foundational operations used to define every protocol.
These operations are designed to align with the physical capabilities of the
digital microfluidic biochips, ensuring that every protocol written in the DSL
maps directly to actions executable on the hardware. From these operations
complex procedures such as polymerase chain reaction (PCR) can be expressed.
A PCR protocol will later be expressed in a case study, in Section 7.5.
==PAGE:40==
26
Language
However, the ability to express protocols alone is not sufficient. To be effective,
the DSL should offer a user-friendly abstraction from the complex actions, allow-
ing users without extensive programming knowledge to create and understand
protocols. As Hudak (1998) states,
“... programs written in a DSL also have one other important characteristic, they
can often be written by non-programmers. More precisely, they can be written by
non-programmers who are nevertheless experts in the domain for which the DSL
was designed. This helps bridge the gap (often a chasm) between developer and
user, a potentially major hidden cost in software development. It also raises an
important point about DSL design: a user immersed in a domain already knows
the domain semantics.” [20]
This highlights the importance of designing the DSL in a way that resembles
the semantics familiar to domain experts. By offering intuitive abstractions, the
DSL can provide accessibility and usability, while maintaining precision, which
is required for microfluidic operations.
To ensure that the DSL adheres to these concepts we have identified some core
design principles [21].
• Domain semantics
The DSL should encapsulate the domain semantics of the DMFB, ensur-
ing that commands align closely with the physical operations possible on
the biochip. Commands should map to real-world actions such as droplet
movement, sensing, and changing temperature while maintaining the ter-
minology familiar to domain experts.
• Structure
The DSL should have a well-defined structure, such that users can easily
identify commands, but it should not deviate from common structures in
programming languages. Therefore, each command should be designed to
act as a function, and common functionality, such as mathematical and
logical expressions, should follow the standard approach given by C#.
• Readability and simplicity
The DSL should be user-friendly, making it easy to read and write, even
for users with minimal programming experience. This is achieved by using
clear and intuitive naming for commands and a syntax that closely resem-
bles the underlying operations. This ensures that the DSL is functional,
accessible and maintainable, letting users focus on designing protocols
without the need to understand the low-level details.
• Real-time
==PAGE:41==
4.2 Language Definition
27
The DSL should support parallel execution of commands, allowing mul-
tiple operations to be executed simultaneously.
Since the compilation
process happens in real-time, the DSL must allow users to enforce syn-
chronization points where necessary. This ensures efficient execution of
protocols while giving the users the ability to maintain control over the
execution order.
• Statically typed
To ensure that users do not receive type errors during the execution of
their protocols, the language should be statically typed.
This enforces
types to be explicitly declared by the user or inferred by the Compiler,
such that type errors can be thrown before execution of the protocol.
Catching errors at this stage prevents runtime interruption, which would
require the program and hardware to be reset.
• Limit types
The DSL should capture the semantics of the domain as precisely as possi-
ble, focusing only on the types essential for expressing DMFB operations.
Therefore, it is unnecessary to include the full range of types commonly
expressed in general-purpose programming languages. For example, nu-
merical types such as ‘int‘, ‘long‘, ‘float‘, and ‘double‘ can be consolidated
into a singular numerical type, simplifying the type system and aligning
it with the domains requirements.
• Scaleable with protocols
The DSL should support the creation of complex protocols, ensuring scal-
ability as procedures become more advanced. This is achieved through
control flow constructs, letting the user create protocols that can dynam-
ically consider properties of the droplets, and make informed decisions
based on logical operations.
These design principles are the foundational pillars when designing the DSL,
ensuring that the language is user-friendly and capable of addressing the unique
requirements of DMFBs.

Data Types and Expressions
The language provides a type system designed to handle droplet operations

Variables
Variables are used to store numerical values that can be referenced
and manipulated within the program. The language includes a single numeric
type, Num, which represents floating-point numbers. This type provides flexibil-
ity for specifying parameters such as volumes, ratios, and positions. Whenever
an integer is needed for example in coordinates, only the discrete component
of Num is used, effectively treating it as an integer, ensuring compatibility with
the DFMBs discrete electrode grid. Using a unified Num type simplifies the lan-
guage and avoids the need for users, that might not be used to programming,
to distinguish between integers and floating-point numbers. By leveraging C#’s
built-in double type, the language ensures robust and accurate numerical oper-
ations. However, this approach might still cause imprecise values in some edge
cases, which should be handled during the compilation process, by introducing
a precision threshold of 10−10. The threshold value of 10−10 is chosen since it
is smaller than the range of typical values in many calculations but still large
enough to account for imprecision.

Commands
To organize functionality and improve comprehension, the language groups com-
mands into four distinct categories: droplet commands, device commands, flow
control commands, and general commands. Below is a description of each cat-
egory, as well as the commands it contains and their functionality.
A more
detailed description and syntax of each command is provided in section 4.4.
Droplet commands
Droplet commands are the commands that directly con-
trol the movement and operation of droplets on the platform.
• DropletDeclaration: Allocates a droplet to a specified position on the
biochip.
• Move: Moves a droplet to the provided position.
• Split: Splits a droplet into two smaller droplets with the volume desig-
nated to the ratio.
• Merge: Combines two droplets into one at a designated location.
• Mix: Mixes a droplet.
• Store: Stores a droplet for a given time.
• Waste: Waste a droplet removing it from the compilation process.
Droplet commands provide functionality that is central to defining the physical
manipulation of droplets and their interactions on the DFMB.
Device commands
Device commands facilitate interaction with external
hardware components on the DFMB including sensors, actuators and dispensers.
• Sense: Sends a request to a sensor and stores the result in a variable.
• Actuate: Sends a command to an actuator to start, stop, or change the
state of it.
• DispenserDeclaration: Declares a dispenser with a given name and sub-
stance.
• Dispense: Dispenses a droplet from a dispenser with a designated volume.
==PAGE:45==
4.3 Language Features
31
The device commands enable the Toolchain to trigger actions on the biochip’s
connected devices and gather real-time feedback during execution. They are
crucial for making a protocol where the actions performed are based on reading.
Flow control commands
Flow control commands manage the logical flow
of the program, and can operate based on sensor readings.
• If-statement: A conditional if statement that can optionally include an
else-statement.
• While-loop: A while loop for repeating operations.
The flow-control commands allow conditional execution and looping, enabling
complex decision-making and iterative processes in the protocol. This allows
for the effective scalability of complex protocols in the DSL.
General commands
General commands provide some auxiliary functions,
for variables, printing and time synchronization.
• Assignment: Assigns a number to a variable.
• PrintStatement: Prints variables to the command line.
• Wait: Halts the compilation process for a specified amount of time, and
forces synchronization up to this point.
• WaitForUserInput: Halts the compilation process until the user provides
a start signal, and forces synchronization up to this point.
General commands support the overall functionality of protocols by handling
variables, providing output, and allowing user intervention. They can facili-
tate debugging, fine-tuning execution, and ensuring proper synchronization of
protocol steps.
4.3
Language Features
In this section, the main features of the domain-specific language (DSL) are
outlined. These features highlight the strengths and simplifications of the DSL,
which further amplifies the usability and potential of the DSL.
==PAGE:46==
32
Language
Figure 4.1: Programable area of the DMFB marked by the red rectangle
Platform abstractions The language should abstract the complexity of know-
ing specific electrode IDs. A DFMB consists of a programmable area that is the
rectangle area of electrodes in the centre, see Figure 4.1. In the programmable
area, each electrode can be represented by an x and y coordinate, with 0, 0 in
the upper left corner. We will use these coordinates to simplify positions in our
language.
Turing completeness
A feature of the language is its Turing completeness,
achieved through the inclusion of control flow constructs such as if statements
and while loops. This allows users to make programs with complex logic and
dynamic decision-making. For example, users can create conditional flows based
on sensor feedback or repeat specific operations until a condition is met, enabling
real-time adaptability in experiments.
Substance support
Another feature is the declaration of substances for
droplets, which makes contamination management easier. Each droplet can be
assigned a specific substance during declaration, enabling the Compiler to auto-
matically track potential contamination risks as droplets interact. This feature
ensures that incompatible substances are never mixed inadvertently and simpli-
fies contamination handling, which traditionally required manual oversight.
Parallelism
The language is designed such that it should be possible to run
in parallel. This significantly reduces protocol execution time by utilizing the
biochip’s resources efficiently. For example, a Move command for one droplet
can be executed concurrently with a Merge command for another droplet, pro-
vided there are no dependencies or conflicts. Importantly, while the program’s
command order matters, the Compiler can reorder independent commands to
enhance efficiency.

5.1
Architecture
For our Toolchain, we have chosen to work with a layered architecture [22].
In this architecture each layer is responsible for a specific set of tasks in the
compilation and execution of the program. The layered structure ensures the
separation of concerns between different tasks and follows a natural flow from
high-level user input to low-level DMFB instructions. The layers interact by
passing intermediate results, such as abstract syntax trees and low-level actions,
ensuring that each layer focuses on a specific responsibility without overlapping
concerns
==PAGE:54==
40
Design
Figure 5.1: High-level architecture diagram of the layers in the Toolchain. The figure also displays intermediate data
representations between layers.
==PAGE:55==
5.1 Architecture
41
As shown in Figure 5.1, the Toolchain consists of four primary layers and an
infrastructure layer. The roles of each layer and how they interact are explained
as follows.
Presentation layer
The presentation is the layer where the user interacts
with the Solution. The layer handles all user inputs, including the high-level
program to be compiled and executed. This layer shows a console for the user
to interact with and inputs from the user in the form of programs can be parsed
to the translation layer.
Translation layer The role of the translation layer is to take the raw high-level
program provided by the user and transform it into a formalized code format
that the application layer can compile. During translation, the layer identifies
independent paths in the program that can be executed in parallel. This al-
lows the application layer to focus on direct compilation without recalculating
parallelization commands, delegating this task to the translation layer.
Application layer
The application layer is where the main compilation task
is performed. It transforms the formalized code into low-level actions that can
be executed on the DMFB, the low-level electrode actions are, once compiled,
passed to the communication layer. Compilation of the high-level code involves
translating commands into specific routes and movements to be performed via
electrode actions. Furthermore, this layer can request and process feedback from
the following layer, the communication layer. This is done when the application
layer needs information from the DMFB chip itself, for instance in the case of
a sensor read.
Communication layer
The communication layer receives electrode actions
for the DMFB from the application layer It translates them into the protocol
required by the specific hardware or simulator, that the code is being executed
on. The communication supports one-way communication sending actions to the
DMFB, and two-way communication, where it requests data from the DMFB
and parses the response to the application layer.
Infrastructure layer
Although the Toolchain can be divided into distinct
sequential layers, some components are shared across multiple layers, this is the
role of the infrastructure layer. This layer contains simple entities and services
that are shared across the layers. This could be classes representing commands
in our language or a runtime exception handler.
==PAGE:56==
42
Design
5.1.1
Design Rationale
The decision to adopt a layered architecture was made because a compiler gen-
erally follows a step-by-step approach and thus fits this structure well. Each
layer handles a distinct set of responsibilities, from user interaction to low-level
hardware communication, this allows for each layer to be developed and tested
independently, focusing on specific responsibilities.
The layered architecture
also allows to modify one or more layers easily without having to refactor the
other layers, as an agreement of communication and separation of concerns have
been made. We also considered using other architecture patterns such as MVC
[23], or CLEAN [24] architecture. We ultimately choose to work with a lay-
ered architecture over the others because layered architecture provides us with
more structure and modularity than MVC. And as the flow of a compiler usu-
ally follows a predictable flow the additional complexity of enforcing the use of
dependency inversion between layers in the CLEAN architecture would be too
cumbersome.

5.5.1
Lexer Parser
The goal of the lexer-parser is to transform our custom microfluidic language
into a set of tokens or objects, thus creating an AST. The lexer-parser should
go through the program top to bottom. Each node in AST should represent
one command in our language, with the condition that commands that contain
other commands such as while should be represented as a node with other nodes
inside it, ie. the while command and the commands contained within the while.
Each node in the AST contains a command type, its parameters, and child
nodes for nested commands. Figure 5.11 shows an example of an AST for a
small program, moving a droplet in a while loop. The full program is the root
of the AST.
5.5.2
Type checker
The generated AST is, after being parsed, given to the type checker. The goal
of the type checker is to detect some of the most common errors in provided
==PAGE:69==

programs such as incorrect variable use, before the program is executed to pre-
vent run time errors. To achieve this goal we have come up with a set of rules
that should be checked by the type checker:
1. Whenever a variable is used it must first have been declare.
2. Whenever a droplet is declared the name provided must be free to prevent
overwritting droplets.
3. Whenever a droplet is used it must already be declared.
4. Different branches in the protocol i.e. if-else statements must result in the
same set of droplets.
5. The set of existing droplets before entering a while loop must remain
unchanged after the loop’s execution.
The type checker traverses the AST iteratively, starting with the first command.
It applies the above rules checking that droplets and variables are defined and
free to use. In the case of commands that contain other commands, we have two
approaches. If the program can run on multiple paths (if-else) the type checker
will run recursively on each branch separately and then verify that both paths
result in the same set of droplets. This check allows us to know the precise
set of droplets after the if-else without knowing what path was taken thereby
enabling us to correctly type check following code. If a part of the code can
be run multiple times (while) that part of the code must not alter the set of
existing droplets. Because the type checker runs pre execution we do not know
how many times, if any, the code should be repeated in the while loop and we
therefore only type-check the body of the while once. This checks ensure that

we prevent errors during run time relating to droplets. For example, a while
loop creating a droplet would only be able to run one time before getting an

As explained in the language definition section the goal is for our program to run
commands in parallel whenever possible. The goal of this part of the translation

mands and their relations (dependencies) to other commands. Nodes in the

5.6.2
Router
The router is responsible for calculating paths for droplets on the DMFB while
adhering to the constraints of the domain. To achieve this, the router utilizes
search algorithms to efficiently find paths that conform to the constraints.
From a path-finding perspective, our Compiler operates within the domain of
multi-agent path finding (MAPF), as multiple droplets can be moved simulta-
neously, in a single protocol. However, the domain introduces an extra level of
complexity with the presence of contamination, a concept that is not usually
considered within traditional path-finding problems. Contamination essentially
acts as dynamical walls, that can be free spaces for some droplets, but blocked
spaces for others.
Path-finding algorithms inherently involve a trade-off between runtime, memory
space, and solution quality. The Compiler operates as a JIT compiler, which is
time critical, and therefore, our primary concern is runtime. We do not consider
memory as a concern, given that the Toolchain is made to run on laboratory
PCs. We therefore prioritize efficiently finding satisfactory solutions over the
quality of the solutions found.
To find the best routing algorithm for our domain, we considered three search
algorithms: A* (A-star) search, priority-based A* search, and conflict-based
search (CBS). These algorithms were chosen since they represent unique strate-
gies for solving MAPF problems, each addressing specific trade-offs between
computational speed and solution quality. A* serves as a baseline, offering a
well-established heuristic-based approach that guarantees optimality in single-
agent scenarios and can be extended to multi-agent domains. Priority-based
A* introduces prioritization to improve computational efficiency in multi-agent
systems, making it practical for real-time applications. CBS systematically re-
solves conflicts between agents, using a combination of high-level and low-level
search, ensuring high-quality solutions even in complex environments. Many
==PAGE:75==

61
more path-finding algorithms exist, however by analysing these three path-
finding algorithms, we try to cover a variety of simple to complex strategies.
In the following, we will briefly describe the strengths and weaknesses of the
search algorithms in an ordinary setting, where we only consider droplets with
a size equal to one electrode and no contamination. We will then analyse the
algorithms in regards to our domain, and outline the choice of routing algorithm
for our Compiler. Lastly, we will describe how the algorithm is adapted to our
domain, and how we handle droplets of variable sizes.
A* Search
A* is regarded as the standard for single agent pathfinding [25], and able to
produce optimal paths given admissible and consistent heuristics [26]. However,
applying an unmodified A* algorithm in a multi-agent path finding (MAPF)
domain reveals challenges.
A* works by exploring nodes based on their evaluated heuristic1 cost, consider-
ing all possible actions at a given state. In our domain this results in up to five
actions to evaluate: up, down, left, right, and not moving. For every subsequent
agent that the A* considers it adds another layer of computational complexity,
which leads to an exponential worst-case increase in runtime.
In a single-agent domain A* will consider up to N actions for every step. How-
ever, in a multi-agent domain A* must evaluate the joint actions of all agents
in each step. For A agents, the branching factor grows to N A, meaning the
number of states expands exponentially with the number of agents [27]. Over
S steps the worst-case state expansion can be expressed as:
O((N A)S)
where
• N is the number of possible actions.
• A is the number of agents.
• S is the number of steps to be performed.
1A heuristic is a function that estimates the cost to reach the target.

While the A* search is very good at finding solutions for conflicting agents, the

based on its heuristic cost, and therefore should not reach its worst-case, it
is highly reliant on the heuristic, and the number of expanded states can still
approach the theoretical worst-case bound.

Conflict-based search
Conflict-Based Search (CBS) is an advanced modification of the A* algorithm
and builds on some of the same concepts. CBS introduces a two-layered ap-
proach to MAPF routing, consisting of a high-level and a low-level search [28].
The low-level search utilizes A* to plan paths for each agent independently,
without considering the actions of other agents. As a result, conflicts where
multiple agents occupy the same location at the same time are likely to occur.
The conflicts are handled by the high-level search, which manages constraints.
Constraints are rules that prohibit specific agents from entering certain loca-
tions at specific times. The high-level search identifies conflicts and generates
constraints. The low-level search re-plans the paths for the agents involved in
the conflicts while taking the constraints into consideration until a conflict-free
set of routes is found. An example of a conflict resolution, using constraints,
can be seen in Figure 5.16. A constraint for the conflicting cell at the time of

Chosen Search Algorithm
Theoretically in a single-agent path-finding problem, the unmodified A* algo-
rithm would be sufficient, as it balances efficiency and solution quality. However,
when considering MAPF, the exponential state space growth of A* makes it un-
suitable for our domain, which prioritizes runtime.
Priority-Based A* Search (PBS) and Conflict-Based Search (CBS) are both ded-
icated MAPF path-finding algorithms but differ in their computational speed
and quality of solution.
Priority-based A* is a modified version of A* that
routes agents sequentially. Once an agent’s path is routed, it is committed, and
subsequent agents route their paths while considering the committed routes of
earlier agents. This approach ensures conflict resolution and avoids entangle-
ment but does not guarantee optimal solution quality. By using single-agent A*
path-finding, the branching factor of PBS follows a linear growth with the num-
ber of agents to be routed. In contrast, CBS employs a more advanced search
strategy by resolving conflicts incrementally through constraint-based planning.
This allows CBS to balance efficiency and solution quality, often resulting in
more optimal paths. However, given the complexity contamination adds to our
domain, CBS must frequently re-plan paths, significantly increasing the branch-
ing factor of CBS. Due to the increasing branching factor, the runtime of CBS
grows to an extent, where CBS becomes unfeasible for a JIT compiler.
Out of the routing algorithms considered for our domain, the priority-based A*
search is the favourable algorithm. It can deliver satisfactory paths while having
a runtime that fits the needs of a JIT compiler.
The described routing algorithms have also been implemented, evaluated and
compared, in order to ensure that the theoretical arguments also held in prac-
tice. The evaluation and comparison can be found in Section 7.3.2. The practi-
cal comparisons align with the theoretical findings, and PBS still becomes the
favourable algorithm.

(PBS) has been shown to be the most suitable algorithm for MAPF in the DMFB
domain. However, some issues are introduced by the complexity of contamina-

Firstly, the priority (order) in which the agents are routed can be adjusted to

Variable sized droplets
In DMFB, droplets can vary in size, which introduces additional complexity
to the routing. Larger droplets occupy multiple electrodes and require careful
planning. To ensure minimal contamination while moving droplets of variable
sizes, and ensure determinism in the movement, we choose to route the droplets
in a snake-like routing pattern. Snake-like routing utilizes a head, body and
tail, to move droplets in a single line. This is achieved by moving the head
and letting the body and tail occupy the previous positions of the head. The
body can be constructed of multiple nodes, enabling the snake configuration to
encapsulate larger droplets.
In Figure 5.18 a snake-like droplet can be observed. The droplet spans four
electrodes, however, by moving the droplet in a snake-like pattern, it only uses
a one-wide electrode path, thereby producing the same amount of contamination

The router utilizes Priority-based A* search (PBS) to balance computational
efficiency and domain-specific constraints.
PBS’s sequential routing handles

exhaustion, and snake-like routing for variable-sized droplets, address solutions

These adaptations ensure robustness by enabling PBS to find valid solutions
whenever possible, making it a reliable and adaptable choice for the DMFB
domain.
5.6.3
Scheduler
The purpose of the Scheduler is to find the optimal positioning for complex
actions to take place while ensuring that other droplets are not affected by
the action and that the action is confined to the restrictions of contamination.
==PAGE:83==
5.6 Application Layer
69
Complex actions involve multiple droplets and/or result in the creation or con-
sumption of droplets, such as the actions of Merge and Split.
An optimal position for a complex action is a position that adheres to the
following restrictions:
1. Does not overlap with other droplets.
2. Conform with the contamination.
These restrictions are called the applicable restrictions, and a position that
passes these restrictions is denoted as an applicable position.
Since multiple positions can be applicable we rank all the possible applicable
positions by a secondary set of rules, such that we can determine the most
appropriate position. The ranking criteria prioritize positions that:
1. Create the least contamination on the DMFB.
2. Requires minimal movement for the droplets, based on their current posi-
tions.
By evaluating positions against these restrictions and criteria, the Scheduler is
able to determine the optimal position that a complex action should be carried
out. Once an optimal position is selected, the Router handles droplet move-
ments, and templates are applied to execute the action.
In Figure 5.20 an example of a merge between a blue and red droplet, resulting
in a purple droplet, can be seen. Prior to the merge, the position of the blue, red
and purple droplets are known, but the position where the merge takes place
is dictated by the Scheduler. This position requires the least movement for the
blue and red droplets, while also creating the least amount of contamination.
The calculation resulting in this position can be seen in the implementation
Section 6.5.3, Figure 6.12.
In summary, the Scheduler determines the optimal position for a complex ac-
tion by applying a set of restrictions and ranking applicable positions based on
predefined criteria.

5.6.4
Orchestrator
The orchestrator is the central controller of the application layer.
Its main
responsibility is to extract executable commands from the dependency graph,
manage their scheduling and routing, and transform them into low-level elec-
trode actions for the DFMB. By handling the dependency graph, evaluating
expressions, and delegating tasks to the scheduler and router, the orchestrator
ensures that high-level protocol commands are executed efficiently.
Command Execution Flow
The orchestrator executes commands in the following steps:
1. The orchestrator queries the dependency graph to identify commands
whose dependencies have been satisfied. These commands are ready to
be executed.
2. Before execution, the orchestrator evaluates all expressions in the com-
mands. For example, in a Move command, the target coordinates might
depend on arithmetic operations involving variables. Expressions are eval-
uated just-in-time when the command becomes executable to ensure that
variable dependencies are correctly resolved.
3. Lastly the commands are processed into low-level board actions. For this
task, the orchestrator might delegate scheduling tasks to the scheduler to
optimize droplet positions for complex operations. and invoke the router
to compute safe and contamination-free paths for the droplets.
==PAGE:85==
5.6 Application Layer
71
Command Handling
The orchestrator categorizes commands into three types, each handled differ-
ently:
• Commands that do not alter droplets: These commands are in-
dependent of droplet movement and include: Variable Assignments and
Conditions. Because these commands do not involve droplets and are re-
solved through dependency analysis, they are executed immediately when
ready, furthermore, they do not directly generate any low-level electrode
actions to be executed on the DFMB. Examples of this can be assignments
of variables or conditional statements such as if.
• Commands requiring scheduling: Some commands require the orches-
trator to interact with the scheduler to determine their optimal positions.
Merge and Split. The scheduler computes the most efficient position for
the action.
if droplets need to be moved to the optimal position, the
router handles the routing. Once droplets are in position, the orchestra-
tor applies a template to translate the high-level command into low-level
electrode actions for the DMFB. Examples of this type of command are
merge and split as the Compiler needs to find a position to perform the
action.
• Commands that move droplets: Droplet movements are delegated to
the router, which computes optimal paths and generates low-level actions
based on templates to ensure droplets reach their goals efficiently while
avoiding contamination. Examples of this command type are the simple
move, but also split and merge since they once scheduled might need to
be moved to a specific position.
Dynamic Routing and Partial Execution
When the orchestrator identifies commands involving droplet movement, such
as Move, Merge, or Split, it delegates their routing to the Router. The router
computes contamination-free paths to move all droplets to their respective goal
positions. However, the orchestrator does not execute the entire computed path
in one go; instead, it processes the routes incrementally to maximize execution
accuracy and efficiency.
The orchestrator begins by sending all droplets that require movement to the
router, which computes their full paths. Once the paths are returned, the orches-
trator executes the routes only up to the point where the first droplet reaches its
==PAGE:86==
72
Design
goal or a stored droplet is freed. At this milestone, the orchestrator updates the
dependency graph to reflect the newly freed commands triggered by the com-
pleted droplet movement, or freed droplet. The orchestrator then reevaluates
the graph to extract the next set of executable commands and reroutes droplets
accordingly.
This strategy has two key benefits:
• Accurate routes and scheduling: By recomputing routes dynamically
after each milestone, the orchestrator ensures that the chosen paths and
scheduled positions are as optimal as possible, given the current state of
the board and dependency graph.
• Avoiding conflicts: Routing droplets to their full goals from the out-
set (even if only a portion is executed) allows the orchestrator to avoid
decisions that might inadvertently block other droplets. This approach
balances foresight (full-path computation) with adaptability (partial exe-
cution).
While this method may result in some droplets being routed and scheduled
multiple times, the trade-off is justified: incremental routing ensures that the
orchestrator continuously adapts to changes in the board state, improving over-
all execution efficiency and preventing potential conflicts between routes.
Variable Size Droplets
Operations can be performed on both smaller and larger droplets. For actions
like merges, dedicated templates are used for specific droplet sizes, and the
appropriate template is selected. For droplet movements, as discussed in the
router, droplets follow a snake-like pattern to minimize contamination. Moving
droplets in this way requires logic to convert, for example, a 3x3 droplet into
a 1x9 snake. This process, known as unravel and ravel, is used to transform
droplets into and out of snake form.
Orchestrator Internal State
The orchestrator maintains an internal state of the board, including the position
of droplets, time and what is contaminated.
==PAGE:87==
5.7 Communication Layer
73
Droplet Positions represent the current locations of all droplets on the digital
microfluidic biochip (DMFB). The orchestrator continuously updates these posi-
tions as droplets move or complete scheduled commands. This tracking ensures
accurate routing, verifies droplet readiness for scheduled actions like Merge or
Split, and determines when a droplet unlocks new commands in the dependency
graph.
The Contamination Map reflects the areas on the board that are contaminated
due to droplet activity.
It is updated dynamically whenever droplets move,
merge, split, or are removed. By referencing the contamination map, the or-

unsafe overlaps or invalid droplet positions. It also validates commands that
declare new droplets to confirm that they are placed in clean positions.

5.7.1
Communication Types
The Communication Layer should support two types of communication, one-way
and two-way communication. The main communication type is one-way com-
munication, as it is used to send electrode activation from the Compiler to the
client. The two-way communication provides a Request-Response model, where
the Compiler can request information from the client, and expect a response.
• Send: Sends data over the communication channel without expecting any
acknowledgement (one-way).
• Request-Response: Sends a data request over the communication chan-
nel, expecting a response from the client (two-way).
The Toolchain does not inherently know the intricate bio-dynamics of droplets,
which makes two-way communication essential. The two-way communication
allows users to design informed protocols, which are protocols where conditionals
depend on the physical properties of droplets. For example, a sensor reading
from a colour sensor might be used to determine the path of a droplet, based
on the actual colour of the droplet.
5.7.2
Communication Services
Communication between each client is handled by a dedicated communication
service, allowing for the use of specific communication protocols and electrode
translations for each client. A Communication Engine is introduced as a man-
aging instance, ensuring that the client communication is handled seamlessly,
across different clients.
The following communication services are supported
by the Toolchain and provide the communication capabilities for two distinct
clients.
• Simulation Communication Service: Facilitates communication with
the Simulation Framework, enabling efficient testing and execution with-
out requiring external physical systems.

Communication Engine The Communication Engine is the main entry point

Simulation Communication Service
The Simulation Communication Ser-

The Physical Communication Service
(PCS) enables hardware-specific communication with the DMFB developed by

carried out.
The PCS requires access to the physical DMFB system, which
should be set up prior to establishing the communication. The DMFB unfor-
tunately does not support two-way communication, and can not respond to
requests. This limits the possible protocols, such that informed protocols are
not supported, yet simpler protocols, where no external information is required
for the protocol to run, can still be executed.

The contamination and merge table defines how droplets interact with each

Translation Layer
The translation layer transforms the protocol into an executable dependency
graph. The translation layer goes through three steps. First reading the input
language and transforming it into an AST in the parser/lexer. Secondly, the
typechecker validates that the protocol provided by the user is sound.
And
lastly, transform the AST into an executable dependency graph ready to be
used by the application layer.
6.4.1
Parser and Lexer
The first step in compiling a program is parsing its content into an internal
representation, often called an Abstract Syntax Tree (AST). For this task, we
use ANTLR (ANother Tool for Language Recognition) [15], a generator that
reads grammar descriptions and automatically produces lexers, parsers, and
ASTs. This allows us to bypass the manual work of lexing and parsing, focusing
instead on program-specific logic. With ANTLR, we define a grammar that it
uses to generate an AST, which we can then traverse to interpret commands
within our program efficiently.
==PAGE:116==
102
Implementation
Grammar Structure
The grammar for the microfluidics language is structured to support a range
of commands for droplet manipulation, conditional logic, loops, and hardware
interfacing. This section provides an overview of the key components within the
grammar, highlighting the main rules and structures.
Grammar representations
At the top level, a program is defined as a sequence of commands, each termi-
nated by a semicolon. This structure is represented by the following rule:
program
: (command ';')* EOF
;
This allows the parser to recognize programs consisting of multiple commands,
which can be droplet declarations, movements, conditional statements, and
more.
The core of the language consists of individual commands, each performing
specific actions.
Commands are grouped under the command rule, as shown
below:
command
: dropletDeclaration
| dispense
| moveDroplet
| split
...
| waste
;
This structure allows the parser to handle a wide range of commands, enabling
both droplet control and interaction with the microfluidic hardware.
Droplet-related commands, such as declarations and movements, allow fine con-
trol over the droplets in the system. Below are two examples:

Internal representation of commands
After ANTLR parses a program into an AST, we can traverse the tree using
ANTLR’s built-in walk function. ANTLR provides a BaseListener, which in-

These methods are
called automatically as the parser enters and exits each rule during the AST
traversal, allowing custom behaviour to be added at specific points in the tree.
To read all commands into the classes that represent them, outlined in section

ANTLR. The custom listener overrides the exit methods, allowing us to capture
and process each command in our language. Our custom listener creates a list
of commands that is filled during the traversal of the AST as each is created
as an ICommand. This list of commands is our internal representation of the
AST that can be type checked and made into a dependency graph. In the code

6.4.3
Dependency Graph
When executing a protocol on a microfluidic biochip, it is important to perform
operations in parallel whenever possible. This parallelization accelerates execu-
==PAGE:121==
6.4 Translation Layer
107
tion and minimizes the time droplets spend idle when no constraints prevent
them from proceeding.
Dependency Graph Structure
As discussed in the design Section 5.5.3, to determine which commands in the
protocol can be executed concurrently, we introduce a dependency graph. This
graph is a directed graph where nodes represent individual commands, and edges
represent dependencies between those commands. Specifically, a node points to
another node if it depends on that node’s execution.
Each node in the graph contains the following fields:
• Command: The protocol command the node represents.
• IsExecuted: A boolean flag indicating whether the node’s command has
been executed.
• Dependencies: A list of nodes this node is dependent on.
These fields enable the graph to keep track of command execution status and
ensure that commands are only executed when all their prerequisites are met.
This design supports efficient parallel execution by identifying independent com-
mands.
Nodes are categorized into three distinct types:
• Standard Dependency Nodes: Represent regular commands and vari-
able assignments.
• While Loop Nodes: Contain an internal dependency graph for the com-
mands inside the loop.
• If Statement Nodes: Contain one or two internal dependency graphs,
depending on whether the if-statement has an else clause.
Categorizing nodes ensures that the graph can represent both simple commands
and more complex control structures like loops and conditionals. This distinc-
tion allows the Compiler to correctly evaluate dependencies within nested con-
structs, ensuring accurate execution order.
==PAGE:122==
108
Implementation
Node Methods and Execution Readiness
All nodes share the following key methods:
• GetExecutableNodes: Returns all nodes that are ready for execution. In
a standard node, this method returns the node itself if it is ready. For
nodes representing loops or if-statements, it returns nodes from the inter-
nal dependency graph that are ready for execution.
• MarkAsExecuted: Marks the node as executed.
• AddDependency: Adds a dependency to the node, used when constructing
the graph.
The dependency graph serves primarily as a container for nodes, and the nodes
themselves manage their dependencies. The graph’s most important function
is GetExecutableNodes, which returns nodes that are ready for execution by
ensuring all their dependencies have been resolved.
Handling Conditional and Loop Constructs
The dependency graph is capable of handling loops and conditionals by recur-
sively checking dependencies within them. This is achieved by encapsulating
internal dependency graphs within the while loop and if-statement nodes.
For example:
• While Loops: Nodes representing while loops contain their own internal
dependency graphs. The outer node checks the entire loop body, ensuring
it only executes when all internal dependencies are satisfied.
• If Statements: If-statement nodes may contain one or two internal de-
pendency graphs (for the if and else branches). Both branches are checked
independently, and external nodes that depend on the result of the if state-
ment will depend on both branches.
Consider a protocol where a droplet is repeatedly mixed until a specific con-
centration is reached. The dependency graph ensures that the mixing step is
re-evaluated after each iteration by representing the loop body as an internal
subgraph.

Creating the Dependency Graph
The process of building the dependency graph starts by processing a list of
commands parsed from the protocol. For each command, a node is created and
added to the graph without any initial dependencies.
Once the nodes are created, the list is traversed to establish dependencies:
1. For the first node, no dependencies exist.
2. For the second node, dependencies are checked against the first.
3. For the third node, dependencies are checked against both the first and
second nodes.
4. This process continues for each subsequent node, ensuring that all depen-
dencies are correctly assigned.
This stepwise approach guarantees that dependencies are correctly assigned in
the order commands are encountered, ensuring that commands dependent on
earlier results are correctly sequenced.
Checking Dependencies
Dependencies between two nodes are identified by comparing the variables or
droplets they access or modify. Each command implements the following meth-
ods to assist in this process:
• GetInputVariables: Returns the names of all variables and droplets re-
quired by the command.
• GetOutputVariables: Returns the names of all variables and droplets
modified by the command.
The system checks for overlap between a node’s GetOutputVariables and an-
other node’s GetInputVariables to establish dependencies. We do not check for
overlap between GetInputVariables and GetInputVariables, as using a variable
without modifying it does not introduce a dependency.

First, PBS generates possible move combinations (permutations), which is sorted
by prioritizing moves that result in a lower contamination factor. It then re-
serves contamination for the goals of the moves, in a reserved contamination
map (RCM), which ensures that contaminating agents do not route over the
goals, causing subsequent moves to become unroutable. PBS then iterates over
each of the permutations (P), and each move in the permutation. PBS uses a
single-agent A* search to route the agents while taking previous contamination,
other agents, reserved contamination, and the previously committed states into
consideration. If a resulting route is not found for a move in the permutation, the
remaining moves in the permutation are skipped in order to minimize the com-

putational complexity. If a resulting route is found, it is committed by adding
the route to the committed states (CS). After iterating through all moves in a
permutation, we check to see if the permutation is marked as a solution, if so
we return the solution without iterating on any further permutations, otherwise
we continue to explore permutations. If no solution has been found through all
the permutations we return an error, marking the moves as unsolvable. In this
sense the PBS uses permutations greedily, accepting the first found solution,
instead of exploring the entire set of permutations and choosing the best one.
Generating permutations
The first step in PBS is to generate permutations of the moves to explore dif-
ferent routing orders. This step can become computationally expensive as the
number of permutations expands factorially with the number of moves (n!),
where n is the total number of moves in the permutation. As an example, if we
were to route 5 moves, thereby 5 agents, simultaneously, the number of permuta-
tions are 5! = 120, and were we to increase the number to 10 moves, the amount
of permutations increases to 10! = 3.628.800. Effectively managing the rapid
expansion of permutations is critical, in order to minimize the computational
strain on PBS.
To handle the factorial growth, PBS utilizes lazy evaluation, which dynamically
generates permutations as they are needed, instead of generating all permuta-
tions upfront. The worst case for the permutations remains O(n!), however, by
lazy evaluating the permutations, it allows the algorithm to exit early, when-
ever it finds a permutation that results in a solution to the routing problem. By
employing lazy evaluation we reduce unnecessary computation and improve the
efficiency of PBS.
Lazy evaluation is achieved by utilizing a combination of IEnumerable and LINQ
in C#. By using IEnumerable permutations aren’t stored in memory, but gener-
ated as needed, when the sequence is enumerated. LINQ methods like Select,
Where and SelectMany allow PBS to define how permutations are generated.
These LINQ methods operate lazily, meaning that they only compute when ex-
plicitly requested, such as during the iteration in a foreach loop. As an example,
if PBS finds a solution permutation in the first couple of permutations, the lazy
evaluation ensures that the remaining permutations are not computed. This
approach conserved memory and reduces the computational load, by skipping
unnecessary computations. The algorithm for generating permutations can be
seen in Code Snippet 6.31.

Low-level Search
To search the individual paths we utilize A* search, as the search is a single agent
path-finding problem. The A* algorithm is well-known and explained in various
sources [25][31][32]. Therefore, we will not explain the detailed implementation
of A*, but instead, explain the heuristic and optimizations we have made to the
core algorithm.
In order to understand the details below, we will quickly cover some general
information. A* works by expanding states based on an f-value f(n) assigned
to each state, favouring lower f-values.
It uses a frontier to hold each state
and ranks the states using a priority queue. The f-value is the addition of the
heuristic h(n) and cost to reach the node g(n), where n is the current node.
The f-value is defined as:
f(n) = h(n) + g(n)
The f-value explains the accumulated cost to reach the goal, based on the esti-
mated cost to reach the goal h(n) and the cost so far g(n).

The first step in the heuristic calculation is to calculate the Manhattan Distance

In order to understand if the heuristic is good, we must analyze if it is admissible
and consistent. An admissible heuristic is a heuristic that never overestimates
the true cost of reaching the goal h∗(n). Formally this can be defined as:
h(n) ≤h∗(n),
∀n
where

Implementation
• n: The current node.
• h(n): The heuristic estimate to reach the goal form node n.
• h∗(n): The actual cost to reach the goal from node n.
Since the only increasing factor in our heuristics is the Manhattan Distance,
which is an admissible function [35], our heuristics adheres to the requirements
of admissibility and will not overestimate the true cost to the goal.
A consistent heuristic is a heuristic where the estimated cost h(n) of reaching
the goal from any node n is always less than or equal to the cost of moving to
a successor node n′ plus the heuristic estimate of the successor node h(n′). For
all nodes n and their successor nodes n′, the following must hold:
h(n) ≤c(n, n′) + h(n′),
∀n, n′
where
• c(n, n′): The cost of moving from n to n′.
The domain for which we route the agents has a constant cost to move to an
adjacent node of 1, meaning that the cost to move from n to n′ will always
be one, given that the successor node n′ is any node reachable by an action,
which is always the direct adjacent nodes in four directions. Given this formal
definition of consistency and the uniform cost of movement being 1, the heuristic
ensures consistent f-values along any path in the search.
Optimizations
Optimization 1
To ensure that we efficiently calculate paths without ex-
panding unnecessary nodes, we introduce tie-breaking into our A* algorithm.
Tie-breaking ensures that if two f-values are the same, the algorithm chooses
the next state based on an informed decision. We use a lexicographical priority
order[36] to break ties based on the cost to reach the node g(n). To implement
this, we introduce tuple-based sorting to our priority queue, this allows us to
first sort all states based on f(g), and subsequently sorted by g(n). We will
favour higher values of g(n), since the agents in these states have traversed a
larger amount of nodes, and will therefore likely be closer to the goal.
==PAGE:139==
6.5 Application Layer
125
As an example, if we were to find two states with the values:
1)
f(n) = 6, g(n) = 1
2)
f(n) = 6, g(n) = 5
We would select the second, since the g(n) value is higher, meaning that the
agent has traversed 5 nodes, whereas the first has only traversed a single node.
From this we can also deduct that the h(n) value is 5 for the first and 1 for the
second, meaning that the estimated cost to reach the goal for the second state
is far better than for the first.
Optimization 2

track of all explored states through a HashSet<State>. This allows for each

6.5.3
Scheduler
In Section 5.6.3, we outlined the overall design of the Scheduler.
The main
purpose of the Scheduler is to determine suitable positions for executing complex
commands while adhering to the defined restrictions and criteria. In this section,
we detail the implementation and practical application of the scheduler.
Commands
The Scheduler is only responsible for finding the position of complex commands,
which are commands where the position cannot directly be determined by the
language definition. Complex commands include the following commands:
• Merge: Defines the resulting position of the merged droplet.
• Split: Defines the resulting positions of the split droplets.
Both of these commands only include positioning related to the end positions.
The position where the actual action is executed is to be found by the Scheduler.
==PAGE:141==
6.5 Application Layer
127
Finding Optimal Positions
To determine the optimal positions for executing complex commands, the Sched-
uler uses a Breadth-First Search (BFS) approach. BFS systematically explores
all nodes in the search space, with a complexity of O(N), where N is the total
amount of nodes. To minimize the computational overhead, the search is re-
stricted to a dynamically calculated bounding box, defined by the minimum and
maximum coordinates of the droplets involved in the command. This restriction
reduces the number of nodes that need to be traversed by BFS. For each node
within the bounding box, the following steps are performed:
1. Validation: Validate that the node adheres to restrictions, such as con-
tamination conflicts and overlap with other droplets.
2. Template Selection: Evaluate the optimal template for the action from
a list of valid templates. The template will define how the action is carried
out.
3. Template Validation: Validate the chosen template against the plat-
form constraints, such as contamination, and overlapping other droplets.
4. Scoring: Assign a score to the node based on minimizing droplet move-
ment and reducing contamination.
If no node is found to be valid within the bounding box, we dynamically expand
the bounding box, until a valid node is found or the bounds are equal to the
platform and no valid node is found.
In the following sections, we will detail the steps involved in finding the optimal
position.
Validation
To validate a node, we use the contamination service to check if the contami-
nation at the position of the node does not conflict with the substanceIds of
the droplets related to the complex command. If there is a conflict, the node is
skipped and the evaluation continues with the next node. If the node is valid,
we continue with Template Selection.
==PAGE:142==
128
Implementation
Template Selection
The Scheduler selects the best template from a list of valid templates provided
by the Orchestrator. These templates are defined by the criteria in Section 5.2.6,
and include variations for the same action (e.g., horizontal and vertical merge
templates).
To find the optimal template, each of the valid templates are scored based
on the Manhattan Distance from the agents current positions to the initial
positions in the templates. The template that provides the best score is chosen
and information about the position and template is stored in a class called
ScheduledPosition. The stored information is:
• The position of droplet 1
• The position of droplet 2
• The position of droplet 3
• The chosen template
Based on the complex command that is scheduled, the droplet positions relate
to specific droplets in the action. For a merge droplet 1 and droplet 2 relate to
the initial positions of the two droplets that should merge, which is extracted
from the template. Droplet 3 relates to the position of the output droplet, that
is created by the merge. For a split command, droplet 3 relates to the initial
position of the input droplet, droplets 1 and 2 relate to the positions of the
droplets created by the split.

6.5.4

The orchestrator serves as the central controller of the application layer, man-
aging the dynamic execution of high-level protocol commands. It extracts exe-
cutable commands from the dependency graph, evaluates expressions, and del-
egates tasks to the scheduler and router. By transforming high-level operations
into low-level electrode actions, the orchestrator ensures efficient execution of
commands on the digital microfluidic biochip (DMFB).

The main entry point for the orchestrator is the Execute method, which orches-
trates the entire execution process. The execution loop continuously queries
the dependency graph for executable commands, evaluates them, and delegates
tasks such as scheduling, routing, and low-level command execution(template
execution).

Applying templates
If a command involves an action that directly moves, splits or merges a droplet,
the orchestrator must find a relevant template to perform the action. When
applying a template the orchestrator selects a template based on the action
type and the size of the droplet. Applying the template itself transforming the
action into BoardAction, the internal representation of electrode activations, the
ITemplate has a function Apply. The Apply takes the position of the droplet that
is being altered and a time when the action should start. The Apply function
can then transform the actions within the template to the specific position and
time requested by the orchestrator. This ensures a simple and accessible way for
the orchestrator to move, split and merge droplets by only providing a position
and a time.
Extraction sub routes
Droplet movements, whether direct (e.g., in a Move command) or indirect (e.g.,
positioning droplets for a Merge operation), are delegated to the RouterSer-
vice. The router computes complete contamination-free routes for all droplets
requiring movement. However, instead of routing each droplet individually as
commands are handled, all droplets needing movement are collected first. After
all commands have been processed by their respective handlers, the router is in-
voked to compute routes for all droplets simultaneously. This approach ensures
that routing is optimized for parallel execution, even though command handling
occurs sequentially.

complete independently of droplet movements, as they become executable at
specific times. To address this, the orchestrator employs an extraction function
to translate only a portion of the total route into low-level DFMB actions.
The extraction function operates by identifying the point at which a new com-
mand becomes free for execution. It achieves this through a two-step process:
• Determining the Maximum Time Step: If certain commands (e.g., Store)

• Traversing Routes: The extractor processes the routes generated by the

maximum time step.
This approach ensures that the orchestrator is left with a series of routes, min-

soon as their dependencies are resolved.

Instantiating WebSocket Communication
The SCS is set up to act as the server in the WebSocket communication protocol.
The Simulation Framework is then able to establish a connection through the
local network to the SCS. To open the communication SCS initializes an instance
of WebSocketService, which handles WebSocket connection and transmission of
data over the TCP connection, on a separate thread. Running the WebSocket
on a separate thread allows it to operate in parallel with the rest of the system,
ensuring that it does not block the Compilers execution. This essentially allows
the Compiler to continue the compilation of the next set of commands, while
the SCS translates and sends the actions to the Simulation Framework.
Synchronization
The Simulation Frameworks internal time is not necessarily bound to real-time
and can proceed faster than the Compiler time. Therefore, there is a need for
synchronization between the Simulation and the Compiler, to achieve this the
SendTimeRequest is used to request the Simulation time.
This procedure is
repeated before each action is sent through the SCS, ensuring that the timing
of the actions aligns with the Simulator.
Sending instructions
When a Send instruction is triggered the SCS processes the list of BoardActions
extracting information, translating the BoardActions into a queue of DTOs,
which is then wrapped in a WebSocketMessage. The WebSocketMessage can be

Flow of the Simulation Communication Service
To illustrate the flow of the SCS, Figure 6.19 showcases a sequence diagram of

receives a trigger to start the SCS, this trigger is raised when the user chooses

Translating Actions
The DMFB is natively built to use BioAssembly instructions, introduced in
Section 2.2.1, to execute actions. Therefore, actions eligible for execution by
the DMFB need to align with the commands specified by the BioAssembly
instruction set.
Actions from the Compiler need to be translated into the platform instructions
SETEL for electrode activation and CLREL for electrode deactivation, both of these
instructions follow a similar format.
<INS> <DVR> <EL#> [EL#] [EL#] [EL#] [EL#] [EL#] [EL#] [EL#]
Where,
• <INS>: The instruction, either SETEL or CLREL.
• <DVR>: The driver id, either 0 or 1.
• <EL#>: The electrode id, for which the action is executed.
• [EL#]: Additional optional electrode IDs, up to 7 additional electrodes.
In order to translate the actions into the required format, we have to extract the
related information. The instruction can be inferred from the electrode state in
the action. If the state of the action is 1 it is identified as an activation and
the corresponding instruction is a SETEL, if the state is 0 it is identified as a
deactivation, and the instruction becomes a CLREL.
On the DMFB the board is split into two sections, each controlled by an in-
dependent driver, the driver ID is used to determine which of the drivers the
action belongs to. The electrodes in a single instruction must all belong to the
same driver. To find the driver id the platform information file, which is pro-
vided by the user, is used to look up the information for the electrode that the
action revolves. Through the platform repository, we can access the platform
information, and extract the driver ID related to the electrode.
We then group the actions that have the same state, driver and should be
executed at the same time.
From the grouping, we can then construct the
platform instructions that are ready for execution, and send them to the DMFB.

Sending Actions
Prior to sending the translated platform instructions to the DMFB, it is neces-
sary to send some pre-execution instructions, that ensure the DMFB is ready
for execution when the action instructions are sent.
To prep the DMFB we first signal for it to turn on high-voltage, which is used
in the electrode activations and deactivations. The instruction SHV 1 280 is sent
to set the high-voltage value, and HVPOE 1 1 is used to signal the DMFB to turn
on high voltage. Secondly, the instruction CLRA <DVR> is sent to both drivers,
which ensures that all electrodes are deactivated before execution begins.
The DMFB is now ready to receive the translated electrode instructions and
execute them. The DMFB executes instructions as they are received, so the
Compiler has to keep track of the time, to ensure that commands are executed
correctly. To keep track of time and send instructions at the correct time, the
PCS has to wait between sending the instructions for the time specified by the
action. Waiting is a thread-blocking process, meaning that the Compiler will
not be able to process any further commands, while it is in the process of sending
the current batch of instructions. To make the process non-blocking, the PCS
is created on a separate thread allowing the separate thread to wait, while the
main thread can continue the compilation of the next set of commands.

7.4
Bounds and Scalability
As our Solution is designed for deployment on platforms beyond the one used
at DTU, it should also be capable of functioning on other platforms. We will in
this subsection explore the performance bounds of the Solution.
First, it is noteworthy that the platform being developed with a programmable
area with a total of 640 electrodes, is one of the largest being developed. Keeping
this in mind the 7 droplets criss-cross scenario is one of the most complex sce-
narios that can be created on this board. This is because computing an optimal
path for droplets is the most computationally heavy part of our Compiler, and
it is especially hard when droplets conflict. The 7 droplets crisscross showcase
this difficult scenario and also show that our Compiler is indeed able to compile
it within a reasonable time. If our Solution is to be deployed on larger platforms
we do not think this would necessarily pose problems for a few reasons.
First of all, as our Compiler is a JIT compiler it can compile while executing.
This means that once the Compiler has compiled the first set of commands it
can start computing the next set of commands while executing the result of the
first set on the biochip. This means that if the Compiler does encounter a very
complex scenario chances are that the Compiler is ahead and the extra time it
takes to compute will not affect the execution of the program.
Secondly, our Solution has no problems with many droplets. To show this we
created two scenarios where we tried to fill the board with as many droplets
as possible. The first test scenario includes 45 droplets that have to move 4
electrodes to the side the code for this scenario can be found in the Appendix

Our Solution introduces new features such as full contamination handling, al-

sizes, applying the strategy of routing droplets as snakes to ensure minimal
contamination on the biochip. The language introduces the concept of control
flow, which allows the user to create protocols with a high level of complexity.
Control flow works strongly with the addition of sensor readings to create a
reactive protocol based on real-life measurements. Apart from sensors, the lan-
guage also integrates communication with actuators and dispensers connected
to the DMFB. Through the use of a configuration folder, the Solution can easily

8.2
Language design principles
In the Language design chapter, we introduced a series of design principles
outlined in Section 4.1. In the following, we will briefly explain how each of the
design principles was realized through the development of the domain-specific
language.
• Domain semantics: Through commands that are directly mapped to
physical operation on the biochip, the DSL encapsulates the semantics of
the domain. Commands such as Move, Mix, and Sense are abstractions of
real-world microfluidic actions.
• Structure: The DSL uses a function-based structure for the commands
with naming conventions mounted in the domain semantics, ensuring clar-
ity and familiarity, for both programmers and domain experts.
• Readability and simplicity: Clear command names and minimal syn-
tax make the DSL accessible to non-programmers while retaining func-
tionality for complex protocols.
• Real-time: By enabling multiple commands to execute simultaneously,
such as moving a droplet while mixing another droplet, the DSL enables
the support of parallelism. Synchronization commands, such as Wait, are
introduced to allow users to manage the execution order.
• Statically typed: The DSL enforces static typing by requiring types to
be explicitly declared or inferred during compilation, preventing runtime
type errors.
• Limit types: The DSL simplifies types by consolidating numerical types
into a single Num type. This aligns with the discrete grid system of the
DMFB while maintaining precision for operations.

• Scaleable with protocols: By introducing the control flow constructs
IfStatement and WhileLoop the user can express complex and dynamic
protocols. This allows the DSL to reach real-time events, such as sensor
feedback.
Through these principles, we believe that our DSL offers a powerful and user-
friendly language, that can be used to design and execute complex protocols.

8.4
Limitations
The if and while loops provide new potential for users using our Solution, but
it also has some limitations.
Due to the design of our language, we had to
implement some rules when using the if and while constructs. The rules were
put in place, in order to catch dangerous code sections prior to execution, that
would otherwise result in a runtime error. For if statements, this means that
both branches must result in the same final droplet state, such that droplet
creation and deletion cannot be conditional. For while statements, this means
that the final droplet state of the statement must be identical to the state before
the statement, such that droplet creation and deletion cannot be done in the
body of the while.
Finding the best solution to any MAPF problem is an NP-hard problem. For
our Compiler, we had to choose a routing algorithm that preferred speed over
optimality, while still finding satisfactory paths. Combining this with the nature
of a JIT compiler, we can end in a situation where we commit to routes that
might have been better if the Compiler was able to look at future movements.
This could end in a state, where a droplet is routed further than necessary,
resulting in more contamination, and further movement.

Chapter 9
Conclusion
This project describes the design and implementation of a domain-specific pro-
gramming language (DSL), compiler, execution engine, and communication pro-
tocol. The DSL is developed to allow users to create complex protocols on the
digital microfluidic biochips (DMFB), encapsulating the domain-specific actions
of digital microfluidics, in an intuitive and user-friendly way.
The usability
is supported by feedback from a researcher, who found the DSL logical and
straightforward. To support the DSL we have created a Toolchain that trans-
lates, type checks, parallelizes, schedules, routes, and sends to the DMFB.
Through this process, the high-level protocol gets converted into low-level elec-
trode activations, that can be directly executed on the DMFB. It ensures col-
lision and contamination avoidance while creating routes for the droplets while
minimizing residual substances that can cause cross-contamination.
To reduce computational overhead the Toolchain is built as a just-in-time (JIT)
system, sending actions to the DMFB as they are resolved. By utilizing the na-
ture of JIT, we can employ dynamic sensor readings through a request-response
communication protocol, allowing the DSL to rely on real-time sensor measure-
ments, directly from the DMFB.
Modularity is introduced through a series of configurable files, that provide an
abstract representation of the internal procedures, allowing platform designers
==PAGE:194==
180
Conclusion
to modify and alter the Solution to comply with specific platforms, enabling
multi-platform support.
Through our case studies, we have shown how a complex protocol describing
polymerase chain reaction (PCR), can be expressed in our language, where
extensive use of sensor readings and actuator requests are required for thermal
cycling. Furthermore, automatic and manual testing was carried out to ensure
the robustness and efficiency of the Solution during and after development.
Through testing and case studies, both on the physical and simulated hardware,
we have shown that the DSL can encapsulate complex protocols and that the
compilation and execution provide efficient and effective results.
With this project, we have made multiple contributions to the field of DMFB.
A domain-specific language targeting domain experts and a solid compilation
Toolchain were developed to simplify droplet operations and hardware inter-
actions. A contamination-aware routing algorithm ensures time-critical path-
finding and is guided through user-defined contamination rules. Through exten-
sive configuration capabilities, the Toolchain allows for multi-platform support,
and templates provide a high-level abstraction of electrode activations. Through
real-time compilation and execution, the Toolchain enables sensor and actua-
tor control, which is essential for protocols such as PCR. These contributions
enhance the usability, flexibility, and functionality of DMFB systems.

Future work
In this section, we will describe ideas that we would have liked to work on, if
given more time for this project, as well as areas for future research.
• Arrays: To allow creations and deletions of droplets within conditional
statements. We suggest the implementation of arrays of droplets. Similar
to other programming languages these arrays could be manipulated and
accessed both inside and outside conditionals.
• Functions: For creations of very large protocols the possibility of declar-

• Inferred positions: Most commands in our language require the user
to specify exact positions. A possible addition would be the ability to
overload some commands to be position-free, and then the Compiler could
schedule positions for the command.
For example, a merge command
requires the final position of the droplet, but it could be implemented
such that the Compiler will select a suitable final position.

ical user interface (GUI) could be developed, where users could seamlessly
configure, choose, and run their protocols. To allow users of the Solution
to test their protocols, before running them on the physical or simulated
