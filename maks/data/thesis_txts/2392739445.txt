ABSTRACT 
The thermoelectric (TE) effect defines the Seebeck voltage 𝑉𝑆= 𝑆Δ𝑇 generated as a result of a 
temperature difference Δ𝑇 across a conducting material with performance described by the 
figure-of-merit 𝑧𝑇= 𝜎𝑆2/𝜅, 𝑆 being Seebeck coefficient, 𝜎 electrical and 𝜅 thermal 
conductivities. State-of-the-art n- and p-type semiconductor materials are established as a 
unicouple with the goal of reaching high power outputs, 𝑃= 𝐼𝑉, long-term stability under 
thermal load and high efficiency, 𝜂> 10 %. High efficiency of 𝜂= 11.3 % but also high p-type 
degradation rate at 4 % over 10 hr under thermal load has been reported in earlier work, and a 
new promising p-type is thus being tested. 
The n-type skutterudite (nSKD) (Mm, Sm)0.14Co4Sb12, where Mm is a misc. metal consisting of 
La, Ce, Nd and Pr, and p-type half-Heusler (pHH) FeNb0.88Hf0.12Sb alloy are used, both spark 
plasma sintered from prepared powder and undergone long-term module testing. Both were 
given a Cr diffusion barrier coating and joined by brazing onto Ag electrodes, nSKD with an 
Alu-Flo™ Al-Si brazing filler. Different brazing temperatures of 500 ℃, 530 ℃ and 550 ℃ and 
2.5 μm diffusion barriers of Mo and Cr were separately tested on nSKD.  
TE properties were measured to be close to reported values from literature but performances 
stayed lower, most significantly for pHH due to high 𝜅 and an 𝑆 deviating from literature at 
higher temperatures above 𝑇> 500 ℃. The Al-Si Alu-Flo™ brazing filler was found to cause an 
interphase Ag-Al reaction layer showing violent growth when kept over time at 𝑇𝐻= 500 ℃ as 
well as 10 μm Si grain concentration near the TE bulk. nSKD showed macro-cracks after 96 hr 
testing.  
2.5 μm Mo and Cr diffusion barriers are found to be well-working and Cr to be stable over long 
term 156 hr testing at 𝑇𝐻= 500 ℃ on pHH. Microcracks are found after 96 hr testing but might 
not be directly linked. The combined unicouple is found stable over 48 hr at 𝑇𝐻= 500 ℃, with 
the output power reaching a constant 0.14 W giving 𝐼= 1.4 A and an open circuit (Seebeck) 
voltage around 𝑉= 125 mV. A calculated ideal unicouple efficiency of 9.4 % is achieved. 
The summarized result of this work is a high-output, high-efficiency, stable, non-degrading 
unicouple based on state-of-the-art-materials, with possible weakening of the nSKD over time 
but well-working and stable pHH.

1. INTRODUCTION 
Functional materials are defined as those having a function other than purely structural[5,6] such 
as energy absorbing materials in car fronts, electrically conducting wires and optically focusing 
glass in lenses. Functional energy materials envelope energy storage, conversion, utilization and

alike, such as battery materials, catalysers, ionic conductors and fuel cell membranes. 
Functional energy conversion materials concern materials that convert between energy forms. 
The subgroup of interest in this thesis is thermoelectric materials, which can convert thermal 
energy (from a temperature difference) directly into electrical energy (a voltage) based on the 
thermoelectric effect[7]. 
1.1. 
MOTIVATION AND THESIS OUTLINE 
Energy harvest, conversion and storage are the key topics of tomorrow's energy and industry[8]. 
The thermoelectric (TE) effect has a huge potential for waste heat recovery[3]. All it requires is 
the presence of a heating and cooling source[7,9]. It is a reversible effect, and while only its 
refrigeration was of interest previously, the rise of interest in environmentally friendly energy 
sources since the beginning of the millennia caused its electricity generation to gain interest[7]. 
A key advantage of thermoelectric devices is theoretically decades of unaided and maintenance-
free lifetime. They have no moving mechanical parts so no internal wear and tear or noise[10] 
and have high reliability[3] while being compact and lightweight[10,11]. With waste heat present 
in literally every process, natural (such as the daily cycle of heated and cooled black roof-tops 
and asphalt roads) as man-made (such as a kitchen refrigerator where energy is spent to remove 
energy from the interior), possible applications are plentiful.  
 
Figure 2 Examples of TE applications. 
Several applications exist at present. Examples in Figure 2 show waste heat recovery from a 
camping pot[12], smart watch charging from the temperature difference between skin and air[13], 
waste heat recovery from a solar panel[14] and electrical powering of a satellite in space[15,16]; not 
to mention larger-scale waste heat recovery from industrial power plants[17] as well as 
biothermal charging of heart pacemakers[16]. The motivated application of this MSc project is 
waste heat recovery from for example a car’s exhaust pipe, where up to 3/4 of the energy 
output is heat loss[18] in the temperature range of 500 −600 ℃[19]. TE devices can reduce fuel 
consumption and outlet gasses by continuously charging the vehicle’s accumulator (Figure 3)[17]. 
This has shown to increase the overall car efficiency by 3 % as reported by Altstedde et al, 2014

DTU Energy has recently successfully developed a TE module using skutterudite materials with 
a maximum efficiency of 11.3 % with the hot side at 600 ℃ and the cold side at 140 ℃. However, 
this module has shown a degradation rate of more than 4 % when tested for 10 days with the hot 
side at 550 ℃ and the cold side at 130 ℃. From that investigation, p-type skutterudite is unstable, 
which is the main reason for the degradation[21,22]. 
The goal of this MSc project is to develop a high-performance material based on half-Heusler 
alloys for building up a stable high-efficiency TE module reaching > 10 % for waste heat 
harvesting in the medium temperature range of 300-600 ℃. State-of-the-art TE materials will be 
used with skutterudite as n-type (nSKD) and a half-Heusler alloy as p-type (pHH).

1.2. 
THE THERMOELECTRIC EFFECT 
Thermoelectrics is the science, technology and study of the phenomenon of electron drift 
associated with a temperature difference[7,23]. When heating and cooling either end of a 
conductor, the more energetic free charges near the hot end at temperature 𝑇𝐻 are “pushed” 
towards the colder end at 𝑇𝐶 causing a net drift[7] (Figure 4). 
 
Figure 4 TE leg schematic with electron charge-carriers 
This “push” corresponds to an electromotive force called the Seebeck voltage 𝑉𝑠
[24], and the 
phenomenon is called the Seebeck effect after the discoverer Thomas Johann Seebeck who 
made the first experiments in 1821[25,26]. The Seebeck effect appears in most conducting 
materials (except for in superconductors below their critical temperature[26]).

thermoelectric power[23] or thermopower[7,9,25]). As 𝑆 may vary with temperature, the general 
expression is[24]

To connect a TE material with an electric circuit, a steady current must be possible. This 
requires one more TE material with different majority charge-carriers for electrons to move 
away from the hot end on one side but towards it on the other. That is achieved with a 
semiconductor of the p-type, in which the charge-carriers are holes h+[1,7,23] (Figure 5). A hole – 
a missing electron – is “pushed” and moves away from the hot side by electrons taking over the 
empty spot, thus moving towards the hot side. The circuit can now be closed. Figure 5 therefore 
shows the smallest possible TE device called a unicouple[7] with the two TE materials called 
legs[7,25] (or arms[7]). While one leg is a p-type, the other is often an n-type semiconductor as this 
material group shows an optimal property compromise.

The Seebeck effect is reversible. Applying a current causes heating and cooling of the respective 
material ends, called the Peltier effect[7] after C. Peltier who discovered the reversibility in

Skutterudite materials, named after the locality of discovery, Skutterud in Norway, by W. K. 
von Haidinger in 1854[49], are body-centred cubic (bcc) crystal structures[30] (Figure 8) with the 
centre atom weaker bound; an "atomic cage"-like structure in which the centre atom can 
"rattle" – it is called a filler or “rattler”[50] or Einstein oscillator[51] – significantly reducing 𝜅[7,10,17] 
while optimizing 𝜎[50]. They generally have a binary 𝒳𝒴3 or equivalently 𝒳4𝒴12 host lattice 
composition, constituting the “cage”, with 𝒳 representing a transition metal[30] such as Co, Rh 
or Ir and 𝒴 elements such as P, As or Sb[51,52]; in this work they represent Co and Sb, 
respectively, since the CoSb3 base structure has shown superior while being lower cost and 
easier to process[51].

lower-cost materials than other most-commonly used TE materials, such as Bi-Te. The pure

composition 𝒵𝑥𝒳4𝒴12, with 𝑥 being relative dopant concentration of the dopant element 𝒵 – 
the “rattler” that fills the “cage”[3]. When largely doped with a heavy-metal atom they are often 
referred to as “filled” skutterudites[8,10].

A brazing filler must have a melting point not only lower than those of nSKD and electrode but 
also lower than the degradation temperature of nSKD, 𝑇𝑑≈870 ℃)[62,64]. Reaching 𝑇𝑑 may cause 
Sb to evaporate from nSKD, changing the composition[38]. Also, it should have an 𝛼 in-between 
those of electrode and nSKD. 
Joining techniques with several fillers are tried in literature, for example with skutterudite 
joined onto Mo electrodes with a thin Ti filler sheet[63,68].  
Brazing is a filler joining process performed above 𝑇> 500 ℃. Below this temperature the 
process is called soldering and the filler a solder. Several commercial brazing fillers are 
available, and the Alu-flo™ HT from manufacturer Johnson Matthey [67] is the choice for this 
work. It is an Al-Si composition with a reported melting range of [575 ; 585] ℃. The 
composition as stated by the manufacturer as well as the EDS-measured composition for this 
work is shown in Table 8.  
 
A diffusion barrier might solve an issue with solid-state diffusion or reaction at the filler/leg 
interface as a thin non-reactive separator[14,61,62]. Such reaction might cause good joining[61] but it 
will alter the composition and thus TE properties as well as bringing the risk of faster 
degradation and micro-cracks[16,62,65]. Adding such a layer has shown to work well with 
sputtering, causing better bonding and thinner layers than for example by sintering of a thin-
film onto the surface[61]. 
So, a diffusion barrier will be added. Common materials for thin diffusion barrier deposition are 
pure Mo, Ni[62], Pt, Au, Cr and Ti[65]. Ni has shown to dissolve into the filler during sintering[62], 
while Au and Pt similarly showed to either dissolve into the bulk or to bond weakly, causing 
easy peel-off after deposition. Cr and Mo are the choices for testing in this work. 
 
Electrical insulation will often be needed without hindering thermal conduction during this 
work. Materials that conduct thermally but insulate electrically are for example alumina Al2O3, 
in the form of solid plates of 1 −3 mm in thickness[24], as well as boron nitride BN spray that 
adds a micrometre thin layer over a surface. The BN spray is used for the first module testing, 
and elsewhere alumina separators are used.

PVD is a gas-phase process in which atomic evaporation happens from a source and impinges 
on the target substrate – the TE pellet – and there atoms bond and eventually cause full 
coating[70]. See a schematic on Figure 16. The PVD method used in this work is non-reactive

When a high-energy ion beam bombards the source material with sufficient momentum, it may 
“knock” loose surface ions that when impacting the target material will adhere. They are drawn 
towards the target by a voltage applied across source and target. The voltage as well as 
sputtering time are main factors determining the coating layer thickness[70].

2.2.3. SEM/EDS 
For high magnification imaging, scanning electron microscopy (SEM) is performed with the 
Hitachi High-Tech Tabletop TM3000 Microscope[72]. With electron beam energies of 
15000 keV, around ∼ x10000 times of magnification is possible, visualizing down to below the 
micrometre range.  
While the naked human eye can see length-scales as small as ∼0.1 mm[73] and optical 
microscopes can magnify ∼ x1000 times[74], enabling a view down to ∼200 nm[73], the best 
electron microscopes can magnify ∼ x1000000 times, visualizing length-scales of less than a 
nanometer[74]. The limit is determined by half the wavelength of that of the microscope’s 
source; that being down to around 400 nm for visible light and wavelengths in the range of 
picometers for electrons[73]. 
 
Figure 25 Returned electrons and radiation at beam impact during SEM (from [75]) 
SEM is a non-destructive method. An electron gun accelerates electrons to high energies of 
millions of eV and shoots this electron beam onto the sample, scanning it over the surface[74]. 
Depending on the depth reached, high- or low-angled electrons as well as electromagnetic 
radiation are returned[75] (Figure 25). In SEM specifically, a detector catches the returned 
secondary electrons. These are escaping from a small depth. The brightness of a spot on the final 
image depends on the material density at that spot; brighter spots mean more electrons returned 
to reach the detector, thus less travelling deeper, meaning higher density[74].  
The procedure as illustrated in Figure 26 is as follows:

• Step 2: The height of the sample in sample holder was adjusted of the TM3000 apparatus. 
The smaller the distance to the detector, the better.

Energy-dispersive x-ray spectroscopy (EDS) is a technology for elemental investigation 
performed on the SEM scan. The system used is Bruker’s QUANTAX 70[76]. EDS catches and 
measures the returned x-ray photons. X-rays result from electrons being “knocked” out of their 
shells in the atom; when this happens, electrons in other shells located farther away will 
transition down to such now vacant sites in lower shell. This reduces their energy, which is 
expelled as an x-ray photon. As all outer electrons make such a down-transition, a unique 
energy distribution is expelled, fitting uniquely as a “finger-print” to the element in question[74]. 
The EDS data is shown as coloured points on the scanned surface image. 
For proper amounts of data points for EDS, scanning for data sampling was done for 300 s.

4. CONCLUSION 
The leg performances in general reached close to but always below values reported in literature 
over the whole intermediate temperature range. pHH shows the worst performance with 
temperature compared to literature, mainly caused by high 𝜅 as well as values of 𝑆 deviating 
from literature at higher 𝑇> 500 ℃. A joining temperature of 550 ℃ was found fitting, and the 
joining time can most likely be reduced significantly in future work. 
The Al-Si Alu-Flo™ brazing filler caused some new interphases to appear. An Ag-Al reaction 
layer appears between filler and electrode and grows violently under operation, possibly 
building up internal stresses that may have caused a cracked TE leg after 96 hr testing at 𝑇𝐻=
500 ℃. Furthermore, existing Si grains/particles in the original filler were purified during 
operation and more sharply defined, as well as being concentrated near the coated TE bulk 
(farthest from the Ag electrode) independent of temperature. The reason for this bias is not 
known. 
Both Mo and Cr showed to be well-working 2.5 μm thick diffusion barriers for nSKD and Cr 
also for pHH that were stable over time under operation. The hardness of the pHH caused a 
more planar surface after polish, giving a more even diffusion barrier. There were no visible 
changes regarding the diffusion barrier on pHH after neither 96 hr nor 156 hr with 𝑇𝐻=
500 ℃, though some microcracks are seen.  
Module testing showed a stable unicouple over the first 96 hr. Linear 𝐼𝑉-curves and almost 
constant unicouple resistance over time indicated a stable and non-degrading microstructure.

The resistance only varied within the small range of 𝑅∈[0.076 ; 0.081] Ω over the entire 
measurement time, with the largest deviations from the stable value seen during heating and 
cooling. The output power reached a constant height of 0.14 W at 𝑇𝐻= 500 ℃ giving 𝐼= 1.4 A 
and an open circuit (Seebeck) voltage around 𝑉= 125 mV, kept unchanged over 48 hr. The 
final pHH leg testing at 𝑇𝐻= 500 ℃ until 156 hr showed gave no relevant results. 
The overall unicouple maximum efficiency is from the measured data and a 1-dimensional 
numerical modelling method calculated to be 𝜂= 9.4%, almost reaching the goal of this project 
of > 10 %. It must be remembered that this value is ideal and neglecting heat losses and contact 
resistances and should be viewed as an upper limit. 
This stable test giving high power output indicates a non-degrading well-working unicouple, 
though weakening of the nSKD cannot be ruled out due to the subsequent crack after the first 
testing, which might have appeared during polishing due to a weakening of the material. The 
pHH type shows promising stability and performance results, in spite of slight micrcracks that 
must be investigated further. It is in general a good choice for p-type replacement. 
4.1. 
FUTURE WORK 
The performance of pHH doesn’t live up to the high 𝑧𝑇 reported in literature, and not much 
other literature gives such good results. Future work should aim for repeating the experiments 
of the Fu et al, 2015 [77], for verification.  
For nSKD brazing, other brazing fillers than the Al-Si Alu-Flo™ should be investigated for less 
Al-Ag reaction layer growth. As nSKD was cracked after 96 hr testing at high temperature on 
the hot side, a numerical model of internal stresses based on the growing Ag-Al phase would be 
beneficial for deeper insight. The influence of this Ag-Al layer on the properties (possibly 
reducing of 𝜎) would also be an interesting future topic. 
Furthermore, the effect causing concentration of Si precipitate particles near the coated TE bulk 
(farthest from the Ag electrode) regardless of temperature and side (hot or cold) of the bulk 
should be investigated. This effect does not seem to have much influence but is an odd 
behaviour that is not understood at this point in time. 
Reduction of the joining time should be possible and is an area to look into before larger-scale 
production. 
The efficiency is calculated high in this work and an actual value should be measured 
experimentally for confirmation. 
The pHH microcracks near the diffusion layer ought to be tested in the future at high hot-side 
temperature over several hours, for example over 300 hr. The cracks may be a result of the
