Preface
This thesis was made at DTU at the department of photonics engineering in Risø, for the
acquiring of the MSc in photonics engineering.
The aim of the thesis consist of three parts:
• Building a Q-switched laser: The building of the Q-switched laser uniﬁes laser theory,
simulations and practical expertise into one. The understanding of how the CW laser works
in general is needed in order to reason how the cavity should be assembled and where to
place the diﬀerent optical elements to get the wanted eﬀect. Simulations of for example
the size of the beam width at diﬀerent places within the cavity is needed in order to decide
where to place the optical elements. Also the resonator stability in order to know if the
design can support a laser mode or not is necessary. Practical skills are necessary when
aligning the mirrors. Since the laser is not visible, knowing how to trace the laser with a
heat sensitive card and helping mirror is crucial. In many situations, the diﬀerence between
getting laser or not is determined by a tenth of a millimeter. Under these circumstances a
steady operation is a prerequisite.
• Developing a simulation program: The simulation program is developed to predict
results and assist in their interpretation. The precision of the simulated results depends
on the accuracy of the used parameters. It is also limited by the assumptions simplifying
the math that is used to describe the processes in the simulations.
• Characterizing the pulsed laser: The laser is characterized to get an understanding
of the physics governing the Q-switched laser. This is achieved by comparing the results
from measurements of the average power, pulse peak power, pulse duration, pulse rise and
fall time with theory and simulation results.
Two diﬀerent types of Q-switched Tm:YLF lasers are built, one using a passive saturable ab-
sorber and another one using a mechanical chopper. The Q-switched Tm:YLF laser using a
Cr:ZnS crystal as passive saturable absorber is known to allow the realization of pulses of 14 ns
in duration and a peak power of around 65 kW [4]. Such an amount of power can easily damage
the cavity mirrors as well as the passive saturable absorber. This happened in the process of
building the pulsed laser: ﬁrst the Cr:ZnS absorber got burned on the surface, this led to a
change in the setup in which the pump mirror was destroyed. These damages show the diﬃ-
culties in handling the high peak power when constructing a Q-switched laser using a passive
absorber. This led to the idea of introducing a mechanical chopper in order to secure results
from some type of a Q-switched laser in order to fulﬁll the aims of the thesis. The mechanical
Q-switching is associated with much less peak power and therefore less prone to laser damages.
The measurements were at ﬁrst taken using a low pump power(maximum 1.08 volt). With a
gradual increase it was then tested whether the cavity could support more power before changing
back to the passive absorber.
The little selection of mirrors available combined with the high pulse peak power even around
laser threshold, resulted in changes of the setup along the way. Partly for reducing the circu-
lating power and increasing the beam width for preventing further damage to the equipment,
but also to accommodate for the changing between optical elements. Therefore the ﬁrst part of
the project was dedicated to aligning and placing of the mirrors, in order to build a stable and
suiteble resonator for the two types of Q-switching elements.

Abstract
This thesis deals with the construction and characterization of two types of Tm:YLF Q-
switched laser, one using a saturable absorber (Cr:ZnSe), and one using a mechanical chopper.
The Tm:YLF crystal can generate high power outputs at wavelength 1.9 µm, due to the long
excited state lifetime and cross-relaxation. These properties of the Tm:YLF crystal make it a
suitable candidate for building a high power passive Q-switched laser. A setup for Q-switching
the Tm:YLF laser with the saturable absorber was developed and tested, and ﬁrst results showed
a high pulse power even for a pump power close to the threshold value. The mechanical chopper
was then introduced because the setup didn’t support the power of the circulating ﬁeld generated
by the Tm:YLF crystal, which lead to laser damages on the pump mirror and the saturable
absorber. A simulation program was developed to predict results, support their interpretation,
and provide information for building an improved setup for the passive Q-switched laser.
Through measurements of the mechanical Q-switched laser, the setup and the properties
of the Tm:YLF crystal were characterized. It was found that average power and peak power
are increasing with pump power. Additionally, the peak power is increasing with rising chopper
frequency. The pulse duration and rise and fall time are decreasing with increasing pump power
or chopper frequency. These ﬁndings are consistent with theory and match qualitatively the
expectations predicted by the simulations. Furthermore, the simulation results predict relaxation
oscillations, which explain the behavior of the after pulses, which were seen in the measurements
of the mechanical Q-switched laser.
The knowledge gained through the characterization of the mechanically Q-switched Tm:YLF
laser creates the basis for building a stable passive Q-switched laser in the future.

Introduction
Mid-infrared lasers have been proven useful in applications supporting diﬀerent ﬁelds like
medical care, remote sensing, lidar, and military [1] [10] [6] [5].
Applications within medical care in cancer detection, exploits within a range of 5-10 µm the
characteristic absorption lines indicating whether cancer cells are present or not [8] [7]. This
wavelength range is not suiteble for detection and for that a given signal within this wavelength
range has to be up-converted. Crystals for nonlinear up-conversion such as Lithium Niobate
which has been the standard so far, is not transparent for wavelengths above 4.5 µm. This
means that the light gets absorbed so that the signal gets lost and therefore other crystals have
to be used. Nonlinear optical crystals such as AGSe can be used for the frequency up-conversion
and also have the advantage of a higher nonlinearity. This crystal is transparent in the wave-
length range of 1.5 up to 18 µm. This means that the standard solid state infrared Nd lasers can
not be used for this purpose. Instead the Tm laser meets the requirements having a wavelength
around 1.9 µm for frequency up-conversion of the signal to around 1600 nm, which is in the
wavelength range suiteble for detection with the InGaAs detector.
The Tm:YLF laser has a wavelength varying between 1.9 to 2 µm and with a high absorp-
tion in the range around 790 nm. Due to cross-relaxation it can create two excitations when
absorbing a pump photon. This characteristic combined with a long ﬂuorescence lifetime of
about 14 ms, gives the Tm:YLF laser a high slope eﬃciency and minimal heating (because of
the low quantum defect) which make it comparable to the diode pumped Nd lasers [12] [11].
A Q-switched laser is capable of generating short pulses with much higher peak power, than
the average power coming from the CW laser. The property of a long ﬂuorescence lifetime in the
Tm:YLF crystal seems promising for its application in the ﬁeld of Q-switched lasers, because
it enables a high peak power pulse even with a low pump power. The high power needed for
the nonlinear up-conversion in order to detect the signals in the infrared spectrum, combined
with the need of a laser with a wavelength above 1.5 µm makes the Q-switched Tm:YLF laser a
promising candidate. Publication [4] has shown that it is actually possible to built a Q-switched
Tm:YLF.
So in order to accommodate the need of a high power laser suiteble for nonlinear up-conversion
within the wavelength range of 1.5 to 16 µm, the building and characterizing a Tm:YLF Q-
switched laser is the aim of this thesis. A simulation program of the Q-switched laser simulating
the photon ﬂux in the cavity together with the population inversion is developed, in order to
predict and interpret the behavior the measured data.
The DTU photonics group located in Risø has long term experience with frequency up-conversion
for detection in the infrared region. The realization of a Q-switch laser using the Tm:YLF, is
expected to support the detection of biomarkers indicating cancer [7] [8].

Laser theory
A laser consist in its simplest form of a gain medium,resonator and a pump. The pump
stores energy in the gain medium in the form of population inversion in order to create laser (Light
Ampliﬁed Stimulated Emission Radiation). The function of the cavity, also called resonator, is
to create a feedback for the light to be ampliﬁed by the gain medium. It also determines which
longitudinal and transverse modes can exist within the resonator. The pump is supplying the
gain by exciting the atoms in the gain material. The process of exciting an atom is known
as absorption, which by the Bohr model is shortly explained as the atom absorbing a photon
whereby the electron jumps to an excited state. The electron can then jump back by emitting
a photon. This jumping back, also called relaxation, can happen in two ways: Either by the
presence of a new photon causing the electron to jump back.
This is done by emitting, in
all aspects, an identical photon to the photon stimulating the process and so this process is
called stimulated emission. The other way happens spontaneously within some average time,
called excited state lifetime in which the emitted photon gets a random direction and phase.
The process of exciting the atoms is quantiﬁed by density N, which is the diﬀerence between
the excited atoms and the non excited atoms. In order to have an amplifying gain medium,
the excited state has to be more populated than the ground state.
This condition is called
population inversion and can be written as N = N2 −N1 > 0, where N1 is the number of atoms
in their ground state and N2 is the number of excited atoms.
The loss of photons in the cavity is unavoidable, and can be divided into two categories of wanted
and unwanted loss. The unwanted loss appears as a lowering of the power conversion eﬃciency
from mechanisms such as scattering, spontaneous emission and non-radiative processes. The
wanted loss is determined through the reﬂectivity coeﬃcient of the output coupler, giving the
percentage of the circulating photon ﬂux that gets transmitted as laser output.
For laser oscillations to occur in the cavity, two conditions must be fulﬁlled. These two conditions
are known as the phase and gain conditions. The phase condition demands that the total phase
shift of the light after propagating one round trip in the cavity, must equal some multiple of 2π,
so that the reﬂected feedback input phase, matches the phase of the input. The gain condition
means that the small signal gain (low circulating power) must be greater than the loss for a
round-trip in the cavity.The laser output wavelength which is emitted when pumping a gain
medium is determined by the energy diﬀerence between N2 and N1.
2.1
Threshold value
In order to start building up laser oscillations, the gain has to exceed the losses in the
laser cavity. This is carried out by the pump, which by absorption stores energy in the gain
medium as population inversion. At this point, there are no substantial circulating power and
the gain coeﬃcient known as the small signal gain is higher than the loss coeﬃcient. As the size
of population inversion (ampliﬁed by the stimulated absorption) gets large, laser oscillations are
then trigged by the spontaneous emission from the gain medium, which enables the stimulated
emission process that is necessary to generate laser. The situation below threshold where N is
linearly increased by the pump is unstable, and it comes to a point where the stimulated emission
will start to saturate the gain. This process is known as gain saturation. At gain saturation the
gain will start decreasing until it reaches to the stable point where it equals the losses. This is
called the steady-state condition and is expressed as
R1R2 e2gl = 1
(2.1)
where R1 and R2 are the cavity mirror reﬂection constants, g is the gain coeﬃcient and l is
the length of the gain medium. The lowest value of pump power which satisfy this condition is
called the threshold value. From this point on, the laser output increases linearly with the pump
power.

2.3
Resonator design
In order to build a laser, one key aspect is to have a stable resonator which is able to
support a laser mode. The resonator, is in its simplest form made out of two mirrors (Fabry-

The feedback factor is
determined by the reﬂectivity of the mirrors, which at the same time determines the output
coupling of the laser.

2.4
Rate equations
The rate equations describes how the population densities in the diﬀerent levels changes
with time. The thulium is considered a quasi-three-level laser and is described by the rate equa-
tions for the three level laser. The three levels are pictured as a ground state, where nearly all
atoms in thermal equilibrium at room temperature without external inﬂuence are located, and
two excited states, which are called level two and three. The lifetime in level 3 is extremely
short whereas the lifetime in level 2 is long. In order to get population inversion, a pump is
used to bring the atom from the ground state to level 3, where it immediately relaxes to level
2. The long lifetime in level 2 and the continuous pumping accumulates the atoms in level 2.
At the point where the gain gets higher than the loss,the spontaneous decay can start the laser
oscillation by stimulated emission.

2.5
Optical properties of the Tm:YLF crystal
The laser crystal Tm:YLF consists of a host material YLF doped with Tm ions which are
the active material. YLF shows valuable properties as host material by having a lower thermal
lensing than YAG and being highly transparent for both, the pump and laser wavelength. The
low thermal lensing originates from the negative thermal dispersion coeﬃcient that counters the
positive dispersion from the thermal expansion causing the crystal face bending. YLF excels
when it comes to non-radiative decay losses, which are lower than any oxides due to the lower
phonon energy. This gives the Tm:YLF its longer ﬂuorescence lifetime [3].
The Tm:YLF crystal can generate laser in the range of 1.9-2 µm and has a strong absorption
band within the range of 770-810 nm [12].
It has a high conversion eﬃciency due to cross-
relaxation between the electronic energy levels of the Tm ion, which gives the advantage of
exciting up to two ions for the ”price of one” absorbed pump photon. Additionally this has the
positive eﬀect that it reduces the heat generation, that usually is associated with the quantum
defect. It thereby counteracts the limitations from the quasi-three-level nature that thulium
exhibits [3]. Since the Tm:YLF crystal is a quasi-three-level laser material, the lower level of the
laser transition lies so close to the ground level that it has a thermally induced population. This
is in contrast to a four level laser material, where the lower laser transition level is energetically
larger compared to the ground level so that this level is thermally empty. This introduces a
saturable loss that increases the threshold and reduces the slope eﬃciency. The saturability of
the induced loss has the eﬀect that at large circulating power, the slope eﬃciency approaches
that of the four level laser [6](p.126).

2.6
Thermo-optic eﬀects in the crystal
An important fraction of the loss in the conversion process is associated with the heat
generated in the crystal. The continuous heat generated in the crystal needs to be lowered for
not damaging the crystal. This is achieved by cooling the surfaces of the crystal. Due to non-
uniform cooling, the temperature in the crystal gets warmer in the center than at the surface
where the cooling takes place, causing a temperature gradient within the crystal. This can lead
to mechanical stress when the inner area of the crystal is constrained to expand due to the
colder outer layer of the crystal, resulting in end-face curvature. Stress fractures in the crystal
from the stress component towards the constraint surface, happen if the heating exceeds the
threshold [3] [6].

Pulsed laser: Q-switching
Q switching refers to the switching between a high and low value of Q, where Q is the
quality factor of the cavity deﬁned as the ratio of energy stored in the cavity to the energy loss
per cycle
Q = 2π E
p/ν
(3.1)
where E is the energy, p is the energy loss and 1/ν is the period. The passive absorber induces a
high loss which prevents laser oscillations because of the increased threshold value. This allows
building up gain, in the form of population inversion, inside the cavity to a degree much higher
than achievable with the lower threshold value. It is now possible to pump the population density
far beyond the threshold value for normal lasing, more energy can be released but in a short
pulse. For this to happen, the Q value has ﬁrst to be switched to a high value (corresponding
to a low loss) thereby decreasing the threshold for the laser. In this master thesis the switching
of the Q value is performed with a passive absorber or a mechanical chopper.
In ﬁgure 9 we see the population inversion is growing as soon as the pump is turned on. As N(t)
increases, the corresponding gain starts to saturate (because of the diminishing amount vacant
excited states ) and the steepness of the curve ﬂattens out as it approaches N0 proportional
to the pump. The dotted line which is proportional to the loss, indicates the two values of
population inversion threshold. At t1, the Q-value or the loss is switched which is proportional
to the decrease in Nt from Nta to Ntb. N(t) is now larger than Ntb and oscillations set oﬀby
spontaneous emission begins the stimulated emission process raising the photon number. In the
middle between t1 and t2, N(t) is less than Ntb. The loss is again larger than the gain, and
the photon number decreases after peaking together with N(t) until t2, where the increased loss
switches the threshold value back to Nta. For the next Q-switching period, the buildup time
interval t2 to ti is shorter and N(t) is less, resulting in less peak power of the pulse.

increasing the pump power leads to a decrease in the fall time of the pulse.

When trying to make a Q switch pulsed laser with this kind of mechanical chopper, two
factors that can eﬀect the pulse peak power have to be considered. That is the lifetime of the
excited atom and the time dependent loss induced by the optical chopper. The lifetime of the
excited atom in the Tm:YLF crystal is 14 ms which corresponds to 71 Hz. This means that
power would be lost due to spontaneous emission when setting the frequency lower than this
and the gain will saturate at some value. Measuring the average power would then decrease
when decreasing the chopper frequency below this saturation value. The other factor is due to
the time dependence of the induced chopper loss that prevents the instantaneous change of Q
value of the cavity. Laser oscillations stops when the gain drops below the loss, so if the chopper
induced loss is not switched fast enough, the gain can drop below the loss before reaching a full
opening of the chopper blade. While the induced chopper loss is dropping and the pump is still
on, the loss can again reach a value lower than the gain and cause a new pulse to arise.

3.3
Saturable absorber
The process of passive Q-switching is created by inserting a saturable absorber into the
cavity which then, due to the high loss from the absorption, increases the threshold of laser (the
size of Nt). The energy levels of the passive absorber is like a 3 level laser but with a weakly
absorbing 4 level from which stimulated emission and absorption can also happen, ﬁgure 14.
From level 1 the photons gets absorbed to level 3, where it decays fast and non-radiatively to
level 2. This increases the loss in the cavity. From level 2 it can either decay by spontaneous
emission or jump by absorption into level 4, from where it decays fast non-radiatively back to
level 2. An important property of the passive absorber is that the lifetime in level 2 is long. This
enables the depletion of the ground state that is needed in order to make the Q-switching. As
long as the absorber is opaque, the energy from the pump laser gets stored in the laser crystal
generating population inversion. As more and more atoms gets excited the loss eventually gets
exceeded by the round-trip gain. The photons can now start oscillating and the energy stored in
the gain medium is now released by stimulated emission bleaching the absorption in the passive
absorber. The lifetime of the excited state in the saturable absorber then determines when the
absorber turns opaque again.

3.4
Simulations
A computer simulation of an experiment can be a useful tool in order to get a good under-
standing of the physics behind the behavior within a corresponding experiment. By comparing
the results of the simulation with the data from a measurement, the simulation can assist in
getting a better interpretation of the data. Also useful information can be gathered about how
to set up the laser and which measurements to execute. Instead of deﬁning the ABCD matrices
and putting in numbers, one can use a program as reZonator to do the calculations, after making
a virtual setup.
A simulation program was ﬁrst made to simulate the Q switched laser using a passive absorber
and then later in the experimental process, the loss mechanism in the program was changed to
a mechanical chopper. The intention with the simulation program was to simulate the eﬀect

When running the simulation spikes is observed as a result of the relaxation oscillations of
the population diﬀerence.
These spikes arises as a mathematical consequence from the rate
equations. The switching of the Q-value leads to a pulse by the conversion of N2 into photons
via stimulated emission. This conversion depletes N2 causing the photon density to drop which
then decreases the process of stimulated emission. At the same time the pump is exciting the
atoms, which creates an unstable situation where the population density N2 increases to a size
beyond the steady-state value. The result is that the population inversion also reaches a size
greater than the threshold value, which then converts into a photon ﬂux beyond the steady-state
value. The high photon ﬂux leads to a likewise higher stimulation emission process that depletes
N2 to a size below threshold. This creates the basis of a new overshoot and the behavior repeats
while approaching the steady-state condition.

5.1
Uncertainties in the measurements
When making a measurement, data is usually not identical when repeating the measure-
ment. This gives an uncertainty to the true or expected value of the data taken from which,
had it been an ideal world, the value of the data would be the same every time you repeated the
same experiment. So the uncertainty in a measurement gives a range to value of which can be
expected from a given measurement. A good way of representing the best estimate of the true
value, is as a the mean value given over from many measurements. This has been done for all
the measurements with the InGaAs detector, where the mean value,unless otherwise speciﬁed,
is given from 100 measurements whenever data is given or plotted. There are diﬀerent kinds of
sources giving uncertainty to the measurements. Mechanical vibrations is a one source which
adds to the uncertainty of the data taken in a measurement. Mechanical vibrations comes from
the mechanical chopper pushing air and also giving small vibrations to the table close to the
laser setup. Vibrations also comes from the water cooling system not letting the water ﬂow 100
% uniformly all the time. The measurements with the InGaAs detector is especially vulnerable
to these because of its use of detection of single pulses. So to compensate for that the measure-
ments is represented by the mean value and its standard deviation describing a range at which
the expected value is to be found with a probability of 68 %. The uncertainty from the setting of
pump power is 0.004 V, due to the lack of digits on the screen. This corresponds to 0.03 W and
is at same size as the width of the dot showing the data. The ﬂuctuating of the electric cooling
system and the mechanical vibrations doesn’t impact much on the thermal detector measure-
ments. This is because the thermal detector is only able of measuring the average power. When
measuring the average power the value was seen to rise and fall within a range of 2 mW. For
that a mean value is given from the two extreme values, giving an uncertainty of 1 mW.

5.3.2
Varying the chopper frequency
To see how only the frequency setting of the mechanical chopper inﬂuences the Q-switched
pulses, experiments were made varying the frequency while keeping other settings constant. The
measurements were done with both the thermal detector and the InGaAs detector. In this way
the inﬂuences on the average power and peak power induced by diﬀerent chopper frequencies
could be investigated. Likewise the eﬀects on the width, rise and fall time of the pulses was
investigated. The pump power were kept constant at 4.31 w, getting an average value from 100
measurements for each data set within each frequency setting with the InGaAs detector. For
the measurements with the thermal detector, the average power were measured over suﬃcient
amount of time to get a mean value. The average power was ﬂuctuating up and down within
a range of 2 mW, following the small instabilities of the cooling temperature in the Tm:YLF
crystal.
So for that made the suﬃcient time needed, corresponded to reaching this pseudo
equilibrium. The uncertainties caused by this eﬀect is not presented in the data plots, since they
seemed insigniﬁcant to the interpretation of the physics behind the overall behavior seen in the
experiment.

the pump power setting due to the limited precision setting is 0.005 V which corresponds to

Experiment: passive absorber
Using the setup described in section 4.4 with M465, the passive absorber was placed as
close as possible to output coupler. This was done so that the passive absorber was as close to
the beam waist where the intensity of the beam is highest. It turned out that the ﬂuence was
above the laser damage threshold of the passive absorber (Cr2+:ZnS) and the experiment was
stopped because of damage to the surface of the crystal. Two precautions were made in order to
decrease the ﬂuence to below the laser damage threshold for the Cr2+:ZnS crystal. The passive
absorber was positioned a little further away from the beam waist to decrease the intensity of
the beam in the absorber and the cavity end-mirror was changed to M482 witch has a larger
output coupling, in order to decrease the circulating ﬁeld inside the cavity.
The experiment was unfortunately again prematurely stopped, this time due to damage to the
front cavity mirror.

6
Discussion
Motivated by the need of a pulsed laser in the wave length range of 2 µm the aim of this
thesis was to build such a pulsed laser. This was combined with the developing of a simulation
program in order to provide a better understanding of the physics of the Q-switched laser.
Through measurements the pulsed laser was characterized in terms of pulse duration, peak
power, average power, rise and fall time.
6.1
Evaluation of the simulation results
In ﬁgure 24 the assumption about the loss proﬁle from the mechanical chopper was in-
vestigated. The transmission proﬁle corresponds to the ﬂipped loss proﬁle, so by measuring the
power of the CW laser through the mechanical chopper with the blade blocking to fully open, the
transmission proﬁle could be obtained and compared with the loss proﬁle used in the simulation.
The assumption was that the proﬁle measured outside the cavity corresponds to one inside. In
ﬁgure 13 the results of the transmission measurements using four diﬀerent chopper frequencies
was presented. The transmission proﬁle with the chopper frequency at 50, 75 and 99 Hz shows
the characteristic shape and behavior. In ﬁgure 24 the loss proﬁle from the simulation imitates
the proﬁle from a transmission of 50% to 100%. Minor adjustments has to be made in order
to get a complete overlap. From 50 % and down the simulation proﬁle gets unacceptable to far
from the measured proﬁle.
From comparing the simulation loss proﬁle in ﬁgure 25 with the simulation of the population
inversion in 26 A is seen that N2 grows until time equal to 0.2 ·10−3 where the loss from the
mechanical chopper is less than 5 %. This percentage increases when increasing the pump power
as seen in ﬁgure 28. The same can also be seen in in ﬁgure 27 A where the pulse appears at that
time. This implies that an eﬀort in improving the accuracy of the loss proﬁle further should be
made ﬁtting especially the last part of the loss proﬁle with the ﬂipped transmission proﬁle from
the measurements.
Figure 27 shows pumping with 4.3 W at the chopper frequency of 99 Hz in ﬁgure A. The
peak power of the ﬁrst pulse is 140.2 W more than the ﬁrst pulse in ﬁgure B, with the chopper
frequency of 50 Hz. At the same time in ﬁgure 26, the population inversion gets larger at the
chopping frequency of 50 Hz because of the longer pumping time. This suggest that a higher
chopper frequency leads to a larger pulse peak power, despite of the larger size of population
inversion generated with the lower frequency. This is contradicted in ﬁgure 23 for the instant
Q-switching. Here the lower frequency of 99 Hz in ﬁgure A, leads to a larger pulse peak power
than for the frequency of 500 Hz in ﬁgure B. The reason for diﬀerent outcome lies in the steep-
ness of the loss proﬁle. Since the loss is instant in ﬁgure 23 The diﬀerence in size between Ni
and Nf is larger for ﬁgure B, which leads to a larger photon number density, and hence larger
peak power.
The loss proﬁle in ﬁgure25 is given by the derivative of the Gaussian proﬁle, is more steep at
99 Hz than at 50 Hz. This results in a larger diﬀerence in size between Ni and Nf at 99 Hz, and
that is why the peak power is larger at the chopper frequency of 99 Hz in Figure 27 A than at
50 Hz in ﬁgure B.
The pulse peak power is expected to increase with pump power, and for that is it surprising
that this is not the case in ﬁgure 28. The explanation is again found in the loss proﬁle. The
size of N gets larger when pumping with 15 W than 4.3 W, and therefore is the gain also larger
than the loss earlier generating laser. Because more time has to pass while the loss decreases
the loss proﬁle can have another steepness and the size of N can drop more. This behavior was
only seen for the simulation with these parameters. Many other simulations showed an increase
in the peak power when increasing the pump power. Comparing the average power within the
ﬁrst 3 milliseconds it is clear that more power is released when pumping with 15 W.

6.2
Evaluation of the measurement results
The measurements presented in ﬁgure 39 and ﬁgure 40 were taken in order to qualita-
tively compare them with the pulse behavior seen in the simulation part and described in the
section 3.1. The behavior of the pulses was slightly changing with time, but the overall picture
was constant. This justiﬁes the qualitative experiment that gives a visual understanding of the
general behavior of the Q-switching with the mechanical chopper. Both measurements show
that the pulse power rises when increasing the pump power. Since the peak power is normalized
to the highest power within the same chopper frequency setup, comparing between the pump
power can only be done within the same chopper frequency. The comparing between chopper
frequency has been more deeply investigated in other measurements (ﬁgure 42). The trends in
both measurements are the same, but is most clear in ﬁgure 40. low pump power results in less
peak power, because less atoms in the Tm:YLF crystal get excited. This gives a smaller value of
Ni which by equation 3.2 gives less peak power. It is also more easy to see how the after-pulses
in ﬁgure 40 increase in number when increasing the pump power and how they decrease in peak
power with time. This same overall behavior is also seen in ﬁgure 39 but here the behavior gets
more complex, where after-pulses can become larger then the previous. The simulation shows
that as soon as the Q-switch stop being instantaneous, then the loss decreases according to the
loss proﬁle given by the complementary error function imitating the opening of the chopper for
oscillation ﬁgure 25. The gain drops faster than the loss, which stops laser oscillations. The
pump is still pumping more gain, while the loss continues to decrease and when the gain exceeds
the loss again, a new but smaller pulse is generated. Therefore the after-pulses increases with
the slower chopper frequency. The more times we get the situation where the gain gets smaller
than the loss before N reach Nf.
The motivation for the experiment behind the data presented in ﬁgure 41 was based in the
variation between each pulses. It was interesting to investigate the connection between the ﬁrst
pulse and its after-pulses. As we saw in ﬁgure 39 it gets messy with to many after-pulses. For
that the experiment were kept as simple as possible, by only considering two pulses. Twenty
measurements were taken and again to keep it simple two only two was plotted.
The mea-
surements were qualitatively showing the same, so by only presenting two and choosing them
out on the criteria of having the largest diﬀerence in peak power was clarifying the interpretation.
In ﬁgure 42, 43, 44 and 46 the measurements of the pulse peak power, width, rise and fall
time were taken by varying the pump power for diﬀerent chopper frequency settings. The mea-
surements were taken for both the 2 and the 100 hole chopper setting. The same procedure was
repeated again with one more chopper frequency setting for the 2 hole chopper setting plotted
in ﬁgure 57, 58, 59 and 60. From the theory explained in section 6 supported by equation 3.1
we would expect the peak power to rise linearly when increasing the pump power as long as we
stay in the unsaturated pump regime. The theory in section 6 about the chopper induced loss
also predicts that a higher chopper frequency causes higher pulse peak power. Both predictions
are supported by the simulations ??. The results show an agreement with the predictions a rise
in power with the chopper frequency and the pump power. But the peak power doesn’t rise
linear with the pump power which is an indication of pump saturation. Approaching the regime
of pump saturation we would expect a decrease in the conversion eﬃciency (equ.2.10) and that
the decrease would be more signiﬁcant when increasing the pump power. Because of the long
excited lifetime of the atoms in the Tm:YLF crystal, the pumping time hence the chopper fre-
quency would also have a signiﬁcance. This agrees very well with what we actually se from data
when inspecting ﬁgure 57 the behavior ﬁts theory about pump saturation. But this behavior is
not seen from the repeated experiment with the 2 hole setting in ﬁgure 57 and since we in the
same range of pump power but without pump saturation, something else has to account for that
behavior seen in ﬁgure 57 that were not present in the measurements plotted in ﬁgure 57.

6.3
Assessment of fulﬁllment of the thesis’ goals
The building of a mechanical Q-switched laser was indeed successful proven by the results
from the measurements of the mechanical Q-switched laser. The characterization of the pulses
was consistent with the theory with only minor exceptions as mentioned earlier in the discussion
of the results.
The results from the measurements conﬁrm to a large degree the predictions given by the theory
and the simulations. The assumption about the loss proﬁle agreed very well with the measured
proﬁle for the chopper frequencies of 50 Hz and higher. This assumption didn’t hold for the
chopper frequency at 10 Hz were some unknown factor changed the loss proﬁle for the last 40
% of the chopper opening. Even though the simulation failed to predict the exact of the pulse
generating, it still gave a qualitative indication of what to expect of the pulse characteristics in
response to pump power and Q-switching. The simulation program was built on the assumptions
of monochromatic light, uniform absorbing in the laser crystal and no external disturbances from
the mechanical chopper vibrations and cooling ﬂuctuations. Accounting for these complexities
would undoubtedly have made the results more precise.
Because a calibration of the power
was not possible comparing the peak power of the measurements with the simulations was not
performed which could have lead to further validation and connection between the results of the

The building of a passive Q-switched laser which was the original goal was only successful to a
minor degree due to the burning of the passive absorber and the pump mirror.
The burning of the pump mirror and the passive absorber indicates pulses of very high power
which the setup was not suited to support. By decreasing the distance of the cavity end-mirrors
the beam waist is increased. It was only possible to decreased the distance down to 145 mm.
The reason is probably because the pump was to focused in the laser crystal. So when increasing
the beam waist, the overlap between the pump beam and the circulating ﬁeld was decreased.
This increases the loss which eventually prevents the laser. A more suiteble setup should have
a less focused pump. This would allow a larger beam waist in order to spare the pump mirror
from a to high intensity ﬂux. The lifetime of the excited Tm atom enables a large output cou-
pling which decreases the circulating ﬁeld and decreases the risk of damaging the components
in the setup. The simulations shows in ﬁgure 22 no suﬀering of pump saturation when pump-
ing the Tm:YLF crystal at 15 W while chopping at 500 Hz. So a large pump given the right
precautions just mentioned in the setup, can be done eﬃcient and without laser damage to the
optical components. More time for a new setup would have enabled further investigations and
experiments with the passive Q-switched laser. Which could lead to the successful building of a
passive Tm:YLF Q-switched laser.
Since the amount of scientiﬁc articles testifying the building of this kind of passive Q-switched
laser (to my knowledge) can be narrowed down to only two [4] and [2], the knowledge about
the Tm:YLF passive Q-switched laser is very limited. This also suggest the possibility of new
discoveries about the Tm:YLF passive Q-switched laser which in combination with frequency
up-conversion could open up for new opportunities and exploitation in the applications of de-
tection.

7
Conclusion
For the simulation of the mechanical Q-switched laser, the loss was assumed to be Gaus-
sian. The accuracy of that assumption was invested in ﬁgure 24 which showed an overlap between
0.17 to 0.59 of the transmission proﬁle.
The simulation with the Gaussian loss proﬁle shows that the higher the frequency the steeper
is the loss proﬁle. The beneﬁt of a steeper loss proﬁle is shown in its extreme condition by the
square proﬁle loss which gave one pulse with much larger peak power. This was conﬁrmed by
the experimental measurements presented in ﬁgure13.
For constant chopper frequency, the pulse peak power was increasing with the pump power,
ﬁgure 42 and 57. This was not conﬁrmed by the simulation results presented in ﬁgure 28 where
pumping with 15.0 W lead to less peak power than when pumping with 4.3 W. But The total
power of the following pulses did that the average power was larger.
For constant pump power, the peak power was increasing with the speed of the blade holes and
therefore the chopper frequency. It was also seen comparing the chopper blade setting at 2 holes
to the setting at 100 holes. Where using the chopper blade setting at 100 holes and 99 Hz, lead to
a peak power of less than 1% compared to the setting at 2 holes in ﬁgure 42. This behavior was
explained in the simulation with the Gaussian loss proﬁle. As the chopper frequency increases,
the blade speed increases which make the loss proﬁle more steep enabling more conversion of
the population inversion into photon ﬂux.
The measurements of the pulse duration, fall and rise time, ﬁgure 58, 59 and 60, agrees with the
theory section 3.1, that the pulse duration, fall and rise time is decreasing with increasing pump
power or frequency.
Both the measurements and the simulations shows that the average power is increasing with
the pump power, ﬁgure 53, 55, 27 and 28.
Measurements also show that the pumping time and hence the frequency of the chopper limits
the average power when approaching the frequency corresponding to the lifetime of the excite
atom in the laser crystal from above. The lower we go after passing this corresponding frequency
of 71 Hz the average power starts to drop even faster.
The original aim of building a passive Q-switched laser was only partially fulﬁlled. When using
the passive absorber, the intensity of the circulating ﬁeld was larger than the cavity was able
to support. This resulted in damage to the pump mirror and the passive absorber, ﬁgure 61
and 62. By introducing the mechanical chopper for Q-switching combined with the simulation,
characterization of the of system was performed. The characterization gave information of how
a new setup supporting the power generated by the Tm:YLF crystal should be build.
The building of a mechanical Q-switched laser was successful. From this setup data gave knowl-
edge about the setup and the properties of Tm:YLF in respect to the pulse duration, peak power,
rise and fall time.
The development of a simulation program in order to predict measurement results and sup-
port the interpretation of the data was only successful in terms of giving qualitative information.
The experience gained through this thesis suggest for future attempts of setting up a passive
Tm:YLF Q-switched laser the following improvements. By decreasing the focus of the pump in
the Tm:YLF crystal, further shortening of the resonator can be performed, section 2.3. This
will stabilize the circulating ﬁeld more and increase the beam waist in order to avoid damage
