Abstract
This Master Thesis deals with the implementation of calculations for added
wave resistance in the maritime programme I-ship.
Although methods for calculating the added resistance have been in develop-
ment since the 1960‚Äôs, it is only within resent years that the added resistance
has become interesting to accurately predict for the maritime industry. This
is due to the added resistance having a signiÔ¨Ācant inÔ¨āuence for slow speeds.
As the fuel prices rises and the CO2-restrictions have tightened, slow speed
have become beneÔ¨Ācial, especially for tankers and bulk carriers.
Two added resistance methods based on strip theory have been implemented;
Salvesen‚Äôs method and Maruo‚Äôs far-Ô¨Āeld method. Both calculates for Ô¨Āve
modes of motion and diÔ¨Äerent headings of the ship in a seaway. The results
for zero forward speed are compared to results from the boundary element
tool WAMIT R
‚ÉĚ.
Finally this thesis documents the updates and additions made to I-ship for
the added resistance implementation and general user-friendliness. The cal-
culations for added resistance can now be automated as parameters can be
read from predeÔ¨Āned Ô¨Āles. This makes it possible to run multiple hulls with
various parameters in succession without interference of the user.

Executive summary
The added resistance in waves has its largest impact on the total resistance at
slow speeds. Due to various factors in shipping and environment, operation
at slow and ultra slow speed has become widely used, especially for tankers
and bulk-carriers. Therefore, the topic of added wave resistance has gained
more interest in the resent years, although prediction methods have been in
development since the 1960‚Äôs.
This thesis deals with the implementation of two far-Ô¨Āeld methods, which
are based on strip theory, into the maritime calculation programme I-ship.
The Ô¨Ārst method is Salvesen‚Äôs method, which is known to give fair predic-
tions for moderate speeds, but has been found to give inaccurate results at
zero and very slow speeds. Apart from the added resistance, Salvesen also
calculates the sideways drift force and the yaw moment. The second method
is Maruo‚Äôs far-Ô¨Āeld method, which has been introduced to give a better pre-
diction of the zero speed calculation.
In order to make the calculation more eÔ¨Écient and able to be automatized,
the original procedures for input in I-ship has been changed. Using Ô¨Āles con-
taining the diÔ¨Äerent parameters and settings for the calculations, instead of
manual input from the user, the added resistance calculation can now be run
in succession for many hulls with various parameters.
Two diÔ¨Äerent hull geometries have been tested using the two methods; a
tanker and a ro-pax vessel. The hulls were tested for various speeds and
reasonable results were found for Salvesen‚Äôs method. For Maruo‚Äôs method,
only the implementation of zero-speed formulation was successful.
The zero-speed results for the tanker have been compared to the commer-
cial boundary element tool WAMIT R
‚ÉĚ, which was used as a benchmark. The
comparison showed that Salvesen over-predicted the added resistance, while
Maruo‚Äôs far-Ô¨Āeld method generally gave a much better estimation of the
added resistance. The sideways drift force by Salvesen was found to gener-

other hand yielded decent results for higher speeds.

Introduction
In traditional propulsion prediction for ships, the total resistance on a ship
has been the sum of calm water resistance plus a range of added resistances
consisting of; windage on hull and superstructure, steering corrections by
the rudder, fouling on the submerged surfaces and the wave resistance. The
added resistance due to waves has been used as a rough proportional increase
in the calm water resistance. This increase have been obtained from mea-
suring the needed propulsion power in a seaway and by monitoring the fuel
consumption. However, due to the increasing optimization of hull designs
and propulsion systems, the added resistance has become yet another pa-
rameter to optimize for. Furthermore, the increased focus on CO2-reduction
has an inÔ¨āuence, as initiatives like the ULYSSES project1 has been launched
to reduce the CO2-emissions of ocean-going vessels. At low speeds, the added
wave resistance has a proportionally larger inÔ¨āuence on the total resistance
of a ship than for higher operation speeds. Therefore, especially through
the resent years where slow steaming and ultra slow steaming has become
widespread operation types for tankers and bulk carriers, the added wave
resistance has gained increased interest from shipowners, ship operators and
designers. The incitement to create tools for reasonable predictions of the
added resistance has therefore increased as well.
The added resistance is a second-order steady state force with respect to
the wave amplitude of an incident wave. Commonly, it is assumed that the
added wave resistance is proportional to the square of the wave amplitude.
Being a resistance, the force is pointing in the opposite direction to the lon-
gitudinal forward speed of the ship. At zero speed, the added resistance is
therefore equal to the drift force in longitudinal direction.
Over the last 70 years, a number of diÔ¨Äerent numerical methods have been
proposed with varying accuracy and complexity. Generally, the calculation
methods for added resistance can be divided into two categories; near-Ô¨Āeld
calculations and far-Ô¨Āeld calculations.
The near-Ô¨Āeld methods integrate steady hydrodynamic second-order pres-
sures on the wet body surface to obtain the resistance. These methods gives
exact results from Ô¨Ārst-order potential functions and derivatives.
The far-Ô¨Āeld methods is based on radiated and diÔ¨Äracted wave energy and
momentum Ô¨āux inÔ¨Ānitely far away from the body. These calculations returns

Maruo (1957) [10] proposed the Ô¨Ārst far-Ô¨Āeld method, which were reÔ¨Āned

were developed in the early 70‚Äôs. These pressure integrations were, however,
greatly simpliÔ¨Āed, due to the lack of computational power of the time. Ger-

prediction of ship motions and Salvesen obtained quite satisfactory results.
Faltinsen et al. (1980) [4] introduced an approach using exact direct pressure
integration with a simpliÔ¨Ācation to the estimation at short wavelengths. This
method gave good results for both the short and long wavelengths. In the
same year, Maruo et al. [11] proposed further development on Maruo‚Äôs work
from the 60‚Äôs. This new method calculated the added resistance for oblique
waves, as well as head seas. Most previous work had focussed on head seas,
although Salvesen (1974) noted that the maximum added resistance could
be found for bow quartering seas for certain hulls.
It is not easy to validate the added resistance methods by experimental data,
as it is diÔ¨Écult to obtain accurate measurements - especially for oblique waves
and low wavelengths. When the wavelength is short, it can be hard to ob-
tain reliable results, as the added wave resistance can disappear in noise from
measurements.

was tested in a towing tank with two wave generators and a special rig for
moving the model. Their work yielded promising results for headings from
head-, over beam- to following seas. Hopefully more of these results will be
available in the near future for calculation validation of the methods.

The program oÔ¨Äers a wide range of tools for technical calculations regarding

The program consists of a number of modules, each with a range of sub-

Discussion
For the implementation of added resistance in I-ship, it was deemed necessary
to increase a number of variables that limited the size of calculations. These
variables were the number of speeds, number of headings and limitations to
the discretization-size of the hull geometry.
The ability to run hull-import and added resistance-calculation without GUI
has successfully been added to I-ship. This has been done by creating two
preparation Ô¨Āles for geometry- and added resistance-module respectively. All
the previously manual input can now be loaded from these Ô¨Āles, making the
calculations more automated and streamlined.
Furthermore, the input arguments given to the program during startup have

Conclusion
For this thesis, an existing code for Salvesen‚Äôs method for added wave re-
sistance should be built into the maritime calculation program I-ship. The
calculations in I-ship should be split into geometry deÔ¨Ānitions and strip theo-
ry/added resistance calculation. Furthermore the calculations should be able
to run without graphical user interface in order to automate the calculations.
Knowing that Salvesen‚Äôs method is inaccurate for zero-speed, another method
should be implemented to obtain more accurate results for low speeds.
Implementation in I-ship
Using two preparation Ô¨Āles for hull lines and strip theory, it is possible to
run I-ship without the graphical user interface from a console in the program
MATLAB. The Ô¨Ārst of the two Ô¨Āles makes it possible to create a hull and
load hull geometry from curve Ô¨Āles. The curve Ô¨Āles can either be written

and lines into I-ship.
The second Ô¨Āle holds all parameters, which otherwise would be given as
manual input, for initiating a project trough the strip theory calculations
and Ô¨Ānally the added resistance calculation by either Salvesen‚Äôs or Maruo‚Äôs
method.
Added resistance methods
Salvesen‚Äôs method calculates the added resistance, sideways drift force and
the yaw moment for diÔ¨Äerent headings and speeds.
Meanwhile, Maruo‚Äôs
method only calculates the added resistance for zero speed. Implementation
for higher speeds were unsuccessful for this method.
Finally the two methods have been compared to the boundary element tool
WAMIT R
‚ÉĚfor zero speed calculations. This comparison showed that Salvesen‚Äôs
method over-predicts the added resistance for all wavelengths, while Maruo‚Äôs
method only over-predicts in long waves.
For short waves Maruo under-

‚ÉĚ, but the same trends and peaks are present
in these two methods. For the zero speed calculation Maruo is therefore a
more accurate method than Salvesen.
The sideways drift force and yaw moment calculated by Salvesen was also
compared to the zero speed WAMIT R
‚ÉĚ-results. It was found that peaks in
Salvesen drift force could be related to peaks in WAMIT R
‚ÉĚand that the

much smaller than the WAMIT R

Two diÔ¨Äerent hull geometries were tested with Salvesen and Maruo. The
Ô¨Ārst was a tanker, while the second was a ro-pax vessel. The Maruo-results

Concluding remarks
It can Ô¨Ānally be concluded that a new version of I-ship can be launched with
the ability to calculate added wave resistance.
The new version are able
to load diÔ¨Äerent geometry types, run added resistance calculations with or
without graphical user interface and can be run from a console. Salvesen‚Äôs
method can be used as a fair estimation of the added resistance apart from
at very slow speeds, beam seas and shallow draught. At zero speed however,
Maruo‚Äôs method is found to be a good estimation of the added resistance.
Future work
A prolongation of this project would be to further investigate the faults in
the implemented far-Ô¨Āeld method by Maruo for non-zero speed. Also, side-
ways drift force and yaw moment could be expressed and implemented from
the Maruo formulation.
The NAPA macro might be further updated to make I-ship load centre-points
at both keel and deck level.
Other parts of I-ship, such as propulsion and manoeuvring, might be reformu-
lated to run without graphical user interface to automate these calculations
as well.
Finally a plotting module might be coupled to the added resistance calcula-
tion in I-ship to plot the results internally. This may require an extension of
color-, curve- and point-types present in the current I-ship program to plot
all desired graphs.
