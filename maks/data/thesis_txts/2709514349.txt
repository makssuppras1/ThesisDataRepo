Abstract 
 
Mesopelagic fish are the most abundant vertebrates in the biosphere. Their great biomass and diversity 
along with their migratory behaviour have an essential influence not only on deep oceans but on global 
ecology as well. However, there is still scarce information on the role and behaviour of these organisms. 
Thus, the present study analyses the lipid composition of 28 species of mesopelagic fish (from 9 
different families) sampled at the Iceland Basin, Irminger Sea, Norwegian Sea, North Atlantic (off the 
Iberian Peninsula) and Mediterranean Sea. It was evident that lipid content and composition varied 
significantly across families and locations and that two main categories could be observed: TAG-rich 
species and WE-rich species. The interaction of these two main lipid classes with key environmental 
variables like salinity, depth and temperature and fundamental ontogenic factors, like fish size, was 
explored through a multimodel approach. It revealed that a large portion of the observed variation for 
TAG and WE ratios could be explained by the aforementioned variables. Such results emphasize the 
importance of elucidating the influence of mesopelagic fish’ behaviour and environment on their lipid 
composition, as their correlation has been demonstrated and could be a useful tool to shed a light on 
their role in global ecology.

Introduction 
 
Throughout the long history of the study of the oceans, the pelagic region of the water column has been 
divided into three main zones: epipelagic, mesopelagic and bathypelagic. ‘Meso’ is a prefix of Greek 
origin which means intermediate or middle, thus, mesopelagic fish refers to those inhabiting the water 
mass between the euphotic (epipelagic) zone and the bathypelagic zone. The former is defined from the 
surface down to 200m depth and, as the light is able to penetrate through the whole column, it’s the 
habitat of the majority of aquatic life. The latter extends from 1,000m to 4,000m below surface and, 
despite holding a notably larger volume than the epipelagic zone, the bathypelagic is remarkably less 
densely populated. The reason behind this is the absence of sunlight at those depths, making primary 
production virtually impossible. Thus, mesopelagic fish inhabit the region that lies between the light 
and the darkness. It starts at 200m below surface, where only 1% of sunlight is able to penetrate, and 
extends down to 1,000m deep, where no visible light is available anymore (Robinson et al., 2010; Staby 
& Salvanes, 2019).

The population of mesopelagic fish is distributed worldwide, including circumpolar habitats. Certain 
species are restricted to specific regions of the world while others are present worldwide in a large 
variety of oceans and/or seas (e.g., Benthosema genus). The remarkable variety within mesopelagic fish 
allows for a vast range of environments and ecosystems in which these fish can thrive. Moreover, thirty-
one families of mesopelagic fish have been identified so far, as listed in Table 1, which gives an idea 
of the immense diversity these fish present (Staby & Salvanes, 2019). 
Apart from their remarkable diversity, it’s worth noting their extensive biomass. Over the past few 
decades, great efforts have been made to estimate the actual reach of the population of these marine 
organisms. Considerable research was conducted during the 1970s, when the FAO (Food and 
Agriculture Organization) started to look for new unexplored commercial resources. At that time, the 
estimation of the biomass of mesopelagic fish was mainly based on trawl catch data, which assessed it 
to be approximately one billion tons. However, such estimation techniques were already considered to 
some extent inaccurate, possibly falling short of the actual dimension of the biomass. Consequently, 
further efforts have been made along these past few years as an attempt to narrow these estimations by 
focusing on the study of Deep Scattering Layers (DSL), this is, dense aggregations of fish, squid and 
other micronekton, along with further acoustic data (as hydro acoustic technology advanced) (Proud et 
al., 2019). Such studies yielded numbers that led to a 10-fold higher biomass than the previously 
estimated, approximately 109 tons, making mesopelagic fish the most abundant fish and, indeed, the 
most abundant vertebrate in the biosphere (Irigoien et al., 2014; Staby & Salvanes, 2019). 
Such a large and diverse community of organisms has an immense relevance in a global scale. As 
explained in following sections, it has a great impact on the carbon fluxes of the ocean as well as on the 
behaviour and functioning of the deep ocean. Nevertheless, despite their ecological significance and the 
possible commercial applications derived from their extensive biomass, mesopelagic fish are still 
understudied, with scarce information for many species and communities (Goetsch et al., 2018).  
Therefore, the present study will attempt to shed light on the life and role of these organisms, focusing 
on the correlation between lipid composition and the habitat and behaviour of these fish. The capability 
of deducing the habitat and the role of mesopelagic fish species by their lipidic content would be 
extremely useful to achieve a thorough understanding of the deep oceans. Such correlation has been 
previously reported by numerous studies during the past few decades, describing a certain correlation 
between the species’ behaviour and habitat with their lipid composition and even fatty acid profile 
(Connan et al., 2010; Falk-Petersen et al., 1986; Stowasser et al., 2012). However, the sample size 
and/or the species/genus diversity covered by the studies are commonly limited. The majority of studies 
performed analyses on species from similar habitats or geographical regions or counted with a limiting 
number of species from different habitats (Childress & Nygaard, 1973; Culkin & Morris, 1970). 
Moreover, despite certain patterns and relationships had been suggested, contradictions and 
discrepancies across the studies are frequent, many of them concluding that further research is needed 
==PAGE:7==

for a solid resolution (Lea et al., 2002). Thus, pursuing to clarify the aforementioned correlation, the 
present study will explore interspecific as well as intraspecific variation across a wide range of locations 
and more than 20 species, regarding a broad set of factors such as ontogeny, geographical region and 
behaviour. Furthermore, as background for the present study, a general overview on the life history, 
behaviour and biology of these organisms will be provided. 
 
 
Life History 
 
Mesopelagic fish have been in this world since time immemorial. They have been living on this earth 
for millions of years, evolving and adapting to the continuously changing environment. As a result of 
the fossilization of their remnants throughout history, today we have certain hints about them being 
present along the evolution of life on earth. Remnants in the form of isolated otoliths, teeth and scales 
from sedimentary rocks were found and analysed to elucidate the origin of these fish (Giles Miller et 
al., 2002). According to the fossil discoverers, remnants belonging to Diaphus and Ceratoscopelus 
genera belonged to the cretaceous period, this is, 145 million years ago. These have been the oldest 
fossils found for mesopelagic fish, along with other relics belonging to the Paleocene (65 million), 
Eocene (54 million years ago) and Oligocene (33 million years ago) (Catul et al., 2011). 
The majority of mesopelagic fish are small, often ranging between 2-15 cm long. However, with such 
a wide variety of families and genera, there are a few larger species that inhabit the mesopelagic zone, 
such as the blue whiting (Micromesistius poutassou), which weight and length can exceed  800g and 
50cm respectively (maximum dimensions recorded) (Fish Base, 2022). Derived from the generally 
small size characteristic of these fish , they usually present low fecundities, most of them lying within 
a range from hundred to a few thousand eggs. Nevertheless, as they have a significantly shorter 
generation time than most epipelagic species, they often show a higher reproduction rate than long-
lived epipelagic species with higher fecundities but notably longer generation times.  
 
However, the reason for higher survival rates of mesopelagic species in early life stages, despite their 
larvae growth rates are comparable to those of other communities, is still unclear. Fecundity and egg 
production can be affected by numerous factors, including environmental variations, food availability, 
breeding season duration, and spawning frequencies, making it difficult to clarify the reasons behind it 
(Rizzo & Bazzoli, 2019). In this case, a feasible explanation could lie in the differences in advective 
loss. As mesopelagic larvae go into metamorphosis (during which the skin becomes pigmented and 
light organs develop) they already start migrating into deeper areas, thus, they are less prone to be 
passively transported by surface currents and end up in threating environments. What’s more, such

small epipelagic fishes) generally decrease with depth, and physical conditions below the mixed layer 
(approximately 30m deep) are stable (Watanabe et al., 2010). Fig.1 shows the correlation between age 
and depth observable in most mesopelagic fish species.

Among some myctophids, this ontogenetic shift in habitat is believed to be recorded in the otoliths as 
accessory primordia, that is, structures that appear as extra nuclei outside the true nucleus of the otoliths. 
Furthermore, these structures in the inner ear (otoliths) are truly useful sources of information regarding 
age and growth of fish species (mesopelagic included), as annuli or daily increments in the otoliths can 
be usually found (Staby & Salvanes, 2019). 
Regarding the growth pattern, certain particularities can be observed for mesopelagic fish. As opposed 
to the usual asymptotic behaviour, in which the majority of fish grow towards an asymptotic size, 
mesopelagic fish can grow almost following a linear profile with age. Then again, certain exceptions 
can be found, as several species do grow increasingly slower as they get older, tending to an asymptotic 
profile (Fig.2).

Several studies also highlight how growth-rate strategies have a strong connection to vertical migration 
behaviour, which will be discussed in following sections. As a regular basis, the daily vertically 
migrating species have relatively slower growth than many of the non-migrators. A solid example can 
be found in the myctophid family, which, being in their majority migrators, have typical asymptotic 
growth curves. In contrast, some deeper/nonmigrating mesopelagic species exhibit more rapid linear 
indeterminate growth patterns (e.g., Borostomias panamensis or Lampanyctus regalis). Studies 
exploring the correlation between growth rates and energy budgets for migratory and non-migratory 
species conclude that, in general, such a difference could be due to different priorities regarding energy 
usage and storage. The reasoning behind it is that migratory species require high amounts of energy to 
undertake their diel migrations, thus, prioritizing energy storage over size is essential. Whereas the non-
migratory species can invest that energy in attaining sizes that minimize predation risk, which can yield 
linear profiles as the ones observed in Fig.2 (Caiger et al., 2021).   
 
In addition, sexual size dimorphism (SSD) can be observed in many mesopelagic species. Life-history 
theory predicts that SSD will arise from both natural and sexual selection, such that variation in 
reproductive success leads to differences in the optimal body size of each sex. Male-biased SSD is 
commonly associated with a high degree of sexual selection (e.g., male-male competition, intrasexual 
combat or territoriality),  whereas female-biased SSD is typically attributed to the positive correlation 
between maternal size and fecundity. In those mesopelagic fish species in which SSD is present, the 
average size of females is larger than for males, mainly attributed to the fecundity selection just 
==PAGE:10==
 
6 
 
mentioned. However, to this also adds the fact that some species (e.g., Cyclothone microdon) can 
change sex with increasing age, this is, with increasing size, changing from male to female sex as they 
grow older (Horne et al., 2020).  
Moreover, other secondary sexual characters can also be found in some species. Among myctophids, 
males have a supracaudal light organ, whereas females have an infracaudal light organ, which could be 
associated with courtship behaviour, despite their functionality is still unclear.  
  
Geographical variations have been found to affect numerous factors such as morphology, life history or 
genetics. Meristic characters of Benthosema glaciale and Notoscopelus elongatus differ between the 
Mediterranean and the North Atlantic, which suggest genetic heterogeneity. Furthermore, populations 
of Benthosema glaciale in West Norwegian fiords are genetically different from each other and from 
the Norwegian Sea population, even presenting a faster growth rate towards a lower maximum length 
in the fiord populations. Other species, such as Ceratoscopleus maderensis, have shown signs of 
geographical variation in the morphology of the otoliths, presenting clear variations among specimens 
from the Canary Islands (Spain), the North East Atlantic and the Strait of Messina (Central 
Mediterranean Sea) (Jawad et al., 2018).   
 
All the factors stated above only serve to emphasise the great richness and diversity present in 
mesopelagic fish. With the greatest biomass among vertebrates and with fossil findings dating as far 
back as the cretaceous period, we can only acknowledge the vital importance these organisms have and 
have had throughout a substantial part of life history on Earth. Such a long and varied life history leads 
us to the conclusion, once again, that significantly more research should be carried out to understand 
the role of these organisms in global ecology.  
 
 
Behaviour 
 
One of the main features of mesopelagic fish’ behaviour is the migration patterns many of these species 
follow on a daily basis. Vertical and horizontal habitat selection of mesopelagic fish has been studied 
indirectly by observing sound scattering layers (SSLs) and individual fish tracks with echo sounders, in 
combination with depth stratified pelagic trawl samples (Geoffroy et al., 2019; Klevjer et al., 2016). As 
a result, four fundamental migration patterns have been described over the years. The first group is 
conformed by migrants showing clear day-night habitat separation with peak abundance above 200 m 
at night, thus, diel vertical migrators (DVM) that swim to shallower areas at night from their usual 
deeper daytime habitat. The second group is integrated by semi-migrants, in which part of the 
==PAGE:11==
 
7 
 
population often remains in the daytime habitat at night. Moreover, the distribution depths of migratory 
and nonmigratory individuals do not overlap. Passive-migrants constitute the third group, in which there 
is no separation of day-night habitats, but the upper limit of daytime distribution depth shifts to a 
shallower layer at night, probably as the fish follow migratory prey. Finally, the fourth group is 
conformed by non-migrating mesopelagic fish, which stay at the same depth range during both day and 
night (Catul et al., 2011; Staby & Salvanes, 2019).  
 
Again, due to the large biomass and diversity these fish present, there is significant variation among 
species regarding migration patterns. What’s more, whether fish migrate, the timing of migration, and 
the vertical extent of the migration varies with species, ontogeny, season, environmental conditions, 
and prey availability. As it can be observed in Fig.3 below, notable differences in these patterns were 
obtained for different geographical regions through the analysis of acoustic backscatter (a proxy for 
biomass), which was recorded through echosounders highly sensitive to the echoes produced by 
organisms (specially species with gas-filled swim bladders) (Klevjer et al., 2016) . 
 
However, despite clear geographical variations, a significant migration pattern can be observed among 
all the locations analysed. Numerous widespread species (including lanternfish, dragonfish and 
hatchetfish) do undertake daily vertical migrations to forage in the productive surface layer. Moreover, 
migrating fish travel at varying speeds, depending upon several factors such as the daytime depth of the 
migrating fish, whether the fish is ascending or descending or the prevailing light intensity. Speeds of 
up to 90 m h-1 have been  recorded and, for shallower distributed fish, speeds tend to vary between 30 
and 60 m h-1 (Staby & Salvanes, 2019).

Fig.3 from Klevjer et al (2016) demonstrates that, within the scattering layer population, high variability 
can be observed in terms of migration. The entire population does not necessarily migrate to the surface 
every night, , as previously mentioned, depending on the life stage and even the sex the fish show 
different migration patterns. For instance, in Norwegian fjords a considerable proportion of the adult 
population of Benthosema glaciale remains at daytime depths during the night, whereas juveniles are 
most numerous in the surface layer. Evidence for depth segregation between the sexes has also been 
reported, e.g., female Maurolicus muelleri specimens tending to stay deeper than males at daytime 
during the Spring in temperate regions. Moreover, according to the season, female specimens of 
Lampanyctodes hectoris have been reported to inhabit either shallower or deeper waters than males 
(Staby & Salvanes, 2019). 
 
Nevertheless, regardless of ontogeny, species and sex, light intensity is considered the fundamental 
factor affecting DVM as well as the daytime depth of mesopelagic fish (and other organisms) (Staby & 
Aksnes, 2011). As a consequence, geographical region and seasonality are two extremely relevant 
variables to consider, as both have crucial influence on light intensity and availability. When 
atmospheric conditions change or vary, subsequent changes in cloud cover and/or water turbidity 
==PAGE:13==
 
9 
 
usually follow, having a tremendous impact in the extent of light penetration in the water column. Thus, 
depending on these changes, diverse DVM strategies are adopted by mesopelagic species to adapt to 
the environment. Many studies already agree that migrant species commonly stay in shallow areas to 
forage at night and then descend deeper to avoid predation. Furthermore, certain studies suggest that 
the ratio between mortality risk and feeding rate tends to the minimum at the so called ‘antipredator 
window’, in which the light allows for visual feeding while minimizing predation risk. Migration 
patterns as well as vertical position could be also adjusted to reach that window, specially while 
foraging. However, as already mentioned, extensive variation can be observed among species and 
within species (Liu et al., 2022;Staby & Salvanes, 2019).  
 
Oxygen concentration is considered as another dominant factor affecting DVM and daytime depth. 
Oxygen minimum zones (OMZ) have been observed to have a significant impact on migration 
behaviours, again with high variability among species and areas. Some species tend to restrict their 
habitat to avoid OMZ while others have been found to perform DVM to the upper OMZ to avoid aerobic 
predators during daytime.

Other patterns have been observed such as migrating through the OMZ core to reach a desired region 
in the water column. Nonetheless, in well oxygenated areas, light intensity remains the fundamental 
factor affecting DVM. In Fig.4 it can be observed how, depending on the geographical region, oxygen 
is reduced to different extents as depth increases; thus, not the entirety of mesopelagic fish deal with

OMZs in the same way and some almost don’t even have to deal with them at all (Liu et al., 2022; 
Robinson et al., 2010). 
 
Such migrational behaviour, combined with the great biomass of mesopelagic fish, has a great impact 
on oceanic ecosystems as well as on global ecology in general. Understanding the mesopelagic habitat 
is crucial to build a comprehensive model of how trophic interactions and food webs actually work. 
Likewise, these migration patterns have a strong influence on vertical carbon flux. By migrating to 
epipelagic regions to forage, mesopelagic fish provide means of rapid transfer of carbon from the 
epipelagic region to the deep oceans, where it can be sequestered for centuries or more. Despite primary 
production in the surface drives the uptake of atmospheric CO2, it only begins to be sequestered once it 
is transferred below the mixed layer and is no longer in contact with the atmosphere. Thus, migrating 
mesopelagic fish provide an active transfer channel of Carbon to the deep ocean through excretion, 
defecation, mortality and respiration. In fact, studies have assessed the respiration of these fish to 
account for up to 47% of the gravitational particulate organic carbon flux in specific geographical areas. 
Per contra, direct measurements of mesopelagic fish respiration as well as the inclusion of other 
contributions to carbon transport (mortality, excretion and defecation) are needed in order to yield a 
realistic estimate of the contribution of these fish. Such contribution is very likely to be higher than the 
estimations made so far and thorough research is still needed to understand the relevance of these 
organisms (Davison et al., 2013; Belcher et al., 2019.). 
However, extensive vertical migrations pose a significant problem to the study of these organisms, 
making them not ideal for laboratory experiments. Both the high-pressure differences experimented by 
migrating fish as well as their own migration instincts make the recreation of their habitat remarkably 
difficult. Attempts to keep light-sensitive mesopelagic fish in aquaria have failed because fish attempted 
to migrate downwards, bumping into the walls until they became lifeless. Certain specifically designed 
spherical containers with water jets have been implemented in an attempt to recreate a suitable 
environment for the study of these fish. Despite the efforts, fish have only survived a maximum of 72h 
in the research projects carried out so far (Staby & Salvanes, 2019). 
 
Adaptations 
 
Mesopelagic fish are subjected to strong gradients in multiple environmental factors that influence their 
life strongly and directly. Vertical gradients in light intensity, temperature, pressure or oxygen content 
are just some of them, leading to multiple and varied morphological and physiological adaptations. 
==PAGE:15==
 
11 
 
Derived from the different feeding habits and preferences present among families and genera of 
mesopelagic fish, numerous morphological adaptations can be found. Generally , mouth morphologies 
are characterized by large horizontal mouths with small teeth, typical of fish that feed on large prey, 
combined with fine gillrakers, typical of fish that feed on small prey. Such an arrangement allows for 
consumption of a broad range of prey; however, diet and prey selectivity often change for almost every 
species studied so far, leading to different mouth morphologies.

Not all mesopelagic fish show a generalist behaviour in terms of feeding, in fact, they can be categorised 
within three major trophic guilds: zooplanktivores, micronektivores, which feed on small fish and 
cephalopods, and generalists, which feed on wide range of taxa such as crustaceans, gastropods or fish, 
among others. Consequently, varying jaw sizes can be found between different species, with plankton 
eaters often presenting small jaws when compared to piscivorous predators (Staby & Salvanes, 2019). 
What’s more, many species have shown changing feeding patterns as their mouths become bigger while 
they grow. Prey sizes have been observed to increase as the size of the predators (and resultantly, their 
mouths) rise, specially for small species such as Cyclothone braueri or Argyropelecus hemigymnus 
(Fig.6). Depending on the species, diet variation regarding the predator size is observable or not, making 
difficult to generate a general statement for all the mesopelagic fish species studied (Bernal et al., 2015).

Furthermore, as in many other life aspects, mesopelagic fish also present adaptations to the ever-
changing environment regarding feeding and diet. Some predators have been found to fed onto different 
prey according to season and prey availability, such as Benthosema glaciale, which preyed on fishes on 
summer and copepods and ostracods in autumn (following the peak of these in the habitat studied, 
Balearic Islands, Spain) (Bernal et al., 2015).

Light stimuli play a significant role in these fish’ morphological and physiological adaptations. Most 
of these adaptations are in fact direct or indirect responses to such stimuli. Due to the low light 
penetration levels at mesopelagic depths, eyes of mesopelagic fish tend to be large with pure-rod retinas, 
characterized with high densities of the photosensitive pigment rhodopsin. Some species have 
developed tubular eyes with large lenses, which provide a larger field of binocular vision as well as 
higher resolution. Such adaptations enable these fish to see in dim downwelling light, being able to pick 
out small planktonic organisms with no or very low visible light around (Haedrich, 1996; Staby & 
Salvanes, 2019).  
Regarding predator avoidance, light availability is crucial to the different adaptations presented by these 
fish. Depending on the depth and thus, the light, varied blending and camouflaging adaptations are 
developed, as the requirements are not the same throughout the water column. Due to the usually 
==PAGE:17==
 
13 
 
shallow distribution of mesopelagic larvae, they are commonly transparent to light, making their 
detection notably more difficult than if their bodies were coloured. However, during and after 
metamorphosis, silvering of the lateral sides or blackening of the skin takes place, as the young fish 
move down to dimer and darker depths. Most of mesopelagic fish in adult stages present silvery sides, 
silvery iris and a dark back. This way, they would be tough to visualize for predators located above 
them, due to the dark back, or at the same depth as them, as the silvering sides recreate the dim light 
present in the environment, therefore hiding their silhouette. Some species, particularly the ones living 
in higher mesopelagic regions, present photophores (the light-emitting organs responsible for the silvery 
sides) ventrally located, as for predators looking from below, dark-bottomed prey would be easy to 
visualize against a light background (Haedrich, 1996; Staby & Salvanes, 2019).  
 
 
 
 
Fig. 7. Left: Benthosema glaciale, obtained from the SUMMER project. Right: Notoscopelus bolini (Gewin, 2016). 
 
 
Additionally, derived from light stimuli, mesopelagic fish had to develop certain adaptations to enable 
them to undertake DVM. They present a particular muscular organization for sustained efforts. Such 
organization counts with a large proportion of red muscle fibres, which are rich in fat, glycogen, 
myoglobin and mitochondria and are richly supplied with blood and oxygen. While white muscle fibres 
work more anaerobically in short bursts, red muscle fibres are capable of sustaining longer efforts. 
However, the high presence of these fibres has a significant metabolic cost, leading to a fine equilibrium 
between red muscle presence and  the vertical distance covered during migration. Those species 
undertaking the most pronounced migrations are the ones with the highest proportions of red muscle 
(Staby & Salvanes, 2019). 
Moreover, there is a difference of a factor of 15 in metabolic rates between species that live at the 
surface and those that come no shallower than 800 m. Such a difference seems to be too great to be 
explained by decreases in temperature, oxygen content, decrease in food availability or increase in 
pressure, characteristic features of high depth environments. Instead, several lines of research indicate 
that such a drastic reduction in metabolic rate could be related to a decrease in swimming capabilities 
==PAGE:18==
 
14 
 
with increasing depths. This idea is supported by the reduction of visible light and predation pressure 
as we go deeper in the water column. Swimming capabilities lose relevance when the predators can’t 
rely on prey visualization to feed, thus, escaping predators as well as chasing down prey become unusual 
practices in deep habitats. Hence, the low metabolic rates found in deep-living fish species would result 
from the relaxation of selection for locomotor abilities, rather than from adaptations to the deep 
environment (Kawall et al., 2002).

Lipid Extraction 
 
The lipids were extracted following Folch’s extraction method, which was implemented on whole 
samples, including gut. The samples, which were frozen at -80 ºC since their collection, were thawed, 
weighed (±0.001) and standard length measured to the nearest mm. After taking pictures of every 
species, the fish was processed according to their weight, as follows: 
- 
Samples below 7cm and/or 3g: samples were placed in a 30 mL extraction glass vial (Schott) 
and extracted with Chloroform:Methanol:KCl according to Folch’s ratio of 2:1:0.75 (by 
volume). For this range of weight and size were 7 mL and 5.5 mL respectively. Subsequently, 
the IKA Ultra Turrax T18 disperser was used to homogenize the sample at 15.000 rpm for a 
minimum of 30 seconds (in case it wasn’t enough for complete dispersion another 30 seconds 
were added). 
- 
Samples above 7cm and/or 3g: larger samples (whole fish) were first placed in liquid Nitrogen 
for 2 minutes. They were then broken into pieces so that the sample in its entirety could fit into 
the grinding chamber, which was coupled to the IKA Tube-Mill Control (1.1). The sample was 
then pulverized for 2 minutes at 5000 rpm. Once the sample was powdered, a sub sample of a 
known weight, approximately 2 g were placed in the 30 mL extraction glass vial (Schott) along 
with the corresponding proportions of Methanol and KCl (again 7 mL and 5.5 mL respectively). 
In case the powder was not properly dispersed, the IKA Ultra Turrax T18 was employed to 
achieve complete homogenization at 15.000 rpm for 30 seconds. 
Both procedures followed the same line after this first step. Thus, according to Folch’s method, to attain 
the volume ratio of 2:1:0.75 Chloroform:Mehanol:KCl, 10 mL of Chloroform were added to the 
samples which were then vortexed (IKA Lab Dancer s040), topped up with Nitrogen (to avoid lipid 
oxidation) and stored at 4 ºC to allow extraction overnight. 
The samples were vortexed and centrifuged at 2500 rpm for one minute. The resulting lower layer, 
containing Chloroform and lipids, was drawn off the vial with the help of a pipette and filtered through 
a phase separating paper (Fisher) to further clean the sample and remove remaining water phase. A final 
volume of 4 mL of Chloroform was added to the original sample (30 mL vial) and the same vortex, 
centrifuge and filtration process was implemented in order to collect any lipidic remnant. The resulting 
lipid samples obtained after filtration were collected in 14 mL vials, which had been individually 
weighed on an analytical balance (±0.000001g). Finally, samples were topped up with Nitrogen and 
stored at -80 ºC until analysis.

Thin Layer Chromatography (TLC) and Flame Ionization Detector (FID) 
 
Prior to the analysis of the lipid samples through the TLC-FID system, samples were split to achieve 
uniformity regarding their lipid concentration. To this aim, the samples in the 14 mL vials were dried 
with Nitrogen gas and weighed on an analytical balance (±0.000001g). The difference between both 
weights, the one just recorded and that of the empty vial, allowed us to compute the total amount of 
lipids present. The dried samples were then resuspended according to their lipidic content and a certain 
volume was drawn into 2 mL vials, aiming at a final concentration of approximately 10 mg/mL. 
Chloroform was then added to make up to a volume of 1 mL. Samples with particularly low lipid 
concentrations were split into a lower final volume of 0.5 mL in order to reach the desired 10 mg/mL 
concentration. These new set of subsamples were topped with Nitrogen and stored at -80 ºC until their 
analysis through the TLC-FID system. 
Individual lipid classes were separated by Thin Layer Chromatography (TLC). This separation was 
carried out through two phases: the stationary phase and the mobile phase. The former was implemented 
with sets of silica-coated quartz rods, i.e., chromarods (SES GmbH), onto which the samples were 
spotted with the aid of an automated sample spotter (Model SES 3200/IS-01). Either 1 µL or 2 µL. Each 
sample was analysed in triplicates. Afterwards, a mobile phase was implemented in order to separate 
the lipids along the chromarods. It consisted of two different development procedures based on two 
solvent systems: a first non-polar solvent system resolving non-polar compounds and a second non-
polar solvent system to separate hydrocarbon from wax esters and triacylglycerides from diacylglyceryl 
ether. Thus, the first solvent system used was conformed of hexane/diethyl ether/acetic acid (60:17:0.2, 
by volume) and the second was based on hexane/diethyl ether (96:4 vol./vol.).  
 
Once the samples were spotted onto the chromarods and prior to their development through the mobile 
phase, they were subjected to a 10-minute hydration process. They were placed in a glass chamber lined 
with a filter paper (to ensure optimal vapour saturation) that contained 80 mL of a supersaturated salt 
solution (40% NaCl). After hydration, the chromarods were developed with the two solvent systems 
mentioned above. Two different glass chambers (again lined with filter paper) contained 80 mL of the 
first and second solvent system, respectively. The samples were developed for 30 minutes in each glass 
chamber, with a 5-minute hydration in the salt-containing chamber in between both procedures. Finally, 
the chromarods were oven-dried at 100 ºC for 2 minutes and immediately analysed to avoid adsorption 
of atmospheric contaminants.  
 
The analysis was performed using an Iatroscan MK 5 TLC-FID analyser which allowed to determine 
the amount of individual lipid classes present in the samples. Operation conditions for the Iatroscan 
were 18 L min-1 air flow, 190 mL min-1 Hydrogen flow and a scan rate of 30 s per chromarod. It was 
==PAGE:23==
 
19 
 
coupled to a compatible computer to record the chromatograms. Subsequently, the peaks obtained for 
each sample were quantified through the i-ChromStar 6.4 software. The peaks were identified by 
comparison to the retention time of known standards of wax esters (from the copepod Calanus 
finmarchicus), triacylglycerol (tripalmitic acid) and cholesterol. 
It’s worth noting that the rods were passed through the FID scanner immediately before sample spotting 
in order to activate silica and assure the absence of any lipidic remnants or contaminants from previous 
analyses. Furthermore, regarding cleanliness, the rods were also burnt (scanned through FID) at the end 
of every analysis, ensuring their neatness for following uses. However, after a certain number of 
analyses, permanent adsorption of lipidic remnants and/or contaminants could be observed on the 
chromarods when scanned. Consequently, a cleaning protocol was implemented.  
For such, the rods were developed in a glass chamber lined with a filter paper and filled with 30 mL of 
a solvent conformed of Chloroform/Methanol/H2O (Milli-Q) (50:20:2, by volume) for 15 minutes. They 
were then oven-dried for 2 minutes at 100 ºC and burnt in the Iatroscan. Afterwards, each rod was 
placed individually in an 35-50 % solution of Nitric acid overnight, which ensured the removal of any 
adsorbed contaminant and/or lipidic remnant. Finally, they were rinsed with distilled water and again 
oven-dried at 100 ºC for 2 minutes before being burnt in the Iatroscan. 
 
 
 
Statistical Analyses  
 
Statistical analyses were performed using R statistical tools. Differences in lipid content (as percentage 
of wet weight) were analysed for all the families and locations involved. Due to irregular sample sizes 
and a lack of normally distributed data, Kruskal-Wallis one-way ANOVA on ranks (H test) was 
performed to explore the differences in lipid content among families and locations. Consequently, 
according to the p-value yielded from such tests, the differences in lipid content were considered 
significant (p-value < 0.05) or not. In case of a significant difference, further analysis on the lipid 
content was carried out through regression models.  
The same statistical procedure was implemented for the lipid composition of the samples. Three lipid 
classes were taken into consideration as percentage of total lipids: wax esters (WE), triacylglycerides 
(TAG) and phospholipids (PL). Thus, total lipids were taken as the sum of those three main classes and 
ratios for WE and TAG were computed accordingly for each of the samples. Kruskal-Wallis one-way 
ANOVA on ranks was then performed on WE and TAG ratios across families and locations and, in case 
the differences turned out to be significant (p-value < 0.05), the ratios were further analysed through 
more comprehensive regression models (explained in the following section).

Regression Models 
 
Using a statistical modelling approach, two main types of models were implemented to assess the degree 
to which the variation in the lipid class ratios could be explained by a certain number of explanatory 
variables (predictors). These variables were conformed by a set of environmental predictors, 
temperature, depth and salinity, included to account for the differences derived from habitat and 
geographical region, weight of the fish, included as a proxy for size, and species, which was included 
as a fixed factor to account for the variability derived from differences across species attributed to other 
variables such as diet, foraging strategy or behaviour (to name  few). The same set of explanatory 
variables was used in both modelling approaches: (1) generalized additive models (GAMs) and (2) 
random forests (RFs). Additionally, after model fitting, the resulting statistical relationships as well as 
the derived response curves between the lipid class ratios and the explanatory variables were compared 
across methods to assess the robustness and sensitivity of our results. 
 Furthermore, we performed a k-fold cross-validation analysis to formally compare prediction skill 
across methods by fitting the models to randomly selected subsets of the original data (amounting to 
75% of the observations). We then assessed the predictive accuracy of both models by comparing the 
observed values with the predicted estimates for the remaining test data sets. This procedure was 
repeated 100 times (each time with a new random and serially independent draw) in order to assess the 
range of uncertainty associated with the predictions (i.e., reflected by the mean square errors ,MSEs, of 
model predictions) (Lindegren et al., 2020; Pecuchet et al., 2017). 
Therefore, we used generalized additive models (GAMs) as they allow for non-linear relationships 
between response and predictor variables. Both lipid class ratios were modelled as a function of the 
predictor variables and were fitted as the response variable with a Beta distribution. In R, such 
distribution allows to implement regression for beta distributed data within an interval of (0,1). Thus, 
due to the presence of zeroes in our data, both for WE and TAG ratios, the response variables were 
truncated to the interval [0.001 , 0.999] to avoid stability issues. The basis dimension (k) for the 
smoothing spline function (s) was restricted to 3 to allow for possible nonlinearities as well as limiting 
the flexibility of model fitting and reducing the risk of overfitting (Lindegren et al., 2020). For both 
lipid classes analysed, a GAM was built following the structure below:

All the possible models, containing from zero to a maximum of 5 explanatory variables, were fitted and 
evaluated using the package ‘MuMIn’ (Multi-Model Inference) in R (Barton, 2022), and the best model 
(defined as the one with the lowest Akaike information criterion, AIC) was adopted. Apart from  
applying standard model checking diagnostics, the relative variable importance (RVI) of each variable 
was computed, based on the sum of weights yielded by the ‘MuMIn’ package for each variable, which 
essentially evaluates the number of models in which each variable was included.  
Random forests were implemented to estimate the nonlinear partial effects of individual variables, 
which are obtained by averaging out the effects of all other predictors on the quantity of interest. Thus, 
it included the same variables as the GAM (Eq.1), but due to the fewer constraints the RF is subjected 
to, it is able to capture interactions between variables that cannot easily be achieved with additive 
models.  
Random forest modelling approach is based on decision trees that rely on bagging, so that individual 
regression trees within the RF are trained on randomly selected subsets of the data. For the present study 
the number of trees was set to a 1000. In each tree, the data is recursively partitioned at each instance 
(i.e., node) based on the strongest associated predictor. Thus, at every node, a random subset of 
predictors is evaluated for partitioning and the final tree prediction is given by the average value of the 
data within each branch. Then, the final forest prediction is computed as the average prediction among 
every tree in the forest. This way, RFs are capable of reproducing complex nonlinear shapes in single 
and multiple dimensions, making them suitable for ecological applications in which complex 
relationships are common (Lindegren et al., 2020).  
Moreover, RVIs were quantified for the variables included in the random forest models. By randomly 
permuting the values of the variable of interest and measuring the percentage of increment of the MSE, 
the relative importance for each variable was quantified. Thus, a variable that caused a large increase 
in the MSE of the model when randomized, would be considered a significant contributor to the fit of 
the model.

Discussion 
 
Previous studies have shown that the main lipid types in mesopelagic fish are WE or TAG, often one 
dominating the other. Moreover, previous research indicates that the role of these lipids in life history 
strategies is of interest, describing relationships between lipid composition and the behaviour and 
habitat of mesopelagic fish (Childress & Nygaard, 1973; Lee & Hirota, 1973). Consequently, the focus 
of this study was set on the lipid classes to shed a light on whether there is an emerging pattern in what 
is the driving force in the accumulation of WE over TAG; as both lipid classes have been linked to 
different traits and functionalities, such as long-term energy storage and buoyancy for WE and readily 
==PAGE:40==
 
36 
 
available energy in the case of TAG (Connan et al., 2010; Lea et al., 2002). Despite not being within 
the main interests and focus of this study, total lipid content was also of interest, so further analysis was 
carried out besides studying the variance across families and locations, which turned out to be 
significant (Table 4). Thus, a GAM was implemented to explore the response of total lipid content (% 
of wet weight) to the same variables considered for WE and TAG analyses (supplementary figure SF.1). 
It was evident how it followed previously described relationships with certain variables such as fish 
weight. The non-linear decrease in total lipid content with increasing fish weight is in agreement with 
previous findings that suggested that specimens reaching larger sizes would have lower lipidic contents 
to lower the energy required per unit of growth (Childress & Nygaard, 1973).  
The GAM also revealed a solid relationship between lipid content and salinity, with the former 
following a clear linear decrease as salinity increased. However, in this study such negative relationship 
is probably to be more related to the geographical area rather than salinity itself. As previous literature 
has suggested, lipid-rich diets characteristic of higher latitudes, which present lower salinities than the 
Mediterranean Sea, are likely to be the reason behind the high lipid contents observed in lower salinity 
basins like the Norwegian and Irminger Seas as well as the Iceland Basin (Falk-Petersen et al., 1986; 
Lee & Hirota, 1973).  
The positive correlation of total lipids with depth is not in agreement with previous studies (Childress 
& Nygaard, 1973; Reinhardt & Van Vleet, 1986). Despite some studies have suggested different 
patterns relating lipid content and depth, they often concluded that further research was needed (Lea et 
al., 2002). Yet, the resulting relationship could derive from the shortage of samples from commonly 
deep-dwelling families, like Bathylagidae, as the majority of the samples analysed belonged within the 
surface and 500m depth. Therefore, as the other variables tested (depth, salinity and fish weight) were 
in agreement with previous studies, further research could be necessary to validate such relationship, 
including a larger number of samples from deeper habitats (500-1000m) and studying other frequently 
depth-related parameters like water content. Moreover, the explanation power of the model was lower 
than those implemented for WE and TAG, with lower R2 and % of explained deviance (supplementary 
table ST.1). Thus, this could illustrate how total lipid content is dependent on more variables than 
particular lipid classes like WE or TAG, requiring a broader comprehensive study to elucidate its 
relationship with the fish’ environment as well as behaviour.  
The mesopelagic fish analysed in the present study could be categorised in two major groups: WE-
containing species and TAG-containing species (Fig.13 and Fig.15, respectively). As already indicated 
by previous research, both categories could be found within the same family, suggesting evidence for 
interspecific variation among same-family species (Connan et al., 2010). Even the species that presented 
both lipid classes (less than 25% of the samples analysed) could be classified within the aforementioned 
categories as they were clearly either TAG-rich or WE-rich, e.g: specimens of Arctozenus risso sampled 
==PAGE:41==
 
37 
 
in the Irminger Sea had WE and TAG ratios of 0.4 and 0.04 respectively, as well as specimens from the 
Norwegian Sea, with 0.5 (WE) and 0.04 (TAG). Nonetheless, some species didn’t have neither WE nor 
TAG but only phospholipids. Vinciguerria nimbaria, the only species sampled from Phosichthyidae 
family, only presented phospholipids (among the three lipid classes analysed, WE, TAG and PL), 
underlining once again the great diversity displayed by mesopelagic fish. However, such a low sample 
size (two samples from the same species at the same location) poses a limitation regarding the study of 
interspecific and intraspecific variation within Phosichthyidae family. Previous research has reported 
several species with such lipid profiles (based on PL), however, these reports were performed mainly 
on species belonging to Myctophidae and Stomiidae (Wilson, 2004), making it difficult to generate a 
solid generic statement for Vinciguerria nimbaria and/or Phosichthyidae family regarding their lipid 
composition.  
The location emerged as an important determinant in the WE content of the fish, which was also the 
case when focusing only on the family Myctophydae across locations (Table 5). Such effect is clearly 
evident in the response displayed in Fig.17.A, which shows how both models revealed a notable non-
linear decrease in WE ratios with increasing temperatures. The same way previous research points at 
an increase of mesopelagic species’ total lipids with increasing latitudes (lower temperatures) due to 
higher lipid contents in prey, WE have also been observed to follow the same pattern (Lee & Hirota, 
1973). Furthermore, the only species sampled by the three research cruises involved in this study, 
Benthosema glaciale (a well-known WE-rich species) (Falk-Petersen et al., 1986), allowed for direct 
comparison of the ratios between the three main geographical areas studied (off Iceland, Norway and 
Iberian Peninsula). Therefore, WE ratios of Benthosema glaciale specimens caught by the three cruises 
were explored (supplementary figure SF.2). It revealed that the WE ratios of Benthosema glaciale 
followed an essentially linear relationship with temperature, clearly decreasing with increasing 
temperatures, thus confirming the mentioned relationship between WE and temperature. Moreover, 
such results could be interpreted as an indicator of intraspecific variation regarding lipid composition 
partly due to differences in geographical area, despite numerous other factors are also likely to have an 
impact. 
However, other studies have shown that certain mesopelagic fish species are capable of modifying their 
dietary fats to suit their own specific characteristics and requirements, which would contradict the 
studies pointing at the diet as accountable for lipid composition (M. Kayama & Nevenzel, 1974). Thus, 
such decrease in WE ratios with increasing temperatures could be related to depth rather than latitude. 
Previous literature has shown increments in WE ratios of mesopelagic fish with increasing depths 
(which could also translate to decreasing temperatures, supplementary figure SF.3) (Mitsu Kayama & 
Ikeda, 1975; Lee & Hirota, 1973). Features such as buoyancy and slow hydrolysis/re-esterification 
would make wax esters more suitable than TAG for deep-dwelling non-migrating species, which require 
==PAGE:42==
 
38 
 
less readily available energy due to lower metabolic rates and non-migrating behaviour as well as lower 
feeding rates (Kawall et al., 2002; Nevenzel & Menon, 1980).  
The same relationship was observed in the present study, in which WE ratios increased with increasing 
depths; despite the parabolic shape of the response curve, which is mainly driven by the few samples 
obtained from depths below 600m. From all the specimens sampled below 600m depth only one species 
showed significant levels of TAG, Bathylagus euryops. The rest of species: Cyclothone pygmaea, 
Cyclothone alba, Cyclothone pallida, Cyclothone braueri, Lampanyctus crocodilus, Lampanyctus 
pusillus, Diaphus holti, Valencianellus (sp) and Sigmops elongatus, fall within the category of WE-rich 
species, as we would expect from previous literature. The absence of WE and the notable TAG ratios 
in a deep-dwelling non-migrant species like Bathylagus euryops could be related to their foraging 
activities and the seasonality regarding food scarcity. The presence of WE in deep-dwelling species has 
often been related to their functionality as long-term energy reserves in periods of food scarcity. This 
way, the period in which this fish species was caught (August 2020) could have been a particularly 
prey-rich season at their location and vertical position, overcoming the usual needs for long-term 
reserves. Yet, this raises another question regarding buoyancy. As non-migrating fish have to maintain 
essentially the same vertical position continuously, the most common observed mechanisms to do so 
are high water content and/or swim-bladders (lipid-filled or gas-filled). However, Bathylagus euryops 
does not present a swim-bladder, thus, further research would be needed regarding, for example, the 
water content of this species, in order to clarify how they maintain their vertical position without 
constant swim (even though their low lipid content (1-3 % of wet weight) could be taken as a proxy for 
higher water contents). 
The other predictor with a significant impact on WE ratios, individual fish weight, revealed an 
essentially linear increase of this lipid class with increasing fish sizes. This could also be related to the 
aforementioned needs for buoyancy and energy reserves of the species inhabiting the lower layers of 
the mesopelagic area. As dee-living species often attain larger dimensions than other migratory species 
inhabiting shallower areas, mainly to avoid predation as they commonly lack highly developed 
musculature to escape, it is likely that size and WE ratios are related positively (Childress & Nygaard, 
1973).  
The significant variation observed for triacylglycerides across families and locations differed from that 
of wax esters. While the analysis of the latter considered all the environmental predictors (as well as 
sample weight) significant to a certain extent, the TAG analysis only revelaed salinity as significant. 
TAG ratios followed a non-linear decrease with increasing salinities. The highest levels of TAG were 
observed in the samples from Irminger Sea as well as from Norwegian Sea with an evident difference 
with respect to samples from the Mediterranean Sea and the Iberian North Atlantic (supplementary 
figure SF.4). However, the reason behind the lack of significance of the rest of explanatory variables 
==PAGE:43==
 
39 
 
remains unclear. Despite scarce information has been unveiled regarding TAG and its relationship with 
the fish’ environment and behaviour, previous literature has described patterns for deep-sea species in 
which TAG levels rose with increasing depths (Voronin et al., 2021), which was not the case for the 
present study. Even so, the most common relationship described between TAG and mesopelagic fish 
species has been for migrating fish, which would need high amounts of readily available energy to 
undergo their diel migrations. This pattern does fit into our data, in which most species studied that 
undertake DVMs, such as Myctophum punctatum, Notoscopelus kroyeri, Chaulodius sloani, 
Argyropelecus hemigymnus, Lobianchia dofleini or Gonichthys cocco, fall within the category of TAG-
rich species (A & Ferna, 2015; Eduardo et al., 2020; Fish Base, 2020). Nonetheless, the fact that TAG 
is hydrolysed at a substantially faster rate than WE would make TAG levels fluctuate frequently and 
strongly, which could likely make it harder to study and to relate with the fish’ features and 
characteristics.  
It is worth underlining the relevance the species factor had for both lipid classes. Regardless of  the 
robust relationships described with certain environmental predictors, the species factor alone was 
accountable for a large part of the explained deviation by the models. Such significance only highlights 
again the broadness of the variabilities observed and how large is the set of factors and variables 
affecting the lipid composition of mesopelagic fish species. Apart from the contribution of crucial 
environmental factors such as temperature, salinity or depth along with main ontogenic parameters like 
size, a significant portion of the variability observed was due to other components such as fish 
behaviour, diet, foraging strategy and any other parameter involved in the fish’ activity and 
environment.  
 
Conclusion 
 
Mesopelagic fish constitute an essential contribution to the functioning of deep oceans as well as to 
global ecology. Despite numerous studies have been carried out during the past few decades, examining 
different traits and aspects related to these fish, they were often limited by access to specimens,  
resulting in low sample sizes and scarce variability of species, family or geographical area. The present 
study analysed 28 species from 9 different families distributed over a wide range of latitudes, allowing 
to explore variability across species and locations.  
The analyses performed showed robust relationships of the lipid content and composition with the 
environment and behaviour of mesopelagic fish. It revealed how much influence key environmental 
variables such as temperature, salinity or depth as well as fundamental ontogenic factors like fish size, 
have in the lipid content and composition of mesopelagic fish. It has been demonstrated how total lipid 
==PAGE:44==
 
40 
 
content as well as the proportion of WE are strongly influenced by the mentioned variables. Moreover, 
the analyses also revealed that TAG is influenced by a different set of factors, presenting only a strong 
relationship with salinity from the variables considered. 
The fact that the relationships found are both in agreement and disagreement with previous literature, 
highlights the need for further and more comprehensive research. Despite a wide range of species, 
families and locations were analysed, there were still irregularities in the sample size, with varying 
representation across families and locations. Thus, further studies should be carried out including more 
samples from deeper regions of the water column (500 - 1000 m deep) and a more comprehensive set 
of variables should be considered. As the present analysis revealed, a large percentage of the deviation 
explained by the regression models was due to differences among species not comprehended by the 
variables here studied. The broadness and complexity of the relationship between mesopelagic fish’ 
lipid content and composition with their environment and behaviour is becoming increasingly evident, 
therefore requiring of more extensive and complete studies to understand their role in global ecology.
