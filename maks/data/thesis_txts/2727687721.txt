Abstract
This project used numerical simulations and experimentally generated data to estimate
time-varying modal properties on a 3-storey shear frame structure. A mathematical model
was developed to simulate vibrations of the structure, serving the purpose of generating
data for design decisions during the construction of a physical test stand and providing
valuable data for modal property analysis using the Functional Series Time-dependent
AutoRegressive Moving Average (FS-TARMA). The numerical model’s estimation of po-
sitions as a function of time aligned well with data from the experimental test stand,
demonstrating its usefulness for preliminary analysis and design. However, it was ob-
served that the base excitation was not fully randomly distributed assumed due to the
interaction between the shaker system and the structure.
The analysis of numerically simulated data using FS-TARMA yielded acceptable es-
timations of the predetermined modal properties with low relative errors. Similarly, the
analysis of experimentally generated data with FS-TARMA provided estimates in the
same order of magnitude as those obtained through Impact Hammer tests using Experi-
mental Modal Analysis (EMA). However, the precise determination of the physical test
stand’s damping ratio is not known, as both FS-TARMA and Impact Hammer tests only
offer approximations and estimations, but both estimators predicted damping ratios in
the same order of magnitude.
Notably, FS-TARMA predicted a slight increase in the damping ratio for the sec-
ond mode, consistent with theoretical, experimental, and numerical predictions when
damping was implemented near a nodal point.
This finding enhanced the credibility
of FS-TARMA’s modal property estimations. However, FS-TARMA overestimated the
frequency decrease when additional damping was introduced, both in numerical and ex-
perimental data. This discrepancy remains unexplained despite the available data and
understanding of the FS-TARMA algorithm. Further research is required to understand
this overestimation and how it is generated.

Introduction
Damping, or the dissipated energy in a system, is one the most challenging aspect to
model in vibration analysis, particularly if the damping forces are much greater than the
other forces in the system [1]. This project aims to acquire new insights and contribute to
new knowledge on estimating time-varying (TV) modal properties, particularly focusing
on damping in elastic structures during operation. The project aims to gain a deeper
understanding and new insights into short-term TV-damping estimation using analytical,
numerical, and experimental methods.
Large complex structures in outdoor environments can not always be considered systems
with time-invariant (TI) modal properties. The systems are instead time-varying (TV),
which complicates the analysis. Time-varying damping is characterized by changes in
the amount of damping over time. In vibrational analysis, understanding the short-term
TV modal properties for structures under operation is essential. It is essential because
a better understanding of the system’s behavior could lead to more eﬀicient engineering
solutions. Short-term damping is defined as damping occurring a relatively short period of
time. Elastic structures under operation are all active, flexible mechanical components or
structural systems exposed to continuous external input loads. An operating system can
be a functioning wind turbine exposed to environmental factors such as wind, tempera-
ture changes, etc. These numerous environmental factors often problematize the analysis
because their random impact is nearly impossible to predict and determine.
Accurate estimation of short-term dynamic properties is essential for the engineering
of dynamic systems. The modal properties of interest are damping, eigenfrequencies, and
mode shapes. These properties are the characteristics of the dynamic system. Precise
estimations of the modal properties are essential for validating and improving computer
models for predicting the system’s dynamics and designing mechanical systems. The dy-
namic properties are products of the structure’s mass, stiffness, and energy dissipation.
The inertia and stiffness of the system are primarily straightforward to determine (with Fi-
nite Element Modeling, analytical formulas, and testing). However, the damping requires
mostly dynamic testing to be appropriately estimated. Having a proper understanding
of the damping in an operating system allows for monitoring the changes in the dynamic
properties. A significant change in the dynamic properties often indicates damage to the
structure. Monitoring a system for potential damage is highly relevant for structures that
are diﬀicult to access, such as rotating machinery and offshore wind turbines.
Damping is a complex phenomenon because of the high complexity of numerous mech-
anisms that dissipate energy which complicates the analysis; therefore, a better under-
standing is crucial when structural designs are pushed closer to the limits. One of the
most famous examples where the limit has been violated is the Tacoma Narrows Bridge,
which opened on July 1, 1940, and collapsed seven days later, on November 7, 1940. Many
papers have been written about what caused the event. Still, in general, most agree that
the bridge collapsed because average winds produced aeroelastic flutter, initiating the
bridge’s first torsional mode shape and causing the bridge to collapse. Aerodynamical
flutter is undesirable because it brings elastic structures into a stage of dynamical in-
stability. The engineers of the bridge needed to understand the system better to avoid
negative damping, causing the energy to accumulate and the bridge to collapse [2]. The
Tacoma Narrows Bridge is a relevant example of what can go wrong when engineers push

the limits too far for a poorly known system.
A better understanding of determining the modal properties is valuable in engineering.
Especially if the modal properties and dynamics of the system are poorly known, engineers
tend to design structures with large safety factors to avoid failure. Knowing the modal
properties of the systems with high accuracy could lead to more eﬀicient engineering
solutions.
One of the first who described damping in a mathematical form was Lord Rayleigh, who
assumed linear viscous damping where the damping force is proportional to the relative
velocity [3]. This damping model is often in good agreement between theory and exper-
iment, as long as the damping forces are small compared to other forces in the system.
Numerous scientists have extensively researched the field. Lazan [4] accounted for ear-
lier works on viscoelastic damping, and damped systems, especially non-linear damping,
have been considered. Later on, Nashif [5] wrote a book that covers different material
damping models and their applications in the design and analysis of dynamic systems.
Woodhouse [6] had extensively examined the field of linear damping models for structural
vibrations, and Adhikari [7,8] contributed to the research of general non-viscous damping
and proposed a new generalized proportional damping model [9,10].
Modal analysis is used to determine dynamic characteristics such as natural frequen-
cies, mode shapes, and damping ratios. The standard techniques for analyzing modal
properties are Experimental Modal Analysis (EMA) and Operational Modal Analysis
(OMA). EMA is the oldest technique requiring experiments in a controlled environment
and an known input excitation. In contrast, OMA is performed when the system is under
operation and does not require a known input excitation [11]. OMA is a vibrational anal-
ysis tool that aims to determine the modal properties of a structure based on only output
data when the structure is under operation. This technique is highly valuable for wind
turbines or similar systems that are too large to respond to artificial excitation (impact
hammer etc.) or if the system cannot have a complete shutdown. OMA is usually used
to analyze modal properties of structures subjected to random excitation generated by
their ambient environment (wind, traﬀic, temperature), as in wind turbines, bridges, and
buildings.
In the early 1990s, a great interest in OMA began, and researchers started to pay
more attention to OMA techniques to determine modal properties of structures. Brincker
and Ventura [12] was among the first who collected both theory and practice of OMA in
a book. Many of the standard tools in OMA are not always suitable for estimating the
modal properties because of the assumptions that the properties are time-invariant (TI).
A deeper analysis of mathematical tools that can handle time-varying (TV) structures is
necessary for more accurate or comprehensively predictions.
In recent years, the OMA-technique is even further developed with advanced models
such as Autoregressive Moving Average (ARMA), Time-dependent Autoregressive Mov-
ing Average (TARMA) and Functional Series Time-dependent AutoRegressive Moving
Average (FS-TARMA). ARMA-modelling is a tool that analyzes a given time series of
data and can predict future values in the time set. The AR-part regresses the variables on
past values, and the MA-part models the error terms at various past times. TARMA- and
FS-TARMA-models are based on the ARMA-model, where the significant difference is it
allows their parameters to depend on time. Rao developed the FS-TARMA [13] models
in 1970, and his models have effectively modeled various TV non-stationary random sig-
nals such as simulation of earthquake ground motion [14], vibration analysis in rotating
machinery [15], and the modelling and vibration analysis of a bridge with heavy vehicle

The main goal of this project is to contribute with new knowledge of the TV-modal
properties with a focus on the estimation of damping for operating elastic structures.
The TV-modal properties will be investigated mathematically and experimentally. The
project will simulate a simple model where all quantities are known and then analyze the
output by OMA and more complex techniques. The project will then investigate how
well or poorly these techniques predict the TV and TI-modal characteristics for a known
system. The same procedure will be acquired for experimental data where the damping
properties are not fully known. The experimental data will be collected from a test setup.
The test setup is a shear frame (SF)-structure and is placed in a laboratory where envi-
ronmental factors such as wind, traﬀic, and varying temperatures can be controlled.
The SF-structure is based on a five-storey steel frame structure mounted on a shaking
table. The test setup has five individual aluminum floors linked to each other, with beams
less stiff in the horizontal direction than in the out-of-plane direction. Each beam between
the floors is in a clamped-clamped configuration, so rotations can be assumed negligible.
These considerations allow for a simple mathematical model with discrete point masses
with only five degree of freedom (DOF) in the horizontal direction. The test stand is
mounted with two pairs of electromagnets, and they are installed on the fifth and fourth
floors. Each pair of electromagnets creates a magnetic field that induces eddy currents in
the floor plate. When the floors move, the eddy currents cause a viscous damping force.
The size of the damping force, depends on the floor’s velocity and the magnetic field. The
voltage in the electromagnets determines the magnetic field. It allows the damping forces
to be controllable during tests or completely turned off by changing the power from the
power supply.
The SF-structure was earlier designed at the Techinical University of Denmark (DTU)
by Xinxin [17], but doing countless hours of operations, the recent years has caused wear
and undesirable clearance are accumulated in the test stand.
An update of the test
setup is necessary; therefore, the project will design and construct a test stand similar
to the original but with an improved choice of floor materials for better implementation
of the damping by the electromagnets. A new shaking table will be implemented since
the original one had too much clearance, and each floor will have a pair of electromagnets.
This report is organized as follows: The background of TV-modal properties for general

The modal properties are a function of the system’s topology,
stiffness, and damping. The modal properties of a system, such as its natural frequencies
ωn, damping ratios ζ, and mode shapes ϕ. The modal properties will remain constant if
the system’s topology, stiffness, and damping parameters are kept constant or are time-
invariant. However, if the damping of the system is time-dependent or time-varying, then
the modal properties will also be time-dependent. This is because the damping affects
the rate at which the system dissipates energy, which can change the system’s response
to external forces and alter its natural frequencies, mode shapes, and associated damping
ratios. Therefore, time-varying damping will lead to changes in the modal properties of
a system over time.

Eddy current damping is a category of damping mechanisms used in different mechani-
cal and electrical systems to decrease the amplitude of vibrations. This type of external
damping functions by inducing eddy currents, which are electric currents that flow in a
non-magnetic conductive material when it is subjected to a changing magnetic field. A
conductive material is a material that allows the flow of electrical currents through it with
slight resistance.
A dynamic system as illustrated in Fig. 2.2 where the non-magnetic and conductive
material continuously moves in the magnetic field B created by electromagnets. The ma-

current in the material to regenerate and produce a damping force F proportional to the

Analytical and numerical analysis
This chapter will explain the development of the method, mathematical-, and numerical
modeling in a step-by-step order as the project envelopes. The developed mathematical
and numerical models are then implemented into numerical software to enable the design
and construction of a physical test stand. The numerical model will be used as a digital
twin to predict the dynamic behavior of the actual physical system, which is constructed
and illustrated in Fig. 4.10. The developed mathematical model predictions are used to
predict dynamical behavior to design a test stand within given limits and requirements.
Along with the development end implementation of the mathematics into numerical soft-
ware, partial results will be illustrated in plots and discussed and argued for their reli-
ability and relevance for the intended application, namely to create artificial data of a
3-DOF system that can substitute the behavior of a physical test stand.
3.1
Multiple degree-of-freedom system
The desired test stand is built on the principle of the original and already manufactured
test stand with 5-DOF illustrated in Fig. A.1 and reported in [17]. The new test stand will
be built on the same principle as a shear frame (SF). A SF-structure has a different number
of floors and is less stiff in the horizontal direction than in the out-of-plane direction. This
allows for relatively larger vibrations in the horizontal direction than in the out-of-plane
and vertical directions because the stiffness is significantly higher.
The SF-structures floors mounted vertically and separated and linked with steel rulers.
The steel rulers are mounted in pairs of two and are opposite each other in the horizontal
direction, and a simplified sketch of the principle of a SF-structure can be seen in Fig. 3.1.
Figure 3.1: Example of a 5-DOF shear frame (SF).
A mathematical model must be developed to predict the SF-structures behavior. To ease
the analysis, a simple model will be developed. Implementing a simple discrete linear
model where each floor is a rigid mass linked with linear springs allows fast computing
of numerical results. The three storey SF could be modeled as a 3D, fine discretized
model with a significant amount of degrees of freedom. This is assumed to be unnecessary
because of the symmetry of the SF-structure. The fine discretized 3D model could provide

too much information that is not necessary. The simple model can be reduced to a 3-
DOF column structure with lumped masses. However, certain properties of the system
will not be described for this type of model. This project is interested in the first three and
lowest modes and is not interested in the much higher eigenfrequencies. For instance, the
eigenfrequencies of the floors or springs/beams will be lost in the simple model because
it does not consider it. The assumptions of the simple linear model are:
• The structure is made of linear elastic materials.
• The structure has a homogeneous and isotropic cross-section.
• The deformation of the structure is small enough that the linear strain-displacement
relationship is valid.
• The floors are assumed to be rigid and can not deform and store energy inside the
floors.
• Each floor is assumed to be lumped masses and acts as point masses where the mass
of the floor only acts in the point.
• Each floor is restricted to only having 1-DOF, and it is only possible to move in
the horizontal direction. The out-of-plane and vertical directions are assumed to be
very small on the physical test stand, so the mathematical model will not consider
this.
• All vibrations are assumed to be relatively small, so beam theory and linear vibra-
tions analysis are valid and fine approximations for predicting vibrations.
• The steel rulers linked to the floors are assumed to be linear massless springs that
can be described mathematically by Euler–Bernoulli beam theory. The rulers are
considered to be perfectly clamped at each end, so rotations and displacement can
be neglected.

Method based on FS-TARMA
In the following, the Functional Series Time-dependent AutoRegressive Moving Average
(FS-TARMA) model will be considered for the estimation of time-varying (TV) eigenfre-
quencies and damping ratios for a simple damping case.
3.3.1
Model estimation
As can be seen in eq. (2.17), it is nonlinear regarding the vector θ, since the residuals and,
therefore, the regression matrix depends on θ. To methods in this project are used to
estimate the AR and MA coeﬀicients, namely the Two-Stage Least Squares (2SLS) and
Two-stage weighted least squares (2SWLS) and named in the code as 2sls and 2swls,
respectively. Both methods are detailed in [26, 32], where they also mention that the
Ordinary Least Squares (OLS) in the 2SLS must be replaced by a nonlinear least squares
solver.
3.3.2
Numerical implementation
The fundamental theory of Functional Series Time-dependent AutoRegressive Moving
Average (FS-TARMA) is described in Sec. 2.3.2 and the basis code is developed by [22]
and is edited by the author for the purpose of this project. The code is implemented into
MATLAB, where small adjustments to the code are established. For instance, the code
was expanded to allow that na could be different from nc. The vector θ had the length
of 2na = 2nc, which was to be 2nm, where nm was then defined to nm = max(na, nc). To
overcome this particular situation, the rows of θ, and consequently Φ and e(θ) as well,
are then replaced with zeros when nm reaches either na or nc.
The basis orders ba and bc are chosen to follow a 1-order polynomial to simulate that the
coeﬀicients change as a step (on/off signal) and to overcome nonlinearities in variables;
the OLS algorithm was replaced by the build-in function lsqnonlin.
3.3.3
Model structure selection
The process of choosing the model and basis orders constitutes a complex optimization
challenge. To improve the FS-TARMA model eq. (2.11) with more terms by increasing the
model and basis orders (na, nc, pa, and pc) could improve the signal yt’s representation
and decrease the magnitude of the residuals. However, such an increase also escalates
the complexity of the model, potentially leading to overfitting. Extremely high model
and basis orders may cause too complicated models, offering only minor performance
improvements. Therefore, the selection of an appropriate and eﬀicient model structure is
essential. The Bayesian Information Criterion (BIC) reduces these dual conditions into a
single measure [23] [26].

This chapter will go through the experimental analysis and the design and construction

4.1
Design of test-stand
In this section, the design of the new, improved test stand will be elaborated on and
explained in detail. The mathematical model (Sec. 3.1) is a digital twin of the 3-storey
test stand. A digital twin is a virtual and mathematical representation of the physical
behavior of the system. The digital twin is modeled mathematically to predict the phys-
ical outputs a given input would create. The insight the digital twin predicts is used to
design and make decisions so the final test stand can meet the general requirements and
limitations. The digital twin is used because it is a more productive way to design and
make adjustments for dimensions in a mathematical model rather than build a test stand
and change the dimensions when it is already build.
The section will go through the design limitations of the test stand and which require-
ments and intentions the test stand should meet for the project. The chapter will go
through the method in each stage of the design phase, and finally, the final design will be
presented.
The design of the test stand is based on a principle of a n number of floors linked
with steal rulers and exposed to basis excitation. The construction principle is of a shear
frame (SF) structure, and a sketch of it is illustrated in Fig. 3.2. The design phase will
build on knowledge from a previous test stand at DTU and customize it to the demands
of this project.
4.1.1
Design limitations
The structure of the new test setup will be designed in a similar way as the original
5-storey shear frame (SF) as illustrated in Fig. A.1a. The improved shear frame (SF)-
structure will be assembled, consisting of three floors with four steel rulers separating
each floor. The configuration of each steel ruler will be clamped at both ends of the
floors. It was desired to reduce the test stand to only three floors because the project
initially only had access to two electromagnetic damper (EMD), and the intention was
to dampen the system as much as possible and have a more straightforward system to
analyze (3-DOF simpler than 5-DOF). Later in the project, one more pair of EMD’s was
given to the project so all floors could be damped equally.
Each floor is chosen to be quadratic, and the height of each floor is determined to be
hfloor = 15 mm because of manufacturing reasons. The reason why the floors are chosen
to be quadratic is that the magnetic field from the electromagnetic dampers will have the

Assumptions
The numerical model is based on small vibrations around the mass’s origin. The beams
are assumed to be fixed, so contributions from rotations at the ends of the beams will
be negligible. The beams connecting the floors will be counted for massless, and the
vibrations of the beams will not be considered. However, the mass of the beams will
be considered by the calculations of the stiffness, which can be seen in eq. (3.2). The
model only considers displacement in the horizontal direction because it assumes that
the stiffness in the out-of-plane direction is much larger than in the horizontal direction.

1600-times larger, and therefore the displacements are not considered1.

4.2
Adjusting mathematical model to experimental
data
One of the project’s key objectives is to develop a mathematical model capable of accu-
rately predicting the physical system’s behavior to some extent. It is desirable that the
mathematical model can predict the system’s behavior with time-varying damping. The
mathematical model of the 3-DOF system is an approximation of the physical system,
and specific assumptions are made to ease the implementation. These assumptions, out-
lined in Sec. 3.1, are based on idealized conditions that may not completely correspond to
reality but are considered to be well close. These assumptions are particularly valid for
small vibrations, and due to factors such as Young’s modulus E and the dimensions of
the elements, rises a relatively considerable uncertainty. The lengths of the elements and
the assumptions made regarding the non-rotatable connections contribute to this uncer-
tainty, while the mass estimates are considered fairly accurate. The mass of each floor is
assumed to be the most precisely measured, whereas the estimation of the stiffness of the
steel rulers, which relies on beam theory, is assumed to be the least precise. The eigen-
values of the numerical model is dependent on the system matrices where the stiffness
matrix is the most uncertain matrix and will in the following be tuned for experimental
data.

The following section presents a description and explanation of the main equipment and
software used in the experiments. It covers the various equipment used in the experiments

4.3.1
Vibrations sensors
For this project, two distinct types of sensors are used to capture the vibrations of the
structure: a laser displacement sensor and an accelerometer.
Displacement sensors
In this project, the laser displacement sensor, optoNCDT 1402, is used for the measure-
ment of displacement of each floor and is illustrated in Fig. 4.10 (C) and Fig. 4.14 (a) and
a manual can be seen in Sec. A.1.3.
The laser displacement sensors measure the displacement from a fixed point to each mov-
ing floor and send the signal to be stored in a data file with FlexLogger. The displacement
sensors work by a technique which is called Laser triangulation, which operates by a laser
diode that projects a visible light spot onto the surface of each floor. The light will be
reflected and imaged by an optical receiving system that can detect position changes.
==PAGE:64==

Accelerometers
The accelerometer, which is highly sensitive to acceleration, detects and measures the
accelerations experienced by the structure at a specific mounting point. In this report,
the accelerometer is mounted using beeswax. The used accelerometer is a Brüel & Kjær
4397, where the product data of the equipment are listed in Sec. A.1.2.
Similar to the laser displacement sensor, the accelerometer records vibrational data
about the structure. However, instead of measuring displacements, it records the accel-
erations at the point where the accelerometer is located. To avoid obtaining insuﬀicient
information that may be overshadowed by noise, it is crucial to meticulously choose the
accelerometer’s mounting location, ensuring it is not placed at a node point. This pre-
caution is necessary to prevent low vibrational amplitudes that could result in limited
data quality. The accelerometer can be seen in Fig. 4.14 (b). The accelerometer works by
the piezoelectric effect. When the accelerometer experiences accelerations due to inertia,
the mass tries to be at rest. This will result in the spring compressing or stretching,
causing a force to be applied to the piezoelectric crystals. The force that generates an
electrical charge across the crystals is proportional to the applied acceleration, which can
be measured and translated to accelerations.
When storing data with accelerometers, there are some challenges related to their at-
tachment to the test stand floors and the mounting location. It is essential to consider
not mounting them on floors near nodal points to avoid catching low vibrational data
for analysis. Additionally, using appropriate adhesive methods is crucial to attach the
accelerometers securely. For this project, beeswax is used, which, if a thin layer is used
to attach the accelerometer, creates a large enough stiffness so the structure of the ac-
celerometer does not interact with the measurements.
In the MEscope software, the data from the accelerometer are applied with a high-pass
filter applied where it passes frequencies f > 0.7 Hz.
4.3.2
Electromagnetic dampers and implementation
The experimental setup includes electromagnets of the type Intertec 400 N 24 V/DC 11
W, which intention is to implement eddy current damping. The electromagnets can be
seen in Fig. 4.14 (c), and a more detailed specification is provided in the datasheet in
Sec. A.1.4. For this project, the EMD’s are controlled with an on/off signal of V = 24 V
and V = 0 V.
The electromagnets are placed in pairs with opposite magnetic poles. A conductive metal,
represented by copper plates mounted on each floor of the structure, is positioned between
the two electromagnets. This conductive material moves perpendicular to the magnetic
field generated between the electromagnets. To maintain a consistent distance between
each floor and the EMD, 3D printed spacers are used and can be seen in Fig. A.3 and (A.4).
The experiments indicated that the distance between the EMD and the floor significantly
influences the damping effect.
If the distance is too large, the magnets provide less
damping due to a weaker magnetic field resulting from an increased separation between the
electromagnets. Conversely, if the space is too small, there will likely be physical contact
between the structure and the electromagnets, leading to undesired friction damping,
which is not interesting for this project.

4.3.4
Impact hammer
In this project, an Impact hammer is used of the type Brüel & Kjær type 8206 and can
be seen in Fig. 4.14 (d). The impact hammer operates by delivering a single impact to
the structure when it is at rest and is not appropriate for measuring structures during
operational conditions, which is the main goal of this project. Nevertheless, this technique
is commonly used and acknowledged in the industry, making it valuable for comparing
modal parameters as a reference and for the experimental set-up where the damping ratios
are not known.
The Impact hammer equipment includes a force transducer and an accelerometer. The
frequency range achievable with an impact depends on the hammer tip chosen for the
experiment. The surfaces’ stiffness and the impactor head’s mass determine the system’s
frequency range. The hammer is equipped with a force transducer at the head, assuming
that the force exerted on the impactor is equal and opposite to that experienced by the
structure (Newton’s third law of motion: Action equals reaction). The system resonance,
which sets the upper limit of the frequency range, is determined by the contact stiffness
divided by the impactor mass multiplied by one-half (


impactor mass
 1
2) [40]. The pulse
duration is directly correlated with the limit, meaning a shorter pulse leads to an increased
range [40]. A hard metal tip instead of a soft rubber tip will result in a larger range.
While the impact magnitude is not crucial for frequency analysis, it should be suﬀicient
to provide a favorable signal-to-noise ratio without causing significant structural non-
linearities. Consistency in the position and orientation of the impacts is crucial, while
it is important to avoid double hits. When using a hammer for excitation, it is crucial
to consider the resulting transient response. The impact force should be controlled to
ensure the structure does not exceed its linear elastic range. The software for the Impact
hammer test is MEScope which allows the analysis of the data from the accelerometer and
hammer into the frequency domain. Pulse is used to fitting the frequency responses and
obtain modal parameters, and a high-pass filter is applied on f = 0.7 Hz for the sensors.
Experimental modal analysis
The impact hammer test is straightforward, well-proven in the industry, and relatively
easy to implement using the software Brüel & Kjær - MEscope.
A short presentation and the basic principles of Experimental Modal Analysis (EMA) will
be given in the following. In Fig. 4.13, the general principle is illustrated. The objective
of the impact hammer test is to obtain a mathematical description of the dynamics and
vibrational behavior of a structural member. [40] In this case, the mathematical descrip-
tion is the natural eigenfrequencies, mode shapes, and damping ratios.
The frequency response function H(ω) illustrated in the lower figure in Fig. 4.13 is defined
as the output divided by the input as:

4.3.6
Signal generator
For this project, two different kinds of signal generators were used. The first one is a simple
signal generator of JOY-IT JDS6600, which is a signal generator that can create various
signals such as sine waws, pulses, and random noise. It was observed that the signal

the same random signal over again. The second signal generator was implemented in
LabVIEW, and the code was received by Professor Jan Høgsberg. The code was adjusted
to fit the project with the purpose of a random signal that is expected to excite all the

4.5.2
Procedures for experiment with basis excitation
The main idea is to generate an experimental time signal of the test stand to be analyzed
with output-only techniques.
1) Turn on the amplifier and check if all displacement sensors are in range.
2) Generate input signal with a signal generator or randomly distributed signal from
LabVIEW.
3) Zeroise the displacement sensors and expose the structure for basic excitations for
around t = 10 min to have the shaker table find its new center of oscillations.
4) Zeroise again and turn on/off dampers for the desired damping case.
5) Initiate the ground floor movement of the shaking table and measure the response
by FlexLogger.
6) Change the damping case during the experiment for the desired damping case.
7) Let the structure vibrate until the end of the test.

Data processing

Results
In this chapter, the main results from the numerical and experimental models will be
presented and described. In the previous chapters, Chap. 3 and 4, the numerical and
experimental models and their implementations are discussed and argued for validation
within the scope of the project.
In the following, numerical and experimental results will be illustrated for different
damping scenarios. The initial scenario involves simulating the dynamics of a 3-DOF.
Initially, there is no additional damping, but suddenly the electromagnetic damper (EMD)
of the 2. DOF is activated, deactivated, and this process is repeated several times. As a
result, external damping is introduced and removed from the system. The second scenario
is similar to the first scenario, but instead, all EMD’s are subjected to multiple activations
and deactivations.
For a more simplified and convenient way of defining the names of the damping cases,
the damping cases will be named the following:
• ”Undamped” or ”Damping case 0”: No external EMD’s will be added to the system.
• ”2. DOF damped” or ”Daming case 1”. Only the 2. floor EMD is activated.
• ”All DOF’s damped” or ”Daming case 2”. All EMD’s is activated.
The three described scenarios and mentioned damping cases will be investigated by im-
plementing them into the numerical and experimental models. The experimental and
numerical models will generate data for analysis with an output-only technique called
Functional Series Time-dependent AutoRegressive Moving Average (FS-TARMA). From
the numerical model, it is possible to generate a time response with prescribed modal
properties, such as eigenfrequencies and damping ratios. The numerically produced time
responses will then be analyzed with FS-TARMA, and the estimated modal properties
will be compared to the prescribed numerically modal properties.
The modal properties of the experimental setup are not fully known. Therefore, an
Impact Hammer test will be made to determine estimates of eigenfrequencies and damp-
ing ratios, which will serve as a reference. The estimates of the modal properties from the
Impact Hammer test will be compared to the experimental data, which is analyzed with
FS-TARMA’s estimation of the modal properties.
The numerical model detailed in Sec. 3.1 will generate data for analysis, while the
experimental test-stand designed in Sec. 4.1 and illustrated in Fig. 4.8 and 4.10 will be
used to generate data for further analysis.

Discussion
In this chapter, a discussion will be presented on the results in Chap. 5, as well as on the
design and construction of the test stand displayed in Fig. 4.8 and elaborated in Sec. 4.1.
The chapter is organized in the following structure: Initially, the implementation of the
numerical model of a 3-DOF shear frame (SF) will be discussed for its usability for data
analysis and representation of dynamic behavior of the same but physical model.
The structure of the chapter will continue with the FS-TARMA estimation on modal
properties of the numerically generated data from the 3-DOF system. This will be fol-
lowed by a discussion on the FS-TARMA estimation based on experimentally generated
data from the 3-storey physical SF-structure. Upon completing the discussions concern-
ing the FS-TARMA technique and its role in estimating modal properties, the focus will
be on discussing the design and construction of the test stand. Following discussions will
evaluate its intended usage and consider whether it meets the necessary usability require-
ments for this project’s needs and for further use on Techinical University of Denmark
(DTU).
6.1
Implementation of the numerical model
In this project, a numerical model is developed to simulate vibrations near equivalent
to those of the physical test stand, thus generating representative numerical data. The
primary aim of this numerical model is to estimate a physical system’s vibrations with rel-
atively high accuracy. This allows for analysis of both systems’ output using FS-TARMA,
yielding comparable and meaningful results between numerical and experimental gener-
ated data for further analysis of this project.
The numerical model is based on ideal assumptions as detailed in Sec. 3.1 and is ex-
pected to provide a reliable estimate for small vibrations. The analysis of Fig. 4.11 reveals
that, from an engineering perspective, the deviation in vibrations (between numerically
generated data and experimental) is relatively small, thereby suggesting the feasibility of
using the numerical model as a substitute for the physical test stand and vice versa. The
minor deviations observed between numerical and experimental vibrations are assumed
to be due to the small influence of noise and non-linearities in the physical test stand
that the numerical model does not consider. It’s important to note that the numerical
model is a simplified 3-DOF representation of a system that, in reality, is continuous
and theoretically has an infinite number of DOF’s, allowing for an infinite number of
eigenfrequencies and modes. The numerically 3-DOF model only considers eigenfrequen-
cies for modes in a single horizontal direction and only predicts three eigenfrequencies.
While the numerical model is built upon ideal assumptions listed in Sec. 3.1, excluding
non-linearities, it appears to be an acceptable approximation for small vibrations within
this project’s scope. However, it should be noted that the model might not perform as
well when exposed to large vibrations. This project did not consider these non-linearities,
but they might induce more substantial deviations as the linear numerical model does not
consider large displacements.
In Sec. 5.1.1 presents a numerical simulation considering the impact of vibrational
amplitude at resonance under three different damping scenarios: Damping case 0, 1, and
2. The simulation indicates a considerable decrease in amplitude for all modes with the
activation and deactivation of damping cases 1 and 2, which correspond to damping on

6.2
FS-TARMA estimation of modal properties
using numerically simulated data
From the numerically simulated response analyzed with FS-TARMA for estimation of
modal properties in Sec. 5.1.2 showed relatively acceptable estimates of the eigenfrequen-
cies and damping ratios with the largest relative error on e = 5.83% and e = −14.88%,
respectively.
The study showed larger deviations on the damping ratios than of the
eigenfrequencies, and the study indicates that if the FS-TARMA overestimated the eigen-
frequencies, then it will underestimate the damping ratio and conversely. This could be
due to the fact that the damping ratio is inversely proportional to the estimated eigen-
frequency as formulated in eq. (3.63), which leads to an increase in damping ratio if the
estimated eigenfrequency decreases and conversely.
An evaluation of the relative errors in Tab. 5.2 and 5.1 indicates that FS-TARMA
estimates the modal properties with a reasonable error margin, which could lead to im-
provement if a more accurate estimation is necessary. This can potentially be achieved
by implementing a more effective model structure order. However, from the stabilization
diagram shown in Fig. 3.10, it is observed that choosing an AR-order of na = 14 would
result in the three eigenfrequency estimations with fine precision, relatively. If a higher
model order is chosen na = 15, for instance, then for the first eigenfrequency, FS-TARMA
is estimating two eigenfrequencies very close to each other. This could be named mode
coupling, where the modes are ”closely spaced” and are described in [12]. However, this
is a phenomenon where two eigenfrequencies are very close to each other in the data set,
which is not the case in the real frequency response from the simulation, where all real
frequencies have a suitable space between each other. It seems that FS-TARMA predicts
an unphysical eigenfrequency that seems only to give mathematical meaning.
In this
model order (na = 15), the model seems to create an artificial eigenfrequency that does
not correspond to reality. This phenomenon could lead to the transfer or sharing of en-
ergy between the modes, leading to an incorrect estimation of the corresponding damping
ratio. Then the model order of na = 15 is unsuitable for analysis because it predicts
results that do not correspond to reality or give physical and meaningful information.
Nevertheless, selecting a lower model order lower than na < 14, brings a trade-off in the

precision of the estimation of eigenfrequency, as observed in the stabilization diagram in
Fig. 3.10. While, for instance, in a model order of na = 13 the estimates for the first two
eigenfrequencies remain precise, the third eigenfrequency estimation suffers a significant
decrease in accuracy. Utilizing stabilization diagrams for different model orders than one
for this analysis could be implemented to find more optimal model orders to estimate the

Conversely, FS-TARMA predicts a considerably more significant decrease in eigen-
frequency when additional damping is introduced in the system. The numerical model
approximates a change to the fifth or sixth decimal place, which is almost invisible in the
plot. However, FS-TARMA predicts a far more noticeable change in frequency, which is
observable in the graph. The reason why FS-TARMA estimates such a dramatic shift
remains unclear and could be connected to the implementation of its basis function. The
basis functions in the FS-TARMA model are configured as a 1-order polynomial, a con-
stant function capable of predicting the model coeﬀicients (AR, MA) to change or develop
as a constant step to another and new constant step (as simulated with the on/off signal
to the EMD’s). This assumption could be reconsidered to find a better assumption be-
cause it seems that FS-TARMA are estimating a larger drop than gives physical meaning.
FS-TARMA also tends to predict a higher eigenfrequency when damping is added to the
system, a prediction that lacks physical justification, for instance, as seen in Fig. 5.13. A
more comprehensive investigation and an expanded data set for analysis could address
this issue, improving FS-TARMA’s capacity to estimate more physical solutions that
correspond to reality.
6.3
FS-TARMA estimation of modal properties
using experimental simulated data
In Sec. 5.3.2, the analysis focuses on the experimentally generated data to estimate the
modal properties. The model structure selection and model validation are also illustrated
and described. The model validation indicates that the error et can be classified as a
Normally and independently distributed (NID) with a zero mean and has no structure.
Moreover, the autocorrelation function (ACF) of the error et does not show significant
peaks at specific time lags. Typically, the presence of a high autocorrelation at a par-
ticular time lag suggests the existence of unaccounted periodicity in the time series [21].
However, in this case, the absence of such behavior, along with the normally distributed
and structureless nature of the error term, indicates the FS-TARMA model’s validity.
From the time convergence plot in Fig. 3.12 that for even a time duration of T =
100 min the eigenfrequncy ω1 does not show a clear convergence. The amount of compu-
tational power needed for analysis over 100 min was not available, and therefore, it should
be considered to run the analysis with a longer simulation time to get modal properties
that are converged.
Both experimental and numerical analyses analyzed with FS-TARMA for modal prop-
erty estimations have revealed that selecting a model order in which FS-TARMA predicts
closely spaced eigenfrequencies often leads to eigenfrequency estimates that lack physical
meaning. Fig. 5.13 illustrates an example where one of the eigenfrequencies was predicted
to increase when damping was introduced to the system while the corresponding damping
ratios were approximately halved relative to the Impact hammer test. This observation
suggests that choosing a model order with closely spaced frequencies yields non-physical
solutions, including negative damping ratios. There are indications that in such cases,

As one of the assumptions of the FS-TARMA model is that all modes should be ex-

One of the project’s central goals was designing and fabricating a robust test stand capa-
ble of generating reproducible experimental data with time-varying (TV) modal proper-
ties. The successful manufacturing of the test stand represented a key milestone in this
project, as the availability of experimental data is vital for analyzing modal properties
in a real physical dynamic system. The project had access to an existing 5-DOF test
stand, illustrated in Fig. A.1, which was operating properly and has been employed for
various research1 at DTU. However, this test stand had accumulated numerous hours of
operation, resulting in undesirable clearance in the shaker system, which could lead to
non-reproducible results that potentially make the control of the movement of the shaker
challenging. Then it was determined in an early stage of the project to manufacture a
new one with a new shaker system provided by the COWIfonden2.

This project aimed to engineer a test stand that could significantly increase damping
ratios using EMD’s. By effectively implementing EMD’s would result in observable devi-
ations in damping during operation, easily observable to the naked eye. Better detectable
or quantifiable damping ratios for output-only techniques, such as FS-TARMA, could
probably detect higher-represented damping better than lower ones and are desirable for
this project.
From prior work [28], investigating the impact of EMD’s on the existing 5-storey test
stand (Fig. A.1), showed their function in increasing the damping ratios in the physical
system. The maximum damping ratio for the first mode was approximately estimated to
ζ1 ≈1%. However, this project’s test stand improved the damping ratio from ζ1 = 2.39 %
to ζ1 = 9.82 %, a change from an undamped system to a system with all activated EMD’s.
This represents an increase of approximately a factor of 4, which is also observable in the
resonance test in Fig. 5.7, where a significant reduction in amplitude can be seen. Several
reasons could explain why the same EMD’s can induce more damping in the new 3-DOF
system compared to the older 5-DOF system. First, the 3-DOF test stand has three
EMD’s, whereas the 5-DOF system has only two EMD’s. Therefore, the total amount of
added damping is higher for a system with more dampers of the same type. Another fac-
tor is the significant weight difference between the two systems. The floors of the 3-DOF
system weigh approximately m ≈0.5 kg, whereas those of the 5-DOF system weigh about
m ≈2.5 kg. If a 1-DOF system is considered, the damping ratio is inversely proportional
to both stiffness and mass if the damping coeﬀicient is kept constant, as stated in [1, eq.
(1.6)]. Therefore, since the mass and stiffness of the 3-DOF system are significantly less
than those of the 5-DOF test stand, it results in a much higher damping ratio for the
3-DOF system. This leads to more significant and detectable added damping, which is
desirable for this project. However, as in eq. (2.1), Eddy Currents are proportional to the
electrical conductivity and the cross product of velocity V and magnetic field B. Keeping
V and B constant, Eddy Currents are increased by switching the material as it is done for
the 3-storey test stand from aluminum to copper, which has higher electrical conductivity
σ. Therefore, this increases the Eddy Currents, resulting in a greater damping force F,
as formulated in eq. (2.2).
Only the three first modes are of interest in the physical test setup because they can
be compared to the modes of the numerical 3-DOF model. Therefore, all modes larger
than ω > ω3 are not relevant in the physical model. It can be observed that there are
no significant modes present near the largest mode in the Fast Fourier transformation
(FFT) analysis of the time response in Fig. 4.16 and 4.18. For this experimental model,
higher modes should be well separated from the first three modes of interest because
higher modes could interfere with and mistake wrong modes. The observation made in
the laboratory, where the structure is in resonance, and in the Impact hammer test shown
in Fig. 5.5 strongly indicates that the first three modes of both the physical and numerical
models possess a similar nature and can be compared for further analysis.
One of the objectives of manufacturing the test stand is that the system can produce
vibrations, so the dynamics of the structure as a function of time can be stored for data
analysis. The displacement sensors record the displacements as a function of time, and
the shaking table provides the structure with the external force that forces the structure
to vibrate or simulate basic excitation. However, it is seen that even though the shaking
table is provided with a random input voltage, the displacements can not be classified as
random input displacements as in the outcome of the FFT analysis in Fig. 4.19. This is
assumed to be because the structure when vibrating, affects the ground floor movements

Conclusion
This project has investigated methods to estimate time-varying (TV) modal properties,
focusing on damping using both numerical simulations and experimentally generated data.
The initial step involved developing a mathematical model capable of simulating the vi-
brations and the dynamics of a 3-DOF shear frame (SF) structure under operation. This
model served a dual purpose - creating data for the design process during the construc-
tion of a physical 3-storey shear frame (SF) test stand and supplying valuable data for
analysis and estimation of TV modal properties with Functional Series Time-dependent
AutoRegressive Moving Average (FS-TARMA). After the model was fined tuned with
experimental data, the model estimation of positions as a function of time aligned rea-
sonably well with the data from the experimental test stand, proving its utility as a tool
for preliminary analysis and design. Nevertheless, it was observed that even though the
input voltage to the base excitation shaker was programmed to follow a uniformly random
distribution, the actual base excitation displacement did not strictly stick to a random
distribution. This discrepancy was attributed to the interaction between the shaker table
and the structure.
The analysis of numerically simulated data using FS-TARMA presented acceptable
estimations of the predetermined numerical modal properties, with the highest relative
errors observed to about e = 6% and e = 15% for eigenfrequency and damping ratio, re-
spectively. The analysis of the experimentally generated data analyzed with FS-TARMA
showed estimations that were in the same order of magnitude as experimental estima-
tions with Experimental Modal Analysis (EMA) more specifically Impact Hammer test.
The actual damping ratio of the physical test stand is not exactly known. Both the FS-
TARMA and Impact Hammer test only provided approximations and estimations, leaving
the determination of the most accurate method unresolved.
Interestingly, FS-TARMA predicts a slight increase in damping ratio for the 2. mode
where damping where implemented in a DOF close to a nodal point, which both theory,
experimental, and numerical also predicts. This finding gave credibility to FS-TARMA’s
way of estimating modal properties corresponding to reality. However, it was noticed
that FS-TARMA predicted a considerably more significant drop in eigenfrequency than
of prescribed values, when additional damping was added to the system for both numer-
ical and experimental generated data. Given the amount of data and understanding of
the FS-TARMA algorithm, the reason for this overestimation of the frequency decrease
prediction remains not yet fully understood.
Selecting a model order with closely spaced eigenfrequencies using FS-TARMA re-
sulted in eigenfrequency estimates with uncertain physical meaning, including non-physical
damping ratios. This project suggests and assumes an energy-sharing phenomenon be-
tween modes that affects damping estimation accuracy, but it can not be determined
if this is the right assumption or not. Further investigation is needed to understand to
which degree the assumptions are actually fulfilled.

Description and Characteristics 
 
The APS 113 ELECTRO-SEIS® shaker has been 
optimized for driving structures at their natural 
resonance frequencies. It is an electrodynamic 
force generator, the output of which is directly 
proportional to the instantaneous value of the 
current applied to it, independent of frequency and 
load response. It can deliver random or transient as 
well as sinusoidal waveforms of force to the load. 
The armature has been designed for minimum 
mass loading of the drive point. The ample 
armature stroke allows driving antinodes of large 
structures at low frequencies and permits rated 
force at low frequencies when operating in a free 
body mode. 
 
The unit employs permanent magnets and is 
configured such that the armature coil remains in a 
uniform magnetic field over the entire stroke range - 
assuring force linearity. The enclosed, self-cooled 
construction provides safety and minimum 
maintenance. Attachment of the armature to the 
drive point is accomplished by a simple thrust rod 
like the APS 8610 - Modal Stinger. 
 
An amplifier, such as the APS 125 - Power 
Amplifier, is required to provide armature drive 
power. 
 
The drive coil is wound in a manner which allows 
series or parallel connection, offering the user the 
choice of standard or low impedance. This option is 
required if the shaker is to be used with the 
APS 125 - Power Amplifier for extended frequency 
range or random noise excitation. 
 
 
Modes of Operation 
 
Free Armature Mode 
In this mode, the armature provides the reaction 
mass for force delivered to the test structure via the 
shaker body. Auxiliary reaction mass may be added 
to the armature to decrease the low frequency limit 
for rated force operation.  
The APS 113 shaker and APS 0112 - Reaction 
Mass may be used in a vertical or horizontal free 
armature mode with rated force down to 2 Hz. Feet 
and carrying handles are provided for ease in 
placement of the shaker on horizontal test surfaces. 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
Fixed Body Mode 
By providing a rigid attachment between the body 
and ground, the full relative velocity and stroke 
capability is available for load motion. Maximum 
rated force can be delivered down to 0.01 Hz and 
70% maximum to 0 Hz.

Performance 
 
One application of the APS 113 ELECTRO-SEIS® 
shaker is to determine the dynamic characteristics 
of mechanical structures. At resonance, a large 
amount of energy is contained in the structure, and 
the shaker must accommodate the resulting 
motion. However, it need only supply the real 
mechanical power dissipated by damping 
mechanisms within the structure. 
 
If a drive point on a structure in resonance is 
vibrating with a velocity of 1,000 mm/s (39 in/s) 
peak and a force of 133 N (30 lbf) peak is required 
to sustain the vibration level, then the shaker will be 
delivering approximately 65 W RMS to the 
structure. Such a load on the shaker is termed a 
matched resonant load, and it is purely resistive 
since the force is in a phase with the velocity. 
 
If the resonant load input is other than 
133 N x 1,000 mm/s, the full 65 W of mechanical 
power cannot be delivered to the structure, the 
system being either force or velocity limited. If the 
resulting maximum response level is not great 
enough, the user may have the option of moving 
the shaker to a drive point having an impedance 
closer to the matched value, or adding more 
shakers to the array driving the structure. 
 
Within the limitations of maximum force and 
velocity, the actual power delivered to a structure is 
a function of the input mechanical impedance at the 
drive point. In typical modal testing, this input 
impedance varies widely in magnitude and phase 
angle. At different frequencies, the input impedance 
of the drive point may appear predominately spring-
like, mass-like, or resistive. Since the object of the 
tests is to establish resonant modes, at which the 
input mechanical impedance of all drive points are 
resistive, the shaker’s maximum performance 
capability is most meaningful stated in terms of the 
force and velocity that can be obtained when 
driving a matched resistive load. 
 
Therefore performance is given in the form of 
graphs which present the envelopes of maximum 
force and velocity delivered to a resonant structure 
as functions of the resonance frequency of the 
structure.

calibration. Acceleration performance of the

The sensor enables an averaging of the measured values with the median or moving average, before they are 
output. This does not reduce the measurement rate.
3.2.7	
Sensor Emulation (Compatibility)
For a replacement or retrofitting, the ILD1402 can also be operated in the configuration of the previous type 
ILD1401. This reduces the data word width of 14 to 12 bit and the functions of the ILD1401 are usable only.
3.2.8	
Command Compatibility
The sensor ILD1402 uses the same commands (Stop, Info, etc.) for the same functions as the type ILD1700. 
This means that existing programs of the ILD1700 can be easily adapted.
More informations, see Chap. 8.4.4.
