Abstract 
Objective: To investigate the electric buses as a substitute for diesel buses and the influence on noise 
emission at places near bus stops and bus paths in urban areas. 
Methods: From noise measurements carried out over 3 weeks in different measuring zones, the road 
noise was explored. Detailed noise measurements of diesel and electric buses in different typical urban 
driving scenarios was compared under controlled conditions. How residents in urban areas experience 
the subjective noise annoyance from bus traffic inside homes was investigated by a questionnaire survey. 
Relevant statistical tests are done to analyse the data from the noise measurements and the questionnaire. 
Results: The result measured differences in noise emission from electric buses and diesel buses. Noise 
measurements showed a significant difference between electric and diesel buses. The electric bus 
emitted 6.6 dB lower noise level than similar a diesel bus while accelerating and 9 dB while idling. The 
electric bus was 10.5 dB less noisy at constant speed of 15 km/h and 8.8 dB less noisy at constant speed 
of 25 km/h. For higher speed of 50 km/h the difference in emitted noise was smaller, 6.4 dB, due to the 
noise from the contact between the tire and road. It appeared that the residents were experiencing a much 
lower annoyance level in their homes after switching the diesel buses from diesel to electric. By 
substituting the diesel buses with electric buses, the annoyance level and incidence of annoyance were 
reduced inside homes in urban areas, where buses often pass by and serves bus stops. 
Conclusion: This study concludes that the engine type is of significant importance according to noise 
emission, especially at low speeds. A substitution of diesel buses with electric buses will mostly have 
influence in places where the speed is low. This implies that electric motors can contribute to a reduction 
of road noise in dense urban areas, on smaller roads in residential areas and other places where the speed 
is low. If speed restrictions below 30 km/h are introduced to reduce the noise in resident areas, then the 
substitution of diesel engines with electric motors would make these areas even less noisy.  
 
Keywords:  
Road noise, Noise emission from buses, Diesel engines, Electric motors, Noise and health survey, 
Noise annoyance, Statistics

1 Introduction 
1.1 Motivation 
Noise is the second biggest environmental problem after air pollution in the cities of Europe. To reduce 
the noise impact of heavy vehicles on the environment, sustainable transportation is required. Diesel 
buses account for high noise emissions, as a result electric technologies develop. 
In 2019 Roskilde Municipality became the first Danish city replacing all city buses running on diesel 
with electrically operating buses. With the twenty new electric buses distributed among 9 out of 23 bus 
lines, all buses running internally in the municipality is now 100% electrical. The rest fourteen bus lines 
running across the municipal borders of Roskilde are still operated by diesel engines. 
With Roskilde Municipality in the lead, they are together with regions and other Municipalities now 
planning to switch twelve more bus lines to be operating by electric motors. The plan on regional and 
communal basis is to step closer to the goal of all busses on Zealand being fossil free. 
1.2 Objective 
The purpose of this study is to analyse electric buses as a substitute for diesel buses and the influence 
on noise emission at places near bus stops and bus paths in urban areas, by measuring the exposure of 
standstill, accelerating, and moving buses at different speeds.  
Make an analysis and evaluation of the difference between electric buses and diesel buses from a noise 
point of view. To determine the effects of the urban bus traffic noise and the health consequences, a 
survey investigation in neighbourhoods near bus stops is conducted. Discuss the consequences of 
exposure to the urban bus traffic based on reading of scientific literature and reports, as well as own 
data.  
After establishing the noise conditions, recommendations are given for the premise of making public 
transport more beneficial in terms of noise and health, by replacing the current diesel buses in cities with 
electric buses.

1.2.1 
Research questions 
 
It was decided to focus the study on the following questions: 
1. Do electric buses emit more or less noise than diesel buses? 
2. How great is the difference between how much noise the buses emit in different operation 
scenarios? 
3. How great is the difference between how much noise different buses emit at different constant 
speeds? 
4. What is known about noise from contact between tyre and road compared to noise from electric 
vehicles? 
5. What are the differences in frequency spectrums of the noise from electric buses compared to 
diesel buses under different operation scenarios? 
6. Does the number of passing buses influence the noise level at busy roads? 
7. Is the noise annoyance experienced in homes along bus paths and bus stops greater by diesel 
buses compared to electric buses in service? 
8. Are young people more or less annoyed by bus noise than elderly people? 
9. Are people living at lower floor heights more exposed to noise annoyance than people living at 
higher floor levels? 
10. How great an impact can substituting diesel engine with electric motor have on the noise level 
in urban areas?

1.3 Traffic noise 
More than 98% of the traffic noise in Denmark derives from the roads [1]. Many people live in areas 
with unacceptably high noise levels and where traffic noise exceeds the limit of what is considered 
healthy. Approx. 785,000 homes in Denmark are exposed to road noise and charged above the guiding 
limit value by the Danish Environmental Protection Agency [2]. 
Weather conditions affect the intensity of the noise when it is dispersed. This is especially true for the 
wind. The wind direction has a considerable influence on how strong the noise is. Tailwinds amplifies 
the noise and in headwinds can slow it down. If you live a few hundred meters from a busy road, you 
may find that the noise is sometimes loud and other times weak, even though the traffic is the same. 
This is due to weather conditions. Close to the road, the noise seems more constant, and the weather 
conditions can be less important [3]. 
Tire noise from contact between tyre and road dominates for the buses when the speed is above about 
40 km/h [1]. Therefore, tire quality and the type of the road surface are of importance for the traffic 
noise level. In slow city traffic, the mechanical vehicle type has an impact on the noise emitted by 
vehicles. This is especially true for trucks and buses. 
1.3.1 
Diesel engine and electric motor 
The noise produced by the diesel engine and electric motor are caused by different explanations. When 
the diesel engines are burning the fuel, it causes a lot of noise. The diesel molecules are big, and the 
engine runs on high compression. During the compression, heat is generated, and it leads to self-ignition 
of the fuel, which produce the clattering noises. Diesel engines work by high compression ratio and 
cause high pressure rates inside the combustions chamber. During blowdown events, which is when the 
exhaust valve opens, a high sound is produced because of the high pressure in the chamber. 
Electric buses work by a battery, which is the source of the energy that moves the bus, and the energy 
goes through a controller that regulates the voltage, which is sent to the motor. The motor powers the 
wheels and moves the vehicle forward [4].  
Battery electric vehicles produce zero emissions and are quieter than the usual diesel vehicle. The 
amount of noise they make while idling should be quieter than the ambient noise surrounding them. This 
means, electric transport will create a calmer city and cleaner air. Electric vehicles should be easier and 
cheaper to maintain, since they have fewer moving parts than diesel vehicles, which means fewer parts 
to maintain and fluids to restore. A disadvantage of the electric motor is the longer recharging time. The 
diesel engine takes less time to refuel.

1.3.2 
Regulations 
In Denmark, the Danish Environmental Protection Agency has decided the indicative limit value for 
road noise in residential areas of 58 dB. If the noise is above 68 dB, the area or the dwellings is referred 
to as "heavily noisy" [5]. In general, noise limits must be complied with everywhere in the concerned 
area, including at the facade of the buildings and the outdoor living areas. However, sub-areas, such as 
parking areas, can be categorized as not sensitive to noise in the local plan. Also in the Building 
Regulations, it is a basic requirement that dwellings and similar buildings used for accommodation must 
be designed, so those who stay in the buildings are not bothered by noise from nearby roads and railways 
[6].

1.4.1.1 
Frequency of sound 
The audible frequency range of the human ear is of 20 Hz to 20 kHz. The hearing is most sensitive in 
the 2000-5000 Hz frequency range. Traffic noise contains sound at all frequencies, from the deep to the 
high. It has in previous studies been reported that for vehicles with diesel engines, the peak part 
concentrates in the frequencies 100 – 400 Hz [9], and for vehicles with electric motors, peaks at middle 
or high frequencies. Higher frequency tones are more easily perceived than low frequency tones, and 
therefore likely to be perceived as an annoying sound by people [10].  
1.4.1.2 
Peak level 
The peak level of the sound pressure level, LcPeak, is the maximum value reached by the sound pressure 
level and no time constant is applied. The signal is the true peak of the sound pressure wave (non-time 
weighted). Unlike the equivalent continuous sound level, the peak measurement is usually C-weighted 
rather than A-weighted [11].

1.5 Consequences of noise 
There is no one-to-one relationship between the measured noise level in dB and the noise annoyance. 
The context of noise annoyance is the circumstances in which the noise occurs, and which persons are 
exposed to the noise. It can be defined as sounds that hurt, irritate, or disturb us. All circumstances and 
conditions can be important for the subjective assessment and can have an impact on the perceived noise: 
• 
the source of the noise. 
• 
the time of day, the duration. 
• 
the place (e.g., indoors/outdoors). 
• 
the surroundings. 
• 
the demographic characteristics (gender, age, education, work position etc). 
• 
the expectations and mood of the recipient, and noise sensitivity. 
Exposure to road noise can be associated with serious health effects. Every year, hundreds of people in 
Denmark are thought to suffer from road noise. In worst case an early death due to exposure to road 
noise [2]. According to the World Health Organization (WHO), traffic noise can lead to annoyance and 
health effects such as headaches, sleep disorders, stress, high blood pressure and increased risk of heart 
disease [14]. 
1.5.1 
Noise in homes  
Noise situations, where the noise of the source as a passing vehicle is transmitted through a path and 
reaching the receiver, is quite common in many cities. The noise will be perceived as a problem when 
the noise level is so high to be an annoyance to the receiver. The degree of the problem depends on the 
intensity of the noise source (such as heavy or light traffic), the length of the road and the distance 
between the noise source and the receiver. The further away from the noisy road, the less noise there is. 
The noise decreases by at least 3 dB each time the distance to the road is doubled. This means that the 
==PAGE:21==
 
Introduction 
 
Page | 10 
noise decreases fast at the beginning when moving away from the road. Therefore, there can be a 
significant difference in the noise in different rows of houses [15].  
Buses are a part of the main public transport. The distance to the bus stops often plays a key factor when 
choosing a location. But the convenience of having easy access to the bus is also at risk of noise pollution 
[16]. Therefore, the impact of noise caused by stopping and taking-off operations as in a bus stop, is 
addressed in this study.  
Buildings, noise barriers and e.g., hillsides in the countryside can shield the noise. A noise shield 
interrupts the direct propagation of the noise from the road to the receiver. The noise reduction is always 
greatest just behind the shielding object and decreases with a greater distance. It is not always possible 
to eliminate the noise from the traffic when setting up noise barriers. In multi-storey dwellings, a noise 
barrier will typically only shield the lower floors. As an alternative, it will be appropriate to insulate the 
facade to achieve acceptable noise conditions indoors.

2 Method 
2.1 Process overview  
The study was designed to measure and collect data regarding the noise level to which the general 
population are exposed to when staying outdoors near the public transport and in their homes. 
To investigate road noise and compare the noise emission generated by the operation of diesel engines 
and electric motors in the urban public transport, different noise measurements was performed: 
• 
The first series of measurements was road noise measurement sessions in urban zones with 
buses. 
• 
The next series of measurements was detailed noise measurements of diesel and electric buses 
in different typical urban driving scenarios to be compared under controlled conditions. 
• 
A questionnaire survey was performed to investigate the subjective perspectives about noise 
annoyance and related health consequences from bus traffic inside homes.  
• 
Relevant tests were done to analyse the data from the noise measurements and the questionnaire  
All the measurements took place for five weeks all in October and November 2021 in Roskilde, 
Denmark. To ensure the stable meteorological conditions (light wind, moderate temperature, and no 
rainfall) at every outdoor measuring session, the air temperature, air humidity and wind speed was 
registered. For short term measurements in this study, it is sufficient to determine the weather condition 
by the location immediately before every measuring session to check compliance with the technical 
criteria. The air temperature is preferably at 15 ºC and the wind speed at maximum 5 m/s [17]. The road 
and the surrounding terrain must be dry and clear of snow and ice. The ground must not be frozen or 
vet, unless specifically such conditions are desired to be investigated. The type and the condition of the 
pavement texture must be registered. 
2.1.1 
Measuring equipment  
To measure the noise level in this study a Brüel & Kjær Type 2250 sound level meter as seen in figure 
2.1 was used. The sound level meter was placed on a tripod and adjusted to the desired height. The 
sound level meter measured the A-weighted, equivalent sound level. A microphone windscreen was 
placed to effectively reduce excessive pressure on the microphone facing winds, giving the ideal 
measurement conditions for outdoor measurements.

2.2 Road noise measurement design 
To answer the research questions about buses and the influence on the noise level in urban traffic, the 
noise emission at busy roads with bus stops was measured by a sampling method. The noise measure 
procedure included discovery of the sampling locations and selection of the time duration for the 
measurement. The measurements took place for 3 weeks, where each measuring zone was measured for 
1 week each on different weekdays. Every measuring session had a 15-minute duration, where every 5 
second the instantaneous sound pressure value was recorded. With the sampling data the equivalent 
sound pressure level in dB could be calculated.  
2.2.1 
Site selection 
The bus stops in the city of Roskilde were investigated by the schedules for buses provided by DOT 
(Din Offentlige Transport), to see what buses were dominating in the zone. Three bus stops at three 
different busy roads were selected to be monitored. All selected zones were in residential or commercial 
areas. The criteria were that both DE and EM buses must serve the zone and the bus stops must be in 
service from morning to evening. The chosen zones were located at the roads: Holbækvej (zone 1), 
Køgevej (zone 2) and Københavnsvej (zone 3). The four buses serving zone 1 from the morning hours 
to the evening hours was equally distributed between DE and EM. Zone 2 was mostly served by EM 
and zone 3 was mostly served by DE. The type of bus dominating in the zone characterizes the type of 
the zone regarding bus engine type. The list of bus types serving in each zones in seen in table 2.1

2.3 Comparative measurement design 
The comparative measurements were designed to investigate the difference in noise level under urban 
driving scenarios: acceleration, idling, and moving with constant speed. The measurements were 
designed according to (DS/EN ISO 11819-2:2017), where the measuring site was constant, but the buses 
and the driving operations were the variables. The measurements were intended to answer the research 
questions about the differences in the noise emission from DE and EM buses [17]. 
2.3.1 
Vehicle units 
The vehicles used for comparison in this study are the following: 
▪ 
Diesel engine: Diesel bus being 2.5 years in operation 
IVECO Crossway, 13m, 2019 
2 axles, 11625 kg weight on the vehicle incl. driver, but excl. cargo. 
 
▪ 
Electric motor: Electric bus being 2.5 years in operation  
Zhengzhou Yutong, 12m, 2019 
2 axles, 14075 kg weight on the vehicle incl. driver, but excl. cargo. 
2.3.2 
Setup 
The buses were set up in the following scenarios: 
• 
idling 0 km/h. 
• 
accelerating between 0 and 25 km/h. 
• 
moving at constant speed of 15 km/h, 25 km/h and 50 km/h. 
==PAGE:28==
 
Method 
 
Page | 17 
The maximum measured constant speed was 50 km/h, the speed limit of the city traffic in Denmark, as 
well 15 km/h and 25 km/h, which is the average speed interval for city buses in Denmark [19]. During 
the idling and acceleration measurements, the sound level meter was positioned 5 meters from the axis 
of movement of the bus. When accelerating, the bus drove up the driving lane from stationary position 
until reaching the given speed of 25 km/h. During the moving measurements, the sound level meter was 
placed in the middle of the 70 m driving lane. The sound level meter were 1.50 meters above ground on 
a tripod. All measurements were conducted with a repetition of three times. The conditions during the 
noise measurements were controlled as possible. The measurements were carried out in a silent area 
without other traffic.

• 
Instantaneous sound pressure level with frequency weighting A every 1 second. 
• 
Equivalent continuous sound pressure level with frequency weighting A, LAeq. 
• 
The C-weighted peak sound pressure level, LcPeak.

2.4 Annoyance survey 
A survey was conducted to measure the experienced bus noise annoyance inside homes in residential 
areas. The survey was based on a questionnaire, measuring the differences before and after DE buses 
were changed to EM buses in the city. The city buses serving the residential areas was changed from 
DE to EM at the same time as the municipality changed the rest of the city buses. This study focuses on 
the relationship between bus noise exposure in the home and the annoyance experienced by the 
respondents. The questionnaire was sent to residents living in two neighbourhoods on roads with bus 
stops and passing buses. Around 140 buses are passing/stopping in the neighborhoods on a regular 
weekday 5:00–0:00. The questionnaire was available for 320 residents in the two neighbourhoods 
indicated om figure 2.6.

asked in relation to the time before and after the buses were changed from DE to EM. The possible 
experienced health consequences of the bus noise were questioned according to the WHO pyramid of 
health effects of noise and the Environmental Noise Guidelines by WHO [14]. It was desired that the 
residents could perceive differences about the annoyance and the health effects. Personal data and

The foregoing study of the noise from DE and EM buses indicates the following: 
 
• 
The differences between the technologies for two common scenarios of operation of urban transport 
units (idling and acceleration) are significant and would be reflected as important changes in 
perception for the recipients of the impact when DE EM units replace DE units. 
 
• 
The trends shown in the figures 3.10-3.12 according to traffic speed for the two technologies 
represent an additional argument to the implementation of EM public transport over DE. The 
exterior measurements of noise levels from DE and EM buses at constant speeds indicate a 
difference of 6.4-10.5 at normal operating speeds in a city environment. Since the dB-scale is 
logarithmic, the perceived difference of 6.4-10.5 dB is between "a clear change" to a "a very big 
change”, perceived as a doubling of the noise level.  
 
• 
The differences in noise levels between DE and EM bus moving reduces as the speed of the bus 
increases. At 50 km/h a significant reduction in the difference is observed, because here the tire 
noise becomes dominant.  
 
• 
As mentioned above, the usual operation of urban public transport buses involves, beside the cycles 
of stopping and accelerating, driving and slowing down over short distances. Therefore, the most 
frequent situations of buses are associated with idling, acceleration, and driving slowly. According 
to the above, the substitution of DE buses with EM buses would generate a significant positive 
impact to relieve noise pollution associated with urban transport systems. 
 
• 
Additionally, the improvement in the performance of transport systems would generate an 
improvement in the acoustic environment of urban places and residential areas, considering that 
traffic represents one of the main noise pollution sources in this type of environment. 
 
• 
According to the results, of noise exposure, it is not different for a person to be sitting or standing 
while EM buses are stopping at a bus stop. On the other hand, it does make a smaller difference 
when DE are stopping by.

3.2.5 
C-weighted peak values 
The peak values (LcPeak) are added elements of quantitative and qualitative evaluation of the noise 
generated by transport systems. C-weighted noise level follows the frequency sensitivity of the human 
ear at extremely high noise levels. The LcPeak is qualitatively relevant because they expose as 
punctual events of very short duration that have the potential to generate discomfort and even damage. 
The more moderate evolution of the SPL generated by the source and the lower the associated LcPeaks 
are, the lower the displeasure. Therefore, by lower and less apparent LcPeak, a better performance of 
the system is expected in relation to noise. 
The peak-value is usually not used for environmental noise measurement and is useless when wind is 
present [11]. Since the weather conditions was registered during those measurements (T=13°C, 
W=5m/s, RH%=70) with the low wind speed, the data is assumed to be valid to examine.

4.1.1.1 
T-test noise levels 
A two sample t-test was used to test whether the means of two populations are different. The null 
hypothesis for this two-sample t-test is: H0: µ1=µ2 (the two means are equal) and the alternative 
hypothesis is HA: µ1 ≠ µ2 (the two means are not equal). If the p-value is less than alpha=0.05, the null 
hypothesis is rejected. This means we have sufficient evidence to say that the mean between the two 
populations is different. 
Since the p-values of the t-tests in tables 4.2-4.4 are less than alpha=0.05 the null hypothesis of the test 
is rejected. This indicates we have sufficient evidence that the mean noise level of buses between DE 
and EM is different in all cases except for the “standing in bus stop” scenario, p =0.1189. For the 
standing noise level there is no sufficient evidence that the mean noise level between DE bus and EM 
bus is different.

significance is indicated with the p-value, which gives that the population correlation coefficient is likely 
different from zero.

4.2 Annoyance analysis 
The fundamental problem with noise is that noise does not accumulate in nature, like other kinds of 
pollutants, but its negative effects accumulate in humans, and negatively affect our society. Traffic noise 
can have a wide range of negative effects on people. The noise is an emotional and attitudinal reaction 
from a person who is exposed to noise in a given context. As mentioned earlier, the noise annoyance 
also depends on the circumstances in which the noise occurs, and which persons are exposed to the 
noise. Therefore, it is essential to investigate whether there is a connection between different people in 
this survey and their noise annoyance. A wide range of psychological and context dependent factors 
means that people experience the noise annoyance differently, and it is therefore dependent on other 
factors than the objective noise level.

5 Conclusion 
5.1 Noise level 
In this study an investigation of diesel engine and electric motor technologies was conducted. Electric 
buses emitted less noise than diesel buses. Noise measurements showed a significant difference between 
electric and diesel buses. The electric bus emitted 9 dB lower noise level than similar diesel buses while 
accelerating and 6.6 dB while idling. The electric bus was 10.5 dB less noisy at low speed of 15 km/h 
at constant speed and 8.8 dB less noisy at speed of 25 km/h. For higher speed of 50 km/h the difference 
in emitted noise was less significant, 6.4 dB, due to the noise from the contact between the tire and the 
road. 
We know the contact between the tire and the road affects the traffic noise at speeds above 40 km/h, 
why the noise measured from buses at low city speeds are not affected by this friction noise. An increase 
in the number of electric buses will only have influence in places where the speed is low. This implies 
that electric motors can contribute to a reduction of road noise in dense urban areas, on smaller roads in 
residential areas and other places where the speed is low. If speed restrictions below 30 km/h are 
introduced to reduce the noise in recreational areas, then the substitution of diesel engines with electric 
motors would make these areas even less noisy.  
The engine type is important according to noise emission, especially at low speeds. This relates not just 
to the noise level but also to the frequency. At higher speeds, the frequency spectrum of the buses did 
not differ as much as at the lower speeds, which was assumed to be according to the dominance of noise 
from contact between tyre and road. Even at lower speeds the electric bus level at 1 kHz was approaching 
to the level of diesel buses, which could be perceived as annoying. 
The overall number of vehicles controls the road noise level. The number of passing diesel buses in a 
zone of traffic, seems to influence the noise level as well, but the influence of individual buses among a 
flow of busy traffic could not be identified. This is another reason the difference between diesel and 
electric buses will primarily be noticeable in quiet areas. 
5.2 Noise annoyance  
Residents living along roads with passing buses and buses stopping/taking-off from bus stops, 
experienced a much lower annoyance level in their homes after switching the diesel buses from diesel 
to electric. Out of the tested factors, only the age of the respondents had an influence on noise annoyance. 
There is a small indication of young people are more annoyed by bus noise than elderly people. The 
==PAGE:76==
 
Conclusion 
 
Page | 65 
floor height does not have a significant influence on the annoyance level among the residents. By 
substituting the diesel engines with electric motors, the annoyance level and incidence of annoyance 
will be reduced inside homes in urban areas, where buses often pass by and serves bus stops. 
The noise advantage of electric buses allows greater freedom when choosing a location for bus stops. 
Traffic planners are often forced to place bus stops away from the houses, because of the disturbances 
from a traditional diesel bus idling and accelerating can cause. Electric buses have in this study shown 
to be much quieter, both while idling accelerating. When the noise from the buses braking and 
accelerating does not have to be considered, traffic and bus stop planning as well as housing construction 
can become more flexible with electric buses.  
5.3 Further research  
The results are consistent with other related studies. But the difference was expected to be more 
significant. More buses are needed to obtain plenty of data for analysis. Only one electric bus and one 
diesel bus were measured in this study for comparison.  
To support the annoyance survey in homes, an indoor noise level prediction could me made by 
measuring traffic noise at facades. Further research about noise levels in apartments, could be in relation 
to the noise level indoors predicted by knowing the noise reducing properties (insertion loss) of the 
façade. By this, different façade and window types could be evaluated in relation to noise level and noise 
annoyance in homes. 
From the survey, some residents commented on the positive influence on air pollution by electric buses. 
Air pollution could also be an obvious parallel study. Air pollution and noise are both social problems 
with serious consequences. This could as well be a comparative study between diesel and electric buses, 
and the consequences in homes.
