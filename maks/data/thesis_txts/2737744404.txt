Abstract
In this thesis, we analyze goal setting and achievement within self-tracking applications using data
from 200,000 smart wristband users, both pre and post-goal setting. We explore how demographic
factors like age and BMI influence goal ambition, noting that younger and heavier users tend to
set higher goals, while gender shows no significant effect. Our findings indicate that sedentary
and low-active users not only set higher ambitions but also exhibit more substantial changes in
initial activity levels. Additionally, we delve into the dynamics between ambition, success, and
performance, revealing that while setting higher goals boosts engagement and activity changes, it
paradoxically reduces the likelihood of achieving those goals. Furthermore, our predictive anal-
ysis identifies goal difficulty and early signs of activity as strong predictors of goal achievement,
emphasizing the importance of early engagement in predicting successful outcomes.

1
Introduction
Humans inherently strive for self-improvement, continually setting goals that push the bound-
aries of personal development. In the domain of well-being, improvement often involves adopting
healthier behaviors. Individuals make various decisions towards this end, such as adopting diets,
engaging in new sports, or quitting smoking. One of the most effective mechanisms for behavioral
change is goal setting, which has been recognized in several well-known behavioral theories [1, 2].
However, while some individuals achieve these goals, others struggle. What factors contribute to
the success or failure of sticking to one’s goals? Is success merely a matter of ambition, or are other
dynamics at play? The literature offers limited answers, especially regarding the role of ambition
in goal-oriented physical activity, often relying on small-scale experiments with few participants.
Now, with the integration of tracking systems in smartphones and other personal devices, a new
range of possibilities for researching behavioral changes has emerged.
Previously, analysis of behavioral changes relied on interviews, questionnaires, or manual logging
of physical activity data. However, with the popularity of app tracking systems, monitoring has
become easier as much of the data is recorded automatically, providing users with real-time mea-
surements of their behavior. These applications collect, quantify, and process data, enabling users
to evaluate their behavior effectively. Examples of these activity tracking systems include apps for
monitoring hours of sleep, biometric measurements, screen time, social media usage, headphone
volume levels, and notably, our case in point: physical activity through step counts.
Regardless of the specific application, the functionality of any tracking system is the same: to learn
more about our behaviour. However, users often seek not only to monitor their behaviour, but also
to improve it. The importance of setting goals for changing health behaviors has been recognized in
several well-known behavioral theories [1, 2] and state that setting specific, challenging goals leads
to higher performance and greater motivation compared to setting vague or easy goals. According
to Locke and Latham[3] ’A performance goal is a personal representation of a desired outcome or
behaviour, such as walking 10,000 steps a day’ which highlights that the difficulty of the goal is
significantly influenced by the individual’s level of ambition. Here, goal-setting theory provides.
On the other side, the process by which a user sets a goal as a tool or guide falls under what is
known in social cognitive theory as the Theory of Planned Behaviour. This theory addresses the
psychological and contextual factors that can impact an individual’s ability to achieve these goals
and possits that the likelihood of achieving a goal is not only determined by the ambition but also
by the context in which the action takes place, social pressures, and the individual’s confidence in
their ability to succeed. [4, 5]
Although the literature on the psychological and socio-cognitive factors behind decision-making to
change behavior is extensive and well-known, little can be found about the role of ambition within
the field of physical activity and its relevance to individuals’ likelihood of success, since it can be
difficult to find records of the process by which goals are both set and subsequently achieved [6].
There has been interesting recent work on goals that address this problem both from the perspective
of weight loss [6–9] and physical activity [10–14], yet these studies have not gone far enough
in exploring the association between performance goal difficulty and physical activity and the
association between performance, goal difficulty and goal achievement. The most relevant articles
examining the interrelationship between goal difficulty, behavioral performance, and success in
reaching goals within the framework of physical activity include the work of Chevance and Baretta
[15], and Moon et al[16]. However, these studies have certain limitations in terms of sample size
(20 and 100 users respectively), the short length of the time interval in which the data were collected
1
==PAGE:24==
(maximum of 80 days), and little information about the users’ pre-goal setting activity profile to
establish a reliable frame of reference for comparing the direct effects of goal setting.
1.1
Scope and limitations
This thesis presents an opportunity to analyze the interrelationship between goal difficulty, change
in performance, and goal achievement using an extensive and heterogeneous dataset. This dataset
allows for extending previous work to a more representative sample of society. The dataset includes
wristband data from 2016 to 2019, including a total of 100,000 users. For each user, the dataset
records the daily step goals set and their physical activity up to 90 days before and 90 days after
the goal-setting date.
The key research questions to be addressed are:
• Study the interplay between ambition and success: Investigate the relationship between
goal difficulty, past activity, and the likelihood of success.
• Analyze the impact of context on physical activity: Examine how context stability and
past behavior influence the likelihood of engaging in physical activity.
• Explore early activity levels and long-term improvement: Determine whether high levels
of activity early in the goal-setting process correlate with sustained, long-term improvements
in physical activity.
• Analyze various factors affecting goal achievement: Investigate how goal completion
rates are influenced by factors such as gender, Body Mass Index (BMI), and age, providing
a comprehensive perspective on physical activity outcomes.
• Predicting goal achievement: Develop models to predict the likelihood of goal achieve-
ment based on the collected data.
Regarding the limitations of this work, it is important to note that the study of human behavior is
inherently unpredictable. Although general trends and results can be identified, individual cases
are diverse and ultimately depend on the unique circumstances of each individual.
When conceptualizing and quantifying ambition through the difficulty of the goal, many external
factors that may influence the user’s intention and motivation are not accounted for. These lim-
itations arise either from the constraints of the dataset or from factors that cannot be quantified
within an objective framework for all users. Consequently, it should be understood that ”ambi-
tion” is measured indirectly through the difficulty of the goals set in relation to past activity. This
measurement may not always correspond to the actual intention of the individual.
On the other hand, the conceptualisation of goal attainment and the framework used to determine
when a goal has been reached is another limitation. Unlike continuously varying variables, such as
weight change, physical activity targets can be achieved discontinuously as they may include rest
days or interruptions due to external causes. Therefore, the binary nature of goal attainment may
not fully capture the nuances of physical activity behaviour and the context in which it occurs.
Furthermore, there is limited work on this specific topic, which adds to the challenges faced in this
thesis. There are no standardized methodologies which limits the ability to draw comparisons and
build on existing findings.

Literature review
This chapter provides a comprehensive review of the literature relevant to this thesis and is or-
ganized into three main sections. The first section discusses the benefits of physical activity and
its importance in combating the challenges of a sedentary society. The second section delves into
the psychology work on the theory of planned behavior and goal-setting behavior. The third sec-

2.1
Physical activity
This section discusses the numerous benefits of physical activity and its critical role in addressing
the challenges posed by a sedentary lifestyle. Additionally, it reviews various studies that support
the widely recommended target of 10, 000 steps per day, providing evidence for the extent of its
effectiveness in promoting health and well-being.
2.1.1
Physical activity and health
The World Health Organization (WHO) defines physical activity as any bodily movement produced
by skeletal muscles that requires energy expenditure [17]. Its absence, physical inactivity, is now
identified as the fourth leading risk factor for global mortality, with rising inactivity levels in many
countries leading to concern about the general health of the global population. The reason for its
noxiousness is that is one of the main risk factors for noncommunicable diseases mortality, with
insufficiently active individuals having a 20% to 30% increased risk of death compared to those
who are sufficiently active [18].
There is irrefutable evidence that regular physical activity can prevent many of those risk factors
[19] and is a powerful tool to address the rise of sedentary behaviours. Observational studies have
shown that regular physical activity and a high fitness level are associated with a reduced risk of
cardiorespiratory diseases, hypertension, coronary heart disease, stroke, diabetes, various types of
cancer and psychological problems like depression, stress or anxiety [20–22]
2.1.2
Optimal target
Around the world, physical activity guidelines are written and promoted by governmental and non-
governmental agencies to recommend the amounts of physical activity needed for health benefits
[23]. There appears to be a some discrepancy in the exact values of steps/day that are being com-
municated to be enough, yet a significant majority indicates the popular and widespread target goal
of 10000 daily steps.
The concept of 10, 000 steps originated from Japan as a result of a successful marketing campaign
of the world’s first wearable step counter, called ”manpo-kei,” which translates to ”10, 000 step-
meter” [24]. Although somewhat arbitrary, the number works as a rough estimate as it has been
proven that setting a higher physical goal leads to higher engagement in physical activity [15, 16],
in many ocassions, regardless of the feasibility of the goal.
However, research has supported this goal to some extent. Current physical activity guidelines
recommend that adults should aim for at least 30 minutes of moderate physical activity (MPA), a
threshold likely to be reached by people who accumulate more than 10, 000 steps per day, especially
if activity lasts more than 5 and 10 minutes [25]. Normative data indicate that healthy adults
typically take between 4, 000 and 18, 000 steps/day, and that 10, 000 steps/day is reasonable for this
population, although present evidence of a low active population group[26, 27]. Further research
suggests that many people fall short for 10000 steps, achieving between 3, 000 to 6, 000 steps daily
and needing a more wide range of activities to bridge this gap [23].

2.2
Behavioural change
This section explores the psychological theories behind behavior change, with a focus on the theory
of planned behavior and goal-setting behavior.
2.2.1
The theory of planned behaviour
The Theory of Planned Behaviour (TBD) is examined in this subsection. It was developed by
Icek Ajzen in 1991 [4] to predict human behavior by examining the relationship between beliefs,
attitudes, intentions, and actions.
It states that intentions to perform behaviors of different kinds can be predicted from attitudes
toward the behavior, subjective norms, and perceived behavioural control; and these intentions,
together with perceptions of behavioural control, account for considerable variance in actual be-
havior. The interrelationship between these predictor is shown in 2.1 and they are defined as
follows:
• Intention: It reflects the amount of effort and willingness an individual intends to put into
performing the behaviour. Intentions capture motivational factors and in general higher in-
tentions tend to increase the probability of performing the behavior.
• Attitude toward behaviour: This is the individual’s positive or negative evaluation of per-
forming the behavior. It is an internal evaluation of outcomes. A favorable attitude possi-
tively influences forming a strong intention.
• Subjective norm: This refers to the perceived social pressure to perform or not perform the
behavior. It is the external evaluation, expectations and motivtions from external factors.
• Perceived behavioural control (PBC): This is the perceived ease or difficulty of performing
the behavior and reflects past experiences and anticipated obstacles. It can often be used as
a substitute for a measure of actual control.

However, perceived behavioural control cannot directly predict behavioural achievement when a
person has little or no control over the behaviour, or when the context in which the action takes
place has been altered or is unfamiliar. In such cases, intention alone will mark behavioural success.
Addtionally, PBC is also influenced by past behavior, which guides future responses through two
processes [28]. First, behaviors that have been practiced repeatedly in consistent contexts become
automatic (habit development), meaning that the actions are performed without much conscious
thought. The frequency of these past behaviors indicates the strength of the habit and directly
affects future performance. Second, when behaviors are performed in changing contexts, conscious
decision-making is needed to carry out the behavior. In these cases, past behavior, along with
attitudes and subjective norms, influences intentions, and these intentions then guide behavior.
Results showed that past behavior was significantly related to behavioral intentions (r = .43,
p < .001) and future behavior (r = .39, p < .001). However, the frequency of past behavior was
a weaker predictor of future behavior in domains encountered only occasionally.
Oulette et al [28] highlights the importance of context in the development of habit and in the
prediction of future behaviour through past behaviour. Understanding a stable context as an envi-
ronmnent where the key features of the context and the individual’s goals remain similar over time
and across different settings, she states that when behaviour has been practiced frequently in con-
stant contexts, future behaviour should be a direct function of past act frequency. Results showed
that for behaviors in stable contexts, past behavior strongly predicts future behavior (e.g., r = .59,
p < .001), whereas in unstable contexts, the prediction is weaker (e.g., r = .27, p < .001).
All in all, past behavior and habits can predict future actions more reliably than intentions, espe-
cially in consistent contexts where repeated behaviors become automatic.
2.2.2
Goal-setting theory
This subsection explores goal-setting theory, the conceptualization of goal performance and the
associations between goal difficulty, goal achievement and general performance.
The goal-setting theory[1, 3] affirms that well-defined goals are more effective in motivating indi-
viduals and improving performance compared to vague or general objectives. It argues that when
individuals have a specific target, such as ”completing 10000 steps daily,” they can focus their
efforts and monitor their progress more effectively, rather than aiming for a general goal like ”in-
crease physical activity”.
Moon et al. (2016)[16] studied the impact of goal difficulty on behavioral performance and goal
achievement. In their experiment, participants were observed for a baseline period of 14 days
before being assigned to different goal groups. The goal groups were as follows: an easy goal
group aiming for a 10% improvement, a medium goal group aiming for a 20% improvement, a
difficult goal group aiming for a 40% improvement, a no-control group with no specific target that
should do the best they can, and a control group with no goal setting. The outcome, measured
in number of days over a week when participatns reached their goals, was evaluated after one
week to show that the medium and difficult goal groups increased their performance more than the
no-control and easy goal groups.
However, Chevance et al. (2021)[15] argued that that type of measurement to evaluate performance
was limiting , and it should be conceptualized as a percentage of the target goal rather than a
sum of binary outcomes. Their experiment consisted on 20 individuals with the same baseline
but 80 days intervention after target. They found that while higher physical activity goals led
to increased activity levels, they also resulted in lower goal achievement percentages and that the
relationship between goal difficulty and physical activity was mainly linear without inconsistencies
across individuals. Lastly, [11] pointed out that if the performance goal is beyond the persons
capabilities in context, it could have detrimental effects on physical activity.

2.3
Large-Scale Analysis of activity data
This section reviews the literature on activity tracking apps and wearable devices, with a particu-
lar focus on studies that utilize large-scale datasets. It covers the technological advancements in
wearable devices and their applications in monitoring physical activity. The section also evaluates
the effectiveness of these technologies in promoting health behavior change, highlighting key find-
ings from large-scale studies and identifying gaps in the current literature. This analysis provides
a context for understanding how modern technology can be leveraged to support physical activity
and goal achievement on a large scale.
Studies indicate that smartphone apps can be efficient in promoting physical activity although the
magnitude of the intervention effect is modest [29]. Engagement is higher in apps that automati-
cally track physical activity (e.g., steps taken), track progress toward physical activity goals, and
are user-friendly and flexible enough for use with several types of physical activity.
2.3.1
My FitnessPal
In their comprehensive study, Gordon et al. [6] explore the goal-setting and achievement processes
within the popular activity tracking app, MyFitnessPal. This study, involving 1.4 million users,
offers a detailed view of how people set and achieve weight loss goals. In MyFitnessPal, users
select their weight loss goals without any guidance from the app, allowing them to set personal and
self-determined objectives. They have the option to include a free-text reason explaining their goal
choice, providing insight into their motivations. Users are responsible for logging all information
related to their weight loss journey, including their weight and dietary intake.
The main findings are:
• Overall completion rates are low, with only 18.2% of weight loss goals being met.
• They predicted goal achievement with an accuracy of 79% ROC AUC. The likelihood that
a user will achieve their goal can be predicted by observing their behavior over the first 7
days.
• Users who set easier goals are more likely to go in the opposite direction and gain weight.
• Easier goals are far more likely to be achieved than harder ones.

• The most predictive features are the initial goal and the user’s motivation, aligning with the
Intention and Perceived Behavioural Control concepts from the Theory of Planned Behavior
[4].
• More difficults goals take longer to achieve. For the easiest goals, half of all users who meet
their goal do so within 100 days. For the most difficult goals, this number is closer to 300

• Younger adults set more ambitious goals than older adults.
• Men are more likely to meet their weight loss goals, even when accounting for the fact that

2.3.2
Other Large-Scale Data Studies
A 2017 study by Althoff et al. [10] analyzed physical activity data from more than 700,000 individ-
uals across 46 countries, collected via smartphone accelerometry. This dataset included minute-
by-minute step recordings, with the average user logging 4,961 steps per day. The study’s objec-
tive was to examine activity inequality using the Gini coefficient, revealing that this measure of
inequality is a more accurate predictor of a country’s obesity rates than average step counts. Addi-
tionally, the research highlighted that in countries with high activity inequality, women’s activity
levels were notably lower than men’s, suggesting a gender-driven disparity. The study also found
that cities with more walkable environments tended to have lower activity inequality. Moreover,
it was observed that physical activity decreases with increasing age and body mass index (BMI),
and is generally lower in females than in males. Crucially, activity inequality predicted obesity
independently of gender and age.
Another work from Shameli et al.[30] investigated the effects of gamification on physical activity,
focusing on how competitions within a mobile activity tracking application influence users’ behav-
9
==PAGE:32==
iors. The study analyzes nearly 2, 500 physical activity competitions over a year, involving more
than 800,000 person-days of activity tracking. The findings revealed that during competitions,
users increased their activity by 23% on average, with significant improvements observed across
all demographics. Competitions with balanced participant compositions, particularly in terms of
gender and pre-competition activity levels, were more effective. Winners increased their activity
by 40−60%, while the last-ranked participants often became less active. A statistical model devel-
oped by the researchers accurately predicted competition engagement, highlighting the importance
of thoughtful competition design to maximize physical activity.
2.4
Summary
This chapter reviewed literature relevant to tracking systems in personal devices and their role
in monitoring physical activity. We explored the significant health benefits of physical activity,
emphasizing its necessity to combat a sedentary lifestyle and the relevance of the 10, 000 steps per
day goal. The theory of planned behavior and goal-setting theory were discussed, illustrating how
personal attitudes, social pressures, and perceived behavioural control influence behavior and goal
achievement. Additionally, we analyzed findings from similar works, particularly those involving
smaller datasets or focusing on weight loss. The main takeaway is that setting higher physical
activity goals generally results in lower goal achievement but encourages greater engagement in
physical activity. This indicates that while performance and goal difficulty are positively related,
goal achievement and goal difficulty are negatively associated.

Dataset

3.1
Data collection
The dataset contains data collected by a global smartphone and electronics company through an
Android mobile app called Lifelog between 2015 and 2019. The application tracks multiple activity
attributes such as movement and app use throughout the day, including walking time, running
time, app usage,sleep time, stress periods, etc. The primary focus for this project is on the fitness
activities regarding step counts and goal setting.
The data come from portable device (Smartband) that can be integrated via the app. The applica-
tion uses an accelerometer to monitor movements. Accelerometers are sensors that measure the
accelerations of objects in motion along reference axes, making them ideal for tracking physical
activity as they can accurately reflect the intensity and frequency of human movement. [31].
Users can log their goals in the app and modify them at any time. For each user and each goal, the
dataset includes activity information from up to 90 days prior to the goal setting and up to 90 days
afterward. Therefore, activity measures are relative to the goal setting day, referred to as time since
goal = 0. Consequently, this configuration may result in overlapping activity data if a user sets
two or more different goals within a relatively short period. This issue will be addressed during
the data processing and cleaning stages.
The data for this project are anonymised in compliance with strict privacy standards and ethical
guidelines. Personal identifiers had been removed prior to this project to ensure that individual
users cannot be traced.

Data validation
This brief section focuses on validating the dataset by ensuring that the recorded values are within
biologically feasible limits. The validation carried out in this section consists of applying the nec-
essary filters so that the data fall within a framework representative of real-life activities. This
includes attributes such as steps, height, weight, age, and gender. After a brief exploration, only

This leads to the removal of 4 users and all their records, with a total of 459 being eliminated.

4.3.2
Normality and Homoscedasticity
Levene Test
The Levene test is a statistical test used to assess whether the variances of two or more groups
are equal. The null hypothesis of the Levene test is that the population variances are equal (ho-
moscedasticity), i.e. if the p-value results to be less than the significance level, the obtained dif-
ferences in sample variances are unlikely to have occurred based on random sampling from a
population with equal variances.
Shapiro-Wilk Normality Test
The Shapiro-Wilk test is a statistical test used to assess whether a given sample comes from a
normally distributed population. The null hypothesis is that the data are normally distributed,
while the alternative hypothesis is that they are not.
4.3.3
Parametric tests
One-way ANOVA
The One-way ANOVA is a parametric test used to compare differences among three or more in-
dependent groups to determine if any of those groups differ significantly from each other. We
28
==PAGE:51==
will use it with Welch’s correction, a variation that does not assume equal variances. This adjust-
ment is crucial for accurate analysis when sample sizes and variances are unequal, making it more
robust against violations of homoscedasticity. Welch’s ANOVA will be used to assess the mean
differences between groups.
Pearson’s Correlation Test
Pearson’s Correlation Test is used to measure the strength and direction of the association between
two variables linearly related. It assumes that the data are normally distributed. A positive corre-
lation coefficient indicates a relationship of increase or decrease in both variables while a negative
coefficient indicates opposite trends. An value closer to zero indicates a not very strong relation
between the variables.
4.3.4
Non-parametric tests
Wilcoxon Rank Sum Test (between-subjects)
The Wilcoxon Rank Sum test is a non-parametric test used to compare data between subjects of two
or more independent samples. The null hypothesis of the test is that there is no significant difference
between groups. It will be used to calculate the Wilcoxon test statistic and corresponding p-values
for all possible pairwise combinations of groups.
Wilcoxon Signed Rank Test (within-subjects)
The Wilcoxon Signed Rank Test is a non-parametric statistical test used to compare two related
samples, or repeated measurements on a single sample to assess whether their population mean
ranks differ. It does not assume normality. The null hypothesis of the Wilcoxon Signed Rank Test
is that the median difference between the pairs of observations is zero. This test will be employed to
calculate the test statistic and corresponding p-values for comparing the pre- and post-intervention
measures within the same subjects.
Kruskal-Wallis Test
The Kruskal-Wallis test is a non-parametric test used to test whether data from two or more in-
dependent groups originate from the same distribution. It does not assume normality in the data,
but it does assume that the data is homocedastic. The null hypothesis of the Kruskal-Wallis test
is that there are no significiantly difference between groups, i.e. low p-values will reject the null
hypothesis meaning that at least one group differs significantly from the others.
Spearman’s Rank Correlation Test
Spearman’s Rank Correlation Test assesses the strength and direction of a monotonic relationship
between two variables, which may not necessarily be linear. It does not require the data to be
normally distributed. Like Pearson’s, a positive Spearman’s coefficient indicates that both vari-
ables move in the same direction, and a negative coefficient indicates that they move in opposite
directions. A coefficient near zero shows a weak monotonic relationship.
4.3.5
Augmented Dickey-Fuller
The Augmented Dickey-Fuller (ADF) test is a statistical test used to determine whether a unit
root is present in a time series dataset. It will be employed to assess the stationarity of a time
series, stationarity referring to whether their mean and variance remain invariant over time and the
covariances between two variables only depend on the time lag between them. The null hypothesis
of the ADF test is that the time series has a unit root, indicating non-stationarity.

4.4.1
Periodicity - PACF
In the analysis of time series data, particularly when examining activity metrics such as step count,
understanding the underlying periodicity is crucial. Many human behaviors, including physical
activity, are structured around weekly routines. These routines often reflect consistent patterns in
daily life, such as work schedules, exercise habits, and leisure activities, which are repeated on a
weekly basis.
To explore the dynamics and periodicity of physical activity within a weekly time frame, ACF
and PACF will be calculated for each time sample of user-target sets. This analysis does not seek
to draw individual term conclusions for each sample, but rather to support the hypothesis that in
general, users’ routine follows a weekly period or in some cases even shorter period.
First, the autocorrelation function, which is the set of linear correlation coefficients calculated for
different orders k, is used to measure the linear correlation between st and st−k, which are number
of steps at time t and number of steps at time t −k respectively.
Secondly, the partial autocorrelation function, is formed by the set of partial linear correlations
obtained for the different values of k. Thus, for two instants of a time series t and (t −k), it
measures the linear correlation existing between the variables st and st−k associated with them,
adjusted for the values taken by the time process in the intermediate periods.
However, the calculation of these two functions assumes a hypothesis of stationarity, i.e. that the
time process has a time-invariant mean and variance.
Physical activity series around a goal setting day in general are neither stationary in variance nor in
mean, so it will be necessary to apply transformations to make them stationary. On the one hand,
to eliminate the trend, a multiplicative decomposition will be carried out and, on the other hand,
to make the series stationary in variance, a Box-Cox transformation will be applied, in particular,
a logarithmic transformation.
The stationarity hypothesis will be tested with an Augmented Dickey-Fuller test, which is a one-
sided test in which the null hypothesis is the existence of unit roots and, therefore, that the series
is not stationary , while the alternative hypothesis is that it is stationary.

4.4.2
Context - Recurrence period density entropy
In the study of planned behaviour, the context to which an individual is subjected plays an im-
portant role . in determining future behaviour. The stability or non-stability of an environmental
context can make a difference to whether habitual responses are automatically made. J.Oulette
and W.Wood define a stable context as an environment where the key features of a context and the
goals of individuals remain similar over time and for different settings [28]. A stable context fa-
cilitates the emergence of habits and thus a repetition of behaviour without extra effort or intention.
In the case in point, physical activity is a behaviour that is strongly linked to the environmental
context and to the emergence of habits. An individual with a more or less stable routine on which
an exercise habit has been built will be more likely to repeat the same activity automatically and fu-
ture behaviour should be a direct function of past activity frequency. The quantification of context
is not a direct measure that can be collected, as it depends on the type of behaviour being analysed,
to which a myriad of factors can be added.
In the previous section, autocorrelation and partial autocorrelation are tools that allow us to analyse
the periodicity of a sample, but lack the power to capture the complexity and repetitiveness of a
signal. Therefore, a new measure will be introduced with the idea of quantifying the context:
Recurrence period entropy density.

from the recurrence analysis of time series data, characterised by measuring the repetitiveness in
the phase space of the system.

4.5
Predictive Model
The objective of our prediction model is to predict goal achievement based on behavioural features,
focusing on identifying the factors that most significantly influence success, i.e. goal achievement.
In the literature on weight loss goals, Gordon et al. (2019) successfully developed a predictive
classification model with 79% ROC AUC accuracy [6]. Inspired by this model, we aim to apply
a similar approach to step activity goals. However, the nature of these goals differs significantly.
Unlike weight loss, which is achieved continuously over time, activity goals can be met intermit-
tently.
From our preliminary analysis, we observed that achieving 100 percent completion every day is
highly unlikely. Consequently, we chose to develop a regression model to predict the percentage
of goal achievement rather than complete fulfillment. This approach allows us to better capture
the nuances of daily activity and goal performance.
33
==PAGE:56==
4.5.1
Regressor Analysis
We evaluate several regression models to determine their effectiveness in predicting goal achieve-
ment, including linear regression, decision tree, random forest, and gradient boosting.
Basic description of the models evaluated:
• Linear Regression: Provides a baseline understanding of the relationships between features
and the target.
• Decision Tree Regressor: Captures non-linear relationships and interactions but can overfit
complex or noisy data.
• Random Forest Regressor: Combines multiple decision trees to reduce variance and im-
prove generalization.
• Gradient Boosting Regressor: An ensemble method that uses weak prediction models,
typically decision trees, to build a strong predictor.
To assess the fit of these models, we compare the R2 metric and calculate the mean squared error
(MSE). The R2 metric, or coefficient of determination, ranges from 0 to 1, where 0 indicates no ex-
planatory power and 1 indicates perfect prediction. MSE measures the average squared difference
between actual and predicted values.
The Gradient Boosting Regressor performs best of all the above mentioned models. More in detail,
this method works as a series of weak models, decision trees, sequentially built, to improve accu-
racy. This method employs gradient descent on the loss function to enhance the model with each
iteration. Parameters like number of estimators, maximum depth, minimum samples per split, and
minimum samples per leaf are adjusted to optimize the model. We use cross-validation to fine-tune
these parameters and control model performance.
Given the computational demands of gradient boosting, we also experimented with the Histogram
Gradient Boosting Regressor. This advanced model speeds up training and reduces memory usage
by binning continuous feature values into histograms, effectively simplifying the problem and
making it especially suitable for large datasets. We found very similar results but with greater
efficiency.
4.5.2
Feature Importance
To find the most predictive features, we employ impurity based importance and complement it
with permutation importance, since impurity-based importance can be biased towards features with
more categories or higher cardinality, potentially overstating their importance.
• Impurity-Based Importance: Gradient Boosting constructs an ensemble of decision trees,
and each tree inherently evaluates feature importance based on the decrease in impurity (such
as Gini impurity) when a feature is used to split a node. This impurity reduction measure is
accumulated across all trees for each feature..
• Permutation Importance: This technique involves shuffling each feature in the validation
set and observing the impact on model performance. A significant deterioration in perfor-
mance after a feature’s values are shuffled indicates that the model heavily relies on that
feature for making accurate predictions.

Results
This chapter presents an overview of the different analyses conducted, together with their results.
First, we explore goal selection and ambition levels, examining how ambition varies across de-
mographic groups. This section includes an examination of a notable trend related to the popular
’10,000 steps’ goal. We then delve into goal attainment, assessing whether individuals achieve
their goals and how goal attainment rates vary across different demographic metrics. We also in-
vestigate the temporal dynamics of goal attainment to determine whether the propensity to achieve
goals has improved or worsened over time. Finally, we evaluate performance independent of goal
attainment to analyse the degree of improvement users experience regardless of whether or not
they achieve their specific goals. Results show ambition varies across BMI and age, but not sig-
nificantly with gender. Younger adults set more ambitious goals than older adults and individuals
with above normal BMI index also set more ambitious goals-
Moving forward, after analyzing ambition (reflected in goal difficulty), success (measured by goal
achievement), and performance as separate entities and examining their associations with demo-
graphic factors and baseline conditions, we shift our focus to explore the interrelationships among
these three critical metrics. This next phase of our study will delve into how ambition, success, and
performance dynamically interact. Results find that goal difficulty positively affects performance,
but does the opposite with goal achievement.
Next, we pivot slightly to conduct a more detailed temporal analysis, beginning by exploring early
behavioral factors. Given our understanding of goal achievement and performance rates, we now
investigate if early behavioral factors can predict the future trajectory of a user’s behavior toward
their goal, focusing particularly on the 14 days preceding the logging of the target. Our findings
show that early activity is positively correlated with the likelihood of reaching targets, making it a
strong predictor of goal achievement.
As part of this temporal analysis, we also examine seasonality trends, notably the spike in targets
logged following January 1st, which suggests a significant influence of New Year’s resolutions on
user behavior. Finally, we conclude our temporal examination by analyzing the recurrence period
density entropy. This analysis helps us draw conclusions about individual contexts and the stability
of their routines, providing insights into the regularity and predictability of their behavior patterns.
The concluding section of the results chapter will highlight the predictive strength of various fea-
ture sets previously analyzed in relation to goal achievement rates. Here, we ask what are the best
predictors for goal achievement by comparing two different models: one that accounts for initial
activity in the first seven days and another that does not. The findings indicate that both early and
initial goal achievements, along with goal difficulty, are crucial in predicting outcomes. Specifi-
cally, the model that does not consider initial activity yields an R-squared accuracy of 0.66, while
incorporating initial activity into the model increases the accuracy to 0.77. This underscores the
significant impact of early interactions on overall goal achievement.

5.6
Predicting goal achievement
In this section, we will explore the feasibility of using user information from the baseline period (all
days prior to two weeks before the goal-setting day) to predict goal achievement over the following
two months of activity.
The section begins by detailing the various variables that will be considered for the model, based
on previous analyses. We will then proceed to analyze the percentage of goal achievement and
evaluate the performance of different models, focusing on features strictly from before target-
setting. Finally, following the criteria outlined in Gordon (2019), we will also incorporate into our
prediction model the behaviors observed during the initial seven days after target setting.
5.6.1
Features used for the model
This section outlines the different feature sets used to predict goal achievement over eight weeks.
• Ambition: Previous observations indicated that achieving ambitious goals is less likely as
the difficulty increases. We measure ambition through the goal’s difficulty and the effort
required, quantified by the target’s standard score.
• Past activity: Baseline activity levels significantly influence the likelihood of reaching tar-
gets. If a user has previously met a similar goal, this positively impacts their perceived
behavioral control. We measure past activity by the average daily steps taken before setting
the goal, the frequency of active days and pre-goal achievement percentage.
• Early activity: Some users begin changing their behavior even before setting a target. This
early activity is a good indicator of future performance. We assess this by calculating the
percentage of days goals were achieved, the percentage of days activity was above baseline
levels, and the initial effort as indicated by the standard score of steps.
• Initial activity:This feature is considered in only one of the models. It includes the same
metrics as early activity—percentage of days achieving the goal, days exceeding baseline
activity levels, and the initial effort, supplemented by the frequency of active days.
• Demographics: We include age, gender, and Body Mass Index (BMI) as demographic fac-
tors that may influence goal achievement.
To effectively summarize and interpret the relationships within the dataset, we plotted a correlation
matrix between selected features, as illustrated in Figure 5.32. This matrix provides a comprehen-
sive overview of how these features interact with one another. Notably, ”Initial Goal Achievement”
and ”Early Goal Achievement” exhibit strong correlations with ”Goal Achievement,” scoring 0.81
and 0.67, respectively. This finding aligns with our expectations based on previous analyses. Fur-
thermore, the variables ”Early Performance” and ”Early Improve Performance” demonstrate sub-
stantial correlations of 0.48 and 0.60, respectively.
On the other hand, our analysis reveals a low correlation between demographic factors and goal
57
==PAGE:80==
achievement. Both ”Age” and ”Gender” show minimal influence on performance outcomes, with
correlations that are insufficient to warrant inclusion in our predictive model. Therefore, we have
decided to exclude these demographic variables.

6
Discussions
In this chapter, we will review the results presented in Chapter 5. We will start by first outlining
and discussing the results on demographic differences on behavioural activity data. Then we will
discuss the findings on the interrelationship of ambition, success and performance. We will end
by presenting the results on the predictive models. Then we will make a summary of what to take
away from this thesis. Finally we will discuss limitations and an outlook for future work.
6.1
Discussion of Ambition and Goal Setting in Physical Activity
Across Demographics
In the app, individuals choose their goals, influenced by their ambition to change their behavior.
We determine users’ ambition by comparing their selected targets to a baseline activity level, which
we establish using data from three months to two weeks prior to the target-setting day. This com-
parison is standardized for each user, providing an ambition indicator. Users exhibiting negative
ambition—those aiming below their baseline—are sometimes removed from the analysis or used
as a reference group to assess the impact of ambition.
While specific literature on ambition in physical activity is scarce, we find parallels in studies
measuring ambition through targeted weight loss percentages. These studies, often utilizing large-
scale data, sometimes show consistent results with our findings.
Our analysis reveals that low-active sedentary users are the most ambitious, while highly active
users are the least. On average, users aim to increase their daily steps by 40%, but the actual
percentages vary by activity level. Sedentary users often target increases exceeding 100%, whereas
very active users generally show little to no ambition, aiming for no more than a 20% increase.
This discrepancy can be attributed to more active users already engaging at healthy activity levels
and merely needing to maintain their status.
Regarding demographic factors, we observe no significant difference in ambition between male
and female users, across different BMI categories, except in underweight individuals where males
display more ambition. This contrasts with other literature, where females often show greater
ambition, particularly in contexts focused on weight loss. However, this difference makes sense
when we think about how weight loss involves more than just exercise, like dieting, and women
might feel more pressure from society to lose weight, which could explain their higher ambition
in that specific behavioural goal.
Ambition also tends to be higher among overweight and obese individuals, likely due to their need
for more substantial behavioral changes to reach targets above their baseline. Ambition varies with
age as well; young and older adults generally show more ambition than those in middle age. This
trend, particularly evident among younger females and older males, reverses when considering
actual targets set, which tend to peak in middle age—a U-shaped curve that reflects health trends
correlated with age.
Users generally aim to enhance their daily activity by an average of 3,000 steps, or just over two
kilometers. However, the same increment requires different levels of effort for users based on
their initial activity levels. Notably, many users set a popular target of 10,000 steps, with over
one-third of our dataset opting for this goal. This target’s popularity leads to a significant uptick in
ambition among sedentary and low-active users (50%), although we found no significant difference
in performance improvement or goal achievement between users who chose the 10,000-step target
and those who selected similar goals. Thus, the value of setting a 10,000-step goal may lie not in the
61
==PAGE:84==
specific target but in the motivational impact of aiming higher, which can lead to more substantial
changes regardless of whether the goal is fully achieved.
6.2
Discussion of the interplay between
ambition-success-performance
The results directly support the main idea of goal-setting theory applied to physical activity, specif-
ically walking: higher ambition improves performance outcomes more than less ambitious and
simpler goals. However, higher ambition can also leads to a lower success.
Performance and success exhibit opposing trends when analyzed with respect to ambition. We also
found that improvement and success do not linearly correlate with goal difficulty. Trying to exceed
baseline activity levels by more than 50% generally leads to the same outcomes. This finding
suggests that users should set ambitious but achievable targets to prevent becoming demotivated
from pursuing unreachable goals. Similarly, aiming for an increase in activity of more than 100%
results in a goal completion rate of no more than 12%. Additionally, individuals with easier goals
often achieve better than their targets suggest.
Users with higher ambition make more significant changes in the first week compared to less
ambitious users, but these changes decline more rapidly over time. Regardless of change rate, all
ambition groups gradually distance themselves from their targets over the weeks.5
About 50% of users maintain the same activity level, while no more than 25% advance to higher
levels. Ambition significantly affects the percentage of users moving from low to high activity
levels. Specifically, ambition positively impacts individuals’ progression to higher activity levels.
Notably, 17% more ambitious users moved from sedentary to low activity levels compared to their
less ambitious peers, and 15% moved from low to moderately active.
Early activity is crucial for developing a positive perception of behavioral control. Users who
meet a step count similar to their target before setting it officially are more likely to achieve higher
completion rates. Additionally, the percentage of early goal completion is positively correlated
with overall goal achievement, and early completion strongly predicts future success. However, the
probability of increasing early achievement by more than 20% does not exceed 10%. Furthermore,
80% of active users had already reached their target at least once in the preceding days, compared
to nearly 50% for sedentary users.
6.3
Discussion of the behavioural context and periodicity of physical
activity
One of the objetives of this work was to examine how context stability could be measured and
what conclussions could be drawn from the measurement.
To that aim, we opted for calculating the recurrent period density period. This measurement aims
to capture the extent to which a time series repeats the same sequence, to try to capture how consis-
tent are individuals’ routines. We calculate the RPDE separately for before and after goal setting
intervention, each for a period of 8 weeks.
RPDE and baseline activity level are negatively correlated meaning that higher values of RPDE
which corresponds to more disorder inconsistent physical activity sequence are associated with less
active users, while lower RPDE values which reflect more stable routines with more repetitive in
both frequency and activity level entries, are associated with more active individuals. We confirm
that the difference are significative between sedentary/low active and more active users.
When comparing recurrence period density entropy values before and after the goal, RPDE suc-
cesffuly identified changes in routine consistency. Both when individuals go from no structrure to
62
==PAGE:85==
more consistent routine and also when they have an consistent activity habit and it loses strength.
However, only significant differences 5.2 in goal completion rates were found for users with a
decreasse in RPDE.
6.4
Predicting goal achievement
To predict goal achievement, we explore two approaches. The first model incorporates factors
such as ambition, past activity, and early activity. This model achieves its best accuracy with an R-
squared of 0.66 using a gradient boosting regressor. Feature selection indicates that goal difficulty,
early goal achievement, and target Z-score are the primary contributors to variance in the model.
While the R-squared is not outstanding, it is significant for predicting behavior using only data
available before setting the target.
The second model focuses on initial activity and achieves an R-squared of 0.774. This improve-
ment is supported by observations that individuals tend to exert significant effort in the initial days
following their goal setting. The most critical features in this model are initial goal achievement,
target Z-score, and goal difficulty. These findings suggest that ambition, reflected in goal difficulty
and target Z-score, remains crucial for prediction. However, the significance of early goal achieve-
ment has been somewhat overtaken by initial goal achievement, relegating early goal achievement
to third or fourth place in terms of feature and permutation importance, respectively.
6.5
Future Work
In this study, context was mainly determined by analyzing patterns of physical activity and their
repetitiveness. However, the tools used for this analysis, specifically Recurrence Quantification
Analysis (RPDE), were found to be weak and overly sensitive. It is evident that an individual’s
behavior could be influenced by numerous external factors that were not considered in this the-
sis. Future research should expand its scope to include additional variables such as screen usage,
mobility patterns, and even dietary habits.
Moreover, a deeper understanding of why individuals decide to improve their physical activity
levels remains a crucial area for exploration. Future studies should delve into the psychological and
environmental triggers that motivate individuals to set and pursue physical activity goals. Many
fitness apps already offer users the option to record their motives for selecting specific goals. This
existing data could be utilized to analyze patterns in goal selection and the underlying reasons.
Additionally, examining the influence of community and social pressure as external conditioning
agents, for example via social media.
Our analysis treated each instance of a user setting a target as a separate event. However, we
observed that many users set more than one target over the four-year period. Future research should
conduct a deeper analysis of these patterns. It would be interesting to explore whether individuals
lower their targets if they find them too challenging, or if they set higher targets after meeting their
initial goals. Additionally, examining the role of feedback in this process, such as how receiving
positive or negative feedback affects subsequent goal setting, could shed light on the mechanisms
of motivation and self-regulation.
Another interesting aspect would be to examine the psychological process of self-efficacy devel-
opment in individuals as they set and achieve goals. Each successful achievement can potentially
increase an individual’s confidence in their abilities, affecting their future behavior and goal-setting
practices. Future work could explore how this evolving self-perception influences subsequent
decision-making and long-term behavior modification.
By including these areas in future research, we could not only expand on the findings of this the-
sis but also contribute to a richer, more comprehensive understanding of the mechanisms behind
63
==PAGE:86==
behavior change. This could ultimately lead to more effective strategies for promoting healthier
lifestyles across diverse populations.

Conclusion
This thesis analyzes physical activity target data from 20,000 individuals, uncovering significant
trends in ambition and achievement. The analysis shows that individuals with higher BMI and
sedentary lifestyles set more ambitious goals, indicating a drive to make substantial changes due
to health concerns. Early activity data proves critical, as it significantly predicts the likelihood of
meeting long-term physical activity targets. Additionally, the study reveals a paradox: the more
ambitious the goals, the lower the achievement rates, highlighting the challenge of setting realistic
yet motivating targets. Predictive modeling confirms that early activity levels and goal difficulty
are paramount in predicting success. These findings emphasize the need for health promotion
interventions to tailor goals more closely to an individual’s initial activity levels and to account
for the complexity of ambitious goal setting. Overall, this research deepens our understanding of
health behavior patterns and informs strategies to enhance support for achieving sustainable health
outcomes
