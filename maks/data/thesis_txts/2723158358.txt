Previous work
Enrico Ghidoni presented an exploratory analysis of methods and tools, used to identify apartments
with improper heating systems in large building blocks and describe their dynamic thermal behaviour.
The information obtained with these tools can then be used to improve the operation of the heating
system in the apartment by reducing the return and supply temperature.
A grey box model of a
generic apartment was created using Modelica in the Dymola environment. The parameters necessary
to describe the thermal behaviour of the system associated with the well-performing apartment were
estimated using ModestPy. These values were used to explore alternative configurations of supply and
return temperatures in the heating system. Two alternative configurations of the heating system were
proposed and simulated in the faulty apartment. The author stated that higher water mass flow rates
allowed for a reduction of the supply temperature, whilst increasing the return temperature. Further-
more, the increase in water cooling reduces the savings in supply energy, While achieving lower return
temperature values and reducing the water mass flow [14].
Francesco Cirone presented a novel methodology to identify hydraulic imbalances, critical zones or
disproportionate set-point temperatures in apartment space heating systems and offer potential opti-
misation measures, through the development of one Grey box model which, in combination with data
analysis and computational methods, can be used to represent the thermal behaviour of multiple apart-
ments. The GBM can be used to detect heat distribution imbalances by relatively comparing flow
coeﬀicient estimates and inspecting the set point and indoor temperatures in the rooms. Disproportion-
ate flow coeﬀicients led to the identification of rooms with too high set points and which were heating
adjacent zones. Furthermore, it was possible to satisfy the combined apartment’s need for heat by
decreasing the supply temperature according to a weather compensation curve scaled by a factor of 0.8
and with fully open valves to improve the eﬀiciency of the heat consumption [7].
Eleni Bitsiou developed two models to study the thermal and energy performance of a non-resident
building, the Frederiksberg Skole, as well as examine and propose potential improvements. One model
was created in IDA ICE (IDA Indoor Climate and Energy) to obtain useful data about the building.
The other grey box model model was created in Dymola software using Modelica language. From the
analysis of the results in IDA ICE, two assumptions were justified in the development of the model.
Furthermore, the annual energy consumption simulated in IDA ICE was lower than that measured by
the metres registered in the building, which meant detecting a fault in the heating system. Furthermore,
in terms of heat balance in the classroom, the primary heat gains and losses were the occupants and
the airflow of the ventilation system, respectively. The Dymola model was exported as an FMU, which
was then used as input to an open-source Python tool, named ModestPy, to estimate the unknown
parameters. The total normalised mean-square error of the parameter estimation using the data from
IDA ICA was lower than that from the sensors installed in the school. The author stated that the
occupancy of a room was very diﬀicult to predict and it was better to activate the ventilation system
throughout the day if the CO2 concentration exceeded the setpoint. In addition, the concentration of

1.1.2
Space heating system
The objective of space heating systems is to meet the thermal comfort requirements of the occupants.
Space heating systems can be divided into direct acting heating and indirect acting heating. A direct
heating system converts fuel into heat in the space to be heated. Indirect heating is the exchange of
fuel energy and heat medium in the substation of the building and then from where it is distributed
around the building and emitted to the space. An example is a radiator system. Radiators combine
both radiant and heat convective methods[31].
1.1 and Figure 1.2 show the one-string radiator circuit and two-string radiator circuit, respectively[18].
With regard to the one-string radiator circuit, there is only one pipe for both supply water and return
water. To be specific, the water flow on the main pipe is split, then a fraction water flow goes into
the radiator and exits back to the main string, continuing to flow to the next radiator. The circuit
results in a problem in that the temperature through each radiator gradually reduces as the water flow
enters each successive radiator. A situation may occur the temperature of the first group of radiator
is very hot, while the last group is very cool.
This is hard to regulate temperature.
As shown in
Figure 1.2, the performance of the two-string radiator circuit is better. One pipe is responsible for
supply water, and the other is for return water from each radiator. The supply water flows into each ra-
diators at the same temperature. A lower return temperature can be obtained than the on-string circuit.
Figure 1.1: One-string radiator circuit.
Figure 1.2: Two-string radiator circuit.
Thermostatic radiator valves(TRVs) are installed on the radiator to control the mass flow rate depend-
ing on the indoor setpoint temperature. A TRV is a self-regulating device with a temperature sensitive
element that opens or closes the valve plug. There are usually 5 setpoint selection on it acrroding to
different desired indoor temperature. The number is higher, the more mass flow rate goes through the
radiators results in the indoor temperature highet. Usually, the recommendation set point is three or
four, they response to the desired indoor temperature 20 ◦C and 23 ◦C respectivelt. The maximum
setpoint selsction is 6, but it can not be set. This is because for most radiators, this design condition
is not considered. High supplt temperature causes high return temperature. Occupants can have a
comfortable indoor environment by installing the TRVs to meet requirments. On the meantime, excess
heat can be avoided, which have a positive impact on energy saving.
A capsule in the valve head
contracts or expands in response to changes in zone temperature, which causes the valve body to open
or close and therefore regulate the flow of hot water into the radiator[5].

1.2
Objective and scope of the research
The goal of the project is to develop a GBM that can identify the performance of the heating system
with limited information about the radiator installation. The model, created in the Dymola environ-
ment with the Modelica language, involves the ventilation system and the heating system. The heat
balance is analysed, and all the heat gain and heat losses are included while developing the model.
The unknown parameters in the model were estimated using ModestPy. The accuracy of the model
can be verified by analysing whether the values found for these parameters match the output of the
model with the measured values. The error between the output of Dymola and the measured value
is analysed, the model is modified, and the known parameters are re-estimated to obtain values closer
to the true physical meaning. Furthermore, using what-if analysis discover the minimum supply tem-
perature and minimum mass flow rate to meet the heat demand at a given setpoint indoor temperature.
The analysis time period was from the 14th to the 21st of November. The methods analyzed were
developed using data measured by sensors installed in the ventilation system, heating system and in
the classroom from Frederiksberg Skole, which is located in the Sorø on the island of Sjælland, in the

2.1
Fault detection and diagnosis for buildings
With the increased demand for indoor comfort, HVAC systems are being used more widely. However,
problems arise during operation, which causes the system to fail to work properly. In order to im-
prove the quality of system operation, it is essential to take effective measures to improve the quality
and standard of fault detection and diagnosis of ventilation systems. With the development of the
social economy and the progress of science and technology, various forms of fault detection and diagno-
sis have emerged, which help HVAC system fault detection and diagnosis have also been effectively used.
15 % to 30 % of the energy utilized in commercial buildings is lost due to equipment that is not
adequately maintained, is deteriorating, and is not being monitored.
With the broad implementa-
tion of automated condition-based maintenance, most of this waste may be avoided. Condition-based
maintenance of engineered systems is built on automated fault detection and diagnostics (FDD) and
prognostics[16].
In the report [17], the types of BMS component issues that impact a building’s energy eﬀiciency are
discussed, and techniques can be used to identify and diagnose them. Moreover, the challenges that lie
in this problem domain is highlighted. Finally, a strategy is provided on how to counterfeit them. It
points out that faults from the wrong configuration are typical faults in new buildings. To be specific,
wrongly configured building equipment, where the setting of the equipment is wrong, and misplaced or
wrongly wired sensors and actuators. Building FDD methods can be divided into three main classes.
They are model- and data-driven methods, and hybrid methods, that represent a combination of the
model- and data-driven approaches. The challenges of FDD for Smart Buildings are the following el-
ements. Firstly, in the literature, very little has been written about detecting errors related to these
problems: incorrectly programmed building management systems, incorrectly configured or conflicting
setpoints, incorrectly configured building equipment, and misplaced or incorredtly wired sensors and
actuators. In addition, another gap is the explanation process in diagnosis because it is significant to
explain why it has occurred as that could improve the processes of future diagnosis and repair, and
prevent the failure from happening in the future. What is more, the availability of data for diagnosis
is limited. The sharing of data where data security is ensured is necessary for this problem area. Fur-
thermore, the combined failures also had not received adequate attention. Figure 2.1 shows the whole
process from collecting data to analyse data to solve the challenge of Fault detection and diagnosis.

2.2
Black-, grey- and white-box model
Three different types of models are analysed and compared in terms of the interpretability and explain-
ability aspects[26]. It is concluded that the gray box model is best suited for modeling the thermal
system of buildings among these models.
White-box models(WBMs) are models based on theoretical knowledge. The physical parameters of the
system are compeletly known. In the process of developing the model, the properties of the system
are reflected realistically and accurately. In addition, the predicted results can be easily understood
and interpreted. However, because it is a model based on the characteristics of the system, it is not
universal. To be specific, it can only be applied to the analysis system study. If it is to be applied to
the simulation analysis of other models, modifications to the model are required to fit the new system.
If the system characteristics are very different between models, the new model needs to be rebuilt. For
large and complex systems, this work is very tedious. IDA ICE and EnergyPlus provide the environ-
ment for modelling WBM.
In contrast to WBMs, the inner workings of black-box models(BBMs) are not known, and diﬀicult to
interpret the meaning. The inputs to the model and the corresponding outputs are known. Because the
inner workings of the model are not fully understood, it is almost impossible to analyse and interpret

the predicted results. Black boxes are not suitable for modelling and analysing the thermal system of a
building. This is because there is no physical meaning between the input and output parameters. The
calculation of BBMs usually are on the use of algorithms. On the other hand, the models require a high
quality and quantity of input data.
Grey-box models (GBMs) are a combination of WBMs and GBMs, which include physical formulations
and algorithms for building systems modelling. Therefore, the model has interpretability and effective-
ness. Some parameters in the model can be calculated from a priori knowledge, and some unknown
parameters are obtained by parameter estimation to simulate the real thermal behaviour. GBMs do
not require the same full knowledge of the model’s internal information as WBMs. Moreover, GBMs
greatly reduce the complexity and increase the generality of the model. The results of the calcula-
tions are also more practically meaningful, which can provide potential approach for optimizing system
thermal behavior.
2.3
Parameter estimation in ModestPy
The goal of parameter estimation in this project is to find the most optimal values for the model parame-
ters within the allowed range. The final values for these parameters can fit the outputs of the model and
the real measured data, and minimize a cost function. ModestPy is a free and open-source Python tool
that is used for parameter estimation in models compliant with Functional Mock-up Interface (FMI),
developed by Arendt et [3].
ModestPy documentation is available on GitHub. The package can be installed using the command pip
install modestpy [22]. Running ModestPy requires defining the file path to assign to the Python script
to specify input files to run. These files are kept in a subfolder named resources. The input variables
(blue arrow) and the output variables (white arrow) of the Dymola model are time series input files
with the file extensions input.csv and result.csv, respectively. In addition, there are two ”.json” files:
a known.json and a est.json. The value of known parameters is saved in the known.json file. The
unknown parameters are estimated in the est.json file, including the initial guess and the upper and
lower bounds. The available algorithms im Modestpy are below:
• Parallel genetic algorithm (MODESTGA): It is a test run for a brand-new kind of distributed
algorithm. It is based on a parallel search conducted by individuals, each of whom has a detailed
description of the issue. By imitating biological principles of evolution, the individuals exchange
information[23]. This algorith is also a recommended by ModestPy.
• Egacy single-process genetic algorithm (GA): The fittest people are chosen for reproduction
in order to give rise to the next generation, which is how natural selection works[15].
• Pattern search (PS): It is a family of numerical optimization methods that does not require a
gradientc[24].
• Truncated Newton method(TNC): It is suitable to optimizing non-linear functions with
large numbers of independent variables. To identify an update to the function’s parameters, a
truncated Newton method consists of repeated application of an iterative optimization algorithm
to approximately solve Newton’s equations[32]
• Quasi Newton method(L-BFGS-B): It is used to either find zeroes or local maxima and
minima of functions, as an alternative to Newton’s method[29].
• Sequential least squares(SLSQP): It is an iterative method for constrained nonlinear optimiza-
tion. In mathematical issues when the objective function and the constraints are twice continu-
ously differentiable, SQP approaches are applied. Each optimization subproblem that is solved
by SQP methods optimizes a quadratic model of the objective under linearized constraints[30].

such algorithm was tested to estimate the parameters of an FMU built from a Modelica model of a

Python is a high-level, general-purpose programming language and supports multiple programming
paradigms, including structured (particularly procedural), object-oriented and functional program-

Different fields require different modelling systems to address specific issues. Models developed from
various modelling methodologies and simulation environments make up a complex system. In order to
implement the simulation of a whole complex system, different programs need to interact with each
other.
This means that the simulation environments from various specific tools collaborate, which
creates a need for model interaction. To accomplish this, EU Modelisar has developed the Functional
Mock-up Interface (FMI). FMI is a free standard that defines a container and an interface to exchange
dynamic simulation models using a ZIP file containing XML files, binaries, and C code [12]. Several tools
are compatible with the FMI standard, including MATLAB/Simulink, EnergyPluse, OpenModelica and
Dymola [13]. The FMI-compliant model is called the Functional Mock-up Unit (FMU). The model built
in Dymola software can be translated to FMU to be used for the parameter estimate with ModestPy.

Theory
In this chapter, the formulae for the heat balance of a room and for the calculation of the heat output

3.1
Building heat balance
Combined with the interaction between indoor and outdoor environments, the indoor thermal environ-
ment is analysed to provide a method for developing an indoor thermal environment load model. The
heat balance in a room is calculated below:
Qhs + Qso + Qocc + Qeq + Qlight = Qenv + Qvent + Qinf + Qv
(3.1)
Where:
Qhs is the heat gain from the heating
Qsol is the heat gain from the solar
Qocc is the heat gain produced from occupants
Qeq is the heat gain from the equipment
Qlight is the heat gain from the lighting
Qenv is the heat loss for transmission through the building envelope
Qvent is the heat loss by the ventilation system to exhaust air from indoor to outdoor
Qinf is the heat loss for infiltration
Qv is the heat loss for venting
All values are in Watt (W).
The heat loss for transmission through the building envelope is calculated below:
Qenv = U · A · (Tint −Tout)
(3.2)
Where:
U is the heat transfer coeﬀicient of the envelope component in W/m2k.
A is the area of the envelope component in m2.
Tint is indoor air calculation temperature in ◦C.
Tout is outdoor temperature in ◦C.

Case study
The research case for this project is described in detail in this chapter. In the first part, the main

4.1
Building description
The studied building, called Frederiksberg Skole, is a non-domestic building located in the Sorø on
the island of Sjælland, in the southwestern part of Copenhagen. The construction of the building was
developed in accordance with a basic sustainability strategy that responds to demanding environmental
conditions and intentionally plans to maximise the most of each square meter.
The building was
classified as a low-energy rating for 2015 and completed in early 2017. The building consists of two
floors above ground and one underground. The ground floor rooms are staff rooms, a nature workshop
for activities, a science laboratory, a cafe area and a kitchen. Classrooms are mainly gathered on the
first floor. The ground floor is used for machine rooms. As shown in Figure Figure 4.1, the whole
building is divided into two main parts. About the orientation of each part, the longest facade faces
north and south respectively. Because it is a newly built building, sensors are installed in this new
building for operator to monitor and control of equipments and systems. These sensors are distributed
in the heating and ventilation systems.
In addition, sensors are installed in the different rooms to
measure indoor temperature and air quality. The data from sensors is used to analyse the thermal
performance of the building, as well as for fault detection and diagnosis.
4.2
The heating system
The hot water from the district heating system provides heat for heating the building. The substation
is indirect connected to the district heating network and is located on the ground floor. The substation
is used for the heat exchange for the SH and DHW. The substation divides the distribution into two
circuits. The primary loop means that the hot water is transported from the heat source of the DH
system to the substation, where the heat is exchanged in the heat exchanger and then the water go
back to the DH network. The secondary loop is the side close to the consumer and heats the rooms
directly. Most connections to the network of the DH system are indirect connections, which is benifit to
allows the distribution to the consumer to be controlled. Figure 4.2 shows the scheme of the substation.
The upper storage (VVB) is connected to the domestic hot water(DHW). The DHW system is not
investigated in this project. The bottom heat exchanger is for the space heating and ventilation system.
In the second loop of the heat exchanger, the main supply water pipe is divided into four riser pipes,
which respectively heat the supply air of the ventilation system of the southern part and northern part
of the building, and heat the rooms of the southern part and northern part of the building. Each riser
rises to each floor and is distributed in sequence. The water returns to the substation in the same
sequence.

5.2
Genetic algorithm in ModestPy
The algorithm used for the parameter estimation is MODESTGA, which is a parallel genetic algorithm.
The setting of MODESTGA options was default, except CPU cores was set to 4. The maximum num-
ber of generations was defined as 50. The population size was defined as 30. The mutation rate was
defined at 0.01. The tournament size wad defined 3 and the solution tolerance was 0.001. The process
of parameter estimation is iterative. The initial value of the unknown parameter is iterated by the next
parameter estimate time. The values found for these parameters are optimal when the error between
Dymola’s output and the measured values is minimized. ModestPy is not only used to estimate the
unknown parameters but also to validate the model. In Model 1 and Model 2, the outputs of model
regarding the room temperature and CO2 concentration are validated by ModestPy.
PGA uses a hybrid strategy. Subpopulations try to find good local minima. After that good local
minima of one subpopulation are spread to neighboring subpopulations. Moverover, this algorithm can
solve complex search problems in very high dimensions[23]. Therefor, it is suitable for the parameter
estimation of the GBM developed in the project. Genetic algorithms are performed synchronously for
each individual in the population of unknown parameters to evaluate the itself. Unknown parameters
are internel indepent. With the synchronization signal, multiple processors jointly participate in the
genetic operations of selection, crossover and mutation performed by all individuals in the global mem-
ory. So this algorithm is a global research and the global optimal solution. In the project, try to run
the Model 2 five times to find the smallest the total normalized root mean-square error is the optimal
solution of the model.

CHAPTER 7
Conclusion
The objective of the project is to propose an experimental method to develop a dynamic model of the
heating system of a building, which helps to provide a method to analyse the performance of the radi-
ator heating system. Using what-if analysis discover the minimum supply temperature and minimum
mass flow rate to meet the heat demand.
The object of this project is a non-domestic building, called Frederiksberg Skole, located in the sorø on
the island of sjælland, in the southwestern part of Copenhagen. Research data can be obtained from
Building Management System (BMS). The current data that can be available in the month of November
2022. Firstly, the Nan values of the data in the dataset for some rooms were checked and the recording
of the valve opening of the supply air and the valve opening of the radiator valves were not captured.
The rooms identified as suitable for analysis were the three rooms in the west zone on the north side of
the building. To be specific, Room 108 was chosen because it is a critical zone. Room 105 has a west
facade and a south facade. Room 107 also has a west facade, the difference is that the room is located
facing north.
The two GBMs of a classroom are developed in the Dymola environment with Modelica language,
namely Model 1 and Model 2, respectively. The factors that affect the heat balance of the room are
considered in the model. They are heat gain from heating and solar, metabolic heat produced from
occupants, heat loss for transmission through the building envelope, heat loss by the ventilation system
to exhaust air from indoors to outdoors, and heat loss for infiltration.
Model 2 is a refinement of
Model 1 with the addition of consideration of heat losses from the air from adjacent rooms entering
the analysed room through the door leak. With regard to the structure of the model for the heating
system in GBM, with the limitation of detailed information on the installation of the radiators, it is
possible to estimate the maximum mass flow rate and the nominal heating power of a radiator based
on the input variables of supply temperature and room temperature set point. ModestPy is a free and
open-source Python tool that is used for parameter estimation of unknown parameters in the GBMs.
The parameters to be estimated were solar heat gain coeﬀicient, external wall resistance, internal wall
resistance, internal thermal mass factor, occupant heat generation effectiveness, number of occupants,
maximum mass flow rate, nominal heating power and the maximum door leak airflow. The analysis
was carried out on all three selected apartments from November 14th to 21st, 2022. The verification of
the parameter estimation results was to match the simulation of the indoor temperature and the CO2
concentration with the data from the sensors.
The outputs of the three rooms in Model 1 show that the simulated CO2 concentrations were in most
cases lower than the measured data. This means that the ventilation system has a fault that the fresh air
can not be deliveried to the room or the room ventilation system is manually shut down by occupants.
The calculation of the concentration in the Dymola model is influenced by the number of people and
the ventilation flow rate provided in the room. The estimated number of occupants remained the same
each day. When the valve opening of the supply air was close to 100 % during the occupied time on that
day, it resulted in a lower simulation CO2 concentration in the room than on other days. It is verified
in Room 105. In Room 107 and Room 108, the output of Dymola regarding the CO2 concentration was
higher in Model 2 than in Model 1. This was because the estimated number of occupants increased in
Model 2. With regard to the indoor temperature, the predicted temperature was basically consistent
==PAGE:64==
7 Conclusion
56
with the recording data, except for a short period of time before the person entered the room and after
they left the room. During these two periods, the temperature from sensors decreased to 18 ◦C, while
the predicted temperature declined to approximately 19 ◦C, which illustrates that some heat losses were
neglected.
The final value obtained by parameter estimation with ModestPy in Model 1 was as initial guess values
of unknown parameters for parameter estimation in model 2. Moreover, the unknown parameter value
named ’maxdoorleak’, which presents the air from the adjacent room entering the analysis room through
the door leak, was added to the est.json file of Model 2. It can be found that the total normalised root
mean-square error (NRMSE) of Model 2 is better than that of Model 1. And the fitting degree between
the fluctuation trend of the simulation temperature and the measured temperature was better than that
of Model 1. To be specific, the temperature began to drop when the ventilation system of the building
was open in the early morning and then increased after people entered the room. The simulation tem-
perature declined after occupants left the room and reach the lowest point. Then the ventilation system
of the building closed and the temperature increased slightly. The reason for this temperature decrease
case was that when the mechanical ventilation of the classroom closes, the ventilation system of the
whole building is not closed at the same time, which results in fresh air supply from the ventilation
system continuing to be distributed to other designated zones with ventilation needs. If the corridor,
which is the adjacent area to the analysed classroom, is still mechanically ventilated, then cold air from
the corridor is brought into the classroom through the door leak and affects the temperature of the
classroom.
The solar heat gain coeﬀicient was different among these three different rooms. It for Room 105 was
the highest, which was 0.41. This is because the room is located facing south and more solar heat gain
can be obtained. However, the solar heat gain coeﬀicient of Room 108 was the lowest because it only
has one northern facade. The model can capture indoor temperature changes caused by solar irradiance
very well. The goal of parameter estimation is to reduce extreme changes in predicted temperature,
making temperature fluctuations smooth and minimizing errors from measured values. The algorithm
used for parameter estimation is a genetic algorithm(GA), which is a global search. The final best values
of parameter estimation are the global optimal solution, not the local one. The unknown parameters
of GBM are internally independent of each other. Therefore, after training Model 2 several times, the
parameter estimation results when the total normalised root mean-square error (NRMSE) cost function
was the minimum, were chosen to be the most optimal result.
By the supply temperature reduced by 12 ◦C and the mass flow rate reduced by 55 %, a constant 21 ◦C
set point temperature in the room (excluding weekend time periods) can be met during the occupied
time period. A constant setpoint temperature is more energy eﬀicient than a set point temperature
that fluctuates each day. Lowering the supply temperature is less energy consumption than lowering
the mass flow.

CHAPTER 8
Suggestions for future
research
Some suggestions are list bellowing in order to improve the the analysis the performance of heating
system of the building with GBM.
If the objective is to model a radiator heating system and analyse the heating thermal behaviour of a
room, it is recommended to choose a room with only one radiator operating. Parametric estimation of
the nominal capacity of more than one radiator of the GBM developed in Dymola will make the model
less accurate. This is because the model cannot predict the state of operation between radiators. When
multiple radiators are combined into a single radiator model inserted into the GBM, it is not possible
to identify which radiator is the real problem. This may take time to confirm manually, reducing the
eﬀiciency of default detection and diagnosis of system faults with the model.
The second suggestion of improvement to the model is to model the heating and ventilation systems
independently. This is because the number of parameters in the model has an impact on the parame-
ter estimation results. The goal of parameter estimation is to find the ogolbal ptimal solution for the
unknown parameters to match the simulation outputs with the measurement results, rather than the
optimal solution for individual parameters themselves. As the number of parameters increases, the
number of unknown parameter inputs to the ModestPy for parameter estimation increases, resulting in
trend fluctuations in the simulated results that do not match the measured values as well.
Chanhanging the indoor temperature set point by using the same constant indoor temperature during
the whole week. This is done to try if the supply temperature can be reduced more to meet the indoor
heat demand. From this study, compared to the daily fluctuating setpoint temperature, the constant
setpoint temperature on weekdays can meet the heat demand of the room by reducing the supply tem-
perature by 12 ◦C.
To reduce the error between the output of the model and the measured values, it is recommended to
use more training data as the input of the model. In addition, a combination of global and local search
methods is used when using ModestPy for parameter estimation.
