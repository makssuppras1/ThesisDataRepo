Abstract
This thesis investigates an expansion of capacity at Copenhagen Central Station (Kû¡benôÙ
havn H appr. KH) by implementing a new tunnel between the northern and southern parts
of the station. The primary purpose of the tunnel is to return trains that arrive at CopenôÙ
hagen Central from the south without the need to reverse at the platforms. The project has
accordingly been named the Return Loop throughout the report. The report introduces
and analyzes the railway network of Zealand, focused on Fjernbanen and the current trafôÙ
fic conditions at KH. The report also includes some of the upcoming projects financed by
the new Infrastructure Plan 2035.
Calculations and analysis show that the Return Loop will enable higher punctuality and
robustness in the network with the expected traffic volume in the future. Currently, the
conflicting approach routing and a low number of platform tracks at KH contribute to delays
spreading between railway lines. In addition, a considerable number of train systems use
the line section between KH and ûsterport, contributing to high capacity utilization.
The Return Loop is evaluated using a simulation model to determine punctuality, robustôÙ
ness, and traffic flow effects. Two scenarios in relation to alternative track layouts at
Copenhagen Central are created. In scenario 2, a simplified track layout at KH is examôÙ
ined with increased approach speed from the south.Calculations for the simulation model
showed an increase in punctuality across the analysis area of 1.0 and 1.3 percentage
points for the two scenarios, respectively. The increase in punctuality is most significant
at KH, though improvements are observed at most stations in the network. In addition, the
line section between ûsterport and KH, where traffic volume is reduced and transferred
to the Return Loop, is calculated to have a substantial punctuality improvement. The two
scenarios enabled higher punctuality by reducing platform utilization from 51 % to 41 and
42 %.
A robustness study showed that the Return Loopãs track layout improves the robustness
of the traffic flow. The number of unscheduled stops on arrival for the south is reduced
from an average of 51.7 to 27.7 per day. Furthermore, the simulation model was used to
assess circumstances when significant disruptions occur. With the traffic level operated
in the model, the Return Loop proved most capable at maintaining punctuality. Based
on the assessment analysis, both Scenario 1 and Scenario 2 were calculated as socioôÙ
economic unprofitable with an internal rate of return of 1.5 and 1.9, respectively. Thus,
Scenario 2 is considered the best alternative despite higher construction costs. However,
many uncertainties in the construction budget can affect the results.
The report recommends further analysis of the Return Loop based on the results calcuôÙ
lated regarding punctuality, capacity utilization, and traffic flow. It is considered relevant to
analyze the project in a larger context with the development of the Danish railway network
with significant infrastructure expansions pooled and assessed in relation to a transport
policy objective. In particular, it is relevant to assess transport objectives for the future
Copenhagen Central and whether the strategic effects concerning capacity requirements,
commuter flow, and international traffic are achieved with the Return Loop.

Introduction
Copenhagen Central Station, from now on Kû¡benhavn H appr. KH, is the largest station
in Denmark measured by the number of trains arriving and departing. Since its commisôÙ
sioning in 1911, the current station has been a central hub for passenger railway traffic in
Copenhagen. Over the last decades, the infrastructure towards Copenhagen has been
expanded. The traffic flow is now integrated with three lines from the south connected
through KH to one line towards the north. However, the number of platforms has been
almost constant since 1911, and the increased traffic and conflicting train paths contribute
to a lack of capacity and lower punctuality. Therefore, this project analyzes an infrastrucôÙ
ture project to increase platform capacity at Kû¡benhavn H and eliminate conflicting train
paths. The project is a new tunnel to return trains arriving from the south, named the
Return Loop. The objective is to analyze effects on traffic flow, punctuality, and robustôÙ
ness to illustrate effects and assess whether an infrastructure project such as the Return
Loop could be interesting for further research. The following section contains the problem
statement for the project and the specific thesis learning objectives.
The report first introduces the current railway network in Chapter 3, focusing on the naôÙ
tional/regional lines (Fjernbanen) of Zealand and the infrastructure around Kû¡benhavn H.
The intent is to create an overview of the infrastructure used in the project and different
conditions concerning rolling stock and shunting. Based on the infrastructure, structurôÙ
ing and choices about traffic patterns are reviewed. The primary purpose is to determine
the number of trains to operate at Kû¡benhavn H. The project is planned according to fuôÙ
ture traffic demand and estimated based on various reports. The chapter also examines
opportunities for increasing train operations.
Chapter 4 analyzes the specific capacity restrictions currently observed in the railway
network, and a more comprehensive study is performed for issues at Kû¡benhavn H. In
addition, findings and ideas that previous projects have analyzed at KH are reviewed. The
results of the capacity restriction analysis are used in Chapter 5 to introduce two alternaôÙ
tive track layouts for the implementation of the Return Loop. In addition, the procedure
and method for the structuring of timetables and differences between the scenarios are
examined.
Chapter 6 reviews the process and method for creating the simulation model in RailSys.
The purpose is to explain the calibration method and the advantages and limitations when
using a simulation program to evaluate train operation. The model makes it possible to
review the results, mainly focusing on differences in punctuality and capacity utilization.
The simulation model is also used in Chapter 7 to analyze specific robustness scenarios.

2.1
Project description
Kû¡benhavn H (Copenhagen Central Station) is one of Denmarkãs largest train stations
with different lines converging in a complex track and platform layout. Two new railway
lines have been built to Kû¡benhavn H in the last 20 years, without the platform capacity
having been significantly changed.This has challenged capacity at the station and conôÙ

The overall objective of this master thesis is to investigate how capacity and timetabling

Loopã. The idea is to create a new singleôÙtrack tunnel north of the station for trains returnôÙ

times. The new Return Loop will eliminate the need for most turnbacks and free up caôÙ

3.1
Method
Since this chapter presents the infrastructure and examines possible extensions of the
current network, it is necessary to determine a base year for the analysis. A base year
indicates when the examining infrastructure project is expected to open in relation to a
scenario with no new project. The chosen base year is especially relevant concerning
other planned projects and the expected traffic volume. For this project, the base year of
2032 is selected. The Danish railway network is undergoing substantial developments,
and major projects will be completed around 2032. These projects include, i.a., the FerôÙ
mern Belt Fixed Link, the signalling program, and complete electrification, requirements
for operating more trains to KH. In addition, the Traffic Plan for the national railway 2017ôÙ
2032, which is introduced later in the chapter, contains data and traffic patterns for the
expected service in 2032. Applying a base year with data is an advantage when creating
timetables and performing the assessment analysis. Furthermore, 2032 is considered the
earliest a major infrastructure project at KH can be completed with the necessary surveys
and construction period.
First, the current infrastructure is reviewed. In a later section, expansion projects of the
existing infrastructure are examined in relation to what to include in the model.

The West Line (TIB 1), Ringsted Line (TIB 6), and ûresund Line (TIB 11) represent what

The maximum speed of the Danish railway network is 160 km/h (180 km/h for certain
rolling stocks). The speed will be upgraded to 200 km/h on some sections of the core netôÙ
work soon in connection with the Signalling Program. The Ringsted Line is constructed for
a maximum line speed of 250 km/h. No train sets are expected to utilize that speed in the
near future, and the speed for this analysis is thus limited to 200 km/h. Lines outside the
core network generally have a lower top speed. All lines on Zealand are electrified (or unôÙ
der electrification) with 25 kV 50 Hz. The exception is the line between Roskilde and ûlby.

The legacy system is still used for most lines. The system has a relayôÙbased interlockôÙ

This paragraph aims to review the method of producing the timetables in the project. Only

This project uses the software program RailSys developed by Rail Management ConsulôÙ
tants GmbH (RMcon). RailSys is a railway simulation and assessment program primarily
designed to support infrastructure managers and operators to calculate, plan, and evaluôÙ
ate timetable projects and infrastructure. RailSys is divided into three main parts: infrasôÙ
tructure, timetable, and simulation. The simulation part of RailSys is explained in more
detail in section 6.
Work in this project is based on an infrastructure model provided by Banedanmark conôÙ
taining the current railway network in Denmark as of 2022. The model is then adapted with
the projects considered a condition for this study, including Infrastructure Plan 2035 outôÙ
lined in section 3.4. The infrastructure is created using different subôÙelements in RailSys.
The most important of these are:
ãÂ Stations: Indicates an area with switches, stopping locations, or both. Only at
stations can trains make scheduled stops. Station boundaries signify when the train
enters and leaves the station and when a station route starts and ends
ãÂ Station routes: Defines which routes can be used through the station. Station
routes are supported by the interlocking of the station. Only the most relevant routes
are implemented to simplify the model for this project. Routes for, e.g., leftôÙhand
running are therefore not included. Station routes do not affect signalling and the
block sections. They are thus only used to plan train paths through a station.
ãÂ Signalling: Signals define the location for the separation of different block sections.
Identical signal locations are used as the current infrastructure unless changes to
the infrastructure require adjustments.
ãÂ Block sections: Indicates a section of tracks between different signals. It is possiôÙ
ble to create multiple block sections from each signal. As with station routes, only
the most relevant for the project are created.
When the Return Loop is completed in the RailSys model, the entire loop is implemented
as part of KH station. The loop is connected at both the north and south end of the
station, requiring it to be either one station or divided into two parts. In a situation with two
parts, the simulation results would not be comparable between the base model and the

6
Simulation and results
This chapter presents the structure and parameters of the simulation model. Settings
and calibration of the model will largely influence the simulation results and thus the data
output of the project. The following sections present the selected settings and methods
for choosing the parameters and calibrating the simulation model. Finally, the chapter
presents and analyzes the timetable and simulation model results.
6.1
RailSys ôÙ Using Simulation
In addition to producing timetables, RailSys also contains the potential to simulate the
traffic set up in the timetables. RailSys is a microsimulation program where each train
is assigned a high level of detail. The timetable is emulated by an algorithm handling
trains based on several input parameters that define how the trains need to interact. The
algorithm then makes choices about dispatching preferences and managing delays. The
algorithm uses multiple iterations of the timetable to create reliable results and minimize
statistical uncertainties. For this project, an iteration level of 500 is found acceptable. 500
is used in similar big simulation projects in Banedanmark with extensive analysis areas
and trains. The number of iterations is a weighting between precision and computational
time.
The use of RailSys has both advantages and disadvantages. The main argument for
using simulation is the possibility of testing infrastructure and timetables early, with a high
degree of detail long before the commissioning of the actual project. In addition, the
simulations can derive accurate punctuality data for different train systems, stations, and
between given timetables. However, it is essential not to interpret simulation results as
a direct replica of the future. The amount of settings and preconditions is crucial for the
output, and these can be difficult to adjust according to a future reality. The settings and
limitations of the model are described in more detail in the following sections.
6.2
Computing delays in the model
The timetable as a baseline is assumed to have no conflicts, rolling stock defects, or
temporary speed restrictions. Hence, no delays will, in principle, occur when simulating.
Trains will in daily operations experience delays creating conflict conditions. Therefore,
a certain number of delay parameters must be added to the models to simulate these.
These parameters are executed based on three types of delays.

6.3.1
Limitation of the dispatching algorithm
The dispatching algorithm in RailSys is assumed to make sensible choices and priorities
similar to a traffic manager in real life. However, some factors can lead to limitations when
interpreting the results from the model:
1. The algorithmãs ability to look far enough into the future
2. The algorithmãs ability to select routes at complex and large stations.
3. The model limited opportunities to manage major delays and incidents.
The algorithmãs limited possibilities to calculate far into the future can, for instance, affect
the prioritization of train departures. An example is a timely regional train that departs from
Ringsted towards Kû¡benhavn H before a delayed InterCity train with a faster schedule and
fewer stops. At Ringsted, the viable option for the algorithm is to depart the regional train
to keep it punctual. Likely, the IC train could, however, have used the run time allowance
to make up the delay further out in the network and arrived punctually at Kû¡benhavn H.
By departing behind the regional trainëƒ the InterCity train will now be significantly more
delayed. Prioritization and input parameters are therefore critical to improving the algoôÙ
rithmãs performance. Issues regarding prioritization are usually encountered during the
calibration phase discussed in the subsequent section.
The second limitation is the algorithmãs ability to make route choices at complex stations.
RailSys routing algorithm is heuristicôÙbased and not optimizationôÙbased. The heuristic
function means that the algorithm will often choose the first feasible solution when enôÙ
countering a conflict, creating limitations to the dispatching priorities. RailSys will be able
to change platforms and routes in case of delays but will, in some instances, prioritize the
planned platform track. The algorithmãs selection regarding reôÙrouting is executed before
entering the station. In actual operation, traffic managers may change train paths at the
last minute if circumstances favor a better routing for the general traffic flow. However,
the calculation complexity becomes too great with the number of trains in the model, and
the algorithm instead prioritizes the planned route. Especially at KH, problems can arise
with route selection. The approach tracks are far from the platforms, and trains spend
prolonged time at the station. In the simulation model used in the project, the number of
alternative tracks and routes is limited compared to reality to improve the algorithm. PreôÙ
selecting the most used paths reduces complexity and forces the algorithm to prioritize
only the most relevant.
Finally, the model has limited options to manage major delays and incidents. Very high
delays would lead to deadlocks in the model. Therefore, the simulation model is planned

according to ãnormalã delay levels. However, how the infrastructure handles incidents is
especially interesting and is therefore analyzed in more detail in robustness studies in
chapter 7.
6.4
Calibration of the model
Usually, the level of punctuality in a base model is determined according to historical data
(For instance, data from RDS). Creating a level of delay that is plausible is then calibrated
accordingly using iterations of the simulated timetable. Each iteration will generate a
punctuality level at crucial stations that can either be approved or rejected. However, with
a timetable planned for 2032, adjusting to these levels may be very challenging. This is
primarily due to:
1. The number of trains in 2032 is significantly higher than in 2021, and thus punctuality
is expected to be lower. However, an unknown factor is the punctuality effect when
implementing ERTMS.
2. Train systems run differently and may therefore contribute to unknown network efôÙ
fects. In addition, the effect of new infrastructure projects is an unknown parameter.
3. Adjustment to a delay level corresponding to 2021 requires a long time and may not
be possible.
The conclusion is that calibration of a delay level set based on historical data is overly
timeôÙconsuming, concerning the limitations of the project, and may lead to incorrect outôÙ
comes. To still use the simulation tool, the model is applied a level of delay that can be
characterized as sufficiently when analyzing Kû¡benhavn H (Typically between 80 and 95
% arrival punctuality depending on the line). Therefore, the level of delay in the model
may not necessarily constitute the expected level in 2032. Consequently, it may be chalôÙ
lenging to compare punctuality to other projects due to different parameters and delay
levels. Hence, the direct purpose of the simulations is to compare internally between the
different scenarios.
By default, only the base model is selected for calibration to maintain credible data outputs.
Selected parameters can then be transferred from the base model to other alternatives,
usually only calibrated to avoid substantial errors. This ensures that the same values
are present in both the base model and the scenarios. Calibration occurs in two phases
ôÙ 1. Corrections of errors that affect punctuality 2. Adjustment of punctuality to a suffiôÙ
cient level. Errors typically occur when train systems acquire significantly higher or lower
expected punctuality between two stations. These noteworthy errors are usually found
using fewer iterations, which is less timeôÙconsuming. When adjusting the level of delay,
a higher number of simulations is needed to eliminate statistical uncertainty between itôÙ
erations sufficiently. This part of the process, therefore, requires the most computational
time.

Robustness analysis
Robustness is a term often used in railway planning that is challenging to quantify and
access. The following chapter describes the robustness studies performed in the project.
The section first describes how robustness is assumed for this project. Then two robustôÙ
ness studies are completed based on the simulation model.
7.1
Method
The first important priority is to assess the term robustness in timetabling. How should
robustness be quantified so that it can be further investigated? Therefore, the first section
examines different definitions of robustness and how it can be used in the project. The
following section reviews statistics from the simulation model, which can evaluate robustôÙ
ness in relation to traffic flow. The RailSys simulation model is then adapted to analyze a
specific issue concerning significant disruptions in the last section. This section also evalôÙ
uates some of the limitations when using the simulation model. The robustness simulation
model uses the same parameters, settings, and timetables as the previously mentioned
simulation model. Due to time limitations, robustness scenarios are only performed for
the base model and scenario 1. Scenario 2 is likely to perform better than scenario 1.
7.2
Robustness in timetables
Robustness for a railway network is an often used term in timetable planning but with no
precise definition. A network with very few incidents and breakdowns could be considered
robust. An extensive network with multiple alternative routes for the passengers in case
of disruptions could also be classified as robust. For this project, however, robustness is
used to express the extent to which the timetables can manage delays. Different previous
analyzes have given different definitions of how this can be expressed:
ãÂ Anderson. E study on assessment of robustness in railway traffic timetables (2014):
The tolerance in a timetable for a certain degree of uncertainty or the capability to
cope with unexpected troubles without significant modifications. [And14]
ãÂ Dewilde, T., Sels, P., Cattrysse, D., Vansteenwegen, P. study on defining robustness
of a railway timetable (2011): A robust timetable minimizes the real passenger travel
time in case of small disturbances. The ability to limit the secondary delays and
ensure short recovery times. [And14]
ãÂ Cacchiani, V & Toth, P. study of nominal and robust train timetabling problems
(2012): A tradeôÙoff between the nominal objective (efficiency) and the robustness
objective must be considered, favoring an efficient use of the railway infrastructure
while guaranteeing solutions that remain feasible under ãsmallã disturbances. [CT12]
From the above, it can thus be concluded that a robust timetable requires the possibility to
absorb delays in case of some uncertainty with the run and dwell times and reduce the imôÙ
pacts of delays spreading to other train systems. Some of these tools are already present
when planning timetables. The timetable must be planned with a run time allowance to
account for minor delays in the network. This project adjusts the run time allowance acôÙ
cording to the line speed or the rolling stock maximum speed. Lines with higher speeds
require a higher percentage of run time allowance.

==PAGE:71==
However, the challenging part is to quantify robustness. Does the addition of the planned
run time allowance designate a robust timetable? If not, how much run time allowance is
required, or are other circumstances more critical to improving robustness. Some issues
were highlighted in section 4.2, e.g., capacity utilization depends on traffic volume, traffic
heterogeneity, and the possible headway.
A manageable way to increase robustness could be to decrease capacity utilization (traffic
volume). Less traffic signifies an increase in headway and less tendency for delays to
spread. However, as the study from Cacchiani, V & Toth, P described, a robust timetable
is a tradeôÙoff with an effective timetable. A timetable with a low number of trains may be
robust but perhaps not attractive due to lower frequency from a passenger standpoint.
The same argument is valid for a timetable with an increased homogenized scheduled or
increased run time allowance. Train systems will have better opportunities to catch delays
on behalf of extended travel time. A theoretical example is a train system calculated to
arrive 8 out of 10 times punctually with a standard run time allowance. An increase in the
run time allowance of 1 min could raise this to 9 out of 10. The train system would then
be more robust, but already onôÙtime departures would require an extra minute of run time.
Therefore, it is not considered attractive to assess robustness by increasing the run time
allowance in this project. Instead, robustness studies focus on traffic flow and disruption.
These studies are explained in more detail in the following sections.

The following subsections analyze the effects of changed travel time, train changes, and

transportation between scenarios distributed and assigned to different routes. A model

sengers who will receive a given benefit or cost from the project.

In order to be able to evaluate whether the project can be characterized as profitable, it

ãÂ Net present value (NPV) compares the amount of cost and benefits over the calôÙ

the cost and benefit streams. For a project to be profitable, the rate must be higher

ãÂ Net Benefit Investment Ratio (NBIR) indicate repayment of public money invested

ãÂ Scrap Value is the value of the project after the calculation period. The scrap value

ãÂ Distortion of labor market indicates additional tax collection as a result of the govôÙ

ãÂ Labor market benefit denotes the impact on transport costs as a consequence

9.2.3
Uncertainties with dwell times at KH
As a starting point, dwell time at KH is based on the assumptions laid out in Appendix
A. It is assumed that there must be a minimum dwell time of 3 minutes for all trains. 3
minutes is considered sufficient because there is often a good distribution of passengers
either boarding or alighting. For example, ICL trains arriving from Odense departing for
ûsterport will have many disembarking passengers and few boarding. However, this
situation will change with the Return Loop, where arriving systems must return to their
destination with many passengers. For example, ICL 3xx is an express train arriving
from Odense that departs as ICL 3xx towards Odense after 4 minutes. This can cause
problems with sufficient dwell time when passengers have to disembark and board, often
with bags. The risk is that more delays may occur if the minimum dwell time in these
cases is not increased.

9.6
Final recommendation
Based on the results calculated regarding punctuality, capacity utilization, and traffic flow,
it is recommended to investigate the Return Loop further. The improvements to traffic flow
must be assessed in a larger context with the development of the Danish railway network
to enable a more considerable amount of traffic through Kû¡benhavn H. Therefore, it is
also recommended that significant infrastructure expansions are pooled and assessed in
relation to a transport policy objective. This would make it possible to assess alternatives
at KH concerning capacity requirements, commuter flows, and the role of Kû¡benhavn H
for regional, national, and international traffic, respectively.
Whether the construction of the Return Loop is the best solution at KH can be challenging
compared to other alternatives being investigated. However, it is considered optimal for
Kû¡benhavn H as a hub to create the opportunity to eliminate the requirement for turnbacks
and promote the opportunity to connect traffic between railway lines. The current allocated
budget of 900 million for the redesign of KH is unlikely to resolve problems of both elimôÙ
inating conflicting train paths and creating the necessary capacity platform increase. As
with the KûR and LOKO projects discussed in section 3.3, it will be possible to solve
subôÙproblems, but further issues will be left until additional funding becomes available.
Meanwhile, significant engineering work has to restrict traffic with great inconvenience to
passengers. As described, various projects are currently being analyzed. However, lack
of space, restrictions of track construction, and projects with other interests have meant
only a few possible locations where tunnel portals can be constructed. Therefore, a clear
strategy must first be developed for Kû¡benhavn Hãs role, and then the most relevant areas
for tunnel portals selected and reserved.

Conclusion
Kû¡benhavn H is the central hub for national and regional traffic in Copenhagen. The
infrastructure is built according to an asymmetrical traffic flow with three lines integrated
from the south connected through KH to one line towards the north. Although some expanôÙ
sions have alleviated traffic congestion in recent decades, issues remain. The number of
platform tracks is relatively low, contributing to a high platform utilization, mainly because
the asymmetrical traffic flow requires turnbacks at the platforms. Conflicting train paths
reduce traffic flow and contribute to network influences which spread delays between the
various lines. Furthermore, due to the low number of platform tracks, there is a significant
dependence on the line section between KH and ûsterport, which contributes to very high
capacity utilization.
This project has investigated capacity, robustness, and punctuality by implementing a
new tunnel to return trains arriving from the south, named the Return Loop. Two scenarôÙ
ios in relation to alternative track layouts at Kû¡benhavn H were analyzed. In scenario 2, a
simplified track layout at KH was examined with increased speed for the approach tracks
from the south. For both scenarios, operational advantages, including higher punctualôÙ
ity and saved operating costs, were estimated when shortening some traffic patterns to
Kû¡benhavn H. Calculations of the simulation models showed an increase in punctuality
across the analysis area of 1.0 and 1.3 percentage points for the two scenarios, respecôÙ
tively. The line section between ûsterport and KH, where traffic volume is reduced and
transferred to the Return Loop, is expected to have the most substantial punctuality imôÙ
provement. The elimination of turnbacks and dwell time reduction likewise contribute to
higher capacity in the platform tracks. Platform utilization is reduced from 51 % to 41 and
42 %, respectively. In addition to higher punctuality, the robustness analysis also estabôÙ
lished that implementing the Return Loop reduces the number of unscheduled stops and
improves traffic flow with disruptions. Especially the number of unscheduled can become
relevant if train operation is to expand further than the traffic volume used in the project.
However, both scenarios are calculated as socioôÙeconomically unprofitable with negative
net present value. Scenario 2 is calculated to be more socioôÙeconomically, suggesting
that the higher construction cost is offset by less delay in the network. The assessment
analysis performed is assumed to have many uncertainties and not included effects. SenôÙ
sitivity analysis demonstrates that including effects like capacity can significantly change
the CBA. In addition, it is considered necessary to analyze the construction budget to a
greater extent, which is associated with several uncertainties.
The report recommends further analysis of the Return Loop to determine the most signifiôÙ
cant benefits and disadvantages accurately. One option for further research is to examine
the Return Loop using the National Transport Model. It will likely be more precise when
investigating route choice and train change effects. It is recommended that a project such
as the Return Loop be examined as part of a more extensive transport policy objective
analysis with a strategy for Kû¡benhavn Hãs role for regional, national and international trafôÙ
fic. Analyzing according to a strategy would clarify whether the capacity requirements for
KH are fulfilled with the Return Loop or whether it is relevant to investigate larger capacôÙ
ity projects. As suggested in the discussion, clarifying gains in connection with changing
traffic patterns via the Return Loop is also possible in further research. In addition, it is
recommended that the simulation modelãs limitations be further examined and defined
whether it has affected the results.

calculate the minimum travel time between each station.

C
Appendix ôÙ Parameters for simulation
This appendix presents crucial input parameters and setting for the simulation model in
the project. Other settings are referenced in section 6.
C.1
Simulation Time
The simulation and results are calculated for a weekday between 5 and 21. This period inôÙ
cludes both rush hours and allows network effects to spread. Typically, punctuality is highôÙ
est with less traffic (early morning, late evening, and night). Adding additional hours and
weekend traffic could provide a more significant misrepresentation of punctuality when
analyzing KH.
C.2
Usage of run time allowance
An essential parameter for the data output is the proportion of the run time and dwell time
allowance used to catch up on delays. Typically, this parameter will depend on driver
behavior and dispatching priorities. Often, however, it will not be possible to use 100 %
as this requires perfect performance when operating the network. This project assumes
that 80 % of the allowance can be used. Presently, there are no standard practices for
the amount of allocated supplement in a planning project. 80 % is assumed to be an
acceptable level that will accommodate different driver behaviors.
C.3
Alternative tracks
Alternative tracks define the number of tracks the algorithm can select at a station. In
the case of delays, the algorithm may deviate from the planned platform to one of the
predefined alternative tracks based on a list of the most suitable tracks for this traffic
pattern. Alternative tracks are made for all directions towards all directions at a station,
as there may be a difference in preferences based on origin and destination. Figure
C.1 displays the number and pattern for alternative tracks at KH on arrival from Valby
towards ûsterport in the base model. The arrows indicate which platform the algorithm
first examines based on the planned platform track.

As described in section 6.3.1, limiting the algorithm number of alternative tracks reduce
simulation time and reduces errors. For example, at KH, there are several additional
possibilities for platform track allocation, mainly tracks 5 and 6 in this traffic connection.
However, using these platform tracks is considered less functional, creating conflicting
train paths. Hence, an initial analysis is conducted to determine the most usable platôÙ

4 alternative platform tracks are selected. 4 has been found through calibration to be a
reasonable relation between flexibility and the algorithmãs limitations. Limiting alternative
tracks is performed at all major stations in the analysis area.
C.3.1
Routing station
Alternative tracks are only relevant where routing stations are assigned. Trains enterôÙ
ing nonôÙrouting stations will consistently be assigned the planned track. All stations with
switches could be designated as a routing station. However, routing stations require a
lot of computing power and significantly increase simulation time. Therefore, only staôÙ
tions with a realistic possibility for overtaking and reôÙplatforming are designated. Routing
stations are selected based on a review of the network. An example is Borup and Viby
Sj, which have switches and options for alternative track use. Borup is set as a routing
station since the station has an intermediate track used for overtaking. Viby Sj is not set
as a routing station as using an alternative route would require leftôÙhand running. Hence,
using this option would only rarely be feasible.
C.4
Settings for overtaking and reôÙplatforming
This section describes the input parameters used in the simulation. The input parameters
define when the algorithm should make selections such as reôÙplatforming, overtaking,
and route choice. The parameters for dispatching can be divided into two categories:
Minimum lateness and intervention range. Minimum lateness defines when a delay is
significant to trigger a dispatching measure. The conflict is ignored if the delay is less than
the given input parameter. More dispatching measures are performed with a lower value
of minimum lateness but with an increased risk of unnecessary actions. The intervention
range defines how far in advance a train may be dispatched. The algorithm performs
dispatching measures long before a conflicting train path with a high intervention range.
A lower time limit causes the algorithm to make choices closer to the conflict itself and
thus better coordination closer to the actual traffic flow.[RMc21]
