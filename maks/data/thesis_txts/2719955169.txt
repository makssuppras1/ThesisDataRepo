Abstract 
Green hydrogen is currently enjoying a widespread momentum in many national and 
international decarbonization strategies. The proposed hydrogen economy not only offers 
opportunities to combat climate change, but also enhances energy security via reducing the 
reliance on imports; a fact that has become ever more apparent as Europe enters new 
geopolitical and energy market reality. This thesis focuses on analyzing the European Union’s 
hydrogen policies, specifically the 2022 REPowerEU plan and its provisions on diversifying 
Europe’s energy mix via the target of 20 million tonnes (of which 10 million imports) of 
hydrogen and 65 GW of electrolyser capacity by 2030. The energy system Balmorel, which 
optimizes investments to meet heat, electricity, and hydrogen demands, was used to analyze 
potential scenarios between the years 2020 and 2050. The research conducted in this thesis 
reveals that reaching these targets is feasible and that hydrogen will constitute a large part of 
the future European energy mix. Transmission corridors between potential importing and 
exporting countries are analyzed in the broader context of a pan-European hydrogen backbone. 
Southwestern Europe shows massive potential for not only cheap renewable energy production, 
but to become a major hydrogen exporter. For green hydrogen, prices in most member states 
are shown to become competitive with conventional hydrogen as early as 2030, with prices 
reaching the €1.00 – 2.00/kg range.

1 Introduction 
Europe is battling record-breaking energy prices as a series of political, geographic, and market 
factors have coalesced into a perfect storm that threatens economic prosperity and the post-
pandemic recovery. At the same time, limiting greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions via concrete 
actions are on the agenda of politicians and policy makers around the European Union (EU), as 
pressures to hit various climate pledges mount. This becomes a challenging dilemma as the EU 
must decide how to manage energy security without losing sight of its climate neutrality targets. 
Alongside the current debate about the best path forward, green and blue hydrogen (H2) have 
entered the spotlight and have been touted as crucial elements in the future energy mix to 
replace conventional hydrocarbon fuels such as coal, oil, and natural gas (NG). These fuels are 
low to no carbon and can fundamentally change the energy supply, consumption, and 
production structures while ensuring that the three pillars of EU energy policy are met: 
sustainability, security of supply, and competitiveness [1].  
Hydrogen, alongside energy efficiency measures, direct electrification and a massive build-out 
of renewable energy sources (RES) comprise the bulk of the EU’s current energy and climate 
strategy. In sectors where electrification is not economical or practical, hydrogen can bridge the 
decarbonization effort gap. These end-use sectors include shipping, aviation, heavy-transport, 
chemical manufacturing, steel, and fertilizers. However, significant challenges to realizing 
hydrogen’s potential remain. These include high production costs, storage and transport 
requirements, infrastructure needs, and an underdeveloped end-use value chain. These issues, 
along with a clear European regulatory and policy framework, must be solved and are key to 
scaling up hydrogen’s competitiveness and allow this nascent industry to develop.  
Currently, European industrial sectors consume an estimated 8.6 Mt of hydrogen annually, the 
majority going towards refining, and fertilizer production. This corresponds to less than 2% of 
Europe’s energy mix. In addition, this quantity of hydrogen is entirely being produced using 
natural gas feedstock and falls into the ‘grey’ category which produces an estimated 70 to 100 
Mt CO2 emissions annually [2]. Additionally, this hydrogen is primarily used locally for on-site 
manufacturing or production and lacks the infrastructure to be used at distant demand centers. 
However, as the hydrogen backbone takes shape, energy analyst experts project that European 
hydrogen demand in 2050 could be as high as 2150-2,750 TWh by 2050 which corresponds to 
20-25% of European final energy consumption [3]. A vast network of hydrogen pipelines,
storage facilities, and demand off-takers across different sectors are envisioned in the future
hydrogen economy.
Target-wise, the EU has presented aggressive renewable energy and electrolyser capacity 
targets. Over time, these targets for RES share in the energy system have evolved. The 2022 rise 
in energy prices has led the EU to raise its hydrogen ambitions beyond its 2021 Fit for 55 
package which was initially set at 5.6 million tonnes (Mt) [4].  The new 2022 REPowerEU 
targets have increased to target to 20Mt (10 Mt domestic, 10Mt import) and signal a strong 
determination to accelerate the transition to clean energy while ending the bloc's dependence 
on Russian gas [5]. 
These ambitious EU goals are not possible to achieve without the commitment of all 27 EU 
member states which are tasked with producing their own national energy and climate plans 
(NECPs) [6]. In the context of H2, all EU member states are encouraged to develop and 
implement their own strategies to enable hydrogen deployment taking the European 
framework and guidance into account. In the latest NECP's, submitted on April 2020, all but two 
Member States mention hydrogen’s role in decarbonizing their respective energy systems. 
However, only some of the plans cover concrete and quantitative measures to facilitate H2 
==PAGE:7==
6 
 
deployment and integration into their existing systems. A study analyzing the NECP's by the 
FCH JU found that only half of the plans have specific goals for hydrogen development, while 
others are focused on Power-to-X demonstration projects which may also support hydrogen. 
Most plans prioritize using hydrogen in transportation, followed by industry [7]. The study 
concludes that the period from 2022 to 2030 is seen as a preparatory phase for hydrogen, with 
a focus on using electrolysis and renewable energy to produce hydrogen, although some plans 
also mention using low-carbon "blue" hydrogen.  
  
1.1 Problem Statement and Project Scope 
The trajectory for green hydrogen and efuels within the EU is complex and faces a myriad of 
considerations ranging from techno-economic ones such as technology readiness, scalability, 
infrastructure, and competitiveness as well as sociopolitical ones such public acceptance, access 
to incentives, and political approval. To address each of these topics on a deep level would be 
outside of the scope of a master’s thesis. However, the aforementioned factors will be presented 
in a simple manner in the context of the EU and supported by a variety of sources including the 
European Hydrogen backbone (EHB), IRENA and IEA analyses as well as academic papers 
examining energy system models on RES and hydrogen build out.  
Firstly, a background theory of hydrogen and efuels as an energy carrier will be presented. 
Demand, production levels, and various technological considerations will be introduced. 
Economic comparisons to conventional technologies will be assessed with a levelized cost of 
hydrogen established. Conversion pathways from hydrogen to various efuel derivatives will be 
presented with various sectoral end-use potentials.  
A policy deep-dive with the EU’s action plans will follow. The development of a comprehensive 
set of policy goals, actionable steps, and regulatory mechanisms. The Individual country level 
plans of Denmark, Germany, and Spain will be examined. This includes country level capacity 
targets, H2 value chains, select projects of interest, and their relation to the EU’s objectives. 
These countries are chosen due to their unique political, technological, or geographic qualities.  
To investigate hydrogen’s role in the EU energy landscape, the energy system model Balmorel 
will be utilized. Balmorel is an open-source energy system model that is used to optimize the 
simultaneous generation, transmission, and consumption of electricity and heat under the 
assumption of perfectly competitive markets. The model aims to maximize social welfare by 
finding the optimal way to satisfy energy demand while minimizing the cost of electricity and 
heat production, storage, transmission, and distribution. The model takes into account technical, 
physical, and regulatory constraints and is designed to support long-term energy planning and 
decision-making [8].For this thesis, Balmorel will be used to examine hydrogens trajectory and 
placement in the European energy mix by 2050. Various scenarios will be implemented that 
constrain or modify input data to simulate potential techno and socioeconomic factors that are 
possible between 2020-2050. These include price spikes to simulate volatility on the 
hydrocarbon markets, limiting cross-border energy flows to simulate protectionist policies, 
simulating the Esbjerg Declaration regarding offshore wind in the North Sea, and modifying 
interest rates to simulate macroeconomic differences between countries.  
Finally, a discussion comparing the results of the Balmorel model to the policy objectives of the 
EU will take place. The model’s electrolyser capacity, production output, and transmission 
geography will be compared to various other hydrogen studies that attempt to judge the 
trajectory of hydrogen in Europe.

Ultimately, this thesis seeks to implement scenarios on the EU’s energy system to determine the

Moreover, to tackle this main research question, sub-questions are presented to help guide the

2 Hydrogen and E-fuels 
Hydrogen is a chemical element that can be used as a fuel for transportation and power 
generation. When it is burned, hydrogen reacts with oxygen in the air to produce water, making 
it a clean and renewable energy source. Hydrogen can be produced from a variety of feedstocks, 
including natural gas, coal, and biomass, as well as water using electrolysis. It can also be stored 
and transported in a variety of ways, making it a flexible fuel option. Despite its potential 
benefits, the widespread use of hydrogen as a fuel is currently limited by cost and infrastructure 
challenges [9]. When combined with carbon, nitrogen, or other elements, hydrogen can be 
further synthesized into synthetic fuels, or “e-fuels” which may display more desirable physical 
or energetic qualities than H2 when it comes to certain practical applications.  
 
Today, a convergence of factors has appeared to make hydrogen a key actor in the energy 
transition away from fossil fuels: global climate change and CO2 emissions, the desire for energy 
independence from foreign oil imports, the need to store variable renewable electricity 
supplies, carbon neutral transport, and the need to decarbonize niche industrial processes such 
as cement and steel production. For these considerations, hydrogen is virtually an ideal energy 
carrier due to its abundance and equal availability to humanity. Even further, some see green 
hydrogen as a “democratizing force” allowing resource poor countries to break free from 
asymmetrical trade dynamics and energy weaponization [10]. This section focuses on the 
history, nomenclature, production, and applications of hydrogen and efuels.  
2.1 A Brief History of Hydrogen 
The interest in hydrogen technologies to reduce dependence on conventional hydrocarbons is 
certainly not new. The invention of hydrogen production dates to the late 18th century where 
the experiments of scientists Henry Cavendish and Antoine Laurent Lavoisier produced 
important discoveries concerning hydrogen, carbon dioxide, air, and water [11]. Practical 
applications of gaseous hydrogen were theorized in the 19th and 20th centuries, with uses 
ranging from internal combustion engines, military applications, and the powering of spacecraft 
via solar powered fuel cells. Part of the interest in hydrogen was prompted by a post-WWI 
interest in energy self-sufficiency and existential supply risk for energy intensive countries. 
==PAGE:9==
8 
 
 
The phrase “hydrogen economy” was first used by General Motors engineers in 1970 who 
theorized that hydrogen could be used as “the fuel for all types of transport” [12]. Interest in 
hydrogen was boosted during the 1973 oil crisis where high fuel prices caused policy makers 
and academics to theorize on alternative sources of energy including renewables and nuclear. 
The year 1973 also marked the establishment of the International Association for Hydrogen 
Energy [13]. Despite the heightened interest, when oil output increased and prices dropped in 
the 1980’s and 90’s, attention and research into hydrogen technologies waned. However, the 
debates around air pollution, greenhouse gas emissions, and ‘peak oil’ that gained exposure by 
governments and policy makers through the late 20th century have continued until today. 
Research and development of fuel cells and electrolyzer technologies have continued in the 
background despite diminished attention,  
 
Certain noteworthy developments of hydrogen have taken place in the 21st century include the 
first H2 locomotive demonstrated in Quebec in 2002, the first power to gas installation (2MW) 
in Germany in 2013, the commercial launching of the first hydrogen fuel cell vehicle (FCV) the 
Toyota Mirai in 2014, the deployment of 1,000 residential fuel cell combined heat and power 
(micro-CHP) units in Europe in 2017, and the début of the first H2 powered tugboat at the Port 
of Antwerp in 2019 [14], [15], [16], [17], [18]. All these projects symbolize milestone 
developments in rail, power, mobility, heating, and shipping sectors, respectively.  
 
Since the early 2000’s the amount of international patents field for fuel cell, electrolysis, and 
hydrogen production processes has experienced substantial growth, as shown in Figure 1. The 
recent trend of securing intellectual property within the hydrogen industry is a strong 
indication that interest and development in this field is rapidly increasing. This suggests that 
key players within the hydrogen space are taking proactive steps to protect their ideas and 
technologies, as they work to establish themselves as leaders in this growing market. This is a 
positive development for the hydrogen industry as a whole, as it implies that more investments 
are flowing into researching and developing novel hydrogen technologies.

Future developments of hydrogen technology focus on materials and processes that make 
electrolysis more efficient and cost-effective. In the ramp-up phase of green hydrogen, 
development cost and scale at maximum efficiency will be important to ensure competitiveness 
with competing technologies.

2.2 Hydrogen Demand  
According to the latest 2022 estimates by the Fuel Cells and Hydrogen Observatory (FCHO), the 
current European H2 consumption is an estimated 8.6 Mt (341 TWh), of which 50% is used in 
refining1 and 29% in ammonia production [20]. The remaining 20% of demand is consumed by 
methanol production, energy, chemical manufacturing, and transport. As of 2022, the transport 
sector only consumed a minuscule 0.02% of the European total, although this figure is expected 
to grow as H2 infrastructure and fuel cell technology expands. The figures below show current 
hydrogen demands by country and end-use, based on the latest FCHO data.

Germany alone represents 20% of the total demand and 19% of the production capacity. Four 
manufacturing intensive countries, Germany, Netherlands, Poland, and Spain are responsible for 
a combined 51% of hydrogen consumption and 48% of supply.  
For future hydrogen demand, several studies have estimated the sectoral demands in transport, 
industry, power, and buildings and project an end-use consumption of 65-93 million metric 
tonnes hydrogen (2,100 - 3,100 TWh) by 2050. These studies include the 2021 European 
Hydrogen Backbone Report [3], the EU Commission Impact Assessment MIX Scenario (55% 
GHG reduction with carbon pricing) [22], The EU Commission JRC LCEO Zero Carbon Scenario 
[23], and the FCH Roadmap Ambitious scenario [24].  
By sector, the categories typically include the following end-uses or applications: 
• 
Transport: fuel cell vehicles (FCVs), heavy transport, shipping, aviation e fuels,  
• 
Industry:  steel, ammonia, high value chemicals (HVC), high temperature industrial 
applications where electrification is not economical 
• 
Power: energy storage, combustion, gas-to-hydrogen power plant conversion 
• 
Buildings: Hydrogen boilers, micro fuel cell CHP’s, hybrid heat pumps  
The various studies all conclude that industrial and transport applications will compose the 
majority of demand, with buildings having the lowest of use. The potential size of the efuel and 
HVC market is a big uncertainty between the studies, with scenarios having widely different

2.3 Hydrogen Supply 
For the current hydrogen market there is usually parity between supply and demand within a 
country as most hydrogen is produced locally on site as captive production. For example, 
Germany accounts for 20% of demand and 19% of supply. While “merchant” hydrogen intended 
to be sold and traded on various chemical markets exists, this style of production only accounts 
for 11% of the EU’s supply in 2022 according to the FCHO.   
Of the 12 Mt of hydrogen produced in the EU, 85% is produced via SMR, 14% via ethylene, 
chlor-alkali, sodium chlorate, or coke oven methods, 0.4% via SMR with carbon capture, and 
0.1% via electrolysis. However, of the electrolyzer production of 15,279 tonnes a year, 85% of it 
is destined for the merchant market which is a big difference between SMR production where 
only 11% is merchant production. Merchant producers of hydrogen include companies such as 
Air Liquide, Air Products, and Linde, who share a 96% market share of merchant production 
[25].

Today, most of the hydrogen produced worldwide is grey, brown, or black meaning H2 is 
produced through hydrocarbon processes, such as natural gas reforming (SMR) or coal 
gasification. Grey hydrogen uses natural gas, while brown and black use brown coal (lignite) 
and black coal, respectively.  CO2 is emitted when producing hydrogen via these processes and 
it is therefore not considered a sustainable long-term option. For SMR, the reaction of natural 
gas and water produces the following reaction:  
CH4 + H2O => CO + 3 H2 
Next, in what is termed the "water-gas shift reaction," the carbon monoxide and steam are 
reacted using a metal catalyst to produce carbon dioxide and more hydrogen 
CO + H2O => CO2 + H2 
For these reactions to occur, high temperature steam in the range of 700 to 1,000°C are needed, 
dependent on the catalyst, and therefore a large amount of outside heat must be supplied [26]. 
These colors of H2 make 99.6% of the 94 million tonnes (Mt) of global production and account 
for an estimated 830 Mt of carbon dioxide per year, equivalent to 3% of global CO2 emissions 
[27].   
Blue hydrogen is a term used to describe hydrogen that is produced through the process of 
natural gas steam methane reforming (SMR) but in which the carbon dioxide is then captured, 
utilized or stored, or "sequestered," in a process known as carbon capture utilization and 
storage (CCUS). Simply put, blue hydrogen is simply grey (or fossil-fuel based) hydrogen plus 
carbon capture.  
Green hydrogen is produced through the electrolysis of water using renewable energy sources. 
In this process, electricity is used to split water molecules into hydrogen and oxygen atoms via 
an electrolyzer. The overall chemical equation and overall decomposition of water into oxygen 
and hydrogen is as follows:  
2H20 => 2H2 + O2  
The hydrogen atoms can then be collected and used as a fuel or as a chemical feedstock for a 
variety of applications. The oxygen atoms can either be vented or fully utilized in various 
industrial processes such as improving combustion efficiency, semiconductor manufacturing, 
wastewater treatment, or the production of medical gases [2]. Despite growing interest and the 
scaling up of electrolyser technologies, green hydrogen today remains 2-3 times more expensive 
than blue hydrogen, depending on the prices of electricity and natural gas feedstocks [28].  
Yellow hydrogen is H2 that is produced using mixed-origin grid energy. Depending on the 
source of generated electricity, this form can either be low-carbon or high-carbon. In some 
instances, yellow hydrogen can also be used by industry to refer to H2 produced using solar 
power and is therefore is simply a subcategory of green hydrogen.  
Pink hydrogen is produced using nuclear energy and is sometimes also referred to as red or 
purple hydrogen. Nuclear power plants could use off-peak electricity to manufacture pink 
hydrogen more efficiently and in greater volumes than renewable energy, then sell the supply to 
industrial customers for an additional revenue stream.  The high temperature waste heat given 
off by nuclear power plants could also be used to improve the electrolysis process efficiency 
[29].  
Lastly, turquoise hydrogen, which sits between green and blue, uses methane as a feedstock in a 
pyrolysis process and produces hydrogen and carbon. However, the carbon produced by the 
pyrolysis process is solid carbon which can be easier handled, stored, or reused over gases 
==PAGE:13==
12 
 
carbon dioxide. If the feedstock methane is biomethane, the process can be considered carbon 
negative. Many experts believe methane pyrolysis represents a relatively under-explored area 
in hydrogen space and could potentially become cost competitive as the process uses far less 
electricity than traditional electrolysis [30].  
As mentioned, production of hydrogen is almost exclusively produced by hydrocarbons. 
Although there is a tremendous amount of hype and potential regarding green hydrogen, it 
barely registers in the hydrogen being produced and supplied today.  
2.6 Electrolyser Technologies 
The two main drivers for green hydrogen to become cost competitive with grey hydrogen are 
the abundant availability of low-cost renewable electricity and the reduction of electrolyzer 
capital costs. Electrolysers can be categorized into three main types: Alkaline, Proton Exchange, 
and Solid Oxide electrolysis. Each technology varies by maturity, operating condition, efficiency, 
and cost.  
Alkaline electrolysis is a type of electrolysis that uses an aqueous solution of potassium 
hydroxide as the electrolyte. In this process, a direct current is passed through the electrolyte, 
causing the water molecules to break down into hydrogen and oxygen gas at the electrodes. The 
most common technology currently, alkaline electrolysis, is relatively simple and inexpensive, 
but it has a lower efficiency and requires more electrical energy compared to other types of 
electrolysis. However, the technology is mature and benefits from stability and a long lifetime 
[31].  
Proton (also referred to as polymer) exchange membrane (PEM) electrolysis is a type of 
electrolysis that uses a proton exchange membrane as the electrolyte. In this process, hydrogen 
ions are transported through the membrane, while oxygen ions are transported through the 
water. PEM electrolysis is typically more efficient and requires less electrical energy compared 
to alkaline electrolysis, but it is also more expensive to operate. 
Solid oxide electrolysis (SOEC) is a type of electrolysis that uses a solid oxide or ceramic 
material as the electrolyte. In this process, oxygen ions are transported through the electrolyte, 
while hydrogen ions are transported through the water.  Unlike the other electrolyzer types, 
SOEC requires very high temperatures of 700-1,000C which can be expensive or impractical to 
maintain if a heat source such as high-temperature steam is not available. However, SOEC 
benefits from very high efficiencies of 90+% with a high H2 purity which means less electricity 
is needed, reducing the main cost driver. While still a maturing technology, Topsoe from 
Denmark is leading next-generation SOEC development and currently has several large-scale 
500MW+ projects in the pipeline [32].

Regarding future development, a comprehensive report by IRENA outlines the main cost 
reduction strategies that can reduce the cost of electrolyser plants by up to 80% with the main 
drivers listed below [28]:  
1. Design and Construction: Increased module size and improved stack design with 
regards to efficiency and flexibility can reduce costs by 33% 
2. Economies of Scale: GW-scale automated manufacturing facilities with high production 
rates, standardized plant design and system components can reduce costs by 50%  
3. Material Procurement: Reduce or avoid the use of scarce materials such as platinum 
and iridium in electrolyser design 
4. Operational Flexibility: Modular plant designs with multiple stack units and power 
supply modes improve efficiency at low load conditions vis a vis the variability of 
renewable energy sources. Integrate energy storage to balance system loading.  
5. Applications: Design electrolyser plant with specific application or end-use in mind. 
Scale, power stability, logistics all need to be optimized for the specific type of plant.  
6. Learning Rates: Studies show that learning rates of 16-21% can be achievable and that 
electrolysers can share a similar curve to solar PV. Electrolysers also benefit from 
battery development as system components (anodes, cathodes, membranes, assembly 
technologies) are shared.

2.7 Power to X 
Power-to-X (PtX) is an umbrella term that refers to a set of technologies that involve converting 
electricity from renewable energy sources into other forms of energy such as gases, liquids, or 
heat (the ‘X’). These technologies can help store excess renewable energy that would otherwise 
go to waste via generation curtailment and can also provide a way to use renewable energy to 
create liquid or gas in applications where electrification is not a feasible or economic option.   
 
Figure 4: Conceptual Diagram of the Power to X concept [36] 
Power-to-Gas (PtG) is the most popular PtX concept as it can provide significant amounts of 
hydrogen or synthetic methane utilizing excess electrical power from renewables to produce 
gaseous fuels. Electrolysis is used to produce hydrogen, which can be used directly or converted 
further. The resulting gases can either be used as chemical feedstocks, in fuel cell vehicles, 
burned to produce heat, or converted back into electricity using gas turbines and gas engines. In 
addition, power-to-gas opens the door to long-term energy storage of clean electricity and plays 
a complementary role to wind and solar energy’s intermittency problem.  
Power-to-Liquids is a process that adds carbon or nitrogen elements to hydrogen to produce 
ammonia, methanol or kerosene, among others. If the added carbon is extracted via direct-air-
capture (DAC) or comes from biomass, the process is low-carbon or carbon neutral. These liquid 
fuels are easier to store and transport than gases and can be used as feedstocks in industry or as 
transport fuels.  
Other touted concepts include Power-to-Heat and Power-to-Mobility. Power to-Heat works via a 
heat pump or an electric boiler using resistance heating. Large-scale heat pumps in district 
heating (DH) systems with thermal energy storage (TES) are an especially attractive option for 
power-to-heat.  Power-To-Mobility refers to either direct electrification of electric vehicles 
(EVs) or producing H2 for use in fuel cell electric vehicles (FCVs). Extensions of this approach 
could include Vehicle-to-Grid (V2G) where EVs act as mobile batteries that can be used in 
demand response and to balance variations between production and consumption.  
Overall, PtX technologies have the potential to play a significant role in the transition to a low-
carbon economy, as they can enable the storage and use of excess renewable energy and help to 
decarbonize sectors of the economy that are currently reliant on fossil fuels. However, these 
==PAGE:16==
15 
 
technologies are still in the early stages of development and deployment, and there are a 
number of technical and economic challenges that need to be overcome in order to realize their 
full potential. 
2.8 Levelized Costs of Hydrogen

2.9 E-fuels 
Electrofuels, also known as power-to-gas/liquids/fuels, e-fuels, RFNBO’s, or synthetic fuels, are 
carbon-based fuels produced by mixing hydrogen and CO2 in a reactor with electricity as the 
primary source of energy [39]. E-ammonia uses nitrogen, typically sourced from air separation, 
as an input instead of carbon They have the potential to be used in all forms of transportation 
and may not require significant investments in new infrastructure (when used in conventional 
combustion engines). The resulting gaseous and liquid fuels have properties that make them 
ideal replacements for fossil fuels, such as high energy density, ability to be stored, transported 
and combusted [40] . While efuels have potential to reduce carbon emissions in the transport 
sector, they come with several downsides including low efficiencies when compared to direct 
electrification, high costs, requiring carbon sources from biomass or CCUS [40]. Furthermore, 
large scale implementation can potentially cause higher electricity prices as demand on RES 
sources is increased [41].

3 EU Hydrogen Strategy 
As the focus of this thesis is on the European Union, the current policies and announced RES and 
hydrogen targets will examined in this section. A history of hydrogen policy, the current 
strategy, and future developments will be presented.  
3.1 Policy History 
While early EU policy (2000s) focused primarily on biofuels and fuels of biological origin, 
debates around the sustainability and impact on land use and food prices have caused biofuels 
to fall out of favor and its use and production even capped in REDII, depending on its origin and 
land-use impact [46], [47].  More recently, focus has shifted towards fuels of non-biological 
origin (RFNBO’s) wherein hydrogen and its derivatives play a central to the EU’s 
decarbonization strategy. The EU's policy history on hydrogen can be broken down into several 
key milestones which are listed below.  
• 
In 2004, the EU established the Fuel Cells and Hydrogen Joint Undertaking (FCH JU), 
which is a public-private partnership that supports research and innovation in hydrogen 
and fuel cells [48]. The FCH JU has provided funding for a wide range of projects, 
including the development of hydrogen fuel cell vehicles, hydrogen storage systems, and 
hydrogen production technologies. 
 
==PAGE:19==
18 
 
• 
In 2007, the EU adopted the "Integrated Strategic Energy Technology Plan (SET-Plan)" 
which identified hydrogen and fuel cells as one of the key technologies for the EU's 
transition to a low-carbon economy [49]. 
 
• 
In 2011, the EU adopted the "Energy Roadmap 2050" which set out a vision for the 
development of a hydrogen economy in the EU and identified key actions needed to 
achieve this goal [50].  
 
• 
In 2018, the EU ‘s “Renewable Energy Directive” recast (RED II) package went into force 
with the aim of promoting the use of renewable energy sources in the European Union 
[51]. It includes the following provisions:  
o A minimum of 32% of renewable energy in the EU's final energy consumption by 
2030 
o A target for the transport sector to achieve at least 14% of renewable energy in 
transport by 2030 (biofuels, hydrogen, and other RFNBO’s)  
o Member States to establish a national long-term strategy for the deployment of 
renewable energy and a national indicative trajectory (NECP’s) [6].  
 
• 
In 2020, the European Commission launched its hydrogen strategy, which aims to 
establish hydrogen as a major source of clean energy in the EU by 2030 [2]. See section 
3.2 EU Strategy for the phased hydrogen targets and objectives.  
 
• 
In 2020, The European Clean Hydrogen Partnership, the successor to the FCH JU, was 
launched with further plans to support the implementation of the EC’s hydrogen 
strategy, strengthen the hydrogen value chain, and stimulate research and innovation on 
clean hydrogen production [52].  
 
• 
In 2021, the “Fit for 55” package with new proposals was presented with the aim of 
putting the EU on course for a 55% reduction in GHG emissions by 2030, relative to 
1990 levels [53]. It includes the following revisions or amendments:  
 
o Increases the EU’s target of 32% renewable energy to 40% by 2030. Specific 
targets are set for various industries (heating and cooling, industry, transport, 
buildings)  
o Use of RFNBOs of of 50% in industry and 2.6% in transport by 2030, totaling five 
million tonnes of green hydrogen per year for industry and five for transport 
[54].  
o REFuelEU initiative for sustainable aviation fuels with specific targets for 
synthetic aviation fuels (PtL based on green H2) for planes departing from EU 
airport [55].  
o FuelEU Maritime proposal which limits maximum GHG intensity of the fuels used 
by ships departing EU ports. It encourages adoption of PtL based fuels and other 
sustainable maritime fuels [56].  
o Energy Tax Directive Revision which gives preferential tax rates to green and 
low-carbon fuels to encourage adoption in the aviation and maritime sectors.  
o Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism (CBAM) applied to hydrogen imports to 
encourage domestic production and to discourage imports of carbon intensive 
hydrogen (grey) “carbon-leakage” from foreign suppliers [57]  
 
==PAGE:20==
19 
 
• 
In 2022, following the Russian invasion of Ukraine and the 2021- present global energy 
crisis, the EU presented the “REPowerEU” plan which proposed even more aggressive 
2030 targets than “Fit for 55”. The main provisions and objectives are presented in 3.3 
REPowerEU and Current Targets.  
In summary, the EU has been actively working on hydrogen policy since the early 2000’s and 
has implemented a number of initiatives and set targets to support the development and 
deployment of hydrogen as a clean energy source. The EU is also working on legislation and 
regulations to create a favorable environment for the development and deployment of hydrogen 
and is investing in research and innovation to support the development of new technologies and 
infrastructure.

3.4 Investments and Financing 
Massive investments will be needed in both renewable energy and green hydrogen 
infrastructure are needed to meet the ambitious EU targets. To meet the H2 production targets, 
the EU estimates that an additional 500 TWh of additional power generation in 2030 Is needed.

300 billion needed for additional renewable electricity production [1].  
For hydrogen specifically, the EU commission expects that the investment needed for key 
hydrogen infrastructure categories by 2030 is approximately €50-75 billion for electrolysers, 
€28-38 billion for EU-internal pipelines, and €6-11 billion for storage.  
The EU plans to increase hydrogen production by accelerating the implementation of state aid 
decisions on Important Projects of Common European Interest (IPCEIs) and by increasing

chain.

4 National Plans for the developing hydrogen value chain  
To study the interplay between EU policy goals and announced national hydrogen strategies, 
three case studies are examined here. The three countries assessed are Denmark, Germany, and 
Spain. Denmark is chosen because it is a country that is eagerly developing electrolyzers and 
P2X technologies despite its minimal hydrogen demand. Denmark has been a historical leader in 
renewable technology (wind) and seeks to advance its position in the new hydrogen economy 
[67]. Germany is chosen because it is the EU’s biggest economy, hosting some of the world’s 
largest corporations, and has a high industrial dependency on fossil fuels. It is energy hungry 
and resource dependent, with a large oil and gas deficit (production minus consumption) [68]. 
Nonetheless, It is also considered a global leader in hydrogen technology with deep pockets to 
aggressively build out H2 infrastructure [69]. Spain is chosen due to its favorable geographic 
conditions and its proximity to potential green hydrogen import corridors with Northern Africa.  
By studying the national plans of three EU member states, hydrogen’s role in future energy 
systems can be examined. Since much of the development of the green H2 economy is 
intertwined to the build-out of renewable energy capacity, they are inherently “technology 
partners”. However, in terms of access to financing and government support programs, it is 
possible that they could be rivals. The role of EU may also vary between countries with some 
choosing to adopt a national policymaking focus while some copying and learning from EU level 
vision. Hydrogens end-use could also vary between national plans with some focusing mainly on 
H2’s role in energy storage and grid stability, while some countries opting for a direct energy 
application as in a feedstock in transportation fuels.

Germany’s approach on hydrogen is guided by “The National Hydrogen Strategy” which was 
published in June 2020 [71]. It is ultimately focused on achieving its goals of climate neutrality

decision to phase out coal-fired power plants, Hydrogen will now play an essential role in 
reducing emissions in Germany’s large industrial sector and an important role in reducing 
transport and heating related GHG emissions.  Economic opportunity in growing a hydrogen 
market and becoming a global leader is also highlighted in the plan. The extensive plan includes 
38 concrete measures for achieving its vision within hydrogen production, transport, heat, R&D, 
EU-level regulation, markets, and international partnerships. The timeline to achieve the goals is 
aggressive, with Germany aiming for an early domestic market ramp-up as early as 2023 and a 
well-defined national and international market by 2030. The 12 main German goals and

5 Methodology and Model Description 
Now that an introduction to Hydrogen, the EU policy objectives, and cost drivers have been 
presented, Balmorel and the energy systems analysis framework will be introduced. The 
objective of this section is to explain the modeling objectives, outputs, and scenario 
formulations created using Balmorel and how they relate to the EU’s hydrogen roadmap. First, 
Balmorel will be introduced, followed by a description of the base model and the various 
scenarios that have been created with the motivation behind them.  
5.1 Balmorel Energy System Model 
Balmorel is a partial equilibrium energy system optimization model focusing on the 
generation, consumption, and transmission of electricity and heat. Released in 2001, Balmorel 
was first used to analyze electricity and CHP markets in the Baltic region and has expanded to 
include several additional countries, generation technologies, and add-on features, owing to its 
modular nature [82]. The model is flexible and can be used to interpret future development in 
energy systems, the effects of energy policies (carbon taxes, emissions), and investment 
opportunities. Written in GAMS (General Algebraic Modelling System), Balmorel solves the 
optimal way to satisfy exogenously defined energy demands while maximizing social welfare 
with respect to electricity generation and distribution while being subject to capacity, energy, 
operational, and technical, and other constraints [83], [84].  
Mathematically, the core model is linear partial equilibrium model. Partial equilibrium, 
used in this context, is a technical term for supply and demand analysis that only considers one 
market and ignores interactions across different markets or the greater economy [85]. The main 
equation, the objective function, maximizes social welfare (i.e. the sum of consumers surplus 
and producers surplus) subject to constraints. This corresponds to the minimization of the total 
system cost. Balancing equations ensure that demand equals the supply of heat, electricity, or 
hydrogen which are geographically and temporally defined. The algebraic formulation of these 
equations as well as the code structure and documentation can be found on the Balmorel 
homepage and GitHub  [86], [87].  
Certain parameters are defined exogenously (imposed on the model) such as fuel prices, 
demands, existing capacities while new generation investments, transmission, and 
decommissioning are found endogenously (determined by model) as part of the optimization 
solution. Energy balance constraints ensure that the amount of energy supplied is equal to the 
amount of energy demanded at every time segment and geographical location. The equilibrium 
condition determines the electricity prices for all locations and time periods, and the optimal 
solution is found along with the corresponding dual variables, also known as shadow prices. 
Shadow prices represent the change in the objective function due to a change in the constraints 
and can be used to determine the marginal value of a particular resource or constraint. 
On the supply side, various generation technologies exist which have their own specific 
efficiencies, fuel types, capital and operation costs, lifetimes, emissions outputs, ratios between 
heat and power for CHP, and production profiles with regards to RES (wind, solar, hydropower). 
Energy storages are implemented and consist of short-term and long-term storage options and 
are restricted by their storage dynamics (i.e batteries vs hydro reservoirs).  
Balmorel can be run in four different modes depending on the analysis type and desired 
model outcomes. The model types vary depending on optimization temporal horizon, whether 
endogenous investments are allowed, and whether multi-year optimization periods may be 
solved. The running mode Balbase 4 is the most advanced model among the Balbase models as 
it allows an optimization period greater than a year as it can employ a rolling horizon approach, 
==PAGE:33==
32 
 
which involves solving a series of partially overlapping multi-year models. The rolling horizon 
approach can be used to make investment decisions on generation by considering the potential 
future impacts of current decisions. It is often used in industries where long-term planning is 
required, such as analyzing supply chains and energy systems [88]. Balbase4 is the latest 
version and used in this thesis.  
5.2 Spatial and Temporal Resolution 
Balmorel uses three layers of spatial resolution to define geographical entities: Country, 
Region, and Area. Each country can be composed of one or many regions which can be 
composed of many areas. Countries are where general economic input such as fuel prices, 
discount rates, and exogeneous resource restrictions are typically defined. Regions are where 
the energy demands are specified, and the energy balance is maintained. Therefore, electricity 
prices are solved on a regional level. Transmission is also defined in the regional layer and 
region-to-region trade can take place (for example, from western Denmark to Northern 
Germany). Areas are used to represent individual geographic characteristics such as wind, solar, 
and hydro potential within a region. For example, the Western Denmark region (DK1) may be 
composed of areas that describe its onshore and offshore wind resources. Areas are also where 
the type and capacity of all generation and storage units are placed. For this thesis, results will 
typically be summarized on the country level. However, some regional-to-regional 
summarization will take place when discussing the flow and transmission of cross border 
electricity and hydrogen. The figure below is an example of Denmark’s regional and area 
makeup (note: areas and transmissions lines are illustrative only).   
 
Figure 17: Spatial Entities: country, region, area 
Balmorel utilizes year, season, and term to define temporal resolution. The model setup can 
include one or more years, each of which is divided into an equal number of seasons which can 
be further divided into an equal number of terms. For example, 1 year with 52 seasons and 168 
terms results in 8,736 time units that are representative of the number of hours in a year. 
Seasonality allows for the representation of renewable energy resources availability. Optimal 
heat and power dispatch are determined in terms and allow for supply-demand equilibrium and 
an hourly solution of electricity and hydrogen price. The more seasons and terms chosen in the 
model set-up the longer the computational time. For this thesis, the years 2020, 2025…,2050 are 
chosen with 8 seasons and 24 terms. This allows for a future-oriented look towards a 2050 
==PAGE:34==
33 
 
energy system without the extreme computational times of including all years, seasons, and 
terms while still being representative of varying resource seasonality and time-based demand 
fluctuation.  
5.3 Model Characteristics 
As Balmorel can be set-up and run in many different configurations depending on the desired 
results, this section describes the main configuration, data inputs, and parameters that were 
used in the base case model and the ensuing scenarios.  
5.3.1 Geographical Delimitation 
A total of nineteen countries are included in the model which are shown in Figure 18. Out of 
these nineteen countries, sixteen of them are EU member states. While non-EU, Norway, 
Switzerland, and the United Kingdom are included in the model while a handful of EU member 
states are not. The non-EU states amount to 81 million people, compared to the 65 million EU 
member states that are not included. The countries that are not included are shown in red and 
are primarily the Eastern European and Balkan countries. The total population of the countries 
included amount to 461 million people, which is 3% more than the estimated total EU 
population of 447 million people. It is assumed that the nineteen countries included in the 
model are a sufficient representation of the EU as a whole and no scaling or demand/population 
weighting will take place in this analysis. The inclusion of the UK is also helpful to provide an 
additional look at the North Sea area and potential offshore wind generation and transmission.  
 
Figure 18: Countries included in the Balmorel model 
Four countries (Denmark, Germany, Norway, and Sweden) are divided further into regions 
which allow for a deeper look at geographic effects within a country such as energy flows, 
regional demand centers, and intra-country transmission dynamics. This is primarily because 
Balmorel has been focused on Northern European and Scandinavian countries during its use. As 
electricity balance is maintained on a regional level, the marginal cost of supplying power is 
observed which is then interpreted as the specific power price in the model. For example, the 
population and demand heavy NO1region where Oslo is located has an average power price 
greater than the sparsely populated and hydropower rich NO4 region which has an abundance 
of cheap supply. Transmission lines can be created between these regions if Balmorel decides 
this is an investment worthy decision.

For offshore wind, countries production and additional generation capacity takes places in

in the onshore wind category, stemming from its protected landscapes and fierce public 
opposition to the turbines. To account for this, Balmorel places limits on the build-out of

The hydrogen demand for each modeled year is sourced from the European Hydrogen

hydrogen demand in 2050 equals 2,300 TWh of which 1,200 TWh is used in industry as 
feedstocks and energy. For the nineteen countries considered in the Balmorel model, the total 
2050 exogenous demand equals 1,080 TWh. Exogenous demand plays an important role in the 
hydrogen build out in the model and essentially frames the amount of electrolyser capacity that 
will be built. Additional discussion on hydrogen demands are given in the section 7.3

5.4.2 No new transmission scenario (NNT) 
For the NNT scenario, investments in endogenous electrical transmission are not allowed. Any

scenario is also ran to analyze how the cost reductions of renewables over time compete with 
conventional generation technologies.

the results from the scenarios on transmission restriction, a North Sea offshore wind hub, fuel 
price volatility, discount rate, and no carbon tax are given. The scenarios effects on generation, 
capacity, price, and transmission of both electricity and hydrogen are presented in detail and

6.1 BASE Scenario

6.1.1 Generation Technologies 
In terms of RES development, wind and solar energy see a massive expansion by 2050, with 
these two forms accounting for 98% of new endogenous generation capacity, and accounts for 
68% of overall cumulative European energy supply between 2020 and 2050. Solar beats out 
wind to become the number one generation technology, with 1,8761 GW of new capacity being 
added versus 511 GW of wind. For wind, onshore and offshore share an 83% to 17% split with 
the majority of new investments going towards onshore build-out. Offshore wind capacity is 
only added in the countries bordering the North and Baltic Seas. This dynamic will be 
investigated more in the discussion section.  
New capacity investments into coal and liquid hydrocarbon generation do not occur, with coal 
being completely removed from production by 2030. Natural gas sees a steep decline in 
investment with less than 1GW of generating capacity running by 2050. Already by 2030, 74% 
of conventional fossil fuel generation technologies have been decommissioned and replaced by 
a mix of RES technologies. This number reaches 99.9% by 2050. Similar declines are seen across 
the entire spectrum of carbon generating fuels such as lignite, fuel oil, shale, and other gases.  
The baseload technologies, hydro and nuclear, see no new endogenous capacity investments but 
remain important producers. Hydropower technologies experience no decommissioning, while 
nuclear sees a decline of 39 GW by 2050 stemming from the retirement of older plants in 
Belgium, Germany, and the UK. Woodchip and municipal waste technologies see minor 
endogenous investments of 8.1 and 4.4 GW respectively but remain insignificant compared to 
the bulk of RES investments. The figure below shows the ranking of various generation

compared to against each other to identify trends and similarities between the modeled 
scenarios from the results section, Next, the results from the BASE scenario will be compared

compared. Any major differences in end-results will be identified and discussed.

7.4 Transmission and Production Geography 
All ran scenarios in Balmorel point towards Southern Europe becoming the dominant green 
hydrogen producer and exporter. Even within the scenarios that attempted to shift the 
geographic center of production North (NSPH, DR), the attractiveness of cheap solar in the 
South overcame these modeled barriers. The largest endogenous H2 transmission capacities 
were created to funnel hydrogen north towards the demand centers in Northern Europe. By 
2050, up to 18 Mt of hydrogen (55% of total production) flows up through the Southwest 
corridor towards Germany, Belgium, and the Netherlands. While minor domestic production is 
seen in these countries, they lack self-sufficiency in both electricity and hydrogen.  
This transmission dynamic is also shown in other studies that identify the “Southwest”, or 
“Iberian Peninsula” corridor as the most promising to deliver the cheapest levelized cost of 
hydrogen, assuming a H2 transmission network is formed to deliver the hydrogen to the 
Northwest demand hubs.  
A study by DNV evaluated four potential hydrogen value chains to supply the large demand 
centers seen in Northwest Europe. The competing corridors included:  
1.
Solar PV in Southern Spain with a long-distance pipeline
2.
Icelandic geothermal sourced liquid hydrogen transported via ship
3.
Offshore wind in the North Sea with electrical transmission to onshore H2 production
4.
Nuclear Powered production (pink hydrogen) with short distance pipelines

the necessary infrastructure, the study concluded that the Spanish solar PV route is the optimal

strategy when considering costs. While each value chain has its own merits and peculiarities, 
the cheapness of solar power tips the study in Spain’s favor. While the pipeline infrastructure

the levelized cost of hydrogen in 2030 is as low as €1.9 /kg.  
The offshore wind option is the second cheapest in terms of cost and benefits from proximity to 
demand off-takers and the lack of public infrastructure. In this scenario, a single developer can 
control the entire value chain of electrical and hydrogen infrastructure and production can be 
executed in the shortest time frame. This may have its own benefits, as faster permitting and 
construction could result in immediate hydrogen delivery to ports, and industrial centers in the 
North Sea area instead of waiting for construction of an extensive H2 pipeline infrastructure to 
be built up via Spain, France that may be burdened with permitting delays. With the lack of a 
hydrogen backbone, the study concludes that offshore wind is the cheapest option, although it 
states that there will be significant overlap between the solar and wind strategies and there will 
likely be no ‘winner-takes-all’ scenario.  While geothermal or nuclear value chains are plausible, 
the study concludes they are less likely to become dominant strategies but may play niche roles 
in price arbitrage and leveling production imbalances.

8 Discussion 
The discussion will be centered around the four key messages. The first will include a discussion 
on the key EU and national level targets and their feasibility. Next, the Balmorel scenario results 
will be discussed in a higher-level context of what they mean for the development and 
placement of hydrogen infrastructure. Thirdly, key policy recommendations from the leading 
hydrogen authorities and their intended impact to overcome the barriers for large scale 
hydrogen adoption will be summarized. Lastly, the limitations of the energy system model and 
further areas of investigation will be discussed.  
8.1 REPowerEU Goals 
From the Balmorel results, it is shown that the 65GW RepowerEU target is feasible by 2030, 
with 72 GW being built in the BASE scenario. The total production in 2030 is ~1Mt short of the 
10 Mt target, however, this result is heavily influenced by the data assumptions, as 89% of the 
301 TWh of 2030 demand is defined exogenously. Other targets, related to renewable 
percentage as total share (45%) are met in the model as are the goals related to renewable 
capacity (1,236 GW), and solar capacity (600GW). However, the reach these targets accelerated 
deployment of renewables above today’s levels are needed as are massive levels of investments 
and planning. 
While these results for energy system transformation look good on paper, the practicality of 
implementing these changes present challenges. The main obstacle to the widespread adoption 
of renewable energy sources is not the technology itself, which is already available, but the 
bureaucratic process for obtaining approvals. It currently takes an average of five to six years to 
approve a wind farm in Europe, and two to three years for a solar power plant [110]. To address 
this issue, the REPowerEU is calling for changes to the Renewables Energy Directive (RED II) 
that would allow countries to define certain areas as "go-to" zones for renewable energy 
projects, where new projects would be permitted within one year. By streamlining the 
permitting process, the transition to renewable energy sources and hydrogen can be 
accelerated [111].

8.2 Key Scenario Takeaways 
For the scenario results, important findings are discussed below in the context of the greater 
themes surrounding energy, policy, and future developments.  
•
Hydrogen development in Denmark
Denmark’s low electrolyser capacity of 2.3 GW by 2050 in the BASE scenario is addressed
when massive offshore wind capacity is placed in the North Sea in the NSPH scenario.
Capacity then ramps up capacity to 14.3 by 2050 and Denmark becomes a net exporter. The
NSPH hub scenario aligns more with real-world factors as Denmark is a signatory of the
Esbjerg Declaration and has plans for up to 35GW of wind, much more than its domestic
electricity needs. Their national target of 4-6GW may even be exceeded, as 7GW of projects
by 2030 have been identified in the Danish hydrogen landscape as of 2022 [70].
Additionally, Denmark’s PtX strategy emphasizes playing an important role in the global
development of hydrogen and efuel value chain, drawing directly on its expertise with
offshore wind, energy systems, and manufacturers of synthesis plants. Furthermore, the
Danish strategy places emphasis on that PtX can become a new export market for Denmark,
contributing to the overall economy and job creation. This strategic long-term thinking on a
national level is not captured in Balmorel.
•
Spain to France hydrogen corridor
The second largest transmission link was identified between Spain and France, amounting 
to 5.5Mt of flow in 2050 in the BASE model. This corridor will become critical for funneling 
cheap solar based hydrogen northwards. The key energy system studies point towards this 
link, including the EHB and DNV reports (described in  
7.4 Transmission and Production Geography). This transmission is critical to fulfilling the 
EU’s long term H2 objectives, and one of the first that will likely be constructed, according to 
the EBH. This dynamic is the one that the announced H2Med Subsea pipeline project plans 
to address, with Spanish Enagás, Portuguese REN, and French GRTgaz TSO’s signing a 
memorandum to construct a hydrogen-only link between Barcelona and Marseille, an 
estimated 450km distance [112]. This project will be submitted as an IPCEI and could 
potentially transport up to 2 million tonnes hydrogen per year (10% of demand), a 
significant part of the REPowerEU target. As of January 22 2023, Germany has also signed 
onto this project, indicating an extension of the pipeline from France into Germany will 
occur [113].  
•
Investment environment and asset based risk
The BASE socioeconomic discount rate of 4% applied to all countries and to all generation
technologies is an oversimplification of real-world conditions and skew the results in the
favor of Southern European countries, as seen in the differences between the BASE and DR
scenario. The DR scenario showed that when equity risk is applied, the investments into
new capacity move North by ~14%.
Furthermore, different assets will come with different degrees of risks. A report by Grant 
Thorton compares renewable discount rates in France and showed that solar PV has a 5% 
discount rate compared to 5.5% for onshore wind and 8% for offshore wind [114]. These 
discrepancies across technologies are often reflective of the technological readiness level 
and perceived risk in the investment. It can be expected that novel and pilot projects within 
hydrogen space will see high applied rates due to technology maturity and the lack of a well-
defined off-taker market for selling the end-product. For LCOH calculations, 
==PAGE:71==
70 
technoeconomic discount rates of 8% used by IRENA and the IEA [28]. For more novel efuel 
projects, these rates can be even higher due to the lack of precedent. To combat risk, 
securing guarantees in the form of production credits, contracts for difference, or PPA’s 
could lower the investment risk of new projects. However, that dynamic was not explored in 
this thesis.  
•
The blue hydrogen non-factor
SMR based hydrogen with CCS did not result in any meaningful percentage of the energy
mix in any of the scenarios in 2030 or 2050.  The only scenario where endogenous capacity
was added was the no carbon tax scenario. Studies show that blue hydrogen may not be as
clean as touted, especially when factoring in methane leakage [90] . The issue of dependency
and security of supply is also not addressed with blue hydrogen, relying on natural gas
feedstocks, much of which will require imports. The main proponents of the blue hydrogen
pathway are oil and has industries, whose true motives for locking in this technology
pathway should be put under intense scrutiny. Some industry experts state that “blue
hydrogen isn’t clean and makes no sense economically — unless you are in the business of
selling methane” [115].
•
Energy nationalism
As seen in the NNT scenario, one country that benefited most with restricted transmission
with respect to energy prices was the renewable resource rich Norway. With new
transmission investments allowed, Norway’s average electricity prices rose ~30% versus
the restricted scenario. For Norwegian residential and commercial customers, this would
mean increased utility bills and decreased industrial competitiveness. Unsurprisingly, with
the energy crisis unfolding in Europe in 2021-present, there has been fierce backlash and
bitter debate against energy liberalization in Norway with their entrance in the EU energy
market in 2018 [116].
Norway is not part of the EU with the main reason being the feared loss of sovereignty over 
its natural resources (fish, oil, and gas) with little domestic economic benefit. 
Unsurprisingly, a large argument for BREXIT came with regards to the UK retaining energy 
sovereignty. Switzerland, also a non-member, prioritizes energy independence over 
electricity agreements with the EU [117]. On the opposite end, Germany, which is not self-
sufficient in any of the scenarios ran, inherently benefits from the wide and diverse build 
out of transmission and interconnections to neighboring countries as shown in the model 
runs. It a has a vested interest in promoting energy liberalization and pan-Europeanism. 
A study by Hansen [118], concluded that renewable energy exports can reinforce 
asymmetric dependencies, weaken energy sovereignty, and that energy nationalism might 
constitute a future constraint against the green energy transition. It would not be 
implausible to see similar arguments against liberalization take place in Spain as its status 
as an EU energy supplier ramps up.

8.4 Model Limitations 
Energy systems models are a valuable tool for comprehending the intricacies of energy systems 
and making informed policy decisions about energy and climate change. They can determine the 
most economical and efficient ways to meet energy demands and analyze the potential effects of 
various technology and policy drivers. However, there are limitations to their usefulness. 
Aspects of the future are uncertain, and stochasticity is not always accounted for in the models. 
Incorporating uncertainty into exogenous demands, fuel prices, and key base assumptions 
would enhance the confidence of achieving final targets. Simplifications and assumptions in 
modeling real-life uncertainty with regard to future prices, technology developments, and policy 
changes, and flexibility can limit the accuracy of the model. 
While not exhaustive, certain model limitations and further areas of investigation include: 
Transmission: Most countries do not include regional resolution and have simplifications 
regarding the placement of transmission lines (center of country).  
Trade: Imports and exports to countries outside of EU not modelled. International price 
competition could impact domestic production. Impacts of a CBAM could be analyzed with 
regards to production offshoring and carbon leakage. 
Demand: Only one dataset of exogenous demand considered related to industry and high value 
chemicals and sourced from EHB. Integrate the model with more studies on demand and 
introduce uncertainty metrics. Optimistic demand situations such as McKinsey’s “H2 
breakthrough” scenario could be run as separate scenarios.  
Balmorel Opti-Flow: Enable OptiFlow framework to include biofuel and certain efuel 
technologies to the available technologies. Allows a better look into multiple inputs and outputs 
of electrolysis/efuel technologies and any interacting flows [121].  This could allow a better 
representation of hitting the EU’s sectoral RFBNO targets.  
Carbon supply:  Biomass carbon stock or point source availability not modeled. See research of 
Lester M, Bramstoft R. et. Al. for analysis on carbon stock impacts on efuels using OptiFlow 
==PAGE:74==
73 
Balmorel add-on [41]. This modeling framework for biomass and point stocks carbon in 
Denmark, could be applied across the whole EU energy system.  
Foresight: Allow perfect foresight for more than one modeled year by enabling YMODELDELTA. 
Although computationally intensive, enabling a rolling horizon will optimize across multiple 
years. Important when considering long-term targets with respect to decarbonization to avoid 
stranded assets and illogical investment decisions.  
9 Conclusion 
Currently there is significant momentum towards promoting green hydrogen due to its 
potential to decarbonize existing facets of the energy system while improving European energy 
independence. This study examines the European Union's policy goals for hydrogen energy and 
the factors that will affect their ability to be achieved. Using Balmorel, this thesis investigated 
potential scenarios for the EU's future energy system to identify key parameters that impact 
electrolyzer capacity and production goals, as well as determining where hydrogen 
infrastructure is likely to be developed.  
The research questions that were earlier introduced are answered below. 
What is the current EU strategy regarding hydrogen and how has it evolved over time? 
While mentioned in earlier climate and environment legislation, the first concrete hydrogen and 
electrolyzer targets in EU legislation appeared in the European Union's " A hydrogen strategy 
for a climate-neutral Europe" plan which specified 40GW of electrolysers and 10Mt of 
production by 2030 in a phased approach. These targets were later increased to 65GW and 
20Mt of hydrogen in the 2022 REPowerEU plan as a response to the Russian invasion of 
Ukraine. On volume, the current objective is to reach 10Mt of domestic production bolstered by 
10Mt of imports. Potential import corridors in North Africa and Eastern Europe are identified, 
however much work has to be done to secure partnerships and clarify the regulations and 
market structure with respect to imports. Currently there are an estimated 39 GW of capacity in 
the pipeline, with 200+ projects in various stages of deployment including crucial pipeline 
infrastructure plans between European member states. There are tangible real-world efforts 
being taken but accelerating project financing and permitting must be done to ensure that the 
targets are met by 2030.  
What is the potential cost trajectory of green hydrogen from now until 2050 and what are 
the influencing factors? 
Based on literature review, delivered green hydrogen in Europe today costs an estimated 
according to the latest FCHO analysis. Comprehensive hydrogen studies state price targets of 
€1.10-3.80/kg by 2030 and <€1.00 – 2.70 /kg by 2050 are attainable depending on a variety of 
circumstances. Influencing factors include the price of electricity, variable renewable energy 
production profiles, transmission method and distance, capital costs of electrolyser stacks, 
degradation, and overall process efficiency.  
Are EU and member state level capacity and production targets aligned with the Balmorel 
modeling results, and if not, what are the possible reasons?  
On an EU level, the electrolyser capacity target of 65GW by 2030 is met in the BASE scenario. By 
2050, electrolyser capacity hits 471 GW. The 2030 domestic production target of 10Mt is not 
met, but Balmorel results come close at 9Mt. The 1Mt gap can be explained by an overall low 
electrolyser utilization rate and missing exogenous demand and an which can be increased with 
==PAGE:75==
74 
a larger off-taker market and improving the overall capacity factor. 10Mt of imports are not 
modelled, however, the IEA identifies only 0.9Mt of secured imports leaving a large gap to be 
addressed. Hydrogen, as percent of the final energy mix reaches 13.5% by 2050, a result which 
aligns with many of the established roadmap studies examining the EU’s hydrogen potential.  
Member state goals were examined in Denmark, Germany, and Spain with the results showing 
that Germany and Spain did reach their respective 5 and 4 GW targets by 2030, while Denmark 
did not reach this target. Adding an abundance of wind capacity in Danish offshore waters 
greatly improves Denmark’s production capacity and causes the model to align more with the 
stated goals of 4-6GW by 2030.  
How do modifying the technoeconomic Balmorel data parameters impact the deployment 
of hydrogen infrastructure, its location, and its cost? 
Electrolyser capacity levels and productions amounts are similar in all modeled scenarios. The 
most interesting results pertain to cost, production geographies and transmission across 
country borders. By modifying transmission restrictions, national level capacities increase for 
import dependent countries and more generation capacity moves closer to the demand centers 
in Northwest Europe. Average prices also increase. The Esbjerg declaration was modeled by 
placing 150GW of offshore wind in the North Sea. This scenario resulted in additional 
electrolyser capacity in the signatory countries with Denmark becoming a net exporter. 
Modifying country level discount rates to become more aligned with equity risk rates resulted in 
additional capacity and production output moving North to countries with more favorable 
investment environment, at the expense of export volumes origination in countries such as Italy 
and Spain. Modifying fossil fuel parameters – fuel price, and carbon tax unsurprisingly resulted 
in varying rates of decarbonization and technology decommissioning, These effects were more 
pronounced on the heating sector. These scenarios also impacted the deployment of SMR based 
hydrogen in 2020-2030, with lower natural gas costs resulting in additional build out at the 
expense of delayed eletrolyser development. Similarly, these scenarios did not reach the 65GW 
REPowerEU target. Final costs in 2050 for green hydrogen in each scenario varied between 
€1.10-1.70/kg which align with the price targets set in IEA and IRENA analyses and making it 
highly competitive with conventional methods used today.  
Final Remarks 
Governments have a unique chance to guide the development of hydrogen, avoid the mistakes 
and inefficiencies of current systems, and shape geopolitical outcomes. It is clear that a greater 
use of hydrogen technology will disrupt existing economic and political relationships. If handled 
carefully, this technology also has the potential to enhance national and regional sovereignty, 
resilience, and cooperation. Most importantly, international collaboration will be crucial to 
effectively manage unknowns, reduce risks, and overcome challenges in the future. Support for 
the creation of low-carbon hydrogen pathways, similar to those for other technologies aimed at 
combating climate change, should be guided by a clear, global vision. Pan-European or national 
strategies alone may be ineffective without considering the larger, international context. This is 
because they can exacerbate existing disparities between countries, making it even more 
difficult to achieve the ambitious goals necessary to limit climate change.
