Abstract
Since the construction industry strives to reduce its carbon footprint, the evolution of wooden
structures has drastically increased in recent years. Although wood has good construction
related qualities, it is often challenging to meet the requirements for the serviceability limit state
(SLS). One solution to this has been timber concrete composite structures (TCC), which are a
promising product for use as slab elements. However, the existing solutions rely on in­situ cast
concrete to achieve ductile failures, which removes the potential of reusing the elements after
the lifespan of the building.
This report presents an experimental investigation of TCC structures where the concrete ele­
ments are prefabricated to facilitate reuse and reduce cement content by using lower­quality
concrete. The experiments were done on 30 test specimens with a notched connection, along
with other varying parameters to explore concrete failure modes and the influence of mechan­
ical fasteners. Push­off tests were performed in a custom­built setup to provide pure shear in
the interface between the two composite materials. Results were retrieved by multiple sensors
that measured relative displacements in critical areas of the specimens. Furthermore, digital
image correlation (DIC) was used to trace strain development for crack propagation and global
deformation.
Results in this study are concentrated on various positions of the fasteners and how it influences
the behaviour of the notched connection. The findings were different failure mechanisms caused
by crack propagation depending on where the fasteners were positioned. Fasteners in front of
the notch resulted in ductile timber compression failures because the screws almost instantly
carried tensile stresses that occurred in the loaded notch area. Fasteners in the middle of the
notch had high stiffness values and a ductile post­peak behaviour, although a brittle concrete
shear failure at peak caused a significant capacity drop. Fasteners in the back of and behind
the notch had reduced stiffness and inconsistent failure behaviour caused by diverse crack
propagation and design flaws in the notch area.
Additionally, other parameters were tested to explore the effect of the fasteners. In­situ cast
concrete, frequently used in similar experiments, had a ductile timber compression failure but
is unreliable when retrieving stiffness data. Inclined screws resulted in a drastically reduced
stiffness and a brittle concrete shear failure. However, the capacity was improved together with
the post­peak behaviour. Having no fasteners resulted in a very brittle concrete tensile failure
that separated the specimens, thus demonstrating the importance of fasteners to prefabricate
composite elements completely.

Introduction
Current climate politics aims to reduce global warming, and every country has pledged to cut
greenhouse gas emissions through the Paris agreement. The construction industry, which rep­
resents 23% of worldwide CO2 emissions and is highly characterised by cement production,
bears significant responsibility for lowering global warming (Huang et al., 2018). In order to
fight climate change, the construction business has concentrated on developing new construc­
tion materials and untraditional solutions.
1.1
Background
The emphasis on adapting environmentally friendly alternatives has led to multiple solutions,
such as low­carbon concrete, which is mixed with other materials to contain less cement. Fur­
thermore, the evolution of glulam timber has resulted in many carbon­neutral small to medium­
sized projects. The reason why wood as a construction material is lucrative from a climate
perspective is the ability of trees to absorb and store CO2 during the life cycle (Nordby et al.,
2015) (Strekerud, 2017). Timber is also light and solid, making it an excellent building material.
However, reinforced concrete is favoured by engineers in larger projects because of its greater
compression strength and life expectancy.
Timber­concrete composite (TCC) structure is a system that has been developed over the last
50 years. It is a construction technique where the tensile strength properties of timber are used
as a beam or deck with a concrete topping resisting the compression forces. This technology
has mainly been used to upgrade existing wooden floors but is now adapted to the construction
of multi­story buildings and short­span bridges (Cecotti, 1995). The combination of the two
materials has many excellent properties compared to using them separately. When used as a
slab element, there are multiple advantages compared to cross­laminated timber (CLT) decks,
such as increased stiffness, improved acoustic separation, and reduced energy consumption
needed to heat and cool the building (Yeoh et al., 2011). TCC is more effective to tensile stresses
and has a lower self­weight than a standard concrete deck, allowing for rapid erection with
prefabrication, reduced seismic action, and reduced CO2 emissions.
The escalation of prefabricated timber and concrete has been essential to cutting construction
costs. It is conceivable to prefabricate products of practically any size, but in practice, the avail­
ability of production equipment and shipping constraints generally limit the size of the objects.
The manufacturer can customise the prefabricated parts for each project, and accurate design

and ready­to­assemble elements make the construction process short. Another advantage of
prefabricated elements is that it is more suited for dismantling and recycling. Nowadays, TCC
is partly prefabricated, where the concrete slab is cast directly onto the timber. Creating a com­
posite deck made of prefabricated concrete and wood could be a massive advantage for the
industry by ensuring a longer life for the elements and reducing the carbon footprint.
The connection system between the concrete and timber elements must be strong and stiff
enough to transfer the design shear force to provide an effective composite action. Literature
has found that notched connections, i.e., where grooves are cut in the timber by computer
numerical control (CNC) machines and filled with concrete, are the most effective connection
system (Figure 1.1) (Yeoh et al., 2011) (Boccadoro et al., 2017). The notches act as shear
keys, minimising the relative displacement between the timber and concrete, and have the
best stiffness and composite action properties. However, the notches reduce the timber cross­
section, leading to critical stress and brittle failure modes (Boccadoro et al., 2017). To avoid
an undesired brittle failure, mechanical fasteners, such as glue, nails, or screws, are commonly
used in the interlayer between timber and concrete. These fasteners prevent uplift between the
two materials and provide effective composite action if used correctly.
Figure 1.1: Illustration of a notched shear connection
The ductility of the notched connection, on the other hand, is vaguer and highly dependent on
the failure mode of the connection. The five failure modes depicted in Figure 1.2 have been
identified in the literature by numerous experiments and different connection systems (Yeoh
et al., 2011). Figure 1.2(a) illustrates timber compression failure (TCF), a ductile failure mode
due to buckling of the timber fibres before ultimate failure. Figure 1.2(b) shows timber shear
failure (TSF), the most brittle failure mode. The three concrete failure modes are more uncertain
and not yet understood in their entirety, making it impossible to predict the connection failure
mode with sufficient accuracy and thereby ensuring a ductile failure.

1.2
Purpose
The study aims to examine which variables contribute to a safer and more predictable failure
for TCC elements with a notched connection. It is necessary to achieve high rigidity while
also preventing the materials from separating to take advantage of the composite structure.
It is also critical to avoid brittle failures, which requires an understanding of how the elements
behave under applied load. Several tests will be conducted to clarify the influence of mechanical
fasteners, with an explicit focus on the position of the fasteners. The study will highlight the
downsides and advantages of the various fastener placements, and by comparing the results
to other typical solutions, a better understanding of the connection is provided. Furthermore,
this report seeks to explain different experimental factors that contributes to solid documentation
and reliable results. Both the setup and the result shall be comparable to existing and future
projects of its kind.

2
Literature study
Multiple TCC push­off tests have been conducted in recent years, focusing on optimising a
notched connection. A literature study of similar tests is presented in this chapter to understand
how the tests should be completed and to comprehend the results. There are varied methods
to retrieve the results, commonly by symmetric or asymmetric test setups, where the specimens
are placed vertically or horizontally. Symmetric tests are conducted with two concrete elements
and two notches subjected to compression, while asymmetric tests only have one notch. Re­
sults from different test setups are not directly comparable, although it explains how the different
connection systems behave.
The articles focus on the different failure modes in TCC push­out tests, most of which are ei­
ther timber compression or timber shear failures. Although most experiments result in timber
failure modes, numerous factors contribute, as they are tested differently. Through this litera­
ture study, explanations of why the different failure modes occur are investigated to understand
the experiments better. Other strategies for measurement tools are also examined to find the
optimal types and placements for these tools.
2.1
Previous notch­connection experiments
A study of previously conducted push­off tests with similar conditions is essential to understand
which parameters contribute to the different failure modes so that the test setup is built appro­
priately. Thai et al. (2020) conducted a test series of 60 specimens where the influence of heel
length, notch depth, screw length, and concrete thickness were tested. The configuration of the
tests was an asymmetrical specimen system, where the samples had two screws inserted in the
middle of the notch. The specimens were placed vertically and the load was transferred to the
timber by an actuator with fully supported concrete on the opposite side. Two analogue laser
sensors (ALSs) were installed 150 cm to the edge of the loaded notch edge to measure the
relative displacements between the two layers. The total time for testing was about 15 minutes,
but the condition for ending varied by whether the charge had a significant drop or the relative
displacement reached 15 mm. All tests were performed with high­strength concrete of quality C
35, while the timber was cross­laminated (CLT) with 23.6 MPa. The notch had 90° angled edges
and two screws with a diameter of 8 mm. Results from the relevant test series, with a constant
notch depth of 20 mm, are presented in Table 2.1 with their additional properties, such as heel
length (HL), screw length (SL), and the concrete thickness (CT ). Kser and Fmax represents the

The tests resulted in nearly 80% of the failure modes being ductile timber failures due to timber
compression in the loaded edge of the notch. Since the timber heel lengths were considerably
long and made of relatively weak timber, this type of failure should be predicted. The other
frequent failure mode was a brittle timber shear failure that occurred when the lamellas of the
heel got sheared off at a displacement between 2 and 3 mm. This was mainly because the
test setup had a metal plate that held the sheared­off lamellas in place and a sleeve around the
screw that prevented the contribution of the screws to the shear loading. Specimens with 350
mm and 300 mm heel lengths showed less consistency than those with 400 mm heel lengths.
The concrete thickness and screw length had little impact on the overall results.

The results showed a ductile TCF due to buckling of timber fibres in front of the notch on all
specimens, which, similarly to Thai, was expected because of the lower timber quality. Having
screws placed in the middle of the notch did not influence the results in terms of failure mode,

2.2
Suggested design from literature
Some parameters should be accounted for when designing TCC elements with notched con­
nections based on the two articles. Having the timber being the weaker element facilitates a
timber failure mode, which is desired as long as it is ductile. A notch depth of 20 mm with 15
times greater heel length results in a ductile failure mode, TCF. Shorter heel length gives in­
consistency and a higher risk of brittle failure, TSF. Including fasteners in the notch is highly
recommended, although the composite action seems to be fulfilled without their presence. The
fasteners are a measure to prevent uplift between the elements, which is necessary to provide
good sound insulation. Screws with a diameter of 8 mm are sufficient for the commonly used
geometry. Slip between the elements should be measured in the middle of the notch and not
on the loaded edge, as cracking in the concrete can impact the results. Gap measurements
were done on the loaded edge of the notch, although adding a sensor on the opposite side will
give measurements for rotation data.
Furthermore, by analysing the different test setups, it was found that some experiments have
measures that hinder typical failures caused by bending of the concrete element. Because of
the eccentricity between the applied load and the bearing edge of the notch, the concrete will
typically lift from the timber element. Zhang et al. (2020) and Ballerini et al. (2002) experienced
this phenomenon and considered it faulty or unrealistic as TCC structures typically will be sub­
jected to bending. Therefore, steel plates were used in both experiments to prevent the uplift
that caused tensile failures in the concrete. This study will not conduct such measures to find
the causes for tensile failure modes. To properly investigate the behaviour of a TCC structure,
a bending test should be conducted afterwards with the knowledge from the push­off tests.

Description of the push­off tests
This section explains the experimental study of a horizontal asymmetrical push­off test on 30
TCC specimens with different parameters conducted at the Technical University of Denmark
(DTU). The components were prefabricated at factories under controlled circumstances before
being shipped to DTU. Contiga A/S cast the concrete elements, and Södra Wood A/S produced
the timber with specific dimensions to fit together as a composite element.

made in vertical lamellas of 33.3 mm width. The concrete quality was C 20/25 reinforced with

and the concrete elements. CNC­cut grooves in the timber had a consistent depth of 21 mm
for all 30 test specimens, with a 10° edge inclination. The notch length was primarily 150 mm
wide, except for six specimens that varied in notch length.

notch (­50 mm) or behind the notch (­100 mm). The two screws were set 50 mm from each edge

degrees of prestressing. These screws were specially designed to apply axial force without
torsion to prestress without risking the screws breaking. Furthermore, tests were conducted
with inclined screws at a 45° angle, without fasteners, and with concrete cast directly onto the
timber elements (in­situ). Table 3.1 summarises the geometry and properties of the different

3.2
Test setup
The test setup was built to be easily replicated in future studies and to obtain results compa­
rable to the literature. The specimens are laid down, where the timber element is supported
horizontally, known as the passive side. Two vertical rollers underneath the timber element
reduce friction and allow for the correct height. A hydraulic actuator with a cylindrical load cell
with a capacity of 222 kN pushes the concrete from the active side. The actuator and timber
support are placed in the centroid axis of the TCC element and are pinned to sustain the load
without taking any moment. A replaceable masonite plate, later aluminum, was used at the
active support to protect the actuator during the tests. All features in the setup were designed
to provide pure shear in the interface of the two composite materials. An illustration of the test
setup is seen in Figure 3.3.
Figure 3.3: Illustration of the test setup for the main push­off tests
The actuator measured the load throughout the test using MTS test software with an accuracy
of 1%. Additionally, LVDT sensors and digital image correlation (DIC) measure displacements
globally and at the notch. The data was used to examine the stiffness and ductility of the con­
nection with force­displacement curves.
From Chapter 2, the LVDT sensors must be placed tactically to give reliable results and be
comparable to the literature. In this experiment, five LVDTs measure horizontal and vertical
displacements in critical regions of the specimens to ensure the collected data are comparable.
The positioning can be seen in Figure 3.4, where an essential one is placed in the centre of the
notch, measuring horizontal displacement used to make the force­displacement curves later
presented in this report. The remaining two horizontal sensors are placed on the active and
passive sides of the notch and are used to compare the slip and stiffness in different parts of

between the marks was imported. These distances create the foundation for all data analysed
later in the software, and accuracy is essential to obtain reliable results. Eventually, the software

3.3
Test procedure
The concrete specimens were delivered from Contiga A/S in four batches with 12 cylinders for
material testing for each batch. As suggested in ISO 554, the timber elements were put directly
into a controlled climate chamber with a constant temperature of 20° and 65% relative humidity.
The timber elements were stored in the compartment until the push­off tests were performed,
and the mass was measured daily by weighing the specimens to track their moisture content.
After a few days, the moisture content was stable (less than 0.1% change over 6 hours), but
the elements were kept in storage due to other factors such as preparing test equipment and
performing the associated tests. The concrete was stored in similar conditions.
While the timber was stabilising, tests were carried out on the rebar and screws used in the
push­off tests. Rebar testing was performed according to DS/EN ISO 15630­1, and the mea­
surements according to DS/EN ISO 6892­1. LVDT sensors, load cells, and camera equipment
also needed calibration before testing began to give precise and trustworthy results. Before
each batch could be tested, nine concrete cylinders from the specific batch were tested for

after the main test so that the concrete quality for each specimen could be determined by in­
terpolation. The first and last specimens tested in each batch and those resulting in timber

4.1

This section concentrates on the early stages of the push­off tests, which last until the eventual
failure. This phase must be thoroughly examined in order to understand why the different fail­

5.1
M­150­(0)+I ­ In­situ series
The initial results for the specimens with the concrete cast directly onto the timber elements
depended on the interface’s cohesion. Cohesion is formed when wet cement penetrates the
wood fibres and forms a bond with the concrete element. An observation made during the tests
was that the cohesion broke for M­150­(0)+I­2 and M­150­(0)+I­3 at respectively 83 kN and
57 kN, creating a loud sound. M­150­(0)+I­1 did not generate any sound, but a sudden slip is
visible at 51 kN, implying that the cohesion broke. For specimen M­150­(0)+I­3, the cohesion
broke at precisely 40% of Fest with no deformation registered at the notch prior. M­150­(0)+I­1
and M­150­(0)­2, on the other hand, lost the cohesion at a notch slip of 0.12 mm, although the
loading was at different stages. A DIC analysis of specimen M­150­(0)+I­1, where no physical
sound was registered, was carried out to understand why the cohesion broke under different
conditions.

fasteners carry the tensile stresses once the notch crack opens and stops the propagation. A
similar model is made for a specimen without fasteners to compare the tensile stresses.
Figure 5.16 represents the strut & tie model for M­150­(0)+0nf­3 and is created on the assump­
tion that the tensile forces flow through the rod between node 2 and node 4, which is located
halfway down the notch edge. To simplify the calculation model, it was also assumed that node
1 shared the exact location as in M­150­(0)­2. From DIC, a tensile notch crack developed from
the loaded edge of the notch, which can verify the placement of the vertical tensile rod. As
a consequence of not having fasteners, the tensile stresses in the concrete tore the element
apart.

Discussion
The presented analysis revealed that numerous factors influence the behaviour of a notched
connection in composite structures. This section discusses each failure mode, design parame­
ters in the push­off tests, and prefabricated TCC structures in general.
6.1
The failure modes
Even though the geometry of the specimens and notch was constant, the varied parameters
contributed to four distinct failure modes. In this section, the failures are merged to discuss
similarities and whether the influence of fastener positions triggered them. In Figure 6.1, the
typical force­displacement behaviour is shown for the four different failure types.
Figure 6.1: Typical behaviour of the experienced failure modes
6.1.1
Concrete shear failures
The shear failure of the concrete notch was the most common failure for the discussed tests, as
well as in the prestressing and notch length series. It was the second most brittle failure due to
a sudden longitudinal crack that, in most cases, sheared the concrete notch off completely. This
led to a significant capacity drop seen in Figure 6.2 before processing to plastic compression
deformation of timber.
Experimental Study of Notched Connections for Prefabricated TCC Slabs
47
==PAGE:56==
Figure 6.2: Force­displacement curves for specimens with CSF
The reason why this failure was so commonly experienced is difficult to determine. Equation 3.2
calculated the estimated shear capacity of concrete to be critical, based on geometry and mate­
rial strength. From the results attained in Sections 4 and 5, the failure happened in the presence
of fasteners. As tensile stresses form in the loaded notch area due to the notch eccentricity, a
crack develops from the loaded notch edge. The crack occurrence engages the fasteners to
carry the tensile stresses and gradually stops further propagation of the notch crack, preventing
a CTF. This allows for further load increasement until the concrete notch suddenly shears off.
To the left in Figure 6.3, a DIC photo of M­150­(0)­2 right before failure shows the sudden shear
crack opening from the not loaded notch edge. The same specimen is seen moments later to
the right, showing the shear crack tearing the notch apart. The reason why the shear crack
opened from this side of the notch is likely caused by tensile stresses behind the fastener as
a result of the notch crack. However, a finite element analysis is necessary to investigate the
origins of the shear crack and is not accounted for in this study.
Figure 6.3: DIC image of a CSF during failure

This type of failure occurred in the test series with fasteners placed in the middle of the notch
and was not prevented by using inclined screws. However, the failure was slightly less brittle
due to the inclined screws’ axially loaded contribution. Neither did the applied prestress of the
screws prevent the failure, as it was experienced for all specimens with varying prestressing
degrees. The only measure that appeared to be effective was with the fasteners positioned
closer to the loaded edge of the notch. This is likely because the tensile stress is highest in this
area, and the screws acted immediately when the notch crack opened.
Figure 6.4: CSF screw hinges on different specimens
The CSF resulted in two plastic hinges on the screws, as seen in Figure 6.4. The hinges were
located in the position of the shear crack and the interface between the materials. Both hinges
are evident for specimen M­50­(0)­2 with a notch length of 50 mm compared to N­150­(0)­1,
in which notch length was longer. This is because the shorter notch length had a substantially
lower capacity, tearing the concrete notch off at an earlier stage. As described in the loading
procedure, both tests continued until the notch slip was measured to 15 mm. This resulted in
an extended post­peak deformation period for M­50­(0)­2 and, therefore, more visible hinges.
Additionally, M­150­(0)­1, which test was stopped immediately after the failure, had no plastic
hinges visible on the screws, which signifies that the post­peak behaviour of timber compression
causes the screw deformation.
6.1.2
Timber compression failures
Also widely experienced was the TCF, which is the most ductile failure experienced in this test
series, shown in Figure 6.5. This failure happened for specimens where the concrete notch
stayed fully or partially intact, so the timber compression strength was exceeded in front of the
Experimental Study of Notched Connections for Prefabricated TCC Slabs
49
==PAGE:58==
loaded notch.
Figure 6.5: Force­displacement curves for specimens with TCF
Although this failure happened due to a surpassed capacity, other parameters contributed to
the individual specimens. For example did M­150­(­50)­1 have an almost entirely sheared­off
concrete notch, only kept together by the screws in the back of the notch. With so many similar­
ities to M­150­(­50)­2, which failed by CSF, it cannot be concluded that the fastener placement
in the back of the notch contributed to a ductile timber failure. Furthermore, the timber com­
pression strength varied greatly between the specimens, which may have contributed to the
results. However, despite having the greatest variation in timber strength, both specimens in
the M­150­(50) series failed by TCF, demonstrating that fasteners placed in front of the notch
cause ductile failure.
The in­situ series had the only specimens with screws placed in the middle of the notch and failed
by TCF. Based on similarities to the reference specimens after the cohesion broke, the timber
failure was most likely caused by a perfect interface in the loaded notch edge and timber strength
slightly below average. As a result, the stiffness and ductility of the failure were improved,
advocating that the notch interface must be uniform in prefabricated elements to compete with
in­situ solutions. The specimens with a notch length of 200 mm had higher concrete shear and
compression capacities and failed expectedly by TCF.
6.1.3
Concrete compression failures
Less prevalent was the CCF, where concrete was crushed at the loaded notch edge, which
happened with two specimens in the entire test series. There are few parallels between the two
Experimental Study of Notched Connections for Prefabricated TCC Slabs
50
==PAGE:59==
specimens other than both being cast in the second batch, where the concrete compression
strength was significantly lower. Still, the results for both are a somewhat ductile failure, even
in the absence of screws.
Figure 6.6: Force­displacement curves for specimens with CCF
Because the failure only occurred for one specimen in each series, characterising how it de­
velops is challenging, and the failure may be described as arbitrary. Furthermore, the failure
happened in combination with TCF for M­150­(­100)­2, which was not the case for the identical
specimen that failed primarily due to CSF. Even if the failure occurs arbitrarily, the presence of
fasteners contributes to a higher load­bearing capacity and a steady failure.
6.1.4
Concrete tensile failures
A tensile failure of the concrete turned out to be the most brittle failure type, where the overall
capacity was reduced, and the elements were entirely separated due to the tensile notch crack.
The failure is a direct consequence of a notched connection without any mechanical fastener
between the two materials, as it only occurred in the no­fastener series. Compared to a CSF,
the failure did not proceed to plastic deformation of the timber or crushing of the concrete as
in a CCF. This proves that the fasteners improve ductility and the post­peak behaviour despite
different failure types.
6.2
Design imperfections
To acquire realistic results when performing push­off tests, everything from the test setup to
the specimen design must be flawless. When different manufacturers prefabricate two distinct
materials, it isn’t easy to achieve precision, and the elements in this experiment were challeng­
Experimental Study of Notched Connections for Prefabricated TCC Slabs

==PAGE:60==
ing to assemble. As a measure, it was decided to extend the not­loaded edge of the timber
notch by 2 mm for all specimens. Although the specimens were put together accurately with
measurement tools and placed carefully in the test setup, a horizontal displacement between
the elements was observed during testing. Additionally, many specimens had different failures
on each side, especially noticeable for M­150­(­100)­1. Alongside physical observations, force­
displacement curves generated from DIC did not match the curves from the LVDT sensors.
Therefore, extensive measurements of the test setup and specimens were completed to track
down the source of this occurrence.
Firstly, the test setup was re­measured to see whether the active and passive support interacted
with an angle. The distance between each corner of the active and passive supports showed
that the maximum difference was around 0.7 mm. Because the distance between the supports
is approximately 1.1 m, it was concluded that the angle of 0.2° did not cause the experienced
rotation in different directions.
Secondly, the camera setup was investigated to explain the differences between DIC and LVDT
measurements. The DIC camera is very sensitive to out­of­plane rotation because of how the
surface is generated by pixel sizes, as explained in Section 3.2. Initially, it was discussed
whether this disturbed the results since the first specimens had a considerable difference be­
tween the slip from DIC and the LVDTs. The additional LVDT placed on the same side as the
camera verified that the specimens had a slight rotation, though less than expected. Figure 6.7
shows that the curve retrieved from GOM Correlate corresponds with the LVDT on the same
side, whereas both are unlike the measurements from the LVDT­side.

The timber elements were measured by the loaded notch edge and the passive support. These
measurements showed that the loaded surface of the notch had a slight angle that varied
amongst specimens. In other words, the notch was slightly longer on the DIC­side for some
specimens compared to the LVDT­side and vice versa. The lengths additionally varied with the

angle at the notch’s loaded edge, ultimately causing the elements to rotate. Consequently, this
makes a non­uniform load transfer seen by the screw loads measured on each side of the spec­

6.3
Practical aspects
Other practical issues must be considered regardless of the type of failure or desired stiffness.
Because both materials are prefabricated, irregularities may occur during installation on the
construction site. Specially designed formwork is required for the concrete notch to fit correctly
into the groove in the timber. If the concrete notch is longer than the timber groove when the
elements are to be assembled, it will be a tedious adjustment as contact in the loaded notch
edge must be ensured. The disadvantage is that the expenses will be considerably influenced
by how standardised the solution is for the specific projects. Especially for the case with inclined
screws, the concrete element must include an angled slit with enough space to tighten the
screws, which will be visible on the element’s surface.
Nonetheless, the primary benefit of incorporating prefabricated concrete in TCC structures is the
possibility of reusing the elements after the building’s lifetime. The experimental study indicates
that assembly of the specimens could be done by using standard tools, and separating the
elements was easily done by either pulling the screws out or cutting them off.

7
Conclusion
An experimental study of a push­off test series of notched connections in TCC structures is
presented in this report. The study aimed to achieve a greater understanding of how the position
of mechanical fasteners, amongst other parameters, influences the stiffness and failure mode
of the connection. It was achieved by analysing results from LVDT sensors appropriately placed
in critical areas of the specimen, and a detailed investigation of the crack propagation through
DIC, which led to the following conclusions:
• The fastener’s position greatly influences the failure type due to tensile stresses that arise
in the loaded notch area because of the eccentricity of the notch in the load transfer.
Fasteners closer to the loaded notch edge reduce tensile stresses in the concrete at an
earlier stage, leading to a ductile timber failure similar to the in­situ cast experiments found
in the literature.
• Fasteners in the middle of the notch achieved the highest stiffness values and load­bearing
capacity. However, the positioning of the fastener led to a brittle concrete shear failure
caused by tensile stresses behind the fastener at higher loading. Installing the fasteners
at a 45° angle further increased the capacity and slightly decreased the brittleness of
the failure. The inclined fasteners, however, reduced the stiffness because of element
separation induced by the applied prestress. Still, the fasteners in the middle of the notch
prevented complete element separation and contributed to ductile post­peak behaviour.
• Having the fastener in the back of or behind the notch lowered the stiffness of the con­
nection and caused inconsistent failure behaviour. Additional research is needed to un­
derstand why these irregularities occur, as two specimens per test series are insufficient
to detect tendencies. However, theories about the influence of crack propagation and the
compression component’s location in the loaded notch have been presented.
• Full prefabrication needs specially designed formwork for the concrete and incredible pre­
cise milling of the timber groove. Inaccurate design led to uneven failures, and less re­
liability in the fasteners since properly installed measurement tools registered horizontal
displacement. However, achieving great results in a fully prefabricated TCC structure
with low­quality concrete is possible, hence reducing CO2 emissions in the construction
industry.
