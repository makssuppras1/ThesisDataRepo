Summary：
Operation of transmission systems with high wind power penetration is 
especially challenging during storm conditions. Under stormy wind 
speed conditions, wind turbines protection system is designed to shut 
down the turbine to avoid excessive mechanical load. Sudden loss of 
wind power from large offshore plants is difficult to forecast accurately 
and it results in a large amount of power imbalance. The severity of 
such a wind power imbalance towards frequency stability needs to be 
studied for the future power system scenario where more wind power 
will appear in the system.
In addition, the overhead transmission lines can also be affected during 
storms, thereby increasing their probability of failure resulting in 
separation of the Danish network from rest of the Continental European 
network. This can result in frequency changes increasing the probability 
of frequency instability; hence, these situations need to be studied and
analyzed in details.
The objective of this Master project is to investigate how the stormy 
weather condition can threat the frequency stability of a highly wind 
penetrated power system and how to avoid the frequency going into 
unstable through controlling other equipment in the system. A simplified 
Danish power system model for frequency stability studies will be 
developed. Two types of unexpected events: ’Large amount of wind 
power loss due to a storm’ and ’the AC transmission failure caused by 
the storm’ will be investigated with the developed model. Suggestions 
regarding how to improve system frequency stability and secure 
operation of the system under storm conditions are proposed.

Chapter1
Introduction
This chapter gives a general introduction to the thesis project. Firstly, background and
motivation of the project is given. Then, the objectives of this project are discussed. Next,
contribution and limitation of this project are summarized. An outline of the thesis struc-
ture is provided at the end.
1.1
Background and Motivation
The main goal of the power system is to supply reliable electric energy to the consumers.
Conventional generation such as fossil fuel plants and hydro power plants convert chem-
ical energy in the fuel and potential energy in the water respectively to electrical energy.
The fossil fuel and water can be stored easily and be utilized whenever needed. Unlike
conventional generation, wind power generation depends on the stochastic and unstorable
wind so that it only produces electricity when wind blows.
However, the exhaustion of fossil fuel and global warming issues caused by CO2 emis-
sion has limited the development of fossil power plant. As CO2 free and renewable energy
,and with an increasingly competitive price, wind energy is widely accepted as a new al-
ternative to meet new electricity demand.
More challenges regarding security and stability of the power system emerge if large
scale of conventional plants are replaced by the less controllable wind turbines. It could
be more critical when the uncertain nature imposes unexpected weather condition such as
a storm to the power system. The storm not only increase the risk of failure of an overhead
line but also make the emergency shutdown of a wind plant plausible. It is important to
study how a high wind penetrated power system could survive from the extreme events
caused by the stormy weather. In this project, the western Danish power system under
2020 scenario with leading wind penetration is chosen as the study object.
As a leading country in wind power utilization, Denmark has the highest wind energy
contribution to the power system. In 2015, 42% of the total electricity consumption was
covered by wind and the government has set the goal to reach 50% by 2020 [1]. As
shown in Figure 1.1, the total installed wind capacity almost doubled in 2016 (5242 MW)

Among the installed capacity in 2016, onshore wind turbine stands for 3971 MW while

a large terrain do not ﬂuctuate with a same pattern and the extreme weather does not in-

tinental European grid only relies on the overhead transmission lines with Germany. If the
overhead lines is damaged by the storm, a large sudden power imbalance would be expe-
rienced by the Danish grid. As a high wind penetrated system, the small system inertia
might not be able to hold the frequency within the safe region and a blackout out might
happen.
Motivated by the Danish ambitious goal towards wind energy utilization and the his-
torical negative impact form the storm, this thesis targets at answering related questions.
The objectives are discussed in next section.

1.2
Project Objectives
The general objective of this Master project is to investigate how the stormy weather con-
dition can threat the frequency stability of a highly wind penetrated power system and
how to avoid the frequency going into unstable through controlling other equipment in the
system.
The speciﬁc objectives included in this thesis are:
• Study the planned power system infrastructures in the future 2020 scenario. Develop
a simpliﬁed power system model for frequency stability study based on the future
scenario. Include the general dynamic model of the essential components that are
important for frequency stability study.
• Development of a simpliﬁed western Danish power system model that includes con-
ventional generation units, wind power plants and interconnections with neighbor-
ing systems.
• Review the historical records of storm events, and study their impact on the power
system. Apply an algorithm that is capable of predicting the wind power storm
behaviour in a power system scale based on historical weather data. Identify a severe
storm in the past that is capable to inﬂuence the wind power production in the whole

interested area; apply the developed wind speed to power algorithm and analyze the
possible prediction error caused by the storm.
• Consider the current power system operation mode, apply the wind power prediction
error to the system model and study how to balance the system through controlling
of other components. Analyze the frequency stability with wind power storm be-
havior.
• Review the current power system defence plans for frequency stability control. De-
velop a control algorithm based on the observed defence plan. Consider high impact
low possibility events: loosing an important transmission due to system, investigate

• Perform sensitivity analyses to these severe events and ﬁnd the stable and unstable
situations. Give suggestions that are possible to improve frequency stability under
severe contingencies.
1.3
Contributions and Limitations
The main contributions of this thesis project are listed below:
• Development of an approach that is able to predict the behaviour of large scale of
wind power in a stormy weather condition.
• Deterministic study on the impact of an AC line failure on the system frequency.
Analyze the different pre-event situations and their corresponding post-event inﬂu-
ence.
The limitations of this thesis project are listed below:
• In this thesis, an aggregated model is used to represent the typical behaviour of the
system components. The key parameters are referred from existing literature and
are not calibrated with industrial measurements. Thus, the model behaviour might
not perfectly comply with the real cases.
• The thesis only focus on frequency stability of the system. Voltage dependencies of
generations and loads are not considered. Voltage stability is not taken in account,
which is also crucial for power system security.
• In aggregated model, central frequency is considered and local frequency ﬂuctua-
tions are not considered. Also, network congestion are not considered in single bus
aggregated model.

1.4 Thesis Outline
1.4
Thesis Outline
This thesis includes 7 chapters and is organized as follows:
Chapter 1 gives an introduction of this thesis by describing the background and moti-
vation, discussing the objectives and listing the contributions and limitations.
Chapter 2 shows the impact of storms on the power system. The historical power
failure events are listed to show the increased risk of outage in a stormy day. In addition,
the wind power storm behaviour and its impact on the power system are illustrated.
Chapter 3 starts with discussing the electrical infrastructures in western Denmark in
the future scenario. Main generating units and interconnections with neighboring areas
are covered. Then, a system dynamic model for frequency stability study is developed
with including related infrastructures. Also, how the imbalanced power would be reﬂected
as frequency deviation of the system is discussed.
Chapter 4 deals with detailed modelling of each component included in the system
dynamic model. The current frequency control approaches are ﬁrstly discussed. Following
that, the modelling of conventional units is presented. The modelling of wind power and
wind power prediction error are then illustrated. Next, the AC connection between western
Denmark and Germany is modelled. This Chapter ends with discussing frequency defence
plans that aims at handling abnormal frequency situations.
In Chapter 5, a typical severe storm is chosen and its historical wind speed data is
obtained. Those wind speed data is applied to the future power system scenario and the
possible wind power prediction error is analyzed. After obtaining the wind power error, the
error is applied to the power system model as power imbalance and the control strategies
of other equipment to compensate the wind power imbalance is studied. The frequency
stability under such a case is concluded in the end.
In Chapter 6, the analysis focuses on the large disturbances caused by losing the AC

with different HVDC ramp rates, magnitudes of disturbance and system inertia to ﬁnd the
frequency secure prior-disturbance region. In addition, the beneﬁt of wind turbine inertia
response towards frequency stability is shown.
Chapter 7 summarizes and concludes this thesis, giving suggestions for further study.

2.2
Wind farm behavior in extreme wind speed
The extreme wind speeds are rare to happen, hence designing turbines to operate in ex-
treme wind conditions is not economically feasible for the manufactures. Wind turbines’
control system is usually designed to stop the turbine through the mechanical break inside
the nacelle when stormy wind speed occurs, which is often referred as High Wind Speed
Shutdown (HWSD) control [8]. The wind turbine emergency shutdown can be explained
by the power curve of a single turbine, as shown in Figure 2.2, the controller send the
shutdown signal to the brake actuator either when:
• Instantaneous wind speed reaches 32m/s
• 30 second mean wind speed reaches 28m/s
• 10 minute mean wind speed reaches 25m/s
To avoid over reactivating of the turbine, a conservative restart wind speed is chosen
as 20m/s, turbines start capturing wind energy again when a 10 minute wind speed is
measured below 20m/s.
With HWSD control, the steep power drop gradient is challenging for the power system
balancing if large scale of wind turbines shut down within a short period. To handle the
problem, Siemens has proposed a new controller named as High Wind Ride ThroughTM,
as shown in Figure 2.3, to improve turbine power performance in stormy conditions. Com-
pared with HWSD, turbine with HWRTTM has a extended operating wind speed range. The
smallest and the severest shutdown wind speed has been improved to 30m/s and 39m/s re-
spectively. Between 20m/s to 30m/s, turbine is controlled to provide power between rated
power to around 40% of rated power. Shutting down of the turbine with HWRTTM has a
smaller gradient as the power only drops from 0.4 p.u. to zero.

Conventional Units
The Combined Heat and Power (CHP) plants produce electrical power and additionally
utilizes waste heat to supply heat to the district heating network. CHP technology is widely
used in Denmark due to the demand for heat in the Danish households, and that it has a
great efﬁciency in fuel utilization.
Among CHP plants, they can be classiﬁed as Centralized Combined Heat and Power
Plants (CCHP) which are usually located close to large cities and Decentralized Combined
Heat and Power Plants (DCHP) that are close to smaller cities or towns. Apart from the
difference in location, the Danish CCHPs are mostly represented by steam turbine power
plant while DCHPs are mostly represented by gas turbine power plant [11].
The main difference between a steam turbine and a gas turbine plant is reﬂected by
the primary mover of the generator. In a steam driven power plant, the water is ﬁrstly
heated by ﬁring the fuel and turns into steam, then the steam spins a steam turbine whose
shaft drives the electrical generator. The gas turbine generators are usually used in two
basic conﬁgurations: Single Cycle (or Open Cycle) gas turbine (OCGT) and Combined
Cycle Gas Turbine (CCGT). In both OCGT and CCGT conﬁguration, the compressed air
is mixed with the gas fuel in a combustion chamber and then the combusted gas moves
the gas turbine which then drives the generator. However, in CCGT conﬁguration, the
exhausted gas from the outlet of the gas turbine is then utilized to heat water in a boiler, the
heated water turns into steam and then rotate a steam turbine that ﬁnally drives a generator.
The gas turbine and steam turbine in CCGT layout can both drive a shared generator or
separate generators. [16]

System Dynamic Model
As aforementioned, the interest of this project is frequency stability. The system is sim-
pliﬁed and modelled towards the requirement of a frequency stability study in this section.
For frequency stability analysis in a synchronized area, it is common to use the model
described by Equation (3.1) [17].
∆PG −∆PL + ∆PEX = ∆ω · (2Hs + D)
(3.1)
where H is the system inertia constant in seconds, D is the dimensionless damping
coefﬁcient that represents the damping effect from the frequency dependent load, ∆ω is
the rotor speed deviation in p.u. and ∆ω = ω −ωs, where ω is the actual rotor speed and
ωs the synchronizing speed. ∆PG and PEX in p.u. denote the total deviation in generated
electrical power and interchange respectively. Besides, ∆PL in p.u. represents the total
deviation of electrical load that is independent of frequency. The lower capital s is the
Laplace operator.
As mentioned before, the west Danish grid is synchronized with the CE grid. It is
feasible to model the complete CE grid through Equation (3.1) to study the overall fre-
quency behavior. However, this approach neglects the local frequency oscillation between
the sub-areas. In this study, as we are interested in the interaction between West Denmark
and the CE grid, the two-area system modelling approach discussed by [17] is used. By
doing so, the western Danish grid is considered as one area and the rest of the CE grid
is considered as another area. The inertia and damping constant discussed in this chapter
only represent the western Denmark.

Boiler model
The boiler in a power plant burns the primary fuel and transfers the produced heat to
the feeding water which then becomes steam. The boiler model shown by Figure 4.4
is simpliﬁed and could be described by three time constant: Tb1, Tb2 and Tb3. They
represent the time delay from the load reference to the turbine main steam pressure Pt.
These time delays are mainly because of slow thermal dynamics inside the boiler, from
fuel combustion to water feeding and then to the steam heating etc., each of these steps
takes some time. It is worth mentioning here that the slow response of steam-electric
power plants is dominated by the boiler delay. A typical overall response of the boiler can
take up to several minutes. The parameters of the boiler model is obtained from [11] and
summarized in Appendix .1.
Steam turbine
The steam turbine is a key component in the thermal power plant, that converts the thermal
energy in the steam to shaft mechanical energy. Numbers of blade are mounted around the
main shaft of the turbine. When the pressurized steam ﬂows through the vanes between
blades, they release the stored thermal energy and push the blade to rotate.
There are different types of steam turbines ranging from from non-reheat, tandem com-
pound single and double reheat, to cross compound single and double reheat [24]. How-
ever, the general turbine model illustrated in Figure 4.5 is able to accommodate all types
[23]. The mechanical power is deﬁned as a function of control valve: CV and steam pres-
sure: Pt. A power limiter is applied before the turbine mechanical power as the unit has a
certain operation limit due to thermal or mechanical constraints. The series of gains: K1 to
K8 represent the steam extraction at different stages of the shaft. K1 and K2 denote very
high pressure extraction fraction, K3 and K4 denote the high pressure extraction fraction,
K5 and K6 are the intermediate pressure extraction fraction and K7 and K8 are the low
pressure extraction fraction [11].

4.3
Modelling of Wind Power
In the future Danish electrical power system, the dominance of wind power becomes more
signiﬁcant. Unlike conventional units whose power can be controlled by adjusting the
input energy, wind power is determined by the incoming wind speed. In this section, wind
speed across western Denmark in a stormy day will be simulated and be converted to
available wind power in the power system. The method proposed by [26] is referred to
model wind speed ﬂuctuations and wind power in the whole western Denmark.
4.3.1
Modelling of wind speed
In order to obtain the wind power behaviour in the stormy weather, it is needed to model
the turbulent wind speed with which the turbine storm controller is able to distinguish
whether to stop the turbine. As discussed in Section 2.2, the 1 second sampled wind speed
is required for the control algorithm, thus the modelled wind speed should at least be
sampled with 1 second. In addition, as the wind speeds vary by locations, turbines inside
a wind farm would not see the same wind speed, which means that they might shut down
at different times [4]. As a consequence, considering the turbulence and coherence of the
wind speed is necessary to model the wind power storm behaviour.
The CorWind software is used to simulate wind speeds in this study. The wind speed
generated by CorWind is based on the historical meteorological data and is able to provide
wind speed time series at the user speciﬁed sites [21]. In this study, the 1 minutes sam-
pled hub height mean wind speed at each 150kV/220kV bus site is generated by CorWind.
Wind turbines connected to each 150kV/220kV bus is considered as a wind farm. Then,
the 10 minutes average wind speed at these farms are calculated based on the 1 minute
sampled wind speed. With the 10 minute mean wind speed, it is able to cover the low fre-
quency (smaller than 1/600 Hz) ﬂuctuation of the wind speed. Then, the Kaimal spectrum
that is speciﬁed by the IEC 61400-1 [27] standard for turbine design requirement is chosen
to simulate high frequency wind speed ﬂuctuations. The Power Spectrum Density (PSD)

The inertia response of wind turbines is now suggested by several European TSOs to help
regulate grid frequency after a sudden and severe disturbance [33]. The disturbance could
be the failure of a large load, a conventional generator or an import transmission line.
A typical way to achieve the inertia response is by implementing a control algorithm,
which varies turbine power output according to the deviation of grid frequency, to a wind
turbine or a wind power plant. This method utilizes the kinetic energy stored on the rotor
mass of the turbine. For the power electronic based turbines (type III and type IV [3]),
the power production could be fast changed by sudden increase or decrease the electrical
torque. For type I and type II turbines, the inertia response is provided by the speed torque
characteristic of the induction machine and it is not controllable. In the future, most of the
turbines would be represented by type III and type IV turbines with better control ability
and power quality, hence the modelling here is based on type III and type IV turbines.
As it is more often to disconnect the wind turbines following an over frequency events,
here the discussion focus on inertia response on under frequency events where extra power
should be provided by turbines. Drawing extra electrical torque from the turbine will cause
the rotor decelerate. If the turbine is operating with low wind speed where pitch control
is not activated, the rotor deceleration might increase the danger of turbine stalling. On
the contrast, if the turbine is operating with high wind speed, the performance of inertia
response could be better as the turbine rotor deceleration could be avoid by adjusting the
pitch angle to give more aerodynamic torque.
In this study, the wind turbine inertial response is simulated through the block diagram
shown in Figure 4.15. The Laplace operator in the numerator aims at calculating the
derivative of the frequency. The constant Kw is the gain that determines the magnitude of
the output, while the constant Pwn is the available wind power when inertial response is
not applied. Pwn ampliﬁes the input signal ∆ω in p.u. to the wind power deviation PW
in MW. In addition, a time constant Tw is applied in the denominator to ﬁlter out the high
frequency component in the input signal and to represent the control delay [34]; it deﬁnes
how fast the turbine could provide the inertial response. A limiter is implemented to

4.5 Modelling of System Defence Plan
4.5
Modelling of System Defence Plan
Following a severe disturbance, for example, losing a large generating unit or an import
transmission line, the frequency could deviate from the normal range. Under this situation,
the imbalanced power should be quickly eliminated to avoid the frequency deviate further.
The fast power balancing approaches used today include HVDC emergency control, under
frequency load shedding and fast wind turbine disconnection. These approaches will be
discussed in this section.
4.5.1
HVDC emergency power control
In a HVDC (High Voltage Direct Current) transmission system, the power from an AC net-
work is converted to DC through a converter station at the sending end, transmitted to the
receiving end through a cable or overhead line and then converted back to AC in another
converter station at the receiving end. The power conversion process in a HVDC system
could be fully controlled by the power electronic components and it is possible to achieve
fast and bidirectional power exchange between two asynchronous AC network.[35]
The previous application has shown that the fast control of power ﬂow in HVDC links
help to enhance the frequency stability of the connected AC grid. On April 10, 1979
the power network in western Denmark was isolated together with parts of the German
network from the CE grid. There was an power deﬁcit on the island of 1150 MW; within
3 seconds, the frequency fell to 48.1 Hz. Part of the load was shed by under-frequency
protection. The Skagerrak (500 MW at that time) increased the power injection to the
isolated system from 50 to 320MW and Konti-Skan (250 MW) from 0 to 125 MW within
3 seconds. On average, the ramp rate was approximate 0.167 p.u./s. Contributed to the
fast power input of 375 MW by the HVDC links, the frequency restored to normal quickly
and a blackout was prevented. [35]
The emergency control of HVDC links is activated when the frequency deviates from
the normal frequency. As for western Denmark, the rule of Continental Europe network
applies which means ±200 mHz is the normal frequency deviation. Once the frequency
gets outside this range, further control action should be activated to avoid frequency de-
viates further. According to ENTSO-E [36], in Nordic system, emergency power control
on HVDC connections is activated in several different steps at frequencies over 50.3 Hz or
below 49.8 Hz.
In this project, the rule of HVDC emergency control in the Nordic system is assumed.
Also, all the HVDC links between western Danish system and neighboring systems are
aggregated as one HVDC link. And the HVDC emergency response is modelled as a
constant ramp with a short communication delay, the key parameters are summarized in
Table 4.2. It is worth mention that the emergency power control should be stopped when
the power imbalance is eliminated, otherwise, a further imbalance could be seen due to
excessive emergency power. The total capacity of the aggregated HVDC link reaches
3740 MW, as shown in Figure 3.1. Based on the ramp rate found in the 1979 event, the
technically available average ramp rate of 625 MW/s could be expected in the future.
However, most of the HVDC converters in the future will be represented by the faster
voltage source converter, thus a ramp rate faster than 625 MW/s could be speculated in the
future.

Impact of wind turbine inertia response
In the previous sections, the wind turbines do not provide any inertia response. In a stormy
day, it could be expected that the wind speeds across a large region is fairly high. As
a consequence, most of the turbines could operate above rated wind speeds where the
pitch control system is activated to limit the turbine speed and power. As discussed in
Section 4.3.4, a good inertia support from the turbines could be possible in this situation.
The increased frequency stability by adding inertia response for under frequency scenarios
will be investigated in this chapter.

the frequency recovering takes more time, however, the severity of slow recovering is less
signiﬁcant than a smaller frequency nadir.
All in all, the inertia response helps to mitigate the frequency drop and faster ramp has
a better effect. In reality, based on the wind conditions, the wind plants operator could
develop a plan determining how much inertia response they could provide and set the
gain, time constant and limiter of the control block. With wind turbine inertia response,
it is possible to reduce or even avoid load shedding and reduce the economical loss of the
contingency.

Chapter7
Conclusion and Further Study
This Chapter concludes the thesis and gives suggestions for further study.
7.1
Conclusions
The master thesis ’Security and Stability of High Wind Penetrated Power System during
Storm Conditions’ aims at studying the frequency stability of a highly wind penetrated
power system under two types of events. One is that large amount of wind turbines are
stopped due to a severe storm and another one is losing the AC connection with the main
CE grid. The conclusions of these studies are listed as follows:
• In the future, due to more closely sited large offshore wind farms, the amount of
wind power loss becomes more signiﬁcant. In case of wind power shut down due
to extreme wind speed events, the analysis showed that the frequency can be main-
tained within the normal range. The main reason for that is that the power imbalance
coming from wind is rather slow and the AC connection with the main CE grid helps
compensating this imbalance. The reserved capacity on the AC lines is important
for wind power loss compensation; it ensures that the AC lines are not overloaded
when extra power is delivered from Germany to western Denmark to support the
wind power loss. More available reserve is required in the system to support the
grid. If enough reserve is available, the wind power loss could be compensated
through the human reaction in the control room by activating manual reserves. A
HWRTTM control helps to reduce the gradient of wind power drop and capture more
wind energy in a severe stormy day.
• When looking at extreme events such as AC lines failure, the frequency nadir is
extreme, especially when combined with small HVDC ramp rate and small system
inertia. In this case, the main source of very fast reserve is the HVDC connections
with neighboring areas. To avoid the risk of blackout, it is necessary to increase the
amount of spinning conventional generator or ensure very fast frequency support
59
==PAGE:74==
Chapter 7. Conclusion and Further Study
from wind power. If the required HVDC reserve (and ramp rate) is not available,
conservative operation of the AC lines could be another option to reduce the mag-
nitude of disturbance and improve frequency security. One advantage of the stormy
condition is the high wind speed across large area. This gives a good opportunity for
wind turbines if they dont shut down to support the grid by virtual inertia response.
An excellent inertial response is able to reduce the uneconomical load shedding and
limit extreme frequency deviation.
7.2
Further study
The suggested further studies are listed below:
• For the wind turbines shut down event, the further study could focus on analyzing
the possibility of such a storm event, and different realizations of a same storm by
CorWind. By doing so, probabilistic evaluation could help the system operator to
deﬁne new defence plans towards such events. Also, the wind farm operators could
judge whether it is feasible to install new controller based on the probabilistic study.
Also, what could be the most economical approach to dispatch the required reserve
could be another option to study. For example, if the reserve in the western Danish
system is not sufﬁcient or too expensive, extra reserve could be asked from the
HVDC lines by integrating the HVDC transmission capacity into AGC control and
manual regulation.
• For AC lines failure event, how the AC lines could be failed in a storm and the
corresponding probability could be further studied. During a storm, the risk of fault
also increases, the voltage drop due to the fault could be another reason for the wind
power to be disconnected. It could be interesting to study whether the current wind
turbine fault ride through capacity is applicable to the future scenarios. The ramp
rate of HVDC links is aggregated in this study, how to dispatch the aggregated ramp
to the individual HVDC links and give less negative impact to neighboring systems
could be further researched as well.
