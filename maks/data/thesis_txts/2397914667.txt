Abstract
Millimeter-wave (30-300 GHz) technologies have been utilised in a wide range of ap-
plications such as high data rate communications, radio astronomy and remote sensing.
Moreover, it has recently recorded a growing demand for high-frequency signal sources
to be used in a whole emerging set of technologies such as imaging and sensing for
biomedical and spectroscopy applications.
The commercially available solutions are usually very expansive. This project aims at
investigating the possibility to develop a high power W-band signal source, with the
in-house technology available at the EMS (Electromagnetic Systems) department of DTU.
Due to the availability of a generator in K-band, the power source is designed as a cascade
of two frequency multipliers operating as balanced doublers, based on an anti-series array
of the commercial Macom MA46H146 GaAs varactor diodes.
Because of its suitability for balanced operation, a passive structure similar to the one
proposed in [1] is chosen. A model of the commercial diode is developed, and used in a
design methodology consisting of an interactive usage of the harmonic balance simulations
(performed in Keysight ADS) and a full 3-D electromagnetic analysis (performed in CST
Studio Suite 2017).
Both devices are fabricated as a hybrid split-block waveguide structure embodying the
PCB on which the array of varactors in anti-series connection is manually soldered. When
the two multipliers are connected and the chain is driven by a source in K-band, the highest
measured output is 1 mW at 83.024 GHz.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Traditionally, the demand for millimeter-wave and submillimeter-wave power sources, to
use as local oscillators, has been dominated by the sectors of radio astronomy, remote
sensing and atmospheric physics [1]. Only recently, due to the development of terahertz
frequency technologies, a broader range of applications is emerging. Research in the
biomedical sector [2, 3], applications in biology [4], security and nondestructive testing
[5], require compact, high power, and affordable signal sources.
The purpose of this thesis is to develop a W-band (94 GHz) microwave signal source to use
inside a polarizer for NMR (Nuclear Magnetic Resonance) applications within the center
for Hyper-polarization in Magnetic Resonance (HYPERMAG) [6] at DTU (Danmarks
Tekniske Universitet). The desired output power for this type of application is ideally 200
mW.
Modern versions of the past generation oscillators, such as the Klystron ampliﬁer and
the traveling-wave tube, are devices capable of very high output power at microwave
frequencies [7]. However, those devices are in general bulky, expensive, and require high
voltages. A cost-effective strategy to produce compact microwave power sources is using
semiconductor devices, which are the core components of frequency multiplier chains. A
frequency multiplier is a device that performs a frequency conversion of the input signal.
The conversion aims at obtaining an output waveform at a frequency that is an integer
multiple of the input frequency. Usually, two RF ports and one DC bias port are present;
the input RF port is meant to be connected to the source power generator at the input
frequency fin, while the output RF port is where the harmonic signal is rendered available.
The DC port serves as a channel to deliver the DC voltage to the electronic components
used in the multiplier.

CHAPTER 1. INTRODUCTION
Various typologies of semiconductor devices can be used to design frequency multipli-
ers. Successful designs have been developed by using high electron mobility transistors
(HEMT) [8], heterojunction bipolar transistors (HBT) [9], and passive components, such
as heterostructure barrier varactors (HBV) [10], Schottky barrier diodes, varistor and
varactor diodes. In particular, the latter category has a positive response in literature,
with regards to the development of high and very high frequency (0.5-1.5 THz) multiplier
chains [11]. The signal source designed in this project consists of a cascade of two varactor
frequency doublers, the chain is pumped by a generator capable of delivering power in a
range between -10 dBm and 30 dBm at K-band frequencies (18-26.5 GHz). The ﬁrst 47
GHz doubler (Q-band) is the driver of the second 94 GHz doubler (W-band) as depicted in
Figure 1.1.
Figure 1.1: Block scheme of the W-band (94 GHz) frequency multiplier chain.
The design of both elements, is inspired by the David W. Porterﬁeld’s work [1] on a
40/80 GHz doubler, which is in turn derived from the Erickson’s balanced schemes [12].
Those circuits consist of an anti-series array of varactor diodes soldered on a PCB, which
is integrated into a metallic split block and mounted across an input and output custom
rectangular waveguide structure. The anti-series diode connection is capable of suppressing
the unwanted odd harmonics, while separating the input and output radiations. Successful
implementations of this design can be widely found in literature [13–16].
The diodes utilised for the development of the multipliers in this project are the commer-
cially available GaAs ﬂip-chip varactors (MA46H146) from Macom [17]. The ﬂip-chip
conﬁguration requires the diodes to be manually glued on the dedicated PCB pads. The
doublers passive networks consist of rectangular waveguide to PCB transitions forming a
hybrid structure which parts are analysed and designed with the aid of an electromagnetic
simulator (CST Studio Suite 2017). The optimisation and testing of the multipliers overall
performance are carried out through a co-simulation procedure involving CST and the
harmonic balance simulator of ADS (Keysight Advanced Design System).

CHAPTER 2
Theoretical background
This chapter offers a brief presentation of the mechanism responsible for the generation of a
signal containing high order harmonics by means of a non-linear component. Furthermore,
the basic theory concerning the varactor diode p-n junction is outlined, the physical and
electrical equivalents are analysed, some considerations regarding conversion efﬁciency
and the packaging parasitics are made. The last part illustrates the circuit theory behind
the balanced topology that is employed in the actual design of the frequency doublers
proposed in this work.

2.2
Varactor device
The variable capacitance diode is a semiconductor p-n junction which is used not for its
rectifying proprieties but rather for its voltage-dependent non-linear proprieties, mainly
provided by the depletion layer of the junction [19].
Varactors normally exhibit a non-linear relation between capacitance and voltage and are
mostly used for harmonic generation at millimetre and submillimeter wave frequencies.
In general, any semiconductor diode can operate as a frequency multiplier by voltage
modulating the nonlinear depletion region capacitance under reverse bias conditions, or by
driving it in the forward conduction region under positive bias voltages. The two modes of
operation are called varactor and varistor respectively. Depending on the bias conditions,
the diode can also work in a region where both nonlinear effects are used at the same time.
The main advantages of using a varactor are the low losses and low noise which lead to
higher conversion efﬁciency. On the other hand, resistive multipliers, due to the absence
of reactive energy storage, have a larger operational bandwidth but suffer from lower
conversion efﬁciency [20].
Commonly used varactor devices are the reverse biased semiconductor abrupt p+n junction
diode made from GaAs (gallium-arsenide) or silicon, or the Schottky barrier diode. The

The formation of the p+n junction creates a contact potential difference between the two
regions causing some of the free electrons of the n region to move to the p+ region while
some holes of the p+ region move to the n region, as a illustrated in Figure 2.3. The
depletion region is formed where unneutralized acceptor (−) and donor (+) ions are left
at the interface of the junction, creating an electric ﬁeld, which opposes further movement
of charge carriers [22]. The electric ﬁeld implies the existence of a potential difference
that is called built-in potential φ. This implies that an actual storage of charge is present at
the junction interface.
==PAGE:27==
2.2. VARACTOR DEVICE

As described so far, the width of the depletion region depends on the doping density and
the material parameters of the semiconductor. If an external voltage is now applied to
the terminals of the diode, it will be either added or subtracted from the built-in potential
voltage φ, and it will give rise to a new net junction voltage. In the case of a reverse
external voltage, the action already brought by the built-in potential is enhanced and thus
the depletion region is widen. If the external applied voltage is in the forward direction,
the depletion region narrows.
Under a reverse bias conditions, there is a very little current since the external voltage
polarity only allows the ﬂow of the electrons from the p+ side to the n side and holes from
the n side to the p+ side but in these conditions there are few electrons (minority carriers)
on the p+ side and few holes on the n side [22].
The depletion layer and the neutral p+, n regions can be seen as an insulator and two
conductors. Indeed, it is possible to recognise the existence of an effective capacitance
in a form similar to the parallel plate capacitor, where the p+ and n junctions act as the
electrodes and the depletion region as the dielectric in between. If a reverse voltage is
applied, the insulating depletion layer widens and further separates the charge. Therefore,
the junction capacitance Cj decreases from its zero bias value to the minimum at the
reverse breakdown voltage Vbr.
The voltage-dependent series resistor Rs(V ), is indicated in Figure 2.3 as the remaining
n doped region below the depletion region. As already mentioned this is the largest
contributor to the losses of the device, principally because the doping and consequently
the conductivity of this region needs to be made small enough, so, the depletion region
can expand to provide the voltage-dependent nature of the depletion layer capacitance. If
the n region is made highly doped and consequently more conductive, the series resistance
Rs is lowered, but the capacitance would remain essentially constant while varying the
externally applied voltage. This shows that ensuring a certain spatial variation of the
depletion region is a basic necessity if one is to obtain a variable capacitance diode.

many parasitic contributions mostly in the form of capacitances and inductances.

Other important conditions involve the input impedance presented to the device at the
second harmonic and the output impedance presented at the fundamental frequency. As
illustrated in Figure 2.6, and explained in [24], those should in both cases be an open
circuit. The reason for this can be seen if we consider the equivalent impedance Zeq(fin)
across the diode terminals at the input frequency. This is, in fact, the parallel combination
of the input and output impedances presented at the diode terminals: ZI(fin) and ZO(fin)
respectively. Since we do not want the output impedance and consequently the output
network to dissipate power at the input frequency, ZO(fin) should be ideally inﬁnite. The
same reasoning can be applied for the second harmonic 2fin, in which an open circuit is
needed as input impedance since we do not want the output signal of the doubler to be
dissipated in the input network.
In summary, this strategy prevents energy to be absorbed in unwanted parts of the structure,
and ensures isolation between the radiations at fin and 2fin, ultimately preserving the
conversion efﬁciency.

Varactor diode modelling
The following chapter aims at presenting the steps that lead to the development of a
computer model of the varactor diode used in this work: the MA46H146 from Macom
[17]. A sample of the diode has been measured in the laboratory through a VNA (Vector
Network Analyzer). The measured data have been exported in the form of a touchstone
ﬁle, and de-embedded from its soldering ﬁxture to obtain the actual S-parameters of the
diode.
A full 3-D model of the MA46H146 is rendered available by the authors of [25]. The
model includes the complete passive semiconductor structure of the MA46H146 chip, and
it is simulated in CST studio. Although very small (less than 0.5 mm), the diode chip has a
ﬁnite dimension that interacts with the electromagnetic waves, and accounts for the device
parasitics. Therefore, the obtained S-parameter ﬁle from the 3-D model is combined with
a default ADS diode to model the non-linear junction. This co-simulation setup allows
to ﬁt the developed diode model to the measurements by modifying some parameters of
the non-linear junction (in ADS environment) such as, the saturation current, the built-in
potential, and the junction capacitance at zero bias voltage.
Having a reliable model of the semiconductor device is a primary need for an accurate
design of any diode application circuit. Indeed, the accuracy in the design of the frequency
doubler circuit is directly related to the accuracy of the computer model.

The output side of the structure consists on a PCB to WR-22 (Q-band) output waveguide

generated in the diodes, and is propagated through the PCB in an unbalanced quasi-TEM

fundamental TE10.
The "hammer-head" ﬁlter section (part of the PCB), located beyond the output waveguide,
is a low-pass ﬁlter designed for DC bias purposes. This part of the structure allows the
application of the DC voltage to the diodes while preventing the leakage of the second
harmonic power from the output probe to the DC circuit. In practice, it works as an
RF-choke.

One of the software utilised for this analysis and design is CST studio suite 2017, an
electromagnetic 3D simulator that offers various solvers. The one used for this work is
the transient solver, a general purpose simulator particularly suited for analysing ﬁelds
propagating through structures or along the lines of a PCB [28]. It uses the ﬁnite integration
technique which consists in dividing the three-dimensional areas of the EM problem into
thousands or millions (depending on the electrical size of the structure) of smaller cells
called "mesh" and numerically solves the Maxwell equations for each sub-region.
Using a ﬁne mesh (high number of mesh cells) leads to higher accuracy of the S-parameter
solution but it requires more computational time and memory from the computer in use.
Since in the structures simulated in this work the output frequency is twice the input
frequency, a part of the doubler speciﬁcally designed to work at the input frequency, can
result in an electrically large structure at the output frequency when the 3D model is
simulated in its entirety.
In order to test the structures designed in CST, the touchstone ﬁles containing the linear

environment, it is possible to connect the diodes and use the integrated harmonic balance

The sections 4.1.1 - 4.1.5 describe the design steps of the Q-band frequency doubler,
starting with the extraction of the input and output optimal impedances of the single diode
and continuing with the synthesis of the passive structure that is divided in three parts:
the input, output, and bias sections. The design techniques are in part inspired by the
methodologies found in [29] and [30], but with the main difference that partitioning and
co-simulating (in ADS) the passive structure is avoided, and the optimisation process is
carried out entirely in CST studio. The decision to not partitioning the design is because the
performance and matching of the network are altered when recombining the individually
optimised sub-networks. Every section is therefore optimised individually by acting on its
critical parameters, but the structure is kept in its entirety during these simulations (except
for the DC bias ﬁlter). Finally, since the DC bias ﬁlter does not affect the aforementioned
RF parts, it is independently designed, and subsequently connected to the multiplier
network.
4.1.1
Optimal input and output impedances
The circuit setup of Figure 4.4 is used to determine the optimal input and output embedding
impedances for frequency doubler operation of a single diode. A power of 14.5 dBm at the
input frequency of fin = 23.5 GHz, feeds the varactor model developed in Chapter 3. The
source impedance at the second harmonic, and the load impedance at the input frequency
are set to an open circuit, for the reasons explained in the Theory Section. Keeping in
mind that is important to try exploiting the most extensive voltage range possible, the bias
voltage, as well as the input power, are set to a value that allows the diode to safely operate
between the built-in forward potential and the negative reverse breakdown voltage.
The optimisation algorithm integrated in Keysight ADS, in combination with the harmonic
balance simulator, is utilised to determine the real and imaginary parts of the source and
load impedances that maximise the output power at the second harmonic, those are found
to be: ZI(fin) = 6.5 + j · 82.2 and ZO(2fin) = 7.8 + j · 22.2.
Theoretically, the maximum output power is obtained when simultaneous conjugate
matching conditions are fulﬁlled at the input and output sides of the diode, meaning that
the actual diode impedances at the fundamental and second harmonic are the complex
conjugate of ZI(fin) and ZO(2fin). Therefore, Zd(fin) = 6.5 −j · 82.2 and Zd(2fin) =
7.8 −j · 22.2.

4.1.3
Input section design
The primary purpose of the input network is to allow the maximum transfer of power,
from the WR-42 waveguide to the diodes located in the PCB, at the input frequency (23.5
GHz). For this purpose, the full-height and the reduced-height waveguide sections should
act as a matching network, to transform the wave impedance of the WR-42 waveguide to
an optimal input impedance presented to the diodes. Figure 4.9 offers an internal view of
the split-block, where the rectangular waveguide sections forming the input network are
indicated.
Figure 4.9: Internal view of the of the split block enclosing the PCB of the Q-band doubler, the
dimensions involved in the matching of the input section are indicated.
The frequency doubler is designed with an array of four diodes in anti-series topology.
This choice is made as a compromise between the enhancement of the power handling
capabilities of the multiplier and the actual possibility to ﬁt the array in the reduced-height
waveguide section.
The reason for the reduced-height waveguide is attributed to existence of a number of
propagating modes supported by the input full-height waveguide at the output frequency.
The lowest order of those is the TM11 which can be potentially excited by the quasi-TEM
ﬁeld distribution of the output generated signal [1]. Modifying the height of the WR-42
waveguide to approximately half of the full-height, ensures that the TM11 and the higher
order modes are cut-off, hence, preventing the propagation of the second harmonic power
towards the input port.

As it can be seen, this structure differs from the "classic" microstrip line because the
substrate is enclosed in a rectangular waveguide whose metallic walls function as an
extended ground plane. In a normal microstrip line, the ﬁeld distribution of the dominant
mode can be approximated with good accuracy by the transversal electromagnetic ﬁeld
(TEM), this assumption is also valid in shielded microstrip lines for frequencies below 5
GHz [31]. For higher operating frequencies the values of the longitudinal components of
the ﬁelds start to rise, and hence, cannot be neglected. This fact invalidates the quasi-TEM
assumption, and the structure needs to be analysed by full-wave methods, where the
complete set of Maxwell equations is solved without any simplifying assumption. This is
the reason why the EM simulations in CST studio are the only tool used to analyse and
design these hybrid structures, part of the output matching network.

arrangement, the length of the probe Lout_probe, the width of its stripline Wout_probe, the

4.1.5
DC bias ﬁlter design
The DC bias ﬁlter is designed as a separate part since it should not make any inﬂuence on
the previously discussed RF parts. In fact, the purpose of this section is to deliver the DC
component to the diodes while providing adequate RF isolation.
The DC bias line is connected to the output probe through a thin metallization, and it
is designed as one compact "hammer-head" ﬁlter section [14], where all the dimensions
indicated in Figure 4.17 are optimised for a band-stop response centred at the output
frequency of the doubler. The ﬁlter works as a resonator and the larger pads, with
dimensions Wb1 and Lb1, mainly contribute to the reactance with a capacitive effect. The
thinner (higher impedance) metal strips, with dimensions Wb2 and Lb2, contribute with an
inductive effect. This type of ﬁlter is adopted after some experimentation with a simpler
stepped impedance ﬁlter. The CST electromagnetic simulations have shown that the
hammer-head solution can achieve a better rejection with a shorter circuit length, leading
to a more compact design where fewer losses are introduced. Since signal isolation only in
the vicinity of the 47 GHz frequencies is required, one hammer-head resonator stage is
sufﬁcient as it is possible to see in Figure 4.18 where the S11 and S21 of the designed ﬁlter
are depicted.

4.3.1
Fabrication
The fabrication consists in the creation of the custom rectangular waveguide transmission
lines, the planar circuit hosting the diodes array, and the bias connection. The planar circuit
is made as a PCB on Rogers 5880 substrate with a thickness of 0.508 mm, and manufac-
tured using a photo-lithographic process available at the EMS department (Electromagnetic
Systems group at DTU Elektro).
The metallic custom waveguide sections of the frequency doubler circuit are fabricated as
a split block by the DTU mechanical workshop. For each one of the two aluminum halves,
shown in Figure 4.25, the cavities are excavated by using accurate milling techniques, and
the circular holes are realised for hosting the screws keeping together the the assembled
block. The holes surrounding the the structure are used to connect the output backshort
mechanism and to assemble the input and output ports to eventual rectangular waveguide
ﬂanges.
Four Macom MA46H146 ﬁlp-chip varactors are glued in the designated metallic pads on
the PCB, as illustrated in Figure 4.27. This process involved the use of a special silver
conductive epoxy adhesive. Due to the small size of the chips, it is necessary to perform
this task with the aid of a microscope.
Once the diodes are glued, the PCB is heated at a constant temperature for several hours to
"cure" the silver glue and stabilise the connections.

connected.

provides the (LO) local oscillator signal. The mixer is needed to extend the frequency
range of the spectrum analyser by down-converting the Q-band output signal to a lower
interval of frequencies measurable from the instrument.

4.3.3
Measurements and limits of the measurement setup
The measurement of the Q-band doubler consists in the acquisition of the output power
data through the spectrum analyser while performing a frequency sweep of the input signal
across the K-band frequencies supported by the input WR-42 waveguide.
The DC bias level is set to -18.5 V as the nominal value suggested by the simulations. The
tunable output backshort plate is initially positioned at a distance from the PCB that is the
same as the simulated structure. Due to the lack of a Q-band attenuator, it is not possible
to directly connect the frequency doubler to the Agilent 11970Q mixer. An attenuator
is needed to avoid any potential risk of damaging the mixer by overdriving it with an
excessive power at the RF port. Moreover, if the output power of the doubler under test
exceeds the gain compression value of -7 dBm for the Agilent 11970Q [34], the mixer
operates in non-linear regime. Hence, the measurement is affected. To partially overcome
this problem, an air gap is left between the WR-22 doubler’s output waveguide and the RF
input of the mixer, as it is possible to see in ﬁgure 4. This conﬁguration makes impossible
the estimation of the attenuation factor caused by the air gap and the misalignment of the
==PAGE:76==
58
CHAPTER 4. THE Q-BAND DOUBLER
WR-22 waveguide ports. Hence the output power measurement of the Q-band doubler
can’t be faithfully reconstructed.
The measurement is then performed with the scope of determining the frequencies where
the output power of the doubler peaks. The available input power, at the WR-42 port of
the multiplier, is kept at a safe level of 13 dBm, preserving the mixer from any potential
damage. The input frequency is swept in a range that goes from 18 GHz to 50 GHz, which
is the maximum operating frequency of the 11970Q mixer. The plot of the Q-band doubler
output power versus frequency is depicted in Figure 4.31 which shows two frequencies
where the power undoubtedly peaks: 42.3 GHz and 43.81 GHz. This means that the
operative point of the multiplier is shifted from the design frequency of 46.68 GHz.
Due to the limitations mentioned above, the Q-band doubler cannot be adequately mea-
sured. However, trying to slightly increase the available input power, shows that the output
power grows rather fast at the frequencies of 42.3 GHz and 43.81 GHz.
Various attempts to ﬁx the ADS diode model and adapt it to the measurements are made.
This includes modelling the varactor with the equivalent circuit of Figure 2.4 and tuning its
parameters. Because several difﬁculties to determine a satisfactory model that thoroughly
agrees with the measurements occurred, it was decided to keep the original diode model
and it was chosen to design the W-band doubler to operate with an input frequency of
46.68 GHz (The simulated output frequency of the Q-doubler). In this way, the design of
both doublers is performed under the same simulation conditions with the expectation to
measure a coherently down-shifted operative frequency in the complete W-band multiplier
chain.

CHAPTER 5
The W-Band Doubler
The following chapter presents the design, simulation results, and fabrication of the second
stage of the multiplier chain: the W-band doubler.
Also for this implementation, the MA46H146 varactor is used. In particular, an array of two
diodes in anti-series is soldered on a planar PCB made with Rogers RT 6002 substrate (5
mil thickness). In general, for higher frequencies, a thinner substrate with a low dielectric
constant is desired, since the thickness of the substrate can become comparable to the
wavelength causing the propagation of unwanted modes and ultimately result in losses
[35]. The W-band doubler is essentially a smaller version of the Q-band doubler and it
follows the same design principles. Therefore, the techniques used to design the passive
embedding network are brieﬂy described, and the differences discussed.
The last paragraph is dedicated to the measurements of the complete multiplier chain,
where the W-band doubler is driven by the Q-band doubler.
5.1
Design, and total structure considerations
Before designing the W-band multiplier, it should be considered that it needs to be
connected to the Q-band doubler, and mechanically ﬁt with its output section. For this
reason, the input rectangular waveguide of the W-band doubler has the standard WR-
22 dimensions. Secondly, the split-block should be large enough for drilling the holes
accommodating the screws connecting the two parts.
Figure 5.1, which represents the deﬁnitive model prior to manufacturing, offers a frontal
view of the complete multiplier chain’s 3-D model (made in Solid Works). The two blocks

CHAPTER 6
Review and Future Work
After the measurements of the Q-band doubler and the complete W-band multiplier chain,
some design issues have been identiﬁed. This chapter addresses these problems and
attempts to give some suggestions for their mitigation and possible future work.
Simulations VS measurements discrepancy
As pointed out throughout the report, there is a difference between the measured and
the simulated results, and this primarily concerns the operational point of the multipliers,
intended as the frequency at which the doublers register the maximum output power.
Due to the rather high operative frequencies (Q-band and W-band), the deviations can be
attributed to various factors such as mechanical inaccuracies of the aluminium cavities,
imprecisions caused by the PCBs manual-cutting, and an increase in parasitics caused by
the non-uniform usage of silver epoxy when glueing the diodes.
Moreover, some inaccuracies can reside in the diode model. After the measurements
of the Q-band doubler, various attempts to modify the model’s parameters and adapt its
simulation results to the output power measurements have been made. Considering the
C-V and R-V characteristics of the single diode, depicted in Figure 3.11, it is found that
for frequencies above 20 GHz there is a discrepancy between the measured and the model
curves. In particular, an imprecise estimation of the varactor capacitance might be the
cause of the shift in the operative frequency. Therefore, more time can be spent on studying
and applying the semiconductor theory for further reﬁnement of diode model.
At the same time, it can be useful to investigate the possibility to use the same concept of
structure to design a "quadrupler" and generate the fourth harmonic directly. Even if the
conversion efﬁciency would suffer from the higher order of multiplication, only one stage

avoids the risk of a mismatch between two cascaded structures. In this way, a direct test
bench of the diode model can be established, and its parameters can be directly adjusted
for operation in W-band.
Practical considerations
As shown in Figure 5.13, the enlarged portion of PCB in the vicinity of the diodes region of
the W-band doubler is introduced with the purpose to prevent the fragile Rogers RT-6002
substrate from bending. This precaution should also be included in the Q-band doubler.
Although the Rogers RT-5880 is reasonably thick (20 mils), the process of mounting /
unmounting the split block can cause an alteration of the substrate position and ultimately
interrupt the RF and DC ground connection between the diode pads and the aluminum
structure.
Finally, the possibility of using a single chip integrating a planar array of varactors can
be evaluated. This kind of chips usually incorporates 2, 4 or 6 varactors in anti-series
topology. In this way, a potential higher number of diodes can ﬁt across the reduced height
input waveguide enhancing the power handling capabilities of the multiplier. Moreover,
they can ease the mounting process and possibly contribute to fewer parasitics.
Measurements
In order to measure the input return loss of the multiplier chain, the inclusion of a direc-
tional coupler in front of the input WR-42 waveguide can be considered. The proprieties
of this 4-port device can be exploited in a measurement setup to discriminate between
forward and reverse travelling waves. If the directional coupler is connected among the
input power source and the multiplier chain, a portion of the power from the forward
travelling wave is coupled at the "forward coupling port". In the same way, part of the
reﬂected travelling wave is coupled at the "reverse coupling port". As follows, the powers
at the coupling ports can be read through a power meter, and the input and reﬂected powers
mathematically reconstructed by knowing the parameters of the directional coupler.
As the last consideration regarding the measurements, it would be useful obtaining a signal
generator in Q-band in order to measure the W-band doubler independently, and hence
determine its optimal frequency of operation and the performance in terms of output power
and efﬁciency.

CHAPTER 7
Conclusion
In this project, a W-band microwave signal source has been designed and fabricated. The
device consists of a cascade of two frequency doublers operating in Q-band and W-band
respectively. An ADS model of the commercially available Macom MA46H146 GaAs ﬂip-
chip varactor has been developed and used in the harmonic balance simulations. A set of
preliminary requirements has been examined and coherently resized to an implementable
solution inspired by the successful Porterﬁeld et al. balanced doubler design [9]. A design
methodology, based on co-simulation procedures involving the interactive usage of CST
Studio Suite and Keysight ADS, has been proposed and analysed.
The designed structures have been fabricated as split-blocks embodying the PCB circuit
and the varactors. The aluminium cavities have been machined by the DTU Workshop
[8], while the PCB’s fabricated with the lithographic process available at the EMS (Elec-
tromagnetic Systems) department. The assembly of the structure parts has been entirely
handmade. It has involved cutting the substrate shapes and glueing the varactors with a
speciﬁc conductive epoxy silver paste. When the input power driving the chain has been
set to an estimated level of 28 dBm at 20.756 GHz, the peak of measured output power
was 0 dBm at 83.024 GHz. The causes of the poor conversion efﬁciency and the shift
from the design frequency (93.36 GHz) determined in the simulations can be attributed to
various factors discussed throughout the report. Those include a difference in the operative
frequencies of the two cascaded doublers and possible imperfections in the developed
diode model.
Although a power level of 0 dBm is not functional to NMR (Nuclear Magnetic Resonance)
applications, it can eventually be ampliﬁed or ﬁnd a possible use as a local oscillator in
communications applications.
