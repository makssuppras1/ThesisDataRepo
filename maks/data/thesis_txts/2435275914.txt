Abstract
To drive forward ﬁelds such as magnonics, it is essential to enable calculation and improve
the understanding of magnon dispersion relations and lifetimes. As quasi-particles describing the
collective dynamic change of the magnetization in excited states, ﬁrst principle calculations are
needed in order to appropriately model the magnon’s interaction with single-particle excitation
continua and other quasi-particles.
In this project, static and time-dependent density functional theory is employed to calculate
the dynamic transverse magnetic susceptibility from ﬁrst principles. A ﬂuctuation-dissipation
theorem is shown to connect the magnon spectral function directly to the transverse suscep-
tibility, and a semi-empirical enhancement condition is proposed taking inspiration from the
Stoner theory of itinerant magnetism. A practical computer implementation of the formalism
has been developed and is tested in some detail. The tests show that qualitative agreement
with experiment can be reached at under-converged computational parameters and strategies
are proposed to improve the convergence.
The theoretical formalism and code is applied to calculate the transverse magnetic response
in bulk iron and monolayer MnO2. Detailed features in the spectra are successfully explained
using the proposed semi-empirical enhancement condition, and magnon dispersions and inverse
lifetimes are extracted from the spectra. Ligand bonds consisting partly of transition metal
d-orbitals are shown to be responsible for monolayer MnO2 having a minority Stoner continuum
with signiﬁcant spectral weight. Thanks to these kinds of bonds, it might be possible to ﬁnd
ferromagnetic two-dimensional semi-conducting materials with both majority and minority spin
waves protected from Landau damping.

Introduction
In magnetic materials, spin ﬂuctuations have consequences for a broad range of properties and
phenomena. The dynamic transverse magnetic susceptibility describes how a collinear material
responds to time-dependent perturbations in the transverse magnetic ﬁeld. It is a linear response
function and is directly connected to the transverse magnetic excitations through a ﬂuctuation-
dissipation theorem[1]. Resultantly, properties given from the susceptibility are derived from
the corresponding magnetic excitations, including properties such as the temperature dependent
magnetization, Curie temperature and neutron scattering cross-section[2, 3, 4].
The type of magnetic excitations contributing in the transverse linear response to the ground
state of a collinear system raise or lower the total projected spin of the system Sz
s = Sz
gs ± 1,
keeping the number of particles in the system constant. Although these elementary magnetic
excitations are many-body excitations, describing a collective change of the electronic structure,
they can be associated to single quasi-particles with wave vectors q and energies ω(q). These
quasi-particles are called magnons and can be pictorially understood as propagating waves of
dynamically rotating magnetization around the ground state magnetization. In a localized spin
picture, this corresponds to the local spins rotating collectively with a wavelength given by q,
why the magnons are also called spin waves. In a band picture of non-interacting electronic
states, the excitations describe states where an electron is moved from an occupied state in
one spin channel to an unoccupied state in the opposite channel. These spin-ﬂip excitations
are called Stoner excitations and even though they do not constitute true excitations to the
many-body system, they limit the magnon lifetime by Landau damping[5]. Because the magnon
quasi-particles are not actual particles but changes in the collective electronic structure, their
interaction with each other and with other quasi-particles is non-trivial. As an example, two
quasi-particles can have regions of attraction but also repulsion in the same material, potentially
pairing them into Cooper pairs responsible for high-temperature super-conductance[6].
1
==PAGE:10==
For the reasons described above, it is important to be able to calculate the transverse mag-
netic susceptibility in a theoretical framework within which all types of interactions can be
accounted for. The linear response time-dependent density functional theory provides exactly
such a framework. Although the LR-TDDFT formalism has been well-known for more than
three decades[7], it has not gained widespread use for the purpose of computing the spectrum
of transverse magnetic excitations. This said, it has proved useful for modelling the magnetic
properties of systems from simple bulk transition metals[8, 9, 10, 11] to adatoms and dimers on
surfaces[12, 13].
In the ﬁeld of spintronics, magnons are investigated as a mean for communication and pro-
cessing of data in the next generation of information technology devices[14]. In this regard, it
is essential to obtain long lifetimes of the magnon states, which has motivated magnon research
with LR-TDDFT to look for materials with magnon states protected from Landau attenua-
tion.
This includes research in Heusler alloys, with magnon states protected by the alloys’
half-metallicity[15], but also a range of studies in magnetic thin ﬁlms[9, 10, 16, 17, 18]. As
a highlight it has been shown theoretically, that a free-standing iron monolayer has reduced
Landau damping due to an enhanced exchange splitting and band narrowing resulting in a less
dense low-energy Stoner continuum[9, 10]. When the monolayer is deposited on a support, en-
gineering of hybridization with the substrate is needed to actually realize longer lifetimes from
the dimensionality reduction[16]. With the newly discovery of ferromagnetic two-dimensional
semi-conductors[19], the possibility opens up to see nanostructured magnonic devices with gap-
protected long-living magnons.
The goal of this project has been to adapt the linear response time-dependent density func-
tional theory formalism to enable magnetic excitation research in the GPAW density func-
tional theory code[20]. In Chapter 1, the Stoner and magnon excitations are introduced using
model Hamiltonians, whereas Chapter 2 introduces linear response theory and shows how the
ﬂuctuation-dissipation theorem connects the magnon spectral functions and the dynamic trans-
verse magnetic susceptibility. In Chapter 3, static and time-dependent density functional theory
is introduced and in Chapter 4 it is applied to linear response theory, completing the theoretical
formalism. In Chapters 5 and 6, the computational details of the implementation are discussed,
tested and considerations on future improvements are proposed. Lastly in Chapter 7, the de-
veloped theory and code is used to characterize the transverse magnetic excitations in both a
bulk itinerant ferromagnet, iron, and a ferromagnetically ordered semi-conducting monolayer,
namely monolayer MnO2.

1.3
General properties of magnons
Without an external magnetic ﬁeld, both the Heisenberg and Stoner models predict that the
magnon frequency goes to zero for q →0 (see (1.13), (1.20)). This is not just a feature of the
models but an example of the Goldstone theorem. For systems which are invariant to continuous
spin rotations, a spontaneous break of this symmetry, has an associated gap-less quasi-particle,
the magnon.
This gap-less magnon, sometimes called the Goldstone mode, is easily understood in the
Heisenberg model: the spin wave for q →0 amounts to a rigid rotation of the spins, which
does not cost any energy due to the symmetry. Furthermore, if a constant magnetic ﬁeld is
introduced as in (1.13), the symmetry has been broken and the mode is no longer gap-less.
Additionally it is seen in both (1.13) and (1.20), that the dispersion of the Goldstone mode

4.3
The ALDA kernel
Even though the deﬁnition of the kernel in (4.15) looks innocent, it is not very trivial.
V i
xc(r, t) describes the single-particle eﬀective time-dependent Coulomb interaction for the given
time-dependent external ﬁeld, i.e. it both depends on the system and the external ﬁelds, es-
sentially describing the system response and self-interaction on all time and length scales. The
kernel then describes how this single-particle interaction is aﬀected by a change in the density,
which thanks to the Runge-Gross theorem corresponds to a change in system/external ﬁelds[36].
In the adiabatic local density approximation, one uses the kernel describing the change in
the system response and self-interaction of a homogeneous electron gas with an adiabatically
varying density and magnetization to a small change in the latter. One would assume this to
be a good approximation for materials where the electrons interacting with the external ﬁeld
in question see a homogeneous electronic environment on their given length scale of Coulomb
interaction. Additionally, the ﬁelds would have to vary on much longer time and length scales
compared to the eﬀective Coulomb interaction. If the static ground state of the material in
question is known to be well represented within LDA and the material only has short-ranged
magnetic interactions, the ALDA should be applicable.

Computational details
Having established how one can use time-dependent density functional theory to calculate
the transverse linear response function, this chapter will present how the formalism is applied
in practise using the GPAW code.
In this chapter a slightly diﬀerent notation is used compared to what is presented above.
Spin indexing is neglected for the most part and the Kohn-Sham orbitals are denoted as ψn with
n being a general state index, later speciﬁed to a band index and a k-point. As non-interacting,
the Kohn-Sham orbitals are single-particle wave functions, which is the terminology used here.
5.1
The static ground state problem
The ﬁrst step in the response calculation is to solve the Kohn-Sham equations (3.8) to
ﬁnd the Kohn-Sham orbitals, eigenvalues and occupation numbers.
The Atomic Simulation
Environment[41] is used to set up the structure in question, determine the k-point sampling for
the ground state calculation and eventually the q-vectors to calculate in the response calculation.
The k-points are sampled on a Monkhorst-Pack grid[42].
The GPAW density functional theory code[20] is used to solve the Kohn-Sham equations.
First, the ground state density and magnetization are found self-consistently, secondly the Kohn-
Sham Hamiltonian HKS = ˆT + ˆVs is fully diagonalized.
To compute the full Kohn-Sham response function in (4.9), one would need an inﬁnite number
of bands, but in practise a choice for the number of bands to include has to be made, eﬀectively
truncating the Hilbert space within which the problem is solved. The sensitivity to this choice

Whenever the ”number of bands” in the calculation is documented, it is

The second important choice is how to represent the Kohn-Sham wave functions. To that

Chapter 6
Applying the formalism in practise:
convergence issues and solutions
Finally, the linear response time-dependent density functional theory formalism presented in
Chapters 2, 3 and 4 can be applied to real materials using the implementation in the GPAW code
presented in Chapter 5. In this chapter, the sensitivity of the procedure to the ﬁnite number of
bands included in the calculation and the plane wave and local ﬁeld cutoﬀis assessed. For this
purpose bulk iron (bcc) is used as a test bed. Additionally, some strategies from the literature
are presented in order to discuss how the convergence issues can be overcome in future work.
At the end of the chapter, comments are made on issues associated with using the formalism
for calculation on two dimensional materials.

6.3
Convergence in plane wave and local ﬁeld cutoﬀ
Within the implemented code there can be several strategies to choosing the plane wave and
local ﬁeld cutoﬀs. The most consistent choice would be to choose a plane wave cutoﬀfor the
Kohn-Sham orbitals, which ﬁxes both the coarse and ﬁne grids, use the interpolation scheme to
get the pair densities on the ﬁne grid and the pair density matrices with all possible local ﬁelds.
Doing this with the current interpolation scheme was found to improve the gap error for low
plane wave cutoﬀs, but increase it for higher cutoﬀs. It is possible that a diﬀerent interpolation
scheme interpolating the pair densities directly, as is done for the regular density, would improve
on this, but in general it is not a desirable direction to go, due to the strong requirements on
computer memory for the calculation. Two matrices of the size of χ+−,[G](q, ω) need to be stored
in the memory when performing the inversion in (4.19). Resultantly, if reciprocal lattice vectors
double the length compared to plane wave cutoﬀneed to be included, the memory requirements
increase approximately by a factor of 2 · 2d where d is the dimension of the system. This would
severely limit the number of systems to which the method is applicable.
For the tests included here, no interpolation scheme is used for the pair densities. The local
ﬁeld cutoﬀis set to be equal to the plane wave cutoﬀfrom the ground state calculation, including
as many local ﬁelds as possible without interpolation.

6.5
On choosing the smearing parameter
Having established how the plane wave coeﬃcients and number of bands are chosen, some
comments on choosing the smearing parameter η are in order. First it should be noted, that
the smearing parameter η should not be chosen much smaller than the frequency spacing in
order to make all present features in the spectral function visible. In practise, this is not the
major limitation. That however, is the resolution in the k-point sampling of the Brillouin zone.
Because this sampling is ﬁnite, only a ﬁnite number of Stoner excitations are sampled and η has
to be chosen so that they are smeared out into a continuum.
In Figure 6.9 an example of two diﬀerent choices for η in bulk iron are shown using a
48 × 48 × 48 k-point grid and a frequency spacing of 5 meV. Using η = 5 meV, the sampled
Stoner state around 380 meV gives rise to a small dip in the inverse enhancement function,
κM(q, ω), just as the collective trend is about to reach full enhancement at κM = 0. As a result,
two peaks are found in the enhanced spectral function, where only the second correspond to a
collective excitation. By choosing η = 20 meV the Stoner excitation is smeared out suﬃciently
to give only one peak in the enhanced spectrum.
The Stoner continuum still broadens the
magnon peak far beyond 20 meV indicating that the Landau attenuation via the Stoner states
shortens the magnon lifetime signiﬁcantly!

6.6
Next steps in improvement of the implementation
For the methodology to be widely applicable, improvements to the implementation are
needed. The ﬁrst step would be to check for the possible improvement in cutoﬀconvergence
using a constant number of plane wave coeﬃcients for each k-point in the ground state calcu-
lation as well as for each q-vector in the response calculation. Also, a better understanding of
the radial kernel’s sensitivity to the number of plane waves is needed. If this sensitivity can be
overcome, converged results should be achievable at much lower plane wave cutoﬀs, which is the
primary motivation for using the PAW formalism at all.

7.2.1
Magnon dispersion
Lastly, the magnon dispersion was calculated using a 24 × 24 × 1 k-point grid, a frequency
spacing of 0.5 meV and a smearing of η = 1 meV. In Figure 7.7 the magnon energies extracted
as the maximum in the spectral function for a given q-vector are presented. Immediately, a

ferromagnetic state not being stable - the used Kohn-Sham ground state is not the true ground
state of the system. When the unit cell is expanded to include four formula units of MnO2,
this is conﬁrmed - the ground state in the super cell is antiferromagnetic[51]. Some clariﬁcation
is in order: Haastrup et al.[49] found the ground state to be ferromagnetic, also using a super
cell, but using the PBE exchange correlation functional.
In order for linear response time-
dependent density functional theory formalism to work, the Kohn-Sham starting point needs to
be calculated using the static limit of the full time-dependent exchange-correlation functional,
otherwise the response to a state diﬀerent than the ground state is calculated. With the adiabatic
local density approximation, studies are limited to systems where the LDA predicts the right
ground state!

Conclusion
In this project, linear response theory has been presented as a mean to calculate the trans-
verse magnetic susceptibility χ+−and through it get access to elementary magnetic excitations.
The spectral functions for Sz
s = Sz
gs ± 1 excitations, S±, have been shown to fulﬁl a ﬂuctuation-
dissapation theorem connecting them directly to the susceptibility: −Im {χ+−} = π(S−−S+).
As a result, the energies and lifetimes of the magnon quasi-particles associated to the excitations
can be extracted simply by identifying lineshapes in the spectral function S+−= S−−S+.
Time-dependent density functional theory has been presented as an exact formalism within
which the linear response can be calculated from ﬁrst principles. Employing a two-step process,
ﬁrst the Kohn-Sham response function χ+−
KS is calculated, from which χ+−can be determined
through a Dyson-like equation. χ+−
KS is given from the spectral function for single-particle spin-
ﬂip excitations, the Stoner excitations. A computer implementation of the formalism has been
developed to the GPAW code. The code has been benchmarked, revealing it essential to have
tight control of the plane wave coeﬃcients expanding wave and response functions. Future steps
towards an accurate and eﬃcient code have been proposed, including the use of sum rules to
break the inherently slow convergence in number of unoccupied bands.
Finally, the adiabatic local density approximation was used to calculate the transverse re-
sponse in bulk iron and monolayer MnO2. The magnetic excitation spectrum of iron has been
compared to experiment and other theoretical studies conﬁrming good qualitative agreement
despite using unconverged computational parameters. A semi-empirical inverse enhancement
function was introduced and eﬀectively used to explain a range of features in the spectral func-
tions, drawing clear parallels to the simpler Stoner theory of itinerant magnetism. Lastly, the
MnO2 calculations demonstrated that d-electrons taking part in ligand bonds can result in mi-
nority Stoner excitations and possibly minority spin waves showing up in the spectrum. The
MnO2 calculations also conﬁrmed that instabilities in the alleged system ground state are re-
vealed in the linear response, constituting both a strength and a weakness for the formalism.
