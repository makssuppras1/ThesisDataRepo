Abstract
Sterols are organic molecules present in plants, animals, and ﬁlamentous fungi, playing
an essential role for the membrane structure of cells. Sterols are synthesized through a
biosynthetic pathway known as the mevalonate pathway. Statins are fungal secondary
metabolites and inhibitors of the rate-limiting enzyme 3-hydroxy-3-methyl-glutaryl-co-
enzyme A reductase (HMGR) in the mevalonate pathway. They are widely used in the
medical industry for treatment of cardiovascular diseases (CVDs).
Aspergillus terreus is a natural producer of one type of statin, named lovastatin. Re-
cent studies revealed that this fungus carries four HMGR-like genes in the genome.
Two of these genes encodes for proteins with a transmembrane domain (ATET09965 &
ATET09520) while the two other encodes for soluble proteins (ATET03447 & ATET02145).
A combination of the presence of a lovastatin gene cluster and four HMGRs indicate that
A. terreus holds self-resistance mechanisms in the genome.
The aim of this project was to investigate how the four HMGRs are linked to self-
resistance in A. terreus. Determination of the intracellular localization of the four HM-
GRs was one approach to characterize them. This was achieved by tagging the genes
with ﬂuorescence proteins. Suggestions on the localization of three HMGRs were made
in these studies, based on the distribution of emitted light observed in the ﬂuorescence
microscope. ATET09520 is believed to be located in the endoplasmic reticulum, whereas
ATET09965 and ATET02145 is suggested to be present in the cytoplasm of the cell. It
was not possible to determine the localization of ATET03347 due to low expression of
emitted light. Construction of deletion vectors were made in order to elucidate the in-
dividual function of the HMGRs in regards to self-resistance against naturally produced
lovastatin. The vectors were transformed into A. terreus by homologous recombination.
Growth were observed on three deletion transformants, but none of them were fully ver-
iﬁed. When deletion strains are veriﬁed, they will be well suited for future investigation
of the individual lovastatin insensitivity.
Furthermore, it was proved that A. terreus
synthesize both the active and inactive form of lovastatin naturally on CYA and YES
media, but not on MM media. These ﬁndings can be useful for designing a future statin
sensitivity assay. Construction of pET expression vectors with integrated HMGR-like
genes of A. terreus were cloned into E.coli BL21 (DE3) for eﬃcient protein expression.
A histidine tagged based puriﬁcation was subsequently made to detect the enzymatic
reaction response upon addition of statins. However, the concentration of expressed and
puriﬁed proteins was insuﬃcient for an enzymatic assay to run. These studies proved
that optimization of the expression and puriﬁcation protocol must be made in the future.

Resumé
Steroler er organiske molekyler, som er essentielle for strukturen af cellemembraner i
planter, dyr og svampe. Steroler bliver syntetiseret igennem en syntesevej kaldet meval-
onatvejen og er reguleret af enzymet 3-hydroxy-3-methyl-glutaryl-coenzym A reductase
(HMGR). Statiner er sekundære metabolitter produceret i svampe, som reducerer dan-
nelsen af mevalonat og derved steroler. Reduceringen sker helt konkret ved, at statiner
inhiberer HMGR. Statiner bliver ﬂittigt brugt i medicinindustrien til at sænke kolesterol-
niveauet i blodet hos patienter med forhøjet kolesteroltal, og derved forhindre udvikling
af hjertekarsygdomme hos genetisk eksponerede personer.
Lovastatin er én type statiner, som bliver naturligt produceret i svampen Aspergillus
terreus. Nyere studier har vist at A. terreus desuden har ﬁre HMGR-lignende gener in-
tegreret i genomet. To af disse har vist sig at kode for proteiner med et transmembran
domæne (ATET09965 & ATET09520) hvor de to andre koder for opløselige proteiner
(ATET03447 & ATET02145). Tilstedeværelsen af både et lovastatin genkluster samt de
ﬁre HMGR-lignende gener tyder på at A. terreus besidder resistensmekanismer.
Det udførte studie havde til formål at undersøge forbindelsen mellem de ﬁre HMGR-
lignende gener og resistensmekanismer i A. terreus. Dette inkluderede undersøgelse af den
intracellulære lokalisering af de ﬁre omtalte HMGR gener i A. terreus, baseret på inkor-
porering af ﬂuorescerende proteiner. Studiet indikerede at den intracellulære lokalisering
af ATET09520 er i det endoplasmatiske retikulum, hvorimod ATET09965 og ATET02145
tyder på at være til stede i cytoplasmaet i cellen. Det var ikke muligt at identiﬁcere
lokalisationen af ATET03447, da denne udtrykte et for svagt signal i mikroskopet. Dele-
tionsvektorer blev også konstrueret, for at vurdere det enkelte gens funktion ift. resistens
overfor naturligt produceret lovastatin. Deletionsvektorer blev transformeret ind i A. ter-
reus vha. homolog rekombinering og vækst blev observeret for tre af deletionsstammerne.
Desværre er ingen af mutantstammerne veriﬁcerede endnu. For fremtidige studier vil disse
deletionsstammer, såfremt de er korrekt kontrueret, være en eﬀektiv måde at analysere
lovastatin sensitivitet på. Det var desuden påvist at A. terreus syntetiserer lovastatin
naturligt på CYA og YES medie, men ikke på MM medie. Denne opdagelse kan også
blive et vigtigt værktøj til at analysere lovastatin sensitiviteten i fremtidige studier. Til
sidst blev der konstrueret et ekspressionssystem for HMGR proteinerne, som blev klonet
ind i E.coli BL21 (DE3). En histidin-baseret oprensning af proteinerne blev efterfølgende
gennemført, for at kunne køre enzymatisk analyse, med det formål at måle resistensfak-
toren for de enkelte HMGR. Studiet demonstrerede at en optimering af protokoller for
hhv. ekspression og oprensning er nødvendig for at gennemføre den enzymatiske analyse.

Introduction
The leading cause of death globally is according to the World Health Organization (WHO)
caused by cardiovascular heart diseases (CVDs). CVDs are a group of disorders involving
heart and blood vessels. Common for most CVDs is that a reduction or total blockage
of blood ﬂow occurs due to formation of plaque on the inner walls of the arteries. The
blockage is usually caused by consistent high levels of cholesterol in the bloodstream,
which over time can limit the oxygen being carried to the organs and can consequently
lead to heart diseases, heart failure, and stroke [1].
In 2015 CVDs represented 31% causes of all global deaths. This equals to 17.7 million
people who died this year, exclusively due to CVDs [1]. Death can in most cases be
prevented by addressing the risk factors in time and by early detection and management
of other established diseases. The leading strategy for prevention and treatment of CVDs
is based on lowering the plasma cholesterol in patients. This is done by a combination
of change in lifestyle and statin therapy. Statins belong to a group of pharmaceuticals
that can interfere with the natural biosynthetic pathway of cholesterol, by inhibiting the
enzyme 3-hydroxy-3-methylglutaryl coenzyme A reductase (HMGR), which is the rate-
limiting step in the synthesis of cholesterol. Statin therapy is very successful and has
been used for more than 30 years in the medical industry. Competition in developing
new generic versions of this pharmaceutical is very high due to a continuously high de-
mand from consumers.
The majority of statins are synthesized naturally by ﬁlamentous fungi. Lovastatin is one
type of statin, naturally produced by Aspergillus terreus. It is believed that this fungus
produce lovastatin to confer competitive advantages against other fungi and organisms in
nature. A. terreus carries four copies of HMGR-like genes in the genome, which indicate
that this fungus possess some self-resistance mechanisms against lovastatin. Identifying
the individual characteristics of the HMGRs in A. terreus can potentially increase knowl-
edge about these resistance mechanisms. This is important for optimizing heterologous
expression of statins and for construction of the most cost-eﬀective production organism
in the future.

Aim
The overall aim of this project is to characterize the four HMGR-like genes in A. terreus
and investigate how they are linked to self-resistance mechanism against lovastatin.
The approach includes deletion of each HMGR in the genome of A. terreus, to investi-

2.1
Filamentous fungi
Filamentous fungi are one of the three major kingdoms of eukaryotes and are fundamental
for life on Earth. The study of fungi is termed mycology and covers the understanding of
fungi and the biological systems to which they contribute [2]. Mycologists have developed
a fungal taxonomy to help characterizing fungi. This kingdom can be divided down to
phylum, subphylum, class, order, family, genus and lastly into species.
Fungi are essential in nature for break down of many complex structures of organic mat-
ters and for decomposition. All fungi are heterotrophic like animals and require energy
from external sources [2]. In contrast to animals, most fungi contain ergosterol in the cell
membrane instead of cholesterol and in contrast to plants, fungi contain chitin in the cell
wall instead of cellulose. They are fundamental as symbionts for plants and furthermore
plays an essential role for the present and future food and medical industry [2].
2.1.1

About 100.000 species in the fungal kingdom has been described so far. The majority
belongs to the phylums of Ascomycota and Basidiomycota [2]. The Aspergillus genus
is a major group, with more than 340 species oﬃcially recognized, belonging to the As-
comycota phylum [3]. This genus is a group of conidial fungi considered as versatile and
diverse with both beneﬁcial and pathogenic features [4].
The Aspergillus genus is considered to be one of the most important genus regarding
the fermentation industry [5]. A. niger and A. oryzae are among the most industrially
important species, responsible for the majority of food fermentation and large-scale pro-
duction of enzymes, organic acids, and other bioactive compounds [3].

2.1.2
Fungal metabolism
The great diversity of fungi is reﬂected by the metabolism, which often consist of both
a primary and secondary metabolism. In general, metabolism describes the chemical
reactions that occur inside cells to maintain a living state. The primary metabolism, or
central metabolism, is fundamental for the cell to live and maintain life. The essential
function of the primary metabolism is to convert nutrients into energy (ATP). Energy is
crucial for development, growth and reproduction of cells, hence the primary metabolism
is present in most organisms [2].
The secondary metabolism exist in addition to the primary metabolism.
These two
metabolisms are coextensive meaning they can occur simultaneously in the same cell.
This is important since the secondary metabolism utilize enzymes, derived from the pri-
mary metabolism, to proceed [2]. The secondary metabolism is a common feature in the
majority of fungi and is also found in some bacteria and plants. This metabolism is not
essential for the fundamental functions of a living cell, but rather appear to be impor-
tant for the cell to confer competitive advantages. Some of the advantages featured by
the secondary metabolism includes intracellular communication, defense and competition
against other organisms [2]. The major groups of secondary metabolites in fungi includes
polyketides (PKs), ribosomal and non-ribosomomal peptides (NRPs) and terpenoids [10].
The group of PKs is relevant for this project and some of the basic concepts will be de-
scribed in the following section.

2.1.3
Polyketide synthesis
Polyketides belong to a large class of secondary metabolites, which are synthesized
through polyketide synthases (PKSs) consisting of multimodular enzymes [11]. Polyke-
tides are an important therapeutic in the pharmaceutical industry and has highly poten-
tial biological eﬀects. Examples of polyketides includes antibiotics, immunosuppressants
and cholesterol-lowering agents, whereas the last mentioned is relevant for this particular
project [12].
PKSs can be divided into three major groups termed PKS type I, II and III. Common for
all three groups is, that they confer the same enzymatic function. However, they appear
in diﬀerent quaternary structures and chain initiation and termination [11]. Furthermore,
these three diﬀerent types of PKSs are subdivided into iterative or non-iterative groups,
depending on their ability to catalyze more than one round of elongation [12]. Fungal
polyketides are usually synthesized by iterative type I PKSs, which holds catalytic do-
mains within large multifunctional enzymes that elongate the polyketide chain iteratively
[12]. Accordingly, only this type of PKS will be elaborated.
The three main domains needed for a catalytic cycle to run in PKSs consist of a β-
ketosynthase (KS), an acyl-transferase (AT) and an acyl carrier protein (ACP) domain.
The KS domain is responsible for catalyzing a condensation reaction by attaching an
extender unit (malonyl-CoA) to a starter molecule (acetyl-CoA), thereby catalyzing for-
mation of C-C bonds. The AT domain is responsible for loading the substrate to the
enzyme and the ACP domain facilitates the movement of substrates and products on
the active sites of the enzyme. Each ﬁnished catalytic cycle will lead to an elongation
of one ketide unit on the polyketide chain [11]. At the end, when a deﬁned chain length
is obtained, the polyketide chain is released from the enzyme complex by a thioesterase
(TE) domain [12]. Figure 2 present the iterative type I PKSs including the main do-
mains explained above. Step 1 in the ﬁgure illustrates the loading of the starter unit
(acetyl-CoA) followed by loading of an extender unit (malonyl-CoA) in step 2. Step 3
illustrates a condensation step catalyzed by the KS domain followed by step 4, which
present the transfer of the chain from the ACP domain to the KS domain in order to
prepare for a new cycle to run. Step 2,3 and 4 makes up the iterative part of the system.
The termination and release of the PKs is entered at step 5 after n times of cycles.
The elongation step can be followed by a reduction step in some cases mediated by β-
ketoreductase (KR), dehydratase (DH) and trans-acting enoyl reductase (ER) domains
[12]. The KR domain will reduce the carbonyl group, the DH domain will dehydrate the
hydroxyl group and last the ER domain will reduce the enoyl group in this reduction
step. Fungal PKSs can be classiﬁed as nonreducing (NR), partially reducing (PR), or
highly reducing (HR) PKSs depending on the presence or absence of a reduction step [12].

2.1.4
Self-resistance mechanisms towards secondary metabolites
Secondary metabolites confer advantageous features to the organism that synthesize and
secrete them. However, it is not always without challenges that an organism produces
these metabolites.
Secondary metabolites, such as antibiotics and mycotoxins, can just as well be toxic for
the organisms that synthesize them, as for other organisms. The ability to avoid self-
intoxication is therefore essential for the organism to survive the production and secretion
of some secondary metabolites. The mechanism by which the organism is resistant to
its own metabolites is called a self-resistance mechanism [13]. Seven of the known self-
resistance mechanisms utilized by both plants, bacteria and fungi are summarized in
ﬁgure 3. The ﬁrst mechanism illustrated (ﬁgure 3, (1)), is secretion of the secondary
metabolites out of the cell by a transporter system. This could for instance be a dedi-
cated transporter able to move ions and complex metabolites from one side of a biological
membrane to the other. This kind of transporter system is often encoded in the gene
cluster. Another mechanism is based on inactivating the metabolite from the inside of
the cell. This can be done by modiﬁcation of the metabolite inside the organism through
interaction with an enzyme, which can either completely inactivate the metabolite or at
7
==PAGE:20==
least make it less eﬀective (ﬁgure 3, (2)). Another way of making the metabolite inactive
is by capturing it inside compartments intracellular, thus preventing it from interacting
with the target protein. This mechanism is called metabolite sequestration (ﬁgure 3, (3)).
Furthermore, a negative feedback inhibition mechanism of the biosynthetic pathway of
the metabolite, is another way to retain a low concentration (ﬁgure 3, (4)).
Lastly, modiﬁcation of the target protein can prevent interaction between metabolite and
target protein. Post-translational modiﬁcations of the target protein or synthesis of new
versions of the target protein, which is insensitive, can prevent this interaction (ﬁgure 3,
(5 & 6)). Also an overexpression of the target protein is a way to reduce the concentration
ratio (ﬁgure 3, (7)) [13]. Common for most of the self-resistance mechanisms is, that they
either inactivate or reduce the concentration of the problematic metabolite at the site
where it can cause damage.
Self-resistance mechanisms are an essential part of A. terreus in regards to the biosyn-
thesis and maintenance of sterols, which will be elaborated in the following section.

The mevalonate pathway, shown in ﬁgure 4, is an essential biosynthetic pathway present

The carbon source of the pathway is acetyl-CoA derived from glycolysis. The ﬁrst step

The S and R nomenclature refers to the stereo-chemistry of the substrates [15].

The
human HMGR can furthermore be regulated by phosphorylation of the enzyme, which
will decrease the activity.
Known and studied HMGRs have been divided into two distinct classes. Class I containing
all eukaryotic and most archaeal HMGRs and class II consisting of prokaryotic HMGRs.
The structure of HMGRs can consist of both a transmembrane part and a catalytic part
or exclusively of a catalytic part. The transmembrane part carries a N-terminal sterol
sensing domain responsible for binding sterol groups and regulates degradation of the
HMGRs [18]. The catalytic part carries a C-terminal catalytic domain consisting of the
HMGR domain. The catalytic part is the essential domain responsible for enzymatic
activity to be present [18].
2.1.6
HMGRs in A. terreus
A phylogenetic analysis made on HMGRs in fungi was carried out by Ana Ley in 2015 [13]
to gain insight into some of the biological foundations of statin resistance. The analysis
revealed that a total of 583 HMGR sequences, originating from 447 diﬀerent genomes were
found in Joint Genome Institute (JGI) databases. The phylogenetic analysis grouped the
genes into ﬁve clades. The grouping did for the majority of the genes follow the taxonomy
at phylum level. However, ascomycetes were grouped into two diﬀerent clades, group A
and group B. The two clades showed signiﬁcant diﬀerences. Group A was characterized
by carrying a HPIH membrane spanning domain, a sterol-sensing domain and a HMGR
domain. The function of the HPIH domain is yet unknown. Group B was characterized
by only carrying one speciﬁc domain, the HMGR domain. The structure of the domain
in group A suggested that these HMGRs are part of the primary metabolism and directly
involved in the conversion of HMG-CoA to mevalonate [13].

2.1.7
Statins
Lipids are a class of natural occurring molecules and a key component in living cells,
which regulate many biological functions by inﬂuencing cell processes. Besides serving as
the main energy reservoir in many cells, lipids also inﬂuence signaling pathways, DNA
replication, protein translocation and is the main structural component of cell membranes
[19][20]. There are diﬀerent classiﬁcations of lipids based on their function. Sterols are
one of them and is the important type of lipids in regard to this project.
Cholesterol, which is the main type of sterol in vertebrates, is an essential part of a
well-functioning cell in the human body and contributes to a range of diﬀerent functions.
Besides being the major component of the cell membrane it also contribute to the syn-
thesis of steroid hormones and bile acids in the human body [21]. Cholesterol can either
be derived through diet or can be synthesized naturally by the mevalonate pathway [22].
The majority of cholesterol contained in the human body is synthesized naturally by
organs [21]. Cholesterol is vital for the human body to exist, but too much cholesterol
can also be life-threatening. Consistent high levels of cholesterol in the bloodstream,
also termed hypercholesterolemia, can lead to cardiovascular diseases (CVDs) [23] [24].
Especially coronary heart disease (CHD) that often leads to vascular damage, such as
atherosclerosis, is at high risk. Atherosclerosis causes accumulation and formation of
plaques in the inner walls of the arteries. The plague will eventually block the arter-
ies and limit the blood ﬂow, which will lead to less oxygen being carried to the organs.
Complication and other diseases can occur as a response to this plaque formation. In
worst case scenario, a person having atherosclerosis could have a heart-attack, stroke or
eventually die [25] [26]. To overcome these severe side-eﬀects, a search for an inhibitor
to the key enzyme in the mevalonate pathway (HMGR) was initiated in 1971. Prior to
this idea many other unsuccessful attempts to ﬁnd cholesterol-lowering agents had been
made. By knowing the metabolism of bacteria and fungi, scientists from Japan, Akira
Endo and his colleagues, assumed that a candidate producing a secondary metabolite to
compete with other microbes that required sterols, had to be among somewhere. In two
years approximately 6000 microorganisms were tested and screened for their ability to in-
hibit the cholesterol biosynthesis. The discovery of mevastatin, also known as compactin,
isolated from Penicillium citrinum was the result of these two years of searching, which
would turn out to be a breakthrough in the pharmaceutical industry [27].
Mevastatin and HMG-CoA has structural similarities in the active binding site and
HMGR can bind to either of the two molecules.
See ﬁgure 6 for illustration of the
chemical structure of HMG-CoA and mevastatin. When HMGR binds to mevastatin,
the enzyme is blocked and cannot interact in the cholesterol biosynthesis. Mevastatin
showed to have an aﬃnity 10.000 times greater for binding HMGR than HMG-CoA and

The biosynthesis of lovastatin
Lovastatin is produced as a secondary metabolite by A.terreus to confer competitive ad-
vantages in nature. More speciﬁcally, lovastatin is a polyketide synthesized through a
polyketide pathway derived from acetate [28]. The gene cluster of lovastatin consist of
18 genes, which is believed to be involved in the biosynthesis in one way or another. Not
all genes are yet identiﬁed. Figure 8 below illustrates the biosynthetic gene cluster of
lovastatin, presenting only the known and studied genes.

Five of these 18 genes, namely, lovA, lovB, lovC, lovD, and lovF has been proved to

PKSs and are recognized as megasynthases, namely the lovastatin nonaketide synthase
lovB and the lovastatin diketide synthase lovF. These two megasynthases are responsible
for synthesizing the backbone of lovastatin [29]. LovB carry the domains that characterize
a PKS including the AT, KS and ACP needed for the catalytic cycle to run and the KR,
DH and ER domains used in the reduction step. However, the ER domain lacks activity
and do not work suﬃciently. LovC in the gene cluster of lovastatin encodes an active
ER and thereby supplement the lack of activity in the lovB gene [30]. LovF is very
similar to lovB, but has an active ER domain. The role of lovF in the gene cluster is
to synthesize a side chain of lovastatin. LovA encodes a cytochrome P450 oxygenase,
responsible for hydrolyzing and connecting polyketide intermediates in the biosynthesis

of lovastatin [31]. The diketide modiﬁed by lovA is covalently attached to the side chain
synthesized by lovF by the gene lovD, which encodes a trans esterase, responsible for

Another gene present in the gene cluster of lovastatin is lovI, which encodes for an eﬄux
pump responsible for secretion of lovastatin out of the cell. This eﬄux pump thereby

Lastly, one remaining gene in the gene cluster has been suggested to play an important
part for A. terreus. Previous studies made by Ana Ley 2015 [13], proved that two genes
from the biosynthetic gene cluster of mevastatin, mlcE and mlcD, were part of the self-
resistance mechanism in P. citrinum. The study proved that it was possible to confer
statin resistance in S. cerevisiae by expressing these two genes heterologously. Later,
a sequence comparison of mlcD and lvrA revealed homologous regions in the catalytic
domains. The two genes showed 40.1% identity suggesting that lvrA plays an essential
role in self-resistance in A. terreus. These ﬁndings are very interesting for this project
and for future investigations of self-resistance mechanisms in A. terreus.

Genetic engineering
Engineering of a genome refers to a process with the aim of making exact targeted modiﬁ-

2.2.1
Damage and repair mechanisms
All genetic information in cells are stored in DNA, which makes it an essential part of
life [33]. The majority of alterations in the chemical structure of DNA can be considered
as damage. Such alteration can lead to interruption of the genetic information in cells,
which can be severe if the damage is not repaired.
DNA damage in cells is a very common thing, which most often does not compose any
danger since the majority of well-functioning cells has the ability to repair it naturally.
DNA damage in cells can cause diﬀerent kind of mutations. Both silent mutations, which
will not cause any harm to the organism, but also mutations causing a loss of function,
which can be lethal if they are not repaired. DNA damage can occur either naturally or
15
==PAGE:28==
it can be caused by exogenous sources such as UV radiation or chemicals for instance [33]
[2]. Several kinds of DNA damage exist in cells, whereas double-stranded breaks (DSBs)
is one of the severe forms. There are two main pathways for DNA repair to cope with
DSBs in eukaryotes, namely homologous recombination (HR) and non-homologous end
joining (NHEJ). HR is the dominant repair mechanism in most unicellular eukaryotes
whereas NHEJ is dominant in most multicellular eukaryotes [34].
As the name reveal, HR is based on replacement of a homologous double stranded DNA.
HR can be used as repair mechanism if an exogenous DNA template is available, carrying
short regions of homology to the sequence surrounding the DSB [35][34]. See ﬁgure 9 (c)
and (d). HR has high frequency for gene-targeting since it is very eﬃcient in making
accurate insertions and repair. HR is also eﬃcient since this repair mechanism ensure
that the gene will function in the same way as before the DSB occurred.
NHEJ on the other hand, has a very low frequency for gene-targeting. The NHEJ pathway
consist of three major steps.
Recognition, assembling and ligation of the DSB. The
ligation occurs independently of DNA homology [35]. This kind of repair mechanism can
lead to a gene deletion or insertion. Neither the gene deletion or insertion will necessarily
leave the gene stable and functional as it was before the DSB occurred. See ﬁgure 9 (a)
and (b). NHEJ is the dominant pathway for DNA integration in multicellular eukaryotes
including ﬁlamentous fungi [36].

Vector construction by USER cloning
Cloning techniques are used in the biotechnological area to create new recombinant DNA
structures, thus creating new genetic compositions. Cloning is a very useful technique
used to obtain a desired expression from a chosen vector. Both construction of tagging
and deletion vectors has been a major part of this project and Uracil-Speciﬁc Excision
Reagent (USER) cloning has been the chosen approach to achieve this. USER cloning is
a technique used to insert one or several DNA fragments obtained by polymerase chain
reaction (PCR) into a vector backbone, by enabling complementary single-stranded over-
hangs [39]. An illustration of the basic concepts of USER cloning is presented in ﬁgure 11.
17
==PAGE:30==
Figure 11: Basic concepts of USER cloning
The picture demonstrates a simpliﬁed version of the mechanism in USER cloning. The vector backbone to the left
carrying a USER cassette, which is digested with PacI and Nt.BbvCI. Fragments of interest are ampliﬁed from a
template, shown to the right, through PCR. Fusion and insertion of the fragments are obtained through a USER cloning

Important for a vector to be USER compatible is to hold a USER cassette, which is
the cloning site responsible for facilitating insertion of DNA fragments [40].
Primers
are the essential part of a USER cloning and consist of two individual parts. The ﬁrst
part is the regular primer part responsible for amplifying the target gene. The second
part is a USER overhang consisting of about 8-12 nucleotides (nt) complementary to the
insertion sites of the vector backbone. Special for the USER overhang is, that it contains
a substitution of a deoxy-thymidine (T) with a deoxy-uridine (U) in the 5’ terminal of the
primer [39]. This substitution will generate a single stranded overhang at the 3’ terminal
of the DNA fragments when treated with a deoxyuridine-excicion reagent, also referred
to as USER enzyme [39]. This USER enzyme is a mix of uracil DNA glycosidase (UNG)
and DNA glycosylase-lyase endo VIII, which binds to the uracil base of the primers and
subsequently release the sequence upstream, thereby initiating a single strand break [41].
The vector backbone also has to be prepared, prior to the actual USER cloning step, by
opening the cloning site and leaving sticky ends. The vector backbone used throughout
this project contain a PacI/Nt.BbvCI USER cassette. This cassette is opened by digestion
using the restriction enzyme PacI and nicking enzyme Nt.BbvCI, leaving single stranded
overhangs. After digestion of the vector backbone, and preparation of the DNA fragments,
18
==PAGE:31==
the fusion and insertion of the fragments can be obtained in a single USER cloning step
mediated by E. coli due to the complementarity of the single stranded overhangs [40].
After transformation in E. coli the desired vector construct can be selected for by the
respective selective markers present in the vector backbone.
2.2.3
CRISPR-Cas9 in ﬁlamentous fungi
A powerful gene editing tool was recently developed and made available for ﬁlamentous
fungi. This tool is called Clustered Regularly Interspaced Short Palindromic Repeats
(CRISPR)-Cas9, originally found in some bacteria and archaea as a natural part of the
adaptive immune system.
The role of this system in nature is to respond to a viral
infection and subsequently eliminate it [42]. CRISPR-Cas9 is a very eﬃcient tool for
controlled and site-speciﬁc targeting of DNA and is widely used in scientiﬁc work [40].
The CRISPR-Cas9 system consist of two individual components. A combination of the
two components contribute to a recognition and generation of a DSB in a target DNA.
Several requirements need to be fulﬁlled for Cas9-mediated editing to occur. See ﬁgure 12
for a simpliﬁed presentation of CRISPR-Cas9 in process. First a chimeric single-guided
RNA (sgRNA) needs to be present, represented as the orange strand in the ﬁgure. This
sgRNA holds a 20 nucleotide long protospacer at the 5-end, which is responsible for ac-
curate targeting by binding complementary to the target gene. The sgRNA also holds a
stem-loop structure at the 3-end, responsible for binding the Cas9 protein. Furthermore,
a short conserved sequence, namely the protospacer-associated motif (PAM) is required.
The PAM sequence must be positioned adjacent to the binding site of the sgRNA at the
target DNA for Cas9 to be active [43]. The PAM sequence varies depending on the Cas9
protein, but 5-NGG-3 PAM sequence has shown to be the most eﬃcient sequence for the
frequently used Streptococcus pyogenes Cas9 protein [40]. Cas9 is a CRISPR-associated
protein consisting of two nuclease domains, represented as the blue bean shaped structure
in ﬁgure 12. These nucleases are responsible for cleavage of the target DNA, hence intro-
duction of a DSB [42]. The DSB is typically repaired by one of the two repair mechanism
in fungi. If the NHEJ pathway is used to repair the DSB a small gene deletion or insertion
will often be introduced in the target site, leaving a mutation that usually knocks out
the gene. The DSB can also be repaired by HR in the presence of a homologous ﬂanking
sequence to the target site [43].

PAM sequence. The DSB will eventually be repaired by either NHEJ or HR. Picture modiﬁed from [43].
2.3
High Performance Liquid Chromatography Mass Spectrom-
etry
A metabolite detection was performed throughout this project in order to verify that the
wildtype strain of A. terreus produce lovastatin naturally. This detection was achieved
by Ultra-high Performance Liquid Chromatography High Resolution Mass Spectrometry
(UHPLC-HRMS) equipped with a diode array detector.
In general, liquid chromatography (LC) is a technique used for separation, quantiﬁcation
and identiﬁcation of molecules dissolved in a solution. High Performance Liquid Chro-
matography (HPLC) does in diﬀerence to LC use a high pressure delivered by a pump
to force the liquid through the columns. UHPLC is a development of the original HPLC,
which oﬀers a much faster analysis time [44].
The analyte molecules has to be converted into a charged ionised state (a gaseous phase)
before going into the mass spectrometer for analysis. This is done by making an ion-
isation of the analytes. The ion source used most widely for biological molecules (and
throughout this project) is the electrospray ionisation source (ESI) [45]. The generation
of ions are obtained by pumping liquid samples of the analytes at high pressure through
a metal capillary at 3000 to 5000 voltage. This will initially form a ﬁne spray of charged
droplets consisting of analytes and solvents. The charged ionised analytes are released
from the solvent through evaporation by nitrogen and eventually transferred into the
mass spectrometer by addition of focused voltage [45]. Inside the mass spectrometer is
located a mass analyzer that separates ions according to their mass-to-charge ratio (m/z).
20
==PAGE:33==
The analyzer used in this project was a quadrupole time-of-ﬂight (QTOF) and this will
be the only technique elaborated.
QTOF analyzer can be separated into three main steps, which takes place in three vacu-
ums in the machine. The ﬁrst vacuum consist of the ion source marked in red in ﬁgure
13. The second vacuum contains a quadrupole mass ﬁlter, collision cells and an ion beam
shaper marked in black in ﬁgure 13. Ions are separated by mass by acceleration through
the mass ﬁlter, then only ions with in a certain range of mass are accelerated further into
the hexapole collision cells [45] [46]. Ions are accelerated to a higher kinetic energy before
entering the collision cells. These collision cells are composed of small metal rods, which
can create an electromagnetic ﬁeld when alternating voltage is applied to the rods. This
electromagnetic ﬁeld ensures that ions above a certain mass is conﬁned. Collision induced
dissociation (CID) is obtained by reaction with a collision gas [46]. The remaining gas
from the collision cells are removed by transferring ion optics in the ion beam shaper
after CID is obtained. The ions will subsequently enter the third and last vacuum, which
contains an ion pulser and a detector, which makes up the TOF part (marked in green
in ﬁgure 13). Ions will travel through a ﬂight tube when high voltage pulse is applied to
the ion pulser. The time it takes for the ions to reach the detector reﬂects the mass of
the ions, from which a computer can calculate the mass-to-charge ratio [46].

2.4
Fluorescent microscopy for intracellular localization of pro-
teins
Imaging ﬂuorescence proteins (FPs) through spectroscopy is a very important tool in
genomic research. The ability to tag and thereby combine FPs with intracellular proteins
of cells make it a very eﬃcient marker for gene expression and protein targeting in a wide
selection of organism [47].
Two well known FPs were used for protein targeting throughout this project. The green
ﬂuorescent protein (GFP) from the jellyﬁsh Aequorea victoria and the red ﬂuorescence
protein (RFP) from the sea anemone Entacmaea quadricolora [48]. It becomes possible
to determine the intracellular localization of a speciﬁc protein by incorporating either one
of the FPs in the genome and subsequently analyze it by the ﬂuorescence microscope.
A beam light in the microscope will expose light with a selected wavelength to the ﬂu-
orescent tagged proteins. The light will be absorbed, which will cause the molecules to
absorb a photon. This state is also known as the excited state. When the photon is
released, the molecule will return to the ground state and emission of light will occur. A
separation of the emitted ﬂuorescence from the excitation light is the main function of
the ﬂuorescence microscope. This makes only the emitted ﬂuorescence light visible in the
microscope [49]. Thereby only ﬂuorescent tagged genes will be visible.

Protein expression, puriﬁcation and analysis
Diﬀerent heterologous expression systems, including fungi, mammalian cells and bacteria,
can be used to express proteins eﬃciently. In this project, expression of the four HMGRs
in A. terreus were obtained by a pET plasmid transformed into E. coli BL21 (DE3).
The pET DUET vector backbone is a commercial vector from Novagen. The vector is
a circular plasmid approximately 5420 bp long. The pET system is the most eﬃcient
expression system of recombinant proteins in E. coli strains. The vector is originally
designed for coexpression of two target genes and contains two multiple cloning sites.
However, the pET vector used in these studies was modiﬁed and one of the two cloning
sites were removed. A T7 promoter and a ribosome binding site is found in front of the
cloning site. The vector also carries a gene coding for ampicillin resistance and a lacI
gene coding for a lac repressor [50]. See ﬁgure 14 (b) for a representation of the pET
vector.
The T7 promoter is highly speciﬁc and only matches RNA polymerase from T7 bacte-
riophage. This is beneﬁcial since leaky expressions of target genes will be prevented.
Target genes cloned into the pET system are expressed by providing the system with T7

RNA polymerase. This speciﬁc polymerase can be transcribed and translated by the E.
coli strain BL21 (DE3) controlled by the lacUV5 promoter [50]. See ﬁgure 14 (a) for
representation of E. coli BL21 (DE3) strain.
The lac repressor will block for binding of the T7 RNA polymerase to the promoter se-
quence. The lac repressor therefore has to be released from the lac promoter sequence
before transcription of the target gene can begin. One of two compounds can release

Histidine aﬃnity tags, also referred to as his-tag, is a technique frequently used for pu-
rifying recombinant expressed proteins. Fusion of a DNA string containing between six
and nine histidine residues to the N- or C-terminus of the recombinant protein allows for
selective puriﬁcation by immobilized metal-aﬃnity chromatography (IMAC) [52]. IMAC
is based on interaction between side chains on speciﬁc amino acids and divalent metal
ions including nickel, cobber, cobalt, iron and zinc, immobilized on a matrix. Histidine
is frequently used as the amino acid since it has the strongest binding aﬃnity with metal
ion matrices [53]. This is primarily due to the imidazole ring on histidine, which serve as
electron donor and can form bonds with the metal ions. Thereby, any peptide holding a
sequence of histidine residues can be retained on IMAC column matrices. These peptides
can subsequently be eluted by adding a solution of free imidazole to the columns, which
will compete with the peptides for binding of the metal ions [53].
Thereby, overexpressed his-tagged proteins can easily be detected after puriﬁcation due
to the high binding aﬃnity between histidine and a range of diﬀerent immobilized metal
ions [52].
The Bradford assay, also called the coomassie brilliant blue protein assay, is a biochemical
analytical technique used to determine accurate concentration of puriﬁed proteins in a
solution by a spectrophotometer. The assay is based on formation of a complex between
a dye, Coomassie Brilliant Blue G, and the proteins.
This complex is formed by a
noncovalent binding of the dye and the carboxyl group of the proteins. The dye binds to
the basic and aromatic amino acids and causes a shift of the maximum absorption of the
dye from 465 to 595 nm, which will turn the color of the solution from red/brown to blue
[54]. If the color of the samples does not change this indicate that an insuﬃcient amount
of proteins is present in the sample. The process of dye binding is complete in 2 minutes
and last stable for about 60 minutes, which means that the measurement should be done
within this time frame [54].
An equation can be written from the relationship between measured absorption and
known concentration of the sample proteins, which is used to make a linear calibration line
[55]. The equation expressed from the graph is used to determine the exact concentrations
of the unknown protein samples.

Experimental strategy
The aim of this project has been to characterize four known HMGRs (ATET09965,
ATET09520, ATET03447 and ATET02145) in the genome of A. terreus and to eluci-
date how they are linked to self-resistance mechanisms. Diﬀerent experiments has been
made throughout this project to investigate the HMGRs. These experiments can be di-
vided into four parts, all contributing to address the aim of the project. The experimental
strategy and design will be presented below.
1) Investigation of biosynthesis of lovastatin in A. terreus on various media
The ﬁrst part of the project was made in order to conﬁrm the natural production of
lovastatin in A. terreus. The wildtype strain was grown on three diﬀerent media and a
chemical analysis by UHPLC and MS was subsequently run to measure the presence of
lovastatin.
2) Deletion of HMGRs in the genome of A. terreus
The second part of the project was based on deletion of the four HMGRs in the genome
of A. terreus in order to explore loss of function or loss of resistance in the fungus.
Deletion vectors were constructed consisting of two fragments, 2000 bp upstream of the
HMGR and 2000 bp downstream of the HMGR. These two fragments were cloned into the
plasmid vector backbone of either p6 (carrying the selective marker pyrG from Aspergillus
ﬂavus) or pAC3 (carrying the selective marker pyrG from Aspergillus fumigatus). Dele-
tion of the HMGRs was based on HR by transforming each of the deletion constructs
into a NHEJ deﬁcient mutant strain of A. terreus.
3) Tagging of HMGRs with ﬂuorescent proteins in the genome of A. terreus
The third part of the project was based on ﬂuorescent tagging with either RFP or GFP,
C-terminally on the HMGRs, in order to determine gene expression and the intracellular
localization. Tagging vectors were designed by amplifying three fragments per construct.
2000 bp in the 3’ end of the HMGR (without stop codon), a RFP/GFP gene, and 2000
bp downstream of the HMGR. These three fragments were cloned into the plasmid vector
backbone of either p6 or pAC3.
4) HMGR expression, puriﬁcation and analysis
The fourth part of the project was made to measure the activity of each of the four
HMGRs, determine the concentration and investigate the individual resistance towards
statins.

==PAGE:38==
An IPTG-induced gene expression of the HMGRs and a histidine aﬃnity tag based pu-
riﬁcation has been the chosen strategy to accomplish an individual expression of the
HMGRs. The experimental design was based on insertion of the HMGRs in the pET
DUET vector backbone and later transformation into the E. coli BL21 (DE3) strain.
This is believed to make up an eﬃcient expression system for the HMGRs. A his-tagged
puriﬁcation of the expressed proteins and a Bradford assay was run in order to deter-
mine the concentration of each puriﬁed HMGR and to later identify individual statin

Chemical analysis by plug extraction
In order to verify that the wildtype strain of A. terreus is producing lovastatin naturally, a

Two diﬀerent protocols has been used throughout the present project for transformation
of recombinant plasmids in ﬁlamentous fungi depending on the selective marker present
in the plasmid.
The ﬁrst protocol explained is a molten agar medium protocol, used for plasmids holding
resistance against hygromycin B. 10 µg/mL plasmid and 100 µL protoplast were mixed.
The mixture was put on ice for 15 minutes and subsequently added 1 mL PCT and in-
cubated in room temperature for 15 minutes. TM sorbitol-glucose supplemented with
500 µg/mL hygromycin B was used for selection of the transformants. The transfor-
mation mixture was added to 12 mL melted TM sorbitol-glucose supplemented with 60
µL hygromycin B and plated on an empty agar plate. The plate was incubated o/n at
30◦C. The following day an overlay solution was prepared consisting of 10 mL melted
TM sorbitol-glucose supplemented with 50 µL hygromycin B. The solution was poured
on the plate as overlay and incubated at 30◦C for about 96 hours.
The second protocol explained is the usual protocol for solid plate transformation, and
was used for plasmids holding a pyrG gene as selective marker. 10 µg/mL plasmid and
100 µL protoplast were mixed and placed on ice for 15 min. 200 µL PCT was added and
the mixture was incubated at room temperature for 15 minutes. The mix was plated on
TM plates supplemented with sucrose and incubated at 30◦C for about 96 hours.
A negative control not containing any DNA was made alongside both transformation
protocols.

4.9
Protoplast preparation protocol
Protoplast was prepared for the pyrG deﬁcient Ku70∆mutant strain of A. terreus
(ATER8). All equipment used throughout the protoplastation were sterile.
ATER8 was grown at 30◦C in 7 days on MM plates supplemented with sterile ﬁltered
uridine and uracil.
The mycelium was harvested carefully from the plate by using a
Drigalsky spatula and 3 mL YPD supplemented with uridine and uracil. The biomass
was transferred to a shake ﬂask added 97 mL YPD supplemented with uridine and uracil
and additionally incubated for about 73 hours at 30◦C and shake at 150 rpm. The days
after, the biomass was ﬁltered through a Miracloth and washed with milli-Q water. The
water was removed from the biomass leaving a solid texture, which was transferred to
two Falcon tubes. 1.6 g glucanex (40 mg/ml) was dissolved in 40 mL APB by very slow
stirring. 20 mL of the glucanex solution was added to each Falcon tube and the mixture
was incubated for another 3 hours at 30◦C and shake at 150 rpm. The mixture was ﬁl-
tered through a Microcloth and the ﬁltrate was transferred to two new Falcon tubes and
centrifuged for 10 minuets at 1700 g. The supernantant was subsequently discarded and
pellet was resuspended in 25 mL ST. This step was repeated twice and the ﬁnal pellet
was resuspended in 2 mL STC. The protoplasts were stored at -80◦C.

4.10
Protein expression protocol
HMGR protein expression and puriﬁcation was made in the histidine tagged vectors to

supplemented with 1 mM IPTG. Samples were incubated at 30◦C at shake o/n. The
pellet was harvested the following day and all Eppendorf tubes were added into liquid
nitrogen to obtain an instant freezing before storage at -80◦C.
4.11
Protein puriﬁcation protocol
An enzymatic lysis was performed on the cells by resuspending the cell pellet with a
lysis buﬀer consisting of a phosphate binding buﬀer added 20 mM imidazole and protease
inhibitor, lysozyme DNAse and MgCl2. The cells were incubated for 30 minutes after
addition of the lysis buﬀer and subsequently put into a container with liquid nitrogen to
freeze them immediately. 3 cycles of freeze and thaw of the samples was made to induce
a mechanical lysis. The cells were then centrifuged at max speed for 30 minutes to clarify
the cell lysate so the supernantant could easily be harvested. The clear and collected
supernantant was puriﬁed on HisSpin Trap Columns from GE Healthcare according to
manufacturers protocol. The eluent obtained from the puriﬁcation protocol was later
up-concentrated by Amicon Ultra 0.5 Centrifugal Filter Device from Merck Millipore by
following the manufacturers protocol. The upconcentrated proteins were washed three
times with a 50 mM TRIS solution in order to exchange buﬀer and remove the imidazole.
The up-concentrated proteins were subsequently recovered and mixed 1:1 with a storage
buﬀer consisting of 50 mM Tris-HCl, 1:200 v/v protease inhibitor cocktail and 10% glyc-
erol.

were prepared to make a linear concentration range. The protein used as standard was

dilution series of BSA were made by mixing with a 50 mM TRIS buﬀer. The dilution
series consisted of 10 dilutions of the sample protein in the following concentrations:
1.5 mg/mL, 1.0 mg/mL, 0.75 mg/mL, 0.5 mg/mL, 0.375 mg/mL, 0.250 mg/mL, 0.188
mg/mL, 0.125 mg/mL, 0.094 mg/mL and 0.063 mg/mL.

buﬀer and added to the 96 well plate. 5 µL of the 50 mM TRIS buﬀer was added to the
blank wells on the plate.

were brought to the spectrophotometer Synergy 2 - Biotek from Holm & Halby. The plate
was shaken for about 20 seconds. Measurement of the samples with an absorbance in the

5.1
Lovastatin production on various media by A. terreus
The ﬁrst part of the present project was aiming on proving that the wildtype strain of
A. terreus is synthesizing lovastatin naturally by performing a chemical analysis.
The output of the UHPLC-HRMS was analyzed by the Agilent Mass Hunter Workstation
software, which is a quantitative analysis program. This software allows to represent data
as an extracted ion chromatogram (EIC). The ﬁrst axis in an EIC represent time, whereas
the second axis represent the intensity of the signal. The software furthermore allows to
select for speciﬁc masses, hence displaying only the peaks of interest.
The exact ionic mass calculated for lovastatin is 404.2563 and can be found on Kyoto
Encyclopedia of Genes and Genomes database (KEGG). Only charged molecules can be
observed by the MS, hence the protonated form of the molecule [M+H]+ is often observed.
Furthermore, sensitivity to form complexes with alkali metal adducts are well known
when analysis are run by ESI-MS-based techniques. In a positive ion electrospray the
most common metal adduct ions observed are sodium [M +Na]+ and potassium [M +K]+
[56]. To see the total observed m/z value for the diﬀerent adduct ion compositions, see
table 7. The total observed m/z values presented in the table, is the exact masses selected
for in the EIC.

6
Discussion
Investigation of lovastatin production in A. terreus on various media
A chemical analysis on lovastatin production was carried out as the ﬁrst part of this
project to verify that the wildtype strain of A. terreus synthesize lovastatin naturally.
Lovastatin was proved to be synthesized in two of three culture media, CYA and YES
media. No lovastatin was synthesized on MM media, which suggest that this media does
not supply with exact nutrients needed for the biosynthesis of lovastatin.
Optimization studies of the culture conditions for a maximal production of lovastatin
in A. terreus has been made by Kamath et al. in 2015 [57]. Carbon and nitrogen are
directly involved in formation of biomass and metabolites and these two nutrients play
the essential role for high yield of lovastatin [58].
These studies provided knowledge
of the best carbon and nitrogen sources. Using yeast extract media as nitrogen source
showed signiﬁcant higher yields of lovastatin compared to six other sources. Compared to
MM media, which contains a nitrate solution, both CYA and YES media contains yeast
extract as nitrogen source, and thereby makes up a better growth media based on this
nutrient. The optimal carbon source is glucose, tested among 10 other carbon sources.
Only MM media has glucose as carbon sources, whereas the two other has sucrose. The
studies furthermore revealed that the pH value of the growth media has a crucial role
to play, since pH aﬀects the transport of molecules across the cell membrane [57]. The
optimum pH value for synthesis of lovastatin is 6. Even a small increase or decrease in
this pH value can lead to a substantial decrease in the yield of lovastatin. The pH for both
YES and CYA media is adjusted to 6-6.5 prior to use. The MM media is not adjusted
prior to use, hence this is unknown. One or more of these culture conditions can be part
of the reason why no lovastatin production is obtained in A. terreus when grown on MM
media.
The peak observed in the time frame between 10-10.5 minutes on the EICs for both CYA
and YES are most likely an indication of the presence of the inactive closed gamma lac-
tone ring form of lovastatin. In large scale fermentation industry, a large amount of this
inactive form of lovastatin is also obtained. A conversion of the inactive form to the active
hydroxy acid form is obtained by mixing with 0.1 N NaOH and ethanol (1:1), heating
the mixture at 50◦C for 20 minutes and subsequently neutralize with addition of HCl [59].
Deletion of HMGRs in the genome of A. terreus
A part of this project aimed to design and construct four deletion vectors, which were
intended to delete each of the four HMGRs in the genome of A. terreus based on HR.
Growth was obtained on the transformation plate for three of the four deletion constructs.
60
==PAGE:73==
It was not possible to verify the mutant strains due to lack of time, which impact the
validity of the studies.
An ineﬃcient PCR protocol can be one reason for the lack of bands on the gel. Insuﬃcient
amount of mycelium added to the reaction could also aﬀect the results, since too small
amounts will not give any ampliﬁcation of fragments and too much DNA can inhibit the
PCR reaction. Another explanation for the missing bands is incorrect construction of
the strains. Random integration of the selective marker pyrG in the ku70∆strain of
A. terreus (ATER8) could lead to successful growth on media without supplements of
uridine and uracil. This would however be must unlikely since the NHEJ pathway is
inactivated in this strain.
If we assume that the deletion constructs are correct and veriﬁed, it would have been
expected that deletion of the two transmembrane HMGRs ATET09965 and ATET09520
in the genome of A. terreus would have had crucial eﬀects on the fungus. This is assumed
since these two HMGRs are closely related to mlcD, which is proved to be involved in the
primary metabolism and part of the self-resistance mechanism against lovastatin in P.
citrinum. Transformation of both of these deletion vectors were carried out successfully
and growth were observed, indicating that the fungus can maintain life without one of
the two HMGRs. It was expected that at least one of these two HMGRs were essential
for the fungus. A suggestion to why each two transmembrane HMGRs can be expendable
is if they can complement each other.
The failed attempt in transforming the deletion vector construct of pAC3_ATET02145_
del into ATER8 could be due to an ineﬃcient transformation protocol or too small
amounts of DNA transformed into the protoplast.
Another suggestion to the unsuc-
cessful transformation is, that this speciﬁc HMGR plays an essential role in A. terreus.
It is thought that the two soluble HMGRs (ATET02145 and ATET03447) are present
in smaller compartments or in the cytosol of A. terreus. It is unknown whether or not
the presence of HMGRs in the cytosol or smaller compartments are crucial for the fun-
gus. Studies has been made evaluating the role of peroxisomes in the biosynthesis of
sterols in mammal cells. Aboushadi et al. (1999) [60], supported the proposal that per-
oxisomes are essential for sterols to be synthesized. A deletion of the speciﬁc HMGR
in the peroxisomes was carried out in these studies, which showed signiﬁcant reduced
rates of sterol synthesized. Later studies carried out by Breitling et al. (2002) [61] ex-
pressed scepticism about these studies due to lack of supportive evidence. However, if
one or more compartment inside a cell play an essential role in the biosynthesis of sterols,
any HMGR present in such compartment will be crucial for the organism to maintain live.
61
==PAGE:74==
Tagging of HMGRs with ﬂuorescent proteins in the genome of A. terreus
Five ﬂuorescence tagged vectors were prepared to be able to determine the gene expres-
sion and intracellular localization of the four HMGRs in A. terreus.
Four out of ﬁve constructs were successfully transformed into ATER8. The RFP tagged
version of ATET03447 did not succeed. It is most likely caused by ineﬃcient transforma-
tion protocol or too little DNA used for transformation, since the GFP tagged version of
ATET03447 and constructs made with both p6 and pAC3 templates has succeeded.
Three of four successfully transformed strains emitted clear ﬂuorescence light under the
microscope. The level of expression from the GFP tagged on ATET03447 is very low, close
to nothing, under the microscope. This low expression might be due to large amounts of
mycelium on the plate, which made it diﬃcult to focus the microscope. Another option is
that the gene expression of ATET03447 is very low in A. terreus or that the intracellular
localization of the protein is found inside a closed compartment of the cell.
HMGR expression and puriﬁcation
A heterologous gene expression of the HMGRs in E. coli BL21 (DE3) combined with a
histidine aﬃnity tag puriﬁcation was made in order to determine the activity and con-
centration of expressed proteins and to investigate statin resistance. Unfortunately, it
was not succeeded to either express all HMGRs nor purify them.
The sequencing results from the his-tagged vectors revealed that both the histidine se-
quence and the HMGRs are cloned correctly into the backbone of pET. No stop codon
was observed in the coding strand for any of the constructs, suggesting that the low ex-
pression is not connected to ineﬃcient or mutated expression constructs.
The approach for expressing and purifying the HMGRs has not been optimal throughout
this project, which makes it diﬃcult to identify where the cause of the problem lie. Nev-
ertheless, the expression of proteins analyzed before puriﬁcation did not reveal activity
for all HMGRs, indicating that this protocol is not eﬃcient.
The cells used for induction in this study were harvested at an OD around 1. Other
studies suggest that this OD is too high and that it is optimal to harvest cells between
OD 0.6-0.8, to be sure that cells are still in the exponential phase. The culture starts to
collect dead cells if the stationary phase is entered and less proteins will be expressed.
An optimization experiment checking diﬀerent OD before harvesting could optimize the
cells that express the proteins.
Both the concentration of IPTG and the time used for induction can have an eﬀect on
the expression of the proteins. The optimal time for induction depends on the stability
of the proteins. A test experiment collecting samples each hour during induction should
be made to determine the stability of each protein. This could reveal at what time of
62
==PAGE:75==
induction the protein is most expressed. Furthermore, the temperature for induction
used throughout this project was 30◦C. Although this was optimal for three of the seven
proteins, this might not be optimal for the remaining. A temperature test experiment
should also be run for each of the proteins to measure the optimal temperature for protein
expression.
Furthermore, optimization experiments for buﬀers, solutions and columns should be car-
ried out in order to optimize the puriﬁcation protocol. However, one attempt to optimize
the puriﬁcation protocol was made throughout this study during the imidazole gradient
experiment. Although the experiment suggested that the optimal concentration of im-
idazole was 40 mM for ATET03447, this can diﬀer between the remaining proteins. A
new experiment should be carried out for each of the proteins in order to determine the
overall optimal concentration of imidazole in the binding, washing and elution buﬀer in
the puriﬁcation protocol. A way to optimize the remaining buﬀers and solutions could
be to analyze concentrations in some of the eluted buﬀers during the binding, washing
and elution steps. If signiﬁcant concentrations of the proteins are found in the eluted
solution from the washing buﬀer for instance, this might suggest that this buﬀer is not
optimal for the speciﬁc purpose.
The binding properties of his-tagged proteins can diﬀer depending on the type of im-
mobilized metal ions column (IMAC) used for puriﬁcation. Nickel charged resins were
used in the columns to purify the his-tagged proteins in this project. It is known that
cobalt-charged resins bind with higher speciﬁcity than nickel-charged resins. Using cobalt-
charged resins gives a smaller protein yield, but higher elution protein purity [53]. Since
it was not possible to purify any of the proteins except for the control with RFP, a cobalt-
charged resin could potentially be more eﬃcient for the HMGRs in this study.
Puriﬁcation of his-tagged proteins can also be obtained in other ways than using IMAC
columns. Proteins are often puriﬁed by solutions instead of columns in large scale pro-
duction. Beads are loaded with the immobilized metal ion of interest and the puriﬁcation
is made in a large beaker instead of small columns. This will leave a larger surface for
the proteins to bind, hence the protein concentration will most likely increase.
To sum up, optimization experiments should be carried out for each of the proteins re-
garding concentration of IPTG, temperature used for expression of proteins, sampling
and harvest time. Furthermore, experiments regarding optimization of columns, buﬀers
and solution should be carried out. A combination of these optimization experiments
would contribute to make an eﬃcient expression and puriﬁcation protocol.

7
Conclusion
Characterization of the four HMGRs in A. terreus has been the ambition to investigate
during this study. The intention of constructing an enzymatic assay to measure the in-
dividual statin insensitivity reactions on the HMGRs was not realized, as the expression
and puriﬁcation protocols must be optimized for these speciﬁc proteins. However, the
achievement of veriﬁed constructions of correct and sequenced his-tagged HMGRs open
several opportunities for future enzymatic assays to be run.
Construction of vectors
carrying ﬂuorescence tagged HMGRs transformed into the NHEJ deﬁcient strain of A.
terreus, made it possible to propose the intracellular localization of the proteins. The
intensity and accumulation of emitted light from the ﬂuorescence microscopy suggested
that ATET09520 is localized in the ER, whereas ATET09965 and ATET02145 is localized
in the cytosol.
At the time I transformed veriﬁed deletion constructs into the NHEJ deﬁcient strain of A.
terreus, veriﬁcation by tissue PCR did not reveal bands on the gel for all mutant strains.
Time was not left to explore the sensitivity of the deletion constructs against natural
lovastatin production in the fungus. However, this investigation is desired to achieve in
the future.
In conclusion, this study contributed to obtain knowledge about the four HMGRs in A.
terreus, which can be used for future perspectives. Self-resistance mechanisms are yet
only a lightly explored area. Identifying how HMGRs are linked to self-resistance mecha-
nisms can, in a broader perspective, contribute to obtain even more eﬃcient heterologous
expression systems for desired secondary metabolites in the future.

8
Perspectives
The experiments made throughout this project can be continued in the future to address
the challenges encountered and contribute to further investigation of self-resistance mech-
anisms in A. terreus.
First, a veriﬁcation of the mutant strains of A. terreus containing deletion and tagging
vectors will be necessary for future studies to be continued. When these strains are ver-
iﬁed they should be added to the collection and further work will be feasible. Veriﬁed
strains with deletion vectors could be plated on both MM media, and on a more com-
plex media (CYA or YES), in order to determine which of the HMGRs is responsible for
conferring resistance in the fungi. Since it was proved in these studies that A. terreus
synthesize lovastatin on CYA and YES media, but not on MM media, it can be used
as an assay for determining the role of each HMGR in self-resistance towards lovastatin.
If growth is observed on MM media and not on complex media for one of the HMGR
deﬁcient strains, this will indicate that this protein is part of a self-resistance mechanism
in the fungus.
For the tagging vectors, it is intended in future experiments to investigate the protein-
protein interaction between the HMGRs, by exploring if they form homodimers or het-
erodimers. It is intended to make a ﬂuorescence resonance energy transfer (FRET) anal-
ysis or split GFP analysis to achieve this. This type of study will furthermore approve
or disprove the intracellular localization of the proteins suggested in this study.
At last, future experiments regarding expression and puriﬁcation of the HMGRs will have
to start over and a new approach will be used. Optimization experiments should be run
for all proteins in both the expression and puriﬁcation protocol. When it is succeeded to
express and purify all HMGRs, an enzymatic assay can be made to detect the enzymatic
reaction response upon addition of statins.
