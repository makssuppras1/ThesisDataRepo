Abstract
This thesis presents the work on the design of an Economic Model Predictive Controller
(EMPC) with soft constraints and Kalman filter. The controller optimizes the heating costs for
electricity based upon varying electricity price signals for a residential research facility house.
The controller inherits a thermal model of the house that is investigated and evaluated
and the benefit of implementing a Kalman filter in order to handle model uncertainties is
proofed.
The proposed EMPC performance is analysed and compared with conventional heating
control system solutions. The sensitivity of the comfort zone choice and disturbances on
the controller results are analysed. Finally, the role of EMPC in the future power grid is
discussed in terms of offering flexibility to the grid by shifting demand towards periods of
energy surplus.
Keywords: Economic Model Predictive Control, Active Demand Side Management,
MPC Controller Design, Building Energy Management System

Chapter 1
Thesis Introduction
1.1
Introduction
The reduction of greenhouse gas emissions and the transformation of the energy sector from
a fossil fuel based towards a renewable energy based system are important issues at present.
Denmark and Germany both established as an objective to increase the share of renewable
energy electricity generation to 50% [7] respectively 35% [16] until 2020. Space heating is
the most important end-use of energy in the residential sector in the EU. In 2012 an average
of 67% [4] of household energy consumption by end-use was used for heating in the EU.
It is because of its magnitude of interest to investigate deeper into this sphere. Apart from
increasing the energy efficiency, lower the environmental impact by building isolation and
use of renewable energy sources, advanced control strategies can contribute in lowering
the energy consumption. At the same time electricity based heating systems (heat pumps
or electric radiators) can increase flexibility of electricity consumption, so called Demand
Response (DR), to integrate high share of intermittent energy generation into the electricity
grid. In times of high renewable energy production the prices of electricity at the spot market
decrease most of the time as a consequent of an excess of energy. Model Predictive Control
(MPC) based electrical heating systems consider the electricity price in the optimization
procedure and can potentially save costs for the consumer whilst stabilizing supply and
demand of electricity in the grid. Especially massive constructed buildings have a large
thermal inertia and therefore an inherent DR potential.
MPC control strategy is based on use of a building model as well as on weather forecasts
and occupancy predictions in order to find the optimal control sequence to be implemented
in the future. The best control sequence is found by solving, at each moment in the decision
process, an on-line optimization problem. MPC’s ability to handle constrained multivariable
systems as well as economic objectives makes this paradigm particularly well suited for the

issue of energy management in buildings and is a intrinsic difference to conventional optimal
control technique. [17] Chapter 3 examines the MPC control strategy in detail.
This thesis is concerned with the development and assessment of Economic Model Pre-
dictive Control algorithms (EMPC) for energy management in a three storey brick domestic
house located in Risø, Denmark. The 150m2 residential building is part of the research facili-
ties at the Technical University of Denmark (DTU) equipped with sensors and controllable
loads and heating equipment. It is interconnected with the SYSLAB facility, a 400 V grid
with a communication platform for smart grid test activities. [25]
Furthermore, the performance of the proposed controller is evaluated and compared with
a conventional controlling solution.
The main research contributions of this work are:
• Detailed analysis of a mathematical model describing the thermal dynamics of Power-
FlexHouse 3 residential building located in Risø DTU campus.
• Design of an Economic Model Predictive Controller for the research facility house in
order to offer flexibility on the demand side of the electricity grid, reduce costs for
electric energy based heating.
• Provide robustness towards the controller to handle model uncertainties, prediction
errors and other disturbances and to guarantee a stable and reliable operation.
• A comparison of the developed EMPC with conventional control solution.
1.2
Thesis Outline
The thesis is organized as follows:
In Chapter 2 the heat dynamic model used for the controller is introduced and described. It
is then transformed in discrete time to make it available to the EMPC. Finally, the model is
evaluated and climate data and electricity prices are investigated.
Chapter 3 introduces the concept of Model Predictive Control and economic MPC
including state estimation, filtering and prediction. The mathematical background, the
controller structure and the implementation in MATLAB is presented.
Chapter 4 documents the simulation of the designed EMPC and interprets the results
with regard to differences to a conventional control strategy. A sensitivity analysis is proceed
to quantify the direct effects of disturbances on the controller behaviour.

Chapter 2
Model for the heat dynamics of the
building
In this chapter the mathematical model is presented describing the thermal behaviour of the
investigated residential house. By means of the differential stochastic equations the model
is transformed in continuous state space representation and in a next step into discrete time.
The model’s accuracy and controllability and observability is then investigated. In (2.5) the
input data used in the controller simulations is presented and evaluated.
2.1
PowerFlexHouse 3 Heat Dynamics Model
Figure (2.1) and (2.2) show pictures of the three-storey domestic brick house, PowerFlex-
House3.

The building model was introduced in [33] using a grey box modeling approach which
is a combination of white box and black box modeling. White box models require a proper
==PAGE:20==
6
Model for the heat dynamics of the building
knowledge about the system physics and do not consider any disturbances. Meanwhile, black
box models are based on information of the inputs and outputs of the system and are used
when only limited information about the system physics is available. In grey-box modeling
first a lumped model of the heat dynamics of the building is created, which is formulated as a
deterministic linear state space model in continuous time. The parameters are then estimated
using measured data and statistical analysis [31]. By using this methodology one can reduce
the weaknesses of white box and black box approaches and the model becomes suitable for
both short-term and long-term predictions.
Assuming each floor as one room or temperature zone with one heater the chosen model has
in total six state variables. Three states describing the internal temperature Ti in each floor
and three representing the temperature of the respective building envelopes Tei. Figure (2.3)
sketches the thermal RC-model of the house for the heat flow where capacitors represent the
thermal capacity of the walls, interior equipment, and floors. The resistors correspond to the
thermal resistance between the two floors, floors and envelopes and envelopes and ambient,
analogously to the flow of electric charge in a resistor, where the temperature differences are
interpreted as potential differences. Note that the dynamics of the rooftop is included into
floor two.

The heat transfer model takes measured disturbances namely the solar irradiance (kW/m2),
the outside temperature (◦C) and the earth temperature (◦C) into consideration. Ventilation
does not directly cause heat transfer, but due to mass transfer heat is transferred. It is con-
sidered for wind speeds up to 5m/s in the thermal resistance of the building walls as shown
in [32]. This approximation is invalid for higher wind speeds though it becomes non-linear.
High wind speeds are considered as noise term. The heat flux (kW) coming from the heater
in each floor is the controlled input in the model. The heaters in the first floor have with
6.138kW the highest power. The power in the second floor is 2.198kW and in the basement
2.708kW resulting in a total heating power of almost 11kW. It has to be noted that the heating
system in PowerFlexHouse 3 is equipped with water radiators meanwhile the system model
is considered to have electric heaters. As a result a faster response to the input signal is
anticipated in the model.

for the computation of the optimal control inputs. The first-order dynamics are represented
by the stochastic differential equations (2.1a) - (2.1c) for the floors and equations (2.1d) -

2.5
Input Data
Besides a proper model of the plant (residential building) EMPC requires a wealth of
input data. As stated before the proposed controller considers electricity prices, ambient
temperature and solar irradiance data for the predictions. The input data for this project
were taken from 17.01.2014 until 31.01.2014 a total of 15 days. That allows a simulation
horizon of 14 days plus 24 hours prediction. One can distinguish between deterministic data
as electricity price and measurement data and forecast data which inherits uncertainties. In
(2.5.2) some methodologies for data evaluation are presented to quantify this uncertainties.
Since the time series of valid data is limited, it is appropriate to apply the presented methods
on larger data sets to reach statistical significance.
2.5.1
Electricity Price
The electricity prices used in the simulation scenarios are hourly spot market prices for
electricity (Elspot) in the eastern Denmark grid (named DK2) published by [9] and plotted
for the period examined in figure (2.11). Power plant operators offer their capacity for
supply 12-36 hours in advance at Elspot. They offer their generation capacity at marginal
generation costs and are then sorted ascending by its price. This is known as Merit-Order.
The cheapest power plants win the bid up to where the generation capacity meats the demand
prognosis. The market maker (Elspot) sets the hourly electricity price equal to the offer
of the most expensive generation plant that is considered for production. Despite their
originally placed offer all suppliers get the same production price. The difference between
marginal generation costs and electricity price is the profit margin. The proposed EMPC
optimizes its control actions by shifting the necessary heat supply towards periods of lower
electricity prices by maintaining the inhibitor comfort and taking into account predicted
disturbances. Since the electricity generation is not entirely based on renewable energy
plants, environmental concerns as greenhouse gas reduction are not considered directly in the
optimisation algorithm. Nevertheless, it could be already reached indirectly by price signals
since renewable energy power plants offer its electricity at low marginal costs. This is to be
examined in the following.

Model Predictive Controller
In this chapter the concept and theoretical background of Model Predictive Control is revealed
starting with a general overview of the MPC control strategy, moving horizon principle and
its advantages in comparison to conventional control strategies. A special case of MPC
policy called Economic Model Predictive Control (EMPC) is described and the mathematical
background and the implementation of the control algorithms in MATLAB is explained based
on the established model from chapter 2. In appendix (A) the MATLAB program code can be
found. The theory behind EMPC was taken from [13], [10], [5] and [19]. Section (3.5) deals
with providing robustness and offset free control to the proposed MPC.
3.1
Overview of Model Predictive Control
Model Predictive Control (MPC), Receding Horizon Control (RCHC) or Model-Based
Predictive Control (MBPC) belong to the advanced control techniques and is according to
[19] the only concept "[...] that is more advanced than standard PID control". Some of the
advantages over other methods listed in [5] are:
• controller tuning is straightforward
• capable of dealing a high variety of process types
• handling of MIMO-systems
• consideration of plant constraints, since the economic operating point of a typical
process unit often lies at the intersection of constraints [26]
The main principle of MPC is to transform the control problem to an optimization one [37].
At each sampling time the control signal u is obtained by calculating an optimal control

3.1.1
History of MPC
The history of MPC and the receding horizon control principle in literature and industrial
application starts in the late 1960s and has experienced a continuous development. Since
then the MPC concept is becoming more and more attractive for a wide band of industrial
applications due to constant progress in MPC research and decreasing costs of microproces-
sors. Many applications can be found in petrochemicals and chemical industries but also
pulp and paper, food processing, aerospace, and automotive industries [5]. Before becoming
a research topic in the academic control community Predictive Control was developed and
used in industry for around 20 years [19].

4.1
Simulation Conditions
The electricity prices and all considered disturbances (see chapter (2.5)) in the model are
identically in the confronted scenarios to guarantee an adequate comparing. One advantage
of EMPC is the fact that it can keep the room temperatures within certain constraints or
comfort zones. According to [34] based on ISO 7730 a bandwidth of operative temperature
in regular buildings from 21 to 23°C reach 90% acceptability from the inhabitants in winter
whereas a temperature band from 19 to 25°C is for 70% acceptable. It is assumed that the
average temperature and in the same way the comfort zone can be lowered during the night
by 1°K. The daytime-comfort zone starts at 07:00 and ends at 19:00. In figure (4.1) the
temperature bands are illustrated by dashed lines.
It is obvious that thermal comfort is a subjective value and depending on individuals.
Due to this fact, two parameters will be compared in the performance analysis: accumulated
electricity costs over simulation horizon and relative costs or average heat price. Which is
calculated as the ratio of electricity costs and energy consumption. In this work electrical
resistance heaters are assumed to be installed, consequently the efficiency for conversion of
energy from electricity to heat is almost 100%.
The initial temperatures of all floors and envelopes are set to 20°C. From further sim-
ulations it was found that the differences the choice of the initial envelope temperatures
disappear after less than 2 days.

Chapter 5
Conclusion and Recommendation for
future work
5.1
Extension to larger units
The extension of the proposed EMPC towards larger buildings with a wide range of tem-
perature zones is realizable but referring to [22] a fully centralized MPC for large-scale
systems (buildings with many rooms) is often undesirable and difficult to implement. This
statement is reasoned with an exponentially growing effort for computation resources and
its inherent drawback of poor flexibility and robustness. Due to that facts [21] proposed
a distributed MPC structure for large-scale dynamically coupled systems. Local MPCs
exchange information regarding their control sequences by a communication network to
reduce the global system performance and the computational demand. One can distinguish
between independent distributed algorithms where each local controller optimizes locally
and on the other hand cooperating distributed algorithms where a global cost function is
considered in all units [28].
5.2
Control Hierarchy
The future electricity grid will be more complex in terms of controlling and more decen-
tralised due to a higher amount of fluctuating energy generation plants. To soften the resulting
challenges it is plausible to include the consumer side as a flexible and controllable actuator
into the grid control. A new stakeholder known as Aggregator could enter in the electricity
market and merge Decentralised Energy Resources (DER) and actively control electrical
consumers. This process is call Active Demand Side Management (ADSM). The aggregators’
==PAGE:62==
48
Conclusion and Recommendation for future work
objective is to create stable states of electricity generation and consumption in a portfolio of
DERs [10]. Figure (5.1) is a schematic overview of a potential integration of the proposed
EMPC into the future energy system. The price signal will be delivered by the aggregator
to target a desired future consumer behaviour. In case of emergency there will be also the
opportunity to intervene into the controller strategy. The EMPC communicates its control
action plan after finding a local optimal solution towards the aggregator. Differing from the
simulations in chapter 4 the EMPC would send a reference temperature for PI/PID controllers
in the zones instead of actively controlling the heaters. This modification could provide
more robustness and direct user intervention into the Building Energy Management System
(BEMS) presumed that the PI/PID controllers can act autonomous.

5.3
Implementation of various devices
[36] proposed a MPC-based Building Energy Management System (BEMS) wherein all
significant energy components as electrical heating, ventilation and cooling systems and heat
pumps are considered. In [10] the implementation of a EMPC solution is introduced for a
building with heat pump, Electric Vehicle (EV) and a solar thermal collector system. [17]
developed a MPC for controlling indoor temperature, CO2 level and lightning including local
solar production.
==PAGE:63==
5.4 Conclusion
49
5.4
Conclusion
Chapter 2 demonstrated that even tough the thermal RC-model of PowerFlexHouse3 inherits
a lack of accuracy, a stationary one-step predictive Kalman filter is capable to guarantee
offset free control. It is recommendable to extend the data analysis of forecast and prediction
errors for the entire year or at least for the heating seasons.
As shown in chapter 4 the determination of the EMPC prediction horizon is not clearly
definable as it depends on the comfort zone choice. Nevertheless, it can be stated that
a longer prediction horizon is the more robust implementation since the relative costs
do increase slighter than in shorter prediction horizon realisations. The computational
requirements increase with the prediction horizon although the algorithm is undemandingly
for the stationary one-step prediction Kalman filter. The proposed EMPC is capable to add a
value for the consumer even without a varying price signal.
It was shown that the choice of the comfort temperature is an important parameter for an
EMPC-based heating system for smart buildings. Future works should therefore investigate
how the information of occupation of inhabitant could be gathered in a sophisticated way.
Knowledge of absence of inhabitants establishes an high energy efficiency potential by
lowering the building temperature during this periods. Companies like Nest Labs and Tado°
are working on GPS-based smart-phone solutions.
