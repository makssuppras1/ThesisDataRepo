Abstract
One of the most signiﬁcant ways to stop global warning and ensure future
energy security, is through energy conservation.
As part of the EU ”20-
20-20” targets [2] and the Energy Efﬁciency Plan 2011, the energy conser-
vation target for the EU is a 20 percent reduction in energy use by 2020
compared to the projected use. Also an indicative target for EU energy con-
servation of 27 percent in 2030 has been endorsed by the European Council.
Smart Homes and the Smart Appliances they consists of are considered
to have an important role in improving the energy efﬁciency of consumer
homes and archive our energy conservation targets. Improving the energy
efﬁciency is not only a Smart Home automation problem, but making con-
sumers aware of how efﬁcient their appliance use is just as important.
This project propose to make collected data from Smart Home devices us-
able to the consumer. A proof of concept solution consisting of visualization
and data aggregation is developed. An aggregation method is suggested for
classifying power consumption of any appliance. This aggregation method is
based on K-means clustering and the method is tested against a real wash-
ing machine. The usage classiﬁcation is presented using the Energy Efﬁ-
ciency Rating, based on the EU Energy Label.

Resume
En af de vigtigste måder at stoppe global opvarmning og sikre fremtidig
energi sikkerhed, er gennem energibesparelser. Som en del af EU ”20-20-
20” mål og energieffektivitetsplanen fra 2011, vil energibesparelsesmålet
for EU være en 20 procent reduktion i energiforbruget i 2020 i forhold til
det forventede forbrug. Desuden er et vejledende energibesparelsesmål for
EU sat til 27 procent i 2030 blevet godkendt af Det Europæiske Råd.
Smarte Hjem og Smarte Enheder som hjemmene de består af, anses for at
have en vigtig rolle i at forbedre energieffektiviteten af forbrugernes hjem
og opnå vores energibesparelsesmål. Forbedring af energieffektiviteten er
ikke kun et Smart Hjem automatiserings problem, men at gøre forbrugerne
opmærksomme på, hvor effektive deres apparaters brug er lige så vigtigt.
Dette projekt foreslår at gøre indsamlede data fra Smart Hjem enheder
brugbare for forbrugeren. Et proof of concept bestående af data aggrege-
ring og dertilhørende visualisering er udviklet. Et eksempel på en aggre-
gering er foreslået. Ethvert apparat forbundet til et Smart Hjem skal have
dets energiforbrug klassiﬁceret og gjort anvendelig for forbrugeren. Denne
metode er baseret på K-means clustering og er anvendt på effekt måledata
indsamlet fra en vaskemaskine. Brugs klassiﬁkationen præsenteres derefter
med en energiklasse vurdering, baseret på EU energi mærkning.

Chapter 1
Introduction
This chapter will begin with the motivation of the project in section 1.1 and
be followed by its description in section 1.4. From the project motivation
and description the goal tree is constructed in section 1.5. The goals are
later reﬁned to solution behaviour goals. The prioritization of each goal is
stated and justiﬁed in the project scope in section 1.6. A suitable develop-
ment method to achieve the goals is described in section 1.7. Finally, an
overview (in section 1.8) describes the upcoming chapters and how they
contribute to achieving the goals.
1.1
Project Motivation
Global warming is by the EU accepted as a result of human actions. Coun-
tries in the EU, including Denmark have set goals for reducing future emis-
sions [VC] [Coma]. Shifting towards more efﬁcient energy use and sustain-
able energy sources will together reduce carbon dioxide emissions and also
solve other non-climate related issues aswell.
The western world is today forced to do business with countries like Russia
and Iran. They possess large parts of the world’s oil and gas reserves. The

Smart Grid
The Smart Grid is a next generation power grid that introduces distributed
energy resources ”DER”. The DERs contribute to the Smart Grid with ser-
vices.
The Smart Grid utilizes two-way communication, making the grid
more responsive to demand. An example of a Smart Grid is illustrated in
ﬁgure ﬁg. 1.2 in the problem description.
The primary value of the Smart Grid is not necessarily the same in every
country. In Europe, we are used to a stable grid with rare outages. The
main incentive for the Smart Grid is a better utilization of renewable energy
sources.

In the US, a more intelligent power grid has been implemented since "The

had no power for a short duration.
The blackout happened because the
grid was short circuited by a tree.
[sW] A solution to this problem is an

The Smart Grid makes the grid able to reroute energy to another energy

The Smart Grid is essential for the future of Smart Homes (Scheduling of ap-

2.1.1
DER: Distributed Energy Resource
The energy resources mentioned in chapter 1 are in the context of the Smart
Grid called Distributed Energy Resources (DERs).
The DER concept can represent many kinds of devices, some more obvi-
ous than others. A DER can be a single solar panel or an entire farm of
solar panels, Smart Homes, building blocks, electric vehicles, supermarket
coolers and freezers, water pumping stations. Any device able to consume,
produce or store energy will in a Smart Grid context be considered as a
DER.
DER(s) that solely consumes energy is called a consumer. A DER that con-
sumes and produces energy for the grid it is called a prosumer.
2.1.2
Demand / Response
One of the advantages of participating in the Smart Grid is Demand/Re-
sponse services. The purpose of Demand/Response services is stabilizing
and making the energy load more efﬁcient.
==PAGE:37==
2.1 Smart Grid

The stabilization and efﬁciency is implemented using load shifting and shed-
ding.
Load shedding
Load shedding is agreeing to limit power consumption for
a limited time (for example by shutting off consuming DERs). Agreeing not
to use energy for a limited time will result in economical compensation.
Load shifting
Load shifting is scheduling load for when the grid is ex-
pected to deliver the load most efﬁciently. Load shifting is both proﬁtable
for the consumer and the utility provider. The energy will be cheaper when
the utility company can avoid turning on regular fossil fuel power plants.
In an OpenADR presentation [Zub], Mark Kerbel lists several business cases
involving load shedding in the industry and commercial settings. The busi-
ness impact of participating in load shedding is evaluated ﬁg. 2.1.
Stabilizing the grid is a valuable service. Stabilizing the frequency in the
grid is done by regulating how much energy being consumed. An owner of
a consuming DER can, therefore, earn money even by consuming energy.

In the Netherlands, the minister of economic affairs tried to make it compul-

2.4
Customer performance
Customer performance is how dedicated the resident is to adapt new behav-
ior and services. The article "Crafting a persuasive Smart Meter customer
experience" [HC] mentions four strategies for enhancing customer perfor-
mance; Vision, Access, Incentive, and Expertise.
Vision is explaining to the customer what is wanted from them. Experiments
in the Netherlands have shown that using feedback to keep the vision clear
made participants able to reduce their energy usage by 12 %. Participants
without feedback reduced their energy usage by 8 %. Simple goals must
explain the vision; a daily goal for demand/response energy consumption,
friendly competition between neighborhoods, cities, etc. or personal goals
for how much energy to save in own household.
Access is supporting the customer with the knowledge and tools they need.
Including user interfaces on the devices the consumer own. Displaying the
information they need and offer a manual override.
The incentive is the external reason for saving energy; mainly achieved
by economic compensation. However, research have shown that a typical
household only can save around 50 USD using demand response, making it
necessary to include other forms of incentives.
Expertise is educating the consumers to use the tools they are provides.

4.1
Power System Communication Standard IEC
61850
The purpose of the IEC 61850 standard is to propose a design of electrical
substation automation.
This project focuses on the data model for com-
munication. However, the standard also proposes a design for: Reporting
Schemes, Fast Transfer of events, Setting Groups, Sampled Data Transfer,
Commands and Data Storage. [Wik]
The basics of the data model for communication is illustrated in ﬁg. 4.2.
A network (for example a Smart Home) can consist of any number of IEC
61850 devices.
An IEC 61850 device is a physical device that consist of
several general power system components, these components are called
Logical Nodes. The IEC 61850 device is then the only visible entry point for
communication to its logical nodes. [MH].
Figure 4.2: The entire IEC 61850 hierarchy from Standard data types to
Logical Devices. [IEC, p. 20]
Each Logical node has one or more elements of data that are grouped ac-
cording to a service. "Temperature measurement (Tmp)" and "Rate of tem-
perature change (TmpRate)" are grouped as "Measured values". The name
of the data is distinct; however, service groupings like Measured Values are
reused for several logical nodes. The data ﬁelds can be optional, mandatory
or conditional, indicated by "O","M" and "C".
Devices are self-describing, meaning that a client application can request
what data a device provides, and by following the standard know how the

K-means clustering
K-mean clustering is an simple and effective unsupervised machine learn-
ing algorithm for clustering populations into k distinct clusters by James
MacQueen in 1967 [Mac67]. The population can have any number of di-
mensions. The number of clusters k is a parameter of the algorithm.
1. Select k random points from the population as seeds (initial centroids)
2. The rest of the points are assigned to the centroid it is nearest. The
distance calculation could for example be euclidean.

4.7.4
Clustering power consumption
Common for all the calculations of Energy Efﬁciency Rating is that they are
based on how long is an appliance consuming energy at a certain rate. For a
television it is simply how long is the television ON, OFF and on standby. The
washing machine has several ON states, the different program for washing
clothes that consumes energy at certain levels.
This section investigates
using a clustering algorithm for detecting these states.
K-means is one of the simplest clustering algorithms available. See general
description in section 4.7.3. This project investigates how well suited the
k-means clustering algorithm is for detecting distinct power states.
The
following method is suggested:
1. Cluster the power measurements into the most simple states; ON and
(OFF+Standby). This is done by using the k-means algorithm (K1) with
2 Clusters. The order of the measurements have no inﬂuence on the
result.
2. Each point is classiﬁed using the trained k-means clustering (K1) from
1.
in the order they where measured.
The points is grouped into
consecutive ON of OFF+Standby measurement periods. The following
information is saved from each period:
(a) Duration
(b) Average power
(c) Energy Consumption
3. Decide the number of "program groups" the ON states must be divided
into.
4. Decide using appliance domain knowledge what distinguishes the pro-
gram groups from one another.
5. Use a new k-means clustering (K2) on the periods with the information
picked from the analysis done in 4. with k clusters decided in 3.
6. Classify each period with K2, the periods are now classiﬁed into pro-
gram groups.
7. Use EU Energy Label to calculate efﬁciency rating with the programs
groups detected.

Solution Architecture
This solution consist of three components; DERs, data aggregators and vi-
sualization applications.
The connection between them is achieved by a
network, and can exist nay number of each component in the system, if
the hardware and network supports it.
Such systems are called Distri-
bruted Systems and is typically organized as client-server or peer-to-peer
(also called centralized or de-centralized system).
The client-server organization makes a clear distinction between servers,
their services, and who is provider/receiver of those services. This clearly
ﬁts the organisation of Smart Homes and the devices they consists of. The
devices work as servers, and provide services like access to the data from
its sensors. The Data Aggregator both have the role as a server and a client.
==PAGE:86==
68
Design
When it receives services from devices it has the client role. When it pro-
vides aggregated data to the visualization application and/or other aggre-
gators it has the role of a server. The visualization application do also both
have the role of the client and service. When it is access from a client it has
the role as server, and when it receives services from the Data Aggregator it
is a client. This clear respsibility is important in relation to security. It may
be possible to secure data on peer-to-peer systems, however trusting the
data really is secured when scattered across the network is another matter.
The client-server load is distributed unevenly. The server is doing most of
the application processing, data management and may even host a database.
The two-tier architecture is selected over the three-tier architecture for sim-
plicity. Both architectures are well suited for this project. There are 2 ba-
sic types of architectures for the two-tier client-server organization; thin-
client or the fat-client architecture (see ﬁg. 5.1). For this project is selected
the thin-client model. When the software needs an update, it is only the
software running on the servers (few in numbers compared to clients) that
needs to be updated. This makes the solution easier to maintain when the
solution is in production.

5.3.1
Data Exchange Data Model
The Data Model in ﬁg. 5.8 included a small subset of the IEC61850 data-
model standard. The IEC61850 datamodel is best suited for the communica-
tion between the energy resource and the Data Aggregator. The IEC61850
datamodel is however not well suited for the Data Aggregator aggregation
results. This is why custom aggregation classes is needed in the data model,
the EEI, DER and DoubleLabel classes.
EEI is used to calculate energy efﬁciency related data. DER aggregates the
interfaces a device provides and stores this information in a single accessi-
ble object. Based on the interfaces it determine the type, role, and service
of the DER. DoubleLabel is used to store two strings for the table visualiza-
tion. The LabeledMeassurement is used by the Bar and Pie chart. These
class could be avoided by simply serializing an array of two strings and let-
ting the JavaScript parser handle the conversion. However the design of
AngularJS prefers Objects with attribute names.
CompositeMeassurement are CompositeData classes SYSLAB speciﬁc data
model that is inspired by the IEC61850. LabeledMeassurement is designed
for this project and is also inspired by the IEC61850 standard. TSTP and
GPSL are directly from the IEC61850 datamodel standard. Composite Me-
assurement is used for single value measurements and its related attributes.
This class is used for the Gauge and Graph. CompositeBoolean is in this
project used for Control. This is the focus of the thesis by Tobias Brasch ”Ag-
gregator for Smart Grid data with visualization” handed in 1st June 2015.

Application Design Architecture
Model View Control (MVC) is an architectural design pattern for separation
user interface from the model and logic of a application. Both the Visualiza-
tion application in the presentation tier and and the Data Aggregator in the
Server Tier will be designed with this pattern. MVC for the Visualization Ap-
plication is designed using the AngularJs framework extension for HTML5,
see ﬁg. 5.17. MVC design pattern for the the Data Aggregator in the Server
Tier will be designed using ASP.Net MVC Web API, see ﬁg. 5.18.
The combination of HTML5, JavaScript and AngularJS makes it possible to
design web application with a clear MVC architecture. The controller up-
dates the model ($scope) with data from RESTful Http calls the aggregator.
The visualizations deﬁned in the View using reusable AngularJS directives is
data binded to the this model. The D3.js JavaScript Library manipulates to
HTML DOM elements making the visualization in the AngularJS Directives
with the databinded Data. The data received follows the Data Model deﬁned
in ﬁg. 5.8.
The Data Aggregator follows MVC design pattern. The Visualization Appli-
cation can be considered as the view, however no View Designed for the ag-
gregator is developed in this project. The Controller is the WebAPI classes
that Http Requests. The Model is the application functionality calculating
the aggregations and the Data Models it uses (see, section 5.3.1)

Abstract— Smart Homes and the Smart Appliances they con-
sists of are considered to have an important role in improving
the energy efﬁciency of consumer homes. [5] and archive our
energy conservation targets. Improving the energy efﬁciency
is not only a Smart Home automation problem, but making
consumers aware of how efﬁcient their appliance use is just as
important.
This project propose to make collected data from any
appliance connected to the Smart Home available and usable
to the consumer. As proof-of-concept, K-means clustering is
applied on power measurement data collected from a real
washing machine. The usage classiﬁcation is presented using

I. INTRODUCTION
One of the most important ways to stop global warning and
ensure future energy security, is through energy conservation.
As part of the EU ”20-20-20” targets [2] and the Energy
Efﬁciency Plan 2011 [1], the energy conservation target for
the EU is a 20 percent reduction in energy use by 2020
compared to the projected use. Also an indicative target for
EU energy conservation of 27 percent in 2030 has been
endorsed by the European Council [3].
The Smart Home together with the Smart Grid has the
potential to strengthen this shift towards a more efﬁcient use
of energy. But the Smart Home will only really make the
home energy consumption more efﬁcient if it involves the
home owners [4].
Smart Homes consist of several appliances, including
refrigerators, heat pumps and washing machines. It is not
unlikely that the home has DERs like solar panels producing
energy for the household and the grid. Getting the knowledge
and understanding to use appliances and DERs efﬁciently
may be a overwhelming task for the average resident.
The goal is to motivate the residents of Smart Homes to
use energy efﬁciently. This project will investigate a data
aggregation method that enables usable visualisation of home
appliance power consumption. The energy consumption must
be explained using well known concepts. This paper suggests
the Energy Labelling of the country the resident lives in. The
EU introduced its Energy Label in 2005. The goal of the
labelling is to inform consumers about the energy efﬁciency
of the appliances being sold. Other regions have their energy
labelling scheme including the US, China, Singapore, Aus-
tralia. The EU Energy label has been a success and is today
recognised by 80% of the EU citizens [6].
Fig. 1.
The EU Energy Label is composed of; Energy Efﬁciency Rating,
Annual Energy Consumption and product speciﬁc information.
The Energy Label is designed to explain the expected
consumption of an appliance and its efﬁciency based on
appliance speciﬁc assumptions.
II. METHODS
The project is divided into the following problems that
needs to be solved:
• Deduce the usage of an appliance based on its power
consumption.
• Estimate expected Annual Energy Consumption and
efﬁciency based on usage
• Compare the estimate to the EU assumed typical use.
• Explain the consequences the usage
This project propose using the machine learning technique
called K-means clustering on the power metering of the
appliance to deduce its usage. It will be tested on data
collected while a real washing machine washes clothes using
different programs and load of clothes.
A. K-means clustering
K-mean clustering is an simple and effective unsupervised
algorithm for clustering a population of points in dimensional
space Rd into k distinct clusters. The result of the k-means
clustering is k center points for each cluster set.
==PAGE:161==
1) Select k random points from the population as seeds
(initial centroids)
2) The rest of the points are assigned to the centroid it is
nearest. The distance calculation could for example be
euclidean.
3) Each cluster centroid is recalculated by the mean of
every point assigned to its cluster.
4) Step 2-3 is repeated until no point changes nearest
cluster (the clusters converge).
The algorithm is effective compared to other clustering
algorithms. The number of calculations is for the algorithm
is O(iterations·clusters·instances·dimensions) In most
real world applications, the number of iterations is decided
in advance based on the trade-off-decision between accuracy
needed and available computation time available.
B. Data preparation
Data preparation is done using k-means clustering with
k=2. This devides the power meassurements into 2 clusters;
high power and low power meassurements.
1) Cluster the power measurements into the most simple
states; ON and (OFF+Standby). This is done by using
the k-means algorithm (K1) with 2 Clusters. The order
of the measurements have no inﬂuence on the result.
2) Each point is classiﬁed using the trained k-means
clustering (K1) from 1. in the order they where mea-
sured. The points is grouped into consecutive ON
of OFF+Standby measurement periods. The following
information is saved from each period:
a) Duration
b) Average power
c) Energy Consumption
3) Decide the number of ”program groups” the ON states
must be divided into.
4) Decide using appliance domain knowledge what dis-
tinguishes the program groups from one another.
5) Use a new k-means clustering (K2) on the periods with
the information picked from the analysis done in 4.
with k clusters decided in 3.
6) Classify each period with K2, the periods are now
classiﬁed into program groups.
7) Use EU Energy Label to calculate efﬁciency rating
with the programs groups detected.

washing machine is. A washing program takes approx 2
hours, but the most signiﬁcant energy consumption is only
in a duration of approx 15-25 min. The signiﬁcant power
consumption is stable in this duration and starts approx. 10
min after the program starts.
If the appliance should be used for services like shifting
and shedding, this information is useful for scheduling when
the washing machine most efﬁciently is washing clothes in
the context of the Smart Grid.
The system is targeted multiple types of users: Residents,
Scientist, TSOs and DSOs.

to support any future DER that may be a part the Smart
Home. Standards make sure that the user do not need a
new user interface for each DER in his Smart Home. This
solution makes use of only Open Source technologies. Open
Source makes it possible to accelerate the development using
the Open Source Community and provide it to the users for
free. The Hardware needed is a small computer (see ??) that
only cost approximately 600 DKK. Since the only cost is
hardware, the solution is a cheap way to upgrade a home to
a Smart Home.
Presenting the Smart Home improves the insight of the
consumption of each appliance, the home overall and the
future Smart Grid. The presentation of data shows the
possibilities of the Smart Home together with the Smart Grid.

the resident. The DSO and TSO can use these results to
coordinate efﬁciently Smart Home energy consumption in
cooperation with the residents. The third case study showed
that a washing machine consumes almost all energy during
a washing cycle in a short period of approximately 20 min
when the water is heated. When the resident participates in
Smart Grid shifting, this is the period the shifting needs to
use for optimization.
Because the solution is generic and supports visualizing
any IEC61850 data from DERs, it is an efﬁcient tool for the
scientist to perform DER experiments. This makes it possible

New Work Item Proposal 
 
IEC 61850 Part 8-2: Specific communication service mapping (SCSM) –  
Mappings to web-services 
Introduction 
The purpose of this new part is to provide detailed instructions/specification as to the mechanisms and 
rules required to implement the services, objects, and algorithms specified in IEC 61850-7-2, IEC 61850-
7-3, and IEC 61850-7-4 while making use of Web services and other application protocols.  
It will rely on the current IEC 61850 part 8-1 for features not relating to messaging services (for example : 
time synchronisation). 
It will extend/improve the first mapping as proposed by IEC 61400-25 (and specifically the annex A), in 
order to : 
• 
Identify a single profile containing a minimal set of Web service specifications needed to support 
the maximum of services as defined in today’s ACSI. 
• 
Comply with the new edition of IEC 61850-7-1, IEC 61850-7-2, IEC 61850-7-3, and IEC 61850-7-
4 
• 
Support the application data models defined in IEC 61850-7-410 and 7-420 
• 
Take advantage of the new class of standard web-services, including WS-Eventing, which offers 
event driven communication capabilities 
• 
Cover the full life cycle of a IEC 61850 system (from configuration, through conformance testing, 
down to maintenance) 
• 
Identify which web services specification should be considered to deploy cyber-security (in 
conjunction with IEC TC 57 WG 15 work) 
 
It will also define the co-habitation rules with other mappings such as IEC 61850-8-1 and IEC 61850-9-2, 
and possibly recommend communication profiles depending on specific application context (inside 
substation, pole-top equipment, inside DER, connection of DER, …). 
 
 
The proposed mapping is a step toward « plug-and-play », thanks to other capabilities offered by web-
services, however this won’t be formally addressed in the proposed scope of work. 
 
The purpose of this part is NOT to : 
• 
Extend the current ACSI with new services, especially the one referring to more advanced system 
management -> this is the purpose of an other work.  
• 
Modify the services, objects, and algorithms models specified in IEC 61850-7-2, IEC 61850-7-3, 
and IEC 61850-7-4 
• 
Introduce competing web services profiles 
 
The structure of the document will be as close as possible to the existing part 8-1 of IEC 61850 
 
In order to be self implementing, this document will also include as annexes : 
• 
The needed extension to part 5 to handle the additional functional requirements considered 
through this work 
• 
The needed extension to part 6 to handle the configuration part of this mapping (SCL) 
• 
The needed extension to part 7-1 to expose the main principles of this mapping 
• 
The needed extension to part 10 to handle conformance testing 
 
The real integration of this new mapping into the IEC 61850 document structure, and the re-allocation of 
the annexes described above, will occur at the next IEC 61850 full edition release (Edition 3).
