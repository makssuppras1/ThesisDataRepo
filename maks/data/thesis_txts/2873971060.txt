Abstract 
Marine environments, such as the Baltic Sea, are increasingly affected by hypoxia, which is 
driven by stratification, global warming, and mainly eutrophication, which threatens life within 
them. Consequently, benthic and demersal species, which live near the non-oxygen replenished 
bottom water, are most negatively impacted, including the Baltic cod. A recently developed 
method uses the stable isotopic composition of carbon (δ13C) in fish otoliths to estimate the 
field metabolic rate (FMR) in wild fish. The present study aims to apply the otolith-based 
method to investigate whether an effect of mild hypoxia is evident in cod from a controlled 
experimental set-up, which could support future FMR investigations on wild cod. The study 
compares the otolith δ13C derived from cod exposed to mild hypoxia and normoxia. The find-
ings in the present study revealed a significantly lower mean δ13C-value in cod exposed to mild 
hypoxia, which suggests that mild hypoxia will lower the FMR in cod. This is further supported 
by a significantly lower end weight in the otoliths derived from the cod exposed to mild hy-
poxia due to decreased cod growth. The study was unable to obtain direct FMR estimates be-
cause of over-drilling of the otoliths. Therefore, for future studies, it should be considered to 
extend the experimental period, use larger cod and therefore larger otoliths, and/or otolith stain-
ing to avoid over-drilling. However, the otolith-based method shows potential to reveal expo-
sure to mild hypoxia in cod, but more investigations are needed to fully understand the effect. 
 
1. Introduction 
Hypoxia is an increasing problem affecting marine environments worldwide. It has been doc-
umented since the mid-20th century and is expected to increase substantially (Diaz and Rosen-
berg 2008; Schmidtko et al. 2017; Oschlies 2021). Hypoxia occurs when the supply of oxygen 
is insufficient or is consumed at a rate higher than the supply can provide (Diaz 2001). A uni-
versal threshold has not been established, but oxygen concentrations of approx. 2 mg/L or be-
low are considered hypoxic (Vaquer-Sunyer and Duarte 2008). Oxygen is essential for most

life on Earth, which can lead to hypoxia having sublethal or even lethal effects on marine or-
ganisms (Schurmann and Steffensen 1992). In fact, it has been considered one of the primary 
contributors to global biodiversity loss (Borges et al. 2022), as low oxygen concentrations can 
cause habitat compression or loss (Eby et al. 2005; Campbell and Rice 2014), reduced recruit-
ment success (Breitburg 1992), alter trophic structures (Hou et al. 2024), and declining fish 
stocks, ultimately impacting fisheries (Scavia et al. 2024). Therefore, hypoxia represents a con-
siderable threat to life that benefits from the goods and ecosystem services the ocean provides. 
Coastal areas experience the highest oxygen decline rates, with over 500 reported areas having 
≤ 2 mg O2/L (Diaz and Rosenberg 2008; Gilbert et al. 2010; Global Ocean Oxygen Network 
et al. 2018). More than 20% of the reported areas are located within the Baltic Sea in Northern 
Europe (Conley et al. 2011). The Baltic Sea is considered one of the most hypoxia-affected 
areas and the world’s largest anthropogenically induced marine ‘dead zone’ (Breitburg et al. 
2018). The first records of hypoxia in the Baltic Sea date back to the early 20th century. Further, 
the extent of hypoxia increased rapidly in the 1960s and has only worsened in recent years 
(Fonselius and Valderrama 2003; Conley et al. 2009; Carstensen et al. 2014; Krapf et al. 2022). 
The low oxygen concentrations are a major environmental threat to life in the Baltic Sea, which 
is already in a poor ecological state (HELCOM 2023). The low oxygen concentrations are 
mainly explained by a synergistic effect of global warming, stratification, and eutrophication 
(Conley et al. 2009; Carstensen et al. 2014; Krapf et al. 2022). The semi-enclosed, brackish 
Baltic Sea experiences seasonal stratification due to rising temperatures during summer (ther-
mocline), and a permanent stratification related to salinity (halocline), which alters the water 
density (pycnocline) (Snoeijs-Leijonmalm and Andrén 2017). When water is stratified, vertical 
mixing becomes limited, resulting in reduced oxygen transport into deeper layers. Conse-
quently, the bottom layers are more subject to hypoxic conditions. Therefore, the Baltic Sea is 
dependent on irregular major Baltic inflows of saline, oxygen-rich water from the North Sea, 
replenishing the deeper waters alongside vertical mixing (Carstensen et al. 2014; Mohrholz 
2018). 
Over the last four decades, stratification in the Baltic Sea has strengthened alongside increasing 
hypoxia, with global warming being a contributor (Liblik and Lips 2019; Litina et al. 2020). 
As global temperatures increase primarily due to anthropogenic activities, e.g., by accumula-
tion of greenhouse gases from fossil fuels, the ocean absorbs more heat. Consequently, warmer 
water reduces the solubility of oxygen, increases respiration, and, therefore, oxygen

consumption (Carstensen et al. 2014; Arora 2020). However, the latter is primarily due to an 
increase in anthropogenically induced nutrient loadings from agriculture, which enhances pri-
mary and further secondary production, as well as other trophic levels (Meier et al. 2018; HEL-
COM 2023). Consequently, more particulate organic material accumulates at the bottom, 
where the heterotrophic microorganisms consume oxygen during aerobic respiration at a faster 
rate. Therefore, eutrophication is considered the main driver of hypoxia in the Baltic Sea (An-
dersen et al. 2017; Mohrholz 2018; Krapf et al. 2022). 
Hypoxia affects predominantly demersal and benthic species, including one of the key species, 
the Baltic cod (Gadus morhua). Since the mid-1980s, cod in the Baltic Sea have been under 
pressure (Bagge et al. 1994; Eero et al. 2023). Together with declining oxygen concentrations, 
cod in this area are now affected by an increasing number of seals (i.e., high predation pres-
sure), a persistent liver worm parasite, and increasing water temperatures (Casini et al. 2016; 
Limburg and Casini 2019; Eero et al. 2023). 
Cod are affected by hypoxia in various ways and at all life stages. Even at the earliest stage, 
egg survival is at risk, as the salinity required for buoyancy is more frequently within hypoxic 
zones (Vallin et al. 1999; Limburg et al. 2011). Three main direct and indirect impacts on the 
Baltic cod because of hypoxia were identified by Casini et al. (2016): habitat compression 
and/or habitat change, scarcity of benthic food, and direct physiological stress. Together with 
historical fisheries and the above-mentioned factors, hypoxia has likely resulted not only in a 
decline in abundance but also in smaller-sized cod (Casini et al. 2016; Limburg and Casini 
2019; Orio et al. 2022). 
The metabolic rate in fish changes in response to their environment and is a proxy for their 
energy use. Food and stored lipids are converted into energy to sustain basic needs and non-
obligatory activities such as swimming, growth, reproducing, and foraging (Claireaux and 
Chabot 2016; Treberg et al. 2016). This requires oxygen, which is the primary limiting factor 
that governs the metabolic rate (Fry 1971). Studies have reported that hypoxia decreases feed-
ing, digestion, and activity in cod, possibly due to a reduction in the metabolic scope (Chabot 
and Dutil 1999; Herbert and Steffensen 2005; Chabot and Claireaux 2008; Petersen and 
Gamperl 2010). The metabolic scope represents the energy available for the non-obligatory 
activities in cod and is the difference between the individual fish’s standard metabolic rate 
(SMR) and maximum metabolic rate (MMR) (Fry 1971; Claireaux and Chabot 2016). The 
standard metabolic rate is the minimum energy cost required for maintaining homeostasis

during rest and fasting, whereas the MMR is the highest achievable metabolic rate in the fish. 
A reduction in the metabolic scope results in less energy available for the non-obligatory ac-
tivities, as energy is focused on sustaining basic needs for survival. Therefore, a reduction in 
the metabolic scope with time can result in reduced growth and reproduction. 
The metabolic rate is commonly measured with respirometry under controlled laboratory ex-
periments, usually investigating one or a few influencing factors at a time (Chabot et al. 2016). 
However, in the wild, cod are exposed to varying factors impacting their metabolism simulta-
neously, such as temperature, prey abundance, and oxygen. Therefore, it is of interest to inves-
tigate the energy expenditure under natural conditions, referred to as the field metabolic rate 
(FMR) (Treberg et al. 2016). The field metabolic rate is the sum of the three metabolic com-
ponents: SMR, specific dynamic action (SDA, energy cost of digestion, absorption, and me-
tabolism of food), and activity metabolism (energy cost of various behaviors such as swim-
ming, foraging, and predator avoidance) (Chabot et al. 2016; Nelson 2016; Treberg et al. 2016; 
Chung et al. 2019b). 
Measuring the FMR in marine environments is challenging, but not impossible (Butler et al. 
2004; Treberg et al. 2016). A recently developed method by Chung et al. (2019b) uses the 
stable isotopic composition of carbon (δ13C) in fish otoliths to estimate the FMR. Otoliths play 
a crucial role in the hearing and balance of teleost fishes. A layer forms daily that consists 
mainly of a calcium carbonate structure (CaCO3) and a protein matrix. As the layer is formed, 
the chemical composition becomes inert, reflecting the environmental setting and the fish’s 
physiological state at the time of formation (Degens et al. 1969; Pannella 1971; Campana and 
Neilson 1985; Campana 1999). 
The carbon in the otoliths originates from metabolically derived carbon from the cellular res-
piration of the diet (typically <20%) and dissolved inorganic carbon (DIC) from the ambient 
water (typically >80%) obtained through the gills and intestinal interfaces. The carbon is in-
corporated into the otoliths as it enters through the endolymph in the inner ear from the blood-
stream (Figure 1). The δ13C from the two sources are distinguishable in the otoliths, as the DIC 
in the water is higher in 13C, while metabolic processes of the diet favour 12C (Kroopnick 1985; 
Kalish 1991; McConnaughey et al. 1997; Solomon et al. 2006). The otolith-based method uses 
the δ13C from the otoliths, diet, and ambient water to calculate the proportion of the respired 
(metabolic) carbon in the otolith that can then be used to estimate the oxygen consumption

representing the time-integrated FMR for the individual fish (Chung et al. 2019b; Chung et al. 
2019a).

This study aims to apply the otolith-based method by Chung et al. (2019b) on cod exposed to 
mild hypoxia in laboratory facilities to investigate if it is detectable and thereby can serve as a 
verification for future FMR studies on wild fish. Further, the potential of using the method to 
reveal mild hypoxic exposure in wild fish is evaluated. A comparison is made between otolith 
δ13C-values derived from cod exposed to mild hypoxia and normoxia in a controlled experi-
ment. It is hypothesized that exposure to mild hypoxic conditions will lower the FMR in cod. 
 
2. Materials and methods 
2.1 Controlled experiment with cod in normoxia and mild hypoxia 
The otoliths analysed in this study were obtained from cod used in a controlled laboratory 
experiment conducted by PhD student Magnus Højen Husen. Wild Baltic cod were caught in 
September 2024 near Copenhagen Harbor in the Western Baltic Sea and transported to the Fish 
Biology Experimental Facility in Lyngby. After four weeks of acclimating to artificial seawater 
in laboratory facilities, the cod were divided into one of two treatments: mild hypoxia or

2.2 Otolith preparation for δ13C and δ18O analysis 
The left otoliths were identified to maintain consistency for the δ13C and δ18O analysis. How-
ever, four left otoliths were missing and were thereby replaced with their corresponding right 
otoliths. Furthermore, two broken right otoliths replaced their corresponding intact left otoliths. 
In total, ninety left and six right otoliths were used for the analysis. 
The surface of the otoliths was thoroughly cleaned following previous methodology (Chung et 
al. 2019b). More specifically, the otoliths were placed separately in test tubes with 250 mL of 
0.2 M NaOH for 30 minutes, followed by a two-minute ultrasonic bath. Subsequently, an elec-
tric toothbrush was used to remove the dislodged contaminants. The otoliths were then rinsed 
three times with MilliQ water and dried for 48 hours at 40°C. The otoliths ranged from 9.65 
mm to 12.20 mm in length and 82 mg to 158 mg in weight, see Appendix 1.  
The δ13C and δ18O analyses required that the outer layers of the otoliths, representing the time 
in the experiment, were drilled into powder. The convex surface of the otoliths was glued to 
microscope glass slides with a 54°C flow point Crystalbond 555. The otoliths were hand drilled 
using a Foredom High Speed Rotary Micromotor with a 0.7 mm diameter cylindrical milling 
cutter under a microscope. Otolith powder was gained from drilling the sides and the concave 
surface of the otolith. The powder was transferred to foil for weighing before being transferred 
to 5.9 mL vials. The analysis required at least 0.06 mg of otolith powder. 
The drilling process of the otoliths was not free of complications and involved some degree of 
errors. In such events, everything was documented and can be divided into three categories: 1) 
multiple drills on the same otolith, 2) otolith breakage during drilling, and 3) otolith powder 
contaminated by the glue. Half and one third of the otoliths in the normoxic and hypoxic treat-
ments, respectively, exhibited at least one of the three mentioned errors and complications.

2.3 Water preparation for δ13C analysis 
Artificial seawater was collected from the inlet pipes before reaching the tanks in the labora-
tory. Four 1 mL samples were passed through a 0.22 µm filter before being transferred to 5.9 
mL vials with a syringe. As for the otolith samples, international standard samples of NBS18, 
NBS19, and LSVEC were prepared, but diluted with MilliQ water. For mass correction and 
instrumental drift, Na2CO3 diluted with MilliQ water was used. Prior to this, all vials had been 
flushed with helium, and 0.5 mL 85% phosphoric acid had been added. The analysis was per-
formed at least 24 hours after the samples had been prepared. Note, the method described in 
the present section was not performed by the author. 
 
2.4 Sandeel preparation for δ13C analysis 
Ten sandeels from the cod’s diet were randomly selected, and muscle samples from both sides 
of the sandeel were dissected, see Appendix 2. One side of the sandeel was left to dry for 48 
hours at 35°C, whereas the other side was used for lipid extraction. The lipid extraction was 
carried out by adding 3 mL of 2:1 chloroform/methanol to each sample. The solution was 
drained after 19 hours, before another 3 mL of 2:1 chloroform/methanol was added and left for 
four hours. Subsequently, the drained samples were left to dry for 20 hours. The dried muscle 
samples from both sides of the sandeels were ground into powder and transferred to tin capsules

2.5 δ13C and δ18O analysis 
The δ13C and δ18O analyses were carried out using a Thermo Scientific Delta V Plus IRMS 
(IRMS) connected to an Isodat software suite for data reporting in delta notation relative to the 
VPDB (Vienna Pee Dee Belemnite). The IRMS measures CO2 with masses of 44, 45, 46 g/mol, 
where 45/44 is a proxy for δ13C, and 46/44 is a proxy for δ18O (Thermo Fisher Scientific 2009). 
The software produces three reference isotope ratio peaks, followed by three peaks from the 
samples. The third sample peak is used for the data processing.  
The IRMS was equipped with a GasBench II while analysing CO2 in the headspace of the vials 
of the otolith and water samples. Three analytical sequences were conducted: two each with 
randomly mixed otolith samples and one with the water samples. The international and in-
house standards were incorporated into the sequences. Due to air contamination in the first 
sequence of the otolith samples, the contaminated samples were re-analysed. 
The δ13C in the sandeel samples was analysed using the IRMS equipped with an Element An-
alyzer. The samples with the tin capsules were combusted, and the released CO2 was measured. 
One analytical sequence of randomly mixed sandeel samples with the isotope reference mate-
rial incorporated was conducted. 
 
2.6 Data handling 
The analysed otolith δ13C and δ18O-values from the IRMS were mass and drift corrected using 
the international and in-house standards. This was carried out in R (version 4.4.1) within the 
RStudio environment (version 2025.05.1+513) with a script written by PhD student Jonathan 
Christensen. Each analytical otolith sequence was corrected, and all data were merged for data 
processing. Four outliers in the δ13C and δ18O data were removed using the Interquartile Range 
(IQR) method with a range of 1.5xIQR. In total, the results from 92 otoliths ended up being 
used in the present study. 
The international and in-house standards were used to correct the analysed water and sandeel 
δ13C-values. No marginal difference was found between δ13C-values in the water samples.

2.8 Statistical analysis 
R within RStudio was used for all statistical analyses. Prior to the final analyses of the data, 
supplementary statistical analyses were performed to evaluate the potential effects of con-
founding factors such as complications and errors during the drilling and the performed drilling 
order, see Appendix 3-4. 
The experimental set-up resulted in a nested data structure. Therefore, for the final analyses of 
the δ13C and δ18O-values from the otoliths, nested ANOVAs were used to assess for between-
tank variation in the means. Further, one-way ANOVAs were used to assess for differences 
between the means of the two treatments. The underlying assumptions of normally distributed 
data and homogeneity of variances were evaluated using Shapiro-Wilk tests and Levene’s tests, 
respectively. To improve normal distribution and homogeneity of variances, the δ13C-values 
were transformed using a cube root transformation. 
A linear regression model was used to analyse the effect of the total cod growth during the 
experimental period and the two treatments on the end weight of the otoliths. The otolith end 
weight was the dependent variable, and the treatment and cod growth were the independent 
variables. Broken otoliths were excluded from the test. Initially, the interaction between the 
treatments and cod growth was included in the model to test whether the correlation between 
cod growth and the otolith end weight differed between the two treatments. However, due to 
insignificance (p > 0.05), the interaction was removed, and a simplified additive linear model 
was used. Shapiro-Wilk tests and Levene’s tests were used to check the model assumptions.

4. Discussion 
It was hypothesised that mild hypoxia would lower the FMR in cod in the controlled experi-
ment. The results revealed a significantly higher mean δ13C-value in cod exposed to mild hy-
poxia compared to those exposed to normoxia (Figure 4), which supports the hypothesis. This 
is further supported by the otoliths revealing a significantly lower end weight under mild hy-
poxic conditions (Figure 8), as otolith growth increases with metabolic rate and somatic growth 
(Hüssy and Mosegaard 2004; Svedäng et al. 2020). To the author’s knowledge, the present 
study is the first of such investigations. 
 
4.1 The effect of mild hypoxia on the FMR in cod 
Since the findings in the present study suggest that FMR is decreased in cod exposed to mild 
hypoxia, it must indicate that the MMR and at least one of the three metabolic components – 
SMR, SDA, and activity – are reduced. Martino et al. (2020) found that with increasing tem-
peratures, the δ13C-values in otoliths from juvenile Australasian snapper decreased, and SMR 
and MMR increased. The same logic might apply to the findings in the present study, replacing 
temperature with oxygen. In Atlantic cod, MMR decreases with declining oxygen (Claireaux 
et al. 2000; Jordan and Steffensen 2007). Additionally, the SMR in Atlantic cod was found to 
decrease in both acute and chronic hypoxia by Petersen and Gamperl (2010). However, despite 
the findings in the latter study, SMR is more likely determined by body mass and temperature 
and remains stable with declining oxygen until the critical oxygen saturation is reached (Schur-
mann and Steffensen 1997; Chabot and Claireaux 2008; Tirsgaard et al. 2015; Rosewarne et 
al. 2016). Therefore, it is more probable that under mild hypoxia, as in the present study, δ13C-
values increase, MMR decreases, and SMR remains stable. 
A reduction in SDA and activity is more likely to cause a decrease in the FMR. The higher 
mean δ13C-value in otoliths derived from cod exposed to mild hypoxia can indicate a reduced 
incorporation of metabolically derived carbon from the diet into the otoliths (Figure 5). This 
finding aligns with the decreased food consumption during the experiment, which was also 
reported in Atlantic cod with declining oxygen concentrations by Chabot and Dutil (1999) and 
implied by Brander (2020). This, together with the fact that there is a positive relationship 
between energy in the meal and SDA according to Secor (2009), suggests that the SDA is 
reduced by mild hypoxia. Further, a study by Jordan and Steffensen (2007) found that under 
hypoxic conditions, the SDA in Atlantic cod was significantly depressed and prolonged, 
==PAGE:24==
Master’s Thesis 
 
22nd of August 2025 
Page 20 of 35 
 
possibly due to a slower gastric evacuation (GE). A slower GE was also observed at 55% ox-
ygen saturation at 6⸰C by Chabot et al. (2015), which is similar to the oxygen saturation for 
mild hypoxia in the present study, yet at 12⸰C. Comparing these findings to those of the present 
experiment supports the notion that mild hypoxia has a similar impact on the SDA in cod. 
However, this response might only occur during chronic exposure, since experimental data 
suggest that digestion is not influenced by acute exposure to hypoxia (Behrens et al. 2012). 
The standard dynamic action occupies a large fraction of the available energy, as found by 
Jordan and Steffensen (2007), leaving less energy for other energy-requiring activities. In ad-
dition, declining oxygen concentrations have been found to impair the swimming (Schurmann 
and Fleng Steffensen 1994; Herbert and Steffensen 2005; Skjæraasen et al. 2008) and growth 
in cod (Chabot and Dutil 1999), where the latter was evident in the present study (Figure 8). 
Nevertheless, linking the otolith δ13C to measured metabolic components during the experi-
ment may have provided a better basis for interpreting the effect of mild hypoxia on the FMR. 
 
4.2 Limitations in estimating the FMR based on the results 
The suggestion that mild hypoxia decreases FMR in cod is based on the analysed otolith δ13C-
values, which show higher values in the mild hypoxic treatment and hence suggest a lower 
proportion of respiratory carbon in the otolith. Estimating the FMRs were not possible, since 
the requirements were not met, see Figure 5 (Chung et al. 2019b). More specifically, most 
otolith δ13C-values overlapped the mean δ13C-value of the ambient water and did not fall be-
tween the diet and the ambient water δ13C-values. The only plausible explanation for this is 
that the drilled temporal average in the otoliths includes time before the experiment. Hence, 
excessive layers in the otoliths were drilled off, and the otolith δ13C-values reflect a combined 
result of carbon incorporated during the experiment and in the wild. 
Given this, the δ13C-values from the diet and the ambient water should have represented both 
the time in the experiment and the wild. In the Baltic Sea, δ13C of the water varies between 
+2‰ and -6.5‰ depending on the season, location, and depth (Filipsson et al. 2017; Torniainen 
et al. 2017), which exceeds the mean value of -6.72‰ from the experimental tanks. From Fig-
ure 5, it appears that the δ13C-values in the Baltic Sea elevate the δ13C-values in the otoliths. 
This might have been avoided if natural seawater had been used in the experiment rather than 
artificial seawater, which is shielded from natural physical and biological processes that alter 
==PAGE:25==
Master’s Thesis 
 
22nd of August 2025 
Page 21 of 35 
 
the DIC (Thomas and Schneider 1999; Filipsson et al. 2017; Relitti et al. 2020). However, in 
the context of the present study, the over-drilling of the otoliths might not have been detected. 
The mean δ13C-value of -20.86‰ from the sandeel diet is slightly lower but close to δ13C-
values measured in previous studies (Käkelä et al. 2007; Jansen et al. 2013). Despite this, sub-
stituting the sandeels in the experiment with a more typical diet of the Baltic cod, e.g., brown 
shrimp, Gobiidae, sprat, and herring (Jacobsen et al. 2023) might have been more representa-
tive. Additionally, the δ13C of the soluble organic matter (SOM) in the otoliths could have been 
used, as it reflects the diet of the temporal average (Grønkjær et al. 2013; Sirot et al. 2017). 
However, the feasibility of the method for the present study can be argued, as the amount of 
SOM required appears to be equivalent to the amount of the drilled otolith powder. 
Nevertheless, it is assumed that the same amount of otolith powder has been drilled off across 
all otoliths, regardless of whether the cod had been in mild hypoxia or normoxia. However, the 
otolith powder from the two treatments might reflect different temporal averages, which is 
supported by Figure 7. δ18O in otoliths is in near-equilibrium with the ambient water and is 
driven by temperature (Thorrold et al. 1997; Høie et al. 2004b). Had the same number of layers 
been drilled off, the mean δ18O-values would have been equal, as all cod experienced similar 
temperatures in the laboratory and were randomly assigned to the treatments (normoxia or mild 
hypoxia). The difference in the δ18O-values suggests that slightly different proportions of pre-
experiment and experiment otolith material were drilled off the two treatments. Possibly, more 
layers have been drilled off the otoliths derived from mild hypoxia, as the end weights were 
significantly lower compared to the ones from normoxia (Figure 8). This is possibly due to 
otolith growth being proportional to cod growth, and the cod in mild hypoxia grew less. Since 
the δ18O-value from the water in the experiment was not yet measured, it is not possible to 
detect in which treatment most layers have been drilled off. 
 
4.3 The potential of the otolith-based method and future considerations 
Applying the otolith-based method for FMR estimations to laboratory experiments comes with 
strengths and limitations. Such studies are limited by the experimental period and otolith sizes, 
as was proven by the present study. In future studies, it should be considered to extend the 
experimental period for as long as possible. By doing so, it minimises the risk of over-drilling 
the otoliths due to the formation of more layers. In addition, the layers will only represent 
carbon incorporated during the experiment. However, using, e.g., natural seawater or a more 
==PAGE:26==
Master’s Thesis 
 
22nd of August 2025 
Page 22 of 35 
 
typical diet of the cod might allow for a more direct comparison between δ13C obtained in 
laboratory conditions and natural conditions. 
Sometimes, circumstances do not allow for a long experimental period, and the use of larger 
cod or alizarin red S (ARS) for otolith staining before the experiment might be favourable. The 
ARS would create a visible benchmark for when to stop drilling (Beckman and Schulz 1996), 
whereas larger cod have larger otoliths, which can allow for the use of a micromill for greater 
accuracy and precision instead of a handheld drill (Høie et al. 2004a). Managing the trade-off 
between the drilling of the layers using a handheld drill and achieving enough powder for the 
δ13C analysis presented a significant challenge. Consequently, complications and errors were 
unavoidable. Nevertheless, the errors did not cause any significant difference within the two 
treatments (Appendix 3). On the contrary, the non-randomised order of the performed drilling 
did. The otoliths derived from cod not subject to respirometry were drilled first, followed by 
the ones that were used in respirometry. A significant difference between these two groups was 
found within the normoxia treatment (Appendix 4). It appears that the otoliths from cod in 
respirometry might have contributed to a lowering of the total δ13C for the normoxic treatment, 
resulting in the significant difference between the two treatments (Figure 4). It can be argued 
that the drilling technique was improved when drilling the otoliths from normoxia (subject to 
respiration). Therefore, if the drilling was randomised, the normoxia (no respiration) would 
likely also have obtained lower δ13C-values. 
Nevertheless, it might be necessary to compare different life stages in such studies due to dif-
ferences in metabolism, diet, and habitats. This is also reflected in the δ13C-values, as juvenile 
cod have more negative values compared to adult cod (Jamieson et al. 2004). However, know-
ing that mild hypoxia is evident in the otolith δ13C can help interpret future FMR estimations. 
This is especially given the current state of the Baltic Sea, with increasing extents of hypoxia 
(HELCOM 2023; Martin and Viktorsson 2024). The otolith-based method by Chung et al. 
(2019b) has the potential to reveal mild hypoxia in fish. Yet, comparing the findings in otolith 
δ13C from cod in experiments with wild cod should be done with careful consideration. To fully 
understand the effect of mild hypoxia on FMR in cod, further investigations are required, which 
could include measuring SMR, SDA, and activity in laboratory facilities. Having said this, the 
present study does indeed advance our knowledge of FMR and the effects of hypoxia.

5. Conclusion 
This study aimed to investigate if the effect of mild hypoxia is detectable in cod used in a 
controlled experiment using the otolith-based method by Chung et al. (2019b) for FMR esti-
mations, which could serve as a verification for future FMR investigations of wild fish. Based 
on a significantly lower mean otolith δ13C in cod exposed to mild hypoxia for six weeks com-
pared to those that had been in normoxia, i.e., controls, it is suggested that mild hypoxia will 
decrease the FMR in cod. This is further supported by a lower end weight of the otoliths derived 
from the cod exposed to mild hypoxia, which relates to the lower cod growth. It is most likely 
that MMR, SDA, and activity are the cause of the decreased FMR, whereas SMR remains 
constant. It was not possible to obtain direct estimates of the FMRs as most of the otolith δ13C-
values did not fall within the range of the mean δ13C-value of the sandeel diet and ambient 
water. This is most likely due to over-drilling of the otoliths, resulting in the temporal average 
including both time during and before the experiment. It appears that the δ13C-values in the 
otoliths are elevated due to higher water δ13C-values in the Baltic Sea compared to the water 
in the experiment. From the δ18O-analysis, it could indicate that the otolith powder from the 
two treatments reflects different temporal averages, due to significantly different means. Pos-
sibly, more layers have been drilled off the otoliths from the mild hypoxic treatment because 
of lower growth. However, since the δ18O in the ambient water in the experiment has not yet 
been measured, it is at this stage not possible to interpret this further. The study was limited by 
the duration of the experimental period and the size of the otoliths. Errors and complications 
occurred while drilling, but this did not seem to have a significant effect on the result. However, 
the non-randomised drilling order could potentially cause the significant result found between 
the δ13C-values of the two treatments. Yet, it is assumed the same results would have been 
found if the drilling had been performed randomly. For future studies, it should be considered 
either to extend the experimental period for the otoliths to develop more layers, use larger cod 
as they have larger otoliths, which allow the use of a micromill, or use otolith staining. Further, 
differentiate between different life stages due to differences in metabolism, diet, and habitats, 
and use natural seawater and a more dominant diet in the experimental set-up. Nevertheless, 
the otolith-based method has potential in revealing mild hypoxia in wild fish. However, further 
investigations are needed to fully understand the effect.
