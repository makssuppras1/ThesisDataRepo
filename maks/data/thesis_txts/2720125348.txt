Abstract
A new form of magnetic levitation was discovered in 2021 [1], which can be accomplished in two steps.
First, spin a magnet (rotor magnet), with a magnetisation perpendicular to the axis of rotation, at speeds
of around 10000 RPM. Then by placing a smaller magnet nearby (floater magnet), this will levitate a few
centimeters below the rotor magnet. The purpose of this work is to answer some fundamental questions
about the phenomenon and collect and organise hints for the mechanism.
Using an experimental setup with the rotor magnet spinning above the floater magnet, several aspects of
the phenomenon were investigated.
By painting the north pole of both magnets, it was shown that the floater magnet precesses with a small tilt
from vertical, so the north poles of both magnets are parallel.
By changing the speed of which the rotor magnet spins (rotor speed), several things can be concluded.
First of all 4 different types of instabilities have been observed with regions of which no instabilities occur.
These are:
• Up-down motion
• Circular movement
• Mix between Up-down and circular modes
• U-shaped mode
It has been seen that the equilibrium levitation distance changes over time with a linear fall rate that de-
creases with rotor speed. Additionally, the minimum levitation distance decreases with rotor speed. The
levitation time for each of these speeds has been measured. Generally, a lower fall rate results in a larger
levitation time and instabilities causing large drops in levitation time. The instabilities generally grow
faster at high speeds resulting in an effective maximum speed.
The lowest speed levitation could occur at, has been measured for magnets of different magnetisation keep-
ing the size constant. From that it could be concluded that increasing magnetisation increases the lowest
speed. Likewise, the lowest speed has been measured for magnets of different sizes keeping the magneti-
sation constant. Here it was shown that larger magnets could levitate at lower speeds.
The component of the floater magnet’s magnetic field, perpendicular to the rotation axis has been mea-
sured using a pickup coil. This disproved one possible mechanism for the phenomenon involving eddy
currents.
In addition to the experiments, the system has been simulated using dipole-dipole forces and rotations.
From these it was possible to conclude that the phenomenon requires the inclusion of additional terms
such as damping or eddy currents.
The magnetic levitation has yet to be understood, with unanswered questions, such as why the two mag-
nets synchronise with north poles parallel. But in this work many results are laid clear for future models
to reproduce.

0.1.2
Levitation technologies that overcome Earnshaw’s theorem
Paramagnetism, diamagnetism and superconductors
Earnshaw’s theorem only considered hard fixed magnetic materials [5, 6]. Paramagnetism and diamag-
netism are dynamic and change with the external field. Paramagnetism aligns with the field and therefore
moves towards the field maxima from equation (4), whereas diamagnetism is antialligned with the mag-
netic field creating a repelling force, allowing the possibility of a local minima [5, 6]. It is possible to levitate
objects as large as a frog using diamagnetism. But due to the low strength of diamagnetism it is only pos-
sible to lift small objects in even the strongest of fields, making it impractical for most applications.
Levitation using a superconductor is also possible for the same reason, because a superconductor can be
seen as a perfect diamagnet [5, 8]. But type 2 superconductors are even more suited for magnetic levitation
due to a phenomenon called flux-pinning allowing increased stability [3]. Due to the perfect diamagnetic
properties of a superconductor they are much stronger than non superconducting diamagnets with the
drawback that superconductors require cryogenic cooling.
Another way of achieving levitation is through dynamical means.
Active magnetic stabilisation
This could be done through active magnetic levitation systems. Here you use coils and precise control sys-
tems to keep the levitation well balanced. An example of this is the magnetically levitated planar actuator
that can freely levitate and be moved around in a 2D plane, and could be of great use for high precision
machines that also need to be isolated from vibrations [4, 9]. In this case, an array of 72 coils are distributed
below the levitating Halbach array. Each of these coils will apply a force and a torque on the Halbach array
when current runs through the coil. The forces and torques for each of these coils are then characterised
individually and combined together in a complete characterisation of the system. The system then uses six
single-input–single-output (SISO) quasi-proportional–integral–differential (PID) controllers to determine
the current through each coil needed to move or keep the Halbach array at the position of interest.
In this category we also find active magnetic bearing currently used for flywheel energy storage [10]. Just
as in a planar actuator, the active magnetic bearings uses a system of coils in combination with controllers
to actively stabilise the system [10]. These active magnetic stabilisation systems generally have all the same

Maglev trains
The most well-known and commercially successful levitation technology is probably the Maglev trains.
Here there are three main types of levitation, Electromagnetic suspension (EMS), Electrodynamic suspen-
sion (EDS) and Hybrid electromagnetic suspension (HEMS). Each of these have their own advantages and
disadvantages but currently the one most suitable for high speed and freight uses is the EDS [2]. Maglev
trains can both use permanent magnets and superconductors to achieve levitation, but due to supercon-
ductors being able to produce higher fields than permanent magnets, superconductor-based designs have
the best performance. EDS designs have coils on the guideway and as the train moves the magnetic field
induces a current in the coils producing a repulsive force that increases with the train speed. This results in
the levitation height changing with train speed and at speeds below 100 km/h it does not levitate and uses
its rubber wheels [2]. Propulsion and guidance of the maglev trains are electromagnetic but, unlike most
other active magnetic levitation technologies, it does not require a control system to have stable levitation
and levitates if the speed is high enough.

The technology most like the kind of levitation investigated in
this work is the spin stabilised levitation. The levitron is a toy
that perfectly illustrates the principles of spin stabilised levi-
tation. The levitron is built up of two parts, an axisymmet-
ric spinning top with a magnetic ring and a magnetic stator
with a particular magnetic field shape [11, 12]. There are two
types of levitron devices but the principle remains the same
for both and only the vertical axis shown in figure 2 levit-
ron will be explained in this work.
Here the levitron spins
around its vertical axis at speeds around 500 RPM [13] and
experiences a magnetic repulsive force from the magnetic sta-
tor with a ring shaped magnet. This pushes the spinning top
upward and applies a torque on the spinning top, due to the
gyroscopic effect the spinning top does not escape and only
starts to precess. The system is stable under precession and
levitation only becomes unstable once the spinning top slows
down.

Previous work on the magnetic levitation phenomenon
The newly discovered levitation phenomenon has some clear differences to each of the other levitation
previously discussed. It levitates a ferromagnet with the use of another ferromagnet spinning at high
speeds. It does not use superconducters and therefore can operate without the use of cryogenics. In-
stead it needs angular speeds in the order of 10000 RPM to produce the levitation phenomenon. 20 times
larger speeds than the levitron. It most likely will not replace Maglev trains since it has drastically dif-
ferent strengths and weakness. But due to its different nature it can lead to new types of innovations.

But since this is the only article on the topic, there are still many unanswered questions. Here is a list of

• Why do the magnets synchronise in a parallel alignment?
• What exactly is the mechanism behind the phenomenon? What are the lift and repulsive forces?
• How does the levitation stability and distance change with motor speed?
• What kinds of instabilities can cause levitation to cease and when do they occur?
• How does the magnet size and magnetisation affect the levitation?
• Does the fast spinning of the rotor produce noticeable eddy currents in the floater magnet?
• How does magnet shape affect levitation?
• What is the phase between the floater and rotor magnet? Does the floater magnet just precess or is

• Is it possible to produce the phenomenon in a simulation, and what terms are needed?
These are many important questions and during this work many of these questions will be tackled. Though

Chapter 1
Experiments
The Chapter will begin with the experimental methods. Showing the experimental setup and how the ex-
periments are conducted. Then the video processing needed to analyse the experiments is shown. Lastly
the rotor magnet is characterised, finding its tilt from horizontal, phase shift and the skewness of the setup
with regards to gravity.
The next part of the chapter shows the experimental results of 5 different experiments.
The first experiment looks at how the rotor speed changes the levitation. Three key aspects that are shown
to change with rotor speed are the levitation distance, levitation time and large periodic motion patterns,
such as the magnet moving up and down or swirling around.
The second experiment takes a look at the low speed region of the first experiment where no modes occur.
Here it can clearly be seen that the equilibrium height of levitation changes over time. This falling rate of
the equilibrium height is investigated and shown how it changes with rotor speed.
The third experiment investigates the lowest speed at which levitation can occur for magnets of different
sizes and magnetisations.
In the fourth experiment the north pole of the floater and rotor magnet have been painted. By filming
the painted magnets levitating with a high speed camera, it is possible to investigate the synchronisation
between the rotor and floater magnet. Here the floater magnet is shown to precess in phase with the rotor
magnet.
In the last experiment a hypothesis of eddy currents was tested. Here it was tested whether the strength
of the eddy currents in the floater magnet, produced by a rapidly spinning rotor magnet is larger than the
component of the floater magnet perpendicular to the rotation axis.

1. Start motor at the desired speed.

1.2.2
Falling rate investigation
During the experiments it was observed that there are two ways levitation ceases
1. The levitation distance gradually increases until the floater gently drops off.
2. The oscillations of the modes grow out of control. When the amplitude increases the restoring forces
weakens leading to larger amplitudes until the restoring force isn’t strong enough and the floater
drops.
In this section the gradual decrease in levitation height is investigated. The low-speed region with no
modes is studied, as the gradual increase in levitation distance dominates this region. The position of the
floater magnet is fitted to a linear curve to find the fall rate. This is done for each experiment between 142.5
Hz and 180 Hz. A few of the fits are shown in figures 1.14 and the rest of the fits in appendix section 3.4.1.

1.2.5
Eddy currents in the floater magnet
While considering the mechanisms needed for the magnets to synchronise as found in using the painted
magnets in section 1.2.4, one possible mechanism for synchronisation is through the generation of eddy
currents in the floater magnet. The idea behind this is that since the rotor magnet is creating a rapidly
changing magnetic field, this field can then induce an eddy current in the well-conducting floater magnet.
Due to the symmetry of the system, it is expected that the eddy current will produce a magnetic dipole with
a direction anti-parallel to the rotor magnet. Since the floater magnet is shown to move with a component
parallel to the rotor these will cancel each other out. The exact cancellation depends heavily on the relative
size of the two magnet dipoles. This experiment assesses the hypothesis by measuring which of the two
are largest. The experiment is based on the setup described in section 1.1.1, with the addition of a pickup
coil and an oscilloscope as can be seen in figure 1.27. The coil is placed so it measures the change in
magnetisation perpendicular to the rotation axis. With the height chosen to maximize the signal of the
floater magnet.

Simulation summary
In this work a dipole-dipole model for the system has been constructed and tested. The dipole-dipole
model contains two parts: Force calculations to update the position and torque calculations to rotate the
floater magnet. These two parts have been implemented separately and then combined together to produce
the full simulations. Each of these two parts have been tested with test cases such as magnet moving along
the magnetic field lines and magnetic precession. And the separate implementations have been compared
to limit cases of the full simulation with great success.
Even though the simulations seem to be correctly implemented, the simulations differ from the experi-
ments quite dramatically. When the position of the floater magnet is locked and it is given time to syn-
chronise with the rotor magnet it will always ends up synchronising in an anti parallel fashion. Without
having seen the phenomenon this is also what could be expected, but it has clearly been shown in section
1.2.4, that the magnets have parallel components in the plane perpendicular to the rotation axis. In the full
simulations with the magnet allowed to freely move, the floater magnet simply gets attracted to the rotor
magnet and they collide after 0.01s.
These results seem to be consistent across many simulations run with different parameters and initial con-
ditions, without any qualitative difference.
Since the simulations seem to be correctly implemented, but still cannot produce the phenomenon it might
be due to something essential missing in the dipole-dipole model. The three main candidates for this are:
1. Angular momentum of a magnetic moment.
2. Electrodynamics such as eddy currents.
3. Inclusion of damping.
These will be discussed in section 3.1.

Chapter 3
Physics of the phenomenon
In this chapter the results from both the experiments and simulations will be combined together to give
an overview of the current hypotheses and what can be ruled out. Afterwards the work is concluded and
finished with what future work is needed.
3.1
Hypotheses behind the mechanism
Simulating dipole-dipole magnet interactions could not reproduce the two key parts of the phenomenon
in this work. First of all the synchronisation found using the dipole model showed the magnets synchro-
nising together antiparallel to each other. Naively this is what could be expected, but it is shown clearly
in the experiments that the magnets synchronise parallel to each other. Second of all it was not possible
to levitate or produce any qualitative resemblance of the phenomenon using full simulations of forces and
torques. The simulations passed all tests in limit cases and against simple known solutions. This hints that
something important for the phenomenon is not part of the simulations. Before moving to the possible
missing terms, it is important to note that a key part of the phenomenon is the parallel synchronisation.
In Ucar’s paper [1], he is able to explain the vertical force balance assuming the parallel synchronisation
and that the precession angle θ ∝
1
rx , where x > 0. This can be derived from the vertical forces between
two dipoles, assuming a small tilt in the rotor magnet direction or the centre of the rotor magnet being
shifted from the rotation axis. Then by rewriting the terms in static components and components that have
a phase difference between the floater and the rotor magnet, attractive and repulsive forces appear. This
only explains the vertical force balance and requires θ to increase as the floater magnet moves closer to the
rotor magnet. But still all of this requires the unintuitive parallel synchronisation. Below three hypotheses
will be presented in order to explain the origins of the parallel synchronisation.
Hypothesis 1. Angular momentum of a magnetic moment
Magnetic moments have an angular momentum given by m = γL, where γ is the gyromagnetic ratio of an
electron with γ = −1.76 · 1011rads−1T−1. The reason why this extra term for angular momentum could be
36
==PAGE:41==
Joachim H.
Master’s Project
Feb 2023
interesting is that it has a component perpendicular to the mechanical angular moment. Additionally due
to precession this term constantly changes direction of it’s angular moment resulting in a non-static impact
on the motion. But due to the size of the gyromagnetic ratio the term ends up being around a factor of a
million smaller than the other terms. Additionally it has been implemented in the full simulations without
any qualitative impact on the dynamics. With this in mind it is expected that this contribution is negligible
for the phenomenon.
Hypothesis 2. Electrodynamics
The system has rapidly changing magnetic fields, therefore it makes sense to consider electrodynamic
effects. The rotor magnet’s rapidly changing field will induce a current in the floater magnet since the
magnet is conducting. From Lenz’s law and the symmetry of the system it is expected that a large part of
the induced currents will create a magnetic dipole antiparallel to the rotor magnet. If the induced magnetic
dipole is larger than the perpendicular component of the floater magnet’s magnetic dipole, the effective
dipole will be pointing antiparallel. This would make the parallel synchronisation stable. But there are
some problems with this hypothesis. It has experimentally been shown that the perpendicular component
of the floater magnets magnetic dipole is larger than the induced magnetic dipole in section 1.2.5. Ad-
ditionally there is no clear reason for why the direction of the magnetic moment and the eddy current’s
induced dipole should be coupled. Still eddy currents are complex and it is not easy to rule out eddy
currents from playing a role in the mechanism.
Hypothesis 3. Dynamically stable with damping
If the floater magnet is slightly ahead of the rotor magnet with regards to rotation phase, it will be experi-
encing a torque to further increase its phase difference. The inclusion of rotational damping might result
in the damping torque cancelling out the magnetic torque. This is analogous to an object at its terminal
velocity where the air resistance neutralizes the gravitational force.
From this mechanism it is expected that there should be a measurable difference between the phase of the
rotor and floater magnet with the floater magnet ahead. In the section 1.2.4 it is found that the floater mag-
net is ahead of the rotor magnet by 6.4◦± 5.1◦, but due to the high uncertainty further evidence is required.
A point against this hypothesis is that since the torque is given by τ = m × B = |m| · |B| sin ϕ, the torque
increases with ϕ. This means that the phase ϕ is expected to be unstable unless the damping force has an
even stronger ϕ scaling. For obvious reasons it is expected that the damping should be independent of ϕ.
With the torque in this form the expected phase between the rotor and floater magnet would be more than
90◦shifted from each other. This would allow the forces to balance out since the torque decreases with ϕ,
at angles above 90◦. It is clearly shown in the experiments that this is not the case.
Since each of these hypotheses have some issues explaining the phenomenon, there is a need for new
hypotheses, which will be discussed in section 3.3.

3.2
Conclusion
In this work several experiments have been conducted in order understand some key aspects of the mag-
netic levitation phenomenon that have not previously been investigated.
The first part of the investigating involves mapping out when the phenomenon occurs. For spherical mag-
nets of different sizes it was found that, increasing the size of the magnet lowers the minimum speed for
which the phenomenon could occur, denoted ”lower bound”. This is most likely due to the larger spheres
having a larger moment of inertia and mass. Keeping the size constant but varying the magnetisation
through the use of controlled heating, it was seen that lowering the magnetisation would also lower the
lower bound, as discussed in section 1.2.3.
Next, it was investigated how the levitation changes with rotor speed.
With the magnet initially levitating, the equilibrium distance would slowly increase until it either reaches
an equilibrium in the high damping, high speed case, or the magnet falling down in the low damping case.
This movement of the equilibrium point as a function of time denoted as ”falling rate” has been measured
by tracking videos of the magnet levitating. It was shown that increasing the rotor speed would slow
the falling rate in the low speed regime. To ensure reproducibility of the experiments the magnet always
started from its minimum levitation distance. This was measured with the clear tendency that increasing
the rotor speed would lower the minimum levitation distance.
It was seen that the magnet had different instabilities forming at different speeds. At low speeds no insta-
bilities would form. Increasing the speed further the magnet would naturally move in a Up-down pattern,
known as ”Up-down mode”. Further raising the speed, the magnet would move in a circular movement
perpendicular to the rotation axis, known as ”Side mode”. At larger speeds the motion would start to mix
between the Up-down mode and the side mode with modal changes occurring mid levitation. The last
mode observed happens at large speeds where the magnet will move in a U-shape due to it being very
close to the rotor magnet. Without including damping, further increasing the speed would lead to the
magnet being flung away.
Levitation time was measured as function of rotor speed. There are two main contributions to levitation
ceasing: The falling rate and instabilities. Generally the falling rate decreases as the speed increases, lead-
ing to increased levitation time with speed. But as the speed increases instabilities start to play a large role
in the levitation time. Here the Up-down mode and the U-mode are quicker at ejecting the floater magnet
than the Mixed and Side mode. Within the side mode regime the largest levitation times are found to be
200 s for a low damping setup.
By painting the north pole of the floater and rotor magnet it has been possible to determine how they move
relative to each other. It has been shown that the floater magnet points upward with a small tilt of around
6.8◦, so the two north poles point towards each other. This is known as parallel synchronisation. Addition-
ally it was found that there is no large azimuthal phase shift between the rotor and floater magnet, with
the floater magnet being 6.4◦± 5.1◦in front of the rotor magnet.
To test a hypothesis an experiment was conducted to measure the importance of eddy current with regards
to the synchronisation mechanism. It was found that the hypothesised dipole field induced by the rapidly
changing field is weaker than the floater magnets component, ruling out this exact eddy current mecha-
nism.

3.3
Future work
As this field is very new there is much to do. Most notably there is no good model for the phenomenon
with many questions yet to be answered. In this work the numerous experiments and simulations allow
us to pinpoint the features of a good model. These features of a good model can be seen below:
• The mechanism for the parallel synchronisation of the magnets should be explained.
• The mechanism for how the floater magnet spins should be understood, and at what speed the floater
magnet spins with.
• It should predict the change in equilibrium height over time, and explain what properties of the
floater magnet change leading to this. This can be tested against the results in section 1.2.2. The best
idea for this is currently the spinning of the floater magnet.
• The stability of the levitation should be known. This involves the restoring forces, and the instabil-
ities forming. The instabilities could be modeled, finding out at which speeds they form and how
damping affects the instabilities. This could be tested against the results found in section 1.2.1.
• The model should predict the minimum levitation speed and its scaling with magnet size and mag-
netic moment. This can be compared to the results found in section 1.2.3.
• Due to the experiments done in section 1.2.5. The mechanism cannot involve eddy currents’ magnetic
dipole field dominating the magnetic field of the floater magnet perpendicular to the rotation axis.
Additionally there are still many experiments to do, such as:

3.5.1
Background and Motivations
Static magnetic levitation is not physically possible, as was proved by Samuel Earnshaw showed in 1842,
i.e. a configuration of permanent magnets cannot levitate another permanent magnet. Magnetic levitation
is only possible if there is either a control system to continuously adjust the position of the levitating magnet
by controlling the magnetic field, or if a diamagnetic material is used.
However, it has very recently been shown that it is possible to levitate a permanent magnet using another
fast-rotating magnet. In this novel magnetic levitation technique, a magnet can be levitated by spinning
another magnet near it at a high speed, several thousand RPMs. However, this knowledge is still very new
and there is a plethora of questions that are currently unanswered such as how does the size and shape
of the levitated magnet influence its ability to levitate, how does the rotation speed of the rotating magnet
change the levitation distance etc..
The idea in this project is to attack the problem both from an experimental side and a modeling side.
Regarding the latter the idea is to use both analytical and numerical integration to obtain the force between
the magnets and from this build a simple theoretical model of the system. For the experimental work, the
idea is to realize a setup with high-speed rotating magnets where the stability of the levitating magnet and
the distance it is levitating can be measured to characterize the achieved levitation.
