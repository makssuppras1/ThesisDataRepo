Master Thesis
Abstract
Monitoring the power performance characteristics, namely the power curve and the
Annual Energy Production, of wind farm turbines is of great importance for all the parties
involved in the development of a wind farm. However, measuring the wind speed a wind
turbine experiences can be challenging.
An alternative method to measure the wind so
as to determine the power performance of each wind farm turbine, as it is introduced by
IEC 61400-12-2, is by utilizing spinner anemometry which is positioned on wind turbine’s
spinner and, therefore, it is not inﬂuenced by the complex ﬂow prevailing behind the rotor.
In 2016, ROMO Wind initiated the "Performance Transparency Project" so as to prove that
the spinner anemometer measurements are very robust in spite of the terrain, the seasonal
variations, and the wake eﬀects. The objective of the current Master Thesis is to investigate
whether spinner anemometer measurements from all the wind directions, including the waked
sectors, can be used in order to monitor the power performance of a wind turbine over time.
The wind farm under investigation is comprised of eight wind turbines and a met mast.
The exact location of the wind farm is unknown. The wind farm is located in a ﬂat terrain
and its surroundings are farming lands and small house groups. The wind turbine which
was evaluated in the present Thesis is the one located closer to met mast. The data of
the evaluated wind turbine and the met mast were pre-processed in order to check whether
there were errors in the respective dataframes and to identify whether there was any sensor
out of operation. Additionally, the free of wakes and the waked directions for the met mast
and for the evaluated wind turbine were determined in order to be able to distinguish the
directions and hence, to answer the research question. Moreover, the "true" wind direction
was determined and the nacelle direction signal was corrected accordingly. Furthermore, a
data ﬁltering was performed in order to ensure the normal operation of the evaluated wind
turbine and of the instruments measuring the wind. Then, the air-density was corrected
according to IEC 61400-12-2, and the power curve and Annual Energy Production (AEP)
was calculated for all the directions, for the free of wakes directions, and for the waked
directions.
From the aforementioned analysis, it was observed that, for low annual average wind
speed, the AEP in 360° was slightly higher than the AEP in free of wakes, probably because
the waked sectors and small compared to 360°. However, a range between AEP in 360°and
AEP in free of wakes needs to be deﬁned in order to determine whether the diﬀerence found
can be accepted or not. Also, it was observed that AEP in wakes was signiﬁcantly higher
than in free of wakes. Two possible reasons were investigated for this diﬀerence; either it
was due to higher turbulence intensity in low wind speeds in wakes, or because spinner
anemometer potentially measured lower wind speed in higher turbulence intensity.
The
turbulence intensity in wakes found to be signiﬁcantly higher than in free of wakes for low
wind speeds. Also, it was found that bin averaged spinner anemometer wind speed was
higher than the bin averaged cup anemometer wind speed for high turbulence intensities.
However, it was also seen for high turbulence intensities, the diﬀerence between spinner and
cup anemometer was ﬂuctuating signiﬁcantly. Therefore, it could not be concluded whether
spinner anemometer measured lower wind speed in high turbulence intensities.

Introduction
A wind turbine is a large structure which extracts the kinetic energy of the wind with its
rotor, converts it into mechanical energy with its shaft, and lastly into electrical energy with
its generator [6]. Monitoring the power performance of a wind farm, turbines by measuring
their power curve and their Annual Energy Production, is of great importance for all the
parties involved in investing, manufacturing, operating, planning, and regulating of wind
farm turbines [5].
In order to measure the power performance of a wind turbine, the wind speed (input)
from near or on the wind turbine, and the electric power (output) need to be measured [7]
[3]. Due to the fact that the electric power output is simple to measure since it is given by
the SCADA in electrical form, the task is to measure accurately the wind speed [7].
As it is described in IEC 61400-12-1 [5], one possible way to measure the wind speed at
the hub height of a wind turbine, is to install a cup anemometer on a meteorological mast
The met mast shall be located at a distance two to four rotor diameter upstream of the wind
turbine and in free of wakes from neighbouring and operating wind turbines and obstacles
[5]. However, as wind turbines are getting taller, the met mast needs to also be taller in
order to measure the wind speed at hub height. This makes the met mast a very costly
solution [7].
An alternative method to measure the wind was introduced in IEC 61400-12-2, where in
this case the wind speed and wind direction sensors are positioned on the top of the nacelle
of the wind turbine [8]. Therefore, in this way, by utilising nacelle anemometry, it is possible
to measure wind and therefore to monitor the power performance characteristic for each
wind farm turbine [8]. However, even though nacelle anemometry is a cheaper solution than
erecting a met mast, it has a signiﬁcant drawback; it is positioned in the nacelle roof, hence
behind the rotor, where the ﬂow is signiﬁcantly complex [1].
Moreover, as it is introduced in IEC 61400-12-2, another way to measure the wind that
a wind turbine experiences is by utilising a spinner anemometer [8]. A spinner anemometer
is comprised of three one-dimensional sonic sensors which are positioned on a wind turbine’s
spinner. The main advantage of a spinner anemometer comparing to a nacelle anemometer
is that it is located in front of the rotor rather than behind, and hence the wind measured
by a spinner anemometer is not inﬂuenced by the complex ﬂow prevailing behind the rotor
([1]-[9]).
According to IEC 61400-12-2, when conducting a power performance test in order to
measure the nacelle power curve of a wind turbine, only the sectors for which the evaluated
wind turbine is free of wakes from neighbouring and operating wind turbines and obstacles
are taken into account. This is because wind in wakes is signiﬁcantly distorted, and this
inﬂuences the power performance of evaluated wind turbine [8].
Since October 2016, ROMO Wind, which exclusively provides commercially the spinner
anemometer (iSpin) technology, initiated the "Performance Transparency Project", or PTP,
having as a scientiﬁc partner the DTU Wind Energy department. The objective of this
project is to prove that the spinner anemometer is able to demonstrate performance trans-
parency. More speciﬁcally, PTP aims to show that the spinner anemometer measurements

Theoretical Background
2.1
Power performance
The main objective of IEC 61400-12-1:2017 [5] is to describe a reliable methodology
which will ensure "consistency, accuracy and reproducibility" in the analysis and the mea-
surement of power performance of wind turbines [5]. Additionally, it provides instructions on
how to analyse, measure and report the power performance testing for wind turbines. The
procedure described in IEC 61400-12-1:2017 can be used to evaluate the power performance
of individual wind turbines at speciﬁc locations, as well as to compare diﬀerent turbine mod-
els or settings. Therefore, the IEC 61400-12-1 shall beneﬁt the parties which are involved
in the manufacture, planning of installation and permitting, operation, and regulation of
wind turbines [5]. Thus, it shall be utilised by a wind turbine manufacturer who aims to
meet power performance requirements, by an operator who may be needed to demonstrate
that the stated or the required power performance speciﬁcations are met, as well as by a
wind turbine planner or a regulator who needs to precisely deﬁne the wind turbine’s power
performance characteristic in regards to regulations or permit requirements. [5]
The power performance characteristics of wind turbines are determined by both the
measured power curve as well as by the calculated Annual Energy Production (AEP) [5].
More speciﬁcally, the measured power curve is deﬁned as the relationship of wind speed and
wind turbine power output and it is determined by gathering simultaneously measurements of
meteorological data, including wind speed, and wind turbine signals, including power output
[5]. Collecting data shall be continued until a signiﬁcant statistical database is established
under diﬀerent wind and atmospheric conditions [5]. The AEP, which essentially estimates
the total energy production of a wind turbine during one year, is calculated by applying the
measured power curve to diﬀerent wind speed frequency distributions at hub height [5].
Regarding IEC 61400-12-2:2013, its main objective is to describe a procedure to verify
the power performance characteristic of a horizontal axis wind turbine [8]. This standard
is expected to be utilised in the case the requirements which are set in IEC 61400-12-1 are
not feasible. The procedure described in IEC 61400-12-2:2013 can be used to evaluate the
power performance of individual wind turbines at speciﬁc locations, as well as to compare
diﬀerent turbine models or settings. One of the main diﬀerences between IEC 61400-12-1
and 61400-12-2 is that in IEC 61400-12-1 the wind speed is measured by an anemometer
positioned on a meteorological mast, located two to four rotor diameters upwind of the test
wind turbine. Therefore, this position makes possible to measure directly the "free" wind
with the least possible interference from the wind turbine rotor. In IEC 61400-12-2, the
wind speed is measured by an anemometer positioned on or near the nacelle of the wind
turbine. Therefore, the nacelle-measured wind is signiﬁcantly aﬀected by the rotor of the
test wind turbine and the nacelle. Thus, in IEC 61400-12-2, methods to determine and apply
corrections for this ﬂow distortion are included. [8]
According to IEC 61400-12-2, in order to deﬁne the power performance characteristics
of a wind turbine, namely its measured power curve and its estimated AEP, simultaneous
measurements of nacelle-measured wind, and wind turbine signals, including power output,
need to be collected [8]. Collecting data shall be continued until a signiﬁcant statistical

2.2.1
Nacelle Anemometery
Nowadays, in order to monitor the power performance of wind farm turbines, and since
it is not viable to erect a met mast in front of each wind turbine, the wind speed and wind
direction are measured with sensors which are mounted on the top of the nacelle of large wind
turbines, also known as "nacelle anemometry" [1]. These sensors are typically comprised of
cup anemometers and wind vanes or sonic anemometers, and they can also be doubled for
redundancy reasons, as shown in Figure 1(a) [1]. Nacelle anemometers’ measurements of
wind speed and wind direction are used as inputs to the wind turbine control system and
they are utilised for yaw control, start up and shut down of the wind turbine, as well as for
power performance veriﬁcation [1].
However, nacelle anemometry’s potential is limited due to the fact that the sensors are
placed on nacelle roof, behind the rotor, where the wind ﬂow is very complex [1]. Therefore,
nacelle anemometers measurements are inﬂuenced by the distortion of the ﬂow due to the
wakes of the blade root vortices and the nacelle, as well as by the mounting of the instruments
[1]. The vorticity in the air ﬂow behind the rotor is shown in Figure 1(b), conﬁrming the
complexity of the ﬂow. Even though the eﬀects of the ﬂow distortion may be corrected for
a single wind turbine by utilising a met mast, for individual wind farm turbines it is more

A signiﬁcant advantage of sonic anemometer is that it can measure the wind with
very ﬁne temporal resolution (high sampling frequency), making it suitable for measuring
turbulence eﬀectively [12]. Additionally, sonic sensors do not consist of moving parts and
hence, they cannot be easily worn [14]. This makes sonic anemometers suitable for long-
term use in weather stations where the accuracy of cup anemometers and wind vanes is
adversely inﬂuenced by salty air or dust [12]. On the contrary, one disadvantage of the
sonic anemometer is that measurements will be inﬂuenced by the ﬂow distortion caused by
the wind ﬂow which past sonic sensors’ heads and their support structure [13]. In order
to minimize this ﬂow distortion, diﬀerent designs of sonic heads have been developed and
utilised [13]. Sonic anemometers perform well under icing conditions due to internal heating
of the tubes and the sensor heads [1].
2.2.4
Operating principle of spinner anemometer
The working principle of spinner anemometer is based on the distortion of the aerody-
namic ﬂow caused by the spinner [15]. More speciﬁcally, it detects three directional wind
speeds in the three positions over the spinner surface where the three 1D sonic sensors are
located. However, due to the fact that the paths of the sonic sensors are perpendicular to
the velocity component caused (induced) by rotor rotation, this velocity component is not-
sensed [2]. In the case the wind comes along the shaft axis and thus the ﬂow is axial with the
ﬂow stagnation point located on the center of the spinner, all three sonic sensors measure
identical wind speeds [1][2]. However, in the case that the angle of attack of the wind on
the spinner changes, as in a skew air ﬂow, and the ﬂow stagnation point moves away from
the nose center, the sonic sensor which is closer to the stagnation point experiences lower
wind speed, whereas the sonic sensor which is further away experiences higher wind speed
[2][1]. This principle can be better understood by taking into account Figure 3. Figure 3
illustrates an axial ﬂow over a spinner with spherical nose which transforms into a cylinder
[1]. The wind which is coming from the right of the Figure 3 is the free wind speed and it is
represented by orange color [1]. At the center of the spinner nose, namely at the stagnation
point (due to the axial inﬂow), the wind speed equals to zero which is represented by dark
blue color. Moving away from the stagnation point along the spinner surface the wind speed

In order to convert the velocities V1, V2 and V3 measured by the three 1D sonic sensors
and the rotor position, φ, measured by accelerometers which are embedded in the root of each
sonic sensor to horizontal wind speed, Uhor, yaw misalignment, γ as well as ﬂow inclination
angle, β, the spinner anemometer conversion algorithm described in [2] is utilised [15]. This
algorithm utilises two constants, namely k1 and k2, which are taking into account the shape
of the spinner, the blade roots design as well as the mounting of the sonic sensors on the
spinner [16]. More speciﬁcally, k1 is mainly related to wind speed measurements, while the
ratio of these two constants, known as kα, namely kα = k2/k1, is mainly related to ﬂow
angle measurements [16]. In order to measure the wind accurately over the spinner, the two
spinner anemometer constants have to be calibrated, ideally to free wind conditions when
the wind turbine rotor is at standstill and points into the wind [11][9]. However, in [16],
Demurtas, G. and Janseen, N. found an innovative way to calibrate spinner anemometer
without having to stop the wind turbine.
It is important to note that, as mentioned beforehand, the calibration of a spinner
anemometer takes into consideration the spinner shape, the design of the blade roots and the
mounting of the sonic sensors, while the Nacelle Transfer Function (NTF), as it is deﬁnes in
IEC 61400-12-2:2013 [8], accounts for the rotor induction [15]. Rotor induction is essentially
the reduction of the free stream wind speed due to the presence of wind turbine rotor.
2.2.5
Mounting of spinner anemometer
According to [9], the air ﬂowing around the spinner of a wind turbine has a stagnation
point at the spinner nose where the wind speed is zero. The sonic sensors of the spinner

==PAGE:22==

anemometer shall be mounted at positions on the spinner surface where the wind speed is
approximately equal to the free wind speed [9].
The three sonic sensors shall be mounted at azimuthally equal distance from the nose
of the spinner spaced by 120°. Additionally, they shall be placed at azimuth positions in the
middle of the blades roots, as well as at the exact same distance from the shaft axis [11][9].
Moreover, the sensors paths of the three 1D sonic sensors shall point forward and they are
mounted to be in plane with the rotor axis [11]. Also, they are slightly tilted backwards in
order undisturbed wind to ﬂow between sensor paths as well as to prevent sonic sensor head
shadow eﬀect. In this way, the component of the wind on the sensors due to the rotation
of the spinner is cancelled out and therefore only the wind along the shaft axis direction is
measured [11][1].
In case there are structures inside the spinner of a wind turbine which prevent mounting
the three sonic sensors at the optimal positions, the sensors shall be placed closer to the
spinner rather than closer to the nacelle in order to avoid possible collision with other
structures [9]. The large spinner size of a modern wind turbine as well as possible defects
of the spinner shape, as for example poor rotational symmetry, makes it challenging to
accurately mount the sonic sensors at equal distance from the spinner nose, spaced by 120°.
These challenges could be tackled in the future by integrating the holes for the sonic sensor
mounting in the spinner mould [15].
2.2.6
Spinner anemometer calibration
As it is shown in Figure 4, the calibration of the spinner anemometer consists of ﬁve
steps.
The zero wind calibration of the sonic sensors is the ﬁrst step in the calibration procedure
and it is executed by the manufacturer. More speciﬁcally, in order to calibrate the individual
sonic sensors at zero wind speed, a curtain is put around the sonic sensor to create zero wind
conditions and the temperature inside the curtain is measured [9]. The goal of the zero
calibration is to guarantee a zero reading which remains stable in the output [17]. Zero
calibration is described more detailed in [9] and [17].
Additionally, in case power performance measurements are conducted according to IEC
61400-12-2 [8], the sonic sensors of the spinner anemometer must be calibrated individually
in a wind tunnel [15]. The calibration of the sonic sensors of a spinner anemometer in a wind
tunnel is performed similarly to the calibration of cup anemometer, as it described in the
MEASNET procedure [18][9]. In 2015, IECRE published an amendment for IEC 61400-12-
2:2013 [8] in order to specify the calibration procedure of spinner anemometer sonic sensors
in wind tunnels [10]. More detailed information can be found in [9] and [17].
The third step in the calibration procedure is the internal calibration. It is conducted
during normal operation of the wind turbine soon after the installation of the spinner
anemometer sonic sensors on the spinner [15]. Internal calibration is important in order
to normalise the three sensor paths to each other [17]. Therefore, for each individual sonic
sensor, the calibration constants, k1 and k2, are adjusted in order to give the same average
value during one rotation [17]. Its objective is to out-compensate the imperfections in the
geometry of the spinner, such as the non-symmetry of the spinner, as well as the errors which

Following up, the fourth step in the calibration procedure is the calibration for inﬂow
angle measurements. As it is mentioned beforehand, the calibration constants, k1 and k2,
take into account the shape of the spinner [16].
More speciﬁcally, k1 is mainly related

measurements [16]. Since the values of k1 and k2 are not known when the spinner anemometer
is installed on the wind turbine for the ﬁrst time, k1 and k2 are initially set equal to arbitrary
default values, usually k1,d=1 and k2,d=1 [16]. The subscript d indicates default calibration
settings. Therefore, the calibration will provide the corresponding correction factors, F1 and
Fα, in order to correct the default values, k1,d and k2,d, to calibrated values, k1 and k2 [16].
According to Chapter 2 of [2], there are ﬁve methods to calibrate a spinner anemometer
for ﬂow angle measurements. These are based on spinner anemometer measurements with
default settings and a reference yaw misalignment signal which is measured with a yaw
position sensor. These methods are based on yawing several times the wind turbine by ± 60
°in and out of the wind in stopped condition in order to compare the changes in yaw position
measured by the sensor with the changes in the yaw misalignment measurement which is
read by the spinner anemometer [16][17]. Hence, the correction factor Fα can be derived from
the analysis of the measurements and it can multiplied with the default value of kα, kα,d, to
give the correct kα calibration constant [16]. Even though a yaw position sensor is commonly

important to mention that the spinner anemometer wind speed, Uhor, "represents the wind
speed at the rotor center as if the turbine was not present", but it is not the actual wind
speed at the spinner of the wind turbine [15]. In reality, the wind speed at the spinner of the
wind turbine will be lower than Uhor at the spinner nose and higher on the side of the spinner
as well as between the blades [15]. Also, due to the induction caused by the rotor during
operation of the wind turbine, the spinner anemometer wind speed, Uhor, is lower than the
free wind speed [15]. Therefore, in order to calculate the free wind speed when the wind
turbine is operating, the spinner anemometer wind speed, Uhor, needs to be corrected by
utilizing the Nacelle Transfer Function or NTF, as it is deﬁned in the IEC 61400-12-2:2013
[8]. However, the main drawback of this method is that the wind turbine has to be stopped
for several hours when the wind conditions are good, which usually is not accepted by the
wind turbine owner [15].
2.3
Performance Monitoring with Spinner Anemometry
As described in subsection 2.1, wind turbines extract the kinetic energy of the wind and
converts it into electrical power with their generator. The power performance characteristic
relates the electrical power to the horizontal wind speed which is measured in free (undis-
turbed) wind conditions, usually in a distance between two and four rotor diameters in front
of the wind turbine under test [3][4].
The power performance characteristic of every wind turbine in a wind farm shall be
monitored in order to observe what energy its wind farm turbine produces. However, it is
diﬃcult to measure the wind quantities, such as wind speed, turbulence intensity, shear, veer,
ﬂow inclination, yaw misalignment, air pressure, air temperature and humidity, at all wind
farm turbines and at all diﬀerent type of sites by using met masts. Nowadays, the way to
handle this issue is to utilize wind speed measurements made by nacelle anemometry in order
to monitor the power performance of each individual wind farm turbine.[3][4] Conventional
nacelle anemometry measurements, made with cup anemometers, wind vanes or 2D sonic
sensors mounted at the back of the nacelle, have very high uncertainties due to the fact
that nacelle anemometers are positioned behind the rotor, and therefore are highly sensitive
to variations in the wind ﬂow conditions, due to the presence of the rotor and the nacelle
structure. However, they are quite often the only available measurements of the wind speed
that each wind turbine experiences in order to derive its power curve. [3][4]
Therefore, by utilising the spinner anemometer technology it is possible to overcome the

In order to transfer this
approach methodically to also other wind farms, iSpin should be installed on all the wind
farm turbines, but only at one as an IEC compliant reference measurement. This will serve
to determine the iSpin free wind speed calibration factor as well as the Nacelle Transfer
Function. Subsequently, after some validity checks, the free ﬂow calibration factor as well
as the NTF which are derived for the reference wind turbine can be applied also to the
remaining wind farm turbines in order to determine their power curves. This approach is
called The iSpin Guardian approach and it is developed by ROMO Wind. [3]
However, in the aforementioned references ([3][4]), even though they investigate the per-
formance of the spinner anemometer in 360°and in free of wakes, they do not investigate the
power performance characteristic, namely the power curve and the Annual Energy Produc-
tion, in wakes and in free of wakes. In the present Master Thesis, a comparison of the power
performance characteristics between 360°and free of wakes, as well as between free of wakes
and in wakes for the spinner anemometer, will be conducted.

3.4
Determination of free of wakes and waked sectors
According to IEC 61400-12-2 [8], in order to measure the nacelle power curve (NPC),
only the wind directions which are not inﬂuenced by the wakes of neighbouring and operating
wind turbines and/or signiﬁcant obstacles shall be taken into account. However, in order to
be able to address the main question of the present Thesis stated in the Introduction, namely
whether spinner anemometer free wind measurements from all the directions (360°), including
the waked sectors, can be used to monitor wind farm turbine power performance over time,
the power performance of wind turbine 11 using spinner anemometer measurements shall
be determined in free of wakes, as well as in wakes and for all the directions. The spinner
anemometer of wind turbine 11 due to the fact that it is positioned on spinner nose of wind
turbine 11, it will not be in wakes of wind turbine 11.
For this reason, the sectors for which the wind turbine 11 and the met mast are in
wakes and free of wakes of the neighbouring and operating wind turbines and/or signiﬁcant
obstacles needs to be determined.
3.4.1
Wake inﬂuence from neighbouring and operating wind turbines
The ﬁrst step of this procedure is to evaluate the wake inﬂuence which is caused by
the neighbouring and operating wind turbines. According to the IEC 61400-12-2 [8], the
minimum distance between the wind turbine 11 and a neighboring and operating wind
turbine shall be two rotor diameters, Dn, of the neighbouring wind turbine or two rotor
diameters, D, of the wind turbine 11, if it has a larger diameter [8]. In the present wind
farm, all wind farm turbines have the same rotor diameter. The neighbouring and operating
wind turbines which are located closest to wind turbine 11 are wind turbine 22 on the north-
west of wind turbine 11, and wind turbine 12 on the south-east of wind turbine 11, as shown
in Figure 7(b). As shown in column four of Table 3, wind turbine 22 and wind turbine 12
are located 4.6D and 4.8D, respectively, from wind turbine 11. Additionally, according to
IEC 61400-12-1 [5], the distance between the met mast and any neighbouring and operating
wind turbine shall be at least 2.5 rotor diameters of the respective wind turbines. As it can
be seen from column four of Table 4, this criterion is fulﬁlled, since wind turbine 11, which
is located closest to the met mast, is located 3.7D away from the met mast.

3.4.2
Wake inﬂuence from obstacles
Regarding the second step of this procedure, the wake inﬂuence of obstacles which are
located near wind turbine 11 or near the met mast needs to be evaluated. More speciﬁcally,
according to IEC 61400-12-2[8] signiﬁcant obstacles, as for example buildings, trees and
parked wind turbines, should not exist in the measurement sector in a reasonable distance
from the wind turbine or the met mast [5]. As it is mentioned in Annex B of IEC 61400-12-2
[8], the dimensions which are important for the determination of signiﬁcant obstacles are the

3.6.1
Description of applied ﬁlters
In section 8.6- Data rejection of IEC 61400-12-2:2013 [8], the circumstances for which
measurements shall be removed from the database are described. This was done in order
to make sure that only data which are obtained during wind turbine’s normal operation
are used and that data are not corrupted in any way. These circumstances were taken into
account during the present ﬁltering procedure.
In order to be able to measure the nacelle power curve (NPC) and calculate the Annual
Energy Production of wind turbine 11, two things have to be ensured. Firstly, it is important
to ensure the normal operation of wind turbine 11 both in free of wakes as well as in 360°,
and secondly, the normal operation of the instrument with which wind speed is measured,
namely cup anemometer on the met mast and spinner anemometer on wind turbine 11. For
this reason measurements which are out of the operational range of wind turbine 11 and
measurements which indicate failure or degradation of the measurement equipment were
identiﬁed and ﬁltered out.
For the better understanding of the further analysis, it is important to mention that
for a 10-minute period, raw spinner anemometer data, SCADA data and met mast data are
recorded with a sampling frequency. Then, the average, maximum, minimum, and standard
deviation of these sampled data are calculated for each signal and inserted in the respective
data set, namely wtg11means, wtg11maxs, wtg11mins and wtg11stdvs for wind turbine 11
and calmeans, calmaxs, calmins, calstdvs for the met mat. It is important to mention that
in case at least one of the sampled data in a 10-minute period, for each signal, is out of the
operational range of the wind turbine, then the whole 10-minute period shall be excluded.

rotational speed to increase and therefore, controller was pitching the blades in order to
secure the wind turbine.

Discussion
As it can be seen from Table 12, for low annual average wind speeds, the AEP in 360°
is slightly higher than the AEP in free of wakes of the spinner anemometer. Additionally, it
can also be seen that for low annual average wind speeds, the AEP in wakes is signiﬁcantly
higher than in free of wakes for the spinner anemometer. These diﬀerences in AEP in 360°
and in free of wakes, as well as in wakes and in free of wakes were investigated further in
the previous Chapter, they were attributed to two possible reasons: either to the higher
turbulence intensity in low wind speeds in wakes, or due to the spinner anemometer possibly
measures lower wind speed in higher turbulence intensities.
5.1
Case 1: High turbulence intensities in lower wind speeds
Regarding the ﬁrst case, the question which arise is how high turbulence intensities in
lower wind speeds can inﬂuence the power performance. The answer lies to the non- linearity
of the power curve. As it can be seen from Equation 9, the power is proportional to the cube
of the horizontal wind speed and linear proportional to the air density and the rotor area.
P = 1
2ρACp(λ, θ)V 3
0
(9)
For wind speeds above cut-in and below rated, the wind turbine is designed to produce
optimal power, namely to extract energy out of the wind with the maximum attainable
eﬃciency. For wind speeds above the rated wind speed, the wind turbine is designed to
constrain the power to its rated value in order to prevent the wind turbine’s generator from
burning out. In the case of high turbulence intensity in low wind speeds, this means that
even though the average wind speed is low, it ﬂuctuates rapidly and signiﬁcantly around
its average value. Fluctuating components of wind speed which are higher than the average
wind speed will result to higher power production than the ﬂuctuating components of wind
speed which are lower than the average wind speed.
This might be more clear with an
example.
Thus, if the average wind speed is 5 m/s, the power will be proportional to
(5 m/s)3 = 125 (m/s)3. The air density and the rotor area are considered to be constant
and the power coeﬃcient for wind speeds above cut-in and below rated wind speed has its
optimum value. In case the wind rapidly ﬂuctuates to 4 m/s, the power will be proportional
to (4 m/s)3 = 64 (m/s)3 , while in case the wind ﬂuctuates to 6m/s, the power will be
proportional to (6 m/s)3 = 216 (m/s)3. Hence, for an average wind speed of 5 m/s, if the
wind speed ﬂuctuates to 6 m/s, the wind turbine will produce 73% more power, whereas if
it ﬂuctuates to 4 m/s, it will produce 49% less power compared to the power would have
been produced at 5 m/s.
However, for wind speeds closer to rated wind speed (near the knee of the power curve),
high turbulence intensities do not aﬀect signiﬁcantly the power production, and hence the
AEP. The reason why might be clearer with an example.
Thus, it is assumed that the
average wind speed is 12 m/s and the rated wind speed, which is the wind speed at the rated
power, is at 12.5 m/s. For an average wind speed of 12 m/s, the power will be proportional
to (12 m/s)3 = 1, 728 (m/s)3. In case the wind ﬂuctuates rapidly to 13 m/s, the power will

be proportional to (13 m/s)3 = 2, 197 (m/s)3, but in this case the rotational speed of the
wind turbine will exceed its reference value, and hence the controller of the wind turbine will
start pitching the blades, which will result in a decrease of the power coeﬃcient, in order to

power would have been produced at 12m/s. Hence, for wind speeds near rated wind speed,
ﬂuctuating wind speed components will not inﬂuence the power production, and hence the
AEP, as much as at low wind speeds.
Therefore, for wind speeds below rated wind speed, wind speed ﬂuctuations above mean
wind speed will result to higher power production compared to the power loss for wind speed
ﬂuctuations lower than the mean wind speed. However, for wind speeds near rated wind
speed, wind speed ﬂuctuations above or below average wind speed will not aﬀect signiﬁcantly
the power production. The case of wind speeds below rated wind speed, and more speciﬁcally
for low wind speeds, was investigated thoroughly in section 4.2. As it is shown in Figure 28,
for low wind speeds, the turbulence intensity in 360° is slightly higher than in free of wakes.
Also, Figure 29 shows that the turbulence intensity in wakes is generally higher than in free
of wakes, but for low wind speeds is signiﬁcantly higher. Hence, it can be seen from Figure 29
and Figure 30 that even though the wind speed in wakes is lower than in free of wakes, the
wind in wakes ﬂuctuates more intensively. This can potentially be a possible reason why the
AEP in wakes and in 360° are higher than the AEP in free of wakes for low annual average
wind speeds.
5.2
Case 2: Spinner anemometer wind speed error in high TI
Regarding the second case, the question which arises is how the possibility that the
spinner anemometer measures lower wind speed in high turbulence intensity, can inﬂuence
the power performance. The answer lies in the AEP calculation. This might be clearer with
a simple example; it is assumed that there are two identical wind turbines, one produces
100 kW at 4 m/s and the second one produces 100 kW at 5m/s.
Since the ﬁrst wind
turbine will produce 100 kW at lower wind speed than the second one, consequently it will
also give higher AEP. This was investigated in section 4.5. The objective of the analysis
in this section was to investigate whether the spinner anemometer underperforms, namely
measures lower wind speeds in high turbulence intensities, and therefore in wakes, resulting
to higher AEP. This analysis cannot be performed when either the spinner anemometer or
the cup anemometer or both are in wakes, due to the inﬂuence of the wakes. Hence, it was
performed when both the spinner anemometer and the cup anemometer are free of wakes,
namely for the wind directions from 158° to 281°. Even though both the cup and the spinner
anemometer are free of wakes, high turbulence intensities might have also occurred. Based
on that, it can be seen from Figure 33 that for low wind speeds (either they are measured
from the cup or the spinner anemometer), the spinner anemometer measures higher wind
speeds than the cup anemometer. Additionally, from Figure 35 it can be seen that for high
turbulence intensities (either they are measured from the cup or the spinner anemometer),

anemometer on wind turbine 11.

Conclusions
From the analysis conducted in the previous chapters, it was found that, for low annual
average wind speeds, the AEP in wakes is signiﬁcantly higher than the respective AEP
in free of wakes for the spinner anemometer (Table 12). The diﬀerences in AEP are also
reﬂected in Figure 27. Two possible reasons were examined for this diﬀerence; either it is
due to higher turbulence in low wind speeds in wakes compared to free of wakes, or it is
due to the spinner anemometer measures, possibly, lower wind speed in high turbulence
intensities. After investigation of the turbulence intensity, turbulence intensity in wakes is
shown to be signiﬁcantly higher than in free of wakes for low wind speeds (above cut-in)
(Figure 29). Also, the possibility that the spinner anemometer measures with a wind speed
error in high turbulence intensity was investigated. It is shown that, when both the spinner
and the cup anemometer are free of wakes, for higher turbulence intensities, either they
are measured with the spinner or with the cup anemometer, the spinner anemometer free
wind speed is higher than the cup anemometer wind speed. However, for high turbulence
intensities, the diﬀerence between spinner anemometer and cup anemometer wind speeds
ﬂuctuates signiﬁcantly. Hence, it cannot be derived a clear conclusion about what aﬀects
the diﬀerence in AEP in wakes and in free of wakes.
Also, it was found that the AEP in 360° is higher than the respective AEP in free of
wakes for the spinner anemometer ( Table 12). The diﬀerences in AEP are also reﬂected
in Figure 26. After investigation of the turbulence intensity, turbulence intensity in 360°
is shown to be slightly higher than in free of wakes for low wind speeds (above cut-in)
(Figure 28). This is possibly because of 360° contains all the directions including the wakes,
which might have an impact on the turbulence intensity of 360° measurements. However,
due to the fact that the wakes sectors for the spinner anemometer is only the 30% of the 360°
directions, this impact is small. As it is shown in the second column of Table 12, the AEP
in 360° is 1% higher than in free of wakes for annual average wind speed of 4m/s and these
two AEPs are getting closer and closer for higher annual wind speeds. Since, it could not
be found a band for which this diﬀerence in AEP is acceptable, it cannot be stated clearly
that spinner anemometer free wind speed measurements from all the directions can be used
to monitor wind turbine performance over time.
Also, the spinner and the cup anemometer wind speeds were compared when both the
spinner and the cup anemometer are free of wakes, and when the spinner anemometer is in
the wakes of neighbouring and operating wind turbines, but the cup anemometer is not. It
is shown that when both instruments are free of wakes they measured wind speed very close.
However, it is shown that when the spinner anemometer enters the theoretical waked sector
(theoretical wind directions above 281.4°), it delays around 15° to sense the wakes. This
could possibly be because the spinner anemometer is in partial wakes, and hence, according
to [2], it cannot detect if the wind turbine rotor operates in partial wakes since it gives a
point measurement. Also, it is shown that when the spinner anemometer is about to exit the
theoretical waked sector (theoretical wind directions close but less than 157.4°), it does not
measure signiﬁcantly lower wind speed than the cup anemometer. This is possibly because,
close to 157.4° direction, even though the spinner anemometer is still in wakes (and the wind
