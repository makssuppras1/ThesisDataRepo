1. Theoretical introduction 
The immune system is the body’s defense mechanism with the ability to recognize and counter 
foreign objects, cancer cells, and pathogens. It can be divided into the innate and the adaptive 
immune system, both consisting of lymphocytes derived from hematopoietic stem cells from 
the bone marrow.   
 
The innate immune system, also known as the nonspecific response, acts immediately after 
tissue damage or a pathogen has overcome the anatomic barrier. Myeloid cells are one of the 
key components of the system that also include both physical and chemical protective 
mechanisms and neutral killer (NK) cells. By recognizing damage-associated molecular 
patterns (DAMPs) and pathogen-associated molecular patterns (PAMPs) through pattern-
recognition receptors (PPRs), macrophages and dendritic cells among other cells are able to 
identify pathogens and danger signals from damaged cells, triggering an inflammatory 
response [1]. A different recognition strategy is applied by NK cells that can identify pathogens 
by a unique mechanism known as “missing-self”. This concept will be further described later.  
 
While the innate system is nonspecific, the adaptive immune system recognizes specific 
antigens of pathogens that are identified by antigen receptor expressed on the cell surface of T 
cells and B cells. The system becomes activated when the first line of defense has failed and 
can be divided into humoral and cell mediated immunity. B cells are part of the humoral 
response where they proliferate and differentiate to plasma cells upon activation. By secreting 
antibodies, plasma cells can activate the complement system and neutralize bacteria and 
viruses, eventually triggering elimination of the pathogen [2]. In contrast, the cell-mediated 
immune response act upon cell-contact with an infected-cell, mainly driven by T cells. This 
makes T cells a particularly interesting for therapies, allowing direct targeting of a pathogen. 
T cells 
T cells originate from progenitor cells and are matured in the thymus into antigen-specific naïve 
CD4 or CD8 T cells, followed by the migration to secondary lymphoid tissue and antigen-
presenting cell (APC)-dependent priming [3]. APCs, usually dendritic cells, present antigens 
through the major histocompatibility complex (MHC) to naïve T cells. When a cognate antigen 
is recognized by the T cell receptor (TCR), the naïve T cells differentiate into effector cells [4]. 
In addition to the TCR response, naïve T cells need a co-stimulatory signal from CD28. This 
is mediated by the ligands B7.1 and B7.2 that are expressed on the cell surface of APCs. The 
response ultimately lead to the release of interleukin (IL)-2, promoting the cell differentiation 
[5]. 
CD8 T cells recognizes peptide displayed by MHC class I (MHC-I), triggering the 
differentiation into cytotoxic T cells (CTLs) and the release of cytotoxic granules [6]. Upon 
activation, most CTLs cells also produced multiple cytokines that contribute the host defense, 
including interferon (IFN)-J and tumor necrosis factor (TNF)-D [1]. While IFN-J promotes the 
cytotoxic immune response by increasing the MHC-I expression, recruiting macrophages, and 
inhibit viral-replication, TNF-D can directly kill target cells through the TNF-receptors [7]. 
Contrarily, CD4 T cells recognizes peptides through MHC class II and depending on the 
==PAGE:6==
 
5 of 53 
cytokine environment differentiate into a vary of subset that all support immune cells, including 
CD8 T cells, through cytokine secretion [8]. 
 
Upon clearance of antigen and resolution of inflammation immune, most effector cells die but 
a small proportion of long-term memory cells remains, generating immunological memory for 
the antigen [9]. This allows the body to mount a rapid immune response upon reinfection by 
the same antigen. 
Memory cells are characterized by being less differentiated, tissue tropic, and cytotoxic 
compared to effector cells while having a greater proliferation potential [10, 11]. In addition, 
memory T cells have also developed an antigen-independent self-renewal mechanism, 
allowing them to persist in absence of antigen stimulation, expanding their life-span 
significantly compared to effector cells [12]. Based on these characteristics and several 
phenotypic markers, memory cells can be divided into stem cell memory T cells (TSCM cells), 
central memory T cells (TCM cells), and effector memory T cells (TEM cells), as shown in 
Figure 1[11].

T cells also express killer immunoglobin-like receptor (KIRs) that recognizes peptide-MHC-I 
(pMHC-I) similarly to TCR. This has been reported in for smaller subset but their functional 
role is not fully known [13-15].  
MHC-I antigen presentation 
When a cell expresses abnormal properties, such as during viral-infection or cancer, immune 
recognition of the cell is mediated by MHC-I molecules. The molecules exhibit enormous 
polymorphism, allowing them to bind and present a wide-range of peptides from the cell. 
MHC-I molecules are mainly recognized by T cells through TCRs but also interact with other 
receptors such as killer immunoglobulin-like receptors (KIRs), giving them a role in both the 
innate and adaptive immune response [3, 16]. 
 
The MHC-I molecule is a heterodimer consisting of a transmembrane D chain that is 
noncovalently bound to the smaller invariant chain, E2-microglobulin (E2M). The D chain

consists of two extracellular domains (D1 and D2) and a transmembrane segment (D3) with D1 
and D2 forming the peptide-binding cleft, enabling the binding of peptides [17]. Usually, the 
bound peptides are 8-10 amino acids long, however, longer peptides have been reported to bind 
at the C-terminal and shortened by exopeptidases present in the endoplasmic reticulum (ER) 
[1, 18]. In Figure 2, the structure of MHC-I and the peptide-binding groove can be observed.

In humans the D chain is encoded by the human leukocyte antigen HLA class I (HLA-I) region 
located on chromosome 6. The region consists of multiple HLA-I D chain genes with the most 
known to be HLA-A, -B, and -C. Each gene has multiple variants or alleles within the population 
and with individuals having two copies of the chromosome, one from each parent. In addition, 
most humans will be heterozygous for the HLA-I genes, allowing a total of six different 
molecules per individual to be expressed with two HLA-A, -B, and -C molecules. With the 
major difference of the allotypes being the D1 and D2 domains, this creates an immense degree 
of polymorphism in the peptide-binding region, allowing a large repertoire of peptides to be 
docked [1, 19].  
 
After ribosomal synthesis of the D chain and E2M in the cytosol, the proteins are translocated 
to endoplasmic reticulum (ER) by the transporter associated with antigen processing (TAP) 
which together with calnexin assemble and partly fold the MHC-I molecule. After dissociation 
of calnexin, MHC-I binds the peptide-loading complex (PLC) consisting of  tapasin, 
calreticulin, and ERp57 [20-22]. The PLC retains the MHC-I in a peptide-binding sensitive 
state, allowing peptides derived from self or nonslef proteins to be loaded in a complex called 
peptide-MHC-I (pMHC-I) [20, 21]. Prior being presented to the MHC-I molecules, peptides 
are generated in the cytosol by proteasome and translocated into the lumen of ER by TAP1 and 
TAP2. Endoplasmic reticulum aminopeptidases (ERAPs) further trim the peptides to be 8-10 
amino acid long [21-23]. After being stabilized from peptide binding, pMHC-I complex is 
presented on the cell surface of APCs. The full process can be seen in Figure 3.

KIR-mediated recognition of MHC-I 
While all MHC-I molecules can be recognized by TCRs, the main ligand for KIRs are the 
HLA-C molecules. KIRs act as a key regulator NK cells and consists of two subfamilies KIR2D 
and KIR3D with receptively contain 2 (D1 and D2) and 3 (D0, D1, and D3) immunoglobulin 
(IG)-like domains in the extracellular portion of the protein. By interacting with receptively the 
D1 and D2 domains of the peptide-binding cleft, the D1 and D2 domains are responsible for the 
MHC-I recognition [16]. These KIR domains also anchor over the C-terminal region of the 
peptide and interact with residue 7 and 8 of the peptide by either supporting or inhibiting the 
binding [24]. 
 
Both KIR families provide inhibitory (designated with a ‘L’) and activating signaling 
(designated with ‘S’) and are distinguished by the cytoplasmic domain consisting of either two 
immune tyrosine-based inhibitory motif (ITIM) and a immune tyrosine-based activating motif 
(ITAM) respectively. While KIRs also recognizes HLA-A, -B, F, and -G with the Bw4 motif 
(residues 77-83 of the D chain) being significant for HLA-B [16, 25, 26], HLA-C is the main 
ligand. The dimorphism of positions 77 and 80 of the HLA-C molecules divides HLA-C 
molecules into either C1, Ser77Asn80, or C2, Asn77Lys80,  classifying two types of KIR HLA-C 
ligands [27, 28]. With the HLA-C1 group consisting of the alleles C*01/*03/*07/*08/ 
*12/*14/*16 while HLA-C2 covers C*02/*04/*05/*06/*15/*17/*18. A similar dimorphism 
exists at position 44 in D1 domain of KIRs by methionine and lysine, determining the receptor’

overview of KIRs and their ligands are shown in Figure 4.

Each NK cell expresses both inhibitory and activating KIRs, differentiated them from a simple 
receptor-ligand arrangement generating a downstream signal. The activation of NK cells are 
instead highly dependent on the KIR-HLA interaction and tolerance to self-molecules. This is 
accomplished by inhibitory KIR being specific for self MHC-I molecules and activating KIRs 
being specific for nonself molecules. The response from the receptors is dependent upon the 
density of MHC-I molecules on the cell surface and strength of the interaction. When the 
inhibitory signal is stronger than the activating, NK cells are in tolerance and cells are identified 
healthy cells, expressing higher number of self-MHC-I compared to stress-induced molecules 
on the cell surface. Activation of NK cells is generated by the downregulation of self-MHC-I 
molecules on the cell surface (known as the “missing-self” model) or the upregulation of stress 
associated self-molecules, triggering lysis of the target [30, 31].  
The loss of MHC-I is a rare event triggered in certain tumor and virus-infected cells to avoid 
CTLs recognition by TCR. By the missing-self model, NK cells are able to eliminate these 
cells, underlying the importance of the KIR-mediated regulation [30]. An overview of the 
dynamics are shown in Figure 5. 
 
Two possible mechanisms have been suggested to explain the development of tolerance to 
normal cells, an “disarming” and an “arming” model. The disarmed model suggests the NK 
cells are initially responsive and interaction with MHC-I specific inhibitory receptors are a 
required to avoid the cells to become anergic. In contrast, the arming model proposes NK cells 
initially are unresponsive and become effector cells by MHC class I engagement of inhibitory 
receptors [32].

Even though the function of KIRs in CD8 T cells has yet to be fully understand, both activating 
and inhibitory functional KIRs have been reported with the ability to modulate TCR signaling 
[13, 33, 34]. However, the KIR repertoire has been reported to be random, distinct specificity 
from NK cells in the same individuals, and independent of self-HLA-I [13]. This indicates the 
function of KIRs on CD8 T cells are distinctive from the one found in NK cells. 
Utilizing pMHC-I multimers to identify antigen-specific T cells 
pMHC-I multimers can be used to identified T cells specific for diseases, including viral-
infections, cancer, and autoimmune diseases. With the ability to recognize antigen-specific T 
cell response, MHC-I multimers can improve current understanding of the pathology and 
enable more specific targeting for adoptive cell therapy. 
 
 
MHC-I molecules are produced either with a UV-ligand, temperature-dependent ligand, or 
empty, depending on the used MHC peptide exchange technology. This is done with the 
purpose of keeping the pMHC-I stable as the peptide binding is crucial for the complex. 
Upon UV exposure, the UV-ligand is cleaved, allowing the user to load a peptide [35-37]. This 
is similar for the temperature induced technology, where an elevated temperature will allow a 
peptide to be loaded [38]. In contrast, the empty-loaded technology is based on a disulfide bond 
between the D1 and D2 domains, composing the peptide-binding cleft. The di-sulfide bond 
mimics the effect of binding a high-affinity peptide into the region, stabilizing the empty 
pMHC-I complex [39]. This allows the peptide to be loaded without an exchange step and has 
also been shown to improve recognition of TCR-specific responses for CD8 T cells [40]. 
 
Following the generation of pMHC-I with a peptide of interest, pMHC-I complexes are 
assembled onto fluroscent-labeled streptavidin (SA) molecule (Figure 6) as a requirement due 
to the pMHC having a low affinity for TCR-binding and easily falls off [41-43]. A pMHC 
tetramer consists of four pMHC-I complexes bound to its SA molecules with one fluorescent 
color, comprising the ability larger panels of peptides in a single sample. Though, 
combinatorial encoding with unique two-color combination enables parallel detection of up to 
36 pMHC tetramer [44, 45].

2. Aim 
While both HLA-A and -B have been widely studied for their interaction with T cells, much 
less is known about HLA–C. This supposedly relates to the assumption that HLA-C plays a 
minor role in the T cell recognition landscape as its cell surface expression has been reported 
to be as much as ten-fold reduced compared to HLA-A and -B [47-51]. While the reason behind 
the lower expression is not fully understand, it has been suggested to be a result of an 
evolutionary selection process as both HLA-A and -B are downregulated in the virus-infected 
cell while HLA-C expression is relatively retained [47-51]. With HLA-C restricted CD8 T cells 
having a role for at least human cytomegalovirus (CMV) [52], Epstein-Barr virus (EBV) [53], 
and human immunodeficiency virus (HIV) [54]. With 54 % of the total CTL response been 
shown to be HLA-C restricted in HIV-1 [55]. Furthermore, some HLA-C alleles have been 
reported to be associated with the autoimmune diseases psoriasis [56] and Crohn’s disease [57]. 
In contrast, a thymic selection process has been proposed, suggesting the lower expression 
provoke HLA-C restricted T cells to have a higher affinity toward peptide-HLA-C complexes 
[58].  
Beside HLA-C’s association to virus infection and autoimmune diseases, HLA-C restricted 
CD8 T cells has been shown to have a role in cancer. While HLA-C restricted neoepitopes have 
been reported for cancer [59-61], many studies only include HLA-A and -B restricted 
neopeptides for T cell response evaluation.  
 
Previous working from xTI on various cancer types, showed an unusually high number of 
HLA-C recognition for CD8 T cells (unpublished data). The unusual high number made them 
challenge the specificity of the observed interaction, leading to the suggestion of a TCR 
independent response. Ultimately, discovering the recognition was TCR-unspecific and 
provoked by KIRs. 
This project aimed to evaluate HLA-C associated interaction mediated via TCR and KIR, and 
characterization functional implication of such interactions in antigen-specific T cell 
identification. More specific the following aims were made:  
 
1. Optimizing HLA-C pMHC multimers for antigen-specific T cell identification 
 
2. Functional evaluation of HLA-C associated TCR and KIR interactions 
 
3. Identification of T cells specific to antigens presented by HLA-C molecules

3.2. Abstract 
Identification of disease-associated peptides recognized by T cells are important to expand the 
understanding of pathogenesis and to improve treatments. While human leukocyte antigen 
(HLA)-A and -B restricted peptides have been widely studies for their role in immune-related 
diseases, much less are known about HLA-C’s role. Aside being recognized by T cells, HLA-
C interacted with NK cells by killer-cell immunoglobulin-like receptors (KIRs) as a key 
regulator. Here, we report KIR-specific binding of HLA-C peptide-major histocompatibility 
complex (pMHC) multimers in T cells for both CMV-derived and cancer-associated peptides, 
comprising the ability to recognize HLA-C restricted peptides for T-cell receptor (TCR)-
specific responses. By blocking KIRs, we were able to distinguish between TCR-specific and 
KIR-specific in T cells, allowing HLA-C driven antigen-specific T cells to be identified. This 
enables evaluation of T cell responses at genome-wide levels for various immune-mediated 
diseases across HLA-A, -B, and -C. 
3.3. Introduction 
T cells perform an important role in the adaptive immune response with the T-cell receptor 
(TCR) of CD8 T cells recognizing class I major histocompatibility complex (MHC-I) 
associated peptides being presented by antigen-presenting cells. A better understanding of this 
interaction is essential to improve current therapies for viral-infections, autoimmune diseases, 
and cancer. The peptide-MHC (pMHC) molecules recognized by CD8 T cells are currently 
being explored with the use of either fluorescently, barcoded, or metal tags, allowing to parallel 
analysis of specificities of multiple peptides at once [44-46, 62]. By identifying antigen-
specific T cells, we are enabling more specific targeting for tumors and viral infections with 
the use of adoptive cell therapy.  
 
MHC-I molecules are divided into human leukocyte antigen (HLA)-A, -B and –C in humans 
with both HLA-A and -B being primarily TCR specific and widely studied. In addition to TCR, 
HLA-C is recognized by killer cell immunoglobin-like receptors (KIRs) that mainly are present 
on NK cells but also are known to be expressed on CD8 T cells. Based on whether KIRs contain 
2 or 3 immunoglobulin (IG)-line domains in the extracellular portion of the protein, they are 
divided into two subfamilies, respectively KIR2D and KIR3D. MHC-I molecules binding will 
either function inhibitory (designated by an L) or activating (designated by an S) for the 
cytotoxic response in NK cells. Only few HLA-A and -B alleles code for proteins that bind 
==PAGE:14==
 
13 of 53 
KIRs while all HLA-C alleles express proteins recognized by KIRs. KIR3D receptors are only 
able to recognize HLA-A and -B alleles while KIR2D recognition is dominated by HLA-C 
alleles with the exception being HLA-A11 [24, 58, 63]. Unlike the TCR, KIR2Ds interaction 
with HLA-C is based less on peptide specificity and more on binding to conserved sequences 
of the HLA-C molecule. Positions 80 of HLA-C are integral for the specificity of KIR2D, 
dividing HLA-C molecules into either C1, Asn80, or C2, Lys80 [27, 28]. Some allotypes can be 
cross-reactive, but most KIRs interact with only C1 or C2.  
 
KIRs have been reported to be present on a small subset of CD8 T cells with expressions 
ranging from 1 % to 38 % (average 5 %) among 44 healthy donors [13]. This may limit the 
current possibilities to identify TCR-specific HLA-C restricted pMHC in T cells, considering 
KIRs recognize HLA-C molecules. As HLA-C-restricted T cells have been shown to be 
involved in defence against viral infections [64, 65] and cancer [59-61], this may constrain the 
ability to detect antigens for improved therapies. pMHC multimers of carrying HLA-C 
associated virus or neoantigen (cancer-specific mutation derived) showed large-scale KIR 
dependent binding in healthy donors as well as in cancer patient samples, thus limiting 
identification of antigen-specific T cells. By blocking KIR receptors using panKIR antibodies, 
we demonstrate an efficient way to remove the unspecific HLA-C-KIR binding which enables 
large-scale T cell detection pMHC methods to be used detecting of HLA-C driven antigen-
specific T cells. 
3.4. Results 
TCR independent binding of pMHC multimers 
Evaluating large peptide libraries using pMHC multimers are widely used to identify antigen-
specific cells. By using DNA barcoded pMHC multimers [46], parallel analysis of >1,000 T-
cell specificities in a single sample is possible which is particularly relevant for personalized 
cancer genomic analysis. The appliance of this method allowed us to screen a total of 5473, 
5073, and 4001 predicted HLA-A, -B and -C neopeptides in five cancer cohort: Renal cell 
carcinoma [66], non-small cell lung cancer (NSCLC), Melanoma [67], and Bladder [68] 
(Supplementary Figure 1A). We detected a total of 145, 96, and 158 HLA-A, -B and -C 
responses respectively. Here, an unusually high number of HLA-C is observed out of total 
HLA-A/B/C when comparing predicted peptides to detected responses (p < 0.0001, χ2 test) 
(Supplementary Figure 1A, Table 1) with HLA-C responses covering the alleles C*0202, 
C*0304, C*0401, C*0501, C*0602, C*0701, and C*0702. For the NSCLC and Bladder 
cohorts, HLA-C restricted neopeptides accounted for the largest fraction of responses, with the 
relative contribution compared to HLA-A responses being significant in both cohorts (p < 
0.0001).  
The high number of detected HLA-C specific T cells made us question the specificity of the 
HLA-C restricted neoantigen as HLA-C has been estimated to be as much as ten-fold reduced 
compared to HLA-A and -B [47-51]. To confirm the lower level of HLA-C expression, we 
evaluated the HLA-C level in mRNA, protein, and peptide presentation (Figure 7A-D). In the 
malignant melanoma cohort, HLA-C clearly had the lowest RNA expression compared to 
HLA-A (p < 0.0001) and –B (p < 0.001) (Figure 7B, Supplementary Figure 1B). The cell

Expression of KIR receptor on T cells mediate unspecific recognition of HLA-C-specific 
pMHC multimers 
We not only observed binding of pMHC multimers to T cells but also to NK cells, predicting 
the receptor causing the unspecific binding to be the HLA recognizing receptors in NK cells, 
KIRs (Figure 8A). Blocking of CD8 was shown to be incapable of removing the unspecific 
pMHC multimers, confirming the source is nonspecifically binding to CD8 receptor. KIRs are 
also known to be expressed in a limited degree on T cells and were shown to co-stain with 
pMHC, indicating KIRs are responsible for the unspecific binding (Figure 8B). By adding the 
antibody first, we displayed the ability to block the response, confirming the source of 
unspecific HLA-C restricted pMHC recognition in T cells. KIRs are known for high cross 
recognition for HLA-C molecules between the receptors, making it natural to choose a panKIR 
antibody (Table 2). However, to identify the specific KIR responsible for the interaction, 
additional three KIR antibodies were tested with none of them showing as strong blocking 
ability as the original panKIR antibody (Figure 8C-D). While the KIR2DL3 antibody showed 
an increased pMHC+KIR+ population likely triggered by being co-stained, KIR2DL1 displayed 
a limited blocking ability, indicating a potential role in unspecific TCR binding. The other 
tested panKIR antibody, KIR2D, displayed a strong blocking capability and decreased the 
pMHC multimer response in T cells by 77.7 %. Thus, we were unable to identify single KIRs 
responsible for this effect, the panKIR antibodies showed high blocking capability, motivating 
the future use of panKIR antibodies. The KIR2D and KIR2DL1/DS1/DS3/DS5 antibodies were 
titrated by cell number and volume (Supplementary Figure 4). 
The prevalence of KIRs in T cells has been shown to be highly individual dependent with an 
average of 5 % in healthy donors [13]. To confirm the reported variations in human, we 
screened a total of 16 healthy donors for surface expression of KIR2DL1/DS1/DS3/DS5 on 
CD8 T cells (Figure 8E, Supplementary Figure 5). The donors displayed a high degree of 
incoherency with the average being 2.93 % with lowest and highest expression being 
respectively, 0.13 % and 11.4 %. This variance could explain the observed differences of HLA-
C pMHC multimer+ T cells across healthy donors. Furthermore, a fraction of the 
KIR2DL1/DS1/DS3/DS5+ T cells were also found expressing the NK phenotypic marker CD56 
(Figure 8F).

To ensure KIR blocking distinguish between unspecific and specific TCR responses, the 
previously identified HLA-C restricted MDS pMHC multimer, causing unspecific recognition, 
were compared to a TCR-specific HLA-A restricted EBV-derived multimer (Figure 8G). In 
CD8 T cells, we were able to block only unspecific interaction and not the TCR-specific 
interaction, suggesting that KIR blocking can be used to identify true antigen-specific T cells. 
This observation was further validated by showing TCR-specific HLA-C restricted viral-
derived pMHC recognition in T cells was unaffected by KIR blocking (Supplementary Figure 
6). Together, these results indicate that KIRs are solely accountable for the recognition of 
allotype mismatched HLA-C multimers in T cells.

Identification of antigen-specific T cells by blocking HLA-C-KIR interaction 
To examine the proficiency of KIR blocking to identify true HLA-C specific T cell responses 
using pMHC tetramers, we evaluated a large-library of 48 HLA-C*02:02 and 39 HLA-C*07:02 
viral-derived peptides in allotype-matching healthy donors. With combinatorial pMHC 
staining, we were able to identify up to 14 peptides in a single flow sample, using a unique 
two-color combination for each peptide. By gating for multimer positve two-color combination 
and negative responses of the remaining colors, we identified a total of 6 pMHC tetramer 
responses across 3 healthy donors with 5 for BC351 and 1 for BC366 (Figure 9A). To identify 
if these pMHC tetramers are TCR-specific multimer binding, we measured IFN-γ and TNF-α 
secretion upon stimulation with identified peptides. This validated TCR-specific activation of 
the CMV-derived peptide FRCPRRFCR identified in BC366 (Figure 9B). However, none of 
the peptides identified using pMHC tetramer binding for BC351 showed T cell activation. As 
a separate control for BC351, we have also used peptide pools in the functional assay to identify 
any interaction that might not have resolved in our combinatorial analysis. We identified 
activation of CD8 T cells specific to peptides in pool A that consisted of 9 unnoticed peptides 
from the combinatorial pMHC staining, probably, triggered by the absence of one-colored 
pMHC multimer controls. This would have allowed the gating to be more qualitative for 6 
colors, resulting in a better discrimination of pMHC binding (Supplementary Figure 7). 
Peptides from pool A were assessed by individually pMHC T cells recognition together with 3 
of the initial pMHC tetramers (Figure 9C), showing a total of 5 responses with 4 of them 
representing pool A. The last response were provoked by 1 of the previous tested pMHC 
tetramers, allowing the peptide to be verified as unspecific.  
Altogether, 48 and 39 HLA-C restricted peptides were tested with allotype-matching pMHC 
tetramers in three healthy donors, confirming only 2 peptides to be TCR-specific across the 
donors, EBV-derived IACPIVMRY for BC351 (Figure 9D) and CMV-derived FRCPRRFCF 
for BC366. With at least 4 of the C*02:02 specific viral-derived peptides displaying TCR-
independent pMHC tetramer binding in BC351, empathizing the need for a method to identify 
TCR-specific pMHC multimer interactions.

3.5. Discussion 
Both HLA-A and -B have been widely studied for T cell evaluation, generating a large library 
of TCR-specific antigen, while HLA-C restricted antigens are less reviewed. We report pMHC 
multimer interaction with KIRs and blocking of these can be used as an efficient method to 
identify TCR-specific HLA-C restricted T cells.  
pMHC multimers are generated with the purpose of identifying a disease-related response in 
CD8 T cells to improve therapies by advancing the understanding of pathogenesis. In this 
study, we observed a KIR-specific interaction of HLA-C restricted pMHC (Figure 8), limiting 
identification and studies of HLA-C antigen-driven T cells (Figure 7).  
 
KIRs act as a key regulators of NK cells activity and predominantly recognizes HLA-C with 
the exception of few HLA-A and -B alleles [70, 71]. In NK cells, KIRs mediate the killing 
mechanism of tumor and virus-infected cells, with both inhibitory and activating receptors. 
However, they have also been reported to be expressed by T cells but their functional role is 
not fully known [13-15]. Previous studies have shown around 40 % of HLA-compatible 
peptides can bind to the HLA-C specific KIRs, making the interaction much less peptide 
specific compared to TCR [72]. This is likely to be the reason for the unusual higher numbers 
of unspecific HLA-C pMHC multimer binding (Figure 7). Interestingly, we were also unable 
to report any correlation between KIR expression in T cells and the TCR-independent pMHC 
binding. Hence, indicating the KIR-pMHC interaction is dependent upon the repertoire rather 
than the total expression. 
 
By showing the panKIR antibody covering KIR2DL1/DS1/DS3/DS5 completely blocked the 
KIR-specific HLA-C pMHC interaction on T cells, we were able to narrow down the KIRs 
accountable for the recognition to KIR2DL1, KIR2DS1, KIR2DS3, and/or KIR2DS5. As the 
KIR3D subfamily has not be reported to recognize HLA-C alleles, classifying the interactions 
to be KIR2D-related was expected [24, 58, 63].  
The inhibitory KIR2DL1, KIR2DL2, and KIR2DL3 are more studied than the activating KIRs. 
For example, the peptide sensitivity of inhibitory KIRs has been reported to vary with studies 
showing KIR2DL2/3 binding to HLA-C1 allotype is more peptide selective than that of 
KIR2DL1 binding to HLA-C2 [28, 73]. Thus suggestion a potential dominate role by 
==PAGE:24==
 
23 of 53 
KIR2DL1. However, blocking of KIR2DL1 did only partly block the unspecific response by 
15.5 %, indicating KIR2DL1 was not solely responsible of the KIR-specific interaction. This 
could be a due to epitope specificity of the KIR2DL1 antibody that may not block the receptor 
completely. The argument is supported by the flow pot plots in Figure 8C, where KIR blocking 
results in a smear of PE-positive pMHC multimers, and needs to be assessed.  
As the inhibitory KIR2DL1 was not shown to be solely accountable for the KIR-specific 
interaction, it seems likely that either the activating KIR2DS1, KIR2DS3, and/or KIR2DS5 are 
involved. The activating counterpart of KIR2DL1, KIR2DS1 originates from same single locus 
as KIR2DL1 and shares a high degree of sequence homology in the extracellular domain. The 
key difference being the amino acid at position 70, threonine in KIR2DL1 and a lysine in 
KIR2DS1 [27, 74]. Additionally, they have both been shown to be sensitivity to peptide 
sequence alterations at position 7 and 8 [75]. However, KIR2DS1-binding is mainly restricted 
to HLA-C2 ligand while KIR2DL1 has displayed a broader HLA-C specificity[76]. The avidity 
of KIR2DS1-binding of HLA-C2 compared to KIR2DL1 has been reported to significant lower 
[27, 74, 75, 77]. This points towards the role of KIR2DS1 to be less than that of KIR2DL1 for 
the KIR-HLA-C T cell recognition and further analysis is required.  
Of the two last receptors covered by the KIR2DL1/DS1/DS3/DS5 antibody, KIR2DS5 is 
known to bind HLA-C ligands, while KIR2DS3 is poorly expressed on the cell surface [78], 
making it reasonable to assume KIR2DS3 either has no or a minor role for the interaction. 
Though, KIR2DS5 has been reported to recognize both C1 and C2 epitopes of HLA-C, studies 
have primarily been focusing on its influence in various diseases [79-82]. Therefore,  much 
remains unknown about the receptor’s binding capabilities and further analysis to clarify its 
role is required.  
Thus, we were unable to identify the KIR or combination of KIRs responsible for the 
unspecific-TCR response observed in T cells with pMHC multimers. However, the KIR-
specific responses are expected to be a combination of KIR2DL1, KIR2DS1, and/or KIR2DS5. 
Interestingly, Schlott et al. have reported KIR2DL2 and KIR2DL3 to be the main contributors 
for KIR-specific pMHC multimer binding [83]. These receptors were not covered by the 
KIR2DL1/DS1/DS3/DS5 antibody, further highlighting the need for additional studies.  
 
CD4, CD8, γδ T cells have all been reported to express KIR [13, 84-87]. For CD8 T cells, KIR 
expression starts to appear on effector memory CD8 cells but are also found in terminally 
differentiated effector CD8 T cells [88, 89]. While the function is not completely understood, 
it has been reported that activating KIRs can enhance functional TCR signaling while inhibitory 
KIRs can modulate the signaling [13, 33, 34]. By comparing the KIR function in NK cell to 
the one seen in CD8 T cell, Björkström et al. reported the KIR repertoire in CD8 T cells to be 
restricted from NK cells and displayed a random and distinct specificity from NK cells in the 
same individuals. In addition, the KIR repertoire was reported to be independent of self-MHC-
I molecules [13]. As the function in NK cells for KIRs are highly dependent on self-MHC-I to 
separate healthy and tumor or virus-infected cells based [90], this indicates the function of 
KIRs on CD8 T cells are distinctive from the one found in NK cells. We were also able to 
report similar findings as there were no correlation between the KIR expression in T cells and 
NK cells across the 16 healthy donors (p = 0.25) (Figure 8E, Supplementary Figure 10). For 
==PAGE:25==
 
24 of 53 
example, in BC366 and BC367 the KIR expression was similar in NK cells while the 
expression was respectively 2.65 % and 0.13 % in T cells. 
In contrast to Björkström et al. [13], a study from September 2021 by Sottile et al. reported that 
in a subset of CD8 T cells that resemble memory CD8 T cells associated with chronic CMV 
infection, a higher number of KIR2DL1-postive cells in C2/C2 individuals were observed, 
suggesting a self-KIR preference [52]. As HLA-C expression is relatively retained compared 
to the downregulation of HLA-A and -B by CMV [54, 91], recognizing TCR specific HLA-C 
responses comprise a therapeutic target for CMV. Contrarily, KIR-specific CMV responses 
could be diseases-promoting as pMHCs would be recognized by self-KIRs in T cells, triggering 
inhibitory effect for TCR recognizing [52, 92]. The HLA-C restricted T cell pattern shown by 
CMV is similar to the responses in other virus including EBV [53] and HIV [64, 65, 93], 
emphasizing the importance of distinguishing KIR- and TCR-specific responses to potentially 
avoid graft-versus-host disease. 
 
Recently, a study by Schattgen. S. et al. showed KIR2D receptors were significantly expressed 
in activated CD8 T cells by measuring HELIOS, an activation marker, indicating a direct 
relation between T cell activation and KIR expression [94, 95]. This follows previously studies 
showing TCR sustains KIR expression and are able to reestablish functional levels of KIR 
expression after downregulation [14]. The KIRs expression in T cells have also reported to 
have a role for the survival rate in various types of cancer patient [96] and 54 % of the total 
cytotoxic T cell response in HIV-1 have been shown to be HLA-C restricted [55]. Together, 
this suggest KIRs to have an influenceable role for CD8 T cells, making it an important topic 
to explore within fundamental and applied science.  
 
While we establish KIR-blocking as a method to identify TCR-specific HLA-C pMHC 
multimers, a comparable method has been reported for CMV-derived peptides HLA-C*07:02 
by Schlott et al. [83]. They established a double-staining protocol where a KIR2DL2/KIR2DL3 
antibody was added after pMHC multimers, allowing them to identify TCR-specific responses. 
While this method is applicable, KIR-blocking removes the need of additional gating and offer 
an additional color for flow cytometry analysis.  
Moreover, we validated HLA-C*05:01 and C*07:02 donor mismatching and C*02:02 
matching pMHC multimers to be KIR-specific, allowing the current understanding of KIR-
specific HLA-C pMHC binding to be expand to both HLA-C1 and -C2 allotypes. Through, 
only three HLA-C allotypes have yet been shown to influence KIR-specific pMHC multimers 
binding in T cells, the problem may occur in other alleles as well. Thus, additional studies are 
required with a larger peptide library assessing the size of KIR-specific T cell responses for 
pMHC multimers. 
 
HLA-C is estimated to be as much as ten-fold less expressed compared to HLA-A and -B [47-
51], however, the reason behind the lower expression is not fully understand. It has been 
suggested to be a result an evolutionary selection process favoring low expression in disease 
associations [97]. Several viruses, including CMV and HIV, retain the HLA-C expression upon 
infection while both HLA-A and -B are downregulated in the virus-infected cell. In addition, 
some HLA-C alleles have been reported to be associated with the autoimmune diseases 
==PAGE:26==
 
25 of 53 
psoriasis [56] and Crohn’s disease [57]. This, together with the hypothesis of T cells having a 
higher affinity toward peptide-HLA-C complexes [58], makes an intriguing case for the 
relevance to asses HLA-C antigen-driven T cells. This empathizes the importance of easily 
detecting HLA-C specific T cell responses and the potential impact of KIR-blocking for pMHC 
multimer analysis.  
3.6. Conclusion 
Thus, we were able to establish a method to allow for the detecting of specific antigen-driven 
T cells. HLA-C restricted pMHC multimers were recognized to bind unspecifically to T cells, 
requiring detected responses to be functional validated. We were able to resolve this obstacle 
by identifying KIRs as the root of the problem. By blocking KIRs, pMHC multimers were 
reestablished as a tool to recognize MHC-I specific responses across HLA-A, -B, and -C, 
permitting the evaluation of T cell responses at genome-wide levels for various immune-
mediated diseases. 
3.7. Future perspective 
For a detailed characterization and functional assessment of T cells expressing KIRs, we have 
performed single cell analysis, allowing us to examine the transcriptome, TCR and KIR 
specificity, and cell-surface phenotype markers on individual cells. With the additional usage 
of KIR for cell sorting, we will be investigating a total of 4 sorted T cell populations: TCR-
specific HLA-C pMHC multimers, KIR-specific HLA-C pMHC multimers, KIR-specific 
HLA-C pMHC multimers with KIR, and KIR recognizing cells. The single cell populations 
are currently being sequenced which will be followed by RNAseq analysis to identify 
similarities and differences between populations.  
 
We showed HLA-C unspecific pMHC responses in T cells are driven by KIRs, however, we 
were unable to resolve the exact KIR or KIRs triggering the recognition. To determine this, we 
are establishing T cells line expressing either KIR2DS1 or KIR2DL1. This allows us to assess  
whether these KIRs contribute to HLA-C pMHC recognition in T cells. Both KIR2DS1 and  
KIR2DL1 has been reported to recognize HLA-C in NK cells together with KIR2DS2, 
KIR2DS3, KIR2DS4, KIR2DS5, KIR2DL2, and KIR2DL3 [16, 25, 26]. The initial 
experiments will be focusing on detecting whether KIR2DS1 or KIR2DL1 contribute to KIR-
specific HLA-C multimer interactions with a TCR-specific HLA-C multimer as a control. 
Based on the findings, we will establish additional T cells expressing either other KIRs or a 
combination of KIRs, ideally resolving which KIRs should be targeted. Additionally, this will 
allow us to investigate the biological function of the blocked receptors. 
 
Finally, a larger assessment of KIR-specific HLA-C pMHC multimer interaction is required to 
identify whether the problem expands further than the allotypes C*02:02, C*05:01, and 
C*07:02. By applying DNA barcoding, we will be able to evaluate T cell responses at genome-
wide levels, allowing us to test a variety of peptides and allotypes. 
Thus, the method has been shown to able identify HLA-C specific pMHC responses in T cells, 
these experiments will allows us to create a stronger data set, potentially allowing the work to 
be published in a journal with a higher impact.

Cancer patients 
Four cancer cohorts were included for T cell neoantigen screens in this study; RCC (n = 6. 
Denoted R#), NSCLC (n = 2, denoted L#), Melanoma (n = 26, denoted M#), Bladder (n = 26, 
denoted R#), and MDS (n =2). Patient samples were kindly provided by the Department of 
Oncology and the Center for Cancer Immune Therapy, Copenhagen University Hospital, 
Denmark (RCC and Melanoma cohorts), the Cancer Immunology Unit, University College 
London Cancer Institute, London, United Kingdom (NSCLC cohort), Genitourinary Oncology 
Service, Department of Medicine, Memorial Sloan Kettering Cancer Center, New York, USA 
(Bladder cohort), and Department of Oncology at the University Hospital, Mannheim, 
Germany (MDS cohort). All samples were obtained under approval by the regional ethics 
committee and donor written informed consent was obtained according to the Declaration of 
Helsinki. 
Cell lines  
Cell lines T1, T2, BT-549 and MDA-MB-231 were kindly provided by the Center for Cancer 
Immune Therapy, Copenhagen University Hospital, Denmark. The EFM192A (DSMZ, 
Cat#ACC258), HEK-293T (American Type Culture Collection (ATCC), Cat#CRL-11268™), 
and SUP-T1 (ATCC, Cat#CRL-1942™) were purchased.  
HLA-C restricted peptide libraries 
A large-library of 78 viral-derived peptides from human cytomegalovirus (CMV), Epstein-Barr 
virus (EBV), and human adenovirus species B (HAdV-B) was used to identify HLA-C 
restricted responses. A total 73 of the peptides were HLA-C predicted peptides from the CMV 
proteins immediate-early-1 (IE1) and the phosphoproteins (pp) pp50, pp65, and pp150. Amino 
acid sequence of the CMV proteins were obtained from Uniprot database [98] and analyzed 
with the netMHC 4.1 [99]  to generate unique 8-11 amino acid long peptides covering the 
complete protein sequences and to predict the binding affinity to HLA-C*02:02 and HLA-
C*07:02. A percentage rank of 0.5% or less was used as the threshold value for strong binders. 
All generated HLA-C restricted HCMV epitopes were examined using the Immune Epitope 
Database and Analysis Resource (IEDB) [69] to identify whether the epitopes were novel or 
known. The published epitopes were included in the peptide libraries even if their rank was 
above 0.5%. 
While the majority of the library consisted of predicted peptides, 5 peptides were found in 
HLA-C response using IEDB or in previous literature. Note, the EBV-derived peptide is 
reported novel in the peptide library as netMHC 4.1 [99] was used to generate the peptides 
from a 15 amino acid long peptide (IEDB ID: 25237). The full HLA-C restricted peptide 
libraries can be found in Supplementary Table 6. 
Peptides 
Sequences of peptides are denoted in single letter amino acid code. All peptides were purchased 
from Pepscan (Pepscan Presto BV, Lelystad, Netherlands) and dissolved to 10 mM in dimethyl 
sulfoxide (DMSO).

Cell samples 
Peripheral blood mononuclear cells (PBMC) were isolated from whole blood by density 
centrifugation on Lymphoprep (Axis-Shield PoC) and cryopreserved at −150 °C in fetal calf 
serum (FCS) (Gibco, Cat#10500064) + 10% DMSO. 
MHC-I monomer production 
All MHC-I monomer types were produced by expressing MHC-I heavy chain and human β2-
microglobulin light chain in Escherichia coli (E. coli). The folding of these molecules was 
initiated in the presence of either UV-sensitive HLA-specific peptide ligands [35-37] or empty 
peptide-receptive HLA class I molecules [40]. Folded MHC-I molecules were biotinylated with 
the BirA biotin-protein ligase standard reaction kit (Avidity LLC, Aurora, CO), and MHC-I 
monomers were purified by size exclusion chromatography (HPLC, Waters Corporation, 
USA). Before storing at −80°C, all MHC-I folded monomers were quality-controlled for their 
concentration, UV degradation, and biotinylation efficiency. 
Generation of fluorescently labeled pMHC tetramers 
Individual HLA-restricted pMHC monomers were assembled by incubating 200 μM peptide 
of each peptide with 100 μg/ml of the respective MHC-I molecules for 1 hour using UV-
mediated peptide exchange or direct binding to empty MHC-I molecules. To generate pMHC 
tetramers, pMHC monomers were conjugated with fluorophore-labeled streptavidin (SA) 
molecules. For 100 μl of pMHC monomers, 9.02 μl [0.2 mg/ml of stock; SA-PE (Biolegend, 
Cat#554061), SA-PE-CF594 (BD Biosciences, Cat#562318, and SA-PE-Cy7 (Biolegend, 
Cat#405206)] or 18.04 μl [0.1 mg/ml of stock; SA-BUV395 (BD Biosciences, Cat#564176), 
SA-BV421 (BD Biosciences, Cat#563259), SA-BV605 (BD Biosciences, Cat#563260) and 
SA-BV650 (BD Biosciences, Cat#563855)] of streptavidin conjugates were added and 
incubated for 30 min at 4°C, followed by the addition of d-biotin (Sigma-Aldrich) at 25 μM 
final concentration. pMHC tetramers were generated in two colors to allow for two-
dimensional staining with fluorescent labels by incubating pMHC monomers and mixed in a 
1:1 ratio. 
Single pMHC multimer staining of NK cells and T cells  
PBMCs were thawed and washed in once R10 (RPMI + GlutaMAX¥ (Gibco, Cat#61870010) 
and 10% FCS) and twice in fluorescence-activated cell sorting buffer (FACS) buffer 
(phosphate-buffered saline (PBS) + 2% FCS). Cells were incubated 15 min with 1 μl of pMHC 
tetramer, 50 nM dasatinib, and 10 μl staining buffer (BD Biosciences, Cat#566385) (final 
dilution 1/10) in FACS buffer at 37 °C. Followed by mixing the cells with 20 μl of of antibody 
staining solution consisting of CD3-FITC (BioLegend, Cat#300406) (final dilution 1/20), 
CD4-BUV395 (BD Biosciences, Cat#742738) (final dilution 1/320), CD8-BV480 (BD

(final dilution 1/320), CD19-BV650 (BD Biosciences, Cat#563226) (final dilution 1/80), 
CD56 ((BD Biosciences, Cat#564849) (final dilution 1/320) and a dead cell marker 
(LIVE/DEAD Fixable Near-IR; Invitrogen, L10119) (final dilution 1/1000) and incubated for

dilution 1/20) was used instead of CD3-FITC. Cells were then washed twice in FACS buffer

HLA tissue typing, mutation analysis and neopeptide prediction 
Tissue typing, sequencing of patient sample materials, and neopeptide prediction were 
performed as previously described in RCC [66], Melanoma [67],  and Bladder [68] cohorts. In 
brief, whole exome sequencing (WES) of DNA (tumor material and germline control) and 
RNA sequencing of tumor were used as input for MuPeXI [100] in order to identify tumor-
specific mutations and predict 9-, 10- and 11-mer peptides with binding capacity to the patients’ 
HLAs. For the RCC cohort, neopeptides were selected with a predicted eluted ligand percentile 
rank (EL%Rank) <2. Whereas for the Melanoma and Bladder cohorts, neopeptides were 
selected with an EL%Rank <0.5 and an expression level >0.1 TPM. For patients with less than 
200 neopeptides using this criterion, the highest-ranking 200 peptides were selected to 
constitute the patients’ neopeptide library. For the NSCLC cohort an approach previously 
described [101] was used for mutational analysis and neoepitope prediction resembling the 
method used for the other cohorts. Briefly, 9-11-mer peptides with binding capacity to the 
patients’ HLAs were predicted with netMHCpan 2.8 [102] and netMHC 4.0 [99] and 
neopeptides were selected based on a predicted binding affinity of <500 nM or an EL%Rank 
of <2% by either tool. 
HLA expression 
HLA expression was measured at three levels,: RNA, protein, and endogenous HLA class I 
embedded peptides. RNA expression analysis was performed on a data from 26 malignant 
melanoma patients. Raw RNAseq were trimmed and quality checked, to a minimum length of 
20 bp and an average phred score of 20 with TrimGalore version 0.4.0 [103] combined with 
Cutadapt [104] and FastQC version 0.11.2 [105]. Kallition version 0.23.1 [106] was used to 
determine the gene expression where TPM from HLA-A HLA-B, and HLA-C were extracted 
from the data set. 
Surface protein expression analysis was performed by extracellular staining and flow 
cytometry on four RCC patient tumor cell lines, three melanoma patient tumor cell lines, and 
two control cell lines; T1 (positive control) and T2 (negative control). Wells with 10^5 cells 
each were incubated in R10 with or without 250 U/mL IFN-ɣ stimulation for 21 hours. 
Hereafter cells were washed twice in PBS + 2% FCS and stained with HLA-A/B/C pan specific 
primary antibodies; anti-HLA-A (Creative Diagnostics, Cat#DCABH-8114)), anti-HLA-B 
(Abcam, Cat#212436), anti-HLA-C (Biolegend, Cat#400301), isotype control (Biolegend, 
Cat#400301) or PBS for 30 min at 4 °C. After two additional washing steps, cells were 
incubated with FITC (fluorescein isothiocyanate) conjugated secondary antibody (Abcam, 
Cat#ab6785) for 30 min at 4 °C, washed twice and finally fixated in 1% paraformaldehyde 
(PFA, Santa Cruz Biotechnology, Cat#sc-281692) until analysis.  
The endogenous HLA class I immunopeptidomes of breast cancer cell lines EFM192A (HLA 
type: A*0101, A*2402, B*0702, B*3901, C*0702 and C*1203), MDA-MB-231 (HLA type: 
A*0201, A*0217, B*4002, B*4101, C*0202 and C*1701) and BT-549 (HLA type: A*0101, 
A*0201, B*1517, B*56, C*0303 and C*0701) were purified by MHC immunoaffinity
