Abstract
While Denmark scores high on societal and economic sustainability in the United Nations Sustainable
Development Goals (SDGs), the nation is failing both on environmental sustainability goals and on contributing
to SDG 2 Zero Hunger. According to the OECD , the largest challenge for Denmark to reach the SDGs lies
within the Danish agrifood system. Additionally, the agrifood sector is experiencing social and economic
challenges, despite Denmark’s general high score within these areas, which hinder an environmentally
sustainable transition.
In literature, a holistic approach with engagement of multiple stakeholders across the agri-food system is
frequently recommended to alleviate agrifood related sustainability challenges. However, most projects have
narrow research scopes and only involves a narrow group of stakeholders. This might be due to difficulty of
including a multitude og parameters from different sciences in a traditional research project.
This thesis aim to contribute to research and practice through an exploratory, applied design project
on improving the environmental sustainability of Danish agrifood systems with consideration for social
and economic factors as well. The high complexity of the task is managed through systems engineering
methodology. Participatory design is used to involve a diverse group of agrifood stakeholders, in order to
combine expertise in sustainable intervention design.
In the design project, regenerative agriculture and it’s associated agrifood system was identified as an
emerging opportunity for increasing sustainability holistically. The concept is attracting increasing support
and commitment from grassroots movements, large food corporations, established farmers and politicians
alike. While the diversity of committed stakeholders presents an opportunity for progress, it also poses a
potential threat. Collaboration between groups with such different interests may be limited or challenging,
slowing development and uptake, and also creating a risk of green-washing and continuation of systemic
problems. We assembled stakeholders across the system to co-develop interventions for the agricultural
consultancy Agrovi, to assist them in advancing development and implementation of ambitious regenerative
agriculture with farmers. The final interventions were a collection of interactive "initiative cards" for Agrovi,
that describe concrete actions they can take to establish thought leadership within regenerative agriculture
consulting, and a poster with visual models, aiming at creating a common language about regenerative
agriculture between stakeholders, in order to overcome collaboration barriers. Apart from the tangible output,
the project has resulted in stakeholders continuing collaboration outside the context of this project. The project
demonstrates how the design field can contribute to much-needed social cohesion and understanding among
agrifood stakeholders, as well as to the co-development of interventions that address holistic sustainability.

Learning objectives
Learning objectives defined by us. After completing our master thesis, we demonstrate that we:
1. can describe the Danish socio-technical agrifood system.
2. can meaningfully scope a project for both radical innovation and impact in a complex sector.
3. can analyze key environmental-, social and economical sustainability challenges in the Danish agricul-
ture system and identify which system objectives a solution should be based upon.
4. can develop socio-technical solutions in collaboration with key stakeholders that address the identified
system issues and evaluate them based on defined system objectives.
5. can design a solution for a complex stakeholder landscape and communicate results in a clear manner,
that is conducive to further work and implementation of the solution.
Learning objectives from the Technical University of Denmark (DTU). According to DTU, a Master of Science
in Engineering:
6. can identify and reflect on technical scientific issues and understand the interaction between the various
components that make up an issue.
7. can, on the basis of a clear academic profile, apply elements of current research at international level to
develop ideas and solve problems.
8. master technical scientific methodologies, theories and tools, and has the capacity to take a holistic
view of and delimit a comple, open issue, see it in a broader academic and societal perspective and, on
this basis, propose a variety of possible actions.
9. can, via analysis and modelling, develop relevant models, systems and processes for solving technolog-
ical problems.
10. can communicate and mediate research-based knowledge both orally and in writing.
11. is familiar with and can seek out leading international research within his/her specialist area.
12. can work independently and reflect on own learning, acaddemic development and specialisation.
13. masters technological problem-solving at a high level through project work, and has the capacity to
work with and manage all phases of a project - including preparation of timetables, design, solution
and documentation.

The Danish agrifood system faces significant challenges in terms of envi-
ronmental, societal, and economic sustainability. Despite the widespread
recognition that a holistic, participatory approach is necessary for sus-
tainable agrifood systems, few initiatives have effectively incorporated all
three aspects of sustainability and involved a broad range of stakeholders.
This thesis aims to address this gap by applying systems engineering
and participatory design methodologies to develop and implement a
sustainable intervention in the Danish agrifood system. By doing so, this
study seeks to test how the complex, interlinked sustainability challenges
facing agrifood systems can be addressed.
The goal of the design project developed from an initial broad focus on
’Designing The Future of Sustainable Agrifood Systems in Denmark’, and
narrowed down to ’Increasing environmental sustainability of the Danish
agrifood system while considering societal and economic sustainability,
by designing interventions for the agricultural consultancy Agrovi that
advance regenerative agriculture in Denmark’.
To ensure tangible impact on sustainability and test the holistic, par-
ticipatory approach in a real setting, we undertook an applied design
project with a client organisation and an advisory board. The client, the
agricultural consultancy Agrovi, was chosen because of their expertise in
farming and because they are a key enabler for changing mindsets and
practices of farmers. Additionally, to address holistic sustainability, an
advisory board was assembled with competencies within field technol-
ogy and automation (Teknologisk Institut), innovation in food systems
(DTU Skylab Foodlab), societal development and politics (The Thinktank
Frej, Guldborgsund Municipality and Business Lolland-Falster), and
regenerative agriculture advocacy (Foreningen Regenerativt Jordbrug
(eng. The Association for Regenerative Agriculture) and the regenerative
experimental farm Øm Klostergaard).
The stakeholders were consulted in continual meetings and assembled
for milesone presentations and a workshop. This collaborative approach
shows promise in developing collective understanding and interventions
that could not only be effective in advancing the environmental sustain-
ability of the Danish agrifood system, but also implementable by being
aligned with societal and economic needs of key stakeholders.

but through the situation analysis, observed that fundamental problems
of mindsets, unaligned goals and information flows were the deepest
potential leverage points for breaking system lock-ins (in line with the
recommendations of Wood et al. 2019). Thus, the final interventions
became:
1. "Initiative cards" for Agrovi, that describe concrete actions they can

consulting.
2. Visual models that make it easier to talk concretely about regenera-
tive agriculture. Can be used by Agrovi with farmers and between
stakeholders.
3. A poster, with some of the visual models, aiming at creating a com-
mon language about regenerative agriculture between stakeholders,
in order to overcome collaboration barriers.
Apart from the concrete interventions, a significant output from the
project was that collaborations between the stakeholders have started
outside the context of the project. As Tittonell et al. 2022 state: a "co-
innovation processes, with all the learning and social cohesion that is generated
through them (...), may be more important at fostering long-term sustainable
transitions than the initially promised outcomes". The process of stakeholder
engagement can in itself lead to a more resilient, adaptive agrifood system,
as described in the EU Agriculture Resilience Assesment Framework
(Appendix B.3). The process led to numerous promising project ideas
that we couldn’t follow through with in the context of this project, but
that can be anchored within the stakeholder group in future work.

1.1 Context of the project
Global context
Agrifood systems are the basis of human survival. They are both deeply
dependant on biosphere resources and a significant contributor to the
degradation of these resources. With the industrialisation of agriculture,
agricultural production per capita has outpaced population growth
and global food production has increased by 145% since the 1960’s. Yet,
undernourishment continues to remain widespread, while obesity is
increasing, causing ill health in both cases (Pretty 2008). At the same
time, roughly 1/3 of food is wasted globally (Gustavsson et al. 2011).
Growing evidence is suggesting that industrial agriculture has reached
critical environmental limits, and that the production growth trend is
likely to start reversing, if environmental impacts of agrifood systems
are not dramatically reduced (Pretty 2008).
Denmark: a country made of fields
Denmark is on a global 2nd place on reaching the UN Sustainable
Development Goals (SDGs), but still largely overshooting the planetary
boundaries and performing poorly on biosphere sustainability and global

In this project the Danish agrifood sector is perceived as an engineering

transformation into a desired agrifood system. This section indtroduces

2.1.1 Engineering system methodology
Maier, Oehmen, and Vermaas 2022b argue that major societal challenges
calls for a systemic approach. Likewise, as shown in section 3, researchers
also call for a holistic, systemic approach to address sustainability issues
in the agrifood system. Of this reason, we address the agrifood system
as an Engineering System.
Engineerging system
Definition by Maier, Oehmen, and Vermaas 2022a “Engineering systems
are defined as complex socio-technical systems that increasingly shape modern
lives.“
L. de Weck, Roos, and L. Magee 2011 has defined five characteristics
for an en engineering system.
==PAGE:32==
10
2 Approach
In the following we answer how the agrifood system fulfills the charac-
teristics of en engineering system:
1) Exists in the real world: The agrifood system is not just an abstract idea
- it does exist in the real world, as it includes tangible artifacts such as
food, tractors, farm buildings etc.
2) Artificial: A great part of the agrifood system has been created by
people. Without humans cultivating the land, the land would just be
nature.
3) Dynamic properties: Many of the properties in the agrifood system
changes. Examples are regulations, consumer behavior and climate
change.
4) Hybrid state: The agrifood system both contain continues and discrete
states. Example of a continuous state is photosynthesis (happens all the
time), an example of discrete state is rain, which happens and stops
again.
5) Some human control: Humans control many things in the agri-cultural
system (e.g. how to cultivate the land, what sort of food we eat). Many
functions also happens autonomously e.g. the seasons and the weather
changes automatically.
Weck et al. 2011 divides the evolution into engineering systems into 1)
Artefacts, 2) Complex system and finally 3) Engineering system. Examples
are respectively 1) a light bulb, 2) the electric power grid and 3) the energy
system.
These levels are used in this project to describe different layers of the
system. It was used to describe our project (e.g Figure 1.2, Section 1 ) and
objectives will be defined according to each level.
For engineering system design it is valid to use multiple theories, per-
spectives and processes. The different approaches bring different insights
about the design problem, and it depends on the context which specific
theory or method to use (Isaksson, Wynn, and Eckert 2022). We use the
methodologies of Systems Engineering and participatory Design. In the
following sections the methodologies will be introduces.

Systems approaches can be divided into "hard", "soft" and "Socio-
technical", where "hard" systems perspective mainly focus on integration
of technical artefacts into larger complex, socio-technical systems, and
"soft" mainly focus on the social aspects. (McKay, Broberg, and M. A.
Robinson 2022).
Systems Engineering originates from the "hard" systems perspective but
has with time developed into a rather socio-technical methodology. As
examples it was developed from the 1950s’"extensive, complex, interdisci-
plinary and large-scale projects – such as landing on the moon" and was in
1970s first time used out-site an engineering context for planning of traffic
and mobility in Switzerland (Züst and Troxler 2006). The methodology
used in this project is based on Züst and Troxler 2006, which takes a rather
socio-technical approach. However, the text book examples are rather
technical and when addressing stakeholder engagement it is stated by
Züst and Troxler 2006 that this is important information to base the goal
definition upon, but they only briefly touch upon how to do it stakeholder
engagement, which is a reason for supplementing this methodology with
participatory design, described in the in the following section.
Systems Engineering was chosen because the approach has a high degree
of structure (McKay, Broberg, and M. A. Robinson 2022) which can help
make the project manageable, which can be challenging when dealing
with lage-scale, complex systems.
The methodology is based on two methodical concepts; 1) the life cycle
model, which in this project is used to understand the development of the
agricultural system in the big perspective and 2) the problem solving cycle,
which the overarching planning of the project is based on. Beyond that
the methodology has a toolbox of qualitative and quantitative methods
for problem solving. The methods applied in this project are elaborated
in section 2.2.

2.1.3 Participatory design
Participatory Design
"Participatory design is an approach to design that attempts to actively
involve the people who are being served through design in the process to
help ensure that the designed product/service meets their needs." [...] A key
characteristic of the participatory design zone is the use of physical artifacts
as thinking tools throughout the process [...]L. Sanders 2008
A reason for designers to engage with stakeholders, stated by Yamauchi
2012, is that designers "typically have little knowledge about a particular
domain. They must learn alot". Likewise we had to learn a lot about the
agrifood system. In this project multiple stakeholders where engaged,
which according to Maier, Oehmen, and Vermaas 2022a is an typical
approach when doing engineering system design as a way to get details
about different system entities and relations while retaining the big
perspective.
So, we had stakeholders as informants in the project. However, we could
have gained expert knowledge from stakeholders without taking a par-
ticipatory design approach, so why did we take this specific approach?
Participatory design differs from other stakeholder interactive methods
(e.g. user centered design) by viewing stakeholders as partners rather than
a subject that is being studied. This means that the stakeholders become
co-designers, where they are perceived as "‘expert of his/her experience’
and plays a large role in knowledge development, idea generation and concept
development".(E. Sanders and Stappers 2008).
Having stakeholders as "partners" alleviates potential challenges when
designing an engineering system intervention.

2.2.2 Situation analysis methods
The purpose of the situation analysis is to make an assessment of the
system in order to later define goals for the search of solutions.
Below follows a description of the methods applied to fulfill thus pur-
pose.
System demarcation
A process in Systems Engineering used to map a system’s components
and interactions between these and to define the boundaries of the
system. Different demarcations are are 1) "Intervention system", 2)
==PAGE:38==
16
2 Approach
"Environment" (everything out-site the intervention system) and 3)
"Area of investigation" (covers the intervention system and the part
of the environment relevant to the task) (Züst and Troxler 2006).
A main activity when working with engineering systems is to actively
and consciously scope the system in a way so that it is clear what is
taken into consideration in the project, and how factors influence across
boundaries. We used system demarcation with this purpose.
Early in the project we mapped components in the Danish agrifood
system as we did exploratory research (Appendix A.2). After starting
focusing on soil-based agriculture, we initiated another mapping of who
could be clients and a part of the advisory board, that we expanded
on throughout the project (Appendix A.1). We used it both to get an
overview of relationships and to remember interesting components and
stakeholders. We drew several demarcations, that became simpler, as
we narrowed the scope. The final system demarcation can be seen in
section 5.5. We took inspiration in an existing mapping of agrifood
systems (Figure 3.2) for categorising the entities in the system.
SWOT analysis
A tool used to identify and evaluate the Strengths, Weaknesses,
Opportunities, and Threats of an organization or a project. Züst and
Troxler 2006 suggests this method to analyse the current system,
by identifying strengths and weaknesses, and the future system, by
identifying opportunities and threats.
SWOT is suggested by Züst and Troxler 2006 to be applied in the analysis
of the current and future state of the system. In this project, the SWOT
analysis was used as a sense-making framework for presenting the
relevant findings from the situation analysis in an manageable way.
Even so, as the scope of the project had a broad area of investigation,
few constraints and many investigated solution areas, the SWOT is also
extensive. The main purpose of the SWOT is thus to be a storage for the
many findings that will be relevant to refer to later. We did this based on
findings from literature review and field work. The SWOT analysis was
done as suggested by Züst and Troxler 2006:
1. Current behaviors were mapped onto the area of investigation in
the system demarcation using post-its in Miro.
2. Behaviors were sorted into strengths, neutral or weaknesses, based
on the project objectives.
3. Important areas in the current situation where defined, and future
trends were then defined mapped with use of post-its on a separate
copy of the area of investigation
4. The trends were sorted into opportunities, neutral or weaknesses,
based on the project objectives.
Cause analysis and 5 Why’s
Cause analysis is the process of identifying the factors that contributed
to an event or problem. "5 Why’s" is a specific cause analysis technique

We did cause analyses on the most central weaknesses and threats in the
SWOT analysis, selected based on the project scope. We did the cause
analysis to understand the true problems and to make the problems
actionable.
Cause analysis of Farmers’ Weakness W3.1 Early in the project, we

2.2.3 Conceptualisation methods
The overall purpose with the conceptualisation methods are to guide the
process of generating ideas and turn these into alternative concepts.
Brainwriting
Brainwriting is a method for generating a great number of ideas. It
differentiates from brainstorming by writing down ideas in stead of
exchanging ideas orally. It is based on the assumption that quantity
lead to quality (Boeĳen, Dalhuizen, and Zĳlstra 2020). Two sort of
brainwriting are:
Brainwalking: The central concept is to build on top of each others
ideas in silence. The idea "walks" from person to person.
Negative brainwriting: The central concept is to open op the solution
space by identifying bad ideas and extract good ideas on this basis.
Brainwriting was used to explore the solution space in the generation of
initial ideas. It was used in the beginning of iteration loops of development
==PAGE:40==
18
2 Approach
which occurred several times throughout the process. This none-oral
method was chosen in stead of brainstorming to avoid "production blocking",
which is "the inability of individuals in groups to express ideas as they occur
since only one idea can be effectively expressed at one time." (Paulus, Kohn, and
Dzindolet 2011). The idea generation in this project always happened in
teams, either in workshops with stakeholders or internally in the research
group existing of two people.
Brainwalking was applied as a brainwriting method, because it makes
it possible to collectively share ideas and hence increase the number of
topics without speaking. This method was used internally throughout
the project, both in the situation analysis and conceptualisation and in
the workshop (Advisory board event 2, Table 2.2).
Negative brainstorming combined with brainwalking was carried out
once internally in the research group, as it opens up for a new way of
thinking and also is a fun exercise, supporting a positive attitude which
enhances creativity (Paulus, Kohn, and Dzindolet 2011). It was not used
in the workshop due to time limitation and as it is our experience that it
can be challenging method to apply the first time.
Design drawing
Design drawing can be used in different ways throughout the design
process with the purposes of either defining, developing or delivering
(Boeĳen, Dalhuizen, and Zĳlstra 2020).
Design drawing was used both for defining, developing and delivering.
Design drawing for defining was typically used for the purpose of
alignment internally in the research group and with stakeholders. Design
drawings for defining could e.g. be a cause-effect tree or a organisational
diagram which we discussed with the client in order to be sure, that we
had understood these subjects correctly. It was also used to summarise
chosen preliminary sketches in previous development iterations, in
order to secure alignment about agreements internally which Figure 2.6
exemplifies.
Design drawing for development was often used in combination with
brainwalking with the purpose of exploring ideas visually, to efficiently
communicate ideas and compare them.
Design drawing to deliver was used to communicate concepts in the end
of development iteration with the purpose of having the stakeholders
evaluate the concepts. These sketches typically had an informative and
appealing character, in accordance to Boeĳen, Dalhuizen, and Zĳlstra
2020.

Morphology matrix
Method that helps to generate solution alternatives in an analytical
and systematic way. It involves a decomposition of the overall function
that the solution must fulfill and combines means that fulfill each
function (Boeĳen, Dalhuizen, and Zĳlstra 2020, Martin and Hanington

correlation between solutions and functions that the solution fulfill.

Collection of objectives that the solution should fulfill. It guides the
search for solution and makes it possible to evaluate solutions (Züst

Objectives are divided into three system levels: artefact level (regards

the specific artefacts designed), complex system level (regards client
organisation and advisory board), and engineering system level (the
agrifood system). Objectives on all levels are taken into account in the
intervention design to best possibly create wanted knock on effects (and
avoid unwanted rebound effects) in the rest of the system. Decisions made
throughout the process were guided by these objectives, e.g. decisions
about the scoping of the project and evaluation of the solutions. Objectives
where developed and edited continually throughout the process, as we
got more information about the system. The objectives on the artefact
level first really became clear after we had decided what concept to design,
because the concepts where too different to have the same objectives on
the artefact level. In design projects, prioritisation of objectives helps
to focus efforts and resources on the most important aspects of the
project (Züst and Troxler 2006). For this reason, the objectives on the
engineerging system level and complex system level are divided into
prioritization of high, neutral and low. Objectives on the artefact level are
further specified into key performance indicators (KPI’s). The KPI’s is a
suggestion for how to measure whether the solution have the wanted
effect, but an actual evaluation of the solution based on KPI’s is not done
in this project.
Weighted decision matrix
An method to evaluate design concepts, where each objective have
been given a specific value according to the importance of the objective.
The decision matrix was used to make a systematic evaluation of five
concepts, where it was difficult to detect why one idea was better than
an other. The evaluation happened through subjectively assessing each
concept and giving them a score. The weighting of objectives was cho-
sen in order to take into account that objectives where prioritized as
respectively high, medium and low. The limitation of the method is that
the concept with the highest score is not necessarily the best. However,
the process of systematic evaluating the concepts can in itself give an
understanding for why one concept is chosen rather than another.

This chapter explains how we apply participatory design and systems en-

complex socio-technical systems. System Engineering (SE) is applied

In reviewed literature on regenerative agriculture (Benson et al. 2023;

Tittonell et al. 2022 states that regenerative agriculture needs a compre-

suit local needs and contexts. Transdisciplinary research that incorporates
farmers’ knowledge and builds on farmer-to-farmer knowledge exchange
is also recommended. It is concluded that co-innovation processes, with

more important at fostering long-term sustainable transitions than the initially

They call for a "coalition of actors to develop an outcome-based framework for
assessing regenerative agricultural practices that can be used universally" and
state that "All stakeholders will have a role to play, including farmers, policy

can play an important role in bringing together these stakeholders to assess,

No analysis was identified on the Danish regenerative agriculture system
as a whole.
Overall, we take away from these sources the following key recommen-
dations:
1. Remember to reflect on and define the desired outcomes of applying
regenerative agriculture (Giller et al. 2021). A way to do this could be
developing an outcome-based regenerative agriculture framework
(Benson et al. 2023), scientifically setting, tracking, and measuring
the outcomes from regenerative agricultural practices (EASAC
2022) with recognition of context-specific differences, in order
to determine regenerative agricultural practices that can be used
universally (Benson et al. 2023; Tittonell et al. 2022).
2. Use collaborative design incorporating farmers, policy makers,
businesses, civil society, academia and donor organizations (Benson
et al. 2023; Tittonell et al. 2022). The resulting learning and social
cohesion from bringing together these actors may have bigger
benefits for long-term sustainable transition than the concrete work
made by this stakeholder group (Tittonell et al. 2022).
3. Explicitly incorporate social, economic and political considerations
in regenerative agriculture (Giller et al. 2021; Tittonell et al. 2022). A
way to do this could be tighter links with the science and movement
of agroecology (Tittonell et al. 2022).

3.2.2 Defining sustainability of an agrifood system
There are several recognized definitions of a sustainable (agri)food system,
and more specifically of sustainable agriculture. They put emphasis on
and detail different aspects but share many characteristics. In this section,
internationally recognized definitions are reviewed from the UN and
well-cited scholars, in order to synthesize a definition at a high detail-level,
which will be useful for the goal definition of our design project.
The Food and Agriculture Organization of the United Nations, FAO,
define a sustainable food system as "one that delivers food security and
nutrition for all in such a way that the economic, social and environmental bases
to generate food security and nutrition for future generation is not compromised.
This means that:
▶it is profitable throughout (economic sustainability);
▶It has broad-based benefits for society (social sustainability); and
▶It has a positive or neutral impact on the natural environment (environ-
mental sustainability)." (FAO 2018a).
As seen in the definition by FAO, sustainability is commonly described
as being divided into social, environmental and economic sustainability,
also referred to as the 3 Ps (people, planet, profit). The three pillars
or three overlapping circles of sustainability are common depictions of
sustainability (see Figure 3.3), though no vigorous theoretical description
was identified in a review by Purvis, Mao, and D. Robinson 2019. A later
paradigm describe the 3 Ps not as separate pillars, but as being contained
inside each other, with environment being the condition for the others
(Purvis, Mao, and D. Robinson 2019). An example of this paradigm shift is
the wedding cake model by the Stockholm Resilience Centre (Figure 3.4).
It is described by professor Johan Rockström to convey that "economies
and societies should be seen as embedded parts of the biosphere”, not as separate
parts (Stockholm Resilience Centre 2016).
To more specifically define goals for sustainable development, the UN
Sustainable Development Goals (the SDGs) is one of the most commonly
used frameworks, adopted by all UN Member States. These goals all
include specific targets UN 2015. All SDGs can be directly or indirectly
linked to the agrifood system (FAO 2016; Stockholm Resilience Centre
2016; Wood et al. 2019). See Appendix B.1 for an illustration of how each
SDG is related to the agrifood system. Specifically for climate change
mitigation and biodiversity, the COP targets for 2050(?). EU Green Deal
Targets and Farm to Fork strategy.
One of the most cited definitions of agricultural sustainability, Pretty
2008, divides agricultural sustainability into 1) resilience (the capacity of
systems to buffer shocks and stresses); and 2) persistence (the capacity
of systems to continue over long periods). The concept of resilience can
be understood through the EU Resilience Framework (Meuwissen et al.
2018), or the simplified version by Röös et al. 2021 (see Appendix B.3).
In the framework, some of the attributes of a resilient system include:
enterprise diversification, stakeholder engagement, farm heterogeneity,
& an open attitude to innovation.

Definition used in this thesis
A sustainable agrifood system in this project will be defined as:
A system delivering food security and nutrition for all (FAO 2018a), in a
way that is:
1. Resilient: able to buffer shocks and stresses (Pretty 2008), from:
a) Environmental events (like drought, pests etc.); b) Economic
changing sales opportunities (due to consumer preferences, value
chain disturbances etc.); and c) Societal loss of identity and capacity
to act as planetary stewards (Röös et al. 2021).
2. Persistent: able to continue for a long time (Pretty 2008), so that
the economic, social and environmental bases to generate food
security and nutrition for future generation is not compromised

3.3.1 Biodiversity
This section will cover SDG 14 & 15, life on land and below water, as a
part of asessing environmental sustainability. Measured by the SDGs,
Denmark has significant challenges on biodiversity below water (goal
14), where efforts have stagnated. Regarding biodiversity on land (goal
15), according to the SDG indicators, challenges remain, but efforts are on
track (Figure 3.5). As other recognized indicators show that Denmark is
one of the countries most challenged on biodiversity in Europe, the SDG
indicators for life on land might be limited in showing a representative
picture in a Danish context. Denmark has the lowest share of habitats
with a good habitat status in the EU, together with Belgium, with only 5%
of Danish habitats being in favorable condition (European Commission
and European Environment Agency 2022).
Life on land
Like in the rest of Europe, agriculture is the main pressure on natural
habitats (European Court of Auditors 2020). Denmark is one of the most
heavily cultivated land areas in the world, with over 60% of the land
being agriculture, leaving very limited space for habitats, as seen in
Figure 3.6. Of the agricultural land, 76% is used for producing animal
feed, 4% is used for producing food for direct human consumption, 12%
is used for industrial food products like sugar beets and malt for beer,
and 8% is producing non-food products (e.g. energy crops, paper, timber,
Christmas trees). Further, an area equivalent of 14% of Danish land area
is used outside of Denmark for soy production, mainly used for animal
feed (Lundsgaard et al. 2022).
Other pressures on Danish biodiversity from agriculture include pesti-
cides, that threaten especially insects (Lundsgaard et al. 2022).
Life below water
The majority of coastal habitats are in bad condition as seen in Figure 3.7.
A contributing factor is oxygen depletion, coming from added nutrients
(nitrogen, phosphorous, carbon, ammonia). Nutrients can be added
through run-off or atmospheric fallout, and come from Danish and
foreign agriculture, and other factors, like waste water emissions and
combustion (Jung-Madsen et al. 2021). Reviewing literature, we were not
able to find a solid summarized estimate on agricultural contributions
to nutrient levels in Danish water bodies. Based on reports with highly
varying numbers from lobby organisations on the subject (e.g. Leif
Knudsen 2017; Lundsgaard et al. 2022), and from a law suit of a professor
from an agricultural lobby organisation (Ingeniøren and Bredsforff 2019),
we conclude that these estimates are complex and the topic highly
political.

that "As consequence, Denmark is at a large distance from meeting targets on

marine pollution (Target 14.1).". The fact nitrogen surplus is so high, and

3.3.2 Climate action
This section will cover SDG 13, climate action, as a part of asessing
environmental sustainability. Measured by the SDGs, Denmark has major
challenges on climate action (goal 13), where efforts are moderately
improving (Figure 3.5).
GHG emissions from Danish agriculture
Today, 35% of Danish green house gas (GHG) emissions come from
agriculture, and in 2030, it is estimated to become 45% (Energistyrelsen
2022). As other sectors reduce emissions, reductions have not been made
in the agricultural sector since 2009 (Klimarådet 2016, fig. 3).
93% of agricultural emissions stem from animal production (including
production of animal feed, which is 31% of total emissions), and 7% of
emissions stem from other crops (Klimarådet 2016, fig. 4) - other crops
are assumed to include crops for direct human consumption, industrial
food processing crops, and non-food crops.
Spill-over effects
What emits green house gasses on a farm? Including fossil fuel consump-
tion on the farm (relevant for not tilling). Including that production of
synthetic inputs are energy intense.
GHG emissions from Danish food habits
The climate impact of average Danish diets per capita is higher than
average for high-income countries. Roughly 2/3 of diet related emissions
come from meat and dairy consumption (see Figure 3.8). According to
the GlobAgri WRR model (World Resources Institute 2016) changing
diets needs to be the biggest reduction contributor to achieve 2050 GHG
reduction targets.

of the Danish agrifood system.

3.3.5 Economic sustainability
This section will relate to SDG 8, Decent work and economic growth. The
overall indicator performance on this goal is high for Denmark (Sachs
et al. 2022). Yet, there are significant economic challenges threatening
the persistence and resilience of the Danish agrifood system.
The Danish agricultural sector faces several economic challenges, includ-
ing rising production costs, which in 2015 ans 2016 exceeded the value
of production (Lundsgaard et al. 2022). This means declining profits
for farmers, and without agricultural aid, many Danish farmers would
only have a small financial surplus (Lundsgaard et al. 2022). Danish
consumers are accustomed to spending a relatively small share of their
income on food, and with increased globalisation, this challenges prof-
itability of Danish farming. According to Rasmussen 2017, the average
Danish household spends 13% of their income on food.

3.4 Summary
Research question 2
What are meaningful objectives for a design project aimed at aiding a
holistic sustainable transition of the Danish agrifood system?
The overall objective of this project is to help transition the Danish
agrifood system towards sustainability.
A sustainable agrifood system in this project is defined as:
A system delivering food security and nutrition for all (FAO 2018a), in a
way that is:
1. Resilient: able to buffer shocks and stresses (Pretty 2008), from:
a) Environmental events (like drought, pests etc.); b) Economic
changing sales opportunities (due to consumer preferences, value
chain disturbances etc.); and c) Societal loss of identity and capacity
to act as planetary stewards (Röös et al. 2021).
2. Persistent: able to continue for a long time (Pretty 2008), so that
the economic, social and environmental bases to generate food
security and nutrition for future generation is not compromised
(FAO 2018a).
The economic, social and environmental sustainability of an agrifood
system can be assessed in part by the performance on the targets defined
in the UN Sustainable Development Goals (UN 2015), COP agreements,
the EU Farm to Fork strategy (European Commission 2020) and the
EU Resilience Assessment Framework (Appendix B.3). Environmental
sustainability (a positive or neutral impact on the natural environment
(FAO 2018a) will be seen as a precondition for a sustainable society
and economy (as in Figure 3.4).
Decreasing environmental impacts of Danish agrifood systems is the
highest prioritised objective of this project, as this is the sustainability
area where Danish agrifood systems have the most critical problems.
Specifically biodiversity will have a high priority in this project, as
Green House Gas emissions are the sole parameter considered in many
other efforts (an effect called carbon tunnel vision) and the planetary
boundaries for biodiversity are further exceeded. Societal and economic
considerations are taken into account, but prioritised less. Considering
those afford successful implementation, and are important for change
towards environmental sustainability, as Danish agrifood systems face
societal and economic challenges regarding continued resilience and
persistence. Contributing to global food security through reducing meat
consumption and production is another objective of the project, as
Danish performance on SDG 2 Zero Hunger has major challenges, largely
due to meat consumption and extensive industrial meat production.

processes in the project can be seen in Table 3.2. A simplified version

in specialty stores. The official government-certification was introduced
in 1987, as the world’s first organic certification (Faktalink n.d.; Landbrug
& Fødevarer n.d.). In 2020, 13% of products sold in Danish retail stores
and 11,7% of agricultural land was certified Danish Organic (Landbrug
& Fødevarer n.d.), attesting to the popularity of the scheme. Whereas
the number of conventional farm holdings have decreased the last many
years, the number of organic farm holdings have remained steady, and a
on average more profitable than conventional farms (Nielsen 2016).
It is described as central to the organic mindset to focus on maintaining
or increasing the fertility of the soil (Landbrug & Fødevarer n.d.), though
this is currently not a part of the certification documentation requirements
(Landbrugsstyrelsen 2022).
Scandinavian organic agriculture typically obtain yields 0-50% lower
than conventional farming (Økologisk Landsforening and Miljø- og
Fødevareudvalget 2016), because it is difficult to optimize nutrients for
crops without synthetic fertilizer, and because pests cannot be controlled
with synthetic pesticides. Biodiversity advocates argue that even though
organic agriculture is less yield efficient, it still produces more calories
and nutrients for humans per hectare, because fewer animals and more
food for direct human consumption is produced in organic agriculture
(Lundsgaard et al. 2022).
Organic agriculture have the challenge that they have to till more in order
to control weeds, which is energy intensive and disturb constructive
biological processes in the soil.
Conservation Agriculture
Conservation Agriculture is defined as “a farming system that promotes
minimum soil disturbance (i.e. no tillage), maintenance of a permanent soil cover,
and diversification of plant species. It enhances biodiversity and natural biological
processes above and below the ground surface, which contribute to increased water
and nutrient use efficiency and to improved and sustained crop production”. (FAO
n.d.). The practice of no tillage is what makes Conservation Agriculture
==PAGE:73==

most unique, as tilling has been central in Danish agriculture for 5000
years (Tænketanken Frej n.d.). 20% of agricultural land globally and
1,6% of the agricultural land in Denmark is cultivated as conservation
agriculture (SEGES Innovation 2021).
Conservation Agriculture stems from the conventional, and not the
grassroots world. It started on the American prairie in the 1940s to mitigate
extensive wind erosion of the plowed land, which had no deep roots to
keep the soil in place (Tænketanken Frej n.d.). It is especially popular in
countries with severe soil fertility and wind and water erosion challenges.
In Denmark, the interest among farmers is growing (Munkholm et al.
2020; SEGES Innovation 2021).
Conservation agriculture is especially challenged by it’s dependence on
pesticides to control weeds. The EU has a strategy to phase out pesticides
(European Commission 2020), which will threaten the existance of con-
servation agriculture, if practices are not developed to be less dependant
on pesticides.
Agroecology
Agroecology is defined as “a holistic and integrated approach that simul-
taneously applies ecological and social concepts and principles to the design
and management of sustainable agriculture and food systems” (FAO 2018b).
It is both a science studying human-biosphere influences within food
production, a set of practices described in 10 interdependent elements,
and a social movement, looking at food systems interdisciplinarily from
farm to fork. Agroecology advocates for a redesign of the global food
system and has a strong political angle on social justice and advocating
indigenous rights, and preserving local knowledge and culture (Cabral
et al. 2022; Ebel 2020).
The agroecological practice elements are: 1. diversity, 2. co-creation and
sharing of knowledge, 3. synergies, 4. efficiency, 5. recycling, 6. resilience,
7. human and social values, 8. culture and food traditions, 9. responsible
governance, 10. circular and solidarity economy (FAO 2018b).

4.3.1 Review of the impact of farm size on sustainability
The sources Ebel 2020; Esquivel et al. 2021; Inwood 2013 are used as
sources on farm size and sustainability.
==PAGE:79==

The paper "The "Sweet Spot" in the Middle: Why Do Mid-Scale Farms
Adopt Diversification Practices at Higher Rates?" (Esquivel et al. 2021)
explores why mid-scale farms tend to adopt diversification practices at
higher rates than both small and large farms. The study uses survey data
from farms in the northeastern United States and finds that mid-scale
farms are better able to balance the risks and benefits of diversification
due to their size, managerial capacity, and access to markets. Additionally,
mid-scale farms tend to have more diversified income streams and are
more likely to engage in direct marketing, which increases their flexibility
in responding to market changes. The authors suggest that policies
aimed at supporting mid-scale farms could have significant benefits for
promoting sustainable and resilient agriculture systems.
The paper "Social Forces and Cultural Factors Influencing Farm Transi-
tion" (Inwood 2013), among other things, describe the difference between
two types of farmers: MG (multi-generational) farmers and FG (first-
generation) farmers. It is described how MG farmers have access to
resources such as knowledge, equipment, land, capital, and credit, which
they can pass down to their heirs, allowing for intense specialization
but also making adaptation to new production and marketing systems
difficult. In contrast, FG farmers struggle to access these resources, face
high barriers to entry, and may have limited farming skills. However,
younger FG farmers are often more entrepreneurial and willing to take
risks, while older FG farmers may struggle to socialize their children into
agriculture and pass on the necessary values.
The paper "Are small farms sustainable by nature? - Review of an ongoing
misunderstanding in agroecology" (Ebel 2020) challenges the widespread
belief in agroecology literature that small farms are inherently more
sustainable than large farms. It argues that studies that allegedly prove
the sustainability of small farms actually compare management strategies,
not farm size. The review suggests that the elevated sustainability of
small farms is not always evidenced, and that small farms are not
necessarily sustainable just because of their size. The crucial factor in farm
sustainability is the farmer’s attitude to farm management, which might
be shaped by the sociocultural status of the farmers typically involved
in small scale farming. The review argues that creating conditions so
that large farms can be successfully operated like biodiverse small farms
should be a mission undertaken in politics and education.
4.3.2 Discussion of focussing on farms <100 ha
We are not refraining from visiting and designing for larger farms than
100 ha, but aim at incorporating considerations for small farms when
designing our interventions. Today, infrastructure and new technology is
primarily developed for large-scale industrial farms, as these players have
the finances to invest in R&D and products, and because agricultural sup-
port (’landbrugsstøtte’) favor large farms, because of the administrative
burden (Nielsen 2016). These types of farms tend to have low diversity in
crops and a smaller range of different activities than small farms, and
work towards standardization (Inwood 2013). Overall, this means that
most sustainable technological development is pushing Danish agricul-
ture towards centralization and standardization, e.g. monocultures, as
described in Section 3.3. However, many stakeholders argue that there

new business models and cultivation systems. Generational change is
described as one of the biggest challenges in Danish agriculture (see

increasingly larger farms, making them heavily indebted and forcing
them to run a multi-million dkk business right at the start of their ca-

This chapter describes and analyses our data collection findings in order
to define where to intervene in the system and what key objectives should
be.
The following research question will be answered in this chapter:
Research question 4
What is the future behavior within sustainability of the Danish
agrifood system without an intervention, with an offset in regenerative
agriculture? Which objectives for Agrovi and their value chain arise
from this analysis?
The area of investigation is introduced in section 5.1, then analysed using
SWOT (Section 5.2) and cause-effect analyses (Section 5.3,5.4), in order
to define the most meaningful objectives for and area of intervention in
Section 5.5.
The analysis takes an offset in key findings from the literature review (3.3)
which are built upon with our field work findings (see an overview of
fieldwork in Table 2.2). The approach to the situation analysis is described
and justified in 2.2.2.
The chapter leads to a definition of objectives for the intervention on
the complex system level, a reevaluation of the true problem and a
demarcation of where in the investigated area the intervention can take
place (intervention system).

▶Function: serving the farmers different needs through consultation

Regenerative agriculture is an emerging approach with several environ-

gradual transition to implement regenerative agriculture with farmers
and for the ecosystem services to start performing to bring the benefits

expertise, because they are owned by farmers’ associations, and have to
spend most of their time on what farmers’ are demanding here and now.
It becomes like the hen or the egg, which should come first? Demand
from the farmers or expertise from the consultants? Based on these
findings, we hypothesise that without intervention, the implementation
of regenerative agriculture will be delayed compared to how fast the
demand will increase. Given the urgency of addressing the pressing
environmental challenges of climate change and biodiversity loss, the
slow implementation of regenerative agriculture represents a missed
opportunity for the sustainability of the Danish agrifood system.
Furthermore, there is a risk that without intervention, food companies
will be able to define regenerative agriculture in a way that suit them
(corporate agriculture, as defined in Figure 4.8), creating a risk of green-

5.3 Findings from cause analysis: Why do few
farmers take up regenerative practices?
In this section, the most relevant reasons for few farmers taking up
regenerative practices will be selected and explained (Weakness W3.1,
Table 5.2 & Threat T3.2, Figure 5.4).
The fish bone diagram is comprised of findings from the data collection,
and was detailed and verified at a client visit (client visit 3, Table 2.2)
and the advisory board workshop (advisory board event 2, Table 2.2).
The approach is described in Section 2.2.2.
The fish bone diagram can be seen in Figure 5.2. A deeper explanation
with supporting field work observations that nuance each fish bone can
be found in Appendix D.2.
As seen in Figure 5.2, the four main causes identified for farmers not
transitioning to regenerative practices are that it is A) difficult to diverge
from the norm, it entails B) high risk, there is a C) lack of evidence,
knowledge and experience, and D) few incentives to transition.
5.3.1 Interpretation
When defining objectives for Agrovi and their supply chain in section
5.5.2, we will focus on the areas marked with purple outline in the model.
These are findings within A) difficult to diverge from the norm and C)
lack of evidence, knowledge and experience. We will focus on these, as
the barriers of few incentives and high risk are expected to decrease
without intervention. This will be further justified in the following.

practices without significant testing and experience, while consultants are
hesitant to invest in developing their expertise without a clear demand for
their services - especially since the consultancies are owned by farmers’
associations and should only supply what farmers demand and run

come first, the supply of or demand for consulting?", leading to a waiting
game. Given the urgency to act on environmental threats, this delayed
implementation is a loss for sustainability of the agrifood system.

by the regenerative frontrunners. Even though the practices of regen-
erative agriculture might overlap more with conversation agriculture
than organic agriculture, the ideologies and discourses are more similar
between regenerative and organic agriculture, as they both originate
from grassroots movements. We hypothesise that the agricultural exper-
tise of established farmers combined with the holistic approach of the
regenerative frontrunners, could lead to a holistically more sustainable
regenerative agrifood system, where realism and ambition is balanced
for maximum impact.
Because of these ideological differences, organic- and conservation agri-
culture farmers and organisations frequently avoid collaboration, even
though their competencies would supplement each other in developing
the practices of regenerative agriculture. If Agrovis’ and the conservation
agriculture farmers’ expertise is not leveraged in the development of
regenerative agriculture, it can delay a broad implementation of regener-
ative agriculture, at a loss to the sustainability of the system as a whole.
Without collaboration, there is also a risk of the definition of regenera-
tive agriculture becoming less ambitious and continue the problems of
conventional agrifood systems, if food companies set the tone and define
regenerative agriculture together with agricultural consultancies accord-

push the ambitions, but might not reach many skilled established farmers
and consultants with their perspectives without an intervention.

and become a frontrunning regenerative consultancy. This involves

6.2.1 Introduction to the concept
How did we go from iteration 2 to this final concept? We continued
working with the LandBO course. A drawing for defining the concept
can be seen in Appendix E.3 We made a prototype of the actual course
material as a power-point presentation, but realised that what we should
contribute with in the course, is a way to speak about regenerative
agriculture. We had earlier on developed early iterations of models for
easing the understanding of regenerative agriculture, which where used
as a starting point for the final concept.
Why? The overall purpose of the final concept is that Agrovi becomes
expert in advising farmers in regenerative agriculture, and position
themselves as the leading Danish regenerative agriculture advisors. This
is important as it 1) makes it more likely that Agrovi attracts stakeholders
leading the regenerative agenda and hence enable that stakeholder
with backgrounds in regenerative, organic and conservation agriculture
begin collaborating and 2) catalyzes the implementation of regenerative
agriculture at the farmer, as there currently is no established Danish

6.2.2 Poster
Why?
The purpose of the poster is to enable the collaboration between stakehold-
ers with different backgrounds, by giving them a common understanding
for what regenerative agriculture is.
Use scenario The poster will be printed out and given to Agrovi and the
other advisory board members. The intention is that the poster hangs on
the wall at their office. This will contribute to a common understanding
between members of the advisory board, which potentially can give rise
to a collaboration between them.
How?
The poster (A0 format) exists of five advises for improving the conver-
sation about regenerative agriculture and includes models (elaboratied
in the following section) that suggests how to understand aspects of
regenerative agriculture.
The five advises are based upon findings from the workshop we facilitated
( Advisory board event 2) and Conference 3 Table 2.2. The following list
shows the five advises and five problems adressed in the poster:
1) Problem: How does one actually practice regenerative agriculture?
There are many desired outcomes of regenerative agriculture and many
suggestions for practices, which makes it difficult to agree on what
the practices should be. Advice: Different practices have different doc-
umented effects. Pick practices based on documented effects, which is
illustrated in a model on the poster. Use practices that have documented
effects, which is summarised in the
==PAGE:114==
92
6 Interventions for regenerative agriculture in Denmark
2) Problem: Conservation and organic agriculturalists have different
views on whether it is most important not to till or not to spray. However,
their combined knowledge can be the key to reaching the application
of no-till organic regenerative agriculture. Advice: Conservation and
organic agriculturalists should cooperate. They should see no-till organic
regenerative agriculture as a common goal with different levels on the
way towards the goal, where they each come from different starting
points.
3) Problem: How should a farmer transition towards regenerative agricul-
ture? There are many practices suggested in regenerative agriculture that
fulfill the same purpose in connection to cultivating crops. Which one
should be done? Advice: Approach the practices as different options that
fulfill different functions for cultivating crops. Pick practices so that all
functions are fulfilled. Do not change everything in one day, but change
current practice with regenerative practices over time in conversation
with your farming advisor.
4) Problem: Everyone discusses and often disagree about the amount
of carbon that can be stored in the ground through no-tillage, which
make stakeholders hesitate about whether to implement it or not. Advice:
Move away from the tunnel-vision on CO2 and no-tillage, because 1)
other regenerative practices have documented effect on CO2 storage and
2) there are many other important benefits from regenerative agriculture
than CO2 storage including increased biodiversity en reducing soil
erosion.
5) Problem: Should regenerative agriculture only take responsibility for
sustainability for soil in the specific field or also for the sustainability of
the bigger agri-food system? Advice: The big picture should be taken
into account. One need to think about what they produces food for. The
sustainability of a field managed with regenerative practices but produces
crops for animals is still not sustainable in the big picture.

6.4 Summary
This chapter addresses the following research question: "What are in-
terventions that can advance the regenerative agrifood system in Denmark &
be implemented by Agrovi?". This includes an elaboration of the alterna-
tive concepts, the final intervention, objectives for the final intervention,

where developed and evaluated. The development can be simplified

across conservation agriculture, regenerative agriculture and organic
agriculture to collaborate and 2) make Agrovi the leading agricultural
consultancy within regenerative agriculture by taking though leadership.

agriculture, which gives sign of a successful implementation.
It can be argued that the intervention have been in an implementation
process throughout the projects, as the engagement of stakeholders in
the development and evaluation phase possibly have made them more
receptive to the final intervention.

7.1 Reflective discussion: Implications and

In the following section we will reflect on the implications and limitations
of the findings related to each research question that was answered in this
thesis. A summary of the findings themselves to each research question
can be found at the end of each chapter, with main points summarized
in the conclusion, section 7.2.
7.1.1 Research question 1: The application of Participatory
Design and Systems Engineering for sustainable
agrifood systems
Research Question 1
How can participatory design and systems engineering be applied to
design interventions that advance holistic sustainable transition of
the Danish agrifood system?
Overall, Systems Engineerging (SE) was used to bring structure to the
process and Participatory Design helped collecting understanding stake-
holder needs and to facilitate co-design.
Findings from applying the approach can be summarised into:
1) The value of Participatory Design goes beyond designing an inter-
vention that fits peoples’ needs. It also plays a crucial role in changing
the involved stakeholders mindset and can be seen as an intervention
in itself. However, the success of the method might be sight to facilita-
tion competencies and personally traits of the people using it, and you
need to compromise with other tasks, as it is time consuming to involve
stakeholders.
2) Although we have used both a method of high structure (SE) and one
of low structure (Participatory Design) it is still extremely difficult to
navigate the balance between flexibility and constraints in a project like
this, where there are no constraints to begin with.
These findings are discussed below.
Participatory Design: High reward, but difficult to execute
We experienced in this project that the process of co-designing within
itself can be change-making. In our project it became a goal to increase
collaboration between stakeholders, and when bringing them together
in workshops, the collaboration had already begun; stakeholders where
introduces to each other, they got to know each others’ perspectives
and to create solutions together. Still after the project, 4 stakeholders
have been in contact with each other, continuing collaboration outside
==PAGE:124==
102
7 Discussion and conclusion
the boundaries of this project, which we are proud of having facilitated
through Participatory Design.
The process of creating also appears to have a motivational effect on
people. We received positive response from workshop participants about
that it "sparked a fire" in them. Perhaps too often, the focus is on the
problems, although it is most inspiring to be a part of the solution.
Co-designing can in the future be used more as a motivational driver for
people, which is crucial for them wanting to engage in the project.
We encourage to apply Participatory Design when trying to make a
change that involve people in some way. However, the methodology has
its limitation.
First of all, it is highly time consuming, because meetings has to be
planned, relations must be nourished, workshops must be planned and
the big amount of data that is generated must be logged and analysed.
Therefor it is important to prioritize who to engage with and how much it
makes sense to engage with them. In this project, we chose stakeholders
based on the expertise we would like to have in the project. We naturally
spend most time interacted with our client, but otherwise the interaction
was based on a gut-feeling on when it would be natural to nourish the
relation and on when we had questions for that specific stakeholder. In a
future project, the Stakeholder Assessment method could be a way to
systematically choose stakeholders and for how much to engage with
them.
Secondly, we assume that interaction between people always to some
degree depend personality. It is possible that the result of the stakeholder
engagement would not have been the same, if it was not specifically
Kristine Fisker and Sara Dømler who carried out the project. This makes
it tricky to ensure the success of applying Participatory Design, and rises
the suggestion that when using this approach the "chemistry" between the
researcher or designers and the engaged stakeholders also is important
when choosing which stakeholders to engage with.
Finally, we also highlight that doing Participatory Design requires the
right skill-set. We have experienced participating in workshops, that
where not well facilitated, including one in this project. A typical flaw
is that the workshops are lacking structure, which results in a fuzzy
process, where people misunderstand each other, or that "workshop" is a
cover name for meetings including different stakeholders but have no
knowledge-sharing or generative elements. If people get bad experiences
with workshops it might result in a bad reputation for the field. We
recommend that people that facilitate co-design take the time to learn
about workshop facilitation both in order to gain good results, but also
in respect for the field.
Navigating levels of constraints with SE and PD as approach
We base this discussion on that a balance of constraints must be reached
in order to perform best possibly, inspired by (BALDER)
This project is characterised by a high level of flexibility in several ways.
From the beginning of the project the only constraints where to make a
project that advance sustainability in Danish agriculture and that fulfills
the requirement of a master thesis. This might be an extreme case, but
we expect that it is not uncommon for a project that aims at designing an

Choosing a client narrowed our scope significantly and have great impact

We applied the System Demarcation method with the goal of understand-

a project, but this is also the time, where your decisions have the most

you do not know anything about? In conclusion, we would wish for

project can be used as a pre-project, where ideas that we conceived but
did not have time to follow can be used as starting points together with
the stakeholders we identified. Projects could e.g. be to make a business
model catalogue for small, regenerative agriculture farms, or to build
a machine that both can do weed control without ploughing or using
pesticides.
7.1.2 Research question 2: Objectives for a sustainable
Danish agrifood systems
Research Question 2
What are meaningful objectives for a project that is aimed at aiding a
transition to a sustainable Danish agrifood system?
An answer to the research question can be found in section 3.4. The
overall objective of the project was to decrease biosphere impacts of the
Danish agrifood system, with a particular prioritization of biodiversity,
==PAGE:126==

with consideration for societal and economic sustainability, which would
facilitate implementation.
The reflections that arose in this objective definition can be summarized
as follows: Firstly, while setting goals can increase objectivity, it might be
most useful in making subjective priorities explicit. Secondly, achieving
scientific rigor can be challenging when dealing with holistic objectives,
but it may be necessary to move outside the comfort zone of research
advance sustainable agrifood research. Thirdly, we are unsure about
global effects of our intervention, but suspect reduced meat production
might be a good objective for global sustainability. Lastly, the objectives
defined might serve as a foundation for future projects.
Goal definition can help with increasing objectivity, but perhaps even
more with making subjective priorities explicit
What should goal definition be based upon? Our experience with the
topic of environmental impacts of Danish agriculture is that it is political
and divisive, and goal definition for a project is inherently uncertain,
especially since the future development of the system is uncertain and
interlinked. This was certainly also the case for our project, where we
grappled with prioritizing limited resources. Due to the broadness of our
defined goals, we didn’t have time to explicitly map trade-offs between
project objectives, and evaluation had to rely on subjective assessments
rather than performance indicators. To mitigate the subjectivity, we an-
chored objectives in what has been defined by recognized international
institutions and researchers, and sought input from experts on our advi-
sory board when evaluating solutions. However, we would argue that
goal definition does not have the sole purpose of making decisions as ob-
jective as possible. It can also serve the function of externalizing decision
making processes and priorities, so it becomes more transparent for the
team members themselves and for outsiders. This is why we initiated
quite a detailed objective definition (3.3.6), even though only simple
objectives were used for explicit evaluation of interventions. We believe
this makes our goals and prioritization easier to understand, critique,
modify or build upon for others. We hope the objective definition in this
thesis can serve as a first iteration to build upon, for future projects that
also aim to advance holistic sustainability of the Danish agrifood system.
For further research, a commonly identified literature gap within re-
generative agriculture is the absence of defining clear outcomes and
associated practices (Giller et al. 2021; Tittonell et al. 2022). This can lead
to unrealistic attribution of positive outcomes to regenerative agriculture
as a whole without stakeholders agreeing on which practices lead to
which outcomes, and without considering trade-offs. In this project, we
used design methods like objectives catalog and morphology schemes,
and believe these have the potential to be utilized furher to close this
gap.
Scientific rigor is challenging with holistic objectives, but it might be
necessary
Usually projects, and especially research projects, focus narrowly on one
or a few sustainability challenges, even though a holistic sustainability
approach is an identified gap in literature (El Bilali, Strassner, and Ben
Hassen 2021; Olesen, Christensen, and Jensen 2021). Such a narrow focus
risks overlooking harmful trade-offs outside the focus area. Still, after
trying to navigate a project with the objective of holistically improving
==PAGE:127==
7.1 Reflective discussion: Implications and limitations
105
sustainability, we can see why most projects focus on a single sustainability
parameter. It requires far more resources to follow a scientifically rigorous
approach, which we often couldn’t do. Still, we believe there is an
argument to be made for trying. To begin to close the research gap on
holistic sustainability, researchers might need to carry out ambitious,
applied projects like this one, where ideal goals have to be compromised
in relation to real world constraints.
Considering global effects - complex or mainly about meat?
We prioritized decreasing biosphere impacts the highest in the Dan-
ish agrifood system, given the country’s high societal and economic
sustainability. However, in countries with lower economic and societal
sustainability, trade-offs between biosphere and societal sustainability
will be more severe. This puts extra responsibility on countries with high
societal and economic sustainability to reduce consumption.
There is a risk that the effects of our interventions could have unforseen
negative global effects, as this was not explicitly investigated. However,
we tried to take global effects into account by using SDG indicators in ob-
jective definition, wherein national contributions to global sustainability
has already been considered. Given that Denmark’s poor performance
on SDG 2 Zero Hunger is largely based on meat production (Sachs et al.
2022) and research identifying changing diets as the main contributing
factor in achieving global biosphere sustainability (World Resources
Institute 2016), we are believing that if a project can successfully reduce
meat production and consumption, it is on the right path towards global
sustainability. It is a complex task involving changing mindsets, behaviors
and paradigms, and re-imagining business models for Danish farmers,
for whom meat production is currently the most profitable option. We
believe a systems engineering and participatory design approach could
be highly useful in such a complex, behavioral transition.
This project’s contribution to the objective of reducing meat production
and consumption is debatable, given the controversial regenerative
practice of integrating grazing livestock as part of soil health regeneration.
However, we argue that this practice can increase total nutrient output
for human consumption per hectare by reducing the number of animals
that can be produced. This is in line with the argument presented by
Lundsgaard et al. 2022 about land use of organic agriculture. By including
regenerative frontrunners in our project, we hope that we contributed
to a development where the regenerative practices are developed for a
wide range of crops for direct human consumption, instead of only feed
crops.
7.1.3 Research question 3: Scoping a concrete project
around systemic sustainability
Research Question 3
How is this design project scoped within the Danish agrifood system
so that it is both feasible, and contribute to holistic sustainability of
the system?
This section discusses the scoping decisions made during the project. In
summary, we highlight the benefits of exploratory projects as well as
==PAGE:128==

the need for new methods to regulate market forces. Additionally, we
acknowledge the north-bias in research and reflect on how participatory
design could make international projects more inclusive and less pater-
nalistic. Finally, we reflect on whether disruptive change can come from
within the established food producing world and suggests the need to
remain open and explorative in our future careers.
Explorative projects can lay the groundwork for numerous projects to
come
As this was an iterative exploratory project, continuous scoping and
pivoting was a key activity throughout the project. If we had focused
on a smaller system from the beginning, we would have had more time
to implement and test. It would also have been easier to communicate
the expected outcome to stakeholders. However, the richness of insights
would have been more limited too. It is our experience that exploratory
projects are seldom allowed in organisations (Fisker and Dømler 2021).
This could be due to the lack of knowing what the outcome of investing
in the project will be, and the uncertainty and even discomfort associated
with such a process. Frequent pivoting is also difficult the larger the
team. Thus, we are happy to have endeavoured into this project in our
master thesis, as it can be the sources of many professional projects to
come in our career. Similarly to what we recommended to Danish med-
tech companies in earlier work (Fisker and Dømler 2021), we encourage
organisations in agrifood to occasionally run exploratory projects with
small teams facilitated by a designer, as a tool to get new perspectives on
business-as-usual and meaningfully define future directions for projects
in the organization.
A need for new methods to scope projects that regulate market forces -
hope for broad participatory scopes
The agrifood system is a grade A example of how unchecked market
forces can fail to work for common societal interest. Negative externalities
induced by agriculture will become increasingly expensive for society as
a whole, but are not payed for by the stakeholders inducing them today
(Pretty 2008) - this includes all of us, as we all consume food, though with
highly varying impact depending on diet. When scoping this project,
we were constantly challenged by the fact that there are currently few
incentives for farmers and consumers to make the environmentally-
friendly choices that would help complex societies persist long term.
This is a struggle that we feel is not sufficiently addressed in systems
engineering and participatory design methodology. We did however
experience that the broad scope and engagement of multiple stakeholders
from different parts of the system in participatory design led people to
reflect on deep systemic food system challenges. Thus, we hypothesise
that participatory design can help facilitate the changes of mindsets,
paradigms and societal goals necessary to make stakeholders able to
rethink current market constraints for project scoping, and how to
contribute to regulating market forces, so they don’t compromise the
persistence of society. Inspired by this property of participatory design,
new design methods should be developed for scoping that go beyond
market forces when they are harmful to society.
Our project contribute to north-bias in research
When debating what to focus on in our thesis, we did consider addressing
agrifood challenges outside of Denmark. We chose to focus on Denmark
==PAGE:129==

107
because we wanted to be able to thoroughly immerse ourselves in the
design problem. From living and studying in foreign countries, it is our
experience that short-term projects conducted by visiting foreigners often
fail to appropriately address the context. Still, we want to acknowledge
that this thesis contributes to the north-bias of sustainable agrifood
systems studies, a gap in research identified by El Bilali, Strassner, and
Ben Hassen 2021. The question is, what does this dilemma mean for
globally responsible design? Should designers from other parts of the
world leave local problems to be solved only by local people? This would
enforce the north-bias further, as many funding opportunities are in
the Global North. Inspired by the methodology for co-creation across
cultures developed by Bille 2019, we see that participatory design could
be moving towards making international projects more inclusive and
less paternalistic. Because of this, we are not dismissive of working on
agrifood projects in foreign countries in the future, if it can be done
in a way where we can work collaboratively with local stakeholders to
ensure that design processes are locally owned, culturally sensitive and
contextually appropriate.
Can disruptive change come from within a broken system?
Finally, we will reflect on our scoping decision to base our project within
the established food producing world. There is an argument to be made
for established organisations like the UN, FAO and the EU having a
blind spot for disruptive potentials to completely rethink the use of space
and resources in agriculture. This is supported by the fact that many
food sources are often left out of conceptual mappings of the agrifood
system (see Appendix B.2). As an example, the RethinkX report (Tubb
and Seba 2019) critique the EU for not investing sufficiently in cell-based
meat, even though this technology might have the potential to completely
disrupt the immense environmental impact of the meat industry. We
believe there is a point to be made both for working within and outside
the system, which is supported by Gordon, Holmgren, and Bengtsson
2022. However, in our future career, we will try to remain open and
explorative, not becoming too stuck in the path dependencies of the
established systems we will work within.

who distributes money, which could be collaboration partners in future
engineering system design projects.

7.2 Conclusion
Societal and environmental factors are generally overlooked in projects
for improving environmental sustainability of agrifood systems and a
narrow range of stakeholders are typically involved, which might re-
sult in fragmented solutions that fail to address broader sustainability
challenges. This thesis address that gap by demonstrating an applied,
exploratory design project with a systems engineering and participatory
design approach, aiming at improving environmental sustainability of
the Danish agrifood system while considering societal and economic
sustainability. The agricultural consultancy Agrovi was chosen as the
client organization, for whom interventions that advance the regenerative
agrifood system in Denmark were designed. It involved an advisory
board across the Danish agrifood system, to ensure that a solution would
fit the different needs of stakeholders in the system.
The contribution of this thesis is thus threefold, demonstrating: 1) How
systems engineering can be applied as a holistic approach to sustainable
agrifood systems, 2) How participatory design can be applied to facilitate
collaboration between stakeholders from many areas of expertise to de-
velop holistic, yet concrete solutions for sustainable agrifood systems, and
3) Concrete interventions for improving sustainability of Danish agrifood
systems in the case of the agricultural consultancy Agrovi advancing
development and implementation of regenerative agriculture.
The following research questions were answered:
1. How can participatory design and systems engineering be applied
to design interventions that advance holistic sustainable transition of
the Danish agrifood system? (Chapter 2)
Systems engineering had helpful methodologies for navigating the com-
plexity of a project aiming at holistic sustainability in an agrifood system.
They helped us sort findings, continuously narrow the scope, define ob-
jectives, discover underlying causes, as well as designing and evaluating
interventions. However, the methodology is complex, and will require
several usage attempts and experience with managing design projects
to be applied effectively. Especially for an exploratory design project, it
comes with a great deal of uncertainty and frustration, requiring many
assumption-based decisions and prioritisation to move forward, and
openness to changing objectives as new insights are gathered.
Participatory design exhibited great results in creating profound prob-
lem insights and designing holistic interventions between stakeholders.
Furthermore, stakeholders have continued collaborating outside the
project, indicating an increased social cohesion from the participatory
approach. These links can lead to a more resilient, adaptive agrifood
system. One disadvantage is that stakeholder enrollment, interaction and
participatory design activities is time consuming and require facilitators
with design and facilitation expertise.
All in all, the positive response from participants, with expressions of
increased motivation and interest in further collaboration, lead us to
conclude that the main contribution of this thesis is applying the systems
engineering and participatory design approach to holistic sustainability
of the Danish agrifood system.

2. What are meaningful objectives for a design project aimed at aiding a
holistic sustainable transition of the Danish agrifood system? (Chapter
3)
A design project aimed at aiding a holistic sustainable transition of
the Danish agrifood system should prioritize the development of a
resilient and persistent system that delivers food security and nutrition
for all within economic, social, and environmental sustainability. As the
biosphere is the precondition for continued sustainability of societies
and economies, biosphere sustainability is paramount, with especial
responsibility falling on countries like Denmark, that has high societal
and economic performance. Still, societal and economic factors are
important to consider for implementation of environmental measures.
This project aimed at improving the critical SDG performance of the
Danish agrifood system on environmental indicators and SDG 2 Zero
hunger, to move towards sustainability, while making implementation
likely through considering societal and economic factors. The main
objective that arose with potential to reduce environmental impacts and
increase contribution to Zero Hunger was reducing production and
consumption of meat. Future systems oriented research should explore
how to solve the societal and economic challenges in such a transition.
3. How is this design project scoped within the Danish agrifood system
so that it is both feasible, and contribute to holistic sustainability of
the system? (Chapter 4)
The project was continuously scoped. The overall scoping decisions were
1) taking an offset in regenerative agriculture, because of it’s promis-
ing sustainability and high commitment from many stakeholders. 2)
a pragmatic offset in farms under 100 hectares, to counterbalance the
current trend of increasing farm sizes, which reduces system resilience
and innovation. 3) choosing the agricultural consultancy Agrovi as client
organisation in order to ensure that we related to scaled implementation
with many farmers for high sustainability impact.
4. What is the future behavior within sustainability of the Danish
agrifood system without an intervention, with an offset in regenerative
agriculture? Which objectives for Agrovi and their value chain arise
from this analysis? (Chapter 5)
Regenerative agriculture is expected to see a large rise in demand, in-
creasing incentives for farmers to take up regenerative practices. Still,
converting to regenerative agriculture for supplying that demand might
be significantly delayed due to the time it takes for farmers and consul-
tants to develop the necessary expertise, and then implement regenerative
agriculture. This is enforced by a culture that makes it difficult to diverge
from the norm for farmers and a lack of collaboration between stakehold-
ers interested in regenerative agriculture. As conservation agriculture
specialists, Agrovi has a head start on developing expertise for becoming
the leading Danish regenerative consultancy, but will likely be left behind
due to a lack resources to work with regenerative agriculture and a lack
of appeal towards regenerative frontrunners.

Advancement of regenerative agriculture start with increased collabora-
tion between ambitious regenerative stakeholders. However, this requires
increased clarity of what regenerative agriculture can be, and a common

Recommendations for the future
We encourage designers and design engineers to undertake ambitious
exploratory sustainability projects within agrifood systems. The agrifood
system serves some of the most fundamental functions for society and
contains many interesting socio-bio-technical challenges with a high po-
tential sustainability impact if they are solved. Because of the complexity
and high stakes, it is both a challenging and highly awarding system
to work with. This thesis can hopefully serve as inspiration for future
projects on how designers can engage in the agrifood system.
We encourage agrifood stakeholders to involve people with design com-
petencies in their projects, to efficiently working towards solving the
numerous complex, interlinked sustainability challenges in the agri-
food system. As demonstrated in this project, design competencies and
methodologies can make the task of tackling cross-cutting systemic prob-
lems manageable for stakeholders, and increase shared understanding
and collaboration for a more innovative, resilient and sustainable system.
Furthermore, it can induce motivation and agency to act on sustainability
challenges, instead of being overwhelmed by the complexity. As one
workshop participant stated: "What you did, it sparked a fire".

D.2 Cause analysis detailing: Why do few
farmers take up regenerative practices?
D.2.1 Difficult to diverge from the norm
A summary of the causes can be seen in Figure D.2.
Our results indicate that most Danish farming communities have long
traditions, experience, and pride in certain ways of doing farming. This
includes pride in having a neat looking (heavily ploughed monoculture)
field, owning big tractors, and continuing in the path of the traditions
learned from former generations and on the agricultural educations.
Our results also indicate that the agricultural system is experienced by
outsiders as a ’closed club’ having little interaction with other systems
that could bring in new norms and ideas. Finally, the norms and culture
in farming communities are found to have a different understanding of
nature, and a risk adversity for trying new things, stemming from how
the agrifood system interacts with the seasons and banks.
What will the neighbours think? In the workshop (advisory board event
2, Table 2.2), an experienced conservation agriculture farmer explained
how difficult it is to diverge from the norm in agriculture. He noted
how the ’grade sheet’, the field, is out in plain sight for the neighbours
to see. Since there is a long tradition for industrial agriculture with
monocultures and ploughing, a good field is associated with a neat field.
This is supported by a survey of farmers by SEGES, LMO, and FRDK
2017, asking farmers to rate fields on whether the farmer had done a bad
or a good job. The most heavily tilled parcels were graded as the best
fields (average 5,1 out of 6), whereas parcels with no tilling and direct
sowing got significantly lower grades (average 3,4 out of 6). A common
barrier for transitioning to new practices is thus the fear of teasing and
loss of professional respect from neighbours, colleagues, ERFA-group
and their agricultural consultants.
This was most clearly exemplified in a talk with The Association for
Regenerative Agriculture (’Foreningen Regenerativt Jordbrug’) (continual
meetings 4, Table 2.2), where we were told that a few conservation
agriculture farmers had come to the association, because they had started
to experiment with spraying ferments on their fields and wanted people
to do sparing with. She told us they were very secretive about it, and
hadn’t shared in their ERFA-group, because they didn’t want to be
teased.
Professional pride, age and training. Farmers are trained in industrial,
monoculture agriculture, and with the average farmer being 57 years
old (Danmarks Statistik 2017), the workshop participants agreed that
many take pride in the more conventional way of farming. At a farmers
event (Conference 2, Table 2.2) and at the client visits (client visits 3,5,
Table 2.2), we talked to several young farmers that will take over their
family farm and want to run it with different practices, like those of con-
servation agriculture practices (many practices overlap with regenerative
agriculture), but where they cannot convince their father, and have to
wait for him to retire to do things differently.

A closed club? Futhermore, the agricultural system was described
as a ’closed club’ in the workshop by a municipality development
consultant in charge of LAG and EU CAP funding (advisory board

for agricultural development. She experienced a lack of interest for
outsiders to come in with new ideas. We hypothesise that this can
further be enhanced by farmers living in rural communities, where they
meet fewer people with alternative perspectives. In our field work, we
experienced mostly curiosity for our presence, but also attention to us
being different. At an event for farmers interested in increasing soil
health and conservation/regenerative agriculture, were asked "Are you
from ’Enhedslisten’ (red: the most left-wing socialist political party in

a teasing friendly tone, but clearly exemplifies how we stood out in a
uniform group of farmers wearing overalls, as some of the only young,
female participants. The feeling of a ’closed club’ should be nuanced by
the fact that we experienced excitement arising from our presence in
general. Many farmers were keen to talk to us and expressed gratitude
for our engagement with farming, and described how they feel that they
stand alone with converting agriculture to more sustainable practices, in
a society they experience as being largely against them.
Different understanding on nature. At the workshop, the regenerative
farmer from Øm Klostergaard described many farmers having a different
understanding of nature than him and the regenerative stakeholders,
describing that many established farmers talk about having the right to
farm the land. As a contrast, he would talk about putting nature first
and using as little land as possible for fulfilling human needs (continual
meetings 6, Table 2.2).
Don’t dare to try new things because of the banks. The advisory board
workshop participants described that the current agricultural system
rely largely on farmers indebting themselves heavily to banks in order to
buy land and to invest in productivity increases (advisory board event 2,
Table 2.2). This is supported by the literature review, section (OBS ref til
societal sust.). It was described at the workshop that this gives the banks

D.2.2 High risk
Figure D.3: Detailing of one bone from

Causes that there is high risk in taking

A summary of the causes can be seen in Figure D.2.
Farmers have good reasons to be risk averse. Farming is a high-risk
activity, because activities follow the seasons. Mistakes and trying out
new things can potentially lead to a whole seasons’ harvest being ruined,
and farmers only have a set number of seasons in their carreer to earn
back the large sums of money they often owe to the banks (farm visit 2,
Table 2.2).
Lack of financing opportunities for trying new practices. In a lot of field
work and in the workshop, it was described to us how many farmers are
under financial pressure (Farm visit 1,2,3, Continual meetings 1, Table 2.2)
- see Section 3.3.5 for a literature review supporting this. As described in
the previous paragraph, farmers are usually in big debt and banks are
reluctant to let them loan money to invest in converting to less tested,
risky practices.
A reason for the financial situation of farmers, leading to risk aversity,
is that Consumers are used to pay very little for food (advisory board
event 2, Farm visit 3, Conference 4, Table 2.2). Danes are among the
people in the world that spend the lowest percentage of their income on
food (Rasmussen 2017, and the few big retailers can push prices down for
agricultural products (related to W6.1, T6.3, Appendix D.1). Even with
heavy Danish and European subsidies, most farmers are not profitable
(Nielsen 2016) If new approaches are tried and fail, they risk betting the
farm, and their Business often tied to house and lifestyle, with their
family heritage, life and identity centered around the farm and doing
farming (advisory board event 2, Table 2.2).

Risk of yield loss, especially in the first years. If farmers convert to
practices they are not familiar with, they risk loosing one or several years’
yields. This can also happen, even if farmers do it right but transition
too abruptly, because there can be a several years long transition period
before the biological system on the field has regenerated and can provide
the services needed to have a good yield output (advisory board event 2,
continual meetings 1,4, Table 2.2).
Uncertainty of what future legislation will favor. There is a high degree
of uncertainty among farmers on what the future legislative landscape
will look like (advisory board event 2, Table 2.2). To exemplify, several
farmers expressed a distrust of political decisions and a frustration over
the upcoming Danish CO2e-tax on agriculture (conference 2, Table 2.2).
Even though many of these farmers were front-runners on implementing
sustainable cultivation practices, and most of them did plant-based
agriculture and not industrial meat production, which account for the
vast majority of emissions, they didn’t trust that the CO2e-tax would
be designed to give them a competitive advantage, but that it would
only make them go out of business. This uncertainty and mistrust lead
to controversy, as it takes many years to convert agricultural practices
as described above, but farmers seem to not feel confident to take the
risk of changing their practices before the concrete design of the tax is
disclosed. The Danish government is waiting for an expert commission
to recommend how the tax should be designed and has not disclosed
how farmers should prepare (SMV Regeringen 2022).
