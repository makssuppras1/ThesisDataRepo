ABSTRACT
This thesis seeks to study the value creation obtained through digitalisation and digital 
technologies for small and medium sized companies (SME’s) in the building renovation 
industry. Digitalisation is a global megatrend, which has already changed many aspects 
of everyday life. The construction industry is gradually becoming more affected by digi­
talisation following the fast evolving digital development.
The construction industry is a large and complex industry, with many actors and stake­
holders working in different sectors and combinations, however, utilising the benefits 
of digitalisation has not been implemented in all areas of the industry. Studies rank the 
construction industry lowest with regard to level of digitalisation, and also investments 
observed in the construction industry are way below other production industries and 
business sectors. The total annual average investment is close to a fourth of the invest­
ment made in the production industry and a third of that in other business sectors. Not 
only is the total investment in the industry low, but the SME’s combined investment is 
less than one fifth of that compared to the large companies. 
The thesis is done in collaboration with Peter Jahn & Partnere A/S (PJP) and the objective 
is to gain insight into the possibilities and limitations for implementation of digitalisation 
and digital technologies in the building renovation industry. The aim is to identify and 
illustrate where value creation can be obtained, in order to create an incentive for SME’s 
to invest in digital innovation, research and development. This thesis analyses two dif­
ferent digital technologies and processes a case study on the digitalisation of workflows 
in one SME (PJP). Understanding every step of a project process as well as knowledge of 
the digital technologies Matterport and Dalux made it possible to illustrate where in the 
project process these tools can be most beneficial. 
Since the initation of this project, Peter Jahn & Partnere A/S, have invested in not only 
the two digital technologies (Matterport and Dalux), but also allocated resources to 
make an employee responsible for digital development moving forward. If PJP is indica­
tive of SME’s within the building renovation, immediate investment in innovation have 
proven to create value, which is why there is a good reason to be optimistic for the in­
dustry and digital development going forward. 
Copenhagen, 13th of August 2017.

1.1 DIGITAL DEVELOPMENT
Digitalisation is a global megatrend, meaning a 
factual macroeconomic force shaping the world, 
backed by verifiable data. Digitalisation can be 
defined as the integration of digital technologies 
into everyday life by the digitisation of anything 
that can be digitised. In other words, converting 
analog information into digital information. Digital 
technology is the branch of scientific or engineer­
ing knowledge that deals with the creation and 
practical use of digital or computerised devices, 
methods, systems, etc. The question of digitalisa­
tion is no longer an ‘if’, but rather a question of 
‘when’, it is integrated into almost all aspects of 
society. According to the Organisation for Econom­
ic Co-operation and Development (OECD) in their 
report ‘An OECD horizon scan of megatrends and 
technology trends in the context of future research 
policy’, megatrends include some of society’s big­
gest challenges and opportunities. Digitalisation 
is particularly interesting because it also plays an 
integral part in the other four megatrends climate 
change, societal change, globalisation and urban­
isation. Digital development is evolving quickly 
and is also beginning to have a larger effect on the 
construction industry. The construction industry 
is a large and complex industry, with many actors 
and stakeholders working in different sectors, but 
unfortunately, the development in direction of uti­
lising the benefits of digitalisation is not spreading 
out to all areas of the industry. Grith Bech-Nielsen, 
network manager for InnoBYG [Appendix A-14], 
agreed that investigating why the building renova­
tion industry is failing to adopt the digital trends 
would be an interesting and relevant question to 
ask and said the following; “It is both socially rele­
vant and exciting to look at digitalisation in relation 
to building renovation and how digitalisation can 
create value.”

As digital technologies continue to develop, new

Robotics & Additive manufacturing
One of the most recognised and fast developing 
robotic technology is flying drones. Drones can be 
used to rapidly gather a great amount of informa­
tion from areas of a construction or construction 
site that was previously inaccessible or hard to 
reach. Although the use of drones is gaining pop­
ularity in the construction industry as a cost and 
labour-saving tool, adoption is not widespread 
(Armstrong & Gilge, 2016). Manufacturing today 
is primarily subtractive (i.e. products are built by 
using material and removing unnecessary excess), 
or formative (i.e. material is forced to take shape 
using a forming tool). Additive manufacturing, 
commonly known as 3D printing encompasses dif­
ferent techniques that build products by adding 
material in layers, often using computer-aided de­
sign software (Barreneche, et al., 2016, pp. 63-64). 
Progressively adding material to make a product 
take shape is an unprecedented approach to man­
ufacturing that warrants new business models and 
implies significant changes to existing industries. 
The combination of computational design and 
machine learning for robotics can help to realise 
bridge the communication gaps between design 
and construction teams. All this results in a coor­
dinated process that increases productivity and 
cost-savings at every stage of a building’s life-cycle 
(Goetjen, Dann, & Berg, 2017). BIM is no longer a 
new technology within the AEC, but despite this, 
the technology still has a lot of untapped potential. 
Current trends involve an increased use of BIM in 
regards to lifecycle management and further uti­
lising BIM during the operation and maintenance 
phase. Virtual Design & Construction (VDC) builds 
on BIM and is a way of working or a combination 
of digital collaboration, processes. VDC is a visual 
management methodology using BIM as a part of 
a whole project lifecycle (Khan, 2017).  
Virtual Reality (VR) and Augmented Rea­
lity (AR)
Virtual environments cover different digital tech­
nologies like ‘Virtual Reality’ (VR) and ‘Augmented 
Reality’ (AR). VR technologies completely immerse 
a user inside a synthetic environment and while im­
mersed, the user cannot see the real world around 
him. In contrast, AR is taking digital information, 
whether it is images, audio, video, touch or hap­
tic sensations and overlaying the information in a 
real-time environment. Augmented Reality can be 
used to enhance all five senses, but its most com­
mon use is visualisation. Unlike VR, Augmented 
Reality allows the user to see the real world, with 
virtual objects superimposed upon or composited 
within the real world. Therefore, AR supplements 
reality, rather than completely replacing it (Azuma, 
1997, pp. 355-356). 
3D Laser Scanning
There are different operating principles of la­
ser scanning, but common is scanning a room 
through a laser by comparison of the transmit­
ted and the received laser beam. Operations are 
automatically repeated thousands of times with 
current algorithms and devices capable of ren­
dering a 3D space with a standard accuracy of a 
few millimeters (Shanbari, Blinn, & Issa, 2016). All 
leading BIM applications now support data (point 
cloud) provided by laser scans. The documenta­
tion of existing building spaces and conditions is 
a key requirement in construction and renovation 
projects. It is expected the use of laser scanning 
devices and point cloud software increase in the 
future (Shanbari, Blinn, & Issa, 2016).

1.1.2 Digital Development and 
Trends
The development of digitalisation and digital tech­
nologies has been expressed as; “Change has 
never happened this fast before, and it will never 
be this slow again”. We are currently experiencing 
the 4th industrial revolution, where technologies 
are getting increasingly connected, connecting the 
physical world of people, products and productions 
in a digital world (Krull, 2017, p. 11). According to 
the publication, ‘An OECD horizon scan of meg­
atrends and technology trends in the context of 
future research policy’, there are currently 40 key 
technologies for the future, divided into four cate­
gories digital, biotechnologies, advanced materials 
and energy + environment. Far from all 40 trends 
are relevant to the construction industry, but the 
digital technology trends Internet of Things (IoT), 
Big Data and Artificial Intelligence (AI) could have 
an impact in the future construction industry. 
The Internet of Things (IoT) promises a 
hyper-connected and ultra-digitally re­
sponsive society
In the beginning of the 1990’s the internet be­
comes available for unrestricted computers and 
the World Wide Web (www.) is made official at 
CERN (Conseil Européen pour la Recherche Nu­
cléaire) making the internet available to the public 
domain (Leiner, et al., 2012), which had until now 
only been a tool available to the scientific commu­
nity. By the year 2015 this number had increased 
to 3.2 billion users (Dadax, 2017) and is expected 
to reach 4 billion by 2020. An internet user is de­
fined by an individual who can access the Internet 
at home, via any device type and connection. The 
Internet of Things (IoT) is defined as the connec­
tion over time of almost any object and device 
to the internet’s network of networks. In public 
space, at the workplace and at home all kinds of 
objects and sensors will gather data and exchange 
firms, governments and citizens access an unprec­
edented amount of data (volume), increasingly 
inform their decision-making with real-time data 
(velocity) and combine a new range of information 
from structured and unstructured sources (varie­
ty), which will generate disruptive impacts on all 
human activities (value). (Barreneche, et al., 2016, 
pp. 52-53)
Artificial Intelligence (AI) abilities are 
likely to reach those of the human brain 
within the next few decades
Artificial intelligence (AI) is defined as the abili­
ty of machines and systems to acquire and apply 
knowledge and to simulate intelligent behaviour 
(Barreneche, et al., 2016, pp. 54-55). This means 
performing a broad variety of human tasks, e.g. 
sensing, processing oral language, reasoning, 
learning, making decisions and demonstrating abil­
ity to move and manipulate objects accordingly. 
these with one another and with humans. The IoT 
really is an Internet of everything, since in addi­
tion to connecting things; it also enables digital 
connections among other elements in the physical 
world, such as humans, animals, air and water. The 
networked sensors and actuators in the IoT allow 
monitoring of the environment, status reporting, 
receiving instructions and sometimes even auto­
mated actions. The Internet of Things promises 
a hyper-connected and ultra-digitally responsive 
society that supports human, societal and en­
vironmental developments. North America, for 
example, is likely to have almost 12 devices per 
capita connected by 2019, Western Europe around 
8 devices (Barreneche, et al., 2016, pp. 27-28). By 
2030, it is estimated that 8 billion people and 25 
billion active “smart” devices will be interconnect­
ed and interwoven by one single huge information 
network. The result is the emergence of a gigantic, 
powerful ‘superorganism’, in which the internet 
represents the ‘global digital nervous system’ (Bar­
reneche, et al., 2016, pp. 50-51). An impact on 
society of that scale will also affect the construc­
tion industry.

1.1.3 Innovation, Research and

try is conservative, and according to two reports 
published in 2016 by the industry interest organ­

itself to be particularly innovative, which is backed 
by lacking investments in innovation, research and 
development. Innovation is the introduction of 
new or substantially different products, processes, 
workflows, organisational forms or methods and 
innovation can be introduced with prior research 
and development (R&D) work (Realdania, 2016). 
The two reports, are both based on key figures 
from the industry, which is referred to as the ‘com­

sub-sectors architects, engineers, manufactures, 
contractors and operations. The reports look at

construction industry, this thesis will refer to the 
classification made by the Crafts Counsel. Small 
companies have less than 10 employees (1-9), me­
dium sized have less than 100 employees (10-99) 
and the large companies are those with more than

to work more with digital technologies has not 
been a talking point. PJP’s aim from collaboration 
on this thesis is to gain insight into the possibili­
ties and limitations of digitalisation and digital 
technologies that concern the building renovation 
industry of residential housing, which they also 
believe, is lacking innovation. As the great Dan­
ish architect Poul Henningsen once said: “Future 
comes by itself, progress does not”. Digitalisation 
is almost inevitable, but in order to embrace the 
possibilities of digitalisation and digital technol­
ogies an effort must be put into achieving it. The 
residential building sector alone has the high­
est value for Denmark, and with a majority of 
SME’s like Peter Jahn & Partnere A/S working to 
renovate, maintain and improve the frames of 
everyday lives it is only fair that support is given 
to this sector. Industry initiatives tend to focus on 
larger projects like new construction and regula­
tion demand that does not account for differences 
in the industries sub-sections. With knowledge of 
the industry’s struggle to innovate, research, and 
develop, a need for concrete examples to help 
investment in digital innovation in the building 
renovation industry is essential. This thesis aims at 
studying the reasons for lack of digital adoption in 
conservative building renovation industry, which is 
in sharp contrast to a world where digital develop­
ment and innovation is evolving faster every day. 
The objective is as described and to support the 
main objective of creating value from digitalisation 
and digital technologies to SME’s, two hypotheses 
were formulated.
1.3.2 Boundary
The construction industry is a large, complex sys­
tem, with intertwined actors and sectors, but on 
basis of the knowledge obtained from the intro­
duction, the thesis is limited to the area where 
the main objective can have the biggest impact. 
Because of investment in innovation and R&D in 
the SME, along with the increased focus on the 
building renovation industry, with residential 
buildings as a majority of that sector, the scope of 
the thesis is defined to these boundaries. In addi­
tion, the publication, ‘White Paper on Renovation’ 
states, that there are three levels of renovation 
and maintenance. The highest level of renovation 
is urban renewal involves renovation on a large 
scale, bringing a whole neighbourhood or open 
areas (public spaces) up to date. The middle level 
of renovation is renewal renovation, which aims to 
renew a property so it is updated to a contempo­
rary level with the building standards. It is about 
replacing existing building parts/sections with bet­
ter components, elements and functions, such as 
modification, improvements or extensions. The 
lowest of renovation is maintenance retention, 
which aims to maintain the property in the state 
it was originally built into. The thesis will focus 
on the middle and lowest level of renovation and 
maintenance. As the thesis is done in collaboration 
with Peter Jahn & Partnere A/S, whose main area 
of work is in the location of the municipality of Co­
penhagen and Frederiksberg it is decided to limit 
the boundary to these two municipalities. The ma­
jority of buildings here are multi-story buildings 
and are a representative proportion of Denmark. 
Public residential ownership is very small com­
pared to the private and general housing segment, 
the project is limited to looking at buildings owned 
by private rental companies, general housing com­
panies and cooperative/owners’ associations. 
The thesis is limited to these boundaries, which 
is illustrated by figure 15. From this point forward 
anything referred to as the ‘industry’, will repre­
sent the boundaries as defined here. Additional 
limitations can occur in the analysis and will be 
elaborated relating to the subject.

1.3.3 Research Outline & Method
To define value creation from digitalisation and 
digital technologies it is necessary to clarify the 
notion ‘value creation’. Value can be two-fold in 
the sense that it can be a direct value you seek 
to create, or an indirect value that is an effect of 
something else. This thesis aims at identifying and 
illustrating direct value creation, but will also dis­
cuss any indirect values discovered as a result of 
the analysis conducted. Value can be many things 
and variate depending on the perspective. There­
fore, is it necessary to understand who the value 
is directed at and how it creates value. The the­
sis is divided into two parts and consists of five 
chapters, see figure 16. The first part frames the 
thesis by stating a problem and analysing it. This 
part aims to illustrate why the objective is relevant 
and answering the main research question: “why is 
the digital development not being adopted by the 
building renovation industry?”. The second part of 
the thesis focuses on the results found from the 
analysis and a case study, which is summarized 
with a discussion and conclusion of the results in 
relation to the thesis main objective and hypothe­
ses. Finally, recommendations for future work are 
stated.
Introduction
The thesis objective was formulated based on lit­
erature reviews of the current understanding and 
expertise in the field of construction and digital de­
velopment (digitalisation and digital technology) 
elaborated in chapter 1. Inspiration also includes 
the day to day experience with this industry from 
working at Peter Jahn & Partnere A/S. To frame the 
approach, a broader screening among interest and 
knowledge organisation, think tanks and industry 
actors has been performed. This chapter describes 
why this thesis (objective) is relevant. 
Analysis
The aim of the analysis is to answer the main re­
search question: “why is the digital development 
not being adopted by the building renovation 
industry?” To help answer the main research ques­
tion two sub question are formulated, “What is 
the difference between new construction and 
the building renovation industry in regards to 
digitalisation and digital technologies?”, which is 
analysed in chapter 2 and “Who are the key stake­
holders and processes in a building renovation 
project and what digital development would cre­
ate value for the identified stakeholders?”, which 
is analysed in chapter 3. The analysis is based on 
literature studies of educational books, scientif­
ic journals and articles, together with interviews, 
presentations and debates. Knowledge on digital 
technologies was together with literature studies, 
gathered from and hands on experience by testing 
a Matterport 3D laser scanner and the web-based 
software program DALUX. The analysis is the the­
sis’ theoretical foundation and provides the basis 
for illustrating value creation from digitalisation 
and digital technologies for SMEs in the second 
part.
Case study
The aim of the case study of Peter Jahn & Partnere 
A/S, chapter 4, is to analyse how plausible the 
adoption of digital technologies and digitalisation 
is for a consultancy company (SME) in the building

value from digital technologies and digitalisation. 
Data and information from real projects executed 
in the period from 2010 to 2017 is provided by PJP. 
Results
Chapter 5 combines the knowledge obtained from 
the analysis of digital technologies, digitalisation 
and digital workflows to illustrate value creation in

dustry. 
Summary
Chapter 6 is a combination of the thesis discus­
sion, conclusion and recommendations for further 
work. The discussion focuses on the plausibility of 
the result from the analysis and proposed value 
creations. The conclusion will conclude on the the­
sis as a whole, the objective and two hypotheses. 
Recommendations for further work will focus on 
areas on which the industry should invest in inno­
vation and R&D.

2.1.1 The Notion of Working Di­
gitally
For a better understanding of the analysis and re­
sults, this section gives a clarification on what is 
meant by digitalisation, digital technologies and 
working digitally. The definition of digitalisation or 
to digitise is simply to convert analog information 
into digital information, but digital information can 
be many different things. For example, digital in­
formation can be a scanned paper document that 
is converted to a digital image on a computer or 
it can be large amounts of datasets collected by a 
sensor measuring the air temperature every sec­
ond. Digital technologies are the instruments used 
to collect or work with the digital information. It 
could be the scanner used to convert the analog 
document into a digital document or the sensor 
used to collect air temperature measurements and 
the program used to work with following dataset. 
Working digitally therefore has different meanings 
for different people or companies. In this thesis, 
the definition or essence of working digitally with 
digitalisation and digital technologies is using digi­
tal information to make better and faster decisions 
or help/assist areas of the building renovation in­

2.2 KEY DIFFERENCES BET­
WEEN NEW & EXISTING 
To understand what and which types of digital 
technologies that can create value for SME’s in the 
building renovation industry, it can be useful to un­
derstand what does not work. As Jacob Lemche, 
partner at Peter Jahn & Partnere A/S (PJP) [Appen­
dix A-13] explained it, “just because it is possible 
does not necessarily mean it creates value”. It was 
meant both in regards to whether ‘working digi­
tally’ actually is better than working analogically, 
but also to that extent that if a digital technology 
proves to create value for one part of the indus­
try it does not necessarily mean it works in other 
parts. The different digital developments from the 
AEC (introduced in the previous chapter [1]) will be 
related to the building renovation industry. Digital 
technologies can be subcategorised into software 
and hardware, see figure 19. Software is a general 
term that describes computer/software programs, 
applications, scripts, and instruction sets, which 
all fall under the category of computer software 
(Christensson, 2017). Software is “virtual”, or not 
physical like hardware, which refers to the physical 
parts of the computer or related devices. Hard­
ware is usually connected to a specific software 
that has been designed to work with the hard­
ware, but many software programs stand alone 
and can work on multiple platforms. Hardware can 
be understood as tools for collecting data from the 
physical world from for example a scanner or sen­
sor, whereas the software is used to process and 
illustrate the data collected.
2.2.1 Key Difference Between 
New and Existing Buildings
There are, of course, different approaches to a 
building project depending on whether it is the 
construction of a new building or the renovation 
and maintenance of an existing. The most obvious 
difference is the presence or lack of a physical build­
ing. The list of differences is long, but in regards to 
digitalisation and digital technologies there is one 
major difference. For new constructions, all infor­
mation is embedded into a project and opposite, 
with existing buildings the information is being 
extracted from the building to scope a project, as 
illustrated in figure 20. For new constructions, a 
lot of work goes into designing, simulations and 
renderings of how a proposed building will look 
and perform by abide with current building regu­
lations. For the renovation of existing buildings, a 
lot of work goes into registration of existing condi­
tions and assessments of different aspects. Those 
are, in terms of working with digitalisation and use 
of digital technologies, the two main different sce­
narios.

Web Based Software Program Dalux
One of the software programs, Dalux, shown 
on Figure 5 is developed by a Danish software 
company (www.dalux.com) for the construction 
industry. It is a web based software program ini­
tially developed to allow architects, engineers and 
contractors to work with registration in 3D models 
in the ‘cloud’ (web browser) making it possible to 
work from tablet devices on the construction site. 
Now the software consists of four different plat­
forms Dalux BIM viewer, Dalux Field, Dalux FM and 
Dalux Project. Dalux Field as the name implies is 
used in the field (on site) as a registration tool in 
different respects, which includes preregistrations, 
supervision notes, deficiency lists and checklists 
etc. According to Dalux’s website the consultant 
can save up to 30 pct. of their time used for on-
site supervision and the contractor can save up to 
25 pct. time on internal work inspection or quality 
control (Dalux, 2017). The software supports dif­
ferent drawing file formats (Revit, IFC, DWG, PDF, 
Scan). PJP decided to test Dalux Field on a larger 
renovation project, using it as a pilot project, be­
fore making a final decision on whether it should 
be used on all projects prospectively. For a de­
tailed description of the project see [Example 1: 
Dalux Field], page 36-37. Dalux Field is a simple but 
effective tool because it allows for the cooperation 
between two stakeholders in terms of the con­
sultant (assisting the builder) and the contractor 
executing the construction. It saves time, because 
the registration made on site are completed as 
they are registered, meaning no need for finishing 
the work when returning to the office. It also saves 
time for the contractor because the registration 
and description of the work is easy to understand, 
reduces miscommunication and allows for the 
main contractor to assign responsibility to sub-con­
tractors. The construction site manager only needs 
to control that the work has been completed as 
described, before marking it as finished. Dalux 
Field allows for the contractor to take a photo of 
the completed work, saving the consultant for an­
other trip to the construction site. In many cases, 
it is the consultant who is hired by the builder to 
conduct supervision of the construction project 
and compiling deficiency reports with what needs 
to be executed or completed before a project can 
be finalised for the hand-over. It is the consultant 
who buys a license (1,500 DKK per month) to use 
the Dalux Field software (Dalux, 2017), and is in 
charge of creating the project and invite the in­
volved stakeholders. The contractor does not have 
to pay anything, and only needs to accept the invi­
tation to gain access to the project. The web based 
software works both on the web browser of a com­
puter and as an iOS or android app on a tablet. A 
project is created around the particular drawing 
for the project and the combination of a drawing, 
picture and description ensures everyone under­
stand exactly where in the building a registration 
is made. Miscommunication is always a factor in 
projects involving different stakeholders and when 
information’s are being passed down command 
chains. A renovation project consists of many pro­
cesses and every time an information is passed 
on there is a risk of some of the information go­
ing missing in translation. With Dalux Field there 
is more transparency with what has been agreed, 
alignment with the work needed to be done and 
where the responsibility for execution is situated 
at all times. Dalux has other great features such as 
Dalux Project which connects components from 
the BIM or web model to the tender-lists so noth­
ing is forgotten or omitted if changes are made 
to the project, which often happens in manual 
transfer from BIM models to, for example, Excel. 
Unfortunately PJP, like so many other SME’s in the 
building renovation industry, do not work with 3D 
BIM models and therefore cannot obtain the full 
value of a software program like DALUX.
3D Laser Scanner - Matterport
The 3D laser scanner called Matterport is originally 
developed in the USA for the real estate industry. 
Matterport built the first end-to-end media plat­
form that allows users to easily create, modify, 
navigate, and build on digital representations of 
real places. To get a better understanding of what 
the tool is capable of, examples are available on 
their website:  www.matterport.com. Because the 
digital hardware technology was invented to be 
used by individuals without the technical knowl­
edge and understanding of 3d scanning technology, 
it was developed with ease of use as a key feature. 
The Matterport was tested in a collaboration be­
tween PJP and DTU to study the capabilities of the 
technology. A detailed description of the tools pre­
cision and how it works was compiled in the report 
‘Matterport in Retrofitting’, see [Example 2: Mat­
terport Scanner], page 38-39. 
The result of the 3D Matterport scanning was pre­
sented to the employees at PJP [Appendix A-6], 
and the reactions where unanimous – everyone 
was impressed. The feedback was that 3D scans of 
this character, price and speed of the processing 
could create value, but it also that it would prob­
ably not be useful in all aspects. Some thought 
would have to go into figuring out where the scan­
ner could create the most value. The Matterport 
was later discussed in a meeting with the technical 
designers of PJP [Appendix A-12] and their feed­
back was that they could see it being used on for 
example staircase renovations where drawing ma­
terials can be inadequate. The technical designers 
also saw great value from a 3D scan of a buildings 
heating central with the many connections of dif­
ferent pipes. Areas where the Matterport lacks 
capabilities for example, is that it does not work 
outdoors, which is where a lot of renovation work 
is usually executed. Kent Friis Petersen, the owner 
of Vest Administration A/S [Appendix A-17], was 
introduced to the Matterport and was likewise 
impressed with the result, but after some thought 
gave an interesting remark. He said, “high quali­
ty pictures of a building components condition 
explaining the reason for the need of certain ac­
tion, says a lot more to a resident than being able 
to walk around in a virtual representation of the 
building they already live in.”. Kent Friis Petersen, 
an engineer with more than 30 years of experi­
ence in building renovation industry, was keen 
on figuring out how the Matterport could be in­
tegrated into the working processes of renovation 
projects, but could not see it creating more value 
for the decision makers of a cooperative or own­
er’s association. The results of the Matterport scan 
were also presented to the private rental company, 
Pears Global [Appendix A-11], who owns approxi­
mately 3,000 housing units in Copenhagen. Their 
reaction, contrary to Kent Friis Petersen’s, was 
that they could see a clear value creation for their 
work with building renovation projects. The value 
of a total spatial visualisation for registration of 
housing units before and after a renovation was 
something they were interested in. Pears Global 
have since asked PJP to use the Matterport tool on 
several of their future renovation projects.
To summarize on the reactions of a digital technol­
ogy like the Matterport 3D laser scanner, there is 
an initial wow-effect followed by a deeper thought 
of what it can be used for. There is value creation 
to be gained from the Matterport, the question is 
where and how it is used optimal and understand­
ing the limitations. In other words, it is a specific 
tool which needs to be assessed by the individual 
company. The same can be said of flying drones 
with high quality cameras. The technology has be­
come so good and obtainable in prices that almost 
all companies are able to afford and use the tool. 
Drones can deliver high quality image of areas of a 
building where residents and others don’t normal­
ly have access, which is a value creation according 
to Kent Friis Petersen [Appendix A-17].

2.2.4 Future Digital Technology 
Trends 
With a better understanding of digital develop­
ment in the building industry it is also interesting 
and relevant to look at which digital technology 
trends might affect the industry.
Augmented Reality (AR) is the next big 
thing
Tim Cook, Apple Inc.’s chief executive, has talked 
up a lot of technologies like driverless cars, artifi­
cial intelligence (AI), streaming television etc. but 
no technology intrigued as much as augmented re­
ality (AR), which overlays images, video and games 
on the real world (Gurman, 2017). Tim Cook has 
linked AR’s game-changing potential to when the 
smartphone was introduced to the world. (See 
examples on the website: https://twitter.com/
madewithARKit). AR is not new, but with the intro­
duction of being able to use it on everyday devices 
like a smartphone, it will without a doubt also have 
an impact on the construction industry. Imagine a 
project proposal of balcony’s to a residential build­
ing this will allow all residents to see their new 
balcony on their phone, how it will look from their 
living room or from the street outside. It will give 
a whole new perspective and understanding of the 
proposed renovations or improvements to a build­
ing. Augmented Reality is also called enhanced 
reality or mixed reality and comes in many forms 
but the core idea behind it is combining the digi­
tal world with the real world as we see it. As Marc 
Durand, Atkins’ new director of digital disruption, 
explains “We are seeing a massive shift into virtual 
and augmented reality, which offers the possibility 
to bring pretty much anyone from anywhere into a 
project eco-system. Immersive environments help 
demystify the understanding of data. Not everyone 
can do coding or manage databases, but every­
one can quickly learn to navigate a virtual model 
and access data through it. People say a picture is 
worth a thousand words, well a good VR model is 
worth a thousand drawings. The technology helps 
ensure a clear and comprehensive approach be­
tween the client, the design intent and contractor 
input.” (Cousins, 2017) 
Next step for building component & ma­
terial manufacturers 
Grith Bech-Nielsen [Appendix A-14] explained in 
connection with BIM, that the next step, and what 
some companies where currently working on, was 
imbedding properties into the digital components 
and materials that are used to create BIM models. 
This is the next level of innovation for manufactur­
ers of building components and materials. As the 
construction industry starts to reorganise around 
BIM and digitalisation in general, the companies 
that go beyond object libraries and embrace digital 
capabilities may gain a first-mover advantage (Aud­
ier, Lahet, Laubier, Guenot, & Wunder, 2017). By 
embracing the possibilities of digitalisation, mate­
rial companies can use object libraries to showcase 
their technical abilities by expanding the informa­
tion that is available on each item. Libraries can be 
set to emphasize a product’s contribution to en­
ergy efficiency and other performance measures, 
as well as to meet various operational and main­
tenance requirements. In regards to the building 
renovation industry, the idea of digital building 
components and materials with physical proper­
ties is just as interesting as it is for the construction 
industry in general, as the whole industry becomes 
more digitalised. 
Sharing data not files
One of the really interesting transformations 
happening in the digital technology industry is 
movement away from traditional software pro­
grams that are installed on a computer or device, 
to web-based software. Web-based software is 
software you use over the internet with a web 
browser. No need for downloading or installing any 
software, or worry about upgrades. An example 
most people are familiar with is a web-based email 
program like Gmail or Hotmail. These are very reg­
ular web-based programs, so the notion is not 
new, but the key difference trending is the power 
of cloud-computing which is allowing for the heavy 
software programs to be run from a browser. An 
example of a traditional software program like Mi­
crosoft office is now available as an online version 
with Word and Excel running through Office 365. 
With the increasing power of cloud computing, 
better internet, broadband and 4G network con­
nections constantly improving, it is a trend winning 
ground fast. Trimble Connect, is a web-based soft­
ware program, like DALUX, which can be explored 
on: www.connect.trimble.com. Trimble Connect is 
a digital collaboration environment where every­
one involved in a construction project knows what 
is happening now and what should be done next. 
Everyone from the ground floor to the top floor, 
on-site and off-site. The program works like DALUX 
on the desktop, web and mobile platform, but dif­
ferentiates by integrating other software programs 
like Revit and SketchUp (https://my.sketchup.com/
app) which just launched as a beta-version. Taking 
it a step further is the company Flux (www.flux.io) 
whose goal is exclusively to share data, and no dig­
ital files. Flux is an open, inexpensive platform that 
allows all members of the building-making commu­
nity to seamlessly share data - different users can 
send selected building data from applications such 
as Revit, SketchUp, Grasshopper, Dynamo, and 
Excel (support for other applications is in develop­
ment). Flux integrates the data coming from these 
different applications and keeps it synchronized, 
accepts and tracks changes, and keeps the pro­
ject participants posted about them; and which, 
of course, includes the requisite visualization tools 
that allows the integrated project geometry to be 
viewed online and shared with others. Flux con­
nects and organises information from separate 
applications enabling designers, engineers, con­
tractors, subcontractors, builders and suppliers to 
use their preferred tools and seamlessly collabo­
rate. But it doesn’t stop there. With the amounts 
of data being gathered and made accessible, com­
panies are operating on a larger and larger scale. 
Cityzenith’s 5D Smart World™ software platform 
(www.cityzenith.com), is a next-generation City 
Information Modelling (CIM) tool, that will revolu­
tionizes the way building professionals (AEC) use 
data to design, collaborate, analyse, present, and 
operate their projects. As Joe Dignan, chariman of 
the Cityzenith strategic advisory board described 
it, “Cityzenith is the lens that allows cities the abil­
ity to turn data into information” and Sandra Baer, 
President of Personal Cities explained, “The data 
visualization platform that Cityzenith has created 
is revolutionizing our ability to make faster, better, 
more informed decisions. With 5D technology, cit­
ies have a new power to transform.” (Cityzenith, 
2017) □

2.3 DIGITAL VALUE CREATI­

With a better understanding of the possibilities 
and limitations for digitalisation and digital tech­
nologies in the building renovation industry it is 
now possible to point to why the digital develop­
ment is not being adopted equally. 
2.3.1 Reason for SME’s lack of in­
vestment in innovation, research 
and development
There are key areas that could explain the lack of 
investment in digital innovation, research and de­
velopment among SME's in the building renovation 
industry. As described, a key difference between 
new construction and building renovation was the 
projects sizes. Because renovation projects typical­
ly include a few elements of a whole building, the 
overall project scopes are generally much smaller 
than of those in the other parts of the construc­
tion industry. This means the companies hired to 
execute the projects are smaller and therefore typ­
ically more specialised in certain areas through a 
whole project life-cycle.  The many SME’s connect­
ed to a project are individual stakeholders and do 
not have the same incentives for optimisation of 
workflows or processes that go beyond their area 
of work. The companies can optimise their internal 
workflows, but in a conservative industry like the 
building renovation, the need to change or inno­
vate has been limited. 
As the study from Realdania highlighted in Chap­
ter 1, the little investment in innovation, research 
and development that did happen, 80 pct. came 
from the large engineering companies and man­
ufacturers (suppliers) with 250 employees or 
more. Large engineering companies like Ramboll 
and Cowi are so big, that they work across a very 
broad stroke of a construction project scope and 
therefore have many different departments with­
in the company that focuses on different aspects 
of a project, which gives an incentive to innovate 
across all areas to increase efficiency and quality. 
The same can be said about the large contractor 
companies MT Højgaard and NCC that will bene­
fit from innovation, research and development in 
other parts of the company internally, giving them 
an incentive. At the same time, the allocation of 
resources both financially and in man-power is 
easier for a large company compared to a SME. 
The investments made from manufacturers/sup­
pliers can most likely be accredited to the high 
focus on energy efficiency of buildings. The Danish 
government’s ambitions of a 100-pct. sustainable 
energy society by 2050 points to energy efficiency 
of buildings as a key (Regeringen, 2011, p. 27). For 
many years there has been political focus on the 
issue leading to tightened regulations, which has 
helped push manufacturers to invest in innovation, 
research and development to abide by these new 
regulations. The building renovation industry has 
adapted to the new regulations using the improved 
material elements and components, but it has not 
created a need for innovation of workflows. There 
just is not the same political prestige in these small 
types of building renovation projects as there are 
in large construction projects, which gets media at­
tention and thereby political attention. It’s easier 
to sell to the public forum.  
With a general lack of resources and political will 
to push for innovation, the building renovation in­
dustry has simply not had an incentive to change 
significantly. But the world is changing with digi­
talisation and digital development gives a new 
incentive for the building renovation industry 
- there is value to be created from it. It is essen­
tial that the industry is supported in a quest for 
digital innovation. The already large numbers of 
options provided by digital technologies can be 
overwhelming, especially for SME’s with limited 
resources. In the end, the real loser from the lack 
of investments in the building renovation indus­
try is the builder (owner) and the residents of the 
housing units in those buildings. Four key reasons 
for lack of investment is summarized, see figure 22.
2.3.2 What would create value 
for the building renovation indu­
stry
Understanding why there previously has been so 
little incentive to invest in innovation for SME’s 
helps to identify the critical elements that needs 
to be accounted for, if adoption of digital technol­
ogies is to be successful. For example, the fact that 
the SME’s have fewer resources means the digital 
technologies need to be simple and cost effective 
if they are to have any chance of being of value. 
The trend of digital software programs becoming 
web-based with cheaper cost through only hav­
ing to buy subscriptions of licenses, depending on 
how much the program is used will make it easi­
er for a SME to invest and try different programs 
because the risk from losing on the investment is 
minimised. Web-based software is clearly the right 
way for the SME’s e.g. one of the key features of 
DALUX was the programs ability to ensure collabo­
ration on a single platform between two different 
stakeholders. This type of collaboration emulates 
the workings of a large company by connecting 
the individual SME’s on a project. Web-based 
programs reduces scenarios where companies 
working with different software’s are forced to 
spend a lot of time on handling information to 
either fit their own or other stakeholders needs. 
A major value creation for SME’s in the building 
renovation industry and industries in general lies 
in digital information in the form of data and not 
digital files. Because there are many different 
stakeholders involved in a renovation project the 
value of exchanging data (information) on a web-
based platform will be a real value creation for all 
stakeholders. Digital hardware technologies are a 
little more complicated and as expressed in the 
presentation of the 3D Matterport scanner, those 
introduced to the technology pointed to differ­
ent areas it could create value and areas where 
it could not. It is a good example of that type of 
digital technology in general, that it is much more 
dependent on the individual company’s way of 
working that will decide if it can create value or 
not. Another thing about hardware technologies 
is their need to operate with some sort of digital 
software, meaning the basis for a good integration 
of a digital hardware technology depends on the 
company’s level of digital adoption.

3.1.1 The Process of a Building 
Renovation Project
The organisation Vaerdibyg, is a collaboration be­
tween some of the construction industry’s leading 
organisations and the initiative is to develop a 
new common practice for construction processes 
across the different stakeholders.  According to 
their publication, ‘Good Advice for the Renovation 
Process’, the construction process in a building 
renovation project is more complex and contains 
more unpredictable factors than new construction. 
Renovation is requires skilled parties to succeed. 
The publication focuses on the different phases of 
a building renovation project and especially the 
transitions from one phase to another. It also fo­
cuses on the interaction between stakeholders, 
because it is here the effective cooperation in a 
renovation process is created. The thesis is limit­
ed to studying the building renovation projects 
involving the three builder types; general housing 
companies, cooperative/owner’s associations and 
private rental companies.
The four main phases 
Independent of the builder type, a building ren­
ovation project typically goes through these four 
phases; idea/planning, design/scope, execution 
and hand-over/operation, see figure 25. An effec­
tive idea and planning phase is about clear goals, 
effective decision making, and a good mapping of 
needs and knowledge of the existing conditions. 
Many possibilities must be sought to find the 
opportunities that maximize value creation in a 
project and the stakeholders must be dressed to 
understand the process. If the initial mapping of a 
project is insufficient the following process can be 
subject to costly project changes. The reason why 
project changes may be necessary can be found in 
what is called the ‘project paradox’, see figure 26.

balance the framework, direction and process in

and more stakeholders as the renovation project

LYSIS OF THE DIFFERENT 
BUILDER TYPES
Stakeholders defines those who need to be con­
sidered in achieving project goals and whose 
participation and support are crucial to a projects’ 
success. Project stakeholders in this case are those 
actively involved in the project, or whose interests 
may be affected as a result of a projects execution. 
3.2.1 Identifying key stakehol­
ders
According to the Builders Association, then funda­
mentally, the builder is the one who pays for the 
renovation of a building and the term often cov­
ers the person, entity or company that orders a 
project stakeholder to perform a specific task. The 
builder is of course a key stakeholder and must be 
aware of its obligation to be an active participant 
in a construction project. Property administration 
companies (administrator) have many roles in the 
industry, but their main objective is to help the 
building owner with administration of their proper­
ties. Administration can be done in many ways, and 
will variate in both responsibility, size and scope 
depending on the type of builder, but are consid­
ered a key stakeholder. A resident of a housing unit 
is either a tenant or the owner of the residence. 
Depending on the type of resident (owner or ten­
ant) the influence on a renovation project can be 
very different. Operations and maintenance, ad­
ministration, debt management, etc. is the solitary 
owners’ responsibility, while a tenant is a clean 
consumer. Regardless of type, residents are con­
sidered a key stakeholder, because they will in one 
way or another be involved in the project. There 
are types of consultancy used in the construction 
industry; client, total, split and sub-consultancy. 
In this industry, the primary type of consultancy 
is the total consultancy. Here a consultancy com­
pany, most likely engineers and/or architects, is 
assigned to manage a project from start to finish. 
When entering an agreement on a renovation pro­
ject, a construction contract with the companies 
that will perform the work is agreed. To this end, 
there are several standardised sets of agreements 
(www.bolius.dk, 2017) called; AB92, ABT93, ABR89 
and AB Consumer. ABR89 is a general provision for 
technical advice and assistance, which regulates 
the relationship between builder and consultant 
and determines the principles to be used for the 
contractual relationship with a given assignment. 
Consultants play a key role in a project and are 
therefore considered a key stakeholder. For these 
types of building renovation projects the AB92 is 
the most commonly used agreement, between 
the builder and contractor. It ensures the involved 
parties agree exactly what the work is about and 
what they each are committed to deliver. AB92 is 
used when the builder (or assigned consultant) 
has prepared the scope of a project which docu­
ments how the project is to be constructed and 
the contractor in this case a main contractor with 
various sub-contractors, only needs to make a 
tender offer and perform the construction work. 
Because the contractors in principal just need to 
follow the instruction given by the project scope, 
they are not considered as key stakeholders. Oth­
er stakeholders involved are the authorities in 
terms of building regulations and construction 
permits. Suppliers who like the contractor will de­
liver the components and materials as descried 
in the project scope. Specialists who might be 
hired to conduct prerequisite investigations such 
as harmful substances or static construction cal­
culations to help define the project scope. These 
three stakeholders together with the contractor 
are not considered key stakeholders in the initial 
decision-making phase of a project, see figure 28. 
A stakeholder analysis will be conducted for each 
of the three types of builders.

Cooperative and Owner’s Associ­

There are some discrete, but significant differenc­
es between a cooperative and owner’s association. 
As a shareholder, you become a member of the 
cooperative housing association and through the 
certificate of ownership you have exclusive right 
of use for an apartment, but a cooperative apart­

book. The price for the housing unit (apartment) is 
not free, but statutory (www.bolius.dk, 2017). As 
an owner, you become a member of the properties 
owner’s association, but an owners’ association 
is not an association in the ordinary sense. It is 
more of a business community that is organized 
by the associations agreed rules and the main 
rights of an owner of a housing unit (apartment) 
is the right to legalize the apartment. This means 
that the owner can pledge the apartment as col­
lateral for loans and that the owner can live in the 
apartment and sell it without asking the owners’ 
association. For both types of associations, the 
legal position of the individual association is gov­
erned by the articles of association, which states 
provisions on deposits, liability, housing tax, board 
of members and the general assembly. The main 
obligation for the apartment shareholder/owner 
is the obligation to contribute to the payment of 
common costs (www.bolius.dk, 2017). As a build­
er, cooperation/owner’s associations are a lot less 
complicated than the general housing companies. 
These housing association’s supreme authority is 
the general assembly where the overall decisions 
of the association are made. The general assembly 
elects a board whose obligations are to manage 
the day-to-day operation of the association and 
to function as the builder in the case of a reno­
vation project. It is common for an association to 
have attached a professional administrator or oth­
er assistance to the board, illustrated by figure 31.

registered accountant who audits the association’s 
accounts. Despite the decision-making constella­

3.2.4 Stakeholder Analysis of a

optimized to get the most out of their lease agree­

3.3.4 Value Creation for Key Sta­
keholders 
The described stakeholders and information in­
teractions are just a fraction of the interactions 
that happen through a whole project. A building 
renovation project is complexed, but divided into 
what is the buildings physical frame and what is 
the builders frame and direction, makes it more 
manageable. 
Eliminating the project paradox
The consultant is the one balancing the capabilities 
of the buildings physical frame and the builders 
frame and direction, which are also influenced by 
the administrators and residents. Every building 
is in one way or the other unique and on top of 
this; the different types of builders have different 
frames and directions of how they manage their 
building. The relationship among the stakehold­
ers are therefore also depending on the type of 
builder in a specific project. Out of the three types 
of builders, the private rental companies are the 
least complicated, as they typically have a clear 
direction for how a building is managed. General 
housing companies are limited by the many stake­
holders involved in the decision-making process, 
and the special regulations from the municipality 
and LFB. Cooperation’s and owners’ associations 
are primarily limited by the financial liquidity. They 
have an incentive to both maintain and add the 
buildings value, which opens the playing field of 
possibilities. This, as a result, puts a greater de­
mand on those having to make the decisions and 
as the builder is an unprofessional in the field of 
building renovation, it can be challenging. Consult­
ants are currently acting as the ‘processing engine’ 
of information when it comes to understanding 
consequences of decisions. Cooperation’s and 
owners’ associations rarely have excess resources 
and lack the will to prioritise something that is not 
absolutely necessary. The result is a forced neces­
sity to make some quick overall assumptions of 
the right direction for a building to work in, leading 
to the project paradox. The complexity of making 
decisions that consider both the economic, envi­
ronmental and social aspect of a building and its 
users, with a general lack of resources, can be a 
reason for the lack of development in the industry, 
with little incentive to consider the whole building 
other than from an economic perspective. Having 
a lot of information, is not the same as having a 
lot of knowledge of a building. If the information 
cannot be processed in relation to the other infor­
mation it clouds a project and adds to complexity 
instead of transparency. Then it is easier to stick 
with what you know. The aim should be to have 
broad valuable knowledge of a building from the 
very beginning of a project, which would coun­
teract the impact of wrong decisions as shown on 
figure 38.
Creating valuable knowledge
Having a complete understanding of a buildings 
physical frame, would create valuable knowledge 
for the stakeholders developing a project scope. 
It would assist a builder in taking sustainable

need. This will allow for better planning of a pro­

or wishes from residents or owners for improve­
ment of the property. Common improvements are 
establishing balcony’s, better common yard areas 
or aesthetic improvements to staircases etc. In this 
step of a process the builder will usually interact 
with the administrator and consultant. Common 
maintenance or repair work does not need a con­

that change the existing construction in a signifi­

5.1 RESULT OF ANALYSIS
Summary of the key results found from the analysis 
and case study of the building renovation industry.
5.1.1 Challenges in the Building 
Renovation Industry
The general challenge for the industry is the fact 
that it primarily is small projects, executed by in­
dependent SME’s working on individual sections 
of project. Most building owners do not have the 
resources to execute a total renovation, includ­
ing all aspects of a building and therefore tend 
to priorities the most necessary measures. Some 
consider renovation as an unwanted necessity,

itself. The general challenges of the industry can 
be summarized to; (1) few resources, (2) not work­

incentive to innovate.

determining a renovation project, information of

lack of incentive to innovate has resulted in a low

more difficult to know which of the digital tech­

individual SME. A demand for simplicity and cost 
effectiveness is essential if the industry is to adopt 
digital technologies.

Not a new idea

building mass. The report also concluded that the 
digitalisation of the existing building mass in princi­

but a very resource intensive project (Hauch, et

in 2010, a lot in the world of digitalisation and 
digital platforms has happened. Ricki Toftelund 
Larsen, Commercial Director for Copenhagen Data 
Exchange (CDE) [Appendix B-9], presented the 
world first data exchange of quality data of any 
sort to anyone interested. He explained that there 
is high interest for gaining data from the exchange, 
but very little activity on providing the exchange 
with data at the current time. This he believes is 
a result of companies not grasping the true value 
of data and the fact that it is a completely new 
concept. Every company produces data and the 
construction industry is no exception, but very few 
companies have structured data that is usable. CDE 
has extended their services to helping companies 
understanding what information they are produc­
ing, how it can be structured as data and then sold 
to others. Good quality data has value in places 
that have not even been considered a possibility.
The idea of a complete digitalisation of buildings 
and entire cities was presented in chapter 2.1, 
with the 5D City by Cityzenith. The web-based data 
platform illustrates the many possibilities with 
creating such interactions of large scale datasets. 
The web-based software programs like Trimble 
Connect and Flux also illustrated the possibilities 
of collaborating across different stakeholders on a 
platform. All cases illustrate clearly that the digital 
technologies to make a platform with processing 
capabilities of illustrating consequences is possible 
and already present. As previously described a lot 
of information is not the same as a lot of knowl­
edge and therefore it is essential to have a clear 
idea of the information (data) that should be inte­
grated into such a platform. What is important in 
order to make sustainable decisions. The 5D city is 
a representation of too broad a scope, by not just 
integrating building information’s, but also other 
city related information like traffic data, pedes­
trian data etc. On the other hand, platforms like 
Trimble Connect, are concentrated on the collab­
oration between stakeholders on a single project, 
which still requires the stakeholders to provide the 
information. This is not sufficient for the industry, 
because it repeats the lack of resources. The aim 
of the platform wanted for the building renovation 
industry is to land somewhere in between the two 
concepts.

6.1 DISCUSSION
This section will discuss the plausibility of the pro­
posed digitalisation of the existing building mass 
and development of a web-based platform for 
sharing of the valuable data. It will also discuss the 
indirect benefits of the digitalisation and digital 
technologies in the industry and how the future 
trends will work with the results of the thesis.
6.1.1 Plausibility
As described in chapter 5, the digitalisation of the 
existing building mass is in principle a simple, but 
very resources-intensive task. By understanding 
the technological capabilities already available 
today with cloud-computing allowing for the re­
al-time processing of data in a web-browser, it 
is plausible to both store and process the digi­
talisation of all buildings as data. The need for 
3D visualisation is not present in the renovation 
industry as it is in the construction of new build­
ings. The quantification and integration of physical 
properties of components and materials is what 
will save the renovation industry time and money. 
The level of digitalisation and use of digital tech­
nologies is low, and if a broad adoption of digital 
development is to succeed the introduction of 
a few smart digital technologies is not going to 
change the conservative industry. Four factors that 
make the digitalisation of the existing building mass 
plausible is the BBR, common use of 20-points list, 
the regulation on energy certificates and the ex­
istence of technical drawing of buildings ‘as built’. 
These four factors each have an essential function 
to create a great digital foundation for the indus­
try. (1) The BBR is ideal to be further developed 
and function as the platform, (2) the 20-point list is 
ideal for structuring the data, ensuring familiarity 
for the industry, (3) the workflow of compiling en­
ergy certificates can similarly be used to collect the 
data needed and (4) the ‘as built’ drawings would 
work well with a digitalisation of the current avail­
able technical drawing material. 
Denmark has a high building standard, as a result 
of high construction regulations, which means the 
existents of technical drawing material going back 
a long time and is stored with the technical admin­
istrations of municipalities. A digitalisation of the 
technical drawings of buildings to digital working 
files would create a basis for continuity and easier 
quantification of a buildings physical frame.  It is 
a question of getting the right framework in place 
and creating an incentive for the actors in the indus­
try to start thinking in digital development. Making 
digital maintenance reports mandatory as a new 
and better version of the energy certificates would 
not be as hard for owners/builders to accept and 
force the SME’s to interact on a digital platform. 
Key elements are in place for the plausibility of a 
digital building mass and as the case study of Peter 
Jahn & Partnere A/S illustrated there is also value 
to be created in digitalisation of the SME’s work­
flows and integration of data. The digitalisation of 
the existing building mass is as much a merging of 
the entities already in place and deciding the best 
way of converting the knowledge and information 
of the industry into data. 
This thesis is limited to the renovation of ex­
isting multi-story residential buildings, but the 
digitalisation of other buildings would be the same 
procedure. The BBR-notice is compliant with all 
buildings regardless of type or ownership. Data is 
data and the value creation from access to infor­
mation and working across platforms are the same 
no matter what section of the construction indus­
try. Because the result of the analysis pointed to a 
general feature in working with data it has a broad­
er perspective and the effects could be significant 
not just to the proposed industry. Big data is much 
more than a buzzword – it is the resource that will 
drive the digital growth of the future (Rostgaard, 
2013). The future trends like the Internet of Things 
(IoT), big data analytics and artificial intelligence 
(AI) are all technological features that will support 
the idea of digitalisation of the existing building 
mass.
The question of digital versus analogue
As the industry is both conservative and has a low 
level of digitalisation many still argue that the an­
alogue way of working is preferred. The incentive 
for significant innovation in the industry has not 
been evident. Some would probably even argue 
that they work digitally, but writing a document 
in Word, saving it as a .pdf-file and emailing it to 
another stakeholder is in principle just as analogue 
as writing a hand letter and sending it by postal 
service, the only major difference here is time. The 
case study of Peter Jahn & Partnere A/S illustrat­
ed a company that most likely represents a broad 
segment of not just the industry, but also other 
businesses in general and how it does not work 
digitally in the sense that has been described in 
this thesis. It is understandable that some might 
question the power of digital technologies if the 
structure is not in place and a manual process is 
just as easy. Many employees at PJP have repeat­

avoid additional costs in the future.

The future trends Internet of Things (IoT), Big Data 
analytics and artificial intelligence (AI) will influ­
ence the world as we know it but how would it 
work with the digitalisation of the existing building 
mass. 
Data, data and more data
The numbers of internet users and connected 
devices on the internet is increasing significantly 
every day. Since the start of this thesis in the end 
of January 2017 approximately 155 million more 
internet users have joined and 990 million new de­
vices are connected. Every new internet user and 
connected devices will produce data and it estimat­
ed that 2,5 quintillion bytes of data (Kwun, 2017) 
is produced every day. A hyper-connected building 
mass with integration of sensor data, smart tech­
nologies etc. provided by an IoT platform could 
become a reality e.g. with the 5D City from Cityz­
enith. The increasing quality of big data analytics 
and AI capabilities will help add knowledge to the 
context of the building mass. Improving cloud 
computing, internet connectivity will add to the ef­
fects of big data and there is no saying where it will 
end. A hyper-connected digital built environment 
could allow for data simulations of for example the 
impact of new building regulation and help those 
framing the construction industry with the effec­
tive regulations. 
Parametric design of a project
If the world becomes as connected as foreseen 
and AI becomes as clever or beyond that of hu­
mans, it could mean a radical change in how the 
design of renovation projects are scoped. Access 
to all data, related to a building and any surround­
ing data connected to the IoT, such as real-time 
insight to consumption, physical conditions of ma­
terials, real-time economy insight of the building 
owner, current construction regulations, weather 
conditions, market economies, loan rates and the 
cost of contractors based on current supply and 
demand. If all this data and more is connected to 
an IoT platform it would be possible to make rec­
ommendations of building measures based on 
virtually all aspects related to it. In other words, 
all parameters imaginable, could after the need be 
involved in a simulation of the best-case scenario 
renovation. This connection of information from 
different sources is in principle what a consultant 
together with the builder and other stakeholders 
does when scoping a project. Development in this 
direction could mean a change in the assignments 
a consultant is hired to execute.
Disruption of the industry
Blockchain is a digital technology not previously 
described in this thesis. Internet applications such 
as web browsers and email programs use protocols 
that define how software on connected devices 
can communicate with each other. Whereas the 
purpose of most traditional protocols is informa­
tion exchange, blockchain enables protocols for 
value exchange (Barreneche, et al., 2016, pp. 70-
71). Blockchain technology offers the opportunity 
to append additional data to value transactions. 
These data could specify that certain rules are re­
quired to be met before the transfer takes place. 
In this way, a transaction would work as an invoice 
that would be cleared automatically upon fulfil­
ment of certain conditions. Such “smart contracts” 
based on blockchain are also referred to as pro­
grammable money. Imaging a digital building mass 
where every component and material was applied 
with a digital currency and when replaced and up­
dated after the execution of a renovation project 
the stakeholders involved would be paid with the 
digital currency without the involvement of banks, 
administrators or other entities. If one or more of 
the future trends described here have the impact 
they are foreseen to have, it could disrupt the in­
dustry. It would most likely disrupt the SME’s who 
are advising others, meaning removing the need 
for the consultant and administrator functions. 
With increasing digitalisation and easy access 
to knowledge provided directly to the builder/
owners, decisions on the measures needed to be 
executed would be possible to take without the 
help of professionals. The industry would most 
likely shift to be more concentrated around the 
actual execution of projects and those involved 
in this phase, meaning the contractors, suppliers 
and specialists (new role for the consultants). New 
business models would emerge and digital special­
ist could thrive. □

6.2 CONCLUSION
This section concludes on the thesis’ overall ob­
jective and the two formulated hypotheses. 
It also concludes on the results of the thesis’ anal­
ysis and the case study of Peter Jahn & Partnere 
A/S.
6.2.1 Results of the Analysis and 
Case Study
It can be concluded that the building renovation 
industry has few resources and a lack of incentive 
to innovate. This has led to a low adoption of digi­
talisation and digital technologies, which the quote 
from Poul Henningsen frames so well, “The future 
comes by itself, progress does not.” The building 
renovation industry needs to start investing in in­
novation, because the possibility for value creation 
is definitely present.
Digital technologies
The study of two digital technologies, the Matter­
port 3D scanner and the software program Dalux 
highlighted key features that work well with the 
aspects of a building renovation project. The study 
of the Matterport also showed how some digital 
capabilities are not applicable with the workings 
of the industry and the value creation from the 
tool is limited to very specific areas. The study of 
Dalux highlighted some key features that really 
work well in the industry that is hampered by lack 
resources and a lack of incentive to invest in inno­
vation. The key feature is the low investment cost, 
it is web-based and creates a platform for digital 
collaboration between consultant and contractor. 
Only the stakeholder responsible for creating the 
project on the platform has to pay, which elimi­
nates any argument for not engaging in the tool 
from those on the receiving end. Dalux is an effi­
cient tool to create and store understandable data 
of different nature during a project. Dalux operates 
between the consultant and main contractor and 
between contractor and sub-contractor, keeping 
track of all interactions making a project process 
very transparent.
It can be concluded from the analysis of the dig­
ital technologies that there are key differences 
between the construction of a new building and 
the renovation of an existing building in regards to 
which digital features that will create value for the 
industry. The major difference is the presence of an 
existing building and the need to extract informa­
tion rather than embed information into a digital 
model. Actually, there are already so many differ­
ent digital technological possibilities that it can be 
difficult to assess and decide the best tool for the 
job, which for the industry with few resources can 
act counterproductive. Not all digital technologies, 
even though they are smart, will necessarily create 
value to a specific company however the industry 
stakeholders do not allocate sufficient man-hours 
to study the many possibilities already available. 
It underlines the importance of understanding not 
only the specific digital technology, but a broader 
idea of what it means to work digitally.  
Working digitally
In this thesis, it has been concluded that work­
ing digitally does not mean the same as working 
with digital technologies and that value creation 
comes from the first of the two variations. The 
case study of Peter Jahn & Partnere A/S illustrates 
the value creation that can be obtained from con­
verting information into structured data providing 
the possibility of working digitally. It can be con­
cluded from the study that other industry SME’s 
should invest in digitalisation of the companies 
work flow. Everyone has knowledge and produces 
information, which can be converted to valuable 
structured data. The most value creation from dig­
italisation and digital technologies can be found in 
data. It is most important for the SME’s and key

Incentive for key stakeholders, SME’s

6.2.2 Objective and Hypotheses
Structuring of data increases knowledge and dig­
ital platforms have the capability of bridging the 
gaps between individual SME’s. Digitalisation can 
create an incentive for the industry to start invest­
ing in innovation, research and development.
Hypotheses
The first hypothesis (1) was formulated; “By 
understanding the differences between new con­
structions and building renovation it is possible to 
identify where digitalisation and digital technol­
ogies can support or create value in the building 
renovation industry of SME’s.” As the results of 
the analysis have shown it was possible to iden­
tify key digital features that can create value for 
SME’s in the building renovation industry. The sec­
ond hypothesis (2) was formulated; “By illustrating 
the possible value creation from digitalisation and 
digital technologies for SME’s in the building reno­
vation industry, they will have an incentive to invest 
in digital innovation, research and development.” 
As the case study of Peter Jahn & Partnere A/S 
clearly shows the understanding of the possibili­
ties from digitalisation and digital technologies can 
give the incentive to invest in innovation, since the 
company on basis of the thesis have bought a Mat­
terport 3D scanner, will implement the software 
program Dalux to be used on all projects moving 
forward and allocating resources to innovation by 
appointing an employee to be responsible for the 
company’s product development.
Objective 
The main objective of this thesis is; “to combine 
knowledge from the construction industry, dig­
italisation and digital technologies to identify 
possibilities and limitations of implementation in 
the building renovation industry, and to illustrate 
where value creation can be obtained for SME’s 
and key stakeholders, to create an incentive for 
digital innovation, research and development.”
It can be concluded that the main objective of this 
thesis has been achieved. Summarizing the results 
of the analysis, the case study and the discussion 
concludes the thesis. The availability and possi­
bilities of digital technologies and digitalisation is 
definitely not what is holding back the building 
renovation industry from innovating itself. This is 
just the very beginning of the digital development 
that will only evolve from this point forward. “Dig­
ital development has never happened this fast and 
will never be this slow again.” □

6.3 RECOMMENDATIONS
This section will describe recommendations for 
further work with the thesis.
6.3.1 Talk to Industry Actors 
Following this project, there are a number of actors 
in or related to the industry who could be interest­
ing to talk to about the subject of digitalisation and 
digital technologies.
The organisations behind BBR
The BBR Act was changed in 2005, so it is no longer 
the individual municipality that is responsible for 
establishing and operating a local building and 
housing register. The assignment is now central­
ised to KOMBIT A/S, owned by KL, and thereby 
ultimately by the municipalities (www.bbr.dk, 
2017). It would be interesting to talk to those in 
charge of the BBR to hear their current visions for 
the registry and the limitations of implementing a 
digitalisation of all buildings physical frame and in­
tegrating it to their platform.
Contractors and other SME’s
This thesis has primarily talked with only one 
consultancy company, Peter Jahn & Partnere A/S. 
The possibilities and limitations of the software 
program Dalux was discussed with the contractor 
Snedkermester Arne Pedersen A/S, but it would 
also be interesting to hear their opinion of the 
Matterport technology in terms of integration with 
their workflows. An idea is that the Matterport 
could work well as a pre-registration tool as it is 
the task of the main contractor to register the con­
dition of the affected areas prior to the execution 
of a project. It could also be interesting to talk with 
suppliers and their possibilities of digital ‘datafica­
tion’ of materials and components.
InnoBYG and other organisations
InnoBYG is an innovation network for energy ef­
ficient and sustainable construction and as the 
thesis has concluded, the digitalisation of the ex­
isting building mass could result in value creation 
of different characters. One of the main value cre­
ations would be the empowerment of sustainable 
decision-making for an industry that at the current 
time is very limited. The digital interaction of quan­
tities and properties of materials and components 
will help calculate the impact of decision on an 
economic, environmental and social level. It would 
be interesting to hear their thoughts on the subject 
and whether the aim of digitalisation is something 
they would be willing to study. The same could be 
done with other interest organisations that work 
with similar issues in the industry. 
6.3.2 Further Studies
There are many areas in relation to this thesis that 
could be interesting to do further studies of. In re­
lation to the digitalisation of the existing building 
mass there are at least two areas of interest. 
Level of digitalisation
The thesis describes the digitalisation of a buildings 
physical frame by data of quantity and properties. 
It would be interesting to make a study of the level 
of detail needed in a dataset to give a satisfying 
representation of a building. A study like this could 
be performed by gathering data of real buildings in 
the structured idea of elements, components and 
materials and relate to the amount of data embed­
ded in 3D models of new construction.
Automation of digitalisation
To support the digitalisation of the existing build­
ing mass studies of automated gathering of data 
would be of real value. An idea could be to create 
algorithms that could analyse the existing techni­
cal drawings of buildings and generate tables of 
quantities. The generated data could then be con­
nected to datasets of material properties. 
6.3.3 Development of algorithms
Data should be considered as raw material and is 
not truly valuable before the interaction of data 
occurs and the key element to that are algorithms. 
An algorithm is defined as a process or set of 
rules to be followed in calculations or other prob­
lem-solving operations, especially by a computer.
Prioritisation of maintenance
Developing an algorithm that can prioritise the 
importance of maintenance measures for a build­
ing based on condition assessment, rest lifetime, 
cost of maintenance or replacement, connection 
to other elements, need of scaffold or other ex­
cess measures. The algorithm should determine 
the most critical set of measure and recommend 
which measures should be executed together. 
Integration of building characteristics
Developing an algorithm that can integrate the 
characteristics of a buildings physical frame with 
the quantities and properties of elements can for 
example help understanding the influence of a 
buildings preservation value on the prioritisation 
of maintenance measures.
Renovate or demolish and rebuild
Developing an algorithm that can determine the 
life cycle of a building based on the quantity and 
properties building materials, the characteristics 
of the building and the economy of the owners 
and recommend if the building should be pre­
served, renovated, demolished or rebuilt. 
These are a handful of ideas and recommendations 
for further research and development into the uni­
verse of digitalisation. Possibilities are many. ■
