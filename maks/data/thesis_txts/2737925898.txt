Abstract
In this thesis, we investigate the uniqueness and stability of the problem of
recovering a function or distribution, f, with compact support from partial
knowledge about its Fourier transform, bf.
We derive a novel stability estimate for the problem of recovering a Sobolev
function f ∈H1
0(−1, 1) from bf restricted to the interval [−B, B]. This stability
estimate depends explicitly on the bandwidth B. The estimate incorporates a
priori knowledge about the oscillation of f. We use the ratio of the L2-norms
of the derivative of f and of f itself to describe the amount of oscillation. We
identify a critical oscillation ratio, beyond which the stability deteriorates. This
critical oscillation ratio is analogous to the Rayleigh and Abel diffraction limits.
We also consider uniqueness and stability of recovering signals that are as-
sumed to be in Hs(R) where s is a real number. Given that f can be written
as a superposition of Dirac delta distributions, one can stably recover f from
finitely many Fourier coefficients assuming a priori that f satisfies a sparsity
constraint.

1
Introduction
In everyday language, the word resolution will often refer to the number of pixels in
each dimension that a given screen can display. In many engineering fields, the term
resolution generally refers to the ability to observe details in an object of interest. For
audio signals, one could imagine using a microphone to record a symphony orchestra.
Here, the resolution of the audio signal refers to the quality of the sound signal –
the accuracy with which the audio signal captures the sound waves produced by the
instruments.
In optics, we may experience a similar phenomenon, whether that is when taking a
photograph with a regular camera, using a telescope to take a photo of stars that are
several million light-years away, or when recording bacteria through a microscope.
When taking measurements of an object, the amount of detail of the object that we
are able to observe is, in general, always reduced compared to the amount of detail
in the actual object. For a given measurement device, the highest possible amount
of detail that can be observed, is referred to as the resolution limit. Super-resolution
is the ”art” of surpassing this limit imposed by the measurement device, often by
utilizing a priori information about the object of interest.
In this thesis, we will study the problem of super-resolving from a mathematical
standpoint. We focus on computational super-resolution, which refers to computa-
tional techniques that one can use after measurements are taken, when reconstructing
an object; that is, the measurement device is fixed. In particular, we focus on the
resolution of recovering a signal from information about the Fourier transform of the
signal. In the next section, we will illustrate the relevance of this problem in the field
of optics.
1.1
An application of resolution analysis: Optics
Although this thesis focuses on the mathematics of super-resolution, let us first con-
sider a concrete application of super-resolution.
Throughout much of history, it has been a subject of discussion whether light is
particles or waves. One proponent of the particle theory of light was Sir Isaac Newton,
who argued that light appeared to propagate in straight lines or so-called rays [NI30].
Experimental work by Grimaldi, published in 1665 (see [Som11]), showed however
that light does in fact ”bend” when hitting an object. What Grimaldi had witnessed
was the concept of diffraction.

the two red dots on the right side of the figure. Light from the red dots passes through

is studied in order to engineer the transfer function in a way that leads to higher
resolution.
1.2
Diffraction limit and super-resolution
In many scientific fields, it is of interest to infer finer details about an object f from
a measurement g = Af. For example, in microscopy, a particular resolution might
be needed for an expert to identify a certain cell type. In fluorescence microscopy
and in astronomy, the object in question will be a set of small colored dots with
varying intensities, typically modeled as a superposition of point sources. For optical
systems, one often talks about the diffraction limit of the system [Lin12]. Several
definitions exist, all of which are rather arbitrary. Roughly speaking, the diffraction
limit is the minimal distance between two point sources, as shown in Figure 2, such
that one can detect that there are two point sources. The most commonly known
are the Rayleigh and Abbe diffraction limits, [SKWSW20]. These definitions share a
common characteristic: They are proportional to λ/NA with λ being the wavelength

optical system.
The term super-resolution refers to methods that allows one to infer details about the
object that are smaller than the diffraction limit. Specifically, the term computational
super-resolution refers to computational methods for recovering details finer than the
diffraction limit, for example by making a priori assumptions about the object.

1.4
Organization of the thesis
The thesis is organized as follows. In Chapter 2, some mathematical preliminaries
are introduced. This includes theory about Sobolev spaces, distributions, the Fourier
transform and harmonic measures. Since each of these subjects is rich enough to fill
out entire books, only the main results with relevance for the rest of the thesis are
presented.
In Chapter 3, we consider the inverse problem of recovering an element, f, which is
assumed to belong to a Sobolev space Hs(R), s ∈R, of Hilbert type. Specifically we
derive stability estimates for the truncated Fourier transform acting on Hs(R). We
also illustrate the results with a numerical example.
In Chapter 4 we will assume that the signal is given by f = PN

The problem of recovering f from the noisy Fourier data gε now enters the domain
of compressed sensing, where the main assumptions being made on the signal f is
related to sparsity. Some of the most notable work in this area was done by Emanuel
Cand´es, among others, with the 2012 paper [CFG14] being very influential.
The main theorems in this thesis that contain new discoveries are given in Section
3.2. Specifically Theorem 3.8, Theorem 3.10, Theorem 3.11 and Corollary 3.12 is new
work.
Lastly, a short word on notation: The letter F is consistently used to denote the
Fourier transform. The signal we want to reconstruct is f. Often, the letter g is used
as the ”observation”. The letter B will always denote the bandwidth, meaning the

Mathematical preliminaries
The Fourier transform can be defined on various vector spaces, and thus the properties
of the Fourier transform depends on the particular space. In the following section, we
will refer to elements of these spaces as signals. The signals are what we are trying to
recover or reconstruct. The signals we consider will sometimes be distributions and
sometimes functions in Lp-spaces.
First we will consider the Fourier transform on the space of square integrable func-
tions, L2.
We then consider the Sobolev spaces of Hilbert type, Hs.
Lastly we
consider the Fourier transform on the space of compactly supported distributions.
The Fourier transform of a compactly supported distribution turns out to have some
nice properties, as it has a holomorphic extension to the entire complex plane.
The topic of complex analysis studies the properties of holomorphic functions, and
thus Section 2.2 contains some results from complex analysis, leading up to the two
constants theorem attributed to Nevanlinna [EN13].
The theory in Section 2.1 can be found in many analysis books.
For the theory
regarding function spaces, see the classical book [Rud87] or the more modern book
[Bre10]. For the theory regarding distributions, see [Rud91] and [H¨or15]. For the
theory on complex analysis in Section 2.2, see the classic book, [Ahl66], and the book
on potential theory, [Ran95].

2.2
Holomorphic and harmonic functions
Because the Fourier transform of a compactly supported distribution is entire, we can
take advantage of this fact, as we use a property of entire functions that allows us to
bound the function in one part of the complex plane by its values at another part.
This is the essence of the two constants theorem (Corollary 2.21). In this section, we
aim to give an almost complete proof of the two constants theorem. This will include
showing the existence of the solution to the Dirichlet problem under rather general
assumptions on the domain and boundary data.
The tool to do this is complex analysis. In this section, we will repeatedly make
use of the extended complex plane C = C ∪{∞}, which allows us to perform some
arithmetic operations at ∞. For a reference on this and for many other basic concepts
in complex analysis, see the book [Ahl66]. In this section, we will use z, ξ and ζ as
elements of C. We shall use subscript x and y to denote differentiation along the real
and imaginary axes respectively.
First we shall introduce the notion of harmonic and subharmonic functions.
Definition 2.11 (Harmonic function). Let U ⊆C be open and connected. A function
u : U →R is harmonic if u ∈C2(U) and ∆u = uxx + uyy ≡0.

which at that point in time had not yet been proved.

We consider the truncated Fourier transform acting on different domains: One well

0(−1, 1).

Remark 3.14 (About super-resolution)
In this chapter we have seen results that are related to the concept of diffraction
limit. In optics, this limit is loosely speaking the smallest observable detail of an
object, or more precisely the minimal distance, d, between two point sources, that
allows one to distinguish the point sources, when observed through an aperture of
width B (corresponding to a band-limiting filter at the frequency B).
In this section we have observed two phenomena that are related to that of the
diffraction limit:
1. For a function f ∈L2(−1, 1), the information contained in Pf, where P is the
orthogonal projection onto the first ⌊2
πB⌋Prolate Spheroidal Wave Functions
can be stably recovered.
2. For a function f ∈H1
0(−1, 1), if ω = ∥fx∥L2(−1,1)/∥f∥L2(−1,1) < B, then f can
be stably recovered.
Here ω and span(f1, . . . , fN) can be interpreted as specifying the amount of detail in
the signal.
Can we recover information beyond these limits, and thereby super-resolving the
signal?
Theorems 3.6 and 3.11 show that this indeed can be done under certain
assumptions, for example on the smoothness of the function. The stability constants
that we have obtained are however very large. Let k be the stability constant such
that ∥f∥≤k∥FBf∥. In order to obtain meaningful reconstructions, the measurement
error ε should be small enough such that ∥ε∥and k−1 are the same order of magnitude.

5
Conclusion
In this thesis, we have examined the uniqueness and stability of recovering a function
with compact support or a superposition of Dirac delta distributions from partial
knowledge of its Fourier transform.
In case the Fourier transform, bf, of a function or distribution f with compact sup-
port is known in an interval with complete accuracy, one can uniquely recover f by
holomorphic extrapolation of bf to all of R.
The stability of recovering a function, f, in the Sobolev space H1
0(−1, 1) from knowl-
edge of the Fourier transform of f in the interval [−B, B] depends on a frequency
number ω defined as a ratio of the L2-norm of the derivative fx and of f. It was found
that when the frequency number, ω, is smaller than the bandwidth B, the problem
of recovering f is well-posed. When the frequency number exceeds B, the stability
rapidly deteriorates. Still we are able to get an upper bound on the stability constant
of exponential type. The stability constant we have obtained depends explicitly on
B, which makes it an improvement of the work in [LS16].
In a numerical example, we recovered a function from samples of its Fourier transform
using the Fractional Fast Fourier Transform [BS94]. In this example we clearly ob-
served an increase in the reconstruction error that occurred as the frequency number
was made larger than the bandwidth B.
In Chapter 4 we examined results on uniqueness and stability of recovering a su-
perposition, f, of Dirac delta distributions using only finitely many samples of the
Fourier transform bf. If f satisfies a sparsity condition that places a lower bound on
the shortest distance between the supports of the Dirac delta distribution, extrapo-
lation in the Fourier domain can be done by solving an optimization problem that
minimizes the Total Variation norm of a complex measure. Hence a uniqueness and
stability result can be obtained.

function frft = my ift(b,B)
% This algorith is taken from the Bailey 1993 article on the FRFT.
% Although one change has been made. The original algorithm can be seen
% from the comment.
m = length(b);
a = 2*B;
beta = a/m;
j = 0:m-1;
gamma = 2/m;
k = 0:(m-1);
delta = beta*gamma/(2*pi);
yj = zeros(2*m,1);
yj(j+1) = b.*exp(pi*1i*(j*m*delta-j.ˆ2*delta));
zj = zeros(2*m,1);
zj(j+1) = exp(pi*1i*j.ˆ2*delta);
zj(j+1+m) = exp(pi*1i*(j-m).ˆ2*delta);
%zj(j+1+m) = exp(pi*1i*(j-2*m).ˆ2*delta);
frft temp1 = fft(yj).*fft(zj);
frft temp2 = ifft(frft temp1);
exp factor = beta*exp(pi*1i*((k-m/2)*m*delta-k.ˆ2*delta));
exp factor = exp factor(:);
frft = exp factor.*frft temp2(1:m);
end
