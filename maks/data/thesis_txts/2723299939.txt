Abstract
Primary hyperaldosteronism (PHA) is a disease potentially concerning over 10% of all hyperten-
sive patients (i.e., high blood pressure), and can without treatment lead to serious health problems
such as damaging of blood vessels and kidney failure, and in worst case cause heart failure. It is
a disease that causes high blood pressure due to excess production of the hormone aldosterone
from the adrenal glands, and is commonly caused by enlargement of the adrenal glands or tu-
mours (noncancerous). Positron emission tomography (PET) is considered a sensitive and specific
non-invasive alternative to adrenal vein sampling (AVS) for identification of PHA. The PET tracer
[11C]metomidate ([11C]MTO) has specific binding to the adrenal gland tissue, but with a short
half-life of 11C (∼20 min). It is therefore crucial to have efficient and time-optimised radiosyn-
thesis and quality control (QC) of [11C]MTO. Thus, the goal of this project was to both implement
an automated radiosynthesis of [11C]MTO on a TracerMaker synthesis module and establish fast
QC methods of the product at Dept. for Nuclear Medicine, Herlev Hospital. For this purpose,
a series of synthesis tests on the TracerMaker module was performed and optimisation of vari-
ous HPLC methods were conducted. For the QC-procedures, two time-optimised HPLC methods
for [11C]MTO were established to respectively determine radiochemical purity (6 min) and enan-
tiomeric purity (9 min). However, the automated radiosynthesis of [11C]MTO yielded low activities
of 0.1-0.9 GBq at end-of-synthesis and included several UV- and radioactive impurities detected
by HPLC. Hence, further improvement of the synthesis is needed to prevent impurities in the final
product formulation.

Introduction
Primary hyperaldosteronism (PHA) is the most common cause for secondary high blood pressure
(secondary hypertension), i.e., high blood pressure caused by another medical condition, and may
concern over 10% of all hypertensive patients [1]. Without treatment, high blood pressure can lead
to health problems such as damaging of blood vessels and kidney failure, and in worst case cause
heart failure [2, 3]. PHA was first described in the 1950’s by J. W. Conn, and is the excess produc-
tion of the hormone aldosterone from the adrenal glands [4]. The condition is commonly caused
by enlargement of the adrenal glands (bilateral idiopathic hyperaldosteronism, IHA) or tumours
(aldosterone-producing adenoma, APA), and responsible for 60% and 35% of the cases, respec-
tively [5]. To accurately treat PHA, it is important to distinguish between IHA (treated medically),
and APA (treated surgically). The cause of PHA is identified by the adrenal vein sampling (AVS)
method and is considered as the golden standard [6]. Though, AVS is an invasive examination
technique, and comes with challenges such as being unreliable due to human anatomy, where
cannulation of the correct adrenal vein fails in 25-50% of the cases. The difficult golden standard
method led to the search for a non-invasive method such as a positron emission tomography (PET)
tracer, which targets the adrenal tissue. The tracer utilised for PET is a radio-labelled molecule and
when injected to the patient there is an emission of a positron (e+) which leads to detection of ac-
tivity in the targeted tissue and functional imaging via high resolution PET images as illustrated in
Figure 1 for a [11C]MTO scan [7].
Figure 1: Illustration of (R)-[11C]MTO uptake in the adrenal glands, and subsequent scan of the adrenal glands via
PET, showing the tumor placed in the adrenal glands.
It has been shown that 11β-hydroxylase (CYP11B1) is a key enzyme in the final step of the biosyn-
thesis of aldosterone in the adrenal glands [8]. Furthermore, it has been reported that CYP11B1 is
extensively detected in APA tumor tissue and proved to be a good target enzyme for adrenocorti-
cal imaging with PET [9, 10]. In 1998, Bergström and his colleagues reported that both (R)-[11C]-
etomidate (R-[11C]ETO) and (R)-[11C]-metomidate (R-[11C]MTO) had high affinity for CYP11B1 as
well as specific binding to the adrenal gland tissue [7]. They concluded that [11C]MTO was a better
candidate due to higher uptake in the adrenal glands and APA’s as well as a lower uptake in the
adjacent organs compared to [11C]ETO. Additionally, they reported that [11C]MTO had a shorter
synthesis time (40 min vs to 60 min), higher yield (10 GBq vs to 1.6 GBq), and specific radioactivity
(120 GBq/µmol vs to 6.4 GBq/µmol). Thus, an accurate and non-invasive alternative to the inva-
sive and technical challenging method of AVS was created, which could not only be utilised for an
easy approach to scan for APA and IHA but also for follow-up procedures [11].
However, the synthesis of 11C labelled compounds is challenging due to the relative short half-
life of 11C (20.34 min), where it decays into the stable 11B through positron emission [12]. Utilised
for PET scans, it is critical that the radiosynthesis is fast as well as the product quality control (QC),
which is a process needed in accordance with good manufacturing practice (GMP) before human
use [13]. Recently, [11C]MTO synthesis were reported by Kovac et. al. Here they used a fully auto-
mated synthesizer to produce [11C]MTO, with a total synthesis time of 18 min [11]. Additionally,
6
==PAGE:8==
QC of the product was conducted and took additionally 15-20 min, yielding a total time of 38 min.
Thus, paving the way for a GMP compliant, time-optimised synthesis and QC to get the highest
yield possible for clinical use.
In this project, the overall aim is to implement a similar GMP compliant [11C]MTO production
at Dept. of Nuclear Medicine at Herlev Hospital, Denmark. Thus, the purpose of this study is to
synthesise, optimise and validate the synthesis of [11C]MTO on a new synthesis module, Tracer-
Maker. Furthermore, quality control analysis of [11C]MTO will be investigated and optimised.

Theoretical background
This section will provide an introduction to the study by first discussing the background and con-
text, followed by the research problem, goal, hypothesis, and approach of the project. The subjects
covered by the theory sections are the disease, introductory radiochemistry, radiopharmaceutical
tracers, radiation dosimitry, synthesis of [11C]MTO, validation methods for [11C]MTO, and permit-

2.1
Primary hyperaldosteronism
Primary hyperaldosteronism is rooted in adrenal glands that does not function normally. The
adrenal glands are situated above the kidneys and secret hormones important for regulation of
various systems, such as blood volume and osmolality [14]. They consist of an inner medulla and
an outer cortex, where adrenal hormones are secreted from the latter (see Figure 2). The cortex is
well-supplied with vessels and obtain blood from branches of aorta, renal arteries, and the inferior
phrenic artery [15]. Moreover, the adrenal cortex consists of three zones (glomerulosa, fasciculata,
and reticularis), c.f., Figure 2, each responsible for production of various hormone classes: min-
eralocorticoids (influence salt and water balances, i.e., aldosterone), glucocorticoids (influence
immune system and metabolism, i.e., cortisol), and androgens (development and regulation of
male characteristics, i.e., testosterone).
Figure 2: Illustrated is the kidneys, the adrenal glands and the zones within the adrenal cortex [16].
Secretion of glucocorticoids is controlled directly by the adrenocorticotrophic hormone (ACTH)
secreted by the hypophysis in the brain through a negative feedback system, whereas the secre-
tion of mineralocorticoids primarily depends on the renin-angiotensin system (RAS) [17,18]. If the
adrenal glands does not function i.e., due to either an over-production or insufficient production
of hormones, various types of complications for the body occur, which can cause severe compli-
cations [19]. Such a disease is PHA and is caused by overproduction and -secretion of aldosterone
in the zona glomerulosa. The elevated aldosterone levels cause retention of sodium in the blood,
which in the body leads to a higher retention of water, excretion of potassium, and increased blood
volume and pressure [4]. The most evident consequence is high blood pressure, but the disease
may also influence many other systems and organs in the body even if the blood pressure is under
control by medication. Such examples could be the higher risk for heart, brain, and kidney com-
plications. [2,3]. PHA occurs independent of the renin-angiotensin system and can be caused by
an adrenal (noncancerous) tumour, enlargement of the adrenal glands, adrenal cancer, or genetic
8
==PAGE:10==
syndrome [20]. In addition to increased aldosterone, a pivotal trait of PHA, is a decreased level of
renin. This is due to the overproduction and -secretion of aldosterone which has a suppressing
effect of the renin production as a consequence of the negative feedback loop as illustrated in Fig-
ure 3 [21]. Renin is a part of the RAS and has a considerable influence on the blood pressure and
fluid balance [22].
Figure 3: Illustration of the renin-angiotensin system (RAS) under the influence of primary hyperaldosterionism,
where the renin is suppressed by the overproduction of aldosterone by e.g., an adenoma (illustrated as the red cir-
cle) [23].
Under normal circumstances, renin secretion from the kidneys is a response of a decrease in the
blood pressure [19]. Renin converts the secreted angiotensinogen (a glycoprotein) from the liver,
to angiotensin I which consecutively is converted to angiotensin II by the angiotensin-converting
enzyme (ACE) primarily found on the surface of the lungs [23]. Angiotensin II stimulates the se-
cretion of aldosterone, which increases the blood pressure and results in suppressed secretion of
renin. When there is an aldosterone-producing tumour, there will be an increase of aldosterone
without the influence of angiotensin II, which in turn will keep suppressing secretion of renin, an-
giotensin I and II. Thus, the ratio between aldosterone and renin increases when suffering from
PHA. The decreased level of renin in PHA makes it possible to differ between primary and sec-
ondary hyperaldosteronism as in secondary hyperaldosteronism both the aldosterone and renin
level are elevated. Thus, to confirm that the patient suffers from PHA, the ratio of aldosterone and
renin in the plasma should be obtained, and is referred to the aldosterone-to-renin ratio (ARR) test.
If the ARR is higher than 31, it suggests the patient suffers from PHA [24]. Combining the plasma
test with a urinal test will further validate the diagnosis as a patient that suffers from PHA will have
high levels of potassium (hypokalemia) in the urine. Though, new and improved renin and aldos-
terone assays led to the discovery that the majority of the patients that suffers from PHA have nor-
mal levels of potassium (normokalemia) [25]. Therefore, if the patient has normokalemia, it should
not preclude the diagnosis of PHA. When the diagnosis of PHA has been confirmed, it is necessary
to determine the cause of the hyperaldosteronism as the treatment will be different whether the
PHA is unilateral (one adrenal gland) or bilateral (both adrenal glands) [26]. Excess secretion of
aldosterone in one of the adrenal glands indicates the presence of an aldosterone-producing tu-
9
==PAGE:11==
mour (APA, or aldosteronoma) or genetic disorders, and is treated surgically or medically. If the
lesions are greater than 3 cm, the APA should be suspected to be a cancerous tumour [20]. Con-
trarily, if excess secretion of aldosterone is measured from both of the adrenal glands it suggests
that the patient suffers from IHA or genetic disorders, and is treated medically [23]. To differenti-
ate between unilateral and bilateral PHA multiple test can be conducted. The considered golden
standard method within characterising sub-types of PHA is AVS [6]. But as AVS is an invasive ap-
proach and comes with several challenges such as being unreliable due to difficulty in cannulation
of the correct vein as well as being cumbersome due to sampling over a longer period. Thus, a less
invasive and more consistent method such as PET scanning are more appealing. Furthermore, the
genetic disorders mimics the renin and aldosterone levels of APAs and cancerous PHA, which AVS
might not be able to differ between [27]. By use of PET this problem can be alleviated as PET imag-
ing will show tumours. Thus, by choice of a molecule specific for adrenal gland tissue, and label it
with a radioactive isotope specific for use of PET, it is possible to differ between both bilateral or
unilateral PHA as well as genetic disorder and tumour.

The enzymes CYP11B1 and CYP11B2 are shown to be key regulators of the adrenal cortisol and al-
dosterone synthesis (Figure 4) [8]. In APA tissue, both CYP11B1 and CYP11B2 are detected in abun-
dant quantities, proving to be possible targets for imaging of the adrenal glands with PET [9, 10].
A well-studied tracer used for imaging of the adrenal glands is the tracer R-[11C]MTO with a high
affinity for both CYP11B1 and CYP11B2 [7]. [11C]MTO is synthesised from its precursor desethyl-
etomidate (DETO) and a radioactive source, e.g., [11C]CH3I (see section 2.3 for details). MTO has
proven to be a better candidate compared to etomidate (ETO) as MTO has a higher uptake in the
10
==PAGE:12==
adrenal glands and APAs, and a lower uptake in the adjacent organs such as the liver. As MTO
has high affinity to both CYP11B1 and CYP11B2, it can be problematic as CYP11B2 is also used
for the bio-synthesis of cortisol in the adrenal gland. Thus, the contrast in PET between adrenal
gland and APA is blurred [6]. To overcome the issue, the non-autonomous cortisol secretion is sup-
pressed prior to the PET scan which will reduce the expression of CYP11B2, and will create a larger
contrast between uptake into normal adrenal gland tissue and the APA. Another solution is use
an inhibitor specific for CYP11B2, leaving only CYP11B1 to bind with MTO. Thus, it is possible to
diagnose PHA and detect APAs in a non-invasive approach using the radioactive tracer [11C]MTO,
which can additionally be utilised for follow-up procedures [11].
2.2
Introduction to radiochemistry and -pharmaceuticals and PET
In hospital departments of nuclear medicine the diagnosis of different diseases is accomplished
essentially by two methods: single-photon emission computed tomography (SPECT) and positron
emission tomography (PET). However, PET will be the main focus as [11C]MTO is a PET tracer.
PET is a tool used for both research and medical purposes, and is heavily utilised within imag-
ing of tumours as well as mapping the human brain [28]. As PET is a highly sensitive method and
detects changes at a molecular level, it can provide early information on diseases long before any
structural or anatomic changes have occurred. To conduct a scan, a radio-labelled molecule, a
tracer, specific for the diseased tissue is needed. The tracer is injected into the body of the patient,
and is left for a short period of time to distribute and accumulate in the tissue of interest. The
patient is then placed in the imaging scanner, where a record of the tissue is obtained as a result of
the radioactive decay of the tracer.
2.2.1
Radiochemistry and radiation
Radiochemistry is the chemistry of radioactive materials, which decay into more stable materials
[29]. An atom consists of electrons (e-), protons (p), and neutrons (n), where the protons and
neutrons collectively account for the atom nuclei (c.f., Figure 5). Nuclides are commonly referred
to by the elemental symbol with a number indicating the mass number, A, e.g., 14N, 12C, and 16O.
A nuclide with a given amount of protons, but differing numbers of neutrons, is called isotopes,
and varies in stability. Though, most of the isotopes is unstable in respect to the natural element,
and can undergo a spontaneous transformation to obtain a more stable isotope. The spontaneous
transformation is called radioactive decay, and the nuclei that sustain radioactive decay is known
as a radionuclide or a radioisotope. When a radioisotope decays, it does so by emission of one
or more of the particles from the atomic nuclei, and results in one (or more) of the three types of
radiation: α, β and γ.
Figure 5: Illustration of an atom, with 14N as an example.
11
==PAGE:13==
α radiation is commonly seen in radionuclides of high mass (A < 80) and is the emission of two
protons and two neutrons in form of the stable 4He, also known as an α particle. Where β radiation
is the process in which the radioisotope emits either an electron, e- (β−decay) or a positron, e+ (β+
decay). The last type of radiation is γ emission. This type of emission is the outcome of a relaxation
of a daughter nuclide in an excited state into ground state. During the relaxation into the ground
state it emits photons, also called γ photons. As the project is based on tracers for PET, the main
focus will be on β radiation.
Stability via β decay can occur through various processes and depends on the radionuclide
in question. The process of a nucleon (i.e., p, n) within the nucleus converting to another type
of nucleon is the common type of β decay. One of the conversions could be the transformation
of a neutron to a proton and is a process commonly known as the β−decay in which it emits an
electron, e-. The process can be written as:
n →p+ +e−
(1)
Thus, in a β−process the radionuclide loses one neutron and gain one proton. Another type of
β decay is the conversion of a proton to a neutron, which can be achieved in two ways. The first
route is emission of a positron (e+), i.e., the positively charged antiparticle of an electron, from the
nucleus. This process is known as the β+ decay and can be written as:
p+ →n+e+
(2)
The other route is capturing of an electron situated in an inner-shell by the nucleus, and is referred
to as the electron capture (e.c.) process. This process can be written as:
p+ +e−→n
(3)
Capture of the extra-nuclear electron leaves an inner shell vacancy which is relieved by one of the
electrons from a higher energy shell. During this transition there will simultaneously occur an
emission of an X-ray photon. Thus, in both β+ decay and e.c. process the radionuclide loses one
proton and gain one neutron. All the β emissions are depicted in Figure 6.
12
==PAGE:14==
Figure 6: Illustration of the two β decay processes [29]. a) an electron is emitted, b) a positron is emitted, c) electron
leaves an inner shell vacancy which d) is filled up by a electron from a higher shell, emitting an X-ray photon.
The three types of emission (α, β and γ) differ in energy and penetrating power [30]. An α particle
only travels a few centimetres and is easily stopped by a thin piece of paper or a layer of dead skin.
However, they generate large quantities of local ionisation energy upon impact, which for living
tissue may cause mutations disruptive of cell processes [31]. The smaller β particles travels up to
a couple of centimetres in living tissue and can be stopped by a few millimetres thick aluminium
sheet. This also imply that the β particles damage a larger area and can damage tissue as well as
causing mutations affecting living cells. Both α and β particles are said to be internal emitters as
their damage to organisms are greatest when internally. Contrarily, γ radiation is said to be an
external emitter as it can penetrate biological materials effortlessly and can only be stopped by an
substantial amounts of heavy metal shielding materials such as lead. Thus, γ radiation can pro-
duce damage without being taken internally.
Despite the radiation being dangerous for its environment, in very low concentrations (<100µg),
the radioactive compounds are used in the pharmaceutical world as diagnostic and therapeutic
tools for different types of diseases and are known as radiopharmaceuticals [32]. The radioactiv-
ity of a radiopharmaceutical is often given in the unit Becquerel (Bq), which is described as 1 Bq
being equivalent to 1 disintegration (decay) per second. When administered, the activity of the
radiopharmaceutical is measured as the specific activity (SA) and is the ratio between the radioac-
tive ("hot") and non-radioactive ("cold") pharmaceutical [33]. The SI unit for SA is GBq/µmol and
can be determined by High-performance liquid chromatography (HPLC). Additionally, the time
at which the radioactivity of the radionuclide is reduced to half of the original value is called the
half-life (T½) and is a value used to determine how much of the radiopharmaceutical can be ad-
ministered [34]. As radionuclides differ in their half-lives, it is important to correct the activity with
regard to the decay, and is calculated by:
A(t) = A(0)e(−ln(2)/T1/2)t
(4)
13
==PAGE:15==
where A(t) is the radioactivity at a given time, t, and A(0) is the activity at time zero [35]. The
decay corrected yield is more accurate in regards to the appropriate amount of dose administered
to the patient. The dose of a radiopharmaceutical administered to a patient is typically scaled
based on body weight, or total body surface area [36]. When the radiopharmaceutical is given as
a diagnostic tool, it may be administered with an activity as little as 0.185 MBq or as high as 1295
MBq [37]. Though, if the T½ is low enough much larger quantities of the radiopharmaceutical
can be utilised, e.g., up to 2 GBq for rubidium-82 (T½ = 76 s) [38]. If given as an therapeutic tool
to treat e.g., certain types of cancers, the dosage administered is often higher than the diagnostic
radiopharmaceutical.
2.2.2
Brief history and introduction to PET and radiopharmaceuticals
The diagnostic radiopharmaceuticals are often referred to as radiotracers and can be described
as a specific radiolabelled molecule which mimic or traces the in vivo behaviour of a natural oc-
curring molecule [32]. Hence, the radiotracer can be utilised to give information of a specific bi-
ological process such as glucose metabolism by the tracer [18F]FDG. They can also be utilised to
get an image of a target tissue as the radiopharmaceutical can accumulate in the specific tissue
area and emit radiation, which then can be detected by imaging instruments detecting potential
tumours [34]. Since the discovery of radionuclides, the use as pharmaceutical diagnostics and
treatments has increased due to the many applications of radionuclides [39]. As seen on Figure 7,
the use of radioactivity have had an important impact on medical diagnostic processes since the
discovery of X-rays by Wilhem Conrad Röngten in 1885. Shortly after, Henry Becquerel discov-
ered natural radioactivity and today the unit Becquerel is widely used to define the radioactivity.
In 1898 Marie Curie discovered radium as the natural source of γ-rays, and even though radium
was the only source of γ-rays for the first 20 years, clinical radiotherapy thrived [40]. Addition-
ally, the radioactivity unit "Curie" (Ci) was named in honour to Curie and is equivalent to 1 g of
radium, and in Becquerel 1 Ci is equivalent to 37 GBq. Following the work of Curie, in the 1930s
the first production of artificial radioactive elements was achieved and in the late 1930s the tracer
principle was discovered [41]. The radiotracer principle was first described by George de Hevesy,
and states that the radiotracer must not disturb or alter a biological process whilst being a part of
the same biological process [42]. By this, it is possible to get an image of the biological process
by the use of a radiotracer. The first radionuclide to be used for in vivo tracking was 131I to study
iodine metabolism in treatment of an overactive thyroid gland, and could be tracked over a long
time period as the half-life of 131I is 8 days [43]. Initially, the detection of radioactivity was per-
formed by using the Geiger-Müller counter which was moved manually over the targeted area on
the patient who had been injected with 131I [41], to measure the uptake of the radionuclide in the
thyroid gland. Though, this proved to be difficult as the counter was insensitive to high-energy
γ-emissions from 131I, and hence there was a need for more sensitive methods.
Figure 7: Timeline of the most historical events of radioactivity and impact on medical imaging techniques [39].

In the 1970s the development of computerised tomography (CT) and magnetic resonance imaging
(MRI) made it possible to determine the exact location as well as the size of a tumor [40]. Further-
more, the invention of PET in 1975 enabled visualisation of the radionuclides within living organ-
isms, thus making it possible to detect molecular and cellular changes in diseased tissue as PET
offers high sensitivity within a range of 10-11-10-12 mol/L as well as independence of the location
depth of the tracer, i.e., good imaging independent of how deep the tracer is located within the
human body [44]. Around same period of time, the well-known and still highly used [18F]FDG (2-
deoxy-2-[18F]fluoroglucose, glucose analogue) was developed to be used in studies of metabolism
in the brain [41]. However, the first glucose analogue was 2-deoxy-D-glucose labelled with the
radionuclide 14C in the 2-position of glucose (c.f., Figure 8) and used for animal studies [45,46].

glucose analogue, 2-deoxy-2-[18F]fluoro-D-glucose, to the right.
But as 14C has a long half-life (5,730 years) and failed to image the distribution of the radioactivity
due to undergoing β decay with maximum emission energy at 156 keV and maximum travel dis-
tance in body tissue at 0.27 mm, the 14C-labelled glucose was not suitable for humans. Fowler and
coworkers came up with the idea to use [18F]-labelled glucose as [18F] is a pure positron emitter
with no γ emission, a low half-life of 110 min, decays to the stable [18O], and is chemically reactive
(easy to react during synthesis of radiopharmaceutical) [41]. At the time, the synthesis of[18F]FDG
took 2 hours to complete with a yield of 8%, and was produced via a radiosynthesis using [18F]F2
and tri-O-acetoxy-D-glucal. Since then, the usage of [18F]FDG as a diagnostic has been expanded
to other parts of the body, and the synthesis-time and yield has greatly been improved [47, 48].
Today, [18F]FDG can be produced in a high yield (60-70%, decay corrected yield) in less than 30
minutes, and is widely used for PET imaging of various types of brain diseases [41].

The PET method relies on detection of γ-ray pairs emitted indirectly by a positron-emitting ra-
dionuclide, as seen in Figure 9 [49]. A three dimensional image is then calculated, which pictures
organs and functional processes in the body. The tracers used for PET is positron emitters (β+
decay) [50]. The emitted positron travels a specific distance depending on its initial energy, and
will over time lose its energy until it annihilates with an electron. Subsequent to the annihilation
a pair of γ photons is emitted in opposing directions, 180°of each other, and with an energy of 511
keV/photon. The energy of annihilation photons, (511 keV/photon), is the same for all positron
emitters [35].

Contrarily, SPECT is a nuclear medicine tomographic imaging technique using γ-rays, which are
measured directly by gamma cameras. Comparing PET with SPECT, the most prominent differ-
ence is the higher sensitivity of detection for PET [38]. The higher sensitivity in PET implies a
higher ability to detect and record a higher percentage of the emitted events. Furthermore, the
utilised tracers for PET have a shorter half-life and it will therefore be possible to inject higher con-
centrations of the tracer without posing additional damage to the patient, making it achievable to
get increased detectable radiation over a shorter period [38].

considered thoroughly in regard to both patient safety and imaging method utilised.
2.2.4
Radiation dosimitry
While PET has contributed significantly to pharmaceutical development, the administration of
radiotracers to human patients exposes them and the staff to a source of radiation [60–62]. More-
over, the radiation of PET radionuclides are associated with high energy of annihilation photons
(511 keV) [52]. Therefore, it is important to focus on radiation dosimitry, protection, and safety
precautions in effort to reduce radiation exposure. Radiation dosimitry is an attempt to explain
the risk of physiological effects, chemical and/or biological changes, radiation would produce in a
target via specific measurements. There are two SI units related to radiation; Gray (Gy) for radia-
tion absorbed dose (J/kg), and Sievert (Sv) for radiation dose equivalent to absorbed in a human.
The absorbed dose (D) is the measurement of the energy deposited in a material by ionising radi-
ation, and is equal to the mean absorbed energy (d¯E) per unit mass (dm), expressed in the unit Gy
(1 J/kg = 1 Gy) [62].

To minimise the radiation dose the human body is exposed to, it is important to consider radi-
ation protection [52]. Protection of external sources of radiation depends highly on four factors:
time, distance, shielding, and activity. The longer the exposure time, the higher radiation dose.
When considering the distance to the radiation source, it is important to note the photon energy
emitted from the radionuclide as some are higher than others. When handling a radiation source
of the positron emitting PET tracer (photon energy of 511 keV), the radioactivity of the radiotracer
also determines the necessary distance to take, as higher radioactivity of the high photon energy
emitting radionuclide increases the radiation dose considerably. B increase of distance, it is possi-
ble to reduce the exposure dramatically, i.e., by doubling the distance from the radiation source it
is possible to lower the exposure levels by a factor of four [66]. Though, as distance is not enough, it
is necessary to shield with barriers of lead (Pb) as it absorbs radiation, and as a result provides with
radiation protection. For production, the radiosynthesis of PET tracers needs to be performed by
automated or remote-controlled synthesis equipment inside lead-shielded fume-hoods (hot cells)
in order to reduce the radiation exposure [67,68]. The higher the energy emitted from the radionu-
clide, the thicker the layer of lead.

2.3
Production of 11C for automated radiosynthesis of [11C]-metomidate
Formation of 11C requires high energy bombardment of 14N with protons (H+) [35]. This is accom-
plished by use of a cyclotron, which is a particle accelerator. The idea of a cyclotron was conceived
in 1929 by the American physicist and inventor Ernest O. Lawrence, and in 1931 the first cyclotron
with a diameter of 11.4 cm was built by Lawrence [52,72]. The principle of a cyclotron is based on
a magnetic field, where the protons are introduced in the centre of the cyclotron, and the acceler-
ation of the ions in an expanding spiral path occurs via an alternating electric field, c.f., Figure 11.
Figure 11: Sketch of the expanding spiral path of the accelerated proton inside the cyclotron, accelerating towards the
target guided by a deflection plate to attract the ion [72]. The alternating electric field is controlled by the oscillator.
To generate the protons for production of 11C, the cyclotron is fitted with an ion source, which
generate protons by ionisation of hydrogen gas [73]. The protons are then led to the centre of the
cyclotron, and accelerated towards the target. To guide the protons, a deflection plate with a nega-
tive charge is placed near a window to the bombardment chamber connected to the target. In the
chamber, the the protons hit the "target" material (gas, liquid, or solid) to create radionuclides,
and when completed, the radionuclides are sent to a hot cell where the synthesis of the radiophar-
maceutical can commence. A cyclotron can be fitted with multiple targets to generate different
types of radionuclides. At Herlev Hospital, Dept. of Nuclear Medicine, the cyclotron is fitted with
targets for production of 18F and 11C, which are led up to the hot cells from the cyclotron (located
in a bunker in the basement) via 35 m stain-less steel pipes (only for [11C]). Additionally, Herlev
Hospital (Dept. of Nuclear Medicine) uses a different route of production of protons. In the centre
of the cyclotron, a hydrogen gas is introduced and split into hydride ions (H-) which are acceler-
ated in a similar fashion as described in Figure 11. Though, instead of a deflection plate, a stripper
is used to strip two e- from H- to create H+ and are subsequently send to the target [74].

For a radiopharmaceutical to be approved and released for clinical use, the manufacturing of the
radiopharmaceutical needs to be in compliance with GMP regulations as well as undergo quality
control prior to human use [87]. Quality control of pharmaceuticals are of great importance as
some pharmaceuticals have a chiral centre, one of which can cause severe side-effects. A great

such side effect [90]. Today, the R-enantiomer of Thalidomide is used for treatment of patients
diagnosed with multiple myeloma (cancer in plasma cells) and severe leprosy (inflammatory con-
dition in fat cells under the skin) [91].

separated into its constituent analytes [93]. The separation is accomplished by distribution be-

2.4.3
Permitted daily dose
Permitted daily dose, or permitted daily exposure, (PDE) is a tool to determine the maximum of
residual active substances the human body can be exposed daily to without causing adverse effects
[104]. PDE is derived preferably from the no-observed-effect level (NOEL) in most relevant animal
studies, and is defined as the highest tested dose without any adverse effect. If NOEL cannot be
obtained, the lowest-observed-effect level (LOEL) may be used instead. By using NOEL, PDE can
be determined by the Gaylor-Kodell method of risk assessment [105]:
PDE =
NOEL ×W
F1 ×F2 ×F3 ×F4 ×F5
(18)
where W is the body weight adjustment factor, and F1, F2, F3, F4, and F5 are modifying factors or
safety factors. The body weight adjustment factor assumes an arbitrary adult human body weight
for either sex of 50 kg. The relatively low weight provides with a safety factor against the standard
weights of 60 or 70 kg that are often used in this type of calculation [106]. Some adult patients
weigh less than 50 kg, which the built-in safety factors used in PDE calculation accommodate for.
30
==PAGE:32==
F1 is a modifying factor that account for extrapolation between species, e.g., mice to humans. The
factor accommodate the comparative ratios between body surface area (Bs) and body mass (M)
for species concerned and human. Bs is calculated as:
Bs = cM0.67
(19)
in which c is a constant often taken to be 10. Thus, F1 can take different values when extrapolating
from various species to human: F1 = 5 for rats, F1 = 12 for mice, F1 = 2 for dogs, F1 = 2.5 for rabbits,
F1 = 3 for monkeys, and F1 = 10 for other species.
F2 is a factor of 10 to accommodate the variability between individuals, and is generally a fac-
tor of 10 for all organic solvents.
F3 is a factor that account for toxicity studies of short-term exposure, and depends on the du-
ration of the study: ½ halflife-time for the considered species F3 = 1 (i.e., 1 year for rodents or
rabbits, 7 years for cats, dogs, and monkeys); reproductive studies (whole period of organogenesis
is covered) F3 = 1; 6-month study in rodents or 3.5-year study in non-rodents F3 = 2; 3-month study
in rodents or 2-year study in non-rodents F3 = 5; studies of shorter duration F3 = 10. In the cases
of studies in-between time points, the higher factor is used, i.e., factor 2 of a 9-month rodent study.
F4 is a factor that can be used in cases of severe toxicity, e.g., non-genotoxic carcinogenicity, neu-
rotoxicity or teratogenicity. In studies of reproductive toxicity, factors of the following values have
been used: fetal toxicity associated with maternal toxicity F4 = 1; fetal toxicity without maternal
toxicity F4 = 5; a teratogenic effect with maternal toxicity F4 = 5; a teratogenic effect without ma-
ternal toxicity F4 = 10.
Lastly, F5 is a factor to be used when the NOEL is not established. When only LOEL is available, F5
= 10 can be used depending on severity of the toxicity.
Thus, by calculating PDE based on modifying factors and body weight adjustment, it is possible to
determine the maximum dose a patient is allowed to receive daily.

Problem and hypothesis
The production of radiopharmaceuticals requires quality control of the drug before clinical use.
As the synthesis of 11C labelled compounds is challenging due to the relative short half-life of 11C
(20.40 min), the quality control of the radiotracer R-[11C]MTO needs to be quick and efficient.
It is hypothesised that it is possible to time-optimise and validate the HPLC methods for quality
control of [11C]MTO synthesised on the new synthesis module TracerMaker.

In this project, the goal is to optimise and validate the HPLC methods for quality control and the

Synthesis of [11C]-metomidate on TracerMaker
After optimisation and validation of the HPLC methods for quality control of [11C]MTO, tests and
further optimisation of the radiosynthesis of [11C]MTO were initiated (see "Experimentals" sec-
tion 6.3). The synthesis of [11C]MTO was carried out by using the TracerMaker Radiochemistry
system, installed in a hot cell. The system was controlled by the software TracerMaker® Software,
in which a text-file (.txt) can be downloaded and initiated. The radionuclide [11C] was produced
in a cyclotron (18 MeV IBA-cyclotron), in the form of [11C]CH4 and was delivered to the Tracer-
Maker module via 35 m long stain-less steel pipes. For the synthesis, a Sep-Pak Plus C18 cartridge
was used to load a solution of 1 mg R-DETO (commercially bought) in 100 µL 2-butanone/TBAOH
(0.19 mg TBAOH dissolved in 5 mL 2-butanone), as well as for purification of R-[11C]MTO with a
solution of 25% ethanol in water for injection (WFI). Additionally, a Sep-Pak Plus Light QMA car-
tridge (Cl- form) was used for supplementary purification of R-[11C]MTO. Afterwards, the product
R-[11C]MTO was eluted into a vial containing 9 mL phosphate-buffered saline (PBS) via 1.5 mL
absolute ethanol (≥99.8%). Finally, the product was transferred to the product vial fitted with the
sterility filter MILLEX-FG SLFG025LS, inserted into a lead container for protection. Pictures of
the TracerMaker can be seen in Figure 51. Additionally, an overview of the total synthesis of R-
[11C]MTO has been illustrated in Figure 50, from the cyclotron to the product vial. A full-sized
image can be seen in appendix section E.

3.3
Permitted daily dose calculation
As a part of the quality control of [11C]MTO, it was necessary to determine PDE for multiple com-
pounds. Previous work had already determined the PDE for DETO and MTO, and subsequently
determined the maximum concentration (Cmax) for both to 0.60 µg/mL. The PDE and Cmax for
MTO and DETO was validated by calculation of PDE. Additionally, in coherence with the com-
position of a standard curve for DMF in GC, PDE and Cmax were calculated for DMF. This was
accomplished by the Gaylor-Kodell method of risk assessment [105]:
PDE =
NOEL ×W
F1 ×F2 ×F3 ×F4 ×F5
(31)
where W is the body weight adjustment factor, and F1, F2, F3, F4, and F5 are modifying factors or
safety factors.
Both MTO and ETO are sedative drugs, ETO being the most potent [118, 119]. As ETO has found
most widespread human use, there exists more toxicological data for this derivative. When ad-
ministered to a patient, both MTO and ETO are quickly metabolised into the inactive metabolite
DETO, which is excreted in the urine [119–121]. The calculation of PDE for MTO was based on the
NOEL (0.73 mg/mg), which was estimated from ED50 (effective dose for 50% of the population)
of loss-of-righting reflexes in rats [118]. As the NOEL was based on rats, the modifying factor F1
= 5 [106] to extrapolate from rats to humans. F2 was set to 10 to account for variability between
individuals, and F3 was set to 10 as the study lasted for a shorter period. As MTO is an sedative
drug, the modifying factor F4 was set to 1, whereas F5 was set to 5 as the NOEL was not determined
but estimated based on the ED50 as well as MTO being a sedative drug. Thus, the PDE for MTO
was calculated to:
PDEMTO = 0.73mg/kg ×50kg
5×10×10×1×5 = 14.6µg/day
(32)
Therefore, the PDE for MTO was set to 14.6 µg/day. Cmax was then calculated based on the total
product volume (Vprod) of 19.5 mL:
Cmax = PDEMTO

= 14.6µg/day

= 0.75µg/day
(33)
Hence, the specification for the final product formulation is Cmax, MTO ≤0.75 µg/day. As DETO is
considered an inactive metabolite, and for ease of the QC-procedure, the specification for DETO
is also set to Cmax, DETO ≤0.75 µg/day in the final product formulation.
For the standard curve for DMF in GC, the concentration limit was calculated, using an equation
from European Pharmacopoeia 10.0 volume I, p. 718:
Concentration (ppm) = 1000×PDE

In European Pharmacopoeia 10.0 volume I, the PDE for DMF was given as 8.8 mg/day. The dose

Conclusion
The optimisation and validation of the HPLC methods for quality control of R-[11C]MTO and ac-
ceptance control of R-DETO were performed and achieved. Based on experiments of different elu-
ent ratios, flow-rates, and isocratic versus gradient method, the final method for the HPLC method
for quality control of [11C]MTO using the C18 column Luna was finalised with the following pa-
rameters: isocratic with 75:25 eluent ratio (TFA (0.05% v/v) in Milli-Q:EtOH), a flow-rate of 1.2
mL/min, run-time of 6 min and at 235 nm. Testing different eluent ratios on the chiral column
CHIROBIOTIC T used for acceptance control of R-DETO, resulted in the final HPLC method of:
isocratic with eluent ratio 75:25 eluent ratio (0.1M sodium acetate buffer in Milli-Q water:MeOH),
a flow-rate of 1.0 mL/min, run-time of 9 min and at 235 nm. Furthermore, it was investigated if
the CHIROBIOTIC T column could be used for quality control of R-[11C]MTO. Though, no separa-
tion of R- and S-MTO was observed and the column could therefore not be used for the specific
purpose. Lastly, the HPLC method for quality control of R-[11C]MTO using the chiral column Lux
was tested at different eluent ratios, and resulted in the final HPLC method with the following pa-
rameters: isocratic with 30:70 eluent ratio (FA (0.1% v/v) in Milli-Q water:MeOH), a flow-rate of 1.5
mL/min, run-time of 8 min and at 235 nm. Additionally, it was tested if the Lux column could be
used for separation of R- and S- DETO, in effort to obtain both RCP and EAP with only one column
as well as being utilised for acceptance control of R-DETO. Though, it appeared that DETO eluted
simultaneously with the injection peak and no separation of the enantiomers. As a result, the Lux
column could not be utilised for the mentioned purposes.
The automated radiosynthesis of [11C]MTO on the TracerMaker module yielded 0.3-0.9 GBq, but
via the quality control after synthesis it was observed that there were impurities in the product
(UV- and radioactive). The measured yield was therefore the total radioactivity of impurities and
[11C]MTO. From calculations based on data from the radio-detector, it was possible to estimate
a yield of 0.1-0.3 GBq of [11C]MTO at EOS. In effort to minimise the amount of impurities in the
product, there was added ascorbic acid as a radiolytic stabiliser and DMF as dissolving solvent
for R-DETO. Neither adjustments reduced the amount of impurities. It was also discovered, that
the original and approved sterility filter for the production of [11C]MTO retained radioactivity (0.6
GBq) due to interaction between the membrane and ethanol. As a result, another sterility fil-
ter was tested and retained minuscule radioactivity (0.03GBq). Furthermore, the quality control
HPLC methods had issues quantifying MTO as the concentrations were too low to detect via the
UV-detector. Nevertheless, the quality control of [11C]MTO appeared to work otherwise, as the GC
measured values of 2-butanone, ethanol, and DMF well below the acceptance criteria, TLC showed
consistently that the concentration of TBA-salt was below the limit, and the pH for all except the
sample with ascorbic acid, was measured to be within the acceptance criterion.

Future work
So far it has been shown that using the HPLC methods for QC of [11C]MTO using the Luna and
Lux column, it was possible to identify both DETO and MTO as well as R- and S-MTO, respectively.
However, it would be more time-optimised if only one column was necessary for determination
of both RCP and EAP of the synthesised [11C]MTO. This would require a column that is able to
separate DETO and R-/S-MTO. Additionally, if the column could separate R- and S-DETO along
with R- and S-MTO, it would reduce the amount of columns used for the production of MTO pro-
duction as there is currently used three different columns; CHIROBIOTIC T column (chiral) for
acceptance control of R-DETO, Luna column (C18) for RCP of [11C]MTO, and Lux column (chiral)
for EAP of R-[11C]MTO. Interestingly, Merck have produced an HPLC analysis of Ritalinic Acid and
Ritalin (Methylphenidate) enantiomers using Astec® CHIROBIOTIC® V2 with T2 Guard Column,
as depicted in Figure 58 [122].

Even though different from DETO and MTO, they do share similar structural aspects as they all
have a phenyl group, an acid or ester and a chiral centre. Though, Ritalinic Acid and Ritalin do
have two chiral centres which might make them easier to separate from each other. Nevertheless,
it could be possible that the same system would work for separation of DETO and MTO enan-
tiomers. Though, the financial aspect might down-prioritise this test as it requires a new column.
The current HPLC methods for [11C]MTO could possibly be further optimised by testing with other
eluent systems of what have been tested with in this project. Furthermore, testing different flow-
rates might also optimise the methods, as seen during the validation and optimisation of the Luna
column.

For the radiosynthesis, the next step would be further investigation of the impurities and low yield
of [11C]MTO. A possible solution for the impurities detected during QC of [11C]MTO could be the
introduction of HPLC purification as a part of the synthesis program, as both the UV- and radioac-

that impurities originating from the 2-butanone/TBAOH solution eluted simultaneously with R-

though the final product formula could just be diluted, the final volume needs to be considered as
too diluted will result in too high volume to be injected in the patient to obtain the radioactivity
needed for imaging.

Eluents for HPLC
For the HPLC methods for quality control of [11C]MTO and acceptance control of R-DETO, there
are used three different columns and three different eluent A. For the HPLC method for quality
control of [11C]MTO using the Luna column, eluent A is a trifluoroacetic acid (TFA) 0.05% v/v in

Lux column, eluent A is a formic acid (FA) 0.1% in Milli-Q water mixture. Lastly, for the HPLC
method for acceptance control of R-DETO using the CHIROBIOTIC T column, eluent A is 0.1M

Trifluoroacetic acid (0.05% v/v) in Milli-Q water
The HPLC-eluent TFA in Milli-Q water is prepared in a 2 L eluent bottle filled with approximately
1.9 L Milli-Q water. A small vial containing 1 mL TFA (commercially bought) is opened and quan-
titatively transferred to the eluent bottle with Milli-Q water. The eluent bottle is then filled with
Milli-Q water to 2 L ± 100 mL, capped and mixed thoroughly. Lastly, the pH is measured via a cal-
ibrated pH-conductivity meter to ensure the pH is 2.3 ± 0.1.
Formic acid (0.1% v/v) in Milli-Q water
To prepare the HPLC-eluent FA (0.1% v/v) in Milli-Q water, a 1 L blue cap bottle is filled with 1000
mL Milli-Q water to which 1000 µL FA is added via auto-pipette. Cap the bottle and mix thor-
oughly. Lastly, the pH is measured by a calibrated pH-conductivity meter to ensure pH ≤2.7. If
the pH is above the level, FA can be added until the pH is below 2.7.
0.1 M sodium acetate buffer
In producing the HPLC-eluent 0.1 M sodium acetate buffer, 7.72 g (± 0.23 g) of sodium acetate is
weighed on an analytical balance and transferred quantitatively to a 1 L blue cap bottle. Milli-Q
water is added to the blue cap bottle until a total volume of 1000 mL. The blue cap bottle is capped
and mixed thoroughly until the salt has fully dissolved. 337 µL of acetic acid (100%) is added to
the sodium acetate solution via an auto-pipette. Cap the bottle again, and mix. Lastly, the pH is
measured via a calibrated pH-conductivity meter to ensure pH of 5.2-7.2.

phate solution and then filled up to the 1000 mL mark with Milli-Q water. The flask is then capped
and mixed again. pH is measured by a calibrated pH-conductivity meter to 6.7 ± 0.4. Lastly, 53 mL

6.3
Automated radiosynthesis
For the automated radiosynthesis of [11C]MTO on the TracerMaker module, a software called
TracerMaker® Software is used to control the synthesis. The software reads text-files (.txt), which
can be altered and loaded into the software. The starting material R-DETO is dissolved in a TBAOH-
solution (either 2-butanone or DMF as solvent), before loading onto the C18 cartridge. The syn-
thesis is inspired by the study performed by Kovac et.al. [11].
TBAOH-solutions
In this project, mixtures with Tetrabutylammonium hydroxide 30-hydrate (TBAOH·H2O) are pre-
pared with either 2-butanone or DMF (anhydrous) (see appendix section I). The 2-butanone and
TBAOH mixture is prepared by weighing 0.18 g of TBAOH·H2O (i.e., 1 equivalent) on the analyt-
ical balance and added quantitatively to a 10 mL vial. The TBAOH·H2O is dissolved in 5 mL 2-
butanone to obtain 1:1 ratio between R-DETO and TBAOH·H2O. The vial is sealed with a septum
cap and mixed. For the mixture with DMF, it is necessary to use an anhydrous DMF. This is either
bought commercially or made by using dried molecular sieves (molsieves). To prepare the anhy-
drous DMF, the molsieves are dried in the oven at 200°C for a day together with the 10 mL vial used
for the DMF. The next day, the dry molsieves are added to the dry 10 mL vial and 5-10 mL of DMF
is added. The vial is then sealed with a septum cap and let dry for a day. In another vial, 0.18 g of
TBAOH·H2O (i.e., 1 equivalent) is added and dissolved with 2.5 mL of the anhydrous DMF. The vial
is then sealed with a septum cap and mixed.
Synthesis on TracerMaker
Before the synthesis, the cyclotron is started and loaded for the specific target (in this case, a 14N
+ H2 target). The following items are collected and brought with into the laboratory in which the
TracerMaker is located: 1 mg R-DETO, TBAOH-solution, syringe and syringe needles, one empty
sealed 20 mL vial, two 20 mL sealed vials with 9 mL PBS, one bottle of WFI, a C18 cartridge, a QMA
cartridge, a sterility filter, lead container for product vial, and three air filters. In the laboratory, the
software TracerMaker® Software is started and the text-file is loaded in the program. The program
is then initiated, and the commands in the program are followed. For loading of the starting mate-
rial onto the C18 cartridge, 50-100 µL TBAOH-solution (i.e., 50 µL if DMF, 100 µL if 2-butanone) is
added to the 1 mg R-DETO vial and dissolved, which is then loaded onto the C18 cartridge. During
the start-up of the program, the bombardment of the target is started to produce the precursor
[11C]CH4. When enough precursor, and when the TracerMaker is ready in the program, the hot
cell to the TracerMaker is closed and the radioactive methane is send up to the module. At the end
of the synthesis, small holes into the hot cell are opened and the product vial in the lead container
is taken out, and placed in a LAF-bench (laminar flow bench). In here, a small sample for QC is
withdrawn. The product vial is measured in the dose calibrator for activity, and the sterility filter
is withdrawn from the product vial and washed before BPT. The BPT is completed by connecting
the sterility filter to an air line, then lowered into a beaker with approximately 100 mL water (only
the needle is submerged) and the pressure is increased. When a fast flow of bubbles is visual in the
water, the membrane of the sterility filter has broken.
6.4
Quality control
For quality control of [11C]MTO, there are used multiple analytical methods. For chemical and
radiochemical purity, an HPLC method using a Phenomenex Luna C18 column is used. For enan-
tiomeric purity, an HPLC method using a Phenomenex Lux (chiral) column is used. As there are
acceptance criteria for content of 2-butanone, ethanol and DMF, a GC method is used, and for
TBAOH content, TLC is used. Lastly, the pH is measured.
76
==PAGE:78==
HPLC
For the chemical and radiochemical purity of [11C]MTO, an validated HPLC method using the
Luna column is utilised. The validated method in this project is with the following parameters:
75:25 of TFA (0.05%):EtOH, 1.2 mL/min, 235 nm, 6 min run, 50 µL injection volume, and at iso-
cratic conditions. In a small vial fitted with a septum cap, 100 µL of the product sample is added
and placed in the tray in the auto-sampler HPLC, and the sample is tested with the mention
method. For the enantiomeric purity of [11C]MTO, an validated method using the Lux column
is utilised. The validated method for the Lux column used in the project is with the following pa-
rameters: 30:70 of FA (0.1%):MeOH, 1.5 mL/min, 235 nm, 9 min run, 20 µL injection volume, and
at isocratic conditions. A sample of 20 µL product is injected manually into the injection loop of
the HPLC, and the sample is tested with the mentioned method.
GC
A sample of 100 µL of the product is added to a small vial with a septum cap, and is added to the
tray in the auto-sampler GC. Depending on the content tested for, the necessary method is initi-
ated.
TLC
For testing of TBAOH content in the product, TLC is utilised. To do so, a glass with iodine and lid
is heated for 1 min. On a TLC plate, two marks is placed with good space between them and 2
cm from the bottom. One mark is annotated as "P" for product, and the second as "R" for refer-
ence. 2 µL of the sample is spotted at the mark "P", and 2 µL of reference sample (containing 130
µg/mL TBAOH) is spotted at the mark "R". Both spots are then spotted with 2 µL hydrogen per-
oxide and heated on low settings to dry. 10 µL of ammonia-methanol is spotted on each spot and
heated again at low settings to dry. When the spots are dry, the plate is inserted into the iodine-
gas-containing glass for 1 min. At the reference sample, a dark sport will form. If no dark spot
is formed at the product sample, the product does not exceed the acceptance criterion of ≤130
µg/mL TBAOH.
pH
For testing of pH of the product sample, 10 µL of the product is withdrawn and dripped onto a pH
strip. It is important that this is conducted inside a fume hood and behind lead bricks, to minimise
the radiation dose. The pH should be measured to be within 6.3-7.1.
