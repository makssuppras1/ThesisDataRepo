Abstract
The purpose of this thesis is to analyse the frequency spectrum of voltage source converters
with regards to factors that contribute towards increasing their harmonic content. The
factors that are to be investigated are the voltage drop of the semiconductors used in
voltage source converters, and the so-called dead-time, needed to prevent current shoot
trough within the equipment. Three converter topologies will be analysed and compared
in this thesis, which are:
• Two-level DC-midpoint converter
• Three-level neutral point clamped converter
• Five-level diode clamped converter
The harmonic investigation showed that the contribution from the semiconductor voltage
drop effectively increased the harmonic content of the converters, where the contribution
increased with added output voltage levels. The harmonic contribution of the dead-time
proved to have the opposite results, that is, the harmonic contribution was lowered with
added output voltage levels. When the contribution from both these factors was analysed, it
was noticed that their harmonic contribution to the load voltage was out of phase with each
other. This showed to have a positive effect on the spectrum because the total harmonic
contribution of the two factors was effectively lower due to the phase difference.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
The basic functionality of a voltage source converter is to convert electrical energy from
one voltage level to another by use of semiconductor devices. A commonly used voltage
source converter topology is the two level DC-midpoint converter. As the name suggests,
the converter has a centre tapped DC supply at its input, and the possibility to generate
two voltage levels at its output

± 1
2VDC

. The resulting output voltage is, therefore,
a square wave, which naturally has a large low order harmonic content. A widely used
way to minimise the low order harmonic content is by using a modulation scheme such as
pulse width modulation (PWM). The effect of the PWM is that the harmonic content of the
output voltage is based around the frequency of the modulation carrier, and can, therefore,
be ﬁltered out more easily since the low order harmonics are minimised. However, there
are factors that have an effect on the low order harmonics that need to be considered, which
is the purpose of this thesis. These factors are:
• The effect of the voltage drops of the semiconductors on the frequency spectrum of
voltage source converters with an inductive load.
• The effect on the frequency spectrum by adding dead-time.
Because semiconductor switches are not ideal components, they do not have instantaneous
switching. It is, therefore, necessary to add a slight delay to the switching to ensure that
switches which are 180◦out of phase with each other do not occupy the ON-position at the
same time. The effect of this delay (usually called dead-time) is dependent on the current
direction and causes the voltage reversal of the output voltage to be delayed by a time
equal to the dead-time. This delay of the voltage reversal only occurs when the current
does not have a path through the anti-parallel diodes. The effect of this alteration from the
predeﬁned switching angles causes unwanted error pulses at the output that has been seen
to contribute to the converters harmonic content[1].

The other factor that is to be analysed from the spectral viewpoint is the semiconductor
voltage drops. When the current runs from the DC side towards the load, the semiconductor
voltage drops are subtracted from the load voltage. However, for an inductive load, the
current zero crossing occurs 90◦after the voltage. This change in current direction causes
the load to release its energy back to the DC side, and as a consequence, the semiconductor
voltage drops are added to the load voltage. The addition and subtraction between the
quarter periods cause unwanted distortion of the load voltage and as such, is likely to
contribute to the harmonic content.

CHAPTER 2
Voltage source converters
topologies
In this chapter, an overview of the functionality of voltage source converters will be
discussed. Since the topologies analysed in this thesis are two-level DC-midpoint, three-
level neutral point clamped (NPC), and ﬁve-level NPC, the discussion in the chapter will
be based on those topologies.
The basic functionality of voltage source converters is to convert electrical energy from
one voltage level to another by use of semiconductor components (switches and diodes).
The switches function in a way that they have two states, which are either ON or OFF and
therefore can be modelled as a resistor with a resistance approaching inﬁnity (OFF-state),
or a resistor with resistance approaching zero (ON-state) [5]. In a non-ideal case, there is
always some ON-state resistance (Hereafter called dynamic resistance) that needs to be
accounted for, and also a time interval when switching between states. This subject will be
covered in later chapters.

Five-level topology
Figure 2.5 shows the arrangement of a ﬁve-level neutral-point-clamped (NPC) inverter.
This conﬁguration falls under the deﬁnition of a multi-level converter and can produce
ﬁve different DC voltages across its load. It is seen straight away that the ﬁve-level
topology is much more complicated than the two and three-level, requiring 22 semi-

Because of the high number of semiconductors, a problem rises with an increase in the
inductances of the converter due to the large amount of wiring, which could increase the
voltage stress on the components [7]. Another drawback would be that the four DC sources
need to be equal in amplitude, which requires a more complicated control equipment [7,
ch.6, p.14]. The main advantage of the ﬁve-level topology is that the voltage across each
semiconductor is minimised due to the series connection of the devices, which offers the
possibility of higher output voltages. Also, the increase in voltage levels has the advantage
that the quality of the output voltage is increased in regards to total harmonic distortion
(THD) [8].

CHAPTER 3
Selective harmonic elimination
modulation
For voltage source converters (and other electrical equipment) there is a level of acceptable
total harmonic distortion (THD) of the equipment. In a two-level converter, the output
is a square wave which contains quite high THD and high amplitudes of low-frequency
harmonics. The harmonic content of the converters can be ﬁltered out with a low pass
ﬁlter, but for harmonic content at lower frequencies, there is a possibility that the ﬁltering
will cause some regulation to the fundamental component[9]. This problem leads to the
primary purpose of modulating the output voltage, which is to minimise the low order
harmonics from the system to make the ﬁltering process easier, and to minimise the effect
on the fundamental.
The modulation technique used in this thesis for calculations, simulations and laboratory
work, is selective harmonic elimination (SHE) modulation, which will be discussed in this
chapter. An introduction to a more widely used modulation technique called pulse width
modulation (PWM) can be found in Appendix A.

CHAPTER 4
Spectral analysis
In this chapter, the spectral behaviour of the two-level converter will be calculated by use of
the Fourier equations shown in Chapter 3. The calculations are performed to see the effect
that the semiconductor voltage drop and dead-time have on the frequency spectrum of the
converter output voltage. The semiconductor voltage drops that are of interest in this thesis
are the threshold voltage and the voltage contribution from the dynamic resistance. For
IGBT switches, the threshold voltage is the gate-source voltage at which collector current
begins to ﬂow, and the dynamic resistance is simply the semiconductor resistance against
current ﬂow through the components. Initially, the frequency spectrum of an unmodulated
output voltage will be calculated, without the semiconductor voltage drop and dead-time
taken into account (ideal case). From there, the contribution of the threshold voltage (VT)
will be accounted for, and then the dynamic resistance (Rd). The effects of the selective
harmonic elimination modulation will be calculated separately from the semiconductor
voltages, both with and without dead-time.

4.2
Semiconductor voltages
The spectral effect that the semiconductor voltages have on the output voltage of voltage
converters is in part based on the converters having an inductive load. With an inductor
connected across a converters output, the current will lag the voltage by 90◦, which means
that the current will ideally change direction after 1
4T. Even though the current changes
direction, the voltage has the same polarity, which means that when the current is ﬂowing
from the DC source (positive current), the load is receiving power and the voltage across
it will be the subtraction of the semiconductor voltages from the DC voltage. When the
current changes direction and becomes negative the inductive load releases its energy and
supplies power to the DC source capacitance, which will effectively create a load voltage
equal to the addition of the semiconductor voltages and 0, 5VDC. This can be explained
by Kirchhoff’s voltage law, which states that the directed voltage sum around a closed
network is zero. Figure 4.2 shows one-half of the two-level converter with the positive
current direction (green arrows) and negative direction (red arrows) shown. Equations 4.5
and 4.6 show the load voltage according to Kirchhoff’s law for the positive and negative
current direction respectively.

Since the threshold voltage adds to or subtracts from the load voltage, the current can
no longer be deﬁned as a triangle function. The reason for this can be explained by the
fact that the slope of the current is dependent on the ratio of the load voltage and load
inductance. When VT adds to the load voltage, the current rises faster because of the
increase in voltage. When VT is subtracted the opposite happens, that is the current will
have a lower rate of change. Because of this, it can no longer be assumed that the current
zero crossing occurs 90◦later than the voltage zero crossing.

4.2.2
Dynamic resistance and threshold voltage, spectral inﬂuences
At this point, the frequency spectrum of the two-level converter has been calculated with
the effect the threshold voltage VT taken into account. Now the task is to also include the
effect of the dynamic resistance Rd of the semiconductors. Since the voltage generated by
the dynamic resistance is dependent on the instantaneous current, it will not be constant
over time like the threshold voltage. Assuming the current is still linear in the four quarter
cycles of the period, the voltage VRd will be a straight line function with different slopes
based on whether the semiconductor voltages are added on or subtracted from the load
voltage.
Because the voltage function is dependent on the current, there is a need to use an iterative
process to calculate an exact voltage function, based on the fact that the slope of the current
changes when the inﬂuence from the dynamic resistance Rd is included in the calculations.
Therefore an initial value of the current has to be used and then recalculated until the
voltage level converges with an acceptable deviation.

4.3.1
Dead-time inﬂuences
Now the inﬂuence of adding a dead-time to the modulated output voltage will be examined.
As previously explained, the need for dead-time rises from the fact that the switches used
are not ideal devices and therefore the turn on and turn off times are not identical [6].
Because of that fact, the switches that are 180◦out of phase could occupy the ON position
at the same time and cause bridge shoot through. By delaying the time for which the
switches turn on, this problem can be avoided, but by doing so, the load voltage reversal
does not occur at the angles calculated for the harmonic elimination. The exact effect that
the dead-time has on the output voltage can be explained as follows:
Let’s assume that the current in the inverter leg shown below is positive (green arrow) and
sw1 switches off, and sw2 switches on after the dead-time. During the dead-time, both the
switches are in the off state, which is of no concern because the current can run through
freewheeling diode D2 to create a negative output voltage. When sw2 switches off, sw1 is
not switched on until after the dead-time. This means that during the dead-time the current
still runs in the freewheeling diode D2 and a negative voltage is still being generated at the
output. It isn’t until the dead-time expires that the current can run through sw1 to create
a positive output voltage. This means that the voltage reversal from negative to positive
gets delayed by the dead-time, which results in the output voltage not reversing at the
calculated modulation angle.

Simulations with component values
To be able to compare the simulation results to the laboratory measurements
performed in Chapter 6, the parameters used for VT and Rd will need to be the same
as given in the datasheets of the components used for the hardware construction. The
datasheet of the IGBT module [2] contains graphs that plot up the collector to emitter
current as a function of the collector to emitter voltage for the IGBT, and the forward
current as a function of the forward voltage drop for the freewheeling diode. These graphs
are used to extract the semiconductor parameters VT and Rd based on the RMS current
through each semiconductor component. To establish the VT voltage, a tangent to the RMS
current value is used to ﬁnd the intercept of the VCE axis on the graph. The value for Rd
is then determined by simply taking the inverse of the slope of the tangent line. For the
freewheeling [2] and clamping diodes [3] [4], the same procedure was performed by using
the graphs that show the instantaneous forward current as a function of the forward voltage
drop.

added voltage levels the dead-time harmonic contribution is lowered. This makes sense
when looking at the explanations in Section 4.3.1 about the dead-time inﬂuence on the
fundamental voltage being regarded as error pulses. The amplitude of the voltage error
pulses caused by the dead-time is lower relative to the fundamental with added voltage
levels. The reason for this is that the error pulses always have an amplitude of VDC for all
three topologies, but, the peak to peak voltage levels are different between the topologies.
For the two-level, the peak to peak voltage is equal to VDC, for the three-level equal to
2VDC, and 4VDC for the ﬁve-level.
The semiconductor voltage drop contribution has also changed compared to the three-level
converter, which had a 3rd harmonic amplitude of −27 dB for the VDC = 15 V case. It
is, as previously stated, expected that the voltage drop contributes more to the harmonic
amplitude with added semiconductor components, which is again valid, seeing that the
5 level topology has a 3rd harmonic amplitude of −25 dB. It is also a factor in the
harmonic increase that for the voltage levels of ±VDC, there is a mismatch of how many
semiconductors conduct current between the current directions (see Section 2.3). For a
positive current direction, there are four semiconductor components that conduct current,
but only two when the current is negative. This causes an added symmetry loss of the
output voltage which contributes to the harmonic content.

6.4
Laboratory results
In this section, the results of the laboratory measurements will be presented. The measured
data is the load voltage, DC voltages and load current where the measurements were
performed according to the description given in Chapter 1. The same inductive load value
was used as in the simulations, or a value of 80 mH. Overall, the measurements gave good
results, except for an issue that came up when increasing the DC voltage levels. When
the DC voltage was increased to around ±100 V, the IGBTs started to switch at a very
high frequency (around 1 MHz). It was initially thought that the problem was due to
the gate drivers since they are dependent on the DC voltage levels, but the oscillations
were seen at the comparator inputs of the control circuit. It was tried to solve the issue
by separating the DC supply from the gate signal parts as well as possible and twisting
the cables to minimise the magnetic ﬁeld around the conductors. This did not prove to
work, and no other practical solution was found to solve the issue. It should be noted that
this issue was only present for the two and ﬁve-level topologies and not the three-level.
This is most irregular since all the control boards were designed in a similar way. Because
of this problem, and lack of time, a decision was made to perform the measurements for
VDC = 100 V instead of VDC = 200 V. This means that the simulations will be conducted
again at the new DC voltage level for comparison with the laboratory results. To view
photos of the constructed hardware, the reader is directed to appendix C.
6.4.1
Two-Level converter
For the two-level converter, the measurements were initially performed without the
selective harmonic elimination modulation, in order to compare the harmonic spectrum of
an unmodulated output voltage, to the spectrum of a modulated output voltage.
Figure 6.15 shows the unmodulated load voltage of the two-level converter (The no-load
output voltage measurements can be viewed in Appendix D). As the ﬁgure shows, the
DC voltage is quite stable which was initially not the case when using only the on-board
capacitance of the power board. The result was that the on-board capacitance of 1 mF
was not high enough to absorb the inductive current running from the load which resulted
in DC voltage ﬂuctuations. Because of this, an extra capacitance of 6, 6 mF was added,
which proved to be sufﬁcient to keep the DC voltage levels stable. This added capacitance
was then used for the other two topologies also.

When inspecting the voltage curve in Figure 6.15, it was seen that the voltage has a slight
DC component because the area under the former half period of the cycle is smaller than
the area under the latter half period. Figure 6.16 shows the output voltage when zoomed in
at the falling edge zero crossing. The ﬁgure reveals that the former half period is about

output voltage, It is probable that a certain amount of even harmonics will be present in
the harmonic spectrum of the output voltage. This is conﬁrmed by doing a fast Fourier
transform in Matlab where the results are shown in Figure 6.17.
The reason for a DC component appearing in the load voltage could be a combination of
many factors in relations to how the gate signals are generated. For example, in the
comparator circuit shown in Figure 6.4, there could be a different behaviour at the
comparator output when the voltage goes from low to high, and from high to low. When
the signal goes low at the output of comparator U12, capacitor C54 is discharged through
the internal impedance of the comparator, which happens very fast. However, when
the signal has to go high, the capacitor needs to be charged before the voltage level
reaches its high value of 5 V. This generates a mismatch in the slopes for the rising and
falling edge of the output signal that could cause unwanted behaviour from the gate signals.
A simpler explanation is that there could have been a slight DC offset in the sinusoidal
input signal to the comparators. Since Figure 6.16 shows that the positive part of the load
voltage is smaller in area than the negative part, there might have been a slight negative
DC component present in the sinusoidal signal.

CHAPTER 7
Conclusion
This thesis has presented the following results:
• Fourier calculations of a two-level DC-midpoint converter topology.
• Simulink simulations of a two-level DC-midpoint converter, three-level NPC and
ﬁve-level NPC converters.
• Laboratory measurements of the three voltage source converter topologies.
The Fourier calculations of the two-level converter presented in chapter 4 matched the
simulation results very well, both for the amplitude and phase of the harmonics. This
accuracy was manifested in the THD between calculations and the modelling being
identical to a fraction of a percent for the ﬁrst 30 odd harmonics.
The simulations were performed with a dead-time of 1, 5
µs and with the
semiconductor parameters VT and Rd extracted from the data sheets of the components
used for the construction of the topologies. The simulations showed that the dead-time
and semiconductor voltage drop harmonic contribution were out of phase with each other
for the minimised low order harmonics. This had the effect that the contribution of the
dead-time and semiconductor voltages phased each other out to a certain amount.
After comparing the harmonic contribution between the three topologies, it was noticed
that the semiconductor voltage drop harmonic contribution increased with added output
voltage levels. This result is consistent with the fact that more semiconductor components
are needed to generate more voltage levels at the output. Also for the ﬁve-level converter,
there is not an equal amount of conducting semiconductors between the current direction,
which creates an uneven addition and subtraction of the semiconductor voltage drops and
101
==PAGE:124==
102
CHAPTER 7. CONCLUSION
affects the symmetry of the fundamental.
The results also showed a decrease in the harmonic contribution due to dead-time with
added voltage levels. Even though the dead-time is not directly dependent on the voltage
amplitude, it is dependent on the number of voltage levels. If the delay of switching that the
dead-time causes is thought of as error pulses in the output voltage, the amplitude of those
error pulses decreases with added voltage levels when normalised with the fundamental.
Therefore the amplitude of the harmonic contribution from the dead-time is also decreased.
The gate modulation/control boards were found to generate a DC component of the output
voltage. The DC component gave ground to an increase in even harmonic content of the
output voltage because of the symmetry loss between the half periods. It was established
in section 6.4.1 that the DC component did not affect the amplitude of the odd harmonics,
but it affected the phase.
The comparison of the laboratory measurements and the simulations showed that the odd
harmonic content for the two-level converter with unmodulated gate signals did match
quite well, but by adding the modulated gate signals, the laboratory measurements started
to deviate from the simulations. This deviation is in all likelihoods because of certain
shortcomings in the control boards. There could have been a small DC component in the
sinusoidal comparators input signal, or the level of the DC voltage comparator input could
have been slightly off. Also, the DC component that generated the non-symmetry between
the half periods could have transposed the switching angles slightly.

APPENDIX A
Pulse width modulation
The primary function of a carrier based pulse width modulation is to control the On and
OFF positions of the switches by comparing a sinusoidal reference signal to a carrier
signal. The carrier signal type varies and is often a triangular wave or a sawtooth wave,
both with left and right aligned edges. The frequency of the sinusoidal reference decides
the frequency of the output voltage, and the modulation signal, the switching frequency of
the converter. This comparison of a low-frequency reference to a high-frequency carrier
is termed naturally sampled PWM [5]. A signiﬁcant advantage of using PWM with a
constant carrier signal is how the harmonic content is concentrated around the switching
frequency and multiplies of it [13, p.92]. It is therefore practical in perspective to the
harmonic content to use a carrier at a much higher frequency than the reference. There is,
of course, a draw back by using high carrier frequency because of the increase in switching
losses. But it is possible with modern IGBT (insulated gate bipolar transistor) switches in
high power inverters to work with a carrier frequency of up to 2 kHz, which is 40 times
higher than the reference [7, Ch.7, p.12]. This would make the process of ﬁltering out the
harmonics much more manageable since the harmonics are so much higher in frequency
than the fundamental.
The amplitude of the carrier wave is usually kept at a constant value, but the amplitude of
the sinusoidal reference is changed to control the output voltage level [?]. By adjusting
the reference amplitude and keeping the carrier at a ﬁxed amplitude, the relative ratio
between the two signals is altered. This ratio is called the modulation index and is given
by equation A.1:

%clc; close all;clear all
% The vector "n" contains the harmonics that are to be eliminated
global n; n = [3,5,7,9];
% Initial guess for angle iteration
For 2 level
angle0 = [0.01,0.02,0.03,0.04];
For 3 level
angle0 = [0.1 0.2 0.3 0.4 0.75];
For 5 level
angle0 = [0.01 0.3 0.5 1];
% A function that computes the left-hand side of the
% fourier equations for selective harmonic elimination
SHE = @Angles;
% Fsolve is used to solve n equations with n unknowns
alpha = abs(fsolve(SHE,angle0));
alpha=sort(alpha);
% Function to calculate the DC values for the
% comparators as a function of elimination angles
DC=DcValues(alpha);
% The Fouries coefficient cn is calculated by using the angles alpha,
% where the number of calculated coefficients is set by variable "N"
N=30;
