Abstract
During the last decades, the increased demand for renewable energy has resulted in rapid
growth in the market share of wind energy. To compete in energy markets without
subsidization, wind farm design is required to transition from pure cost minimization to
accounting for the time-varying value of energy.
This thesis investigates the differences between the value metric Cost of Value Energy
(COVE) and the metric Levelized Cost of Energy (LCOE) as objective functions for
wind farm layout optimization. The optimization is performed using the python library
TOPFARM developed by DTU Wind Energy. The wind and price data used is from the
Danish electricity market zone "DK1".
The LCOE objective function is seen to outperform the COVE objective function in terms
of COVE and LCOE. Both objective functions produce similar layouts except for the edge
case, where revenue is only generated from the wind directions between due east and due
south. For this scenario, the two objective functions produce different optimal layouts. It
is suggested that the two objective functions yield similar layouts, as minimizing the ratio
of cost to revenue and the ratio of cost to AEP for the given data set is accomplished by
the same layouts. Both objective functions are seen to prioritize minimizing wakes from
the dominant wind directions.

1. Introduction
In this chapter, the motivation for this study, a review of the relevant literature, a set of
research questions, and the thesis outline are presented.
1.1
Motivation
Throughout the last decades, awareness of climate change has increased gradually. An
essential step in reducing global carbon emissions is transitioning from using fossil fuels to
generating power using renewable energy technologies. In Northern Europe, wind energy
is the Variable Renewable Energy (VRE) technology that is integrated mostly into the
electricity system.
Historically, wind energy has been more expensive than fossil technologies[1, 2], and wind
turbines have required subsidies to make renewable energy projects profitable for the
project owners. This resulted in wind turbine owners receiving most of their revenue from
Feed In Tariff (FIT) support that ensured a fixed price per unit of electricity[3]. This
support scheme is advantageous for energy systems with a low share of wind energy as
it incentivizes maximum generation through a secure revenue stream. When generators
receive a fixed price per unit of electricity, they strive to minimize the Levelized Cost of
Energy (LCOE). LCOE is calculated by dividing the total lifetime cost of the generator by
its expected lifetime electricity generation. The advantage of designing a wind farm with
the lowest possible LCOE is that it ensures the cheapest possible electricity generation
from the wind turbines. Furthermore, it is a simple metric that ignores the relationship
between energy generation and market dynamics, and it can easily be used to compare
different energy projects to each other.
As the market share of wind energy increases, the FIT support schemes tend to be sub-
optimal for the energy system. For systems with a high wind penetration, the merit-order
design of the energy markets results in wind energy generation being negatively correlated
with the electricity price due to its non-dispatchability[4]. I.e., when a high percentage
of the demand is covered by wind energy, the electricity prices decrease significantly and
sometimes even go negative. This is often referred to as the "self-cannibalization effect"[5].
In this system, a fixed price per energy unit and generation maximization results in a net
loss of welfare as generators are incentivized to generate electricity at all times, even when

the electricity cannot be used.
To avoid excess generation, support schemes that penalize electricity generation at nega-
tive prices or the price of zero have been developed. An example of this is the Contract
For Difference (CfD) support scheme[6] which also attempts to mitigate the variation
in revenue due to changing electricity prices over time. As the share of wind energy
increases even further, the availability of support for wind farms decreases due to the lack
of flexibility and dispatchability. This is forcing wind farm developers to move away from
the dependency on support schemes for profitability towards being able to compete on
the free market. This has led to energy economists considering moving away from the
relatively simple LCOE metric and focusing on more complicated metrics that address the
value of energy more accurately to increase the profits of wind energy projects[7].
Designing wind farms according to value metrics should theoretically be more profitable
than designing for the state-of-the-art metric LCOE. To the author’s knowledge, there
is limited research on the difference between designing wind farms according to metrics
that accounts for the time-varying value of energy and LCOE, and these differences are
explored in this thesis.
1.2
Literature Review
This section provides an overview of the current literature on wind farm design, wind farm
optimization, competitiveness metrics for comparing energy projects, and how to increase
the value of energy.
1.2.1
Wind Farm Design
Due to the large fixed cost associated with developing an offshore wind project, offshore
wind turbines are installed in clusters, as some of the expenses, such as wind resource
assessment, and environmental assessment, do not scale with the number of turbines.
These wind turbines are usually installed in a limited area to reduce the costs of cables
and to ease the process of turbine maintenance. The drawback of installing turbines close
to each other is an increased effect of wake losses. The wakes of wind turbines have a
decreased wind speed and an increased amount of turbulence which results in reduced
power generation for turbines affected by wakes. The wake loss of offshore wind farms has
been shown to reduce energy production up to 10%-20%[8].

Historically, the design objective of wind farms has been to maximize the Annual Energy
Production (AEP) or to minimize LCOE. For both design objectives, it is desired to install
the turbines in positions where the wake loss has the least impact on the performance of
the wind farm. The modeling of wakes is very complex as the evolution and recovery of
the wakes are affected by the inflow wind speed, turbulence intensity, wind shear, etc. Fur-
thermore, the wakes from multiple turbines interact, which adds even more complications
[9].
The wakes can be modeled using several different methods, which include analytical
engineering models and Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD). The CFD models are
high-fidelity but use a lot of computing power. The analytical engineering models are the
opposite. These use much lower computing power as they are based on simplifications of
physical parameters. Some parameters vary from site to site and require ad hoc tuning
to fit the specific site conditions, resulting in the engineering models’ accuracy varying
from case to case. Through time engineers have become better at predicting wakes and
designing wind farms which can be visualized by examining the three Horns Rev wind
farms in Figure 1.1.
Figure 1.1: Layouts of the three Horns Rev wind farms. Image from[10].
Horns Rev 1, 2, and 3 were commissioned in 2002, 2009, and 2019 respectively, and it can
be seen that the layout of the wind farms has changed significantly from 2002 to 2019.

During this period, the distance between the turbines has increased a lot. It is not clear
from the figure how much is due to the advances in wake modeling, as the size of the
rotors has grown significantly during this period. For context, the diameter of the rotors
in Horns Rev 3 is more than double the size of the rotors in Horns Rev 1. The shape of

Horns Rev 1 is seen to have a simple diamond-shaped layout that is rotated to minimize
wakes from the west. As the wakes are very sensitive to changes in wind direction, the
power output is also, which causes the electricity generation to be less predictable and
more difficult to integrate into the grid[11]. This is one of the findings that has changed
the design from Horns Rev 1 to Horns Rev 2, which consists of rows of turbines aligned in
slightly different directions.
This is also present in the layout of Horns Rev 3. However, the turbines are not placed in
straight lines but rather in arcs, and the distance between the turbines is not always the
same. It is seen that the layouts over time have changed from simple to more complex
geometry. Since the development of Horns Rev 3, the layouts in the literature have changed
further. With the addition of mathematical optimization to the design process, the layouts
are becoming even more irregular.
1.2.2
Wind Farm Optimization
Mathematical optimization can be applied to several aspects of wind farm design. It can
be used to optimize the number of turbines, the type of turbines, the control strategy, the
layout, and the cabling of the wind farm. The wind farm layout optimization problem
is highly complex, and there have been studies researching wind farm optimization since
the 1990’s[12]. Since then, many other studies have been conducted examining different
design objectives and optimization algorithms.
Wind farm optimization is categorized as Multidisciplinary Design Optimization (MDO),
which considers interactions between multiple components using coupled models while
simultaneously optimizing the design variables in terms of all the components. This leads
to better system performance compared to sequentially optimizing the design variables for
the different components. Ideally, wind farm optimization uses components that include
disciplines such as aerodynamics, structural analysis, control strategy, and economics.
Optimization problems that include many detailed models can be too complex to solve
as an MDO problem, and it might only be viable to use sequential optimization even
4
==PAGE:14==
CHAPTER 1. INTRODUCTION
February 12, 2023
though the solution is sub-optimal. Alternatively, the models can be simplified, which also
compromises the solution[13].
The wind farm optimization problem can be solved as a continuous problem or a discrete
problem. The discrete optimization divides the area available for the wind turbines into
grids with wind turbines in the center. The optimization is binary, and the turbines cannot
be moved in the cell. It is only determined if the turbine should be installed or not. If
the resolution of the grid is increased, there are more turbine positions to choose between,
which in practice, gives the optimization the possibility of moving the turbines to obtain a
better solution. Using a discrete approach gives a smaller solution space which can reduce
the computing time if the grid resolution is low[9]. If the resolution is fine, the quality of
the solution is improved, but this also increases the size of the problem as a huge number
of combinations needs to be considered leading to a high computational cost[14].
If the continuous approach is used, the turbines can be placed anywhere in the wind
farm area. A category of continuous optimizations is the gradient-based approach. The
optimization uses steps based on the calculations of gradients to determine an optimum
iteratively. A drawback of the gradient-based approach is that the optimum is not guaran-
teed to be a global optimum. Furthermore, for gradient-based optimization to be used, the
examined functions should be smooth and continuous, and gradients should be available.
For complex optimization problems, it can be complicated to calculate gradients. The
gradients can be estimated using finite differences, but this is an approximation that comes
with inaccuracies[13].
An alternative to gradient-based optimization is gradient-free optimization. Gradient-
free optimization is often based on heuristic or meta-heuristic algorithms like genetic
algorithms or particle swarm optimization. Gradient-free optimization is generally more
robust than gradient-based optimization, requires less time to set up, and has a higher
chance of reaching a global optimum. The drawback of gradient-free optimization is that
it is significantly slower than gradient-based optimization, and the difference increases as
the dimensionality of the optimization problem increase[13]. Even though the gradient-
free heuristic methods are slower than the gradient-based optimization, they are most
commonly used in wind farm layout optimization studies[9].

The Value of Energy
The value of energy can be examined by using the value factor. If the benchmark price
that is used is the average spot market price, the value of VRE can be compared to the

rest of the energy that is generated in the system. Due to the non-dispatchability of VRE,
the value factor is typically below 1, meaning the value of VRE, on average, is less valuable
than the energy in the grid. For systems with a high share of VRE, the value factor can
drop significantly, and for the US grids "ERCOT" and "SPP", it has been estimated to
be below 0.5[18] between 2019 and 2021. In these grids, the electricity generated from

more VRE to a system does not always decrease the value factor if multiple types of VRE

present such as wind and solar, and only one of the technologies is installed at a large

above 1. This was shown in California[19] from 2013 to 2017, where the share of energy
generated from solar is much higher than the share of energy generated from wind. As
more solar energy was installed, the value factor of wind energy reached 1.02 in 2016.
It has been suggested that the value of wind energy can be increased by introducing low
specific power wind turbines, overplanting turbines, combining technologies, spreading out
turbines over larger areas, entering other energy markets, and developing new support
schemes[3, 20, 21].

2.1.3
Statistical Model
To compute COVE and LCOE, the AEP and the revenue generated from the wind farm
need to be computed. To avoid the complications of trying to predict future wind condi-
tions, electricity demand, and electricity prices and how they are affected by a presumed
increase in VRE generation during the lifetime of the project, a statistical approach is
taken to model future electricity prices.
The hourly wind speeds, wind directions, and electricity prices for 2020 and 2021 are
discretized, and a frequency distribution of all combinations of wind speed, wind direction,
and electricity price is calculated.
The wind speeds are discretized in intervals of 1 m/s between the cut-in wind speed of 4
m/s and the cut-out wind speed of 25 m/s. The wind directions are discretized in intervals
of 4 degrees. Finally, the prices are discretized in intervals of 10 €/MWh ranging from 0
€/MWh to 530 €/MWh, which covers all the prices in the data set within the operating con-
ditions of the turbines. The negative prices are filtered as it is assumed that the wind farm
owner would be able to shut down the operation of the turbines at negative electricity prices.
For wind speeds, wind directions, and electricity prices, the value assigned to the intervals
is the mean value of the two endpoints. I.e., the wind speeds between 4 m/s and 5 m/s are
assigned the value 4.5 m/s. Generally, as the resolution of the discretization is increased,
the probability mass converges toward the probability density. Increasing the resolution of
the wind speed and wind direction discretization, however, has the drawback of increasing
14
==PAGE:24==
CHAPTER 2. METHODOLOGY
February 12, 2023
the computational effort of the optimization, which is driven by the number of AEP
computations. The AEP computations are performed using PyWake, which is described
in Section 2.2.2.
Determining the resolution of the discretization is essentially a trade-off between accuracy
and computational speed, and the resolution is chosen iteratively. This is done by running
a set of simulations at a fine discretization and comparing the objective values to the
objective values of a set of simulations with half the resolution. If the objective value
changes less than a tolerance of 0.5%, the results are still sufficiently accurate, and the
resolution is halved yet again. The tolerance of 0.5% is chosen based on it being used in
convergence studies in the IEC 61400-12-1 standard for wind energy generation systems[30].
The process is repeated until the objective value changes more than 0.5%. The resolution
is chosen as the lowest resolution that still satisfies the tolerance. Only the resolution of
either wind speed, wind direction, or price is changed at a time, i.e., the price resolution is
halved while the resolution of wind speed and wind direction is kept constant.
The frequency distribution from the discretization is used to model the relation between
wind speed, wind direction, and electricity prices used in the AEP and revenue computa-
tions. This assumes that the wind speed, wind direction, and electricity price distribution
of 2020 and 2021 are representative of the 25 years that the simulated wind farms are
assumed to operate. This is unlikely to be accurate. It is, however, convenient for modeling
as predicting future electricity prices is very complex and subject to a large degree of
uncertainty. There are, to the author’s knowledge, no other available models that can
be used to model future hourly electricity prices for the Danish electricity market. The
statistical model is therefore used for this purpose.

Optimization Components
In this section, the components that are used in the optimization problem are presented.

2.3.1
Annual Energy Production
In the optimization, the AEP is computed using the "aep" method from the WindFarm-
Model object in PyWake. The method calculates the total AEP of the wind farm using a
frequency distribution of wind directions and wind speeds. This frequency distribution is
generated using the statistical model described in Section 2.1.3. As the same frequency
distribution is used for all years, the AEP is identical for all years of operation.
The frequency distribution only contains wind speeds between 4 m/s and 25 m/s, which is
the range of wind speeds within the operating conditions of the wind turbines. As the
hours outside the turbines’ operating conditions are excluded in the frequency distribution,
the turbines are effectively being modeled to have zero hours without power generation,
resulting in an overestimated AEP. This results in the model outputting artificially low
values of COVE and LCOE. As this overestimation of AEP is present for both objective
functions, this does not affect the relative differences between the two objective functions.
If the reader wishes a deeper understanding of how the AEP is computed, the source code
can be found in the PyWake documentation [36].

low wind speeds, as wind speeds are negatively correlated with electricity prices. Both
cases provide a business case for lowering the specific power of turbines even though the
total cost of the turbines is increased.

2.4.3
Cost Model
In the optimization, the cost model used is the DTU cost model that is predefined in
TOPFARM. The cost model is used to compute annual costs, which are used for computing
COVE, and LCOE. The model is valid for offshore wind turbines, and the prices are based
on 2017 euros.
The cost model uses the number of turbines, distance from shore, project duration, discount
rate, RPM, rotor diameter, rated power, hub height, water depth, cable cost, and AEP as
inputs. Most of these inputs can be varied between turbines, but for simplicity, all inputs
except for AEP are kept the same for all turbines.

The cost calculations are split up into calculations of Development Expenditures (DEVEX),
Capital Expenditures (CAPEX), Operational Expenditures (OPEX), and Abandonment
Expenditures (ABEX).
Of these expenditures, the CAPEX is modeled in the greatest detail as it contributes the
most to the total project cost. The CAPEX is computed from three main costs. The
material cost of turbines, the material cost of foundations, and the balance of plant costs.
The three main costs are modeled from the cost of many smaller elements, but these are all
scaled with the number of turbines, the rated power, and the rotor diameter. Furthermore,
the turbine cost is scaled with the rated RPM and the hub height, the foundation cost
is scaled with the water depth, and the balance of plant cost is scaled with the distance
from the shore and the cabling cost.
The DEVEX and OPEX are as the CAPEX modeled in smaller elements, but both have
significantly fewer than the CAPEX. Both expenditures’ elements are scaled with the
number of turbines, the rated power, and the distance from the shore. Finally, the ABEX
is calculated purely as a function of the CAPEX, and it is defined as 3% of the CAPEX.
When the annual costs are computed using the cost model, the DEVEX and CAPEX are
used for year zero, the ABEX and the OPEX are used for the last year, and the OPEX is

Constraints
The constraints used in the optimization problem are a boundary constraint and a minimum
spacing constraint. The boundary constraint is defined by the boundary of the site, which
is a square with a side length of 6 km. The area of the farm is chosen iteratively to be
large enough for the turbines to be able to expand during the optimization. It is assumed
that the wind conditions are the same within the site boundary. The minimum spacing
constraints define the minimum allowed spacing between the turbines. The minimum
spacing between the turbines is defined as three rotor diameters.

To avoid getting stuck in the same local minimum during the different optimizations, the
initial positions of the turbines are varied between the optimizations. The initial positions
are randomly generated, and the seed is specified for each optimization to be able to
reconstruct the optimizations if necessary.

The optimization is performed using the "EasySciPyOptimizeDriver" object from OpenM-
DAO in TOPFARM, and it uses the gradient-based Sequential Least Squares Quadratic
Programming (SLSQP) algorithm. For the AEP component, the gradients are computed
using the "autograd" function from PyWake, and for the rest of the components, the
gradients are computed using finite differences.
As it is unlikely that the gradient-based optimization algorithm finds a global optimum,
ten simulations with different initial positions are run for each case study. These ten initial
positions are the same for all sets of optimizations. The tolerance used as the stopping
criterion is 10−3, and this tolerance is chosen iteratively. If the tolerance is lowered even
further, the objective value does not improve or improved very little with a significant
increase in computing time.
The value used as "expected cost" input is 10−6. The expected cost determines the initial
step size of the optimization, and it is chosen iteratively. The choice is based on the value
results in converged layouts for all optimizations. The structure of the COVE optimization

2.6
Assumptions and Limitations
There are many limitations to the use of the model, and it is based on assumptions and
simplifications. These are described in this section.
No environmental assessment has been conducted
To ensure that a wind farm does not negatively impact its surroundings, it is required to
conduct an environmental assessment before construction. This is out of the scope of the
project, and it has not been examined if the construction of a wind farm in the analyzed
area could be approved.
Predefined square boundary of the wind farm
The boundaries for wind farms are not defined before examining the site. They are usually
determined by physical constraints at the site, such as water depth, soil properties, shipping
areas, flora and fauna, etc., which can make some locations within the boundary ineligible
for installing turbines.
Predefined number and type of wind turbines
To optimize the profits of a wind farm project, the number and type of turbines would
ideally be a design variable in the optimization problem. Due to the added complexity and
computational effort of making these design variables in the optimization problem, this
is not attempted. The number and type of turbine are examined later in the sensitivity
analysis
29
==PAGE:39==
CHAPTER 2. METHODOLOGY
February 12, 2023
Wind turbines are generated using interpolation
The wind turbines used in the sensitivity analysis are not thoroughly designed. They are
generated using a function that only outputs rotor diameter, specific power, power curve,
and thrust coefficient curve. It is not known if these turbines have properties that are
comparable to already implemented wind turbines and if the cost model can be used to
accurately predicts their costs.
Simplified cost model
The model used to compute the cost is heavily simplified compared to an economic model
that would be used to evaluate projects for actual construction. Many of the inputs are
simplified and assumed to be identical for all the turbines in the wind farm. This is
quantities such as water depth and the distance to shore. The model uses prices in 2017
euros, and it is based on empirical data, which is unlikely to scale accurately. The cost
model is used to evaluate the differences between the layouts, and the economic results
cannot be compared to real wind farm projects.
Historical data is representative of the future
The analysis in this thesis is based on wind and price data from 2020 and 2021. It is
assumed that the data is representative of the lifetime of the project, which is unlikely
to be accurate as the prices have historically changed over time. It is also assumed that
the seasonal wind and price change are represented in the statistical model. The relation
between wind speed, wind direction, and electricity price from the model cannot be used
for other geographical locations, electricity markets, or periods of time.

Amplified Price Fluctuations
In this study, the data is modified artificially to amplify the high and low prices. This
seeks to increase the correlation between wind direction and electricity price, potentially
increasing the benefit of optimizing for COVE. The wake expansion is kept as k = 0.1,
and a wind direction discretization of 15 degrees is used.
The fluctuations of the prices are changed by multiplying them by a scaling factor. The
prices in the statistical model are changed to:
ρscaled = ρavg + max [−ρavg, α σ(WD,WS)]
(4.1)
Where ρavg is the mean value of the electricity price, σ(WD,WS) is the difference between
the average price and the price for the given wind speed and wind direction, and α is a
scaling factor that is used to amplify the price fluctuations. In the model, a lower limit of
the term α σ(WD,WS) is set to −ρavg, which filters the negative prices.
It is expected that an increase in α will increase the correlation between wind direction
and electricity price. The price that is used for normalizing the revenue is unchanged,
ρavg = 56.67 €/MWh. As only the revenue function is affected by the change, the layouts
for the LCOE optimizations are not changing from an increased scaling factor. Layouts

4.2
Discussion
Generally, the COVE optimizations generate slightly more revenue, have a slightly higher
value factor and have a significantly higher cable cost than the LCOE optimization. This
indicates a small difference between the two objectives. The LCOE optimizations, how-
ever, are seen to outperform the COVE optimizations in terms of both metrics, and no
visual difference between the layouts of the two objective functions is found. The results
indicate that there is no benefit of optimizing for COVE instead of LCOE for the given data.
From the wind rose in Figure 3.1 and the plot showing the electricity price as a function of
wind directions in Figure 4.11, it is seen that the dominant wind directions are correlated
with the lowest electricity prices and that the direction with the least wind is correlated
with the highest electricity prices. Furthermore, it is known that the total revenue gener-
ated from the wind farm is proportional to the electricity price and the AEP.
If the wind direction is more correlated with the total generation than the electricity price,
the two optimization objectives may result in similar layouts as both objectives effectively
try to minimize the ratio of cost to AEP, which is the definition of LCOE. If this is true,
it is not surprising that the LCOE objective function outperforms the COVE objective
function, as both effectively would seek to minimize LCOE. This hypothesis is investigated
in the sensitivity analysis in Section 5.1.1.
The main motivation for optimizing for COVE instead of LCOE is that COVE captures
the time-varying value of the energy in the grid. As the value factor of a grid decreases, the
difference between using COVE and LCOE as objective functions is expected to increase.
From the optimizations, it is seen that the value factor of the energy from the wind farm
is approximately 0.89. This is unexpectedly high as the market share of wind energy in
60
==PAGE:70==
CHAPTER 4. WIND FARM LAYOUT OPTIMIZATION
February 12, 2023
the Danish grid for 2020 and 2021 was around 45% [38]. For comparison, the value factor
of the US grids "SPP" and "ERCOT" was estimated to less than 0.5 between 2019 and
2021, and the wind penetration in these grids was 32% and 22% [18]. The value factor of
0.89 is consistent with analysis from IEA[43], and it is suggested that the high value of
energy from wind in Denmark is contributed to the availability of hydro-storage in Sweden
and Norway.
If the value of wind energy in the Danish grid is decreased, the difference between using
COVE and LCOE as objective functions is expected to increase. This is investigated in
the sensitivity analysis in Section 5.1.2.
4.3
Sub-conclusion
The case studies in this chapter are conducted to answer research questions 1, 2, and 3.
The chapter investigates how wind farm layouts and the value of energy change when wind
farm layouts are optimized for COVE compared to LCOE. Furthermore, it investigates
how changing the statistical model impacts the layouts.
The key findings are:
• Optimizing for COVE results in a higher value factor than optimizing for LCOE. This
does not result in increased performance, as the LCOE optimizations outperform
the COVE optimizations for both metrics.
• Optimizing for COVE and LCOE yields similar optimal layouts. This does not
change when the price variations between adjacent wind directions are decreased
and when the high and low electricity prices are amplified.
• Changing the revenue function to be heavily dependent on the wind direction result
in different optimal layouts for the two objective functions.
• The value of wind energy is estimated to be 0.890, which is higher than initially
expected, as the data set has a wind penetration of approximately 45%.
• Changing the resolution of the discretization of the wind directions affects the results.
Fine resolution results in resolved AEP calculations but has the drawback of the
electricity prices varying significantly between adjacent wind directions due to a lack
of samples in each wind direction bin. The price variations can be decreased by
decreasing the resolution, which results in unresolved AEP computations.

5.2
Sub-conclusion
The sensitivity case studies in this chapter are conducted to answer research question 4.
The chapter investigates how sensitive the COVE and LCOE objective functions are to
changes in key parameters, and how these changes impact the optimized layouts.
The key findings are:
• The COVE and LCOE functions change similarly when the turbine spacing and
alignment are changed. This indicates that optimizing for COVE and LCOE leads
to similar optimal layouts with the given data.
• The value factor is reduced from 0.890 to 0.863 by using data from a period with
higher price volatility. This change is not significant enough to result in different
optimal layouts from the two objective functions.
• Using turbines with a decreased specific power results in a decrease in COVE and
LCOE. Furthermore, the value factor is increased, and the wake losses are similar for
the different turbines. As the specific power decreases, the difference in performance
between the two objective functions is reduced.
• Increasing the number of wind turbines decreases the value factor even if COVE and
LCOE are improved.

6. Conclusions and Further Work
In this chapter, the four research questions are answered, and recommendations for further
work are proposed.
6.1
Conclusions
This thesis aims to investigate how the design of wind farms changes when they are
optimized using the metric COVE and the metric LCOE as objective functions in an
energy system with a high share of wind energy. This is investigated through wind farm
layout optimization case studies and sensitivity case studies that are used to answer the
formulated research questions.
How does the layout of wind farms change when they are optimized for COVE
compared to LCOE?
No significant difference between the layouts optimized for COVE and LCOE is found.
This does not change when the price variations between adjacent wind directions are
decreased and when the high and low electricity prices are amplified. Only for the edge-case
scenario where the revenue function is changed to only generate revenue between due
east and due south, the COVE and LCOE objective functions yield significantly different
layouts.
How does the value of energy generated from wind farms change when the
layouts are optimized for COVE compared to LCOE?
The value of wind energy is seen to be slightly higher for layouts optimized for COVE
compared to layouts optimized for LCOE. The increase in the value of energy does
not result in increased performance, as the LCOE optimizations outperform the COVE
optimizations for both metrics.
How can the dynamics between generation, demand, and electricity price be
modeled, and how does the modeling impact the design of the wind farm?
The dynamics between generation, demand, and electricity price can be modeled using
a statistical model. The model generates a frequency distribution of all combinations
85
==PAGE:95==
CHAPTER 6. CONCLUSIONS AND FURTHER WORK
February 12, 2023
of hourly wind speed, wind direction, and electricity prices from the input data. This
frequency distribution is used to model demand, generation, and electricity prices for all
years of operation for the wind farm. It is found that the resolution of the wind direction
discretization in the model changes the layouts. Fine resolution results in resolved AEP
computations. It also causes the electricity price to vary significantly between two adjacent
wind direction bins due to a lack of samples in each bin. The price variations can be
decreased by decreasing the resolution of the wind direction discretization, which results in
unresolved AEP computations and an overestimated AEP. With the low-resolution wind
direction discretization, the turbine positions are changed significantly less than for the
high-resolution wind direction discretization.
How sensitive are COVE and LCOE as objective functions to changes in key
input parameters, and how do the changes impact the optimized layouts?
The COVE and LCOE functions respond similarly to changes in turbine spacing and
alignment for the given data. This indicates that the COVE and LCOE objective functions
are equally sensitive to changes in the input parameters. This is verified by changing the
cabling cost, the turbine type, and the number of wind turbines. When the turbine type
is changed to turbines with lower specific power, the difference in performance between
the objective functions decreases. For all sensitivity studies, the two objective functions
yield similar optimized layouts.
6.2
Further Work
In this section, the recommendations for further work are presented.
• It is proposed to re-do the analysis with data from a location with a significantly lower
value factor of wind energy. This is expected to increase the benefit of optimizing
for COVE compared to LCOE. It is suggested to use market data from one of the
two US grids, Southwest Power Pool (SPP) or Electric Reliability Council of Texas
(ERCOT), as both have value factors of wind energy that are estimated below 0.5[18].
• The statistical model assumes that the wind and price data used as inputs are
representative of the wind and price data throughout the lifetime of the project. As
this is unlikely to be accurate, it is proposed to investigate alternative methods of
determining the dynamic relation between generation, demand, and electricity prices.
As this is very complex, an alternative could be to incorporate the uncertainty of
how the market conditions change in the statistical model.
86
==PAGE:96==
CHAPTER 6. CONCLUSIONS AND FURTHER WORK
February 12, 2023
• It is suggested to change the statistical model to calculate the probability mass
using a low resolution of wind direction discretization and then refine the resolution
using interpolation. This will result in less varying prices at a fine discretization
that allows for resolved AEP computation. Alternatively, a criterion that requires a
minimum number of hours per wind direction can be implemented as suggested in
the IEC 61400-12-1 standard [30]. If the minimum number of hours is not met, the
standard suggests additional filtering or adding other steps to correct the lack of
convergence in the data.
• The cost model used in the thesis is based on empirical data. As the cost model is
unlikely to scale accurately when the size of the turbines increases, it is suggested to
investigate how the cost model scales and possibly recalibrate it for larger turbines.
• To reduce the complexity of the optimization problem, the turbine positions are
the only design variables used. To add more depth to the optimization model, it is
proposed to investigate if the turbine type or the number of turbines can be added
as design variables.
