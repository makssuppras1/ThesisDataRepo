Abstract
This master thesis presents two numerical models for fast computation of ﬁrst- and
second-order loads and responses on ﬂexible oﬀshore wind turbine structures.
The ﬁrst model deals with a slender, ﬂexible, bottom ﬁxed vertical cylinder in
unidirectional, irregular and focused waves.
The ﬁrst-order loads are computed in
the frequency domain by numerical depth integration of complex Fourier amplitudes
of the velocity potential. The second-order loads are computed though ﬂexible body
extension of a recent model by Bredmose and Pegalajar-Jurado [3], in which quadratic
force transfer functions are diagonalised and eigen decomposed. The ﬂexible cylinder
force displays equally high accuracy as the rigid body force, however at increased
computational cost due to discretisation in the z-direction.
The second numerical model is designed for a ﬂoating semi-submersible structure
consisting of two parallel cylinders ﬁtted with heave plates, joined by a rigid beam
with hinge connection above the water level. The ﬁrst- and second-order loads are
computed in surge, heave and pitch on each cylinder using the above fast method for
the depth integrated surge force and pitch moment. The heave plate loads consist
of Froude-Krylov and added mass force terms and Morison drag term with sea state
dependent coeﬃcients. A linear frequency-domain response model with constant co-
eﬃcients is adapted. Validation against experimental response measurements showed
good performance of the model up to f = 1.6 Hz at which point the slender body
assumption is breached.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
1.1
Context and motivation
This thesis is centred around utilising and expanding the applicability of the recent
model, which presents a fast method of computing 2nd-order wave forcing on a bottom-
ﬁxed monopile using a rigid-body assumption by Henrik Bredmose and Antonio Pegalajar-
Jurado [3]. The model reduces the computational cost of 2nd-order wave load contri-
butions from order N 2 to N log N, where N is the number of discrete frequencies.
For long time series of wave data, this reduction results in a signiﬁcantly lower time
consumption involved.
Ocean engineering and ocean-based renewables play a signiﬁcant role in mitigating
the eﬀects of climate change. The High Level Panel for a Sustainable Ocean Economy
present the potential for ocean based renewables to contribute with 5.4 GtCO2e emis-
sion reductions by 2050 [7]. This is equivalent to 10 % of the annual reductions required
to live up to the Paris Agreement target. Compared to the projected 0.8 GtCO2e by
2030, this will require extensive research and development of reliable and cost-eﬀective
oﬀshore renewable energy solutions.
Wind power is a mature and wide spread form of renewable energy with a global
installed capacity of more than 650 GW in 2019 [6] and further 70 GW installed
yearly at current rates [9]. The majority of existing and planned wind energy projects
consist of onshore wind farms, but with an increasing percentage of oﬀshore wind being
installed. Oﬀshore wind and in particular ﬂoating oﬀshore turbines have shown larger
power outputs than their onshore counterparts[18]. It is an emerging technology, which
remains subject to extensive research and development. There remain some technical
challenges to overcome before full scale commercial oﬀshore ﬂoating wind farms become
a low-risk, cost eﬀective alternative to onshore and nearshore wind farms.
Fast calculation of accurate loads is essential to the feasibility of oﬀshore structure
design processes, as it allows for thorough exploration of the solution space thus pro-
viding good conditions for accurate design of natural frequencies avoiding over design
of safety margins.

1.2
Learning objectives
The aim of this thesis is to develop and use a fast model for calculation of 2nd-order
loads and response on a ﬂexible wind turbine structure on the basis of an existing recent
model for rigid bodies. The following learning objectives will be fulﬁlled through the
project:
• Apply current research in higher order wave loads and numerical methods and
attempt to solve the challenge of high computational cost of loads and response
of ﬂexible structures.
• Develop a numerical model that is applicable on a simple ﬁxed monopile structure
and with some modiﬁcations on a multiple degree of freedom ﬂoating structure.
• Analyse existing data and apply phase separation methods to evaluate the ef-
fect of distinct higher order load contributions on the structural response and
determine natural frequencies of the structure.
1.3
Methodology
The ﬁrst step will be to set up a frequency-domain model solving for loads and re-
sponse of a bottom-ﬁxed monopile in 1st-order random waves and validate against
a time-domain model.
Then, the model will be extended to second order using a
slow double-sum method and fast Fourier transformation respectively (DFT and FFT).
Next, a fast load model will be set up using quadratic transfer functions and eigen-
value decomposition. This will be validated against the slower FFT model. This load
model will then be used in conjunction with available data from previous experiments
to validate the fast model against the results of subjecting oﬀshore structures to higher
order wave loads in wave tank testing facilities. Data analysis will be conducted using
four-phase analysis, which is a method of separating incident wave, load and response
contributions of diﬀerent order, to evaluate and understand the eﬀect of higher order
loads on structures.

CHAPTER 4
Numerical model on a ﬂoating
structure
This chapter presents the numerical model for a scaled test model of a ﬂoating oﬀshore
wind turbine support structure, which has been created as the main scope of this work.
Initially, the equations of motion will be presented for two diﬀerent conﬁgurations
of the model, one where it is ﬁxed such that rigid-body motions are prevented, and
one where it is freely ﬂoating and moored with pretensioned cables. Then, the model
matrices for mass, added mass and stiﬀness are derived using mass and dimension data
for the ﬂoater. Then, the forces on the two cylinders are derived to the 2nd-order.
Three rigid-body degrees of freedom, surge, heave and pitch are considered, and the
1st and 2nd order loads are derived for each cylinder in each degree of freedom. The 2nd
order loads are found using the eigenvalue decomposition method from Bredmose and
Pegalajar-Jurado [3], adapting the quadratic transfer functions to the ﬂoating case in
surge and heave and deriving new QTF for the heave loads. This will enable to use of
equations of motion and load fourier series to be used to compute the response of the
structure using the same approach as for the monopile, only this time with a coupled
multiple-DoF mechanical system. Calibration of damping factors and validation of the
model is done in chapter 5, where physical data is included.

bodies using a ﬂexible centre beam. The system will be solved by describing the loads
on each cylinder C, and subsequently transform them to the global system G, which
is centred in the global point of ﬂotation using transfer matrices that will be derived
in the following subsections. The response is computed in G in the frequency domain
with constant model coeﬃcients and load Fourier series.

In theory the ﬂoater has two ﬂexible resonance conditions related to the bending
of the ﬂexible beams. These two are hereafter denoted bird ﬂy-mode and S-mode and
refer to the cylinders moving in phase like a bird ﬂapping its wings and in opposite
phase thus making an s-shape with the ﬂexible beam.
Two diﬀerent cases, relating to two diﬀerent mechanical models are considered:
1. Fixed - Global rigid body motion is prevented by clamping the ﬂoater using a
rigid beam attached to the centre piece. 2 ﬂexible modes are considered. One for
each cylinder.
2. Floating - 3 global rigid body modes and 1 ﬂexible mode. This is obtained by
mooring the ﬂoater with horizontal springs and measuring response in the global
as well as local coordinate systems.
For each of these conﬁgurations, two diﬀerent arrangements of the ﬂoater geometry
are tested, using two diﬀerent layouts of the ﬂexible centre plates. One, with a 12 mm
plate attached above the top beam and the centre piece, and one with a 12 mm plate
attached both above and below that top beam and centre plates. These two diﬀerent
layups will be referred to as "1·12 mm" and "2·12 mm" or simply the single and double
lay-up. The main diﬀerence between the two lay-ups will be the bending stiﬀness of
the ﬂexible plate, however there will also be a small diﬀerence in cylinder mass mC
and centre of gravity zg.

CHAPTER 5
Data analysis and model validation
This part of the report will concern validation of the 2nd-order mathematical model,
which has been described in the previous chapter. The validation will be done with
physical data from experiments performed at DHI in Hørsholm, DK, in the spring of
2020 as part of the FloatStep research project.
The ﬂoater was tested in both the ﬁxed and ﬂoating conﬁguration in three envi-
ronmental conditions EC, each consisting of a pair of Hs, Tp and γ. Tests were made
in regular waves, irregular sea states and focused wave groups for all three EC and
for four distinct phase translations for the irregular and focused waves, enabling four
phase separation of the response. The response on the ﬂoater was measured for all 6
rigid-body modes in three positions: on top of each cylinder, and on top of the centre
piece. This is seen in ﬁgure B.1 in appendix B. Furthermore, decay tests are conducted
for both the ﬂoating and ﬁxed conﬁguration
The model validation conducted in this work will focus on comparing the model and
experiment response for the ﬂoating conﬁguration, as this has the highest resemblance
to the real life applications of a semi submersible ﬂoater with heave plates used as
a support structure for an oﬀshore wind turbine.
The numerical model loads are
computed using a time series of the same length and discretisation as the experimental
signal, maximum frequency fHighCut = 4Hz, Nmodes = 20 and Nz = 24.
Detailed plans of the testing regime, mechanical drawings of the ﬂoating structure
and setup properties can be found in [23]. The data processing will be aided with
Matlab tools developed by Stefan Emil Steﬀensen for his master thesis [19] .
The coordinate system of the experimental setup is shown in ﬁgure 5.1, where it
is seen that it diﬀers from the model global reference frame in the orientation of the

Pre-prosessing of data
To ﬁlter out signal noise, two types of simple ﬁlters are used. For time signal noise, a
simple ﬀt-ﬁlter is used with a high cut frequency of fHighCut = 4 Hz, which removes
all Fourier components above fHighCut. This can be done as pre-processing of the data,
as no information of value is lost. For noise on the frequency-domain signal, a moving
average ﬁlter is applied on the Fourier amplitudes or the PSD-signal when plotting.
This ﬁlter replaces a spectral value with the average of the n points on either side.
This ﬁlter cannot be used before plotting the spectrum as it must be applied on the
absolute of the frequency domain signal and thus removes phase information.

5.3
Response analysis and damping calibration
The damping matrix obtained in the previous section covers the structural damping
of the resonance modes i.e. the damping that occurs in still water. In waves, the
structure will be subject to further damping which will depend on the sea state and
model geometry. This damping is of a much more complex nature, and understanding
it is a ﬁeld of research in itself. [14], [21] and [10] are all concerned with the damping
of heave plates, but are only concerned with 1 DOF systems, and their results can
therefore not be exploited here as other than a benchmark of magnitude of the heave
damping coeﬃcient.
For the calibration at present, focus is on sea states which produces 2nd-harmonic
driving response for the ﬂexible modes. This type of condition is of particular interest
in the model validation because these will give the clearest view of the performance
of the model in terms of 2nd-order loads and response. The ﬁrst response condition
dealt with is EC3 for the double layup. Subsequently the damping will be calibrated
for EC6 response of the ﬂoater with single layup centre plates and lastly EC11 and
single layup damping will be calibrated.
In the following, the damping ratios will be calibrated using the full measured
response of the test scale structure compared to response computed using the measured
wave as input to the model. The calibration will be done iteratively by adjusting the
eigen-vector base damping coeﬃcients ζn, aiming for δσ < 1% relative deviation of the
model response time series standard deviation. This entails equal area under the power
spectral density PSD curve of the respective signals and thus enables good comparison
of the spectral density. The method doesn’t necessarily yield the best ﬁt in the time
domain as the damping ratio may be too large or small due to inaccurate response
amplitudes in for example the linear or sub harmonic frequency range, thus causing
the model resonance amplitude to vary signiﬁcantly from the measured. In that sense
this method of calibrating ζ is more demanding than, say just ﬁtting the response
amplitudes at the natural frequencies.
The method is further iterated in the sense that the damping is ﬁrst calibrated
using the full measured response in comparison with ξ(1), the response computed from
1st order loads only. This is followed by comparison of the full measured response with
ξ(1) + ξ(2)

response ξ = ξ(1) + ξ(2)

The damping matrix has the largest impact on the response at the natural frequen-
cies of the model, as this is where the mass and stiﬀness coeﬃcients cancel out each
other. The further from the natural frequency the smaller the eﬀect. This means, that
the calibration of damping can be used to manipulate wrong forcing to look right at
the natural frequencies, but will not help disguise model error in other parts of the
spectrum.

5.4
Harmonic Separation Analysis
The four phase separation method, which is described in Section 2.1.3 allows for sep-
aration of the ﬁrst four harmonics of the input wave, load signal or response signal,
provided the same wave sequence is run by the wave maker with a 90°shift in phase.
Since phase shifted data is available for both focused wave groups and irregular sea
states, separation of harmonics is a useful tool in the model validation to determine
how well the model predicts response of the ﬂoater.
The numerical model does not distinct between sum and diﬀerence frequencies,
which are computed by the same force terms in the numerical model. This means that
the numerical 2nd-order inertia response ξ(2)
I
will compare to both the 2nd harmonic
and the low-frequency part of the 4th harmonic experimental signals. Mathematically
the reason is that ξ(2)
I
encompasses both A2Q20 and A2Q22 cos(2θ), where Q20 and Q22
are the 2nd order diﬀerence and sum harmonic wave to response transfer functions
respectively. Similarly, both ξ(1) and ξ(2)
D
fall within the 1st harmonic response, but
due to the term cos θ| cos θ| in the drag force, ξ(2)
D will also induce response in the 3rd
harmonic. Therefore, the phase separation analysis will be conducted using a simple
phase inversion method, sorting the response in odd and even harmonics as shown in
Section 2.1.3, eq. 2.34 and eq. 2.35.
The analysis will start with comparison of even and odd response PSD’s for the
centre of the ﬂoater. Then, the pitch motion at the stern will show the performance
of the response transfer function. Lastly, ﬁgures with full 4 phase separated measured
response are given as comparison with the model response components.
Focus in the analysis will be on the pitch and ﬂexible modes as these are of most
interest. The pitch response is critical in the use of the ﬂoater model as foundation
for a wind turbine. It will be felt by the nacelle as variations in the relative wind
speed, and with a hub height at ∼119 m for the DTU 10 MW reference turbine, even
a small pitch angle will yield a large deﬂection at hub height. The ﬂexible mode is
important for strength reasons. The ﬂexible mode on the ﬂoater in question has been
designed to bridge gaps in the current knowledge on slender semi-submersible wind
turbine foundations. The ﬂexible plates and their diﬀerent natural frequencies allows
a study in the eﬀect of structural stiﬀness on deﬂection and fatigue life of a ﬂoating,
ﬂexible structure.

CHAPTER 6
Discussion and Conclusion
The main goal of this thesis has been to construct a model for fast computation higher
order loads and response on a ﬂexible oﬀshore wind turbine structure. This chapter will
present the main outcomes achieved through the work and provide a brief discussion
of the strengths and weaknesses of the model.
6.1
Main ﬁndings
The thesis work began with a ﬁrst and second-order frequency domain wave load model
for a slender, ﬂexible monopile wind turbine structure. The structure was already fa-
miliar through the course 46211 Oﬀshore Wind energy.
The recent model for fast
calculation of load on a monopile by Bredmose and Pegalajar-Jurado [3] was success-
fully adapted to accommodate the ﬂexible nature of the monopile by including a mode
shape vector in the quadratic load transfer functions. The resulting ﬂexible monopile
model proved to be capable of obtaining the same accuracy as the standard rigid-body
formulation of the fast eigenvalue decomposition model at slightly higher computational
cost. However, the model proves much faster than the reference numerical model of
Sharma and Dean, 1981 [17].
By then expanding the scope of the thesis to include the ﬂexible ﬂoating structure,
the fast second-order load model of [3] was again utilised under new conditions. Two
distinct mechanical models were set up for the ﬂoater: the ﬁrst with two ﬂexible modes
and no rigid body motion, and the second with three rigid-body modes and one ﬂexible
mode. The responses of both were found using a linear frequency domain response
model.
For both models the modal matrices for mass, added mass and stiﬀness were derived
using mechanical drawings and measurements of the ﬂoater geometry conducted during
the FloatStep lab tests in the spring of 2020.
The stiﬀnesses of the ﬂexible hinge
plates were determined through a calibration with the ﬂexible mode measured natural
frequencies and the modal matrices.
The loads on the ﬂoater were derived and computed for each cylinder in surge,
heave and pitch using the fast eigenvalue decomposition method. For surge and pitch,
transfer functions were adapted from the monopile numerical model, whereas the heave
load was derived for the occasion. Quadratic transfer functions were derived for Froude-
Krylov and added mass inertia force terms and a Morrison drag term was computed

directly in the time domain.
Validation of the numerical model for the ﬂoater was conducted through comparison
with experimental results for responses of the ﬂoater in irregular waves. The main
ﬁndings are summarised below:
• The comparison of model natural frequencies with those measured showed a
maximum deviation of 3.2% seen for the pitch natural frequency in the single
lay-up.
• Structural damping was calibrated in the eigen basis and obtained 1% deviation
of the signal standard deviation for near all cases. The only exception being the
heave response to the smallest sea state EC3 for which the standard deviation
was too small even with inﬁnitely small damping.
• A poor ﬁt of response time series was observed for the EC3 and EC6 response.
The response to the EC11 sea state showed a particularly close resemblance to
the measured response signal.
• Through harmonic separation of the measured response a good match between
the experiment and model was shown. The odd harmonic responses match for
all modes and sea states and the even harmonic response ﬁt improves from EC3
to EC6 and again to EC11. The best ﬁt is seen in surge.
• For frequencies above 1.8 Hz, the numeric model over predicts the response due
to violation of the slender body assumption which is key to the Morison force
model.
• Two unknown resonance frequencies were observed in heave at constant f for all
sea states and hinge plate conﬁgurations.
Generally it was found that the response Fourier amplitudes in the numerical model
match those of the experiment, and thus it is concluded that the force and response
models are eﬃcient and eﬀective tools to assess the loads on a ﬂoating oﬀshore wind
turbine.
6.2
Model discussion and future work
Concluding on the ﬁndings of the report, the numerical model is capable of predicting
ﬂoater responses in the ﬂoating conﬁguration. The performance of the model was seen
to increase with increasing Hs and Tp, and the surge response performed best on all
three analysed sea states. This section will brieﬂy discuss strengths and weaknesses of
the model and suggest some possible improvements which could be studied in future
work.
The main and most obvious strength of the numerical model is the computational
speed which is obtained through use of the eigen value decomposition method [3]. First
and second-order loads and responses of the ﬂoater subject to an irregular sea state
with duration of 25 minutes, in 4 degrees of freedom took 20 seconds to compute on an
old laptop. Such computation times allow second-order analysis at very early stages in
the design process of oﬀshore structures. This has the potential to facilitate the design
of smarter and more cost-eﬃcient oﬀshore wind turbine structures as it allows for more
==PAGE:99==
6.2. MODEL DISCUSSION AND FUTURE WORK
87
accurate load calculation and ﬁner safety margins in the initial phases of exploring the
design space. At later design stages, more elaborate numerical models are developed,
which gives a deeper understanding of loads and response but less room for geometry
improvements.
A clear weakness of the model is the violation of the slender body assumption
for frequencies larger than f = 1.1 Hz, which potentially yields inaccurate results
in relation to the ﬂexible mode natural frequencies at 1.6 Hz and 3.74 Hz for the
single and double lay-ups respectively. However, the wave forcing is mainly applied at
much smaller f, within the the frequency range of the slender body assumption, which
furthermore proved to have a small eﬀect up to f = 1.8 Hz. As the high-frequency range
cannot be avoided in the case of natural frequencies at large f, a mitigation strategy
could be the inclusion of an extra frequency dependent damping term to account for
the diﬀraction potential. This can be implemented in the linear response model at no
signiﬁcant computational cost.
The numerical model for the ﬂoater is relatively simple in the sense that it neglects
the eﬀect of radiation and diﬀraction potentials, applies loads in the undeformed sys-
tem and uses absolute rather than relative velocity components in the drag terms thus
ignoring eﬀects of the motions of the structure on itself. For a bottom ﬁxed monopile
with distance to the next cylinder > 100Dc, the interactions between cylinders is non-
existing. But when the distance is only ∼7Dc some nonlinear eﬀects of motion are
bound to arise. In the validation of the second-order load model, an interesting ap-
proach could be to compare model results to similar results by more sophisticated
models i.e. WAMIT software to include radiation and diﬀraction potentials and fre-
quency dependent damping coeﬃcients or a CFD model. Although it is expected that
more sophisticated models would give better results, and it would also be fascinating
to study the improvements of model results relative to increased computational costs.
Through calibration of the damping of the model response, a good ﬁt of power spec-
tral densities was obtained with the experiment results. However, ﬁtting the damping
through comparison of the response can potentially hide errors in the force model by
empirically adjusting the response such that it ﬁts. For a situation such as the present
where only response data is available, it means that the accuracy of the force model
can’t be aﬃrmed with certainty.
Following the observation in Section 5.4 that the heave natural frequency is not
excited in both EC3 and EC6, it is worth investigating further if this error arises from
a heave force model fault or inaccurate assumption. such as the constant added mass
constant Cm,p, or if the excitation is simply due to resonance induce by the high level
of interaction with the three rigid body modes which are not in the model.
Further research into the second-order heave force on a cylinder with a heave plate
attached could consist of validating the heave load against data for the load on a heave
plate, such as the results of [19], which are conducted on a single cylinder in both ﬁxed
and forced oscillations in waves. Heave plates are widely studied in literature both
experimentally ( i.e. [10], [14], [21],) and numerically ([11], [20]), however the research
is centred around forced oscillations of heave plates through application of external
loads, and in the literature study conducted for this thesis it was not possible to ﬁnd
use of an analytically expressed second-order wave load model on a cylinder with a
heave plate.
Further investigation is proposed into the boundary values of the ﬂexible mode
stiﬀness, allowing for a numerical study of the eﬀect of the ﬂexible mode stiﬀness on
