Abstract
A critical objective in our shared fight against climate change is to transform our energy depen-
dence on fossil fuels towards clean, affordable and sustainable energy sources. Wind energy is a
primary candidate for this transition, with virtually unlimited potential. Offshore energy islands
are currently seen as a stepping stone to exploit this wind power and help the sustainable trans-
formation. These hubs are self-suﬀicient and decentralized systems that provide several unique
advantages for generating, storing and distributing energy. The first energy island in the world
is projected to operate in Denmark by 2030. It will be located 100 kilometres from the Danish
coast in the North Sea with an initial capacity of 3 GW. While this development represents an
enormous step towards a sustainable future, it requires the power system operators, research
community and technology developers to embark on a vast endeavour. These small hubs will
be dominated by power-electronics-based resources, demanding new techniques to operate them
reliably and safely.
One of the first North Sea Wind Power Hub models was developed by the PowerLabDK team
using a low inertia topology.
This system consists of five offshore wind farms connected to
an energy island that uses two HVDC lines to transmit the power to the Danish and Dutch
national grids. This independent system relies on a synchronous condenser connected to the
hub, providing inertia and acting as a voltage and frequency reference. The model is accurately
simulated in a Real-Time Digital Simulator (RTDS), with several strategically placed Phasor
Measurement Units (PMUs) that enable dynamic system tracking.
These PMUs constantly
provide accurate instantaneous values that describe the system’s operation, such as voltage and
current phasors, as well as frequencies.
This thesis provides real-time analyses and visualisation tools that enhance operational aware-
ness, helping the operator effectively monitor and control the hubs. Although the developed
solution is directed towards the NSWPH, the implemented techniques apply to any energy
island model. The proposed methods contribute to the new state-of-the-art monitoring and
control systems by (i) providing accurate real-time performance updates, (ii) extensive data sets
evaluations, and (iii) translating data into effective countermeasures.
Through an integrated operation, multiple assessments lead to the proposed solution that boosts
the user’s ability to monitor and supervise an energy hub’s live state. The tool’s strength relies
upon the variety of assessments provided, tackling different resources and phenomena with
different levels of complexity and interaction. Several theory-driven analyses are translated into
real-time methods leveraging PMU measurements. For instance, operational insights into the
dynamic behaviour are computed using established small-signal stability methods. Furthermore,
security assessments yield power-to-X activation routines to ensure N-1 contingency guarantees.
The developed methodology leverages a high-speed, closed-loop communication framework that
acts as a bridge to connect the live system measurements to the control room. The data man-
agement platform reads, organizes and publishes the PMU snapshots while sending controlling
actions to the system on demand. High-performance Python code accesses and handles the data
to provide real-time analytics and trend capabilities.
I
==PAGE:6==
The result is an interactive and intuitive solution consisting of two Graphical User Interfaces that
convert unstructured data streams into valuable analyses and performance metrics. Within a
few milliseconds, the GUIs provide meaningful insights about the hub. It also depicts targeted in-
formation regarding the converters’, HVDC lines and synchronous condenser’s operating points.
The research presents a robust foundation and lead framework for developing real-time, effective
tools that use PMU snapshots to monitor power systems. The methodology is eﬀicient, scalable
and adaptable, combining the flexibility of the Python language with its well-established libraries.
The solution can significantly improve the tracking and governing techniques of the energy
islands, thereby promoting their final development and implementation for a more sustainable
future. The complete implementation is open-sourced to promote future developments of these
platforms with the potential to form the new generation of modern power systems supervision.

Approval
This thesis has been prepared over five months in the Department of Wind and Energy Systems
at the Technical University of Denmark, DTU, in partial fulfilment for the Master of Science.
The research has been conducted from January to July 2023.
This project consists of three integral parts. The first is this formal written report providing
comprehensive information about the entire project. The second and third part are two open-
sourced code repositories that showcase the proposed solutions discussed in the report. Their
hyperlinks are available in EnerGuard: Hub supervisor and EnerGuard: Modes supervisor.

Introduction
Climate change has been a hot topic for the past decades. The footprint of human activity
in the environment has more significant consequences than anticipated.
Future forecasts by
the scientific community warn that if society does not swiftly stop polluting and starts taking
care of our planet, the harm done to the environment could reach a point of no return. Some
scientists even go as far as stating that humans have already surpassed the breaking point [1].
The question now is how severe society will allow the consequences to be.
The public is becoming more aware of the situation. From RIO 1992 to the COP27 held in
Egypt last November, governments, organizations, and individuals meet regularly, intending
to accelerate global climate action [2, 3].
The primary goal of these submits is to enhance
collaboration and coordination between parties and encourage ambition. The outcome of this
last summit was to recognize the emergency, accelerate action, move away from fossil fuels,
mobilize climate finances, and complete the Paris Agreement.
The energy transition is one of the most discussed topics since the start of climate change aware-
ness. Fossil fuels are the prime drivers of global warming due to their abominable greenhouse
gas emissions [1, 4]. At the same time, they are the backbone of the international economy.
All parties want the transition to green energy as swift and smooth as possible. However, no
one wants to collapse the world’s economy while doing it. The urgent transition to clean, green
energy is always at the forefront of these summits as a balancing discussion between phasing
them out and maintaining the economy. What is clear is that the conversion will require a
multi-faceted approach that needs the technology, finances, and lawmaking sectors to step up.
Green energy refers to energy generated from renewable and naturally replenished sources with a
minimal environmental impact. Its’ main advantages to fossil fuels extend beyond the drastically
lower carbon footprint. Renewable energy is unlimited on the planet, increases energy security,
is cheaper to collect, and decentralizes energy generation. However, the technology and methods
used to collect, distribute and operate green energy require swift improvements if the energy
systems need to phase out fossil fuels.
There are several challenges to transition to a society that relies upon green energy. The primary
ones are the intermittency of renewable energy and its location.
Unlike fossil fuels, system
operators do not possess the ability to decide when and where they generate. This inconsistency
poses significant diﬀiculties to their integration, as the current technologies and techniques are
designed for operated central power plants. On top of that, the technology for green energy
is less advanced than it would be required, and its costs are still too high for most countries.
Finally, there are several technical challenges regarding its eﬀiciency and durability.

2.1.2
North Sea Wind Power Hub (NSWPH)
This thesis will focus on the NSWPH project. This system is still under active development as the
involved companies investigate and evaluate the most suitable conditions. One of the primary
technical challenges of developing an energy island is deciding the system’s topology. Many
configurations are theoretically feasible, but which ones perform best is unclear. The island could
operate based on many technologies, such as a low-inertia system with grid-following converters,
a zero-inertia system with one or more grid-forming converters without inertia, or even a multi-
terminal DC configuration [7].
Every combination has advantages and disadvantages.
This
work will analyse the low-inertia energy island previously developed by the DTU team with
PowerLabDK’s support.
The system comprises five wind farms, a synchronous condenser, and two HVDC links to the
Netherlands and Denmark. The wind farms are formed by type D wind turbines with 800 MW
of rated capacity. These wind farms are connected to the hub through transformers and cables.
Each WF is located at a different distance from the hub. The 2100 MVA HVDC links connect
the energy island to the onshore grids using Voltage Source Converters (VSC) operating in grid-
following mode. A synchronous condenser on the island provides a stiff voltage source that the
converters can follow and frequency control capabilities.
This converter-dominated power system still relies on the dynamics imposed by a synchronous
machine to operate. Although the synchronous condenser does not provide any energy output,
it acts as a reference to the converters and as a balancing component. This configuration is com-
monly known as a low-inertia power system. Power electronics resources dominate the network
but require a synchronous machine to provide its reference and rotating mass. The benefit of
choosing this topology for the NSWPH is that the technology relies on well-established theory.
Synchronous machine modelling has been studied for decades [8]. Grid-following converters have
also been applied for a long time on applications like HVDC links. The primary concerns with
this approach are the grid-following converters’ interactions developing in fatal behaviours under
specific and unknown circumstances [9, 10], and how to operate and supervise such systems.

2.1.3
Phasor Measurement Units (PMUs)
Developed in the 1980s, PMU technology introduced the first devices that directly measure
electrical phasors, such as current and voltage phasors, at any given bus of an electrical network
[11, 12]. PMUs use a shared time synchronization reference generally provided by GPS. These
devices can sample the waveforms of the electrical phasors at a very high frequency.
This
sampling speed allows them to compute magnitudes and angles of the measured characteristics.
Together with the system’s frequency, these devices pack accurate information about the phasors
and frequency, sending everything to the control centre instantaneously.
PMU technology has a direct and relevant application in power systems due to its accurate
and instantaneous measurements. Power systems are constantly subject to load and generation
imbalances. Hence they provide critical real-time information that helps the operator maintain
a stable and reliable power system.
In AC electric systems, the voltage angle helps determine the direction and magnitude of the
power flows.
PMUs are the first technology that allows operators to obtain precise phasor
information critical for monitoring and visualizing the operation, opening the door to a new
generation of wide area supervision and control platforms.
This technology is slowly being
integrated into the central operators of power systems. However, although the presence of PMUs
in power systems has increased significantly during the past decades, their implementation is
not as widespread as operators would like. Economic reasons have always constrained how many
were installed, as a single PMU can cost up to 70 thousand dollars [13]. Therefore, in literature,
researchers have explored how to optimally place these devices in a power system to get the
most amount of information without installing them in every bus, see [14–18].
Operators have widely used SCADA to monitor and control the power network remotely. This
centralization of measurements enhanced many tools that made the systems more optimized
and stable.
However, as the power systems evolve into a more dynamic behaviour, current
SCADA measurements fall behind due to their latency and lack of precision [19]. Thus, PMUs
are slowly taking over due to their increased speed, accuracy and time-synchronized information
that fast-changing systems require.
Figure 2.5: PMU synchronization on the IEEE 5-bus system.

allows for the definition of PMU devices in their simulations publishing messages with their
information.
The frequency of the messages received from the simulation can be separately
defined. The standard practice in this thesis will be to receive ten snapshots every second.

3.1
Power system stability introduction
Power system stability is the property of a power system to maintain its operation in an equi-
librium setting during normal conditions and after a disturbance. Maintaining this stability
is a critical task for operators which requires continuous analysis and planning.
Instability
can appear differently depending on many system parameters, requiring many assessments and
decisions to be taken in real time [25].
Until the last decades, power systems have been dominated by synchronous machines responsible
for supplying the power demand. Because these generators need to operate in synchronism, the
large focus of research has focused on the machines’ rotor angles dynamics and the relation
between phasors angles and power transmission.
These properties will always be present in
systems where a synchronous machine operates, such as the NSWPH. Although power electronics
adds new phenomena, it does not alter the fundamentals discussed in this section.
When assessing the stability of a power system, the primary issue is ensuring that the network
will react to a transient disturbance of any size safely. The system must absorb the constant
small disturbances like shifts in load profiles and large disturbances such as unexpected generator
malfunctionings or line trips. These disturbances analyses generally involve simplifications of
the system to only focus on the factors that affect its response.

The system’s ability to maintain an equilibrium between generation and load can be classified
into three primary categories: angle stability, voltage stability, and mid-term and long-term
stability. The following sections introduce these concepts based on Chapter 2 of [25].
Rotor angle stability
The ability of synchronous machines to remain in synchronism, i.e. the rotor speed of all devices
is equal and matches the electrical frequency, defines the rotor angle stability. Therefore, the
interest of the problem is in the electromechanical oscillations and how the power outputs of the
synchronous generators change when their rotors swing.
This work will consider that synchronous machines have the armature winding on the stator
and the field winding on the rotor. This is a common practice as the currents induced in the
armature winding are usually higher and require more heat dissipation and maintenance. The
machine term refers to both generators and motors. Its designation ”synchronous” means that
the frequency of the induced voltages and currents in the stator depends on the rotational speed
of the rotor. The electrical power output of a generator is defined by the angular separation
between the rotor field and the stator’s magnetic field. This angle separation is maintained by
the mechanical torque given to the rotor, as the stator and rotor fields oppose the rotor spinning.
Unlike in direct current power transmission, where the power flows from high to low voltage,
alternating current power flows due to angle differences between buses. Therefore, the nonlinear
relationship between power and angle is paramount to understanding the network’s behaviour.
Suppose a simple system with two machines connected by a line is considered. In that case, the
abovementioned relation can be written as (3.1). EG and EM are the internal voltages of the
machines and XT is the sum of reactances present. The angle δ is defined as the angle difference
between the generator and the motor’s internal voltage angles.
P = EGEM

This equation defines the power transmitted from the generator to the motor.
If the angle
difference in degrees is 0 or 180 there is no power transfer. In between, a power transfer charac-
teristic is determined, with its maximum at 90 degrees, seen Figure 3.2. Beyond the maximum
power Pmax, the power decreases with every increase of angle separation, eventually leading to
instability. This nonlinear relation between angles defines the power transfers in all types of AC
systems.
Rotor angle stability is usually divided into two categories, small-signal stability and transient
stability. Small-signal stability refers to the capacity of the power system to remain at a stable
operating point after a small disturbance.
Transient stability refers to the capacity of the
power system to remain in a stable operating point after a grave disturbance. These types of
contingencies are not frequent, and the systems are normally operated considering them.
Small-signal stability can happen because of two factors, insuﬀicient synchronizing torque or
insuﬀicient damping torque. These terms are explained by Equation (3.2), which defines the
electrical torque of a synchronous machine when subject to a perturbation.

If there is insuﬀicient synchronizing torque TS∆δ, the machine’s rotor will start drifting out of
synchronism with a gradual change of rotor angle. This is usually referred to as aperiodic drift
due to the inexistence of oscillations in the process.
In the case of insuﬀicient damping torque TD∆w, the rotor oscillations are amplified, leading to
instability in the system. It is usually described as oscillatory instability due to the continuous
increase of the rotor oscillations.
Transient stability results from a significant disturbance in the system, such as short circuit
faults or generator losses. These disturbances generally cause wide deviations in the operating
conditions. Voltage and frequency are affected, which implies variations in the rotor angles and
speeds. If the system can withstand the fault, an increase in rotor angles will be seen, leading
to damped oscillations to a new operating point. However, if the fault is too severe, the rotor
angle will continue to increase until the system loses synchronism. This can happen in the first
swing or the following ones.
Voltage stability
Voltage stability refers to the ability of a system to sustain a stable voltage profile during normal
conditions and following a disturbance. Voltage instability can lead to poor system performance
and eventually black-out when the voltage drops uncontrollably below acceptable levels. Voltage
stability is closely linked to reactive power. Therefore, the general focus is on improving reactive
power support. However, rotor angles can also be associated with voltage stability as a machine
losses synchronism. In this case, a progressive voltage drop would be seen instead of a rapid
decline [27, 28].
Mid-term and long-term stability
Mid-term and long-term stability refer to the problems that arise in power systems after signif-
icant contingencies that are not modelled by the conventional transient analysis. They appear
as the result of large deviations in the system’s operating point and generally take seconds to
minutes to appear.
Therefore, these problems are slower and longer phenomena that appear after substantial contin-
gencies due to poor equipment and protection responses. Both phenomena can cause significant
problems to the operation of a power system and require effective monitoring and control to
detect and prevent these types of instability [29].

exponential term become zero and therefore are stable modes. Positive eigenvalues make the
exponential increase, which leads to aperiodic instability. It is called aperiodic because real
eigenvalues do not present any oscillations. Complex eigenvalues come in conjugate pairs and
represent oscillatory modes. They show fluctuations, with the real part being the damping and
the imaginary part the oscillation frequency. If the real part is positive, the exponential will
increase to infinity leading to instability. If it is negative, the oscillations will dampen out.

4.1
Stationarity analysis
This section describes the time-series study used to monitor the energy island state. This analysis
yields straightforward and relevant statistics of the data harnessed to determine if the system’s
operation is stationary or transitory. The monitored variables are the phasors and frequencies
the PMU measurements provide.
Stationary data means that the statistical properties are not time-dependent. Therefore, the
data’s mean, variance and autocorrelation throughout time remain constant. Determining this
time series stationarity has been a popular topic in statistics and data mining. There are many
methods focusing on establishing a universal solution. Its applications go from stock forecasts
to health indicators monitoring. However, due to the wide range of data characteristics and
complexity, there is no consensus on the best technique to determine stationarity.
A common approach to determining stationarity is looking for transitory periods, known as
change-point detection. Ranges of consecutive time series data are compared in search of sta-
tistical metrics that change significantly. When the statistical metrics change, the data is in a
transient region. When there is no significant difference between metrics, the data is in a steady
state. Typical approaches are the generalized likelihood ratio estimation (GLR) or the cumu-
lative sum, which compare the probability distributions of two continuous time series ranges
[37]. Other common techniques include computing moving statistical measures like the variance,
mean, or average regression models.

4.2
Trend analysis
This analysis aims to detect slow drifts of variables that are not easily spotted by graphic visu-
alizations. These trends or patterns occur during large time ranges and can be reliably detected
by implementing logic operations that constantly track and compare the desired values with the
previous recordings. A key application of this method involves long-term and mid-term stability.
This analysis will be implemented on two different fronts: frequency and voltage. The choice of

Page 35 of 93

these variables is not arbitrary and obeys the power systems’ behaviour. Frequency reflects the
active power balance in the system, with imbalances translated into fast perturbations. Volt-
age, on the other hand, mainly reflects reactive power on a local scale. Although voltage and
frequency are interconnected variables, they are both more sensitive to different phenomena.
Hence, it is important to consider both magnitudes.
Frequency is a global parameter. All the buses in the NSWPH are synchronized to the syn-
chronous condenser’s rotational speed. Only one frequency measurement of the system is enough
to monitor the system. Voltage, unlike frequency, is a local property. Thus it requires moni-
toring multiple buses evenly distributed along the system to ensure a correct application. The
main thread of the developed application constantly tracks the critical variables’ values to detect
unusual increasing or decreasing slopes. As seen in the stability section, there are critical failures
that take up to several minutes to become fatal. Therefore, constant analysis of the received
data is paramount to avoid final scenarios.
The ramp-up rates of the wind farms determine the time scale of the historical data considered
in this analysis. Increasing or decreasing the generation will directly affect the tracked metrics.
Therefore, the method must consider bigger time scales that capture slower and more consistent
trends. The applied time range will be 120 seconds for both voltage and frequency. This value
is determined by the time a WF would take from no loading to maximum capacity in a single
ramp-up.
4.3
Limits tracking
Establishing variable limits plays a critical role in monitoring any system. The operator knows
that the system operation is secure and reliable as long as the critical variables stand within
certain limits. A breach of any of these limits means the system’s security is not guaranteed.
Surpassing a set boundary might still not be critical for the system. However, it can lead to
cascading effects.
Therefore a warning must be issued immediately to decide if any control
actions are necessary. In such a case, the operator must know the location, seriousness, and
information about the violation.
Critical variables such as frequency, voltage magnitude, voltage angle differences, and current
magnitude will be tracked. The main thread issues a descriptive warning if any of the specified
limits are violated. These restrictions are established based on the studied system evaluation
and the defined base.
4.4
Converter monitoring
The converter’s operational state is posed to be one of the most relevant parameters to track in
modern systems to supervise their operation and ensure their stability. The main reason is that
these systems will have no or almost no inertia, and therefore, the interactions between converters
will determine the status of the hubs [40, 41]. The interrelation between these resources will
develop new unknown phenomena, which could create serious system risks. Furthermore, power
electronics obey significantly faster dynamics, demanding real-time solutions to understand their
state.
The research in power electronics-based systems is currently in an initial state.
The
technology has always relied on synchronous machines that provide a reference for the ”grid
following control”. However, these new setups require a new ”grid forming control” critical to
maintaining the stability and reliability of the system [42].

4.5
Short circuit detection
Short circuits are rare phenomena in power system operations that occasionally happen without
warning. These anomalies are often the result of environmental factors or equipment failures.
To protect power systems against the peak currents of these faults, devices such as fuses and
circuit breakers have been installed all over the systems. These devices’ primary purpose is to
disconnect the fault from the system. Generally, they also provide descriptive signals to the
SCADA when activated. PMUs offer a new and updated way to detect and analyse the results
of a short circuit.
Although they do not provide security features, they can report a more
advanced and continuous overview that helps the control centre swiftly grasp the situation and
make real-time decisions. Faults of this nature re-define the system state instantaneously. The
operating settings swiftly adapt to the new conditions. By using PMUs to supervise the system,
the core operations can monitor real-time values of the pre-event and post-event, yielding a
detailed and accurate diagnosis of how the situation unfolded. Moreover, as PMU data provides
detailed information about the complete operation of the system, it could also be used to define
countermeasures that are automatically dispatched under the operator’s overseeing.
The developed application further exploits the benefits of PMU technology by implementing
short circuit detection features. Power systems instantaneously react to short circuits and line
event trips, directly observed on frequency and power flow time series. The core logic constantly

5.1.2
Real-time data stream incorporation
This first part implements the previously defined small-signal stability method [20] explained in
Section 5.1. The techniques required to achieve a real-time analysis remain unchanged. Nev-
ertheless, there are several considerations to take into account to incorporate a real-time data
stream into the assessment while complying with the execution times required by the interface.
First, all the model calculations must be done at the start of the simulation only to perform
essential operations during the assessment.
This differentiation between model and analysis
operations enables the method to yield results in a matter of a few milliseconds multiple times
every second. Secondly, the PMU measurements must be streamed into the state vector through
translation layers that ensure the correct mapping. Thirdly, the model must store and compute
the minimum information to boost performance. Incorporating time-stamped measurements
into the technique enhances accuracy and usability, as it sources the data directly from the
system. The improved new PMU data feed avoids the computation of the power flow problem
using Newton-Raphson methods, which is the standard practice when using SCADA data to
compute the voltage phasors. Therefore, the developed strategy is more accurate and eﬀicient,
saving computation operations, time and accumulated errors.
This replacement requires the model to completely transition to a PMU data pipeline. A con-
junction of both SCADA and PMU is impossible due to their different sampling frequencies,
which would include measurements from different periods in the state estimation. If the data is
not synchronised, common assumptions such as ignoring the second and higher order terms of
Taylor’s expansion series would not be accurate [43]. It is important to note that this assessment
only has validity if the PMU measurements represent a quasi- or steady-state operation.

data is sourced from a pipeline thread in real time, streaming measurements multiple times a
second. The application first processes the data into the correct mapping and applies the pre-
assessment techniques. Next, the program decides which mathematical model is used based on
the operator input and system logic. The models have been established and pre-loaded in the
initialization routine and are available on demand. The data and the state estimator are then
used to compute the assessment operations and return the findings to the enabling interface.

5.1.5
Model assumptions
This thesis does not aim to present a flawless model. The goal is to provide a framework of
tools and ideas that can be of interest to small-signal stability assessment applications. Hence,
the model is only verified inside this thesis scope and seemingly analysed through its numerical
results. Nevertheless, several potential improvement areas for real-time applications are observed.
The most noticeable arises from the presence of the 17 zero modes. Zero modes generally indicate
that not all the state equations are linearly independent. Hence, there is redundancy in the
mathematical description, as the system is overspecified. The state vector definition requires a
detailed study to determine why there are zeros in the system and then decide if the zeros are
assumed, or the description is changed. In addition, the polar formulation would provide more
descriptive insights. This work will not focus on the state-space definition; the model is assumed
to be correct within the project’s scope.

sum of all the participation factors corresponding to a mode should equal one, i.e. a 100%.,
each factor representing the influence of each state variable on the system’s mode. However,
the calculation of the participation factor is mode-specific. Therefore, the sum of participation
factors does not necessarily need to equal one. Because the used model is not perfect, there
might be participation factors that make sense mathematically, but that do not correspond to
the system’s behaviour.

5.2.1
Security in energy hubs
The current national grid operators and researchers have established many security assessments
and measures to ensure smooth operation. These assessments are currently critical and essential
for supervising the grid’s settings. However, these techniques were not instigated from day one
of the power system’s existence. They are based on decades of operation experience, research,
and, last but not least, critical errors. Power outages have been a fundamental part of learning
and understanding the grids. Blackout detailed studies have been instrumental in developing
tools and methods that monitor and supervise power systems. Although the current methods
are not watertight and extensive disruptions can still occur, such as the 2021 Texas power crisis
[45], they have been reasonably consolidated.
When talking about OEH, many unknowns remain to be discovered. The frontier technology
involved and the iterative refinement history of the power system industry induce deep concerns
among the field experts. The proposed technology poses a paradigm shift in the system dynamics,
with much faster phenomena and no previous experience. This fear motivates the community
to thoroughly research and analyse security threats in power electronics-based systems.
There are several critical challenges in implementing energy islands, four of which are subse-
quently outlined. Firstly, the traditional security techniques consider systems dominated by the
dynamics imposed by the rotor of synchronous machines; however, power electronics dynamics
require faster methods. Secondly, unlike the grids, which generally operate in conjunction with
the neighbouring systems, the hubs are decoupled from the interconnected systems. Thirdly,
due to the small number of resources, a fault in any of them can induce much bigger problems
than in a large system. Finally, and as previously said, there are many unknowns in the power
electronics devices and how they interact.
From the regulatory point of view, energy islands still require established standard policies and
practices. Nevertheless, the research community strongly believes in technology as a stepping
stone to better and improved systems. There is a global commitment to take advantage of the
new characteristics and ensure the hubs have a smooth and reliable integration.

General considerations
As mentioned in Section 2.2, RSCAD is insuﬀicient to develop a platform with the desired
capabilities. Therefore, Python is selected as the natural choice to leverage past implementations
and exploit the best of its performance and libraries.
The language is consistent with the

selected language is amongst the most used programming languages in the world. This success
is due to the development pace attributed to its high-level syntax, simplicity and extensive
library support. Furthermore, it provides high integration with multiple servers, databases and
protocols. Python’s recent progress in parallel computing capabilities increases its convenience
against other similar languages. All the previous advantages come with a significant sacrifice,
speed.
Python is an interpreted language that prioritizes accessibility and cannot compete
with other compiled languages such as C++ or Java. Nevertheless, its developers have made
several upgrades to close the speed gap, although the difference could be significant in real-time
applications.
Graphical User Interface (GUI) is the current state-of-the-art approach for converting raw data
into visual figures. These interfaces visually provide information while allowing users to interact
with the platform. Information is better and quicker understood visually than numerically. The
application exploits this fact to provide simple and direct charts, graphs, and maps with different
features.
To improve the performance of the developed figures, this project will leverage the capabilities of
C++ by interacting with Qt. Qt is a high-performance, open-source framework written in C++
that enables the development of Graphical User Interfaces and other applications. It comprises
a broad range of libraries, tools and Application Programming Interfaces (API) easily adapted
across platforms. Therefore, the developed interfaces can run on Windows, Mac OS, and Linux.
Furthermore, it contains multiple defined interface sets and graphic features, which enhances
the developing task.
The package is available in several versions, although Qt5.15 and Qt6.5 are only currently
supported. These two versions have a similar syntax but require different developing skills. Qt6
was first published in 2020, 8 years later than Qt5. It was conceived as an improvement of
the Qt5 core and to enhance compatibility with new C++ standards. While Qt6 presents a
more optimized framework, Qt5 is still a more widespread tool. There are more support and
development projects in Qt5, and therefore the development of the interface is faster.
The
version decision is generally decided on a case-by-case basis.
This project will leverage both versions. The first GUI tool is developed in Qt5, allowing for
faster learning of the Qt framework and its syntax. Once the first draft has been developed,
the GUI will be migrated to Qt6 to boost performance and styling. The change to Qt6 syntax
also makes the interface more future-proof. Migrating to Qt6 will provide a program in the
most updated version. This framework is available in Python through the PyQt package, which
interacts with underlying Qt C++ functions. As stated in the introduction, the application is
open-sourced to provide a framework and enhance future developments.

6.4.2
Application threads
The main core of the application consists of three classes. These classes depend on each other
to run, but at the same time, they run independently on a different thread. The GUI takes the
main thread. At the same time, the other two classes, responsible for the server communications
and data pipelines, run on complementary threads.
This setup is fundamental to the high-
performance behaviour required. The application would not be possible if it did not leverage
threading capabilities. In a single-threaded GUI, the tasks would constantly overlap and block
each other, resulting in a non-responsive GUI. Nevertheless, the thread numbers must always
be kept to a minimum, as the added complexity in the code and synchronization can generate
important issues. To avoid the synchronization issues in this application, the threads transmit
the data to the GUI through signals and slots, only emitted when certain conditions are fulfilled.
The presented application requires three threads for three primary reasons. Firstly, an interface
can only operate in the main thread. Therefore, there has to be a separate background process
that streams the data into the interface without blocking its operations. Secondly, decoupling
the server from the interface provides an extra layer of control to fetch data asynchronously from
the server when there are multiple collection speeds. In addition, it allows swift change between
servers and feeding from different sources. It also enhances the performance while changing tabs.
Thirdly, it opens the door to the user sending information to the server, avoiding the typical high-
speed two-way communications performance issues. In conclusion, these two additional threads
reduce the computational burden of the main thread to keep a smooth operating interface.
GUI operations centre
The main class running on the main thread is labelled as GUI operations centre. As previously
mentioned, this class is responsible for the performance of the interface. It initializes the other
threads and launches and maintains the GUI operations. During regular functioning, it basically
consists of an event loop, continuously running, waiting for any event to happen in the interface.
An event could be any interaction defined in the program, such as a key press, a mouse click
or application logic events. Once any event happens, the thread dequeues the task, realizes the
corresponding operations and redraws the necessary parts of the GUI. The arrivals of new data
are also added to the event queue to update the information being shown.

initialized, there is an infinite event loop that is constantly running, waiting for any event to
happen. This event can be an interaction of the user with the server, an event triggered by the

application to the operations centre except for the data update. This operation requires the

6.4.4
Parallelization of assessments
Motivated by the previously explained pursuit of parallelization strategies, the interface enables
the user to combine and redefine the information seen in the interface. The decoupled main
window’s conceived architecture theoretically allows the user to link as many interfaces as wanted
to the data pipeline.
However, from a practical perspective, the machine specifications and
the inability to transmit processed data between assessments significantly constrain the real
parallelization capabilities. Therefore, two bypass these two restrictions, this section introduces
the development of a second strategy to parallel compute interfaces.
The idea is straightforward.
The user might need to display simultaneous assessments that
use the same processed data. Processing data is one of the key determinants of the system’s
performance, as each analysis requires thousands of operations. Therefore, instead of processing
the data multiple times, the main application processes and publishes it in a new dataset to the
server. Then, a second GUI in the same or another computer can log in to this new dataset and
update it every time new data is published. This strategy enhances the platform’s performance
in a parallel computing setting as it avoids duplicated computing-intensive operations.
The
communications lag with the server is negligible, almost unnoticeable to the human eye. It is
important to note that the server’s structure is critical for the presented approach. This strategy
is only feasible due to the architecture of the SW-Platform.
Figure 6.11 illustrates the procedure. The main GUI fetches, filters and stores the real-time data.
This information will go through the specific operations determined by the visible layout and
update the interface. Once displayed, instead of discarding the processed data, the information
is translated and published into a new server client, making it available to other subscribers with
access to the same IP address. The publication layers gather the processed data and the user’s
input, filter undesired content, and upload it to the server. The results are directly fetched
and translated into a new GUI that handles the illustration. This approach is at the operator’s
disposal on demand, unleashing a new and faster way to display several assessments concomi-
tantly. There is an infinitesimal communication lag between the main and additional interfaces.
However, when dealing with many operations, the communication lag becomes smaller than
data re-processing.

All the graphics included in the GUI must have a common characteristic, speed. The swift

of plots. This work will use three libraries: PyQtGraph, Matplotlib, and QWebEngineView.
PyQtGraph will be the default library used to do scatter and line plots. Matplotlib will host
complex figures and graphs. Finally, QWebEngineView will display the interactive map. More

when it comes to customization and versatility of figures, Matplotlib is a much more solid choice.
Matplotlib is a much more established and feature-rich library that enables better capabilities
and designs. Moreover, it is the preferred framework for embedding functionalities of external
libraries. This compatibility will be used. Although its developers have published several perfor-
mance improvements, such as the animation class ’FigureCanvasQTAgg’, the complexity of the
potential plots is significantly slower. Therefore, PyQtGraph will be used for straightforward
illustrations that do not require many features, while Matplotlib’s animation backend will be
used to render more complete assessments. Finally, QWebEngineView is combined with HTML,
CSS and JavaScript to display a particular library of interactive maps named Folium.
Heretofore, the use of Graphics Processing Units to render the GUI has remained unaddressed.
These units can render in highly parallel flows to significantly accelerate graphics tasks while
offloading the demand on the CPU. However, the actual benefit from its use severely depends
on the complexity of the application. In the developed solution, the plots do not involve the
rendering of large datasets or 3D animations. Therefore, the speed improvement is generally
negligible in 2D figures. The selected classes combined with performance methods achieve an
already fast enough result. Although this work will neglect its application, it is worth mentioning
that PyQtGraph incorporates OpenGL support, a cross-platform to interact with GPUs. Hence,
if future assessments reach the complexity of 3D animations, they can reuse several parts of the
developed illustration widgets classes.

Rendering strategies
This subsection introduces several strategies that enhance the solution’s performance. They
aim to skip as many unnecessary and redundant operations as possible during the application

operation. Several developed strategies require a deep understanding and intuition of power
concepts and the target system to be supervised and controlled. Otherwise, the platform might
ignore valuable information or miscalculate certain parameters.
Pre-computation operations and static figures
The fewer operations computed in real time, the faster the system will update the data and the
more responsive it will be. Therefore, a thorough effort is made to do as many operations in the
initialization as possible. The same strategy applies to the figures loading. The initialization
defines every layout, which is static during the whole operation. The only things that are being
updated are the points and lines within the fixed layout.
This strategy is applied to every assessment developed. A simple example can be seen in the
02_NSWPH_Digraph file. The init method initializes the figure, parameters, operations and
positions. This way, the update function in line 50 only focuses on the new powers.
Filter unnecessary updates
This section avoids unnecessary updates on the figures that do not explicitly show time-varying
properties. Although these plots change over time, they illustrate interactions and relationships.
An instructive example could be a directional graph. Powers flows do not change directions
every iteration. Therefore, these figures implement a conditional that constantly contrasts the
new information with the previous status to decide whether the plot must be updated. If the
relationships stay constant, the updating part is skipped, and the previous figure is reused.
This information contrast enhances the re-utilization of as many previously rendered figures as
possible. An illustrative application of this strategy can be seen in the 02_NSWPH_Digraph
file. The current powers are fetched from the graph in line 56 and compared to the new powers
in line 58. If the power directions do not change, the digraph is reused until a link switches.
The same strategy is also applied to writing the connection flows and loadings, seen in lines 80
and 99.
Using NumPy arrays
NumPy functions and classes play an important role in swiftly processing the received data from
the server. Its high eﬀiciency in array operations boosts every core and specific operation of the
solution. Refer to [50] for more information about their implementation.
Down-sampling of data with insights
To further improve the platform’s performance, the application reduces the number of redundant
operations. This particular strategy requires a deep and critical understanding of the system
and its operation. The risk of missing information or being inaccurate is high. The NSWPH,
the wind farms have the same ramp-up and -down characteristics. Therefore, it is faster to
assume that the wind farms are performing similarly and leave the individual monitoring only
for critical tasks such as monitoring.
A clear example of this approach can be seen in the 01_Interactive_map file. The function that
computes the power directions only considers WF 1; see line 197. However, the short circuit and
event monitoring of all the wind farms is still tracked, as it is the input of the update method
in line 143.

7.9
Frequency tracking
The frequency tracking plot provides real-time insights into the dynamic behaviour of every
bus. Frequency is a direct consequence of the system’s balance. If generation and transmission
are stable and equal, the system will remain constant at 50 Hz. However, as soon as a slight
imbalance appears, the frequency will deviate from the nominal value. In systems with low-
inertia such as the NSWPH, monitoring the frequency is fundamental to overseeing the operation,
as the spinning reserve is very limited. In stable operations, the system has enough inertia in
the SC to return to the nominal value.
If, at some point, there is not enough, the system
risks collapse. Therefore, frequency is a critical value in assessing the stability, balancing and
performance of the system. Although the frequency will be almost identical across all the buses,
the assessment features a selective visualization. This capability might not be relevant in a
stable operation but can become useful in short circuits and other critical operations.

with high fluidity to always take the maximum screen space. Therefore, the screen divisions are

comparisons. These bar plots show the magnitude of the most significant state variables in a
horizontal setting that allows the operator to compare the different participation values and
extract conclusions. Therefore, this GUI consists of only one window that features a toolbar to

Conclusion and Future Work
This Chapter 9 presents the conclusions of this thesis and discusses the future outlook of the
project based on its contributions.
9.1
Conclusion
The overall purposes of this project can be divided into two main directions. The first one ad-
dressed the development of real-time assessments that provide a complete situational awareness
of an offshore energy island, enhancing the operator’s ability to monitor and control the system.
These assessments yielded the most critical and relevant information effectively. The second
one established an interface solution built to translate information from one or multiple servers
into visual widgets in a few milliseconds multiple times a second. This GUI interactively hosts
several assessments simultaneously or on-demand, leveraging the power of multiple high-level
languages and multi-threading capabilities.
Ensuring a stable and reliable power system operation is an extremely complex task which re-
quires deep knowledge and information about many factors and interrelations. To provide a
swift and effective understanding of the system’s state to the operator, this thesis proposes sev-
eral assessments. Their combination achieves a complete overview of the energy island. These
assessments include a wide variety of graphics, numerical analyses and background operations.
Graphics refer to visualization widgets that display the current settings or evaluations. Nu-
merical analyses concern detailed and realistic mathematical models to compute the system’s
status. Background operations are generally implemented in the core logic and keep constant
track of the variable patterns and trends. These last two generally rely on tables and numerical
indicators, although graphics are also used. Every assessment can be shown unescorted or in
parallel, contingent upon which indicators are targeted.
The proposed assessments are specifically conceived to monitor the performance of an offshore
energy island. This new technology presents a paradigm shift in how electrical energy systems
operate, as power electronics dominate a system with almost zero inertia. The supervision and
control techniques have still not been established, and therefore the proposed solution opens
the path towards this new generation of tools. The system tackles the unknowns of dealing
with a system at the forefront of research with three strategies. These entail different levels
of complexity related to electrical engineering and power system operations concepts. Firstly,
studying and illustrating the fundamentals of power transmission. Although power electronics
introduce new phenomena to the grid, key concepts such as Ohm’s and Kirchhoff’s law or power
factors are still present. Secondly, paying close attention to the operation of the new devices.
Monitoring critical aspects of the converters’ controls and parameters provides insights to help
understand their behaviour. Thirdly, established assessments such as small-signal stability and
security studies are still relevant to understanding and governing the new dynamics.
The second part of this thesis creates a novel and advanced solution that redefines the current
state-of-the-art systems for supervision and control systems. This objective demanded a solution
that displayed the previously developed theory and methods for power systems into a powerful,
interactive, responsive and user-friendly platform.
This aim is achieved by developing two

GUI that embed all the assessments into an easy-to-navigate, adaptable and fast interface that
leverages the benefits of PMU measurements.
One of the key focuses of the solution was to ensure that the translation from raw data to
illustrative plots and tables was almost instantaneous. This speed provides unprecedented value
to the platform. Real-time power system information enhances systems’ security, reliability and
eﬀiciency, as the operator can use the information for near-instant decision-making. Conceiving
the desired speed required a combination of a thorough understanding of the energy island model
and coding structures. The application must compute the least amount of operations possible.
Among the dominant approaches are the differentiation between main and specific logic and
the early initialization of layouts and models. Furthermore, the interface leverages Python and
C++ languages combined with a three-thread structure that converts raw data into elaborate
analyses in a few milliseconds.
The principal challenge of the supervision platform development was to ensure that the speed
requirements were fulfilled while implementing the relevant assessments and information that
conceived the energy hub supervision platform. Every implementation decision was a conundrum
between prioritizing speed or features. Both of these competing factors influence the monitoring
platform value. To maintain the balance that yields the maximum effectiveness, the platform was
first constructed with a basic framework and all the foreseen functionalities implemented. Once
the first draft was implemented, a long process of language, packages, strategies and methods
optimization took place to reduce the initial computing lag. The result is a highly responsive
and customizable interface that features a high-performance rate.
The included power system assessments are based on current state-of-the-art theory and estab-
lished methods. Although these assessments build on top of fundamental power system knowl-
edge, as the research of low-inertia systems advances, new interesting analyses might become
relevant. Therefore, the solution is implemented in a highly maintainable and adaptable manner
that facilitates the integration of new modules or the adaptation of the existing ones. The goal
is to establish a monitoring platform that hosts the supervision tasks of real-time simulations
of future energy island research projects. The system ensures its relevance and functionality by
conceiving a design intended for offshore hubs and using the latest frameworks available. The
framework is open-sourced on GitHub. Finally, it is worth mentioning that despite the bias
towards energy hubs, most of the developed assessments and applications can be extrapolated
to any power system that wants to rely on PMU technology as the supervision and control
platform.
9.2
Future outlook
This thesis opens up several promising research perspectives related to offshore energy islands
and the supervision and control of the systems. The current section highlights four possible
directions.
The proposed solution builds on top of highly effective and fast hardware and software, which
allows for almost immediate access to the time-synchronised PMU snapshots. However, data
sourcing is not the only operation supported. As seen in the developed interface, the solution
also entitles almost instantaneous two-way communication, enabling control actions transmission
back to the server.
This capability provides an immense potential to better control power
systems. Fast counter-measures routines could be automatically implemented to control the

system with the operator’s superintendence.
These automatic control methods are in high
demand, as energy islands incorporate more uncertainty and technologies into the grid. These
applications can take several approaches. Two foreseen applications are management systems
to control power dispatch and shedding, and cybersecurity measures.
The previous control applications and the supervision methods have a huge potential for arti-
ficial intelligence and machine learning methods. These methods are already being deployed
across a wide range of industries, having increased their trustworthiness drastically over the
last few years. Although this work did not consider these algorithms, it is seen as a natural
next step. The tons of data available from the PMU provide a valuable dataset for monitor-
ing and control development.
Tools such as reinforcement learning and decision trees could
be used to manage and optimize the operation based on previously taken decisions. Machine
learning and optimization tools could be used to balance supply and demand with power-to-X
resources. Furthermore, artificial intelligence can also be applied to converter control strategies.
The defined model implements a phase-locked loop to control the power electronics resources.
While this strategy has been state-of-the-art for recent years, there are promising results that
include machine learning algorithms to boost their performance. Their application is generally
divided into three sections: design and optimization, control, and maintenance. The previous
ideas represent a fraction of the AI applications that can be implemented in this area, which is
extremely digitalized and requires fast methods.
GPUs present a bright future for graphics rendering performance. Although the current ap-
plications are only directed towards three-dimension and complex animations, they are slowly
getting towards lower-level applications. With these devices being included in almost all of the
new general-purpose computers and the recent additions of support for this technology, parallel
processing is expected to take a principal role in the next few years. However, the current state-
of-the-art framework presents many compatibility issues and added complexity in exchange for
an almost negligible speed gain. The PyQtGraph backend already includes support for paral-
lel computing. Therefore, several included figures, specifically the line plots, could be easily
adapted to this technology. If future required assessments demand a high compute, the backend
class could be adjusted to leverage GPU-enabled graphs.
This thesis has presented several assessments specifically optimized for offshore hub monitoring.
The main goal of these assessments was to showcase the capabilities of the studied technologies.
Although the thesis employed state-of-the-art theory and framework, the tackled field advances
at a very fast rate.
As the research of the low-inertia systems advances, more insights and
improvements will be developed, which could potentially add more value to the developed ap-
plication. A natural next step in the supervision field would be to improve the assessments and
models incorporated to include the newest developments.
