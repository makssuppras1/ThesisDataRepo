Abstract
This report investigates the bond strength of three di↵erent mortars to investigate their poten-
tial for brick reuse. The investigation methods includes the shear strength test DS/EN:1052-3
and the torsion strength test develop by SBi (K. F. Hansen, 2006). The methods are applied
on small masonry specimens made of the same ﬁred yellow soft-mud bricks, with an initial
rate of water water absorption of 2.1 [kg/(m2·min)]. The selected mortars are a 35/65/500
air-lime/hydraulic lime mortar (KKh) a 35/65/650 air-lime/cement mortar (KC), along with
one 9 % air-lime mortar (K). Specimens were constructed and tested after 28 days and 56 days
and then subject to cleaning.
The torsion test displayed signiﬁcant di↵erences between the torsion strength of the mortars,
ranging from 192 kPa ± 15 kPa for the air-lime mortar, to 847 kPa ± 80 kPa for the air-
lime/cement mortar, after 28 days. The shear strengths were quite similar, with little variation,
where the shear strength for the air-lime/hydraulic lime mortar was 115 kPa ± 15 kPa and the
air-lime/cement mortar 163 kPa ± 10 kPa, after 28 days.
None of the mortars strengths increased during the 56 days of curing.
None of the test methods caused signiﬁcant failures in any of the bricks and it was possible to
clean all specimens by hand, for a quality suitable for reuse. It would however not be possible
to clean the bricks constructed with the two hydraulic mortars with the current automated
machinery at the Danish brick reuse facilities. Further work will be needed to determine the
extend of the application of these test methods and and mortars.

INTRODUCTION
1
Introduction
The interest in reuse and recycling is growing in all parts of society, as both conscious decisions
to help the environment and reduce the impact of our doings and as governmental legislation
making demands of the industry. One of these processes in the construction industry is the
reuse of bricks - a process which have been applied throughout history, as new materials were
costly and physical work was cheap and available in abundance. In these modern days, where
production of new materials is less expensive and easier than the reuse of old ones, the process
of reuse requires both a desire for the reused product, and for the surrounding legislation to
allow for the product to be reused.
Currently a brick which have been torn down, cleaned and prepared for reuse is 2 - 3 times more
expensive than the priciest new bricks on the Danish market. The product have a di↵erent
aesthetic, with rough edges and corners, varying dimensions and colours along with a large
variation for the material properties. The main driving forces behind the reuse of bricks are
therefore environmental interest and public image.
The current source for reused bricks in Denmark are buildings constructed before the 1960s,
where the bricks were mainly tied together with air-lime mortars. These bricks come apart
easily, and the mortar will fall o↵by its own or can be scraped o↵with simple abrasive tools.
Most mortars used since the beginning of the 1960s contain hydraulic binders, which makes the
cleaning of bricks much more diﬃcult, as the mortar often binds itself strongly to the brick,
causing the brick to fail before the mortar fails. The joint itself is to hard to be ground down by
simple operations and requires intense use of power tools to achieve a brick free from mortar.
There are several decades worth of air-lime mortar constructions left to supply the current
demand for reused bricks. This will however not last, as all masonry from the 1960’ until now
are laid with mortars with hydraulic binders, posing a problem for the reuse of these buildings.
Development of methods for cleaning bricks laid with hydraulic mortars are attempted, but
another approach would be to change the mortars applied in the construction of new buildings.
Purpose
The purpose of this project is to investigate the possibility of cleaning and reusing of bricks
laid with mortars with di↵erent hydraulic contents.
This was attempted by construction masonry specimens of three di↵erent mortars, testing their
bond strength and attempts to clean them o↵. The methods available at DTU for investigation
of the strength between brick and mortar are the shear strength test (DS/EN:1052-3) and the
torsion strength test (K. F. Hansen, 2006). These are applied to gain an initial idea of the
process diﬃculties in the bond strength of hydraulic mortars, before an extended investigation
of several mortars in larger scale specimens and longer curing periods.
Scope
This project investigates the shear and torsion strength of specimens constructed from ﬁred
yellow soft-mud bricks from Egernsund Wienerberger A/S and three di↵erent mortars; a 9 %
air-lime mortar, a 35/65/500 air-lime/hydraulic lime mortar and a 35/65/650 air-lime/cement
mortar, all from Vejle Kalk- og Mørtelværk A/S. The strengths are tested after 28 and 56
days of curing, after which removal of the mortar is attempted. The project does not include
investigations of the bond strength of reused bricks or larger scale testings of masonry.

INTRODUCTION
As masonry stands in varying weather conditions for several years before being torn down, the
laboratory investigations of small scale specimens, stored at constant humidity and tempera-
ture, are a large simpliﬁcation of the actual constructions and properties of actual brickwork to
be reused. The project is therefore as concerned with the methods applied as with the results
for the individual mortars. Many variables can a↵ect the bond between mortar and brick, and
it have only been possible to initiate the investigation of a few, such as the water content of
the mortar and the storage of the specimens.
Reservations
As this project is supposed to be the initial work for a further study in mortars adhesion
to bricks and the following cleaning and reuse of these bricks, it tries to aim wide to map
potential paths while simultaneously ﬁnding and possibly assessing some of the pitfalls for
further investigations.
There are therefore several minor deviations from the norms along
with some mistakes, which must be taken into consideration if the results are to be used for
comparison. These are described in their relevant segments of the report and listed in the
discussion.
Background
The process of brick cleaning was investigated before a testing program was planned and samples
were constructed. This involved a visit to Gamle Mursten ApS on Funen, to learn about their
production processes and products. The focus here was to investigate the properties and bond
strength of cleaned bricks and several samples were brought to DTU. In the mean time, it was
investigated whether the Karsten tube method could assist in mortar screening for existing
brick work, as mortar identiﬁcation is a diﬃcult process. The results of this project is placed
in the appendix.
As an attempt to get a wide understanding of the diﬃculties in the application of reused bricks
in new construction work, several architects, masons and mortar companies were contacted,
both as part of this project and in former projects by the same author. These will not be
discussed directly in the report, but they lay the foundation for many of the practical aspects.

Theory
Clay and lime, CaCO3, are signiﬁcant parts of the Danish geology, which in many parts of the
country are available in wast amounts only a few meters below the surface. They have been
signiﬁcant resources throughout the Danish history and shaped much of the Danish construction
industry. Clay bricks, lime mortar and cement are some main construction materials made from
these resources. This section will not discuss the speciﬁc production methods of these materials,
but serve to highlight some aspects of these materials and their production, which are important
for the documentation and discussion in this report.
2.1
Air lime mortar
Air lime is used as a simple binder in most bricklaying mortars in Denmark. CaCO3, in the
form of limestone, is dug up from the ground and heated, to produce quicklime, CaO (C 1).
The Danish lime depositions are very pure and can be mined free of any signiﬁcant impurities
such as clay or sand mangler.

The quicklime is then hydrated (C 2), and mixed with sand to form a mortar. The mixing
order and quantities of water and sand a↵ect the ﬁnal mortar properties.
These mortars
are distinguished by name where the Danish methods and names are discussed in Stenholt-
Jacobsen, 2021. The mortars used in this project were made from hydrated lime, slaked with
excess water and mixed with sand at room temperatures. This is generally referred to simply as
a wet lime mortar. It is generally required for more water to be added in order for the mortar
to have a suﬃcient workability for bricklaying. The hydrated lime will then harden by reacting
with CO2 in the air, forming CaCO3 again (C 3). This is called carbonatation, but is mostly
referred to as carbonation and will be referred to as such for the rest of this report.

For the carbonation to occur, the atmospheric CO2 must be dissolved in water to form carbonic
acid, H2CO3 (C 4), before it reacts with Ca(OH)2. As the di↵usion of H2CO3 in water is slow
comparatively to the di↵usion of CO2 in air, the rate of carbonation depends on the water
content in the mortar. The ideal humidity for carbonation is around 60 % RH, while little to
no carbonation takes place the closer it gets to 0 % or 100 % RH (Elsalamawy et al., 2019).
Even at ideal humidity, carbonation of a lime mortar is a slow process and it can take several
years for the mortar in brickwork to fully carbonate.
2.2
Hydraulic binders
As air lime does not harden in the presence of excess water, some mortars are made with the
addition of hydraulic binders. The most common hydraulic binders in the Danish mortars are
hydraulic lime and cement.

Hydraulic lime is made by burning limestone containing a certain amount of clay, at higher
temperatures than normal lime calcination. If the lime and clay mixture is from a natural
source, it is referred to as natural hydraulic lime (NHL), while a fabricated mixture is referred
to as hydraulic lime (HL) or formulated lime (FL) (DS/EN:459-1).
The main hydraulic binder in a natural hydraulic lime is the clinker mineral dicalcium silicate,
2 CaO · SiO2 or C2S (in cement chemist notation). A natural hydraulic lime, with a compressive
strength of minimum 5 MPa (NHL5), can contain approximately 40 %-md 2 CaO · SiO2, 20 %
CaCO3 and 20 % Ca(OH)2 Nordisk NHL, 2022. Used as a mortar, the strength development
for a mortar mixed from NHL and sand, will both come from the carbonation of Ca(OH)2 and
the hydration of 2 CaO · SiO2. The strength development of di↵erent cemet clinkers can be
seen on ﬁgure 3, where 2 CaO · SiO2 displays a slow strength development comparatively to the
other common cement clinker 3 CaO · SiO2.

2.3.1
Capillarity
A ﬁred clay brick is a porous material with an ap-
proximate porosity between 30 and 50 % depending on
constituents and ﬁring process (Nielsen et al., 1997).
The pores of bricks generally have a suﬃcient thinness
to generate a capillary suction. This is important for
the bricklaying process, as the initial bond strength be-
tween mortar and brick is caused by the capillary suc-
tion of water from the mortar into the pores of the brick.
This bond is then strengthened through the hardening
of the air-lime or hydraulic binders. The workability of
a mortar must therefore comply with the suction of the
bricks and the environmental conditions regarding tem-
peratures and humidity, to ensure a good brick laying
process.

If the mortar is too wet in relation to the brick, there will be no initial bond strength between
each course of bricks, because the bricks will get fully saturated before the mortar is dry enough
to sustain any load.
If the mortar is too dry, it will be sucked dry before the next brick is laid, and there will be no
initial bond strength.
2.3.2
Moisture content
The pore-size distribution for a brick ranges from about 10 nm to 1 mm, with the majority
of pores being between 100 nm and 10 µm. Moisture can condense in the pores even at low
humidity, as described by the Kelvin equation (P. F. Hansen, 1995). With few pores below 100
nm, only a small amount of moisture will condense in the pore system at humidities below 100
%. This is shown on the moisture absorption and desorption curves for bricks, displayed on
ﬁgure 4.
2.4
Testing methods
Masonry is always seen as a combination of bricks and mortar, an isolated investigation of one
or the other will therefore always lack a the aspects from the interactions between the two. As
most brick walls in Denmark are half brick walls tied to the load bearing structure. All brick
investigation methods in Denmark are correspondingly based on samples simulating a half brick
wall.
While some methods investigate the interactions of mortars and bricks on larger specimens,
several courses high and a couple of bricks long, most strength parameters used for masonry di-
mensioning are based on smaller samples of 2-3 bricks, testing the bond strength, shear strength
and in some cases ﬂexural strength. The test methods and specimen sizes are displayed in table
2. The Danish Technological Institute are currently the only ones in Denmark who are capable
of investigating the ﬂexural and bond strength following DS/EN:1052-2 and DS/EN:1052-5.

5
Torsion strength
The specimens for the torsion test (K. F. Hansen, 2006) were made by joining two bricks with a
135 mm long mortar joint. The bricks were laid in a system of guide rails, developed speciﬁcally
for these construction of the torsion specimens, ensuring alignment and uniformity for all the
samples. The guide rails were adjusted to ensure a minimum average joint thickness of 12 mm
for the selected bricks. Because of the selected brick-type’s variations in dimensions, the joint
thickness varied from 11 - 18 mm.
5.1
Construction of specimens
The bottom brick was laid out on the guide rails, mortar placed on the exposed surface and
straightened to a uniform shape with a trowel, before the top brick was placed and pressed
down to an even and parallel joint. The bricks were then immediately ﬁxed together with an
F-clamp, whereupon excess mortar was scraped o↵and the joint surfaces struck with a 10 or
12 mm joint trowel, depending on the individual brick shape. The sample was then carried out
of the rail system by the clamp handle and stored according to the mortar. These steps are
displayed on ﬁgure 8.
Striking the joint surfaces served 3 purposes:
• It pushed back some mortar into the joint, after the top brick was placed, which possibly
created a slightly larger contact area for capillary suction, where the mortar cross section
dimensions would be more representable for the actual joint, than a rough scraped joint.
The rough scraped joint is displayed on ﬁgure 17f
• It made the joints more consistent in structure, compaction and dimension
• The smoothed even surface would make the water transport in and out of the joint more
consistent.
The clamping pressure for the specimens were applied as evenly and consistently as possible,
but it would not be possible to measure the actual force applied for each specimen. They were
all constructed and clamped by the same person, where the clamps were attempted tightened
to the same tension level by feel. DS/EN:1052-3 speciﬁes a clamping stress between 2.0 - 5.0
kPa, equalling a load of between 3 and 7.4 kg. A later measurement of the clamping force
applied to the specimens did however measure between 10 and 25 kPa (approximately 15 - 37
kg). The clamping pressure was considered soft when applied, but it should have been even
softer to equal that of DS/EN:1052-3.
The air-lime mortar and sometimes the air-lime/hydraulic lime mortar could generate squeeze
out if the clamp pressure was applied to fast after the top brick was placed, because of their
high workability. In these situations, the clamp pressure were applied more gradually but to
the same stress level, to avoid a reduction of the joint thickness.

Mortar prisms
Three sets of six 40 x 40 x 160 mm prisms were made for each mortar, following DS/EN:1015-11,
with some slight alterations. Firstly, the absorbent ﬁlter paper was used for all three mortars,
even though the two hydraulic lime mortars had a higher hydraulic binder content than 50 %
and therefore should be produced without absorbent ﬁlter paper. This was done as an attempt
to make the measurements more comparable within this project. For comparison with other
projects, the compressive strengths of mortar prisms, determined by use of absorbent ﬁlter
paper, are in DS/INF:167 referred to as ML, while compressive strengths determined without
ﬁlter paper are referred to as MC, where MC = 1
2ML. Here all compressive strengths are ML,
but they will be referred to simply as fc,m in the following.
Secondly, the absorbent ﬁlter paper described in DS/EN:1015-11 cannot be obtained anymore.
Former projects at DTU have therefore used an alternative ﬁlter paper from VWR, the circular
qualitative ﬁlter paper of grade 413 with a diameter of 240 mm. This was then cut to size, -
approx. 170 x 170 mm. Further details for this paper is given in the appendix, section 12.1.7.
Thirdly, the compaction of the mortar in the moulds was done with a vibrating table, as this
was considered to be more consistent than the processes described in DS/EN:1015-11 or in
other projects, such as Houen et al., 2019. This process is not included in DS/EN:1015-11,
but a comparable norm for production of prisms out of NHL (DS/EN:459-2, 2021) requires the
vibration table to run for 120 s in total, where the ﬁrst 45 s are spend placing the mortar in
two layers. An oscillation of 0.75 mm ± 0.1 mm is required for the process.
As 120 seconds of vibration for a wet mortar would make a distinct separation of the components
of the mortar, the mould was ﬁlled in two layers, with only a 4 second vibration after the
placement of each layer. This drastic reduction made a compaction comparative to that of
the applied jolting method in Houen et al., 2019 for the air-lime/hydraulic lime mortar and
the air-lime/cement mortar, but the air-lime mortar was not fully compacted doing this short
procedure.

Casting of mortar prisms
The prisms were cast as shown on ﬁgure 26. Here a mould holding 3 prisms was prepared by
oiling the steel sides and placing ﬁrst the 6 layers of absorbent ﬁlter paper and then the cotton
gauze at the bottom of the mould, before it was reconstructed. The mould was then ﬁxed to
the vibrating table with a mortar funnel on top. It was ﬁlled with mortar in two layers, with
a 4 s vibration of each layer. The second layer is slightly overﬁlling the mould. The mould
was then released from the vibration table and the mortar funnel removed. Most of the 1-2 cm

The top was then covered with a sheet of cotton gauze, 6 layers of absorbent ﬁlter paper and
ﬁnally a glass plate, before it was stored in a plastic bag with a load of approximately 4 kg on
each glass plate.

Reuse of bricks
The reuse of bricks starts with the tear down of masonry. The general process is for demolishing
companies to knock the walls down and bring the suitable bricks to a site for sorting, cleaning
and stacking, ready for reuse. Several companies in Denmark, such as Gamle Mursten ApS on
Funen, specialize in the ”production” of reused and repurposed bricks. They cooperate with
demolishing teams, developers and local authority to plan which constructions or parts thereof
would be suitable for reuse and should be transported to their facilities.
(a) Delivered bricks ready for sorting
(b) Sorting machine in action
Figure 31: Bricks being sorted at Gamle Mursten ApS
The bricks experience rough handling throughout the entire process, initially as the walls are
toppled, whereupon the loose bricks are placed on trucks with excavators. The bricks are then
o✏oaded at the cleaning facility, where they are stored in exposed outdoor piles, before being
mechanically sorted into construction waste and individual bricks.
The sorting machine is
shown on ﬁgure 31. The bricks which have not separated when they arrive at the facility are
discarded by the machine along with other construction waste such as wood, steel, tiles and
concrete.
The individual bricks are then fed into a specially designed transport conveyor belt, where the
mortar is rasped o↵the bedsides and one stretcher by vibrating thick steel mesh. If the mortar
is left on these surfaces, the brick is discarded or the mortar is removed by use of a masonry
hammer or angle grinder.
Mortar left on the header is removed by hand with a masonry
hammer, before the bricks are stacked on quarter pallets and wrapped in plastic, ready for
delivery and use. Some of the ﬁnished and discarded bricks are displayed on ﬁgure 32.
The bricks at Gamle Mursten ApS are sorted into three categories: red, yellow and Flensborg
bricks.
Each category can be a mix of many di↵erent types of bricks from many di↵erent
constructions. This is to ensure a high production throughput with few di↵erent storage options
and changes between brick types in each production run. It furthermore enables them to supply
costumers with bricks with a few days notice, along with the ability to maintain a constant
number of bricks for a construction project, without being limited by the size of the original
construction, supplying a speciﬁc brick type.

9
Discussion
9.1
Method precision
The results displayed are based on processes with many possibilities for variations between the
sample sets. To clarify the foundation for the later discussion and data comparison, the most
prominent variations are gathered here as an overview. Any of these should be considered or
avoided in further work.
The bricks used in this project were not preconditioned in any way, as they were delivered
directly from the brickyard.
They were assumed dry, but did actually display a range of
di↵erent water contents. A higher water content reduces the initial rate of water absorption,
which can lead to reduced bond strengths for the mortars. None of bricks refused the mortar,
and it was therefore unclear to what extend this might have a↵ected the results other than
reducing the strength results slightly for 1/4 of the specimens.
A more signiﬁcant variation is caused by the bricks being from the discarded pile. This have
lead to variations in the properties of the individual bricks potentially causing some variations
between each set of measurements. For the torsion test, which displayed signiﬁcant strength
di↵erences between the mortars, such an inﬂuence might have been minor. The shear test
displayed minor variations between the individual mortars, where a di↵erence in the bricks for
each set of measurements might have a↵ected perceived shear strength.
The mortars were not fully documented at arrival either.
Although this might not a↵ect
the actual measurements, it decreases the documentation accuracy and repeatability of the
processes. Furthermore, the mixing time for the air-lime/hydraulic lime mortar was inaccurately
reduced from 20 to 10 minutes, reducing the possibility to make comparisons between mortars.
The changes made to the individual test methods did further reduce the repeatability. They
might be valid changes, but further care should be taken to ensure a sound and repeatable
procedure. This includes the loading rate of all the tests, the workability measurements and
the prism casting process, where absorbent ﬁlter paper were used for all three mortar types
and a new compaction method was attempted.
9.2
Mortar prisms
Because the absorbent ﬁlter paper were used for all three mortars, the compression strength
for the hydraulic mortars are assumed to be twice as large as the results for similar mortars
made without absorbent ﬁlter paper. Figure 37 compares the strengths from this project, with
the values presented from KALK and DS/INF:167 in table 1, but the compressive strengths

The
ﬂexural strength have not been scaled, as nothing is mentioned in DS/INF:167 regarding ﬂexural

Even with the scaling of compression strength, the compressive strengths for the hydraulic

The strength of the air lime mortar is comparable with the hot-mixed 9 % air-lime mortar from

air-lime) to display such high strengths, but further studies of the speciﬁc mortar is required
for a more in depth comparison.

Strength development
It was quite signiﬁcant for both the torsion test and shear test to lack any strength development
during the longer curing of 56 days. There were unfortunately not enough time to measure the
strength development of the mortar prisms, as that could hint of a potential tendency for the
mortars. The mortars from KALK, which are the most documented mortars available, display
a slight increase in ﬂexural strength after 56 days, while the main increase occurs after 180 and
270 days, for a KKh 20/80/400 KALK A/S, 2023.
The increase in torsion strength of air-lime mortars is documented in Nielsen, 2010, where the
calculated cohesion is shown to increase doing 80 days of curing. The increase was however
only demonstrated in 12 % and 15 % air-lime mortars. For the 9 % air-lime mortar, no increase
was documented.
As specimens have been saved for most of the mortar samples, follow up measurements will be
made to investigate the strength development after a much longer curing period than 56 days.
Before another measurement is made, it will remain guesswork whether the shear or torsion
strength increase or stay unchanged for these 3 selected mortars.

Torsion and shear strength methods
These two methods are not directly comparable, as they represent di↵erent ways of determining
a bed joint failure in masonry. It is however signiﬁcant how di↵erent results they produce. A
comparison of the strength measurements after 28 days is displayed in ﬁgure 38. The torsion
strengths for the individual mortars follows roughly the same tendency as the compressive tests.
Any correlation between the the degree of compressive strength and torsion strength would be
far fetched, as they represent two complete di↵erent setups and failure mechanisms.

Reuse of bricks
It was possible to remove the mortar from all the specimens tested, although some diﬃculties
occurred for the hydraulic mortars. It was not possible to rub the mortar of them, as with the
air-lime mortar. Instead a hammer was needed to shear o↵the remaining mortar plate. With
a slight polishing of the air-lime/cement mortar, all bricks would be considered completely
reusable.
The manual cleaning process did however indicate that the hydraulic mortars would not be
cleaned by passing through the rasping machine at Gamle Mursten ApS, as these rely on steel
mesh to grind through the mortar.
This was idealised specimens as well, much di↵erent from 50 - 150 year old masonry, with
mortar more than one side. In order to properly investigate whether a speciﬁc mortar can be
cleaned from the brick, it must be applied in a realistic way, representing the actual masonry.
As DS/EN:1052-1 and DS/EN:1052-2 both investigate full wall segments, they would supply a
better source for cleaning than the torsion and shear tests.
9.5
Mortar and brick selection
For initial work, the speciﬁc selection of mortars and bricks would suﬃce. The bricks should
however be selected as a good representation of the actual bricks in use in constructions, with
a more thorough initial investigation. Wire cut bricks could be considered, as they have more
consistent dimensions than softmud bricks. Many brickyards are starting to produce softmud
bricks with large voids to reduce energy and material consumption. These might be relevant
contestants for investigation as well.
For mortars, an air-lime mortar of 13.5 % or higher could be considered, to continue the re-
application of such mortars in modern masonry. Here the e↵ect of the di↵erent slaking method
could be investigated for larger scale specimens.

Further investigations of any combination of the selection of prescribed masonry mortars would

9.6
Further work
As this project was the initial work for a larger project investigating the process of brick reuse
and the possible mortars which can make future brick reuse possible, each attempted method
have sprouted several further areas of interest and subjects of investigation.
The ﬁrst step in any further work would be an evaluation and comparison of methods, ap-
proaches and results with the Danish Technological Institute, as they perform the main bulk of
brick, mortar and masonry investigations in Denmark. A dialog have been established and some
questions have been asked, as part of this project, but further corporations will be valuable for
future projects, to avoid redundant investigations.
Brick reuse
As the initial problem is the cleaning of bricks, further work should include a more thorough
investigation of removing di↵erent mortars from di↵erent bricks, both from laboratory speci-
mens and fro actual demolishing sites. In continuation of this, the impact of di↵erent storage
methods for masonry specimens should be investigated further, as the current methods seem
unclear and without relation to the actual climate masonry experiences.
Based on well considered storage methods, the long term bond strength between brick and
mortar should be investigated with respect to reuse of the bricks, as arguments based on 28
days of curing of laboratory specimens does not represent the actual use case of the mortar.
These documentations should be supported by a LCA for the di↵erent mortar types having a
speciﬁc focus on the di↵erence in binder and sand ratio, along with the required construction
precautions for the speciﬁc mortar such as ﬂexural joints, the repairability of brickwork with
di↵erent mortars and ﬁnally the potential for brick reuse at the end of construction life.

mortars and determine the e↵ect of di↵erent levels of precompression.
• Document the relation between the torsion test and the ﬂexural test of wall segments.
• Document the di↵erences in measurements for mortar prisms of the same mortar, con-
structed with and without suction paper
• Investigate the di↵erences in properties related to mortar setting while constructing sam-
ples - ie. opening time.
• Record the air content of the mortars in use.
• Document the di↵erences between wet at dry mortars in both prism test, bond, shear
and torsion test.

Conclusion
The aim of the project was to assess the possibility of cleaning the bricks for reuse after con-
struction with the three mortars, 35/65/500 air-lime/hydraulic lime mortar (KKh) a 35/65/650
air-lime/cement mortar (KC), and 9 % air-lime mortar (K).
An almost complete mortar removal was achieved for all three mortar types. The mortar was
removed by hand, where the air-lime mortar could be removed by rubbing the bricks against
each other, while the hydraulic lime mortars had to be sheared of with a hammer. This would
leave the surface of the brick mostly free of mortar, but the air-lime/cement mortar did cause
tear-out of the bricks and left mortar residue which had to be further removed with the ﬂat
side of the hammer.
The the strength of the bond between brick and mortar for the di↵erent mortar types was
attempted quantiﬁed by the torsion and shear test methods. The di↵erent torsion strengths
of the mortars corresponded with both the strength distribution of the prism tests for these
mortars and somewhat with the degrees of diﬃculty in brick cleaning. Here the air-lime mortar
displayed the lowest torsion strength, with 192 kPa ± 15 kPa and the air-lime/cement mortar
the highest, with 847 kPa ± 80 kPa, after 28 days.
The shear strengths did not follow this trend, as these measurements were both lower than the
torsion strengths and quite similar for all three mortars. Further investigations of the shear
test method must be made, where all three levels of precompression are applied, as per the full
test method for initial shear strength, following DS/EN:1052-3. For this project, all specimens
for both torsion and shear test were precompressed with 100 kPa ± 1 kPa.
The bond strengths were measured after 56 days of curing as well, but no signiﬁcant strength
development could be determined for any of the mortars via any of the two methods. Other
works with similar mortars indicate the requirements for a longer curing period of minimum
180 - 270 days, for an increase in the joint strength. This was not possible to produce within
this project, but extra specimens are stored to be tested at a later point.
The application of the test methods was therefore possible but the results not fully useful, as
further method documentation are required to ensure an accurate testing of masonry specimens
which comes close enough to represent actual brickwork.

The bricks were assumed comparatively dry, and used straight from the pallet. Later measure-

for the darkest bricks.

be attempted. Here the bricks could be dried in an oven before use, and left to cool in dry

12.1.7
Absorption paper
The paper requirements described in DS/EN:1015-11 have a mass of 200 g/m2 ± 20 g/m2 and
an absorption of 160 g/m2 ± 20 g/m2. The density and absorption parameters for the circular
qualitative ﬁlter paper from VWR of grade 413, were determined in a former project (Stenholt-
Jacobsen, 2020). Here the paper was determined to have a mass of 73 g/m2 ± 1 g/m2 and
a single sheet water absorption capacity of 180 g/m2 ± 10 g/m2. When used for the prism
casting the absorption capacity of one paper was estimated to 70 - 90 g/m2 depending on the
papers placement in the mould. The criteria described in DS/EN:1015-11 have therefore not
been met. The use of alternative ﬁlter papers will not be discussed further in this project.

12.2.7
Shear test failure evaluation
The test method aims for a shear failure in both joints but DS/EN:1052-3 does not describe
whether these should occur at the same time. Failure in the ﬁrst joint either occurs where
the mortar slides clean from the brick surface or as a failure through the mortar. When the
specimens fail, they are kept together by the clamping pressure. The stress exerted by the
clamp increases when the joint fails or start to fail.
Most specimens constructed with air-lime mortars failed through the mortar, where the failure
started at the bottom of the joint and climbed up, while the stress increased in the precom-
pression load cell. This is demonstrated on ﬁgure 48a, where the sudden gradual increase in
normal stress occurs while the joint fails.
For most of the specimens, where the failure occurred between mortar and brick, the normal
stress increased suddenly, while the shear force dropped. This can be seen on ﬁgure 48b, where
the failure sometimes was audible. The failure in the bond did however create a similar gradual
increase in the normal stress as the for a mortar failure, as can be seen on ﬁgure 49b. This only

Abstract
This report describes the use of the Karsten tube penetration test to investigate the water
absorption of joint mortars in masonry. It discusses the diﬃculties and variations experienced
through the setup process and the actual measuring.
It demonstrates how its possible to distinguish between di↵erent mortar types based on
the binder type and composition. It highlights how any mortar containing hydraulic binders
displays a very low water absorption compared to more traditional air lime mortars.
Five
di↵erent mortars with varying degrees of known history and known composition are investigated
and a general range for the water absorption after 15 minutes for the Karsten tube penetration
test is constructed. This could be used for comparison for similar measurements in order to
determine the mortar type in unknown brickwork.
It is generally demonstrated how the water absorption for air lime mortars are much higher
than any mortar containing hydraulic binders. The absorption level for air lime mortars ranges
from 5 - 11 ml/cm2, while fully cured hydraulic mortars range from 0 - 1 ml/cm2. A short
discussion of the curing time for hydraulic lime mortar is furthermore included.

INTRODUCTION
1
Introduction
The Karsten tube penetration test is a simple analysis method used to assess the permeability
and water absorption of porous materials. The main application is not to determine speciﬁc
material properties, but to evaluate and compare measurements for di↵erent materials at dif-
ferent times. The method was ﬁrst mentioned in 1963 and have since been used and adapted
to suite di↵erent scopes and purposes.
The purpose of this report is to investigate the use of the Karsten tube penetration test to
determine the mortar type for joint mortars in brickwork and the possible diﬃculties associated
with this.
1.1
Similar projects
A student project from 2014 from DTU, Jensen (2014), describes the use of Karsten tubes to
assess the permeability of air lime mortars, air lime and cement mortars along with di↵erent
bricks. The study did mainly investigate laboratory made mortar plates, of approximate 150 x
150 x 25 mm, or very wide mortar segments in existing brickwork, with an estimated minimum
width of 35 mm. These does not represent the common masonry joint width of 12 - 15 mm in
Denmark.
The results were based on around 16 - 20 measurements for mortars, taken for di↵erent binder
ratios at di↵erent ages. The aim was to determine a possible age tendency in the permeability
of the mortar surface. Two important conclusions were drawn from these measurements:
• A clear distinction could be made between the permeability and water absorption of air
lime mortars (6,6 % and 9 %) and a cement containing mortar (50/50/700).
• A distinct drop in water absorption and permeability as the mortar cured. For the air
lime mortar, this was considered to be caused by the formation of a thin lime membrane
on the surface, acting as a water barrier.
The study investigated ﬁve di↵erent lab-made mortars, with know mixing proportions, while
only two actual brick walls was tested, for a total of four samples.
An article from 2013, Hendrickx (2013), describes how the measurements from the Karsten
tube analysis can be used to estimate the soptivity of the material based on the size of the
wetted area. This is based on assumptions of a somewhat symmetric circular wetting. These
assumptions are mainly valid for relatively homogeneous materials with no hard transactions.
It describes how the Karsten tubes test in general can be diﬃcult for masonry joints, because
the quality of the measurement is dependant on the quality of the bonding between the mortar
and the masonry. Masonry which is re-pointed with a di↵erent mortar than the bedding mortar
can further the variation in results, by di↵erence in bonding between the pointing mortar and
the original bedding mortar.
No other articles demonstrating values for Karsten tube penetration test for masonry mortar
joints or discussing the application for mortar investigation could be found. The Karsten tube
penetration test is manly re↵ed to as a reference test or a point for a general investigation,
with no discussion of the method or the actual application of the tube and the setup on
the brickwork. It might be considered to simple an investigation process to require further
documentation or discussion. The aim of this report is to demonstrate some of the possible
variations and diﬃculties related to Karsten tube penetration tests for masonry joints, including
comparable results for di↵erent mortar types.

METHOD
3
Method
The measuring equipment for the Karsten tube penetration test primarily consists of a mea-
suring tube with a volumetric scale, which can be placed on the surface of the material. The
connection between the tube and the material is sealed with a modelling clay - plastiline, re-
ferred to as putty in the following. The aim of the investigation is to determine the amount of
water absorbed in a speciﬁc period. For this analysis, measurements were taken after 1, 5, 10
and 15 minutes after which the test was ended.
For these tests, the vertical glass tubes with a 90◦angle and an internal opening diameter
of 25 mm were used. They had an approximate total volume of 20 ml, and a measuring scale
from 0 - 4 ml, with 0,1 ml subdivision.
3.1
Setup
The primary aim was to investigate the water absorption of di↵erent joint mortars. The tubes
were therefore centered on the mortars, where the connecting brick surfaces were covered. To
reduce variations and ensure the same initial conditions, the moisture level of the brickwork
and mortar was measured to ensure a dry or semi dry testing surface.
The ﬁrst step in the setup was to mask the bricks with aluminium tape, allowing for 1 - 2
mm of exposed bricks between the tape and the mortar joint. This was to ensure a minimal
stain of the brickwork from the putty. The gap was then sealed with putty, ensuring a total
cover of the brick and the ﬁrst millimeters of mortar. The ﬁnal ﬁxing of the putty must be
done with moist ﬁngers, to ensure it sticks to the surface of the material. The mortar joint was
covered by pressing the putty into the joint and overlapping it with the ﬁrst putty layers.
The putty covering the central mortar was then trimmed into a rectangle.
Because of
variations in the brickwork it was diﬃcult to get a consistent cross section for the surface
mortar. The aim was to cut a 13 x 24 mm rectangle, but the cross sections varied from 2.2 cm2
to 3.4 cm2 across the measurements, due to di↵erences in the joint widths. Each cross section
was measured in order to normalize the measurements. After measuring the exposed mortar
section, the tube could be pressed into the putty. Ensure a complete seal by pressing the putty
towards the tube rim and give the tube an extra press. The setup process is demonstrated on
ﬁgure 3.
Leakage can both occur from under the rim, under the putty and under the aluminium
tape. Rough mortar and bricks with cracks or large voids can be diﬃcult to seal completely.
The setup must therefore be observed for leakage doing the entire measurement. If leakage is
observed, the measurements must be discarded.
3.2
The testing
Start the stopwatch when the tube ﬁlling is initiated. The tube is then ﬁlled to the 0 ml marker,
which takes between 15 - 30 seconds, depending on the bottle used and the permeability of the
mortar. The tube must be reﬁlled each time it reaches 2 ml to ensure an even water pressure
doing the testing. The ﬁlling is demonstrated on ﬁgure 3. After reﬁlling, note down the time
and the reﬁlled amount. A minimum of measurements must be made after 1, 5, 10 and 15
minutes where the same approach is made, noting down the water level and reﬁlling to the 0
ml mark.

4.2
Striking/Pointing methods
An initial point for investigation was the possible absorption di↵erence between di↵erent point-
ing ﬁnishes. Smoothing out the surface layer of the mortar could potentially make the surface
less permeable and decrease the water absorption, compared to a more rough surface with
slight to no ﬁnish. Most modern brickwork in Denmark is ﬁnished by giving the surface of
the mortar a ﬁnal press with a rounded object, creating a slight curve in the joint. These are
known as concave or keyed joints. This method does not smoothen the surface or increase the
concentration of binding material in the surface of the joint.
A common ﬁnish method in the ﬁrst part of the 20th century and earlier, was the struck
or weather joint ﬁnish. This joint was made with an angle away from the facade, with the
intend to run of rainwater from the joint. The ﬁnish of these joints were made very smooth by
repeatedly pressing and dragging the trowel along the joint, bringing out the ﬁne particles and

4.3
Brick type
An other observation made for the di↵erent masonries, was the di↵erent brick colours inﬂuence
on the absorption rate and the shape of the wetted zone. The di↵erent chemical compositions of
bricks, along with the di↵erence in burning method and temperatures for the individual bricks
a↵ect the properties of the ﬁnal brick. Generally yellow bricks are more porous than red bricks,
with a higher water absorption, Miljøstyrelsen (2003).
Many masonries might be described by one general colour, while the actual make-up of
the bricks includes a variation of di↵erent colours and material properties. The building at
Plantevej, investigated in this project, is characterised as a yellow building, but the bricks
varies from slightly green, via yellow to orange and red. These di↵erences might inﬂuence the
KT measurements, in relation to the colour of the bricks adjacent to the investigated mortar
section. A set of measurements are displayed on ﬁgure 5, to demonstrate the observed brick

The comparatively fresh 35/65 hydraulic lime mortar, PV:HL, displays a signiﬁcantly larger
absorption than the 2 year old hydraulic mortar with unknown binder compositions.
This
variation could be caused by a possible larger hydraulic component in BB:HL, but the curing
process for hydraulic lime is likely to be the main reason. The hydraulic component in hydraulic
lime mortar is mainly belite (C2S), Nordisk NHL (n.d.), which have a much slower curing process
than alite (Herholdt, Justesen, Nepper-Christensen, & Nielsen, 1985, p. 276). For comparison,
it takes approximately 150 days for belite to reach the 28 day compressive strength of alite. If
the change in pore structure follows the same trend, it would be a major contributor to the
observed di↵erences.
There seem to be no signiﬁcant di↵erences between absorption of the cement based mortars
and the fully cured hydraulic lime mortar. They all display very little or close to no water
absorption in the 15 minute testing period.

CONCLUSION
5
Conclusion
The Karsten tube penetration test can be used for mortar analysis, to distinguish air lime
mortars or similar non-hydraulic mortars for mortars with a hydraulic component. Although a
larger range of mortar types and mortar masonry combination exists which are not investigated
to their full extend in this project, it is considered reasonable to assume similar values for the
water absorption would occur for other combinations of binder material and brick types. This is
based on the inﬂuence experienced for the di↵erent brick types and joint types. The comparable
values for each of the mortars tested in this project are displayed in table 2.
In addition to the demonstrated values for water absorption for di↵erent mortars, it was
demonstrated how a fully cured (two year old) hydraulic lime mortar display the approximate
same water absorption as a cement based mortar. It was furthermore demonstrated how the
di↵erence in pointing methods and surface ﬁnish did not inﬂuence the water absorption regis-
tered with the Karsten tube penetration test, while the surrounding bricks might inﬂuence the
absorption level.
Further work: Determine the moisture properties of the given mortars in regard to trans-
port and moisture in the masonry - is the transport in KC and HL the same over time, contrary
to popular belief? Does the ﬁnish of the mortar inﬂuence the moisture transport for the whole
brick work? What are the moisture transport properties of brickwork with one type of bedding
mortar and another for pointing? Which di↵erence does the acid wash make to the permeability
of the mortar, both immediately and over time?
Some of these aspects will be covered in the ﬁnal thesis.

6.1
Wetted zone shapes
As mentioned in the method, the homogeneity and water transportation of the joint can be
judged by the shape of the wetted zone. The wetted zone might however be diﬃcult to determine
in darker mortars and bricks.
Figure 7 displays the measurements for a pointed 36/65 NHL mortar, 25 days after curing.
These measurements are quite varied, although taken from within 1 meter of each other. The
di↵erence in the measurements are assumed to be caused by the quality of the pointing, i.e.;
the bond between the bricks and the mortar, the bond between the pointing mortar and the
bedding mortar and the bond between di↵erent parts of the pointing mortar. If the pointing
mortar is not pressed well enough into the raked area, onto the old bedding mortar, the water
transport will happen mainly in the new pointing mortar. Variations in the surface conditions of
the bricks before jointing can inﬂuence the water transport though the bricks, while di↵erences
in the compacting of the mortar can create di↵erent pore structures.
In order to distinguish between the di↵erent shapes of the wetted zones, the following vocab-
ulary is created for this report; Circular (C), Oval (O), Branching (B), No wetted zone (NZ).
These are illustrated on ﬁgure 8, along with their corresponding measurements displayed on
ﬁgure 7. C was not observed for these measurements.

Of the four wetted zones displayed on ﬁgure 8, only the oval zone is accepted without
further consideration. A measurement with slight or no visible wetted zone is only accepted,
if this is general for the rest of the masonry and across the variations of brick types in the
construction. Measurements with no wetted zone but with a comparatively high absorption
should be investigated for larger pores or cracks in the joint, which could allow water to run
though an otherwise impermeable mortar.
The branching zones should always be considered in relation to other measurements on
the same masonry. Mortars with a high porosity might extend the wetted zone long across the
joints. Such measurements should only be rejected if it’s not general for the rest of the masonry
or if it’s know that the masonry is pointed.
Of the ﬁve measurements on ﬁgure 7, #4 is accepted and #2 is depending on further
measurements of the same pointing. #3 could have been accepted if it was unknown that the
masonry was pointed, and if #1 and #5 had not been branching so much, thus indicating faults
or variations in the pointing.
