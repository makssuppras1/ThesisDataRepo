Abstract
In the present study, an investigation has been carried out with the aim of clarifying the
relationship between subjective and objective measures of airborne sound insulation.
The investigation was performed by conducting a listening experiment, in which subjects
rated a range of living-sounds, presented through diﬀerent wall types. The objective
measures are characterized by single-number quantities, and the subjective measures are
characterized by the responses of the participants in the listening experiment.
The responses were analysed using several diﬀerent methods, giving an elaborate clariﬁca-
tion of the results.
Further discussions and considerations were made, regarding the present and previous re-
lated studies, with the aim of clarifying any diﬀerences that were found. By the performed
analyses, it was found that the overall best predictor of the subjective responses were the
weighted sound reduction index, Rw.

1. INTRODUCTION
1
Introduction
It has for many years been known, that the acoustical comfort of dwellings can have adverse
physical and mental eﬀects on the occupants. In this regard, an increase in the both the
awareness and demand of satisfactory sound insulation of home environments have been
seen. This applies especially for multi-storey housing, where not only environmental noise
exposure is present, but also noises generated by close-living neighbours.
These types of noises which can be prevalent in dwellings, are generally categorized as
either impact noises, or airborne noises.
Impact noises can be considered a type of noise that is generated by the direct, physical
contact with the building elements of the dwelling; i.e., the walls, ceiling and ﬂoor. Such
noises can be caused by footsteps, cupboards being shut or furniture being moved, etc.
These sounds are then transmitted through the diﬀerent elements, by ﬂanking transmission
paths.
Airborne noises, on the other hand, are sounds that are transmitted by airborne sound
waves. This type of noise can in dwellings exist in many forms, such as music being played
by a sound system, or by people talking, etc.
In the present study, only the airborne type of sound, will be considered.
To ensure a adequate amount of airborne sound insulation of dwellings, and hereby a high
degree of acoustical comfort, methods have been developed to adequately measure the
eﬀective airborne sound insulation of buildings and building elements.
In Denmark, the sound reduction index(apparent sound reduction index, R′, for ﬁeld
measurements) is being used, which is deﬁned in ISO 140-4[3], in which the measurement
procedure of elements, and calculation of the sound reduction index, is described. The
sound reduction index is deﬁned in ISO 140-4, as
R′ = 10log
W1
W2+W3 dB
(1)
where W1 is the sound power incident on the element, W2 is the power transmitted
through the element, and W3 is the transmitted sound power of ﬂanking elements or other
components, which should only be included if present at signiﬁcant inﬂuence[3].
To describe the overall eﬀective sound insulation properties of building elements, so called
single-number quantities(SNQs) have been developed, in which the sound insulation across
diﬀerent frequency bands is taken into account. In Europe, several diﬀerent SNQs are
applied in building regulations, however the weighted sound reduction index(Rw) is used
in most European countries[36]. In the US, the sound transmission class (STC) is used.
With the application of these SNQs, it is possible to not only quantify the eﬀective airborne
sound insulation of building elements, but also to determine limits, for which the SNQs
will ensure a satisfactory amount of sound insulation of dwellings. Considering the SNQ
an objective measure of the of airborne sound insulation, it is also necessary to investigate
the relationship between this objective measure and the subjective evaluations hereof.
Hereby, an adequate value of the SNQ can be found, at which a satisfying amount of
sound insulation can be ensured.

1. INTRODUCTION
With the aim of investigating the relationship between the subjective and objective meas-
ures, several studies have been conducted, in which listening experiments have been
carried out. In these experiments participants perceive sounds through diﬀerent wall types
with varying SNQ values, and give their evaluations of these sounds in terms of diﬀerent
subjective measures, such as annoyance, dsiturbance and loudness [[19],[30], [28], [32],[38]].
In this manner, a correlation can be found between the diﬀerent wall types (and hereby
SNQ values), and the subjective measures, as such making it possible to quantify the
adequate value of a given SNQ at which the subjective ratings are low.
Diﬀerences have been seen in previous studies, regarding which SNQ is the most precise
descriptor of the subjective evaluations. The diﬀerences can arise from several things,
since the parameters of the diﬀerent studies have not necessarily been equal. It can be
assumed that the subjective evaluation of a sound does not only depend on the sound
level, but on other factors as well, such as the spectral and informational content of a sound.
In previous studies, one focal point of the investigations have been whether the frequency
content from 50-80 Hz have inﬂuence on the subjective evaluations. It has been argued
that Rw, which takes into consideration the third octave frequency bands from 100-3150
Hz, is adequate in terms of determining subjective evaluations[[19],[38]].
Considering the issue that building elements achieve weaker sound insulation in the lower
frequency bands compared to the mid-high frequency bands, gives rise to a certain scepti-
cism about the claim that the use of SNQs including these bands is unnecessary. However,
it is obvious that the issue of weak sound insulation in the low frequencies is only prevalent,
if the noises actually include these frequencies.
In the present study, an experiment have been conducted, seeking to extend the invest-
igation of previous studies and hereby either validate or contradict previous ﬁndings,
including the question about the importance of the low frequencies. The methodologies
and surrounding parameters of previous studies have been taken into consideration, during
the design and setup of the experiment.
As such, a listening experiment was, in which 23 participants were asked to evaluate 12
unique sounds, in terms of disturbance and unacceptability. The sounds were presented
through 7 diﬀerent walls, represented by audio ﬁlters, for a total of 84 sounds perceived
by the participants.
The subjective ratings from the participants were then analyzed by comparing the responses
with the SNQ values of the applied walls. Hereby, the correlation between the could
be investigated, using diﬀerent regression models. To describe the relationship between
the two measures, the coeﬃcient of determination, R2 was used. Further analyses were
conducted by a statistical approach, using a mixed model to investigate the diﬀerent
factors that could have aﬀected the responses from the participants, and to further conﬁrm
the ﬁndings of the regression models..

2.1
Sound insulation
Airborne sound insulation in general, regards the ability of an element to insulate sound
from one side of the element, to the other; i.e. the insulation of sounds coming from one
room to the other, through a wall, ﬂoor or ceiling element. When a sound wave hits an
element, part of the sound power carried by the wave will be transmitted through the
element and part will be reﬂected.
Denoting the power of the incident wave Pi, the power of the reﬂected wave Pr and
the power of the transmitted wave Pt, equation 2 describes the relationship of these
quantities[37].
Pi = Pr + Pt
(2)

describes the relationship of these, and is deﬁned by equation 3 as
τ = Pt

2.1.1
Acoustical comfort and noise
Noise is, by deﬁnition, unwanted sound. Noise can be perceived as an intrusion of a person’s
sense of privacy, and can cause emotional distress, since the noise is often out of control of
the person perceiving it. Noise can not only cause general annoyance, but also be directly
interfering with a range of everyday activities, such as speech communication, listening
to music, TV or radio broadcasts, concentration during mental activities, relaxation and
sleep[2].
The adverse eﬀects of noise exposure on both physical and mental health, has been known
for years. Because of the knowledge gained, related to the adverse eﬀects, the awareness

2. BACKGROUND
2.1.2
Single number quantities
To ensure a satisfactory amount of sound insulation of partitioning elements of dwellings,
such as multi-storey homes in which neighbour noises are often prevalent, the require-
ments used in building regulations have developed through the years[34]. These building
regulations are designed, such as to be able to limit the amount of noise exposure that
occupants are subjected to, using diﬀerent objective descriptors, known as single-number
quantities(SNQ). Today, a wide range of SNQs exist, with the common purpose of precisely
describing the sound attenuation characteristics of an partition; i.e., the basic principle of
the SNQs is to be able to conveniently quantify the eﬀectiveness of a building elements
ability to insulate sound.
Before the application of SNQs as they appear today, earlier sound insulation requirements
were sometimes based on the sound insulation of other constructions, such as "a sound
insulation as good as a 1/1 stone brick wall or another construction providing at least
the same sound insulation"[34].
This manner of comparing the sound insulation of
diﬀerent partitions were obviously less speciﬁc than todays SNQs, resulting in potentially
unsatisfactory sound insulation. These comparative methods then developed into more
speciﬁc descriptors, such as arithmetic averages of 1/3 octave band attenuation values
across a frequency range[34]. From this point, the SNQs as the appear today, were
developed, using the concept of reference curves and maximum unfavorable deviation, for
calculating the single-number quantity.
ISO 717 was the ﬁrst standard to include these types of SNQs, and the updated version
of ISO 717[9] includes the deﬁnition and calculation method of the current international
descriptors, used for airborne sound insulation, such as the weighted sound reduction index,
Rw.
Examples for calculating the SNQs are given in the standard, and the method can be
described through the following procedure:
1. The measured transmission loss at one third octave bands of the partition is compared
to a reference curve, which is given in ISO 717.
2. The reference curve is then shifted in increments of 1 dB, toward the measured curve,
until the sum of unfavourable deviations is less than 32 dB.
3. The single-number quantity of the partition is then found as the value in decibels,
at 500 Hz, on the reference curve.
The unfavourable deviation is a measurement at a certain frequency, which has a lower
value than the reference curve.
In USA and Canada, the sound transmission class, STC, is used instead of Rw. The
STC is deﬁned in the standard, ASTM E413. The procedure for calculating the STC is
similar to that of the European Rw; however, two main diﬀerences can be found. First,
the STC is calculated using measurements in the third octave bands from 125 Hz to 4000
Hz, whereas Rw is calculated from 100 Hz to 3150 Hz. Furthermore, the STC also has a
so-called "8-dB rule", which relates that any single unfavourable deviation cannot exceed
8 dB(The maximum sum of the deviations is still 32 dB for STC).

types of noise sources. The most widely used C-corrections, that are also included in
the standard, consists of C and Ctr, for pink noise and traﬃc noise, respectively. The
adaptation terms have been included as separate numbers to continue the conventional
use of the reference curve of the sound reduction index[9].

The eﬀect of including the C- or Ctr adaptation terms is, that some walls will receive a

frequency range that is taken into account. As such, lightweight walls may still achieve
high SNQ values, if only the frequencies above 100 Hz is considered.

Other SNQs which are used in regulations in some European contries include the Weighted
normalized level diﬀerence, Dn,w and the Weighted standardized level diﬀerence, DnT,w.
All of the existing SNQs, used for building regulations today, are calculated in either one
octave or one third octave frequency bands, such as to ensure that the frequency bands at
which most of the undesirable noises are prevalent, can be taken into account.
An alternative method to express the insulation characteristics of a partition, could be to
consider the varying transmission losses at diﬀerent frequency bands through an element,
giving a more elaborate description of these characteristics. However, the SNQs serves
as a more convenient method for presenting these transmission losses as a single number,
calculated with diﬀerent weightings for diﬀerent frequency bands. Not only are the SNQs
convenient, but they also serve as an ’easy to understand’ quantity, for the general public,
such as prospective occupants of a home, seeking information about the sound insulation

In Denmark, the weighted sound reduction index, Rw, is used in building regulations,
as the objective measure of airborne sound insulation, using the conventional frequency
range from 100 Hz to 3150 Hz. By only taking these frequency bands into account, it is
clear, that the eﬀectiveness of the sound insulation of partitions below 100 Hz and above
3150 Hz is not taken into account, which can very well inﬂuence the resulting degree of
acoustic comfort of occupants. This frequency range is based on the assumption that
most prevalent noises occur within that range - however, such an assumption may prove
to be unjustiﬁable, since sounds can also occur below and above this frequency range, in
particular from music played by high quality audio equipment.
In particular, issues may occur in the one third octave bands from 50 Hz to 100 Hz, since
the airborne sound insulation at these lower frequencies is often considerably worse than
at the mid-high frequencies.
To avoid this issue, Sweden as the only European country to do so, have applied low-
frequency adaptation terms for use in building regulations. England and Wales have
also acknowledged the issues of unsatisfactory low frequency insulation, by applying the
spectrum adaptation term, Ctr[34], in which a higher emphasis is put on the low frequencies.
It is important to realise that while the SNQs do give considerable information about
the overall airborne sound insulation of partitions, two SNQs of equal value may have
very diﬀerent characteristics across the considered frequency bands. An SNQ may have
very good attenuation characteristics at high frequencies, but may on the other hand
also have very bad attenuation at low frequencies. As such, the importance of not only
which frequency bands should be considered, but also how these frequency bands should
be weighted in relation to the disturbance they may inﬂect, becomes clear. Hereby, the
importance of the research carried out to clarify these eﬀects also becomes apparent.

2. BACKGROUND
2.2
Literature study
The diﬀerent single-number quantities and regulations that have developed through the
years, all seek to fulﬁl a common purpose; to ensure a satisfactory amount of sound
insulation for occupants, so that a high degree of acoustic comfort can be achieved and in
turn, an overall increase in well-being and quality of life.
It is clear, however, that many factors inﬂuence the determination of what can be considered
suﬃcient in terms of sound insulation, whether it be the prevalence of speciﬁc sounds,
the frequency and informational content of these, the sound insulation characteristics of
the walls, etc. Furthermore, the subjective evaluation of airborne sound insulation also
varies between each individual, since all individuals have diﬀerent opinions about what is
acceptable or satisfactory, and what is not.
As such, relating the objective with the subjective can be a diﬃcult task, and in this regard,
a number of studies have been conducted, with the aim of further clarifying the relationship.
2.2.1
Hongisto et al.
Hongisto et al. conducted an experiment, with the aim of determining which standardized
SNQs could best predict the subjective ratings of airborne sound insulation[19]. The exper-
iment focused on "living sounds", i.e. sounds that are prevalent in a home environment.
In the experiment, 59 subjects listened to 6 diﬀerent sound types coming through 9 diﬀerent
walls, for a total of 54 sounds perceived by the subjects.
The walls applied in the experiment contained a wide range of Rw values, and signiﬁcantly
diﬀerent SRI values across the third octave frequency bands from 50 Hz to 5000 Hz. The
walls were characterised by standardized SNQs, deﬁned in ISO 717[9] or the American
ASTM E413. One exception is Rspeech, which is a modiﬁcation of Rw + Copt, developed for
structures used between communication rooms, introduced by Park et al.[19][30].
Most of the considered SNQs consisted of variations of the weighted sound reduction index,
Rw, using the spectrum adaptation terms C and Ctr. Diﬀerent variations of the adaptation
terms were used, extending the frequency range to include the low frequency third octave
bands from 50 Hz to 80 Hz, and/or high frequency third octave bands from 4000 Hz to 5000
Hz. The American STC and STCno8 were also considered, where STCno8 is a variation of
STC, excluding the 8-dB rule of individual maximum allowed deﬁciency in a frequency
band. By excluding the 8-dB rule, the SNQ will only be determined by the sum of the
deviations, and not by any single frequency band with large deviations - i.e. "dips" in the
transmission loss curve can be ignored to some extent, although still aﬀecting the 32-dB rule.
The sounds that were included in the study, were all sounds, determined to be common
"living sounds", that could be heard from neighbours in a home environment. The six
sounds were characterized as Guitar, Music(Traﬃc), Music(Living), Baby cry, Loud speech
and Dog bark. The sounds consisted of varying frequency and informational content.
Two of the six sounds - the two music sounds - can be considered to include noticeable
low frequency content, whereas the other four did not. Furthermore, four sounds were
considered wideband, and two were narrowband.
All sounds in the experiment were bandpass-ﬁltered in the range from 50 Hz to 5000
Hz. It is argued by Hongisto et al., that the purpose of this ﬁltering is to ensure that

Further analysis was done by Virjonen et al.[40], using the results found by Hongisto
et al.[19]. In this study, the aim was to ﬁnd a new reference spectrum, instead of the
conventional spectrum deﬁned in ISO 717. Algorithms were implemented, to ﬁnd the
spectra for each sound, in which the highest R2 value were achieved. It was found, that
the optimal spectra varied between each sound; however, an overall optimal reference
spectrum, Lopt was found, taking all sounds into account. The optimal reference spectrum
that was found, is similar to that of Rw + C50−5000 above 315 Hz, and puts signiﬁcantly
less emphasis on the frequencies below 315 Hz, compared to Rw + C50−5000.
Virjonen et al. argues that the reference spectrum of C50−5000 overestimates the signiﬁcance
of the low frequencies of living sounds. It is, however, mentioned that this is not the case
for the sound Music(Traﬃc), in which most of the energy is contained within the low
frequencies. Hereby, stating that C50−5000 overestimates the importance of low frequency
content does only hold true, when not considering sounds with signiﬁcant low frequency
content.
Virjonen et al. mentions that it is diﬃcult to take into account all types of living sounds,
using only one SNQ or reference spectrum. The reference spectrum, Lopt is suggested to
be used for living sounds that does not include signiﬁcant low frequency content, whereas
C50−5000 could be used for sound with signiﬁcant low frequency content. However, as a
ﬁnal conclusion, it is suggested that a new SNQ, using the found reference spectrum Lopt
could be used for all living sounds as a compromise.
In regard to the ﬁndings by Virjonen et al., the actual validity and application of the
optimal reference spectrum may be considered questionable. To ensure that the reference
spectrum truly does represent all living sounds, a larger amount of sound samples with
varying spectral content should be included in the study. Furthermore, it is clear that the
issue of using a single SNQ as an overall descriptor for all living sounds, is still present for
the calculated optimal reference spectrum.
Another study was done by Hongisto et al., in which the subjective evaluations of two
wall types - heavy and light - were compared. The responses were gathered through a
questionnaire provided to the occupants of dwellings containing either type of building
elements. The aim of the study was to investigate whether the reports of disturbance
or annoyance were diﬀerent for the two building types. It was found that only slight
variations could be seen in the amount of satisfaction between the two building element
types. Occupants of buildings with heavy partitions reported more disturbances related to
impact noises, whereas occupants of light partitioned buildings reported more disturbances
from airborne sounds. Furthermore, it was found, that while the satisfaction with the

2.2.2
Park et al.
A similar study was done by Park et al.[30], in which participants listened to speech and
music sounds through 20 simulated walls. The speech sounds consisted of three diﬀerent
harvard sentences, and the music sounds consisted of three diﬀerent genres of music; rap,
house, and pop, giving a total of 120 sounds that the participants listened to, and evaluated.
The music sounds contained signiﬁcant energy in the low frequency range, whereas the
speech sounds contained most energy within the range from 100 Hz to 1 kHz.
The walls that were included in the experiment by Park et al., consisted of three diﬀerent
construction types, Wood studs, Steel studs, and Concrete block. The STC rating of the
walls varied from values of 35 dB to 58 dB, and the Rw values varied from 37 dB to 56
dB. The transmission loss across frequency varied widely between the 20 walls, of which
the heavy concrete partitions in general had increased low- and high frequency sound
insulation compared to the light partitions.
In the beginning of the experiment, the participants ﬁrst heard 12 sound samples consisting
of 6 speech sounds, and 6 music sounds, to get familiar with the sounds. The sounds in this
test phase were chosen to represent the full range of sound levels used in the experiment,
and as such the test phase consisted of both rehearsal of the procedure and familiarisation
with the sounds.
The sounds in the full experiment were presented in randomised order, using three diﬀerent
randomised patterns. The presentation for each participant were then chosen from one of
the three randomized patterns.
Two separate groups of participants completed two diﬀerent evaluation tasks. One group,
consisting of ten participants, evaluated the sounds in terms of annoyance, and the other
group, consisting of twenty participants, evaluated the sounds in terms of loudness. Fur-
thermore, an extra annoyance test was conducted for 18 participants, in which a reference
sound was presented before each sound. The reference sound consisted of the same type of
sound to be rated, but was presented through an "average" wall type, each time. Hereby,
the participants could use the reference as an anchor throughout the experiment, to help
determining the annoyance evaluation of each sound.
The results of the experiment were analyzed in a similar manner as done by Hongisto et
al., in [19], using the squared correlation coeﬃcient to determine the relation between the
subjective and objective measures. In the experiment by Park et al., a linear regression

eau eﬀects at the maximum and minimum ratings. Hereby, a ﬁt using the Boltzmann
equation was done, following the S-shaped curve, after which the R2 values were calculated.

the annoyance ratings of the speech sounds, compared to the music sounds. Furthermore,

the variations of Rw that limited the inﬂuence of low frequencies resulted in increased R2
values. Likewise, the variations that increased the inﬂuence of low frequencies gave rise to
higher R2 values for the music sound type.
In the same study, Park et al. further analyzed the responses by considering the arithmetic
average of the transmission losses of the walls, hereby making it possible to ﬁnd exactly
which eﬀect the included frequency range had, on the annoyance ratings for a speciﬁc
sound.
From this investigation, it was found that for the music sound, the highest correlation
was found using either only the low frequencies(variations between 63 Hz and 630 Hz),
or the high frequencies(variations between 2000 Hz and 6300 Hz). For the speech sound,
it was found that the highest R2 values were achieved when including the mid-frequency
range; i.e. the worst R2 values were found, by only taking either very low or very high
frequencies into account.
In general, it is concluded by Park et al., that the SNQs that best predict the subjective
ratings of music sounds are not equal to those that best predict speech sounds. It is

Mortensen
In 1999, F.R. Mortensen also conducted an experiment, investigating the subjective evalu-
ations of noise from neighbours, with a focal point of the investigation being on the low
frequencies of sound insulation[28].
The experiment included both impact and airborne noise types. Three diﬀerent sounds
were used, of which two were impact noises; male walker and children playing, and one
was an airborne noise; music.
The music sound consisted of the pop song by "Together Again" by Janet Jackson, which
Mortensen argues contains a characteristic bass that varies in frequency, and which in
company with the bass drum is very audible[28], and as such may potentially be perceived
as annoying.

2. BACKGROUND
Extensive research was done by Mortensen, to ﬁnd suitable wall types for the experiment.
Many laboratory measurements were taken into account, to ﬁnd a general pattern for
diﬀerent classes of construction types, mainly Light, Light-medium, Medium, Medium-heavy
and Heavy.
From this investigation, ﬁve diﬀerent wall-ﬁlters were designed, which all had large vari-
ations in transmission losses in the third octave frequency bands below 160 Hz. The
walls had equal transmission losses above 160 Hz. By such a design of the walls, it was
possible to particularly examine the eﬀects of only the low frequency parts of the wall-ﬁlters.
The sounds were presented to the participants once through each of the ﬁve wall types.
Furthermore, the wall-type characterised as "Light-medium" was used to present the
sounds at four diﬀerent levels, as well. As such, a total of 3 ∗8 = 24 sounds were presented.
It is mentioned by Mortensen, that subjects also heard one repetition for each sound, and
also two training presentations, for a total of 50 sounds heard by each participant. Each
sound sample had a length of around 2 minutes, which is considerably longer than those
used by Hongisto et al. and Park et al.
The presentation order of the sounds were randomised individually for each participant.
The subjective evaluations of the participants consisted of a questionnaire, which the
participants could answer, after hearing the sounds.
The questionnaire consisted of four diﬀerent questions in total, where three of these, in
summary were questions about the annoyance, disturbance and loudness of each sound,
on a continuous scale from "not" to "very". The fourth question was a yes/no answer,
regarding whether or not the participant would be annoyed by the perceived sound, coming
from a neighbour.
The responses from the participants were ﬁrst analyzed through ANOVA(analysis of
variance) tests, regarding the parameters, type of level, type of noise, sex of subject, con-
struction type, age of subject and Block(repetition). For the airborne sound, music, it
was found that all of the parameters had signiﬁcant inﬂuence on all of the subjective
evaluations. One exception was block(repitition), which did not aﬀect the yes/no evaluation
of annoyance.
Furthermore, the eﬀects of LAeq and C-correction were also included in the analysis.
It was found that an increase of presentation level in the low frequencies produced a
higher rating of all subjective measures, although not as much as an overall increase in
presentation level across all frequencies. For disturbance it was found, that the diﬀerence
between an overall level increase, and an increase in just the low frequencies were smaller,
than for the other subjective measures, loudness and annoyance.
In conclusion, it is mentioned by Mortensen, that the subjective evaluations of sounds
through lightweight walls were in general higher(i.e. worse) than that of heavy weight walls,
stating that the only diﬀerence lies in the amount of low frequency content transmitted
through the walls. Hereby, it suggested to extend the frequency range of the objective meas-
ures down to 50 Hz, when considering the quality of insulation of a construction or dwelling.

2.2.4
Rychtáriková et al.
Other notable experiments have been conducted with a similar aim as the previously
mentioned, however using an alternative methodology.
One of those was done by Rychtáriková et al.[38]. In this study, a similar listening ex-
periment was conducted, investigation the correlation between Rw + C50−5000 and the
perceived loudness of living sounds.
The sounds that were included in the experiment, consisted of recordings made in 10
diﬀerent households, so that a high degree of realism could be achieved, regarding the
stimuli. A range of diﬀerent living sounds were recorded, however, it is mentioned by
Rychtáriková et al., that 51% of the recorded sounds consisted of music, with substantial
low frequency content. In total, 64 living sounds were used.
The sounds were ﬁltered by two diﬀerent wall types, where one was characterised as
lightweight and one was heavyweight. The walls had large variations in the transmission
loss across the diﬀerent third octave frequency bands; in the third octave frequency bands
from 100 Hz to 3150 Hz, the lightweight wall achieved the highest attenuation, for a
maximum diﬀerence of around 20 dB from 250 Hz to 2 kHz. Below 100 Hz and above 3150
Hz, the heavyweight wall achieved the highest attenuation. Large variations were seen in
the low frequency range, up to around 80 Hz. Both walls achieved the same SNQ rating of
52 dB, using Rw + C50−5000, however a rather large diﬀerence was seen for Rw + C100−3150,
for which values of 67 dB and 52 dB was found, for the lightweight and heavyweight wall,
respectively.
The sounds were presented to the participants in an anechoic room, through headphones.
Each sound sample was played through one wall ﬁrst, and then the other, after which the
participants rated which of the two sounds they thought were the loudest. The rating
procedure was done, using a 2-alternative-forced-choice task[27].
It was found that, using presentation levels of the stimuli of around 20-40 dB, the sounds
through the heavyweight wall were perceived as being loudest. Rychtáriková et al. does,
however, mention that some exceptions were found, for very bass heavy sounds, in which
the lightweight wall was perceived louder.
Further analysis was done by the authors, investigating whether the A-weighting2, on
which Rw + C50−5000 is based[38], could be considered a good predictor of loudness at
higher presentation levels. This analysis was done in a separate experiment, in which the
participants listened to the same stimuli at levels from 50-70 dB.
It was found that by increasing the presentation levels, the correlation between the per-
ceived loudness and the SNQ increased signiﬁcantly. The authors hereby argues that only
for unrealistically high presentation levels, should SNQs that take the low frequency third
octave bands into account be used.

2.2.6
Rindel
A recent review of the previous studies related to the investigation of subjective evaluations
of airborne sound insulation, was made by Rindel[18].
In the review, Rindel addresses some of the ﬁndings of the previous studies, and the
methodological weaknesses that may have inﬂuenced these ﬁndings.
In the review, the overall research question that has been the focal point of the previous
studies, is also put into question.
Rindel mentions, that the purpose of the SNQ is to evaluate the acoustical properties of
sound insulation constructions, and for setting minimum requirements for use in building
regulations and as such, to ensure protection against noises from neighbours. It is stated,
that ﬁnding the best predictor of the subjective evaluations as an average of typical sounds
is not suﬃcient to ensure protection. Rather, the SNQs should not ensure protection
20
==PAGE:32==
2. BACKGROUND
against many, or typical living sounds; they should ensure protection against the worst
case scenario, i.e. the most annoying sounds. Herein, it is given that protection against all
sounds is also ensured.
It is clear that the point made by Rindel is very valid, regarding what the actual research
question should be, in relation to ensuring protection against neighbour noises - however, it
is not necessarily an easy task to ensure said protection. The general solution related to how
to achieve suﬃcient protection is fairly simple: the SNQs should include the frequencies
taking the worst case scenario into account, and values used in regulations should be suﬃ-
ciently high to ensure the attenuation of these sounds. However, the diﬃculty lies within
determining what the worst case scenario actually is, and to which degree it is possible to
be certain of this. Furthermore, economics have, and likely always will be, a heavily inﬂuen-
cing factor in terms of what is applied in building regulations. As such, it is not possible to
simply set the value of the SNQs at a very high level, since this is in many cases not feasible.
Regarding the previously mentioned studies, comments were given by Rindel, about the
methodologies and ﬁndings of these.
In relation to the concluding statements made by Hongisto et al., about the necessity of
including the 50 Hz extended frequency range in the SNQs, it is argued by Rindel, that
Rw was found as the best predictor for an average of the 6 sounds types. As such, since
only 1 of the 2 music sounds contained signiﬁcant low frequency content, the "weight" of
the remaining 5 sounds will be heavier in the resulting average. It is also mentioned by
Rindel, that the highest R2 value in the study was found between Music(traﬃc) and the
two SNQs, Rw + C50−3150 and Rw + C50−3150, which were not reﬂected in the conclusion
made by Hongisto et al.
Regarding the further analysis and corresponding optimal reference spectrum that was
suggested by Virjonen et al., it is mentioned by Rindel, that because of the low emphasis
on frequencies below 315 Hz, the application of such a reference spectrum would almost
certainly lead to increase noise complaints. It is argued by Rindel, that noise complaints
are not caused by an average of diﬀerent sound types, but are mostly caused by loud music
with rhythmic bass.
In regard to the study by Rychtáriková et al., it is mentioned by Rindel, that because of
the very large diﬀerences in Rw seen for the two considered walls, of Rw = 52 dB and
Rw = 69 dB for the heavyweight and lightweight wall respectively, the results showing that
the 50 Hz extended frequency range should not be used in regulations is to be expected.
It is argued by Rindel, that a more balanced experiment would have been to include walls
of equal Rw values. In this case, it may have been that the heavyweight wall would be
rated as being more quiet across all the considered third octave frequency bands, instead
of just the very low. As such, satisfactory overall protection would be ensured. Finally, it
is argued by Rindel, that a diﬀerent conclusion can be drawn from the experiment, than
what was made by Rychtáriková et al.; Rw + C50−3150 and Rw + C50−3150 are more valuable
SNQs, since it was seen that even for lightweight walls with relatively poor low frequency
SRI values, no serious problems were found.
In regard to the importance of the low frequencies of airborne sound insulation, it is
concluded by Rindel that, contrary to what have been reported in several studies, suggesting

3
Experimental setup
The main purpose of the present study is to investigate the relationship between the
subjective evaluations of disturbance and unacceptability, and the objective measures of air-
borne sound insulation; the single-number quantities - this investigation was carried out by
conducting a listening experiment similar to those of previous studies described in Section 2.
The experiment was conducted with a laboratory setup, in an acoustically treated room,
meant to replicate the acoustical conditions of a living room. In total, 12 unique sound
samples were included in the experiment, to represent normal living sounds of a home
environment. The sounds were presented to the subjects, using two loudspeakers. The 12
sounds were ﬁltered through 9 diﬀerent walls, giving a total of 84 sound samples. Each wall
was characterized by 11 standardized SNQs, making it possible to compare the subjective
evaluations with the SNQ values for each wall.
The subjects listened to the 84 airborne living sounds in randomized order, and gave their
subjective evaluations of the sounds, while listening to them.
The diﬀerent parameters of the experiment was inspired by those of previous studies.
In particular, the study by Hongisto et al.[19] has served as an overall methodological
guideline, as it was considered a very well conducted experiment. As such, since several
factors in the experimental setup was inﬂuenced by those found in the study by Hongisto
et al., the two experiments will also be reasonably comparable in many regards, making a
comparison of the results of high interest.
3.1
Experimentation room
The room in which the experiment was conducted, was the IEC listening room on the
campus of the Technical University of Denmark(DTU), Kgs. Lyngby, building 354.
The height, width and length of the room was measured to 2.75 m, 4.6 m and 7.36 m
respectively, giving an area of 93 m2.
The rectangular room is normally used for diﬀerent purposes, however, no other activities
were performed in the room, during the time at which the experiments of the present
study were conducted.
Several absorbers/reﬂectors are mounted on the four walls of the room, and large rect-
angular elements are installed at two corners of the room, as a measure to control the
acoustical properties, such as the reverberation time and low frequency room modes. In
Figure 3, a sketch and picture of the room can be seen.
At a distance of 1.8 m, from the back wall, the room can be divided by a curtain, which
in the present study was used to represent the partitioning wall between receive and
source room. The sounds of the experiment were presented to the subjects, using two
loudspeakers, which were positioned just behind the curtain. The subjects were seated at
a distance of 2.2 m from the curtain, facing the curtain, approximately in the middle of
the room. The distance from the subject to either one of the loudspeakers were 2.4 m, and
the distance between the two speakers were 1,7 m. The area of the curtain was measured
to 10.6 m2.

although barely audible. For this reason, it was attempted to avoid conducting experiments

In total, 12 sounds were used in the experiment, which were all considered realistic living
sounds, that could be prevalent in a home environment. All sound samples have a duration

when determining which types of sounds should be included in the experiment. It was
decided that the sounds should consist of varying frequency content, i.e. both high- and
low frequency sounds, varying presentation level, both loud and quiet sounds, and ﬁnally
for some sounds to be potentially emotionally aﬀecting. As such, the intention was that it
should be possible to get a reasonable contrast in the responses, for diﬀerent independent
variables.

In general, the level of the sounds were set to levels that were deemed realistic for the
speciﬁc sound type. In earlier studies, [[19], [30]], the sound pressure levels of the sounds
were set, so that all or most receive room sounds were audible through all the included
walls. However, by adjusting the source level to ensure audibility of all sounds, means that
the resulting source levels becomes unrealistically high in some cases.
It was in the present study decided, that instead of ensuring that all sounds would be
audible, the level of the source room sounds should be set at realistic levels. Hereby, it is
clear that some sounds may be inaudible through walls achieving high sound insulation,
which was indeed the case in the present study.
A description of the 12 sounds, the motivation for choosing each sound and the presentation
level is given below.
Sound 1, Action movie:
Sound 1 is a sound sample from the movie "The Avengers" during an action scene, in
which talking, shouting, ﬁghting and dramatic orchestral music can be heard. The most
prominent part of the sound is the orchestral music.
The motivation for choosing the sound was, that it was considered a good representation
of a typical action scene from a movie, which could be played from a television set.
The sound is a broadband sample, containing spectral content in all of the considered third
octave frequency bands(50 Hz to 5 kHz), including considerable high- and low frequency
energy.
The sound pressure level of the sound sample was set to 70 dB, as this was considered a
realistic, yet slightly loud listening level in a home environment, for such a sound type.
Sound 2 & 10, Baby cry & Scream:
Sound 2 is a sound sample of a baby crying inconsolably. Sound 10 consists of two
females(an adult and a child) screaming madly. In sound 10, no words are being spoken
or shouted, and the two individuals always scream at diﬀerent times, i.e. they are not
necessarily related. In both of the two sounds, slight pauses are present, between the
outbursts, but in general, they are both continuous throughout the 32 seconds they last.
The motivation for choosing these sounds were, that both sounds can be prevalent in
a home environment, and will also be audible by neighbours in many cases, due to the
rather high produced sound pressure level. Furthermore, both of these sounds were
deemed as being potentially emotionally aﬀective, which may inﬂuence the subjective
responses[[12],[14]].
The sounds are narrowband, containing most spectral content within the third octave
frequency bands from 500 Hz to 5 kHz. It should be noted, that a low frequency artifact
is present in sound 10, in the one-third octave frequency band of 80 Hz. However, this
artifact only occurs in the beginning of the sound, and is around 20 dB lower than the
most prominent parts of the sound, which are present at the same time. As such, it can
safely be assumed that the artifact have not aﬀected the subjective evaluations of the
sound, as this particular part was most likely inaudible for the participants.
For sound 2, the sound pressure level was set to 70 dB, which was considered a reasonable
level for the nature of the sound[13], however possibly slightly quiet. Sound 10 was set to
80(measured at 79) dB.

compared to the remaining 22 subjects.

To acquire subjects for the experiment, the method was equal for all of the subjects; they
were at some point asked by the author, about whether they would like to participate in
the listening experiment.
Out of the 22 subjects, 12 were Danish, while the nationalities of the remaining 10 subjects
varied between UK(1), Lithuania(1), France(1), Germany(3), Greece(2), Equador(1) and
Norway(1).
Out of the 22 subjects, 13 can be assumed to have a higher than normal knowledge about
acoustics, either being a current student or having studied acoustics.
The genders of the subjects consisted of 18 males and 4 females.
The ages of the subjects varied from 23 to 29, with a mean of 26.3, standard deviation of
1.8 and a median of 27.
The requirement for the subjects to be considered valid for the experiment was that they
had normal hearing. Before conducting the experiment, each of the participants were
asked if they knew whether they had normal hearing or not. If a participant answered
that they did not know, or that it had been a long time since they have had their hearing
examined, an audiogram6 was made for the participant.
The audiograms were made using calibrated equipment, connected to an Interacoustics
AC40 audiometer, speciﬁcally for that purpose, so that high measurement precision was
ensured. The procedure of conducting the audiometry was done by the author, with
instructions from the Hearing Systems department of DTU and in accordance with ISO
8253-1[8].
All subjects were deemed as having normal hearing, with the exception of one, in which a
slight hearing loss was noticed, in one ear. The subject still completed the experiment,
and after comparing this subject’s responses with the remaining participants’ and ensuring
no outliers or deviations were found outside of the standard deviation, it was decided to
include the responses from the subject in the analysis.
When the subjects were asked if they wanted to participate in the study, they were
explained the nature and purpose of the experiment, i.e. that it was an investigation
regarding the relationship between the subjective and objective evaluations of airborne
sound insulation. The participants signed a participation agreement, and was told that
they could end the experiment prematurely at any time if they wished. They were also
told that they could take breaks whenever they wanted, which none of the subjects chose
to do.
The subjects received monetary compensation for the time that they spent doing the
experiment. For most subjects, the fact that they would be compensated for their time,
were not known until after the experiment had been done.

3.6
Subjective evaluations and instructions
In the beginning of the experiment, the subjects were seated in a chair at a table con-
taining a screen, mouse and keyboard, which were used by the participants to give their
evaluations of the sounds. Before starting the experiment, the subjects were explained the
purpose of the experiment, and that they would be giving their subjective evaluations of
84 sounds. The subjects were instructed in how to use the PC, how to control the pace of
the experiment, and that the loudness of the sounds they would hear could vary widely,
with some sounds being inaudible.
The subjective evaluations from the participants were given in terms disturbance and unac-
ceptability, following the recommendations of ISO 15666[5] on noise annoyance assessment,
by using both a verbal and numerical scale.
The evaluation of disturbance was done on an 11-point numerical scale from 0 to 10, where
0 was denoted "Not at all disturbing" and 10 was denoted "Extremely disturbing". For
the disturbance evaluation, the subjects were presented with the question "On a scale
from 0 (not audible) to 10 (extremely disturbing), how disturbing is the sound?". The
subjects were also verbally instructed to set the slider to 0, in case the presented sound
was not audible, to further clarify this extreme of the scale. The evaluation was done
using a continuous slider.
The evaluation of unacceptability was done on a 5-point verbal scale, in which the points
were denoted "Not at all", "Slightly", "Moderately", "Very", and "Extremely". The
35
==PAGE:47==
3. EXPERIMENTAL SETUP
unacceptability rating was presented with the phrase "Imagining you are at home in a
relaxed state of mind, how unacceptable is the sound?". The evaluation was done by
pressing one out of 5 buttons.
Before rating each sound, the participants were presented with the paragraph "Imagine
you are alone at home in a multi-storey building in silence and peace. You are in a relaxed
mind set. You are reading a magazine or a book or you are browsing the internet and you
start to hear a sound from a neighbouring dwelling behind the wall."
The sounds were presented to each subject in an individually randomized order.
The chosen subjective evaluations of the experiment are in good correspondence with
those used by Hongisto et al., which consisted of loudness, disturbance and acceptability.
In relation to the subjective evaluation of loudness, it is argued in [19] that disturbance
and acceptability "gives more information about the potential negative eﬀects of neighbour
sounds, which are relatively silent but contain information which may disturb the task at
hand.".
The subjective term, unacceptability was used instead of acceptability, to avoid confusion
for the subjects. If acceptability had been used, the two evaluations would have opposite
meanings, i.e. one being positive and one being negative, possibly causing wrong(opposite)
ratings in some cases.
In this regard, it is clear that the two subjective evaluations are of similar nature - this
also became apparent by questions asked by a few subjects, such as "what is the diﬀerence
between the two?". In this regard, the diﬀerence between the two measures were explained
by giving an example in which a sound may be disturbing, yet uncontrollable by the
neighbour, and hence possibly still be acceptable. Further explanation about the two
measures were not given, to avoid any biasing eﬀects, and in all regards, all subjects were
given the same verbal and written instructions.
All instructions were given in English, in which all participants were ﬂuent.
Before the experiment was started, the subjects completed a familiarising- and rehearsal
phase, with the purpose of familiarising the subjects with the practical task of rating the
sounds, and to give the subjects an idea of the loudest and quietest sounds included in the
experiment.
During this phase, the subjects were presented with a total of 16 sounds. The ﬁrst 12
sounds consisted of each of the 12 unique sound samples, through the wall 7, having the
lowest overall sound insulation. In this phase, the 12 sounds were all clearly audible,
familiarising the subjects with the type of sounds in the experiment. After presenting the
12 loudest sounds, 4 of these were presented through wall 1, being either inaudible or very
quiet.
Hereby, the subjective scaling of the participants could be calibrated to the objective
loudness scale of the presented sounds.
During the familiarising phase while listening to the 12 ﬁrst sounds, the subjects were also
asked four diﬀerent questions about their opinion about the presented sound. These four
questions consisted of the following:

All of the ratings that were given by the subjects were done so, using a graphical user

Figure 8: Graphical user interface used by the subjects to rate the sounds and control the

By using the GUI, the subjects had full control of the pace of the experiment, with the
ability to choose when the should start playing, pause, resume and to listen to the sound
again.
An exposure time of 12 seconds was used in the experiment, in which the subjects were
forced to listen to the sound, before being able to give their subjective evaluation of the
sound. This duration was decreased for the familiarising phase, to 6 seconds. During the
familiarising phase, it was noted, that most subjects chose to listen to each full sound
sample of approximately 30 seconds, before evaluating the sound.
The experiment was continuous throughout, and no subjects chose to take any breaks
during this time. After the experiment had been completed, the subjects left the experi-
mentation room, after which most subjects related their experience with the experiment,
gave feedback and opinions. In this regard, the general interest in the experiment did not
appear to vary widely between the subjects that were related to the study of acoustics
and those that were not. The participants were then thanked for their time.
The total amount of time that was spent by each subject on the experiment varied,
depending on how many questions were asked, how interested they were in the experiment,
and whether they had to have an audiogram made or not.
The time spent on the audiometry measure, before the experiment, varied from 15-25
minutes, generally. The familiarising phase was mostly completed in 10-15 minutes. The
ﬁnal experiment took approximately 25-40 minutes.
As such, the entire experiment was completed in approximately 1-1.5 hours, for most
subjects.

The relation between the objective and subjective measures, will be investigated in a
manner similar to the methods that have been applied in previous studies[[19],[30],[32]], by
ﬁtting appropriate equations to each of the 84 sounds, and calculating the related, squared
Pearson’s correlation coeﬃcient, R2, and root-mean square error(RMSE).
Three diﬀerent ﬁttings will be investigated; one using a logistic model, and two using
linear models.
The logistic function follows an "s-shaped" curve, and is useful for describing subjective
data, related to noise exposure, as described in the "Genlyd" report, by Delta[17]. In [17],
it is argued that if all moderators are constant, then a louder perceived noise will result in
larger annoyance.
As such, it can be assumed that using a deﬁned scale from 0-10, all values which, on an
unlimited scale, would fall below 0 will be set to 0, and all values above 10 will be set to
10. Hereby, the responses will follow an s-shape, including the plateau eﬀects of the values
approaching 0 and 10.

4.3
Disturbance - Linear regression
The disturbance ratings of the 84 diﬀerent sounds were also investigated using two linear
regression models. Generally, the methods of earlier studies have varied between using
linear and non-linear data. By linearising the disturbance ratings, the overall method will
be similar to that of Hongisto et al., while also making it possible to investigate whether
the results of the transformed models reﬂects those of the non-linear models.
The ﬁrst linear model was derived by only considering the most linear part of the logistic
model used in the previous sections.
By excluding the most extreme cases of the subjective evaluations, it was possible to
avoid any plateau eﬀects in the data, in which the responses to the sounds were near zero.
The regression was done, using only the subjective evaluations within the range of the
linear part of the logistic regression. The boundaries of the linear part was taken as the
bend-points of the logistic equation, at which the slope of the logistic function starts to
change, approaching the upper and lower plateaus[39].
As such, only the observed data points of which the disturbance ratings were within the
boundaries set by the bend points, were considered in the linear ﬁt.
The resulting regression models for each sound, can be seen in Figure 13, for Rw. It should
be noted, that a diﬀerent scale of the x-axis in Figure 13 was used for sound 7.
The second linear regression model was derived by applying a transformation on the
subjective evaluation of disturbance in order to linearise the responses. The transformation
is described by Equation 14, in accordance with [17].
At = ln
A
u −A
(14)
where At is the transformed subjective evaluation of disturbance, A is the untransformed
disturbance and u is the the upper limit of A(10 for disturbance).
The resulting regression models for each of the 12 sounds can be seen in Figures 14 and 15
for the considered SNQs, Rw, Rw + C50−3150, Rw + C50−5000 and Rw + C100−3150.
In relation the to linear transformed disturbance ratings, these will henceforth be denoted
with the subscript T; disturbanceT.

5. ANALYSIS AND DISCUSSION
5

The ﬁndings of the present study will in the following sections be analyzed and discussed.
Comparisons will be made with the ﬁndings of previous studies, and the main diﬀerences
in the methodological approaches that can have caused any diﬀerences, will be discussed.

General analysis
A general overview of the responses from the participants can be found in Figure 16, where
the average(across subjects) disturbance and unacceptability can be seen, for each of the 12
sound samples, through each of the 7 walls. Larger sized ﬁgures can be found in Appendix C.
In both ﬁgures, it is apparent that the overall dampening of walls 1 and 2 causes quite
low subjective responses for almost all sounds, with the exceptions being the three music
samples, 6, 7 and 8, and sound 11, Scream. Since wall 1 has a higher degree of insulation
across all frequency bands compared to wall 2, it would be expected that all the subjective
ratings of wall 1 are lower. This is generally also the case, except for sound 2 (Baby cry).
Since all parameters of the two sound samples(sound 2 through wall 1 and 2), are the same,
except for the dampening caused by the walls, it is diﬃcult to explain why the average
rating is higher for wall 1 than it is for wall 2. It should, however, be noted that both of
these ratings are quite low and that the slight diﬀerence might be caused by uncertainty
in the responses of these sound samples, which were played at a very low sound level, at
which they are near inaudible.
For wall 3 and 4, which in general have similar transmission losses in the third octave
frequency bands above 100 Hz, it is seen that most sounds achieve approximately the same
ratings. For the sounds 3, 5 and the four music samples, 6, 7, 8 and 9, the diﬀerence in
disturbance ratings seems to be fairly similar, where no large distinguishing eﬀects of the
low frequencies can be seen. In fact, on further inspection of the music sounds, 6-9, it is
found that sound 6 and 9 achieves almost identical disturbance rating, whereas sound 7
and 8 achieves opposite disturbance ratings between the these walls.
Some variation between the two walls are also seen for sound 12(vacuum), where wall 4 is
rated more annoying. This, however is mostly likely caused by the tonal nature of sound
12, which is very audible in the frequency range from 400 Hz to 800 Hz. In this range, it
is seen from Figure 5, that wall 3 also achieves a higher dampening than wall 4. Sound 10
is also rated more disturbing and unacceptable, for wall 3. This is most likely caused by
the slightly higher dampening of wall 4, in the high frequency range.
Comparing wall 5 and wall 6, it is seen from Figure 5, that wall 5 achieves a considerably
higher degree of sound insulation(up to 10 dB) in the low frequency range from 50 Hz to
200 Hz. Above 200 Hz, wall 6, on the other hand, achieves higher insulation (up to 20
dB, compared to wall 5). Considering the mostly high frequency sounds, 2, 4 and 10, a
clear diﬀerence is also found in the subjective evaluations, where wall 5 is rated higher
on the subjective measure. In general, most sounds achieve a higher subjective score
for wall 5, however, the diﬀerences are smaller for sounds of considerable low frequency
content, such as sound 3 and 5. For the two music sounds, 7 and 8, wall 6 is rated more

of dependence between the two measures. Although the dependency is apparent, it is also

5.2
Statistical analysis
To further investigate the diﬀerent parameters related to the subjective evaluations, and
how they potentially inﬂuence the responses of the participants, these parameters have
been analyzed by constructing linear mixed models[26] for each case.
The mixed model is a statistical model, containing both random and ﬁxed eﬀects. The
ﬁxed eﬀects that are included in a mixed model, accounts for factors that can be considered
known, controlled or systematic, whereas the random eﬀects are of uncontrollable nature.
As such, in unison the ﬁxed- and random eﬀects will, in a well-built model, be able to
account for ideally all the variance in an observed data set.
The analyses will all be applied on the linear transformed subjective evaluations of dis-
turbance. The applied transformation is described by Equation 14.
The general form of the mixed model can be described by Equation 15
S = Xβ + α + ϵ
(15)
where S is the dependent observed variable(subjective evaluations in the present study),
X is the coeﬃcient of the ﬁxed eﬀect, β is the vector containing the ﬁxed eﬀect values, α
is the random eﬀect and ϵ is the error term related to unexplained variation in the data.
Several mixed models were designed with the aim of clarifying whether and how the
subjective responses are related to certain objective descriptors of the experiment. Each
model that was designed, was then compared to a similar model with the given eﬀects
excluded. The comparison was done by examining the related p-values, achieved by
through an ANOVA test in the statistical software, R.
A general model was built, in which the ﬁxed eﬀects of wall, sound, average A-weighted
sound pressure level in receive room, and average A-weighted sound pressure level in source
room was investigated. Separate models including one of the individual ﬁxed eﬀects were
ﬁrst compared with a model containing only the random eﬀect of the subjects. The model
including one of the four ﬁxed eﬀects is described by Equation 16.
Sij = A + αi + Bj + ϵij
(16)
where A is the intercept, i is the subscript for the participants i = 1..22, and j is the
subscript for the value of the ﬁxed eﬀect, i.e j = 1..7 for the wall type and j = 1..12 for
the sound type, etc.

given by Equation 17.

Further analysis was done, regarding the four considered SNQs. A model was built,
consisting of either one of the four SNQs as a ﬁxed eﬀect, and the random eﬀect. These
models were then compared with each other, in order to investigate which of the four
SNQs could best explain the variation in the data. First, the model containing Rw as a
ﬁxed eﬀect was compared to the other three models. It was found that no signiﬁcance
was achieved in either case (p = 1 for all cases). This procedure was then repeated for

instead of the remaining two, and using Rw + C50−3150 instead of Rw + C50−5000, reaching
signiﬁcance of p = 2.2e−16 in all of the described cases.
As such, it was found that in general, Rw could be considered the best overall predictor of
the subjective evaluations.

four SNQs. Exceptions were found for the sounds 5, 7, 8 & 9, in which a signiﬁcant

instead. These 4 sounds all contain considerable low frequency energy, possibly explaining
the diﬀerence.

5.4
Findings of earlier studies
The main purpose of the single-number quantities is, to conveniently quantify the sound
insulation of buildings or building elements, which in term should ensure a high degree
of acoustical comfort and satisfaction in a given environment where noise exposure may
be prevalent. Whether the diﬀerent SNQs that are used in building regulations actually
fulﬁl this purpose, have been a focal point in the present study and those that have been
conducted previously, in [[19], [30], [28], [38].
In general, from the results of earlier studies it is apparent that several conclusions about
the eﬀectiveness of the SNQs can be drawn.
First, no single SNQ can be considered a satisfactory predictor of all living sounds, due to
the widely varying frequency content of those sounds. Some neighbour-caused noises may
mainly consist of mid-high frequency content, such as a baby crying, electrical appliances
or pets. On the other hand, sounds which occurs from neighbours playing music from a
stereo set, may include a very high amount of low frequency energy. This is an issue that
has been increasing, with the better and higher quality audio equipment being available
to the consumers, also resulting in increased playback levels[35].
While the mid-high frequency sounds may in general be more prevalent, these types of
sounds are often attenuated to a much higher degree than the low frequency sounds are,
due to the general better sound insulation of partitioning elements at higher frequencies.
As such, it is of importance to determine to which degree the SNQs can predict the
subjective responses regarding all types of sounds.
Secondly, and closely related, it is obvious for all of the SNQs, that the ability to predict
the subjective responses, depends on the frequency content of the sounds that are present.
As such, this raises the question of whether the SNQs should even be compared, since they
"measure" diﬀerent quantities of the subjective responses.
Thirdly, the focal point of the several diﬀerent studies that have been conducted similar
to the present, have been on whether or not the amount of low frequency content present
in the sounds, actually inﬂuence the subjective responses. In this regard, the conclusions
drawn from the previous studies are not entirely in agreement.
In the study done by Mortensen[28], 5 walls were included with varying low frequency
SRI values and equal mid-high frequency SRI values. For the airborne neighbour noise of
music, which was included it was found that the annoyance ratings of the sounds coming
through lightweight walls were generally higher than when they were presented through
heavyweight walls with higher low frequency insulation. As such, it was concluded that the
subjective responses were dependent on the low frequencies of the music sample. Hereby,
this study signiﬁes that to ensure a high degree of acoustical comfort in dwellings, SNQs
which do take the low frequencies into account should be used by regulations.
The study conducted by Park et al.[30] included 20 simulated walls with varying SRI values
across the one-third octave bands from 50 Hz to 5000 Hz. The airborne sounds of music
and speech was included, representing a a sound sample with signiﬁcant low frequency
content(music), and a sound sample with mainly mid-high frequency content(speech). In
this experiment, it was found by Park et al., that the best predictors of the annoyance

that puts highest emphasis on the high frequencies. For the music sound, the three SNQs
including variations of the traﬃc-adaptation term, Rw + Ctr,100−3150, Rw + Ctr,50−5000 and

In the study by Hongisto et al., 9 diﬀerent wall types of varying SRI values, and 6 diﬀerent

It is concluded by Hongisto et al., that the 50-80 Hz third octave bands should not be
included in the SNQ, for predicting the subjective evaluations of airborne sound insulation.
Finally, the study by Rychtáriková et al.[38], investigated the SNQ, Rw + C50−5000 as a
descriptor of perceived loudness. Two walls were included; a heavyweight and a lightweight,
containing very diﬀerent SRI values. A range of living sounds were included, of which half
included signiﬁcant low frequency content. It was found by Rychtáriková et al., that when
the sounds were played at a realistic presentation level (20-40 dB LAeq was used in the
study), the heavyweight wall was rated as being the loudest, and it was concluded that
Rw + C50−5000 overestimates the importance of low frequencies. When the presentation
levels were increased 30 dB, the lightweight wall was rated as loudest. As such, it was
argued by Rychtáriková et al., that only at realistically high presentation levels will the
low frequencies become important.

5.6
Methodology of earlier studies
It is clear that the overall methodology that is applied while conducting a listening ex-
periment regarding airborne sound insulation, has a signiﬁcant eﬀect on the results that
are found. As such, it could be argued that all of the inﬂuencing parameters related to
the methodology should be reasonably similar, to be able to make realistic comparisons
between the studies.
In the study by Mortensen, the focal point of the experiment was to determine the im-
portance and inﬂuence of the amount of low frequency sound insulation of walls. As such,
5 walls was used, with varying SRI values below 160 Hz and with equal SRI values above
160 Hz. Mortensen found that the walls which had the lowest SRI values in the considered
20-160 Hz frequency range received the highest subjective ratings.
In this regard, it could be argued that the study was rather an investigation of whether a
higher presentation level of the sounds in the low frequency range actually had an impact
on the subjective evaluations. Considering the experiment from this point of view, it is
obvious that the walls with the least amount of sound insulation will receive the highest
subjective evaluations. These results do not, however reveal anything about the actual
degree of importance of the low frequencies, since there are no other walls to compare
with, in which the SRI values vary in other frequency bands.
In the study, only one sound sample was used, which was a music sample. The sound
sample included signiﬁcant low frequency content, making it suitable for the purpose of
the experiment. However, due to the extensive amount of diﬀerent living sounds that
occupants of a dwelling can be subjected to, it is not a justiﬁed assumption to make, that
this particular sound sample should represent the subjective evaluations of all such living
sounds in general.
The presentation levels used by Mortensen are generally very high. Mortensen used a
source level of 100 dB LAeq, which is indeed a signiﬁcantly louder source level than found
in any of the other similar studies. As such, it is possible that the resulting subjective
responses are based on unrealistically high presentation levels, which would not occur in a
71
==PAGE:83==
5. ANALYSIS AND DISCUSSION
typical home environment. Furthermore, as described above, the very high presentation
level will likely have caused a signiﬁcantly increased loudness perception for the low
frequencies in particular. It is for the low frequency range hereby very important, to use
realistic presentation levels, as the perception of the low frequencies will rather quickly
shift from barely audible to very loud, compared to the mid-high frequencies.
The exposure time used by Mortensen was 2 minutes., which is higher than what is used
in other studies. It is a safe assumption, that the noise exposure time has an eﬀect on
the subjective evaluations of annoyance and disturbance[25], and that the high exposure
time is a better representation of a realistic scenario for most sounds. For most neighbour
noises, it is unlikely that the sound will only be present for less than a minute, especially
for a music type sound.
Finally, the analysis that was conducted by Mortensen, was based on SRI values extending
down to 20 Hz, at which the values were set to be equal to that of the corresponding
SRI value at 50 Hz, since no data was available on the walls below 50 Hz. As such, an
assumption is made about the sound insulation below 50 Hz, possibly basing the results
on untrue SRI values. However, it is unknown to which degree these very low frequencies
will actually have aﬀected the subjective evaluations, since some will possibly be inaudible.
It is clear, however, that an increased exposure time will increase the overall time that
each subject will have to spend on the experiment, making it diﬃcult to increase, if many
sound types or walls are being used in the experiment.
The age of the subjects varied from 16 years to 52 years, representing a rather broad range
of ages. It is to be expected, that not only is the hearing threshold diﬀerent between
the subjects, but also the individual sensitivity towards noise exposure. The eﬀect of age
on the responses was conﬁrmed by Mortensen, which stated that the annoyance ratings
generally increased with the age of the participants. It is clear, that covering a broad range
of ages, and an equal amount of both genders for the participants, will best represent the
general population.
In the study by Park et al., a total of 6 sounds were used; three of which consisted of
speech samples, and three which consisted of music sample of diﬀerent genres. As such,
these two sound types were well represented, however, they do not necessarily represent
other types of living noises.
The overall aim of the study was not focused on low frequencies, but rather ﬁnding the
best descriptor for the subjective evaluations. As such, 20 walls was included with varying
SRI values across the third octave frequency bands, and with a range of Rw values from
37 dB to 56 dB. These Rw values are rather low, and represent only the worst cases of
partitioning elements in regard to airborne sound insulation, as seen in the classiﬁcation
schemes in Table 2.
The overall eﬀect of using walls with low sound insulation will be, that the presented
sounds in the receive room will be increased.
The source room levels were set to 72.1 dB LAeq and 75.2 dB LAeq for the music and speech
samples respectively. The sound pressure level of the speech sample may in this regard be
considered to be higher than typical conversation levels.
The background noise that was present in the listening room was very low (reported as 12
dBA), which was then artiﬁcially increased, using a broadband noise with considerable
low frequency energy. The resulting background noise used in the experiment was 34.7
72
==PAGE:84==

dBA, which may be higher than what is typically present in dwellings at the most silent
periods, during which neighbour noises will also be most annoying. The eﬀect of having a
background noise present at a too high level will be, that some sounds of the experiment
are possibly masked by the background noise[21].
The presentation order of the sounds were randomized; however only 3 diﬀerent randomiz-
ation orders were used for all subjects. Whether or not the use of just 3 diﬀerent orders
has an eﬀect on the subjective responses is unknown.
In the study by Hongisto et al., 6 living sounds were included, representing a broader
range of typical sounds that can occur in a home environment. However, only 2 of those
sounds included noticeable energy in the 50-100 Hz range. Furthermore, out of those 2
sounds, only 1 could be considered to contain signiﬁcant low frequency energy, with a
traﬃc noise-like spectrum. Hongisto et al. states that the results of the study signify that
the low frequency extension down to 50 Hz, of the SNQs, is not advisable. It is clear, that
there is an overwhelming weight on the high frequencies in this study, since most sounds
did not include signiﬁcant low frequency energy. As such, the concluding statement about
the low frequency importance can only be considered to be viable for that particular study.
The subjective evaluations consisted of disturbance, loudness and acceptability. It is unclear
whether and to which degree the use of either annoyance or disturbance has an eﬀect
on the subjective evaluations. Some participants may perceive the two terms diﬀerently,
resulting in potentially diﬀerent responses. In the study by Mortensen, both of these
measures were included, and slight diﬀerences were found in the responses. It was reported
that the diﬀerence between an overall level increase and a level increase for just the low
frequencies were smaller for disturbance than for annoyance. However, the diﬀerences that
can occur when using these subjective evaluations may only be present in a study that
includes both, since the subjects are, in that case, aware that the two measures are not
necessarily the same.
The presentation order of the sounds consisted of a pseudo-randomized order, in which one
sound was presented through all of the diﬀerent walls, before a new sound was presented,
repeating the process. As such, the participants heard the same sound 9 times through
diﬀerent ﬁlters, after which a new sound was presented. This may increase the participants
attention towards the diﬀerences in the wall type, since the presented signal is kept the
same. In this case, an increased contrast would likely be seen in the evaluations of the
diﬀerent walls. However, using such a presentation order can not be considered a realistic
representation of how neighbour noises occur. In eﬀect, the inﬂuence of using such a
pseudo-randomized presentation order is not known, and hence it may be advised to use a
randomised order to avoid potentially related issues.
The source room sound pressure level of the sounds were set at a level at which they were
barely audible through the wall that achieved the highest amount of sound insulation,
with an Rw value of 75.1 dB. As such, the levels were not necessarily set at realistic levels
that represent a realistic situation. The sound, Loud speech was set to 82 dB LAeq, which
is 15+ dB above typical conversation levels and hence can not be considered to give a
realistic representation of this sound type.
It is mentioned by Hongisto et al., that to ensure that the presented sounds only contain
frequencies which are considered by the SNQs, a bandpass ﬁlter was applied to all of the
sounds. Hereby, the frequencies outside the range of 50-5000 Hz should be attenuated,

not necessarily remove the frequencies outside of the given range, but rather decreases the
inﬂuence of those.
The analysis that was conducted, mainly consisted of calculating and comparing the R2
values between the diﬀerent SNQs and presented sounds. However, no further analysis
was conducted in relation to what may have caused the results and to which degree of
certainty it can be concluded that the best overall predictors of the subjective measures

In the review paper by Rindel[18], it is argued that the sound pressure level of the sound

energy, the emphasis was rather on the mid-high frequencies, in the determination of the
best overall SNQ and as such, may not provide a truthful picture of which SNQ can be
considered best.
The study by Rychtáriková et al. used a diﬀerent overall methodology compared to the
previously mentioned. The aim of the study was to investigate the loudness perception
of sounds coming through two walls, and as such, an experimental setup was used, to
decrease any other inﬂuencing factors, such as background noise or the eﬀect of a room.
The participants were seated in an anechoic chamber, and the sounds were presented
through headphones. As such, no background noise was present during the experiment,
avoiding the inﬂuence of a potential masking eﬀect. In this regard, it is again arguable,
in which manner using an experimental setup, rather than a realistic setup, inﬂuences
the subjective evaluations of the presented sounds. The sounds that are presented to the
participants may be more audible and unaﬀected by external factors, however, the setup
does not represent a realistic situation in a dwelling.
The two walls that were used in the study consisted of a light- and heavyweight wall. The
walls achieved equal values for the SNQs that included the 50-100 Hz frequency range.
However, for the SNQs that only considers frequencies above 100 Hz, the lightweight wall
achieved signiﬁcantly higher values, with a diﬀerence of 13 dB and 15 dB for Rw +C100−5000
and Rw + C100−3150 respectively. Very large diﬀerences was seen in the SRI values of the
walls, in the 200-2000 Hz range, with a maximum diﬀerence of 20 dB. It is clear that in
such a comparison, only the sounds that contain a very signiﬁcant amount of low frequency
energy will be rated as being louder through the lightweight wall. As such, the study can
be considered an investigation of whether the 50-80(from which the walls had equal SRI)
Hz range is more important for loudness perception, than the 80-3000 Hz frequency range.
The sounds that were included in the experiment consisted of a wide range of recordings
from several dwellings, to ensure that realistic sounds were used, and that a good overall
representation of all living sounds were used. It is reported that 51% percent of the sounds
consisted of music "with substantial bass content". However, no further information is
given about the spectra of the sounds.
The listening test was carried out with a 2-AFC procedure, which is a diﬀering method

5.7
Methodology of present study
In the present study, 12 diﬀerent sounds were included. These sounds consisted of varying
frequency content, and two sounds contained a high degree of informational or emotional
content, i.e. Baby cry and Scream. The sounds were chosen to represent a wide range
of possible realistic neighbour noises, and to adequately cover the full frequency range
of the considered SNQs, from 50 Hz to 5000 Hz. The sounds without signiﬁcant low
frequency content, consisted of both emotional and non-emotional content. The sounds
with signiﬁcant low frequency content, consisted of four music samples and three non-music
samples. As such, a wide variation of sounds types, and corresponding spectral content
was included.
Regarding the spectral content of the songs; in retrospect, the two sounds Blender and
Vacuum were intended to represent broadband mid-high frequency sounds. However,
Vacuum in particular, included a low frequency artifact at 100 Hz, which excludes this
particular sound from being completely rid of low frequency energy. Nonetheless, the eﬀect
was not seen in the results, as the SNQs that best predicted the subjective evaluations
of this sound was Rw and STCno8 one of which takes the 100 Hz third octave band into
account, and one that does not. Hence, it appears that the subjective evaluations were
not based on the 100 Hz artifact.
Such low frequency components were also seen for Blender and Scream; however, for
these sounds, the low frequency components were around 20 dB lower than the the most
prominent parts, which justiﬁes the assumption that the low frequency components which
can be seen in the measured spectra, did not inﬂuence the subjective evaluations. This
assumption is in good agreement with the results, in which it was found that the worst
predictors were the SNQs that include the 50 Hz extension.
The pre-processing of the sound ﬁles consisted of two diﬀerent ﬁlters. A bandpass ﬁlter
was applied, with the aim of removing frequency components outside the range of those
considered by the included SNQs, i.e. outside the 50-5000 Hz range. However, applying
a bandpass ﬁlter in the manner described in Section 3, does not completely remove the
frequency components outside of the given range, but rather attenuates these components.
An eﬀect of applying the bandpass ﬁlter is also, that the sounds do not sound natural,
but become rather harsh. It is unknown whether this may aﬀect the subjective responses,
and the application of the bandpass ﬁlter is generally a trade-oﬀbetween ensuring a more
truthful picture of what the SNQs actually measure, at the cost of further sound processing
and realistic sounds. Furthermore, in a realistic scenario, it is to be expected that some
sound types, mainly music, will contain frequencies both above and below the 50-5000
Hz range. As such, for the general purpose of ensuring attenuation of all sounds, the
75
==PAGE:87==
5. ANALYSIS AND DISCUSSION
application of such a bandpass ﬁlter may not be advisable.
The second ﬁlter that was applied, was the room-ﬁlter, with the aim of ensuring an
appropriate presentation of all of the considered third octave bands. The eﬀect of this
ﬁlter could also be heard in the sounds, but to a much lesser degree. It is assumed that
the application of this ﬁlter did not aﬀect the subjective responses noticeably.
The levels at which the sounds were set, were chosen mainly to represent realistic levels
as they may occur in a home environment. In this regard, it is seen that compared to
the previous studies, the overall trend is that the levels were lower, for most sounds in
the present study. Determining the levels at which to set the sounds is again a trade-oﬀ
between using realistic levels at the cost of non-zero subjective ratings of the sounds.
When a high number of sounds are inaudible, these will achieve very low(ideally zero)
subjective ratings, which in turn results in fewer data points from which the regressions
and corresponding R2 values can be found. As described in Section 3, a mistake was made,
in which the sounds were presented at a level 3.5 dB lower than intended. In relation to
the previous studies, this eﬀect caused an even larger diﬀerence in the presented levels,
compared to those of previous studies. Yet, it was found that the results corresponded
reasonably well with those of previous studies.
The walls which were included in the present study, covered a wide range of Rw values,
from to 45.5 dB to 74.8 dB. Furthermore, the walls were chosen to represent diﬀerent wall
types, reﬂected in the SRI values, in which a maximum variation of 20-30 dB was achieved
in all considered third octave frequency bands.
By including more walls in the study, the contrast in the results may have been higher,
simply because any statistical analyis is highly dependent on the sample size, of which in
the present study was limited by the number of walls. All of the regression models are
dependent on the number of walls that are included, to get the best possible ﬁt. As such,
a high wall number would result in an increased converging of the results.
As mentioned previously, the amount of time that the participants have to spend on the
experiment is obviously dependent on three factors: the amount of sounds, the amount of
walls, and the exposure time, i.e. the duration of the sounds.
From previous studies, in which estimates of the amount of time spent for each participant
were given, a similar estimate was made for the present study. In this regard, it was
considered reasonable compromise to include an exposure time of 12 seconds, for the
participants to fully determine the two subjective measures of each sound. By using an
exposure time of 12 seconds, it was possible to keep the total duration of the experiment
within 60-90 minutes. In this regard, it was noted that several subjects mentioned that
the expected duration of a sound type would probably aﬀect their given evaluations. An
example was the sound, Vacuum, for which a few subjects mentioned that they did not
expect the sound to be present for long, hereby making it less disturbing and unacceptable.
The subjective evaluations of disturbance and unacceptability, were included in the present
study. These subjective measures were chosen as a compromise between using terms that
would be comparable with those used by Hongisto et al., while at the same time following
the recommendations given by ISO 15666[5], including both a numerical and verbal scale.
In the study by Mortensen, both measures were included, and while a slight diﬀerence was

seen between the two, this diﬀerence was mainly present as an overall shift in the ratings,
as the annoyance ratings were slightly higher than disturbance ratings, in all cases.
The participants consisted of both males and females. However, a large percentage of the
participants were male. To best be able to represent a broad population, and ensure that
the study is viable for as many cases as possible, may be important to include participants
of all ages and both genders. In the present study, the participants were rather asked if
they would like to participate in the experiment, and were not hired for the purpose. As
such, it was not possible to ensure that an equal spread in age and gender was included.
It was found by Mortensen, that females rated the sounds high in all subjective measures -
however, the diﬀerence between the male and female responses were mostly seen as an
overall shift, meaning that the trends in the responses were the same.
A large spread was also seen, regarding the nationalities of the participants of the present
study. It is possible that diﬀerences exist in the overall sensitivity to noise exposure between
individuals, caused by diﬀerences in expectations and previous experiences, across diﬀerent
countries. No noticeable variation was found between Danish subjects and subjects from
other nations, however.
The results that were found in the present study, were analyzed in several manners, in
order to best ensure validity of those results.
Three diﬀerent regression models were used, of which the corresponding coeﬃcient of
determination, R2, was calculated for each sound and model. Park et al., used a regression
model based on the Boltzmann equation, and Hongisto et al. used a linear regression. In
the present study, the eﬀect of using either one was investigated, showing very similar
results.
Furthermore, a regression was done across all of the observed data points, to further
examine which of the SNQs could be considered the best overall predictor of the subjective
measures. In this regard, a bootstrap analysis was conducted, to clarify to which degree of
certainty these ﬁndings can be assumed true.
Finally, a mixed model analysis was conducted, in which diﬀerent aspects of both the
source- and receive signals and wall parameters were investigated.
By analysing the results in diﬀerent manners, it is clear that statements about which SNQ
is the best predictor for certain purposes, can be made with a higher certainty.
As mentioned previously, the results and corresponding conclusions that can be drawn
from a study such as the present, is highly dependent on the experimental setup that is
being used.
Overall, it is found that in many cases, decisions have to be made about whether the
nature of the experimental setup should be done with a high degree of correlation with
a real scenario; i.e. realistic sound levels, taking the eﬀect of the room into account,
using unmodiﬁed sounds, e.g. Or, whether the experiment should be based on the aim
of achieving more distinguishable results, from which clear trends and tendencies can be
seen.
Both of these aspects of the experimental setup are of high value, however, not easily
achieved simultaneously.

6. CONCLUSION
6
Conclusion
In the present study, an investigation was carried out with the aim of determining the
relationship between the subjective and objective measures of airborne sound insulation.
The investigation was performed by conducting a listening experiment in which subjects
rated 12 diﬀerent sounds, presented through 7 diﬀerent walls.
The responses from the subjects and corresponding correlation with 11 considered single-
number quantities were analyzed using three diﬀerent regression models, consisting of two
linear models and a logistic regression model. The relationship between four SNQs of
main consideration in the study was also investigated with an overall regression across
all observed responses, and a bootstrap analysis was made, to investigate the certainty of
which any diﬀerences could be concluded.
Furthermore, the responses were analysd using a generalized mixed model, in which
diﬀerent parameters and aspects of the experimental setup was included and examined.
It was found, that the overall best predictor of the subjective responses, taking all of
the 12 diﬀerent living sounds into account, was Rw. However, the diﬀerence between
the conventional Rw and variations including the C-adaptation term, with an extended
frequency range, were not signiﬁcant.
The best predictors for the individual sound types varied, and it was found that for the
sounds that include signiﬁcant low frequency energy, the SNQs that either contain the
Ctr-adaptation term, and/or include the 50-80 Hz third octave frequency bands achieved
the highest squared correlation coeﬃcients.
The mixed model analysis conﬁrmed that the eﬀects of source- and receive room sound
pressure level, Zwicker loudness, and amount of low- and high frequency energy contained
in the sounds, all aﬀected the subjective evaluations.
In general, it can be concluded that no single SNQ can satisfyingly predict the subjective
evaluations of all living sounds, simply because the frequency content of those sounds
varies signiﬁcantly. As such, it is apparent that the eﬀectiveness of the SNQs as predictors
of the subjective measures depends heavily on whether or not the frequency content of the
sounds is equal to the frequency range considered by SNQs.
Regarding the importance of low frequencies, it is apparent that while the low frequencies
may not be important at low levels, the actual inﬂuence of the low frequencies increases at
higher presentation levels.
Because of the rather small diﬀerences in the overall prediction eﬀectiveness between
Rw and the variations including the C-adaptation term, including the 50-80 Hz third
octave bands, it is from the present study considered a better overall solution to use either
Rw + C50−3150 or Rw + C50−5000 for building regulations.
This is particularly the case, when considering the actual purpose of the use of SNQs in
reguilations; to provide adequate sound insulation for occupants and ensure a high degree
of acoustical comfort. Even though the use of Rw would likely result in satisfactory sound
insulation for most living sounds, it is not guaranteed that it would do so, for sounds that
include signiﬁcant low frequency content.

7. FURTHER INVESTIGATION
7
Further investigation
It has been seen that many factors can inﬂuence the results of a listening experiment such
as that of the present study. As such, it is clear that it is of great importance to specify
exactly what the main purpose of the investigation should be, and let the surrounding
parameters of the experiment be set to best achieve the goal that has been set.
If the only purpose is to ﬁnd the SNQ which achieves the highest correlation for all living
sounds, then a large range of sound should be included. However, if the main purpose of
the investigation is to clarify the importance of low frequency content in the subjective
evaluation, then it may be better to include fewer sounds, but with very speciﬁc variations
in the 50-80 Hz third octave bands.
Hereby, it becomes less diﬃcult to determine exactly which parameters of the experiment
aﬀected the results.
Through the present study, the application of the mixed model analysis has proven to
be highly useful. It is suggested to include this analysis tool in future studies, and to
develop more elaborate models, to extract further information about the relationship of
the subjective and objective measures.
A general mixture of both realistic and non-realistic parameters has been used in the
present and related previous studies. As such, a general trade-oﬀbetween a high con-
trast/certainty of the results at the cost of realistic scenarios have been seen.
It may be, that these types of experiments should rather be conducted in a manner similar
to that of Rychtáriková et al., in which the experiment used a laboratory, and as such more
unrealistic setup. By using a such a method, the results become clearer and unaﬀected by
other factors, such as background- and external noises.
Furthermore, approaching the investigation from a more theoretical approach, rather than
realistic/practical, it would be possible to better examine speciﬁc parts of the experiment.
For example, instead of using general living sounds, it could be of interest to use artiﬁcial
sounds that have been processed for a speciﬁc purpose. By using sounds with varying low
frequency modulation, it would be possible to investigate the eﬀect of the thumping type
noise and bass variations, found in music sounds.
It could also be of interest, to further investigate the eﬀect of loudness in diﬀerent sound
types. By presenting a broad range of spectrally diﬀerent sounds, all presented at the
same level, the importance of the actual frequency content would become clearer. In this
regard, it may also be necessary to use artiﬁcial sounds, so that any informational content
can be avoided.
It appears that the overall research question can be approached from two diﬀerent per-
spectives; whether the task at hand is to ﬁnd the SNQ that best predict the subjective
responses for a wide range of living sounds, or whether it is to ensure protection against
noise for occupants of multi-storey dwellings.
If the latter is the case, then it may be necessary to redirect the aim of these types of
experiments, towards an investigation of how total protection is ensured, and in this regard,
which sounds are most annoying, which are most prevalent and which walls best attenuate
these sounds.

It would be of great value to conduct additional surveys in actual dwellings, to give further
clariﬁcation to these questions.
It is reported by Rindel[18], that the sounds that causes a large amount of noise complaints,
and most likely generate the highest annoyance and disturbance, are the music type sounds
with signiﬁcant low frequency content.
Further research may be necessary, to be able to conclude exactly which types of noises

future studies. Furthermore, an extension could be to analyze exactly what parts of those
particular sounds causes the high subjective ratings. As such, specifying the overall most
annoying or disturbing sound would make it possible to determine which SNQ should be
applied in building regulations to ensure protection against those noise sources.
