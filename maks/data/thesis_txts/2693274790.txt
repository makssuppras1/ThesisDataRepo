ABSTRACT
With a tremendous increase in the growth and potential of wind energy there is an increased
need for the improvement in technology to ﬁnd better ways to make wind farms more efﬁ-
cient and economically fancier. Wind farm layout optimization helps tackle this problem by
producing more efﬁcient layouts as compared to those from traditional approaches. This thesis
proposes an integrated approach to the wind farm layout optimization problem to check if it
produces better results than the sequential approach currently practised. The optimizations
are done with respect to the Annual energy production (AEP) and the cable costs. In the
sequential approach the layout is ﬁrst optimised with respect to AEP and then the cable layout
is optimised exclusively and the IRR is hence calculated. The integrated approach solves the
problem by taking the IRR as the optimisation function and the AEP and cable costs as design
variables. This ensures the trade-offs are evaluated in a more efﬁcient manner as compared to
the sequential approach. The entire study was made using TOPFARM which is an open-source
python library created by DTU for the purpose of wind farm optimization.
The results show that the integrated approach produces better results than the sequential
approach. It does so by maximizing the AEP and/or minimizing the cable cost. Counter-
intuitively it also produced a better result by increasing the AEP and the cable cost to achieve
a better IRR as compared to it’s counterpart.
Furthermore a practical use case of the integrated optimization was carried out by multi-
objective optimization to establish a pareto front between AEP and cable costs. Various sensi-
tivity analyses were done for the same.

INTRODUCTION
"We are the ﬁrst generation to feel the impact of climate change and the last generation that
can do something about it"[1]. One of the many ways to tackle the growing problem of climate
change is the adoption of renewable sources of energy. Wind energy is one of the fastest
growing industries in the world at the moment. The IEA’s world energy outlook predicts wind
energy to be the leading source of energy in the European union by 2027 among other sources
such as gas, nuclear, solar, coal and oil[2].
Figure 1.1: Forecast of wind industry [2]
In order to reach the goals set by the countries to tackle climate change and also to reduce to
cost of wind energy to make it even more affordable, major advancements in technology are the

need of the hour. The levelized cost of energy (LCOE) is a metric which can be used to compare
alternative methods of energy production. The LCOE can be seen as the average minimum
price at which the electricity generated by the asset is required to be sold in order to offset the
total cost of production in it’s lifetime[3]. In wind energy the LCOE is calculated by taking
into account the capital cost(CAPEX), the operational cost(OPEX), the ﬁxed charge rate(FCR)
and the annual energy production(AEP) of the wind farm. Advancements in technology help
reduce the LCOE of the wind industry which in turn reduce the cost of electricity produced.
The ultimate aim is to completely transition in to renewables as the only source of energy.
As mentioned in [4] this is done by a three step process. The phase one is to ensure that the
electricity produced by new solar and wind projects costs less than those produced by new
coal and gas plants.In phase two, renewable energy reaches parity with power generated by
legacy fossil fuel plants and in phase three, clean energy becomes cheaper than that produced
by existing thermal plants.
The trends concerning the LCOE of the renewable are promising as can be seen in the ﬁgure
below. LCOE for onshore wind energy is currently at around 0.039$/kWh which falls signiﬁ-
cantly lower than the fossil fuel cost range. Though there has been a considerable reduction in
the LCOE for offshore wind which stands at 0.084$/kWh, it still has not gone below the fossil
fuel cost range.
Figure 1.2: LCOE trends between renewables and fossil fuels [5]
Optimization is a topic that is familiar in the world of engineering in areas with com-
plex systems including numerous variables. Wind farms being complex systems fall into this
category and can beneﬁt out of optimization methods to ﬁnd the most optimum solutions.
Wind farm layout optimization is done to ﬁnd the most optimal position of the wind turbines
within the wind farm. The optimization can be done with respect to either a single objective
or multiple objectives. The optimization process helps maximise output from the wind farms
2
==PAGE:17==
which in-turn helps reduce the LCOE of the wind farm.
This project investigates a wind farm layout optimization approach in contrary to a conven-
tional approach being practised at the moment to check if the newly proposed method brings
better gains in the life cycle of the project.
1.0.1
Project motivation
The motivation for the project is to challenge a practice that has been the norm in the industry.
The planning of a wind farm takes place in a sequential manner. The life cycle of a project starts
with the prospecting stage. This is followed by the siting and development phase. Once done,
the project goes into the construction phase and eventually starts operations [6]. The wind
farm design is explained in detail in section 2.1. The period between siting and construction
can take anywhere between 3 to 5 years [6]. During the siting phase of the project the wind
turbine positions are placed taking into account the wind resource and the AEP of the wind
turbine and this is followed by drawing the array cable layout speciﬁc to the ﬁxed wind turbine
positions. This initial layout goes into multiple iterations, owing to various factors that could
affect the current position of the wind turbines during the siting phase up until the project is
commissioned. An alternative to this sequential approach is an integrated approach wherein
the optimal position of the wind turbines are determined taking into account both the AEP
and the cable costs. This optimization problem falls under the NP-hard category of complexity
[7]. This would result in an exponential increase in the time taken to solve a large instance of
the problem.
Since we aim to determine the optimal turbine positions with respect to AEP and cable costs
potentially 3 years before the installation phase, there also exists a challenge to address any
potential variations in the cable costs and the resulting optimized layouts.
The optimization processes are carried out using TOPFARM [8], an open-source python library
created for the purpose of wind farm optimizations. It uses OPENMDAO [9] packages for
optimization and Pywake [10] packages for AEP computations with the use of various wake
models. TOPFARM offers a wide range of ﬂexibility as compared to it’s counterparts such as
Openwind [11] which is widely used in the industry. Unlike commercial softwares, TOPFARM
allows individual developers to couple their own wake or cost model to the existing built-in
set for custom optimizations. This project also aims to test and showcase the capabilities of
TOPFARM that will be beneﬁcial to wind farm developers from a practical stand point.
1.0.2
Project objectives
The objectives of the project lie between comparing the sequential approach and the integrated
approach while also showcasing the capabilities and functionalities of TOPFARM. The speciﬁc
objectives include:

• Comparison between various optimization algorithms namely- Sequential least square

1.0.3
Project description
Although the integrated approach which is proposed in this project is a general concept which
applies to both onshore and offshore wind farms, the wind farm used to obtain the results

Concept of optimization
Optimization is deﬁned as the process of ﬁnding the "best" possible solution for a problem
by changing variables that can be controlled, often subject to constraints [13]. It involves the
process of ﬁnding the maxima or minima of a particular objective function. Optimization is a
method of solving a problem which has it’s application in various ﬁelds such as economics,
biology, physics and engineering. Optimization applied to engineering systems with multiple
disciplines is termed Multi-disciplinary optimization (MDO). Wind farms are systems where
multiple disciplines come together to work in tandem in order to contribute to the objective.
A simple analogy for the concept of optimization would be the case of a shower faucet with
hot water and cold water taps. Here the right temperature of the water would be the objective.
One could achieve a range of different water temperatures by adjusting the hot and cold taps
which would be the variables for this problem. The constraints for this problem would be

LITERATURE REVIEW
One of the earliest works in wind farm layout optimization was done by Mosetti et al. [18].
The work explored layout optimization using genetic algorithm. Optimization was done with
respect to both energy production and cost of wind farm over the entire lifetime of the project.
This work paved the path for future research related to wind farm optimization. This work
was followed by multiple research activities where the wind farm optimization problem has
been explored with various optimization algorithms.
Wind farm layout optimization problems can be broadly split based on whether the layout is
split into pre-divided grid points for possible wind turbine locations [18] or continuous search
space throughout the layout of the wind farm [19] [20].
In general optimization algorithms are categorized as gradient-based and gradient-free algo-
rithms. Gradient-based algorithms is based on solving problems by computing derivatives.
Gradient-free algorithms are used when the derivative of the objective function is not available.
Gradient-free algorithms are more popular in wind farm optimization problems due to factors
such as computational time and the ability to identify global minima. This will be explained
in detail in the next chapter. Among gradient-free algorithms, genetic algorithm is popularly
seen in the literature [21] [22]. Other algorithms include Random search algorithm [23] [24],
particle swarm algorithms [25] [26] among others. This thesis explores sequential least square
programming, random search and genetic algorithm for the study.
There has been limited literature when it comes to integrated optimization in wind farms. Of
the two most notable works, the one by S.Sanchez et al. [25] explores the difference between
sequential and integrated approaches for a reference offshore wind farm. The integrated opti-
mization is done with respect to the energy production, electrical system and support structure.
The optimization algorithm chosen was the particle swarm algorithm. The comparison metric
16
==PAGE:31==
used for the work was the LCOE of the wind farm. The ﬁndings from the work show that the
integrated approach consistently outperforms the sequential approach even though. The major
reduction in costs between the two approaches was seen in the costs relating to the electrical
collection. The difference between the mentioned project and this thesis is that this thesis does
not consider the support structure optimization module. When it comes to the optimization
algorithms the thesis compares SLSQP, Random search and Genetic algorithms. Particle swarm
optimization was not considered as it was not a part of the in-house optimizers available as
part of the TOPFARM library. LCOE is used as the evaluating metric in the mentioned project
whereas IRR is used as the evaluating metric in this thesis.
A.Pillai et al. [26] also performed optimization of offshore wind farms using particle swarm
optimization. The optimization coupled the AEP module, electrical cabling module and the
cost module. The LCOE was again used as a comparison metric in this research. The ﬁndings
of the work conclude that the particle swarm optimization provides better LCOE as compared
to the genetic algorithm. It also goes on to claim that with the particle swarm algorithm results
were to be obtained at a lower computational cost as compared to the genetic algorithm.

4.4.1
SLSQP
SLSQP is short for Sequential least square quadratic programming. It is a gradient based
optimization algorithm. It is used for computing non linearly convex optimization problems.
The requirement for SLSQP is that the objective function is twice differentiable. Gradient
based optimization algorithms search the design space by computing derivatives for the
objective function. Gradient based algorithms always end up ﬁnding the local minima or
maxima. The derivative gives the step and direction to the next point in the design space. The
fact that SLSQP uses derivatives to perform the optimization limits it’s applications to only
continuous objective functions. In this thesis the SLSQP is only used in the sequential approach
to maximize the AEP.
The parameters used for the SLSQP algorithm are as follows:
max iterations: The maximum number of iteration for the process
tolerance: The tolerance limit. It sets the precision goal for the vaue of the objective function in
the stopping criterion.
expected value: It makes the algorithm scale the value of the objective function to the expected
value which helps with converging to the goal faster.

Multi-objective optimization
The integrated approach considers the inter dependencies between the various components
within the system. To further exploit the dependencies a multi-objective optimization can be
performed. It helps with quantifying trade-offs between multiple objectives and constraints. It
also provides a family of designs rather than a single design. This particularly helps in cases
where a decision needs to be deferred to a later stage as more information is gathered [13].
This proves to be an improved approach to start the wind farm layout design phase. In the
industry the layout design phase begins with one (mostly regular) layout. During the course
of time up until the commissioning phase the initial layout goes into multiple iterations based
on the new information gathered relating to factors such as permits, environmental reasons
and so on. With multiple design layouts upfront it gives the developer multiple options to
assess and quantify the trade-offs between various objectives. In this thesis the multi-objective
optimization is done with respect to the AEP and cable costs.

In TOPFARM the pareto optimization is achieved with the use of a post constraint operator.
The entire process takes place iteratively. In each iteration one objective is allowed to scale to a
speciﬁed value. Then maintaining that objective constant the other objective is optimized. In
the next iteration the ﬁrst objective is scaled to the next value. The process continues till all the
speciﬁed values of the ﬁrst objective goes through the optimization cycle.

5.1
TOPFARM
TOPFARM is the tool used to solve the optimization problems in this thesis. One of the
motivation behind the thesis is to also test the capabilities of TOPFARM and it’s applications
from a research and industry perspective. TOPFARM is a framework which houses all the
components required for the optimization problem such as the design variables, constraints
and the drivers. As mentioned earlier it uses the OpenMDAO package for the optimization
and PyWake for AEP computations which will be explained later on in this chapter.

5.1.1
Design variables
The design variables are components that keep changing in an optimization problem. They
are called into the TOPFARM problem. Common design variables used include wind turbine
positions and wind turbine types.
5.1.2
Constraints
Constraints are the limitations of an optimization problem. TOPFARM has a list of built
in constraint components. However this is open to customization and new additions. The
constraint components now available include the turbine spacing constraint and the layout
boundary constraint. Various boundary shapes such as square, rectangle, circle, polygon and
convex hull are all part of the layout boundary constraint component.
5.1.3
Drivers
The driver refers to the optimization algorithm that loops over the entire workﬂow of the
problem until it arrives at an optimal solution. The drivers available in TOPFARM at the
moment include- SLSQP, COBYLA, Genetic algorithm and Random search. Each driver comes
with it’s own set of parameters speciﬁc to each algorithm.
5.1.4
TOPFARM problem
The TOPFARM problem is a container which houses the design variables, constraints and
the driver. It also couples a plotting routine to plot every instance of the iteration. A recorder
component is also present which records a list of all the intermediate variable values that can
be saved for further post processing.

5.2
PyWake
PyWake is an open source python library used for calculating wind farm ﬂow ﬁelds, power
production and annual energy production. It acts as an interface to TOPFARM to aid the AEP
calculations. It comes with a pre-deﬁnes set of sites, wind turbines and engineering wind farm
models. In addition to this the user can add their own site, wind farm or wind farm models to
benchmark using PyWake.
5.2.1
Site
For a given position, reference wind speed and reference wind direction, the site object provides
the local wind condition in terms of wind speed, wind direction, turbulence intensity and
the probability of each combination of wind direction and wind speed. Furthermore, Site site
object is responsible for calculating the down-wind, cross-wind and vertical distance between
wind turbines [10]
5.2.2
Wind turbines
For a given wind turbine type and effective wind speed, the Wind Turbine object provides the
power and thrust coefﬁcient, as well as the wind turbine hub height and diameter. [10]
5.2.3
Engineering Wind farm models
The engineering wind farms models in PyWake are composed of one of two wind farms models
in combination with a wake deﬁcit model, a superposition model and optionally a blockage
deﬁcit and a turbulence model. [10]
5.2.4
Advantages of PyWake
• The increased level of ﬂexibility gives users the option to build enterprise wide tools
• It helps users to design their own siting workﬂow
• It helps bench marking with own data from the users
• Users can also design their own wake models based on data or physics

SIMULATION AND RESULTS:
This chapter includes all the results and discussion related to the thesis. It begins with a
comparison study on the various optimizers used in the study. These optimizers are then used
to compare the sequential and integrated approaches. The study is done on 3 cases of increasing
number of wind turbines in a site. Further on to exploit the inter dependencies between AEP
and cable cost a multi-objective optimization is performed using the best optimiser from the

7.1
Comparison of optimizers:
The ﬁrst study is on the comparison of the optimizers. The optimizers used include SLSQP,
random search and Genetic algorithm. This comparison is made for AEP optimization of the
3 cases. The comparison is made with respect to the time taken for the convergence and the
quality of the results.
An important factor used in these simulations is the SMART START algorithm. The SMART
START algorithm is a feature within the TOPFARM environment. It is aimed at helping
the algorithm with a better initial guess as compared to the un-optimized initial starting
point. It provides the initial guess by iteratively placing turbines at locations with least wake
interactions. This has proven to greatly improve the quality of results and also the time taken
for the optimizations. As you can see in the results none of the optimizers start from the zero
point. It helps not only with the time taken for the optimizer to converge but also with getting
to better minima.

The parameters for SLSQP remain the same for all cases. For the maximum iterations a
number was chosen that could not practically be reached allowing the optimiser to converge
on it’s own. The expected cost of 1e-3 was chosen by various trials. The expected cost pa-
rameter is expected to scale the value of the objective function to reach convergence quicker.
Repeated trials showed that values lower than 0.1 helped the optimiser converge quicker than
for values higher than 0.1. One thing to be noted is that the behaviour of the optimiser became
more erratic as the value kept going further away from 0.1 towards 0. The erratic behavior
refers to suddenly showing spikes in the convergence. The spikes are indicative of unfeasible
results. 1e-3 was chosen as the tolerance value as it hit a middle ground between achieving the
convergence quicker and also at the same being less erratic in behavior.
The random search is an algorithm that can run for several hours if not days by slowly improv-
ing the results. Thus a maximum time parameter was the only way to stop the optimiser. The
values for this parameter were chosen based on practicality. The maximum step parameter
refers to the distance the algorithm jumps in the design space during the search for a feasible
solution.

focuses on multi-objective optimization. The multi-objective optimization helps establish a
pareto front to evaluate the trade-offs between the design variables. It also helps to deﬁne the
slopes for the trade-offs. The slopes help us to identify if the best solution for a particular set of
design variables has been attained. In-depth analysis of the various plots in the pareto front
also help us to get a deeper understanding as to how the optimiser works.

steep increase in cable costs. To understand this the respective layouts were analyzed for the 9

the wind turbines close to each other. This would have a higher increase in wake interactions
on the turbines and placing the turbines closer would reduce the length of the cables. As the
AEP limit is increased the algorithm tries to push the turbines apart. At ﬁrst it tries to cluster
the turbines in two’s. This can be seen from the layout at 210 Gwh. It ﬁnally ends up clustering
the turbines in 2 groups of 4 and 5 parallel to each other. As the turbines are clustered in groups
of 4s and 5s the algorithm makes use of the higher diameter cables to connect them to the
substation. Thus the cable cost is also optimized while also minimizing the wake interactions.
With respect to the IRR for the various points in the pareto front the layout design with the
least AEP and cable costs is also the design with the least IRR as can be seen in Figure [7.17].
The layout with the highest IRR is the ﬁnal layout on the other end of the pareto with the
highest AEP and cable costs. From 0.81 to 0.85 of the AEP the change in IRR is in the range of
0.8%.

Relationship between convex area and cable costs:
The ﬁnal section of this chapter explores the relationship between the convex area of the
layout and the AEP and cable costs. The convex area of a layout is the area enclosed by all the
outermost turbines in the layout. The convex area of the optimized layout is always expected
to be smaller than the outer boundary of the layout.
In cases where a wind farm location is up for bid the outer boundary is pre-deﬁned. But if
the convex area of the best case is lesser than that of the pre-deﬁned case, in certain regions,
the developer could make a case for receiving increased subsidies. In certain cases because of
certain ﬁsheries zones the wind farm location is pushed further offshore [32]. The wind farm
developer could be interested to look at the tread-offs between various convex areas and it’s
corresponding effect on cable costs and AEP.

CONCLUSION:
The chapter summarizes all the results and the high level takeaways derived from the thesis.
• For a ﬁnite computational time the random search algorithm produces better optimals
and also converges faster to the optimal as compared to the SLSQP and genetic algorithm.
This study was fueled by the use of the SMART START algorithm
• The integrated approach produced the best IRR value in all the 3 cases considered as
compared to the sequential approach. It did so by identifying trade-offs between AEP
and cable costs focussing on the main objective function of IRR.
• The multi-objective optimization was used to identify the trade-offs between AEP and
cable costs. The IRR is lowest for the layout with lowest AEPs and lowest cable costs
where the turbines are close together. The highest IRR was found out to be for the layout
with the highest AEP and the highest cable cost in the pareto front where the turbines
are far apart and clustered.
• The pareto front helped identify slopes for trade-offs between AEP and cable costs. This
helped reﬁne sub-optimal results.
• For a given layout, the AEP and cable costs are inversely proportional to the minimum
spacing between the turbines. The highest IRR is obtained from the layout with the
lowest minimum spacing.
• The AEP and the cable costs are directly proportional to the convex area of the layout

8.1. FUTURE WORK:
8.1
Future work:
1. Research could be done to compare the performance of the random search algorithm
with the particle swarm algorithm which has been used in other studies in similar topics.
2. The thesis concludes that the integrated approach produces better results than the
sequential approach but from a practical stand point it does not produce the literal "best"
solution. This integrated approach used in this thesis provides a best initial guess for the
next step. The next step would undergo a complex optimization process which would
require more time and resources. This could be explored in the future.
3. This study saw that the AEP had a bigger inﬂuence on the IRR than the cable costs.
Future studies could focus on other design variables and identify ones which has bigger
impacts on the objective function.
4. The layouts used in the thesis did not include any exclusion zones. It would be an
interesting study to see how the algorithm optimizes the turbine positions with the
addition of exclusion zones.
5. The thesis used a ﬁnite time to evaluate the performance of optimisers. Study could be
done to explore results without this constraint on the optimisers.
