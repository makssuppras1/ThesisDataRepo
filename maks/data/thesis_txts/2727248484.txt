Abstract 
Invasive species are non-native organisms primarily introduced into an ecosystem by humans, 
where they become established and cause harm to the native species and/or because of harmful 
habitat modifications and biodiversity loss. Due to their often wider physiological tolerances 
compared to co-existing native species, invasive species can endure more diverse environmental 
conditions. It is of great concern that future effects of climate change, such as increasing 
temperatures and, in the aquatic environment, decreasing oxygen levels, likely will further provide 
invasive species with environments that allow them to dominate and outcompete native species.  
This thesis aimed to investigate the hypoxia tolerance of round goby (Neogobius melanostomus), 
an invasive fish species in the Western Baltic Sea originating from the Ponto-Caspian region. The 
study focused on assessing the effects of temperature on the critical oxygen level (O2crit). The 
experiments were conducted in controlled laboratory settings, with fish acclimated to three different 
temperatures: 5, 10 and 25°C. The hypothesis was that O2crit would increase with increasing 
temperatures because the basic energy demands of the fish (measured as the standard metabolic 
rate; SMR) expectedly increase with increasing temperatures. 
The SMR increased significantly with increasing temperatures, whereas O2crit did not show a 
statistically significant difference among the tested temperatures, being 1.4 ± 0.8 mg O2/L for 5°C, 
2.0 ± 0.9 mg O2/L for 10°C and 2.1 ± 0.7 mg O2/L for 25°C. These results suggested a relatively 
stable O2crit level across the present temperature range.  
A literature review, including other invasive species, revealed that European catfish, crucian carp, 
common carp, and Nile tilapia could endure concentrations lower than O2crit of round goby. Notably, 
round goby's hypoxia tolerance was much higher than that of mosquitofish. Round goby's hypoxia 
tolerance compared to native Baltic species found that sandeel exhibited lower tolerance levels than 
round goby. At the same time, sole and cod exhibited about the same tolerance across different 
temperatures. Round goby's hypoxia tolerance and temperature adaptability indicated potential 
success in a warmer, more hypoxic Baltic Sea environment.

1 Introduction 
1.1 Invasive species 
Invasive species are non-native organisms primarily introduced into an ecosystem by humans, 
where they become established and cause harm to the native species and/or because of harmful 
habitat modifications and biodiversity loss (Byers et al., 2023). Invasive species can be all 
species inside all taxa. The same goes for aquatic invasive species in aquatic environments, 
which hold salt and freshwater environments. Examples of aquatic invasive species are the 
Polychaeta Marenzelleria spp. (Norkko et al., 2012), the aquatic plant Ludwigia grandiflora 
(Billet et al., 2018), the bivalve Limnoperna fortune (Oliveira et al., 2010) and the invasive fish 
round goby (Behrens et al., 2022). The common thing for invasive species is the harmful effects 
they cause on the environment. For example, the invasive aquatic plant may outcompete native 
plant species for light, and invasive fish may outcompete the other fish by outcompeting for 
food and habitat. 
The most common way of introducing aquatic invasive species is shipping, mainly through 
ballast water. However, other routes of introductions may be attachments to the hull of ships, 
where the organisms (e.g., bivalves) are transported to new environments. Shipping is vital for 
world trade and accounts for about 80% of transported goods (Tamburri et al., 2001). 
Vessels pump water into ballast tanks to increase the draft (stability); the water can hold a 
variety of organisms, including fish and other marine life. Usually, water is pumped in one port 
and discharged in another, which is how non-native species are introduced (Tamburri et al., 
2001). Another route of introduction is escaping from aquaculture, such as the escape of 
Atlantic salmon (Salmo salar). There can be a great release of farmed Atlantic salmon if net 
pens break during stormy conditions, as there can be up to 650,000 fish per net pen. Atlantic 
salmon are native to the Northern Atlantic Ocean and are farmed in several countries, including 
==PAGE:7==
2 
 
Chile, in the Southern Pacific Ocean. These releases of non-native species threaten local 
biodiversity, mainly because the released fish will compete for the same resources as native fish 
species, i.e., food and are a threat to disease transmission to native fish populations (Marshall 
et al., 2018). 
Climate change has become an increasingly significant global issue, with its consequences 
affecting various aspects, such as increasing temperatures, changes in precipitation, melting ice 
and sea-level rise (IPCC report, 2022). This also includes the spread and impact of invasive 
species since invasive species usually come from warmer climates (Boone & McCleery, 2023). 
Due to their often wider physiological tolerances, the changing climate creates favourable 
conditions for invasive species, thus increasing their potential to cause significant harm to 
ecosystems and economies (Pratt et al., 2022). In addition, they can cause widespread 
ecological disruptions, leading to the extinction of native species, degradation of natural 
habitats, and changes in ecosystem functions (Green et al., 2012). Invaded ecosystems can be 
reduced in the abundance and diversity of commercial and recreational fish species, leading to 
decreased fishing industry revenues and reduced angler opportunities (Cambray, 2003). 
1.2 Climate change and hypoxia 
Climate change is driving hypoxia through various mechanisms, including increased water 
temperatures, which lead to the water being able to hold less dissolved oxygen (DO), altered 
ocean currents and circulation patterns, and changes in precipitation and runoff from agriculture 
(Diaz & Rosenberg, 2008). One definition of hypoxia is when DO levels in the open ocean, 
coastal waters and freshwaters are insufficient to support aquatic life (Breitburg et al., 2018). 
Aquatic ecosystems undergo significant impacts on their physical, chemical, and biological 
processes because of these changes. Altering ocean circulation patterns directly affects the 
distribution of oxygen-rich surface waters and oxygen-poor deep waters, leading to hypoxia. It 
==PAGE:8==
3 
 
can cause areas previously well-oxygenated through the water column to become hypoxic as 
the deeper, oxygen-poor waters are prevented from mixing with the oxygen-rich surface water 
(Deutsch et al., 2011). The depletion of oxygen levels in water bodies can have catastrophic 
effects on aquatic organisms and cause long-lasting damage to the ecosystem (Diaz & 
Rosenberg, 2008). Hypoxia thresholds tend to vary significantly between marine and freshwater 
taxa. Fish and crustaceans are generally the most sensitive, while bivalves and gastropods show 
more resilience (Keeling et al., 2010). As hypoxia becomes more widespread in many parts of 
the world, it poses significant threats to aquatic biodiversity, fisheries, and water quality (IPCC 
report, 2022).  
Climate change can also impact nutrient levels in the ocean, which then again affects oxygen 
levels by increasing primary production. For example, warming waters can lead to increased 
stratification, which is the layering of water with different densities and properties, caused by 
temperature, salinity, and oxygen content (Large et al., 1994). This can prevent nutrients from 
being mixed throughout the water column, limiting the growth of phytoplankton, a primary 
producer of oxygen in the ocean. Additionally, nutrient pollution from agricultural and urban 
runoff can exacerbate hypoxia by causing excessive algal growth, leading to increased oxygen 
consumption as the algae die and decompose (Livingston, 2007). 
1.3 Round goby 
The round goby (Neogobius melanostomus) is a benthic fish belonging to the Gobiidae family 
(MacInnis & Corkum, 2000). It typically grows up to 18 cm in length, although it can 
sometimes reach up to 25 cm (Sapota, 2004). The round goby is native to the Black and Caspian 
Seas in Eurasia and was first detected in the Baltic Sea in the 1990s (Sapota, 2004). Since then, 
the fish has been introduced to the Baltic Sea several times, including the inner Danish waters 
(Azour et al., 2015; Kotta et al., 2016) and the North American Great Lakes (Kornis et al., 
==PAGE:9==
4 
 
2013). However, the most likely vector of the round goby introduction to the Baltic region was 
through ballast water from ships (Kotta et al., 2016). 
Round goby has a high tolerance towards a broad range of temperatures and salinities (Behrens 
et al., 2017, 2022; Christensen et al., 2021). It has a whole palette of prey it can feed on. 
However, in some instances, it can have a negative impact on specific macrobenthos, such as 
on Cardiidae bivalves and Neritidae gastropods in the Western Baltic Sea (Henseler et al., 2020; 
van Deurs et al., 2021). 
Round goby has a unique reproductive strategy: Females lay their eggs in crevices or other 
sheltered areas, and males guard the eggs until they hatch. They are known to spawn multiple 
times yearly and have high reproductive output, which allows them to colonise new areas and 
expand their range rapidly (MacInnis & Corkum, 2000). In their native environment, the ratio 
between males and females is about one, while in the introduced settings, it is about three to 
one. As a result, there is an overproduction in males, possibly because they are more exposed 
to predation when guarding the eggs, which is part of their invasion strategy (Sapota, 2004).  
It is an aggressive fish, which has helped it to become a successful invasive species in many 
regions where it has been introduced. There have been observed differences in behaviour, with 
individuals on the invaded fronts being more aggressive and bolder, which could be the driver 
for the invasion of round goby (Ericsson et al., 2021; Thorlacius et al., 2015). 
1.4 Oxygen demands of fish 
In fish, the standard metabolic rate (SMR) measures the energy a resting, fasting individual 
needs to sustain life. The importance of fasting is to avoid the specific dynamic action (SDA), 
which is increased oxygen consumption due to food digestion (Wang et al., 2006). Fish are 
ectothermic animals, meaning they have cold blood. Their energy use increases with rising 
temperatures because they rely on external heat sources to regulate their body temperature. The 
==PAGE:10==
5 
 
oxygen uptake rate is often used as a proxy for fish’s energy demands, and oxygen demands 
will typically be higher at higher temperatures (Christensen et al., 2021). When the fish 
encounters decreasing levels of DO, it can maintain its SMR until a certain level of DO; it is 
then said to be an oxygen regulator. However, with low DO, its respiration rate will decrease at 
some point. In other words, the organism cannot maintain its SMR and eventually dies. It is 
then said to become an oxygen conformer, and this oxygen level where the fish can no longer 
maintain its SMR is termed the critical level of oxygen, O2crit, or Scrit, depending on whether 
measuring oxygen levels or saturation. The sensitivity towards low oxygen and how this varies 
between species can, in this way, have important implications for understanding how species 
respond to environmental changes (Ultsch & Regan, 2019).  
A study on round goby in the Great Lakes focused on assessing the effects of wastewater 
effluent on different physiological aspects, such as hypoxia tolerance (McCallum et al., 2017). 
The study determined the hypoxia tolerance of the fish using intermittent respirometry. The 
experiments were conducted in cages in situ, meaning in the place where they were caught. 
However, no such field or lab experiments have been done on the hypoxia tolerance for round 
goby that have adapted to the environment in the Baltic, even though the Baltic Sea, to a greater 
and greater extent, is characterised by declining oxygen content (Zillé & Conley, 2010). The 
main reason for the increasing hypoxia in the Baltic, especially in the last 50 years, is 
industrialised agriculture and human activities. These anthropogenic sources are rich in 
nutrients (nitrogen and phosphorus), which leads to eutrophication (excess amount of nutrients), 
leading to algal blooms and oxygen depletion (Zillé & Conley, 2010). 
This master’s thesis aims to investigate the critical oxygen level of round goby from the 
Western Baltic Sea under different water temperatures of 5, 10 and 25°C. The hypothesis is that 
the critical oxygen level will increase with rising water temperatures, as rising temperatures 
will cause increasing standard oxygen demands. To test this hypothesis, intermittent flow 
==PAGE:11==
6 
 
respirometry was applied, a conventional method to determine energy used in fish and other 
aquatic organisms (Steffensen, 1989). This study will provide valuable insights into how round 
goby will perform in a future, more hypoxic Baltic Sea. 
There are many examples of aquatic invasive species, and the general idea is that they are 
tolerant towards a broad range of environmental conditions, including water oxygen levels. As 
a background for the present laboratory experiment, a small literature review will investigate 
the hypoxia tolerance of other successful aquatic invasive species. It will provide valuable 
insights into the importance of hypoxia tolerance for invasion success.

2 Methods 
2.1 Fish and holding facility 
The experiments were done in the fish stable at DTU Aqua, Lyngby, Denmark, using round 
goby (Neogobius melanostomus). Before starting the experiments, the fish (originally caught in 
Karrebæk, Denmark) were kept in a large holding tank in a recirculating aquaculture system 
with aerated water (DO: 90-100% air saturation) at a temperature of 10°C, salinity of 10‰ and 
with a natural light cycle (12:12 light-dark). The 40 fish used for the experiment were checked 
to see if they had previously been injected with a passive integrated transponder (PIT) tag and 
inserted with one if not. The PIT tag was used to keep track of fish IDs, as fish were not kept 
in individual containers. In addition, the condition factor (Equation 1) was calculated for every 
fish, and an ANOVA test was done beforehand to determine any difference between the 
experiments.

2.2 Acclimation to the experimental temperatures 
It was determined to conduct the experiment at three different temperatures, with 10 and 25°C 
serving as pilot temperatures. After these were completed, 5°C was chosen as the last 
temperature since O2 Crit was not found to differ significantly between 10 and 25°C. 
The fish were split into three groups of twelve, and each group was put into a 700 L holding 
tanks to acclimate. Each tank was further subdivided into four compartments, with three fish in 
each compartment to account for the effect of the tank. At the beginning of acclimatisation, the 
temperature of the tanks was set to 10°C, 90-100% air saturation, and salinity of 10‰, i.e., the 
same as the holding tank. Furthermore, a continuous flow of 10°C water was added to the 10°C 
tank. In contrast, a reduced flow was added to the 5 and 25°C tanks to avoid significant 
==PAGE:13==
8 
 
temperature changes. Adding water flow to the tanks was important for maintaining water 
quality as it removed waste and debris. 
The first tank was maintained at a steady 10°C ± 0.5 throughout the acclimation period, with 
the start of the acclimation process coinciding with the introduction of the fish to the tank. The 
second tank was steadily heated to 25°C ± 0.5, increasing by 2°C daily. The water temperature 
was increased by incorporating four titanium rod heaters of varying wattage, three rods of 300W 
and one of 500W (AB Aqua Medic, Bissendorf, Germany). In addition, a 10 L/min pump was 
added to aid in mixing the water and achieving an even temperature throughout the tank. The 
third experiment was conducted at 5°C ± 0.5. This was done by cooling by 2°C daily from 
10°C; two coolers were added to the tank (TK 2000; Teco, Fornace Zarattini, Italy). Finally, all 
fish were kept in their acclimation tank for three weeks at the designated temperature to adjust 
to their new environment fully and acclimatise to the new temperature (Christensen et al., 
2021). 
During the acclimation period, the fish were fed an Aller Ivory Ex 3mm (Aller Aqua, Denmark) 
feed of about 5-10g per tank. The fish at 5 and 10°C were fed three times per week, while the 
fish at 25°C were fed four times per week to account for the higher metabolism at the higher 
temperature (Christensen et al., 2021). Due to the different metabolisms at the different 
temperatures, the fish at 5 and 10°C were starved for three days, and the fish at 25°C were 
starved for two days before conducting the experiments. On the days between feedings, the 
tanks were carefully cleaned with a net with small enough meshes to collect remaining fish feed 
and faeces, ensuring that the water quality remained optimum. The inorganic nitrogen in the 
form of ammonium (NH4+), nitrite (NO2-) and nitrate (NO3-) and pH were measured two times 
a week in each compartment of the tanks to ensure that they remained within acceptable levels. 
The levels were continuously maintained at optimal levels, with NH4+ kept below 0.05 mg/L, 
==PAGE:14==
9 
 
NO2- below 0.01 mg/L, and NO3- below 20 mg/L, the pH level was registered at a steady 8.1 
(Christensen et al., 2021). 
2.3 Respirometry setup 
The respirometry chambers were placed in a dark and shallow tank to keep the fish calm, as 
well as enable easy entry of the fish into the chambers. In addition, a tarp was placed around 
the area to prevent any disturbance caused by human presence. However, visual checks were 
still conducted to maintain the fish’s welfare. 
An intermittent flow respirometry setup was used in the experiment. Four chambers were used, 
three for fish respiration and one for measuring microbial respiration. Two 5 L/min flush pumps 
powered the first two chambers, while the third and fourth chambers were powered by a 10 
L/min flush pump, as seen in Figure 1. This ensured that the flow rate was at least five times 
the chamber’s volume per minute, meaning that 99% of the water was replaced (User Manual 
AutoRespTM 2.2.0, Loligo Systems, 2016, Denmark).In addition, a float was added to the 
outflow flush tube to keep it over water to ensure that it could be visually seen if the flush pump 
was working and that new water did not enter the chamber.

As seen in Figure 2, each chamber had a recirculation loop with a PVC tube containing an

The rate of oxygen consumption (MO2) was calculated using Equation 2. V was the volume of 
the respirometry minus the volume of the fish, the volume of respirometry was the chamber of 
1080 mL, the volume of the tube of 45 mL and subtracting the volume of the fish. The weight

oxygen solubility (mg O2 mm/hg/L), were needed for the equation. However, the software had 
everything it needed to calculate MO2 typed into the computer, such as the temperature, 
barometric pressure, and salinity. 
For each experiment, three fish were carefully taken from one tank section, with one section 
being tested daily, four days per temperature. If one fish had died, then only two fish would be 
tested. All experiments were initiated at around midday. The experimental tank was thoroughly 
cleaned with chlorine between experiments to decrease bacterial respiration.

The bacterial respiration was determined by averaging the oxygen consumption rate in 
mgO2/L/hr in the fourth chamber. This value was then subtracted from mg O2/kg/hr of fish to 
estimate the fish's respiration rate accurately. The SMR was calculated from the mean of the 
lowest 10% of MO2 values before O2crit measurements (Chabot et al., 2016). The O2crit 
calculation curve was found by looking at the data and finding out where the values for 
mgO2/L/hr fell below the SMR value and kept declining.  
2.5 Literature review 
The study of hypoxia tolerance in an aquatic invasive species required a small literature review. 
This was to provide a better insight into how aquatic invasive species cope with low oxygen 
levels, as this leads to a better prediction of their spread. The first step was to determine the 
most reliable search engine for the literature search. A comparative analysis was conducted 
using the same search terms in both Web of Science and Scopus, and it was found that Web of 
Science produced the highest number of results. More specifically, 183 articles were found on 
Web of Science, while Scopus produced 134 articles. Therefore, Web of Science was selected 
as the preferred search engine for the study. This was no guarantee that Web of Science provides 
better articles, just that more articles were given for the exact search. The search string used for 
Web of Science was:  
==PAGE:21==
16 
 
(ALL= (invasive species)) AND ALL=(anoxi* OR hypoxi* or "low oxygen" or "oxygen 
depletion") AND ALL=(aquatic or aqua or water). 
The search used a combination of terms related to invasive species, hypoxia, and aquatic 
environments. Although only one term was deemed needed for invasive species, most articles 
about NIS or Non-indigenous marine organisms (NEMOs) were all obtained from the search 
because the articles mentioned invasive species. The results were then carefully reviewed by 
downloading the titles and reading the abstracts. The decision to keep or discard an article was 
based on its relevance to this thesis. Any articles unrelated to hypoxia and aquatic invasive 
species were discarded. The kept articles were grouped into different categories based on which 
category their aquatic invasive species belonged to. These groups were teleost, amphipods, 
bivalves, macrophytes and annelids.

3 Results 
There was some variation in weight between the fish in each group, varying between 56 and 
159g for the fish acclimated to 5°C, between 67 and 126g for fish acclimated to 10°C, and 
between 55 and 125g for fish acclimated to 25°C. However, when comparing the weight of the 
fish between each acclimation group, the mean for 25°C was 91g ± 23, for 10°C 99g ± 20 and

insignificant (F=0.7, p=0.5).  
The values in condition factor for fish in each group varied from 1.4 and 2.2 for the fish 
acclimated to 5°C, between 1.4 and 2.3 for fish acclimated to 10°C, and between 1.4 and 2.3 
for fish acclimated to 25°C. The condition factor means for the groups were 1.8 ± 0.3 for 5°C, 
2.0 ± 0.3 for 10°C and 1.8 ± 0.2 for 25°C (Table 1), and the ANOVA test was insignificant 
(F=0.6, p=0.55).

4.1.1 Round goby 
Two studies were found relating aspects of round goby concerning low oxygen conditions. The 
first one found that low oxygen saturation levels could increase the ecological impact of round 
goby (n=40) relative to a native species of bullhead (Cottus gobio) in France. The experiments 
were conducted in a lab. Round goby had a significantly higher feeding rate than the native 
species at low oxygen levels. The first experiments were conducted at supersaturation (11.5 mg 
O2/L, 90% air saturation) to depletion (3.6 mg O2/L, 30%) at 19 ± 2°C. Figure 12 showed no 
significant difference at 11.5 mg O2/L, but at 3.6 mg O2/L, round goby had a much higher 
feeding rate than bullhead. This result was significant, with the 95% confidence interval not 
overlapping between the two fish (Dickey et al., 2021). 
==PAGE:27==
22 
 
 
Figure 12: 95% confidence interval of prey consumed of the invasive round goby and native bullhead at three 
different oxygen levels (Dickey et al., 2021). 
 
The second article on round goby showed that their adaptability was attributed to their tolerance 
for various environmental conditions, including poor water quality and contaminants 
(McCallum et al., 2017). These experiments were conducted in the wild on round goby found 
in Hamilton Harbour in Canada on the Great Lakes. Their objective was to test the effects of 
wastewater on round goby. They were tested close to the outfall of the wastewater downstream 
and at the site of capture for reference. The fish were put in cages for 21 days at each site at 
varying distances from the effluent water. Their method was using stop-flow respirometry. The 
fish acclimated in the respirometry chamber for ten hours before their O2crit measurements. 
Their protocol was to reduce the air saturation from ∼100% to 10% air saturation by 10% 
reduction every 20 min. The chamber was closed at 10% air saturation, and the fish consumed 
the remaining oxygen until 0.5% air saturation. Their results showed that round goby had an 
O2crit of 3.8 mg O2/L near the wastewater, 3.7 mg O2/L downstream and 3.9 mg O2/L at the 
reference site. The sites had no statistical difference (McCallum et al., 2017). 
4.1.2 European catfish 
The European catfish (Silurus glanis) is a large predatory fish native to freshwaters in mainland 
Europe but has become an invasive species in Southern and Western Europe. In Lake Elbe in 
the Czech Republic, catfish (n=19) were electro-fished and tagged for this experiment. DO was 
measured at ten reference points at dawn for each day. Previous data research used 1.5 mg O2/L 
==PAGE:28==
23 
 
as the limit between sufficient and deficient DO concentrations. Catfish exposed to deficient 
DO values of 1.3 to 2.4 mg O2/L tended to relocate to areas with more favourable DO 
conditions. These results were highly significant for DO under 1.5 mg O2/L (Daněk et al., 2014). 
4.1.3 Crucian carp 
Crucian carp (Carassius carassius) is a unique species that has adapted to survive in small 
ponds with anoxic conditions and ice cover during winter, where no other fish could survive. 
Olsén & Bonow (2022) did a literature search and found that crucian carp could survive anoxia 
(0 mg O2/L) for up to 5.5 months in natural ponds and in laboratory experiments for 4.5 months 
at 3°C. The physiological mechanisms behind this ability included using liver glycogen as an 
energy source and the excretion of ethanol instead of lactate. The species also had a wide 
temperature tolerance, ranging between 0 and 38°C (Olsén & Bonow, 2022). 
4.1.4 Common carp 
A study was conducted in the Ebro River in Spain, where common carp (Cyprinus carpio) 
(n=20) were caught by electrofishing and tagged with a transmitter. Ten omnidirectional 
receivers were used to track data from fish within a 400m radius. To gather data on DO and 
other water components were collected from the Catalan Water Agency and vertical water 
profiles. They found that the common carp was a species that exhibited apparent differences in 
activity patterns between day and night. Being in deep water with low activity at night to 
shallower water during the day. They extensively used hypoxic waters (<1.1 mg O2/L), 
especially at night. They mentioned that common carp could survive 0.5 mg O2/L for several 
days (Benito et al., 2015). 
4.1.5 Mosquitofish 
One study related the aspects of mosquitofish (Gambusia holbrooki) concerning low oxygen 
conditions. The first article focused on the hypoxia tolerance of the invasive mosquitofish 
==PAGE:29==
24 
 
(n=10) compared to the native pygmy perch (Nannoperca australis) (n=10) in the Australian 
floodplains. These tests were done in a laboratory, using intermittent flow respirometry to 
measure SMR and O2crit. The fish were acclimated to the test temperatures for two weeks and 
were fasted for 48 hours before testing. The metabolic experiments conducted at 25 and 29°C 
showed that mosquitofish had a decreasing O2crit of 6.2 mg O2/L at 25°C & 5.3 mg O2/L at 
29°C. In contrast, the O2crit of pygmy perch significantly increased with the increase in 
temperature, 3.9 mg O2/L at 25°C & 7.6 mg O2/L at 29°C (Stoffels et al., 2017). 
4.1.6 Nile tilapia 
The invasive African Nile tilapia (Oreochromis niloticus) in tropical freshwaters in south-
eastern Mexico was compared to the Mayan cichlid (Mayacheros urophthalmus) Gracida-
Juárez et al. (2022). Nile tilapia and Mayan cichlid were placed in a pond to acclimate for at 
least two days. Then the fish were tested in pools 138cm in diameter and 30cm in depth in a 
laboratory, with 31 replicates of five Mayan cichlid and five Nile tilapia and 33 replicates of 
five Mayan cichlids and ten Nile tilapia. In addition, underwater cameras were added to the 
pools. The focus was to observe how different environmental factors affected the behaviour of 
the two fish species. Mayan cichlid is a hypoxia-tolerant species (<4 mg O2/L) but showed 
lower activity in high temperatures and low oxygen environments. The results showed that Nile 
tilapia appeared more consistent in their behaviour, even in lower oxygen levels. The lowest 
tested was 2 mg O2/L, but it mentioned that Nile tilapia could thrive in conditions lower than 
1.5 mg O2/L, although they grew slower (Gracida-Juárez et al., 2022).

A study examined the invasion risk of different freshwater habitats in Germany. The reduced

4.2.1 Corbicula largillierti 
The study on the bivalve species Corbicula largillierti (C. largillierti) studied their hypoxia 
tolerance. They acclimated the bivalves at 25°C for 4 weeks after capture in a laboratory. To 
find the hypoxia tolerance for invasive species, the oxygen consumption per unit of time (vO2) 
was plotted against the function (pO2), which was the oxygen levels in the environment. They 
used four chambers, three for the bivalves and one blank for background respiration. The 
oxygen level in the chamber started at about 100%, then the chamber stopped flushing and 
started again when the concentration was 0% (Rodriguez et al., 2020). 
The results showed a minimum environmental oxygen level tolerance of 3.9 mg O2/L. In 
contrast, another native bivalve species, Corbicula fluminea, was also sensitive to hypoxia but 
was more tolerant than C. largillierti (Rodriguez et al., 2020). 
4.2.2 Zebra and quagga mussels 
One study focused on zebra mussels and another combined zebra and quagga mussels. Gantz 
et al. (2022) captured Zebra mussels (Dreissena polymorpha) in San Jose Reservoir in 
California, U.S. The mussels were put in a cage connected to a subsurface buoy attached to an 
anchor. They were periodically observed for mortality. For the methods in this study, there was 
an oxygen threshold of hypoxic <2 mg O2/L and normoxic >2 mg O2/L. Zebra mussels were 
found to have low mortality rates in stratified water with hypoxic conditions of 1 mg O2/L 
(Gantz et al., 2022).

Both zebra and quagga mussels are considered invasive species in Western Europe. De Ventura 
et al. (2016) took six mussel populations gathered from four European locations and tested them 
in a laboratory. They were acclimated for at least four weeks. The objective was to assess the 
survival at two temperatures (11 & 18°C) crossed with four oxygen levels (6%, 33%, 66% and 
90% air saturation). The results found survival differences among oxygen and temperature 
treatments of zebra and quagga mussel populations from Western Europe depended more on 
population origin than species identity. Quagga and zebra mussels showed similar sensitivities 
to low oxygen, but on average, quagga mussels had somewhat higher mortality than zebra 
mussels in the low oxygen treatment, independent of temperature. On the other hand, zebra 
mussels had low survival and low oxygen regulatory capacities under hypoxic conditions (0.56 
mg O2/L) (De Ventura et al., 2016). 
4.2.3 Pontogammarus robustoides 
The Ponto-Caspian amphipod Pontogammarus robustoides (P. robustoides), introduced into 
Lithuanian inland waters over 50 years ago, has significantly impacted local crustacean 
populations. Their enhanced hypoxia tolerance gives them a competitive advantage over native 
species. From laboratory experiments, the LC50 was 0.49 mg O2/L of the tested P. robustoides. 
These experiments were conducted in closed bottles, allowing for self-induced hypoxia 
(Šidagytė & Arbačiauskas, 2016). 
4.2.4 Bythotrephes longimanus 
The introduction of the invasive amphipod, Bythotrephes longimanus (B. longimanus), into 
North American lakes since the early 1980s has significantly impacted local food webs. 
Findings revealed that B. longimanus (n=690) exhibited a hypoxia tolerance limit (LC50) of 
1.65 mg O2/L DO (95% confidence interval: 1.59, 1.72 mg O2/L), which was significantly less 
than the native Leptodora kindtii (L. kindtii), which exhibited an LC50 of 0.58 mg O2/L DO 
(0.51, 0.65 mg O2/L). The experiments were done in the laboratory, with different experiments

bottles for 12 hours to measure LC50, always having one control bottle with normoxic

4.3 Macrophytes 
The plant’s physiological anoxia tolerance could be increased by reducing the production of 
ATP. Using other pathways of ATP production by glycolysis or fermentation and synthesising 
compounds that protect cells from toxins produced during anoxia (Catford & Jansson, 2014). 
Human-made structures such as marinas reduce oxygen availability in marine environments, 
making it difficult for native fauna species to survive. In contrast, invasive fauna species could 
tolerate lower oxygen levels and maintain their function, giving them an advantage in these 
environments. This was particularly true for upstanding invasive macrophytes with lower 
critical oxygen levels than upstanding natives. The results suggested that artificial structures 
provided windows for invasion by creating environments that were hostile to native plant 
species but still allowed invasive species to thrive (Lagos et al., 2017). 
4.3.1 Ludwigia grandiflora 
The invasive plant, Ludwigia grandiflora, is rapidly spreading in wet meadows in France, 
leading to a decline in the area’s nutritional value (fodder value). The study on this invasive 
plant showed its remarkable ability to adapt. The adaptations may be forming more 
advantageous roots, aerenchyma or enlarged lenticels. These adaptations improved oxygen 
capture and distribution in the plant (Billet et al., 2018).

Studies have also investigated the impact of invasive macrophytes Egeria densa and

4.3.4 Lemna minuta 
Lemna minuta (L. minuta) in freshwater ecosystems could negatively affect water quality by

the invertebrate community. L. minuta was linked to decreased biodiversity and dramatically

4.3.5 Marenzelleria spp. 
The invasive polychaete Marenzelleria spp. has been observed to thrive in areas with impaired 
bottom-water oxygen conditions in the northern Baltic Sea, specifically in hypoxic zones where 
oxygen levels were below 2 mg O2/L (Norkko et al., 2012). Its long-term bio-irrigation 
activities in these areas significantly impacted sedimentary phosphorus dynamics, potentially 
leading to a switch from a seasonally hypoxic to a normoxic system. At the same time, the 
species may provide ecosystem services by enhancing phosphorus retention in sediments. The 
most important means of eutrophication mitigation remained the continued reduction in 
external nutrient loading. Marenzelleria spp. posed a significant threat to terrestrial and aquatic 
ecosystems worldwide, negatively affecting biodiversity, ecosystem functioning, and species 
interactions. In the Baltic Sea, Marenzelleria spp. has become a dominant member of 
communities, particularly in more profound, hypoxia-affected areas where oxygen levels were 
below 2 mg O2/L, following a boom-and-bust pattern. Boom-and-bust is when there is a 
massive increase in the numbers of a species that then rapidly decreases again (Kauppi et al., 
2015).

5 Discussion 
5.1 Interpretation of derived results 
Round goby exhibited O2crit levels comparable to those of well-established Baltic species like 
cod and sole. When compared with sandeel in the Baltic Sea, the round goby's hypoxia tolerance 
was higher. It was worth noting that while there were indications that most other invasive 
species may withstand lower oxygen concentrations than the round goby, it was important to 
recognise that these findings did not point to O2crit measurements for these invasive species. 
The round goby displayed a greater level of O2crit compared with mosquitofish. 
In the present study, SMR increased as expected with the increasing temperatures; thus, round 
goby’s metabolism increases with temperature. The metabolism at 25°C was about twice as 
high at 10°C and six times higher than at 5°C, and overall, the SMR at each of the three 
temperatures are highly comparable to the values found in a recent study on the same species, 
investigating its thermal optimal and tolerance limits (Christensen et al., 2021). 
Before the O2crit measurements, it was hypothesised that O2crit would be higher at higher 
temperatures where the fish was stressed, i.e., at 25°C and lowest at either 5 or 10°C. The 
reasoning for this hypothesis was that SMR increases with temperature while oxygen saturation 
decreases. It was expected that the fish needed to pump more warm water relative to cold water 
for the same amount of oxygen (Schurmann & Steffensen, 1997), which could lead to a higher 
O2crit because SMR is maintained by increasing ventilation when oxygen levels are decreasing 
(Randall, 1982).  
However, the present results did not match the hypothesis, as the O2crit did not differ between 
the three groups of fish acclimated to the different temperatures (Figure 9), suggesting that at 
least for temperatures between 5 and 25°C, the round goby could compensate for changes in 
SMR with temperature. This could mean that the testing was done inside a temperature range 
that the round goby was comfortable at, even though there were significant changes in SMR 
==PAGE:36==
31 
 
between 5 and 25°C. Future experiments could test either below 5 or above 25°C, which could 
have a higher chance of getting a statistical difference. An experiment tested at 28 or 30°C 
would possibly reveal a significantly increased O2crit. As seen by Christensen et al. (2021), SMR 
for round goby has an abrupt increase from 25 to 28°C and has an increase in mortality at 30°C. 
5.2 Hypoxia tolerance of round goby 
Dickey et al. (2021) used a hypoxia level of 3.6 mg O2/L at 19 ± 2°C to test prey consumed for 
round goby and the native bullhead. The bullhead decreased prey consumed, while round goby 
were mainly unaffected, potentially enhancing its impact. The findings in this thesis could 
explain these results, but there would need to be an experiment also at 19°C to be conclusive. 
Between 10 and 25°C, the O2crit value was around 2 mg O2/L, meaning that at an oxygen level 
of 3.6 mg O2/L, the round goby had not reached its O2crit level. Thus, leading to the round goby 
out-competing the bullhead. 
The results of McCallum et al. (2017) showed that round goby adapted to the Great Lakes in 
Canada had an oxygen tolerance of 3.8 mg O2/L near the wastewater, 3.7 mg O2/L downstream, 
and 3.9 mg O2/L at the reference site. There was no statistical difference between the three sites. 
The water temperature on site varied around 17 - 23°C. However, the values for the round goby 
adapted to the Western Baltic compared to the ones adapted to the Great Lakes were 
significantly lower. There could be multiple reasons; the first could be that there was a different 
hypoxia tolerance of round goby depending on origin due to adaptation. As seen in Behrens et 
al. (2017), there can be stark differences between round goby populations in their salinity 
tolerances. This could be the same for hypoxia tolerance. The second could be the testing 
method; testing was done in a lab with controlled environments, while McCallum et al. (2017) 
acclimatised the fish in cages at different sites in the wild. The article did not mention fasting 
of the fish, possibly because this could not be fully controlled in cages in the wild. Thus, the 
==PAGE:37==
32 
 
SDA could increase the O2crit measurements from increased oxygen consumption due to food 
digestion. Another reason could be all the different stressors from not being in a controlled 
environment. These could be the sounds of different predators or sharp temperature changes 
that could prevent the fish from entirely calming down. 
5.3 Hypoxia tolerance for native species of the Baltic Sea and round goby 
According to ICES, cod (Gadus morhua), lesser sandeel (Ammodytes tobianus) and sole (Solea 
solea) are important commercial species in the Baltic (ICES, 2022). Experiments on cod found 
an O2crit of 1.7, 2.1, and 2.5 mgO2/L at 5, 10 and 15°C, respectively. O2crit for sandeel was 4.4 
mg O2/L at 10°C (Behrens & Steffensen, 2007) and  for sole 1.9 mg O2/L at 19°C (van den 
Thrillart et al., 1994). All three were conducted in a laboratory using respirometry.  
Round goby had a lower O2crit than sandeel, about half of the oxygen level, i.e., round goby 
seemed more tolerant towards low oxygen than sandeel at 10°C. The O2crit values for cod 
increased with increasing temperature. At 5 and 10°C, the O2crit values of cod were very much 
comparable with the O2crit values of round goby, not considering the results for cod at 15°C 
because it was most reliable to compare the same temperatures. The O2crit value for sole tested 
at 19°C was not comparable to our results because there was too much temperature difference. 
However, sole seemed to have a high hypoxia tolerance from its O2crit value. 
Cod and sole were tested at a different temperature than sandeel but seemed to have a much 
higher hypoxia tolerance than sandeel. This could be because sole and cod are demersal fish 
species living close to the bottom, where hypoxia usually occurs. Although sandeel are buried 
in sandy seabed in the winter, they are considered a pelagic fish species, where hypoxia is 
uncommon.

5.4 Interpretation of literature review 
The general trend for the fish species in this literature review was that most, when compared, 
were more hypoxia tolerant than the native species, except mosquitofish at colder temperatures. 
Different physiological mechanisms allowed them to survive in hypoxia. For example, crucian 
carp was unique because it used liver glycogen as an energy source and excreted ethanol instead 
of lactate, tolerating anoxic conditions. Mosquitofish had the lowest hypoxia tolerance but still 
had a higher tolerance than the native species at warmer temperatures. 
For the bivalves, there was a trend for zebra and quagga mussels that their hypoxia tolerance 
depended on where they were sampled. As also seen in Behrens et al. (2017), for fish, this 
indicated that environmental adaptation could significantly impact physiological tolerances 
across taxa. The bivalve C. largillierti was not found to have a higher hypoxia tolerance than 
the native bivalve. This showed that not all invasive species were more hypoxia tolerant than 
the native. 
There were two amphipods in this literature review, P. robustoides and B. longimanus. P. 
robustoides was found to be very hypoxia tolerant, while B. longimanus was not found to be 
more hypoxia tolerant than the native species by having a lethal concentration three times higher 
than the native species. This again showed that some invasive species were better in hypoxic 
conditions than others. 
Invasive macrophytes proved to have very adverse effects. They were strongly associated with 
hypoxia, meaning that in hypoxic environments, they were the most dominant macrophytes in 
invaded systems. Their physical mechanisms allowed them to tolerate low-oxygen 
environments and maintain function. Their effect on the environment ranged from limiting 
light, thus decreasing primary production, and promoting internal phosphorus loading, which 
could lead to eutrophication. 
==PAGE:39==
34 
 
There was one annelid species in the literature review, Marenzelleria spp. It became more 
dominant in more hypoxic bottom waters. Marenzelleria spp. could lead to enhancement in the 
environment. Its bio-irrigation activities impacted phosphorus sedimentation, which could lead 
from a hypoxic environment to a normoxic one.  
Tamburri et al. (2001) showed that deoxygenation increased mortality in ballast water, which 
decreased the surviving organisms in the water. This, on the other hand, could be negative. As 
seen in Figure 11, not all organisms died, meaning that the ballast water could release the most 
hypoxia-tolerant individuals. Releasing hypoxia-tolerant individuals into new environments 
could have unintended ecological consequences. 
Overall, there was a broad representation of taxonomic groups for this literature review on 
aquatic invasive species and hypoxia tolerance. Most invasive species exhibited a high hypoxia 
tolerance with a few exceptions. Invasive macrophytes dominated in hypoxic environments and 
were hypoxia generators in most cases. The ability of invasive primary producers to generate 
hypoxia, combined with the hypoxia tolerance of most aquatic invasive species, could create a 
double negative in invaded ecosystems. 
5.5 Hypoxia comparisons of invasive fish in the literature review 
Most other invasive fish in the search had a higher hypoxia tolerance than round goby. The 
European catfish, crucian carp, common carp and Nile tilapia demonstrated a hypoxia tolerance 
of 1.5, 0, <1.1 and 1.5 mg O2/L, respectively. It was worth noting that each species may have 
additional adaptive mechanisms and behaviours contributing to their overall success as invasive 
species in different environments. Most noteworthy was the Crucian carp, which could go for 
many months in an anoxic environment. The experiments did not consider O2crit but supplied 
concentrations where fish died or were not seen because they stayed away from these 
concentrations. Plambech et al. (2013) showed that fish could survive for shorter periods of 
==PAGE:40==
35 
 
time in waters below their O2crit value. In concentrations below O2crit, the fish primarily relied 
on anaerobic metabolism resulting in lactate accumulation (Plambech et al., 2013). Even though 
the reported numbers were lower for the other fish, these were most likely values lower than 
their O2crit. Most round goby experienced concentrations well below their O2crit tolerance when 
testing but survived. It could then be hypothesised that round goby had the same, if not lower, 
O2crit than European catfish, common carp, and Nile tilapia. 
The O2crit values at 25°C of mosquitofish were comparable with round goby tested at the same 
temperature (25°C). Mosquitofish had an O2crit value of 4.2 mg O2/L, higher than round goby. 
This was evident that round goby were better equipped for a water temperature of 25°C. On the 
other hand, mosquitofish were also tested at 29°C. Christensen et al. (2021) showed that round 
goby increased mortality in increasing water temperatures from 28 to 30°C. The increase in 
mortality could be partly explained by the increase in SMR from 5 to 25°C (Figure 8), which 
was over six times higher, leaving round goby stressed at 25°C or higher temperatures. This 
could mean that round goby were better adapted for more hypoxic waters, but mosquitofish 
could survive in warmer water than round goby, going above 28°C. 
5.6 Condition of tested round goby 
When the results were being discussed, it was important to first look at the weight and condition 
factor to see if there was any significant difference between the experiments, as this could lead 
to different results. No statistically significant weight difference was essential because oxygen 
uptake is not linear with weight, as larger fish use less oxygen per time per weight (Chabot et 
al., 2016). Knowledge about the condition factor is also beneficial as it provides information 
about the health of the fish, whereas a lower condition factor generally means the fish is in poor 
condition. This could be due to factors such as stress, disease, or food limitation. Not 
surprisingly, the condition factor for wild round goby varies in nature. A recent study made on 
==PAGE:41==
36 
 
round gobies in a European river found that females had condition factor ranging annually 
between 1.25 ± 0.11 and 1.75 ± 0.3 and between 1.25 ± 0.13 and 1.54 ± 0.32 for males 
(Dashinov & Uzunova, 2021). This thesis did not take the sex of the round goby into account, 
but the condition factor did not vary significantly depending on the sex of the fish. The lowest 
condition factor was 1.4, and the highest was 2.3, showing that these round gobies are in good 
condition, likely because they have been in the laboratory for a long time and were well-fed. 
5.7 Behavioural response 
One thing almost all fish had in common was a reaction after starting O2crit measurements, i.e., 
when the respirometer chamber was closed, as seen in both Figures 4 and 5. This could indicate 
that the fish were stressed, potentially moving around, which was not intended in starting O2crit 
measurements. They should be as calm as possible and preferably not react. There were two 
different types of reaction; either the reaction started immediately when the flush pump was 
unplugged, and the chamber closed (as seen in Figure 5), or it occurred later during the period 
of O2crit determination (as seen in Figure 4), maybe as a response to a declining oxygen level 
inside the chamber, trying to escape this. In the first case, why the fish should react to the flush 
pump being stopped is unclear. The fish could respond to new noises, such as the sounds of the 
pumps being unplugged. It could also be hypothesised that the fish were accustomed to the 
pressure in the measurement cycle and reacted when the water did not flush. These cycles lasted 
for 7.5 minutes, and some fish were tested for about 24 hours, meaning that some fish 
experienced about 192 measurement cycles. Unplugging the flush pump abruptly could lead to 
fluctuations in the chamber pressure, which the fish possibly could feel. 
From graphs like Figures 4 and 5, how many reactions could be counted for fish used in the 
results. From the data for 5°C, seven fish reacted right after O2crit measurements started, and 
nine had a later reaction. At 10°C, one fish reacted at the start of O2crit measurements, and six 
==PAGE:42==
37 
 
fish reacted later to hypoxia. At 25°C, three reacted to O2crit measurements, and six fish 
responded to hypoxia. In hindsight, these reactions could have been observed by using a GoPro 
camera. The video footage could have told if and when the fish got stressed, possibly from 
seeing a correlation between response and the unplugging of the flush pumps, or if, after some 
time, they started swimming in response to lower oxygen levels. This footage could help 
improve the data quality. 
5.8 Limitations of thesis 
Despite its contributions, this study had several inherent limitations that should be 
acknowledged and considered when interpreting the results. First, the sample size was 25 fish 
used for the results over three temperatures. Increasing the number of each experiment would 
increase the statistical power of the results or at least keep the same number of fish in each 
experiment. 
There was no statistical difference between the experimental temperatures in O2crit. Expanding 
the temperature range to even warmer and colder could lead to a significant difference.  
There were two reactions seen from the fish when O2crit measurements were conducted. By 
adding a GoPro camera and viewing the fish that had a reaction right when O2crit measurements 
started, a possible reason for this could be found and eliminated. This reaction was a stress 
response, most likely to surroundings outside the experimental setup and not due to hypoxia. 
Expanding the literature search to have two search strings, one to include O2crit or Scrit and one 
without. This would increase the chance of finding more O2crit values for invasive fish while 
not limiting articles not focusing on O2crit or Scrit. Because this study focused on O2crit, it was 
best to use O2crit, or Scrit of other invasive fish species, instead of, for example, their lethal 
concentration (LC50). Because as seen in the literature, fish have been observed in 
concentrations significantly lower than their O2crit, which could be misleading. 
==PAGE:43==
38 
 
When the flush pump did not flush the old water out, the accumulation of waste products could 
occur in the chambers during O2crit measurements. Inorganic nitrogen or pH measurements 
could be measured in future experiments to account if water quality affected O2crit values.

6 Conclusion 
This study revealed that within the temperature range from 5 to 25°C, the critical oxygen level 
(O2crit) of round goby from the Baltic Sea is independent of temperature. Furthermore, the 
species seemed more tolerant to low oxygen levels as compared to the native co-occurring 
sandeel but about as hypoxia tolerant as cod and sole. This may give round goby a competitive 
advantage over some native species in a warmer and even more hypoxic future. 
Interestingly, round goby generally exhibited a lower hypoxia tolerance than most invasive fish 
in the literature review. Although these findings indicated that round goby might better adapt 
to hypoxic conditions at lower temperatures, temperature and not hypoxia may be the limitation 
when going above 25°C. 
In future experiments, it would be interesting to see how salinity could affect O2crit in round 
goby by doing experiments ranging from freshwater to a salinity of 30‰. According to the 
findings of Rosa et al. (1996), salinity can have a significant effect on O2crit values.
