ABSTRACT
This thesis work aims to investigate the optimal integration of a hybrid power plant to fulﬁl the en-
ergy demands of a steel production plant. Steel is the most vital engineering and building material
in the world. It is used in all aspects of our life; automobiles and construction products, refriger-
ators and washing machines, cargo ships and surgical scalpels. Although steel is fully recyclable,
very durable, and requires relatively little energy to produce compared to other materials, it still
negatively impacts the environment. About a quarter of the world’s steel is produced using the elec-
tric arc furnace, which uses high-current electric arcs to melt steel scrap into liquid steel of speciﬁc
chemical composition and temperature.
In India bulk of electricity production is achieved by coal in a thermal power plant, which makes up
around 50% of the entire generation. After China, India is the second-largest coal consumer glob-
ally, overtaking the United States in 2015. With the rise in greenhouse gases such as carbon dioxide,
its high time to regulate the impact of such greenhouse gases stringently. According to the latest
developments, fossil fuel reserves are on the verge of depletion. Hence we must ﬁnd a solution to
conserve non-renewable resources and replace them with other renewable resources such as solar
power. This work primarily aimed at ﬁnding optimal scheduling and meeting the power demand
proﬁle by utilising available renewable energy resources. This report has attempted to scrutinise
the leverage of distributed generation from the available data. Considering solar energy alone can
vary, they give a dependable wellspring of vitality as a distributed generator when associated con-
currently. Therefore, consolidating a solar energy source with storage technologies provides reliable
generation by using a self-sufﬁcient renewable energy system based on sunlight for a selected area
in the Nallalam region, Kerala, India.
The primary objective of this project was to optimise and analyse the minimisation of CO2 emis-
sions and quantify and justify new technologies that can be used in place of existing technologies,
which in turn aim to contribute to the complete elimination of fossil fuels by 2070. The goal was
also to justify the choice of technology based on its advantages and its economic feasibility using
the HOMER software. Two cases are investigated; Case 1 is a hybrid power plant that uses lithium-
ion batteries for storage. Case 2 is a hybrid power plant with a hydrogen energy storage system
integrated for energy storage. A sensitivity analysis was also conducted by connecting the system to
the grid, and the percentage share of grid load was tweaked to ﬁnd the optimal design for the hybrid
power plant. This enables a comparative analysis of results to assess the potential of renewables in
decreasing the emissions and the capital required to achieve it. The combination of photovoltaics,
batteries, hydrogen energy storage systems and grid systems has delivered optimal results, provid-
ing vitality with almost 0% unsatisﬁed load and least amount of surplus electricity generation with
minimal net present cost and least carbon footprint.

INTRODUCTION
In past decades, climate change has been a discussion subject even in most remote parts of the
world. Many countries are making great efforts towards reducing the effects of climate change. But
the big question remains the same, Are the countries doing enough? Some countries still refuse
to work towards climate change. Climate change mitigation calls for investments and integration
in renewable energy sources. In Europe, especially in the Nordic countries, billions of euros are
invested in developing and researching innovations of renewable technologies. Compared to the
west, the Asian countries are well behind in sustainable development initiatives. This is mainly due
to the social imbalances in various sectors in Asian societies. Due to the large population in Asian
countries, the energy demand increases at a high pace. It may come as a surprise, but most countries
with current fossil fuel power generation still don’t have a 24/7 electricity supply in the residential
sector. Establishing a movement towards a fully green energy system under such circumstances is a
challenge.
1.1. FORMULATION OF THE PROBLEM
A Hybrid Power Plant (HPP) is a combination of two or more power generation technologies to
improve system efﬁciency and can be considered a "modern environmental friendly solution" to
ﬁght climate change. The HPP can be delivered in standalone or grid-connected mode, making
it a good option for remote locations. Renewable energy sources such as solar and wind depend
on natural/climatic properties that are not easy to predict; Therefore, combinations of these two
sources can increase the system’s overall effectiveness[1]. A viable surrogate option is the union of
these disparate renewable sources with fossil derivatives to form a composite imperative system [2].
The Indian government pledged to reach net-zero emissions by 2070 at Cop26 global climate sum-
mit[3]. To achieve this target, innovations in the upcoming decade will be crucial. To commercialise
new low-emissions processes, including those that integrate Carbon Capture, Utilisation and Stor-
age (CCUS) and hydrogen, and to realise the long-term, a transformational change is required. The
governments will have to play a key role by providing RDD funding, creating a market for near-zero-
emissions steel, adopting mandatory CO2 emissions reductions policies, expanding international
cooperation, and developing supporting infrastructure [4].
Steel production is one of the high CO2 intense industrial processes, and the CO2 intensity of crude
steel production has been relatively steady in the past few years. About a quarter of the world’s
steel is produced using the Electric Arc Furnace (EAF), which uses high-current electric arcs to melt

The learning objectives of this study are-
• Research about relevant technologies.
• Modelling the hybrid power plant using a commercial tool namely Homer Pro.
• To quantify and justify new technologies that can be used in place of existing technologies

This project investigates how a steel casting plant that emits large amounts of Carbon-dioxide and
Green House Gas (GHG) into the atmosphere can become carbon neutral. A Hybrid Power Plant
(HPP) is designed and modelled for a selected location in the southern Indian state of Kerala, where
wind and photovoltaic systems will be used to generate electricity. The electricity generated from
these renewable energies will be used to melt the steel in the electric furnace. The excess electric-
ity from renewable sources is then stored either in Lithium-ion (Li-ion) batteries or converted to
hydrogen. The stored hydrogen can be converted back into electricity with a fuel cell when the pro-
duction from renewable sources is not sufﬁcient. Hence, the system will be autonomous. Technical
and economic analysis is carried out on this hybrid system, and this project’s feasibility is evalu-
ated. A sensitivity analysis will be performed by connecting the HPP to the grid and altering the
percentage of renewables used by the system. The results are then analysed to see how they affect
the ﬁnancial parameters.

requirements of future renewable energy systems.

2.1. STEEL CASTING
Steel is an alloy of iron and carbon containing less than 2% carbon and 1% manganese, as well as
small amounts of silicon, phosphorus, sulfur and oxygen. Steel is the most critical engineering and
construction material in the world. It is used in all aspects of our life; automobiles and construc-
tion products, refrigerators and washing machines, cargo ships and surgical scalpels. Most of the
steel produced globally is manufactured through the Blast Furnace-Basic Oxygen Furnace (BF-BOF)
technique. There are several different grades of steel with different compositions used for various
purposes throughout the world. World crude steel production reached 1,878 million tonnes (Mt) for
the year 2020[7].
Steel casting is generally carried out at high temperatures of around 1550 to 1600°C. This high-
temperature operation creates numerous problems and the volumetric shrinkage that occurs dur-
ing freezing. While steel products require large amount energy to make them, they can also provide
savings over the product’s life cycle, sometimes greater than the energy consumed during produc-
tion. Example, a wind turbine with three megawatts can deliver 80 times more energy than is re-
quired to manufacture and maintain it over the period of 20 years.
2.1.1. CASTING PROCESS

the Electric Arc Furnace route, often referred to as the "primary" and "secondary" paths. The for-

project, HPP is designed and modelled for a EAF steel casting plant. The main raw material for arc
steelmaking is scrap steel. Its availability at low cost and of the right quality is essential for casting.

breakers, an electrode voltage control tap changer and a furnace transformer, is installed in a con-

The roof moves away after tapping the heat, and the hearth is inspected and repaired if necessary.
Melting begins at a low power setting until the electrodes are burned into the light scrap at the top of
the charge, protecting the sidewalls from overheating during higher power melting. The unmelted
scrap is left against the furnace wall for protection, a second bucket is loaded, and the same melting
process is carried out. Sometimes, a third or even a fourth bucket needs to be loaded when casting
very light scrap[9].
After melting, the carbon content in the steel is about 0.25% above the ﬁnal tapping level, which
prevents excess oxidation of the melt. Slag foam is often produced by injecting coal or a mixture
of lime and coal, which reacts with the iron oxide in the slag to produce carbon monoxide gas.
This foam protects the sidewall and allows for a higher power setting. Depending on requirements,
the carbon content of the steel is reduced by blowing in oxygen or increased by carbon injection.
Samples are taken, the temperature checked, additions made, and when all conditions are right,
the furnace is tapped by rotating it forward so that the steel ﬂows over the spout or through the
vertical hole into a ladle. A quick reverse tilt is applied when the slag appears and dumped through
the furnace’s rear door into a slag pot. The total heating time is one to four hours, depending on
the type of steel produced, i.e. the amount of reﬁning applied and the auxiliary heating used. As

oxidizes their bodies. Depending on the work process, three to six kilograms of the electrodes are
used per tonne of steel.

2.2. HYBRID POWERPLANT
A Hybrid Power Plant (HPP) refers to a energy system in which multiple energy conversion devices
are used together to fulﬁll the energy requirement. These systems are often used in isolated envi-
ronment and normally include at least one renewable energy source in the conﬁguration. HPPs are
more environmental friendly compared to the conventional system which are mostly based on a
single fossil fuel source. Hybrid energy systems may also be used as part of distributed generation
application in conventional electricity grid. Usually, a grid-connected HPP is prefered for locations
near a power grid because energy storage systems are currently unsatisfactory[14].
HPP have numerous beneﬁts compared to pure solar or other renewable power plants or pure stor-
age plants.
• In most cases, wind and solar resources complement each other, leading to higher capacity
factors and more stable power output over time with fewer ramping issues and instantaneous
peaks than pure wind or solar plants.
• When wind and solar resources are not correlated, HPPs can be beneﬁcial in dispatching
power to the market. For example, suppose electricity market prices are negatively corre-
lated to wind power (due to the high share of wind). In that case, the HPP can potentially earn
revenue from the market with the solar resource.
• In most cases, only a single grid connection point has to be set up, reducing infrastructure
investment costs and further reducing the overall project costs.
• With appropriate HPP conﬁguration, land can be used more efﬁciently, and a system with
higher installed capacity and energy production per square meter can be designed.
• HPP can facilitate the acceleration of rural electriﬁcation. They can partially provide planned
power distribution to meet load demands in areas where the power grid is too weak to provide
a reliable power supply.
• Developers can create synergies in the development and approval process and system opera-
tion and maintenance[15].

2.2.1. SOLAR PV
A solar cell is a semiconductor component that generates electricity when sunlight falls on it. The
electricity produced by a single cell is relatively small, and hence usually, a lot of solar cells are
interconnected and laminated to form a module. Photovoltaics (PV) modules are typically 1.6 to 2.1
m2 in size and have a power density range of 160-220 Wp/m2. The lifetime of these modules are
a minimum of 25 years[18]. Solar cells can be classiﬁed into different categories according to their
absorber material-
• Crystalline silicon (cSi); The most commonly used substrate material is made from puri-
ﬁed solar-grade polysilicon raw material and comes in the form of monocrystalline or multi-
crystalline silicon wafers. Monocrystalline solar cells are made from wafers cut from a cylin-
drical ingot of high-purity monocrystalline silicon. In contrast, multi-crystalline solar cells
are made from wafers cut from square blocks of casted silicon with 5-50mm monocrystalline
grains.
• Monolithic solar cells composed of compounds of Group III and Group V elements (Gallium,
Arsenic, Indium, and Phosphorus) are often deposited on a Germanium substrate. These
materials can be used to make highly efﬁcient multifunctional solar cells, used mainly for
aerospace applications or in concentrated photovoltaic (CPV) systems.
• In Thin-ﬁlm solar cells, the absorbing semiconductor layer can consist of materials such as
amorphous/microcrystalline silicon (aSi), cadmium telluride (CdTe) or Copper Indium Gal-
lium (di) Selenide (CIGS). These materials are then placed on the glass-covered solar module
in a micrometric thin Layer. Tandem-bond and triple-bond thin-ﬁlm modules are commer-
cially available. In these modules, several layers are deposited on top of one another to in-
crease efﬁciency.
• Perovskite solar cells are basically Dye-Sensitized Solar Cells in which an organometallic salt
is applied as an absorbent material. Perovskites can also be used as absorbers in modiﬁed
(hybrid) polymer / organic solar cells. Perovskite-based solar cells have shown remarkable
advances in evaluating efﬁciency under laboratory conditions over the years. However, the
potential of perovskites raises serious toxicity concerns. The best perovskite absorbents con-
tain soluble organic lead compounds, which are toxic and hazardous to the environment to
the extent that they require exceptional precautionary measures. Therefore, perovskite’s en-
vironmental and health effects are analyzed before it is ﬁnally considered a useful absorbent

UTILITY SCALE PV WITH TRACKING SYSTEM
Many large PV systems installed today have solar panels mounted on ﬁxed structures, resulting in
less generation when the sun moves away as they are only set at a certain angle. To overcome this,
a solar tracking system was introduced. With trackers, the solar panels automatically adjust their
position following the movement of the sun. A Photovoltaics solar tracker is a system that aligns
a Photovoltaics solar module with the sun. Trackers are used to minimize the angle of incidence
between incident sunlight and a Photovoltaics system. This increases the energy generated from a
ﬁxed amount of installed power generation capacity. The main advantage of a tracking system is
that it can collect solar energy for the longest duration of the day.
To improve generation and make the most of the land available, it is imperative to install trackers.
The additional beneﬁts obtained by installing trackers outweigh the additional investment costs of
trackers. The photovoltaic industry in India has seen historically low rates that put pressure on the
IRR for developers. Installing trackers helps reduce this pressure on the designer by improving the
IRR by 2-3% compared to ﬁxed-tilt systems.
The most common tracking system used today are
• Single Axis Solar Tracking System:
These have a single degree of freedom that acts as an axis of rotation; hence there is always
perpendicular incidence between the sun and the panels. The axis of rotation of uniaxial

spaces to prevent gas mixing. The electrolyte is an aqueous solution of potassium hydroxide (KOH).
Water feeds the cathode, whereby the OH ions are transported through the membrane during the
electrolysis operation to form hydrogen and oxygen[29].
In PEM electrolysis, two electrodes are in contact with a proton exchange polymer electrolyte mem-
brane, typically perﬂuorosulfonic acid (PFSA), forming a membrane-electrode assembly (MEA).
Membranes generally are catalyst coated and in contact with carbon or expanded metal meshes
or metal felts. Commercially used state-of-the-art (SoA) catalysts have a platinum cathode and an
iridium oxide (IrO2) anode. The reaction occurs by transporting H+ ions across the membrane,
water is supplied at the anode and hydrogen is formed at the cathode[29].
In the case of SOEC, the electrolyte is an oxide-ion conductive solid phase component, typically
yttria-stabilised zirconia (YSZ). The cathode is made of nickel combined with YSZ to facilitate hy-
drogen generation. The anode comprises mixed ionic-electronic conductors such as lanthanum
strontium manganate (LSM) with YSZ. During the operation of a SOEC, O2 is transported through
the solid electrolyte. An advantage of the SOEC is the possibility of converting steam and CO2 into
synthesis gas at the same time (co-electrolysis)[29].

2.2.5. HYDROGEN STORAGE
Recently, hydrogen has attracted a lot of attention due to the rapid expansion of the renewable en-

2.2.6. LITHIUM-ION BATTERY
A Lithium-ion battery consists of two porous electrodes, which are separated by a permeable mem-
brane. The pores in the electrodes and membrane are ﬁlled with liquid electrolytes. Lithium salt
(e.g.LiPF6) is dissolved to form Li+ and PF6 ions in the electrolyte. Both positive and negative elec-
trode materials can react with Li+ ions. In the Li-ion battery, the negative electrode is usually made
of carbon, and the positive electrode is made of lithium metal oxide. Usually, the negative and pos-
itive electrodes are also called anode and anode, respectively. Electrons cannot travel through the
electrolyte. The membrane physically separates the two electrodes to prevent electrons from pass-
ing from the negative to the positive electrode, causing an internal short circuit in the battery[30].

2.2.7. FUEL CELL
A fuel cell is a class of devices that convert the chemical energy of a fuel directly into electricity
through electrochemical reactions. A fuel cell is similar to a battery in many ways, but it can pro-
vide electrical energy for a much more extended period. This is because a fuel cell continuously
receives fuel and air (or oxygen) from an external source, while a battery has a ﬁnite amount of
fuel and oxidizing material that is depleted with use[32]. The Proton Exchange Membrane (PEM)
fuel cell consists of a cathode and anode made of graphite and a proton-conducting polymer as the
electrolyte, as shown in Figure 2.16. Low-temperature PEM fuel cells operate at temperatures below
100°C (typically around 80°C) because the membrane must be saturated with water[25].

5.2. FUTURE WORK
Utilizing the thermal heat from hydrogen processes:
In order to reduce energy consumption, scrap can be preheated with heat from fuel cells in both
batch and continuous processes. Scrap preheating to 500°C (930°F) cuts power consumption by 40
to 50 kilowatt-hours per ton and decreases the taptotap time and electrode consumption. Scrap is
sometimes preheated inside the electric arc furnace by oxy-fuel burners, but this requires a large
exhaust system to treat the ﬂue gases. By applying these processes and using the electric arc fur-
nace to melt scrap, energy and electrode consumption in the steel production can be reduced to
just 360-kilowatt hours per tonne and three kilograms per tonne. The heating times are reduced to
around one hour. Applying methods initially developed for the basic Oxygen processes allows the
EAF to approach BF-BOF steelmaking rates[9]. Low-temperature fuel cells and electrolysers were
used in this project, but in the future, if the scalability of high-temperature hydrogen systems is not
==PAGE:58==
5.2. FUTURE WORK
46
a concern, they can be used in HPP and further increase efﬁciency and reduce overall energy con-
sumption for steelmaking. A resistance heater (often called an electric boiler) can also be added to
the HPP to convert excess electricity into heat energy that preheats the scrap metal in the EAF. This
component is called a thermal load controller in Homer, available on the Components tab.
Integrating carbon capture and storage (CCS) in steel making:
Steelmaking processes are energy and carbon-intensive industrial processes. Among the various
options that steel companies can incorporate to reduce their carbon footprint, CCS remains one of
the most effective and least intrusive ways to achieve deep emissions reductions. Carbon capture
technologies are mature and can be retroﬁtted to existing plants, maintaining existing plants with-
out disrupting current steel production processes. Therefore, CCS can achieve signiﬁcant CO2 emis-
sion reductions in steel production in synergy with other more innovative low-carbon approaches
[56].
Using salt haverns for hydrogen storage:
Using natural salt havens to store hydrogen can signiﬁcantly reduce the cost of hydrogen storage.
The price of hydrogen storage assumed in this project is USD 130 per kilogram; By storing the hydro-
gen in caverns, this can be reduced to USD 5 per kilogram. The storage of hydrogen in salt caverns is
similar to that of natural gas as they have similar cavern design, construction and operation require-
ments. They only differ in the materials in access wells, cave heads and transmission infrastructure.
Hydrogen in salt havens could be stored for a longer time.
