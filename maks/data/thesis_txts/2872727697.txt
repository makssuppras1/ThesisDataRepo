Abstract
Lactic acid bacteria (LAB) are among the oldest and most widely used microorganisms in
food fermentation, primarily due to their their safety, and their resistance to harsh environ­
mental conditions. LAB play a crucial role in both the food and pharmaceutical industries.
In particular, certain LAB strains contribute to the formulation of probiotics, which are as­
sociated with various health benefits, including improved digestive function and enhanced
immune response.
This study explores the use of 3D­printed microstructures, fabricated via two­photon poly­
merization (2PP) based 4D printing, for in situ monitoring of LAB fermentation. The pro­
posed method enables real­time detection of lactic acid production without disturbing the
cell culture environment. It is designed to be simple, non­invasive, and free from cross­
contamination risks.
Several microstructure designs were tested using an iterative approach to optimize their
dimensions and printing parameters. Comparative cell seeding experiments confirmed
that the 3D­printed sensing microstructures were suitable for detecting lactic acid through
their swelling response in acidic conditions.
This thesis demonstrates the potential of integrating pH­responsive hydrogels with 2PP ­
based 4D printing for cell culture and microbial fermentation monitoring applications.

Introduction
Probiotics are living microorganisms that offer numerous health benefits when consumed
in adequate amounts [1, 2]. They support digestive health, enhance immune function, and
contribute to a balanced intestinal microbiota. Some probiotics also produce antibiotic
substances that help them prevail in the intestinal environment and suppress harmful
pathogens [3].
Lactic acid bacteria (LAB) play a critical role in the food industry, particularly in dairy
products and probiotics [4]. LAB are the oldest known method for food preservation and
contribute to the qualities of food, such as odor, color, texture, and taste [4]. They mainly
active through their metabolism, converting sugars into lactic acid, which lowers the pH
of the food matrix, and other by products [5].
Traditionally, monitoring lactic acid production has been based on electrochemical pH
meters. However, these devices have several limitations, including inaccuracy in small
volume analysis, signal drift without recalibration, and require disruption of liquid cell sam­
ples, increasing the risk of contamination [6].
Two Photon Polymerization (2PP) is a high resolution 3D printing technology capable of
fabricating structures with submicrometer precision. It supports a broad range of pho­
topolymerizable smart materials, making it highly suitable for advanced applications. By
integrating micro 4D printing with 2PP, it becomes possible to utilize pH­responsive hy­
drogels to create responsive and functional microstructures [7, 8].
4D printing, an evolution of traditional 3D printing, introduces time as a fourth dimension,
enabling printed structures to change their shape in response to external stimuli. At the
microscale, 4D printing allows the fabrication of dynamic structures with tunable functions,
opening new possibilities for responsive systems [7].
1.1
Purpose of the project
This project aims to develop cell culture scaffolds with integrated sensing microstructures
through micro 4D printing, by combining 2PP with a pH responsive hydrogel. By utiliz­
ing the high resolution capabilities of 2PP and the stimulus responsive behavior of smart
materials, advanced scaffolds are fabricated for in situ monitoring of the cellular environ­
ment. The study will focus on the design, fabrication, and evaluation of these scaffolds,
assessing their feasibility for real­time cell population monitoring
The research involves comparing various geometries and dimensions of the printed struc­
tures to determine the optimal printing parameters that yield the highest pH response,
thereby enhancing their effectiveness for bacterial monitoring.
Overall, this project combines microfabrication and microbiology, with the potential to con­
tribute to the development of more efficient methods for bacterial detection and monitoring
in future research applications.

Theoretical Background
2.1
4D printing
Additive manufacturing (AM) also known as 3D printing, is defined by the American Soci­
ety for Testing and Materials (ASTM) as the ”process of joining materials to make objects
from 3D model data, usually layer upon layer, as opposed to subtractive manufacturing
technologies” [9]. In contrast, subtractive manufacturing achieves the desired geome­
try by cutting the material [10]. AM was first introduced by Charles Hull in 1986 when he
patented Stereolithography (SLA). 3D printing has evolved over the years alongside tech­
nological advancements. AM offers numerous advantages, such as customized products,
freedom of prototyping and reduced waste. The ISO/ASTM 52900:2015 standard identi­
fies more than 50 AM methods, reflecting the growing diversity of techniques [11]. With
the emergence of novel materials and fabrication strategies, AM now finds applications in
industries such as biomedical, construction, aerospace, and more [12].
The concept of 4D printing was first introduced by Skylar Tibbits, the director of the Mas­
sachusetts Institute of Technology (MIT) Self­Assembly Lab, in collaboration with Strata­
sys Ltd. Their goal was to develop self­assembling, programmable materials for industrial
applications [13]. In this technology, the original definition of the fourth dimension refers
to time. Specifically, 3D printed structures can undergo changes in shape or functional­
ity after fabrication in response to external stimuli [14, 15]. Over time, this concept has
evolved, and the new definition is ”the shape, property, and functionality of 3D structures
could evolve with time when they are exposed to a predetermined stimulus, such as heat,
water or solvent in general, light, pH, etc” [16]. Figure 2.1 illustrates this principle.
Figure 2.1: Schematic illustration of 4D printing. Drawn in BioRender.
The most commonly used materials for microscale 4D printing are polymers, such as hy­
drogels. Other polymers that have been explored are shape memory polymers (SMPs),
liquid crystal elastomers (LCE), or composites for applications across various sectors, in­
cluding microrobotics, smart devices, and biomedical engineering [17]. The integration of
smart materials into AM represents a significant advancement in the technology adoption
[12].
This project focuses on 2PP, a light­based printing technique, for the fabrication of mi­
crostructures used as sensors in cell culture applications. To enable the characteristic
dynamic functionality of 4D printing, 2PP must be combined with smart materials that
respond to external stimuli.

Theoretical Background
2.2
Smart materials ­ 2PP resins
In microscale 4D printing, structures are fabricated using smart polymer based materials
and can alter their shape in response to external stimuli. Their shape morphing capabil­
ities typically result from volume or shape changes, making them valuable in microscale
applications where dynamic functionality is desired [17]
In 2PP, photoresists primarily undergo radical polymerization initiated by highly focused
light. These photoresists typically consist of two key components, a polymer precursor
and a photoinitiator (PI) [17]. Upon light absorption, the PI generates reactive radicals
that initiate cross­linking of the polymer chains, transforming the liquid resin into a solid
[18, 19]. A more detailed discussion is provided in Section 2.3. The choice of monomers
and cross­linker is crucial, as it not only defines the mechanical and chemical properties
of the resulting material but also enables the responsiveness to external stimuli [20].
2.2.1
Hydrogel
Hydrogels are networks of hydrophilic cross­linked polymeric chains that can absorb large
amounts of water leading to their swelling. The degree of their swelling depends on the
volume fraction of the polymer, the average molecular weight between cross­links, and
the material’s mesh size [21]. Hydrogels offer several advantages, including high water
absorbency, biocompatibility, a neutral pH, optimal porosity, and high solubility, making
them an excellent candidate for biological applications [22].
2.2.2
pH responsive Hydrogels
Hydrogels play a prominent role in the microscale 4D printing field due to their inherent
responsiveness to environmental stimuli such as temperature, light, pH, electric fields, and
magnetic fields [23]. Among these, pH­responsive hydrogels are especially significant
due to their tunable behavior under different pH environments.
To enable pH responsiveness, the hydrogel formulation often includes monomers like
acrylamides and actylates, which contain ionizable functional groups such as amines and
carboxyl groups. These functional groups respond to changes in pH by altering their
ionization state, which modifies the swelling behavior of the hydrogel [19].
pH responsive hydrogels can be classified as ionic or amphionic. Ionic hydrogels are fur­
ther divided into anionic hydrogels, which contain negatively charged functional groups,
and cationic hydrogels, which contain positively charged functional groups. Amphionic
hydrogels contain both types of functional groups [24]. In ionic hydrogels, swelling is
influenced by osmotic pressure and ionic interactions between charged polymer chains
and surrounding free ions [24]. In anionic hydrogels, acidic functional groups such as
carboxyls ionize under basic conditions, increasing electrostatic repulsion between neg­
atively charged chains and enhancing water uptake [17, 25]. However, once ionization
reaches saturation, further increases in pH have minimal effect on swelling behavior [26].

2.3
Two­photon polymerization (2PP)
Two­photon polymerization (2PP), also known as Direct Laser Writing (DLW), is a light­
based 3D printing technology used for fabricating arbitrary 3D micro­ and nanostructures,
achieving submicrometer resolution with feature sizes below the Abbe’s diffraction limit
[17, 29]. It operates by using a tightly focused near­infrared (NIR) femtosecond laser to
initiate polymerization within a liquid photosensitive resin with a high numerical­aperture
(NA) objective [30]. The solid structure is built layer by layer by scanning the laser within
the photoresist. Each layer consists of voxels, the fundamental building block of a 3D
structure where the polymerization occurs at the focal point of the laser beam. [17, 8].
The 2PP process consists of three main stages as described in the following equations:
Initiation, where the PI molecule absorbs two photons and enters the excited state. This
leads to bond cleavage and the generation of reactive radicals. Propagation, where these
reactive radicals initiate polymerization by reactive with monomers. The active radicals
react with additional monomers, leading to propagation of the polymer chain through re­
peated addition. Termination occurs when two active polymer chains combine, forming a
stable polymerized voxel [31].

2.3.1
Two­photon absorption (TPA)
2PP relies on two­photon absorption (TPA), a non linear absorption process that was first
theorized by Maria Göppert­Mayer, a german physicist, during her doctorate dissertation
in 1931 [32]. This phenomenon was experimentally observed thirty years later by Kaiser
and Garret, who demonstrated two­photon excitation in CaF2:Eu2+ crystals using red light
from a ruby optical maser, resulting in blue fluorescence [33]. However, it was not until
the 1980s and early 1990s that 2PP emerged as a method for writing simple structures
[34].

Figure 2.3: Jablonski diagram comparing OPA and TPA. Images inspired by [31, 35].
In the process of TPA, a PI molecule is excited from the ground state S0 to the excited state
S1 by simultaneously absorbing two photons via a virtual state with a lifetime of femtosec­
onds (10−15 s) [36, 31]. Figure 2.3 shows the Jabloski diagrams for both OPA and TPA. In
OPA a single photon excites the molecule by absorbing (ultraviolet) UV light. In contrast,
in TPA two photons are absorbed by NIR light. The energy sum of the two photons must
be equal to the gap between the atoms ground and the excited state. However, during
the TPA process, each photon has the same frequency and half the energy required for
the single­photon excitation [37].
TPA is a non­linear process in which the energy absorbed is proportional to the square
of the NIR light intensity (I2). In contrast, OPA is a linear process, where the energy

The voxel defines the spatial resolution in 2PP. It’s size can be controlled by properly
selecting the processing parameters. Polymerization occurs only when the light intensity

The optimal processing window is between the polymerization threshold and the onset
of bubble formation. Beyond this range, the material will either fail to polymerize if the
intensity is below the threshold or begin to deform if the power exceeds the threshold. As
illustrated in Figure2.4, the closer the laser power is to the polymerization threshold, the
smaller the voxel size becomes, allowing for higher resolution. Conversely, higher laser
power increases the voxel size and reduces the resolution. Therefore, operating near the
polymerization threshold yields the best resolution [39].

2.4
Lactic Acid Bacteria (LAB)
Lactic acid bacteria (LAB) are a group of non­spore­forming, Gram­positive bacteria with
a spherical or rod shape. They are characterized by their ability to produce organic acids
as the end product of carbohydrate fermentation [41]. They exhibit characteristics such
as resistance to acidic conditions, aerotolerance, and immobility. While they grow under
anaerobic conditions and do not rely on oxygen for energy production, they can also
survive and grow in its presence. [42]. Their main distinctive feature is the production
of lactic acid as the primary end product of carbohydrate fermentation [43]. LAB has
two main fermentation pathways: homofermenation, in which only lactic acid is produced,
and heterofermentation, in which the end products are lactic acid, acetic acid, ethanol and
CO2 [41]. LAB are widely used in the food industry for food preservation, as they create
an unfavorable environment for pathogens, add flavor, and extend shelf­life. LAB exhibit
significant genetic diversity and inhabit various environments. Consequently, there are
no universal constraints on parameters such as pH, temperature, and other factors, since
these conditions depend on the specific LAB strains [44].
In recent decades, the focus has shifted from merely satisfying hunger to ensuring ade­
quate nutrient intake and promoting a healthier lifestyle. Many studies suggest that LAB
involved in food fermentation produce various bioactive compounds within the food ma­
trix, contributing to health benefits [44]. A key compound that contributes to these health
benefits is probiotics, which are primarily delivered through fermented foods [45]. Ac­
cording to the Food and Agriculture Organization of the United Nations (FAO) and the
Word Health Organization (WHO) probiotics are ”live microorganisms that, when admin­
istrated in adequate amount confer a heath benefit to the host” [46]. They help maintain a
balanced intestinal microbiota, thus supporting proper intestinal function and preventing
many gastrointestinal diseases [45]. The minimum daily effective dose required to benefit
the intestinal microenvironment is around 106 ­ 109 live microbial cells per day [45]. Due
to their long history of use in food fermentation and their role in improving the functional
properties of food, LAB have been listed as Generally Regarded as Safe (GRAS) by the
United States Food and Drug Administration (FDA) on the strain lever or as Qualified
Presumption of Safety (QPS) at the species level by the European Food Safety Authority
(EFSA) [47].

3.1
2PP Set up
The fabrication of the microstructures was carried out using the Nanoscribe Photonic Pro­
fessional GT+ (Nanoscribe, Karlsruhe, Germany), a 3D printer based of the principles of
TPP, designed for ultra­precise and rapid microfabrication. Nanoscribe was the first com­
pany to commercialize 3D 2PP printers, prior to this, custom­made setups were available.
The printer includes a femtosecond (Ti:sapphire) laser source with the center wavelength
at 780 nm, laser power 50 mW at LaserPower 100% and PowerScale 1, pulse duration
100 fs and 200 fs, and repetition rate 80 MHz [30].

3.1.1
Preparation and Optimization of Print Jobs
Upon designing the microstructures using Fusion 360, the models were exported in Stan­
dard Tessellation Language (STL) format. These STL files are then imported into the
DeScribe software (version 2.7, Nanoscribe, Karlsruhe, Germany), which generates the
corresponding General Writing Language (GWL) files.
Describe includes an import wizard where the .STL file is loaded and allows one to select
key system parameters and define the trajectory of the laser within the resin. After loading
the .STL file into the software, the first step is to center the model and align the coordinate
system origin (x=0,y=0) with the geometric center of the design. This ensures that printing
begins from the stage origin and allows the galvo scanner to make full us of the printing
field. The software automatically converts the dimensions from millimeters (mm), the unit
used in Fusions360, to micrometers (μm).
Describe offer recipes with printing parameters for the commercial resins. However, since
this project involves a custom­made hydrogel resin, the system parameters had to be
manually adjusted. In the following steps, the slicing distance and hatching distance were
defined. Slices correspond to the layered cross­sections of the model along the Z­axis,
while hatches are parallel lines within each layer in the X–Y plane.
The surface of the printed structures can be smoothed by adding contour lines. In this
case, a contour line is a continuous outline that defines the outer boundary of the printed
layer. Figure 3.2 is a schematic illustration of a sample with contour line.

print settings. A separate parent file ,_job.gwl, which includes the _data.gwl file and De­

from which the printing process is initiated.

3.1.2
Sample Preparation
Once the print files have been prepared, the fabrication process can begin. In the oil
immersion configuration, the borosilicate round glass is taped into the sample holder, the
resin is deposited onto the top the silanized substrate and the oil is then applied to the
opposite side of the glass, which comes into contact with the objective. It is important that
both the resin and the immersion oil are aligned at the same central spot to ensure proper
focusing.
However, the hydrogel used in this study is a highly volatile material due to the presence

3.3.1
Actuation of pH responsive hydrogel
The swelling response of the hydrogel is a fundamental criterion for characterization and
evaluation of its performance. To assess this, actuation tests were performed using so­
lutions of varying pH levels. The AAc hydrogel has a pKa of approximately 4.3. At pH
values above pKa, the pendant carboxylic acid groups become deprotonated, forming
negatively charged acrylate ions (–COO­). This introduces negative charges on the poly­
mer chains, leading to electrostatic repulsion and increased osmotic pressure. Together,
these effects promote water uptake and cause the hydrogel to swell. In contrast, at pH
values below pKa, the carboxylic groups remain protonated (–COOH) and neutral, re­
sulting in no charge repulsion. As a result, the hydrogel contracts and releases water,
entering a more compact state. Upon removal from the solution, it shrinks further, reach­
ing its driest form. Therefore, pH lower than pKa (in this case 2.3), where the gel is fully
contracted, is used as the reference point to compare swelling behavior under different
pH conditions.[49].
A total of seven pH solutions were prepared by my co­supervisor, Marcin Piotr Piekarczyk:
2.3, 4.4, 5.8, 6.7, 7.4, 8.8, and 12. After the fabrication and development of the samples,
a square glass cover was placed again on top of the tapes to form a cell. For each test,
10 μL of the respective pH solution was pipetted into the cell. Capillary forces facilitated
the diffusion of the solution, ensuring that the structures were fully immersed.
The imaging of the structures was performed using a digital microscope with a 1500x
objective to capture high resolution images. After imaging, the solution was carefully
removed from the cell using a small piece of clean wipe. This process was repeated for
each of the seven pH solutions.
Following the initial test, the samples were then sterilized overnight in 96% ethanol and
rinsed with sterile MilliQ water. The same actuation procedure was repeated to determine
whether overnight sterilization affected the swelling behavior of the structures.
Additionally, a second set of pH solutions was prepared by my supervisor using MRS
(De Man, Rogosa and Sharpe) medium. This was done to evaluate the hydrogel’s pH
response under biologically relevant conditions, beyond the standard solutions.
The images obtained with the microscope were analyzed using ImageJ software. Their
swelling response was calculated using the equation 3.1.

4.1.2
Parameter optimization of sensing microstructures
After selecting the optimal structure and dimensions, the next step was to optimize the
printing parameters. The default printing settings for the hydrogel included a LP of 90%
(see Table 3.1). However, LP was reduced to 70% due to significant bubbling observed
during printing, which was attributed to the height and bulk of the design. These parame­
ters were subsequently refined to enhance the performance of the printed structures. To
achieve a hydrogel with a high swelling response, there are generally two approaches:
one is to modify the chemical composition of the hydrogel, and the other is to adjust the
laser polymerization conditions [51]. In this study, the chemical composition was kept con­
stant, as alteration was beyond the scope of our work. Therefore, the focus was placed
on tuning the laser processing parameters.
A laser processing window needed to be identified, defining the range of parameters
where the exposure dose is sufficient to produce stable structures, while also allowing
for high swelling response. This optimization was carried out through multiple parameter
sweeps. In each parameter sweep, an array of the structures was created with varied
selected parameters along the X and Y axes. LP was selected for the X axes, and SS
for the Y axes. After printing, the optimal parameter sets were identified through visual
inspection using a digital microscope, which provided detailed insight into the morphol­
ogy of the structures. These findings were further validated via actuation tests to evaluate
swelling performance. .
In the parameter sweep analysis, in addition to LP and SS, the number of contour lines was
also investigated. Structures were printed with 0 or 1 contour lines to test their print quality
and performance in subsequent actuation tests. In the initial experiment, LP ranged from
50% to 90% in increments of 10%, and SS varied from 5000 μm/s to 8000 μm/s in incre­
ments of 1000 μm/s. For each parameter combination, an array of four sensing elements
was printed. The results of this initial parameter sweep are presented in Figure 4.7.

5
Conclusion
Two­photon polymerization (2PP) is one of the most innovative techniques for rapid proto­
typing and the fabrication of complex 3D micro­ and nanostructures. When combined with
smart materials, it enables the emerging technology of micro 4D printing, where printed
structures can actively respond to environmental stimuli. In this project, 2PP was suc­
cessfully used to fabricate pH responsive hydrogel microstructures designed to monitor
lactic acid production in LAB cultures, as well as scaffolds with integrated microstructures
that would provide structural support for cell attachment .
After multiple design iterations, the microstructures were developed to swell in response
to pH changes, acting as sensing elements for lactic acid monitoring. Several factors were
investigated to optimize their performance, including geometry, dimensions, and printing
parameters. Among these, laser power and scan speed were found to have the most
significant impact on swelling behavior. The optimal parameters were LP: 60 % and SS:
7000 μm/s. These produced the highest swelling response while maintaining structural
integrity.
For the scaffold fabrication, different shapes were explored. We integrated two patterns
into the scaffolds, each designed to influence bacterial adhesion differently. The first con­
sisted of parallel grooves, replicated from a previous master’s thesis, which enhanced
bacterial adhesion. The second pattern was designed with a bacteriostatic effect in mind,
aiming to inhibit bacterial accumulation. It was positioned around the sensing microstruc­
tures to test whether it could prevent them from being fully covered with cells. Extensive
testing showed that the optimal scaffold printing parameters were LP: 80% and SS: 8000
μm/s. However, in the final round of fabrication, these parameters no longer yielded con­
sistent results. This inconsistency highlights the challenging nature of hydrogel based
printing.
Despite these difficulties, the hydrogel material demonstrated robustness, after two rounds
of sterilization, its structural and swelling properties remained unchanged. It also proved
to be non­cytotoxic, showing no abnormal behavior during cell experiments.
Unfortunately, the imaging process during the bacteria culture experiments presented un­
expected challenges. The outdated microscope setup made the data collection in both
experimental iterations difficult. Although modifications were made, such as changing the
well plate configuration and using sample holders to enable imaging with larger objective,
the quality of the data remained insufficient.
Because not all samples were imaged at every time point, the results were analyzed col­
lectively across the four prepared samples. The swelling behavior of the microstructures
at various time points reflected changes in the surrounding pH. Starting at an estimated
pH of 6, the structures reached a swelling level consistent with a pH of 4.3 after 24 hours,
closely matching the measured pH of 4.29 obtained using a pH meter.
Although the project faced several technical challenges, its primary objective was achieved
somewhat successfully. The results demonstrate that the fabricated microstructures can
detect pH changes in their environment, validating their potential for in situ monitoring of
lactic acid production during LAB fermentation.

Conclusion

5.1
Future perspectives
This project bridges the fields of 2PP printing with stimulus­responsive materials and mi­
crobiology. Although both areas are well­established and extensively studied, their inte­
gration in the context of developing microscale sensing systems for monitoring bacterial
metabolic activity is a novel approach and requires deeper investigation.
One of the key areas that needed improvement was the scaffold fabrication process. Hy­
drogels are soft materials, and during 3D printing they are prone to bubbling and burning,
especially when printing larger structures. Nearly all printed scaffolds exhibited some
degree of deformation.
Despite multiple rounds of parameter sweep analysis to identify ideal settings, we faced
persistent issues. Specifically, during the final round of printing for the second iteration
of the bacterial experiment, the scaffolds failed to print correctly, even when using the
two parameter sets that had previously yielded the best results. This failure might be
attributed to the new batch of hydrogel, but it underscores a broader issue that printing
large hydrogel structures is unreliable.
A more robust solution would be to fabricate the scaffold using a more stable material,
such as IP­S, which is known for its reliability in printing larger, more complex geometries.
Furthermore, studies have shown that LAB adhere better on IP­S compared to hydrogel.
The sensing microstructures, which printed well during our tests, could then be fabricated
directly on top of the IP­S scaffold.
Unfortunately, this approach was not implemented due to time constraints. Aligning and
printing with two different materials is a more advanced technique that requires additional
training, which was not feasible within the scope of this project.
Another method we considered was first to print the hydrogel scaffold, allow it to cool for
a few minutes, and then print the sensing microstructures on top. This strategy aimed
to reduce deformation, especially in the cylindrical structures, which consistently showed
the most deformation as a result their added height to the scaffold. These cylinders were
also the primary focus of our sensing experiments. However, this method also falls under
advanced printing techniques and would have required training.
Due to our collaborative timeline with an external lab handling the bacterial culture, we
were unable to adjust the schedule. The bacterial experiments required five full days
(two for preparation and three for execution), and the other lab had their own constraints.
As such, we could not afford the additional time needed to explore these more complex
printing approaches.
During the cell culture experiments, we encountered numerous issues. The imaging setup
at the KU lab was particularly challenging, as the microscope used was quite outdated.
In both iterations of the experiment, the microscope battery died during imaging, forcing
us to pause and wait several hours for it to recharge before continuing. This significantly
disrupted the workflow and limited the imaging consistency.
If this experiment is repeated or similar experiments are planned in the KU lab in the future,
the imaging protocol must be revised. One promising alternative would be to use an
inverted microscope and flip the sample so that the top of the structures faces downward.

A.3
Silanization
Silianization bath is prepared under appropriate solvents fume hood.
Solvents used
throughout the process are collected and disposed of in C­waste on an ongoing basis.
The whole set up will be properly marked with safety labels during operation
1. Wash the coverslips with the following solvents:
• Acetone
• Isopropanol (IPA)
2. Dry the substrates with N2 / air gun. Place the substrate in appropriate plastic hold­
ers.
3. Put the holders with coverslips inside oxygen plasma chamber and run the process
for 15 minutes. Use program P2 and adjust the time accordingly. Make sure oxygen
line is connected. After the process is done, run the same procedure for 10 minutes
in order to clean the chamber from potential polymer residues
4. While the plasma is running, prepare fresh silanizing solution in teflon beaker for
silanization:
• 100 mL ethanol
• 1 mL silanizing agent (mix well with ethanol) (use 19G needle + 2 mL syringe)
• 0.5 mL of our 30% acetic acid (add just before the substrates)
Adjust the volume based on the number of sample holders. Multiply the solu­
tions volume by four when using one sample holder, and by three when using
two sample holders.
5. Incubate substrates in silanizing solution for 1 h at room temperature, use lid (second
teflon beaker) to prevent evaporation.
6. Rinse with IPA
7. Dry the substrates with N2 / air gun.
8. Bake the coverslips for 1 h at 60 °C on a hotplate, with silanized layer facing up.
9. Place the dry substrates in a petri dish or substrate holder.
10. Store in the dark and use within a month (note silanization date on storage lid).
For self­protecting measures, following equipment must be worn or ready to use in the
close vicinity:
• Gloves: in case of unavoidable skin contact, disposable gloves are used. Pay atten­
tion to whether the glove used is marked as applicable for the work task according
to the ISO EN374 standard. In practice, the application time can be significantly
shorter than the breakthrough time measured according to the standard, due to
several influencing factors (e.g. temperature, thickness)
• Safety glasses: Use equipment for eye protection tested and approved under ap­
propriate government standards EN 166(EU).
• Appropriate fume hood suitable for solvents.
• Laboratory coat or cleanroom gown.

A.4
Resin Preparation
1. Select one brown bottle with photoinitiator (PI, BAPO) and check the labelled weight
2. Calculate the necessary volume os satomer (SR9035) and acrylic acid (AA):
• SR9035 (µL) = PI (mg)*105
• AA (µL) = PI (mg)*19.5
3. Using 1 mL Eppendorf pipette with a cup tip, add SR9035 to the PI bottle.
4. Cut two pieces of parafilm suitable for small bottle lids.
5. Using the 200 µL Eppendorf pipette, add AA to the PI bottle. Close both the AA
bottle and the resin mix bottle as soon as possible in order to minimize AA
evaporation, and wrap them with parafilm.
6. Vortex the resin well to ensure mixing.
7. Sonicate the resin (5 min) to ensure that the PI dissolves.
8. Vortex the resin again after sonication, then left it rest until any bubbles inside dis­
appear.

A.9
Project Plan
This section presents the planned tasks for the thesis project, details the outcomes achieved,
discusses any deviations from the initial plan, and includes the corresponding Gantt chart.
Conceptualization and Planning
In this part, the alignment printing training was not completed, as it was not directly rele­
vant to the experiments. All other tasks were completed within the planned schedule.
Preliminary Studies
During this period, the majority of the relevant literature directly related to the project was
collected to help identify existing work and guide the direction of the project. However,
additional publications were reviewed throughout the project. The first draft of the litera­
ture review was not submitted according to the original schedule but rather two months
later. All other tasks were completed within the planned schedule.
Phase One ­ Fabrication of sensing elements using pH responsive hydrogel
During this phase, multiple designs were fabricated and evaluated according to sched­
ule. The morphological analysis of the structures was primarily conducted using a digital
microscope. SEM was utilized in the subsequent phase, while confocal microscopy was
not used at all. All other tasks were completed within the planned schedule.
Phase Two ­ Integration of sensing elements into scaffolds for cell culture
applications
During this phase, the sensing microstructures were implemented only on top of the scaf­
folds. No experiments were conducted to validate the optimal placement, as the posi­
tion had been determined during the design phase. Three scaffold shapes with varying
heights were fabricated and tested through experiments. However, during the final fab­
rication process, the scaffolds failed to print correctly and were burned. As a result, the
bacterial experiments could not be performed with the scaffolds and were instead carried
out using only the sensing elements. All other tasks were completed within the planned
schedule
Thesis writing
The first draft was written and submitted on time, and initial feedback was provided ac­
cording to the schedule. Following revisions based on this feedback, the final report was
submitted by the deadline.
