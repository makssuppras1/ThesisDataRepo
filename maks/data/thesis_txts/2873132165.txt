Abstract
Stroke is the leading cause of disability worldwide and while effective therapies to help
in rehabilitating post­stroke disabilities are known, they have issues with adherence and
motivation in a home setting. The major focus is on lower limb rehabilitation in the acute
stage of the post­stroke recovery massively increases the chances for the patient to be
able to walk again, giving them a better quality of life. This leads to upper limb rehabilita­
tion being delegated to a later point, usually after the window in which a patient will see
the most improvement. The result being that patients will often see much slower progress
in their fine motor recovery of the hand leading them to be demotivated. This combined
with the apathy and depression that can happen after a stroke makes it much more diffi­
cult to stay engaged in a home­based rehabilitation therapy. This thesis aims to alleviate
this, by designing and developing a mixed reality game based on hand rehabilitation ex­
ercises, which includes gamification elements to increase engagement and motivation.
The game developed is a LEGO stacking game in which the player has to stack LEGO
DUPLO bricks on top of each other, which the game can recognize using a state­of­the­
art object detection model, that is trained on a dataset created during the thesis. As the
game progresses, a larger Lego build is built brick by brick to keep the player’s interest
until the build is completed. The game supports changing the difficulty in a wide variety of
ways, from changing the size of the stack, to where on a table they need to place them.
The game has been tested throughout the development process on non­target users to
ensure playability and performance and with continuous feedback from experts. During
gameplay data is collected about the users hands, fingers, etc. This is used to create a
heatmap visualization and 3D reconstruction to display hand movements over a game­
play session for later review. The final test on a target user group showed that the product
has potential to be used in rehabilitation, which was validated through conversations with
healthcare professionals and other experts and while it has some issues, it shows a great
potential in the future of mixed reality in rehabilitation.

Introduction
With an increasing number of elderly people, strokes are becoming more and more com­
mon, putting more pressure on elderly care and the healthcare sector. Approximately
60% of people who experience a stroke will suffer from some kind of loss of motion in
affected limbs and often also suffer some form of neurological issues as well. Through
physical therapy, a patient is able to regain some or all of their mobility in the affected
limbs. The two main approaches to stroke rehabilitation are either in a rehabilitation cen­
ter or at­home therapy. At­home therapy has proven to be as effective as rehabilitation in
a rehabilitation center, if the rehabilitation schedule is adhered to. The biggest reason for
not adhering to the schedule is lack of motivation, equipment, variation in intensity and
length of the exercises and potential neurological issues.
With a background in computer vision and previous experience in game development,
our main contribution to the field of at­home stroke rehabilitation, is the use of extended
reality (XR) technology in combination with state­of­the­art computer vision methods, to
combine the real and virtual world. This allows rehabilitation exercises to be designed as
games, that is played in 3D space using physical objects like LEGO DUPLO bricks, that
can interact with virtual elements.
Earlier works have explored the idea of using computer aided exercises to improve moti­
vation and adherence in at­home rehabilitation of upper limbs. While showing promising
results, they suffer from one of two problems. One problem being portability, due to a big­
ger setup and/or amount of items needed for the exercises. Another problem is hardware
limitations, like camera quality and display capability.
Rapid development in extended reality (XR) technology, like virtual reality (VR), mixed re­
ality (MR) and smartglasses, fixes many of the problems of the earlier works, by providing
high­quality hardware in a portable and user­friendly form factor.
1.1
Scope
The goal of this thesis is to create a proof­of­concept prototype exercise game for upper
limb rehabilitation following stroke, using extended reality (XR) technology like augmented
reality (AR) or mixed reality (MR).
The primary focus of the game is on hand fine motor skills using LEGO DUPLO bricks.
We narrow the scope further to focus on GRASP­like exercises, such as picking up, mov­
ing, stacking and separating LEGO DUPLO bricks.
Game design principles like performance feedback and adjustable difficulty are incor­
porated to increase motivation and to make the difficulty level match the user’s current
abilities. Difficulty adjustment is done by tuning parameters, like the number of bricks,
min and max stack height, point distribution, etc. A dynamically adjusting difficulty was
not implemented, as it was meant to rely on an AI model and personalized medical data,
which is outside the scope of the thesis.
The LEGO DUPLO bricks used in the game are limited to 2x4 bricks in the colors: red,
green, blue and yellow, but the detection model is also able to distinguish between the
LEGO DUPLO figures: big penguin, small penguin, lion, sheep, pig and human, see fig­
ure 1.1. These are used to change the difficulty of the game, to predetermined presets
based on what figure is in view.

Future development could include more complex builds, like small structures, but this is

not being sufficient for consistent detection of more complex builds.
During gameplay, data on hand, finger and head movement is recorded and saved. This
data is used to make a 3D reconstruction after the play session. The 3D reconstruction
is used to visually show what data is possible to collect. Further data analysis is outside
the scope of this thesis, due to time constraints.
Three types of tests were performed on the game.
• Test 1 ­ Functional and performance tests to eliminate bugs and ensure consistent
performance.
• Test 2 ­ Focused on gameplay and model robustness and tested on non­stroke­
affected adults outside of the thesis.
• Test 3 ­ A proof of concept test on young adults suffering from various degrees of
hemiparesis and observed by a physical therapist.
Long­term clinical outcomes are outside the scope, due to the time frame of this thesis.

1.2
Research Questions
Our main research question is ”How can AI­driven computer vision and gamification im­
prove the engagement of at­home rehabilitation exercises?”. After outlining the scope,
the focus of the thesis changed, from the impact of such a rehabilitation game, to whether
it is even possible to make such a game. Therefore, one of the original research sub­
questions, ”How does real­time AI­driven feedback impact motivation and engagement of
rehabilitation exercises?”, was replaced with new sub­questions that aligned more with
the direction of the thesis.
Research sub­question
­ How might one design an XR­based rehabilitation game in a way that makes it en­
gaging for the user?
­ How might one design an XR­based rehabilitation game in a way that allows adjust­
ment to multiple levels of disability?
­ How accurately can computer vision and AI track and assess the hand and finger
movement?
­ What are the challenges of building a rehabilitation game with consumer XR tech­
nology and real­time computer vision?

Background
This chapter gives an overview of some of the current stroke rehabilitation approaches.
Looking at common challenges like adherence and motivation, and specific rehabilitation
methods such as the GRASP program. It discusses gamification and the emerging use
of technologies like computer vision and smart devices in rehabilitation and what their
impact is.
2.1
Home­based stroke rehabilitation
Stroke is one of the leading causes of death and disability in the world. It is estimated
that over 100 million people worldwide are living post stroke and that one in four people
will experience a stroke in their lifetime [1] and of those people upwards of 60% will suf­
fer from some kind of spasticity which leads to weakness and loss of motion in affected
limbs. Physical therapy is used as a tool to alleviate this spasticity and regain mobility in
the limbs affected by the stroke [2]. An important factor for post­stroke recovery is reha­
bilitation, but how this rehabilitation is done can vary greatly. The two main approaches to
rehabilitation are either based in a rehabilitation center, where the patient goes to receive
therapy or they can sometimes receive therapy at home. While home­based therapy can
vary quite a bit for the individual, it often leads to better outcomes for the patients. There
are also other benefits that favor home­based rehabilitation, as it is often less costly and
usually preferred by the individual [3]. While there are benefits to the home­based ap­
proach of rehabilitation, one should be aware of some of the downsides, like a lack of
equipment and variation in intensity and length of the individual therapies. However the
biggest problem seen in at­home rehabilitation is the lack of motivation and adherence to
the therapy. Post stroke survivors often suffer not only from physical disability, but also
neurological issues as well, one of them being post stroke apathy. This apathy makes it
harder for the individual patients to adhere to their therapy [4].
An important factor to consider for rehabilitation is the time from stroke onset till the reha­
bilitation begins. It has been shown that starting as early as day 1 or 2, depending on the
type of stroke [5] and doing intensive training 2­3 months after onset provides the patient
with the best outcomes [6]. A common misconception about long­term rehabilitation not
providing results comes from the plateau that often sets in after the first 6 months where
progress happens spontaneously, however performing rehabilitation post 6 months still
shows that significant recovery can be made, suggesting that timing is important but not
required for good outcomes [7].
Lower limb rehabilitation is often the first focus of post­stroke rehabilitation as helping the
person walk is a big part of regaining their independence and getting them discharged
from the hospital setting [8]. This means that the first critical window to rehabilitation is
taken up by regaining mobility in the affected leg and the upper limbs are delegated till
later on as it isn’t as important as getting people walking. Both patients and therapists
indicate a lack of focus on upper limb mobility as a part of the rehabilitation process, with
the main complaint being the lack of guidance and, effective and affordable tools [9, 10].

2.1.2
Gamification in rehabilitation
Motivation can be a major issue in rehabilitation and approaches to making it more fun
and engaging have been implemented in various different ways. There is good evidence
that shows that using gamification elements in rehabilitation provides better outcomes,

such as virtual reality (VR) and mixed reality (MR), gamified exercises and vision­based

increase engagement and improve the quality of unsupervised at­home rehabilitation.
Colomer et al. (2016)[17] developed a mixed reality (MR) tabletop system for upper limb
rehabilitation after stroke. The system works by having a projector above a table that
then projects virtual environments onto a table, allowing patients to interact with tangible
objects. The interactions are captured using a Microsoft Kinect, allowing the projected
virtual environment to adapt based on the user’s interaction and give audiovisual feed­
back. They used an A­B­A study design, where the A phase consisted of 30 sessions
of conventional physical therapy and the B phase consisted of 30 training sessions with
their experimental system. This demonstrated improvements in activity­level based on
the Wolf Motor Function Test, Box and Block Test, and Nine Hole Peg Test, which all par­
ticipants performed before, after and between the phases. Their approach emphasized
intensity, task specificity, and personalized progression. It is important to note that the
personalized progression was not automated, but determined by a physical therapist.
Rubino et al. (2024)[16] introduce Track And Intercept Task (TrAIT), which is a semi­
immersive, gamified rehabilitation exercise, that is capable of adapting in difficulty, based
on the user’s performance using a challenge point framework. TrAIT was performed us­
ing an apparatus consisting of a 46­inch monitor with a Microsoft Kinect camera on top,
positioned 72 inches away from the user. Prior to each session, the exercise was cali­
brated to each individual’s arm active range of motion, by having the user reach as far
as they could to the 4 corners of the screen. With performance­based difficulty scaling
and adaptive feedback, they effectively personalize the exercise to the user’s capabilities.
TrAIT has shown improved upper limb movement speed and engagement across a wide
range of stroke impairments, after 4 weeks of 2­3 practice sessions per week.
Earlier work by Burke et al. (2008)[19] explored the use of vision­based games specifi­

gameplay. Their goal was to improve the motivation and support of unsupervised rehabil­
itation, which can be performed at home. Their system also introduces the concept of a
patient profile, this profile contains data about the patient, including their previous scores
and timestamps, allowing for a graphical representation of the patient’s progress over
time, other profile data includes the speed and length of each game. All this data is used
to help a physical therapist determine the right difficulty. Due to hardware limitations the 2
prototype games developed focused primarily on gross motor reach. As colored tracking
markers were used to detect movement, only a limited number of points were trackable.
Tada et al. (2024)[25] introduced a MR system using Microsoft’s HoloLens 2 to perform
an integrated evaluation of spatial cognition and motor function in stroke rehabilitation.

4.1
Rehabilitation Training Strategies
Effective rehabilitation for individuals recovering from stroke, often involves a combination
of motor and cognitive interventions. The following training strategies are commonly used
to target physical and cognitive aspects of rehabilitation. The exploration of how to incor­
porate technologies such as virtual reality (VR) and how it can support and/or enhance
these approaches is increasing.
4.1.1
Repetitive Task Training
Repetitive Task Training (RTT) is a rehabilitation technique that entails performing the
same movement or functional task repeatedly to improve motor function. RTT is often
used as part of the rehabilitation process after a stroke or traumatic brain injury. An RTT
task could consist of a whole complex task that utilizes the entire leg or arm, such as
taking a step or drinking from a cup. A task could also be a subtask of a more complex
one, like bending and straightening one’s knee or curling one’s fingers to grip around a
cup. This makes RTT a flexible rehabilitation technique that can be modified to fit the
patient’s ability.
A meta study shows RTT in comparison with the usual rehabilitation process or placebo
groups, that people who practiced RTT tasks showed more improvement in arm and hand
function, walking distance and walking ability. The quality of the results is limited due to
poor and inconsistent study details and results, making comparisons hard across studies
in this area [27].
4.1.2
Cognitive Training
The literature surrounding cognitive training in post­stroke and traumatic brain injury pa­
tients shows strong evidence when measured on patient­reported outcomes concerning
activities of daily living (ADL) compared with measured performance in specific, non­ADL
functions within different cognitive domains [28].
Cognitive training can be used in both a restorative and compensatory manner. Restora­
tive approaches attempt to repair or restore an impaired function. Compensatory ap­
proaches teach and carry over new skills, strategies or accommodations to help compen­
sate for deficits when the original function might not be able to be fully recovered [29].
When it comes to cognitive training, exercises can be split into subcategories.
Attention exercises can be done on different levels. The first and most basic level is
focused attention, which is the ability to respond to stimuli in any form, e.g. visual, tactile,
and auditory. The second level is sustained attention, this includes the ability to maintain
attention for a duration of time e.g. listening to a lecture or reading a book. Next is
8
Computer Vision and AI­Driven Post­Stroke Hand Rehabilitation
==PAGE:17==
selective attention this is similar to sustained attention, but with an additional distraction
present that requires active filtering to remain on target. Alternating attention is the next
level this requires the ability to shift attention between stimuli e.g. having two tasks to
work on, then pausing the first task to work on the second and then returning to the first
task again. Lastly divided attention this entails completing two tasks simultaneously or
responding to and processing two types of stimuli accurately e.g. cooking and having a
conversation simultaneously.
Memory skill exercises in cognitive rehabilitation, are usually aimed at short­term memory
or delayed recall. In both cases, restorative and compensatory approaches are consid­
ered. Restorative approaches include trials of spaced, distributed practice or retrieval,
along with errorless learning, which is an instructional technique where errors are mini­
mized or avoided, prior to advancing to the next step of a task [30]. Compensatory ap­
proaches can be further divided into internal and external strategies. Internal strategies
include the use of visualizations, associations, repetition and semantic elaborations, in­
cluding the use of associations with preexisting knowledge or identifying salient features
in the target memory. External strategies cover the use of external information storage
that is not the brain, examples of this are calendars, to­do lists, labels, pictures, alarms or
the use of technology for prospective reminders [31].
Executive Functioning skills are a big part of the successful carryover of memory and
attention strategies. For stroke patients executive functioning is at risk for impairment.
If executive functioning impairments are severe, target interventions towards increasing
awareness to cognitive impairment and training of individual functional tasks might be
needed, with the understanding that the skills acquired may not carry over to untrained
tasks [32]. The goals of executive functioning skills in rehabilitation therapy include im­
proving attention to detail, problem solving, accuracy in reasoning and tasks mimicking
demands for return to independent living [29].
For mild to moderate severity deficits
in executive functioning, training focused on metacognition has shown results [28, 31].
Metacognition is the awareness of one’s own thought processes, including understanding
specific changes in cognitive functioning and anticipating their impact. A metacognitive
strategy is dual task training, which has some support as a restorative approach to exec­
utive functioning for a trained task [33]. Most studies with dual task training have been
done on patients diagnosed with traumatic brain injury, however dual task training is ap­
plied in other rehabilitation therapies, such as physical therapy, occupational therapy and
post stroke rehabilitation [28].
VR technology is being explored more and more, when it comes to rehabilitation interven­
tions [27]. VR is capable of providing simulations of ADL while also enhancing cognitive
skills such as information processing, attention, memory and executive functioning. Early
findings indicate that a combination of VR with traditional training strategy, carried out by
rehabilitative therapists can lead to significant improvements [34].

certain task or goal. In this case the player will not have concern for anything else than
what they are doing, leading to a change in perception of time. In this state a player
might feel like minutes have passed when in actuality hours might have passed instead.
Achieving flow requires a balance between the player’s skills and the challenge at hand
matching perfectly. If the challenge is too big and the skill too low, then the player will

4.2.2
Player Types
In game design one of the more prevalent frameworks being used is Bartle’s Player Type
Taxonomy. This taxonomy originally stems from an interest in figuring out the different
types of players that played Multi­User Dungeon (MUD) and how they interacted with
each other. It defines 4 different player types, what their goals are and how they interact
with the game world and other players, being able to be put on some sort of abstract graph
[36] which can be seen on figure 4.2.
Achievers gain enjoyment from collecting points and leveling up. They interact with the
game world to level up or obtain points and only really interact with people to either gain
knowledge, team up to achieve their goals or defeat players that are in the way of them.
Explorers are all about interacting with the world to explore the story or how the world
works. While they might interact with the world or other players to gain points, they gen­
erally only do this as much as they need to be able to access other parts of the world and
learn more about it
Socializers are not interested in the world itself. They instead enjoy playing with others
and the game world itself is just a way to facilitate this interaction. Their goal is to get to
know people and develop relationships with other players.
Killers are the last type and as their name suggests they are more interested in winning
over other players than becoming friends with them. They derive their enjoyment from
being better than others and showing their superiority over other players that they defeat.

Objects
In gamification and game design, objects refer to the items, tools or components that the

Objects serve a functional purpose, such as showing player progression through the pow­

serve as rewards and enhance the feeling of progress and accomplishment [38].

level, collect a set of items or achieve a certain score. In gamification objectives are crucial

can also be layered providing short­, mid and long­term goals to keep players engaged

the sense of progress that is vital for maintaining flow [40, 35]

4.2.4
Point Sampling
In procedural generation one might need to place objects on a surface to populate it. It
could be some objects that need to be placed en masse like grass or trees in a forest.
However, how one chooses the points at which to place these will yield vastly different
results. The most obvious way is to just uniformly randomly generate points and use
those. This is quite efficient and can work somewhat well, the issue is that while the points
are chosen uniformly at random, the spacing between them can vary greatly. As seen on
figure 4.3 this results in some points being quite far away from each other and some points
being clustered. For applications such as forest generation, this would lead to odd­looking
forests with areas with no trees and areas where trees would merge into one tree. So for
those types of procedural generation another approach ought to be considered. The next
obvious choice would be to take a grid of points and just move them ever so slightly apart,
a so­called ’jittered grid’. This approach provides more spaced out points than uniformly
random, but can still yield clustering that seems unnatural, as seen on figure 4.3. What
appears to be missing is a requirement that each point should have a minimum distance to
any other point. One such algorithm that generates evenly spaced points is the Poisson
disc sampling algorithm [42]. A Poisson disc sampling, is a set of random points that
satisfies the disc constraint, that is that each point has a disc around it in which no other
point lies within. There are several ways to do this. The naive way is to just choose a
random point, check if it’s closer to any other point in the set of accepted points, if it is
throw it away and try a new point and if it isn’t then add it to the set of accepted points.
This is easy to implement, but has the issue of taking longer and longer for each point
added to the set. There is also the issue of when to stop, it is not clear when a maximal
set has been reached and no more points can be added.

A faster way of performing the sampling process is by using the fast Poisson disc sam­
pling algorithm [43]. Instead of randomly choosing points and comparing them to every
single other point, locality is taken into consideration such that only the closest points are
considered. This is done by performing the sampling in a grid, in which every point is only
compared to the closest grid points. This solves the issue of having to check against ev­
ery single other point. The way to solve the issue of when to stop is by generating points
based on the current set of points. Every generated point is added to a list. For every
point in the list, k candidate points are considered around a point in the set, the points
being at a random distance of between r and 2r away from the point. If the candidate
point satisfies the disc constraint then it is added to the set of points and added to the list
of points from which new candidate points can be generated. Once every point in the list
has been exhausted the generation is done.

4.3.1
Image Types (RGB VS HSV)
The classic way to represent an image for a computer is in the RGB format, this format
splits every pixel into three color intensities, red, green and blue. This is due to the way
most monitors work by representing colors as a mix of these three colors. This makes it a
very efficient way to represent images as it directly corresponds to what a monitor would
display, however it has some issues for computer vision applications. If one wants to find
red objects in an image, it is not enough to just look at the red channel as the mix of the
three channels makes up the color.
The HSV/HSL format meaning hue, saturation and value/lightness, is a more human­
interpretable way of defining color. Hue adjusts the color, saturation adjusts the saturation
and value/lightness adjusts how bright the color is. A nice way of representing this is with
a color cylinder representation seen in figure 4.4, where the rotation around the cylinder
is the hue, the distance to the center representing the saturation and the height is the
lightness/value.

amount of photons that hit it, which means that if twice the number of photons reach the
sensor, then the recorded signal is twice as big. The issue here is that human eyes do
not perceive light in the same linear way, instead light is perceived non linearly resulting
in cases where a doubling in recorded light will not be a perceived doubling in light. To
account for this a scaling is applied in the form of a power calculation.

4.3.3
Depth Sensor
Depth sensing has many uses, from 3D scans [45] to robotics navigation [46]. Depth
sensors work in a wide variety of different ways depending on how they’re made and for
what purpose they’re used. The one that most think about is likely LiDAR (Light Detection
and Ranging), in which a laser is used to shoot light at a point and then the light that returns
from the light reflecting off the surface can tell you how far away the surface is based on
how long it took for the light to return to the sensor [47]. LiDAR is useful for a wide range
of distances and can be accurate for long ranges. Another way to perform depth sensing
is by using 2 cameras and computing stereo depth from them. There are several ways of
doing this, some do it passively by just using the image in both cameras and employing
feature matching and photogrammetry to find the points in a 3D space, some also have
an active component where an infrared pattern is projected onto a surface. This also
helps in cases where the surface might be flat and lack any discernible features. They
are normally used for low­range applications [48].

camera projection relies on a few different parameters. The first is how big the back plane
of the box is and how far away the back plane is from the hole in the front of the box, this
being known as the focal length denoted as f.

5.1.2
Annotation
Data annotation is one of the most time­consuming parts of creating a dataset, as such
creating a process to make the process faster was important for this project. There are
already existing tools out there for data annotation2 and they generally support automatic
annotation. However most of these solutions are either not free or lack features. This
generalized automatic annotation is not very good at a specialized case like annotat­
ing DUPLO bricks in an image. As such, a custom annotation tool was developed in
Python using both a generalized automatic annotation with Segment Anything Model 2
(SAM2)[50] and using a previous version of the object detection model trained during the
project.
The image annotation process is simple. First a collection of images or video is opened
by the annotation tool and is then processed into a format consisting of the original image
and a scaled­down segmentation mask generated by SAM2. This is then saved and used
for annotation, the reason for saving it this way is that the SAM2 model can often take a
bit of time to process an image, therefore having it preprocessed makes the annotation
process feel smoother.
When a processed image is ready for annotation, it is shown to the annotator as can be
seen in figure 5.2. The annotator can then click the presented segmentation to include the
bounding box of a segmented object in the annotation. The annotator is also presented
with a copy of the original image, this is to help if something isn’t clear on the segmenta­
tion, due to the color overlay making it difficult to distinguish colors. As mentioned earlier,
a generalized segmentation model is not really optimal for DUPLO bricks as it tends to
combine bricks into one segmentation mask or entirely miss the bricks. This also tends to
happen if we use other prompts, like text or point­based ones, instead of automatic mask
generation. In these cases the user can press and hold the mouse wheel and manually
draw a bounding box. The annotation tool also supports using a previously trained model
to help click on the segmentation for faster annotation. In figure 5.2, it is shown as a small
magenta circle where the model predicted a brick.
To select a different class for the object, the alpha­numeric keys are used. This worked
out fine during this project, but if more object types were to be added to the dataset, it
would require another solution, as the number of classes would exceed 10. The anno­
tated classes are:

turned out to be quite a hassle later on.

Design Considerations
When designing an MR application there are a lot of design considerations, some of the
most important ones for our MR application are:
• Performance Constraints: MR headsets, like the Meta Quest 3, have limited CPU
and GPU resources compared to desktops and extended high processing loads can
lead to overheating and cause throttling or shutdowns.
• Framerate and Latency: Consistent high framerate (Ideally 60+ FPS) is essen­
tial for immersion and to avoid motion sickness, and keeping latency under ~20ms
ensures input and environment updates feel natural.
• Cybersickness: Users may experience some sort of motion sickness in an XR
environment. It comes from sensory mismatches, especially between visual input
and the vestibular system (inner ear balance).

6.4
System Design
An important consideration when designing the game was the modularity of the system.
Having modularity is often a good idea when designing a game and deciding on interfaces
between different modules. This allows for individual parts of the game to be switched
with systems that work better. In the context of this project, as we’re two people working
at the same time, having this modularity allows for more efficient concurrent work. The
biggest separation of modularity was in separating game logic from the inference logic
and the interfacing between the two. On the game side this allowed for rapid prototyping
of different games while keeping the inference engine the same. At the same time it also
allowed for changing between on­headset and off­headset approaches for inference and
comparing the performance between different approaches, without changing any of the
game code. The actual realization of this system uses a producer and consumer pattern
with event handlers, which are being invoked by the inference module, to tell a game that
an update has been made. This reduces the need for result polling that would otherwise
have to be used. It also allows for the separation of UI updates and game loop updates
into separate parts making the game code easier to structure.
The data coming from the inference module contains the following:
• Type of object detected
• Image space center
• World space coordinates (If they exist)
As an additional part of information, if the game uses stacks, the inference module allows
for stack detections to also be enabled and sent as a separate event.
By having a producer­consumer pattern, this allows for other modules that might want the
data of the detections, to get them without having any direct dependence on each other.
This is used by the data logging module to help log the information about the detections.

When creating an MR application/game the three most commonly used engines are Un­

The main player type that we considered when designing the game was the achiever, as
the goal of the game is to improve your fine motor skills, while providing data to encour­
age incremental progress. That being said, other player types could also be considered.
Explorers could be encouraged, by having something that gets unlocked only by playing

seems to be very little reason to include this player type in this kind of game.
6.5.3
Game Elements
Key game elements we added to our hand rehabilitation exercise are: randomness, visual
effects, progress tracking and adjustable difficulty.
Randomness
In an attempt to make our game feel less repetitive and more engaging, we introduce
randomness to make every level unique and so that no session is the same. There are
two places randomness is used, first in the generation for the stack(s) the player has to
build and second to determine where to place the stack(s).
To ensure consistency, like alternating colors and giving us some control when generating
stacks, true random is not used. Instead we use an algorithm that given values for, min
and max stack size, number of bricks to use and slice method, generates valid stacks.
This is done by first making one big stack of alternating colors using all the bricks, this big
stack is then shifted. The amount of bricks needed is then taken from the bottom up of the
big shifted stack. Based on the min and max stack size and slice method, the selected
part of the big shuffled stack is divided into one or more stacks that the player has to build.
Generating stacks to build this way, gives the illusion of true randomness while remain­
ing consistent, adjustable and adaptable. Meaning that LEGO bricks can be added or
removed, difficulty can increase or decrease and it will still generate consistent and valid
stacks. The slice methods are.
Max will, try to create as many stacks of max size as possible, this does not guarantee a
minimum size.
Min will create as many stacks at the minimal stack size as possible, this does not guar­
antee a minimum size.

Adjustable Difficulty
The difficulty of our game can be adjusted in a number of ways, from the number of red,
green, blue and yellow bricks the game is played with, to the settings of the game and
how many levels are needed to complete the progress build.
Settings of the game include:
• Number for bricks to use
• Minimum stack height
• Maximum stack height
• Slice method used
• How close points can be to each other
• Minimum distance from player to closest point
• Maximum distance from player to furthest point
With these settings, games like moving one brick around the whole table, to a game of
stacking eight bricks in the right order at the same place repeatedly, and everything in
between are possible. The number of levels needed to complete the progress build, can
be chosen by setting the number of bricks added to the progress build to a specific number
or a range between two numbers. All of this makes the difficulty of our game adjustable
to a wide range of functional impairments.
While testing different settings, it became apparent that having to change the settings
manually would not be feasible without having to interrupt the play session. Therefore, a
way to change the difficulty of the game on the fly without distracting the player too much
was devised. The object detection model has figure detection functionality and as such
this became the way to change the difficulty while the game was running. If it detects a
figure, it then changes the settings to fit the profile of that figure. As an example, the pig
figure could change the difficulty to be easy and the sheep could change the difficulty to
be hard. This figure detection also allowed us to change other things while playing, using
them as a sort of user interface.
6.6
Model Inference
For running the model inference there are only two options, being either on or off the
headset. There are pros, cons and other considerations, that need to be kept in mind
when deciding on the best options.
6.6.1
On­headset
Performing model inference on the headset itself requires some design considerations.
There is very limited processing power available when a game has to run at the same
time with the inference while ensuring a consistent and high FPS. If a target of 60 fps
is set then the entire frame processing, including the game logic, rendering and model
inference has to run within just 16.7ms. This leaves very few options for real­time 60 fps
model inference, either the model has to be very small and/or the inference code has to
be optimized towards just a single model architecture. This means running in real time
isn’t feasible and a near­real­time approach was used. Instead of running the entire model
every frame, the execution of the model was split over multiple frames, allowing smooth
frame rates while still being able to run near real­time.
30
Computer Vision and AI­Driven Post­Stroke Hand Rehabilitation
==PAGE:39==
Choosing which part of the hardware to run the models on was not as obvious as first
assumed. The usual way to do it is to always run inference on a GPU as they are special­
ized for parallelized vector calculations, but in the case of the Meta Quest 3, running the
inference on the GPU caused some weird stuttering behavior. It is assumed that this is
either, due to the limited GPU resources available being needed for rendering and taking
any of them for inference will result in unreliable compute resources, or that when running
inference on the GPU, extra overhead from copying the data to and from the GPU and
waiting for the GPU to be ready for these operations will also introduce some amount of
latency.
The CPU on the other hand might run a bit slower for inference, but saves the overhead
from copying and waiting for the GPU, as the memory is already available to the CPU.
It also allows for just using part of the frame time exclusively for model inference, this
amount of time can then also be changed dynamically to ensure the frame time doesn’t
exceed some specified amount.
6.6.2
Off­headset
Running the inference on the headset has a few limitations on processing power, so of­
floading it to another device or server, with more powerful hardware might seem like a
good idea, as this could result in more detections per second making the overall game
seem more responsive. However this introduces many new issues like data transport,
communication overhead and requiring extra hardware.
Data transport is a particularly big issue. While the images being transported are not nec­
essarily that large and resizing the image could be done before sending it, there is still
the issue of encoding and compressing the image to a size that can be sent to another
device. The next issue is the transport process itself. A cable would be the fastest way,
but tethering is not going to be an option for user­friendliness reasons. Wireless would
be the best option, but here there are issues with reliability and latency as it tends to be
slower. The extra hardware required is also an extra cost and another barrier to user
friendliness.
After weighing the pros and cons of the two approaches and performance testing them
both, the overall conclusion was that, while there are issues with the on­headset perfor­
mance, due to limited hardware and issues with the inference library, it still performed bet­
ter than the off­headset solution. The overhead from encoding and compressing seemed
to be the main limitation in the implementation, often taking more time than running the
inference itself depending on the encoding used. As such the chosen solution was to
run the inference on the headset CPU while splitting it over multiple frames to achieve a
smoother experience with more reliable frame times.
6.6.3
Preprocessing
Before being able to use the image with the model, it needs to be preprocessed. This
would be fairly easy to do in Python, but as Unity is optimized towards a graphics pipeline
and not a machine learning pipeline, there are some steps that need to be performed
before putting the images through the model.
Gamma correction is done by the engine during rendering, however this is usually ap­
plied to the entire frame at once. This poses some issues as the textures themselves are
gamma corrected and will not appear as one would normally perceive them. As such, this
correction has to be performed before feeding the image into the model. The standard
gamma value of 2.2 is used for this.
Letterboxing is not strictly necessary to perform, however the model will perform much
better if done as it is trained on letterboxed images. This is fairly simply done by resizing
Computer Vision and AI­Driven Post­Stroke Hand Rehabilitation
31
==PAGE:40==
and adding black bars as needed to maintain the aspect ratio of the image while keeping
it in the size of 640x640 as required by the model.
Both of these steps are implemented together in a single step in a shader to save on
computational power.
6.6.4
Postprocessing
For post­processing the library supports automatic type conversion into any type of output
of the model, even customized types as long as the memory layout is correct. It also has
support for multi­dimensional arrays which makes data access simpler. However most of
this can be optimized greatly to avoid type and out­of­bounds checking, as those ended
up being very slow. Due to this, the data post­processing into usable types in the game
and event handling, is done by getting all the tensor data in a single large array and
accessing it by our own implementation. This saves a lot of processing time on type
checking and dimensional bounds checks, making it much more efficient for repeated
access. This data is then converted into a set of bounding boxes which are then filtered
based on confidence score and then performing non­maximal suppression to remove any
significant overlapping between bounding boxes, prioritizing bounding boxes with a larger
confidence score.
6.7
Physical to Virtual
The interaction between the physical and virtual world is what separates MR from VR and
AR. It does however, pose some interesting challenges when trying to interpret the real
world from the virtual world.
6.7.1
Room Scan
The Meta Quest 3 has a room scan feature in which the user scans the room that they
are in and this information can then be used to provide information to games about the
environment around them. This includes where objects are, how big they are and a label
of what the object is. In the case of our game the scan provides information about the
table the player is playing at. It also provides the option to create your own objects if
the scan doesn’t recognize an object. This scanning feature works more or less without
issues, with the only exception being with the user­created objects. These are for some
reason missing information about the top of the surface of an object.
6.7.2
Depth Camera
The Meta Quest 3 features a structured light depth camera in addition to its RGB cameras.
Knowing how to find correspondences between the depth camera and the RGB cameras
allows one to get the depth of a certain pixel. While structured light depth sensors can be
fairly accurate, the sensor in the Meta Quest 3 has some issues with differentiating the
depth of small objects on surfaces, unless the user is very close to an object, it will show
as the same depth in the depth texture.
6.7.3
Image Space to World Space
Having a separate sensor for measuring depth makes it much easier and more reliable to
go from image to world space. By knowing the camera extrinsics and intrinsics and know­
ing how to find correspondences from the depth camera in the RGB cameras, allows for
reverse projection of a point from camera space to world space. This is implemented
directly in the Meta Quest MR SDK, but that implementation does have some downsides.
The biggest being that it only supports this in real­time, so the game first has to run model
inference and then do the camera projection, in that small time frame, the player could
move their head, giving a slightly inaccurate world space point.

==PAGE:41==
To find a DUPLO brick in world space coordinates, the output of the model inference
is processed and each of the centers of the bounding boxes in image space can be con­
verted to world space. Providing a semi­accurate place of where that brick is in the world.
Finding a stack of bricks in the image is however a bit more involved. First there is the is­
sue of detecting the stack, this can be done in a few ways. The image space coordinates
of the bricks do tell one something about the bricks in relation to each other, however
image space is not scale invariant, this means that bricks further away in the picture and
bricks closer in the picture would appear to be different sizes, making a stack detection
algorithm very unstable if only considering the brick positions.
Another idea was to use the world space coordinates of the bricks as these would be
scale invariant due to the depth component. It would then be possible to compare posi­
tional heights of the bricks and then figure out the stacks that way. This did work to some
capacity, however as the depth sensor is not accurate enough with smaller objects like
the DUPLO bricks this turned out to be quite unstable as well.
As it seems like neither the world or image space coordinates of the bricks are accurate
enough to detect the stacks reliably, the choice to include a second detection model to
only detect stacks was made. This model finds the bounding box of a stack of bricks in
the image. The image space bounding boxes of the bricks can then be used to determine
which bricks are in the stack and using the relative position of the bricks the order of them
can be found. To find the position of the stack in world space, the bottom brick of the
stack is used. The position of this brick is fairly accurate as it is close to the surface, so
even if the depth sensor shows it as being part of the surface, this is not an issue. This
approach fixes the issues of the world space being too inaccurate and the image space
having scaling issues. It does include a second model, which will take up additional com­
putational power, but due to issues with the inference library and the approach taken to
split inference to multiple frames makes the latency increase negligible.

Data Logging
One of the benefits using computer vision in rehabilitation is the ability to collect data about
the patient in new and continuous ways. Instead of only collecting data once a week at
a checkup with the therapist, the ability to continuously monitor the user from session to
session and track their progress could help lead the way to a more optimized treatment
plan, as it can adapt itself to the patient. It also allows one to monitor for adherence to the
treatment plan.
6.8.1
Collection

types of data would be relevant for this. For this use case there are many relevant data
points to track like, the position of the hand, each finger joint and head, as well as the

the current position of the bricks on the table, where the stacks the user is supposed to

Most of the tracking is not that difficult to do, the position of the DUPLO bricks and stacks to

is. The hand and finger tracking is a bit more problematic. The Meta Quest 3 already has
a built­in hand tracking tool, however it comes with a ton of assumptions about how the
hand is supposed to look and how it interprets occlusions of fingers. The intended use
of this hand­tracking model is for controlling the headset with your hands. It works quite
seamlessly and fast with the hardware and as such it was the model used. The data about
the table can be tracked by retrieving it from the room scan made before playing the game.

7.1.1
Game Performance
Due to platform limitations and the need for consistently high FPS, performance optimiza­

played a key role in how we optimized the game. While we did develop some tools our­

has to be made in order for the results to be the same for both the Python implementa­

bricks on the table. A level is completed when all stacks are built correctly and placed

a finetuned state­of­the­art detection model (YOLO [55]) and the built­in deep camera of
the Meta Quest 3. To show the player the progress made and give them a goal to work
towards, we made a virtual lego model of a bee, figure 6.10, composed of 231 bricks,
where each time a level is completed more bricks are added to the bee, a image showing

making it possible to fit the difficulty of the game to the user’s current capabilities and
needs. By just changing the settings, the game can target elbow and shoulder movement,
fine motor skills in hands or something in between. The four LEGO DUPLO figures lion,

8.2
Test Results
Testing was performed continuously throughout the thesis to ensure that the final game
was in a working state and could be played by others. The result of which has been
condensed to the following.
8.2.1
Performance Tests
Performance testing showed that the biggest bottleneck was, unsurprisingly, the model
inference. The model itself runs in 20­30ms which is quite fast however, the library used,
appears to have a bug or issue with performing safety checks on the output and copying
the data to a usable buffer afterwards, despite all the work being done on the CPU. The
delay is around 150­200ms and does not seem to be something that can be resolved
as it relates to how the inference library performs type checking. The library supports
doing Non Maximum Suppression on the resulting bounding boxes, however the way it’s
implemented, seems to be much slower than implementing it ourselves, likely taking much
longer due to a copying step required before doing the suppression.
Another large performance issue, was related to the progress build tracking. As it’s being
built brick by brick it has a lot of potential overhead. At the start it was created as a bunch
of different bricks and this poses an issue for performance, as in Unity every game object
has a large amount of overhead. This created performance issues as the progress build
was built, making virtual elements jitter as the frame rate got too low. The usual way to
fix this is to just have the progress build as a single mesh, but in this case as it’s being
built piece by piece, having to save 200+ different models of different stages is inefficient.
Instead the model gets converted into a format where the studs are represented in a voxel
grid with information about which brick they belong to. By doing this, the grid can then
be used to determine which studs are part of the current stage of building and if they are
visible or not. This is then used to dynamically create a new mesh during runtime. So
every time a level is completed, a few bricks fall down onto their spots, then the mesh
is reconstructed to include the new bricks and displayed. This creates a seamless and
optimized way of progressively displaying the mesh, while still keeping the frame rate
high.
The performance test also helped with some smaller parts of the game code. There
were some unoptimized algorithms and some implementations that heavily relied on the
garbage collector. These were subsequently fixed after running the profiler.

9.1
Inference
The large delay that occurs after inference and before the data is copied, appears to be an
Android issue. After being stumped for a while looking at the documentation, we turned
our attention to the Unity Forums, seeing if others had similar issues. There we found at
least two examples of people running similar models on Android [56, 57], albeit on other
devices and they seemed to have similar issues. There seems to be no definitive reason
for this, the documentation states that inference runs one layer at a time1, this should
mean that after running all layers the output should be available in some form, but due to
type safety requirements, this is not immediately available to the developer. There seems
to be no way of getting the output without type safety as of writing this, even though the
documentation indicate a way of accessing the tensor data directly if it’s on the CPU2.
To check if this was truly an android issue, the same detection process was copied and
performed on a PC with a webcam and the delay does not appear to happen on there. This
doesn’t verify that it’s an Android­only issue as the hardware is vastly faster on the pc than
on the headset, but the complete lack of wait time needed for type checking compared to
the android environment highlights that more focus needs to be put into fixing this issue.
Attempts to fix this have included using other inference libraries. These include using
ONNXRuntime3 and TensorflowLite4 , however while these did not have the 200ms delay,
they ran substantially slower at 80ms+ inference, without the ability to do layer­to­layer
inference easily in the implementations we tried. In the future if the type checking cannot
be circumvented or the delay hasn’t been resolved, it might be beneficial to implement this
part ourselves to allow for substantial speedups, or modify the other runtimes to support
layer­by­layer inference.
While this delay is quite substantial and reduces the detections per second to somewhere
between 4­5, this has the benefit of allowing us to run another model while the data is
being copied. As the stack detection currently relies on a second inference model, this
downtime while copying can be used to run that model, as it does not appear to affect the
copying time and allows us to make use of the downtime for something useful. This means
that fixing the delay, will also entail that the use of a second inference model will slow down
the detection process quite significantly and will then be the focus of optimizations.

9.2.1
Depth Camera
During development, many issues seemed to arise from the depth camera accuracy, while
we assumed in the beginning that it would be quite accurate, at some point we decided
to investigate how accurate it truly was. While the depth texture is available in a prepro­
cessed format used for depth checking, the actual raw depth texture is not easily available.
The provided documentation primarily focuses on its use in occlusion of real­life objects
in front of placed virtual objects. However as Unity packages tend to be designed in a
way, where the code is provided and compiled with the game, we could access scripts
and shaders that interact with the depth texture. Having access to the source code of the
packages helped tremendously, not only in figuring out how to access the raw depth tex­
ture, but also for debugging and finding the root causes of many issues in the inference
library. After getting access to the depth texture, we could then take all the pixels and
unproject them to world space coordinates, to do this one must know the focal length of
the camera and this information is also not easily accessible. So some estimation and
trial and error was performed here to get a focal length value that matched as good as
possible. While we didn’t have the time to perform this, camera calibration to get a more
accurate focal length, should still be possible despite not having an RGB output, by match­
ing the depth camera output with known points in real life. After doing this we saved the
point cloud and viewed it in MeshLab [58], which can be seen in figure 9.2 where some of
the issues with the depth mapping are visible. It seems to do a smooth interpolation be­
tween points which leads to some slopes forming around the boundary of some objects.
We also observed the issue of stacks being interpreted as half on and half off the table,
leading to a weird sloped stack effect that explains some of the issues we had with stack
detections based purely on 3D positions.
While looking into the depth camera accuracy, we also decided to look at the feasibility
of using the depth camera to do 3D build detections. As can be seen in figure 9.1, this
idea was quickly scrapped as the builds are not large enough to be distinct on the depth
texture. The depth mapping would maybe work well if the person sat closer to the objects
however they would likely have to be so close to them that it would be impractical to do.

9.4
Game
Our game is a combination of two GRASP exercises, namely ”Lego”(Appendix A.4.1) and
”Waiter – Bean bag”(Appendix A.4.2) with added game elements like randomness, visual
and sound effects, and adjustable difficulty. These game elements are added to make
the exercises feel less repetitive and more engaging. Each level is played by using 2x4
LEGO DUPLO bricks to build the displayed stacks and place them at their designated
spot. The game is designed as an RTT exercise, where the goal is to regain hand and
finger mobility through repetitive tasks. In our game the repetitive tasks are picking up,
moving, stacking and separating 2x4 LEGO DUPLO bricks.
9.4.1
Difficulty Adjustment
Using the DUPLO figures to change the difficulty of the game, made it easy to do so,
especially when testing the game on others. The limitation of only having four presets,
one of them being random, was not enough to both show the full range of difficulties
and be adjustable beyond easy, medium and hard, making it impossible to make small
adjustments in difficulty on the fly. The limit of four presets even though we have six
figures, is because the two penguins have other debugging purposes and as such couldn’t
be used to set difficulties.
9.4.2
Cognitive Challenge
During the target audience test, test persons 1 and 2 found the tasks for moving, stacking
and separating LEGO DUPLO bricks too easy. For them to achieve a state of flow, they
need something more cognitively challenging than the procedurally generated stacks.
This can be seen as them being outside the target users or as an interesting area for
study in future works.

Virtual Bricks
An interesting observation we made during the testing of the various stages of the game, is

the virtual bricks are semitransparent. People sometimes try to grab a virtual brick to use

9.5
Research Questions
Our main research question for this thesis was ”How can AI­driven computer vision and
gamification improve the engagement of at­home rehabilitation exercises?”. To try and
answer this, we look at the following sub­questions.
How might one design an XR­based rehabilitation game in a way that makes it
engaging for the user?
During the project we looked at different ways of increasing engagement of the game,
by adding visual and audio effects and progress tracking to the game. During the test,
we saw that those effects, especially the progress build had a great effect on keeping
the interest of the player. This was also one of the points given during feedback, that
having more things to build and more visual and sound effects would make the game
more interesting to play. An example given was having more variety in visual effects, like
the board­clearing effect and other animations.
How might one design an XR­based rehabilitation game in a way that allows
adjustment to multiple levels of disability?
As development went on, we found more and more ways to adjust the difficulty, some
were implemented and some were only conceptualized. The implementations include
changing the distance of where to build the stacks, stack heights, stack counts and how
many DUPLO bricks were used, but others like forcing the positions of the spawned points
to be on one side to make more or less use of that side.
The difficulty levels used during testing were shown to only fit test person 3, however
with some settings tweaking, test person 4 would also be able to play the game at a
suitable level, by changing it to only requiring moving a single brick around. Test persons
1 and 2, who had the most mobility, would likely not gain much from making the stacking
game more difficult and instead the game would need to be modified to contain cognitive
elements, like having a memory element where you not only have to build the stacks, but
also remember the order of the bricks. It could also be considered that a different strategy
would be needed for them and the game developed for this thesis would not be a suitable
exercise for people with their capabilities.

How accurately can computer vision and AI track and assess the hand and finger
movement?
In the game, the default hand and finger tracking model provided by the Meta Quest SDK
was used. This might not have been the best choice of model. While it’s very easy to
use and implement in this setting and get the data for the 3D reconstruction, it doesn’t
have the accuracy that is needed for a thesis like this. In the future another model should
be considered, maybe another hand tracking model like MediaPipe [54] would be good
enough, but this was not tested on the Meta Quest 3, as when testing it on the Ray­Ban
glasses, the quality of the detections was limited by the low video resolution.
The hand position tracking seemed fairly accurate in any setting. When tracking the hand
of a non affected person, it seemed to work well and track the fingers accurately, but
when trying to use rubber bands to restrict hand movement or when testing it on actual
test persons, it became clear that the assumptions, that the model makes to fingers that
are partially or fully occluded was making the finger tracking too inaccurate to be used in
a proper setting.
What are the challenges of building a rehabilitation game with consumer XR
technology and real­time computer vision?
The biggest challenge faced here wasn’t necessarily the processing power of the headset,
but more the software solutions available. The camera on most consumer XR technology
is quite good, but there is a massive issue with access to the cameras, due to privacy
issues. For the first bit of the thesis when the Meta Ray­Bans were used, a workaround
had to be used to get access to the camera 6.2.5 and it was only the week we got a Meta
Quest 3, that the camera access for that particular headset was made available. The Pico
series of headsets has more access on their enterprise side, but those would not be con­
sidered consumer grade in the context of this thesis. Not having access to the cameras
cannot easily be fixed by writing your own library, as it is limited at the firmware level.
The next biggest issue, which could likely be fixed with a homemade implementation, was
the inference process. The models themselves are lightweight and can run without much
issue, the specific implementation used here limited us quite a bit in how real­time the
object detection could actually be.
The last issue is the quality of the accessible RGB and depth cameras. At least for the
Meta Quest 3, the quality itself leaves a bit to be desired. The lack of easy access to both
cameras at the same time, issues with the depth camera for small objects and the lack of
high­quality images complicates the entire process. Getting access to the stitched view
and using that in some capacity would increase the field of view and quality of the image,
making the experience of playing the game much smoother.
The conclusions of our research questions are, that we through AI­driven computer vision
and consumer­grade hardware, were able to make a hand rehabilitation exercise game
prototype, played in a mixed reality setting, that through several different tests shows high
engagement and is playable in an at­home setting.

Both the hardware and software have some limitations, like the Meta Quest 3 not having
eye tracking, the game relying on automatically table­recognized and the stack detection
not always working as intended.
9.6.1
Eye Tracking
Eye tracking shows quite a bit of promise in this field, as it can provide insight into how
fatigued people are [59]. This insight is useful to help determine how to adapt the difficulty,
but due to the current landscape of consumer­grade hardware for MR, the feature set

that one could want does not appear to be available in a single package. Eye and hand

does not seem to come easily. While we primarily looked into the Pico Enterprise and

there weren’t any products available at least in the consumer­grade market at this moment
that check all the boxes. So the best compromise was the Meta Quest 3 as it had all the
wanted features except the eye tracking.
9.6.2
Room Scan
For the game to know where the table is, it uses the room scan made with the Meta
Quest 3. The room scan contains 3D bounding boxes with labels, of different objects
like table, couch, storage, screen, etc. These are automatically created when making the
scan, however it is not perfect and you are therefore able to add, remove and edit the 3D
bounding boxes manually. When a 3D bounding box of a table is automatically created
it comes with some extra properties, specifically a rectangle describing the top plane of
the table. It is this rectangle we use as the play area for our game, but if the 3D bounding
box of a table is manually created or an existing 3D bounding box is relabeled or edited,
then there are no rectangle describing the top plane of the table, resulting in our game
not knowing where the table is and therefore not playable.
So our game is only playable on tables that are automatically recognized during the room
scan. This is mostly a problem on round tables, as they are marked with a 3D bounding
box that we can’t edit, meaning that stacks might need to be placed mid­air.
9.6.3
Detection Models
The models trained in this project work well most of the time. The individual detection
does however struggle in some situations. During the meeting with LEGO, the table used
seemed to cause some issues with the detection of the small penguin figure, while at
Vejlefjord Rehabilitering, it would detect a small penguin even though it wasn’t there. This
suggests that the model isn’t completely background invariant as we would have wanted
it to be.
The stack detection is heavily dependent on what angle it is looked at from. If the angle
is too steep it won’t always detect all of the bricks. This can be alleviated by tipping the
stack ever so slightly to make it easier for the model to see, but it remains one of the
bigger issues with the model.

Future Works
For future works we have identified some areas that could be explored further. These
areas were identified by looking at things that were outside the scope due to time or
technical limitations, ideas formed through conversation with patients and experts, and
observation of target users playing the game.
10.1
Data Analysis
The game tracks and records hand, finger and head movement, but it is only used to
make a 3D reconstruction to demonstrate that the data is being collected. Future work
could look into the potential insights and statistics that can be derived from the recorded
movement. This could include, total hand travel distance, individual finger range of motion
and the degree to which a hand can open or close.
10.1.1
Eye Tracking
An increasing number of XR headsets and smart glasses now include built­in eye tracking.
Access to this data could provide a powerful metric for assessing a patient’s energy level
and visual attention. For example, it could help determine what objects or areas a patient
is focusing on, for how long and offer valuable insight into cognitive engagement and
fatigue [59].
10.2
Adaptive Difficulty
The limitation of only having three difficulty presets, could be fixed by adding adaptive
difficulty. Using game statistics like, time per level, stack precision, etc. changes could
be made to the settings, to make the difficulty level better match the player’s capabilities.
With the use of external personalized medical data the difficulty level could become more
personalized to try and maximize the rehabilitation aspect. With the right data the adaptive
difficulty could be controlled by for example a reinforcement learning model.
10.3
Dashboard
During the last test we discovered the importance of the physical therapist being able to
see what the patient sees. Future work could look into making a dashboard for the physical
therapist, where they can tap into the video feed from the MR headset. Additionally, this
dashboard may include access to the game settings, allowing the physical therapist to
change them when needed.
Logging data like, current settings, patient performances and physical therapist changes,
could be useful data for training an AI, that can adapt the difficulty of the game.
10.4
More Cognitive Challenge
Our game focuses mainly on RTT, so future work could look at ways to make the game
more cognitively challenging. Through conversation with experts and patients three ways
to make the game more cognitively challenging were proposed.
Vanishing stacks: The idea being that the virtual stacks, which show you what to build,
will only be shown for a few seconds. The patient then needs to remember what stacks
to build and which what point they should be placed. The difficulty would be adjustable
through settings like, how long the stacks are shown for and how often they reappear.
52
Computer Vision and AI­Driven Post­Stroke Hand Rehabilitation
==PAGE:61==
Build Structures: Here the idea is to get the patient to build something more complex
than a stack of alternating colors. This could be a house, a pyramid or an animal like a
dog or a horse. The challenge here is in recognizing different builds, especially if not all
the bricks are visible at once.
Memory Game: This idea is to make a new game, where the focus is on the cognitive
part of rehabilitation. Such a game could take inspiration from the game ”Kim’s Game”1,
where one has to memorize objects and/or positions of them.
10.5
More entertainment
Future work should look into adding more visual elements and effects like, more progress
builds, clear effects and stack spawn effects. Observations and conversations, during the
last test with the patients, revealed that they were engaged and interested while complet­
ing the progress builds for the first time, but when they found out that there was only one
progress build the engagement and interest were not as high.

of upper limbs of post­stroke patients, which is often a neglected part of the stroke recov­
ery process due to the large focus on getting people to walk again, as this provides the
biggest increase in quality of life immediately after the stroke. The effect of this is that
once patients are able to go home, they have less support for upper limb rehabilitation
and a lack of resources and will have to keep up with self­administered protocols. This is
the area that this thesis aimed to address.
Through the use of commercially available consumer­grade hardware, the developed ap­
plication is supposed to provide a low­cost, user­friendly and engaging way to perform
and track exercises with LEGO DUPLO bricks based on the GRASP exercises waiter ­
bean bag and Lego. The exercises are able to be performed in a home setting using a
Meta Quest 3 Mixed Reality headset, in which the user only has to put on the headset to
begin the exercises. The application also helps track and provide continuous data about
the exercise sessions that can then be compared to previous sessions.
Although the thesis itself doesn’t determine any long term clinical outcomes for use of the
product developed, the tests performed and feedback received from experts and from the
patients that participated in the tests, shows potential for such a solution to help increase
engagement for patients and provide valuable feedback and data to therapists, not only
in the context of stroke patients, but also for more general use in hand rehabilitation or
fine motor training of the hands.
It also serves as a proof of concept to further explore and develop rehabilitation solutions.
In the future, as mixed reality devices get better and smaller, newer smart glasses that
provide an even more frictionless experience will become available to the consumer mar­
ket. These could form the basis of another thesis, which could further explore and develop
new rehabilitation exercises for such smart glasses.
In summary, this master’s thesis is a step in the right direction towards higher engage­
ment of upper limb at­home rehabilitation exercises. With the use of consumer­grade
mixed reality hardware we have combined physical and virtual interactions in a gamified
rehabilitation exercise using LEGO DUPLO bricks.
