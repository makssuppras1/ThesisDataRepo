1. Abstract 
Systemic lupus erythematosus (SLE) is an autoimmune disease affecting multiple organs including 
the heart and the kidneys. One major complication of SLE is the development of lupus nephritis 
(LN), an immune complex mediated glomerulonephritis (GN) leading to kidney injury. Despite 
current treatment options, up to 60 % of severe GN cases will reach end-stage renal disease 
(ESRD). Thus, it is highly relevant to develop improved treatment therapies. Qualified and 
validated animal models resembling human LN are a necessity in preclinical research to investigate 
new therapeutic strategies for LN patients, or by helping to identify potential new drug candidates 
or biomarkers.  
The aim of the thesis was to establish and mechanistically characterise the passive nephrotoxic 
nephritis (NTN) model in C57Bl/6 mice resembling aspects of human LN. Prior to this thesis, a 
pilot study was performed to find the preferred dose of nephrotoxic serum (NTS) that is able to 
induce NTN in C57Bl/6 mice. Using intravenous (i.v.) injections of 250 µL NTS efficiently induced 
proteinuria (PU) indicating renal dysfunction and GN. In addition, the NTN induction was 
confirmed in a confirmatory study demonstrating abnormal PU and albuminuria. The presence of 
GN could be confirmed by histological PAS stain depicting mesangial expansion and increased 
cellularity within glomeruli.  
From the mechanistic characterisation of the passive NTN model performed in this thesis, it was 
demonstrated by FACS analysis of the leukocyte distribution, that the number of peripheral and 
renal neutrophils was elevated on day 3 and 10, respectively, indicating that neutrophils might play 
a role in the pathogenesis in the passive NTN model. The acute-phase reactant Serum amyloid P 
(SAP) was quantified by ELISA and found to be significantly increased at 7-8 hours and 3 days 
post NTN induction indicating an acute inflammatory response. In addition, measurements of the 
inflammatory cytokine G-CSF supported the involvement of an acute inflammatory response by 
revealing a major increase at 7-8 hours post NTN induction. The role of neutrophils in the NTN 
induction was investigated by a neutrophil depletion study. This study was challenged since only 
half of all neutrophils were depleted on the day of NTN induction, thereby not allowing strong 
conclusions. However, it was shown that partial depletion of neutrophils did not lead to reduced PU 
or to reductions in the SAP and G-CSF response. In conclusion the passive NTN model has been 
established and exhibits potential to be implemented in preclinical research within human LN.

3. Preface and acknowledgement 
 
The purpose of this thesis was establish and mechanistically characterise the passive nephrotoxic 
nephritis (NTN) model to evaluate on its applicability in the preclinical search of new drug 
candidates for human lupus nephritis (LN). 
 The rationale behind the choice of the passive NTN model was based on the fact that the passive 
NTN model is quick to conduct compared to other animal models for LN and it is independent of 
immunostimulatory components such as Complete Freund’s Adjuvants (CFA).  The read-out for the 
model was chosen based on literature on the NTN model together with literature on methods for LN 
diagnostic.   
To understand the basic immunological mechanisms driving the renal dysfunction in human LN, a 
thorough survey of the literature on the field was made and included in the theory section. The 
mechanistically characterisation of the passive NTN model in this thesis was based on areas 
highlighted in the literature within the field of LN including the involvement of inflammation and 
neutrophils. Hopefully, the establishment and characterisation of the passive NTN model will help 
the development of new therapeutic options for patients with LN.

4. Introduction 
Systemic lupus erythematosus (SLE) is an autoimmune disease that affects multiple organs, 
including the heart, the lung, the skin, and the kidneys 1. SLE is characterised by loss of tolerance 
against nuclear autoantigens and autoantibody production which is hypothesised to be caused by 
dysregulations of the adaptive immune system, especially B and T cells, but lately also of 
neutrophil cells 2,3. SLE mainly affects young women and is believed to be triggered by various 
environmental factors in genetically susceptible individuals 4.  
One major complication of SLE is the development of lupus nephritis (LN), an immune complex 
mediated glomerulonephritis (GN) leading to kidney injury.  
Renal involvement occurs in 20-49 % of patients during their disease and is the strongest cause of 
patient morbidity and mortality 3,5.  
Symptoms of LN may include high blood pressure, edema, proteinuria (PU), and microhaematuria. 
If any significant results indicate active GN, a renal biopsy is conducted in order to classify the 
level of GN and to choose the best possible treatment. Today treatment options for GN include 
symptomatic treatment and strategies to delay disease progression. The standard treatment of a 
severe form of SLE-associated nephropathy includes corticosteroids combined with an 
immunosuppressive drug. Despite treatment, up to 60 % of severe GN cases will reach end-stage 
renal disease (ESRD) meaning that the kidneys no longer are able to work well enough to live 
without dialysis or a transplant. The ESRD is permanent and to date no treatments exist to reverse 
the condition. Immunosuppressive drugs are not an optimal treatment due to the induction of an 
increased risk of infectious or neoplastic complications, malignancy and many of these drugs also 
impact the cardiovascular risk adversely 6. Thus, it is highly relevant to find better and more 
specific treatment therapies.  
Several LN animal models have been developed through the years and have given rise to bringing 
new biotherapies into clinical phases, including cell interacting drugs and drugs targeting identified 
important innate immune mediators 7. The animal models have led to great progress within the field 
of LN, however the disease mechanism is still yet not fully understood and the missing knowledge 
could be crucial in finding new better treatment options and earlier diagnosis of SLE patients with 
LN complications 4. 
Overall the aim of this thesis was to establish and characterise the passive NTN model resembling 
aspects of the human GN, which might be suitable to investigate the underlying disease 
mechanisms, to identify possible novel targets and evaluate new treatment therapies.

5. Background 
 
5.1 Systemic lupus erythematosus 
Systemic lupus erythematosus (SLE) is a systemic autoimmune disease with various disease 
outcomes from mild to fatal organ involvement with clinical presentations covering almost all 
organs and tissues. SLE is characterised by multiple serological aberrations, including a high 
number of autoantibodies and both circulating and tissue deposited immune complexes which lead 
to tissue and organ damage 8,9.  
Lupus is the Latin word for wolf and was used already during the Middle Ages to describe erosive 
skin lesions resembling a wolf’s bite. Lupus refers to the malar rash seen in approximately 50 % of 
all SLE patients and was later described with a butterfly metaphor. The classical lupus/butterfly 
rash presents acutely as an erythematous, elevated lesion and should be differentiated from other 
causes of facial erythema 10. 
The incidence of SLE seems to display ethnic and geographic variation. Generally, estimated 
incidence rates in North America, South America, and Europe range from two to eight per 100.000 
per year and the prevalence of SLE is higher among Asians, African Americans and Latin 
Americans. The disease is about ten times more frequent in women and is usually triggered in the 
child-bearing ages 10,11. 
The pathogenesis remains elusive and has been explained by different hypotheses through the years. 
However, the trigger of SLE is believed to be caused by interactions among genetic and 
environmental factors. Abnormalities in control of self-tolerance lead to loss of self-tolerance. The 
loss of tolerance to self-antigens leads to an expansion and proliferation of an autoimmune response 
which eventually leads to sustained disease and end-organ damage 12.  
Due to the extreme heterogenicity of SLE, it is proposed that SLE is a syndrome rather than a single 
disease. LN is one of the most serious complications of SLE, and leads to PU, acute and chronic 
kidney inflammation and in worst case ESRD 7,13. 
 
 
5.2 Pathogenesis of lupus nephritis 
LN is a form of GN, which is characterised by glomerular lesions and deposition of immune 
complexes formed by mainly immunoglobulin (Ig) G, but also IgM, and IgA autoantibodies (mostly 
against nuclear structures) bound to autoantigens 3,14. Cytokine overproduction and complement

deposition in the glomeruli is also observed, probably as a consequence of immune complex 
formation. Furthermore LN is characterised by infiltration of inflammatory cells contributing to the 
pathogenesis of LN 15,16. Two theories of how autoantibodies form immune deposits have been 
proposed, 1): through direct binding to glomerular antigens or 2): trough deposition of preformed, 
circulating immune complexes. Furthermore, several hypotheses of the contribution of various 
immune cells and mechanisms of the pathogenesis of LN have been raised. B and especially T cells 
have been documented to play an important role in the disease development and recent studies 
revealed an important role of neutrophils 17–19. 
 
5.3 The kidney - function and structure 
The kidney produces urine by selective removal of substances from the blood plasma during blood 
filtration. The reabsorption of water, ions, salts, sugars and other carbohydrates is tightly controlled 
to compose the urine to fit the body’s environment and requirements at that time.  
The kidney’s functional part or the parenchyma is divided into the renal cortex and the renal 
medulla. The cortex forms an outer shell and columns that lie between the individual units of the 
medulla and the medulla is composed of a series of medullary pyramids. Each medullary pyramid 
and its associated shell of cortex, comprises a functional lobe of the kidney. The kidney structure is 
illustrated in figure 1 below 20,21.

5.5  Histological presentation and patterns of glomerular injury in LN 
The histological presentation of GN is heterogeneous, with varying involvement of increased 
cellularity in glomerulus and reduced Bowman’s space. However, an invariant finding is the 
accumulation of immune complexes within glomerular membranes, which often is accompanied by 
expansion of vascular and/or mesangial extracellular matrices (mesangial expansion) and the GBM 
thickening as the disease progresses (Fig. 3) 22.

5.6 Diagnosis of LN 
The diagnosis of LN requires integration of symptoms of the patient, physical examination and 
results of diagnostic tests. Various manifestations at disease onset and during disease progression 
have been identified and should indicate the possibility of LN. However, many of these features are 
also observed in other infectious and systemic rheumatic diseases causing kidney injury and make a 
specific diagnosis difficult. 
So far, PU is the most dominant feature of LN and especially albuminuria is a good indicator of 
GN, therefore urinalysis is the most effective method to detect and monitor renal disease activity 23. 
An increase in the urine volume, a phenomenon called polyuria, can also be a sign of reduced fluid 
reabsorption due to damaged tubules within glomeruli. Renal biopsies are performed to document 
renal pathology and classify the renal injuries histologically. The classification system is illustrated 
in table 1.

Often PU is accompanied by albuminuria and glomerular haematuria, the phenomenon of red blood 
cells in the urine, which can be observed microscopically. Haematuria indicates inflammatory 
glomerular or tubulointerstitial disease. Blood samples are analysed for high creatine levels which 
indicate kidney dysfunction and blood serum are screened for antinuclear antibodies (ANA’s). 
ANA’s are present in the majority of LN patients, but it is also present in some healthy individuals 
and people with scleroderma and rheumatoid arthritis. If a positive ANA is revealed, a check for 
more specific antibodies, a so-called ANA panel, is performed. These antibodies include anti-Smith 
and anti-dsDNA antibody which is most associated with the presence of nephritis. However, the 
sensitivity of the antibodies is questionable.  
It remains a challenge and a time-consuming task to diagnose LN as the current laboratory markers 
lack sensitivity and specificity for differentiating renal activity and damage in LN 10,24. 
 
5.7  Current Treatment 
The immunosuppressive therapy is divided into an induction phase targeting inflammation and 
glomerular injury, and a maintenance phase aiming to reduce the long-term risk of renal failure. The 
induction therapy depends on the class of the disease. Milder forms of LN are usually treated with 
corticosteroids alone, a group of natural and synthetic analogous secreted by the pituitary gland. 
More severe forms of LN require a more aggressive induction therapy consisting of corticosteroids, 
often prednisolone, and an immunosuppressive agent, often cyclophosphamide or mycophenolate 
mofetil. Studies show that a discontinuation of immunosuppression of less than three years often 
==PAGE:15==
14 
 
leads to a relapse of LN. However, the immunosuppressive agents can cause several adverse effects 
and the dose should be adjusted to cause as few side effects as possible, but still be effective. 
Infections are an enormous problem among LN patients during treatment and accounts for 20 to 55 
% of all deaths in LN patients. The infections are presumably caused by immunosuppressive agents 
making the patients more susceptible to infections, especially by treatment with high-dose 
immunosuppressive agents 13. 
 
5.8  The role of T cells in LN 
T helper (Th) cells play an essential role in adaptive immune responses and are among the most 
remarkable cells within the inflamed kidney in LN patients. Th cells induce injury of glomeruli 
primarily through effector responses, such as release of chemokines and recruitment of 
mononuclear cells such as lymphocytes and macrophages. The recruited mononuclear cells release 
tissue factors and TGF-β that initiate fibrin deposition and extracellular matrix accumulation, thus it 
is speculated that T cells may be the source of permeability factors that contribute to PU and 
glomerular injury 18,25. 
T cell responses are initiated when a mature naive CD4 or CD8 T cell meets an activated antigen-
presenting cell (APC) displaying the appropriate peptide:MHC complex comprised of a 
decomposed antigen presented by a major histocompatibility complex (MHC).  CD8 T cells 
recognise peptide:MCH class I complexes and differentiate into cytotoxic effector T cells. CD4 T 
cells recognise peptide: MHC class II complexes and differentiate into one of the effector subsets; 
Th1, Th2, Th17 or T follicular helper (TFH) cells. MHC class I is expressed on all cells of the body 
except red blood cells, whereas the expression of MHC class II is restricted to cells of the immune 
system including dendritic cells (DCs), macrophages, B cells and T cells (T cells only in human, but 
not in mice) 26. 
One consequence of GN is a unregulated Th cell responses. GN has largely been explained by the 
Th1-Th2 paradigm which is a polarisation of the specific immune response into a predominant Th1 
or Th2 profile.  Th1 or Th2 dominance seems to be LN class dependent 27. However most articles 
describe dominance of a Th1 response as a hallmark of LN 28,29. Recent findings also suggest a role 
of Th17 cells in the development of GN due to elevated amounts of IL-17A in patients with LN. 
However, its precise role is still not fully understood 30. 
T cells are believed to play a role in the demonstrated loss of tolerance in LN patients.  Auto/self-
reactive T cells are present in normal circulating peripheral blood. If these auto-reactive T cells are 
==PAGE:16==
15 
 
not regulated by T regulatory cells, which have the potential to suppress autoreactive T cells, the 
autoimmune response can spread. The autoreactive T cells can activate autoreactive B cells, which 
have internalised peptides, and thereby differentiate the B cells into autoantibody excreting cells, 
thus intensifying the disease (Fig. 4). The association between T and B cells seems important for the 
development of LN as T cells provide help for B-cell production of autoantibodies. 31,32.  
 
5.9 The role of B cells in LN 
Traditionally B cells were believed to only play a role in producing autoantibodies, which form 
immune complexes with autoantigens and deposit in glomeruli and initiate inflammation. 
Today B cells are considered to contribute to the pathogenesis of GN by several mechanisms.  They 
can function as APC’s and present autoantigens to T cells and act as efficient activators of antigen-
specific T cells. Furthermore, B cells produce pro-inflammatory cytokines and may regulate T cells 
and DC’s through these and by regulatory antibodies 17,33. 
Studies indicate a high prevalence of intra-renal B cells in GN 34. A study by (Steinmetz)35 
examining B cells in LN patients, discovered that most B cells displayed a mature non-antibody 
producing phenotype with an antigen presenting ability 35. The study constructed a hypothesis of 
that local B cell infiltration could play a role in tissue injury and is consistent with the theory of B 
cells activating autoreactive T cells by presenting autoantigens 34. Circulating autoreactive B cells 
bind autoantigens released from dying cells and become activated by auto-reactive T cells that 
recognise the presented peptide of the B cells. The B cells differentiate into plasma cells, secreting 
self-antigen specific antibodies (autoantibodies) which may mediate tissue damage through effector 
functions and result in a positive feedback loop, see figure 5.

5.10 Dysregulation of apoptotic cell clearance – a source of autoantigens 
The process of apoptosis is a critical mechanism in immune tolerance. Apoptotic cells must be 
removed fast from tissues by phagocytosis to avoid that they elicit inflammatory immune responses. 
Apoptotic cell accumulation can provoke a chronic inflammatory response and may lead to 
breakdown of self-tolerance 37,38.  
When apoptosis proceeds, apoptotic blebs will be formed at the surface of the apoptotic cells which 
contain clustered autoantigens from the dying cell. Normally clearance of apoptotic cells is 
associated with active suppression of the immune response to self-antigens within the apoptotic 
cells and blebs, but an abnormal clearance of apoptotic cells can lead to autoimmunity 39. Studies 
reveal that these blebs are able to induce maturation of DC’s causing them to present modified 
autoantigens arising from apoptotic cells, to autoreactive T cells or B cells. The T cells become 
activated and activate autoreactive B cells that have ingested modified autoantigens from apoptotic 
cells. This results in the production of autoantibodies directed towards autoantigens 40,41. 
The aspect of self-reactivity seems crucial for the development of LN, and loss of B cell tolerance 
which occurs early in LN may trigger other immune abnormalities. Normally B cell tolerance is 
established at several checkpoints throughout B cell development, but in LN patients an important 
peripheral checkpoint that operates to sense autoreactive B cells in the mature naïve compartment, 
has been shown to be faulty. The consequence is that the autoreactive B cells are no longer 
prevented in expanding into the memory compartment and up to 20 % of peripheral naïve B cells

are shown to still be reactive with nuclear self-antigens. 11,42. The loss of tolerance is illustrated in

5.11 The role of neutrophils in LN 
Besides neutrophils role of the innate response to pathogens, it is believed that they also possess 
important regulatory mechanisms in many inflammatory conditions. Studies reveal an important 
role of neutrophils in many autoimmune diseases and that they interact with the adaptive immune 
system 43,44. Neutrophils are the most abundant leukocyte of the human body comprising 40-60 % 
of the white blood cell population 45. In healthy individuals neutrophils exist in a resting state to 
protect the surroundings from their toxic intracellular contents. Activation involves priming of 
neutrophils by agents including bacterial products and cytokines or chemokines such as G-CSF, 
TNF-α, and IFN-γ. Afterwards the primed neutrophils are mobilised to the site of infection or 
inflammation to encounter activating signals to trigger bacterial killing  45. The priming cytokines 
are upregulated in LN and indicate a role of the neutrophils. The involvement of neutrophils in the 
adaptive immune response occurs by communicating with T cells and DC’s 45. The communication 
with T cells could have consequences for proliferative GN because neutrophils can direct the

5.12 The role of other cells in LN 
Macrophages are known to infiltrate the glomeruli after their activation by chemokines and are 
believed to promote renal injury 14. 
Macrophages secrete a wide range of molecules that may cause renal injury, such pro-inflammatory 
cytokines and growth factors. Macrophage derived pro-inflammatory cytokines, such as TNF-α and 
IL-1β, stimulate mesangial cells and renal tubular epithelial cells to produce chemokines and 
express adhesion molecules which possible contribute to the renal damage 49. 
Macrophages possess multiple roles and macrophage kidney infiltration could also promote renal 
repair since they are efficient in phagocytising and removing apoptotic cells, deposited immune 
complexes and fibrin 50.‘ 
DC’s from LN patients display an abnormal phenotype characterised by accelerated differentiation, 
maturation, and secretion of pro-inflammatory cytokines, however their functions in autoimmune 
diseases are yet not fully understood 51. Commonly, DC’s circulate in peripheral tissues, capture 
pathogens or dying cells, and present antigens 15,52,53. DC’s are very powerful presenters of various 
antigens to mostly T cells, but also B cells, during the induction of an immune response. However, 
DC’s also contribute to the maintenance of peripheral tolerance by processing autoantigens derived 
from apoptotic cells, particularly when DC’s are exposed to large amounts of apoptotic cells. 
Therefore, DC’s contribute to the maintenance of tolerance, but they also can contribute to 
autoimmunity by presenting auto-antigens. 
Furthermore, DC’s can produce various complement factors such as C1q and C3 that activate 
complement which is believed to participate in the pathogenesis of LN 54.

5.13 Characteristic Cytokines of LN 
Cytokines are soluble messenger proteins produced and released by immune cells to modify and 
regulate functional activities of individual cells and tissues. Cytokines play a pivotal role in the 
differentiation, maturation, and activation of various cells. In healthy individuals, cytokines are 
present at low concentrations and may not even be detectable in body fluids or tissues 55. As before 
mentioned, loss of tolerance and thereby autoantibody excretion can lead to tissue damage. When 
tissue damage occurs, tissue macrophages and other immune cells release pro-inflammatory 
cytokines and chemokines, and attract additional immune cells from the blood to the site of 
infection. The released cytokines initiate an inflammatory response, and cause the dilation of blood 
vessels allowing leukocytes to enter the infected tissue. Anti-inflammatory cytokines are immune-
regulatory molecules that control the pro-inflammatory cytokine response. A dynamic balance 
exists between pro-inflammatory cytokines and anti-inflammatory components of the human 
immune system 56. 
A dysregulation of pro-inflammatory cytokines is likely to play a role in the loss of immune 
tolerance that leads to LN, and in triggering of glomerular inflammation and driving of the 
irreversible renal damage 27,57. 
Multiple cytokines have been identified in the disease activity of LN. The pro-inflammatory 
cytokines IL-1β, IL-6, IL-17, IL-18, IFN-α, IFN-γ, TNF-α and the anti-inflammatory cytokines IL-4 
and IL-10 are most associated with LN. However, the expression of various cytokines seems to be 
dependent on the class of LN 27,57. The role of the mentioned cytokines is described in table 2 
below.

5.14 Granulocyte colony stimulating factor 
Colony stimulating factors (CSFs) have in the last decades been demonstrated to stimulate the 
growth of hematopoietic stem and progenitor cells, and later CSFs also have been shown to act on 
mature myeloid populations as pro-survival, activation and/or proliferation factors. G-CSF’s 
stimulation of neutrophil function and number regulation seems important in LN. The neutrophils 
are regulated by induction of proliferation and maturation of myeloid progenitors, and by increasing 
the neutrophil release from the bone marrow 65,66 Studies indicate that G-CSF activated neutrophils  
induce organ damage due to  tissue infiltration and release of pro-inflammatory cytokines, and is 
supported by increased levels of G-CSF in LN patients 67. G-CSF is produced by fibroblasts, 
macrophages, bone marrow stromal cells, and endothelial cells, and the expression of G-CSF is 
often induced by a host response to infection or injury causing an inflammatory milieu 68. G-CSF 
acts through the G-CSF receptor (G-CSFR), which is expressed on neutrophils 66. On the contrary,

G-CSF has also been reported in studies to show pro-Th2 and anti-inflammatory properties and 
thereby play a role in the Th1/Th2 balance 69,70. Thus G-CSF may play a role in down-regulating the 
activation and expansion of self-reactive T cells which seems to be a key mechanism for the control 
of T cell-mediated diseases. 
More knowledge of the link between G-CSF and LN is needed, but there is growing evidence that 
G-CSF is an essential mediator in innate immune responses linking adaptive and innate immunity, 
which is of particular interest in infectious diseases and autoimmunity 68. 
 
5.15 C-reactive protein 
C-reactive protein (CRP) is a pentraxin family member, and is a phylogenetically highly conserved 
plasma protein, with homologs in vertebrates and many invertebrates. CRP increases in the 
presence of inflammation in the body, and an elevated CRP level is considered a non-specific 
"marker" for disease 71. Elevation of CRP is among the characteristics of LN. A study indicates that 
CRP consistently is present in renal immune deposits in LN patients, in all renal compartments. The 
deposition could be explained by binding of CRP to autoantigens deposited in the kidneys 72. 
CRP is considered as an opsonin that facilitates removal of dying tissue. However, CRP may also 
amplify damage by activating complement, monocytes and neutrophils triggering the release of 
cytokines that mediates tissue damage. The mediation of both cases happens through the Fcγ 
receptor, which is the primary receptor for pentraxins 73.  
In mice strains, CRP is not an acute-phase reactant, but the acute-phase protein Serum amyloid P 
(SAP) is a marker of inflammation in mice like the CRP is a marker of inflammation  in humans 74. 
 
5.16  Animal models for human diseases 
Animal model approaches offer effective and safer treatments for the future, and animal models 
have been used for research in centuries by scientists to obtain knowledge of animal and human 
biological structure and function. An animal model describes a biological phenomenon which the 
species has in common with the target species. An animal model should not be seen as identical to 
what that is being modelled, but more as a set of analogues between the system that is being studied 
and the human disease mechanism. Animal models are very useful for a better understanding of 
disease mechanisms and also for the evaluation of new therapeutic drugs. An animal model for 
disease must be similar in its pathology to the disease conditions in humans. In vitro studies such as 
cell-based assays and tissue culture studies can provide useful information, however they cannot 
==PAGE:24==
23 
 
clarify the complex interactions among different cell types of tissues and organs. Cells cannot be 
considered as separate components because all living organism are dependent on homeostatic 
processes to live and perform their functions within their environment. Therefore animal models 
give the most similar disease conditions to humans to investigate disease mechanisms, evaluate 
treatments and identify possible toxicities before clinical trials in humans. The most common 
animal models are the induced (experimental) models and the spontaneous (genetic, mutant) 
models. In induced models the condition to be investigated is experimentally induced in healthy 
animals 75,76. 
Animal models are also essential to understand the pathogenesis concerning the development, 
progression and resolution of kidney diseases. For a long period of time, rodents and non-rodent 
models have been created and applied within nephrology. Rodent models are preferred for a number 
of reasons including low cost, low maintenance requirements, fast proliferation and the ease of 
handling. The research with LN models has provided knowledge about the crucial interaction 
between inflammatory cells and cells intrinsic to the kidney which describes the pathogenesis of 
GN 77. 
 
5.17 Animal Models of LN 
Numerous spontaneous and induced murine models have been employed to obtain knowledge of the 
aetiology of SLE. Spontaneous mouse models have led to the identification of several gene 
candidates of SLE induction. Induced models have provided knowledge of cellular mechanisms 
involved in the onset and progression of disease.  
It is not possible to achieve all characteristics of the human SLE in one single mouse model, 
however, the kidney disease and immune responses in each LN mouse strain would represent a 
certain aspects of the human disease setting. Additionally, it is an advantage that mouse models 
give the ability to study the initiation of early renal change and the role of individual pathogenic 
factors leading to disease progression before the manifestation of clinical decreased kidney 
function.      
It is important to be aware that strain, gender, age and body weight can provide different outcome 
of a particular experiment. Especially different strains often display heterogeneous development of 
renal lesions after injury 78.

5.18 Spontaneous LN models 
Some of the most commonly used spontaneous mouse strains include the New Zealand Black/ New 
/Zealand white (NXB)/(NZW) F1 hybrid model, MRL lpr/lpr, and BXSB 79. These strains 
spontaneously develop anti-dsDNA antibodies, glomerular immune complex deposits, and fatal 
GN. A disadvantage of the spontaneous models is that the state of kidney pathology manifests over 
a long time, normally several months, before any features of chronic progressive GN is shown. Due 
to the spontaneous development, these models are challenging to manipulate experimentally, and 
their use is time-consuming 80. 
 
5.19 Induced LN models 
Induced mouse models can develop nephritis after exposure to certain environmental triggers. This 
method induces a rapid onset from a known starting point, thus the study of nephritis induction is 
more controlled compared to the spontaneous models of LN where the onset of disease can be 
several weeks to month. The most common induced models are the pristane-induced lupus model, 
the induced chronic graft-versus-host disease (GVHD) and the NTN model. 
The pristane-induced lupus model induces immune-complex deposition in the kidney leading to 
severe PU and nephritis. However the development of nephritis features including moderate PU and 
proliferative GN is reported to take up to 6 month after pristane injection 79,80. Furthermore various 
models of the GvHD have been used as models of LN as it is an LN-like disease. In this model 
autoantibodies are detected within 10 – 14 days. The model also shows kidney injuries similar to 
typical lesions of human LN, however the lesions appear 12 to 14 weeks after the induction 81,82. 
The long timelines of many of the LN mouse models are a disadvantage, as each study will be very 
time-consuming. 
 
5.20 Nephrotoxic Serum Nephritis 
NTN is a rodent model for studying the pathogenesis of induced GN. The Japanese pathologist M. 
Masugi invented the first working model of NTN in 1934, and from thereof the synonym Masugi 
nephritis exists. NTN is characterised by a rapid loss of kidney function, indicated by severe PU. 
The loss of kidney function is caused by the induction of GN which can lead to tubulointerstitial 
injury and subsequent fibrosis 83,84. 
Two types of the NTN model exist, a passive and an accelerated version. In the accelerated version 
mice are immunised with heterologous IgG in complete Freund’s adjuvant (CFA), typically raised 
==PAGE:26==
25 
 
in sheep or rabbit. The mice are afterwards injected intravenously with hyper immune anti-GBM 
sheep or rabbit serum, named nephrotoxic serum (NTS) leading to binding to the GBM in kidneys 
and IC formation and deposition. In the passive NTN model mice are infused with sheep or rabbit 
anti-GBM serum alone without prior immunisation 85. 
Dependent on the sera, various phenotypes of the model is observed, suggesting the possibility of 
different mechanisms of mediation of injury caused by different sera. Therefore the NTN model 
needs to be established and characterised when using another type of sera or even another batch can 
affect the required dose of NTS and the subsequent phenotype 86. 
NTN can be divided in two separated disease phases: an early heterologous (acute) phase as a result 
of linear anti-GBM antibody deposition and an ensuing autologous (chronic) phase resulting from 
immune response against the planted antibodies 83. 
 
5.20.1 Phase 1: The heterologous phase 
The heterologous develops within minutes after the NTS injection due to glomerular deposition of 
heterologous antibodies contained in the NTS. The antibodies bind to antigens of the glomerular 
endothelial wall and GBM, which locally triggers an innate immune activation, where pro-
inflammatory chemokines and cytokines are secreted. The glomerular anti-GBM antibody 
deposition leads to GN characterised by PU and albuminuria which is identified approximately 12 
hours after NTS injection. The injected antibodies act as a foreign antigen in the host and a high 
numbers of foreign antigen induce an adaptive immune response against the heterologous 
antibodies 84,87. 
Glomerular injury is indicated to be dependent on complement activation and neutrophil-dominated 
inflammation. Complement proteins bind to the conserved Fc region of the injected nephrotoxic 
IgG antibodies that is deposited in glomeruli. Furthermore the Fc region of the IgG’s may recognise 
membrane receptors of infiltrating leukocytes, thus the deposited antibodies may be the cause of 
accumulation of complement proteins and leukocytes in the kidney 88.  
C5a plays a major role in triggering neutrophil and monocyte chemotaxis and activation. C5a is an 
effective chemoattractant, and initiates accumulation of complement and phagocytic cells at sites of 
infection 83. C5a increases migration and adherence of neutrophils and monocytes to vessel walls 
and also modulates the balance between activating against inhibitory IgG Fc receptors on 
leukocytes, thereby enhancing the autoimmune response. Further C5a plays a role of pro-
==PAGE:27==
26 
 
inflammatory molecules by its release of histamine and TNF-α 83,89. The heterologous phase is 
illustrated in figure 7a. 
 
 
5.20.2 Phase 2: The autologous phase 
The autologous phase develops within approximately one week. It is characterised by the 
emergence of systemic adaptive immune responses against the heterologous antibody planted in the 
glomerulus. It is characterised by increased albuminuria, glomerular leukocyte infiltration and 
glomerular injury 83. 
Many studies reveal a Th1 response, which stimulates macrophages to mediate renal damage, and 
renal dendritic cells promote the T cell response. Activated T cells induce B cell maturation in 
lymphoid tissues resulting in plasma cells producing autologous antibodies directed against the 
injected heterologous nephrotoxic antibodies deposited in glomeruli. The pathogenesis is illustrated 
in figure 8. Pre-immunisation accelerates the autologous phase leading to severe nephritic PU and 
severe tubulointestinal fibrosis within 3-6 weeks 83. As previous mentioned, the NTN model 
displays various phenotypes dependent on the batch of NTS, thus the NTN model requires 
establishment and validation before the model can contribute to preclinical research within the field 
of LN.  This leads to the main objective of this thesis: to establish and validate the passive NTN 
model.

6. Rationale and Aim 
 
Although there has been progress within the field of LN research, and several biologic agents 
interfering with more specific targets, e.g. type I interferon, is currently in clinical trials, the 
possibilities of treating LN are still not fully explored. In order to develop improved treatment 
therapies and better diagnostic markers, animal models of high quality resembling human LN are 
needed. 
An animal model displaying certain aspects of the human disease LN enables the study of relevant 
underlying pathways. However, findings in an animal model cannot be expected the same in 
humans. Furthermore, a qualified and validated animal model would be beneficial for the 
investigation of possible targets, identification of new drug candidates or biomarkers, as well as 
further exploring the mechanism of action, of a certain drug candidate.  
Several animal models have been used for preclinical research in the field of LN. However the 
classical spontaneous LN models, such as NZB/WF1 and MRL lpr/lpr, develop LN several months 
after the initiation of the study, which makes the studies in these models very time-consuming.  
The inducible LN models, such as the NTN model, give the possibility to test various treatments in 
a shorter and more mechanistic setting. The NTN model, which resembles certain aspects of the 
human LN, is a model characterised by an IC and complement dependent induction of renal injury

are emitted and signals are gathered by an array of photo-detectors and optical filters. Beside the 
fluorescence signals, laser light is scattered from the flowing cells always at the same wavelength.

orthogonal to the incident laser correlates with the intracellular granularity, see figure 10.

7.13 Statistical analysis 
Statistical analyses were performed using the software GraphPad Prism Version 6. To summarise, 
normal distributed data of two groups were compared using a student’s t-test. Non-normal 
distributed data of two groups were compared using Mann Whitney U test. 
Normal distributed data of more than two groups were compared by one-way ANOVA with Dunn’s 
multiple comparison test. Non-normal distributed data of more than two groups were compared 
using Kruskal-Wallis with Dunn’s multiple comparison test. 
 
8. Prior work 
A pilot study was conducted with the aim to determine an efficient dose to induce GN in a passive 
NTN model using NTS which contains antibodies against glomerular structures. The doses 25, 100 
and 250 µL NTS were evaluated. To evaluate the induction of renal dysfunction and possible GN, 
the protein excretion into urine (PU) was measured on day 1, 2, 4, 7, 8 and 9 during the study. 
   
8.1 Proteinuria measurements in the pilot study  
The routine dipstick evaluation of protein in the urine is not specific, but it is a rapid and 
useful screening test to monitor GN. PU is a sign of glomerular filtration dysfunction since the 
glomeruli no longer prevents large molecules such as proteins from being excreted with the 
urine. 
A small volume of urine was collected on a dipstick of each mouse and measured immediately. 
The measurements provided a PU score utilising following scoring system: Score 1:0-
30mg/dL, 2: 30-100 mg/dl, 3: 100-300 mg/dL. 
The mice given the dose of 250 µL NTS revealed a consistent mean PU score of 3 throughout the 
study of 10 days. The mice receiving the dose of 100 µL NTS showed a mean PU score of 3 on day 
1, but decreased from day 4 until day 7, and the lowest dose of 25 µL NTS did not differ from the 
control group (Fig. 15a). The area under the curve (AUC) was calculated to depict the PU scores 
over time in order to evaluate the consistency of the scores.   
 It was shown that the group of 250 µL NTS displayed very consistent PU score of 3 until day 10 
where the study was terminated (Fig. 15B). 
The PU results suggest the dose of 250 µL NTS as the most likely dose to induce GN.

8.2 Weight measurements to monitor the health of the mice in the pilot study 
Body weight (BW) loss, besides a moribund appearance, is a major indicator of pain and distress in 
mice. However working with experimental animals will always affect the general wellbeing of mice 
to some extend by experimental approaches. Thus, it is important to monitor experimental mice by 
their body weight to ensure their general health. In case of reaching the humane endpoint of 20% 
body weight loss, mice were sacrificed. BW was measured on day 0,1, 4, 7 and 8 throughout the 
experiment. The NTS i.v. injection resulted in reduced BW in the mice injected with 250 µL NTS 
compared to the mice that received 25 and 100 µL NTS and the PBS control mice.   
The mice in the group of 250 µL NTS lost BW until around day 7 where the BW had dropped 
approximately 8 %, but after day 7 the mice gained weight until day 8 (Fig. 16A). This indicates 
that induction of 250 µL NTS in the passive NTN model does not significantly affect the general 
health of the mice since the crucial 20 % bodyweight loss never was reached (Fig. 16B).

9.1 Establishment of the passive NTN model 
The passive NTN model is a mouse model which is induced experimentally by injecting NTS into 
the mice without prior immunisation. The main objective of this thesis was to establish a passive 
NTN model induced by a specific NTS, containing sheep anti-rat glomerular Ab’s. The 
establishment of the model using one specific batch was performed because the required dose for 
NTN induction and thereby GN development is supposed to be batch dependent. The number of 
raised antibodies against various glomerular structures can differ from batch to batch within the 
same animal species, but also endotoxin contaminations can play a role. 
In order to validate the establishment of the passive NTN model, the clinical read-outs PU and 
albuminuria which also are utilised in human diagnostic of LN, were implemented at different time 
points during the studies. Furthermore, the welfare of the mice is an important factor when 
establishing a mouse model to comply with animal welfare regulations, and to evaluate if a mouse

9.1.1 Investigation of the renal impact by urine analysis 
To evaluate the impact of the NTS transfer and to determine if GN could be induced with 250 µl 
NTS in C57Bl/6 mice, the urine of all mice was analysed for PU and albuminuria. Additionally, the 
urine volume was estimated to identify possible differences of urine volume excretion between the 
NTS and the control group. 
 
9.1.2 PU in the establishment of the passive NTN model 
The routine dipstick evaluation of protein in the urine is not specific, but it is a rapid diagnostic tool 
and a useful screening test to monitor PU. PU is a sign of damaged glomeruli which no longer 
prevents large molecules such as proteins from being excreted with the urine. 
To monitor PU development in the passive NTN model, the urine protein levels was semi-
quantitatively assessed by dipstick analysis on day 1, 3, 7, 8, 10, 14 and 17 after NTN induction. 
Drops of urine were applied on a urine dipstick and determined for each mouse.  The measurements 
provided a PU score utilising following scoring system: Score 1:0-30mg/dL, 2: 30-100 mg/dL, 3: 
100-300 mg/dL.  The score of 0-2 is within a normal level in C57BL/6 mice. Thus, a mouse is 
classified as having abnormal PU when exhibiting a PU score of 3, which is 100-300 mg protein/dl 
urine. 
The mice were scored 7 times during the confirmatory study to evaluate the renal impact.  
Already one day after NTS transfer, mice were displaying mean PU score 3 and continued to do so 
until termination of study, whereas control mice showed non-clinical scores between 0-2 (Fig. 
17A). 
The area under the curve (AUC) was calculated to depict the PU scores over time in order to 
evaluate the consistency of the scores. The NTS group displayed a rather consistent score of 3 until 
day 10 where 10 mice of each group were terminated (Fig. 17B). From day 10 until day 17, the 
mean PU score of the NTS mice slightly decreased (Fig1C). Three of the last 10 mice in the study 
from day 10 to day 21 showed a continuous mean PU score of 2, and 6 mice displayed a mean PU 
score of 2 on day 17 (data not shown). 
Taken together, PU was only induced in NTN mice, but not in control mice. This confirms the 
findings of the pilot study (section 8), where 250 µL NTS efficiently induced PU.

9.1.3 Albuminuria in the establishment of the passive NTN model

progressive GN. Thus to support results generated by PU measurements, albuminuria was evaluated

calculate the total amount of uric albumin, and to investigate if any urine volume difference

Polyuria).  The albumin concentration of the collected urine samples from the NTS and control 
group was analysed by ELISA. Comparing the total volume of urine calculated for 24 hours, the

observed on day 2/3 (Fig. 18C).

B). Data from the pilot study showed that 100 µL NTS resulted in a significant increase of the urine 
volume on day 9/10 whereas, 25 µL NTS did not (data not shown).

9.1.4 Histology 
To confirm GN development in the passive NTN model, histopathological analyses were performed 
on selected representative kidneys in this study. PAS staining illustrates a wide range of tissue 
carbohydrates, including glycogen, and mucosal substances such as glycoproteins, whereby basal 
membranes and mesangial expansion can be illustrated. In particular PAS staining is commonly 
used to identify renal damage and GN can be confirmed by this method. 
Three representative mice from the group of 250 µL NTS on day 10 and 21 were chosen together 
with three controls and PAS stained terminated on day 21. The kidney sections were collected from 
approximately same area identified from the kidney structure. One kidney section from each mouse 
was evaluated blindly (each mouse was given an additional number and evaluated without knowing 
the treatment of the mouse) by light microscope. 
It was evaluated whether glomeruli of the NTN mice revealed mesangial expansion and/or 
increased cellularity compared to the control mice. The results illustrated abnormal glomeruli of all 
6 NTS mice both on day 10 and 21 (Fig. 20). The three analysed NTN mice on day 10 showed 
approximately 50% affected glomeruli, whereas the three analysed NTN mice on day 21 showed 
approximately 80 % affected glomeruli (table 13). These results suggest that the glomeruli affection 
is increased over time. The NTN mice displayed mesangial expansion and/or increased cellularity 
both on day 10 (data not shown) and 21 (Fig. 22B). In addition to the abnormal glomeruli, the NTN 
mice revealed tubular atrophy and dilations as well as protein accumulation in several tubules (Fig. 
21B). No sign of renal damage was observed in the control mice (Fig. 21A). The tubular atrophy 
and dilations seemed more widespread on day 21 compared to day 10 indicating a continually 
disease progression from day 10 until day 21 (Fig. 21A andC). Furthermore the protein 
accumulation is indicated to be intensified day 21 since the tubules were more dilated and the 
observed PAS stain was more intense in these kidney sections (Fig. 21B and 21D). The PAS stain 
demonstrates an induction of GN by the dose of 250 µL NTS confirming the establishment of 
kidney pathology in the passive NTN model with PU and albuminuria as clinical readouts.

9.1.5 The body weight as a health monitor 
Body weight loss, besides a moribund appearance, is a major indicator of pain and distress in mice. 
However it is expected that certain experimental procedures will affect the general wellbeing of 
mice to some extent. Thus, it is important to monitor experimental mice by their body weight to 
ensure their general health. In case of reaching the humane endpoint of 20% body weight loss, mice 
were sacrificed. BW was measured on day 0, 1, 2, 6, 7, 10, 14 and 17 in the experiment. As 
depicted in figure 23A, the injection of 250 µl NTS i.v. resulted in reduced body weight compared 
to the PBS control group. Mice in both groups started at a comparable BW, but only mice in the 
NTN group dropped approximately 10% BW until day 6 after NTS transfer (Fig. 23B). The mice 
from the control group lost approximately 4% of their BW on day 2 after the PBS i.v. injection, but 
recovered their BW again at day 6. Mice in the NTN group regained BW after day 6 and stabilized 
on approximately 2% BW loss compared to the PBS control group (Fig. 23B). This indicates that 
the passive NTN model is mainly having transient effect on the general health of the mice. 
 
In summary, the passive NTN model has been established by inducing GN with 250 µL NTS in 
C57BL/6 mice. This was determined by the induction of PU, increased urine volume and 
albuminuria both in the pilot and the confirmatory study, showing highly reproducible results. 
Furthermore, renal dysfunction could be confirmed by PAS staining depicting mesangial expansion 
and increased cellularity in NTN mice. None of the mice died from ESRD or as a consequence of 
NTN induction and only transient weight loss was observed following NTS injection, indicating 
that the passive model of NTN can be a useful experimental and pharmacological model of GN.

9.2  Mechanistic characterisation of the passive NTN model 
So far, the results have shown a rapid GN induction demonstrated by PU, albuminuria and 
mesangial expansion of glomeruli. To be able to understand what drives the development of GN in 
the passive NTN model, the mechanisms behind the disease development needed to be analysed. In 
order to mechanistically characterise the passive NTN model, the distribution of leukocytes in blood 
and kidneys was analysed upon NTN induction, as well as the involvement of the acute phase 
protein SAP and the inflammatory cytokine G-CSF which is important for neutrophil recruitment 
from the bone marrow into blood. 
 
9.2.1 The distribution of CD45+ cells in the blood 
To evaluate the involvement of various leukocyte subsets in the passive NTN model, the number 
and distribution of T cells, B cells and neutrophils was investigated in blood by FACS analysis.  
Blood samples were collected from the control, the 25, 100 and 250 µL NTS group on day 3 and 
day 10 in the pilot study. The blood was stained for CD4+ and CD8+ T cells, B cells and 
neutrophils in order to gate the cell populations (Gating is shown in section. 7.8). The data from the 
25 and 100 µL groups are not shown since the dose of 250 µL was most efficient and utilised in the 
confirmatory study. 
The results of the total number of leukocytes on day 3 showed a significant decrease in CD4+ T 
cells, CD8+ T cells and B cells in the NTS group compared to the control group. In contrast, the 
leukocyte neutrophil subset was significantly increased in NTN mice (Fig. 24A). 
The percentage of CD4+ T cells, CD8+ T cells, B cells and neutrophils of all leukocytes was 
calculated to depict the cellular distribution of the different cell types from NTN mice in 
comparison with the control mice (Fig. 24B and C).  These data suggest that the NTN induction has 
initiated cellular changes in blood on day 3 following injections of NTS.

9.2.3 SAP analysis 
The NTN model is characterised by an acute, and a chronic phase, described as the heterologous 
and autologous phase in the introduction. Thus the involvement of the acute-phase reactant SAP 
was investigated during the NTN induction.  
Blood plasma was collected 7-8h, 3, 10 and 21 days post NTN induction and analysed by ELISA. 
The results showed significant differences between the NTS and control group 7-8h, 3 and 10 days, 
but not 21 days post NTS injections. 7-8 h after NTN induction, the SAP analysis revealed a 
concentration of approximately 1,3x107 ng/mL SAP compared to the baseline level of 
approximately 4,0x106 ng/mL SAP in control mice (Fig. 27A). The SAP concentration peaked on

SAP level was decreased to approximately 1,5x107 ng/mL (Fig. 27C) and day 21 the NTN mice 
revealed a SAP conc. close to the baseline level in the control mice (Fig. 27D). Taken together, the 
SAP analysis indicates an acute response already detectable 7-8 h post NTN induction, peaking 
around day 3and slowly decreasing thereafter.

9.3 Effect of neutrophil depletion in the passive NTN model 
Due to the increase in neutrophils in blood on day 3 and 10, the role of neutrophils of the passive 
NTN model was evaluated. A neutrophil depletion study was performed using a neutrophil 
depleting monoclonal antibody (mAb) directed against Ly6G (anti-Ly6G mAb) on neutrophils.  
In this study 4 groups of 10 mice were either treated with 1mg/mouse anti-Ly6G mAb (clone 1A8), 
isotype control mAb (IgG2a), or PBS 24 h prior to the NTS injections. The mice were boosted with 
0.5mg/mouse on day 3, 6 and 8 during the study. A fourth naïve group functioned as an untreated 
control group. 
The specific mAb anti-Ly6G (clone 1A8) was chosen since it was previously demonstrated to 
deplete neutrophils and commonly is used in neutrophil depletion studies in mice.44. 
A depletion check was performed by FACS on day 0 before the NTS injection and 7 days after. 
Blood was collected, and single cells were stained for T cells, B cells and neutrophil cells (Gating is 
shown in section 7.12). 
The results showed a significant decrease of the neutrophil population in the anti-Ly6G treated 
group on day 0 compared to the IgG2a, PBS treated and the control group (Fig. 31A). However, the 
data clearly demonstrated that the depletion was insufficient and not all neutrophils were depleted. 
The neutrophils of the anti-Ly6G mice comprised approximately 6 % of the leukocytes on day 0 
compared to the neutrophil distribution of the control group which displayed approximately 11 % 
(Fig. 31A). On day 7 after NTN induction, the neutrophil population was significantly increased in 
all NTN groups compared to the control group receiving PBS instead of NTS on day 0 (Fig. 31B). 
However, no depletion of neutrophils was observed in the anti-Ly6G mAb treated group. The 
fraction of neutrophils of all leukocytes was comparable between the anti-Ly6G depleted, and the 
PBS and isotype control group. The gating strategy from day 7 revealed that not all neutrophils 
were gated into the neutrophil population due to a too high forward side scatter (FSC), 
unfortunately excluding a part of the neutrophils. This could also explain the relatively low 
neutrophil fraction in the PBS control group between day 0 and 7. Taken together, these results 
indicate that the neutrophil depletion by anti-Ly6G mAb was not efficient, and 50 % of all gated 
neutrophils were still detectable on the day of NTN injection.

10. Discussion 
 
10.1 Establishment of the passive NTN model 
The pathogenesis of the complex systemic autoimmune disease SLE is not fully elucidated which is 
complicating an effective treatment of the majority of patients. One major complication of SLE is 
the development of LN, an immune complex mediated GN leading to kidney injury and finally 
ESRD. Over decades, several animal models have been utilised in preclinical research within the 
field of SLE, however the classical spontaneous mouse models develop LN after several month 
making the research very time-consuming. In order to develop improved treatment therapies and 
better diagnostic markers, animal models of high quality resembling human LN are needed. Thus 
this thesis aimed to establish and validate a passive NTN model displaying a rapid GN 
development. The establishment of a qualified and validated NTN model would be very beneficial 
for the investigation of possible targets, identification of new drug candidates or biomarkers, as well 
as further exploring the mechanism of action of a certain drug candidate. 
A literature study was performed in order to identify NTS dose candidates for NTN induction. 
Different papers describing NTN induction in mice in the non-accelerated model, utilised doses 
between 100 and 300 µL NTS injected i.v. 87,90,91. Thus the literature indicates that the passive NTN 
model could be induced with various doses.  
To determine the efficient dose of NTS for GN induction, a pilot study was performed evaluating 
the doses of 25, 100 and 250 µL NTS. The low dose of 25 µL NTS was included because an 
evaluation of the lowest possible dose would be attractive in order to obtain a sensitive model for 
treatment studies. As a primary read out to evaluate the optimal dose of NTS, the PU measurements 
were chosen as a fast and rapid tool to monitor renal dysfunction. Normal urine contains a variety of 
proteins including serum proteins and proteins derived from the kidney and urinary tract. The 
proteins are essential components of any living individual and the kidneys play a major role in the 
retention of these proteins, using renal tubules to reabsorb the proteins as they pass through the 
glomerular filtration barrier and glomerular capillary structure hinders passage of proteins into the 
Bowman’s space. Thus an increased quantity of PU would usually indicate a filtration defect of the 
glomeruli 23,92. The excreted urinary proteins are a consequence of enlarged pore size of the 
glomerular barrier. The pore size has been demonstrated to increase with approximately 20-25 Å in 
GN patients 93. The most significant protein is albumin, but also leakage of IgG is often found in the 
urine of patients with GN 23,93.  
==PAGE:64==
63 
 
In the pilot study it was demonstrated that the dose of 250 µL NTS induced PU in the mice. This 
was determined by a consistent mean PU score of 3 throughout the study of 10 days. The rapid 
abnormal urine protein level is also observed in a study, that induce NTN by injecting 200 µL NTS 
87. 
Thus the dose of 250 µL NTS was chosen as a preferred dose in the passive NTN model. 
When establishing an animal model, it is important to confirm the results gained from an initial 
pilot study, where in this case, the aim was to determine the optimal dose in one or more subsequent 
studies. High reproducibility and validity are important criteria for a successfully established mouse 
model to find applicability in preclinical research. Therefore, a replication study was conducted 
with the aim to confirm PU induction by the dose of 250 µL NTS. 
The confirmatory study also showed, as demonstrated in the initial pilot study, that 250 µL NTS 
was able to induce PU in C57BL/6 mice. The rapid induction of PU already on day 1 after NTS 
injection points towards a very rapid and acute renal injury mediated by the heterologous anti-GBM 
IgG in NTS. 
To support results generated by PU measurements, albuminuria was measured as a more precise 
read-out to monitor GN. Albuminuria is a pathological condition where more than the normal 
amount of albumin is present in the urine. Albuminuria can be a sign of protein leaking through the 
kidney, most often through the glomeruli, and in humans with severe glomerular injury, the albumin 
is the most excreted protein in the urine, generally accounting for more than 50 % of the urinary 
proteins 23. The addition of albuminuria provided a sensitive read-out compared to PU, that 
provided semi-quantitative data due to the large range of concentration in each score, as e.g. score 3 
indicate a PU level between 100 and 300 mg/dL.   
The pilot as well as the confirmatory study demonstrated an increase in urine volume on day 9/10 
supporting the sign of renal dysfunction 94. 
The results of the albuminuria strongly support the hypothesis of a rapid an acute renal injury 
demonstrated both in the pilot and the confirmatory study by a significantly increased albumin 
concentration, as well as significantly increased 24 hours albumin excretion in urine both on day 2/3 
and 9/10. So far, the urinalysis of the pilot and the confirmatory study revealed a rapid increase of 
PU and albuminuria, indicating a rapid onset of renal dysfunction after NTS injection. 
Taken the results of PU, urine volume and albuminuria together, a high correlation between the 
pilot study and the confirmatory study has been demonstrated, and the results indicate that renal 
dysfunction occurred in the NTN model.  
==PAGE:65==
64 
 
Histology is an essential tool to demonstrate whether pathological damage of glomeruli is present. 
Thus, to confirm presence of renal dysfunction and to investigate if the renal dysfunction was 
connected to GN in the passive NTN model, the histopathological PAS stain was performed. The 
PAS stain illustrates tissue carbohydrates including mucosal components e.g. glycoproteins 
enabling demonstration of basal membranes and mesangial expansion. Kidney sections from 6 
representative mice from 10 and 21 days post NTN induction and control mice were PAS stained 
and evaluated by microscope to observe if any renal injury was present. The evaluation was based 
on abnormal glomeruli by looking at mesangial expansion and increased cellularity within 
glomeruli. The kidney sections from the NTN mice showed approximately 50 % and 80 % 
glomeruli affection on day 10 and 21 respectively, demonstrating that the dose of 250 µL NTS is 
able to induce GN in C57BL/6 mice. This observation was consistent with other studies that have 
observed GN 7 and 10 days post NTN induction by injection a dose of 500 and 250 µL NTS 
respectively 95,96. However, it would add more power to the analysis, if more than three mice from 
each time point had been evaluated. The glomeruli damage seemed exacerbated on day 21 
compared to on day10 indicating a continued disease progression from the disease onset. 
Furthermore, additional signs of renal dysfunction in form of tubular atrophy and dilations were 
observed in the NTN mice both on day 10 and 21. The tubular dilations seemed to be more frequent 
in the NTS group on day 21 in comparison to day 10, supporting the indication of continuing 
progression of renal pathology. The increased cellularity suggests a proliferation of glomerular cells 
which could be a sign of renal damage in accordance with literature describing glomerular cell 
proliferation as a central feature in the active glomerular injury 97,98. The tubular dilations are likely 
to be caused by protein accumulation when proteins are excreted in the glomeruli as a consequence 
of a defective filtration barrier and insufficient reabsorptive capacity. 
Finally, the NTN induction did not show any sign of affecting the welfare of the mice. The body 
weight measurements demonstrated that the NTN induction caused a maximum of 10 % BW loss, 
and furthermore, the NTN mice gained weight during the last days in the study. The humane 
endpoint of 20 % body weight loss was never reached, thus the passive NTN model is demonstrated 
as a feasible model to apply in preclinical research within the field of GN. 
All together, the results of PU, albuminuria and PAS staining indicate that a passive NTN model 
has been established with PU and albuminuria as read-outs. An important aspect in the evaluation of 
the establishment of the model is its reliability. Reliability refers to the stability, consistency and 
reproducibility of the model and ideally every study performed under the same conditions should 
==PAGE:66==
65 
 
yield similar results. From the pilot and the confirmatory study focusing on the 250 µL NTS dose, it 
can be emphasised that the passive NTN model yields reproducible responses indicating a model 
with high reliability. 
 
10.2 Characterisation of the passive NTN model 
Another important objective of this thesis was to characterise the underlying mechanisms 
responsible for the observed disease in the passive NTN model. Characterisation of a model is 
important to in order to understand what drives the disease development and thereby clarify the 
usefulness of the model and its similarity to human disease. Characterisation of an animal model 
can be infinite due to the large amount of mechanisms to investigate and to new arising discoveries, 
thus a focus area was chosen in this thesis. The very rapid onset of PU in this model indicated a 
very acute response in the passive NTN model, thus the involvement of the murine acute-phase 
reactant SAP was investigated. The SAP is utilised as an inflammatory marker in mice, whereas 
CRP is an inflammatory marker in humans. Thus SAP in mice and human CRP results can be 
somehow correlated 72. Additionally, neutrophils are suggested to play a key role in the 
pathogenesis of LN. They are the first leukocytes to arrive at a site of inflammation and they have 
the potential to coordinate every stage of inflammation from induction to resolution and tissue 
repair 19,46. Therefore the role of neutrophils was chosen as another focus area. Neutrophil 
distribution in blood and kidney was analysed by FACS upon NTS injection. Additionally, the 
involvement of the pro-inflammatory cytokine G-CSF, was evaluated due to its capability of 
recruiting neutrophils from the bone marrow into the blood 99. 
To investigate the involvement of various leukocyte subsets, in particularly neutrophils, the 
distribution of CD4+ T cells, CD8+ T cells, B cells and neutrophils in blood was analysed by 
FACS.  The total number of leukocytes on day 3 showed a significant decrease of CD4+ T cells, 
CD8+ T cells and B cells, but a significant increase of neutrophils in the NTN mice compared to the 
control mice. On day 10 the difference in T cells between the groups had vanished, but B cells were 
still found to be significantly decreased in NTN mice. There could be an agreement between the 
observed decrease in B cells in this thesis and a study of peripheral B cells in patients with LN. In 
the study by (Kolaczkowska) 100  B cell lymphopenia (abnormal low level of B cells in the blood)  
and  lowered expression of the CD19 protein on all B cells was observed in LN patients. It is 
believed that the B cell lymphopenia is caused by a plasmablastic differentiation and a tissue or a 
bone marrow homing of B cells. However it is unknown if this condition favours lupus 
==PAGE:67==
66 
 
development or if it is a consequence of a mechanism controlling B cell activation during remission 
of LN. The reason for the decrease in B cells was not further investigated in this thesis, but this 
could be another aim in the future. 
Furthermore, the neutrophil population was still significantly increased in NTN mice on day 10, but 
had decreased compared to day 3. This finding suggests a major acute neutrophil response to NTS 
injection which slowly decreases over time. In accordance with this suggestion, literature describe 
neutrophils as a major player during acute inflammation 101. Neutrophils are the first leukocytes to 
be recruited to an inflammatory site and furthermore, neutrophil accumulation in kidneys is 
observed in patients with GN 102 . 
The total leukocyte distribution found in the kidney of the NTN model showed similar pattern as the 
leukocyte distribution seen in the blood. Again a significant decrease in B cells and a significant 
increase of neutrophils was found in the kidneys of NTN mice on day 10. The T cells, macrophages 
and DC’s of the NTN mice did not show any difference from the control mice. However, since the 
kidneys in this project were isolated without prior perfusion to remove blood, it was not possible to 
distinguish between subsets residing in the kidney or those coming from the blood compartment. It 
would have been interesting to conduct an additional study using perfused kidneys to investigate the 
leukocyte subsets in the kidney by FACS. Moreover, evaluation of kidney sections by 
immunohistochemistry would be very beneficial for identification and location of specific cell types 
or immune complexes within the kidney. Immunohistochemistry is a very specific technique for in 
situ localisation of a tissue constituent by means of a specific antigen-antibody interaction, where 
the antibody is tagged with a visible marker 103. Application of this technique would enable 
distinction of cells within the glomerulus and the interstitium, which is the tissue surrounding the 
loop of Henle in the renal medulla 104. 
In summary, the reduced B cells in NTN mice post NTN induction cannot be explained currently, 
but the elevation of neutrophils indicates an acute response of neutrophils in accordance with 
literature describing the neutrophils as a key player in the acute phase of inflammation. The finding 
of the increased neutrophils directed the study towards an investigation of the involvement of the 
acute-phase reactant SAP, which is a sensitive indicator of inflammation. SAP elevation has been 
demonstrated to be associated with various stimuli, including inflammation 105.  
Furthermore, the neutrophil increase led to the investigation of the involvement of the inflammatory 
cytokine G-CSF. G-CSF participates in many inflammatory responses by its capacity to recruit 
neutrophils from the bone marrow into the blood 99. 
==PAGE:68==
67 
 
The investigation of SAP indicated an acute-phase, possibly associated with inflammation. 
It was found that SAP was significantly elevated in NTN mice 7-8 hours, 3 and 10 days post NTS 
injection. This rapid increase of SAP detected already 7-8 hours after NTN induction could be 
explained by a very rapid onset of the acute/heterologous phase demonstrated in prior study by 83 
which describes that the heterologous phase develops within minutes after NTS injection. This 
study explains that the rapid development of disease is due to glomerular deposition of antibodies 
contained in the NTS against glomerular structures. 
Furthermore, the investigation of G-CSF revealed a significant increase in NTN mice 7-8 hours, and 
10 days, but not 3 and 21 days post NTN induction. The G-CSF response of the NTN mice had 
decreased to baseline levels on day 3 and only a slightly increase was observed again on day 10. 
However, since these data do not represent measurements from the same mice measured on day 3 
and day 10, the apparent increase on day 10 could be explained by individual differences of the 
mice. The major increase of G-CSF in mice 7-8 hours post NTS injection, was approximately 20 
fold higher than the baseline of the control mice, but the G-CSF level rapidly decreased again to a 
level close to baseline on day 3. This indicates that G-CSF was rapidly induced and suggests an 
acute neutrophil-driven inflammation following NTS injection as previously suggested in the 
literature 99. In addition, other studies by (Cox and Wang)106,107 G-CSF injections in humans and 
rodents lead to an increase in neutrophils that correlate with the G-CSF concentration in serum. The 
possible correlation between G-CSF and neutrophils in the passive NTN model could be clarified 
by neutralising G-CSF in the NTN mice and see if an increase in neutrophils still thereby abrogated. 
Furthermore, the urinary G-CSF concentration in NTN mice was significantly increased on day 2/3, 
but not day 9/10 supporting the suggestion of a major acute neutrophil induction via G-CSF. 
However, the G-CSF could arise from the kidney and/or the blood as a consequence of the 
demonstrated glomeruli filtration defect shown by PU and albuminuria induction and mesangial 
expansion. Thus it is unknown if the production of G-CSF in the kidney had increased, or if the G-
CSF only passed through the kidney due to the mentioned glomerular filtration dysfunction. This 
uncertainty could be investigated by e.g. measuring G-CSF in perfused homogenised kidneys by 
ELISA. 
 
10.3 Investigation of the role of neutrophils in the passive NTN model 
The aim of the neutrophil depletion study was to investigate whether neutrophil depletion has an 
impact on NTN induction, as neutrophils were found elevated in blood and kidney. Several studies 
==PAGE:69==
68 
 
describe neutrophils to possess important regulatory mechanisms in many inflammatory conditions, 
as well as in many autoimmune diseases 43,44. Anti-Ly6G is a specific mAb directed against Ly6G 
on neutrophils, and was chosen to induce neutrophil depletion in this study because it has been 
shown to only deplete neutrophils 44. Here, mice were treated either with 1mg/mouse anti-Ly6G, 
isotype IgG2a or PBS 24 hours prior to the NTS injections, and boosted with 0,5mg/mouse on day 
3, 6 and 8. The neutrophil depletion was evaluated by FACS analysis on day 0 before NTS injection 
and 7 days post NTS. The results from day 0 showed a 50% decrease of neutrophil fraction, shown 
in percentage of CD45+ leukocytes, in the group treated with anti-Ly6G compared to the isotype or 
PBS group. However, on day 7 the neutrophil fraction was similarly increased in all the NTS 
injected groups treated either with anti-Ly6G, isotype IgG2a or PBS when compared to the non-
NTS injected control group, as observed in the peripheral leukocyte distribution in the confirmatory 
study. These results indicate that the anti-Ly6G treatment did not efficiently deplete the neutrophils 
before the NTS injection, and furthermore did not show a consistent depletion after NTN induction. 
However, the gating of neutrophils both on day 0 and 7 did not capture all neutrophils due to a too 
high FCS (See section 7.12 fig. 14). The FSC should have been lowered to capture all neutrophils.  
Nevertheless, it can be concluded that the depletion was not sufficient, although the gating on the 
neutrophils was not optimal. To investigate the effect of the neutrophil depletion on the induction of 
NTN, PU was measured during the experiment. PU was not reduced by treatment with anti-Ly6G 
mAb, as PU was comparable to the isotype and PBS control group, demonstrating that 50 % 
depletion of the neutrophil population at the time of NTN induction do not have any influence on 
the development of renal dysfunction. 
Furthermore, the SAP measurements in blood plasma on day 10 revealed that the SAP 
concentration was significantly increased in all NTS injected mice compared to the non-NTS 
injected control group. Again, partial neutrophil depletion by anti-Ly6G did not show any reduction 
in plasma SAP levels. Thus, these results underline that anti-Ly6G treatment did not affect the acute 
phase of the passive NTN model in this study. The insufficient depletion by anti-Ly6G might be 
explained by the observed elevation in G-CSF. Anti-Ly6G blocks neutrophil recruitment through a 
β2 integrin-dependent mechanism 108. However, G-CSF stimulates the cell surface expression of β2 
integrin 109. Thus, the G-CSF might counteract the blocking by anti-Ly6G. It would be interesting to 
see if a higher dose of anti-Ly6G is able to prevent the recruitment of neutrophils into the periphery.  
All together, the role of neutrophils in the initial phase of the NTN induction remains inconclusive 
as half of the neutrophils were still present following depletion, which could be sufficient to induce 
==PAGE:70==
69 
 
NTN. The role of neutrophils in NTN induction needs to be further investigated. The role of 
neutrophils could also be explored by induce NTN in a neutrophil-deficient mouse strain, such as 
the Mcl-1∆Myelo mouse strain 110 or by injection C57BL/6 mice with a higher dose of anti-Ly6G. 
 
11. Conclusion 
 
The aim of this thesis was to establish and characterise the passive NTN model as a model of 
human LN. The experiments performed in the previous work and this thesis has led to the 
conclusion that the passive NTN model could be reproducibly established using i.v. injection of 250 
µl NTS for disease induction in C57Bl/6 mice. This conclusion was supported by the induction of 
significant PU, albuminuria and mesangial expansion in model indicating renal dysfunction and 
signs of GN.  
From the mechanistic characterisation of the passive NTN model performed in this thesis it could 
be concluded that the number of peripheral neutrophils and neutrophils in the kidney were elevated 
on day 3 and 10, respectively, indicating that neutrophils might play a role in the disease 
pathogenesis in the passive NTN model and in GN development in general. Furthermore, the 
investigation of the acute-phase protein SAP was found in plasma at 7-8 hours, and peaking at day 3 
post NTN induction, demonstrating an acute inflammatory response in the passive NTN model as a 
response to the NTS injection. This conclusion was further supported  by an increased concentration 
of G-CSF in plasma found 7-8 hours post NTN induction as part of the acute inflammatory 
response which could be involved in the observed increase in neutrophils, however, this correlation 
needs to be investigated further. Finally, the role of neutrophils was investigated in the passive NTN 
model using a depletion method. Unfortunately, the conclusion from this study was challenged 
since only half of all neutrophils were depleted on the day of NTN induction, thereby not allowing 
strong conclusions. However, it was shown that partial depletion of neutrophils did not lead to 
reduced PU or to reductions in the SAP response. Taken together the results presented in this thesis 
demonstrate the establishment of the passive NTN model and confirms that the model reproducibly 
develops renal dysfunction and GN possibly mediated by neutrophils, following an acute 
inflammatory phase.  
Overall, the GN in the passive NTN model resembles aspects of human LN, and therefore might be 
an applicable model in preclinical research to investigate new therapeutic strategies for LN patients 
or by helping to identify potential new drug targets or biomarkers.

12. Perspectives 
The establishment of a passive NTN model has been confirmed by the results of PU, albuminuria 
and mesangial expansion. However the model has not yet been fully characterised as this is also a 
very time-consuming process. It could be shown that the model is characterised by an acute immune 
response and a rapid recruitment of neutrophils into the blood. The role of the neutrophils needs to 
be clarified in order to evaluate neutrophil targeting as a possible drug candidate in the treatment of 
LN. Additionally, blocking of G-CSF would be beneficial to investigate if G-CSF recruits 
neutrophils and how blocking of G-CSF affects the NTN induction in the passive NTN model. 
Furthermore, additional histopathological studies should be conducted as histology is an efficient 
tool to demonstrate renal injury, and supplementary immunohistochemistry studies could 
investigate the presence or lack of specific cell types or deposition of immune complexes, including 
IgG deposition within the glomeruli. 
This thesis has provided insight into the acute phase of GN, thus it would be interesting to look 
more into the chronic phase by conducting studies with a longer duration time in order to 
investigate the autologous phase (chronic) in passive NTN model, whereby the underlying 
mechanisms of progressed GN can be investigated. 
These findings could give an improved insight into the pathogenesis of LN and give the possibility 
to apply the passive NTN model in preclinical research to identify potential targets, identify new 
drug candidates or biomarkers, as well as to further explore the mechanism of action of a certain 
drug candidate.
