Abstract
Cities are transitioning towards more sustainable living, and one of the major focus ar­
eas is transportation. Notably, vehicle traffic accounts for air pollution, accidents, and a
variety of other externalities. The congestion (or road) pricing policies are a frequently
debated but not yet widely adopted measure to reduce car traffic. This study’s major goal
is to demonstrate the ability to better develop, test and analyse policies on road pricing
schemes. By developing and expanding cutting­edge simulation frameworks via incor­
porating and assessing congestion pricing algorithms, this study brings novel dynamic
congestion pricing control formulations closer to practice. Particularly, two frameworks
were developed, on top of the SimMobility simulator, where the first uses a Bayesian
optimisation algorithm considering average social welfare at the equilibrium state of a
system, and the second utilises the day as a context variable for Bayesian optimisation
and targets the overall system ­ algorithm equilibrium. These frameworks were simulated
and tested under different settings, using SimMobility’s prototypical city environment. It
was found that both frameworks were able to identify a similar optimum toll profile, with
the second framework being more computationally efficient. Nonetheless, it was seen
that the number of iterations, the increasing search space and dimensionality, as well
as relevant, manually set, control variables, could limit their performance and credibility.
Lastly, the capabilities in terms of disaggregated and aggregated analysis provided from
the incorporation of SimMobility in the frameworks emerged during the comparison of the
base case with the optimum toll case. There, it was showcased how the outcomes of
the selected policy in terms of network, zone and individual traveller performance can be
analysed and compared in depth. Therefore, designated congestion pricing policies can
be better evaluated before their implementation.

Introduction
Transport accounts for over a quarter of Europe’s greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions and
is the leading driver of urban air pollution. Based on European Commission’s findings [1]
the transport sector is not presenting a similar decrease in emissions as others, with the
first decline in emissions having started in 2006 but still levels have not reached 1990’s
levels. In the centre of it are road transport emissions, generating more than 70 % of all
GHG emissions from transportation in 2014. On top of that, domestic transport emissions
in the European Union (EU) climbed gradually between 2013 and 2019 as passenger
and inland freight volumes increased. Even though COVID­19 reduced that tendency,
forecasts for the coming year reveal that if no more initiatives are made, an increase
may be observed until 2025, while based on the following predicted reductions, transport
emissions in 2030 will still be around 9% higher than in 1990 [2].
Internationally, different policies and incentives have been decided, while at the EU level,
the target is to cut GHG emissions from transportation by 90% by 2050. An essential
objective is to internalise external transportation expenses so that individuals, who utilise
transportation, assume the entire cost [3]. A strategy that has gained attention both in the
literature and in practice, is congestion (or road) pricing and can be used to internalise
traffic external costs at the city level or even beyond [4]. In chapter 2, various settings,
concerns, and pertinent research findings are presented, while an overview of price opti­
misation strategies and transport network modelling approaches is also provided.
This study aims to develop approaches for optimal design and evaluation of congestion
pricing policies. Following the available research and leveraging the current knowledge,
the study brings emerging dynamic congestion pricing control formulations closer to prac­
tice, by developing and extending state­of­the­art simulation frameworks. Two frame­
works were developed based on the working paper of Liu et al. [5], where similar formula­
tions were tested on a Macroscopic Fundamental Diagram (MFD) model for the morning
commute problem. However, this study uses a different simulation model which provokes
higher complexity and differentiation in concepts but provides the possibility for thorough
evaluation and analysis of policies. The study also extends the optimal toll query to an
all­day problem with multiple trip purposes.
Specifically for the developed frameworks, initially, a model­free Bayesian optimisation
(BO) formulation and a comprehensive agent­based mobility simulator were combined,
targeting to identify the best congestion price scheme, by improving the system’s social
welfare (SW). In the same vein, a second framework with contextual Bayesian optimisa­
tion (CBO), which incorporates the day as a context variable, was also applied. With the
usage of an experimental design, the performance of both frameworks was analysed and
compared to each other. As for the frameworks’ further goals, both aim to resolve a high
dimensional toll specification problem with the fewest amount of iterations considering
that large­scale simulators are highly complex and computationally intensive.
Lastly, regarding this research’s content, firstly an extensive literature review is presented
in chapter 2, followed by background information for the simulator and a detailed descrip­
tion of the developed frameworks, in chapter 3 and chapter 4 respectively. Furthermore,
experiments were designed for the verification and evaluation of the methodology. The
results and findings are presented in chapter 5, while the conclusions can be found in
chapter 6.

Literature review
2.1
Modelling and simulation
Modelling and simulation are used in a variety of fields, ranging from social sciences
to operations research [6], [7]. One significant advantage of simulation modelling is its
ability to model the entire system and its complex interrelationships. Consequently, the
model could expose the interactions among the system’s components. Considering that
real­world systems are mostly too complex to represent analytically, simulation­based
modelling can provide an easy way to replicate a system and enable the exploration of its
behaviour, dynamics, and stability under various conditions [6], [8].
In transportation science, simulation and transport models are widely used to forecast
the impact of various transportation scenarios and policies. These could be relevant to
fluctuations in external factors such as the economy, land use, and demography, or to
changes within the system such as new public transportation lines, new infrastructure,
and congestion pricing control [9], [10]. Also, they can be used for assessing strategic
decisions, such as the selection of a port over another [11] or for analysing traffic bot­
tleneck phenomena [12]. Traditionally, trip generation, trip distribution, modal split, and
network assignment constitute the main four elements of transportation models [9], [13].
On top of that, transport models are used to be classified based on the level of aggrega­
tion of those components, with microscopic, mesoscopic, and macroscopic models being
the main identifications [14].
In contrast to the traditional modelling approach, which is trip­based focused, the evolution
of activity­based models has radically transformed it by implying a tour­based design. As a
result, the aforementioned modelling steps have also been transformed accordingly [15].
It has been shown that the tour­based models can outperform the traditional four­step
models in several performance indicators, and thus provide a more realistic representation
of the system [16]. Also, it is important that the activity­based approach can account for a
considerably higher flexible travel­choice behaviour, including for instance the possibility
of an individual not making a trip or the aptitude of chaining multiple activities in the same
tour [17].
Noteworthy is that a relatively new approach, which has gained attention over the years
and is strongly connected with the activity­based paradigm, is agent­based modelling and
simulation (ABM). The possibility of individual­based simulation, which encompasses the
thorough modelling of systems with high complexity, and the increasing computational
power capacity on recent infrastructures, which allows for faster aggregation of results,
might contribute to ABM’s recognisability [7]. In general, there is no official definition of
an agent since it might relate to different system components while analysing various
aims. Many might regard an agent to be any distinguishing portion of a program (e.g.,
model, platform, or module) or any form of autonomous entity (e.g., institution, company,
or unique individuals) [18]. Despite this, the majority of agent­based models presented
in transportation science are individual­based, with agents acting as individual travellers
[19].
There are several examples of integration of agent­based with activity­based models such
as Tel Aviv’s activity­based model and the MATSim agent­based model [20], the SimMo­
bility simulator [21] and the combination of GTAModel and Nexus [22]. Nevertheless, it

Last but not least, for a more macroscopic and aggregated behaviour of the system the

2.2
Congestion pricing
Traffic congestion has become a major issue across the world, with numerous strategies
and solutions having been explored to address it and its associated repercussions, which
include increased air pollution, accidents, and strong negative impacts on the overall city’s
quality of life [4], [26]. Congestion pricing control constitutes one of the main traffic man­
agement measures which have been researched a lot but not widely applied during the
past decades [26], [27]. However, certain implementations have produced positive re­
sults, such as the Stockholm example, where congestion charging received widespread
public support [28], and the Milan case, where a considerable improvement in air quality
was observed [29].
As initially stated by Pigou [30], congestion pricing serves as a technique for internalising
the cost of externalities caused by road users. Since then, many first­best pricing ap­
proaches have been developed. In those settings the system optimum (SO) is meant to
be identified by maximising social welfare (SW), considering that no fixed cost or external
effects are in place [31], [32]. Complementary to congestion pricing theory, the concept of
Wardrop equilibrium as well as William Vickey’s landmark studies on dynamic congestion
were vital moments for the evolution of congestion pricing research [33].
A more realistic approach, instead of first­best pricing, is based on the second­best con­
gestion pricing criteria. Under those conditions, some internal and/or external system
abnormalities have been identified. Government budget constraints, the inability to im­
plement pricing in road sections, technological limitations in tolling applications, and gen­
eral economic sector consequences are some of the complications that have been widely
discussed in the literature [34], [35]. On top of these, it has been discussed that road
pricing should not only maximise a system’s short­run performance but should also target
variables for obtaining the optimal long­run behaviour [36].
Finally, it is worth pointing out that the modelling of urban road pricing in practice revealed
a significant difference between an idealised method and what can be practically applied
in large­scale network simulators due to city obstacles and limits [37]. Thus, the modelling
of congestion pricing with the usage of high­complexity large­scale simulators and their
high required simulation time still constitutes a very challenging research stream.
2.3
Tolling schemes
In general, various tolling schemes, that are mainly linked with the second­best approach,
have been researched and implemented throughout the years. Regarding the classifica­
tion of tolls, this occurs according to numerous factors. These can be the kind of scheme
(e.g., facility­based, area­based, or distance­based), the level to which charges fluctuate
across periods, additional characteristics of toll variation, and equipment [27]. The main
ones identified in the literature are presented below.

• The distance­based tolling is when the charges are related to a travelled distance
in the respective facilities. The specific framework is very flexible as it can cover a
complete area of a network, specific routes, or even be part of a high­occupancy
toll (HOT) lane setting. The toll may be calculated based on a linear or quadratic
function [27]. The implementation of various tolling zones, where methods for the
design of zones and their associated impacts have been researched in [38] and [39],
is a significant factor that greatly influences the effectiveness of this policy.
• Toll cordons are a type of pricing system wherein vehicles pay a toll to enter a cordon
in either the incoming or outgoing direction or even both ways. The City of Stockholm
was an example of such a congestion pricing cordon­based policy in action [27], [28].
• Area­based systems can be viewed as a more flexible generalisation of a cordon­
based scheme, where vehicles pay to move within an area without necessarily
crossing its limits [27]. In 2003, London implemented this strategy for taxing au­
tomobiles in the city centre and a reduction in traffic delays was achieved [40].
• Facility­based tolls on roads, highways and other infrastructure have been imposed,
and are by far the most popular method of road pricing, albeit tolls targeted to price
congestion have only been put on a few cases [27].
Ultimately, tolls can include a time dimension, in which they fluctuate over periods, and/or
additional settings such as varied costs depending on the kind of vehicle or discounts for
pre­purchases and monthly/yearly passes [27], [41].
2.4
Pricing optimisation
As previously discussed, optimum congestion pricing control seems to be a very com­
plex problem since decisions on system representation, pricing schemes, and methods
for determining the optimal price can vary markedly. The most typical price optimisation
approaches are either with nonlinear programming or a bi­level optimisation setup, both
of which entail a high degree of complexity (NP­hard). Moreover, because of the practical
implications they face, the first­best solution is generally used as a benchmark in many
studies [35], [34]. As a result, for the second­best prices, which are closer to practice,
numerous approaches have been developed, with the most commonly used methods for
calculating prices being heuristics, meta­heuristics, approximations, and trial and error.
For instance, in [35], a concentric model with a combination of a heuristic grid search
method is used for calculating a second­best optimum price for cordon and flat­distance
rate setting. Similarly, in [42] heuristic algorithms were used to answer second­best con­
gestion pricing problems. Additionally, in a cordon­based scenario, solution algorithms
for determining the optimum tolling pricing as well as the placement of the cordon were
examined by Luo et al. [43].
In [34], a dynamic network simulator that treated mode choice and departure times en­
dogenously was used to test and compare six different link­based tolling settings. After
fifty iterations, the stationary was able to be attained, and for the calculation of toll pricing,
several numerical optimisation or analytical methodologies were employed to approxi­
mate the optimum for the representative response surfaces in each scenario. Gu and
Saberi’s [44] research placed a stronger emphasis on departure time with the creation
of a simulation­based optimisation (SBO) method for optimum distance­based pricing,
paired with a large­scale agent­based traffic assignment model that assessed scenarios
with and without departure time rescheduling.
An important aspect of congestion pricing that has been studied is the day­to­day traffic

proceeds in conjunction with the iterative process from the initial state to system equi­

Bayesian optimisation (BO) is a comparatively recent pricing optimisation approach.
Since the pricing problem is NP­hard and does not have simple analytical solutions,
black­box optimisation approaches are often used, and the BO has been demonstrated
to be a very promising strategy. First, Zhong et al. [50] presented a BO technique for

and land usage were investigated. Particularly, an integrated transport assignment and
land usage model was employed in the study and was combined with an active learning
algorithm that featured the multivariate­multiobjective BO method. In the same vein, Liu,
Jiang, and Azevedo [51] investigated a BO approach combining a trip­based MFD model
with distance­based area­based pricing schemes. The toll profile was created based on
time­of­day pricing, with the dimensionality impact on the BO under question. As a result,
it was numerically discovered that as the search space expands, performance decreases

As for BO’s suitability, it can be claimed that because of its ability to balance exploita­

by the integration of problem­specific information within the BO design. More specifically,
the incorporation of problem­specific information in the covariance function increased the
accuracy of the surrogate model. That opens a discussion on the usage of low­level
transport models to reinforce the computational performance of demanding solutions for
large­scale detailed simulation­based transport optimisation problems.
Lastly, another data­driven approach, which has also been researched, utilises reinforce­
ment learning (RL) algorithms for answering optimal price control problems. Specifically,
the morning commute problem has been addressed by Sato, Seo, and Fuse [53], where
the RL algorithm utilises the output of a small­scale network model to update the predic­
tions. Similarly, Nguyen et al. [54] propose an RL technique for a nonlinear congestion
pricing model that considers manual and automatic toll lanes based on wireless commu­
nication technologies. Contrarily, Wang, Jin, and Zheng [55] place the RL algorithm under
the spectrum of cooperative traffic decisions to achieve the best reward for the system.

3.1.2
Interaction with toll prices
Given that the study’s major goal is to use the congestion pricing formulation to minimize
congestion and improve the system’s overall performance, it is reasonable to provide
further information on how tolls are integrated into SimMobility’s design.
Firstly, tolls convey an impact on the pre­day model. The pre­day model is based on the
Day Activity Schedule (DAS) technique and is constructed by using a system of intercon­
nected discrete choice models with an emphasis on options associated with daily activity
and mobility. The toll prices in that set of models can have an impact on models that are
responsible for the mode and destination choices for various trip types, in the intermediate
stop models, as well as on models that account for the time of day the trip is made [56].
In addition to the pre­day model, the within­day simulation is also impacted by tolls. The
within­day simulator models several passenger decision­making processes both before
and during trips. More specifically, the toll affects the private traffic pre­trip behaviour
models, which are related to the route choice and departure time [56]. Those relationships
are the ones that would be analysed in depth for the purpose of this study.
3.1.3
Learning process
The MT model provides two learning options. Using these along with running the simula­
tors iteratively, could allow the system to reach equilibrium.
The first learning approach is known as within­day learning. More specifically, an activity
schedule is initially given and the default travel time is derived for each link. That happens
by adding up the default travel times, computed based on the length and the free flow
speed, of the link segments. Each time a within­day simulation is conducted, the link
travel times experienced by each vehicle are recorded in an output file. Then, based on
the new experienced link travel times, the corresponding table in the database is updated
by a store procedure. Lastly, a similar update process applies to public transit, but this
was outside the scope of this research [56].
On top of that, the agents’ transport mode selection and their relevant travel information
can also be updated. In this case, DAS is generated by the pre­day simulator and used
as input to the within­day one. By iterating the pre­day, agents produce new plans based
on their experiences, which consequently allows a learning process. This is enabled by
updating the travel times aggregated at the zonal level and the relevant information. This
process is referred to as day­to­day learning (see fig. 3.1a) [21], [57].
Hence, by combining all the aforementioned, the MT simulator could be used in different
settings and run for numerous iterations until an equilibrium has been met. For this re­
search’s experiments, the DAS file was considered fixed and, thus, only the within­day

Previous studies
Until now, SimMobility has only been used in one thesis to evaluate congestion pricing.

BO is an optimisation method, where the main components are a model of the objective
function and an acquisition function. More specifically, it develops a surrogate for the
objective and quantifies the uncertainty in that by utilising a GP regression, fitting the
data, and then selecting the next sampling point based on the acquisition function [63]. In
this case, the objective function is the value of SW which aims to be maximised globally,
whereas the acquisition function predicts the toll variables for the next tolling rates to be
simulated.

4.2.4
Exploration and exploitation
As mentioned in the acquisition function, the exploration and exploitation trade­off is an
important part of the overall process as it defines the convergence speed of the optimisa­
tion as well as the possibility of converging to local or global extrema.
In general, the UCB becomes an entirely exploitative technique when the factor ρ = 0,
where the solution with the best­projected mean gets very high expectations. Thus the
process may converge too rapidly to a local maximum. On the contrary, the functions can
be fully exploratory when the ρ takes big prices and evaluates the location with the largest
posterior uncertainty. As a result, it may eventually reach the global optima, while the rate
of convergence may be slow [66]. In general, ρ = 2 can be chosen as the default factor
to establish an adequate balance between those two areas.
4.2.5
Initial sampling
An initial set of points is essential for enhanced performance and the initialization of the
BO approach. As a result, a space­filling experiment design can be employed to pro­
vide appropriate starting data to be used during the BO, covering the search area. Both
frameworks employ this technique.
The selected method for randomly sampling parameter values is the Latin hypercube sam­
pling (LHS). It is extensively used in Monte Carlo simulation because it may significantly
minimise the number of runs required to obtain a relatively accurate result. In other words,
when only a handful of the input variables dominate the output, there is a significant ben­
efit in the LHS. This strategy assures that each of those components is represented in a
completely stratified manner, regardless of whether components are crucial [67].

5.2.5
Conclusion
Ultimately, it has been found that both frameworks can indeed achieve the imposition of a
levy that raises the SW of the systems. The fact that both frameworks reached the same
toll profile is a clear sign that the global optimum, within the respective constraint, has
been discovered. Nevertheless, it is impossible to calculate the optimum toll analytically
due to the system’s nature and consequently compare it with the result.
In terms of performance, framework 2 can be seen to significantly outperform framework 1,
even in ’fair’ conditions. A major difference arises from the structure of the two frameworks
where the equilibrium process per toll needed for framework 1 adds a significant number
of iterations to the sample data. That can be overcome in framework 2, where the overall
equilibrium process (system & GP) allows much fewer iterations.
In other directions, the improvement from the implementation of the selected toll was
showcased. It was seen that the travellers have changed their behaviour by departing
earlier in the morning to avoid a higher toll price. Overall, the system has an increase
of 5,5% in terms of SW and the average paid toll was 3.31 $. Moreover, the most gains
in travel time have been identified in the southeast part of the city, while the bigger CS
losses were presented in the northern region. Furthermore, the link densities during the
peak hour have been improved. A shortcoming of the toll was also identified, as travellers
departing after the peak hour faced a high toll charge and added scheduled delay. Con­
sidering all the above, a deeper comprehension and analysis of the congestion pricing
policy has been attained.
Lastly, it should be noted that in this case, both frameworks cannot explore further a
potentially better solution due to the existence of an UB on the tolls paid as well as the
algorithms’ bounds. Furthermore, a similar toll profile might be discovered, which could
further improve SW, if the search space is defined closer to the proposed variables. The
following experiments will focus on alleviating these issues.

During this research two frameworks combining a detailed agent­based simulator, namely
SimMobility, and machine­learning­based optimisation approaches (BO and CBO), were
developed and tested. Their main goal was to determine the optimum congestion pricing
policy of a 24­hour day by optimising the system’s SW. To begin with, as presented in
experiment 1, both frameworks were able to pinpoint the optimum distance­based toll
design with UB at $10. Nevertheless, in terms of performance, the second framework
proved that needs much fewer iterations. This is of paramount importance considering
the required computing power SimMobility and similar simulators need.
As for the importance of SimMobility’s integration to the frameworks, the advantages of
the analysis of a congestion pricing policy through a detailed agent­based simulation were
also showcased in experiment 1. The selected policy was analysed in depth and interest­
ing findings were presented. First, the policy was able to improve the SW of the system,
as well as the TTI during the peak period. Also, by diving into more disaggregated met­
rics, the improvements in link­level density, during the peak period, were presented. In
the same vein, by using a zonal level analysis, it was possible to identify the regions with
bigger travel time gains and CS losses per traveller, which were the southeast and north­
ern parts of the city respectively. Thus, the ability for in­depth analysis of the proposed
tolling scheme, enabled by the simulator, can reinforce the planning of better conges­
tion pricing policies. All things considered, experiment 1 showcased how the developed
frameworks can design and assess congestion pricing policies, but also indicated their
associated capabilities and challenges.
Moreover, as presented in experiment 2, framework 2 could not find a pure distance­
based toll that improves the SW in this environment setup. That was confirmed during
trial 1, while an interesting limitation of the framework during trial 2 was attained. This is
connected with the framework’s inability to identify a solution when the amplitude’s search
space significantly decreased. This may be due to the relatively small number of trial’s
iterations and/or the inherent variability of the system. In this sense, framework 2 can be
effective for pinpointing the best toll region quickly and efficiently in a large search space,
but it becomes more difficult for it to recognize what different impacts similar tolls may
convey on SW.
Finally, framework 2 in experiment 3 could not locate the same solution or discover a
better toll profile than in experiment 1. The expansion of the size and dimensions of the
search space could be the reason for this. Thus, a higher number of iterations or a higher
acquisition weight may be necessary to resolve this issue.
6.2
Limitations
To begin with, the use of the virtual city constitutes a limitation of the current research as
it is not a real­world system and can consequently impose relevant shortcomings. One is
related to the reduced network capacity needed as well as the low number of travellers
outside the peak hours. These can cause issues with the framework tests. For instance,
the high toll setting outside the peak hours in experiment 3 could have been prevented if
travellers were more dispersed during the day.

On top of that, the high simulation time required for SimMobility limits the number of ex­
periments and trials that can be done. Although this research is focused on alleviating
this issue for the congestion pricing problem, the time needed for the full development
and testing of solutions is still very high, and hence the need for further development is
also necessary.
6.3
Future developments
Based on the findings and limitations of this research, some main interesting directions for
further development were identified. Specifically, regarding the developed frameworks, it
would make sense to further verify and test them for a real­world city environment. In
addition, the pre­day simulator, which was included in the experimental design of this
study, should be included in the simulation. This would bring the frameworks closer to a
real­world setup, where travellers could change their DAS preferences.
In other research directions, it would be also useful to test the performance of the frame­
works in various acquisition functions, as they may improve their performance. In addi­
tion, another intriguing area of research might be the balance between exploitation and
exploration, where different acquisition weight settings could be explored. In particular,
for framework 2, a dynamic setting where the CBO becomes more exploitative over days
can improve the framework’s performance. Additionally, for framework 2, configuring the
scaling parameter in RSW for the change of day (a) requires additional testing, as this
might have an immediate effect on the framework’s performance.
Lastly, it would be interesting to add and test different toll settings by adjusting the ex­
isting frameworks. In this research, the two toll schemes tested were a distance­based
toll with an UB and one without. Beyond these, an interesting set­up could be a more
comprehensive mixed fixed distance­based toll with ranging lower and upper limits. Like­
wise, introducing diverse toll zones into the system may be crucial in light of how the toll
of experiment 1 affected various regions in different ways.
