Abstract
In the quest of de-carbonizing society, the demand for sustainable energy is rapidly in-
creasing. Offshore wind power has a vast potential and is becoming a major source of
green electricity. Green hydrogen(Power to X,PtX) produced by wind power, is promis-
ing solution to de-carbonize energy sectors other than the power system.
In the 2030s an energy island will be built in the North Sea which will act as a collector
hub for several GWs of wind power. PtX production on the energy island is possibility.
Such an offshore construction is an untried concept and many questions are unanswered
regarding its operation. One question is how such an offshore grid handles a sudden
disconnection to the onshore grid.
To answer this question a model of an energy island has been built with grid forming
wind turbines. With grid forming the control the wind turbines are able emulate the
behavior of an synchronous generator with the benefit of being unrestricted of physi-
cal constraints. Emulated inertia and virtual admittance can thus be changed online.
These properties have been investigated during an transition from island operation to
grid connected mode.
It was found that that reducing the inertia constant had a highly stabilizing effect on
the re-synchronization. Reduction of the virtual admittance resulted in reduced oscil-
lation but had the adverse effect of limiting power transfer capability which resulted in
unstability.

1 Introduction
1.1
Background
At the 2015 United Nations Climate Change Conference(COP 21) 196 parties signed an
agreement to limit global warming to 1.5◦C above pre-industrial levels[19]. To meet the
agreement many nations have deployed ambitious goals towards a carbon neutral society.
The European Union, with its 27 member states, has responded to the COP 21 by being
carbon neutral by 2050[7]. En route to the 2050 net zero target the European Commis-
sion’s(EC) has furthermore made a 2030 commitment to reduce greenhouse emissions
with 55 % relative to 1990 levels[21].
These ambitious goals require substantial decarbonization across energy sectors. In Fig-
ure 1.1 a pie chart of the global CO2 emissions by sector is shown. The largest CO2
emitter is the power system with a share of 38 %. In 2019 fossil fuel based power plants,
i.e. coal, natural gas and oil, produced 63% of the total power production[2].
It is expected that energy from renewable sources, such as wind, solar, hydro, and
biomass will be able to supply the majority of the energy demand. The International
Renewable Energy Agency(IRENA) estimates that the share of renewable energy needs
to cover 65 % of the global energy consumption in 2050[4]. In 2018 the renewable energy
share covered 18% of the global consumption[4].
Namely wind and solar power are excepted to become the dominant sources of renewable
energy. In the power system alone, IRENA forecasts that by 2050 wind and solar power
will cover 60% of the global energy demand[4]. On a shorter time base the International
Energy Agency forecasts that by 2026 large energy consumers such as China, the United
States and Europe share of wind and solar power will account for more than 20% of the
electricity production[3]. Such large integration of renewable energy into the power sys-
tem can lead to decarbonization of other energy sectors as well. Through electrification,
sectors such as district heating and cooling plants along with buildings can be heated
with heat pumps and cars and other forms of passenger transportation can be converted
to electric vehicles. This is known as sector coupling. In total around 30 % of the sectors
shown in Figure 1.1 can be directly electrified[4].
However the remaining part, mainly industry and freight transport, currently have no

Another major challenge is the integration of large amount of variable renewable en-
ergy(VRE) into the power system. In order to maintain a stable frequency a power
system must always be able balance generation with the load by continuously changing
the generators’ power output. Due to the stochastic nature of wind and solar energy
VRE generators cannot be dispatched at will like conventional power plants. As electric-
ity production is planned ahead of time weather forecasts are needed to schedule VRE
production. Due to the uncertainty of forecasts actual VRE production may therefore
be less or more than scheduled. Less VRE production than scheduled can pose a risk to
the security of supply if not enough reserve production is available. If VRE production
is larger than scheduled the VRE generators need to curtail production, which can be
regarded as spilling free energy.

1.2
Motivation for Green Hydrogen and Power to X
The problem of decarbonizing the hard-to-electrify energy sector and the challenge of
fluctuating VRE may be solved by the production of hydrogen from renewable sources.
So called green hydrogen is produced through electrolysis where electricity from renew-
able sources(green energy) is used to split water into hydrogen and oxygen. The potential
use for green hydrogen is very broad. Green hydrogen can be used directly in industry
or fuel cells applications or can be converted to other energy forms known as Power to
X(PtX). Electrofuels such as e-methanol, e-jet fuel and e-diesel are examples of PtX
where green hydrogen is chemically combined with carbon from non fossil sources[23].
Green ammonia is another simpler PtX, because it is carbon free, and can be used both
as fuel and for industrial purposes. In 2016 ammonia along with the production of poly-
mers and resins accounted for 65 % of the global hydrogen demand[4]. The remaining
35% is used in other industry[4]. Considering that 96% of hydrogen is produced from
fossil sources[4], the potential for decarbonization the hydrogen industry is immense.
Green hydrogen can also add flexibility to the power system. Because it is much cheaper
to store gas than electricity excess VRE production can be used for hydrogen produc-
tion[20]. This stored hydrogen can then be converted back to electricity and be used for
balancing in periods where VRE cannot meet the demand[20].
The realization of large scale green hydrogen depends heavily on driving costs down.
The Levelized Cost of Hydrogen(LCOH) from VRE sources needs to be competitive
with (grey) hydrogen from fossil sources. LCOH from VRE sources is determined by the
cost of electricity, electrolyser technology and electrolyser placement[20].
1.3
Green Hydrogen and Offshore Wind Power
The promising potential of a green hydrogen industry have been recognized by global
actors. It is estimated that by 2050 green hydrogen could be consuming up to 18 %
of the global energy demand[4].
In 2020 the world only had 0.3 GW of electrolyser
capacity installed[3]. Already by 2026 18 GW of electrolyser capacity is scheduled to be
commissioned[3]. In October 2021 global announced electrolyser projects reached 260
GW[3]. To power these electrolysers The IEA estimates that an additional 475 GW of
VRE could be commissioned[3].
The European Union is the global leader in planned electrolyser capacity[3]. the EC has
agreed on a capacity target of 40 GW installed green hydrogen production by 2030[22].
4
==PAGE:10==
This will require substantial corresponding VRE. Offshore wind energy is expected to
become a major European source of VRE. By 2050 the EC targets 300 GW of installed
offshore wind power[8]. Owing to its vast wind resource The North Sea is expected to
become a major contributor. The Danish part of the North Sea alone has a wind re-
source of 40 GW[12]. In order to feasibly integrate this vast amount of power the Danish
government has decided to build a 3 GW energy island in the North Sea(NSWPH) by the
2030s which will act as a power hub, collecting North Sea wind power and transporting
it to shore[9]. A 2 GW power hub will also be built in the Baltic where the Danish island
Bornholm will act as a base. Green hydrogen production and PtX will be a part of the
concepts[9]. The exact configuration of the NSWPH is not yet decided but one suggested
solution is to place the electrolysers offshore on the energy island. Alternative placements
could be onshore or in the wind turbines themselves. In a techno-economic analysis of
the three mentioned configurations researchers found that the island placement yielded
the lowest LCOH, in a scenario where hydrogen production was prioritized[20]. In the
opposite case, where electricity production is prioritized and hydrogen is produced from
excess wind power, researchers found that the cost of electricity could be reduced by
13% with island hydrogen production[20].
The concept of not only using wind energy to feed the power system but also electrol-
ysers is new territory for the wind industry. Siemens Gamesa, a global wind turbine
manufacturer, has recognized this potential and have already made a pilot project in
Denmark where an onshore 3 MW wind turbine powers an electrolyser, disconnected,
also known as islanded, from the grid[16]. However, green hydrogen production with
offshore wind farms are still years away from being realized and much modelling work
is needed beforehand. That is why Siemens Gamesa, as an external collaborator, has
jointly initiated this project, which is to investigate the operation of an offshore wind
farm connected to an energy island with hydrogen production. A hydrogen prioritized
scenario will be assumed, where the role of the grid connection is to absorb excess wind
power not consumed by the electrolyser.

rounding wind farms feed the island with power which is exported to multiple countries.
The island hub will modular so additional wind farms and countries can be connected.

1.5
Motivation
As the energy islands are new concepts new challenges also arises. These challenges
involve both the onshore integration of the massive amount of VRE and the operation
of the energy island itself.
Historically the power system has been powered by syn-
chronous generators(SGs) with large spinning masses(inertia) which acts as a stabilizer
during frequency events. VRE generators are different from SGs because they are grid
connected through power electronics(PE) which decouple their inertia from the power
system. Another difference is their short circuit(SC) rating. Where SGs can offer SC
currents many times its nominal value, PE based VRE cannot not.
The European
Network of Transmission System Operators for Electricity(ENTSO-E) regard the two
aforementioned properties as a great concerns in VRE dominated power system[15].
The operation of the island will also face several challenges. One is the operation of the
multi terminal, possibly multi vendor, HVDC system[10]. Another challenge is the role
of the wind farms. Here the multi-vendor question also applies. Another question is how
6
==PAGE:12==
to form the island AC grid and how to integrate the electrolyser plant. There are two
primary control objectives for the island AC grid:
• Active power/frequency control.
• Voltage/reactive power control.
Active Power and Frequency Control
Active power and frequency control depends on the operation mode of the island. A
nominal mode would be where the wind turbines are in Maximum Power Point Track-
ing(MPPT) mode, thus producing all available wind power, and feed it to the electrolyser
and grid connection. In this mode a constant 50 Hz frequency could be imposed, set by
one of the island HVDC converters which would effectively form the island AC grid. The
wind turbines would then synchronize to this frequency and be in grid following mode.
This decoupling of active power and frequency would however pose a problem if the grid
connection would trip. The energy island would then be in an islanded operation mode.
Two challenges arises: Who should form the island AC grid and set the frequency? and
how should the wind farms power the electrolyser load?
As no grid former would be present the wind turbines cannot be in grid following mode.
Their control structure would have to change to a grid forming(GF) to sustain the load.
In essense a GF converter acts as virtual synchronous generator(VSG) with emulated
inertia and impedance equivalent to a traditional SG[18]. GF converters are however a
fairly new concept and only limited practical experience have been made[18]. Further-
more the literature on GF wind turbines in island mode is sparse.
The question about how to power the electrolyser depends on the electrolyser’s ability
to consume the instantaneous wind power and the electrolyser’s power rating. If the
power rating matches the wind farm’s rating and the electrolyser is able to perfectly
follow the wind power, the wind turbines could impose a constant 50 Hz frequency and
be in MPPT mode. Due to the high variability of wind speed it is unlikely that the
electrolyser plant will be able to follow the the wind perfectly. Secondly fluctuating
power is never desirable for reliable operation of loaded equipment. Additionally if the
wind farms power rating is not matched by the electrolyser or if a full or partial load
trip would occur, MPPT mode and constant 50 Hz is not an option. A better option is
keep the wind turbines at near constant power output, thus spilling some of the wind
power, and let a droop controller handle load variations through changes in frequency.

Voltage / Reactive Power
Control of voltage and reactive power are closely linked. Voltage should be kept close
to nominal value at all busses. Using Danish grid code as a guideline a tolerance of
± 10 % of nominal voltage is accepted[11]. As offshore wind farms are comprises of
many km’s of both collector and transmission cables, a lot of capacitive reactive power
is generated. This requires compensation in order to avoid over voltage and is handled by
shunt reactors. Time varying reactive power due fluctuating load currents also has to be
regulated and is a task for the wind turbines, island HVDC converters and tap-changing
transformers. As modern wind turbines and HVDC converters are predominantly of the
Voltage Source Type(VSC), independent voltage or reactive power control is possible
and completely defined by the control system[1].
1.6
Objective of Report
Based on the above outlined challenges the scope of this report will be defined. The main
objective will be to investigate a sudden trip of the grid connection and subsequent re-
synchronization. As emulated inertia and virtual impedance can be changed online it
would be of interest to analyse this unique property on a grid re-synchronization.
• Which control parameters allows for stable operation in both grid connected and
island mode?
• How does inertia effect the transition from island to grid connected mode?
• How does virtual impedance effect the transition from island to grid connected
mode?

2 Network Modelling
This chapter will document the network model. Power ratings will be decided and a
delimitation will be explained. Then the wind turbines will be aggregated and passive
components will be described. Electrical parameters such as resistances, inductances
and capacitances for cables, transformers etc. are based on the CIGRE C4.49 425 MW
reference wind farm model[6].
The future offshore energy island in the North Sea will serve as a basis for the modelling
work in this report. As its commissioning lies a whole decade ahead, many details of
its topology are still undecided. The energy island topology used in this report will
therefore not necessarily be representative with the final topology once it is built.
2.1
Power Ratings
The power rating of the energy island will be determined by the wind power capacity. As
previously mentioned the North Sea energy island is scheduled to have an initial capacity
of 3 GW, with option to a +10 GW expansion. In a preliminary report ordered by the
Danish Energy Agency, a standardized 1 GW wind farm layout was used to size both the
North Sea energy island and energy island Bornholm[13]. The energy islands are sized
by clusters of about 1 GW wind farm. A 15 MW wind turbine rating is assumed along
with 66 kV collector cables. According to the report, one collector cable has maximum
rating of 75 MW. Consequently a maximum of 5 15 MW wind turbines can be connected
in series to form one feeder string. Depending on the distance to the energy island the
wind turbines could either be connected directly to the energy island on a 66 kV collector
cable level, or if the wind farm is placed further away, through substation transformers
with 220 kV cables. The report found, after consulting Energinet, that 500 MW would
be the maximum capacity for a 220 kV cable[13]. Each 1 GW wind farm would therefore
consist of two sub farms of 500 MW, with two 220 kV substation transformers. To limit
the size of the model a single 1 GW wind farm will be used, modelled as two 525 MW
wind farms.
No concrete decisions have yet been made regarding electrolyser placement or power
rating. For this report the electrolyser is placed on the island. In order to justify the

2.2
Delimitation of Model Framework
In order to keep the model framework within reasonable complexity, some simplifications
are needed which are explained in detail below. The model will be built in MathWorks
Simulink® and using EMT simulations.
Wind Turbines
The wind turbines are large machines consisting of rotor, pitch & yaw mechanisms,
possible gearbox, axles, bearings, generator, machine side converter, grid side converter,
converter filters, step up transformer and switch gear. A fully described model would
require accurate mechanical and electrical representations of the all the aforementioned
components which is beyond the scope of this report. Instead, only the electrical side
will modelled. An invariant wind speed across each 500 MW wind farm is assumed which
justifies an aggregation of the 35 wind turbines into an equivalent single wind turbine.
A type 4 wind turbine will be used. A direct drive version of a generic type 4 wind
turbine is shown in Figure 2.1. In a conventional grid following mode, the role of the
machine side converter(MSC) is to control the voltage and frequency of the Permanent
Magnet Synchronous Machine(PMSG) to keep an optimal tip speed ratio. The role of the
grid side converter is to control the DC voltage and power factor along with AC voltage
at the wind turbine point of connection(POC). The machine side, i.e rotor, Permanent
Magnet Synchronous Machine(PMSG), and machine side converter, will be simplified

HVDC Links and Grid Connection
The HVDC system will either be a multi terminal system, or conventional point to point
connections. Regardless of what system will be used, the offshore converter’s objective
is to help form the island AC grid, i.e voltage and frequency, and send excess power not
consumed by the electrolyser. The onshore converters’ objective is to extract the power
in the DC-link and transfer it into the onshore grids. This is done by controlling the
DC link voltage. A complete HVDC representation requires modelling of both offshore
and onshore converters and their respective controllers. The focus of this report is on
the control of the wind turbines, which is why the HVDC system will be simplified. The
HVDC system and onshore grid will instead be modelled as a single voltage source behind
an impedance. It assumed that the onshore grids are strong and can sink whatever power
sent to them, while keeping a constant 50 Hz frequency. Thus the HVDC connection
will effectively be modelled as lossless line. Converter dynamics, hardware limitations
and harmonics are therefore neglected for this study.

3 Grid Forming Wind Turbines
This chapter will document the GF control of the wind turbines. As both wind farms are
aggregated into two identical wind turbines, it will be sufficient to present the control
of only one. As mentioned in the introduction, a GF converter emulates the behavior of
the synchronous generator(SG). This implies near instantaneous response to frequency
events(inertial response) and faults such as short circuits. To ensure a stable perform-
ing GF converter in both weak and strong grids, ENTSO-E[15] has recommended the
following requirements:
• Sink for harmonics and unbalanced currents.
• Fault current contribution.
• Robustness against control interaction of other converters.
• Survival of system contingencies.
3.1
Grid Forming Topology
There are many different GF topologies to choose from. The first concept of a Vir-
tual Synchronous Machine(VSM) is called VISMA and was developed in 2007 at the
Clausthal University of Technology in Germany[18].
The VISMA model is a virtual
representation of a complete salient SG, and is therefore a high order model[18]. Since
2007 many other topologies have emerged. There are different views on how to clas-
sify the topologies. Some use the electromechanical(inertia) emulation and others use
the amount of inner control loops. Common for them all however, is that two blocks
are required to build a VSM: an electromechanical block and a electromagnetic block.
An additional block containing auxiliary loops such an AVR, could be needed. These
blocks are shown in Figure 3.1. The role of the electromechanical block is to synchro-
nize with the grid by aligning to the grid angle θ. Virtual inertia and damping can be
implemented in this block. The role of the electromagnetic block is to specify the ref-
erence voltage magnitudes for the VSM. Virtual winding parameters of a SG or virtual

impedance/admittance can be implemented in this block. The auxiliary block can for
example contain an Automatic Voltage Regulator(AVR).

3.2
Synchronous Power Control Topology
The grid forming topology chosen for this study is called Synchronous Power Con-
trol(SPC) and is based on the work done in [5].Fundamentally a SPC based GF converter
mimics the VSM by controlling the converter as if it was a controlled current source.
The electromagnetic block consist of a virtual admittance which determines the reference
current of the current source. The SPC equivalent of the wind turbine GSC is shown in
Figure 3.2. It may seem counter intuitive to control a VSC as a current source but the
advantage is that it allows for straightforward implementation of current control and a
current limiter for protection.

5 Case Study of
Re-Synchronization from Island
mode
In this chapter a study of a sudden island transition and subsequent re-synchronization
will be conducted. The objective is to investigate the influence of GF control parameters
on the aforementioned transitions. It has already been validated that model performs
stable during an island transition. It is expected that the re-synchronization will be
much more demanding for several reasons. For one the grid connection will likely be out
of phase with the island grid. According to the swing equation, as shown below, this
would result in power fluctuations when the breaker closes:
∆P = 2H d2δ
dt2 + D∆ω
(5.1)
Ideally the phase difference should be zero, but this would imply zero power flow be-
forehand which might not possible. The two systems may also be asynchronous, i.e.
have different frequencies. This would mean that the phase difference would a function
of time. Thus the voltages would periodically be in phase, which would be the ideal
time to close the breaker, but would also require precise monitoring and timing. Thirdly
the voltage magnitudes could also be different which could lead to undesirable reactive
power exchange and non-symmetrical currents.
5.1
Study Cases
Study Case 1
Based on the above analysis two study cases are proposed. The first study case will
investigate the effect of the inertia constant on the re-synchronization. Evident from the
swing equation, active power change is directly proportional to inertia. Thus reducing
the inertia constant should be beneficial. Unlike a conventional synchronous generator
41
==PAGE:47==
the inertia constant of the GF converter is not a fixed mechanical property, but can
be changed online. The first study case will therefore reduce the programmed inertia
constant by a factor of 20 for the first 0.5s of the re synchronization. The proportional
gain of the PLC is likewise increased by a factor of 5 during the first 0.5s, to increase
damping.
Study Case 2
the second study case will investigate the effect of virtual admittance on the re-synchronization.
By decreasing the virtual admittance it is believed that the converter is more able to
sink harmonics and power fluctuations during the re-synchronization. The second study
case will therefore decrease the admittance by a factor of 20 for the first 0.5s of the
re-synchronization.
Simulation Setup
To compare the study cases equally the simulation set-up will remain the same through-
out. The model is started in grid connected mode. The wind farm is set to produce 0.7
pu(367.5 MW) throughout the simulation. The electrolyser is consuming 0.25 pu(262
MW) throughout the simulation. At 4s the grid connection trips, and island mode is
active. The wind farm curtails its production to match the electrolyser load. At 6s the
the breaker to the grid connection closes. The wind farm then increases power to 0.7 pu
again. Doing this power up during re-synchronization, is unrealistic but will serve as an
extreme test case.

5.3
Discussion
The main finding of the study case investigation is that reduced inertia and increased
PLC damping is beneficial during re-synchronization.
The effect of reduced virtual
admittance was reduced voltage and power oscillations during the re-synchronization.
However, a decrease in virtual admittance reduces power transfer capability and as the
studied grid was weak, there is very little room to vary the virtual inertia. It has to be
mentioned that the study cases are extreme representations of a real re-synchronization
scenario. A more realistic setup would be to simulate the re-synchronization in scenario
without the wind farm power increase and with a much smaller phase angle difference.
5.4
Future Work
Future work could involve a more realistic re-synchronization scenario. The effect of
virtual inertia could be investigated further to see how much reduction is needed to
achieve stable re-synchronization. Wind Farm II could also be modelled in a tripping
scenario where one of the wind farms would trip due to some fault and subsequently re-
synchronize after fault clearing. The dynamics of the DC link should also be included.
Ideally the full machine and aerodynamic model is needed to verify the grid forming con-
48
==PAGE:54==
trol. This especially involves the assumption that wind turbines can provide the power
requested by the grid forming controller. Finally the simplified models of the electrolyser
plant and HVDC connection should be replaced by accurate converter models.

6 Conclusion
In this report a model of the future energy island in the North Sea, has been built and
studied. The model consist of a total of 1025 MW offshore wind power, an island elec-
trolyser plant and a grid connection to shore. The objective has firstly been to build the
model and apply grid forming control to the wind turbines and secondly to investigate
the stability of a sudden trip and subsequent re-synchronization of the grid connection.
The first objective has been partly achieved.
A functional model has been built and validated in both grid connected and island mode,
but some simplification were necessary. One of the two identical wind farms had to be
omitted to reduce simulation time. Additionally the DC-link controller in the aggregated
wind turbine model was replaced with a stiff DC voltage source due to difficulty in fine
tuning the damping.
Two study cases have been performed to meet the second objective. The first study case
investigated the influence of online reduction of inertia during the re-synchronization
transition. The second study case investigated the influence of online reduction virtual
admittance on the re-synchronization transition. The two study cases were compared
against a base where the inertia constant and virtual admittance were kept at nominal
values throughout the simulation.
The base case resulted in instability after the re-
synchronization. The first study case showed that a low inertia constant during the
transition resulted in a stable system afterwards. The second study case showed that
a reduced virtual admittance could reduce oscillations but had the adverse effect of
limiting power transfer capability which eventually led to instability.
