Understanding the value of offtake structure in hybrid PtX plants
Abstract
The urgent need for a greener energy system, as reflected in the European Commission’s climate
change targets, has placed power-to-x (PtX) at the forefront of strategies to decarbonise hard-to-
decarbonise sectors. This sector is crucial in supporting the transition to sustainable energy and
relies on the development of hybrid PtX power plants. This thesis investigates the under-researched
off-take structures of hybrid PtX plants by considering the impact of different off-take structures on
plant operation and economic feasibility. This gap is addressed by developing a deterministic model
of a hybrid PtX plant with an electrolyser, co-located renewable energy sources, hydrogen storage
and potential grid connection and its associated off-take structure. An initial hybrid PtX plant is inves-
tigated, followed by analyses on the influence of hydrogen purchase agreements, RFNBO Delegated
Acts considerations, and electrolyzer modeling complexity. Using a case study inspired by an actual
PtX project, the analysis shows that grid-connected configurations offer superior flexibility and revenue
potential compared to off-grid setups, improving operational and economic performance. Conversely,
off-grid configurations require strategic hydrogen storage and flexible purchase contracts to ensure
viability. In addition, hydrogen contract design, delivery methods, RFNBO compliance and electrol-
yser modelling complexity have a significant impact on operational strategies, highlighting the need
for careful consideration in feasibility assessments.
Resumé
Det presserende behov for et grønnere energisystem, som afspejles i Europa-Kommissionens mål for
klimaforandringer, har sat power-to-x (PtX) i spidsen for strategier til at dekarbonisere sektorer, der
er svære at dekarbonisere. Denne sektor er afgørende for at støtte overgangen til bæredygtig energi
og er afhængig af udviklingen af hybride PtX-kraftværker. Denne afhandling undersøger de under-
belyste aftagestrukturer for hybride power-to-x-anlæg (PtX) ved at overveje indvirkningen af forskel-
lige aftagerstrukturer på anlæggets drift og økonomiske gennemførlighed. Dette hul adresseres ved
at udvikle en deterministisk model af et hybrid PtX-anlæg med en elektrolysør, samlokaliserede ved-
varende energikilder, brintlagring og potentiel nettilslutning og den tilhørende aftagerstruktur. Et in-
dledende hybrid PtX-anlæg blev undersøgt, efterfulgt af analyser af indflydelsen af brintkøbsaftaler,
RFNBO Delegated Acts-overvejelser og elektrolysørens modelleringskompleksitet. Ved hjælp af et
casestudie inspireret af et faktisk PtX-projekt viser analysen, at nettilsluttede konfigurationer giver
overlegen fleksibilitet og indtægtspotentiale sammenlignet med off-grid-opsætninger, hvilket forbedrer
den driftsmæssige og økonomiske ydeevne. Omvendt kræver off-grid-konfigurationer strategisk brint-
lagring og fleksible købskontrakter for at sikre levedygtighed. Derudover har design af brintkontrak-
ter, leveringsmetoder, RFNBO-overholdelse og kompleksitet i elektrolysørmodellering en betydelig
indvirkning på driftsstrategier, hvilket understreger behovet for omhyggelig overvejelse i gennem-
førlighedsvurderinger.

Introduction
1.1
Background
The energy transition requires the decarbonisation of hard-to-abate sectors with low carbon sources.
In this context, renewable energy generation capacity is increasingly being installed. The intermit-
tent nature of renewable energy resources, such as wind and solar, can be mitigated by hybrid plant
solutions, which allow the integration of one or more renewable energy generation units with energy
storage or conversion systems. These systems can also reduce the risk of potential grid congestion.
Within these hybrid systems, hybrid power-to-x (PtX) plants can offer more flexibility by producing fu-
els with the renewable electricity while contributing to the decarbonization of hard-to-electrify sectors.
The upcoming development of hybrid PtX plants is characterised by a variety of energy vectors, each
associated with different value streams such as electricity, hydrogen, ammonia, methanol and more.
One of several value streams can be considered in a project. Hybrid PtX plants are defined associat-
ing numerous potential combinations of assets, including energy generation, conversion and storage
units. These hybrid PtX plants have multiple off-take options such as fuel off-take with a consumer,
interactions with electricity day-ahead markets or participation in balancing markets.
The range of options available and the novelty of fuel off-take arrangements presents a significant
challenge in determining the optimal off-take structure for a hybrid PtX plant. Key elements to con-
sider include electrolyser operating constraints, storage requirements, off-take logistics and the finan-
cial considerations of off-take contracts, including factors such as time resolution, associated penal-
ties for supply shortfalls and delivery flexibility, as well as plant capital and operational expenditure.
In addition, regulatory considerations relating to the certification of renewable hydrogen production
through the RFNBO Delegated Act in Europe add complexity and additional considerations that could
jeopardise the viability of PtX projects.
In the face of these challenges, planning the operation of a hybrid PtX plant becomes a highly complex
task, resulting in high investment risks. Successful implementation requires a global understanding
of the relationship between different energy vectors, market dynamics, regulatory perspectives and
technological capabilities.
1.2
PtX: A sector in development facing challenges
The PtX sector is seen as a solution to decarbonise hard-to-abate sectors where electrification is lim-
ited or not possible. Green electricity is used to produce ’green fuels’ that can be used in a variety
of industries such as chemicals, metals or oil refining, or as a fuel for the transport sector, with a
focus on shipping and aviation. The most promising and envisaged production pathways using PtX
production are hydrogen, methanol and ammonia [22]. In addition to the above, PtX can also help to
reduce the curtailment of renewable energy sources (RES) in regions with a high penetration of RES,
maximising their use and supporting further expansion of RES, as is the case in Denmark.
At the European level, ambitious targets have been set for the share of renewable energy in energy
consumption. In 2023, the Renewable Energy Directive (RED III) set a target of 42.5% of European
energy consumption to come from renewable sources by 2030[10]. This announcement clarifies the
rules for the production of Renewable Fuel of Non-Biogenic Origin (RFNBO) [7] and sets targets for
the production of RFNBO compliant hydrogen. 42% of the hydrogen used should come from RFNBOs
by 2030 and 60% by 2035.
The PtX sector is expanding with numerous projects still at the feasibility stage, as shown in Figure 1.

The International Energy Agency [22] describes the current uncertainties underlying the development
of PtX projects. It states that securing off-takers, developing appropriate regulations and financing,
and reducing the risks associated with investment and financing costs are essential to ensure the
success of PtX plant development.
Given these uncertainties, techno-economic modeling and analysis of off-take structures for hybrid
PtX plant can be useful in helping developers and investors to assess and mitigate the risks associated
with projects.
1.3
Research gap
The new interest in hybrid PtX plant has motivated a large amount of literature. Most of the literature
deals with the optimization of a hybrid plant by considering the design or operation of the plant under
the assumption of a fixed off-take structure.
As a illustration, several papers investigating the need of a complex modeling of the relationship for
power consumption and hydrogen production in an electrolyzer assume only a fixed hydrogen off-
take contract [28] [5]. Moreover, Campion et al. (2023) [6] describes the optimal sizing of a hybrid
power-to-ammonia plant with solar and wind sources with different configurations (behind the meter
operation and grid connected) while the ammonia off-take structure is considering a constant delivery
volume and frequency. Zheng et al. (2022) [33] addresses the optimal operation for an hybrid PtX
plant by implementing a bi-level optimization model that reveals optimal investment plans consider-
ing various operational strategies like minimizing the IRR (internal rate of return) or maximizing the
hydrogen production. However, in that configuration, the hydrogen off-take is not constrained by any
Page 2 of 67

specific contract.
The above approaches are limited in the sense that they do not fully consider the dimensions of
the off-take contract for a hybrid PtX plant and do not fully address the implications underlying such
a contract. Indeed, off-take purchase agreements consist of two distinct dimensions: the physical
delivery and the financial contract. Essentially, the physical delivery addresses the operational and
logistical aspects, while the financial contract focuses on the economic and pricing elements of the
off-take agreement. This duality in the consideration of hydrogen off-take agreements is often not
considered. A small change in the off-take contract could result in a large deficit in the economics of
the plant. This is an issue because changing the design of the plant after commissioning is critical
and requires high investment costs and possibly additional permits. Understanding the relationship
between the plant design and the potential off-take structures in a hybrid PtX plant is therefore critical.
In addition, the regulatory framework for hybrid PtX projects is often overlooked. Regulations such as
the Delegated Act on the production of Renewable Fuels of Non-Biogenic Origin [7] can significantly
limit electricity imports in the case of a grid-connected setup. These regulations surrounding the de-
velopment of the PtX sector are still evolving and partially in place. However, this will evolve over the
next decades and considerations of the impact of RFNBOS or other regulations need to be included
in the analysis. At this stage, this is largely overlooked in the current literature.
In this sense, the objective of this work is to investigate, based on a fixed plant design, how the opti-
mal operation of hybrid PtX plants is influenced by the plant configuration, the structure of the off-take
contracts and potential regulatory aspects.
1.4
Thesis objectives
These considerations mentioned previously lead to a list of research questions that address several
focus areas that will be investigated in this thesis.
Configuration and design of the plant: Firstly, the configuration of the plant is essential to investi-
gate due to the variety of configurations and off-take opportunities. Off-grid configurations, consisting
of PtX plants connected only to behind-the-meter renewable assets, or grid-connected configura-
tion, can influence the operational strategy on the hybrid PtX plant. The capacity of the components
also plays a role in the flexibility, economic viability and optimal operating strategy of the plant. This
configuration and design of the plant is often considered to be fixed when considering the operating
strategies of the plant. This limitation needs to be further addressed.
• What are the main considerations when designing a PtX plant with a direct grid connection
versus an off-grid or partially grid-connected setup?
• To what extent can the design of the plant have an impact on the economic viability and optimal
operating strategy for a hybrid PtX plant subject to hydrogen offtake contractual obligations?
Hydrogen off-take structures: In addition, there is also a lack of research into the design of hydrogen
off-take contracts. Most of the current literature examines plant operating strategies based on fixed off-
take contracts. The contract elements to be considered: price, volume, delivery schedule, penalties,
physical off-take could drive the business case and these considerations need to be assessed.
• How can modeling and analysis of different hydrogen off-take structures for a hybrid PtX plant
improve understanding of the opportunities and risks associated ?
• How can a deeper understanding of off-take structures and their sensitivities improve the nego-
tiation of hydrogen off-take agreements?

Literature review
This chapter present a literature review of existing papers, open source and regulatory materials
related to hybrid PtX plants techno-economic modeling, off-take structures, and an introduction to the
regulations for RFNBOs considerations.
2.1
Existing work regarding hybrid PtX plant modeling
As mentioned in the introduction, several studies have been carried out on the modeling of hybrid PtX
plants, but part of the off-take structure has mostly been overlooked.
2.1.1
Work addressing operational strategies of a hybrid PtX plant
The following section describes several work that has been done considering the operational strate-
gies of the hydrid PtX plant.
Campion et al. (2023) [6] developed a model to optimise investment and operating costs based on
techno-economic data, different power profiles and hourly grid prices. The analysis focuses on am-
monia synthesised from electrolysed hydrogen produced using electricity from wind and solar plants
or grid imports, and compares different weather profiles and electrolyser technologies. The study also
considers the carbon intensity of the ammonia produced, based on the carbon grid intensity of the
location considered. In this analysis, the contract regarding the ammonia offtake structure is consid-
ered with a constant volume and price.
Zheng et al. (2022) [33] presents a two-stage multi-objective optimisation framework to identify op-
timal investment plans that take into account different operational strategies, such as maximising
economic revenue and green hydrogen production. It aims to investigate the interrelationship be-
tween planning and operations of a hybrid wind-based PtX plant. The hydrogen demand considered
is fixed and based on a daily load profile corresponding to potential industrial consumption profile.
The main findings of the analysis are that there is a trade-off between investment costs and optimal
operational performance, as increasing system flexibility to increase profits also increases costs. In
this sense, different operational objectives, such as maximising hydrogen production or maximising
economic viability, lead to different component optimal capacities in the design.
2.1.2
Work addressing electrolyzer efficiency modeling impact on the operational strategies
The hydrogen production curve of an electrolyser is not convex and several models have been de-
veloped to approximate the efficiency curve of an electrolyser.
Campion et al. (2023) [6] model to optimise investment and operating costs uses a constant linear
efficiency for the electrolyser. The reason for this is to avoid a mixed integer linear or non-linear prob-
lem formulation and to keep the computation time reasonable.
On the other hand, Baumhof et al. (2023) [5] has investigated the need to increase the complexity of
the electrolyser modeling. It analyses the impact of adding different levels of electrolyser detail to the
optimal dispatch problem of a hybrid PtX plant in the day-ahead time stage. Several numbers of op-
erating states (on, off, standby) and linearisation segments were used to approximate the non-linear
hydrogen production curve. This investigation was carried out assuming a fixed amount of hydrogen
to be delivered on a daily basis.
Raheli et al. (2023) [28] adds to the modeling complexity by considering linear relaxation of the
piecewise linear approximation and conic relaxation of a quadratic approximation.Other modeling
approaches. In this analysis, the impact of electrolyser modeling on the operational results of the

plant has been made considering a minimum hydrogen volume requirement.
It can be seen that electrolyser modeling can affect the initial design of the plant due to the approxima-
tion to the real efficiency curve that is made. In the previous work mentioned, the impact of electrolyser
modeling on the operation of the plant was addressed. However, considering a fixed offtake structure
and assessing the optimal design of the plant based on different electrolyser modeling efficiencies
could also lead to different optimisation results.
2.1.3
Work addressing off-take structures modeling
A few papers initiated the analysis of the off-take structure of hybrid PtX plants. The following de-
scription corresponds to the only relevant paper I have found that addresses the impact of different
hydrogen off-take structures on plant operating strategies.
Schrotenboer et al. investigate optimal control strategies for the operation of an electrolyser coupled
to a wind farm [31]. The paper explores optimal strategies for operating integrated energy systems
that combine renewable energy production with hydrogen storage, using Markov decision process
theory to develop policies for day-to-day decisions about energy storage, electricity market transac-
tions and hydrogen sales. Practical considerations include power purchase agreements, fluctuating
electricity prices, different distribution channels, green hydrogen off-take agreements, and market
price uncertainties.
The hybrid plant is designed to include a co-located wind farm with the electrolyser, storage and the
ability to interact with the electricity grid. In particular, the paper addresses several aspects of hydro-
gen off-take agreements, such as hydrogen sales periods and hydrogen pricing. The main conclusion
of the paper is that the design of the hydrogen purchase contracts is crucial for defining the economic
feasibility of a hybrid PtX system.
Despite the efforts to develop an understanding of the modeling and development of hybrid PtX plants,
it appears that few studies have investigated the importance of off-take structures. For example,
as mentioned above, most of the studies either focus on a specific hydrogen off-take contract and
investigate the optimal sizing without considering the optimal operations. Finally, a limited number
of the analysed publications address the issue of regulatory aspects such as RFNBOs. Based on
such considerations, an additional motivation is present to provide further understanding to these
unanswered questions.

A hybrid PtX plant involves the integration of multiple energy sources and technologies to convert
electrical power into various forms of energy, such as hydrogen, ammonia, or methanol. This thesis
is targeting the production of hydrogen but can be used as inpiration for further investigations regard-
ing ammonia or methanol production. Several arrangements and organizations of the plant based on
the integration of potential renewable energy sources and its connection to the electrical grid can be
considered when developing a project. Aurora Energy Research has been assessing several plant
configurations that can be considered when developing a hybrid PtX plant [30]. Based on geograph-
ical constraints, grid availability, initial investment decision and off-take structure adopted, several
configurations can be implemented as described in Figure 2.

The two main categories of electrolyser configurations are stand-alone electrolysers and co-located

This section describes the RFNBO Delegated Act developed by the European Commission to define
hydrogen production from renewable sources. This content will be used as a basis for the analysis
and discussion of the impact of the requirements for producing RFNBO compliant hydrogen on the
optimal operation strategies and economic feasibility of hybrid PtX plants.
2.4.1
RFNBO delegated act description
In January 2023, the European commission clarified the production of renewable liquid and gaseous
transport fuels of non-biological origin. This clarification applies for fuels produced inside or outside
the European Union [7]. Two delegated acts have been developed to supplement the Renewable
Energy Directive (RED II):

• Rules for the production of renewable liquid and gaseous transport fuels of non-biological origin
(RFNBO),
• Methodology for assessing GHG emissions savings from renewable liquid and gaseous trans-
port fuels of non-biological origin and recycled carbon fuels.
Specifically, the RFNBO delegated act establishes rules for determining when electricity used to pro-
duce hydrogen, methanol, ammonia, and other e-fuels through electrolysis and less common produc-
tion pathways can be considered fully renewable [11].
To be eligible for RFNBOs compliance, the maximum greenhouse gas emission intensity threshold
defined by the European Commission has been set at 70% below a fossil fuel benchmark of 94

In this sense, there are two ways to produce RFNBO is the light of the Delegated Act:
• Fully renewable electricity: electricity obtained from a direct connection or grid connection
with PPAs. In the situation where the RFNBO criteria, detailed in the next part, are met, all
quantities produced qualify as RFNBO.
• Grid sourcing: If the carbon intensity of the sourced electricity from the grid results in a GHG in-
tensity of the produced hydrogen lower than the 70% threshold (3.38 tCO2eq/tH2), the RFNBO
production is determined using the average share of renewable energy sources (%) in the coun-
try of production as reported two years prior to the one in question, as described in Article 27.3
of the RED. In this configuration, a portion of the hydrogen produced is considered RFNBO and
the remaining portion is considered low carbon hydrogen.
The criteria implemented to measure the renewable content of the electricity consumed for the hydro-
gen production are the following:
Additionality:
This condition ensure that the renewable electricity come from assets that would not
exist in the absence of RFNBO hydrogen production. A series of conditions have been explicited in
the delegated act to define the additionality criteria:
• The renewable energy asset’ operation needs commissioning date must not be earlier than 36
months before the electrolyser commissionning date
• The renewable energy asset has not received any public financial support in the form of oper-
ating aid or investment aid.
Several exemptions have been addressed regarding the additionality requirement in grid where the
carbon intensity is low and a limitation in terms of renewable asset developement can occur. The
following cases are subject to an exemption:
• The electrolyser is located in a bidding zone with more than 90% RES-E share.
• The grid carbon intensity where the electrolyzer is connected is lower than 18 gCO2eq/MJ.

Geographical correlation:
The delegated act states that in the case where there is a bilateral PPA
between a renewable producer and a fuel producer, a geographical correlation must be met. The
geographical correlation is met if one of the following criteria is met:
• The renewable production and the electrolyser need to be located in the same bidding zone.
• The RES-E is located in an interconnected bidding zone (excluding offshore bidding zone) and
the prices for the relevant period on the day ahead market were higher than in the bidding zone
where the electrolyser is located.
• The renewable asset is located in an offshore bidding zone that is interconnected with the bid-
ding zone where the electrolyzer is located.
Temporal correlation:
Similarly, the delegated act states that in the case of a bilateral PPA between
a renewable energy producer and a fuel producer, a temporal correlation must be met. This criterion
was developed to ensure that the hydrogen produced and counted as fully renewable is actually
sourced from renewable assets. The temporal correlation criteria are currently quite loose and will
become more stringent towards 2030. The following criteria define the temporal correlation.
• Until 31st December 2029 the electricity consumed for the production of RFNBO and the elec-
tricity generated in the PPA must be matched within the same month. Starting from 1st January
2030, it must be within the same hour.
If the electrolyser operates during hour when day ahead electricity price in the bidding zone is lower
than 20 EUR/MWh or 36% of the ETS carbon price (EUR/t CO2), there is an exemption for the tem-
poral correlation.
If the electricity used complies with the guidelines of the RFNBO delegated act, the electricity is
considered to be entirely renewable and, consequently, the hydrogen produced and its derivatives
are compliant with the RFNBO. However, in the case where the PtX plant is also sourcing power
from the grid, without complying with the RFNBO delegated act, the electricity can still be considered
partially renewable and can partially count towards RFNBO production.

2.5
Hydrogen pricing
The price of hydrogen is a key parameter in the hydrogen purchase agreement that will be signed
and will significantly determine the economic viability of the plant. However, as there is currently no
wholesale market for hydrogen and green hydrogen accounts for less than 1% of the volume pro-
duced worldwide, there are still many uncertainties about the potential price of hydrogen.
A recent report by the Boston Consulting Group [25] highlights that the price of green hydrogen in Eu-
rope is expected to be higher than previously thought, ranging from €5-8/kg by 2030. This increase
is driven by regulatory challenges, rising energy costs and supply chain issues in wind power and
electrolyser manufacturing.
The main cost driver is the price of renewable electricity, which has increased due to supply chain
constraints and competition for green electricity. This makes green hydrogen less competitive com-
pared to other decarbonisation technologies, posing a risk to its uptake.
In addition, uncertainty about future costs is making potential buyers reluctant to commit to long-term
contracts, potentially stalling market growth. Policymakers are urged to implement regulations and
incentives to reduce costs and stimulate demand to ensure the sector’s growth and competitiveness
in the global market.
Based on this recent analysis, the base price for the hydrogen purchase contract is assumed to be 5
EUR/kg. This price is however largely higher than hydrogen production price via methane reforming
for instance and cannot compete against fossil fuel based production [22].
Mechanisms are available to bridge the gap between conventional hydrogen production price and
green hydrogen price. As an example, the European Commission has established its first hydrogen
pilot auction in 2024 [9], awarding nearly €720 million to seven renewable hydrogen projects in Eu-
rope. The winning bidders will produce renewable hydrogen in Europe and will receive a subsidy to
bridge the price difference between their production costs and the market price for hydrogen.

The aim of this chapter is to describe and justify the methodology and reasoning that have been built

To illustrate the thesis and give more context to the analysis of a hybrid PtX plant off-take structure, it

refinery and natural gas blending purposes. The electrolyser is based on alkaline technology and has
a capacity of 100 MW. Electricity will be supplied from co-located wind and solar power plants and
from a grid connection. The aim of the project is to demonstrate the lowest possible cost of green H2
production by operating a 100 MW alkaline electrolyser integrated with solar and wind energy [21].

3.2
Modeling framework
A mathematical model has been developed to address the research questions highlighted in the intro-
duction. The aim of this model is to provide an understanding of the dynamics and optimal operations
of a hybrid PtX plant. The model is structured to answer the research questions as following:
• The model is based on key parameters such as plant configuration and design, energy flows
input, cost parameters and RFNBO regulation requirements.
• The model incorporates the terms of hydrogen purchase agreements, including pricing, delivery
schedules and contractual obligations.
• The electrolyser, as a critical component of the hydrogen production process, is modeled with
different complexities regarding the efficiency to study the importance of an accurate electrolyzer
model on the optimal operations results.
• An additional model as been develop to consider the Renewable Fuels of Non-Biological Ori-
gin (RFNBO) with additional considerations for low carbon and RFNBO compliant hydrogen
differentiation.
The model is designed to be a flexible tool that can be adapted to different potential projects and
configurations. It allows for changes in plant design, energy sources and hydrogen off-take, making
it versatile for different scenarios and future developments.
In the following section, the model that has been developed is described with the introduction to the
decision variables, constraints and objective functions associated to the hybrid PtX plant.

Revenue and penalty calculation regarding hydrogen purchase agreement:
The hydrogen pro-
ducer is subject to a contracted revenue, related to the contracted price and volume, and also subject
to another financial stream in the situation where there is a mismatch between the actual delivery and
the contracted volume.
The mechanism that can be designed is inspired by the electricity market. Indeed, the contracted
volume and price are similar to the electricity volume and bid that has been validated during the
day-ahead auction, and a balancing market is in place to adjust the potential mismatches in the final
delivery. The balancing mechanism implemented in this thesis is based on the two-price balanc-
ing scheme from the electricity market. An over-delivery compared to the contracted volume will be
paid, but at a price lower than or equal to the initial contracted price, while an under-delivery will be
translated as a payment back to the buyer at a price higher than or equal to the contracted price. This
creates an incentive for buyers to deliver the contracted quantity while providing the flexibility required
by the predominantly renewable power profile of the hybrid PtX plant.

3.4.1
Contextualization
This section addresses the modeling modifications that have been made to analyze the implications of
considering RFNBO considerations in the case where a grid connection and electricity import without
a green PPA is considered. The description of the RFNBO delegated act and the contextualization
in the light of the thesis is available in §2.4. As explained in this section, in order to be able to certify
the hydrogen produced by the plant while consuming electricity from the grid, the hydrogen produced
must have a carbon emission factor corresponding to 70% of the GHG emission savings compared
to conventional hydrogen production pathways benchmarked at 94gCO2eq/MJ.

In the context of the thesis, where renewable electricity is sourced in a co-located behind the meter
configuration, the only increase in carbon intensity of the hydrogen produced is due to the electricity
import and, more specifically, the carbon intensity of the grid. In the situation where the GHG reduction
target is met, the consumption of electricity from the grid induces the production of two types of
hydrogen: RFNBO-compliant H2 and low-carbon H2. These two different certifications may not have
the same pricing in a hydrogen purchase contract, as the off-taker may have a different willingness
to pay for the different types of hydrogen. For that reason, the modeling of the plant considers two
separated hydrogen streams after the electrolyzer.

Rationale
The analysis is organised to provide a comprehensive understanding of the hybrid PtX offtake struc-

Figure 17: Overview of the analysis
As described in Figure 17, the analysis starts with a base case analysis, which serves as an initial

analyses will consider the grid connected configuration and the islanded configuration as these are
the most common configurations for hybrid PtX plants as mentioned in §2.2.

• Hydrogen offtake: This section examines the design parameters of hydrogen purchase con-

• RFNBO considerations: In this section, the impact of Renewable Fuel of Non-Biological Origin

production, as highlighted in Table 6, an in-depth analysis of different modelling approaches is

modeling complexity is assessed by comparing linear and piece-wise linear efficiency models,
This structured approach aims to provide a global and nuanced understanding of the dynamics of the
hybrid PtX plant, addressing both operational results and economic feasibility.

Figure 45 also represents an increase in electricity imported from the grid and then a decrease as the

capacity is installed, the hybrid PtX plant relies on importing electricity from the grid. The aim is to
maximise the production of RFNBO hydrogen because of the pricing considered: the low carbon hy-
drogen has a base price corresponding to 75% of the RFNBO compliant hydrogen. To ensure RFNBO
production, imports will take place during periods when the share of renewable energy in the grid is

produced. As the installed renewable capacity increases, the plant shifts to importing at times when
on-site renewable energy is insufficient, and thus with a higher carbon intensity. This transition with
an increase in the grid import may temporarily increase carbon intensity. However, as renewable
production continues to increase, the plant’s need for grid imports decreases, leading to a reduction
in overall carbon intensity. This curve needs to be studied in more detail to fully understand what’s
behind it.
The main difference between siting a plant in Portugal and Poland is the amount of electricity that
can be sourced. The low carbon intensity of the Portuguese grid allows for more grid imports and
significantly increases the economic viability when installed renewable capacity is low. At high levels
of installed renewable capacity, grid imports are reduced and therefore the carbon intensity threshold
has less impact.
It can also be noted that the installed renewable capacity co-located with the hybrid PtX plant is a key
parameter that will determine the amount of RFNBO compliant hydrogen produced and impact the
economic feasibility of the project and grid imports. In fact, the RFNBO compliant hydrogen produced
is defined by taking into account the renewable electricity from renewable generation facilities and the
renewable percentage of the grid electricity mix.

7
Conclusion
The upcoming development of PtX plants, driven by the energy transition and the need to decarbonise
hard-to-abate industries, has led to numerous publications and literature focusing on techno-economic
models for hybrid PtX plants. In most cases, the design or operation of the plant has been evaluated
considering a fixed off-take structure. Few studies have attempted to analyse the impact of variations
in the off-take structure on plant operation strategy and economic feasibility.
To fill this gap, an analysis was carried out to understand the intricacies underlying the offtake struc-
tures in hybrid PtX plants. A deterministic model was developed based on a plant consisting of an
electrolyser, behind-the-meter co-located wind and solar plants, hydrogen storage and a potential
available grid connection. The developed model has been used in a case study inspired by the
GreenH2Atlantic project. The analysis has been initiated by investigating the base case considered
and the impact of modifications to the initial design on the operational strategies and economic feasi-
bility of the hybrid PtX plant. Deeper analyses have followed to address the influence of both financial
and physical hydrogen purchase agreement parameters, the regulatory impact of the RFNBO Dele-
gated Acts considerations and the electrolyser modelling complexity.
The following findings emerged from the analysis.
The grid connected configuration offers greater flexibility and higher revenues than the off-grid config-
uration. This configuration allows better optimisation of operations, as it can draw power from the grid
during periods of low renewable production thus benefit fully from the hydrogen purchase agreement
contracted. This capability increases the overall economic performance and operational flexibility of
the plant.
The islanded configuration relies solely on renewable generation and lacks the ability to adjust opera-
tions to maximise economic viability. In this scenario, hydrogen storage becomes essential to provide
the necessary flexibility to balance production and demand. In addition, the design of hydrogen pur-
chase contracts that include provisions for flexibility is critical to ensuring the economic viability of the
project, as it allows for better management of hydrogen production and offtake.
Hydrogen purchase contracts need to be designed with caution as their impact varies depending on
the configuration considered. Parameters such as contracted volumes, prices, both base and bal-
ancing, will have a major impact on both economic feasibility and operational strategy for the grid
connected configuration, while the islanded configuration will be subject to limited flexibility.
The method of physical delivery for hydrogen purchase contracts has a significant impact on op-
erational constraints. The use of tube trailers for delivery creates more logistical challenges and
constraints for the hydrogen producer if sufficient storage capacity is not available. In contrast, con-
tinuous delivery by pipeline provides a more stable delivery method.
To implement these RFNBO compliance considerations, it is crucial for the modeling of the plant to
consider the amount of RFNBO hydrogen and low-carbon hydrogen after the electrolyser in separate
streams to keep track of the carbon intensity of the hydrogen that is sold to the offtakers.
It is observed that RFNBO compliance considerations limit grid imports for hybrid PtX plants in regions
where the carbon intensity of the grid is high.
Moreover, the installed renewable capacity is a key parameter to consider in order to produce suffi-
cient RFNBO compliant hydrogen.
The complexity of modelling the electrolyser can be significant in scenarios with high flexibility and
large electricity imports. Furthermore, the constant linear efficiency model maximises hydrogen pro-
duction, resulting in higher grid imports and electrolyser utilisation, whereas the piece-wise linear
efficiency model prioritises operational efficiency due to its efficiency curve. This difference in strat-

egy means that the optimisation results may introduce an error in estimating the pre-feasibility of the
plant compared to real-time operation and must be carefully considered.
7.1
Future work
This thesis explored the modelling of a hybrid PtX plant in order to understand the offtake struc-
ture of this type of plant and to propose guidelines for PtX developers to ensure optimal operation
and economic feasibility. Through the construction of the proposed methodology and the underlying
assumptions, an attempt was made to provide an answer to this question and some insights were dis-
covered. However, given the importance of this issue and its complexity, further research is required
to fully understand the opportunities and risks associated with the development of hybrid PtX plants.
In this respect, as the thesis, numerous questions and areas of interest have emerged, but haven’t
been fully addressed.
Given the rapid growth and interest in PtX projects, it is expected that hybrid PtX plant projects will
proliferate and there is a need to gain an overall understanding of the opportunities and risks underly-
ing the offtake structures. Consequently, several potential areas for further investigation and research
are suggested.
• More detailed financial analysis: Conducting a detailed financial analysis to accurately assess
the economic viability of hybrid PtX plants is critical to PtX project development and hasn’t been
addressed in this work. This analysis would provide developers with a clearer understanding
of investment returns, operating costs and potential financial risks, ultimately leading to better
decision making.
• The implementation of stochastic programming would allow for better management of uncer-
tainties in PtX plant operations, such as fluctuating energy prices and variable demand. This
approach can optimise decision making under uncertainty, leading to more robust and reliable
operational strategies. Going a step further, the implementation of risk considerations could be
studied using more advanced modelling approaches, such as chance constraint robust optimi-
sation.
• Study of pricing dynamics: An in-depth study of the price dynamics between electricity, low
carbon hydrogen and Renewable Fuels of Non-Biological Origin (RFNBO) compliant hydrogen
is necessary to understand market interactions and competitiveness. This analysis would help
to understand the optimal strategies for the hybrid PtX plant based on the prices considered.
• The implementation of additional components to the hybrid PtX plant would allow the analysis
to be used for several different projects. The inclusion of battery energy storage or alternative
fuel production pathways such as ammonia or methanol have not been considered in this work,
but these plants are currently under development. Moreover, other off-take structures such as
the participation in the balancing markets could be an interesting opportunity to investigate.
