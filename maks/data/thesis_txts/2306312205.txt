1 Executive Summary 
The current digital era is a revolution in many ways and in particular in the entrepreneurship 
world. Never before, such a great deal of entrepreneurs decided to create their businesses. 
Helping them to decide and designing the right tools to generate a thriving business is more 
important than ever. In this view, the Business Model Canvas (BMC) from Osterwalder and 
Pigneur is the most business model generation tool used by first-time entrepreneurs. They 
highly appreciate its visual aspects and its easiness to use. Nevertheless, the Canvas has a major 
drawback: it does not take into account the learning process of unexperienced entrepreneurs. 
Indeed, entrepreneurs have to learn about the skills they need to design a great business model 
while actually designing it. That is the reason why this thesis aims to help do so by improving 
the Business Model Canvas. Thus, the author has analyzed the literature on business model and 
business model generation and he has gathered a pool of entrepreneurs who have been surveyed 
and interviewed. The collected theoretical views and empirical data enabled to design a 
Business Model Generation Framework (BMG Framework) composed of a business model 
design process, the BMC and supporting tools to learn and master the required design-related 
skills. This Framework has been tested with first-time entrepreneurs, adapted and validated. 
This leads to a visual tool that can be used by entrepreneurs to wisely design their start-up 
business model. This thesis can be considered as a first step into the customization of business 
modeling tools for entrepreneurs and first-time entrepreneurs so they will be able to meet the 
business challenges of our new digital world.

2 Introduction 
Entrepreneurs are challenging the current world along with the digital era. More and more 
young people start their own business or are deeply willing to. A whole ecosystem made of 
incubators, universities and specialized stakeholders struggles to help them succeed. 
Nevertheless, the entrepreneurial journey for unexperienced entrepreneurs is made of hard 
obstacles to override. Most of the time, those unexperienced entrepreneurs have a product but 
no idea on how they can make a business out of it. Therefore, their main issue is to design the 
start-up company’s business model. The concept of Business Model (BM) is globally well-
known and it has been discussed for decades by scholars, practitioners, policy makers and 
businessmen. Even though no final definition has emerged, an agreement on what is should 
contain has been reached. It actually answers four key questions: what is the customer? What 
is the value sold to it? How does the company make money? And does the company deliver 
the value? To support the thinking and design process, tools have emerged. The most known 
is the Business Model Canvas, introduced by Alexander Osterwalder and Yves Pigneur. This 
is a simple canvas, divided into nine blocks and balancing between the customer side, the 
company side and the financial side. Despite its easiness to use, it is still a challenge for 
unexperienced entrepreneurs to master it and achieve relevant work. For this specific audience, 
learning new skills to make their use of the Business Model Canvas relevant is fundamental. 
That is why the central question of this master’s thesis will be as follows. 
How can the Business Model Canvas be adapted to take into account the learning process of 
unexperienced entrepreneurs while designing it? 
In order to deal with this issue, an action research approach has been applied. Theoretical basis 
on business model and business model generation has been studied. It has been combined with 
surveys and interviews from a pool of European first-time entrepreneurs to lead to the 
conceptualization of a new visual tool: the Business Model Generation Framework (BMG 
Framework). Then, this tool has been investigated, tested and validated by the same pool of 
first-time entrepreneurs. The whole process leads to an actionable and visual Framework which 
can be used by first-time entrepreneurs to design their business model quickly while learning 
key skills without too much efforts.

3  Literature Review 
The roots of the Business Model (BM) concept can be dated back 1954. They lie in the four 
Peter Drucker’ questions which are: «who is the customer”, “what does the customer value”, 
“how do we make money” and “how can we deliver value to customers at an appropriate cost” 
(Drucker, 1954; Magretta, 2002). But the conceptualization of the concept has occurred 
through the strategic management literature (e.g., Amit & Zott, 2001; Hedman & Kalling, 
2003; Zott and Amit, 2008). Since 2000, BM has been extensively discussed by scholars, 
practitioners, policy makers and business people, from big corporations to start-up companies 
(Timmer, 1998). Researches and experiments have been led in a great deal of different fields, 
from strategic management (Zott and Amit, 2007, 2008; Magretta, 2002; Teece, 2010), 
information systems (Osterwalder et al., 2005), innovation (Chesbrough and Rosenbloom, 
2002) and entrepreneurship (Morris et al., 2005). The great variety of researches and related 
scientific fields has led to many definitions of what a BM is. At the moment, no consensus has 
been found yet, mainly due to the multidisciplinary approaches of BM. The different definitions 
mostly differ on the objectives and the components. For instance, Morris explains that a 
Business Model is “a concise representation of how an interrelated set of decision variables in 
the areas of venture strategy, architecture, and economics are addressed to create sustainable 
competitive advantage in defined markets” (Morris, 2005). In this case, the scale at which a 
Business Model should apply is a market. Other researchers tend to be more specific, focusing 
on a venture and arguing that “a Business Model is a conceptual tool containing a set of objects, 
concepts and their relationships with the objective to express the business logic of a specific 
firm” (Osterwalder et al., 2005).  
Despite the lack of unified definition, key BM components have emerged (Zott et al., 2011). 
Those key components are often called building blocks and they belong to one of the four 
following categories: Strategic Choices, Value Network, Create Value and Capture Value (SM. 
Shafer et al., 2005). Strategic Choices are about the notions of customer, value proposition, 
capabilities and competences, revenue and pricing, competitors and many more. The Value 
Network is about Suppliers, Customer Information, Customer Relationship, Information 
Flows, Product and Service Flows. Lastly, the categories “Create Value” and “Capture Value” 
are respectively composed of Resources – Assets – Processes and Activities on the one hand, 
and composed of Cost – Financials and Profits on the other hand (Table 1).

Based on the specified building blocks, many attempts of model design have been made and 
they eventually have led to two outstanding models. The first one is the Central Business Model 
Dimensions introduced by Gassman (Gassman et al., 2014; Figure 1), which suggests a 
conceptualized view of the Business Model divided into four central dimensions: the “who” 
(who is the customer), the “what” (what the customer values), the “how” (how is the value 
proposition created) and the “value” (why the business model is financially viable and why it 
is profitable).

The second attempt is the Business Model Canvas (Osterwalder et al., 2005). It groups the 
fundamental components of a Business Model into four pillars: the product (the value 
proposition), the customer interface (customers’ segments, customer’s relationship and 
distribution channels), the infrastructure management (key resources, key activities and key 
partners) and the financial structure (cost structure and revenue model). The nine building 
blocks make the Business Model Canvas (Figure 2).  
 
Figure 2. The Business Model Canvas by Osterwalder and Pigneur (2010). 
 
Quite interestingly, the Gassman’s model and the Osterwalder’s model are closely interrelated 
(Figure 3).

Figure 3. The link between Gassman's model and the Business Model Canvas 
 
Nowadays, the Business Model Canvas (BMC) is widely spread among the business 
community and among first-time entrepreneurs. It aims five main objectives: “finding the right 
model”, “testing the model before a full-scale launch”, “inducing the market to adopt the new 
model”, “continuously adapting the model in response to market feedback”, “managing 
uncertainty” (Osterwalder et al., 2010). Moreover, it offers major advantages. Firstly, it is 
visual. Thus, all types of business designers are able to understand it quickly and use it right 
away. Secondly, it facilitates team work. The BMC actually provides a shared language for 
colleagues to discuss and argue. Lastly, it has a great user interface and is easy to use. Teams 
commonly use flipcharts, pens and post-it notes to design business models at will (Amarsy, 
2015). That is the reason why it has spread worldwide, especially within the start-up 
community. 
Nevertheless, this model is sometimes criticized on the elements that may be lacking such as 
the customers’ needs and pains and the correlation with the suggested value, the strategic goals 
and the vision (Kraaijenbrink, 2012) or such as the influence of the business environment on 
the company. Thus, attempts of completion have emerged coming to the Business Model 
Environment (Osterwalder et al., 2010), the Value Proposition Designer (Osterwalder, 2013), 
the Lean Business Model Canvas (Maurya, 2010) and the Advanced Business Model Canvas 
==PAGE:8==
P a g e  8 | 98 
 
(King et al., 2010). Each of those models are interesting to analyze. The Business Model 
Environment takes into account the direct influence of external forces on the company. Those 
forces are divided into four categories: (1) the key trends (regulatory, technologies, social, 
economics), (2) the market forces (needs, demands, switching costs), (3) the macro-economic 
forces (global market conditions, capital markets, economic infrastructure, commodities) and 
(4) the industry forces (stakeholders’ influence). All of them surround the regular BMC and 
are expected to be investigated careful over time by business managers (Figure 4). The Value 
Proposition Designer is a focus on the relationship between the customers and the value that 
the company aims to provide them with (Osterwalder, 2013). It helps business designers think 
on the best value proposition to offer to the customers in order to whether release their pain or 
to create a gain (Appendix 1). The Lean Business Model Canvas advocates that the Lean 
method should be implemented into the BMC in order to easy the business design process and 
the business creation process for start-up companies (Maurya, 2010). Therefore, the building 
blocks of the regular BMC are rearranged. The “key partners” block is turned into a “problem” 
block; the “key activities” dimension is replaced by a “solution” block, the “customer 
relationship” component becomes the “unfair advantage” (information no one has but the 
company), and the key resources are turned into the key metrics (Appendix 2). Lastly, the 
Advanced Business Model Canvas has been introduced by King (2010), specifying the key 
elements within the infrastructure-related part of the BMC. All those models create an 
ecosystem of tools that are useful for business designers. 
 
Figure 4. The Business Model Environment introduced by Osterwalder. 
==PAGE:9==

Nevertheless, the outpointed drawbacks often come from a misunderstanding of the expected 
output of the Business Model Canvas and the attempt for improvements tend to increase the 
complexity of the regular BMC. Anticipating the potential counter-propositions Osterwalder 
and Pigneur specified a BMC design process divided into five phases: (1) mobilize, (2) 
understand, (3) design, (4) implement and (5) manage (Osterwalder et al., 2010). First, 
mobilizing is meant to gather all the necessary elements to design the business model, 
especially a multidisciplinary and diverse team. Secondly, understanding is getting immerged 
in the target market and gathering all the key information to design a wise business model. 
Then, designing is making and testing different business models until the business model 
design team gets the most viable one. The forth step is the implementation, which is also the 
time of truth. The business model is tested and assessed. The last phase is the adaptation and 
modification of the business model depending on market’s feedback. All those five phases 
belong to an iterative process that is expected to lead entrepreneurs and business designers to 
the right market fit (Figure 5). 
 
 
Figure 5. The iterative process to design a business model with the BMC. 
 
Last but not least, some skills are fundamental to achieve a high business-model quality: 
customer insights, ideation, visual thinking, prototyping, storytelling and scenario design 
(Osterwalder et al., 2010). First, Customer Insights is knowing the techniques to investigate a 
market, talk to the customers and promote a customer-centric business model design. Secondly, 
Ideation is the process of generating, developing, and communicating new ideas (Graham et 
al., 2004). Thirdly, Visual thinking is the skill of representing ideas visually and being able to 
==PAGE:10==
P a g e  10 | 98 
 
convey the true meaning of them. Then, Prototyping is the making of a non-definitive product 
to test the market response. Many specific techniques exist and are currently developed. 
Storytelling is the skill to tell stories about the business model and creating links between the 
building blocks to convey meaning to the audience. Eventually, Scenario design is about 
creating different scenario and testing them. Those six skills belong to the Skill Golden Circle 
for business model generation (Figure 6). When the BMC is understood, the process design is 
known and the required skills are mastered, then the business model design team can create 
innovative business models. 
 
 
Figure 6. The Six Skills Golden Circle for business model generation. 
 
As a conclusion, the BMC is a fairly recent model but it has quickly spread worldwide. 
Business designers and entrepreneurs are using it every day. It has been completed by new 
models to tackle specific design issues such as the Business Model Environment and the Value 
Proposition Designer. Yet and despite its appearance of simplicity, the BMC is not a simple 
tool to use. One of the key challenges for business designers and especially young and 
unexperienced entrepreneurs is to gather all the required elements to get the most out of the 
BMC. In that way, models, techniques and education should be combined.

4 Empirical analysis of the BMC use among unexperienced entrepreneurs 
4.1  Introduction 
Even though the BMC is widely spread worldwide, it does not meet all the needs of first-time 
entrepreneurs. They are a very specific audience who rarely find the right tools for their 
situation. In order to understand them deeper and according to the action research methodology 
that is used in this paper, European first-time entrepreneurs have been surveyed, interviewed, 
investigated and analyzed. This is what follows. The purpose of the coming lines is to answer 
four simple questions: (1) Do first-time entrepreneurs actually use the BMC and to what extent? 
(2) Do they know how to use it (related to the design process)? (3) Do they have to learn skills 
before or while using it to design their business model (related to skills)? (4) How do those 
first-time entrepreneurs feel about the BMC and would they recommend it to friends launching 
a venture? The method, the audience composition, the results and their analysis are explained 
in next paragraphs. 
4.2 Method & audience 
To be investigated the previous questions require both a quantitative analysis and a qualitative 
analysis from the first-time-entrepreneur audience. The quantitative analysis consists of a 
questionnaire that has been spread to several destinations around the world for unexperienced 
entrepreneurs to answer questions about the BMC and their relationship to it. The qualitative 
analysis is about interviewing few first-time entrepreneurs to have the opportunity to rebound 
on answers and dig into specific topics. Initially, both the data gathering and analysis had been 
meant to occur in parallel. But it has been realized that the quantitative and the qualitative 
approach could feed each other to provide more interesting outputs. Therefore, a first 
questionnaire has been sent to a population of first-time entrepreneurs, enabling to receive first-
shot answers. Then, the interviews have been led and the questionnaires have been re-designed 
to fit the entrepreneurs’ concerns more closely and determine if those were shared by a large 
number of the population (Figure 7). 
 
==PAGE:12==

Figure 7. Qualitative and quantitative information gathering and data analysis 
 
Beyond the regular questions on the respondents, the first questionnaire has contained the 
following questions: (1) Did you use the BMC to design your business model? ; (2) If so, how 
convenient was it? ; (3) What are the steps you have been through to design your BM using the 
BMC? ; (4) Did you have to learn some knowledge while or before using the BMC in order to 
be efficient using it? The second questionnaire has been fed by the results of the first 
questionnaire and the interviews. Then, some questions have been added to it: (5) how useful 
was the BMC in your opinion afterwards? ; (6) did you learn to use the BMC on your own? ; 
(7) If not, who helped you learn how to use it? (8) What are the skills you had to learn to use 
the BMC efficiently? (9) Would you recommend this tool to a friend of yours? All in all, the 
two questionnaires have been designed to be filled in within fifteen minutes and people could 
answer few sentences along with numbers.  
On the contrary of the questionnaires, the interviews contained few and global questions. 
Therefore, first-time entrepreneurs could give a global answer and the interviewer could rebond 
on specific elements. Four main questions have been asked: (1) what is your business about? 
(2) Did you use the BMC to design your business model? (3) How much did you like it? (4) 
Did you struggle to use it? Those questions are not simple to answer. They actually open a 
wide range of potential answers and related topics that enables the interviewer to collect various 
empirical data. In particular, the advantages of the BMC, its drawbacks, the entrepreneurs’ 
opinion on the BMC and the way they use it are key information to collect. 
In total, thirty-nine entrepreneurs have been surveyed twice. Five of them have been 
interviewed. All of them are unexperienced entrepreneurs and they share some more 
characteristics. Indeed, they all have at least a Bachelor of Science and they belong to top 
universities around Europe such as DTU (Denmark), The Imperial College (UK), Centrale 
Supelec (France), EPFL (Switzerland), TUM (Germany) and TU/e (The Netherlands). 
Moreover, they are quite young since their age is between 18 and 29. Those common points

The gross results for the first questionnaire are lots of learnings. First of all, it appears that 
almost all the entrepreneurs have heard about the BMC (37/39, 95%), probably from lectures 
or conferences they have attended. Yet, they don’t use it as much as it could be expected since 
only 15% of them used it a lot when designing their business. Most of the entrepreneurs used 
it just a bit (18/39, 46%). Among the first-time entrepreneurs who know about the BMC and 
use it, only 4 of them think it is a convenient tool (11%) whereas the majority of them (27/37, 
73%) believe that it has been somehow convenient to use. Moreover, when asked about their 
business design process, all the entrepreneurs complete three important steps: mobilize, design 
and implement. In other words, they gather their teams, they organize design meetings, and 
they implement their final business model into their real business with the support of the BMC. 
Nevertheless, only 23 surveyed entrepreneurs out of 37 (62%) are aware that they need some 
understanding on their business, their market and even on the tools they use. And they are even 
less for the management phase (15/37, 41%). As a summary, unexperienced entrepreneurs 
know about the core process of business model design through the BMC but they don’t dig into 
==PAGE:16==
P a g e  16 | 98 
 
the required knowledge to understand their market and the business environment where they 
operate, to use the tools efficiently and they also forget to modify and improve the BMC after 
testing it in the field. Last but not least, almost no entrepreneur has acquired a lot of knowledge 
using the BMC (2/37, 5%). Most of them agree that they have more or less learn a bit of 
knowledge (34/37, 92%).  
Eventually, the results of the first questionnaire may seem counter-intuitive. In fact, it appears 
that most of first-time entrepreneurs know about the BMC but they don’t use it extensively. 
Therefore, it can be expected that some issues in the tool may prevent them from applying it 
much more. Moreover, the entrepreneurs mostly follow a three-phase process to design their 
business through the Business Model Canvas: mobilize – design – implement. In their mind, 
the knowledge and skills required to use the BMC efficiently is not that obvious. And they 
mostly forget to come back to their BMC after testing it in the business field. Eventually, the 
BMC appears as an easy-to-use and immediate tool for business model design but no learning 
process is obviously associated with it.

First of all, it is noticeable that the figures for the common questions of the two questionnaires 
have somehow changed. This is probably due to the natural fluctuation of answers when 
surveyed people hesitate between two answers. Yet, the previous analysis of the first four 
questions’ results remains. On this strong basis, the added questions enable to dig into the topic 
of BMC use. 
When first-time entrepreneurs are asked if they think that the BMC has been useful for them 
and their business, a great majority says that it has actually been (25/37, 68%). They add that 
most of them have learnt how to use the BMC on their own or in teams (30/37, 81%) without 
==PAGE:18==
P a g e  18 | 98 
 
any external advice or mentorship. They mostly use Internet and books, where they have an 
unlimited and immediate access to information and sources to understand fast. Mentors and 
professors are not the main sources of their learnings (2/37, 5% for professors). Nevertheless, 
it does not mean that they have not attended any lecture on the BMC but that the actionable 
knowledge that they have gained does not come from the standard institutions but from their 
own work and from external sources. Concerning the skills they applied while designing the 
business model through the BMC, Ideation (19/37, 51%), Visual Thinking (13/37, 35%) and 
Prototyping (27/37, 73%) are the most pointed out. Last but not least, most of the first-time 
entrepreneurs would totally or somehow recommend the BMC for a fellow first-time 
entrepreneur (32/37, 86%). 
As a conclusion, the BMC seems to be a well-known and spread tool that almost all the 
entrepreneurs have heard about or even tested. They really believe that it is useful to understand 
the content of a business model and useful to design it. Nevertheless, the two questionnaires 
reveal two main issues. First, unexperienced entrepreneurs are not completely comfortable with 
the business model design process that underlies the BMC. Most of them apply a three-step 
process (mobilize – design – implement) and forget about to key phases which are the 
understanding of the market and the tools and the management phase when entrepreneurs are 
expected to design their business model over to adapt to the market reality. Moreover, it does 
not seem obvious that the BMC cannot be used efficiently without some specific knowledge. 
It seems that most entrepreneurs in the surveyed population rely on skills they actually learn 
during their engineering studies (Ideation, Visual Thinking and especially Prototyping). All of 
those considerations can be summarized in the fact that first-time entrepreneurs may need 
education on the design process with the BMC and the skills they actually need to perform a 
great work at business model generation. This may actually be a role for professors, since they 
are under-represented in the sources of knowledge on this matter. In what follows, the 
qualitative results specify the first-time entrepreneurs’ education needs and draw some 
guidelines on the right methods to meet them.

4.5 Takeaways on the empirical analysis 
A great deal of first-time entrepreneurs in the studied pool of entrepreneurs know at least the 
basics on the Business Model Canvas. They often use it in teams to design their potential 
business models during meetings. Nevertheless, their feeling about the BMC can seem 
paradoxical: they have the impression that it is useful but they do not like it and do not use it 
very often. This paradox is explained by the fact that the BMC enables people to think about 
the right questions on their business model - the customers and the value, the infrastructure and 
the financial model – but it may seem a bit too superficial. This impression is shared by many 
first-time entrepreneurs and comes from two main issues. First, the start-up founding teams 
may not have all the required skills to perform a high-quality work with the BMC – the skills 
being Customer Insights, Ideation, Visual Thinking, Prototyping, Storytelling and Scenario 
Design (Osterwalder et al, 2010). Secondly, the previous analysis has outlined that most of the 
founding teams do not follow the entire business model design process suggested by 
Osterwalder and Pigneur. They actually forget about the “understand” and the “manage” 
phases. An additional problem is also that they don’t feel secure about their entrepreneurship 
journey and they may expect too much from the Business Model Canvas. In other words, they 
may have expectations that are too high compared to what the tool can enable to achieve.  
Before the start-up teams can take their responsibilities on learning the skills they need and on 
following the right design process, they have to be aware of the issue. In that sense, the BMC 
can be adapted and conveyed by external stakeholders so the start-up companies can receive 
the support to achieve high-quality business model design. This opportunity is investigated in 
the next part.

5 Conceptualization: analysis of potential tools to complete the Business 
Model Canvas 
5.1 Introduction 
The objective of the conceptualization is to analyze the models, techniques and methods which 
may complete the Business Model Canvas so first-time entrepreneurs can (1) understand and 
apply the business model design process correctly, (2) determine what skills are already 
mastered by the start-up founding teams and how to acquire the basics on the lacking ones, (3) 
achieve innovative business model at the end of the process. Those three objectives will be 
called the Design Process Improvement (DPI) and the Learning Process Improvement (LPI) in 
what follows. Yet, it does not go without constraints, since the context of start-up companies 
is very specific. In fact, two conditions should be respected. First, the “augmented” BMC 
should conserve its advantages, meaning its visual aspect and easiness to use. Second, all the 
supporting tools that could be associated with the BMC should be quick and easy to learn.  
5.2 Analysis methodology and criteria 
Within the upcoming analysis, both the Design Process (DP) and the Learning Process (LP) 
are tried to be improved through conceptualization. Concerning the Design Progress, tools are 
suggested to facilitate it and help business model design team achieve a high-quality work. 
They are assessed according to specific criteria that depend on the design process phase. For 
the Learning Process, key skills for business model design are determined and different means 
for start-up founding teams to acquire the knowledge are assessed. All the tools and the models 
are extracted from the main stream literature of business strategy and entrepreneurship. All 
related sources are given. 
5.3 Design Process Improvement 
Designing a business model with the BMC is a 5-phase process, as explained previously. This 
process is actually rarely linear and steps are often performed in parallel. This is especially the 
case for the understanding and the designing phase which are particularly interconnected. In 
fact, the overall process is rather a cycle than a linear process to be operated once (Figure 10).

5.3.1 Mobilize Phase 
The first step aims to mobilize the right team, define the scope of the start-up company and 
plan the project and how the five phases will be performed. One of the challenges is to gather 
a team that will be open-minded, effective and efficient enough to innovate on business 
modeling. This mainly comes from the composition of the team. In fact, it should be cross-
functional and it should gather people from different backgrounds and different experiences. 
Nevertheless, tools can also help improve the creative thinking within the business model 
design team. Those tools are actually commonly used by workshops’ designers and facilitators. 
Most of the time, business model design team organize a meeting, also called a BM design 
session, when they think over the BMC. During this meeting, creative thinking tools are more 
than useful. They are actually creative exercises to do at the beginning of the session. The main 
criteria to choose them are that they are easy and quick to use, funny and not so demanding in 
terms of brain energy. Moreover, they should not require lots of materials. In that view, three 
workshop exercises can be particularly analyzed and are known worldwide as mind-releasing

5.3.3 Design Phase and Implement Phase 
The design phase is the realm of the BMC. Therefore, no other tools should be competing with 
it. And the implementation phase is about executing the business model that the start-up 
business model design teams have prepared. From a design process point of view, no tool can 
support this phase. As a conclusion, no specific tools will be assessed for the design and the 
implementation phases. 
 
5.3.4 Manage Phase 
The manage phase’s goal is to create the infrastructure to follow the evolution of the business 
conditions (business environment and customers’ evolution) and to compare it with the start-
up company’s business model over time. The underlying purpose is to trigger business model’s 
revisions whenever needed so it always fits the market and the business environment. In order 
to achieve such an ambitious task, responsibilities should be assigned within the company. One 
of the most known tool of responsibility assignment is the Responsibility Assignment Matrix. 
Many alternatives and adaptations exist and they should be assessed (PACSI, RACSI, RACI, 
RACIQ …).

5.3.5 Conclusion on Design Process 
Empirical data from first-time entrepreneurs have shown that they actually apply three of the 
five required steps for a great business model design process. Therefore and from the design 
process point of view, there is a key challenge: offering entrepreneurs supporting tools so they 
go through the whole design process as conveniently as possible. That is the reason why the 
mobilize phase has been suggested to implement creative thinking exercises to improve out-
of-the-box thinking. The understand phase can be supported by dedicated tools and it has 
seemed that the Business Model Environment is the best one. Concerning the design and the 
implementation phases, the only real tool is the Business Model Canvas. And the manage 
phase, that is often forgotten, can be supported by a specific Responsibility Assignment Matrix 
that has been conceptualized in this part. All in all, those models and techniques aim to facilitate 
the business model design process. Nevertheless, first-time entrepreneurs are unexperienced in 
entrepreneurship and they have to learn skills while they are designing their business model. 
Therefore, learning process supporting tools are also required.  
 
5.4 Learning Process Improvement 
The Learning Process is the acquisition of key skills for business model design by the start-up 
team. It is important to specify that the point is not to say that each member of the start-up 
==PAGE:35==
P a g e  35 | 98 
 
company should master all the skills, but that all the skills should be mastered by the start-up 
team overall. Thus, two steps are particularly important and call for tools, techniques and 
methods. The first step is the determination of what skills are actually present within the 
entrepreneurial team. The second step is to find ways to acquire the missing skills without 
allocating it too much time.  
 
5.4.1 Present skills determination 
The objective of this part is to offer the start-up teams the means to assess their own skills. As 
mentioned earlier, six main skills are required for a high-level business model design: 
customers’ insight, ideation, visual thinking, prototyping, storytelling and scenario design. 
Each of them should be mastered by the start-up team, by one member at least. Then the first 
step of the first-time entrepreneurs’ learning process is to determine whether the skills are 
mastered by the team or not.

created its own. Therefore, the best way to make a competency matrix customized for start-up 
companies is to focus on the key elements. There are three: the list of the team members, the

5.4.2 Learning the missing competencies 
In practice, very few start-up companies succeed in gathering the six key competencies for 
business model generation right away. The reason is that teams are formed according to a 
common set of ambitions, value and a shared mindset. Therefore, the start-up companies, and 
especially the first-time entrepreneurs have to find ways to learn. In this paragraph, the focus 
is put on ways to help first-time entrepreneurs acquire the required knowledge as fast as 
possible. The supporting tools and models will be dealt with later on. 
Based on the empirical data that have been gathered among first-time entrepreneurs, it has been 
showed that most of them are looking for immediate access to information and answers to their 
questions. They basically use Internet and some books. Nevertheless, they regret that no 
experienced entrepreneur explains them the basics of how the BMC works. Actually, they 
believe that interesting talks such as the TED Talks, lasting 10 to 15 minutes, could meet all 
they need. This kind of talks is inspiring, time-saving and contextualized. Therefore, the best 
way for unexperienced entrepreneurs to learn the competencies of business model generation 
that they do not have could be to organize a training program based on short talks on each of 
the six key competencies. 
5.4.3 Skills and supporting tools to learn about “Customers’ Insights” 
The “customers’ Insights” skill is the ability of a business designer to understand his/her 
customers and to design value propositions, distribution channels and customer relationships 
that fit information on customers. This skill enables to design a customer-centric business 
model in contrast with the old-fashioned organization-centric model. According to Osterwalder 
and Pigneur, there are six key questions to answer during the investigations that are led among 
customers (Table 15).

5.4.4 Skills and supporting tools to learn about “Ideation” 
The objective of this step is twofold: first, the start-up team has to generate as many business 
models as possible to increase their chances to issue promising models. Secondly, the team has 
to select the best business models so they will be tested. Ideation can be supported by tools 
related to the fields of creative thinking and inspiration can be obtained from workshops’ 
designers and facilitators.  
A great deal of workshops’ designers use a single or double-diamond structure to organize their 
workshop (Figure 17). In the specific case of business model generation, one diamond is quite 
enough.

In a very concrete way, let’s imagine that the business model design session is expected to last 
four hours. Then the idea generation phase should last up to two hours, as well as the synthesis 
phase. At the end of this process, the good business models should be clearly determined. 
Moreover, it is possible to go into more details in the creative session organization. Workshops’

the team members actually write on the Business Model Canvas or pin post-it notes on it: they 
concretely generate a view of the business model they have discussed. Most of the time, the 
last two steps (Discussing and Building) are iteratively performed. Examples of useful

5.4.5 Skills and supporting tools to learn about “Visual Thinking” 
The objective of Visual Thinking are twofold. First, it aims to depict a big picture of the 
business model in a glance. Indeed, it is extremely important since any element of the business 
model is interconnected to all of the others. Modifying one is modifying all. Secondly, 
designing visually the business model enables the design team members to be all on the same 
page and share the same language. Therefore, discussions can go faster and deeper. 
There is no real supporting tool for Visual Thinking, but the Business Model Canvas itself. 
Nevertheless, techniques of visualization exist and should be applied by start-up companies. 
The two most important are the use of post-it notes and visual storytelling. Those are 
complementary. Thus, they are not compared and selected in this part, but they are 
interconnected so business model designers can use them together. 
The first technique to tackle is the post-it notes use. The basic principle is: (1) the business 
design team prints or draws a big BMC and put it on a wall; (2) when talking about each of the 
nine blocks, ideas are written on the post-it notes and assigned to the dedicated block on the 
giant BMC picture; (3) the ideas are discussed, argued and selected all along the way. 
Moreover, psychologists have shown that if someone gets an idea during a brainstorming phase 
and try to retain it, he/she cannot think and follow the brainstorming flow and his/her 
concentration drops down. In order to avoid this phenomena that hurts the idea generation, 
pots-it notes are marvelous. The only thing to do is, for the participant who has an idea which 
is not in the current discussion topics, to write this idea on a post-it notes, put it aside and come 
back to it when it is the right moment. Proceeding that way avoids the loss of precious ideas 
and enables all the design team members to focus 100% on discussing the current matter. 
Moreover, authors like Osterwalder and Pigneur suggests three additional guidelines to follow: 
(1) use thick marking pens, (2) write only one element per post-it note, and (3) write only few 
words per note to capture the essential point (Osterwalder et al., 2010). In order to facilitate the 
understanding and adoption of the previous golden rules, a “Golden Rules for Visual Thinking” 
==PAGE:45==
P a g e  45 | 98 
 
poster should be prepared and pinned on the wall for every business model designers to see 
(Figure 20) 
 
Figure 20. Golden rules for Visual Thinking applied to business model generation 
 
The second Visual Thinking design technique is the visual storytelling. The concept of 
storytelling is explored in more details further but some elements can already be shared. The 
basic principle is that the business model designers choose one of the five ways to explore a 
business model (Figure 19) and create a story from that point. They can actually pretend to be 
a customer (customer-centric view), or a resource manager (resource-driven view), or a value 
proposition designer (offer-driven view), or a start-up’s financial agent (finance-driven view), 
or all of them in one (multiple-epicenter driven). Then the business model designers imagine 
the experience of the character they have chosen throughout the BMC. At each key step, they 
write it on post-it notes. As the process ends, they have an overview of the experience of the 
character, its constraints, the value created along the way, etc. This technique is quick and 
enables to learn a great deal of information.

5.4.7 Skills and supporting tools to learn about “Storytelling” 
Start-up companies can grow partly because the founding team succeeds in engaging people in 
their business and their vision. Therefore, knowing how to communicate is fundamental. There 
are many ways to do so, but one of the best ways is to tell a story. The objective of storytelling 
is to place the listeners into a story so they can feel the experience and perhaps they can 
recognize their own. Storytellers make the ‘new’ tangible, clear and they manage to engage 
people. This is why it is often the most efficient technique. Even though communication skills 
are somehow natural (some people are better at this than others), techniques can be taught so a 
fair storytelling can be performed. Four elements have to be taken into account to create a good 
story: the perspective to adopt, the overall story plan, the communication techniques and the 
communication media. 
There are basically two main perspectives to adopt for a story: the customer’s perspective or 
the company’s perspective. The choice depends on the audience and on the context. If business 
model designers pitch their business model before investors, then they will adopt the 
company’s perspective so they can clarify the business infrastructure and how they will make 
money, so the investors will get a return on investment. But when pitching to journalists, the 
customers’ perspective is better. In fact, through newspapers, blogposts or TV spots, business 
model designers will potentially reach their customers’ segments. So they have to provide a 
feeling of understanding about their pain or their willingness to get some gains (Figure 22).

5.4.8 Skills and supporting tools to learn about “Scenario Design” 
The objective of scenario design is alike the objective of visual thinking, prototyping and 
storytelling: it aims to make intangible things tangible. Two types of scenario can be applied 
(Osterwalder et al., 2010): customer-setting oriented or business-environment oriented. The 
first one focuses on the user’s experience and analyze all the potential ways the user could use 
the product or service, or substitute it. In best cases, such scenario are mixed with customers’ 
insights to provide a strengthened analysis. The second type of scenario is about anticipating 
the potential changes in the business environment and the way the business model could be 
affected. They are all summarized in the Business Model Environment model (Figure 3). 
For the customer-setting-oriented scenario design, the idea is to start thinking from one of the 
customers’ segments and depict the journey of the customer on the customer side of the BMC. 
In other words, you successively ask those three questions: (1) how is the customer addressed 
(customer’s relationship), (2) is the value proposition the right fit for this specific customer’s 
segment, (3) is the value delivered wisely for this specific customers’ segment. Once the 
customer’s journey is depicted, then the scenario designers should ask the question “what if”. 
What if I provide a web-platform-based service and the customer has just a mobile device? 
What if he has Safari instead of Chrome as a browser? Answering those questions enables the 
scenario designers and the business model designers to forecast all the possible issues from the 
business model. Thus, they can look for potential solutions that could strengthen the business 
model. 
The business-environment-oriented scenario design is a complete different exercise and the 
scenario designer should apply the following process: (1) they brainstorm on the main external 
issues that could have an impact on the start-up company, (2) they select the two most important 
(3) and they draw a two-axis graph, write the two trends and study the four potential scenario 
(Figure 24). This is also called Future Scenario.

In order to understand the process, an example is necessary. Let’s assume that the start-
company is provided drugs to African people in a very poor and politically unstable country. 
Two main issues could be the cost of drugs from western laboratories and the chances of a war 
to start. In order to design a future scenario, the following graph is drawn (Figure 25).

Figure 25. An example of a business-environment-oriented business model scenario design 
 
Four business model canvas are positioned in the four quarters and it is for the business model

conditions. What if the country is at war and the price of the drugs skyrockets? What is there 
is a war but the drugs stay cheap? What happens if it is a peace time but the drugs are 
expensive? What if they are cheap in peace time? For each of the four cases, the business model 
will be completely different, especially on the infrastructure side since the start-up company 
has to adapt its operations to the external conditions.  
All in all, scenario design helps the business model design team to think of potential 
alternatives for business model and to manage the risk of market conditions’ shift by 
preparation. Therefore, this is a very important skill to master in a start-up team.

7.3 Adaptation on the Business Model Generation Program 
Unlike the BMC, the BMG Framework makes the business model design process central and 
the BMC becomes the key tool for the design phase of the BM design process.

execute it, (2) create learning videos on the different tools so people can rely on them whenever 
they need to learn, (3) create a visual tool exactly as the BMC so it can make the Framework 
more actionable.

8 Conclusion 
Starting a company is one of the most difficult professional choice to make and it is especially 
true for first-time entrepreneurs who have to learn about everything while executing. For one 
decade, the Business Model Canvas has been the most used model for entrepreneurs to think 
and design their business model. It enabled them to think over the right questions and to fill in 
an easy-to-use and understandable model. Nevertheless, this model is not meant for 
entrepreneurs. Scholars and strategy consultants use it a lot as well. In other words, it does not 
take into account the specificities of first-time entrepreneurs. Therefore, this thesis aimed to 
build a new framework based on the Business Model Canvas: the Business Model Generation 
Framework. This Framework’s central element is not the Business Model Canvas but the 
Business Model design process. Then, the BMC is the central tool. In order to support the 
learning curve of unexperienced entrepreneurs on business modeling, design tools and learning 
tools have been provided, conveying the key elements to take into account while being an 
entrepreneur. Last but not least, programs for video lectures have been provided. All of those 
elements have been summarized in the Visual BMG Framework, which is the tool that this 
thesis aims to promote. Tests have been led among entrepreneurs as part of the Visual BMG 
Framework building process, but the length of a thesis is not enough to have a clear view of 
how this tool can be used by a great deal of first-time entrepreneurs. Therefore and as a final 
wish, it is expected to be used and improved by researches and entrepreneurs over time.
