Abstract
In the contemporary world, CO2 is one of the primary source of anthropogenic
greenhouse gas emissions, causing the well established problem of climate change.
Many methods and technologies have been developed in response to the urgent need
to address climate change and reduce greenhouse gas emissions, particularly CO2.
One of the established methods is using an absorption process with an alkaline
solvent.
To this day it has been observed that regenerating the solvent with a
desorption process in a stripper, is rather energy demanding. Alternative methods,
such as solvent regeneration with an electrochemical cell, are being explored as it
offers the possibility of reducing the energy demand while maintaining a high purity.
The project was carried out to analyse a rather unexplored field, CO2 capture cou-
pled with electrochemical mediation regeneration process, conducted with potassium
hydroxide as solvent. For an easier operation, the system was split in two parts, the
absorber and the electrochemical cell and operated in isolated modes.
The absorption process was examined for two main parameters, capture percentage
and the loading of the CO2 rich solvent, by varying parameters such as the liquid
and gas flows fed to the absorber. For each experiment the system was operated
until a steady state was obtained. The research outcomes suggested that to achieve
a higher capture percentage of CO2 in the column, low loading of the lean solvent is
beneficial. Furthermore it is favourable to operate the system at a high liquid gas
ratio, that is, at high liquid flow compared to the gas flow injected.
Analysis of the results from the electrochemical cell showed that the system operated
with good functionality in batch mode, however when compared to a theoretical
model established for the system, it could be concluded that the gas flow meter
used did not display accurate results. The experiments conducted in continuous
mode never reached a steady state, which was further confirmed by comparison to a
theoretical model. Possible causes of the failure could be linked to a leakage observed
in the system, pump calibration, lack of flow measurements within the system and
the procedure used.
Evaluation of the energy consumption for solvent regeneration revealed that, for
all experiments, it was roughly 4.0-6.0 times higher if calculated for the measured
gas flow than if calculated from the gas flow based on Faraday’s Law rate constant
and roughly 1.8-6.5 times higher than the modelled value. Additionally, the results
showed 5.0-18.0 times higher energy consumption if compared to energy consump-
tion values from other studies in the field.
The study provided useful insight on the mini pilot plant for CO2 absorption with
an electrochemical regeneration process, using KOH solvent. However, deviations
from expected trends in the absorption process and obstacles encountered during the
operation of the electrochemical cell suggest that further improvements of the system
must be made to integrate the two parts and further for the system to compete
against already established technologies in the industry. Despite the challenges met
during the study, a fully functional process seems promising towards upscaling of an
energy-effective process with the implementation of further optimization, for CO2
capture.

Introduction
Climate change has been a rising problem for the last several years, where CO2
emissions to the atmosphere is one of the main contributor to the problem. The
higher demand for industrial activities such as combustion of fossil fuels and burning
of oils and natural gas has led to increasing concentration anthropogenic greenhouse
gases in the global atmosphere. The greenhouse gases further blanket the Earth as
they trap the sun’s heat, which leads to global warming and even further climate
change.
Warming of the Earth’s surface entails impacts to ecosystems, people,
communities and infrastructure, where it increases the frequency of extreme events,
such as wild fires, severe storms, rising of the ocean and warming of the ocean, to
name a few [1]. These extreme events provoke the security of food supplies and
water, hence the human health [1].
Solutions to climate change are often complicated and take a long time to develop
since the problem is composed of many elements, such as burning of fossil fuels,
deforestation, increase in livestock farming, fluorinated gases etc. Due to this and
the fact that the energy demand in the world is only increasing, solutions are being
developed for each and every element that slows down the effects of the climate
change rather than prevent it. An example of one of those solutions is carbon cap-
ture, which allows the continued use of fossil fuels in power plants however at the
same time minimizes CO2 emissions to the atmosphere [2]. Today, majority of ex-
isting post combustion CO2 capture technologies are amine based, which commonly
involves disadvantages in regards to solvent loss or volatility, degradation, corrosion
and high heat of solution [3]. Hence alternative solvents are being examined such
as potassium hydroxide, KOH. Despite KOH being a promising solution, there are

2.2
CO2 Capture Technologies
Carbon capture is a process which aims to trap CO2 and hence lessen the impact
of greenhouse gas emissions to the environment and the effects of climate change.
The process removes carbon dioxide, CO2, from other gases released by industrial
and energy-related sources [15]. The main application of CO2 capture is used more
for large scale point sources as for smaller sources the capture gets more diﬀicult
and expensive [16]. The captured CO2 can then be stored underground, used for
industrial purposes or used for enhanced oil recovery [15]. Power generation, cement
production, refineries, the production of chemicals, and other industrial processes
can all benefit from the use of carbon capture technology. In power generation,
carbon capture is often combined with carbon storage, in which the captured CO2
is stored underground or in depleted oil and gas fields, instead of being released into
the atmosphere. [15]
Capture of CO2 from the combustion of fossil fuels and/or biomass can be done
using one of the four methods below [16]. A simple flow sheet of the four methods
can be seen in Figure 2.5. The main principles of each method will be explained
separately in the paragraphs below.
• Pre-combustion capture - occurs before the combustion process.
• Oxy-fuel combustion capture - occurs after the combustion process in nearly
pure oxygen, as the CO2 generated in the oxy-combustion is separated.
• Capture from industrial process streams.
• Post-combustion capture - occurs after the combustion process.

In pre-combustion capture, the primary fuel is gasified in a reactor with air,
oxygen or steam to produce syngas, primarily composed of CO and hydrogen. The
syngas is then reacted with steam in a water gas shift reactor to give CO2 and more
hydrogen [17]. Afterwards, CO2 is removed typically through a physical or chemical
absorption process, where physical solvents such as polypropylene and methanol are
used commercially and chemical absorbents, such as carbonates. The removal of
CO2 yields a fuel rich in hydrogen which is then burnt. These system can achieve
more then 90% CO2 capture through solvent or absorbent pre-combustion capture,
however it reduces the eﬀiciency [17]. Furthermore, these systems need a gasification
unit which causes it to become more expensive [17].
Several technologies are used when it comes to pre-combustion capture systems, such
as steam reforming and partial oxidation of gas and light hydrocarbons, gas heated
reformer and gasification of coal, petroleum residues or biomass [16]. One of the
most known technology is the integrated gasification combined cycle (IGCC), which
uses gas turbines and synthesis gas for power generation [16]. When it comes to
carbon dioxide capture, it has some advantages over other technologies, such as low
levels of air-pollutant emissions when removed from syngas and significantly smaller
process stream volumes when compared to flue gas streams from combustion [16].
Oxy-fuel combustion capture can be divided by two main types, that is oxy-fuel
combustion systems and a more recent technology, the chemical looping process [16].
The oxy-fuel combustion systems is a combustion process where burning of fuel is
done by using nearly pure oxygen instead of air, which results in a flue gas stream
that is mainly composed of CO2 and water vapor. This process eliminates nitrogen
emissions from the flue gas. In these systems, the oxy-fuel combustion chamber
provides heat to a separate fluid by heat transfer through a surface [16]. It can
be used to generate electricity in a boiler with a steam or gas cycle or for process

The absorption solvent based method relies on a reaction between the captured CO2
and a chemical solvent mediated by temperature or pressure swings of the solvents
[20]. This method is the most widely used post-combustion CO2 capture method

main heat exchanger with a pump to a desorber or stripper to regenerate the solvent.

in the stripper and sent for compression, as per Figure 2.6 [17, 21]. The solvents typ-

diethanolamine (DEA), or methyl diethanolamine (MDEA) in aqueous solution [17,
19]. Solvents such as aqueous ammonia, amino acid salts and special amines, have

CO2 absorption, aqueous potassium hydroxide solution reacts with the CO2 in a
packed bed or spray tower and forms potassium carbonate, K2CO3, and water. The
reaction is exothermic and reversible, making it possible to regenerate the absorbent
which is an environmentally desired feature of the solvent [30]. Furthermore is the
solvent rather inexpensive and is non-toxic. This method is well established and
effective for CO2 capturing from industrial gas streams [26, 30].

2.2.2
CO2 Capture Coupled with Electrochemical
Regeneration of the Solvent
For CO2 absorption processes, it is of high importance to lower the cost of the CO2
separation process by improving the current procedures and solvent, as the cost is
a limiting factor for large-scale applications [26, 19]. As has been mentioned before,
one major obstacle in the chemical absorption method is the development of solvents
with low energy requirements in the capturing process as the solvent regeneration
is one of the most energy intensive steps of the process [19, 22, 31].
As an alternative to amine scrubbing, the technology of using alkaline solutions
as absorbents has been studied. In this type of a process the capture of CO2 is
similarly, typically performed in a two step process consisting of CO2 capture in an
absorber and electrochemical regeneration system [22] as illustrated in Figure 2.7.
The absorption is performed with either solid sorbents or aqueous solutions. The
solid sorbents could be alkaline earth metal oxides, layered double oxides, carbon or
metal organic frameworks which have an advantage over amines as they are cheap
materials, considered to be less corrosive and more selective for the process [22].
The aqueous solutions being used are alkaline hydroxides and carbonates and have a
number of advantages over amines, including lower toxicity and improved resistance
to flue gas contaminants [20], where alkaline hydroxide solutions such as NaOH and
KOH have received the most research attention for scrubbing purposes [22, 31]. The
main focus of this study is to improve the research on CO2 absorption by aqueous
solution of potassium hydroxide coupled with electrochemical regeneration of the
solvent, hence the research will be conducted from here on with that solvent.
A general description of the process as it will be conducted in this study hence
includes a packed column with an aqueous potassium hydroxide, KOH, solvent used
to absorb CO2 from a flue gas source which results in a CO2 rich solution. After the
absorber, the CO2 rich solution is pumped to an electrochemical unit, which consists
of stacks of bipolar membranes (BPM), anion exchange membranes (AEM), and/or
cation exchange membranes (CEM). In the electrochemical unit, electrical potential
is applied to oxidize the CO2 captured in the CO2 rich solution which produces a
concentrated stream of CO2 is which is then removed from the system. The solvent
is then regenerated in the electrochemical cell and recirculated to the absorber [22].

3.1
Absorption of CO2 in Aqueous Potassium
Hydroxide Solvent
The absorption part of the system consists of flue gas stream, an absorber, KOH
solution stream or CO2 lean solution stream, clean gas stream and a CO2 rich
solution stream. The flue gas stream is an artificial flue gas stream comprised of
carbon dioxide, CO2 and air and it will contain about 15% CO2. All the gas flows
are lead to the fume hood from cylinders located in the same room.
Packed columns are often used in thermal engineering processes such as rectification,
absorption and extraction and are mainly used for counter-current gas/liquid flow.
As CO2 capture with electrochemical scrubbing involves an absorption process, a
packed column will be used.
It is filled with a certain type of packaging, 3mm
Dixon rings.
The rings have a random structure in the absorber to achieve the
desired contact surface which guarantees a homogeneous gas and liquid flow across
the cross section of the column. The selection of packaging depends on the corrosive
properties of the system to be separated and the operating temperature [32]. The
details of the selection will however not be elaborated here as it was not a part of
the thesis project.
Other important parameters of packed columns are the diameter and the height.
The diameter distinguishes how much gas can be treated. Too small diameter leads
to too much gas flow which further causes the residence time in the column to be
too short. For the design of the column used in this project the inner diameter, di
is 5.7 cm or 0.057 m. The height of the column is an important parameter as it
can influence the capacity, eﬀiciency and the pressure drop in the column [32]. The
longer the column is, the longer time for the adsorption process to take place, which
leads to a higher eﬀiciency. However, increasing the bed height beyond a certain
limit may not lead to an increase in capacity nor eﬀiciency due to mass transfer
limitations. Furthermore, increasing the bed height leads to a higher pressure drop
which can consequently limit flow rate through the column [32]. The height suitable
for this project was determined to be 1.5 meters and the column is designed for a
counter-current operation as liquid solutions will be fed to the column at the top,
while a gas mixture, representing the artificial flue gas, enters the column below the
packing section.

Different solutions were made for the different types of experiments conducted in

In this project all the solutions made are based on molality (m), that is moles of

to change with vapor pressure and temperature and is hence undesired. Therefore
molality is used, as its value does not change with temperature.

4.4.4.3
Modelling Electrochemical Cell Experiments
In this study, the application of the extended UNIQUAC model with an Excel inter-
face built by researchers in the field was used as a theoretical model. The model is
a thermodynamic activity coeﬀicient model designed for electrolyte solutions, both
aqueous and mixed solvents. The model input variables were the loading, αrich, and
molality of the carbon rich solution, the mass of solution in each recycling loop, the
removal rate of potassium ions, the time interval of the experiment and the room’s
temperature and pressure.
With the given inputs the model calculates the output moles of the relevant species
in the system for liquid, gas and solid phase. As there is no solid phase in the
process of CO2 capture coupled with electrochemical regeneration of the solvent, the
model only gave results for the liquid and gas phase. From the outputs, interesting
parameters could be examined, such as the development of pH levels over time
and the gas production in the system. Modelling the system allows for enhanced
understanding of the system and a better interpretation of the data obtained from
the experiments.

In the absorption column, the solvent is fed from the top and the gas enters the col-
umn from the bottom. As the solvent flows down the column the chemical reaction
between the liquid and gas initiate and the solvent increases in carbon loading. At
the pinch point in the absorber, the solute concentration in the gas phase is equal
to the solute concentration in the liquid phase. At this point the driving force for
the mass transfer is low, which lowers the mass transfer rate and is presented as
the lowest point on the temperature profile [35]. However, the highest point on
the temperature profile has the greatest CO2 absorption rate and is often called a
temperature bulge [35].
When the amount of solvent is insuﬀicient, the absorption rate tends to be greatest
at the top of the packed column and the pinch point is at the bottom of the column.
However for a scenario where the solvent is in excess relative to the amount of CO2
in the gas fed to the column, the pinch point tends to be on the top of the column
and the absorption occurs at the bottom. This trend can be seen for L/G ratio of
0.47 from Figure 5.4, that is the temperature bulge is at the top of the column which
further means that the reaction mostly takes place there. Then when increasing the
liquid flow in the columnm, referring to the latter two cases from Figure 5.4, the
temperature bulge moves to a lower point in the column. In these two cases the
temperature bulge is more obvious for L/G ratio of 2.67 as the temperature at the
middle of the column is higher. If the liquid flow would be increased even further
compared to the gas flow in the column and become excess compared to the gas,
then the temperature bulge would be expected to be observed at the bottom of the
column as the liquid would absorb the gas as soon as it is fed to the absorber.

flow of the lean solvent out of the system. Releasing the pressure by opening the
pump head on pump A2 was used during the batch experiment, however it is not the
most convenient option when working in continuous mode, as it would add to the
requirements of monitoring the operation. Both pumps moreover play an important
role in maintaining the mass balance of the system correct. Hence looking at the
acid side of the system, three pumps are directly related to the pressure on the acid
side, that is pumps A1, B1 and B2. Pump A1 was used to regulate the flow from the
rich solvent and is essential in the system setup. Pumps B1 and B2 were then used
for the recirculation of the solvent in the system. The two pumps were originally
placed at the spots seen in Figure 5.12 to maintain the liquid level of the gas-liquid
separator. As the separator has an open gas outlet leading to the gas flow meter,
pressure is released from the system at that point.

bined with the liquid from pump A1 in a small three way piece. Combining two
liquid streams with different flow rates can cause a pressure build-up as when the
two streams are integrated together, the total flow rate increases. As a result of
this finding, a try-out experiment was conducted without pump B2 as presented in

Conclusion
The project covered in this report aimed to optimize CO2 absorption coupled with
an electrochemical regeneration process. For experimental operation, the mini pilot
plant operated in this project was split up in two parts to simplify the process, the
absorption process or absorber and the regeneration process or the electrochemical
cell, which were operated in isolated modes. Experiments were conducted on the ab-
sorber to analyze it’s functioning and to optimize it’s performance. Operating and
configuring the system around the electrochemical cell proved to be considerably
more challenging than initially anticipated. The electrocemical cell itself seemed
sensitive to its surroundings and the experiments conducted on it, as it broke down
several times during the short period of the experimental phase. Despite the chal-
lenges encountered during the experimental phase, valuable results were obtained
that can contribute to future work.
From the literature review it is evident that pollution from greenhouse gases, espe-
cially CO2, is undeniably a significant problem in our contemporary world. Different
technologies as well as different solvents for CO2 capture were studied through the
literature review. The studies showed that there is a need for less energy intensive
and more inexpensive solutions for large-scale applications. The absorption process
conducted with KOH solvent, coupled with electrochemical regeneration of the sol-
vent meets the demands of the market, however the process definitely needs further
studies and optimizations before application to large-scale industries.
The analysis of the experiments conducted on the absorber showed that the two
main parameters examined, capture percentage and rich loading, are not affected in
the same way for the parameters varied in each experiment. The analysis indicated
that in order to achieve a higher capture percentage of CO2 in the absorber a
solution with relatively low loading or even no loading is beneficial. Furthermore,
it is more effective to have a relatively high liquid flow and lower gas flow, which
further gives a large L/G ratio.
Furthermore, there was a greater systematicity
and predictability observed for the tendencies of CO2 capture compared to the rich
loading as the capture percentage mostly followed expected patterns. In contrast,
the trends observed for the rich loading showed less variation and were less influenced
by the parametric variations. The experiments however showed that to obtain the
highest rich loading, the system should be operated with a high loaded lean solvent,
with low gas flow and hence a low L/G ratio.
Based on the interpretation of the results from the electrochemical cell experiments,
the system showed a good functionality when operated in batch mode. Two theo-
retical models based on the removal rate of the cell, calculated from the measured
gas flow on one hand and Faraday’s Law on the other hand indicated that the gas
flow meter did not display accurate results of the gas flow, where the same factor
was observed between the two removal rates and the respective gas flows. For the
experiments conducted in a continuous mode, it is evident that the system never
reached a steady state and hence there is no significant difference in the trends ob-
98
Optimization of CO2 capture coupled with electrochemical mediated regeneration process.
==PAGE:113==
served for the pH and conductivity levels in continuous mode if compared to batch
mode. The extended UNIQUAC model was used to model the system in steady
state, where comparison between the experiments and the model did not give good
results. Several possible reasons could be the cause of the deviations observed, such
as the leakage observed in the cell during the experimental phase amongst the effect
of the calibration of pump A and unknown flow rate of the overflow. Furthermore
the procedure used for the experiments could possibly be a cause for the system to
not reach a steady state.
Evaluation of the energy consumption for regeneration of the solvent showed that
for the measured gas flow of CO2, the energy consumption is about 4.6-6.0 times
higher than for the estimated gas flow calculated from the removal rate of K+ in the
cell for all experiments and roughly 1.8-6.5 times higher than the modelled values.
Similarly, is it 5.0-18.6 times higher if compared to energy consumption values from
other studies in the same field. As a strong hypothesis has been established on the
gas flow meter, it can be concluded that the energy consumption calculated from
the measured gas flow rate does not represent the real energy consumption of the
system.
Deviations from the expected trends and the steady state were observed with anal-
ysis for the absorber experiments, where several reasons could have influenced the
results. Moreover, the operations on the electrochemical cell did not go as expected
and as a result of the analysis, it has been identified that the system could be im-
proved in several areas to enhance its functionality. From the findings of the project,
recommendations on the system improvements to address the challenges identified
during the experimental phase can be made for absorber and the electrochemical
cell in isolated modes.

Recommendations for Future Work
Absorber
For future experiments performed on the absorber, few things could be improved
for more precise and reliable results. The improvements that could be effective are
listed down below.
• The calibration of pump A could include set points for lower flow rates. At
the lower flow rates it would be beneficial to measure more set points for a
better precision of the actual flow rate regulated by the pump.
• For even better results from the calibration of the pump, several calibration
curves could be made for the different solutions used for the experiments.
• Similarly for the gas flow calibration, even though the set points used for the
calibration curve fitted the set points used in the experiments better than for
the liquid flow, it would be beneficial to include more measurements for better
precision.
• It would be advantageous to replicate the experiments to confirm the reliabil-
ity and validity of the results obtained from the initial experiment. Replication
could also provide a better overview of discrepancies or inconsistencies that

methods, approach or tools used that might have contributed to the differ-

Including these improvements in the methods and experiments performed on the

effect on the results from the system. After implementation of those improvements
the process could additionally be examined for variation of other parameters such
as the inlet concentration of CO2, as if the process would be applied to an industrial
scale it would be beneficial as different industries have a different composition of
flue gas.
Electrochemical cell
As the results of the electrochemical cell indicate, the system has a great potential
of being a good mitigation action to climate change, however it needs to be im-
proved in many ways before it can be applied to pilot scale measurements and even
further to industrial scale. Recommendations for the next steps in developing the
electrochemical cell part of the mini pilot plant are listed below.
• The leakage observed in the system has to be addressed to obtain a better
operation of the system. As the leakage seems to be within compartments of
the cell, hence the cell should be disassembled and examined. Any defects in
the membranes could cause the leakage. If that is the case the membranes
should be removed or exchanged for new intact membranes.
• The model presented for the batch mode experiment indicated that the gas
flow measured from the system is inaccurate. Hence further examination on
the readings of the gas flow meter should be conducted.
• The pH level on the alkaline side stabilized at 14.43 in all the experiments of
the electrochemical cell. This is due to the fact that the probes have a range
of 0-14. Using probes with a wider range would be beneficial for the analysis
of the data from the system and it would make it easier to determine whether
the system is in steady state or not.
• As the procedure used for continuous mode operation did not give steady state,
the procedure could be changed to some extent for further investigation of the
process. A recommendation for a different procedure is described below.
– Each experiment could be started up in batch to identify the systems
behaviour. Then from the batch mode, as soon as CO2 is being desorbed
in the system, that is at α = 1, the influent flow could be injected to
the system and the overflow opened between the alkaline side and the
outlet of the system. Then to obtain a steady state within the system,
the current applied could be adjusted.
• Testing of the system is recommended to be conducted with a high loaded
rich solvent and a low molality. This would ensure that less time is spent on
each experiment and makes the procedure more eﬀicient for the researcher.
Moreover, it would be beneficial time-wise to make test runs with a high

• When a fully functional operation has been reached for the electrochemical
cell, the experiments from the experimental plan set up for the project can
be conducted and an optimization process started for the system. The opti-
mization should be aimed to regenerate the solvent by minimizing the energy
consumption of the cell so the system can compete with other technologies
already being implemented to industrial scale.
The implementation of these improvements should give a clearer image of the results
and tendencies of the absorber and allow for further optimization of the absorption
process. Furthermore, addressing the challenges met during the experimental phase
of the electrochemical cell will allow for a fully functioning system and an easier
integration of the two units.
Overall, this study provided useful insight into the process of CO2 absorption with
an electrochemical regeneration process using KOH solvent, even though the system
was only operated in two isolated modes. The process behaviour and performance
of the absorber was studied using systematic examination of various parameters,
including liquid and gas flow and loading of the lean solvent. Similarly the regener-
ation process was examined, however in less extent to parameter variation but more
towards a good functionality. From the study it is evident that the regeneration
process needs further research and development to reach the desired functionality.
In conclusion the study provides valuable insights for future studies in CO2 capture
with an electrochemical regeneration process, with the aim of addressing the global
greenhouse gas emissions problem.
