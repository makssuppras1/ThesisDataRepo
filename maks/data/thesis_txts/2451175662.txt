Abstract
The aim of this thesis is to characterize and follow the development of the microstruc-
ture of nitriding steel(31CrMoV, 15CrMoV5-9, 34CrAlNi7 and 34CrAIMo5) treated
by diﬀerent nitriding process.
The compound layer and microstructure of 4 grades nitriding steel nitrided with
various nitriding methods were investigated. A relatively high nitriding potential
experiment was performed to understand the diﬀerent behavior of various steel dur-
ing the nitriding process. A thermobalance was used for investigating the kinetics
of the compound layer formation with precise control. In order to characterize the
early stage of compound layer formation, low potential nitriding was applied. The
project focus on the characterization of the microstructures of the nitrided layer.
The microstructures of the bulk and the nitrided layers were investigated with a
combination of X-Ray Diﬀraction (XRD), Light Optical Microscopy (LOM), Scan-
ning Electron Microscopy (SEM) and Electron Backscatter Diﬀraction. The results
of the material characterization give a detail description of the microstructure and
some insights into the kinetics governing the formation of the compound layer.
Diﬀerent morphology of the nitrided layer was identiﬁed between the steel with
various alloying elements, particularly Aluminum. A unique, plate-like structure
was observed in the steel with Al as nitride forming element, the mechanism was
discussed based on the literature. The development of the nitrided layer in all 4
grades of steel was investigated by changing the time and nitriding potential during
the nitriding process, providing a wide range of characterized surfaces in nitriding
steel.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Gaseous thermochemical surface treatment by nitriding was
rst recognized and developed in the early 1920s and continues to play an important
role in the industrial heat treatment of steel[1]. In order to improve the ability of
compo- nents to resist fatigue, wear and corrosion, this treatment is often used in
the manufacturing of gears and bearings for automotive and aerospace applications.
The nitrided case microstructures determine the properties of the nitrided steels.
In order to ensure the desired microstructure evolution, suitable process parameter
selection and accurate control of the process are important. Understanding of the
nitriding mechanisms is therefore essential to control the process.
1.1
Problem background
The case developing during nitriding can be subdivided in a compound layer created
at the surface and a diﬀusion zone underneath[2].
The compound layer usually
consists of iron (carbon)nitrides γ′ −Fe4N1−x and ϵ−Fe2(N, C)1−Z, which improve
wear resistance and corrosion performance in an atmospheric environment.At the
nitriding temperature, the diusion zone grows into the steel and, depending on the
composition of steel, consists of an interstitial solution of nitrogen in the iron lattice
and alloying element nitrides,(e.g. CrN and AlN)[2]. This can be illustrated by
Figure 1.1.1.
The hardenability of the material can be tailored by adding diﬀerent elements, the
steel specialized for the nitriding purpose usually contain the important elements like
chromium, nickel, aluminum, molybdenum and vanadium[3]. The diﬀusion zone can
signiﬁcantly improve fatigue performance by creating compressive residual stress[2].

Chapter 2
Theory
2.1
Gaseous nitriding
Nitriding is a thermochemical diﬀusion process, which involves the diﬀusion of ni-
trogen into the surface of the workpiece and improves the material performance by
a modiﬁcation of the chemical composition and microstructure in the area adja-
cent to the surface[4]. This case-hardening process for the steels usually carried out
at a relatively low temperature and does not cause a phase transformation as in
carburizing[5]. For this reason, nitriding is widely used in industry because the pre-
cise dimensional control of the component can be achieved. The nitriding processes
can be classiﬁed according to the media used to provide the nitrogen, the donating
source can generally be gases (gaseous nitriding), plasma (plasma nitriding), liquids
(salt-bath nitriding)[5].
During the gaseous nitriding, nitrogen is diﬀused into the steel surface from a con-
trolled atmosphere which usually consists of an ammonia/ hydrogen gas mixture by
holding the workpiece at a suitable temperature, typically between 500 to 590◦C.
The gaseous nitriding provides more accurate control of the chemical potential and
nitrogen activity among the various nitriding methods, which is essential for the
components with complicated geometrical shapes achieve homogeneous hardening
of the surface[6].

2.3
Development of the compound layer
2.3.1
Kinetics of compound layer formation
If the development of iron nitrides is not allowed due to a low chemical potential of
nitrogen in the nitriding medium, the nitrided zone consists only of a ferrite with
concentration depth gradient of dissolved nitrogen in iron matrix; if the nitriding
potential is suitable for formation of nitride, the local concentration of nitrogen at
the surface determines the type of nitride(cf. the Lehrer diagram in Figure2.2.3).The
stages of compound layer formation during gaseous nitriding of iron at temperature
below 592◦C are illustrated by Figure 2.3.1[11].
Usually, the ﬁrst phase formed
thermodynamically on the surface of iron is γ′ −Fe4N1−x. The driving force for γ′
nucleation is the exceeding the maximum solubility of nitrogen in the iron matrix
adjacent to the surface[5]. The actual nitrogen content at the iron surface determines
the incubation time for the nucleation of γ′ nitride, which is the net eﬀect between
the ﬂuxes of nitrogen atoms arriving at and leaving from the surface[12].
The
dissociation rate of ammonia(reaction 2.1) is controlling the ﬂux of arriving nitrogen
atoms at the surface; the reactions 2.2 and 2.3 represent the nitrogen atoms diﬀusing
into iron solid and desorbing from the surface as nitrogen gas respectively. Together
they control the the nitrogen content at the surface. The second phase ϵ Fe2N1−Z
can form on the top of γ′ nitrides, as shown in Figure 2.3.1b. The growth of duel
phase ϵ/γ′-bi-layer is a diﬀusion controlled step, which is signiﬁcantly inﬂuenced by
nitrogen diﬀusion. After coalescence growth follows a parabolic time dependence[2].

2.3. DEVELOPMENT OF THE COMPOUND LAYER
Porosity can be formed in the compound layer during the gaseous nitriding pro-
cess, which can be explained by various theories. Th hypothesis ﬁrst proposed by
Pˇrenosil[13] indicate that porosity is a result of nitrogen gas development inside
the solid is well accepted. The porosity does not only created on the top ϵ nitride
layer but also occur in other Fe-N phases such as γ′ nitrides, austenite and even
ferrite[14]. The development of N2 is usually observed in the region with highest
nitrogen content(driving force for N2 formation), which is grain boundary near to
surface[2](Figure 2.3.1d). These locations are the energetically favored nucleation
sites because iron atoms have be removed before a void can develop.. As the nitriding
continue, the coalesce of these pores lead to the formation of pore channels(Figure
2.3.1e), which is connected to the outer nitriding atmosphere and allow iron atoms
diﬀuse towards the surface to react with nitrogen and develop new nitrides[15].

2.4. NITRIDING OF THE STEELS
In the case of steel nitriding, carbon can change the morphology of the nucleation
process. The amount of ϵ and γ′ nitrides in the compound layer depends on the
carbon content. Although carbon in the steel has limited inﬂuence on the thickness
of the compound layer, it considerably aﬀects the ratio between diﬀerent iron nitrides
in the compound layer. Usually, steel with higher content of carbon can lead to a
greater ϵ content in the compound layer. Typical nitriding steel with around 0.4 wt%
carbon can develop approximately equal ϵ and γ′ phase by gaseous nitriding[16].
2.4
Nitriding of the steels
In the previous sections, the discussion mainly focuses on the compound layer for-
mation of the pure iron, the topic about the diﬀusion zone and nitriding of the
iron-based alloys was omitted. The diﬀusion zone of steel without nitride-forming
alloying elements is only solution of nitrogen[2]. For other nitriding heat treatable
steel a diﬀerent picture emerges.
2.4.1
Nitride forming elements
During the nitriding of steels, the dispersed alloying element nitrides formed at
the diﬀusion zone are able to improve the material performance signiﬁcantly. The
hardness of the component surface is increased by the precipitation hardening of the
diﬀerent nitrides and the compressive residual stress was induced into the material
due to the misﬁt with iron matrix. The typical alloying elements used in nitriding
steels are Cr, Al, Mo, V and Ti, which are stronger nitride former than Fe. The
various Me-N interaction in ferrite can alter the microstructure of the compound
layer and diﬀusion zone signiﬁcantly. The alloying elements Me can be classiﬁed
into two categories by the interaction between Me and dissolved nitrogen atoms
N[17]:
• Weak interaction elements: The formation rate of the nitrides are the same at
any depth below the surface and there is no nitrogen gradient. If a foil with
limited thickness is nitrided, the nitrogen saturation of the matrix throughout
the foil will happen before alloying elements nitride form. Alloying elements
Mo and Si are belong to this group.
• Strong interaction elements: All the alloying elements atoms will precipitate
as nitrides in the area near surface and create a sharp case/core boundary.
Nitrogen is absent in the core.
Alloying elements belonging to this group
are Ti and V. Cr has an intermediate performance and acts strongly at high
content.

Beneath the compact layer, the iron nitride prefers to grow along grain boundaries

also formed along zones between continuous precipitation(CP) and discontinuous

during the nitriding. This can lead to a competition between the iron nitride for-
mation and alloying element precipitation. For some elements such as Al and Mo,
the solubility of these nitrides in the iron nitride can be small. As a consequence,
absorbed nitrogen can diﬀuse deeply into the material due to diﬃculty in the forma-

2.4.3
Development of diﬀusion zone in the presence of al-
loying elements
During the nitriding process, if alloying elements such as Cr, Al, Mo, V and Ti
exists in the solid solution of the matrix, a dispersion of nano-scale platelike nitrides
will be formed and these nitrides like CrN, TiN and VN have a NaCl type lattice
structure[2]. A hard diﬀusion zone is developed and is rate-controlled by the diﬀusion
of nitrogen through the case. The capacity for binding nitrogen is related to the
content of strong nitride forming elements, the higher the content of alloying element
the larger is the capacity for binding nitrogen in the diﬀusion zone. As a result, the

3.4. MATERIAL CHARACTERIZATION
Etching
In order to reveal the microstructural features in the samples, creating diﬀerences
for material characterization. Two etching solutions were used in this project. The
etching solution attacks the microstructure of samples and creates the height diﬀer-
ence. As a result, various features under the microscope can be easily observed with
reﬂected light microscopy.
Nital is a solution of nitric acid and alcohol, which gives a good overview of the extent
of the diﬀusion zone. In this case, 1% Nital solution with one drop of concentrated
HCl added was used to investigate the compound layer at relatively high resolution.
The etching time is around 5 seconds.
The standard Murakami etching, i.e an alkalic potassium ferrocyanide solution, was
used to stain the locations where carbon is present. The Murakami etchant has the
ability to darken the area where carbon exists in the steel. It can also be used as
a color etchant of stainless steels. The nitrided samples are etched for 3.5 min at
55◦c.

Backscatter Electron Imaging
Backscattered electrons (BSE) are high energy electron as a result of elastic scatter-
ing of the primary beam by the nuclei of atoms in the specimen. During the elastic
scattering, there is no loss or very little energy lost for BSEs, which allows them
to escape from a relatively deep level of the interaction volume. The information
gathered through BSEs is usually come from a greater interaction volume, which
leads to a poorer laterial resolution. On the other hand, the compositional contrast
is achieved because the backscatter coeﬃcient(the ratio of backscattered electrons
to incident electrons) increases with the atomic number, resulting in brighter areas
in regions containing relatively heavy atoms.

Result and interpretation
This chapter encompasses the results acquired with the characterization methods
from the previous chapter(Chapter 2). The research is separated into 3 part to in-
vestigate the development of compound layer during nitriding. The ﬁrst subsection
include the results form high nitriding potential experiment respectively, which re-
ﬂect the diﬀerent stages of compound layer formation. The second subsection covers
the results for the nitriding by a thermobalance, which is focused on the diﬀerent
behavior of various steel during nitriding. The crosssection of the samples has been
investigated with LOM and SEM in diﬀerent etched condition. The XRD analysis
was performed mainly on the samples from low nitriding potential experiment to
have a better understanding of the early stage of nitriding. Few samples were se-

during nitriding.
4.1
High nitriding potential
A preliminary nitriding process was carried out at a relatively high temperature(570◦c)
with a pure ﬂow of the ammonia (400 ml/min). All four diﬀerent steel grades were
treated for 1, 4, 9 and 16 hours in order to see the compound layer development dur-
ing nitriding process. This section investigates the inﬂuence of nitriding parameters
on all four grades of steels.
During the nitriding of ferritic iron, the dissolution of nitrogen into the ferrite matrix
control the formation of γ′ −Fe4N1−x monolayers and of ϵ −Fe2(N, C)1−Z/γ′ −
Fe4N1−x bilayers. The γ′ nuclei grow both horizontally and vertically on the surface
of ferrite, eventually, a γ′ nitride layer is formed and isolates the nitriding media
from the substrate. On the top of γ′ nitride layer, ϵ nitride nucleates if nitriding
potential permits. The growth of mono- and bilayers thickness follow the parabolic
law. Although parabolic time dependencies do not apply for the total weight gain
of the specimen during the whole process, it dose hold for the weight gain only
associated the nitride formation[24]. The relation between weight gain and nitriding
time is shown in Figure 4.1.1. The design of the nitriding time was initially based
on parabolic time dependencies of nitride development, but the pattern observed
in this experiment does not show a clear parabolic growth of weight gain. It can

Chapter 5
Discussion
5.1
Characterization of the compound layer
From the LOM and SEM characterization of nitrided sample, a layer structure on
the surface can be obtained by nitriding. The layer formed on the surface is most
likely the compound layer as reported in the literature. In order to conﬁrm the
results, Nital and Murakami etching was applied. In general, the compound layer
consists of non-metallic phases formed by iron and nitrogen, which does not aﬀect
by the etching solution. This layer stays white when the steel is etched by Nital
solution. In this project, the compact white layer is not observed after the Nital
etching. XRD phase analysis was applied to identify the phase composition after
the nitriding, γ′ −Fe4N1−x and ϵ −Fe2(N, C)1−Z phases were found on the surface
of the sample for a 16 hours nitriding at a higher nitriding potential. The remain-
ing ferrite on the surface can be found in the early stage of nitriding. The results
indicate that the layer formed is consists of ferrite and iron nitrides before a full
compound layer is formed.
5.2
Development of the compound layer
Thermodynamically , γ′ −Fe4N1−x phase will ﬁrst form during the nitriding. The
driving force for γ′ nucleation is exceeding the maximum solubility of nitrogen in
the iron matrix adjacent to the surface. The γ′ nuclei grow both horizontally and
vertically as the nitriding proceeds, eventually, a γ′ is formed and isolates the ni-
triding medium form iron substrate. If the nitriding potential is suitable, ϵ nitride
will grow on top of the γ′ phase. In certain circumstance, ϵ can form even before
the isolation. This process can be seen from the XRD results shown in Figure 5.2.1.
At the early stage of nitriding, the peak intensity for the γ′ and ϵ nitride is relatively
low compared to the ferrite. The diﬀraction peaks of the ferrite matrix are very
much broadened due to the microstrain development caused by the precipitation of
alloying element nitride[25], The distribution of composition also contribute to the

5.3
The inﬂuence of Aluminum
Aluminum was shown to have a huge inﬂuence on the microstructure and com-
pound layer formation. As a ”weak” interaction element, the precipitation of Al
nitride can be slow during the nitriding. This delayed precipitation can leads to
a signiﬁcant formation of misﬁt-strain ﬁeld. The slow precipitation also competes
with the iron nitride formation, which creates diﬃculty for the formation of iron
nitride and precipitation itself. During the 30 minutes low penitential nitriding,
a visible nitrided layer can emerge in steel without Al while this layer cannot be
formed in 34CrAINi7 and 34CrAlMo5 within this short period of time. The delay
of the nitride formation allows more absorbed nitrogen to diﬀuse deeply into the
specimen. As discussed before, a peculiar, plate-like morphology can be produced,
as shown in Figure 5.2.2a. This weak interaction eﬀect caused by Al indicates that
nitriding steel having strong nitride forming alloying elements along withe the ele-
ments cannot form nitride or having negligible solubility in iron nitride allows the
modiﬁcation of compound layer. Thus, the morphology and microstructure of the
compound layer can be modiﬁed and incorporating in the iron matrix and avoiding
the brittle outer layer of nitriding.

Chapter 6
Conclusion
The microstructure of nitriding steel(31CrMoV, 15CrMoV5-9, 34CrAlNi7 and 34CrAIMo5)
has been investigated using light optical microscopy, scanning electron microscopy
(backscattered electrons imaging modes) and X-ray diﬀraction after nitrided with
various parameters.
The results of the preliminary characterization of the high
penitential nitrided steel at 570◦C shows a layer structure was produced after the
nitriding. After Nital etching, this layer does not become the conventional ”white
layer” reported in the previous literature, which means a compact compound layer
is not formed. The micrographs obtained by SEM show that this nitrided layer is
easily attacked by Nital solution compare to the core material. The XRD analysis
indicates this layer is consists of iron nitrides γ′ −Fe4N1−x and ϵ −Fe2N1−Z, which
is the compound layer. The formation of the porosity and channel can be observed
in the samples as the nitriding process proceeds. The behavior of this layer is similar
to the compound layer reported in the literature, but the results from LOM after
Nital etching suggest that the layer formed is not fully non-metallic iron nitride
layer. The relationship between the weight gain and time is not clear based on the
mass measured before and after nitriding, the development of the compound layer
is not illustrated in high nitriding potential experiment as well.
In order to analyse the kinetics of the compound layer formation, the samples of
34CrAINi7 and 15CrMoV5-9 were selected to perform nitriding in thermobalance
with continuous weight measuring at 530◦C. The parabolic time dependence of the
weight gain was found during the nitriding, which matches the compound growth
theory described in the literature[24]. The diﬀerent morphology of the nitrided layer
was observed after the nitriding. The plate-like feature was found in the 34CrAINi7
steel with Al as an alloying element, while the compound layer formed in 15CrMoV5-
9 has a uniform transition zone with the core material. The mechanism of compound
layer formation is discussed based on these results.
The early stage of the nitriding process was investigated in the low nitriding po-
tential experiments. The gradual iron nitride development is characterized by XRD
analysis and SEM. The layer produced by nitriding is consists of ferrite, γ′ and ϵ
nitride. The rate disparity of compound layer and porosity formation indicates the
diﬀerent mechanism of iron nitride formation in the steel with Al as alloying ele-

6.1
Future work
First of all, the EBSD analysis of the compound layer needs to be ﬁnished in or-
der to have a better understanding of the development of plate-like iron nitride. It
could also be a useful characterization technique to investigate the phases present in
the layer microstructure since the layer formed is not fully iron nitride in this project.
The characterization of the alloying elements nitride in the nitrided layer and dif-
fusion zone should be analyzed. The results from this project show a big diﬀerence
of the microstructure when various alloying elements present in the steel, partic-
ularly Aluminum.
The structure and properties of the AlN can be investigated
through Transmission electron microscope. The formation of the unique, plate-like
iron nitride can be related to the solubility of AlN in γ′ nitride according to the
literature[21]. The interaction between the AlN and iron nitride could be an inter-
esting topic to investigate.
The growth of the compound layer in nitriding steel could be related to the defect
existing in the material before nitriding. The morphology of the needle-like iron
nitride suggest this unique shape has a preferred orientation relationship with the
ferrite matrix. The relationship between the defect density and the morphology
could be studied in the future. In the industrial application, the steel is manufac-
tured by diﬀerent methods before nitriding. The eﬀect of the defects created by
manufacturing on nitriding should be investigated.
