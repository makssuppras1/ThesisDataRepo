ABSTRACT 
 
European flat oyster (Ostrea edulis) is an important native species with an important economic 
and ecological value. Its natural distribution covers all along the western European coast and 
the Mediterranean Sea. However, most of the natural oyster beds have disappeared due to 
overfishing and disease. In this study, we evaluated the settlement preferences for spat-on-shell 
on two oyster shell species (Crassostrea gigas and Ostrea edulis) and the traditional cultch 
(powder) method.  
For more than a decade, European flat oyster has been produced at the Danish Shellfish Centre 
(DTU Aqua) using the traditional method with settlement rates of ca. 50 %. Spat-on-shell is a 
method that has been used for restoration programs with other oyster species, Crassostrea 
virginica. Currently, restoration of Ostrea edulis is being done and spat-on-shell could be a useful 
for the success of this programs. 
We evaluated in hatchery conditions the flat oyster larvae settlement success on Ostrea edulis 
and C. gigas shells and the larvae settlement preference by including two extra factors regarding 
the shell orientation and side (facing up VS facing down, inside valve VS outside valve). We also 
investigated the vertical distribution of the settled larvae by pouring the larvae at different 
heights. In the last experiment we tested the possibility to mark O. edulis larvae with Calcein. 
The results show that the cultch has a higher settlement rate than the spat on shells. The larvae 
do not show any preference regarding the species and orientation, but they prefer to settle on 
the outside side of the shells. The results also show that larvae prefer to settle close to the 
substrate. Unfortunately, the larvae died after being exposed to Calcein and not marked larvae 
were found.

1. Introduction 
1.1. 
Ecology of European flat oyster 
1.1.1. Biology 
Ostrea edulis (Linnaeus, 1758), commonly known as European flat oyster, is a bivalve belonging 
to the phylum Mollusca. It has an oval shape and a rough surface, which is built by depositing 
consecutive concentric layers of calcite and aragonite, being both alternative forms of calcium 
carbonate (CaCO3) differing in the molecular ultrastructure (Bayne, 2017; Carriker, 1996). These 
concentric layers give to the shell a foliar appearance. The inside of the valves is smooth and 
with a delicate pearly material texture. The colour varies from pure white to more bluish-grey, 
often, it has darker blue dark regions where the abductor muscle is inserted. O. edulis is an 
inequivalve species, which means that the valves are asymmetric, the left valve is concave and 
it is where the body of the oyster rests.  The right one is flat and works as a lid. The outside 
colour of the shell varies from white and creamy mixed with brown and blue concentric bands 
(FAO, Ostrea edulis).   The common maximum size rarely exceeds 11 cm but they can grow up 
to 20 cm and their lifespan is within 5 – 10 years but they may exceed 20 years old in 
extraordinary cases (Roberts et al., 2010). 
O. edulis is an active suspension feeder of the suspended material in the water column. The 
feeding process has been divided in six differentiated processes (Wildish and Kristmanson, 
1997): (1) transport of seawater passing the gills, (2) capture of particles at the filtration surface 
of the gill, (3) sorting the particles between the ones to be ingested and rejected, transporting 
them to the mouth. (4) ingestion, (5) transport and sorting in the intestine, (6) uptake of 
nutrients across intestine surface. 
The diet is based on microalgae between 3-20 µm (Figueiras, 2017), the available in the water 
column, other sources include bacteria, particulate organic matter (POM) and particulate 
detritus (Korringa, 1952; Yonge, 1960). Oysters employ a ciliary pump to move water from 
inhalant chamber through the gills to the exhalent chamber (Jørgensen et al., 1986; LaBarbera, 
1984).

O. edulis is a protandric hermaphrodites (Coe, 1932a; Joyce et a., 2013), which means that first 
matures as a male and after the first spawning, changes to female and to male again in a cycle 
throughout its entire life (Cole, 1942). Adults of O. edulis become mature after between 2 – 3 
years (figure 2). In other oyster species, like the Pacific oyster Crassostrea gigas, fertilization 
occurs in the water column after both sexes release their gametes. However, O. edulis has the 
peculiarity that fertilization occurs inside of the female in the pallial cavity; the sperm travels in 
the water in the form of packages and egg fertilization can be made with sperm from different 
males. Eggs hatched in the mother result in trocophore larvae (< 140 µm) (figure 2). Around 7 – 
10 days after fertilization, between 1 and 2.5 million veliger D larvae can be released into the 
water column (Helm et al., 2004). The following stages are defined as veliconcha, umbo and 
pediveliger (240 – 300 µm) (Helmer et al., 2019). At this latest stage, larvae are a few days away 
of being ready to settle; on each side of the body of the larvae, a clear black spot called “eye-
spot” appear; they are the two adductor muscles, anterior and posterior, symmetrically located 
in relation to the hinge (Cole, 1933). Because they can easily be seen due to the transparency of 
the shell and mantle at this stage, the eye-spot is used as a reference of knowing when the larvae 
are ready to settle. Another clear sign is the crawling with the foot. Once they have settled, they 
metamorphose and become spats (Perry and Jackson, 2017) (figure 2). The time required since 
the attachment until the end of the cementation varies between 11 and 110 min (Cranfield,  
1973a).

The reproduction cycle of O.edulis can be affected by temperature and photoperiod, the former 
being the most important controlling factor (Loosanoff, 1962). In hatcheries these factors have 
been used to synchronize the reproductive cycles in some bivalves such as great scallop (Pecten 
maximus) (Saout et al., 1999) and during conditioning experiments in Crassostrea gigas (Chavez-
Villalba et al., 2002). Temperature works as a controlling factor but not the photoperiod when 
uncoupled with temperature (Joyce et al., 2013).  
 
 
  
 
 
The substrate is important because larvae are selective during the settlement. There are cues 
that drive these preferences; an example of a strong one is the presence of other congeners 
(Bayne, 1969; Lok and Arcali, 2005; Woolmer et al., 2011; Perry and Jackson, 2017; Rodriguez-
Perez et al., 2019), which explain the gregarious behaviour. During settlement the mortalities 
can reach the 90 % (Cole, 1951). In natural conditions, O. edulis can be found in rocky, muddy 
sand, gravel with shells or firm mud bottoms ( Tillin & Hull, 2013). Hence, it seems that as long 
they have a point to settle, they are flexible in terms of the type of bottom, however, they always 
prefer their congener’s shells (Tamburri et al., 2008; Walne, 1958)

O. edulis habitats are determined by certain ranges of the following parameters. The ideal 
salinity is between 30–32 mg L-1 (Hutchinson and Hawkins, 1992) even though is an euryhaline 
species (Perry and Jackson, 2017). The temperature affects the salinity tolerance. It is an 
ectotherm species so its metabolism changes according to the external temperature. The 
feeding and growth are influenced by temperature; the optimal growth rate has been defined 
at 17°C, and for short periods up to 25°C (Korringa, 1952; Buxton et al., 1981; Hutchinson & 
Hawkins, 1992). Below 10°C O. edulis switches to a low metabolic state and this reaction might 
be what allows it to tolerate the freshwater impute during winter (Hutchinson & Hawkins, 1992). 
Temperature also plays an important role on recruitment, specifically, in Denmark and Norway; 
where warm summers are linked to good recruitments (Spãrck, 1949). 
1.1.2. Spatial distribution 
The natural geographic range of O. edulis covers from 65 °N in Norway, all the west coast of 
Europe and the British Islands, continues towards North of Africa and then into the 
Mediterranean and the Black Sea (Yonge, 1960). However, translocations have increased its 
boundaries outside Europe (figure 3). They were made based on scientific criteria and 
commercial purposes; there are just a few studies on commercial translocations (Lemer and 
Planes,2012; Bromley et al. 2016).  
Regarding O. edulis translocations, Bromley et al. (2016) made a literature review to collect all 
the translocations occurred throughout a period of 200 years. From a total of 92 translocations, 
75 were within Europe, 11 from European countries to non-Europe locations and 6 from stocks 
outside the natural distribution range of O. edulis to other parts of the world. The biggest 
translocation consisted on 18 million from the Netherlands to Limfjorden (Denmark).  
(Loosanoff, 1955; Bromley et al., 2016).

1.1.3. Ecosystem services and threats 
It is a sessile and gregarious species (Rodriguez-Perez et al., 2019) and it forms complex biogenic 
reef structures typically not deeper than 10 m depth (Günther, 1897). Flat oyster has been 
observed covering large areas; as an example, in Christianen et al., 2018, a reef of 39.6 hectares 
with an average density of 6.8 ± 0.6 oysters m2 was measured. Shellfish beds provide ecosystem 
services. For instance, flat oysters function as potential settling substrate for other sessile 
species, nursery and foraging habitats for invertebrates and fish species; they also clear the 
water of particulate organic matter mainly as phytoplankton, which benefits the submerged 
aquatic vegetation by increasing the light penetration (Lemasson et al., 2017). The benefits of

the reefs also include economic ecosystem services as protecting shorelines and providing 
nursery habitats for different commercial fish species valuated in of 55 000 – 99 000 $ per 
hectare per year (Grabowski et al., 2012).  
O. edulis is an important species due to the services it provides, but it is also vulnerable due to 
the many threats it faces. They can be divided in two groups: i) direct, including diseases and 
fishing pressure and ii) indirect, the ones affecting the oyster habitats like temperature, oxygen 
concentration and bottom alteration. A selection of the most damaging are detailed in the two 
sections below.   
Direct impacts 
Bonamia ostreae, firstly described in 1979 by Pichot et al. (1979) in France, is a protozoan 
(Acetosporea) considered a disease affecting old and large individuals (Culloty and Mulcahy, 
1996; Kroeck & Montes 2005; Arzul et al., 2011). Breeders can transmit the disease to the 
offspring (Arzul et al., 2011), even though the parents are not infected (Flannery et al., in 2016); 
a possible explanation is that the Bonamia ostreae cells could have entered the pallial cavity 
during respiration and feeding infecting the larvae but not the adults. Larvae, juveniles and other 
species (zooplankton) work as a vector for spreading the disease (Wilson, 1987). Crassostrea 
gigas might also be a vector but recent studies were not conclusive and further investigation is 
required (Lynch et al., 2010). The control of the disease relies on two strategies: first, controlling 
the translocations between infected areas (Morgan et al., 2017) and second, selection families 
of O. edulis, which have developed resilience against the infection (Egerton et al., 2020). 
Marteilia refringens is a protistan parasite belonging to the order Paramixida (Canier et al., 2020) 
also responsible of the severe decrease of European flat oyster populations in the 20th century. 
Other bivalve species can act as vectors, such as mussels (Mytillus edulis), cockles (Cerastoderma 
edule) and the scallop (Argopecten gibbus). The effect of this parasite in their hosts is a reduction 
of their condition by infecting the digestive system. Mortality occurs during the second year 
after the infection during the sporulation of the parasite (Canier et al., 2020). There is no 
treatment available for the disease, and like the case of Bonamia ostreae, control of the 
translocations is the optimal prevention tool (Bower et al., 1994). 
During the period 1970-1980, almost all the European flat oyster populations across Europe 
were infected by Bonamia ostreae and Marteilia refringens, however, Danish flat oyster 
population were not affected by this wave of parasites during that period (Madsen et al., 2013). 
The threat of Bonamia sp. being present in European waters led to an import ban of oysters to 
the Limfjorden. Despite this measure, 34 years later, in 2014 B. ostreae appeared in oysters from 
aquaculture facilities located in Limfjorden in zones 5 and 13 (figure 4) (Madsen et al 2013); and

a few years later, the presence of the parasite was found in the wild individuals  (Nielsen and 
Petersen, 2019). The results of an extended sampling carried out between 2016 – 2018 showed 
that B. ostreae was present in different in more zones than 2014, thus, since 2016 Limjforden is 
not considered Bonamia-free anymore (Nielsen and Petersen, 2019). This new condition has 
brought together an increase in the risk of introduction of alien species, diseases and parasites, 
which might harm the native species; the reason was that the import of bivalves to Limjforden 
was no longer banned.  
The biological threats for O. edulis are not limited to the two most known protozoan diseases. 
There are other organisms from wide type of geneses like, polychaetes (genus Polydora), 
sponges (genus Cliona), other protozoan (genus Heximata), bacteria (genus Norcadia and Vibrio) 
and a virus responsible for the gill disease (Bower et al., 1994).

natural oyster beds (Kammernan et al 2019). Fishing pressure has a double impact: The removal 
of individuals and the destruction of the oyster’s habitat by disturbing the bottom. Over time, if 
the fishing effort persists on an oyster population it will end up provoking a negative impact 
(Woolmer et al., 2011). In Denmark, since 2004, the stock assessment is based on the total

Indirect impacts 
Oysters are vulnerable to changes in their ecosystem and considering that a big proportion of 
O. edulis are located in coastal areas, human activity could induce indirect impact through 
ecosystem alteration. Recruitment is affected by temperature (Perry and Jackson, 2017). A 
population could experience reduction due to recruitment failure; this situation has happened 
in Limfjorden (Denmark) where consecutive bad recruitments caused a natural declined of the 
population (Spãrck, 1949).  
In terms of oxygen, oysters are able to withstand hypoxia as long as the temperature is low, if it 
is low enough, they can survive even weeks out of the water (Korringa, 1952; Yonge, 1960). 
However, in locations very sheltered with low water exchange, high concentrations of organic 
matter coming from run-offs or algal blooms could lead to a high eutrophic conditions resulting 
in oyster mortality due to oxygen depletion (Lenihan, 1999; Bricker et al., 2008). Ostrea edulis 
can suffer mortality due to toxic algal blooms (e.g. Gonyaulax sp. and Gymnodinium sp.) 
(Shumway, 1990). The last indirect threat considered relevant is the bottom alteration. Oysters 
are not able to surface once buried into the sediment. Thus, intense sediment input into the 
oysters habitat might smother them to death, as reported by Yonge (1960) due to flooding and 
sediment runoff from land. 
1.2. Flat oyster production  
1.2.1 European production 
European flat oyster is one of the bivalves with the longest tradition of harvesting and 
aquaculture production, and  it was the first oyster species harvested in France by dredging 
natural beds (Goulletquer and Heral, 1997; Edwards, 1997; Buestel et al., 2009).  
The landings of native Ostrea edulis in Europe have been declining since the 18th century (Héral 
and Deslous-Paoli, 1991). In the last fourty  years the production declined dramatically after an 
output peak of 30.000 tonnes in 1961. (figure 5). As a consequence of this decline there was a 
shift to the production of Crassostrea gigas to substitute Ostrea edulis (figure 6). 
==PAGE:13==

France could be a good example to understand the evolution of O. edulis production and how 
the producers reacted to overcome the big mortalities occurred in the past. In figure 6, it can be 
seen how O. edulis was always with lower population and how in 1972 Crassostrea gigas was 
introduced (Buestel et al., 2009). 
The decrease of the European flat oyster populations was not only constricted to some 
countries, the decline affected countries at a global scale; (FAO, 2020). In the particular case of 
Spain, O. edulis was reported to be declining in 1778 ( Pazó, 1987); a little bit later, the situation 
in the north of Europe followed the same trend. In England, the production fell from more than 
2000 tonnes per year in the 1902s to a few hundred in the early 1990s (Laing et al., 2005). In 
Scotland the situation was similar where regions that once supported significant oysters 
populations like Firth of Forth, after 2000 experienced the disappearing of O. edulis (Smyth et 
al., 2009).

collboratively (Saurel et al., 2019). Some of these events are supported by trend-setting chefs 
and gourment bloggers.  
1.2.3.  Aquaculture production 
The Ostrea edulis spats can be entirely produced in a hatchery (figure 7) or obtained by placing 
collectors (e.g. bags with mussel, bags filled with oyster shell, Chinese hats, PVC tubes, Vexar) in 
the areas with adults, and during the period when the free-swimming larvae are present in the 
water column (Van der Brink et al., 2013). It has been found that the most efficient collectors 
were the bags filled with oyster and mussel shells (Van den Brink et al., 2013). The period for 
placing the collector determines the success; a period between May and mid-June is considered 
to be the one when the maximum spat recruitment is most likely to occur (Lok and Acarli, 2005).  
Hatchery production has the advantage that the environmental conditions can be totally 
controlled (Lallias et al., 2009; Šegvić-Bubić et al., 2020). Then, population can be reinforced with 
these spats, potentially, being resilient against bonamiosis. There are, to my knowledge, only 
four commercial hatcheries currently producing spats of O. edulis: SCARPO (Norway), Ostrea 
Sverige AB (Sweden), Seasalter Shellfish Whistable (UK), Roem van Yerseke S.A. (Netherlands) 
(Lallias et al., 2010), Novostrea (France) On top of that, three research centres: IFREMER 
(Argenton - France), Università di Corsica (France) and the Danish Shellfish Center (DTU Aqua, 
Denmark). 
On the other hand, the major drawback of hatchery production is the potential loss of genetic 
diversity (Appleyard and Ward 2006; Taris et al. 2007; Lind et al. 2009; Lallias et al., 2010). The 
reasons could be the small size of the broodstock and the variance in reproductive success

among breeders (Herbinger et al, 2006). These could have a serious impact in the long-term 
hatchery stocks leading to inbreeding depression (Naciri-Graven et al. 2000; Taris et al. 2007).   
 
 
 
 
1.3. Flat oyster restoration 
Reports of the extensive functional loss of shellfish reefs globally has encouraged to allocate 
efforts on restoration and creation of shellfish reefs (Beck et al., 2011). Initially, the drivers for 
making oyster restoration were to sustain the fishery (Kennedy, 1989), but with time it became 
clear that restoration was important due to the ecosystem services that oysters provide (Lallias, 
2010; Peyre et al., 2014; Pogoda, 2019). However, data evaluating the outcome of restoration 
actions is not abundant (Kennedy et al., 2011). Furthermore, evaluations of restored reefs

appear to be guided more for research questions rather than management needs (Peyre et al., 
2014). 
Restoring activities are considered as any kind of measures or actions which pursuit the recovery 
of the original status of any ecosystem that has been adversely affected (European Parliament, 
1992, 2008). In order to define the approach and strategy for a restoration program, it is 
important knowing what the aimed original status is. There are three strategies used for 
molluscan restoration plans: adult transplant, juvenile seeding and larvae release. Shellfish 
restoration projects are usually based in the second strategy, which consists of hatchery-
produced spats that are release in the targeted area (Lallias, 2010). However, restoration 
programs for O. edulis based on hatchery production will face the issue of not being able to 
produce larvae enough (Laing et al., 2006). 
In the last decades, many scientific studies have assessed the role of O. edulis in the environment 
(Kerckhof et al., 2018; Pogoda, 2019). The capability of O. edulis to form biogenic reef structures 
by shell aggregation (Merk et al., 2019), and the importance of their habitats in terms of 
biodiversity (e.g. increasing production of fish species) and improvement  of the water quality 
(zu Ermgassen, 2016; Smyth et al., 2018), make the recovery of these reef the main goal for 
O. edulis restoration programs. All the efforts related to restoration of O. edulis in Europe have 
been gathered under the Native Oyster Restoration Alliance (NORA) born in 2017. It includes 
stakeholders from governmental agencies, science, non-governmental institutions, oyster 
farmers and other stakeholders. The aim is to bring together all interested parties to exchange 
knowledge, which will lead to re-establish the native European flat oyster in the European seas. 
NORA organizes international workshops yearly and from the first one made in 2017, a set of 
recommendations were listed: (1) Produce sufficient oyster for restoration, (2) identify suitable 
sites for restoration, (3) provide suitable substrate, (4) respect Bonamia-free areas, (5) create 
common monitoring programmes and (6) preserve genetic diversity (further discussed in 
Pogoda et al., 2019). In 2018, an equivalent network to NORA was created for UK and Ireland 
(nativeoysternetwork.org). 
Restoration initiatives started at the end of 20th and beginning of 21st century in Strangford 
Lough in Northern Ireland (Kennedy and Roberts 1999), Spain (Perez Camacho, 1987; Guerra, 
1998), and Limfjorden (Denmark) (Laing et al., 2006). The non-European case of Chesapeake Bay 
in USA, where a total of 4.75 million spats were planted in 1997 and 2002, showed that using 
great numbers of oysters is not enough to be successful (Mann 2000; Mann and Evans 2004). 
==PAGE:17==
16 
 
These past experiences and knowledge are the base for documents as NORA workshop 
recommendations from 2017.  
The way the larvae or juveniles are released into the environment can vary but a common 
method used is the spat-on-shell or also called “cultched seed”. It consists of oyster seed 
attached to a large piece of cultch or empty shell. The reasons why this method is often used for 
restoration is because it mimics the natural reef structure and therefore larvae might be less 
vulnerable to predation (Westby et al., 2019). This methodology has been commonly used with 
Crassostrea virginica, in fact, the “University of Maryland Centre of Environmental Science” has 
developed a solid protocol to produce them. They developed a successful and stable 
methodology that give them the option to sell spat-on-shell on their webpage 
(http://hatchery.hpl.umces.edu). The situation for Ostrea edulis is completely different, it is not 
possible yet to find spat-on-shell production for this species. This is expected, considering that 
there are not many hatcheries capable to maintain a stable production of O. edulis (section 
1.2.3). 
Methods to assess the efficiency of the restoration programs are an important factor that should 
be considered. Therefore, being able to determine if the larvae remain in the location where 
they had been placed is important (Gancel et al., 2019). This means that mark-recapture 
strategies are needed. An option used for marking bivalves is Calcein; it is a fluorescent chemical 
marker that gets 100 % incorporated in calcified tissues and it glows under UV-light. The fast 
shell growth of oyster larvae allows to mark cohorts in a short period of time (Linard et al., 2011; 
Gancel et al., 2019). In a recent study, Gancel et al. (2019) tried mark-recapture with Calcein of 
Crassostrea virginica larvae and they found positive results.

1.4. Aims and hypothesis 
The aim of this thesis is to increase the knowledge of the settling preferences of Ostrea edulis 
larvae to produce spat-on-shells i) on a small scale and comparing settlement and survival 
percentages with the traditional powder cultch method and ii) on a larger scale for larger 
production. In this manuscript I am going to describe three experiments, the one defined as 
“Spat-on-shell experiment (SOS)” represents the core of the thesis because it is the one 
providing more information. The hypotheses that I am going to test are the following:  
 
− 
There is no difference in larvae settlement between traditional settlement cultch (oyster 
powder) and spat-on-shell method. 
− 
There is no difference in O. edulis larvae settlement between O. edulis and C. gigas shell

− 
There is no difference in O. edulis larvae settlement between inside and outside valves 
of the settling shells. 
− 
There is no difference in O. edulis larvae settlement depending on the settling shell 
orientation: shell facing up or down. 
This experiment was conducted on a one layer of settling shells in traditional hatchery conditions 
where a single tank can produce up to 100 000 spats on individual grain size (powder cultch). To 
upscale this experiment for large scale spat-on-shell production, several layers of shells are 
usually placed in large tanks. This configuration lead to a new set of questions: When pouring 
the larvae on top of the layers of settling shells, would the layers affect the settlement, and how 
deep the larvae would settle? this led  to the second experiment defined as the “depth 
experiment” and the hypothesis that is going to be tested is: 
− 
There is no difference in settlement density on shell clutch at different height of a tank 
filled up with layers of shells, when pouring larvae at the water surface. 
A third experiment defined as “Calcein experiment” was designed due to the difficulty related 
to the counting of spats. During the counting of the spats attached to the settling shells, I 
experienced serious problems to see the spats, particularly, on the outside part of the O. edulis 
shells. Thus, considering the resources that I had available at DSC, I decided to try a method to 
make the larvae easier to be seen. The idea was trying to dye the larvae at their veliger-D stage 
with a chemical that under UV light would shine. The aim was to test whether it would be 
possible or not to see spats shining under a UV lamp.

2.1. Danish Shellfish Centre, European flat oyster hatchery

water temperature around 20°C and with a flow of 25 L h-1. The food was pumped and manually 
adjusted according the oysters needs.

Filtration is an essential part in a hatchery because it acts as a barrier against the biological 
threats that can affect the oysters in the hatchery and the particles that can disrupt the normal 
functionating of the mechanical parts. These two factors combined are the reason that DSC has

a general filtering system down to 1 µm. Limfjorden has a big proportion of this sediment 
composed by mud, which is often resuspended increasing the turbidity. These particles must be 
retained before entering the system because they could block pipes and break pumps. These 
particles could also bring pathogens or bacteria attached to them. The increase in the particle 
concentration in the water has a direct impact in the oysters; they would produce more 
pseudofeces in order to eliminate this excess of material (Helm et al., 2004).  
Filtration is also to prevent the incoming of organisms such as ciliates, parasites, larvae of other 
bivalves, eggs of any species and pathogens. That is why besides the mechanical filtration there 
is also an UV-light. The problems that organisms can create for a hatchery are: biofouling, 
diseases and biological contamination. 
The water is filtered from the Limfjorden; this water system inside the hatchery can either be 
flow-through, which refers when the water is only used one time or alternatively a RAS 
(recirculated aquaculture system) system, which as the name indicates consists of re-using the 
water constantly with 10% renewal daily. At DSC, both systems are used for research purpose. 
Flow through is also used for biosecurity reason, in order to prevent cross-contaminations 
between animals coming from different locations. The water is pumped from the fjord has two 
sets of filtrations: (1) mechanical filtration consists with sand filter (400 µm) and (2) series of 
cartridge filters up to 1 µm pore sizes with UV filtration. After the general filtration, the water 
line is divided in two lines; one feeding both RAS systems and the other one line for the 
microalgae room with an extra cartridge filter of 0.2 µm and UV-light. The RAS systems are 
partially thermoregulated, water can be heated up, but it cannot be cooled down. Despite all of 
this, during the summer period, some small blue mussels had to be removed from some of the 
broodstock oysters. 
2.1.3 Food supply – microalgae production 
The food consists of four different microalgae species cultured at DSC. The water quality 
requirements to produce them are higher than for the rest of procedures in the facility. 
Therefore, there is a water subline filtered down to 0.2 µm. This filtration would avoid the entry 
of any potential ciliates into the system, that could feed on the microalgae and also any other 
species that could contaminate the cultures. The air supplied is also filtered through a filter of 
0.22 µm to prevent contamination by bacteria, ciliates or dust. Despite all that, sometimes there 
is contamination due to cyanobacteria that ruins the cultures. 
The species used with its correspondent culture concentration and sizes are the following

European flat oyster has specific food requirements based on the cell size and the nutritional 
composition. Therefore, the diet is different for each of the stages (table 1). Essential fatty acids 
(EFAs) are important to bivalve-growth and development (Palacios et al., 2005). These can be 
supplied by using Chaetoceros sp and Rhodomonas salina; being the last one strongly 
recommended due to its proven positive effect during gametogenesis (González-Araya et al., 
2012; González-Araya et al., 2012b). The problem of these two species is that they are too big 
for the larvae stages. In fact, Rhodomonas salina is only used to feed the broodstock. 
Chaetoceros sp. can produce chains and it has big spines hampering its ingestion for the larvae. 
Size limitation is the reason why Pavlova gyrans and Tisochrysis lutea are included in the diet, 
besides being a good source fatty acid DHA for the first development stages of the larvae. For 
instance, Tisochrysis lutea is beneficial for feeding the broodstock because adults do not feed 
on them and it can be eaten by the larvae when they are still inside the mothers, after hatching 
(Gonzalez et al., 2011). The food ratio recommended for O. edulis is 6 % of dry weight algae/dry 
weight oyster day-1 oyster-1 (Pogoda and Buck, 2011; Maneiro et al., 2017; Robert et al, 2017; 
Maneiro et al., 2020).

2.2. Experiments 
2.2.1. Spat-on-shell experiment (SOS)  
In this experiment, the larvae settlement percentage on three different substrates is compared. 
The traditional method used at DSC (powder made of Crassotrea gigas shells) is compared 
against adult empty shells of Ostrea edulis and Crassostrea gigas; each treatment has been 
triplicated. At the same time, the larvae preferences in term of orientation and position on the 
shell has been tested; it was made by defining two subfactors. First, the difference between 
inside and outside of the shells. Second, the effect of shells orientation, whether there is a 
difference on the settlement percentage based on if the shells are facing down or facing up. 
Triplicate of the three treatments were randomly distributed in nine distributed within the 
settling section consisting of a row of 10 tanks.  
Substrate preparation 
O. edulis shells were fished in April and were kept outdoors exposed to sunlight and rain. This 
would allow to kill any disease or pathogens and also get rid of the grit (Ozbay, 2017). C. gigas 
shells were collected on the upper side of the intertidal zone of Nykøbing Mors beach; I selected 
the ones looking dry, clean and without organic matter between a size range of 8-12 cm.  
However, to be sure of removing any type of material attached to the shells surface, oysters 
shells were cleaned in two steps. First, they were kept in a freshwater bath for 24 h. Second, I 
rinsed them with high-pressure washing machine. Then, they were left in the raceways to dry 
out more than 24 h. The oyster powder does not require any preparation, it can be put directly 
into the water.  
I selected the shells for each of the treatments trying to have the same number for each of the 
subfactors, but it was not possible for all the tanks. The criteria for defining the amount of shells 
to put in each of the tanks was to try covering as much surface of the settling tank as possible 
(figure 13). After selecting the shells, I made a hole with a drill and 4 mm bit in each of them for 
tagging the shells facing down.

larvae would be ready to settle. For the shells treatments the same protocol was used. First, 
each shell was numbered with a pencil. Second, putting them very carefully, one by one, at the

2.2.2. Depth experiment 
In this experiment, it has been tested if the larvae stay at the surface or if they use the whole 
water column when they are put to settle. The experimental designed consisted on two 
cylindrical tanks of 90 L, each representing one treatment, were filled with C. gigas shells up to 
50 cm. The first treatment, defined as “top”, consisted in pouring all the larvae after the tank 
had been filled with shells and seawater. The difference of the second treatment, called “layers” 
was that the larvae were not all pored together. It consisted on dividing the tank and the larvae 
into six layers and batches respectively. After filling up the tank with one layer of shells and 
water, one larvae batch was poured; this process was repeated for the rest of the layers. The 
expected result was that we were going to find a more homogenous vertical distribution in the 
tank divided into layers compared to the one poured from the top. 
The approach to this experiment was to do it as simple as possible and the procedures were, 
deliberated, less careful. The reason for that was testing the sensitivity of the O. edulis larvae. It 
was a design without water exchange and with shells that were not as clean as for SOS 
experiment. Therefore, in case of being able to have spat-on-shells under such circumstances 
would show that high quality facilities and strong monitoring might be unnecessary if the aim is 
just to produce O. edulis spat-on-shells. 
Shells were collected at the same place as the SOS experiment and cleaned with the same 
procedure as described earlier. 
Larvae, experimental design and procedure 
The larvae came from a spawning of the Broodstock 2, from Nissum. The larvae were reared also 
based on the descriptions provided in section 2.1. We used for 182650 larvae for this

4. Discussion 
 
European flat oyster is a keystone species, which serves as essential habitat where many other 
species find shelter, spawning grounds and protection (Grabowski et al., 2012; Rodriguez-Perez 
et al., 2019; Christianen et al., 2018). This species has experienced dramatic population loses 
during the 20th century mainly from overexploitation; leading, in some regions like German 
North Sea, to the extinction of historical wild populations (Pogoda et al, 2019). Although wild O. 
edulis populations are still exploited in certain countries such as Denmark, under strict 
management rules (TAC), restoration measures and programmes (RESTORE I, RESTORE II, 
PROCEED(Pogoda et al, 2019)) have been recommended under several European policies 
(Habitat Directive, Marine Strategy Framework Directive OSPAR) (Pogoda et al, 2019). One way 
of restoring is to transplant seeds produced from hatcheries. However, flat oyster hatchery 
production is still minor compared to C. gigas (FAO) due to i) the complex life cycle, sensitive 
larvae and difficulty to produce and ii) vulnerability to disease and predation threats in grow-
out phase of production. One technique to produce spats for restoration is spat-on-shell has 
widely been used in USA for Crassostrea virginica restoration programs (Meryland university). 
In this study, we achieved to produce spat-on-shells for Ostrea edulis, something that has not 
been found in the literature. We compared the larvae settlement rates using the traditional 
method (C. gigas powder) for food production with the settlement on spat-on-shells. We found 
that there were more than double (42%) larvae settled on the traditional powder than on the 
spat-on-shells (20 %). The traditional method value is within the range that the DSC team usually 
obtain, while 5-21% of the larvae poured for settlement in the SOS experiment did not 
metamorphose and were found at the bottom of the settling tanks (table 3).  This could indicate 
that a large number of larvae used were not completely ready to settle. Spats were also found 
on individual grains at the bottom of the settling tanks with both O. edulis and C. gigas shells. 
They were sorted by size into < 1 cm and > 1 cm. The proportion of each of those in the tanks 
was different (table 3). A possible explanation for this size difference would be food availability, 
which has a direct impact on O. edulis growth. Some of the tanks had periods of more than 12 h 
without food due issues with the feeding system. Monitor the chlorophyll-a would be strongly 
recommended for future experiments. For this experiment it was not monitored because of time 
availability.  
Regarding settlement substrates, oyster larvae have preference for congeners shells (Lok and 
Acarli, 2005). In this study we also compared the settlement rates between two species shells, 
C. gigas and O. edulis. The results show that the settlement is not statistically significant different 
==PAGE:44==
43 
 
between them. It is a positive result in terms of practicality, cost and management: O. edulis 
shells are more difficult to gather from the sea bottom than the C. gigas ones easily harvestable 
from the shore. Thus a future production of spat-on-shell for European flat oyster by using C. 
gigas shells is recommended. 
Another factor evaluated regarding the settlement was if exist differences between the inside 
and outside faces of the shells. Our results show that there is a difference between them and 
that the outside is the preferred face for the larvae to settle.; 70 spats shell-1 inside and 89 spats 
shell-1 outside. This corroborate the results of Lok and Arcali (2005), who studied O. edulis 
settlement on shell collectors filled with O. edulis shells where the same behaviour was found. 
In the case of collectors exposed in the nature, the driver for this behaviour could be a higher 
silt concentration on the outside face compared to the inside (Quayle, 1969). In our experiment, 
shells were only cleaned mechanically. During the cleaning, the dirt was more easily removed 
from the inside valve than from the outside, although, the structure, composition and porosity 
are different between the inside and outside. Therefore, a higher presence of silt could be an 
important factor to consider and it could explain also our results. 
Studies that used collectors of different types, they observed that larvae tend to settle on the 
covered parts of the collector. Hence, it has been stated that the shadow has an impact on the 
settlement behaviour (Kennedy et al., 1990; Buitrago and Alvarado, 2005). Orientation was 
another factor studied in this work to see if larvae prefer to settle on the most covered spots. 
However, it was found as non-significant factor. The settling tanks used for this experiment were 
covered by a mesh inducing shadow in the tank, which means that is possible that we had 
neutralized the shadow effect. For future experiments it would be more accurate to have some 
of the settling tanks uncovered to evaluate the effect of the shadow.  
Something important that must be taking into consideration is the problems that I have 
experienced for counting the spats. It was difficult to see the spats settled on the outside of the 
O. edulis shells. At the moment of counting, the size of the bigger spats was about 3 – 4 mm, 
while there were still spats of the approximately 500 µm. In order to check the counts, the shells 
were counted for a second time (figure 19). The results show that for the majority of the shells, 
the first count was bigger than the last one, which could indicate that some of the spat died; 
however, for a few shells the second one was greater. This could be due to miscounting the first 
time due to poor visibility and also potentially some miscounting the second time. The problems 
related to the counts was the reason to try to mark the larvae the Calcein experiment.  
==PAGE:45==
44 
 
This study is aimed at producing knowledge that could upscale spat-on-shell production for 
future restoration programs. Therefore, it must be taken into consideration the limitations 
present in a commercial hatchery in terms of technology, time-consumption and resources 
optimization. This means that efficiency and cost reduction has been always in mind during the 
present study. In Delaware University trials on large scale production of spat-on-shell were 
carried out with C. virginica. They stacked group of shells in big tanks and then poured large 
amounts of larvae, greater than 1 million (Ozbay, 2017). In this study, we studied how deep the 
larvae swim and settle in a similar design. Our results show that larvae can travel through the 
water column, but they concentrate close to position they were poured. When poured at the 
top of the stacked shells (“top treatment”), the top layer number had a density five times 
superior than bottom one. On the contrary, when poured on regular interval along the vertical 
gradient (“layer treatment”), less larvae had settled at the top than at the bottom of the tank. 
What these data show is that, even though some larvae tend to sink, the majority would remain 
and settle at the top of the later they were poured under laboratory condition where no current 
except than the one created by the aeration is affecting the larvae swimming.  
O. edulis larvae are considered delicate and probably not capable of tolerating intense 
procedures (Pascal Barreau pers. com.). The depth experiment was designed in a specific way 
that would test the resilience of the larvae. Before starting it, we were expecting potentially high 
mortalities due to three main reasons (1) temperature, (2) oxygen saturation and (3) 
manipulation. This experiment was carried out in the hatchery where, in summer, the 
temperature of the building could rise above 25 degrees.  The reason why O2 was also 
considered a potential issue was due to the fact that the water it was not going to be exchanged 
and that shells were dirtier than for the SOS experiment, which could lead to O2 drop to organic 
matter decomposition. At the end of the experiment, unsettled larvae was higher than in very 
controlled hatchery setup (first experiment): 84 % and 95 % for “top treatment” and” layer 
treatment” respectively against 78% for C. gigas shells. In Davis and Calabrese, 1969 it is stated 
that satisfactory growth for O. edulis larvae occur within a temperature range of 17.5 – 30 °C 
with a survival rate higher than 70 %. In our experiment, the temperature (20 and 21.4 °C) for 
any of the layers was within the range defined by Davis and Calabrese, 1969. Regarding the 
oxygen, the saturation never went below of 95 % for any of the layers, thus they were in good 
oxygen conditions (Bayne 2017). Another factor that could have affected the settlement is the 
amount of food and bubbling in the tank. Chlorophyll a concentration were very high and could 
have disturbed the larvae. The manipulation could also be responsible for the larvae loses. Be 
noticed that we are defining the mortality based on the spats recovered at the moment of the 
==PAGE:46==
45 
 
counting, not the spats right after the experiment was finished. This means that the 
manipulation comprehends: (1) pouring the larvae in the tanks, (2) removing the spat on shell 
by hand from the tanks and place them on transferring trays, (3) transfer the shells to another 
tank for rearing (also without water exchanged) and (4) change of water every two days by 
emptying and filling the tank again. Point number four was a critical; the way this step was made 
the first week was by transferring the trays with the tanks to one tank to another. During this 
manipulation, left overs of settled spats on broken shells and powder at the bottom were 
counted up to 1500 individuals in one occasion. At this stage they were small easily confused 
with dirt. Hence, the high mortality is also due to the methodology with high losses from 
manipulation. 
We have tried to replicate the protocol used in Gancel et al. (2019) where Crassostrea virginica 
larvae were marked with Calcein but using O. edulis larvae instead. We also wanted to know if 
the larvae would settle after being marked. Unfortunately, the methodology did not succeed 
because most of the larvae died and did not incorporate the Calcein. However, it was useful to 
detect and learn from the mistakes that could easily be corrected in future attempts. Three 
suggestions are given: (1) Test with different concentrations in order to the define the range 
that O. edulis are able to tolerate. A concentration of below 100 mg L-1 did not affect the survival 
of C. virginica larvae (Gancel et al., 2019) but O. edulis could have a different tolerance rate. (2) 
Divide the experiment in two phases, one for testing the marking and after checking that is 
possible to mark the larvae, test it they would settle after being marked. By doing this, it would 
be possible to use a larvae tank instead of the beakers, which is the third suggestion. Larvae 
tanks have four advantages. First, it would require less manipulation making the methodology 
less stressful for the larvae. Second, using a higher number of larvae, which would increase the 
success chances even though the mortality is high. Third, using the whole tank would skip the 
pipetting step of collecting the larvae, which I believe to have an impact on the larvae. Fourth, 
once the exposure time finishes, the tank could be emptied following the hatchery methodology 
which is known to be tolerated by the O. edulis larvae. By using beakers, once the exposure time 
was finished the water with Calcein had to be removed by sucking out the water with a siphon 
covered with a mesh to avoid the larvae being sucked out, but this was stressful for the larvae 
(3) Using water filtered up to 0.2 µm and 0.22 µm filters for the airline to reduce contaminations 
to the minimum.

5. Conclusion 
Producing spat-on-shells with Ostrea edulis larvae under hatchery conditions is possible and 
they can be produced either on Crassostrea gigas or Ostrea edulis shells. However, the 
settlement rate obtained is slightly minor compared to the traditional method of C. gigas cultch. 
Larvae have shown preference for settling on the outside of the shells; they do not have any 
preference regarding the orientation of the valve. When larvae are put to settle in a deep tank, 
they prefer to settle close to the position of pouring. Therefore, the set up suggested to produce 
O. edulis spat-on-shell would be placing Crassostrea gigas or Ostrea edulis shells in layers 
overlapping each other in way that, there would be enough space for the larvae to swim through 
them. This ensemble should have approximately a maximum of 10 cm height. Further studies 
are needed in order to improve the settlement rates and to study the correlations between 
larvae concentration and spat density on the shells, which would help to offer spat-on-shells 
with different densities based on their future use. 
Calcein marking for O. edulis would be a useful too due to its potential for detecting spats on 
the shells and further research should be done to successfully mark the larvae. This technique 
combined with image recognition could help to improve the quality and shortening the time 
required for counting spats-on-shells.

an intensity of around 100 µmol m-2 s-1. They are kept at lower ambient temperature than the 
hatchery. The water used is autoclaved seawater filtered down to 0.2 µm. During that process, 
all the steps are conducted close to a flame to avoid contamination. New Erlenmeyer bottle 
receive nutrients (table 2) and a few drops of the old culture until some colouration of the 
seawater is visible (figure 14).

Single use 50 L bags (upscaled production) 
The two previous steps are used to inoculate the 50 L single use bag and upscale the production 
(figure 13_C).  Single used bags are filled up with 50 L of seawater UV - filtered down to 0.2 µm, 
the light exposure is 24 h day-1 and they are aerated also with 12 h day-1 with air plus 1% of CO2

Bags are filled with the filtered seawater. Then, nutrients are injected (table 2) and the airline, 
is added at the bottom of the bag.  
Nutrients

in the form of solutions that we refer to as medium. Based on the chemical requirements of the 
species two types of macronutrients medium are produce at DSC. One for Chaetoceros sp. called 
“Chaetoceros medium” and another one defined as “general medium” for all the other 
microalgae species. The ingredients are shown in Table 1 and table 2. For chaetoceros sp. A 
silicate solution is made by mixing of 400 g of silicate in 10 L of demineralized and autoclaved 
water.
