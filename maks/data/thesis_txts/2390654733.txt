2. Abstract 
Adequate solutions to eliminate nitrate from marine aquaculture effluents are required as more 
seawater land-based recirculating systems are being implemented. Biological denitrification is such 
a suitable process, in which under anoxic conditions, heterotrophic bacteria use a carbon source as 
electron donor to convert nitrate into nitrogen gas, the latter one being released into the 
atmosphere. In order to perform this process, several types of reactors can be used. The up-flow 
sludge blanket (USB) is of special interest, as large amounts of bacterial biomass can be 
concentrated in low volume due to the formation of granular sludge, spherical aggregates with 
high density and excellent settling properties. This characteristic allows a higher denitrification rate 
per reactor volume, reducing infrastructure complexity, space requirements and operational costs. 
The aim of the current study was to evaluate the performance of an USB as a candidate for 
denitrification in seawater RAS effluents. Three 10 L reactors (replicates) were used to (1) evaluate 
the formation of granules using waste from a rainbow trout seawater RAS as inoculum and (2) 
evaluate the denitrification rate under the effect of different up-flow velocities. Reactors were fed 
with sodium acetate and sodium nitrate (250 mg NO3
--N/L in the first and 100 mg NO3
--N/L in the 
second phase), keeping a C:N ratio of 6. The obtained granules showed variable diameters reaching 
up to 9.6 mm and a minimum sludge volume index of 7.58 mL/g. A maximum denitrification rate of 
322 mg NO3
--N/g VSS per day was achieved at the third up-flow velocity (0.97 m/h). At higher 
velocities, the biomass started to get fluidized progressively, until granules were no longer present 
and bacterial aggregates predominated (7th up-flow velocity at 2.11 m/h). The obtained results 
indicate granular sludge for denitrification was successfully developed and under optimal 
conditions the USB displayed a potential to remove 14.9 kg NO3
--N/m3 reactor per day, 
representing a promising alternative for denitrification in seawater recirculating aquaculture 
systems.

3. Introduction 
3.1. Aquaculture and environment 
Historically, the global production of animal protein has been mainly dominated by poultry, cattle 
and pork. However, the world population is increasing rapidly and by 2050, 9.1 billion people will 
inhabit the planet (FAO, 2014). Higher amounts of quality protein are required but environment 
associated problems and low production efficiency call for new, more sustainable, alternatives.   
Fish farming has various advantages over land species: High densities can be reared in a relatively 
small area, the products contain high nutritional value (Timmons and Ebeling, 2010) and less 
carbon emissions are produced (SINTEF, 2009). Moreover, feeding conversion rates are by far 
lower compared to common livestock, requiring slightly more than 1 kg of feed to produce 1 kg of 
salmon or 1.8 kg of feed to produce 1 kg of tilapia (Boyd et al., 2007) in contrast to 2.99 and 10.4 
kg of feed to produce 1 kg of pork and beef, respectively (Tolkamp et al., 2010).  
Despite the above mentioned, aquaculture still has important challenges to overcome in terms of 
environmental impacts. Waste produced from faeces and unconsumed feed represents a source 
of organic carbon that must be properly treated to avoid becoming a source of pollution (Cripps 
and Bergheim, 2000; Sharrer et al., 2010; Suhr et al., 2015; Letelier-Gordo et al., 2017). Moreover, 
inorganic nutrients, such as phosphorous and nitrogen are precursors of eutrophication. The 
release of these nutrients from aquaculture results in more severe environmental regulations 
(Dalsgaard and Pedersen, 2011; Suhr et al., 2014). In Europe, due to the water framework directive 
(Directive 2000/60/EC) and particularly in Denmark (Danish Ministry of Environment, 2012), this 
regulation strengthening has become a barrier for aquaculture development (Klas et al., 2006; van 
Rijn et al., 2006) encouraging the need for new, more sustainable solutions.    
3.2. RAS systems  
Recirculating aquaculture systems (RAS) appear to be the most promising way to overcome the 
above mentioned environmental challenges. They have the capability to reduce water usage while 
improving waste and nutrient management (Martins et al., 2010; Timmons and Ebeling, 2010). 
This technology, defined by Rosenthal et al., (1986) as: “systems in which water is (partially) re-
used after undergoing treatment” has evolved over the years gaining complexity and efficiency 
but always keeping the same basic concept. Modern RAS systems are generally composed of 
mechanical filters to remove solids (e.g. drum filters, sand filters, swirl separators, cartridge filters, 
etc.), ultra violet (UV) lamps to control bacterial abundance and occasionally other specialized 
components, e.g. protein skimmers or ozone generators, etc., in addition to the core of any RAS: 
the biofilter. This unit is where bacterial communities convert the toxic ammonium (NH4
+) 
excreted by fish into nitrate (NO3
-), a much less toxic compound that has a 96h LC50s >1000 mg/L 
in fresh water fish (Colt, 2006) in a process called nitrification. Nitrate accumulates in the systems 
and is usually only balanced by the addition of make-up water (Masser et al., 1999; van Rijn et al., 
2006; Timmons and Ebeling, 2010). Therefore, if no treatment is applied to the effluent, a constant

discharge of nitrate into rivers, lakes, groundwater or the ocean would represent a potential 
source of pollution, promoting eutrophication. This could lead to nutrient imbalances and harmful 
algae blooms, phenomena associated with important losses and damages worldwide (Pearce et 
al., 2017). 
3.3. Denitrification  
A few alternatives for nitrate removal have been developed for water treatment. Among those are 
physical processes such as reverse osmosis, nanofiltration, electrodialysis or ion exchange; 
however, none of them are suitable for aquaculture as energy consumption and costs are too 
high. Biological denitrification, on the other hand, is much less energy demanding making it more 
attractive and feasible for RAS systems (Boley, et al., 2017). 
This process is from the reverse of the one carried out in the biofilter. However, instead of 
converting the nitrate back to ammonium, the end product is nitrogen gas (N2), an innocuous 
compound (Henze et al., 2002; Klas et al., 2006) which constitutes almost 80% of the atmosphere 
on Earth. In order to perform denitrification, heterotrophic facultative bacteria reduce nitrate 
under anoxic conditions using a carbon source as electron donor. This could be external 
(methanol, acetate, glucose, etc.), increasing the costs but allowing a better control over the 
process; or internal, termed single sludge denitrification, in which wastes produced by fish are 
hydrolyzed and fermented to obtain readily available carbon sources allowing a simultaneous 
reduction of organic matter and nitrate (Jewell and Cummings., 1990; van Rijn et al., 2006; Suhr et 
al., 2015; Letelier-Gordo et al., 2017). 
Despite the fact that aerobic and anoxic respiration processes in facultative bacteria are almost 
identical; from an energetic point of view, the first is favorable. If oxygen is present, 
microorganisms will switch their metabolism to aerobic and denitrification would be inhibited 
(Henze et al., 2002). Nevertheless, the oxygen concentration experienced by the bacteria is not 
the same as the one measured in the liquid phase because of the formation of different biomass 
aggregates such as flocs or biofilms where the oxygen penetration would depends on the 
aggregate characteristics (Henze et al., 2002). 
Although denitrification treatment has been rarely applied to aquaculture in the past, it is 
receiving increasing attention, particularly in marine systems, as rearing densities are getting 
higher and water exchange reduced, leading to high and problematic nitrate concentrations 
(Timmons and Ebeling, 2010) as the disposal of saline wastes entails complexity and costs. This 
promotes focus on denitrification in order to improve the development of sustainable 
aquaculture. 
The process of denitrification consists of 4 steps with intermediary compounds (eq. 1). By means 
of the nitrate reductase enzyme, bacteria convert nitrate into nitrite (NO2
-), the latter being an 
inhibitor of denitrification at high concentrations (Mara and Horan, 2004). Under circumstances of 
low Carbon:Nitrogen (C:N) ratios  the process cannot be completed and nitrite builds up. However,

Furthermore, some denitrifying bacteria lack the necessary enzymatic machinery to perform the 
complete denitrification process favoring the accumulation of nitrite, while others have their onset 
from this step and complete the process (Gerardi, 2005).  
The second step consists of the reduction of NO2
-
 to nitric oxide (NO), a compound that is 
converted in to nitrogen dioxide in the atmosphere. Afterwards, dinitrogen oxide (N2O) is 
produced, a well-known gas with applications in medicine and used as a power booster for engines 
but which is also a strong greenhouse gas and plays an important role in the destruction of the 
ozone layer (Ravishankara et al., 2009). The final step comprises the reduction of N2O to nitrogen 
gas, the latter one being released into the atmosphere.  
If denitrifying bacteria experience stressors such as; sudden changes in conditions or a lack of 
organic matter or other nutrients, imbalances can occur, which result in the release of 
intermediate compounds (Henze et al., 2002). 
3.3.1. 
Phosphorous and Sulfate 
Phosphorous (P) is another nutrient which plays an important role in eutrophication, especially in 
fresh water, as it represents one of the limiting factors for algal and cyanobacterial growth 
(Correll, 1998). Effluents from RAS systems may contain high amounts of this nutrient in form of 
orthophosphate (PO4
-), the only form of P that autotrophs can assimilate (Correll, 1998). 
Denitrification and phosphorous removal can be achieved simultaneously, as some denitrifying 
organisms are capable of accumulating polyphosphates under certain conditions, the so called 
DNPAOs (denitrifying polyphosphate accumulating organisms). This allows for the reduction of 
three potential pollutants (organic carbon, nitrogen and phosphorous) during biological 
denitrification (Kishida, et al., 2006) 
Sulfate reducing bacteria (SRB) are microorganisms capable of using sulfate as a final electron 
acceptor and a carbon source as an oxidizing agent under anaerobic conditions. The end product 
of this reaction is hydrogen sulfide (H2S), a gas with a characteristic “rotten eggs” smell, which has 
several undesirable effects; it is toxic to humans and animals in relatively low concentrations (Syed 
et al., 2006) as well as to bacteria, as it can diffuse through cell membranes causing protein 
denaturation (Appeals et al., 2008). It can also corrode sewage systems and infrastructure (Firer et 
al., 2008) and, if generated, it can inhibit denitrification and other wastewater processes due to 
competition for organic carbon sources by the SRB (Muyzer and Stams, 2008; Lu et al., 2016; Tal et 
al., 2009). Timmons and Ebeling (2010) stated that hydrogen sulfide production is favored by low 
redox conditions during denitrification and that minimum lower range for nitrate-N of 10 to 50 
mg/L is necessary to avoid problems with this toxic gas.

3.3.2. 
Alkalinity 
The nitrification process carried out in the biofilter consumes alkalinity at a rate of 2 mEq per 
oxidized mole of NH4
+, a loss which is commonly counterbalanced in RAS systems by the routine 
addition of sodium bicarbonate or other base. As opposed to this, biological denitrification 
produces alkalinity by the release of hydroxyl ions in a rate of 1 mEq per mole of NO3
- reduced 
(Timmons and Ebeling, 2010; van Rijn, 2006), representing a partial recovery of pH and alkalinity if 
the effluents are recirculated back to the system, reducing associated costs.  
3.3.3. 
Dissimilatory nitrate reduction to ammonia 
Dissimilatory nitrate reduction to ammonia (DNRA) is another NO3 reducing pathway. The end 
product of this process is NH3/NH4
+ (depending on the pH), the undesired toxic compound 
excreted by fish and oxidized in the biofilter. Therefore, DNRA has to be avoided in order to 
prevent a counterproductive effect during denitrification. It has been described that high C:N 
ratios tend to favor DNRA bacteria over denitrifiers, therefore this has to be kept between 3 to 6. 
However, it also depends on the nature of the carbon source (van Rijn, 2006; Washbourne et al., 
2011). 
3.3.4. 
Anammox 
A different way to reduce nitrogen compounds from wastewater is the anaerobic ammonium 
oxidation. Here, nitrite and ammonium are used as electron donor (instead of nitrate) under 
anaerobic conditions. The end product, as well as in denitrification, is nitrogen gas (Mulder et al., 
1995). This biological process has been described as a promising and cost-effective alternative due 
to lower organic carbon requirements and as it would further imply the absence of a biofilter, 
subsequently saving alkalinity and energy (Rong-rong et al., 2016). However, the technology is 
fairly new and further research is required before it can become a feature of modern RAS.  
3.4. Denitrification Reactors

heterogeneity is reflected in the carbon sources utilized as electron donors as well as in the 
parameters used to express denitrification levels, impeding a comparison. Table I summarizes 
present studies on reactor types in fresh as well as marine RAS.

Rijn (1995) and Shnel et al. (2002). Here, the water was pumped up from the basin of a two-step

3.5. Up-flow sludge blanket  
Another type of reactor, the one applied and analyzed in this study, is the up-flow sludge blanket 
(USB). The principle of this reactor differs from all previous mentioned configurations. In this case, 
biomass does not grow attached to any media and no biofilm or flocs are formed. Instead of being 
mixed inside the reactor, the bacteria are compressed at high densities, leaving a clear separation 
between water and biomass. This occurs due to the formation of bacterial granules with diameters 
up to several millimeters and very good settling properties (Laguna et al., 1999; Liu et al., 2006; 
Wang et al., 2014). The structure of these reactors consists of a vertical cylinder, often called 
column, with an outlet on the top and inlet in the bottom and usually provided with an inverted 
cone at the top as gas-liquid separator (figure 2). The water is pumped in an up-flow direction 
going through the dense biomass layer called blanket (or bed), where the denitrification process 
occurs. Therefore, the up-flow velocity has to be as high as desired without flushing out valuable 
biomass, which means lower than the settling velocity of the bacterial granules. The first approach 
to this reactor type was proposed by Lettinga et al. (1980), emphasizing their excellent 
performance in anaerobic treatment processes and pointing out its potential for VFA (volatile fatty 
acids) formation as well as for anoxic denitrification. To date, extensive information on granular 
sludge in anaerobic and aerobic environments can be found in literature, while information on 
anoxic granulation is scarce (Wang et al., 2014).

3.5.1. 
Important parameters for granular sludge characterization  
TSS and TVSS: Total suspended solids (TSS) and total volatile suspended solids (TVSS) are two of 
the main parameters to characterize any kind of wastewater treatment sludge (called mixed liquor 
suspended solids and mixed liquor volatile suspended solids in traditional wastewater treatment, 
MLSS and MLVSS, respectively). Both are described in standard methods (APHA/AWWA/WEF, 
2005), the first gives information about the concentration of solids (not dissolved) of a sample 
considering organic and inorganic fractions, while the second indicates only the volatile (organic) 
fraction. The ratio between both parameters (TVSS/TSS) is a useful parameter to determine the 
percentage of bacterial biomass within the total granule mass. 
SSV30: Settled sludge volume after 30 min. Used to determine the SVI (see below). Corresponds to 
the volume of sludge settled in a measurement cylinder 30 min after gentle but complete mixing 
of a 1 or 2 liter liquor sample (APHA/AWWA/WEF, 2005). 
SVI: Sludge Volume index. Measured in mL/g, is the results of the division of the SSV30 by the TSS, 
indicating the volume used by 1 g of sludge after 30 min of settling. Gives information of the 
settling properties of the granules being the lower, the better (APHA/AWWA/WEF, 2005).  
==PAGE:14==
 
10 
 
 
Settling velocity: Velocity at which the granular sludge settles. Measured in different ways 
depending on author. The technique used in this study is described in the materials and methods 
section. This parameter determines the highest up-flow velocity at which the reactor can work 
without flushing out granular biomass.  
Up-flow velocity: Velocity at which the water flows up inside the reactor, measured in this work as 
m/h. Must be lower than the settling velocity of the granular sludge in order to keep the biomass 
inside the rector preventing flushing it out.  
Hydraulic retention time: Inversely proportional to the up-flow velocity. Is the time spent by the 
water inside the reactor, usually in the order of hours.  
3.5.2. 
Granulation strategies 
Controlling up-flow velocity: This strategy stands by the principle that light bacterial biomass (e.g. 
flocs and floating filamentous bacteria) would be flushed out from the reactor, while heavy 
aggregations would stay at the bottom turning spontaneously into granules. Effluents can also be 
recirculated allowing the light biomass to re-enter the reactor getting attached to the heavy 
aggregates which aids in granule development (Hosseini et al., 2013; Val del Río et al., 2015).  
Promoting mineral precipitation: It has been described that the increase of the pH produced by 
denitrification itself can promote precipitation of CaCO3 or Ca3(PO4)2 if calcium ions are present 
(Green et al., 1994) favoring not only granulation but also helping the removal of phosphorous and 
calcium from the treated effluents (Val del Río et al., 2015). Liu and Sun (2011) reported a positive 
correlation of extracellular polymeric substances (EPS) formation and calcium concentrations 
suggesting that this mineral promotes the development of mature granules. Additionally, Wang et 
al, (2014) proposed a “string bag” hypothesis arguing that granules are formed by an inorganic 
nucleus of calcium carbonate or other mineral substance surrounded by a matrix of cocaceous 
bacteria and wrapped by a layer of filamentous bacteria all stick by action of the excreted EPS 
(figure 3).

Tarre and Green (1994), Liu and Sun (2011) and Val del Río at al. (2015) studied the role of mineral 
content on the granulation, arguing that calcium carbonate concentrations (and other carbonates) 
and alkalinity are enhancers for the process. The last authors also studied the effect and 
optimization of the up-flow velocity on the granulation and performance of an USB.  
Kishida et al. (2006) and Wang et al. (2014) focused their studies on the composition of granules. 
The first emphasizing on the utilization of DNPAOs and the latter analyzing whole granular 
microstructure. An approach to identify key parameters that are necessary to obtain good 
denitrifying granular sludge was conducted by Jin et al. (2012). Hosseini et al. (2013) studied the 
influence of pH and inhibitory effects of high nitrite and nitrate concentrations.  
In general, information on the use of up-flow sludge blankets reactors, specifically for 
denitrification of effluents from recirculating aquaculture systems, is scarce. To date, no study has 
been performed testing the behavior of an USB using seawater and sludge coming from a 
saltwater RAS.

4. Objectives  
General 
Evaluate the performance of an up-flow sludge blanket reactor as a candidate for denitrification in 
seawater recirculating aquaculture systems  
Specifics 
 
Develop anoxic granular sludge for denitrification using organic wastes from a seawater 
RAS and identify relevant parameters for its correct development. 
 
 
Identify an optimal operation point with a maximum denitrification rate testing different 
up-flow velocities.  
 
 
Evaluate possible accumulation or removal of nitrogen, phosphorus and sulfate 
compounds compared among the different up-flow velocities.

5. Material and methods 
The study was divided into two main phases: (1) Granulation and (2) Capacity evaluation. Parts of 
the methodologies will be explained separately for the two phases.  
All experiments and analyses were conducted at the experimental facilities of DTU Aqua, Hirtshals.  
5.1. Reactors and inoculum 
Three up-flow sludge blanket reactors were installed in parallel as replicates, each 3 m in height 
and 86 cm in diameter. However, reactors were set up to work until a height of 1.8 m, 
representing app. 10 L of inner volume. Internally, a metal grid, supported by a stem and 
connected to an electric motor was used in order to keep the content well mixed. The rotation 
speed was set to 25 RPM during the whole experimental period. Seven ports with ball valves, were 
distributed along the height of the column at 5, 30, 55, 80, 105, 130 and 135 cm. These were used 
as sampling points (Figure 4).  
 
Figure 4: Schematic diagram of reactors and set up. A: Granulation phase configuration. B: Capacity 
evaluation phase. Not to scale. 
==PAGE:18==
 
14 
 
The inoculum was prepared using sludge from a rainbow trout (Oncorhynchus mykiss) pilot-scale 
RAS system, with a salinity of 25 ppt. Three glass 10 L beakers provided with magnetic stirrers, 
were installed and initially 4 L of sludge taken from the swirl separators of the RAS and 6 L of 
water from the same system were mixed in each beaker. Daily, the stirrers were turned off for 30 
min to let the mixture settle and then 4 L of supernatant were removed. Subsequently, 2 L of fresh 
sludge and 2 L of RAS water were added to each beaker. After this process, 20 mL of a solution 
containing 100 mg/L of sodium nitrate (NaNO3) were added. The procedure was repeated during a 
week until obtaining denitrifying sludge by checking with MQuantTM nitrate colorimetric stripes.  
To inoculate the reactors, all denitrifying sludge was poured into a 50 L bucket and pumped inside 
the reactor using a peristaltic pump (Longer Pump BT300-2J) with three pump heads (Longer 
Pump YZ1515x). During this procedure the sludge was manually stirred in order to keep the 
mixture homogeneous.  
5.2. Phase I: Granulation 
During this phase, reactors were set up as batch, recirculating the effluent. Silicon hoses were 
connecting the outlet (at 1.8 m height) directly to the inlet, located at the base of the columns. 
The before mentioned peristaltic pump was installed and configured at 12 RPM delivering 55 L/d 
corresponding to an up-flow velocity of 0.4 m/h and an hydraulic retention time (HRT) of 4.6 h. 
Every other day, the pump was turned off for 30 min to allow settling of the sludge and the 
supernatant was removed from the seventh port, at a height of 1.55 m representing a volume of 1 
L. Reactors were subsequently refilled with the same volume of seawater.    
Daily, the columns were fed with a solution containing 15.17 g of sodium nitrate (NaNO3), 19.48 g 
of sodium acetate (NaAc) and 150 mL of Milli-Q® water, corresponding to a final concentration of 
1500 mg/L of COD (chemical oxygen demand) and 250 mg/L of NO3
--N (nitrate nitrogen) in the 
reactors. This lead to an obtained C:N ratio of 6, granting enough carbon for denitrification but 
preventing dissimilatory nitrate reduction to ammonia (Henze et al., 2002; Pagacova et al., 2010). 
The COD value was calculated by multiplying acetate mass by a factor of 1.07 according to Henze 
et al. (2002). 
At day 23, nitrate and acetate concentrations were duplicated aiming for 500 mg/L of NO3
--N. 
Later in the experiment, at day 44, feed concentrations were adjusted back to the original values.   
Every second day, the added solution was supplemented with a media composed by the following: 
phosphate (0.16 mg KH2PO4/mg N), ammonium (0.50 mg (NH4)2 SO4/mg N, 0.42 mg NH4Cl/mg N) 
and micronutrients (14.11×10−2 mg MgSO4/mg N, 11.41×10−6 mg CuCl2/mg N, 15.78×10−5 mg 
CoCl2/mg N, 3.00×10−5 mg MnCl2/mg N, 3.68×10−5 mg Na2MoO4/mg N, in proportion to the nitrate 
concentration. The formula was modified from Val del Río et al. (2015) using the same compounds 
but excluding the ones that are naturally present in seawater (seawater composition obtained 
from Turekian (1968)). 
==PAGE:19==
 
15 
 
During this phase of the experiment, characteristics of the sludge and granulation process were 
monitored. The sample was taken from the highest sampling port where sludge was still present 
(Day 0 to 16: port 4; day 17 to 30: port 3; Day 31 onwards: port 2). TSS, TVSS and SSV30 were 
measured. SVI as well as the TVSS:TSS ratio were calculated.  
Twice weekly, a sample was taken from each reactor and observed under the microscope. 
Granules (if present) were photographed and the diameters measured.  
5.2.1. 
Denitrification rate in granulation phase  
At day 57, the denitrification rate (in terms of mass) of the granulation phase was calculated. For 
this, reactors were fed as explained before and samples were taken from the seventh port every 
two hours during a time span of 16 hours in total. The sludge column was measured, and the 
volume calculated. Furthermore, TSS and TVSS were measured and the denitrification rate was 
determined by plotting the remaining nitrate-N concentration in the reactors over time (see eq. 3).  
5.2.2. 
Settling velocity  
At the end of the granulation phase (day 58), the settling velocity of the granular sludge was 
determined using a technique modified from Standard Methods (APHA-AWWA-WPCF, 2005). A 
glass measurement cylinder of 1 L was filled with water from the effluent and granular sludge, in a 
proportion relative to their volume percentage inside the reactor. Previously, sludge and water 
had been mixed gently by using a 1 L plastic bottle turned around 3 times. The content was then 
poured into the cylinder and stirred gently using a paddle with back and forth movements. The 
settled volume was recorded every minute for the first 10 minutes, and subsequently at 15, 20, 25 
and 30 minutes. Only the points where the curve showed a zero-order tendency where used. A 
trend line was added and the slope recorded. Converting milliliters to centimeters (1 mL from the 
cylinder = 0.3 cm), settling velocity was calculated.  This value was used to define the maximum 
up-flow velocity in the next phase. 
5.3. Phase II: Capacity evaluation  
Configuration of the reactors was modified in order to obtain a flow through circulation. A 600 L 
tank was used as sump, from where seawater was pumped continuously into the columns. A feed 
solution, containing NaAC, NaNO3
- and 1.7 L of Milli-Q® water was placed in 2 L glass bottles. The 
lids contained two hermetic ports with silicon hoses, one of which was going through a peristaltic 
pump (Longer Pump BT100-2J) and further connected to the inlet of the reactor while the other 
left free with an inverted air non-returning valve in order to prevent negative pressure inside the 
bottles by action of the pump, but guaranteeing that neither volatilization nor the escape of 
acetate from the bottles can occur (figure 4B). 
Eight up-flow velocities were tested in this experiment (table III), starting from the same velocity 
as used in the granulation phase, corresponding to 0.4 m/h, increasing gradually and ending with

5.6. Analytical methods

analysis. Samples for the capacity evaluation were not centrifuged and only filtrated using the 
same mentioned procedure and likewise stored at 4° C for later analysis.

determined using Standard Methods (APHA-AWWA-WPCF, 2005). Anions were analyzed using ion

organic acids – 250/7.8 column (Methrom, Sweden). Used supresor: 0.1 M LiCl; Used eluent: 0.5 
mM H2SO4.

6. Results and Discussion  
6.1. Phase I: Granulation 
The experimental period for the granulation phase lasted 61 days in total, day 0 being when the 
inoculum was poured into the reactors and day 61 being the last day before changing the 
configuration of the system from batch to flow through.  
6.1.1. 
TVSS and TSS 
At day 0, the concentration of TVSS in all reactors was 16,467 mg/L. However, immediately at day 
2 it dropped to 4,500 ± 543.65 mg/L (Figure 5A) representing a difference of 73 %. Regarding TSS, 
initial concentration was 16,933 mg/L and at day 2 5,978  mg/L ± 857.36  (Figure 5B), representing 
difference of 65%. The ratio between TVSS and TSS at day 0 and 2 was 0.97 and 0.76, respectively 
(Figure 5C), indicating that most of the biomass reduction belonged to the TVSS. This fact might be 
explained by hydrolysis activity in the reactors due to action of the up-flow velocity and better 
mixing than in the initial inoculum beakers.   
After day 2, both parameters showed relatively steady increases, reaching 19,233 ± 1,151 mg/L 
and 27,911 ± 2,107  mg/L (TVSS and TSS, respectively, figures 5A and 5B) at day 59 (last sampling 
day of granulation phase). These values are in the same order than found in literature (see table 
II), despite high variability.  
The ratio between TVSS and TSS remained relatively steady around 0.7 reaching a value of 0.69 ± 
0.01 at day 59, indicating a high percentage of mineral content in the sludge.  
After 60 days denitrifying synthetic waste brines, Belivaski et al. (2010) achieved a ratio of 0.8 with 
20 % mineral content using acetic acid as electron donor.  However, after 110 days, the ratio 
increased to 0.9 reducing the percentage of mineral content. As opposed to this, Jin et al. (2012) 
developed mature granular sludge at day 45 with approximately 75 % of volatile fraction, arguing 
that these characteristics are appropriate for granular sludge.  
The high mineral content exhibited by the present work may be the result of the operation 
strategy.  During the granulation phase, the effluent was recirculated and only a fraction of the 
supernatant was replaced with seawater leading to a constant increment in pH (figure 8B) and 
therefore promoting mineral precipitation (figure 15). This mineral content was not flushed out 
from the reactor, as water replacement was performed at the 7th port, at 135 cm reactor height.

The high standard deviation observed for the settling velocity was a result of different behaviors

measurement method, as some authors performed more complex techniques to measure this 
parameter arguing that classic methods are not accurate enough. Ghangrekar et al. (2005)

while Liu et al. (2006) used a modified sedimentation balance, according to a standard method 
developed by Paques BV, Balk, The Netherlands (Hulshoff 1989). A further explanation of the poor 
settling velocity could be related to the heterogeneity of the sludge as in the same sample large, 
medium and small granules as well as important amounts of flocculent-like biomass were 
observed (see Granules Diamater below).

replaced at day 49. The other 2 reactors started to show similar problems which led to the 
decision of replacing them all at day 61.

6.2. Phase II: Capacity evaluation  
The second phase of the experiment started at day 61. Initially, 8 up-flow velocities were planned 
to be tested, however at the 7th velocity (from day 109 to day 115) sludge started to get highly 
fluidized causing a sudden increase in the sludge column height. Large amounts of biomass started 
to be flushed out from the reactors, getting accumulated in the reservoirs from where the samples 
were taken and big accumulation of floating biomass started to appear in the surface of the water 
column, clogging the outlet of the reactors. This event determined the end of the experiment as at 
this point no granular sludge was observed and flocs with gelatinous appearance and floating 
characteristics predominated.  
6.2.7. 
TVSS and TSS  
Both, TVSS and TSS behaved similar during the second phase of the experiment (Figure 16). At the 
beginning of the trial, TVSS values showed 31,552 ± 1,295 mg/L, while at the second up-flow 
velocity (day 76) values increased considerably up to 55,156 ± 1,306 mg/L. From this point on, 
TVSS decreased continuously until the 7th up-flow velocity at day 111, where it was only 7,511 ± 
1,046 mg/L, a value only comparable with the one obtained at the beginning of the granulation 
phase.  
At the second up-flow velocity (day 76), TVSS was significantly different from all the other values 
except from day 83, corresponding to the 3rd up-flow velocity and 46,300 ± 2,367 mg/L of TVSS. 
Similarly, the 3rd up-flow velocity showed significant differences with all the other velocities, 
except for the one corresponding to the day 76. The 7th TVSS measurement was significantly lower 
than the 4th, 5th and 6th, corresponding to days 91, 98 and 104, respectively. 
At the beginning of the experiment, TSS values were 81,990 ± 4,445 mg/L and increased up to 
121,867 ± 2,268 mg/L at the second up-flow velocity being significantly higher than all other 
velocities, except for the 3rd (99,189 ± 5,574 mg/L). The 3rd as well as the 1st velocity were 
significantly higher than the 4th, 5th, 6th and the 7th, where the value dropped to 11,044 ± 502 mg/L, 
being significantly lower than all the other values.  
Interestingly, the TVSS/TSS ratio showed a complete inverse trend, increasing over time. After 
changing the reactors configuration, the volatile fraction of the biomass supposedly started to get 
flushed out from the reactors, likely because the effluents were recirculated, retaining light 
bacteria inside reactors during granulation phase. At day 69 (first up-flow velocity), the ratio was 
0.39 ± 0.02 and increased progressively until up-flow velocity 6 (day 104) where it reached a 
similar value compared to the one obtained in the previous granulation phase (0.72 ± 0.05). The 
6th and 7th ratio were significantly higher than the 1st, 2nd, 3rd and 4th (0.39 ± 0.02, 0.45 ± 0.01, 0.47 
± 0.01 and 0.52 ± 0.01, respectively), while the 5th ratio (0.61 ± 0.01), corresponding to day 98, was 
significantly higher than the 1st, 2nd and 3rd. The 4th ratio was only significantly higher than the 1st.

7. Conclusions and future perspectives  
After the first phase of the current study, granulated sludge was successfully developed, achieving 
granules up to more than 9 mm and good settling properties. However, the composition of the 
biomass was very heterogeneous resulting in a mixed aspect constituted by big granules, irregular 
aggregates and dispersed bacteria. Sudden changes in conditions contributed to this 
heterogeneity leading to the conclusion that these perturbations should be avoided.  
Important parameters for the granule development were identified and monitored. The minimum 
obtained SVI was optimal, while TSS and TVSS were increasing and decreasing accordingly with the 
evolution and aspect of the granular sludge. The ratio between TSS and TVSS indicated an 
important mineral fraction, which was confirmed by micrographs caption. This fraction aided in 
the granulation process giving the granulated sludge a heavier constitution. The determined 
settling velocity was very low taking into consideration other parameters of the granular. Other 
methods to determine this parameter more accurately should be considered. 
Dynamics of sulfate indicated neither accumulation of this anion, nor generation of hydrogen 
sulfide confirming the C:N ratio and applied strategy were adequate for the process. It is 
noteworthy that this high ratio is very unlikely on a commercial scale or using single sludge as 
carbon source. Under those scenarios, a buildup of hydrogen sulfide should not occur. 
Orthophosphate was not observed in any of the samples and total phosphorous results indicated a 
decline in concentrations, most likely because of the flushing-out effect of the reactors as no 
sources of phosphorous were present at the second phase. Special attention should be given to 
nitrite concentration, as apparently incomplete denitrification occurred during the course of the 
experiments. The ratio between NO2 + NO3 and TN in addition with the ammonium concentration 
indicates no presence of other forms of nitrogen in the effluent.  No dissimilatory reduction to 
ammonium occurred indicating that the C:N ratio was adequate and not excessive for the purpose 
of this study.  
The maximum denitrification rate achieved in this study represents a big potential for the USB, as 
under 0.97 m/h of up-flow velocity and its associated sludge characteristics, the reactors reported 
a removal of 321.9 mg NO3
--N/g TVSS per day, equivalent to 14.9 kg NO3
--N/m3 of reactor per day.  
In a scenario of producing 1,000 tons of rainbow trout per year, 3.65 m3 of granular sludge would 
be needed to denitrify 100 % of the produced nitrate, therefore 3 reactors with a capacity of 1.5 
m3 would be adequate to achieve complete denitrification. These findings profile the USB reactor 
as a promising candidate for seawater RAS effluents denitrification.
