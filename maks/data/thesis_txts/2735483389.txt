Abstract
The thesis addresses the research gap in tracing the renal arteries. The importance of renal arteries is due to the
direct correlation between kidney-related diseases and renal artery abnormalities, along with their importance in
nephrological surgeries. The project’s primary aim is to develop and evaluate an automatic centerline extraction
of the renal arteries’ vascular tree in contrast-enhanced CT scans by a deep reinforcement learning (DRL)
method.
The proposed solution suggests two modules: a Path Tracer model based on a value-based DRL
method employing a Double Deep Q-Network (DDQN) and a Branch Point Detector based on a 3D regression
network. The Path Tracer predicts the action of a single agent along an undivided centerline of the renal artery,
and the Branch Point Detector predicts a branch point probability for each centerline point. Combining the two
modules yields the potential of extracting the full vascular tree. However, combining them is out of the scope
of this thesis. Additionally, the study focuses exclusively on the left renal artery. The dataset consists of 62 CT
scans. The test of the Path Tracer model revealed a high variance in the predictions, thereby revealing poor
generalization capabilities. The Branch Point Detector successfully predicts branch point probabilities for each
centerline point. However, the highest errors of the test set reveal challenges in estimating or evaluating the
centerline points due to complicated data. Therefore, we need to access more data and improve the reliability
of the ground truth data for training and testing the modules further before we can combine them.

Introduction
The renal arteries, branching from the aorta, deliver a significant volume of oxygenated blood essential for
kidney function. These arteries are vital for the kidneys to perform their primary roles: filtering blood and
regulating blood pressure. The renal artery’s dual function includes providing the kidneys with oxygenated
blood and facilitating the filtration process—removing waste products and excess fluids excreted via urine while
reabsorbing minerals and nutrients [1]. The health and efficiency of the renal vasculature are essential for the
renal functionality. Therefore, identifying abnormalities in the morphology of the renal vasculature is critical for
diagnosing and treating kidney-related diseases [2]. The following fig. 1.1 provides a reference for the anatomy
of interest.
Left renal artery
Renal hilum
Right renal artery
Aorta

Figure 1.1: 3D figure of the abdomen illustrating the renal arteries branching off the aorta delivering blood to
the kidneys.
To address the necessity of identifying abnormalities in the renal arteries, an efficient method of representation
is required. A centerline representation effectively captures the artery’s trajectory and facilitates the extraction
of vascular properties [3], including branching patterns, curvature, vessel radii, and length. Such an approach
provides clinicians with valuable insights for early identification of potential issues enhancing the diagnosis and
treatment of kidney-related diseases [4].

==PAGE:13==
Medical imaging relies heavily on computed tomography (CT) scans, one of the most utilized modalities due
to their rapid acquisition, low risk, and high-resolution capabilities [5]. Despite their efficiency in capturing
detailed images, interpreting these scans demands considerable time. Since the renal arteries are small vascular
structures greatly intertwined with proximal anatomical structures [6], it is a tedious and time-consuming task
to manually extract the centerlines of the renal arteries. Manual annotation, while necessary, is a labor-intensive
process prone to variability and potential bias across different experts.
Automated centerline extraction minimizes the time and labor associated with manual extraction, ensuring
consistent and unbiased results. Its adaptability across various imaging modalities, not limited to CT, enhances
efficiency and robustness. This approach not only saves valuable time but also strengthens the reliability of
diagnostic outcomes, crucial for effective patient care and treatment decisions.
1.1
Literature Study
This section presents a literature study of centerline extraction of anatomical tubular structures to create an
overview of applied methodologies for automatic extraction and what experience has been gained when applying
those methods.
The most explored data for centerline tracking of vessels in medical imaging is coronary arteries.
It is a
fundamental phase of coronary analysis as it allows for analysis of the morphology that might lead to important
findings crucial for blood circulation. Likewise, there is reason to believe that the morphology of the renal
arteries affects the renal function. Yet, studies of attempts to track the centerline of the renal arteries are few.
Studies for extraction of the renal vascularity focus on the renal artery network in the kidney in micro-CT
scans applied in the industry, on animals, and in pre-clinical studies [7] and not the renal arteries. Thus, the
extraction methods from these have not proven clinical application for the diagnosis of a patient.
Traditionally, the centerline extraction algorithms have been divided into three categories: (1) Direct cen-
terline tracking, (2) Model-based optimization between two points, and (3) Minimal path techniques.
The direct tracking methods usually rely on the eigenvalues of the Hessian matrix to estimate the vascular
direction. However, it suffers from sensitivity to noise due to reliance on local intensity variations.
The optimization-based methods are classically performed by gradient descent schemes to fit a predefined model
but suffer from occasional suboptimal solutions due to local minima issues.
Minimal path tracing methods transform the centerline extraction problem into finding the shortest path in a
weighted graph, where the weights are determined by the image characteristics such as vesselness, medialness,
and tubularity. However, the cost functions might impose problems of accumulative errors and shortcut prob-
lems [3].
Their efficacy highlights the complexity of accurately modeling vascular structures and the ongoing need for
developing more robust algorithms.
With the introduction of Deep Learning (DL) across numerous fields, it is now being utilized as an ensemble
method with Reinforcement Learning (RL) as the state-of-the-art method for centerline extraction also known
as Deep Reinforcement Learning (DRL) [8]. This method utilizes Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs) for
cropped segments of 3D images to estimate the sequential steps of an artificial agent navigating through a vessel
from a starting point to an endpoint and thereby extracting a centerline. The agent receives feedback for each
step and thereby gains experience in optimizing its decision-making. RL alone requires tedious and large tables
to adjust for the decision-making. Therefore, it is very convenient to employ a CNN to substitute the tables
utilized in RL. The combination of DL and RL has freed each other from burdens such as tedious Q-tables in
RL and heavy modeling due to large image dimensions in DL in DRL methods.
There are two main streams within DRL methods; value-based and policy gradient-based. Typically, the value-

The aim of obtaining information about arteries has also been attempted by alternative models for segmen-
tation. The segmentation models will lead to the employment of grand CNNs which are time-consuming and
heavy models. Although Zhang et al.[17] prove successful with high extraction accuracy, the model suffers from
discontinuous geometries because it is insensitive to the geometric properties of branching. From a clinical point
of view, it is insufficient to consider these segmentation results as vessels need to be connected for analysis of
the flow [17].
Generally, centerline extractions have produced challenges of respectively maintaining vascular connectivity and
continuity and identification of branches.
To address the first challenge of continuous and connected vessels, Zhang et al. [11] proposed in 2018 a DRL
approach in which a DQN [9] is included to predict stepwise state-action value for each action. The DQN is
trained for tracking the centerline of the thoracic aorta on more than 2000 3D volumes of multiple modalities
including contrasted CT, non-contrasted CT, C-arm CT, and MR images. The agent has a 6-direction action
space in these 3D volumes and a point-to-curve reward function. The method succeeded in tracking the cen-
terline in real time and outperformed a supervised 3D CNN with a similar architecture as the DQN for four
different types of images. The model does not attempt to detect bifurcations disabling further applications for
detecting vascular trees.
Similarly, Zhang et al. [12] developed an algorithm for centerline tracking and introduced a Branch-Aware
Coronary Centerline Extraction (BACCE) method in 2020. The model detects branches and is faster due to a
larger action space of 26 directions, including diagonal steps, and the introduction of a double deep Q-network
(DDQN) instead of a DQN. DQNs are known to lead to overestimation of the state-action values. Thus in-
troducing a DDQN leads to a more robust decision-making [18]. The full BACCE model consists of a tracker
(agent) and a detector referred to as the branch-aware part. The branch detection is achieved by implementing
a 3D dilated CNN which identifies the lumen radius and returns a branch probability for each step. When a
branch is detected an additional agent is initialized for tracking branches.
Likewise, Li et al. [14] report of DRL model with a Multi-task Discriminator for the detection of bifurcations
and terminations simultaneously. Thereby, the model tracks tree-structure centerlines for coronary arteries in
CTA scans. Different from Zhang et al. [12], the agent decides in which order to track branches supported by
a dynamic reward. The agent avoids backtracking by setting a landmark where the raw image is manipulated.

Su et al. [10] introduce centerline extraction of the cerebral anterior vessel tree based on a policy gradient-based

In 2023 Fu et al. utilize a DDQN as an optimization tool for a preliminary coronary network on X-ray Coronary
Angiographies (XCAs). Thus, the centerline extraction step is divided into three parts: image thinning, segment
re-connection, and DRL optimization. The image thinning and segment re-connection deliver the preliminary
coronary network to the DRL model where the reward function captures the deviation between the preliminary
coronary network and the action of the agent. The method demonstrates high accuracy on a clinical set and a
third-party set. However, the average optimization time is 150s, and the model operates on 2D data indicating
that implementing it with 3D data could result in high processing times. Due to the many steps and high
processing time, it is not fit for CT scans with an increasing dimension.
The majority of DRL-based tracing of anatomical structures rely on reward functions which depend on the
proximity to annotated ground truth GT. Yet, in some instances the GT is unavailable or sparse due to chal-
lenges of securing precise annotations.
Therefore, Dai et al. [13] suggest in 2019 to utilize a 2D synthetic training dataset for an actor-critic DRL-
method which is an improvement of the policy-gradient method with a value-based critic to ensure faster
convergence and avoid local maxima. The actor controls the behaviour of the agent, and the critic measures the
quality of the action[8]. Their method and implementation proved that a model could be trained on synthetic
data and tested on two-photon microscopy images of mice yielding comparable performance to the standard of
the time for neuronal microscopy.
In 2022 Shin et al. [15] propose a model for tracking small bowels by training on annotated GT paths and some
weakly annotated samples with segmentations but without a marked GT path. To do so, they suggest a reward
function based on geodesic distance measures and use an actor-critic algorithm. The model demonstrated the
ability to train a tracker using scans with GT path along with weak annotations without GT path.
DRL methods have been successfully applied for centerline extraction based on the presented studies in this
section. Despite advancement and an increase in attempts at segmenting and tracking different anatomical
structures, there remains a research gap in tracing renal arteries in clinical medical imaging. Therefore, it is
relevant to investigate if a DRL method can sufficiently extract the centerline of the renal arteries.
1.2
Aim of the Thesis
This project aims to develop and evaluate an automatic centerline extraction of the renal arteries’ vascular tree
in contrast-enhanced CT scans by a deep reinforcement learning method. The clinical relevance is established
by the direct correlation between kidney-related diseases to abnormalities of the renal arteries and their impor-
tance in nephrological surgeries [4].
To accomplish centerline extraction of the renal artery’s vascular tree we propose a larger framework where
two modules are combined. However, combining the two modules are not feasible within the scope of this
thesis. Therefore, we suggest a two-fold solution where we aim to:
• Develop and validate a Path Tracer model: Can we trace the renal arteries in CT scans by deep
reinforcement learning?
• Develop and validate a Branch Point Detector model: Can we predict if a point on the centerline
is a branch point or not?

2.1
The Kidneys
Below the ribcage on each side of the columna is a kidney in the shape of a bean. These organs are vital and
fill out crucial roles in maintaining homeostasis (inner balance of the body) [1] by excretory, regulatory, and
endocrine activities.
The aorta is the large blood vessel branching off the left ventricle of the heart and extending upwards into
the chest forming an arch. From the arch, it ascends downwards into the abdomen to supply the rest of the
body with blood. Several arteries branch off to distribute blood to the rest of the body. Some of these are the
renal arteries supplying the right and the left kidney with blood pumped from the myocardium into the aorta
and through the left and right renal arteries to the kidneys. The kidneys, the abdominal part of the aorta, and
the renal arteries are displayed in fig. 2.1 [1].
The kidneys are dependent on a constant and sufficient blood supply and it shows as the kidneys are supplied
with approximately 20% of the cardiac output from the aorta through the renal arteries [19].

2.1.1
Filtration
The main function of the kidneys is to filter the blood and remove waste products and excessive fluids by
excreting them through the urine. This filtration process is facilitated by the glomeruli, which are the kidney’s
filtration units. The renal arteries supply these units with oxygenated blood and enter the kidney through the
renal hilum, which acts as a gateway for the entry and exit of the kidney for blood vessels, but also nerves,
lymphatics, and ureters. When the blood exits the kidney it goes through the renal vein exiting at the renal
hilum into the inferior vena cava [2]. In fig. 2.2 the kidney, renal arteries, and relevant anatomy are illustrated.

The function of the kidneys is measured by the glomerular filtration rate (GFR), which is estimated using a
mathematical formula (eGFR). The estimate measure eGFR is based on the level of creatinine in the blood,
and the extraction of the creatinine is solely facilitated by the kidneys through glomeruli. Creatinine is a waste
product, thus an indicator of kidney damage as creatinine builds up in the blood if the filtration in the kidneys
is insufficient. The eGFR is given in mL/min and normalized to a 1.73m2 body surface area [2].
2.2
Renal Arteries
Each kidney is supplied by a single main renal artery branching from the abdominal aorta in a direct course
towards the renal hilum as a single and undivided vessel [4]. The renal arteries branch off at the abdominal
aorta as a lateral branch and divide into an anterior and posterior branch near the hilum (see fig. 2.2b). At
the root of the renal artery, the diameter measures approximately 5-6mm and the arteries hold a total length
of 4-6cm[21].
However, there are vascular variations of the renal artery which does not align with the above definition. Vari-
ations in the renal structure are common and have been widely reported. They may vary in level of origin,
number, and structure. Some studies point to approximately 50 % of patients having variations in the structure
of the renal artery. Renal artery variations are mainly divided into early branching in approximately 10 % of
patients and accessory renal arteries in approximately a third of patients. Also, differences in the right and left
structure of the renal artery are observed. Anatomically the abdominal aorta lies more on the left side resulting
in the right renal artery being longer in most patients [19].

The presence of accessory renal arteries and bifurcations plays a significant role in the risk of hypertension,
as evidenced by their higher prevalence among hypertension patients. Renal Artery Stenosis (RAS) is recog-

nal arteries.[22]. The accurate mapping of renal artery morphology, including the vascular centerline and its
branches, is of great importance in modern medical practices, particularly for guiding interventional surgeries
and enhancing computer-aided detection of pathologies [3]. The extraction of the renal artery centerline facili-
tates the identification and treatment of abnormalities that could compromise the vascular supply to the kidneys.
2.2.1
Additional/Accessory Renal Arteries
The most common renal vascular variant is additional renal arteries which occur in almost a third of patients
[23]. These arteries may originate from the aorta and terminate in the kidneys alongside the main renal artery.
The variation of an additional renal artery (ARA) is more frequently observed for the left kidney [4] and they
may arise more frequently below the main renal artery. In some cases, they do not enter the renal hilum but
instead enter at the upper or lower pole of the renal capsule [21]. The presence of accessory arteries or renal
artery branches is higher in the group of patients diagnosed with hypertension. Therefore, it has become crucial
to understand the variation in the renal vasculature [22]. An example of an ARA is shown in fig. 2.3a.
2.2.2
Early Branching
Another notable variation is the early branching of renal arteries, where the division into segmental arteries
occurs before the artery reaches the renal hilum [19]. Both the main renal artery and the accessory renal artery
can branch off to either the renal hilum or outside. This early division can have implications for surgical proce-
dures, renal imaging, and the management of renal pathologies, necessitating detailed anatomical understanding
for accurate diagnosis and intervention. It is common practice to recognize early branching in renal transplant
to accomplish a successful connection of the aorta and the renal artery [21].

Computed tomography also referred to as CT is widely used for medical imaging as it is a non-invasive and

inner body. The patient is scanned on a bed as shown in fig. 2.4a where the X-ray source and detectors rotate

The organs are shown with different intensities in the image due to the different tissues’ absorption of X-rays.
Tissues with higher densities will be displayed with higher intensities in the CT scan also known from planar
X-ray images. This is due to the attenuation of the primary X-ray beam. When it passes through the patient it
results in scatter and absorption processes which are characterized by the linear attenuation coefficient. Each
tissue has an attenuation coefficient which represents different intensity values in a CT scan. These intensity
values are known as Hounsfield Units (HU) given a fixed scale for describing the change in attenuation. The
scale is set given the attenuation of water and air [24].
Yet, clinical imaging utilizes contrast agents that operate as a contrast enhancer of certain structures or fluids
within the body by absorbing more X-rays. When absorbing more X-rays, the attenuation coefficient changes
and the tissue appears with larger intensities in the image. Contrast agents are typically injected into the blood
which is of great convenience for examining cardiovascular structures. Cardiovascular structures can be difficult
to visualize. However, the contrast agents allow to enhance the contrast and also distinguish between veins and
arteries. To capture the differences the imaging has to be timed so that it matches the contrast agents’ flow.
First, the contrast agent is injected into the arteries and flowing rapidly which is considered the arterial phase.
After the contrast agent enters the venous phase by flowing through the veins[26]. Depending on which phase
the images are acquired the arteries and veins will appear brighter respectively. As abnormalities in arteries in
general pose a greater threat to health than abnormalities in the veins it is important to consider the arteries.

3.1
Reinforcement Learning
Reinforcement learning (RL) is one of the three machine learning paradigms that create a setting which tries to
mimic the decision process of humans. The goal is to teach an artificial agent how to navigate in an environment
based on maximizing a cumulative reward. The agent is supposed to select the action which in the end leads
to the largest cumulative reward based on the current state. The agent is sequentially acting by observing the
current environment and rewards (see fig. 3.1). Everything that the agent cannot control is considered as the
environment [27].
A discrete global clock t = 1, 2, ... defines the order in which actions occur and is also referred to as time steps.
For every time step t the agent is in a position pt. In this position, the agent has access to a local environment,
also called the state st. Based on the current state st and reward rt, the agent has to decide on which action
at ∈A to make. Given the action, the environment changes and a new state st+1 is entered and new feedback
to the agent is given in the form of a reward rt+1.

3.1.1
Markov Decision Process
The Markov Decision Process (MDP) is a mathematical framework that is used for decision-making problems
and is a standard formulation for sequential learning. Thus, the RL problem presented can be formalized using
the MDP and clarify the role of the components of RL such as states, actions, rewards, and policies.
An MDP model consists of S a finite set of states, A a finite set of actions, T a transition function between
states, and R a reward function.
• S: A set of possible environmental states s ∈S.
• A: A set of possible actions a ∈A.
• T : A transition probability defining the probability of state st leading to following state st+1 after taking
action at.
For each action a, there is a transition probability that the state s will go to state st+1,
T (s, a, st+1), based on a probability distribution over a set of possible transitions.
• R: A real-valued reward function giving the expected immediate reward after transitioning from st to
st+1 after action at.
T and R define the model of the MDP. There are legal and illegal states and the set of actions which are ready
to be applied in a state s ∈S is denoted A(s). The transition probability given all actions a, and all states st
and st+1 is 0 ≤T (st, at, st+1) ≤1 and for all states P
st+1∈S T (st, at, st+1) = 1.
This project deals with episodic tasks referring to a system modeled in episodes. An episode is the progress of
the agent from the first state to the goal state by moving in sequential steps. The goal state is a subset of the
finite set of states G ⊆S also referred to as a goal state area. The agent progresses sequentially through the
states until reaching G where the episode ends. The task of finding a renal artery centerline deals with a goal
state as the end of the renal artery. Yet, there is no fixed number of steps for the agent to take, so the task
deals with an indefinite horizon task where each episode can end but the episode can have an arbitrary length.
Putting these together the Markov Decision Process is a tuple of < S, A, P, R > [27].

we have to set out for a good policy π. The agent has to learn a strategy for its decision-making which is

aims at controlling the environment modeled as an MDP. The policy π is a computable function that outputs

3.2
Deep Learning
Deep Learning (DL) is a subfield of Machine Learning (ML) that utilizes multilayered artificial neural networks
[30]. Since its origin, it has been applied for various applications, and within medical imaging, 3D Convolu-
tional Neural Networks (CNNs) have become widely popular. The neural networks find patterns in the data
and leverage it for predicting a target output [31]. Thereby, A 3D CNN learns the spatial features by taking an
input of 3D such as the states s that are the environments used to navigate in the CT scan for an RL method.
Therefore, CNNs are applicable for estimating q-values in the context of Q-learning for a DRL model.
Page 12 of 63
==PAGE:24==
DL methods are often supervised implying that they use labeled data to train the network to predict new
outcomes. In the training process, the data is fed to the model whereafter it adjusts its weights until the model
has been fitted appropriately. This involves the model learning according to a loss function that evaluates how
well the model is performing on the data set. The loss function influences the update of the model’s weights
through backpropagation for fine-tuning the network parameters.
Training a DL model involves several phases, namely training, validation, and testing. The training phase
focuses on learning from the data, the validation phase on tuning the hyperparameters and avoiding overfitting,
and the testing phase on evaluating the model’s performance on unseen data. This approach ensures that the
model generalizes well to new unseen data that have not been presented to the model yet.
DL is widely applied for regression and classification problems. While classification tasks involve predicting
discrete labels, regression tasks predict continuous outcomes. This distinction influences the architecture and
function of the neural networks designed for these tasks.
Depending on the task at hand and data there exist many types of architectures.
3.2.1
3D Regression Network
Regression problems require different architectures based on the data and the problem at hand. Therefore, it
is important to consider the input dimensions and output dimensions of the neural network.
A 3D CNN is composed of convolutional layers that perform convolutional operations with different kernels on
the 3D input data e.g. CT scans. When the model trains, it is the values of the kernels that are adjusted and
down-sampled by a pooling layer. From the convolutional operations, it extracts and learns spatial features,
and patterns across the three dimensions making it excellent for volumetric data to capture the features in all
dimensions.
In general, the architecture requires the last layers to be fully connected so that each neuron is connected to
every neuron in the previous layer allowing the network to learn complex patterns. The fully connected layers
are typically followed by the convolutional layers to integrate the features extracted from these layers into a
structure that can be used to make predictions [31].
Deeper Neural Networks are difficult to train. However, it might seem desirable to add layers to learn more
complex features. Yet, it has been shown, that there is a maximum depth threshold. The introduction of
residual neural networks enabled the training of much deeper networks by using residual blocks and skip con-
nections. The residual network (ResNet) is trying to learn the residual mapping F(x) instead of having layers
that attempt to learn the underlying mapping H(x). The residual mapping represents the residual function
F(x) = H(x) −x which recast the underlying mapping to H(x) = F(x) + x. Therefore, the skip connections
pass on the input x to the block from the initial layers as information to the deeper layers (see figure fig. 3.2).
This will not introduce any additional parameters. The output of a residual block is the sum of its input x and
the residual mapping F(x) [32].

Originally, ResNets are applied for classification problems. However, these networks can be adapted for regres-
sion by modifying the final layers. For a regression task, the network’s last fully connected layers are adjusted
to output a single value or a vector of continuous values, depending on the problem at hand. This modification

3.3
Deep Reinforcement Learning
Due to the high representation of Deep Learning (DL) in recent contexts, Deep Reinforcement Learning (DRL)
methods have been suggested as a combination of RL and DL. The combination is a synergy that allows the
aforesaid policy and value functions to be estimated by neural networks. In the context of medical imaging,
convolutional neural networks (CNNs) bring a great advantage in dealing with the high dimensionality of the
data which traditional RL methods struggle with.
DRL still relies on the same approaches of estimating the value function or the policy function. In this project
as mentioned the focus is on the value-based method Q-learning.
3.3.1
Deep Q-Networks and Double Deep Q-Networks
The combination of DNN and Q-learning in RL gives rise to Deep Q-networks (DQNs). The core idea behind
DQNs is to use a deep neural network to approximate the q-values given the Bellman equation (see eq. (3.5)),
which is the function used in Q-learning to estimate the value of a given action in a given state. Therefore, the
DQN is simply a function estimator of the q-values. DQNs are currently the most utilized DRL method aiming
at learning the policies directly from high-dimensional data.
Double Deep Q-Networks (DDQNs) addresses the problem of overestimation in Q-learning. It was first im-
plemented in 2015 by Hado van Hasselt et al.
in their paper “Deep Reinforcement Learning with Double
Q-Learning" [18].
The challenge of Q-learning is that the Q-function is based on estimating future rewards rather than true re-
wards. Often this will lead to an overestimation of the q-value because an inaccurate model of the environment
is used or more simply put, the agent expects higher rewards than actual rewards, leading to compromised
decision-making. This is due to the maximization step in Q-learning (see eq. (3.6)) where the maximum is
taken of the q-values for the estimated action steps, leading the agent to prefer overestimated values rather than
underestimated values.

ϵ-Greedy Algorithm
The agent is not given a policy at first and must therefore discover which actions yield the largest rewards
by trial and error. For each action, there is a probability distribution for the generated reward. It is further
assumed that there is at least one action which will generate a maximum numerical reward. The probability

distribution is unknown to the agent, but the agent is aiming at identifying the action leading to the best
reward. At any point, the agent is facing the choice between making an action that will trigger larger rewards
or exploring to improve its knowledge about its current action. The key idea is to balance exploration and
exploitation. The ϵ-greedy strategy is a simple strategy to tackle the problem regarding the urge to choose
the strategy known to work versus exploring unnecessarily by choosing to explore or exploit randomly. The
agent selects an action yielding the highest q-value by the probability of (1 −ϵ) and a random action by the
probability of ϵ. By presenting the agent for random actions the probability distribution of the rewards given
different actions will become more familiar to the agent. Naturally, ϵ should decrease with time as the agent
becomes more experienced and so will the need to explore. The ϵ-greedy strategy used to train the DQN is as

4.4.3
Detection of Starting and Endpoint
The detection of landmarks, specifically the start and end point(s) of the renal artery centerline, is essential
for accurately establishing the artery’s trajectory. The renal artery branches from the aorta where it has its
starting point and terminates in the lumen of the kidney. For the Path Tracer model only a single endpoint
is identified for the longest branch whereas multiple endpoints are found to extract the centerline tree for the
Branch Point Detector. These landmarks, marking the extremities of the renal artery, are identified through
different methodologies.
The starting point, representing the renal artery’s origin from the aorta, is supposedly located at the midpoint
of the interface between the aorta and renal artery segmentations. This midpoint is approximated through a
straightforward morphological operation: dilation of the aorta’s segmentation, followed by identification of the
center of mass of the resultant overlapping volume with the renal artery segmentation. Initially, the aorta’s
segmentation is dilated (see fig. 4.4a), creating an overlapping area with the renal artery segmentation that
signifies their shared boundary (fig. 4.4b). The center of mass within this overlapping volume is then designated
as the starting point (fig. 4.4c).

For the Branch Point Detector endpoints are determined for each branch. If there are more branches, there

applying the Dijkstra algorithm. When extracting the centerline tree we must consider that there are multiple
endpoints. These endpoints are identified by finding local maxima using Dijkstra’s algorithm, thus accounting
for the presence of multiple endpoints. To accurately identify these endpoints, Dijkstra’s algorithm is combined
with the detection of local minima to pinpoint the endpoints of each branch segment and not just the renal
artery’s main course.
The Dijkstra algorithm is implemented through the library vtk in Python also known as the Visualization

4.4.4
Centerline Representation and Extraction
The centerline representation of the renal artery, despite its simplicity, offers a nuanced depiction of the artery’s
structure, capturing its curvature and branching patterns. This representation is particularly useful for measur-
ing both topological and morphological characteristics of the arteries, which are vital for planning and executing
interventional procedures like stenting and angioplasty [40]. Consequently, the extraction of centerlines is a crit-
ical step that facilitates a range of medical applications.
The process for defining the ground truth centerline involves the segmentation of the renal artery and the
identification of its start and end points. The starting point marks the beginning of the centerline, while the
endpoint signifies its termination. The segmentation works as a binary mask of the voxel volume. The binary
mask of the renal artery segmentation is turned into a surface representation by the Visualization Tool Kit in
Python applied as the library vtk [39]. The surfaces are essentially collections of triangles connected at their
edges.
The sequence of points forming the centerline between these two extremities (start and endpoints) is derived
using a Voronoi diagram. The steps from a segmented surface to a centerline are illustrated in fig. 4.5 and are
executed in Python with the Vascular Modeling Tool Kit library vmtk [41]. In this context, the Voronoi diagram
is utilized to approximate the center of the largest sphere that can be inscribed within the artery at any given
point along its length. The centerline, therefore, is determined by the path that minimizes the integral of the
radii of these inscribed spheres, effectively seeking the shortest path that maximizes the clearance from the
artery walls, a concept known as the "radius metric" [37]. This tool works for finding the centerline with and
without branches.

This project aims to extract the centerline of renal arteries by a Path Tracer model based on a DRL model as

The Path Tracer holds the important function of tracing the renal artery from its starting point to a single
endpoint without considering bifurcations and additional branches.
The DRL model requires a starting point, a raw scan, and a ground truth annotation of the centerline for
the training process. During training for each episode, the agent starts at the starting point and according to
the current policy the agent travels around in the raw scan until the episode terminates given some stopping
criterion elaborated on in section 5.1.4. The agent encounters 3D states s of the size of 41 x 41 x 41 voxels
centered around the agent’s current position pt in the raw scan. The choice of action for the following state
st+1 is based on this small piece of information.
To realize a DRL model, I have been given a DRL framework from my supervisor that has been modified
to trace renal arteries. This DRL model utilizes a value-based method Q-learning. More specifically, it utilizes
a Double Deep Q-network for estimating the state-action values q. Based on the estimated q-values, the agent
takes a step.
To construct a centerline representation of the renal arteries from CT scans, such as those obtained from the
CGPS study, it is important to understand that these scans produce volumes composed of voxel grids. This
voxel-based structure is advantageous because centerline extraction algorithms are designed to work with data
in a rectilinear grid format when using a discrete action space A [42]. Within this 3D voxel grid, the centerline
is conceptualized as a curve that follows a 26-connected path, meaning that any given voxel is in contact with
26 other voxels.
The agent can move around in an action space A of 26 directions from its current position pt. The action space
A is a discrete set of the 26 directions available for the agent at any time step t. These 26 directions are given
by the Cartesian coordinates so that the agent can move to all the adjacent voxels including diagonally. The
agent navigates by taking step by step size ω. For this model ω = 1 which corresponds to moving a single voxel
per step. A set of vectors with (x, y, z) coordinates are definitive for the action space relative to the current
position of the agent pt. The difference between the current position pt and the consecutive position pt+1 is

5.1.1
Path Tracing Reward Function
The aim of using reinforcement learning for centerline tracking is to predict a path as close as possible to a ground
truth annotation of a centerline in a volumetric 3D image I. In this project, the data applied as images are CT
scans. The ground truth annotation, denoted as GT, consists of K points, formulated as GT = {g0, g1, . . . , gK}.
At every time step t the agent selects an action at based on the current state/environment st until termination
of the tracking creating a path denoted P consisting of T points, formulated as P = {p0, p1, . . . , pT }. Based
on each action at a scalar reward rt is given to evaluate the effect of going from st to st+1. The reward works
as a response to the effect of the action and is therefore based on the two measurements, the proximity to the
GT, and the progress of the agent along the centerline. To quantify these two measurements the distance to
the closest point gk on the GT is found to the agent. However, the closest point gk is not necessarily a point
on the GT but might be on a line segment in between two points of GT.
To design a reward function that captures both the proximity to the GT and the progress along it, we in-
corporate two measures. First, the distance from pt to the closest point gk determined as d = |gk −pt| is applied
in the reward function to penalize an agent moving further away from the GT.
Secondly, an additional measure, the distance value vk, is introduced to indicate the progress along the center-
line toward the end-point. The distance value vk is the accumulated distance from the start-point along the GT
to the current point gk. To assign a distance value to the closest point gk in between to annotated GT points
gk−1 and gk+1 the measured distance values vk−1 and vk+1 are weighted to find the distance value vk as shown

5.1.2
Replay Memory
Replay memory is utilized during the training process of a DQN. Without using replay memory the samples fed to
the DQN would consist of consecutive experiences in the environment. Hence, there would be a high correlation
between the samples causing instability in the training. The samples are used to update the DQN, thus it is
important to avoid high correlation. Therefore, replay memory is introduced which essentially is a data buffer
for storing past experiences e observed by an agent while interacting with the environment. Each experience
stored consists of a state st, an action at, a reward rt, and the following state st+1, et = {st, at, rt, st+1}. These
experiences are stored in a buffer with a fixed capacity and retain the most recent ones. Mini-batches of a set
size are randomly sampled from the replay memory as the DQN is trained leaving for several advantages such
as breaking the correlation in consecutive experiences, stability in the training process by making the network
less dependent on the order of experiences, and improved learning from a broader range of experiences [44].

5.1.3
Path Tracing Evaluation Metrics
The performance of the Path Tracer is evaluated upon the model’s ability to predict the path P = {p0, p1, ..., pM}
of the ground truth centerline points GT = {g0, g1, ..., gN} correctly. A straightforward approach to compare
the centerline P to GT would be considering the overlap of the two centerlines. However, it is not as simple as
comparing the overlap of two position points in the image grid. Points represent a sparse volume within large
volumes, such as large CT scans. This sparsity challenges metrics that depend on overlap, due to their sensitivity
to the size of the overlap. Instead of considering the overlap of the predicted path P and GT a proximity measure
is implemented to account for data sparsity in the considerably relatively larger image volume. This is addressed
by rewarding proximity between P and GT by introducing a distance measure between points on P and GT.
While evaluating the similarities between the actual centerline GT and the predicted centerline P, we address
potential proximity variations, accounting for pm-to-gn and gn-to-pm distances. This accuracy measure takes
into consideration the variability in length between the two centerlines, as illustrated below:

5.1.4
Stopping Criteria For Path Tracing
To succeed the agent has to reach the end-point of the renal artery. During training, when the agent is no
further than 4 voxels from the end-point an episode terminates as it is considered that the agent has reached
its goal state. Yet, the agent might find itself in situations far away from the end goal due to an explorative
policy during training. Consequently, other stopping criteria are necessary when training and are implemented
as means to avoid states that hinder a training to completion such as occilation and reaching states outside the
image, so that the agent is not stuck in unfavorable states. Therefore, the stopping criteria are:
• End goal reached: The agent has reached the end-point within a 4-voxel distance and is considered to
have succeeded. The end-point is considerably close even though a margin of 4 voxel is introduced and
oscillation is avoided in some cases when the agent has a favored policy.
• Maximum number of steps exceeded: The agent has exceeded the maximum number of steps/actions.
• Deviation: The position of the agent pt has exceeded 20 voxels.
• Oscillation: The agent has visited the current position more than four times.
By introducing these stopping criteria the agent should learn more effectively. The policy that the agent learns
during training should ideally lead the agent to the endpoint and is considered to start oscillating around the
endpoint. Hence, the oscillation of the agent during testing is the stopping criterion.

The Path Tracer previously described in chapter 5 is implemented as a DRL model. The core functionality
involves navigating an artificial agent along the renal artery’s centerline to a single endpoint, guided by the
agent’s policy. The implementation of the Path Tracer disregards branch points and only considers a single
main branch. However, in some cases, the renal arteries present bifurcations which imply multiple branches and
thus more than a single endpoint.
To enable the Path Tracer to trace branches in order to achieve tracing of the full vascular tree of the renal ar-
teries it is relevant to consider branch points because they introduce another branch which needs to be detected.
The idea is to implement a Branch Point Detector that detects if the agent is approaching a branch point or
not. As a baseline model, it is relevant to consider each position of the agent pt and detect if the current state
st is approaching a branching point or not.

Discussion
In this project, a Path Tracer model and a Branch Point Detector model have been employed separately on
environments cropped around centerline points in CT scans. The Path Tracer model was built on a DRL model
applying Q-learning by a DDQN. The agent was trained to follow the centerline of the renal arteries in each
CT scan. Yet, the agent is not aware of branch points in cases of a branching structure. Therefore, a separate
Branch Point Detector model was employed to detect if the environments/states s the agent meets along the
centerline are branch points. The Branch Point Detector is implemented by a 3D ResNet18 model modified to
function as a regression network to deliver a probability of each centerline point being a branch point.
Finally, the two models, a Path Tracer model, and a Branch Point Detector model have been trained and tested.
The results obtained in section 7.1 and section 7.2 will be discussed in terms of their achievements, limitations
and their joined possible prospects.
8.1
Insights from Model Results
The Path Tracer model learns patterns by employing a ϵ-greedy strategy during training. This is reflected
in the training curves of the loss, the cumulative rewards, and the modified training DICE score. However,
the training curves reveal an unstable training with fluctuations in the measures. The fluctuation in the loss
during training can be a result of employing a DDQN. While the DDQN solves the problem of overestimation,
it is still trying to hit a moving target. The target network’s weights are periodically updated with the policy
network’s weights, meaning that the goal is moving for each update. This moving target is known to create
instability for training a DDQN [46]. The exploration strategy given by the ϵ-greedy strategy also introduces
some stochasticity. It is a well-known trade-off of exploration vs. exploitation to the agent. This balance might
introduce variability in the loss, especially in the beginning when the agent imposes new behaviors more often.
Due to the fluctuations in the training curves, it is the trends which have been commented upon.
The training set provided a modified DICE score around 0.8. However, the validation set returned a modified
DICE score of plateauing around 0.22.
This points to the model having a limited ability to generalize on
new unseen data due to the large discrepancy between the validation modified DICE score and the training
modified DICE score. This observation is supported by the findings during the testing of the Path Tracer model.
The Branch Point Detector model displayed the ability to learn the patterns of the local volumes by returning
an RMSE= 0.0081 for the test data set given predictions of probabilities in the range [0, 1] indicating that it
successfully managed to appoint branch probabilities to a centerline point gk. These results support the as-
sumption of employing an untrained 3D regression network instead of a pre-trained model. Pre-trained models
are typically trained on larger and more diverse data sets. This is a rather simple regression task and therefore
the data is only needed to operate in a smaller domain. Models that have been pre-trained are more accessible
for 2D images with a different number of channels which is a mismatch of the data utilized for this project
where the model operates on 3D CT scans. Also, pre-trained models in deep learning often have a large number
of parameters. This can lead to unnecessary additional processing resources and increased prediction time.
In light of this, the model was not chosen to be pre-trained. Instead, the model was trained from scratch on
Page 54 of 63
==PAGE:66==
task-specific data allowing the model to learn features directly relevant to the small local volumes centered
around the centerline points gk.
There are other relevant models which could be employed for this task. Deliberately, the most shallow ResNet
was selected to not overfit the data. Given the test set RMSE = 0.0081, the Branch Point Detector predicts
efficiently. The largest errors do reveal complicated structures in the environment which will be elaborated on
in section 8.5.
8.2
Reflections upon Model Improvements
The possible areas of improvement are identified based on the results of the training and testing of the models.
The agent in the Path Tracer model seems to progress proximally to the ground truth centerline in the first
part of the tracing. However, several premature terminations are observed when the agent approaches the renal
hilum where the agent’s policy seems to be inefficient. A suggestion to make the agent more robust could be to
initiate episodes at a random point along the ground truth centerline during the training instead of consistently
starting at the starting point of the renal artery. Thereby, the agent can be more explorative closer to the
endpoint during training to gain more experience around the renal hilum.
It is not only thought that the agent could benefit from being more explorative for the full course of the cen-
terline, but the model could potentially benefit from being more explorative during testing. The results from
the Branch Point Detector reveal the presence of tubular-like structures frequently in the environments around
the centerline point in the projection figures of the CT scan. This means that the agent in many states st will
encounter nearby tubular structures in its position pt. This is suspected to influence the decision-making of the
agent given the results of the Path Tracer. Thus, the agent could enter states, where the agent to its current
policy π might ’stubbornly’ choose to follow the wrong tubular structure. Hence, it is thought that the agent
can benefit from applying the ϵ-greedy strategy during testing and inference which is presented in the article
from Mnih et al. [47]. Introducing a vague level of exploration with a fixed level of ϵ during testing could allow
the agent to get out of making a less beneficial decision.
The test samples for the Path Tracer model revealed several cases where premature termination is thought
to be associated with the agent’s policy being affected by bifurcations. Even though it has been stated that
branching is not considered for the ground truth centerline it seems to affect the agent’s policy as a branching
point is an introduction for another path that the agent might find natural to track. Thus, branching cannot
go unnoticed when reviewing the results from testing the model as it nevertheless will affect the policy of the
agent. Yet, it is also important to consider for extraction of a full vascular tree including branches.
The goal is to extract the full centerline of the vasculature of the renal arteries before they enter the renal
capsule. The Branch Point Detector model showed great potential in assigning branch point probabilities to
the ground truth centerline points gk. Yet, the data set which the Branch Point Detector is trained upon does
not include environments that the agent in the Path Tracer model might encounter if the agent deviates from
the ground truth centerline. Therefore, the trained Branch Point Detector model has only demonstrated great
results for an agent following the optimal policy for all data. This could pose a problem for the implementation
of combining the two models. Thus, the Branch Point Detector model could benefit from being trained, vali-
dated, and tested on a data set that includes environmental states around the centerline as well.

8.3
Prospects of Combining a Path Tracer and a Branch Point De-
tector
There are several ways to approach a combined implementation of the two modules: the Path Tracer model
and the Branch Point Detector model. Leaving branch probabilities instead of a classification network enables a
wider prospect of implementations. It gives the opportunity to return to points assigned a high probability and
decide how to proceed to extract a full vascular network. For network extraction, we can mainly look into the
two methods Breadth-First Search (BFS) or Depth-First Search (DFS) for utilizing the additional information
the Branch Point Detector provides. In the context of the vascular tree of the renal arteries, it is suggested that
the agent could proceed by using a BFS method and progress along the centerline until reaching a bifurcation.
In the bifurcation, the agent would split into each branch and proceed. Zhang et al. [12] apply a BFS method
approach by initiating an additional agent whenever the agent(s) encounter a bifurcation. Focusing on the DFS
method, the agent will explore as far as possible along each branch before backtracking [48]. Li et al. exhibit
good results by using a DFS method. When the agent meets a bifurcation it proceeds along one branch and
closes off the others, where it will return to and detect the rest of the branch. Hence, a BFS and a DFS approach
for network extraction have proven effective.
Before moving on with a combination of the two individual modules, the Path Tracer model and the Branch
Point Detector model, which leaves plenty of considerations, it is foremost important to deal with the prospects
of improvement and limitations of the method.
8.4
Limitations
Most important, is it to touch upon the limitation in terms of the Path Tracer model’s inability to generalize for
new data which is also reflected in the results from testing the model. There is a large variability in the quality
of the predictions yielding modified DICE scores from 0.179-0.963 for a test dataset of 12 samples. Except for
the sample with the largest modified DICE score, the Path Tracer does not show the ability to reach the goal
state but terminates prematurely. In some cases it was termination in bifurcations, in a branch other than the
main branch, in an early branch, and in surrounding tissue due to deviation from the main centerline close
to the renal hilum, or termination beyond the endpoint. The agent decision-making suffers closer to the renal
hilum and when encountering bifurcations. Therefore, it is thought that the dataset does not fully capture the
variation in the data.
The total data set providing 62 scans split into a training set of 37 scans, a validation set of 13 scans, and
a test set of 12 scans is not sufficient and is thought not to capture the variation in the data. Zhang et al. [12]
succeed in training a model for the coronary arteries with only 8 CCTA scans with 4 arteries in each but as it
will be discussed more thoroughly the data of renal arteries are suspected to be more complex. Earlier studies
for the coronary arteries suggest larger data sets as well of 140 CTA scans for the model Li et al. [14] employ
and 125 samples for the model made by Su et al. [10]. Therefore, it is thought that an increase in the amount
of data could potentially increase the model’s generalization ability but also the quality of the ground truth
data should be trusted.
8.5
Data Quality
The Branch Point Detector pointed to issues regarding the ground truth annotation. The local volumes resulting
in the largest errors for the Branch Point Detector where the target probabilities were all annotated as having
zero probability returned larger predicted probabilities. When inspecting these specific samples it became ev-
ident that the samples unveil complicated states s that are difficult to define as capturing a branch point or
not. These observations collectively point to a critical need for an annotation process, that can accommodate
Page 56 of 63
==PAGE:68==
clarification and ambiguities in the data, thereby ensuring higher reliability in the ground truth. Utilizing a DL
approach requires a certain quality of the annotated ground truth data since it is a data-driven approach from
which the model needs to learn effectively.
One of the main differences in the data compared to data presented in the Literature study section 1.1 is
that the acquisition protocol of the scans targets the anatomical structures of interest [12][11][10][15]. In this
data set the protocol is optimized for maximum contrast in the myocardium which results in varying contrast
of the renal arteries. The great variation in the contrast of the images due to timing differences of the injection
of contrast fluids and imaging is complicated to master during imaging as there is variety for every patient.
Therefore, it is considered an unstable factor in terms of variational intensities and is inevitable. Furthermore,
the complicated structure of the kidney leaves a complex problem for improving the policy for the agent when
approaching the renal hilum that also welcomes other tubular- and vessel-like structures (renal vein, nerves,
and ureters).
8.6
Choice of Method
The data quality and quantity pose a challenge in determining the drawbacks of the DRL method for path
tracing of the centerlines of the renal arteries.
The DRL method is implemented to learn the image features of the CT scans to determine the path of tubular
structures more specifically, the renal arteries. Therefore, the model uses a value-based Q-learning method to
predict the q-values by a DDQN built on two separate CNNs that are thought to capture the features of the
renal arteries. Through the training, the model achieves a training modified DICE of around 0.8 demonstrating
that the model is capturing the features of the renal arteries in the training data set. Yet, its generalization
to new data is challenged, potentially due to the limited size of the dataset. It is expected that other models
such as segmentation models would require larger amounts of data as well, and therefore it is not considered
beneficial to apply these types of models for centerline extraction of the renal arteries.
If the project was to be built on a larger data set, it is available due to the CGPS and the collaboration
with the Copenhagen University Hospital Rigshospitalet. Initially, one might assume that the pipeline for ex-
tracting ground truth centerlines presents a greater challenge. It appears to be a lot of manual work, yet it is
only the renal artery segmentation which is a time-consuming manual task. The rest of the data acquisition
pipeline is handled by automated existing methods resulting in a data acquisition pipeline that requires less
effort. The process of extracting the centerline of the renal arteries and detecting the starting and endpoints
is automated once the manual segmentation of the renal arteries is completed. Hence, it is not thought that
the DRL method is more time-consuming for preparing the data for the model since it is expected that other
methods require a segmentation ground truth representation of the data as a minimum as well.
Another advantage of a DRL approach is the ability to learn from high dimensional data while keeping the
processing time lower and providing a continuous path of the renal artery centerline. Other DL methods utilize
large CNNs for full scans or regions of interest which produce a large number of parameters to be updated.
Furthermore, these models suffer from discontinuity which is not desirable for studying properties of vessels.
The data utilized for this study for the renal arteries are contrast-enhanced CT images but with a focus
on enhancing the contrast in the coronary arteries which necessarily is not equal to enhancing the contrast in
the renal arteries. Therefore, it is not obvious that the centerline of the renal arteries should be successfully
traced by employing the same model as Su et al. [10]. They utilize a PPO method in DRL which optimizes the
policy directly. This method delivers higher stability during training by clipping an objective function. For the
Path Tracer model in this project, unstable training is observed, but it is more crucial to deal with the issue
concerning the reliability of the ground truth annotation of the renal arteries. Because changing the method of

the DRL model will still require reliable data in an appropriate amount as pointed out in section 8.5.
It is not uncomplicated to acquire new data if we were to enhance the contrast of the renal arteries in the

data is available, the annotation pipeline still has to create reliable data. The goal was to achieve generalization
across the existing dataset, which surely requires a larger amount of data. Therefore, the most important task
to improve the model is to make sure that we can rely on the ground truth data and understand the complexities
of it and its anatomical structures.
8.7
Clinical Relevance
The framework for automatic centerline extraction of the renal arteries is at its current operational level not
applicable in the clinic, as it is not reaching generalization capabilities for the data, nor capturing the full
vascular tree with branching if they occur. The main goal is to provide clinicians and healthcare professionals
with a robust tool which can provide important information for kidney-related diseases to remove demanding
labor time.
The Branch Point Detector reveals that the annotation procedure of segmenting the renal arteries is important
but also a difficult task.
It is a time-consuming process highlighting the need for an automatic process of
identifying the renal arteries. Once a robust model returning a reliable predicted centerline is achieved, it is
possible to unfold 3D structures providing a different visualization of tubular structures. For the renal arteries,
the centerline extraction of the vascular tree could be used to straighten out the curved vessels. This allows for
examination of the lumen of the renal arteries, which are more easily accessible and obvious to analyze. Thereby,
stenosis or length of branches could be more easily detected improving the pipeline of diagnosing RAS, planning
stenting, or preparing for transplant surgeries [4]. Thus, successful renal artery centerline tracing could advance
the field of diagnosing kidney-related diseases by contributing to different visualization possibilities and mapping
of abnormalities within the renal arteries.

Conclusion
The thesis aimed to develop and evaluate an automatic centerline extraction of the renal arteries’ vascular tree
by a Deep Reinforcement Learning method in contrast-enhanced CT scans. To do so two sub-objectives have
been defined.
First, the goal was to develop and validate a Path Tracer model based on a Deep Reinforcement Learning
(DRL) method with Q-learning to let an artificial agent navigate from the starting point of the renal artery
to the endpoint. The project successfully developed a Path Tracer by employing a Double Deep Q-Network to
guide an artificial agent along the path of the renal arteries in CT scans. The model was trained on 62 scans
and showed promising results of the artificial agent progressing along the renal artery’s centerline. However, the
validation and test phases revealed that the model lacks generalization capabilities due to a large discrepancy
between training and validation accuracy scores. The training set presented a modified DICE score of approxi-
mately 0.8 while the validation set held a modified DICE of approximately 0.22. The poor generalization ability
provided a wide range of modified DICE scores from 0.179 to 0.963 for the test set. Nevertheless, it also brought
insights into the agent’s behavior when inspecting its progress along the 12 test samples centerline. There are
examples of the agent deviating from the path close to the renal hilum or, if the samples presented bifurcations,
the agent could in some incidences follow another branch than the main one or simply stop in the bifurcation.
Secondly, the goal was to implement and validate a Branch Point Detector model to predict branch point
probabilities for the centerline points. A modified 3D ResNet18 was employed for the regression task of predict-
ing probabilities with success. The training, validation, and testing phase returned accurate predictions with
an RMSE of 0.0081 for the test set, displaying great generalization probabilities.
One of the most important findings is that the data is complex. Therefore, the ground truth annotation is not
a straightforward task. This was revealed by inspection of the 3D volumes around the centerline points yielding
the largest prediction errors for branch point probabilities showing tubular-like structures that were difficult to
interpret.
Overall, the two modules contribute to reaching the goal of extracting the centerline of the renal arteries’
vascular tree. However, we need to access more data and improve the reliability of the ground truth data for
training and testing the modules further before we can combine them.
