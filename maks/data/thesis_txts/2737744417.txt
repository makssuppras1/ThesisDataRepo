Abstract
Wildfires pose a significant threat to ecosystems, property, and lives globally, ne-
cessitating the development of efficient and cost-effective detection technologies.
This thesis presents the design, implementation, and evaluation of a novel wild-
fire detection system employing three main components: deterministic computer
vision, machine learning (ML), and You Only Look Once (YOLO) object detec-
tion. The deterministic module is divided into three main segments: color-based
smoke filtering using RGB, HLS, and YUV color spaces; motion detection utiliz-
ing optical flow analysis and edge detection module employed to label significant
structural changes between evaluated frames. The system aims to identify the
early stages of wildfires using standard video streams captured during daylight
hours, focusing on minimizing computational and thus financial expenditures.
The core of the proposed solution is an optimized algorithm tailored to analyze
video feeds at a near real-time rate to detect signs of wildfires, such as smoke
and small flames, with minimal delay. Extensive testing was conducted to as-
sess the system’s feasibility, reliability, and resource efficiency. The evaluation
involved various videos showing wildfires and normal forested areas to measure
the detection accuracy and computational load under different conditions. The
results demonstrate that the system can sufficiently reliably detect early signs
of wildfires with a reasonably high degree of accuracy while maintaining low
computational requirements. This thesis contributes to wildfire prevention by
demonstrating that advanced detection technologies can be both lightweight
and effective. The findings support the potential for broader adoption of simi-
lar technologies in real-world applications, demonstrating a feasible possibility
to implement large-scale wildfire detection tools tailored for specific use cases
provided sufficient data.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Problem Outline
Wildfires pose a significant threat to ecosystems, property, and lives globally.
The increasing frequency and severity of these events, exacerbated by climate
change, emphasizes the critical need for effective early detection and prevention
strategies. Traditional wildfire detection technologies, including lookout tow-
ers and satellite imaging, often suffer from delays and have limited coverage,
highlighting the need for more modern, real-time detection systems.
The primary challenge addressed in this thesis is the early detection of wild-
fires using real-time video analysis. Early detection is crucial to preventing the
spread of wildfires and reducing their impact. Current technologies either lack
the required precision or are cost-prohibitive for widespread implementation.
Therefore, the goal is to develop a cost-effective, efficient, and reliable system
capable of detecting the initial signs of wildfires, such as smoke and small flames,
using standard video streams.
The limitations of current systems are versatile. Lookout towers require constant
human monitoring and can only cover limited areas. While offering broader
coverage, satellite imaging often fails to provide real-time updates and can be
obstructed by cloud cover or smoke. These shortcomings can delay detection,
allowing wildfires to spread uncontrollably. Furthermore, advanced detection
technologies like infrared sensors are often too expensive for widespread deploy-
ment, particularly in resource-limited areas. Hence, there is a pressing need for
an affordable, scalable solution that uses widely available technology.
==PAGE:12==
2
Introduction
This thesis focuses on real-time video analysis to bridge the gap between ac-
curacy, cost, and accessibility. Real-time video feeds from standard cameras
are increasingly ubiquitous, providing a viable data source for continuous mon-
itoring. Integrating machine learning and computer vision techniques into this
framework can significantly enhance detection capabilities, enabling quicker and
more reliable identification of early wildfire signs.
1.2
Thesis Content
This thesis presents the design, implementation, and evaluation of a novel wild-
fire detection system that leverages computer vision, machine learning (ML),
and the You Only Look Once (YOLO) object detection framework. The sys-
tem aims to identify the early stages of wildfires by detecting initial signs, such
as smoke and small flames, using standard video streams captured during day-
time hours. The proposed solution incorporates multiple advanced techniques,
including color-based, motion-based, and edge-based detection methods, which
are integrated to enhance the accuracy and reliability of the detection process.
The core of this work is a lightweight algorithm that processes video feeds to
detect potential wildfire indicators. Our approach includes developing a deter-
ministic component for initial detection, training an MLP classifier for refined
analysis, and employing the YOLO model for robust object detection.
Ad-
ditionally, we implemented a scoring system to combine the outputs of these
components to produce a comprehensive fire likelihood score. The project is
structured as a proof-of-concept, focusing on exploring and validating different
detection methodologies rather than creating a production-ready application.
The work includes extensive testing and evaluation using diverse datasets to
assess the system’s performance and reliability. By combining these techniques,
we aim to contribute to developing more effective wildfire prevention strategies,
demonstrating the potential for broader adoption and practical implementation
in various settings.

2.1
Wildfires
Wildfires, often called forest fires, are uncontrolled fires that spread rapidly
through vegetation and wildlands. They can ignite and propagate due to dry
conditions, high temperatures, wind, and fuel availability, including trees, brush,
and organic debris. While wildfires are a natural component of many ecosys-
tems, playing an important role in clearing dead material and enabling certain
species to regenerate, they can become highly destructive, particularly when
intensified by human activities or extreme weather conditions [1].
The evolution of a wildfire is significantly influenced by weather, terrain, and the
type of vegetation present. Wildfires tend to move faster uphill than downhill
because the heat rises, drying the vegetation above and making it more suscep-
tible to combustion. Hot weather conditions can also dry vegetation, making
==PAGE:16==
6
Background and Related Work
it highly flammable, whereas wind can carry embers long distances, igniting
new fires far from the source. This can lead to rapid and unpredictable wildfire
spread, especially in regions with dense vegetation.
Wildfires are frequent natural disasters in regions such as Siberia, California,
British Columbia, and Australia, and in areas with Mediterranean climates or
taiga biomes [2]. Globally, human activities have increased wildfire impacts,
doubling the land area burned compared to natural levels.
This increase is
driven by climate change, including more intense heat waves and droughts,
land-use changes, and wildfire suppression efforts [3].
2.1.1
Wildfire Implications: Risks and Dangers
Wildfires pose significant risks and dangers to both human life and the envi-
ronment. One of the most immediate threats is to human life, as wildfires can
spread rapidly, often outpacing evacuation efforts. The intense heat, smoke,
and flames can cause severe injuries or fatalities to individuals caught in their
path. In addition to the direct threat to life, wildfires can destroy homes, in-
frastructure, and entire communities, leading to substantial economic losses and
long-term displacement of residents [4].
The smoke generated by wildfires also poses severe health risks for humans, es-
pecially for vulnerable groups such as children, the elderly, and individuals with
respiratory conditions. Inhaling smoke can cause a range of health problems,
from minor irritations to severe respiratory issues and cardiovascular complica-
tions. The extensive distribution of smoke can affect air quality far beyond the
immediate vicinity of the fire, impacting large populations and raising significant
public health concerns [5].
Environmentally, wildfires can have devastating effects on ecosystems. They
can lead to biodiversity loss as plants and animals are killed or displaced. The
destruction of vegetation can result in soil erosion, leading to sedimentation
in waterways and affecting water quality and aquatic life. Moreover, wildfires
release large amounts of carbon dioxide and other greenhouse gases into the
atmosphere, contributing to climate change [6]. The loss of forests, which act as
carbon sinks, further intensifies this problem by reducing the Earth’s capacity
to absorb carbon dioxide.
Economically, the costs associated with wildfires are substantial. These include
firefighting efforts, evacuation, emergency response, and long-term rebuilding
and recovery expenses [7]. The loss of timber and other natural resources can

2.1.2
Wildfire Prevention Efforts
Efforts to prevent wildfires and mitigate their impact are multifaceted and
involve a combination of strategies. Fire management practices such as con-
trolled burns or prescribed fires reduce the fuel available for potential wildfires.
These controlled burns are carefully planned and executed to minimize risk
while achieving ecological benefits, such as promoting the growth of fire-adapted
species and reducing the likelihood of larger, uncontrolled wildfires [8].
Another key aspect of wildfire prevention is creating and maintaining defen-
sible spaces around properties, particularly in areas prone to wildfires. This
involves removing flammable vegetation, creating firebreaks, and using fire-
resistant building materials [9].
Public education campaigns are paramount
in raising awareness about the importance of these measures and encouraging
individuals to take proactive steps to protect their homes and communities.
Technological advancements are also playing a significant role in wildfire preven-
tion and detection. Satellite imagery [10, 11], remote sensing [12], and drones are
increasingly used to monitor large areas for signs of wildfires. These technologies
can provide early warning and real-time information to help guide firefighting
efforts and evacuations.
Additionally, computer models and simulations are
used to predict fire behaviour and assess the potential impact of wildfires under
different scenarios, aiding in planning and resource allocation [13].
Policy and regulatory measures are essential in supporting wildfire prevention
efforts.
This includes implementing and enforcing land-use policies that re-
duce fire risk, such as zoning regulations that limit development in high-risk
areas. Governments also invest in firefighting resources and infrastructure, such
as training programs, equipment, and communication systems, to effectively
enhance the capacity to respond to wildfires [14].

2.2.1
Cameras
In this subsection, we explore several types of cameras used in wildfire detec-
tion, including thermal cameras, night vision cameras, and standard visible light
cameras. Cameras are among the most commonly used hardware solutions for
detecting wildfires due to their ability to monitor large areas and provide real-
time data. Each type of camera offers unique advantages in detecting fires under
different conditions, enhancing the overall effectiveness of wildfire detection sys-
tems.
Visible Light Cameras
Visible light cameras capture images using the visible spectrum of light, ranging
from 400 to 700 nanometers, similar to how the human eye perceives the world.
They focus light through a lens onto a sensor, such as a charge-coupled device
(CCD) or a complementary metal-oxide-semiconductor (CMOS), which converts
the light into an electrical signal to create a digital image [16]. These cameras
are widely used in various applications, including photography, video recording,
security surveillance, and scientific research. In the context of wildfire detection,
visible light cameras can monitor large areas and detect smoke and flames during
daylight conditions, providing valuable real-time data for early intervention and
fire management.
Thermal Cameras
A thermal camera is a non-contact device that detects infrared energy (heat)
and converts it into a visual image. Unlike standard cameras that capture im-
ages using visible light, thermal cameras create images based on heat emitted by
objects [17]. This infrared energy is a part of the electromagnetic spectrum, dis-
tinct from visible light. Thermal cameras consist of a lens that focuses infrared
energy onto a thermal sensor, processing electronics, and mechanical housing.
The thermal sensor, which can have various pixel configurations (such as 80 ×
60 to 1280 × 1024 pixels), detects infrared energy and translates it into images.
These images are produced through digital or analogue video outputs. Due to
==PAGE:19==

9
the larger wavelengths of thermal energy compared to visible light, thermal cam-
eras typically have lower resolutions than visible light cameras, as each sensor
element must be larger to detect the infrared energy effectively [18].
Ther-
mal cameras have numerous applications, including night vision, surveillance,
firefighting, and building inspection, particularly in environments where visual
observation is difficult. They can also be practical for wildfire detection, as they
can easily identify the source of starting fires with much larger temperatures
than the surroundings.
Nightvision Cameras
A night vision camera is a device designed to capture images in low-light or
no-light conditions. It uses infrared (IR) technology, where infrared LEDs illu-
minate the camera’s field of view with light invisible to the human eye. The
light reflects off objects and is detected by the camera’s image sensor, typically
a charge-coupled device (CCD) or a complementary metal-oxide-semiconductor
(CMOS). These sensors are sensitive to IR light and convert it into electrical
signals to produce video images. The resulting footage is usually monochrome,
appearing in shades of green or black and white, as the camera relies on the
intensity of the infrared illumination rather than visible colors [19]. This tech-
nology enables the camera to produce clear images even in complete darkness,
making it an essential tool for surveillance and monitoring in various environ-
ments, including wildlife observation, security or military applications [20].
2.2.2
Existing Products
Several solutions are already available for wildfire detection, leveraging advanced
technologies to monitor and identify fire risks. This section will describe some
existing products and systems, highlighting their capabilities and effectiveness.
These solutions provide valuable insights into the current state of wildfire de-
tection technology and its use in practice.
IQ FireWatch
IQ FireWatch is an automated system designed for real-time detection of smoke
and hot spots, enabling prompt wildfire response [21].
It was developed by
IQ Technologies for Earth and Space GmbH. Utilizing a multispectral sensor,
the system synchronizes data processing across various regions and conditions,
ensuring accurate calibration for different environments and weather scenarios.
Key components of the IQ FireWatch system include:

• Monochrome sensor for daytime surveillance
• Near-infrared sensor for nighttime detection
• RGB sensor for additional spectral information
• Thermal infrared sensor (optional) for detecting hot spots
The system’s multispectral sensor unit, shown in Figure 2.1, capable of rotating
360 degrees every 4-6 minutes, captures and processes images from preset posi-
tions in real-time. Upon detecting smoke, an alert is sent to a wildfire control
center for verification, followed by notification to relevant fire control centers.
This hardware and software integration allows IQ FireWatch to cover a surveil-
lance radius of up to 20 km under most conditions, extending to 50 km or more
in optimal scenarios [21].

Issues and Limitations
Current wildfire detection technologies and products face several significant is-
sues and limitations. Considering the systems described in Section 2.2.2, one
of the primary challenges is the high cost associated with these products. Most
solutions, except Exci, require users to install specialized camera-sensor units
with advanced technologies like infrared, thermal or air quality sensors. These
hardware components can be very expensive, and the user cannot reduce the
cost by cutting the number of units as they need to cover the entire monitoring
area for the system to work correctly. Additionally, the software tools needed for
monitoring video feeds, providing alert capabilities, and other functionalities are
usually sophisticated and thus generate substantial costs on their own. The fi-
nancial barrier limits the widespread adoption of these technologies, particularly
in regions with limited resources.
Another major issue is the complexity of the technology and algorithms re-
quired for accurate wildfire detection. While these systems are highly effective,
they often involve intricate setups, like the adaptation of space technology for
==PAGE:24==
14
Background and Related Work
terrestrial use, reliance on satellite data, and the implementation of extensive
machine learning models that require vast training data to achieve sufficient ac-
curacy. Integrating multiple sensors, such as thermal, infrared, and air quality
sensors, into a single monitoring unit also adds to the complexity. Although
rapid advancements in computational tools and capabilities of modern devices
may mitigate this issue in the future, such sophisticated systems are currently
not entirely feasible in many areas. There are also regions less prone to wildfires,

Despite their accuracy and advanced features, the deployment of wildfire detec-

Detecting wildfires in real-time is pivotal for minimizing damage to ecosystems,

2.4.1
Overview of Existing Research
Research in wildfire detection has notably increased in recent years, particularly
in developing smoke detection systems using video technology. These systems
often employ cameras sensitive to visible and infrared (IR) spectra. Numerous
video-based smoke detection algorithms have been created and tested in both
experimental and real-world settings, and camera networks are being expanded
to enhance the timely detection of wildfire smoke. The research generally focuses
on detecting flames and smoke separately, leveraging their static and dynamic
characteristics through handcrafted feature approaches. There has also been
interest in fusing visible and thermal images to create multimodal detection
systems, which aim to improve accuracy [32]. Despite advancements, existing
systems still have unresolved weaknesses. To address these, the latest trend in-
volves hybrid approaches that combine handcrafted features with deep learning
techniques, aiming to mitigate the limitations of current methods and enhance
the effectiveness of wildfire detection systems. This growing but still limited
field indicates substantial room for further exploration and innovation.
==PAGE:27==
2.4 Related Work
17
One of the older fire and smoke detection methods involves combining classical
image analysis techniques with classifiers for decision-making. The first exam-
ple is a four-stage smoke detection algorithm suggested by T. Tung and J. Kim
[33]. Their solution involves segmenting moving regions in video frames using
an approximate median method and clustering candidate smoke regions with a
fuzzy c-means method. Parameters such as motion vector, surface roughness,
and area randomness are extracted from these regions and used to train an SVM
classifier, which then determines the presence of smoke for the smoke alarm sys-
tem. Similarly, the algorithm introduced by B. Ko, J. Kwak and J. Nam [34]
uses temporospatial characteristics of smoke such as color, wavelet coefficients
or motion orientation, and two random forest (RF) classifiers trained using inde-
pendent temporal and spatial feature vectors. The final decision is based on the
average probability of two RFs, which improves detection accuracy and reduces
false positives caused by similar objects like clouds and fog. Additionally, quite
recently, Liu et al. [35] proposed a lightweight platform for early smoke detec-
tion based on a cascade classifier characterized by Local Binary Pattern (LBP)
features and trained using the AdaBoost algorithm in OpenCV. This method
aims to ensure both detection accuracy and real-time performance of the smoke
detection system
Another quite popular technique for wildfire detection is motion detection, which
uses optical flow analysis combined with additional methods like Convolutional
Neural Network (CNN). The first example would be an algorithm suggested
by Yu et al.
[36] utilizing foreground image accumulation and optical flow
techniques to differentiate between flame and smoke regions. By analyzing the
motion features of smoke and utilizing color models, it can detect fires with
smoke, fires with only smoke, and fires with both flame and smoke in real-
time video sequences. A more deep-learning approach introduced by Luo et
al. [37] combines motion characteristics and CNNs to identify smoke in visual
scenes accurately. The algorithm first extracts suspected smoke regions based
on motion features and then utilizes CNN to automatically extract and analyze
features within these regions, achieving accuracy rates of up to 99%. A com-
parable smoke detection method, combining a traditional Kalman filtering for
motion detection with a lightweight CNN for region classification, was proposed
by Gagliardi et al. [38]. The algorithm automatically selects specific regions
of interest within the image, generates bounding boxes for grey-colored moving
objects, and verifies the presence of smoke in these regions using the CNN, en-
abling real-time smoke detection in video sequences. Moreover, Wu et al. [39]
also utilize a combination of dense optical flow and CNNs. Their algorithm
operates in two stages: first, it pre-positions potential smoke areas based on
dynamic characteristics to reduce computational complexity; second, it uses a
ResNet-based classifier that combines the optical flow of the blue channel with
RGB color channels to improve detection accuracy and reduce false positives.

In academic research, CNNs have also been employed individually to detect
smoke and flames in forest environments. Namozov et al. [40] introduced a

high detection accuracy makes real-time detection in complex environments ef-
fective.

Morphological Operations
Morphological transformations are basic operations that manipulate the shape
of objects in pictures, typically performed on binary images. These transfor-
mations require two inputs: the original image and a structuring element (or
kernel), which determines the specifics of the operation. Instead of convolu-
tions, the kernel is used for simple tests on the pixels - checking the values of
neighbouring pixels and adjusting the value of the currently considered pixel
if needed. Similarly to image kernels, structuring elements can have various
neighbourhood sizes.

3.2.2
Color-Based Smoke Detection
According to previous studies [45], smoke exhibits a specific greyish or light blue
color, and the sudden appearance of smoke typically decreases the chrominance
channel values of image pixels. Using these observations, we developed this part
of the algorithm with two main components: color value rules [49] and detection
of decreases in the U and Y channels in the YUV color space [50, 51].
Color value rules filter the image early to detect pixels that might be considered
smoke pixels. We use two color spaces: RGB and HLS. The second method
works on a pair of images and utilizes the characteristics of YUV color space.
RGB Color Space
The RGB color space represents images using Red, Green, and Blue color chan-
nels as shown in Figure 3.6. Each pixel is a combination of these three values.
Smoke detection using the RGB color space can be effective because smoke
typically has a lower intensity in the red and green channels than in the blue
channel, giving it a characteristic greyish or bluish tint. By applying thresholds
to these values, we can filter out pixels that do not match the expected color
profile of smoke.

HLS Color Space
The HLS color space (also called HSL) stands for Hue, Lightness, and Satura-
tion. Unlike RGB, which represents color as a combination of primary colors,
HLS separates color intensity (lightness) from color information (hue and sat-
uration), which is presented in Figure 3.7.
Smoke pixels typically have low
saturation and high lightness, making them distinguishable in this color space.
By filtering pixels with low saturation and high lightness, we can effectively
identify potential smoke pixels.

YUV Color Space
The YUV color space separates an image into three components: Y (luminance),
U (chrominance blue), and V (chrominance red), shown in Figure 3.8. The Y
component represents the brightness or intensity of the image, capturing the
grayscale information. The U and V components represent the color informa-
tion, where U encodes the difference between the blue component and a reference
value, and V encodes the difference between the red component and a reference
value, thus allowing for efficient color manipulation and compression. For smoke
detection, we use the Y and U channels. We calculate the differences in the Y
and U channels by comparing pairs of images. A significant decrease in these
values indicates the presence of smoke. We apply a threshold to detect smoke,
determined by these differences’ mean and standard deviation.

3.2.3
Motion-Based Smoke Detection
Our wildfire detection algorithm’s motion-based smoke detection component
leverages optical flow techniques to identify significant and consistent move-
ments in video frames, potentially indicating the presence of smoke or fire. This
approach analyses changes between two frames captured one second apart, fil-
tering out minor movements to focus on major changes in the landscape. The
main objective is to detect areas with potential smoke or fire while minimizing
the impact of irrelevant motion, such as moving clouds or flowing rivers.
Theory of Optical Flow
Optical flow is a method used to estimate objects’ motion between two video
frames. It provides a dense vector field where each vector represents the displace-
ment of a pixel from the first frame to the second. The cv2.calcOpticalFlowFarneback
function in OpenCV implements the Farneb¨ack method for calculating dense
optical flow [55].
The Farneb¨ack algorithm approximates each neighbourhood of both frames by
quadratic polynomials. Observing how an exact polynomial transforms under
translation estimates displacement fields from the polynomial expansion coeffi-
cients. This involves the following steps:
1) Polynomial expansion: Each pixel neighbourhood is approximated by a
quadratic polynomial

the angle represents the direction of the motion. Converting from Cartesian
to polar coordinates allows us to more easily identify and threshold significant
motions based on their magnitude, regardless of direction, and to visualize the
motion directions more intuitively.
3.2.4
Edge-Based Smoke Detection
In the deterministic part of our wildfire detection algorithm, one of our original
methods is edge-based smoke detection. Initially, edge detection was intended
to delineate the border between the forest and the sky, to identify the sky
area on the image and mark it as a region of decreased sensitivity for motion
detection. This was our initial idea of leveraging the problem of clouds - they
are, in principle, very similar in shape and color to smoke, and moving clouds
produced a lot of false positives in our motion-based smoke detection. However,
after some testing, edge detection revealed high sensitivity to various contours
and edges like trees and cloud outlines, making it unsuitable to find a border
between the forest and the sky.
Nevertheless, after analyzing the masks resulting from edge detection applied
on images of wildfires, we discovered that the difference between the masks from
frames one second apart provided a reliable estimate of smoke regions. Hence,
we tried applying exclusive disjunction (XOR operation) on the edge-detection
masks to eliminate the common static area of the images - usually the forest part
and sky. The results looked very promising after some morphological processing
to remove noise and highlight the regions of interest. That is why we decided
to use this method as another part of our deterministic algorithm and not just
as a pre-processing step. This method also relies mainly on the detected change
between the two compared frames, however, thanks to extensive processing it is
more resistant to noise and small irrelevant changes in the scenery.

Moreover, the size of the kernel in the Sobel operator significantly affects the
results of edge detection.
Larger Sobel kernels tend to be more sensitive to
noise, averaging over a larger area and potentially resulting in blurrier images
that may not detect fine details well. They also require more computational

thicker edges. In contrast, smaller Sobel kernels are more sensitive to small-scale

computationally efficient. However, they may be more susceptible to noise and
can produce more false edges, resulting in thinner detected edges.

characteristics and the application requirements. It involves a trade-off between
edge detection accuracy, sensitivity to noise, computational complexity, and the
desired thickness of the detected edges. After testing and careful consideration,
we decided to use 5x5 kernels for the edge detection task, as we needed the
detection to be more robust against noise while maintaining sufficiently low
computational complexity to process the video frames in real time. The accuracy
for finer details was of smaller priority.

BLOB Analysis
Blob analysis is a fundamental machine vision technique used to analyze con-
sistent image regions, making it ideal for applications where objects are distin-
guishable from the background. This method is highly flexible and performs well
across various visual inspection tasks. However, it requires a clear background-
foreground distinction and is limited to pixel-level precision [57].
Our algorithm utilises blob analysis to process objects that remain in the binary
mask created after applying morphology operations on edge detection results.
Since these objects are our potential smoke candidates, we sort them into clusters
based on their distances from each other, using their centroid property. The
clusters are then relabeled so each cluster represents a single object, and the
five largest objects are selected. If all five blobs exceed a specified threshold
(determined through manual testing to be approximately 0.01 of the processed
frame’s area), they are classified as a smoke region, indicating fire detection. If
not all blobs meet this size requirement, the algorithm returns an empty mask,
indicating that no fire or smoke was detected.

3.3.1
Feature Descriptors
This section explores the theory behind the feature descriptors used for training
our MLP classifier. A feature descriptor is a technique that extracts character-
istic details from interest points or an entire image. These descriptors provide
a numerical “fingerprint” that helps differentiate one feature from another by
encoding significant information into a series of numbers [58]. Mathematically, a
feature descriptor is represented as a vector with one or more dimensions known
as a feature vector. This vector captures the descriptor’s attributes within a
multidimensional feature space, allowing for precise identification and analysis
of various features within the data [58].
Since the general purpose of this work was testing the feasibility of various
methods for wildfire and smoke detection, we opted for simple and most com-
mon feature descriptors that can be extracted using the OpenCV library. The
variation and number of the descriptors can influence the final accuracy of the
classifier; however, this was not the main focus of this work. We aimed for a
sufficiently reliable representation of the general classifier approach, and hence,
we have chosen only four well-known features to work with. We also could not
use too many feature descriptors for the task, as some of them can be computa-
tionally expensive, and we needed to optimize the algorithm for approximately
real-time use. Moreover, we wanted the implementation to be straightforward
and not too complicated, but there is room for extension and improvement in
future work.
Hu Moments
Hu Moments are image descriptors used in computer vision to characterize the
shape of an object in an image. Proposed by Ming-Kuei Hu in 1962 [59], these
moments capture essential properties like the area, centroid, and object orienta-
tion. They are implemented in OpenCV through the cv2.HuMoments function.
Hu Moments produces a feature vector of seven values that quantify the shape,
which can then be compared to other shapes for similarity. However, they are
sensitive to the accuracy of the centroid calculation and may not perform well
in noisy or occluded environments [60].
Histogram
An image histogram is a graphical representation that shows the distribution of
pixel intensities in an image. It is composed of bins, each representing a specific
range of intensity values, and the value of each bin indicates the number of
pixels with intensities in that range. Histograms are computed by examining all
pixels and assigning them to the appropriate bins. They are valuable tools for
==PAGE:47==
3.3 Machine Learning Approach
37
inspecting images, allowing one to quickly identify background and grey value
ranges and detect issues like clipping and quantization noise. The number of
bins is typically proportional to the square root of the total number of pixels in
the image [61].
Local Binary Patterns (LBP)
The Local Binary Pattern (LBP) is a popular texture descriptor in computer
vision, known for its simplicity and effectiveness in tasks such as object recog-
nition, face detection, and texture classification. Introduced in 1994 [62], LBP
is based on appearance features and is invariant to illumination changes. It
compares the intensity of a central pixel with its surrounding pixels in a small
neighbourhood, assigning binary values based on whether the surrounding in-
tensities are greater or less than the central pixel.
These binary values are
concatenated into a binary number representing the neighbourhood’s texture
and can be used to construct a histogram of texture distribution within an
image [63].
Histogram of Oriented Gradients
The histogram of oriented gradients (HOG) method is a feature descriptor tech-
nique used in computer vision for object detection, focusing on the shape of an
object by counting the occurrences of gradient orientations in local regions. It
generates a histogram using the magnitude and orientation of gradients, making
it superior to simple edge descriptors. Like scale-invariant feature transform de-
scriptors (SIFT), HOG captures the structural features of an object, providing
detailed shape information. This method is particularly effective for accurately
detecting and recognizing objects in images [64].

learn intricate features from the data.
The Multilayer Perceptron (MLP) is designed to overcome the limitations of
single-layer perceptrons by allowing for non-linear mappings between inputs and
outputs. An MLP consists of an input layer, one or more hidden layers with
multiple neurons, and an output layer, as shown in Figure 3.12. Each neuron
in these layers can use various activation functions, such as ReLU or sigmoid.
MLPs fall under feedforward algorithms, where inputs are combined with initial

Cross-Validation
To evaluate the multiple model settings, we used a method also available in
the scikit-learn library called cross-validation. Cross-validation is a statistical
method used to estimate the performance of machine learning models. It in-
volves partitioning the dataset into a set number of folds (in our case, 5), training
the model on a subset of the data, and validating it on the remaining fold. This
process is repeated until every fold has been used as a validation set exactly
once. The results from each fold are then averaged to produce a single perfor-
mance metric. This method provides a robust estimate of model performance,
ensuring that the results are not dependent on a particular data split.
Hyperparameter Tuning
Initially, we considered using GridSearchCV for hyperparameter tuning. It per-
forms an exhaustive search over a specified parameter grid, evaluating every
possible combination of hyperparameters. While this method guarantees find-
ing the optimal set of parameters, it is very expensive computationally and was
infeasible due to the extensive time required. Testing approximately 2500 con-
figurations with 5-fold cross-validation would result in training around 12,500
models. Even with a high-performance CPU, this process would take at least 5
days, making it impractical for our needs. Furthermore, GridSearchCV does not
support GPU acceleration, limiting its usability for large-scale testing.
Hence, to identify the best hyperparameter configurations, we used another
method for hyperparameter optimization, RandomizedSearchCV. It performs a
randomized search over hyperparameters, sampling from specified distributions
rather than exhaustively searching all possible values. By specifying n iter, the

the prohibitive cost of an exhaustive search. The best-performing configuration
from this randomized search was then used to train the final MLP classifier.

Fire and Smoke Decision Methodology
To accurately determine the presence of fire or smoke in the processed video,
a dedicated algorithm segment is required to process the detection results from
each component for each frame. Firstly, the results from the deterministic algo-
rithm need to be converted into a binary decision. Additionally, the outcomes
of all three detection algorithm components must be integrated into the final
decision, taking into account their distinctive strengths and weaknesses and how
they interact with each other.
4.5.1

into binary decisions.

The following method transforms the binary masks from the deterministic al-
gorithm into a decision about whether there is or isn’t smoke in the processed
frame. Firstly, the fire likelihood score is initialized to zero. The initial frame
is divided into a grid of a specified size (e.g., 10 pixels). The density of true
values from the masks is calculated in each grid cell. The fire likelihood score is
then increased based on the density of true values in the masks and the weights
assigned to various detection combinations.
The algorithm proceeds as follows:
1) Initialization: The fire likelihood score is set to zero. The height and
width of the frame are divided by the grid size to determine the grid cell
dimensions.
2) Weight Assignment: Different weights are assigned to different com-
binations of detections from the color, motion, and edge masks. These
==PAGE:89==
4.5 Fire and Smoke Decision Methodology
79
weights reflect the importance of each combination in indicating the pres-
ence of smoke. For example:
• All three masks detecting smoke simultaneously have the highest
weight.
• The presence of smoke in both color and motion masks has a high
weight.
• The presence of smoke in either color and edge masks or motion and
edge masks also have significant weights.
• Detection by only one mask (color, motion, or edge) has a lower
weight.
3) Grid Processing:
• The frame is divided into grid cells based on the specified grid size.
• For each grid cell, the algorithm checks for the presence of smoke in
the color, motion, and edge masks by determining if any pixel in the
cell is marked as true (non-zero value).
• The density of true values in the cell is calculated by averaging the
number of true pixels across the three masks.
4) Score Calculation:
• A presence tuple is created for each cell, indicating the presence of
smoke in the color, motion, and edge masks.
• The weight corresponding to the presence tuple is retrieved from the
weight dictionary.
• The segment score is computed by multiplying the detection weight
and segment density by the weight multiplier. This score is added to
the fire likelihood score.
• The total possible score is also incremented by the maximum possible
score for the cell configuration.
5) Normalization: The fire likelihood score is divided by the total possible
score to obtain a normalized score between 0 and 1.
6) Threshold Comparison: The normalized score is compared against a
predefined threshold to determine if the frame likely contains smoke - it
is detected if the normalized score exceeds the threshold.
All described weights, parameters, and threshold values were obtained after
manual testing on our specific set of videos and generalized to work best in as
many different scenarios as possible. These values can be further fine-tuned

is initialized to zero. The input video is then read by taking two frames simul-
taneously, separated by a fixed time interval (e.g., one second). For each pair of
processed frames, the detection algorithm is executed. Based on the binary out-
come of the detection, the general score is either incremented or decremented by
a specific weight assigned to each component (the score cannot go below zero).
These weights may differ for increment and decrement values.
After adjusting the likelihood score, it is compared against a predefined thresh-
old. If the score exceeds the threshold, the function outputs that a fire was

detected and terminates. If the score remains below the threshold, the pointer
in the video is advanced to the next frame, and another pair of frames are pro-
cessed. This process continues until all frames in the video have been processed,
at which point the function outputs that no fire was detected.
Figure 4.19: Algorithm flow over video timeline.
Figure 4.19 illustrates the algorithm’s flow. For a video captured at 30 frames

the weights specified in the configuration.

4.5.3
Deterministic Guard
After multiple tests, we devised an additional idea named deterministic guard
to enhance the system’s reliability. This component ensures that the system
only detects smoke if the deterministic component has detected at least one
instance of smoke in the processed video. This approach reduces false positives,
particularly in videos without smoke.
The deterministic guard modifies the algorithm described in the previous section
by adding a condition that requires the deterministic component to have at least
one positive detection before the system can declare the presence of smoke. This
additional check provides an extra validation layer, helping improve the system’s
overall accuracy.
Figure 4.20 illustrates the performance of the deterministic component in clas-
sifying frames as containing smoke or not for both videos with smoke and those
without. The graphs show the percentage of frames in each video that the de-
terministic component classified as containing smoke, with indexes of videos for
which the result was 0% colored in red. With the guard present, those videos
would not be classified as having smoke even if the other two components in
the system (Classifier and YOLO) outweigh the total likelihood score over the
threshold.
The graphs in Figure 4.20 demonstrate that the deterministic component effec-
tively detects smoke in videos where smoke is present while rarely producing
false positives in videos without smoke. This validates the inclusion of the de-
terministic guard as a valuable addition to the overall likelihood scoring system.
Section 5.5 presents two sets of results: one with the guard active and one with-

Evaluation
The primary distinction between our evaluation methodology and those pre-
sented in the literature is that we assess videos entirely rather than treating
individual frames as standalone images. Most works discussed utilized frames
extracted from videos, resulting in many testing images being nearly identi-
cal. In such cases, a single correct (or incorrect) classification can be repeated
multiple times for similar frames, potentially skewing the final results to either
side.
This possibility and other factors contributed to our decision to treat
each video as a single sample. Still, our approach makes it more challenging to
achieve high accuracy, as each misclassification significantly impacts the overall
score, especially with our limited sample size of 60 videos.
Conversely, other studies evaluate their algorithms on large image datasets,
which is preferable, however, can also produce different results depending on
the number and variety of testing images. For instance, Namozov et al. [40]
used 2440 images with additional augmentations, obtaining AR of 97.15%, while
Luo et al. [37] employed two large datasets containing 3428 and 5834 images and
achieved 99.06% accuracy. Other studies, including [38], [39], [42] and [45], test
their algorithms on several dozen videos but evaluate them by extracting single
frames and treating them as separate images. For example, [45] derives 3600
images from 48 test videos, and [39] generates 19,004 images from 15 videos. [38]
used 2 datasets: Firesense [82] and AdViSED [83] with 42 videos. The results
presented in the table for [38] are the better ones the algorithm achieved on the
Firesense dataset. The results for the AdViSED dataset were a bit worse, with
DR at 90.49%, FAR at 27.26% and AR at 84.38%. Lastly, studies such as [33],
[34] or [35] evaluate up to 10 videos, analyze each video separately by frames,
and then average the results. Because of this, the rates for [35] presented in
Table 5.4, are averages we calculated from the individual video scores reported
in the paper.
Overall, the best scores achieved by our algorithm are comparable and fairly
close to other solutions, especially considering the relatively small number of
samples we have used for testing. Even with this limitation we managed to
obtain better accuracy rates than [35], [38] and [45] and up to only 6% lower
score than the best-performing methods like [37] or [39]. Moreover, our false
alarm rate was significantly lower than most of the solutions, losing only to [37],
[39] and [40], which proves our method has a potential for further exploration
and real-world application.

Discussion
Our wildfire detection algorithm results are promising, demonstrating good over-
all system performance. Without the Deterministic Guard, the system achieved

the final accuracy of 86.67%, which improved to 93.33% with the guard en-

on. Moreover, by adjusting appropriate parameters, the FPR and FNR values
could be swapped, as, in the case of wildfires, a false alarm is preferable to not
detecting signs of fire at all.
Key Findings
The system demonstrated significant potential in addressing the problem of
early wildfire detection. Through rigorous testing and evaluation, several key
findings were observed:
• System accuracy: The final version of the system achieved an overall accu-
racy of 86.67%. Integrating the deterministic component, edge detection,
retrained classifier and YOLO models significantly enhanced the system’s
performance.
• Integrated component performance: The combined approach of using mul-
tiple detection methodologies (color-based, motion-based, and edge-based)
along with the classifier and YOLO model provided a robust framework
for accurate detection.
The system’s overall performance was notably
better than any single component, demonstrating the effectiveness of the
integrated approach in improving detection accuracy.
• Parametrization and customization: Thanks to the likelihood scoring sys-
tem, our solution leaves room for customization of the algorithm be-
haviour. By adjusting the appropriate weights of each component, thresh-
old values and components’ parameters we can manipulate the algorithm’s
detection sensitivity to suit a specific use case. For example, by lowering
threshold values for deterministic and YOLO components we can make
the system more sensitive to detecting signs of fire if the system were to
be deployed in an area with high risk of wildfires.

Chapter 6
Conclusions
Wildfires continue to pose a significant threat globally, causing extensive damage
to ecosystems, property, and human life. The increasing frequency and intensity
of these events, driven by climate change, underscore the urgent need for effective
early detection and prevention strategies. This thesis tackled the challenge of
using real-time video analysis to develop a cost-effective, efficient, and reliable
system that detects early signs of wildfires, such as smoke and small flames.
6.1
Summary of the Work
The work presented in this thesis encompasses the design, implementation, and
evaluation of an advanced wildfire detection system. The system leverages com-
puter vision techniques, machine learning models, and the YOLO object de-
tection framework to identify smoke and fire in video streams captured dur-
ing daytime hours. The core of this solution is a lightweight algorithm that
processes video feeds to detect potential wildfire indicators.
The system in-
tegrates three primary components: a deterministic component that employs
color-based, motion-based, and edge-based detection methods; a classifier com-
ponent that uses machine learning techniques to refine detection accuracy; and
the YOLO model for robust object detection. This project serves as a proof-of-
concept, exploring and validating different detection methodologies rather than
creating a production-ready application.
Our contributions include:
==PAGE:126==
116
Conclusions
• Developing and implementing a deterministic component that combines
three image analysis techniques: color-based detection to identify potential
smoke regions by analyzing color distributions, motion-based detection
to capture dynamic smoke patterns using optical flow, and edge-based
detection to detect changes in image edges indicative of smoke.
• Training and integrating an MLP classifier to enhance detection accuracy.
• Incorporating the YOLO object detection model for real-time detection of
smoke and fire.
• Implementing an algorithm that transforms multiple binary masks into a
boolean output indicating the presence of fire, and developing an overall
fire likelihood score calculation used by the entire system to enhance the
reliability and accuracy of fire detection.
• Designing a comprehensive evaluation framework to test and validate the
system’s performance.
The results demonstrate the system’s potential for early wildfire detection, show-
ing significant promise for further development and deployment in real-world
scenarios.
6.2
Future Work
While the developed system shows considerable promise, there are several av-
enues for future research and development:
• Real-time processing: Optimizing the system to process videos in real
time is a crucial next step. This involves enhancing the efficiency of the
algorithms and potentially leveraging more powerful hardware.
• Enhanced dataset: Expanding the dataset to include more diverse and
challenging scenarios, such as videos with very early signs of smoke, could
improve the system’s robustness and accuracy.
• Advanced models: Developing specialized models for detecting very small
occurrences of smoke could enhance the system’s detection capabilities.
• Field testing: Conducting extensive field tests in various environmental
conditions will be crucial for validating the system’s performance and mak-
ing necessary adjustments for real-world deployment.

• Scalability and Integration: Exploring methods to scale the system for
large-scale deployment and integrating it with existing wildfire monitoring
networks will be important steps toward broader adoption.
6.3
Final Remarks
This thesis has demonstrated the feasibility and effectiveness of using advanced
detection technologies for early wildfire prevention. The developed system, with
its potential for high accuracy and real-time processing capabilities, represents
a significant advancement in wildfire detection technology. Continued research
and development in this area hold the potential to further enhance the capabil-
ities and impact of such systems, contributing to better wildfire management
and prevention strategies globally.
