Abstract
Accurate forecast of wind power generation is crucial for power agents to remain prof-
itable on the various electricity markets. Past research has established that exploiting
spatio-temporal correlations between wind farms can improve the quality of predictions
at a speciﬁc site, but this practice would require competing agents to collaborate. The
recent concept of data marketplace can bring competitors together, as buyers paying
to improve their predictions, or as sellers generating a new revenue stream from data
sales. A particular mechanism, already applied to wind forecasting, promisingly oﬀers
agents to trade without disclosing their identities or records. This thesis oﬀers to develop
this initial market algorithm to treat agents more equitably and give more control to
sellers on the utilisation of their knowledge by competitors. To do so, alternative and
additional methods for certain steps of the algorithm are deﬁned. The diﬀerent options
for each method are incorporated in market simulations to be tested over 30 prediction
periods. A synthetic pilot dataset is used ﬁrst, followed by a more realistic setup using
historical wind power observations from northern Europe. Results are analysed from the
viewpoint of agents with diﬀerent purposes, as well as implementation feasibility. The
examination of diﬀerent market metrics shows that the most relevant market mechanism
would conserve the original market price calculation, process buyers in a random sequence
and include a normalisation of sold features. Moreover, the addition of seller-controlled
privacy would attract more sellers, that were originally apprehensive of sharing their
historical observations with competitors.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Background and motivations
i.
Renewable energy development and grid stability
The stability of the electrical grid, connecting producers to consumers, depends highly on
output predictions from power generators. Buyers and intermediary agents regulating the
grid (like transmission system operators, managing national grid infrastructure) rely on
the accuracy of forecasts to maintain a balance between power supply and consumption
and avoid shortages. Some generation infrastructure like coal-fueled or nuclear plants are
controllable: operators decide in advance of the amount of power to be dispatched to the
grid. On the contrary, renewable sources, including wind, are intermittent and producers
of renewable energy can only try to forecast the amount of power a site can deliver during
a time window.
Governing bodies have high ambitions to develop renewable energies: the European Union
aims at multiplying by 6 between 2018 and 2040 the installed oﬀshore wind capacities in
member countries [6]; the International Energy Agency estimates that half of Europe’s
electricity will be supplied by renewable sources in 2030 [7]. As the share of renewable
energy grows in European energy mixes, this generation forecasting task will become
1
==PAGE:14==
more and more important for grid stability, which translates into ﬁnancial stability of
power generators.
ii.
Electricity markets
One way for producers to sell electricity is on electricity markets, which favors cheap
power and accurate forecasts.
Nord Pool, the electricity market of northern Europe,
is composed of diﬀerent trading markets, where producers can commit to delivering a
set amount of power with diﬀerent lead times and diﬀerent durations. The day-ahead
market is used to schedule oﬀers one day in advance, up to 36 hours before delivery, and
the intraday market for trading operations close to generation times. [8] For example, a
producer can sell the bulk of the prediction generation early before delivery, then correct
their oﬀer by selling or buying smaller amounts on the intraday market as the generation
time gets closer and predictions are improved.
Power sales accepted by the power market operator are binding, and producers must
deliver the determined amount of power at the right time. Otherwise, if their generation
does not match their commitments, they can be subject to imbalance costs from regulation
services. [9].
iii.
Prediction in the renewable power industry
The proﬁt of a power producer on the electricity markets depends on many parameters:
predicted volumes sold, real volumes produced, market prices, imbalance prices... To op-
timize this proﬁt, agents engage in forecasting exercises for wind power [10, 11], market
prices [12, 13] and develop power oﬀer strategies that cover both sales and imbalances
prices [14].
A wind agent can use their own local wind observations to estimate their future gen-
eration. Known meteorological correlations between geographic areas can make the ob-
servations of agents in other locations useful to this agent. In Scandinavia, the western
2
==PAGE:15==
winds reach Jutland before more eastern regions [15]. A wind farm can produce weather
observations in Jutland (Nord Pool area DK1) that can be used to predict wind power
generation in Zealand (DK2) or Scania (SE4), as visible in Fig. 1.2 and 1.3. In a carefully
designed framework, power agents from DK1 could sell their observations to other com-
panies located in DK2 or SE4. Agents in Jutland would generate an extra revenue stream
and their buyers would improve their proﬁts on the power market thanks to improved
generation predictions.

1.2
Research question
i.
Constraints on data trading between wind power agents
Trading wind observations poses a challenge to both parties involved, owners and users.
Establishing a payment system to incentivize data owners to share their observations
would turn these agents into sellers and buyers, with conﬂicting interests.
A wind power agent sharing their recent wind observations to any external party is
vulnerable. A dataset can be replicated and communicated freely to other parties with no
control from the original owner. This knowledge can be used against sellers on electricity
markets, resulting in a loss of proﬁt. If they were to provide their observations to an
external agents, sellers might also prefer to operate anonymously.
On the other side of a hypothetical marketplace are buyers. Buyers would come to the
marketplace with the objective to improve their forecasts of wind power. They need
guarantees that the purchased data is proﬁtable to them, i.e.
that the new forecast
generates extra proﬁt on the power market according to their private strategies, and that
the payment for the new forecast does not cancel out this proﬁt. Like sellers, they do not
want to disclose their original or improved forecasts to other agents.
4
==PAGE:17==
That reluctance naturally shared by agents is especially strong in the context of wind
generation forecasting, because all agents considered are direct competitors. Wind power
producers from diﬀerent regions (and even countries) are trading in the same power
markets to satisfy the same demand. Oﬀering knowledge to an agent from a data market
represents a competitive threat on the electricity markets that must be compensated
through adequate ﬁnancial compensation and subject to the control of sellers.
ii.
Research question
Starting from existing research results, the aim of this project is to specify a data trading
procedure that protects all agents involved.
Data sellers should be compensated for sharing their features according to their usefulness
and so as to cover the proﬁt lost on the electricity market to their data-buying competi-
tors; their features should not be disclosed to other agents and could be withdrawn at
any time.
Buyers should obtain a wind generation forecast that improves their proﬁt on the power
market, and pay according to, although less than, this proﬁt. All operations should be
done with reasonable running time, because buyers must be able to use the procedure to
improve their forecasts at the same pace that the electricity markets expects producers
to make their generation oﬀers.
1.3
Literature review
This section intends to provide an overview of how academic research has recently ad-
dressed the diﬀerent notions related to the research question.
5
==PAGE:18==
1.3.1
Wind forecasting using geographically dispersed observa-
tions
At least three publications have conﬁrmed the relevance of using wind observations from
multiple locations to predict future power generation at a speciﬁc site. [16] successfully
formulates the forecasting exercise as a mixed-integer non linear optimisation problem.
[17] conﬁrms the increase in accuracy of wind forecasts using multiple points for their
prediction task, compared to relying solely on local data. [18] used the records from
multiple wind farms to forecast the density function at a particular location, with datasets
provided by Ørsted. All publications support the intention to use records from diﬀerent
locations to predict the generation of a certain point of the grid. However, none oﬀer
incentives to agents to share their records with competitors.
1.3.2
Recent trading mechanisms for replicable data
The core of this thesis being the exchange of observations between agents, only such dig-
ital goods are included in this literature review. Limited digital goods such as ad space
[19, 20] are not examined.
The ﬁeld of data marketplaces comprises many aspects of data trading, like agent anonymity,
privacy compensation, data quality or agent utility. Each research work seems to focus
on a selection of these concerns depending on the priorities of target agents.
In seller-controlled procedures, buyers have to apply to sellers and be selected in order
to receive the features on sale.
In [21], individual sellers choose to which company they wish to disclose their information
against payment. [22] suggests to deﬁne a utility indicator for buyers to identify the most
useful sellers, who can then accept or reject the sale.
In other frameworks, power dynamics are more even and buyers have more inﬂuence
on their outcome. Many highlight the necessary compromise between data quality and
6
==PAGE:19==
budget.
[23] proposes that buyers deﬁne their ideal features and their ﬁnancial valuation, before
paying sellers based on the gap between the ideal and available features. [24] oﬀers a
algorithm based on optimisation problems, aiming at maximises the utility of three types
of agents: sellers, buyers, and intermediary collectors. [25] considers a seller’s privacy as a
commodity to be traded, and buyers can choose between optimizing the received accuracy
or their costs. [26] proposes that buyers bid for a dataset according to the amount of
knowledge they need to make a decision, while sellers prepare diﬀerent versions of their
features to respond to a buyer’s oﬀer. This relation between data quality and buyer
payment is also present in [5]. The algorithm from [5] additionally protects sellers by
never disclosing their features to buyers. Instead, a market operator ﬁts them to a buyer’s
forecasting model to return an improved prediction against payment. Their method to
allocate sellers’ features to buyers is included in [27], among data transformation, multi-
party computation protocols, decomposition methods and others.
1.3.3
Data trading in the energy industry
i.
Privacy of power consumers
Privacy of data sellers in the energy industry has been treated mostly from an end-
consumer perspective. In the treatment of private agents, compensation against privacy
is used rather to encourage sellers to keep sharing their data, rather than recover a proﬁt
missed because of the buyers’ increased knowledge.
[28] applies notions of cooperative game theory to calculate the payments to private
consumers in exchange of their consumption metrics. [29] deﬁnes a privacy compensation
function, ensuring that individual sellers are paid enough to recover the lost privacy. [30]
proposes a model maximising utility, sellers being compensated for privacy and buyers
looking for the highest data quality.

ii.
Two approaches to exchange data between generators
Contrarily to consumers’ records that are directly disclosed to buyers, trading data be-
tween industrial competitors requires to keep the value of sold features secret to other
agents.
[31, 32] formulate the forecasting exercise as a decomposed optimisation problem where
each agent is isolated in a sub problem. Competitors do not see one another’s dataset
throughout the calculation steps. Both article do not consider ﬁnancial operations be-
tween agents or mention agent’s goals (like simple accuracy of power prediction, or more
complex strategies like deﬁning the most proﬁtable oﬀer across electricity markets). [4]
is the direct application of [5] to wind prediction, and therefore includes a payment step
from a buyer to their sellers, exclusively based on the buyer’s perception of their ﬁnancial
gain from the improved prediction.
1.3.4
Takeaways
The current state of academic literature covers the spatio-temporal dependencies be-
tween diﬀerent wind farms, as well as consideration for sellers’ privacy and reluctance to
share their identity and/or records. However, no known publication oﬀers a data)trading
procedure where privacy is calculated using the seller’s perspective and features are not
directly disclosed to buyers.
Out of the two frameworks related to wind prediction that do not disclose features, [4]
is the closest to fulﬁlling the expectations of agents, expressed in section 1.2, and the
remaining gaps can be ﬁlled by the contributions of the present project.

1.4
Contributions
The main goal of this project is to reﬁne the data market algorithm developed in [5] and
[4] to accommodate a wider range of commercial wind power agents.
i.
Buyers sequence
Buying agents expect the marketplace to generate a proﬁtable and reliable prediction
to later use on the electricity market.
The proﬁt on both markets is subtly related
to the order in which buyers are processed by the algorithm, with the possibility that
precise sequences could improve or reduce the buyers’ satisfaction. This thesis investigates
the existence and relevance of such sequences. Alternatively, a random sequencing is
considered to set all agents on equal footing.
ii.
Regular price update function
The quality of the wind prediction produced by the algorithm depends on a reference
parameter, the market price, that is initially drawn from a probability distribution. This
method allows occasionally extreme values to interfere with the quality of the prediction.
Using the mean of the distribution instead would let the reference price to evolve gradually
and oﬀer more consistent predictions to buyers.
iii.
Normalisation of features on sale
On the opposite side of the market, diﬀerent sellers hope to sell their features, yet some
sellers face a constant disadvantage. If one seller oﬀers the wind power observations from
on wind farm, the amplitude of the features on sale are (approximately) proportionate to
the capacity of the wind farm. All sellers features receive noise from the same distribution,
which will aﬀect features of lower amplitudes more strongly than others. Two alternative
9
==PAGE:22==
allocation function are proposed, both including a systematic normalisation of features,
with two slightly diﬀerent distributions for the additional noise.
iv.
Seller-controlled privacy
Finally, this thesis introduces a new concept of seller-controlled allocation. Until [4],
sellers were considered to be agents willing to create a new revenue stream from the sales
of features. Because sellers and buyers are potential competitors on the electricity market,
oﬀering features to a buyer can increase their competitiveness at the sellers’ expense. The
sellers are oﬀered the opportunity to declare a minimal noise they want to allocate to
their features during the allocation step, so that their revenue from data compensates
the privacy and proﬁt lost to buyers.

2.2
Data-trading mechanism for prediction tasks
The trading algorithm introduced by Agarwal et al in [5] is composed of a brief initiali-
sation, followed by a potentially inﬁnite number of iterations, each iteration processing a
single buyer. After the initialisation is complete, agents are free to edit their attributes
(e.g. their bid price) between two iterations.
2.2.1
Initialisation
During the initialisation phase, the Market Operator receives all necessary information
from agents and sets up internal parameters. No calculation is actually performed since
this step is simply a preparation for the ﬁrst iteration of the data-trading mechanism.
2.2.2
Market iteration
One iteration corresponds to one buyer being handled by the Market Operator, from
calculating the new market price to receiving the improved prediction and paying sellers.
The buyer is assimilated to their iteration, both being designated by the same index i.
All elemental functions called during an iteration must be computationally simple enough
15
==PAGE:28==
to allow a large pool of agents in the market. [5] insists that one iteration should have a
computational complexity polynomial in M, and that this complexity should not increase
with N. Otherwise, the marketplace risks not being able to run at a sustainable speed
when the number of agent grows. This complexity requirement, called computational
eﬃciency, is the ﬁrst desirable property introduced to justify the quality of the market
design.
i.

for each possible price and take a value that is likely to favor the buyer in the next steps
of the mechanism.

2.3
Application to wind generation forecasting
Gonçalves et al have applied this marketplace mechanism to wind generation predictions
tasks in [4]. The speciﬁcities of the energy industry induce changes to the algorithm,
especially in the management of agents.
22
==PAGE:35==
2.3.1
Limited pool of competing agents
The energy industry, and more precisely wind power generators, constitute a limited
pool of agents, with hybrid seller-buyer proﬁles. Buyers come to this marketplace to
improve their forecasts, hoping that sellers’ local observations will help them increase
the quality of their own. Buyers are also aware that their own measures are valuable to
market competitors. This is why buyers, when they are not subject to an iteration, are
systematically considered part of the set of sellers.
Selling data to an unknown agent means sharing one’s private knowledge to increase a
potential competitor’s proﬁtability. The access to features on sale must be restricted, and
sellers must be compensated enough for their revelations to balance out the competitive
edge they may lose.
The framework proposed in [5] and applied to wind generation
forecasting in [4] respects the ﬁrst condition of conﬁdentiality, although the payments are
not designed to make up for the lost competitiveness.
2.3.2
Repetitiveness of the prediction tasks
The universal market mechanism from [5] has been adapted in [4] precisely for the repet-
itive (hourly, daily) forecasting tasks required to place oﬀers on electricity markets. To
maintain the quality of their predictions, buyers would have to participate in the data
market with the same periodicity T as the electricity market of their preference. Cou-
pled with the ﬁxed number of buyers, the market mechanism follows a periodic sequence
processing the same N buyers every period, instead of a linear queue of unique buyers as
assumed in [5].

Chapter 3
Proposition of alternative market
methods
The mechanism presented in the previous chapter constitutes a strong basis to trade data
between wind power agents. The original framework from [5] is deliberately generic, and
[4] tailored it to the routine of predicting wind power generation. One iteration of the
algorithm returns an improved prediction task to one buyer, who pays sellers according
to their own measure of quality without accessing information from sellers. Yet, it does
not completely meet the expectation laid out in the introduction. This chapter is an
attempt to deﬁne alternative methods to insert in the applied mechanism from [4], so
that the market mechanism fulﬁlls the conditions of sellers and buyers.
Each step of the market mechanism represents an elemental function connecting a buyer
to their sellers. Alterations to the allocation function (calculating features provided to
buyers) aim to put sellers on equal terms, regardless of their importance in the wind
power industry. Moreover, the creation of a new attribute for sellers allows them to limit
the competitiveness lost to their data buyers on power markets. Intentionally ordering
buyers before processing and exposing them to a more regular market price curve could
generate higher gains. Section by section, the alternatives are described and compared
qualitatively to the original functions from [5] and [4]. Their quantitative analysis will
24
==PAGE:37==
be initiated in chapter 4.
3.1
Price update function
The price update function was ﬁrst introduced in subsection 2.2.2. It generates a market
price pi at the very beginning of each iteration that will aﬀect agents in many ways:
• determining the quality of the features allocated to the buyer via the allocation
function AF(pi, bi, XS);
• calculating the payment to extract from the buyer with the revenue function RF(pi, bi, Yi);
• as a consequence, aﬀecting the revenue returned to sellers.
3.1.1
Original function
The original function deﬁned in [5] and reused in [4] draws a market price from a pre-
deﬁned list, following a probability distribution updated by the Multiplicative Weights
method. This method progressively increases the likelihood of the most beneﬁcial prices,
while relatively decreasing that of extreme values. Nonetheless, because the ﬁrst itera-
tion starts with a uniform distribution, the price update function will maintain strictly
positive probabilities for all possible prices.
If an extremely low price is picked, the buyer will receive a high-quality improved task
but the revenue function will extract a low price and sellers will earn a meager revenue.
An extremely high price will prevent buyers from obtaining a satisfactory gain, whatever
their bid price.
Moreover, this probabilistic approach could be against some buyers’ interests. In a
situation where half of the buyers favor low prices and the other half rather high prices,
the series of weights will be homogeneous. Unfortunately, buyers will be as likely to be
25
==PAGE:38==
processed using a price advantaging them than one working against their interests.
3.1.2
Alternative function
Instead of selecting a market price randomly based on the Multiplicative Weights distri-
bution, the Market Operator could calculate the mean of this distribution. The weights
would be updated in the same manner, and the price would represent a balance of all
prices and weights. Each buyer would be exposed to a market price depicting the whole
pool’s preferences rather than one favored by another agent with competing interests.
Undesirable extreme prices would be avoided too, so buyers would obtain predictions of
more consistent quality.

3.2
Optimised buyers sequence
The buyers sequence refers to the order in which buyers are processed by the Market
Operator. It is closely related to the market price pi and the Price Update function PF.
Indeed, as explained in subsection 2.2.2, the market price carries a history of all previous
buyers’ attributes. The position of a buyer in the sequence deﬁnes the extent of the
history this buyer is exposed to.
In the case of the wind generation forecasting task, this sequence is repeated for each
prediction period. The ﬁrst buyer of a period is treated with the information of buyers
from the precedent period, while the last buyer of a period beneﬁts from other agents’
knowledge for that same period. This last buyer has the advantage of being treated with
more recent and possibly more relevant parameters.
More generally, the position of a buyer in the sequence inﬂuences the relevance of the
parameters used by the Market Operator. Its impact on the buyer’s gain will also depend
on their personal sensitivity (such as the quality of their initial prediction task, or their
spatio-temporal correlations with sellers at other locations).
3.2.1
Original sequence
[5] and [4] imply that the buyers sequence is a queue of N treated in by order of arrival.
The ﬁrst case study of [4] later uses a ﬁxed pool of 3 agents {1, 2, 3} in the same order for
all periods of the simulations. The original buyers sequence is deduced to be the ﬁrst in,
ﬁrst out order of the ﬁrst prediction period, repeated in the case of the repetitive genera-
tion forecasting task. In the applied mechanism from [4], a buyer’s sequence "handicap"
is constant.
27
==PAGE:40==
3.2.2
Possible alternative permutations
For a given marketplace, there may exist a particular buyers sequence that optimizes the
buyers’ welfare. This sequence could be one of the N! permutations of [[1, N]]. A test
period of the marketplace with diﬀerent permutations could be used to identify the most
promising one, to be repeated for all market periods.
If such a sequence cannot be identiﬁed, because it does not exist or requires too much
computational resources, a random sequence can be applied. At the beginning of each
market period, the list of buyers would be shuﬄed. Buyers would experience a varying,
and eventually even, treatment from the Market Operator.
Whether the sequence is repeated or shuﬄed at every period, the choice of a speciﬁc
sequencing will need an extra step in the mechanism to ﬁrst identify all participant
before ordering them.
3.3
Normalising allocation function
The current version of the allocation function does not take into consideration the unique-
ness of each dataset provided by sellers, at the expense of agents with features of lower
magnitude.
Both universal and applied frameworks have their allocation functions add the noise
directly to sellers’ raw features, without any pre-processing. Features can have diﬀerent
amplitudes and will not be aﬀected by the noise with the same severity. For example,
the Nissum Bredning oﬀshore wind farm has been commissioned in 2018 with a capacity
of 28 MW [39]. If this wind farm operates for the usual 20 years, it will co-exist in the
North Sea west of Jutland with the future 10-GW energy island VindØ that should be
inaugurated in 2030 [40] [41]. If both wind farm companies participate in the marketplace
as sellers and oﬀer their raw power generation observations, the allocation function will
aﬀect them diﬀerently. The VindØ dataset would contain values with an amplitude over
28
==PAGE:41==
300 times greater than the Nissum Bredning dataset, but both would receive the same
additional noise. Initially strongly correlated and equally useful to a buyer, the allocated
VindØ features would produce higher marginal gains than Nissum Bredning and get
larger shares of the buyers’ payments. If the Nissum Bredning agent took the initiative
to normalize their observations before the initialisation phase, this amplitude gap could
be even greater and lessen even more the revenue from features of low amplitude.
Neither [5] nor [4] specify the standard deviation σ of the additional noise, but the
simulations from [4] were made using a ﬁxed value, equal to half the average standard
deviation of the features (c.f. script in Appendix A.1). Again, using ﬁxed parameters to
treat diﬀering features may harm the buyers’ gain as well as the sellers’ revenue.

3.4
Seller-controlled privacy
The last point of interest is not an alternative but an addition to the reference mechanism.
The identity and knowledge of the marketplace sellers are protected by the third party
Market Operator. Sellers are ﬁnancially compensated for the marginal gain they oﬀer
to buyers, yet the payments received may not cover the cost of their lost privacy and
competitiveness. Indeed, the payment from a buyer is calculated based on the buyer’s
valuation of gain, not the sellers’ valuation of lost privacy. This unilateral valuation can
make a buyer pay a small value for a prediction that will be very detrimental to sellers on
the electricity markets. Secondly, the payment calculated by the revenue function is lower
than the buyers’ gain valuation and sometimes split between multiple agents; even if a
buyer and a seller had similar valuations of gain/loss, the seller would receive a reduced
amount of this value.
The suggested solution is to let sellers decide of a minimum noise level added by the
allocation function. At each iteration, seller m’s features would be degraded by a minimal
noise of αm · N(0, σ2). The noise threshold αm of the seller corresponds to the noise factor
that balances out their revenue with the loss of competitiveness on the power market from
their data allocation.

Chapter 4
Analysis of alternative market methods
The previous chapter has introduced alternative methods to insert in the market mech-
anism of reference [4]. The propositions for the buyers sequences, Price Update function
and Allocation function have been deﬁned in mathematical terms and their potential to
satisfy agents’ expectations brieﬂy described.
Naturally, this next chapter focuses on deﬁning an objective analysis of the alternatives.
It contains a description of the market where the new methods are incorporated and
the agents to be processed. This framework is implemented in Python to simulate 30
instances of hour-ahead prediction. Two categories of assessment criteria are employed:
theoretical market properties evaluating operational feasibility, market metrics quantify-
ing the agents’ outcomes. All functions will be ﬁrst tried on simple synthetic datasets
and then confronted to realistic values from the electricity market Nord Pool.
4.1
Objectives of the analysis
The intention behind this chapter is to qualify the diﬀerent alternatives introduced in
chapter 3 through two diﬀerent lenses. Market properties describe the ability of a mech-
anism to be implemented at a large scale in a stable manner; quantitative metrics assess
33
==PAGE:46==
whether agents could ﬁnd beneﬁts in participating in a marketplace built on such meth-
ods.
Results from diﬀerent methods can be compared for analysis purposes and to identify
a smaller list of methods that could be incorporated in a future marketplace.
Some
alternatives could show better market properties while others could deliver better imme-
diate results to agents (measured by the market metrics presented in subsection 4.3.2).
If this is the case, a single best market mechanism may not be identiﬁable. Instead, a
reduced number of possible mechanisms could be isolated to satisfy diﬀerent priorities of
the Market Operator (e.g. mechanism stability, agents satisfaction, etc.).

4.3
Assessment criteria
Assessment criteria are the main measures used to evaluate the quality of a market
mechanism. The four desirable market properties are traits that ensure smooth market
operations. They have been introduced from section 2.2 along with their related market
methods and are reused in this section to study the resilience of alternative methods.
Market metrics quantify the evolution of the market and its outcomes for agents, from
buyer and seller perspectives.
4.3.1
Qualitative criteria: market properties
Market properties have been introduced in section 2.2 where the main methods from [5]
enforcing them are described. Together, the four properties ensure that the marketplace
can satisfy a large number of agents over a long time perspective, while preserving agents
from fraud.
New market methods can make a market mechanism lose some of the properties, namely
computational eﬃciency, truthfulness and revenue maximisation. Evaluating the prop-
erties of the alternative methods identiﬁed in chapter 3 is done by comparison with the
original functions. This analysis relies on the assumption that the original framework
from [5] respects all studied properties below.
i.
Computational eﬃciency
A market is deemed computationally eﬃcient if its time complexity is polynomial in M,
the number of sellers, and independent from the number of buyers N. This property will
be particularly important if the data marketplace is to be really implemented. Buyers
would wish to receive their improved forecasting fast enough after the last updates of the
features, to use them to make oﬀers on power markets.
37
==PAGE:50==
The evaluation of computational eﬃciency excludes the prediction models and gain func-
tions employed by the market mechanism. These functions can vary from an iteration to
the next, and for this reason considered a buyer attribute rather than a market method.
ii.
Truthfulness
Truthfulness is the property governing the buyers’ bidding decisions. At every iteration,
a buyer is asked to provide their bid price, or public valuation of gain, bi. In a mechanism
respecting this property, the buyer is encouraged to give their honest valuation of this
attribute, because the Allocation function is deﬁned such that an increase in bi will result
in an equal or higher gain. This property stems from the fact that the quality of the
allocated features is monotonically increasing with the bid price (i.e. that the additional
noise decreases with bi). In a non-truthful mechanism, a dishonest buyer could ﬁnd a
lower price b′
i to use as bid and that could deliver a higher gain than their real valuation
of gain bi.
The bid price inﬂuences the buyers’ gain, as well as the payment to sellers and the price
update for the next iteration. The truthfulness property protects other sellers and future
buyers from deceitful bidding strategies.
iii.
Zero regret or revenue maximisation
The property of revenue maximisation (or zero regret) describes a mechanism that pro-
duces a series of market price such that, as the number of buyers N increases, the dif-
ference between the buyers’ payment and the payment based the best price in hindsight
goes to 0. This property is particularly dependent on the function that produces these
market prices, the Price Update function.
38
==PAGE:51==
iv.
Excluded properties
Two other properties that have been mentioned in chapter 2.2, fairness and robustness
to replication, are not part of this analysis. They are addressed here once for the sake
of exhaustiveness but will not be studied further. Shapley fairness refers to a revenue
division with multiple quality, and in particular a division of payments between sellers
that relies on their marginal contribution to the buyers’ gains. Robustness to replication
supports honest participation in the data marketplace, discouraging agents from taking
advantage of the fair allocation. This second property is speciﬁcally implemented through
the Revenue Division function PD too.
The revenue division function is not aﬀected by the alternatives proposed in the previous
chapter; these two properties are always preserved and need not be evaluated systemati-
cally in the case analysis.
4.3.2
Quantitative criteria: market metrics
Market metrics measure the activities of the market, in order to study the diverse func-
tions of interest and their outcomes for agents. Simple metrics are direct outputs from
functions, while composed metrics are build intentionally for the purpose of the analysis.
i.
Simple metrics
Gain
A buyer’s gain is measured by their gain function introduced along with other
attributes in section 2.1. Expressed in EUR, it quantiﬁes the ﬁnancial beneﬁt a buyer
has found on the data market, compared to improving their prediction task solely based
on their private observations.
It can also be presented in %, in which case the gain
represents the relative quality improvement between the market-improved task and the
privately improved one.
39
==PAGE:52==
Payment
The payment is the second metric relative to buyers. It is the amount in
EUR that the buyer pays to the Market Operator to receive their improved task. It is
directly calculated by the Revenue function RF:
ri = RF(pi, bi, Yi)
(4.4)
Revenue
The revenue is the amount in EUR transferred by the Market Operator to
the sellers in response to the buyer’s gain. The vector Ψi calculated by the Revenue

a buyer’s gain and the more useful a seller, the higher the payment:

Composed metrics

The payoﬀis the balance of all known operations related to the market place:

For one agent, it represents the value this agent has created for themselves by participating
in the market. Summed over the pool of agents, it represents the total value created by
the marketplace, also equal to the summed gain (as payments and revenues cancel one
another). It is the main indicator to judge the quality of a market mechanism.

5.1.2
Price Update function
Computational eﬃciency
Most operations done by the original (algorithm 1) and
alternative (algorithm 3) Price Update functions are the same, especially the update of

to draw a value, or as multiplicative coeﬃcients in a sum, respectively. The sum from the
alternative function requires a number of additions and multiplications that grow with
the size of the price vector |Bp|, which is unrelated to M or N, and not aﬀecting the
eﬃciency property.

The diﬀerence between the original Price Update function
and its alternative is the loss of probabilistic notions. In the original proof of revenue
maximisation in appendix B.1, the expected gain over all possible prices at iteration i galg

to a probability distribution, but simply parameters used to calculate a weighted sum
(equal to the mean of the price distribution in the original framework). As a consequence,
most quantities used in the original proof of revenue maximisation are not deﬁned for
the alternative Price Update function, which does not respect this property in the sense
deﬁned in [5].
5.1.3
Normalising data allocation
Two properties, truthfulness and computational eﬃciency, are related to the allocation
function.
47
==PAGE:60==
Computational eﬃciency
The complexity of the new allocation is that of the original
function combined with the normalisation operations: calculating of the features means
and standard deviations, subtracting the mean and dividing by the standard deviation.
The calculations are done for the features of one seller, a vector of length T. Normalising
this speciﬁc vector does not depend on the number or existence of other sellers, so the
complexity of the normalisation is constant with M. Normalising M vectors is linear
with M, which is better than polynomial and satisﬁes the property of computational
complexity.
Truthfulness
There are multiple ways to interpret the allocation of normalised features
to study truthfulness:
• Sellers upload already normalised features, later allocated by the original allocation
function with a ﬁxed noise variance;
• Sellers features are treated by a normalising transfer function during the upload,
saved under their normalised form described by equation 3.1 and allocated by the
original allocation function with a ﬁxed variance;
• Sellers features are saved under their raw form and the normalising allocation func-
tion assigns features from equation 3.2 or 3.3 to buyers.
In all approaches, the features undergo a treatment that does not alter their quality,
before being degraded by a noise which amplitude factor decreases when the bid price
bi increases (the market price pi being ﬁxed before the buyer bids).
Buyers are still
increasing the quality of the allocated features and the task obtained from their input to
prediction model when they increase their bid prices: truthfulness still holds.
48
==PAGE:61==
5.1.4
Seller-controlled privacy
Computational eﬃciency
The diﬀerence between the original Allocation function
and the one augmented by parameters αm is located in the noise amplitude factor. At
iteration i:
• max(0, pi −bi) is independent from M, while
• max(0, pi −bi, αm) is calculated for each seller.
Fetching a value in the vector α and calculating the max function is constant with M, so
the calculations over the whole set of sellers is linear with M. Including loss of privacy does
indeed add extra operations, but they are simple enough to preserve the computational
eﬃciency.
Truthfulness
In the original Price Update function, the nature of the max function
makes the noise amplitude factor of all features decreases linearly when bi increases, until
the tipping value pi −bi = 0 where the factor stabilises at 0.
In the new function, the same dynamics apply, bi has the same inﬂuence on the factor
until pi −bi is small enough that other arguments of the max function become dominant.
The tipping point is now diﬀerent for each seller and may appear at a lower value of
bi than in the original mechanism.
Increasing a buyer’s bid price still decreases the
noise amplitude factor and increases the quality of the allocated features, preserving the
truthfulness property.

intention of deteriorating features equitably.

the mean of the features and less deteriorating than with σ = 1.

tends to produce slightly better returns than σ=0.5.

Privacy control
This last section intends to demonstrate the functioning of noise thresholds set by sellers
to control their privacy.
Noise thresholds are decided by sellers before their features
are allocated, with diﬀerent consequences on other agents. The analysis below does not
aim to identify a best method or an optimal value for the new attribute, but rather to
highlight the eﬀects of the new feature.
63
==PAGE:76==
Fig. 5.6 presents the gain lost by buyers A and B when their main sellers (respectively
B and C) apply two diﬀerent noise thresholds. The buyers bid the same price (5 EUR)
and are subject to the same original Price Update function.
As expected, the higher the noise enforced by the main seller, the lower the gain for a
buyer. Buyer A is the most aﬀected by the noise enforced by seller B: applying a noise
threshold of simply αB = 1 makes buyer A lose 70% of their gains, and over 90% when
seller B chooses a stronger privacy level of αB = 3. Implementing the same unit noise
via αC = 1 hurts buyer B’s gain more moderately as they lose only 50% of their gain.
The reason for these diﬀerent reactions is the ability of a buyer to redirect their data
exchanges towards another seller when their preferred source is too degraded. Buyer
A is very dependent on seller B, even when that seller protects their features with a
high noise thresholds. This relation is visible in Fig. 5.7, all of buyer A’s payment is
consistently oﬀered to seller B. On the contrary, buyer B is able to shift their purchase
towards buyer A when their usual buyer C restricts their features. This way, buyer B
reduces their loss of gain. The sold features and prediction tasks of Nord Pool agents
have the same nature, wind power generated during one hour. If agent A exclusively uses
agent B’s features to predict their generation, the prediction of A (then converted into
observations and features on sale) contains a lot of information about the features of B.
When agent A later sells these features on the market, B can reuse them to predict their
future generation if seller C restricts their information.

The relation between the revenues perceived by sellers and the noise thresholds are a

Naturally, when a seller m chooses a noise thresholds αm > 0, their buyers will obtain

loses 78% of their revenue (from 136.90 to 20.66, see Table 5.18) when they choose αB =

The revenue of a seller also depends on others’ privacy choices. The amount received
by seller m is their personal share from the revenue division, multiplied by the buyer’s
payment calculated over the whole set of sellers. If a buyer gains and pays less because
an important seller m1 restricted their features, the amount perceived by another seller
m2 will be a share of a lower payment and will depend on whether the buyer redirects its
revenue division from m1 to m2.

Chapter 6
Conclusion
6.1
General conclusion
The aim of this master thesis is to deﬁne a data-trading mechanism for wind power
generators to convert past local wind observations into accurate generation predictions,
exploiting established spatio-temporal dependencies between wind farms. The partici-
pants in such a data marketplace are also competitors on power markets, and therefore
expect a data market algorithm to protect their identities, data, and proﬁtability.
The foundation for this mechanism is a centralised marketplace deﬁned in [4], where
buyers and sellers provide their information to a Market Operator in charge of producing
the improved forecasts and coordinating ﬁnancial operations. The algorithm is speciﬁcally
designed for power agents to be synchronized with power markets. This work is itself
based a more universal algorithm for agents of any industry to punctually purchase an
improved prediction [5].
The present thesis augments this framework with considerations of fairness across agents
and a feature enabling sellers to control the utilization of their features. A deliberate
sequence of buyers is proposed for each prediction period, to expose buyers to market
parameters that maximize their proﬁts, or a randomized treatment if such a sequence
67
==PAGE:80==
does not exist. An alternative calculation of the market price is also suggested to return
predictions of more regular quality to buyers. On the other side of the market, equity
among sellers is introduced by normalising their features on sale, so that agents with
higher installed capacities do not outweigh smaller agents when marginal contributions
are estimated for payments. This normalisation is completed with two distributions of
allocated noise. Finally, sellers are oﬀered the choice to determine a minimal noise to
allocate to their features before they are used for a buyer’s prediction model, so that the
buyer’ gain does not cause a detrimental loss of competitivity to the sellers when they
meet again on the power markets.
One by one, alternative methods are inserted in a market simulation for 30 prediction
periods to produce market metrics and analyze their relevance for the diﬀerent types
of agents. Simulations are run ﬁrst for a simple, synthetic pool of three agents with
diﬀerent bid prices, then in more realistic conditions where nine Nord Pool regions act
as participants providing their historical wind generation records. Alternatives are also
studied theoretically, to determine whether they respect desirable market properties, i.e.
feasibility criteria.
In all cases, no speciﬁc sequence of buyers is preferred, since a sequence preferred by
a buyer can be detrimental to another, and a buyer’s best sequence depends on varying
factors like bid prices. If a particular order is not preferable, then the best approach is
to shuﬄe buyers for each period so that they are not exposed repeatedly to the same
disadvantage.
The smoother alternative for the Price Update function shows interesting results for Nord
Pool cases, but not for all bid prices of synthetic agents. This indecisive result, coupled
with the loss of the market property of revenue maximisation, does not encourage the
use of this new function.
Not all agents prefer normalised features, but this approach has more to do with best
practice and equity between sellers than performance. The second element of the alloca-
tion function studied is the standard deviation of the basic additional noise. Buyers and
68
==PAGE:81==
agents in general prefer a low deviation that generates higher gains and payoﬀs, while
sellers prefer a high deviation producing higher revenues.
Finally, the development of a new attribute for sellers, the noise threshold, allows sell-
ers to moderate the use of their features by buyers and the sellers’ potential losses on
power markets, as intended. No additional market properties are lost by the alternative
methods proposed.
6.2
Future work
The work reported in this master thesis challenges some assumptions or default methods
applied in [5] and [4]. It deﬁnes best practices, such as normalising features and shuf-
ﬂing the sequence of buyers. It also highlights conﬂicting interests between agents, like
the standard deviation of the additional noise. In other words, this thesis characterizes
patterns and dynamics between functions.
However, this thesis cannot conclude on the exact values of some parameters that require
a dialogue with real wind power agents. For example, a wind power agent on power mar-
kets can estimate their personal valuation of gain βi and the related bid price bi. Knowing
the magnitude of the bid prices is necessary to deﬁne a vector of possible prices Bp to
use for a real data marketplace. Together, market and bid prices act as a multiplicative
factor for the basic noise N(0, σ2), which sets the standard deviation of the complete
additional noise (amplitude factor) x (basic noise). The natural development of this work
is to identify relevant values or functions for the parameters used by the Market Operator
and the agents’ attributes, for example by collaborating with an industrial partner. This
real power agent can further support the evolution of the trading mechanism by sharing
their own prediction models and historical datasets, like Ørsted did for the research in
[18].
