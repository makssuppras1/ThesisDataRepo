Abstract
In the steam generator of nuclear power plants, water hammer can occur easily as the
result of the sudden closure of a valve. This thesis deals with different methods in order
to simulate and explain the impact of a water hammer phenomenon on a feedwater ring
of a nuclear power plant.
In this thesis, the method of characteristics and the finite element software ANSYS have
been used to simulate water hammer events in flexible pipes. ANSYS allows detailed
3D computation while the method of characteristics is a 1D method which partially takes
into account the fluid¬≠structure interaction (only for the wave speed). For taking into ac¬≠
count the pipe flexibility, the the method of characteristics has been improved using the
Joukowsky‚Äôs equation. Sensitivity studies on ANSYS have been conducted to be able to
simulate correctly the models.
The main objective of the thesis is to simulate water hammers in the feedwater ring to be
prepared if a water hammer occurs. Two main studies have been conducted. The first
one aims at studying the strength of the terminal end of a feedwater ring. This study has
been improved by evaluating the impact of J¬≠tubes present in this kind of feedwater rings.
The second one is about the time history of the induced reaction forces in a water hammer
load configuration.
This thesis has been done in FRAMATOME‚Äôs office.

Introduction
Nuclear power plants have large pipe systems. They delivers high temperature and high
pressure water from some points to other. In some pipes, there is presence of radioac¬≠
tive elements. The main objective of a producer of an nuclear electric producer is not
productivity but safety. Therefore, those pipe systems require to be correctly sized.
In the working conditions, a valve can suddenly close or steam and liquid water can be
present in a pipe. Those configurations are starting points of water hammers. Those
configurations can happen [1]. Then, it is necessary to take into account water hammers
event when one size a pipe and its supports. In a pipe, the high variation of pressure can
induce large plastic deformation in the structure (see Figure 1).
Figure 1: Expansion joints on a steam line that have been destroyed by a water hammer
[2]
In nuclear power plants with pressurized water reactor, the water arrives in the steam
generator (SG) from the feedwater ring. If one suppose that a breach occurs upflow the
SG, the water in the SG will go in the direction of the breach as the pressure is lower.
However, the presence of a check valves will stop the fluid flow. The kinetic energy be¬≠
comes pressure and a water hammer has occurred. This kind of water hammers can have
amplitudes up to 150 bar.
In nuclear power plants with pressurized water reactor, the SG is a component where there
are both liquid and vapour. This is a configuration for water hammer to occur, especially
in the feedwater ring [3, section 3.3.4]. This kind of water hammer in the configuration of
mass flow rate, temperature and pressure can have an amplitude up to 400 bar.
For those reasons, the impact of a potential water hammer in the feedwater ring needs to
be studied [4, page 479].
Experimental studies have been carried out to quantify water hammers for nuclear appli¬≠
cations [5], [6]. However, it is not the objective of the thesis. The objective is to know the
impact of a water hammer on the structure.
First, the theory of water hammer based on the acoustic equations is developed. In this
part, a 1D solution has been found out: the method of characteristics. This methods
correspond to the summation of two waves traveling at the speed of sound in opposite
directions. This method has solutions for different boundary conditions: pipe junction, null
displacement and imposed pressure. The theory of the fluid structure interaction (FSI) is
developed and leads to the Joukowsky‚Äôs equation and to the matrix formulation used in

finite element software such as ANSYS. From the acoustic equations and the FSI the¬≠

different methods are developed to solve water hammer events. In this thesis, only the

1.1
Description of a nuclear power plant
A nuclear power plant produces energy from the fission of atoms. For a Pressurized Water
Reactor (PWR), the main steps of production of electricity are:
1. From the controlled fission of uranium (also named fuel) in the reactor, thermic en¬≠
ergy is given to the water of the primary loop. This water goes then to the steam
generator (description in section 1.2) where it is cooled with the water of the sec¬≠
ondary loop due to heat transfer. This ‚Äúcolder‚ÄĚ water then goes back to the reactor
to cool the fuel. In a PWR, the pressure is controlled in order to keep the water
liquid.
2. The water in the secondary loop is heating up in the steam generator and becomes
steam. This steam then goes to a turbine. The steam drives the turbine to rotate
and the generator produces electricity. The steam then goes to a condenser where
it is cooling with water of the third loop. Due to its cooling, the steam becomes liquid
and then goes back to the steam generator.
3. The water of the third loop is taken from for example the sea or a river. It goes then
to the condenser where its temperature increases. This hot water is then living the
loop to the nature from a tower as vapour or to the sea or river as liquid.
Figure 1.1: Nuclear power plant with a pressurized water reactor [7]
1.2
Description of a steam generator
A steam generator (SG) has two main goals: cooling the primary loop and vaporizing the
water of the second loop.
For the primary loop, hot water from the reactor arrives in the inlet. It then goes in the
tubes where it losses a part of its heat. Finally, it leaves the SG by the outlet.

For the secondary loop, water arrives in the SG from the feedwater inlet. This water is
distributed to the SG by the feedwater ring. By gravity, the water flows between the inner

tubes containing the hot water of the primary loop, its temperature increases and starts to
boil. The vapor goes up. However, it is a wet steam and to protect the downflow blades of
the turbine, the proportion of liquid must be as low as possible. To minimize this proportion
of liquid, separators are present. So, the dry steam leaves the SG by the outlet and the
recirculated liquid goes back between the inner and the outer shell.

2.5
Water Hammer
In this thesis, the origin of a water hammer is not studied but this part aims at contex¬≠
tualising. The resulting pressure wave is assumed to be the input for the calculations.
However, hereby are presented two origins of water hammer and a method to get an idea
of the amplitude of the pressure wave.
2.5.1
Monophasic water hammer
In a pipe with moving fluid, when a valve suddenly closes or a pump trips, the fluid must
suddenly stop. Due to energy conservation, the kinetic energy of the fluid becomes pres¬≠
sure energy. The pressure wave moves upstream at the speed of sound.
For the feedwater ring, a scenario could be:
1. A breach occurs upstream of the ring
2. Due to high pressure in the SG, the water goes upstream to the breach as the
pressure is lower (at the atmosphere pressure).
3. Between the breach and the ring, there are a check valve. It disables the fluid to go
upstream. So, the fluid has to stop and the water hammer occurs.
2.5.2
Two¬≠phase water hammer
In a pipe with 2 phases fluid (steam and colder liquid), the temperatures tend to be uniform.
The steam gives its heat to the liquid and condensates. As the steam has a lower density
than the liquid, vacuum momentarily exists. This vacuum is replaced by incoming steam
(which has a velocity). At a moment, the way for the steam to replace vacuum is blocked
by the liquid. The steam which is now blocked inside this new pocket collapses. Its
previous boundary hit each other which generates a water hammer1. This phenomenon
is summarized by Figure 2.5.

Conclusion
Throughout this project, the water hammer impact on structures has been studied. In
order to get the pressure wave induced by a water hammer, the classical method of char¬≠
acteristics has been developed from the acoustic equation. This method allows an easy
and quick resolution of problems. However, it does not completely take into account the
FSI and it is a 1D method. For those reasons, the use of more sophistical methods is
necessary: a finite difference method and a finite element method. The finite difference
method has been programmed on Python from the acoustic equation. This method takes
into account the the fluid structure interaction but it remains a 1D method. The finite
element method has been used on the commercial software ANSYS which allows 3D
computations with FSI. The 3 methods have been compared on 2 different systems: one
with rigid boundary conditions (no FSI) and another one with flexible boundary conditions
(with FSI). For the first system, the 3 methods fits perfectly which validates the different
methods. For the second method, the FDM has required some hypotheses to model the
flexible boundary conditions. The FDM and the FE method give similar results but the
method of characteristics cannot get the influence of flexible structure. The method of
characteristics has been improved by using the Joukowsky‚Äôs equation. This improved
method requires the displacement of some particular points (such as the center of the ter¬≠
minal end of a pipe) which is calculated in the FE method. This improved method allows
to find differently the pressure history, and then to validate the simulations. Due to diffi¬≠
culties of implementing the FDM for complex geometry, this method is not use for further
calculations.
In order to have an efficient mesh and a correct time step, sensitivity studies have been
done. They are based on the total energy. It leads to a method which is only based on
one characteristic of the input signal: its frequency. The mesh result is that for a mesh
which uses quadratic elements, a wavelength requires 5 elements. For time stepping, a
period of the input signal needs 12 time steps to be defined correctly.
At this point of the project, the developed theory and methods have allowed to start analy¬≠
ses on feedwater ring. The first analyse was about the plastic deformation of a bolt under
a water hammer load. During the study, some hypotheses have been done such as ne¬≠
glecting the J¬≠tubes. This analyse takes into account plasticity and contacts between
different elements. Simulations have been carried out. The pressure history has been
validated by the improved method of characteristics and the events of plastification of the
bolt have found out: plastification occurs when the pressure reaches a new maximum.
From those results, a relation between the residual elongation of the thread and the input
pressure has been calculated.
Then, the influence of J¬≠tubes has been studied in order to know if it is reasonable to
neglect them. Simulations have been done for several number of J¬≠tubes to quantify the
effect of J¬≠tubes. It has been found out that the pressure magnitude decreases with the
number of crossed J¬≠tubes and that a part of the pressure in the wave becomes negative.
From those results, a method to predict the influence of J¬≠tubes based on the method
of characteristics and an equivalent electric system has been developed. This method,
which remains incomplete for some instants, allows a good approximation of the impact of
J¬≠tubes on the pressure wave. From this method, the previous study with plastification has
been recalibrated and has allowed to modify drastically the relation between the residual
elongation and the input pressure.

Finally, simulations have been carried out in a complete feedwater ring. Those simula¬≠
tions aimed at getting the influence of the model of the supports and the material damping
on the reaction forces in the supports. The pressure results have been compared with
expectations from the theory and the J¬≠tubes influence. The comparison has shown a
difference which is due to the curvature of the ring. Indeed, all the theory is based on
straight structures. The reaction forces have been described and it allows to know which
supports require the strongest sizing. It has been shown that flexible supports have lower
reaction forces than rigid supports. In this study, the influence of J¬≠tubes has been taken
into account and it has been shown that it is necessary to take them into account. In¬≠
deed, they have an influence on the pressure wave. Moreover, they make the system
unsymmetrical which lead vertical forces.
Future aspects
For the J¬≠tubes model, it has been chosen to consider an imposed boundary conditions in
the outlet. The relevance of this boundary should be studied either throughout simulations
or experimentally.
In the last simulations, the supports have been defined as linear springs. It could be inter¬≠
esting to model them as non linear due to the presence of gaps which leads to possible
contacts. The theory of wave in a toroidal pipe could be interesting to be able to quantify
the pressure distribution in the pipe.
In all models, the input wave has been assumed as a rounded square signal. It might
be interesting to look further if it is possible to model for example a valve closure in a FE
model. The determination of the input wave could also been defined in a fluid dynamics
computation.
For the European Pressurised Reactor, the feedwater ring geometry is different: it is
shorter and has deflectors. The deflectors have a geometry incomparable to J¬≠tubes. So,
the wave will have a different behaviour and that will have an impact on the structure and
the supports.

Problem

coherent as there are neither input of energy nor dissipation (due to damping). For the
case 2, the total energy is not constant. By comparing the different strain and kinetic
