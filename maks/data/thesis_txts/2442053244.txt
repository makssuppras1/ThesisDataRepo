Abstract 
This thesis applies system identification techniques to model the dynamics of differential drive mobile robots. 
A grey box modelling approach is used to estimate the load distribution parameters, namely; mass, moment 
of inertia and center of gravity. The model structure is derived from physical principles of motion of rigid 
bodies on a rotating reference frame. The model data is obtained by conducting uncoupled experiments, in 
which only one degree of freedom is in motion, on two different mobile robot platforms. Besides numerical 
simulations, the method was applied to experimental data to validate the results. For the estimation, the 
non-linear least squares algorithm was used and has produced results that are statistically good.

1 Introduction 
Mobile robots are becoming increasingly important in advanced applications since they have the capability 
to move around and perform tasks in their environment with limited human interaction. In the industry the 
mobile robots have many applications in areas of; health care, food service, security and distribution. 
The differential drive mobile robots are the most popular wheeled robots, since they offer high mobility and 
good stability. This thesis deals with the motion of the differential drive mobile robots. The motion is planned 
in a motion controller in terms of position estimation and path planning.  
The goal of the motion controller is to derive the required input to the DC motors. The design of the controller 
is based on reaching a goal, usually in terms of desired velocities. The robot motion is handled using the 
kinematic model to predict the position. The predicted position is then corrected using sensor measurements 
(e.g. odometry).       
A kinematic model is based on the geometry of the robot and neglects the forces acting on it. In order to deal 
with the dynamic variation a dynamic model is essential. The dynamic model considers the influence of forces 
acting on the robot, which for high velocity or heavy load cannot be neglected.  
The dynamics of mobile robots are highly non-linear and involve non-holonomic constrains which makes the 
modeling difficult. The problem is that the exact formulation of the dynamic model depends on the 
knowledge of several parameters that may vary over time, namely the mass, moment of inertia and center 
of gravity.  
The purpose of this thesis is to build a dynamic model for differential drive robots and identify the unknown 
parameters. The goal is to develop an integrated estimation system that uses the dynamics of mobile robots 
together with sensory data to estimate the parameters of load distribution. The idea is that parameters can 
be used in motion controller to achieve better performance, but the implementation of the controller will be 
left for future work.  
The estimation algorithm will be implemented on two different mobile robots to test the accuracy and 
effectiveness of the proposed method. The main use case for the application is for estimating the load 
distribution parameters of a transportation robot, Gopal400, at the company of Robotize. The parameters 
will prove useful for the operation and control of the platform.    
For the application of load transportation with mobile robots an on-line estimation of the load distribution 
can also be useful for fault detection. For example, to issue an alarm if part of the load is dropped during 
transportation. But the main purpose of the algorithm is to prevent that from happening in the first place by 
using the parameters to put restrictions on the control according to the load.

1.1 System identification 
The approach of this project is based on the system identification theory. 
A system is represented in two different ways, the real system and a model of the real system.  The real 
system is what happens in reality. It follows the laws of nature, may it be physical, chemical or biological. The 
real system consists of data collected from experiments conducted on the system. The model is constructed 
to mimic the behavior of the real system. It is represented as a set of mathematical equations that we believe 
best describes the laws of nature governing the system. 
To test our believes, a set of experiments are carried out on the real system. The results are then gathered 
and compared to the results predicted by the model.  
The procedure of system identification can be stated by as follow: 
- 
Construct the model structure  
- 
Experimental design/collect data 
- 
Parameter estimation  
- 
Model validation 
The context of the thesis follows the steps of system identification.

2 Robotic Framework 
This chapter presents the platforms used during the project. The purpose is to state the nominal values of 
the parameters to be estimated. 
The experiments of this project have been carried out on two different platforms, the SMR and the Gopal. 
Both of which are differential drive robots with two driving wheels at the back and two castor wheels in front 
to stabilize the robot.  
Due to its simplicity and mobility, the differential drive robot is the most common type of mobile robot. It 
consists of two drive wheels, each powered an electrical motor, and no steering wheels. The robot is steered 
by the difference in the two driving wheels, hence the name “differential drive”.

3 Mathematical Modelling 
In this chapter a general mathematical model of the differential drive mobile robot is presented and analyzed. 
The kinematic and dynamic system properties are described along with a model of the DC motors. 
In order to make the analysis within the scope of this thesis, following conditions are assumed:  
- 
The body of the mobile robot is rigid and not affected by outside interference force. 
- 
The robot moves on a flat surface without any slope. 
- 
The friction between the wheels and the robot, the wheels and the ground are neglected. 
3.1 Kinematic model 
Kinematic modeling deals with the motion of mechanical systems without considering the mass of the 
platform or the forces that affect its motion. By expressing constrains on the motion of the individual wheel, 
the movement of the entire robot can be computed as the average of the motions of each wheel.    
To describe the motion of the robot in its environment, two different coordinate systems (frames) are to be 
distinguished.  
- 
The Inertial coordinate system (world coordinate system), which is a global frame that is fixed in the 
plane in which the robot moves.  
- 
The robot coordinate system, which is local frame attached to the robot, and therefore moving with 
it.   
The origin of the robot frame is point A, defined as the midpoint between the rear axle. The center of mass 
of the robot is in Point C, symmetric to the origin A at a distance d. In Figure 5 the two frames are shown 
relative to the robot.

The effect of friction is considered in the estimation process. 
5.1 Experiments 
The experimental design task is to create the experiments that produces appropriate data for the model to 
use. In the case of the dynamics of the mobile robot the two terms in the dynamical model from (34)(35) 
describes the wheel accelerations for translational and rotational motion. 
Therefore, two experiments are designed in order to isolate each term individually: a forward and test and a

5.1.1 Low speed test 
The friction is a force that resists the motion of two objects in contact. The materials in contact, the wheel 
and the ground, will deform slightly due to the pressure of contact. The deformation will show of as 
microscopic bumps and ridges which catch on one another when the wheel is moving on the ground.   
In order to start rotating the wheel a force is applied to it. The wheel is held in its place by the static friction.  
At solid surfaces, the static friction is due to the surface roughness of the objects in contact and its value 
depends on the type materials that are in contact. The friction is higher for rough and dry surfaces and lower 
for wet and smooth ones. 
The static friction is acting against the force applied so the force must be greater than the static friction in 
order to start the motion. When the force applied is greater than the static friction, the robot “breaks free” 
and starts sliding and the static friction becomes kinetic friction. Although a force is still needed to keep the 
robot sliding, it is much less than the force needed to “break free”. 
An experiment was planned to investigate the effect of friction on the robot. The idea is to determine how 
much force needed to overcome the friction in terms of input voltage or current.  
The experiment was to find the lowest speed possible for the robot to drive smoothly and record the voltage 
or current needed to drive the robot. The idea is that the input used as the lowest velocity is the closest 
possible input needed to overcome the friction.  
This test is not meant as a part of the online estimation algorithm but as an offline test to estimate a constant 
friction which will be taken into account for better estimate of the parameters.   
 
5.1.2 Forward test 
To collect the forward acceleration data the robot is set to accelerate forward for a distance or period of 
time. The distance or time is of no interest in this test as long as the data provided is sufficient. The amount 
of data sufficient to make the estimate will be investigated.  
The purpose of this test is to estimate the mass by isolating the forward movement and thereby minimizing 
the effect of the inertial parameters. The data of interest during this test is the acceleration only.  
Therefore, the data processing is considered in the algorithm to make sure only the acceleration is evaluated 
in the algorithm since it is only the acceleration that depends on the mass. 
 
5.1.3 Turn test 
In this test the robot is set to rotate around its own axis. The result should be that the center of the robot 
moves in a circle. It was shown in chapter – 4 model simulation when applying opposite voltages on the two 
wheels the robot will rotate in a circle. The rotational acceleration can then be isolated in order to estimate 
the inertial parameters.

6 Parameter Estimation 
Estimating the parameters of the mobile robot will take basis in the dynamic model from (34)(35)(36)(37) 
and a set of input-output data gathered by the experiments explained in previous chapter.  
For the parameter estimation, the non-linear least squares algorithm is used. An implementation of the 
algorithm is made available in MATLAB using the System Identification Toolbox.  
The model and the data are treated as two systems. The data has an unknown structure and the model has 
unknown parameters. The two systems are compared, and the task of estimation algorithm is to find the best 
fit between them.   
 
6.1 Grey box model 
A system can be described as mathematical model in terms of two information sources.  
The first is the information from well-known relationships about the structural knowledge on the 
construction of the system or physical knowledge based on laws of physics. This is referred to as a white box 
model and has a deterministic structure.  
The second is the real information about the system itself gained from collecting data on experiments 
conducted on the system. This is referred to as a black bot and has a stochastic structure.     
Combining the two approaches a grey box model can be built. The grey box model is based on prior physical 
knowledge (white box) and data of the system (black box). 
Most mathematical model are grey box models therefore the definition of a grey box model is broad 
depending on the amount of prior knowledge versus collected data there is available. In other words, a grey 
box model can have different levels of greyness, the method is summarized in Figure 25.

The experiments of this project are divided in two groups: Experiments performed by means of set-up and 
simulation experiments.  
In this chapter, the estimation algorithm will be tested in a simulation using a set of emulated input-output 
data. Firstly, the test is to verify the model, so the noise is neglected, and the system is assumed to be

Hereafter the non-deterministic case will be investigated, which means that the data will be added noise to 
see if the estimation algorithm will be able to handle measurement noise before testing it on the robot 
measurements.  
The estimation algorithm is tested by simulating the output of a robot wheel velocities using the dynamics 
equation from (34)(35)(36)(37) and estimating against the dynamic model based on the same equations, 
but with a different initial guess on the parameters. The tests will be conducted for extremum cases of initial 
estimates to ensure that the model fit do not get stuck in a local minimum. 
In the experimental design, two tests were designed to be conducted to estimate the parameters of the 
robot. A forward test where the robot is set to drive forward by applying the same input voltage to the 
simulation 𝑣𝑟= 𝑣𝑙.  A turn test where the robot is set to rotate in a circular path by applying opposite voltages

11 Conclusion 
The motion of differential drive robots was examined, based on their geometrical properties, leading to a 
kinematic model. The model was extended to include the dynamics of the robot by considering the forces 
that act on the robot. The dynamic model was derived using well known first principles and applying them to 
a moving reference frame. The model was tested in a simulation and was verified using simple experiments 
in the simulation to test the motion.  
An online integrated estimation algorithm for estimating the load distribution parameters was proposed 
based on the dynamic state space model and the input-output model. The algorithm was tested using input-
output data collected from experiments conducted on two different robots, the SMR and Gopal. As part of 
the method, a data processing procedure was presented to enhance the data to be considered, making the 
estimation effective and faster. The effect of friction was measured and considered in the algorithm.   
As a result, the experiments carried out on the SMR provided a successful estimation of the mass but with a 
substantial error of up to 15%. This is mostly due to measurements errors and noise. It was also shown that 
the effect of friction on the SMR was significant, which also lead to uncertainty in the estimation.  
The experiments carried out on the Gopal robot provided a successful and very accurate estimation of the 
mass. Different loading conditions and different accelerations were considered, all resulting in a relatively 
small error, less than 4%.  
The overall effectiveness of the estimation is dependent on the accuracy of the recorded data.  As a result, 
the estimation of COG was not successful. The data extracted on the acceleration related to rotational 
movement was not sufficient to make a proper estimate. This was due to the fact that the Gopal robot is 
heavily restricted for rotational movement.  
On the other hand, the estimation of COG for the SMR resulted in relatively close estimations. But since it 
was not physically possible to conduct the test for a different COG the test is not conclusive.

eq1b=subs(eq1a,taul,tauml*N-wdl*jm*N*N)

eq2a=subs(eq2,taur,taumr*N-wdr*jm*N*N)

assume(r>0) 
assume(L>0) 
assume(I>0)
