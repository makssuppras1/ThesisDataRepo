Abstract  
This thesis aims at identifying the barriers present for companies when implementing electric vehicles 
(EVs) in their daily fleet of service as a replacement of the internal combustion engine (ICV). These 
barriers are both monetary as well as non-monetary values, causing a calculation of the total cost of 
ownership (TCO) for the ICV and EV to be a necessity in the project. Environmental-friendly 
commercial vehicles have not experienced a breakthrough in the commercial sector, despite being 
available on the market for several years. The total market share in the commercial sector in Denmark 
for these sustainable transportation solutions is slowly increasing each year but remains at just below 
1%.  
 
To identify the barriers of a companies transition from conventional vehicles to EVs, a Danish 
company, that replaced an ICV with an EV in their fleet for a test-period, provided data and 
information about their experience with the EV and its performance. The most severe barriers 
belonging to the transition of EVs from a company perspective were evaluated to be the limited 
driving range and investment cost, while the branding potential from owning a sustainable 
commercial vehicle was perceived as the most important advantage.  
 
The provided data, combined with information from other previous studies, would be fundamental 
for the calculation of the TCO for the EV. Three scenarios were then developed with the target of 
testing the importance of using data from a use case when calculating TCO.  
It was discovered that the most realistic option when calculating TCO was a combination of using 
previous literature and data from the Danish company, because the data from the test-period showed 
an insufficient number of driven kilometers. The results from the calculation of the TCO showed a 
general advantage when investing in ICVs compared to EVs across every scenario and regardless of 
a purchase or leasing deal. Because the present technology for the EV still is under development and 
the prices can change rapidly in the near future, a sensitivity analysis of the different parameters 
entering the model were executed. This analysis revealed a clear need in terms of either high amounts 
of subsidies or a reduction in the costliest parameters for the EV to break even with its conventional 
counterpart.  
 
Because a decision rarely is based purely on monetary values, the multi-criteria decision analysis 
(MCDA) technique SMART was adopted. The KPI’s/criterions entering the analysis were firstly 
==PAGE:5==
4 
 
found from previous similar studies, and afterwards discussed and weighted together with the 
spokesman of the Danish company who provided the data and test drove the EV for a period.  
The ICV and EV were then provided with a score on an assessment scale based on their performance 
on each of the criterions. A final normalized score of the two alternatives were then able to be 
calculated, based on the criteria weights and the given score for the alternatives on the assessment 
score. The final score for both alternatives were then calculated for three different scenarios that 
attempts to capture the importance of the economical factor and the point of view when deciding the 
weights of the criterions by introducing a governmental perspective. 
The MCDA revealed a clear advantage for the EV when economy was not included, but the highly 
weighted monetary values by the company gave the ICV a higher final normalized score when every 
criterion were included. The scenario that included criterion weights from a governmental point of 
view showed an advantage for the EV, because the TCO is not weighted as high for governments.  
 
Finally, a recommendation for the Danish company was conducted, with the conclusion that an 
investment in EVs is an inevitable option due to upcoming regulations. The transition, however, 
should be taken with cautious steps, because of the current barriers and rapidly evolving technology.

1 Introduction 
The CO2 emissions from the Transportation sector are the only major European economic sector 
where CO2 emissions have seen an increase since 1990 and it continues to increase, as demand for 
mobility grows (EEA2, 2019). Light commercial vehicles (LCVs) accounts for around 7% of these 
emissions, and the average emissions of newly registered LCVs in Europe saw an increase from 2017 
to 2018 (EEA4, 2019). The stress imposed by this on urban systems is expected to further increase, 
due to the fast growth of commercial vehicle activity. A possible technology-oriented solution is 
electric commercial vehicles (EV) that, along with improvements in vehicle routing efficiency, can 
lead to mitigating the effect of commercial vehicles in urban areas without radical activity reduction.  
The scope of this thesis is defined within the transportation sector, which includes a large variety of 
segments and transportation options. The focus within these transportation segments lies within 
Urban Freight Transport (UFT), which is a segment of freight transportation and used primarily to 
carry goods using commercial vehicles out of and within urban areas (Wang, et al., 2018). 
 
 Problem statement 
This project concerns the identification and development of a set of KPI’s based on company needs 
for urban mobility and the aim to promote more sustainable transportation solutions. This is done in 
collaboration with a Danish company that has agreed to test whether an EV solution can meet the 
requirements of the commercial sector. The company has a delivery service with a postcentral site 
and provide delivery services within the western part of Denmark, and as a part of the study the 
company has replaced one of their ICVs in their fleet to an EV. The project aims at developing a 
framework for determining the attractiveness of replacing ICVs with EVs from a company 
perspective, and thus result in a greener profile without extra costs. A multi-criteria based model is 
in this context developed to assess both the environmental as well as strategic impacts of the EVs 
performance compared to conventional vehicles undertaking the same type of tasks.  
 
1.1.1 Learning objectives.  
To answer the problem statement the following learning objectives needs to be fulfilled. 
1. Perform a literature study on the state of the art within assessment of EV solutions 
o Incl. Total Cost of Ownership (TCO) models and wider impact studies 
2. Define a set of KPIs to be used for the assessment of the test case 
3. Analyze data from the user case of EVs and compare it to its counterpart

4. Set up assessment framework using MCDA and TCO models and apply this to the case 
5. Discuss results and potential barriers that still need to be overcome with regard to the 
implementation of EVs 
6. Provide recommendations for the company and further work within the field 
 
 Reading guideline 
Chapter 1 introduces to the subject of this thesis and presents the problem statement, learning 
objectives and reader guideline. Chapter 2 goes through the background for the thesis, that describes 
how the sustainable development goals helps defining areas to alter and how Denmark are coping 
with sustainable development goals. The environmental state and freight transportation contribution 
in EU is then presented, followed by an overview of how freight transportation in Denmark is divided 
on types of vehicles. The primary goal of chapter 2 is to supply the reader with an overview of the 
current environmental state of EU and Denmark and highlight the potential and importance of 
sustainable alternative fueled LCVs.  
 
Chapter 3 introduces the use case made with a Danish company and DTU. The case is based on a 
single EV doing services in the western part of Denmark in the city of Kolding. The conventional 
vehicle chosen for comparison of the EV is then introduced, where the difference of the two vehicles 
is described. The company then provided data from test-period, that were firstly analyzed and 
evaluated, and later used to support the model parameters to calculate a TCO. Chapter 3 also includes 
an interview as a part of the case, where questions were asked to support the choice of criterions and 
belonging weights, that later were used to conduct an MCDA. 
 
Chapter 4 introduces the TCO for both vehicles, which is the first model used for evaluating the two 
alternatives. The TCO evaluates the economic aspects of the two alternatives from the parameters 
related to each alternative. The chapter includes a state-of-the-art section where earlier studies of the 
TCO is taking into consideration, before forming the TCO suitable for this project. Finally, a 
sensitivity analysis of the TCO model is realized, where potential break-even points between the TCO 
of the EV and ICV when adjusting model parameters is investigated.  
 
In Chapter 5, the Multi-criteria assessment model is introduced. The SMART-technique is used 
together with direct rating and value tree to determine the final weight of the criterions. The chapter

includes three scenarios where the two first is based on the weights given by the company, and the 
last is based on a government point of view. The two alternatives are in the sensitivity analysis tested

2 Background 
Before developing an in-depth analysis, it is important to understand the belonging challenges and 
current situation of the subject. Therefore, this section firstly introduces the overall UN goals towards 
securing a sustainable future, and Denmark’s contribution towards reaching these. The scope is then 
narrowed down towards the environmental dimension in EU, with focus on transportation and its 
contribution towards CO2- and other air pollutants. Because the following chapters in the report is 
themed around freight transportation and LCVs, a more in-depth explanation of the current situation 
of freight transportation in EU, with the upcoming and available alternative fueled LCVs is then 
presented. Ending the section is an overview of the penetration of these alternative fueled vehicles in 
Denmark, and what challenges yet is to overcome for a faster transition.   
 
 The UNs contribution towards reaching a sustainable future 
In late 2015, countries of the UN agreed to meet the 17 Sustainable Development Goals (SDG’s) of 
the 2030 agenda for sustainable development as presented on figure 1 (United Nations, 2020). They 
were later officially set into action on January 1st of 2016. The SDG’s where build on the success of 
the Millennium Development Goals (MDG’s) and has the overall objective to end all form of poverty, 
fight inequalities and tackle climate change, while ensuring that no one is left behind.  
 
Figure 1: The 17 Sustainable Development goals of the 2030 agenda (United Nations, 2020) 
The SDG’s are unique because the three dimensions of sustainability, economic, social, and 
environmental, all are integrated in the goals, recognizing that ending poverty and inequality, creating 
==PAGE:13==
12 
 
economic growth, and preserving the planet are inextricably linked together. Four years after signing 
the 2030 agenda for sustainable development, countries worldwide have taken action to integrate the 
SDG’s into their own national development plans to align policies. While the recent sustainable 
development report 2019 show that advances have been made in some areas, monumental challenges 
still remains to be seen, where actions against climate change is the most urgent. If the high amount 
of CO2 emissions is not controlled better in the future, the effects of pollution can be catastrophic, 
and in worst case, irreversible (United Nations, 2019).

 Environmental state in EU 
The recent years electrification of the road transportation has been on the proposed answers, to reduce 
the overall amount of CO2 emissions, where the transportation sector is a major contributor. In 2017, 
the transportation sector contributed with approximately 20-25% of CO2-emissions in Europe, see 
figure 3 (EEA1, 2019). The four sectoral aggregations which has seen an increase in CO2 emissions 
since 1990 are CO2 biomass, international aviation, international shipping and transportation. The 
percentage development of the different sectors can be seen int the right side of the figure 3.   
 
Figure 3: CO2 development in different sectors from 1990-2016 (EEA1, 2019) 
Transportation are the only of the 4 major European economic sector (Energy supply, Agriculture, 
Industry and Transportation (EEA2, 2019)) where CO2 emissions have seen an increase since 1990 
and it continues to increase, as demand for mobility grows. The decrease in CO2 emissions in the 
transportation sector from 2007-2012 were a result of the economic recession, where the demand for 
freight transportation decreased by 11% and passenger transportation saw a slight reduction as well. 
Ever since, the demand for both freight- and passenger transportation has been growing steadily each 
year.

Air pollutants can, at high amounts, have a negative influence at the environment, but are often

 Freight transportation in EU 
I general for Europe, freight transportation represents about 20-30% of vehicle kilometers and is 
contributing between 16% to 50% of the air pollution in cities. From these vehicle trips, only 12% is 
done by heavy good vehicles, while 88% is done by LCVs (Lebeau, et al., 2015). Especially in dense 
urban areas, the emissions have negative effects on the human health, due to the concentration of 
vehicles. This is one of the critical reasons, why many big cities want to reduce the emission from 
the vehicles driving on the streets of the cities.  
Since the transportation sector is one of the most polluting sectors, European concerns from different 
sides, have pushed the automotive industry in years and forced the industry to make their ICV more 
emission friendly and economic. However, it appears the technology of automotive industry for ICV 
may have reached the limits for further development, which can lead to a push forward for new ways 
of transportation, powered by alternative fuels generating less pollution (Casals, et al., 2016). 
Technologies like electric hybrids (PHEV and HEV) and the EVs, are among the most popular cars 
to compete on the new market, while fuel cell/hydrogen electric vehicles (FCEV) is a relatively new 
competitor. Despite these new alternatives having zero- or low local emissions, alternative fuel 
vehicles only represent 1.7% of new sales and 1.3% of the total market share for LCVs in 2018 
(ACEA, 2019). These sales- and market share numbers implies that the alternative fuel vehicles have 
==PAGE:21==
20 
 
some additional challenges for implementation, that is not present for the petrol or diesel vehicles. 
Therefore, a comprehensive overview of the alternative fuel vehicles and their characteristics, 
strengths and weaknesses are shown on table 3 (Wang, et al., 2018) (Conserve Energy Future, u.d.).  
 
Table 3: Alternative fuel type LCVs characteristics, strengths and weaknesses (Wang, et al., 2018) (Conserve Energy Future, u.d.) 
The three vehicle technologies each have different strengths and weaknesses, but with the similar 
strengths of zero- or low local emissions, that leads to potential branding of conserving the 
environment for companies. Furthermore, they all share the same weakness of reduced capacity due 
to the battery present in the vehicle, and a higher price than the ICV counterpart because the 
technologies still is being developed and is expensive.  
None of the 3 vehicle types are dominating one another by standing out in every category, making it 
difficult to conclude which of the vehicle type fits a company best, and the value of a trade-off 
between categories can be a subjective case.  
With the alternative fueled vehicles having zero- or low local emissions, they immediately improve 
the environment in a city, when they are replacing an ICV driving on diesel or petrol. The electricity 
used to power the vehicle and the additional production of a battery is however still polluting 
elsewhere, which is not shown by the local emissions. Several studies have therefore made an attempt 
on capturing the emissions from the individual vehicle’s contribution during their while lifetime, 
because a wider perspective regarding the emissions of the alternative fueled vehicles were necessary 
to assess whether they are as sustainable as often claimed. The broadest way of assessing these 
contributions is to perform a Life Cycle Assessment (LCA). In a report that reviewed 44 different 
==PAGE:22==
21 
 
LCA’s discovered that different articles were promoting the introduction of EV’s as a way of reducing 
CO2 emissions in the transportation sector, while others did not see it as a sustainable solution. The 
review also discovered big differences in the type of energy mixes, which have a larger effect on the 
overall emissions shown in the LCA than other productions (Marmiroli, et al., 2018). The general 
result from the reviewed LCA’s however, is the alternative fueled vehicles receiving a lower total 
amount of CO2 and other air pollutants emissions compared to their diesel- and petrol counterpart.

In Denmark, the most popular alternatives for freight transportation LCV’s are EV’s and different 
types of electric hybrids (Wang, et al., 2018). Since 2015, hybrids (PHEV and HEV) and EV’s have 
seen a small increase in the total percentage of the total fleet size of LCVs in Denmark, as shown on 
figure 9 and table 4 below.

increase is not to be seen. The number of electric LCVs for the Danish market shows a 0.3% of the

the environmental-friendly alternatives, together with fiscal systems and political demand for 
sustainable services as they do in Copenhagen. Similar tendencies are also present in many other 
countries in Scandinavia. In Norway, which is the front runner in the sustainable transition from ICVs 
to EVs, the same patterns, with only 0.7% of investments in battery electric LCVs, are realized

One of the main barriers in the devolvement of specifically EV’s, are the batteries due to its belonging 
uncertainties; The driving range when fully charged, the price and the environmental effects from

4 TCO 
This chapter covers all parts of the Total Cost of Ownership (TCO). The first part explains the concept 
of the TCO, followed by a state of art section, where earlier studies concerning the same subject are 
used to gain knowledge within the field. In the third part, a calculation of the TCO model is 
formulated and the different parameters and their values are decided. The results are evaluated in 
the last part of this chapter, where a sensitivity analysis is performed. 
 
 The concept  
The TCO of a given product, can be defined as an effort to identify all the relating costs of owning 
and operating a product over its expected lifespan (Dooley, 2016). The total cost of a product is rarely 
only based on the purchase cost, but also the ongoing process of maintaining that product throughout 
its lifespan. Compared to constraining the decision for purchasing a product to the purchase price 
only, a TCO represents the full picture of what investments is needed through the process of 
purchasing a specific product. Therefore, a TCO analysis is excellent at highlighting the difference 
between a purchase price and a long-term investment, which makes it a useful tool for decision 
makers in a numerous of different fields.  
In general, purchase- and operation cost is often dependent on the type of working field where the 
purchase takes place, as the size of the product and requirements for operating a given product is 
different. Managers of companies often found that the support for hardware and IT software would 
cost between 5 and 8 times the purchase price, which obviously makes TCO an important calculation 
to include in that field (PPC, n.d.). In the transportation sector, the divergence between the purchase- 
and operating cost is often based on what type of technology construct the vehicle is. Typically, an 
EV would have a higher purchase cost than conventional vehicles, but the EV performs better from 
an operating point of view (Economicshelp, n.d.). 
The TCO does however not account for any benefits or externalities, but is solely based on raw 
numbers from expenses, which is limiting its potential for decision making. Ideally, if the purchase 
of a product would make some sort of profit, the calculated TCO should be combined in a cost-benefit 
analysis (CBA), to capture every aspect of investing in the product. If a decision for purchasing a 
product only is based on TCO, it is a minimization of the costs, instead of a maximization of the 
benefits. If a product however does not hold any tangible benefits, TCO is a good method of simply 
==PAGE:30==
29 
 
minimizing costs. This would, however, still not account for any externalities, which again raises the 
question of rather TCO is a reliable stand-alone decision tool or not. 
Externalities occur as an offspring of producing or consuming a good, which causes an impact on 
third parties not directly related to the project (Economicshelp, n.d.). Externalities can be either 
positive or negative and can resolve in hidden costs or benefits. A common example of a hidden 
benefit caused by an externality, is companies investing in environmental-friendly alternatives. 
Environmental-friendly alternatives normally reduces pollution compared to other solutions, which 
increases the positivity towards the brand of the company by becoming more sustainable. Therefore, 
any hidden benefits or costs caused by the externalities of a product or service is important to include 
in decision making, as the effects can be quite significant.    
 State of the art 
Although EVs are known to perform better than conventional vehicles both from an environmental 
and economic point of view during operation, they also have the reputation to have a significant 
higher purchase cost (Casals, et al., 2016). This high purchase cost has often proven to be the reason 
for consumers and managers to choose another technology than electric engines, when purchasing a 
new vehicle (Lebeau, et al., 2015). This statement raises the question, of whether a calculation of 
TCO between the different vehicle technologies and classes, could influence the choice of these 
potential buyers.  
A TCO in general often consists of one-time costs and recurring costs. For ownership of vehicles, the 
one-time costs often represent the purchase price, the resale value, registration taxes and subsidies, 
while the recurring costs represents fuel cost, maintenance, insurance and taxes (van Velzen, et al., 
2019), as illustrated on figure 13 below. 
==PAGE:31==

Figure 13: Cost parameters included in a TCO 
Taxes appear on both one-time costs and recurring costs because of registration tax when purchasing 
a vehicle and some possible recurring taxes for owning a vehicle. These taxes often highly depend 
on the country of focus and can variate a lot. The purchase price can in some scenarios be entered as 
a recurring cost if the buyer chooses an instalment-payment instead of a paid at once alternative.     
Several studies have investigated how the TCO variates between vehicle technologies and classes. 
The methods and findings from these earlier studies regarding ownership of vehicles can be useful 
for this project, because an accurate calculation of TCO is important in order to make solid decisions. 
Since acknowledged researchers developed these previous studies of TCO, they will form a solid 
foundation for a method to calculate a more precise TCO.  
For selection and finding the relevant studies, it was decided not to include any published before 
2010, since this point in time was the expansion period for the EV industry (IEA, 2015). Furthermore, 
the studies should be written as scientific papers and focusing on calculation of TCO for electric 
powered engine vehicles as one of their alternatives. Even though this project is written with a focus 
on LCVs, this was not considered a requirement for the studies, since relatively few had this exact 
focus. Furthermore, the studies must give an attempt to clarify why their method of calculating a TCO 
of the vehicles included is a more accurate one compared to any previous studies with the same 
objective.  
A various amount of studies calculates TCO as part of their research, however, the procedure is often 
poorly explained, and backed up with few arguments supporting their decisions when choosing values

The key takeaway from these studies depends on the area of focus for a given project of development. 
For this project, the method of calculation is considered as one the important aspects, because the 
economic factor often highly influences companies’ decisions. The studies offer various alternative 
ways of calculating one-time- and recurring costs, depending on their assumptions. The recurring 
costs are discounted with either the present value factor or the reversed CRF, giving the exact same 
result. Therefore, the recurring costs for this project, independently of other assumptions, will be 
calculated and discounted with the present value factor, because it is the simpler method. The 
calculation of the one-time costs can variate depending on the underlying assumptions, since the 
method for a paid at once- and instalment deal is different. An instalment deal would require the use 
of the CRF, but this method brings a high level of complexity. One problem with assuming instalment 
deals, is the difference from dealership to dealership, where the interest can variate. Since adding this 
complexity and possible confusion would not be beneficial for the project, the one-time costs are 
assumed to be a paid at once and discounted by the present value factor. Note that this decision only 
effects the purchase cost (with the included registration tax) and the resale value.  
An alternative purchase option not considered by any of the studies, is a leasing deal of the vehicle. 
None of the studies mentioned have discussed the possibility of a leasing option, making an 
interpretation of their decision difficult. A reasoning for their decision of leaving the calculation of a 
leasing deal untouched, could be the missing factor of ownership when leasing a vehicle, or that 
leasing of alternatives fueled vehicles was not a possibility at that point in time. The relevance of

 Calculation 
The calculation of TCO for the two vehicle types, conventional- and the electric powered engine, will 
consider the alternatives of leasing and buying the vehicles. The reasoning for not excluding a 
purchasing option for the calculation is, as explained earlier, that 23% of company LCVs is 
purchased. Therefore, a TCO calculation for both alternatives seemed necessary, while a comparison 
of these 2 options is an interesting subject to discuss.  
Most studies do not consider a use case when calculating TCO, but purely relies on information given 
from dealerships or other studies. Therefore, to capture the difference from relying on information 
from dealerships and other studies and working with data from a user case, both TCOs for the 
alternatives of leasing and purchasing will be calculated with data from the user case provided by 
GLS, and by the information given by other studies and dealerships in different scenarios.  
The extraction of parameter values is described in the following section, followed up by a final model 
used for purchasing and leasing deal.

DKK the MRT is 0.46 DKK exclusive tax for each km driven (FDM, 2019). The costs of the MRT

or an EV. The results are presented as DKK/km instead of the total amount of money related to a 
case, because a companies value of owning a vehicle, is using its full potential. Theoretically, the 
TCO of a vehicle would have a lower cost if never used, compared to one doing services. Therefore, 
showing the actual total amount of DKK related to a case would cause an unnecessary bias towards 
scenarios with a higher annual mileage. As a result of this decision, it is important to note that the 
purchase option withholds 2 times the total mileage of the leasing case (due to the longer ownership 
period), when interpreting the results between the two ownership options. Therefore, the holding 
period is further investigated in the sensitivity analysis.

 Sensitivity analysis 
The results showed a general higher cost of the EV for both leasing and purchasing options, when 
compared to the ICV counterpart, but the technology related to an EV is still in its evolving phase 
and has not seen a stable period yet (BloombergNEF, 2020). Therefore, it would be interesting and 
relevant to test if the TCO of the two vehicle types would have a break-even point, when the values 
of the parameters for the EV used in the TCO model are changed. Instead of only testing cost 
parameters influence on the EVs TCO by changing it with a predefined percentage, it was decided to 
run multiple models, where all the different direct cost parameters for the EV and indirect cost 
parameters for both vehicles, enters the model as variables instead. The variable is entering the model 
as a percentage of the before given cost parameter value, going from 0-100%, which then becomes 
the run time of the model. The rest of the parameters included in the model is the same as in scenario 
3, where only 1 of these is reverted to a variable for each model-run. The starting point of 0% was 
chosen because any negative values from the cost parameters would not make sense, while the gap 
of 100% was chosen because any further increase of a cost parameter for the EV simply would 
increase the EVs TCO further above the ICVs. Note that the cost parameters in the model only 
changes for the EV, because the EV still is a new vehicle and the prices of these parameters are 
assumed to be much different in the future (BloombergNEF, 2020). When testing the influence of the 
indirect cost parameters, as years and annual mileage, it was decided to make the change for both 
vehicles, because they are assumed to be independent of type of technology.

5 MCDA 
This chapter contains the parts of the Multi-Criteria Decision Analysis (MCDA). The first part 
introduces the theory behind MCDA, secondly the SMART-technique is introduced and used to 
formulate a framework. After having formulated the framework three different scenarios is testing 
the performance of the alternatives according to the ranked criterions. A sensitivity analysis is the 
last part of this chapter, where two new alternatives are tested against the existing ones.   
 
 Introduction 
When decision makers make important decisions between alternatives, they should be supported with 
all aspects needed to decide with a wider perspective. The Multi-Criteria Decision Analysis (MCDA) 
methods offer the possibility to evaluate and rank several alternatives based on different evaluation 
criteria with varying scales or units (Kügemann & Polatidis, 2019). This makes it possible to evaluate 
the different alternatives that currently dominates the market of LCVs by including other 
measurement than just monetary measures, which often is the procedure today. The new criterions 
with non-monetary values will be weighted among each other because they are not of equal relevance. 
The purpose of a MCDA is not searching for some kind of hidden truth – but rather to assist the 
decision-maker in mastering the (often complex) data involved and advance towards a solution 
(Barfod, et al., 2016).

 SMART 
SMART is a technique used to evaluate a finite number of decision alternatives under a finite number 
of performance criteria. The purpose of the analysis performed by SMART is to rank the alternatives 
in a subjective order of preference to rate the overall performance of the alternatives due to a proper 
assignment of numerical grades (Lootsma, 1999). 
The SMART-technique is completed though a nine-stage process, which steps are shown below 
(Barfod & Leleur, 2019): 
● Stage 1: Identify decision maker(s) 
● Stage 2: Identify the issues 
● Stage 3: Identify alternatives 
● Stage 4: Identify the criteria 
● Stage 5: Assign values for each criterion 
● Stage 6: Determine the weight of each of the criteria 
● Stage 7: Calculate a weighted average of the values assigned to each alternative (Scale for 
assessment of alternatives) 
● Stage 8: Make a provisional decision  
● Stage 9: Perform sensitivity analysis 
An explanation of the nine steps and how they relate to this specific project is followed below. 
 
5.2.1 Stage 1: Identify decision makers 
The first stage in the SMART-technique is to identify the decision makers. In this case the decision 
makers are the one in charge of purchasing or leasing vehicles for a company, who has the goal of 
achieving a greener profile or preparing their business for the future. An example could be a fleet 
manager which needs to find a balance between many different parameters when investing in a 
product. 
  
5.2.2 Stage 2: Identify the issues 
The overall issue of the implementation of EVs in UFT, is the pace of the transition. The amount of 
EVs within freight transportation is increasing each year, but the overall percentage of EVs in 
company fleets is still very low because of the limitations belonging to the EV. These limitations and 
barriers that is slowing down the process of implementing EVs in freight transportation is further 
elaborated in stage 4.  
==PAGE:67==
66 
 
 
5.2.3 Stage 3: Identify the alternatives 
Due to the scope and a specific use case, only two types of vehicles have been chosen as alternatives. 
Fitting the case, the two alternatives are two Volkswagen Crafter, one with a diesel engine and with 
an electric engine powered by a battery. In the sensitivity analysis, an FCEV and PHEV are included 
as additional alternatives.  
 
5.2.4 Stage 4: Identify the criteria 
To identify the criterion’s which fitted the most important aspects when purchasing or leasing an 
LCV, the importance of gaining knowledge from other studies became convenient. The research area 
of the MCDA technique with main focus on implementing alternative fueled LCVs in freight 
transportation is, however, still a relatively new area, causing the material to be limited. 
(Barfod, et al., 2016) introduced an expert-based technique to investigate the most important factors. 
The method was called COPE-SMARTER and used the SWOT technique to identify challenges and 
opportunities for electric commercial vehicles. They identified a set of criteria’s and divided them 
into categories as, challenges, opportunities, and policy initiatives. The challenges consisted of 
financial, physical, operational, psychological, and technological challenges of the EVs.  
Another article aims to systematically classify and analyze the literature applying MCDA methods to 
evaluate road transportation fuels and vehicles. They chose 40 articles of relevance, evaluated them, 
and found 41 relevant evaluation criteria and listed them of occurrence from most used to least used. 
They discovered that CO2 emissions is the most common evaluation- and environmental criteria, 
while the final cost of fuel and vehicles where the most important economic criterions (Kügemann & 
Polatidis, 2019).  
A third study introduced technology as a fourth main criteria to the already existing three pillars of 
sustainability (environmental, social and economic) when they attempt to collect factors on the 
employment of EVs (Wang, et al., 2018). Adopting and integrating a fourth dimension to the pillars 
helps to divide the criterion’s up in smaller segments to easier weight them later.  
From these examples it was possible to collect knowledge on how to choose the right criteria’s aiming 
to fit the case and project. These previous studies had done a lot of work in collecting several 
criterions, which is the most used in studies and the criterions experts saw as opportunities, threats, 
weaknesses, and strengths. The criterions found to be of importance for companies committed to 
replace an ICV with an EV in their fleet can be seen on table 26 below,

The economical dimension includes all the monetary values related to the vehicles captured in the 
TCO. The TCO includes all cost from purchasing or leasing an LCV, maintenance, fuel consumption, 
insurance, resale value and taxes of the vehicles during the lifetime of ownership. The economy is 
excluded in the first scenario, because it is necessary to investigate how the two vehicles perform 
with equal costs. Since the EV has a higher cost than the ICV, it is necessary for the EV to achieve a 
higher score when the economy is not included, because a lower score without any economic criteria 
would make the EV an redundant alternative in the other scenarios. Later, in scenario 2 and 3, the 
economy dimension will be added to the model. 
The technological dimension is the new added pillar in the pillars of sustainability. This new 
technological dimension describes a group of factors, which concerns the difficulties of the transition 
from combustion to EVs in the context of LCV-UFT. Even though it is well known that EVs are more 
energy efficient than their counterparts, this still face some technological issues which limit their fast 
expansion. These limitations are driving range, capacity of vehicle and public charging possibilities. 
The public charging possibilities is assumed to be of relevance for vehicles and especially for EVs 
due to the relative short driving range and this issue is also mentioned the articles of (Barfod, et al., 
2016) and (Kügemann & Polatidis, 2019), which also argues the relevance for this to be evaluated. 
The charging infrastructure is also a limitation for many users since different connectors, sockets and 
payment system, all makes it less convenient to own an EV, which effects the desire to purchase an 
EV (Wang, et al., 2018).  
The second sub-criteria under the technological dimension is Driving range. It is one of the most 
severe challenges the EVs are facing and was considered the second most severe challenge in the 
expert panel of COPE-SMARTER (Barfod, et al., 2016).  
The third sub-criteria under the dimension of technological is capacity of the vehicle. The capacity of 
the vehicles is especially important for logistic companies like GLS, which is a delivering service and 
earns their money on delivering at a low price. Too big differences in the capacity leads to a bigger 
fleet of vehicles or lost revenue from fever deliveries.  
==PAGE:70==

The ability to meet future demands is a criterion with the attempt to assess the alternatives ability to 
cope with future demands from potential customers and regulations from the government. As stated 
in the background section, the municipality of Copenhagen is favorizing sustainable services, and 
maybe other municipalities will follow through. 
 
The social dimension captures some of the social aspects that are related to the concerns of public 
participation. Therefore, the project collects some of these social concerns related to the light 
commercial vehicle urban freight transportation (LCV-UFT). The first criteria are the public image, 
which capture the improvement of public image by using a given technology of a LCV. The second 
is the driver satisfaction which concerns the health and wellbeing of the drivers. The public image 
aspect is to discover the benefits from being a pioneer in the field of the EVs and a more sustainable 
choice of delivery. If the company have ambitions by trying to cope with the SDG’s and make a more 
sustainable future, it is assumed to be honored by the public and the company will profit from this, 
due to better sales (Wang, et al., 2018). This means if a company invests in better and greener 
alternatives, it will improve the public image, hence they contribute to a more sustainable future from 
the qualities of the greener alternative. Driver satisfaction is capturing the effects drivers experience 
of driving an EV. The comfort for the driver is a somehow important factor for the investors of new 
vehicles, because a higher comfort and healthier work environment lead to happier employees, which 
assumes to lead to a better workplace and a positive attitude from the drivers.  
 
The environmental dimension is one of the hottest topics, particularly in Denmark. It describes the 
factors that has been selected to concern the environmental state and quality of the LCV-UFT. In this 
section, the factors that are been assessed is GHG and air pollutants, and noise. 
 In (Kügemann & Polatidis, 2019), that found 41 criterions from 40 articles, GHG emissions had the 
highest occurrence and air pollutants where the fourth highest on occurrence. The GHG and air 
pollutants is only based on tailpipe/local emissions of the alternatives.  
Noise is a neglected criterion, the effects of the noise the drivers are exposed to affects the health and 
their wellbeing. Noise of the vehicles is, however, not only affecting the driver’s wellbeing, the noise 
also effects the health of the people living in dense areas which is exposed for high levels of noise 
especially in cities (Nicolopoulou-Stamati, et al., 2005).

5.2.5 Stage 5: Assign values for each criterion 
To support the analysis and get precise scores for the criterions, a decision maker interview with the 
Assistant Director Operations in GLS Denmark was conducted (see Appendix 1). The main purpose 
of this interview was to gain knowledge of the company and discover how the EV had performed 
during the test-period. Another purpose was to rank the importance of the different criteria’s chosen 
for evaluation. Direct rating was used to construct the value scale, with the scale of the ranks variating 
from 1 to 10, were the lowest value 1 is perceived as “not important” and highest value 10 is “very 
important”. He was firstly asked to rank the importance of the four main criterions, economic, 
technological, social and environmental, followed by a ranking of the sub-criterions within each main 
criterion. These scores where used in scenario 1 and 2, to determine which vehicles had the best 
performance. 
In scenario 3, which is the case from a governmental point of view, the intention was to make a 
ranking of the criterions of importance to the city, where sustainable criterions often are preferred 
over economic benefits. The scenario is aiming to investigate the sustainable goals for big cities and 
the rankings are based on the Copenhagen’s “solutions for sustainable cities”.  
 
5.2.6 Stage 6: Determine the weight of each of the criteria 
The weight of the criterions is conducted by a multi-level value tree. It was decided to use the value 
tree because the 4 first level criterions, economy, technology, social and environment, does not hold 
the same number of sub-criterions. The manager where firstly asked to rate the importance of the four 
dimensions of economical, technological, social and environmental on a scale of 1-10 due to 
importance. The sub-criterions were then ranked with the same scale compared to the other sub-
criterions within its own dimension, hence independently of the other sub-criterions from the other 3 
dimensions. 
In the value tree, each of the first level criterions have a set of sub-criterions. The criterions sharing 
the same parent (either upper- or bottom-level) within each family is being normalized to sum to 1. 
The cumulative weight of a criterion is the product of its relative weight in comparison with its 
siblings and the relative weight of its parent (Barfod & Leleur, 2019).  
 
5.2.7 Stage 7: Scale for assessment of alternatives 
Each of the alternatives are scored on a 5-point Likert scale from 1 to 5 for each criterion, where 1 is 
very bad and 5 is very good and the middle point 3 is defined as neutral. The scale of 1 to 5 where

GHG and air pollutants range of the scale, is based on how vehicles are performing today and decided

In the economy criterion, the range of the scale are based on the TCO for leasing, because 77% of all

The range of the scale driving range is based on the vehicles included in this project and other similar

The capacity of a vehicle is consisting of two elements, the payload (kg) and the available volume

the two vehicles were made on the basis that they should have roughly the same characteristics in

6 Discussion 
The first part of the discussion takes a wider perspective with regards to the two analyses in the 
project, the TCO and MCDA, followed by other potential barriers that slows down the 
implementation rate of EV’s in the Danish companies’ fleets.  
 
 TCO 
From the results of the TCO for leasing and purchasing of vehicles, it is not surprisingly the ICV, 
which is the most economically feasible, even though the EV has less costs of operation in the period 
of ownership. The three scenarios all show the same picture, where the ICV is most cost competitive 
of the two alternatives. In the purchase scenario, the purchase cost of the EV is the major reason, for 
the EV not to get more or equally economical as the ICV, even though the distance were 15,000 km 
per year for a period of 8.6 years. In the Leasing scenario it is the same picture, where the high 
monthly payment of the EV makes it difficult to breakeven with ICV in the short period of time the 
leasing agreement lasts. 
The results are as expected, the EV is not economical competitive to the ICV if the decision of 
ownership only is based on the findings from the TCO. The choice of alternatives was based on the 
case because reliable data from the EV could help to build a precise model, but the results from the 
report may look even worse for the EV, if different alternatives in the same car class were introduced. 
The Crafter-series is considered a premium product in the LCV class and there are cheaper Crafter-
alternatives available on the market. The included ICV for this project was chosen because it had 
similar characteristics for an EV (HP, capacity, trailer loads), but a company could choose a cheaper 
option, and almost half the monthly payment if so. The electric crafter only had one alternative and 
is tied to the version chosen in the project. The presumed outcome of the introduction of a cheaper 
ICV with worsen characteristics may have made the difference between the EV and ICV even larger. 
The depreciation rate of the vehicles, which influences the TCO, was set to 25% every year for the 
EV and 18% for the ICV. From the sensitivity analysis it was discovered that a depreciation rate of 
17.5% or lower for the EV would make it as economically competitive as ICV. The market of EVs is 
still relatively new and there is not a lot of data on the resale of EV’s, especially not on EV’s in the 
LCV class. This is primarily caused by the technology still being under development and there is still 
not that many vehicles on the market. As the technology evolves and EV’s are getting more 
competitive and cheaper, the depreciation rate is also expected to decrease. Another uncertainty that 
may affect the resale of EV’s, is the battery. GEOTAB have made analysis of more than 6,000 EV’s 
==PAGE:87==
86 
 
to investigate the degradation of the batteries. They have discovered that the average battery declines 
2.3% every year, which is 27 km lost after five years. Another finding is that most batteries may 
outlast the usable life of the vehicle (GEOTAB, 2020). This would maybe justify that the depreciation 
rate could be a bit lower than the 25% per year, which is used in other papers, and in this project as 
well. This would lead to a better TCO for the EV, but it is doubtable that the depreciation rate gets 
below the 17.5% as long as the ICV still is the most preferred transportation mode. 
 
 MCDA 
The first result of the MCDA shows that the EV has a competitive advantage, when the model only 
includes non-monetary criteria. This would only be a legit result, if the two vehicles purchase cost 
were the same. Most of the criteria values are based on measurable values, like GHG and air 
pollutants, driving range, capacity in vehicle, public charging possibilities and Noise, while the last 
three parameters public image, driver satisfaction and ability to meet future demands are difficult to 
measure. This means that performances of the vehicles are based on the interview with the company, 
and the assumption made on general information and common knowledge. Another approach on how 
to capture the effects of the ability to meet future demands and public image than the objective 
opinions would be a project in itself to quantify more precise values. The final score of the alternatives 
are therefore based on qualitative measures, that results in some uncertainty entering the model.  
The criterion ranges on the assessment scale also causes some uncertainty in the model, because they 
are defined based on an attempt to create a fair and objective scale. These ranges are heavily 
influencing the final score of the alternatives and could have been different in another research paper 
using the same method as in this thesis. Therefore, it is important to agree that the defined ranges are 
acceptable, before any interpretation of the final scores. 
The subjective weights of the criterions based on the interview with GLS can variate depending on 
the type of company, and is therefore, similar to the other defined weights, causing an unknown 
amount of uncertainty entering the model. This uncertainty is however captured in the analysis testing 
for potential break-even points between the EV and the ICV, depending on the company weights, and 
to what extend they needed to change their believes for EV to be the more attractive alternative.  
 
 Charging Infrastructure and energy supply – transition to EVs  
In the Interview with the assistant director of operations from GLS, he mentioned problems with the 
charging infrastructure, that worried him. As he said: “We want more electric cars than just this one 
==PAGE:88==
87 
 
and we are also in dialogue with various suppliers who can supply these electric vehicles. We just 
have this problem, that we cannot get enough power at these depots. This is probably a strategic 
challenge and a slightly longer-term issue that we will face, and it is not only us who are everyone 
else who would like a greener fleet” (see Appendix 1). What if a company wants to invest in the new 
technology but the infrastructure or energy supplier is not capable of deliver the right amount of 
energy needed? And if it is possible to upgrade the energy network, who will pay for it, is it the 
company who needs the energy or is it the government who asks for a greener future? If it is the 
company who must pay the price, it raises the cost of investing in EVs even further. This can end up 
in several scenarios; the first possible outcome could be that the company would not or could not 
afford to invest in EVs due to the extra expenses. Another outcome could be that the costumers would 
have to pay for the extra expense in form of higher delivery cost of packages.  
The assistant director of operations is not the only one who are worried about this situation. Danish 
Energy, which is a non-commercial lobby organization for Danish energy companies, made an 
analysis in May 2019, of what needed to be done to reach a capacity of the energy network to be able 
to handle the great amount of EVs of the future. The main conclusions of the analysis were: 1) The 
existing electricity grid cannot handle the task. 2) Investment in the billion DKK class is needed if 
we are to ensure that the electricity grid capacity is ahead of the number of electric cars. If it is 
politically decided to pursue “The Smart Road”, where we both reward and make it easy for Danes 
to charge the EVs at times when there is no pressure on the electricity grid, it can reduce the 
investments needed greatly. This keeps the price per kilowatt-hour delivered at rest. 3) If it is 
politically maintaining the electricity companies' existing investment framework, it will not reach the 
goal. 4) Approx. 250 charging stations every single day until 2030 is needed to be implemented, so 
there are enough charging stations in the cities and on the residential roads. Political initiatives are 
intended to secure investment in charging stations (Dansk Energi, 2019).  
These four main conclusions from Danish energy all states that there is a need of political action to 
change the investments in this sector, if the desire is to reduce the amount of diesel and petrol fuel 
vehicles in favor for EV’s. If the goal of all vehicles purchased after 2030 is EV’s, there is a lot of 
work to be done, before the infrastructure is able to charge all EV’s in 2030. One of the 
recommendations from Danish Energy is that employees with an EV, or charging hybrid car, as a 
company car must be able to get an employer-paid remote-controlled charging stand installed at 
home, without taxing the value of the charging stand. This could be a solution for the company to the 
==PAGE:89==
88 
 
problem with too many vehicles charging at the same place, because the employees are not dependent 
on having to park at the company to charge the vehicle. 
Another article response to the Danish Energy’s recommendations to invest in the Danish grid. The 
article claiming that the Danish grid can withstand the pressure of 1 million EV’s (Thingvad, 2019). 
The article gives Danish Energy right in that fact that if every EV is charging in the same time it will 
give problems in the weak ends of the grid. But data from “Nissan many cars”, indicates that not 
every EV needs a recharge every day. The data from DTU’s “transportvaneundersøgelse” concludes 
that in average the Danish passenger vehicle drives in average 45 km per day. This also confirms that 
every car does not have to charge every day and may bring down the voltage on the grid. The article 
also states, that if the charging of EVs is spread over a whole day, the grid will not have the before 
mentioned problems. The article also states that the user in the end of the grid will have problems if 
changes is not to be seen (Thingvad, 2019).  
 
 Battery technology 
One of the main concerns of investing in EVs is the driving range of the vehicle. It was also one of 
the main concerns from the assistant director of operations. The driving range is an important factor 
for a logistic company, since they often are depending on the vehicles ability to deliver the packages 
to the lowest price possible, where a factor as driver salaries needs to be kept down, to minimize the 
company costs. So, if the driving range of the vehicle is insufficient and cannot deliver the packages 
of the day without a charging break, it will not be able to help companies minimize their costs. As 
long as the driving range of the EV is not for filling the needs of the delivery services, it will not be 
able to replace the ICV. The assistant director of operation stated that they would have to replace the 
ICVs with EVs by a factor of 1.4 to obtain the same delivery standards as today (see Appendix 1). 
So, while the driving range is still a main concern, EVs cannot replace the ICV 100% by now.  
 
The technology of EVs evolves rapidly these years and several of the big established car manufactures 
has joined the production of EVs, which add on to the developments pace. Some of the main players 
in the field of becoming the greatest EV manufactures also invest high amounts of money in the 
development of batteries. One of the latest investments in battery technology is the Volkswagen 
group, who invested 1.3 billion DKK in a start-up company, that develops solid state battery 
technology, which is expected to be the replacement of the lithium-ion batteries (Volkswagen, 2020). 
The solid-state batteries are expected to have a higher energy density, which is approximately 2.5 
==PAGE:90==
89 
 
times larger than the current lithium-ion batteries. The battery improvements of the EVs will cause 
the EVs to be close at having the same driving range as the ICVs. With these new batteries, the EVs 
are expected to raise the market shares and be a breakthrough for passenger EVs. This development 
in the technology may also affect the market of LCV’s even though these to markets cannot be 
compared.  
 
The new batteries are still in the development phase and not ready to be mass scaled yet (Godske, 
2020). As the companies compete to be the first to minimize the volume and weight, and at the same 
time maximize the energy capacity of the batteries, the prices on the most expensive part of the 
vehicle is getting cheaper every year. As stated in the TCO section, the battery is close to one 100 
dollars per kWh, and when the prices on EV’s batteries get below 100 dollars per kWh, the price is 
seen as the breakeven with ICV counterpart according to BloombergNEF (BloombergNEF, 2020). 
So, when the battery prices get below this point it is expected that the EVs starts to be equal or more 
competitive than the ICV, because this change both effects the purchase price, resale value and battery 
replacement price. 
 
 Company and consumer WTP 
How should the company achieve a sustainable profile by investing in EVs while being a financial 
business, if the EVs will not become cheaper or economically equal to the ICV within a shorter 
number of years? What measures can be taken by the company to achieve these goals of providing a 
greener alternative than today? 
Based on the idea that more customers are willing to use the company’s services if the company 
invests in EVs is uncertain, together with the possible effect it will have on the company. It is a 
difficult topic to answer as it is difficult to know if customers are willing to accept this offer or not. 
It is difficult to interpret whether it will generate more sales as they now offer something the other 
dealers do not have for the same price. In recent years, Denmark has had climate and sustainability 
on the agenda and large sections of the population say that they will act more sustainably 
(Verdensmål, 2020).  
 
An option is that the company can set the price of all their services to cover the cost of purchasing 
the EVs. How much is customers willing to pay extra, if they know that the company provides a more 
sustainable service than other companies with the same delivery? This procedure of making all their 
==PAGE:91==
90 
 
services more expensive to pay for a more sustainable services, is probably the right thing to do in 
the future, but may not be the right solution right now. Some potential customers may choose other 
alternative services because they are cheaper, and the company could therefore lose part of their 
costumer market share. 
A second option is that the company offers a service where customers can choose to have their 
packages delivered with the use of an EV as a sustainable alternative at a higher delivery cost, but are 
customers willing to pay for a more sustainable service? One product that has already made its way 
into Denmark is organic products, the Danes are acting more and more ecologically and thinking 
more about sustainability than before (Verdensmål, 2020). It is likely that several costumers are 
willing to pay more for a sustainable service, as seen with organic products. However, it may have 
some logistical problems that companies offer two different delivery options that cannot be delivered 
with the same vehicle. These logistical issues can lead to longer waiting times for the costumer, since 
the transportation of packages needs to be economical for the company. This type of service may 
only be available in cities because of the short driving ranges of the EVs and the logistical problems.

7 Recommendation and conclusion 
The recommendation and conclusion are divided in three sections. The first part is a recommendation 
for investment in vehicles, the second part is recommendation for further work and research, and the 
last part is a conclusion for the project.  
 
 Recommendation for investment in EVs  
It is highly recommended for companies to have a test-period before a potential investment in an EV. 
The test period of the vehicle should be of a similar or longer time-period as GLS’s, to investigate 
how it fits the companies needs and if it can replace the ICV without raising too many issues. 
 
Companies which operate in bigger cities and have daily trips will possibly be affected of regulations 
made by governments or the cities in where the companies operate. Copenhagen has already 
introduced a special regulation, where companies with EVs in their daily fleet of service are preferred 
over other companies without EVs to bid on projects or services for the city (Københavns Kommune, 
2019). It is without a doubt not the last regulation the companies who operates in cities are facing, 
since Copenhagen has the overall goal of becoming a sustainably city and fossil free. Furthermore, 
the recommendation for companies which mainly operates in the suburbs and rural areas are not to 
invest in EV’s because the limited driving range of the EV, at this point in time, cannot fulfill their 
needs. 
 
If the company focusses only on the economic aspects of the owning a vehicle, the EV would not be 
the choice of vehicle because of the higher TCO, independently of ownership option. The EV would 
only be able to be as attractive as its counterpart, if the EV were subsidized with governmental means. 
This type of evaluation, which only focuses on economic aspects, is however not recommended. From 
the MCDA section it was discovered how the different criterions added knowledge of the two 
alternatives into the decision. The results from the MCDA, with the companies point of view, the 
ICV still has a small overtake with a 51% of the normalized score, to the EVs 49% normalized score. 
This means that the ICV still performed better, based on the weights by the company, even though 
the two vehicles were close. The sensitivity analysis of the alternatives showed that no counterpart 
available on the market can overcome the ICV. If the weights of the criterions are based on the 
government point of view, the EV is the most attractive choice compared to the company point of 
view. Hereby, if a company is willing to change their priority or the weights of the criterions, 
==PAGE:93==
92 
 
investing in EVs could be the right decision. Therefore, it is recommended that companies with an 
economic opportunity to change their weights, compared to GLS’s, should consider leasing an EV. 
The period of the leasing agreement of four years is preferred over the possible purchase of an EV, 
because the technology of EV’s still is under development. With the leasing agreement, the company 
is less likely to miss the opportunity of being stock with a car, with little to no value after the 
ownership period ends. Another reason for investing in EVs is the opportunity to be a first mover in 
the market and gain new shares from the sustainable services and gain knowledge of how to 
implement EVs in their fleet, without too many upcoming problems. Furthermore, it is recommended 
that the transition from ICVs to EVs should be taken with cautious slow steps, so the company is 
more flexible if changes happen. 
 
 Recommendation to further work  
It is recommended that the test-period of an EV is longer than the one used in this thesis. The 
information for the longer period should be used to test how weather and better driver knowledge of 
the vehicle influences the consumption of the EV. From these data, it would be possible to determine 
how air conditioning and weather conditions as cold, heat and wind affect driving range and 
performance. 
At the same time, it is recommended that the vehicle should be used on longer daily routes than the 
average 32 km per day and the max of 50 km, which could also further clarify the driving range and 
test the performance of the EV. 
Another recommendation is that data from companies ICVs should be collected and analyzed. To 
build a more accurate comparison of the TCO, it is interesting to know what their actual consumption 
is for an ICV. Furthermore, an interesting investigation is whether the daily routes in the larger cities 
is shorter compared to deliveries in more sparsely populated areas, which can help answer if the EVs 
are an alternative that is ready to solve the tasks for the business in cities. The data from the ICVs 
could also be used to determine how many vehicles the company realistically could replace with EVs, 
when considering their limited driving range.  
 
 Conclusion 
The TCO showed that the EV still needs to be cheaper or more efficient to compete with the ICV to 
be the attractive alternative. The framework for determine the attractiveness of EV’s discovered eight 
different KPI’s/criterions which all should determine the performance of the alternatives for a greener 
==PAGE:94==
93 
 
company profile and still being able to fulfill the same tasks. The first scenario, without the economic 
aspects to owing a vehicle, gained the EV a highest performance score. In the second scenario the 
TCO is applied as a ninth criterion and the results of this scenario shows that the ICV performs 
slightly better than the EV. The last and third scenario, with weights of the criterions from a 
government perspective, shows the same results as the first scenario, with a higher score for EV. The 
sensitivity analysis showed that the FCEV scored highest in the first scenario, while the results were 
unchanged in scenario 2 and 3, with ICV scoring highest in scenario 2 and EV as the dominant vehicle 
in scenario 3.

Economic 10 
Purchase/ rental cost? 10 
Alt hvad der har med økonomi at gøre er meget vigtigt. Så det skal være en tier, for det har stor 
betydning for vognmændene, det er jo dem som skal leje eller lease de her elbiler. Hvis den er dobbelt 
så dyr som en alm. Varevogn, så har det en stor betydning for deres økonomi og omsætning. Så det 
er helt klart noget af det mest vigtige for vognmændene. 
Operating expenses? 7  
Igen, når det har noget med økonomi at gøre så er det meget vigtigt, selvfølgelig er det rigtigt at en 
elbil er billigere at vedligeholde i forhold til en dieselbil, det er noget man skal have med ind i 
beregningen, når man gerne vil investere i en elbil. Selvfølgelig koster den noget mere når man køber 
den, men vedligehold det er noget billigere. Hvis man kunne have den længere, ville det nok være 
mere økonomisk.  
Det er ikke lige så vigtigt som rental cost men lige efter. 
Tax expenses? 6 
Det er forsat noget der påvirker økonomien så det er også vigtigt. Hvis en vognmand har ti biler og 
skal betale grøn ejer afgift er det jo 120.000-130.000 om året i forhold til en elbil som er nogle tusinde 
kroner. Så det er jo noget af en forskel.
