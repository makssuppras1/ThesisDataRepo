Abstract
The development of eﬃcient damage detection techniques is a ﬁeld of great interest
that attracts growing research attention over the last decades. The present project
focuses on the application of damage assessment in numerical models and evaluates
the inﬂuence of damage presence in structural systems. To achieve that, the Total
Modal Energy (TME) method is selected to identify damage locations based on
changes in dynamic characteristics. The primary advantage of TME algorithm relies
on the involvement of several modal parameters, such as the natural frequencies and
the mode shapes. The numerical models investigated are a simple 3D steel frame
and a 2D oﬀshore wind turbine, both developed in MATLAB.
Throughout this study, the performance of the proposed method is evaluated in
depth, considering diﬀerent damage scenarios and various parameters.
The ob-
tained results are presented and extensively discussed, while several limitations are
addressed. Overall, TME method shows high robustness in the vast majority of the
investigated cases, where both noise-free and noise-polluted conditions are examined.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Motivation
Structural Health Monitoring (SHM) is an area of growing interest, as it is rapidly
expanding throughout the ﬁelds of civil, mechanical, and aerospace engineering.
Detecting damages at the earliest possible stage is of great importance, therefore, it
gradually attracted the researcher’s attention over the recent decades. As a result,
numerous Structural Damage Detection (SDD) algorithms have been developed to
ensure safety and reliability, while reducing maintenance costs [e.g., 1, 2, 3].
A classiﬁcation system (Rytter, 1993) for the SDD methods deﬁnes four levels of
damage identiﬁcation, as follows [4]:
• Level 1: Qualitative indication of the damage presence in the structure
• Level 2: Information of the probable geometric location of the damage
• Level 3: Quantiﬁcation of damage severity
• Level 4: Information about the actual safety given a certain damage state
The present work focuses on Level 2 damage identiﬁcation, where the presence of a
potential damage along with its location are investigated by a proposed algorithm.
Among several SDD categories, Vibration-Based Damage Detection (VBDD) may
be the most emerging and promising. The basic idea behind VBDD methods con-
cerns changes in the dynamic characteristics of a structural system, such as natural
frequencies and mode shapes. These dynamic properties can be obtained by taking
measurements from speciﬁc locations of a structure, using sensors. Subsequently,
several techniques, such as Operational Modal Analysis (OMA), can be utilized
to transform these measurements into dynamic characteristics. Finally, changes in
these dynamic characteristics may reﬂect potential damages on the structural sys-
tem, therefore, they can be utilized for damage detection, localization and quantiﬁ-
cation [e.g., 5, 6, 7]. A general VBDD procedure can be visualized in the ﬂowchart
of Fig. 1.1 below.

Especially for oﬀshore structures, the probability of developing damages during their
service life is relatively high, since they are subjected to extreme loads such as strong
winds or large waves, as well as they are prone to fatigue and corrosion. On the top
of that, they are structures of high importance, mainly due to economical reasons,
as they produce a high percentage of the global green energy, while they are great
sources of oil and gas gathering. Additionally, their location make them not easily
accessible, thus visual inspection is an ineﬀective and costly procedure. Considering
the above, SHM and SDD are deﬁnitely essential for oﬀshore structures and their
application is widely spread in the civil engineering industry [e.g., 8, 9].
1.2
Objectives
The main objective of the present MSc project is the application of a VBDD method,
that makes use of the aforementioned dynamic changes to identify potential damages.
Since this work is a numerical study, the implementation of the selected VBDD
method focuses on Finite Element (FE) models. As a result, the desired dynamic

characteristics of the investigated structures are obtained from FE analysis and not
from real measurements.
As Fig 1.1 indicates, the damage assessment is achieved by comparing two stages of
a structure, an initial undamaged stage along with a current potentially damaged
stage.
Consequently, the scope of this study focuses on extracting the dynamic
properties in the two stages using numerical models, and subsequently apply VBDD
by utilizing potential changes between them.
1.3
Thesis structure
The present report is structured following the workﬂow of the MSc project through-
out the spring semester of 2021. Initially, an extensive investigation of the existing
literature is carried out in order to be familiarized with the fundamental theory and
evaluate the performance of the diﬀerent VBDD methods.
After this investigation, a VBDD method is selected for application on numerical
models. To achieve that, two FE models are developed, one simple steel frame and
one more complex model of an oﬀshore wind turbine. Subsequently, the selected
damage assessment method is applied and its performance is extensively evaluated.
Finally, the overall conclusions of the method are discussed, while several recom-
mendations for further investigation are being proposed.
Following the described workﬂow, the present document is structured accordingly.
Chapter 2 presents a literature review based on numerous widely spread VBDD
methods, while in Chapter 3, the theoretical background associated with a selected
group of VBDD algorithms is described in detail.
In Chapter 4, the numerical
models developed for the application of damage assessment are presented. More-
over, Chapter 5 and Chapter 6 include the most critical damage detection results,
where the damage inﬂuence on several parameters is investigated. Furthermore, in
Chapter 7, the results obtained by the selected method are extensively discussed for
both models. Finally, the overall conclusions extracted through out this study along
with further work recommendations are presented in Chapters 8 and 9, respectively.
An extensive quantity of damage assessment results, containing all the cases and
parameters investigated in this project are included in Appendix A and B. In Ap-
pendix C the developed MATLAB scripts for the numerical models and the damage
assessment algorithm are presented.

Chapter 2
Literature review
This chapter is dedicated to the research work and the existing literature that has
been developed on SDD over the recent decades.
At ﬁrst, a general description
of SDD principles is presented, while the research subsequently focuses on VBDD.
Among VBDD methods, Modal Strain Energy methods show high robustness and
gradually attracted a lot of interest.
As a result, the present study turned into
this direction, therefore, the vast majority of the investigated literature concerns
MSE-based methods.
Initially, SDD was limited in visual inspection, however, the large size of civil struc-
tures, in combination with the fact that several structural members are not easily
accessible, made this procedures ineﬃcient and time-consuming. Over the recent
years, the ﬁelds of SHM and SDD showed tremendous developments and a sig-
niﬁcantly large number of techniques have been developed to detect, localize and
quantify structural damages [e.g., 1, 2, 3].
SDD algorithms can be classiﬁed into local and global damage detection methods.
Local SDD methods in principal focus on detecting damages in small structural sys-
tems, without taking into account the dynamic response of the system. A relatively
short extend of literature can be found in local SDD methods, where tools as Acous-
tic Emissions (AE), Laser Testing Methods (LTM), Radiographic Testing (RT) and
Ultrasonic Testing (UT) are utilized for local evaluation of structural members [1].
On the contrary, global SDD methods, where VBDD is included, focus on the vibra-
tion response of a monitored structure to evaluate the overall structural health. As
already described, real-time measurements are taken from strategic locations of the
structure, as they can be subsequently transformed into the desired dynamic char-
acteristics. Therefore, VBDD methods utilise changes in these dynamic properties
between two stages of a structure, and based on that, can detect potential damages
and make decisions on the structural health.

2.1
Vibration-Based Damage Detection methods
The research on VBDD initiated in the late 1970s, particularly in the aerospace
and oﬀshore oil industries. Due to its high eﬃciency and robust performance along
with the necessity of monitoring civil structures, VBDD rapidly expanded in civil
engineering industries. As a result, several investigations have been conducted and
numerous VBDD algorithms have been proposed over the recent decades [e.g., 1, 2,
3].
Yin et al. proposed a two-phase methodology, where the Natural Excitation Tech-
nique (NExT) along with the Eigensystem Realization Algorithm (ERA) are utilized
to obtain the dynamic characteristics of a transmission tower. In the second phase of
this investigation, the transmission tower model is divided into sub-structures, where
the damage locations are introduced by causing stiﬀness loss in speciﬁc elements.
The damage assessment is achieved by the proposed dynamic reduction method,
where the damage locations are detected and the damage severity is quantiﬁed [5].
In another study, a damage detection scheme utilizing Hankel matrices is proposed
to monitor potential damages in structural systems. In this case, a residual is intro-
duced based on the diﬀerence between the Hankel matrix of a reference (undamaged)
state, and the corresponding Hankel matrix of a potentially damaged structure. The
proposed damage detection approach is evaluated in both numerical simulations and
experimental data from a full scale bridge [6].
2.2
Modal Strain Energy-based methods
Furthermore, a great deal of research has been conducted in the ﬁeld of VBDD using
Modal Strain Energy (MSE) based methods. Thanks to its adaptability and eﬀec-
tiveness, MSE is among the most successful damage indicators, therefore, various
MSE-based methods has been published over the years. Modal strain energy, deﬁned
as the product of stiﬀness matrix and the second power of mode shape component,
may be one of the most promising modal parameters to identify structural dam-
ages, since it incorporates the physical properties along with the system vibration
behaviour [7].
Stubbs et al. are the pioneers to introduce the concept of modal strain energy for
damage assessment and localization. In their extensive research, they introduced
a damage index where the mode shapes before and after the damage occurrence
are required [8]. Subsequently, Kim and Stubbs demonstrated the eﬀectiveness of
Stubbs’ index by implementing the proposed damage detection algorithm in a nu-
merical model of an oﬀshore jacket structure, obtaining extremely promising results
[9].
Another widely spread MSE-based algorithm is the Modal Strain Energy Change
(MSEC) method, which utilizes potential changes in the modal strain energy of each
==PAGE:17==
Chapter 2. Literature review
6
element. Shi et al. used this MSE change before and after the damage occurrence
to introduce a damage detection index, called Modal Strain Energy Change Ratio
(MSECR). The robustness of this method is based on the fact that the damage index
includes element parameters, thus this makes the method very eﬃcient for damage
localization. However, a signiﬁcant disadvantage of MSECR is its poor performance
in damage detection under noisy or incomplete mode shapes. As a result, MSECR
method is usually limited to a theoretical basis for other damage detection methods,
instead of being used as an individual method by itself [10].
Hu et al. proposed a development of the previous MSE-based algorithms by intro-
ducing the Cross-Modal Strain Energy (CMSE) method for severity estimation. The
modal information required is limited to the ﬁrst few modes and, more importantly,
this modal data do not needed to be paired (i.e. obtained from the same mode)
between the undamaged and damaged structure [11]. Shortly after, Asgarian et al.
combined MSECR and CMSE to apply damage assessment in an oﬀshore platform,
by detecting the damage locations using MSECR and subsequently evaluate damage
severity with CMSE [12].
To implement the MSE methods described above, the mode shapes are required to
be known. Due to noise presence in measurements in combination with the fact
that it is impossible to monitor every singe point of a structure, the obtained mode
shapes involve uncertainties. Nevertheless, natural frequencies can be measured sig-
niﬁcantly easier with better accuracy. As a result, Wang developed the iterative
modal strain energy (IMSE) method to quantify damage severity, using only low or-
der frequency measurements [13]. After further developments, Wang et al. utilized
IMSE to identify both damage locations and severity. Furthermore, they demon-
strated the robustness of IMSE by applying the method in a 3D oﬀshore structure
when limited, spatially incomplete modal information is available [14].
Besides MSE, Modal Kinetic Energy (MKE) can also be involved in VBDD show-
ing high eﬀectiveness. A key advantage of MKE is its association with the modal
frequencies and the mass matrix of the system, in contrast to the other MSE-based
methods where only the modes shapes and the stiﬀness matrix are involved.
Li et al. proposed a new damage detection algorithm called Total Modal Energy
(TME) method, to identify damage locations by utilizing both MSE and MKE. In
this study, the performance of TME method is evaluated in comparison with three
traditional MSE indices. To achieve that, both numerical and experimental inves-
tigation is performed on a tripod-type oﬀshore wind turbine, where TME method
showed higher robustness, especially in multi-damage scenarios and noise-polluted
conditions [15].

3.1
Equations of motion
The motion of a linear, MDOF system with N number of Degrees of Freedom
(DOFs), is described by a set of N linear equations. These equations are formed
by a balance between the external dynamic load vector p and the internal forces
caused by the stiﬀness, mass, and damping of the structural system. As a result,
the equations of motion for an MDOF system are expressed as
M¨u(t) + C ˙u(t) + Ku(t) = p(t)
(3.1)
where M, C and K are the mass, damping and stiﬀness matrices, while ¨u, ˙u and
u represent the accelerations, velocities and displacements of the structural system,
respectively.

Chapter 5
Damage detection - Steel frame
In the present chapter, TME method is applied for the damage assessment of the 3D
steel frame. An extensive investigation is carried out by evaluating several damage
scenarios and numerous parameters that may be aﬀected by a damage occurrence.
In this investigation, structural damages are simulated by reducing the Young’s mod-
ulus of speciﬁc elements. Therefore, several damage scenarios are investigated and
the performance of TME method is evaluated by considering the presence either of
one damaged element or a case when multiple elements are damaged simultaneously.
The chapter is divided in two main sections, where initially no noise eﬀect is consid-
ered, while in the second section, the inﬂuence of noise on several modal parameters
is examined.

Chapter 7
Discussion
In this section, the results presented in the previous Chapter 5 for the steel frame and
Chapter 6 for the oﬀshore wind turbine, are discussed. Along these lines, it is desired
to acquire numerous conclusions for TME method performance, while identifying
advantages and disadvantages. The discussion is divided into two sections, where
the damage assessment evaluation for the steel frame and the oﬀshore wind turbine
are presented separately.
7.1
Four-story steel frame
The sensitivity analysis for the 3D steel frame, presented in Chapter 5, showed the
high robustness of TME method in both noise-free and noise-polluted conditions.
Overall, the damage assessment of the frame appeared to be eﬀective in both single-
damage and double-damage cases.
At ﬁrst, a general and obvious conclusion is the reduction of natural frequencies
due to the damage occurrence. As already discussed in Chapter 5, structural dam-
ages, produced by a stiﬀness loss in speciﬁc elements, result in a natural frequency
reduction, due to the proportional relation between them.
Furthermore, the inﬂuence of damaged members on speciﬁc modal frequencies was
evaluated, depending on their orientation in the global system. As observed in this
analysis, if a structural element shows a parallel deformation in a speciﬁc mode
shape, it appears to not contribute to the reduction of the corresponding modal
frequency.
On the contrary, if a member shows a deformed shape in a speciﬁc
mode shape, then it causes a modal frequency reduction. This is a conclusion of
high importance, as absence of modal frequency change could potentially lead to
erroneous damage assessment, according to the damage index expression in Eq.
3.30.
However, TME method shows once again high robustness, even in cases when the
natural frequencies are not aﬀected. To clarify this further, Table 5.1 demonstrates

7.2
Oﬀshore wind turbine
The damage assessment of OWT showed similar, robust results of high accuracy, in
both noise-free and poise-polluted conditions. In Chapter 6, it was observed that,
in most of the damage scenarios, three available modes were enough for TME index
to identify the damage locations. This is of course the case, since the OWT model
is limited to two dimensions, therefore, damage detection is expected to require less
number of modes involved.
To begin with the single-damage scenarios, TME index precisely detected the dam-
age locations in both jacket structure and the turbine tower, when three modes were
considered.
Following the same eﬃcient performance, TME method correctly identiﬁed the struc-
tural damages in the double-damage investigation. After several combinations be-
tween jacket and tower structural members, the damage assessment showed again
high eﬃciency, when the ﬁrst three modes were taken into account. Although robust
performance was generally achieved considering only three modes, in damage case
E (Table 6.3), TME index missed the damage location of element 8 in the jacket
structure (Fig. 6.8). However, as the number of involved modes was increased to
four, the damages were correctly located in both elements.
This inability of TME method to detect the damage location when three modes are
available can be once again explained considering the mode shapes of OWT. In Fig.
4.11, it was observed that the jacket structure had no contribution to the ﬁrst mode
shape. Although the single-damage scenarios are correctly located with 3 modes
involved, a poor performance of TME is observed when more damaged elements
are included. In this particular case, it is observed that when two elements of the
jacket structure are damaged, three modes are not enough to detect the damage
locations, simply because in one of the considered modes the damaged elements had
no contribution to the vibration mode shape.
Additionally, it is worth noticing that this is also conﬁrmed in the steel frame case,
where in damage scenario E including the horizontal elements 30 and 32, TME
index failed to detect both damage locations considering three modes. As a result,
a general conclusion derived from both numerical models is that, as the number of
damaged element increases, more modes are required to achieve successful damage
detection, especially when elements that do not contribute in a speciﬁc mode shape
are investigated.

Chapter 8
Conclusions
In the present study, the main objective was to investigate the ﬁeld of damage
detection and particularly assess structural damages developed in oﬀshore structures.
Initially, an extensive investigation was conducted to specify a suitable SDD algo-
rithm that meets speciﬁc requirements and personal scientiﬁc interest. Since it was
desired to pair the damage detection with a structural dynamics perspective, the
ﬁeld of VBDD seemed ideal for this purpose. Among other VBDD techniques, TME
method was selected to implement damage assessment in numerical models. This
selection was made based on various diﬀerent parameters, with most important one
being the combination of several dynamic characteristics (i.e. natural frequencies
and mode shapes) that TME method involves. Therefore, the desire to evaluate
several dynamic properties in a damaged stage of a structure, led the investigation
to that direction.
Furthermore, the numerical models are carefully selected, focusing on speciﬁc targets
with the most obvious one being the application of damage detection on an oﬀshore
structure. Apart from that, in the early stages of the investigation it was desired to
focus on the eﬃcient development of the damage detection algorithm in MATLAB.
Therefore, an initial simple numerical model without structural complexities would
facilitate this purpose. To achieve the above, two numerical models were decided to
be developed, one simple steel frame working as a baseline structure, along with a
more complex oﬀshore wind turbine model. Moreover, to include uncertainties and
invalid real-measurement data, noise conditions are introduced in speciﬁc modal
parameters.
Overall, TME method showed high robustness in both models’ investigation, as de-
scribed in Chapter 7. Damage locations are correctly identiﬁed in the vast majority
of damage scenarios, even with low order modes considered. In addition, TME index
conﬁrmed the initial assumption that the damage detection performance becomes
signiﬁcantly more precise, as the number of modes involved increases. Finally, TME
method provided eﬃcient results under noise conditions, applied on diﬀerent modal
parameters, while investigating several noise levels.

Chapter 9
Further work
The present study focused on the damage assessment of structural systems, based
on potential changes in their dynamic characteristics between diﬀerent stages.
Throughout the investigation several conclusions were derived regarding damage
inﬂuence on structures, however there is clearly plenty of room for future work.
Consequently, the last chapter of this report is dedicated to further investigations
and challenges that can be addressed in the future.
The sensitivity analysis conducted in Chapter 5 showed high robustness, as TME
method detected successfully the damage locations in the vast majority of the cases.
However, the investigated steel frame was a simple numerical model that of course
does not represent real structures.
For this reason, the evaluation of structural
health in more complex 3D models, such as large buildings or bridges, would be a
much more competitive challenge for TME method.
Regarding the oﬀshore structure, the numerical model developed based on real di-
mensions and properties, however, it was limited to two dimensions.
Therefore,
an obvious challenge for further study is the development and application of dam-
age detection in a 3D oﬀshore structure model. This will signiﬁcantly increase the
structural challenges during the implementation, but on the other hand, the damage
detection will be examined in a more precise, compared to real conditions, model.
As repeatedly mentioned in this project, the dynamic characteristics utilized by
VBDD are obtained from real measurements on the structure. Since this work is
limited to numerical models, these dynamic properties were estimated using FE
analysis, which obviously is not the case in real-conditions damage detection. Con-
sequently, an interesting further investigation could be the application of damage
detection using real measurements obtained from existing structures.
On the top of that, the obtained modal parameters in this study were accurate
results from numerical models, where modal information on every single node of
the structure can be derived. On the other hand, the available data in damage
assessment of real structures are obviously limited, since the sensors are placed
66
==PAGE:78==
Chapter 9. Further work
67
in speciﬁc locations. Therefore, as a continuation of the previous suggestion, an
additional investigation could be the damage assessment of structures when limited
modal data are available from speciﬁc locations.
Last but not least, the implementation of external loads in the investigation could
be a signiﬁcant development to this study. In the present work, the damages were
simulated as an arbitrary stiﬀness reduction of speciﬁc elements. An alternative idea
is to involve external loads in the numerical models, such as seismic or wave load
scenarios. As a result, a potential damage is created when a speciﬁc cross-section
exceeds the yield strength, showing inelastic behaviour.
Therefore, the stiﬀness
reduction of the system can be estimated, using OpenSees or other software products
that provide the structural matrices, instead of arbitrary assume it. Along these
lines, a potential investigation could be for instance the estimation of an earthquake’s
magnitude, that will cause several damages to a structural system.

% Clear workspace and figures
clear all

% number of nodes
N = 20;
 
% number of elements
n = 32;
 
% Length of elements based on the coordinate system XYZ
L = Length(N,n);
 
% Material properties 
nu = 0.3;   % Poisson' ratio [-]             
E = zeros(n,1);  G = zeros(n,1);  rho = zeros(n,1);  me = zeros(n,1);
 
for i = 1:n
E(i) = 210000000000;     % Young modulus of steel material[N/m2 = Pa]
G(i) = E(i)/(2*(1+nu));  % Shear modulus [Pa]
rho(i) = 7850;           % Material density [kg/m3]
end
 
% HEB 200 - Cross-section parameters 
Iz = 5696*10^-8;    % Moment of intertia around strong axis [m4]
Iy = 2003*10^-8;    % Moment of intertia around weak axis [m4]
It = 59.28*10^-8;   % Tortional moment of inertia [m4]
A = 78.08*10^-4;    % Area of the cross-section [m2]
 
% Mass per unit length in kg/m
me = rho*A;         
 
% Elements connectivity
[X, C] = Connectivity(N,n);
 
% Angles between local and global axes
angle = Angles(n);
 
% Rotation matrix [Gamma]
Gamma = Rotation(n,angle);
 
% Global and elemental stiffness matrices
[K, Ke] = Stiffness(n, N, E, G, L, A, Iz, Iy, It, C, Gamma);
 
% Global and elemental mass matrices
[M, Me] = Mass(n, N, me, L, A, Iz, C, Gamma);
 
% Eigenvalue problem (Undamaged stage)
[V, D] = eig(K, M);
omega = sqrt(sort(diag(D)));
period = (2*pi)./omega;
freq = 1./period;
Phi = V;
