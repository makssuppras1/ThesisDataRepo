Abstract
Atlantic Cod (Gadus morhua) and Common Sole (Solea solea) are economically
significant species in the North and Baltic seas. For this reason, the collection of general
knowledge, such as accurate stock parameters, is important to monitor the stock through
environmental changes. Otolith microchemistry is a technique which focuses on several
elemental compounds to accurately age fish when typical visual aging methods are not
sufficient. For this study, several elemental isotopes (57Fe, 39K, 25Mg, 31P, and 88Sr) were
tested via laser ablation and elemental analysis to see if they are viable age identifiers for
sole. Subsequently, the isotopes cemented that otolith microchemistry succeeded in ac­
curately aging cod. For sole, chemical aging varied among ICES subdivisions, with 25Mg,
57Fe, and 31P being the most successful in determining the correct age of specimens
at 23%, 19.6%, and 16.6% respectively. Linear regressions indicated overlap between
subdivisions, a lack of individuals younger than age 3 in subdivisions 20 and 22, and sig­
nificant relationships between chemical and visual ages, particularly in subdivisions 20
and 4B, and 22 and 4B. This study demonstrates the complexity and potential of otolith
microchemistry for age estimation in Atlantic cod and common sole, with 31P and 25Mg
proving to be reliable indicators for cod, and sole varied by subdivision. The findings
underscore the need for strategic approaches and well­tested analytical methods to en­
hance the accuracy and utility of otolith microchemistry in fisheries management across
stocks and species.

1
Introduction
Atlantic Cod (Gadus morhua) and Common Sole (Solea solea) are species of dem­
ersal fishes that are commercially exploited and ecologically important to the ecosystems
they inhabit. They are distributed throughout the Northern Atlantic Ocean through Scan­
dinavia, causing them to be economically valuable to coastal countries such as the United
Kingdom, Norway, Denmark, and other European Union member states. Atlantic cod’s
popularity and importance led it to be coined, “the most important demersal fish” by the
Food and Agriculture Organization (FAO) in 2023, as they accounted for over 60% the
economic value of total landings. Sole is also commercially sought after, with their 2022
landings totaling under 10 500 tons alone in the North Sea ICES Subarea 4 (ICES Sole,
2022). However, the landings of both demersal fishes have greatly reduced in recent
years. In 2022, the International Council for the Exploration of the Sea (ICES) reported
there were less than 10 429 tons of sole and 17 789 tons of Atlantic cod landed in Sub­
area 4, Division 7.d, and Subdivision 20 of the North Sea ecoregion. The numbers from
both stocks have greatly reduced over the past six years mainly due to unsustainable
fishing practices, climate change, and lack of distinction between populations (Sguotti et
al., 2019).
The current status of the Atlantic cod stock is dismal, with the fishing pressure being
below maximum sustainable yield (FMSY) and the spawning stock biomass being below
maximum sustainable yield (MSY) and other important biological parameters: Blim, Btrig­
ger, and Bpa (ICES Cod, 2022). The status of the sole stock is more successful than the
cod, with the fishing pressure being above FMSY and the spawning stock biomass is be­
low MSY Btrigger and between Bpa and Blim (ICES Sole, 2022). Currently, both stocks
are at fractions of their historic levels causing stress on the environment and economies
that rely on their landings.
In order to understand and mitigate future consequences of climate change, along
with being confident in stock data, new techniques are being rapidly developed­ one of
which regards the accuracy in aging stock specimens. The traditional method for aging
fish entails visually inspecting the calcium­based otolith under a microscope and counting
annuli, or rings, that grow as the fish ages (Hansen et al., 2018). Visual ageing can also
include the use of dyes that assist the pronunciation of annuli.
Visual ageing has been the traditional technique since the beginning of stock man­
agement; however, this is only viable if the annuli are countable. The increasing ocean
temperatures­ a direct consequence of climate change ­are causing annuli to become less
visible as the winter and summer conditions have become more alike. A typical otolith
would have countable annuli, with dark rings that are developed during winters, where it
is colder, the fish experience less food, and have a lower metabolism. Opposite to the
dark sections, the clear rings are developed during summer, where the fish is experienc­
ing growth and favorable conditions. As the fish ages, and its growth begins to plateau,
the annuli will form closer together. This can be an area where visual age estimation fails
in correctly identifying all annuli, especially if the otolith’s growth pattern is irregular or not
symmetrical (Pannella, 1971).
There are several reasons why visual age estimation may be skewed or inaccurate
across all species of fish. The possibility of inaccuracy increases as climate change con­
tinues, therefore a standardized approach must be developed that negates any human in­

exactness and can tackle the readability issues that come with climate change. Recently,
a method has been developed to address this problem: chemical ageing via otolith micro­
chemistry. This method involves exploiting the elemental isotopes that biomineralize into
the calcium carbonate (CaCO 3) otolith as it grows (Sturrock et al., 2012). Through the use
of laser ablation and mass spectrometry, an otolith is transected with a laser and debris
is funneled into a mass spectrometer. In the mass spectrometer, elements are divided
by their atomic weights and recorded. The output is elemental isotope concentration over
the distance of the otolith transect.
Not all elemental isotopes are created equal in their ability to indicate age and show
physiological or environmental conditions. Elemental isotopes Magnesium25 (Mg),
Phosphorus31 (P), Potassium39 (K), Strontium88 (Sr), and Iron57 (Fe) are chosen because
previous studies have identified them as indicators of environmental changes that fish
may experience throughout their life history. Fluctuations in these elemental isotopes
can indicate changes in environmental factors such as salinity and temperature, as well
as physiological changes including maturation and migration (Thorrold & Shuttleworth,
2000).
This method has already been proven in literature to be successful for several com­
mercial stocks, most notably the cod stocks. However, it has only been recently explored
for flatfish such as European Plaice (Pleuronectes platessa L.) (Sturrock et al., 2015).
This study tests the viability of chemical ageing via otolith microchemistry for sole, com­
pares its applicability to that of the already cemented Atlantic cod, and tests if there are
any statistical differences between sole ICES subdivisions.

Methods
2.1
Data Collection
Atlantic cod otoliths were collected during North Sea survey cruises in January,
February, July, and October of 2020, and additional otoliths were collected in January,
February, March, October, and November of 2021. These cruises targeted ICES subdi­
visions 21, 22, and 23. A total of 620 cod otoliths were collected from individuals aged
6 and below. Common sole otoliths were collected in June of 2020, and in May and
June of 2021, from ICES subdivisions 4B, 20, and 22, unlike the cod. A total of 60 sole
otoliths were collected from these sampling surveys. Onboard the vessel, all otoliths were
extracted and given identifiers. They would later be prepared and visually aged in a lab­
oratory under a dissecting microscope. The reader’s age approximation would then be
recorded.
For otolith microchemistry, all otoliths were first prepared for laser ablation by gentle
cleaning to remove any biological tissue still attached. Next, the otoliths were set in a
resin for sectioning. Once the resin had cured, the otoliths were cleaved in half using a
rotating saw. After sectioning, pictures were taken, and notations were made.
The otoliths were transported to the Geological Survey of Denmark and Green­
land (GEUS) for element abundance analysis, a method that was developed by GEUS
and the National Institute of Aquatic Resources, Technical University of Denmark (DTU
AQUA). Laser ablation was conducted at GEUS via its LA­ICP­MS facility, which houses
a NWR213 laser system coupled with an ELEMENT 2 double­focusing, single­collecting
magnetic sector field ICPMS (Supplementary Table S1). Equipment specifications, set­
tings, and operating conditions are listed in Supplementary Table S1. The LA­ICP­MS
has been chosen by previous studies as a preferred process for in­situ data samples that
are retrieved from organic specimens (Hansen Serre et al., 2018). All otoliths followed
the same process of laser ablation, where a transect was followed by the 40 µm circular
laser at a speed of 5 µm/s. Simultaneously, particle debris from the laser was siphoned
out and measured by the ELEMENT 2 magnetic sector field. A 30 second washout was
then conducted at the conclusion of every otolith scan, where the ELEMENT 2 magnetic
sector field was able to dilute elemental isotope debris from the previous scan so that
it would not influence the next. Once every otolith was successfully scanned, data was
compiled and forwarded for analysis.

dataset to narrow down which isotopes had the highest clarity and quality, meaning they
had stronger signal noise than background noise. The isotopes with highest signal­to­
noise ratios were: 57Fe, 39K, 25Mg, 31P, and 88Sr (Figure 2.1).

3
Results
3.1
Data analysis within species: Cod
The Atlantic cod otoliths reflected promising results and found that several elemen­
tal isotopes followed an oscillating seasonal trend, where there would be a minima or
maxima. Several isotopes that proved to be exceptionally well at providing this were 31P,
and to a lesser extent 25Mg and 55Mn. Within the cod otoliths, the visual age was best
estimated by 31P, followed by 25Mg and 39K. 31P correctly aged 83.4% of fish, while 25Mg
and 39K achieved accuracies of 60.6% and 37.3%. 79 cod had at least four of the tested
elements recording verified ages.
The linear regression model for cod in the subdivisions also supported the same con­
clusion as the minima table. The highest slopes were found in 31P and one in subdivision
23 for 25Mg, with the highest being the 31P slope of 0.697, confidence interval [0.582,
0.812] (Supplementary Table S2). As seen in Figure 2.4, all of the slopes statistically
deviate from the dashed red line slope of 1. This means that explaining the relation­
ship between true age and chemical age, based on ICES subdivision and element, is
not a simple 1­to­1 ratio. Instead, that there are other outlying factors that need to be
addressed in order to determine the applicability of certain elements in the varied ICES
subdivisions. However, this relationship can be further investigated visually by analyzing
the otolith concentration profiles and seeing if there are any seasonal patterns or oscilla­
tions revolving around periods associated with behavioral adaptations, such as migration.
These oscillations within certain elements can explain reasons behind the figure’s slopes.
An example of a cod specimen that showed preferable otolith microchemistry results
was F2_12_3_2. A preferable result is one where seasonal oscillations are visible and
consistent throughout the otolith profile, also that the number of minima detected reflects
the same as the visual reader. In this fish, there were visible oscillating trends in all tested
elements, with 3 out of the 5 reading an accurate age. Towards the end of the otolith
profile, a rapid increase in concentration for all elements can be explained due to organic
residue in the form of leftover tissue that was not properly removed prior to laser ablation
(Wood et al., 2022).
3.2
Data analysis within species: Sole
The common sole results varied greatly within and among ICES subdivisions. The
elemental isotope concentration figures differed on an individual­to­individual basis; how­
ever, there were noticeable patterns within some ICES subdivisions. Firstly, the top three
elemental isotopes that were most successful in determining a correct age 25Mg, 57Fe,
and 31P. 25Mg correctly aged 23% of sole specimens, 57Fe aged 19.6%, and 31P aged
16.6% (Table 3.2). These results were found by doing a correlation to see how much each
isotope can correctly describe the age of the fish. Table 3.2 shows that the highest and
strongest correlation is with isotope 25Mg. Relative to the cod results, the elements had
a low correlation and success in determining age.
The two linear regressions were ran. The more encompassing regression attempted
to visualize any relationship between ICES subdivisions. This linear regression output for
sole, which took into account all elements and predictions, showed that there was an
overlap between subdivisions. It also highlighted the lack of individuals younger than age
3 from ICES subdivisions 20 and 22. (Figure 2.5)

4
Discussion
4.1
Findings of Cod data analysis
The linear regression for cod between ICES subdivisions and element gave an output
where the most­fit element, or the element with the slope closest to 1, was 31P across all
subdivisions (Figure 2.4; Supplementary Table S2). However, 25Mg had a notably high
slope in subdivision 23 (Figure 2.4; Supplementary Table S2). This deviation could be due
to the local water qualities of subdivision 23. Subdivision 23 is the Øresund (Sound) which
is located between Denmark and Sweden, where fresher Baltic and more saline North Sea
waters mix (Supplementary Figure A1). Since 25Mg levels can reflect changes in somatic
growth and metabolism, the seasonal and tidal variations in this area can lead to oscillating
salinity trends and varying food supply, particularly when one sea has greater influence
over the water column than the other (Heimbrand et al., 2020). This area of plasticity can
cause cod to grow dependent on which sea is more influential over the water column due
to the temperature (Smoliński et al., 2020).
On an individual level, the Atlantic cod data analysis cemented prior knowledge
surrounding cod otolith microchemistry.
25Mg and 31P were the best elements to esti­
mate age, so it is not surprising as they were the best to exhibit distinct seasonal vari­
ations. Previous studies, such as Hansen et al. (2018) and Hüssy et al. (2021), have
demonstrated the consistent seasonal patterns of these elements. Within the same stud­
ies, it was proven that 31P indicates metabolic activity and seasonality as well. Along
with metabolic activity, elemental isotopes can indicate physiological development and
migration.
31P has been shown to signal maturation and seasonal metabolism in cod
(Hüssy et al., 2016). As seen in Figure 3.1, fish F2_12_3_2 undergoes high 31P spikes, of
metabolism within the late summer to early autumn. These spikes represent that the fish
is experiencing periods of high growth, where foraging is plentiful. For this specimen, it is
suggested that it was hatched away from the coast, and then migrated closer as it aged,
with it recruiting to the fishery, or becoming mature, after age 4. This is representative of
recorded cod behavior, where eggs are hatched within the water column, and use cur­
rents to drift to coastal nursery habitats (Wright and Rowe, 2018). For cod, 88Sr has been
a proven indicator of salinity and temperature changes, which also leads to a suspected
migration since high concentrations correspond to lower temperatures and higher salinity,
indicative of more offshore habitats (Heimbrand et al., 2020; Hansen Serre et al., 2018).
4.2
Findings of Sole data analysis
The common sole data analysis provided insightful evidence about the viability of
chemical aging and otolith microchemistry for the species. As seen in the linear regres­
sion across ICES subdivisions, there was a large overlap between areas (Figure 2.5).
However, this outcome was lacking much significance, as there were limited younger in­
dividuals in areas 20 and 22 compared to 4B. This could be due to low salinity levels in
these areas as they are heavily influenced by the Baltic, which decreases further east­
ward. Younger sole are therefore found only in low abundances in these more eastern
subdivisions (Boje et al., 2019). Unlike these other areas, 4B had recorded individuals as
young as 2, which were likely spawned from a different stock than those from the other
areas (Boje et al., 2019).

5
Conclusion
The Atlantic cod data provided insights that cemented previous knowledge about
the applicability of ageing via otolith microchemistry. For cod, 31P emerged as the most
consistent elemental isotope for estimating age across different ICES subdivisions. This
is reflective of its strong correlation with metabolic activity and seasonality.
Besides
phosphorus,25Mg also showed notable variability, especially in subdivision 23, which can
be attributed to the ever changing salinity of the Øresund, thus impacting metabolic indi­
cators.
On an individual level, cod analysis further confirmed the importance of 31P and 25Mg
in indicating seasonal growth patterns, with significant activity observed during the late
summer to early autumn (Figure 3.1) These findings are consistent with previous studies,
reinforcing the reliability of these elements in tracking physiological changes, migration,
and maturation in cod.
In contrast, the common sole analysis highlighted different isotopes as significant de­
pending on subdivision. While there was clear overlap in the linear regression between
all subdivisions (Figure 2.5), this was most likely due to the restrictive data. There was
also overlap on the more in depth linear regression, however the optimal isotope var­
ied between areas (Figure 2.4). 39K in subdivision 22, the Belt Sea, proved effective in
age estimation. 25Mg and 88Sr were more reliable indicators in subdivisions 20 and 4B,
aligning with their ability to reflect somatic growth and maturation.
The individual analysis of sole underlined the complexity of otolith microchemistry.
25Mg, 57Fe and 31P accurately indicated age through their minima detection. The inclusion
of winter bars helped show the patterns of oscillations on the individual level. Elements
such as 25Mg, 57Fe and 31P had a reoccurring relationship where minima would fall close
to, or inside winter zones (Figure 3.2). This is logical as sole lack the foraging opportunities
that are present during the summer periods.
To better understand otolith microchemistry and chemical aging of sole, several
changes would be implemented in a future study. The most important change would
be a narrowing of the laser used in laser ablation. Similar studies which were done on
European Plaice (Pleuronectes platessa L.) used a laser size of 20 µm, with a 8 µm anal­
ysis window, which would provide more clarity to the results (Sturrock et al., 2015). A
smaller laser and narrowed analysis window, it is believed that more data points would be
collected on the outskirts of the otolith, which would in turn help detect minima accurately.
Another improvement would be to individualize the scan time based on isotope, where
molecularly heavy isotopes have longer scan times (Sturrock et al., 2015). Lastly, a col­
lection of more younger sole individuals would improve the data analysis output. Due to
the restrictive amount of individuals younger than 6, large confidence intervals were set
in the statistical outcomes (Figure 2.3, Figure 2.5).
Overall, the findings of this study highlight the complexity and potential of otolith mi­
crochemistry and chemical aging. The variability in element effectiveness across differ­
ent species, stocks, and environments highlights the importance of tailored approaches,
considering local ecological factors, and species­specific metabolic processes. Future re­
search should focus on refining analytical methods and exploring additional elements to
improve the accuracy and applicability of otolith microchemistry for fisheries management
and conservation efforts.
