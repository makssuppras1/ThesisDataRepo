Abstract
Landﬁlling is the most used waste management technology in most parts of the world and Ghana is not
an exception. In Ghana, the landﬁlls have high organic composition, which under anaerobic conditions,
produce landﬁll gas; mainly methane and carbon dioxide. The methane is a greenhouse gas that has a
high potential of causing global warming. To reduce the effect of landﬁll methane in Ghana, the pos-
sibility of implementing the technological biocover methane-oxidation solutions in Ghana is analyzed;
focusing on the Ohwim landﬁll as a study site. The Ohwim landﬁll was screened for methane concentra-
tions using the FID and the biogas detector. Concentrations of above 1867 ppmv were reported for the
vents and sloped areas on the landﬁll. The landﬁll surface, however, had low methane concentrations.
The variation in concentrations on the landﬁll surface and at the edges could be due to the blockage of
vertical transport or the generation of a natural biocover on the surface. Further, the methane generation
was estimated using LandGEM , to give 300 Mg/yr and 800 Mg/yr for k values of 0.05y−1 and 0.3y−1
respectively. From LandGEM, a methane load of 35gCH4m−2 d−1 was estimated and ﬁnally used in
the oxidation model, LandSEM, to simulate methane oxidation efﬁciencies. The load was assumed to
be constant and uniformly distributed in baseline and conceptual biocover models of thicknesses 0.2m
and 1m respectively. With varying Vmax values, the baseline biocover simulation had oxidation efﬁ-
ciencies of 12% and 22% for Vmax of 150nmols−1 g−1
drysoil and 300 nmols−1 g−1
drysoil respectively.
These oxidation efﬁciencies did not account for the methane ﬂux through the vents and sloped edges
of the landﬁll. The conceptual model, taking into consideration the hot spots of the landﬁll, reported
oxidation efﬁciencies of 32%, 52%, 68%, 77% and 85% for Vmax values of 150, 300, 500, 700 and
1000 nmols−1 g−1
drysoil . Relating Vmax to the organic matter content of the biocover and the capacity
of methanotrophs in oxidizing methane, the trend could suggest that with the right amount of organic
rich material, under suitable conditions, the biocover technology could be implemented in Ghana.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Waste management is an essential part of human existence. As long as human beings continue to go
through their daily activities; be it industrial, agricultural or domestic, the generation of waste is an
obvious expectation. The solid waste management practices in the ancient times posed less difﬁculty
as compared to the current situation, since, inhabitants were mostly few and the habitats were sparse
(Fei-Baffoe et al., 2014; Robinson et al., 2003). Presently, economic growth, population growth and
urbanization have led to the problematic nature of waste management due to the fact that, there are
less or no lands for waste disposal (Pariatamby et al., 2015; Bogner and Matthews, 2003). This, on the
other hand, has had a concomitant effect in the form of competition for space (land) and the creation of
nuisance such as emissions, odor, ﬂies etc. (Christensen, 2011).
However, there has been an appreciable evolution in the management of municipal solid waste (MSW);
from the open dump method to the current integrated waste management practices. The integrated waste
management practices aim at incorporating the prevention, reuse, recycle and recovery techniques as
well as the use of engineered landﬁll facilities with emission control systems (Sadasivam and Reddy,
2014a; Christensen, 2011). According to the European Parliament and Council Directive (2008), the
hierarchy of waste ﬂow should begin as much as possible with waste prevention or source reduction.
Inclusive in this hierarchy are reuse, recycling, recovery (incineration) and lastly, landﬁlling. It is,
therefore, appropriate to follow the hierarchy in designing a waste management system thereby render-
ing landﬁlls as the last option to be considered. Nonetheless, landﬁlling has since historic times been
the main form of waste management practiced in many parts of the world (Christensen, 2011).
Unlike most developed countries, developing country's ﬁrst approach to solid waste disposal is to
landﬁll and this could be attributed to the lack of funds and technical know-how in practicing other
advanced waste management technologies like incineration, recycling and even composting (Greedy,
2016). About 80% of the generated solid waste in most developing countries are landﬁlled and in India,
almost 90% of their solid waste is deposited in unengineered landﬁlls (Robinson et al., 2003; Morris
2
==PAGE:18==
et al., 2012). In 2007, about 200 million tons of solid waste were generated in Africa with over 50%
not being properly disposed (Robinson et al., 2003). The amount of waste landﬁlled each year through-
out the world, irrespective of the development status or technological advancement of a country makes
landﬁlling seem inevitable. The United States of America (USA), for example landﬁlls about 50% of
its generated MSW, depicting landﬁlling as the most used waste management approach in most parts
of the world (Sadasivam and Reddy, 2014a). Usually, the MSW landﬁlled in these countries have high
organic content.
The landﬁlling of MSW with high organic composition results in the generation of landﬁll gas (LFG);
predominantly methane and biogenic carbon dioxide (Sanderson et al., 2008; Philopoulos et al., 2008;
Jones and Nedwell, 1993; Willumsen, 1990). The LFG is produced as a result of anaerobic decomposi-
tion of organic waste and it is composed of 50-55% v/v methane and 40-45% v/v carbon dioxide (Bajar
et al., 2015; Sadasivam and Reddy, 2014a; Scheutz et al., 2009a; Czepiel et al., 1996). The methane
from the LFG is considered a greenhouse gas (GHG) with a high potential of causing global climate
change through the absorption of infra-red radiation reﬂected from the surface of the earth (IPCC, 2006;
Börjesson and Svensson, 1997). According to IPCC (2006), methane can last a period of about 12
years in the atmosphere. Methane, also, has a global warming potential (GWP) of 28 over a period of
100 years; making it a more powerful greenhouse gas with a contribution of 18% to the total radiative
forcing leading to climate change (IPCC, 2006).
Landﬁlling is considered one of the major and signiﬁcant anthropogenic activities that emit methane into
the atmosphere (IPCC, 2007; Rodriguez-Iglesias et al., 2005). Previous studies have reported emissions
between 10-70 teragrams (Tg) per year and these values are about 11% to 12% of the yearly anthro-
pogenic methane production (Bajar et al., 2015; Mønster et al., 2015; Abushammala et al., 2014; Garg
et al., 2007; Berger et al., 2005). Also, an estimate of 20 to 60 Tg of annual methane emission from land-
ﬁlls have been reported by Robinson et al. (2003) in their study. According to Pendolovska et al. (2013)
and EPA (2011), anthropogenic methane emissions from landﬁlls in the USA and the European Union
(EU) member states accounted for 4907 Gg and 3052 Gg respectively in 2011. The Intergovernmental
Panel on Climate Change (IPCC) 4th assessment report also made a projection of the landﬁll methane
emission reaching 820 to 1000 Mt CO2eq in most developing countries by 2020 (Bajar et al., 2015;
Pachauri and Reisinger, 2007). According to Christensen (2011), methane emissions from landﬁlls ac-
3
==PAGE:19==
count for 5% of the total anthropogenic GHG emissions. Robinson et al. (2003), in their study, reported
that developing countries account for almost 40% of the yearly global landﬁll methane emissions.
Considering the high amount of methane emitted from landﬁlls in developing countries, and its effect on
climate change, there is the need to implement a comprehensive technology that could reduce methane
emissions. This is very necessary because, Abushammala et al. (2014) asserts that a reduction of the
30 to 40 million metric tonnes of annual methane emission would put a stop to the rising methane
concentrations and this could be achieved at least half way by the use of current technologies .One
technology could be the recovery or extraction of the methane for the purposes of heat and energy
generation but most countries ﬁnd it difﬁcult to implement this due low ﬁnancial and technical resources
(Mønster et al., 2015; Abushammala et al., 2014). Even in countries where gas extraction is practiced,
there could be leakages or vents that allow for fugitive emissions which would have to be mitigated.
A solution to the above would be to implement the biocover technology on small and less methane-
generating landﬁlls. This technology uses methanotrophic bacteria to degrade methane through an ox-
idation process to produce biogenic carbon dioxide. The biocover technology has been used in places
like Denmark and it has proven to be efﬁcient (Scheutz et al., 2014; Fredenslund, 2010). This study
therefore, seeks to investigate how realistic it is to implement such a techology in Ghana considering
the difference in climatic conditions between Ghana and countries like Denmark where it has been im-
plemented. If the implementation of a biocover technology is found viable in Ghana, it will be a better
way of mitigating landﬁll methane considering the cost involved and the availability of biocover ma-
terials. Installing collection systems on all the small and unengineered landﬁlls in Ghana would not
be economically feasible since these landﬁlls may generate less or low-quality methane (Bajar et al.,
2015; Roncato and Cabral, 2011; Bogner et al., 2010). In this case, the biocover technology stands as a
possible choice.

2.1

Methane (CH4) is the simplest of the alkane group and a major part of natural gases. It is a radiative
gas that occupies a keen role in greenhouse effect; making it a GHG (Etiope and Klusman, 2002; IPCC,
2006). Its ability to absorb and re-emit heat has a tendency to keep the atmosphere warmer than expected
(Brander and Davis, 2012). Landﬁlling is one of the main sources of CH4 (Ortiz-Llorente and Alvarez-
Cobelas, 2012). Methane from landﬁlls is mainly as a result of anaerobic methanogenic archaea acting
on organic matter as shown in Figure 2.1 and Equation 2.1 (Liu and Greaver, 2009).

CO2 production and emission, on the other hand, may be as a result of natural or anthropogenic pro-

2.2
Biocover Technology
Methane emissions from open landﬁll surfaces, top cover cracks, ﬁssures, and vents could be oxidized
to ensure the loss of its greenhouse gas potential (Christensen, 2011) . This form of oxidation can be
initiated on landﬁlls with the use of biologically active materials such as compost, sludge etc. serving
as biocover systems (Scheutz et al., 2014, 2009c). A biocover system is therefore set up to facilitate the
process of alleviating the impacts of LFG emissions through biological oxidation.
2.2.1
Methane Emission from Landﬁlls
Landﬁlls come with a major problem in the form of LFG generation (Sadasivam and Reddy, 2014b).
The LFG generation follows four main reaction phases namely aerobic decomposition or hydrolysis
(Phase I), acidogenesis or fermentation (Phase II), acetogenesis (Phase III), and methanogenesis (Phase
IV) (Pariatamby et al., 2015). The hydrolysis stage is where water molecules split the organic macro
molecules into smaller units. This is followed by the acidogenesis where the hydrolyzation products are
processed into volatile fatty acids, ethanol, H2 etc. In the acetogenesis phase, an acetate is formed and
it is further used in the methanogenesis phase to produce methane. The reaction chains for acetogenesis
and methanogenesis which basically cause the production of the methane are shown in Equations 2.3,
2.4 and 2.5.
Acetogenesis
2CO2 + 4H2 →CH3COOH + 2H2O
(2.3)
Methanogenesis
CH3COOH →CH4 + CO2
(2.4)
CO2 + 4H2 →CH4 + 2H2O
(2.5)
When the production of CH4 through the above reactions continue for a period of time, the landﬁll
is most likely not able to contain the CH4 load. The CH4 is then released with increased pressure
within the landﬁll (Pariatamby et al., 2015). The major channels of CH4 emission from landﬁlls include
diffusion and advection. The diffusive transport is induced by variations in concentration whereas the

advective movement is as a result of pressure gradients in the form of wind or pressure built up from
gas generation on landﬁlls (Scheutz et al., 2009a).

Factors Affecting Methane Generation and Oxidation
Some physical, biological and chemical properties have the tendency of inﬂuencing the generation and
oxidation of methane on landﬁlls. Such factors include moisture content, organic content, waste volume,
soil/biocover material type, biogas ﬂow rate, pH, precipitation, wind and soil cover conditions such as
permeability and porosity (Roncato and Cabral, 2011; Scheutz and Kjeldsen, 2004; Durmusoglu et al.,
2005; Kjeldsen and Fischer, 1995). The permeability of the biocover is of much importance not only
for its oxygen transport but also for the water holding capacity (Moon et al., 2014; Fredenslund, 2010;
Pedersen, 2010; Jain et al., 2005).

The ability to retain water is very important for a sustained microbial population; which in turn increases
methane oxidation (Pariatamby et al., 2015) . Low moisture content puts stress on the methanotrophs
whilst excess water content affects the penetration of oxygen (Pariatamby et al., 2015; Aishah and
Baharain, 2011; Fredenslund, 2010; Pedersen, 2010). The methanotrophs are most active in a suitable
condition in the biocover, hence, an addition of some organic waste to the landﬁll material could improve
the microbial community (Pariatamby et al., 2015; Moon et al., 2014).
Also, the pH of the landﬁll cover material should ideally be between neutral to slightly acidic to enable

in the landﬁll could also have an effect on the oxidation. The studies of Pariatamby et al. (2015) and

as much as possible reduce LFG generation.

of biologically active landﬁll covers that comprise ﬁlter materials capable of supporting methanotrophic
communities under suitable conditions (Christophersen et al., 2001; De Visscher et al., 1999; Kightley
et al., 1995). This form of landﬁll cover, normally called biocover, has the basic design as shown in

The biocover normally comprises a permeable gas distribution layer with the sole purpose of increasing
homogeneity of landﬁll gas ﬂuxes and an overlying oxidation layer that supports the methanotrophic
bacteria that uses the methane (Figure 2.5) (Sadasivam and Reddy, 2014a; Scheutz et al., 2014; Ped-

The choice of a biocover material cannot be underestimated since it has a great impact on the ability
of the system to aid methane oxidation. An unsuitable material under unsuitable conditions may rather
lead to methane production (Scheutz et al., 2009a). Due to cost and material availability, most current
studies have resorted to the use of waste materials such as compost, sewage sludge and farm residues
such as straw and rice husk which are equally good and easily available (Bajar et al., 2015; Sadasivam
and Reddy, 2014a). With a mix portion of 3 to 1 compost and sand biocover, Khoshand (2014) proved
that there was no notable reduction in the methane oxidation rate.
Whalen et al. (1990) reported a methane oxidation rate 61 g CH4m−2day−1 when they used topsoil
from a landﬁll consisting of sand mixed with brown and gray clays at a temperature of 25 ◦C. They
reported an enhanced methane transport through the air soil pores leading increased methane oxidation
rates. Recently, Bajar et al. (2015) conducted a laboratory study to investigate the efﬁcacy of rice

oxidation rate of 76.83 ugCH4g−1
dwh−1 was obtained with 6% (w/w) rice husk proportion and 40% (v/v)
initial CH4 concentration using 5 h incubation time. The study proved that with much knowledge about
the various interactive variables such as rice husk proportion, temperature, methane concentration and
incubation time, methane oxidation can be enhanced.
Several other studies have also included sophisticated materials in their biocovers. Abdolahzadeh et al.
(2010), in their research work used a biocover made of yard waste compost and topsoil from a solid
waste landﬁll coupled with a passive methane oxidation biocover and reported an increase in the con-
centration of oxygen and its distribution. Lu et al. (2011) performed an experiment using a breathing
biocover system (a mixture of yard waste compost and landﬁll cover soil) equipped with a passive air
venting designed to boost the oxygen supply of the biocover, thereby, increasing methane oxidation.
The study saw an increase in oxygen concentration.
Similarly, Chi et al. (2012) conducted another study using yard waste compost and topsoil with a passive
air diffusion system as biocover. From the results, the oxygen concentration increased with the highest
methane oxidation rate being 31.34 mol m−3 day−1. Also, with the enhanced air distribution and
diffusion, the type I methanotrophs experienced an increased growth. Finally, Zhang et al. (2012) ran

2.3
Landﬁll Gas Modelling
Estimation of LFG generation and oxidation have been reported a number of literature for different
locations in the world. Most of these studies resorted to the use of models and some basic landﬁll or
waste information (data) in making their estimates. In this section, two LFG models namely Landﬁll Gas
Emission Model (LandGEM) and Landﬁll Surface Emission Model (LandSEM) are studied. Further on
in this study, the LandGEM will be used to estimate methane load per day and this value will be used in
the LandSEM to estimate methane oxidation efﬁciencies.
2.3.1
Estimation of Methane Gas Emission - LandGEM
Landﬁll gas modelling is the process of using past and predicted future waste disposal data to estimate
and forecast gas generation. A number of landﬁll gas models namely, IPCC, Afvalzorg, Gassim and
LandGEM, having their own limitations and strengths are currently being used and also found in liter-
ature (Scharff and Jacobs, 2006). Most of these LFG modelling tools are used in generating LFG data
estimates to satisfy regulatory and non-regulatory environmental needs. For the purposes of this study,
the LandGEM was chosen since it is the most used LFG model. It is easily available, user-friendly and
also requires less data input to estimate the evolution of cumulative landﬁll gas emissions (Emkes et al.,
2015; Sil et al., 2014b; Faour et al., 2007).
LandGEM is a software with a Microsoft excel interface developed by the Ofﬁce of Research and
Development of the United States Environmental Protection Agency (USEPA) for the quantiﬁcation of
uncontrolled LFG emissions from MSW landﬁll sites (Alexander et al., 2005; Paraskaki and Lazaridis,
2005; Pelt, 1998). The USEPA in their quest to respond to the Clean Air Act amendments of bringing
the large US landﬁlls into air quality program and extend them for regional emission inventories put
together the LandGEM model(Sil et al., 2014a) The model is based on the ﬁrst order degradation (FOD)
equation as shown in Equation 2.8 (Keelson, 2013; Oonk, 2010; Thompson et al., 2009). From the
equation, one can deduce that the methane generation and emission are assumed to be in a steady and
linear decrease to the degradation of organic matter in time.

LandGEM assumes that after anaerobic conditions are established in the landﬁll upon an initial waste
disposal, methane generation is expected to be at its peak and decrease exponentially with time as
organic material gets degraded. LandGEM is also a single phase model that does not allow for the
distinguishing of waste categories but considers only the total annual waste disposed (Mou et al., 2015b).
The model comes in versions 2.01, 3.01 and 3.02. The current version is 3.02 and it replaced the other
two in May 2005. The new version allows users to estimate total LFG generation using either a user
speciﬁc methane concentration or a 50% default methane concentration value. The input data needed
to run the model are the annual mass of waste deposited on the landﬁll Mi, the year of commencing
and closure. The span of waste acceptance is limited to 80 years but multiple model runs could be done
when waste acceptance is expected to be more than that (Alexander et al., 2005).
The main model parameters are the methane generation rate constant (k) and the potential methane gen-
eration capacity (Lo) (Chalvatzaki and Lazaridis, 2009). Lo, gives the amount of methane gas expected
to be produced by a metric tonne of decaying or decayed waste. Estimates from USEPA show that the
suitable values of Lo are within 56.6 and 198.2 m3 per Megagram. It depends on waste composition and
its degradable organic content (Karanjekar et al., 2015; Sil et al., 2014a). The more the organic content
of the waste, the greater the Lo value. Since water does not generate LFG, the Lo value is determined
18
==PAGE:34==
by the dry organic content of the waste and not the wet weight (Alexander et al., 2005). The other model
parameter of importance is the k. It describes the rate at which the waste decay and generation of LFG
occur on landﬁlls. The unit of k is per year and it mostly depends on waste moisture content, available
nutrients, pH and temperature (Karanjekar et al., 2015; Alexander et al., 2005). As long as a landﬁll
continues to receive waste, the methane generation at the site increases rapidly but declines after landﬁll
closure. A higher value of k is best used for high methane generating landﬁlls (Kamalan et al., 2011).
Most low-organic waste categories also show lower k values (Mou et al., 2015b).
Lo and k could be determined using site-speciﬁc data. In the absence of such data, LandGEM has default
values representing conventional landﬁlls, arid areas and bioreactors landﬁlls that could also be used for

2.3.2
Estimation of Methane Oxidation-LandSEM
Gas transport and biological oxidation in cover soils have been and continue to be simulated in math-
ematical models to estimate methane oxidation in landﬁlls (Abichou et al., 2011). Several researchers
have reported early mathematical modeling efforts (Hilger et al., 2000; De Visscher et al., 1999; Yuan
et al., 2009). Nonetheless, the mathematical models were not accounting for the dynamic variation of
heat and water ﬂow even though the actual sites experience changes in weather (Abichou et al., 2015a,
2011). Abichou et al. (2015a) and Abichou et al. (2011), report that the early models simulated results
from laboratory columns held under constant environmental conditions while in actual landﬁll cover
setting, water content, temperature and barometric pressure constantly change depending on climatic
conditions. An improvement of these models was that from Spokas et al. (2009), since it was able to ac-
count for changes in moisture content and soil temperature. Yet, it still had the limitation of considering
only diffusion as the gas transport mechanism.
In an attempt to design a model that could be more realistic in comparison to the ﬁeld conditions,
Abichou et al. (2009) and Yuan et al. (2009) came out with a model approach that uses the combination
of water and heat ﬂow with a gas transport and oxidation inclusive. The numerical model is the Florida
State University (FSU) model; developed by the research team in the named University (Yuan et al.,
2010). The recent version of the FSU model, renamed LandSEM, comes in the form of a graphical
interface (Abichou et al., 2015b,a). This model is used to simulate methane transport and oxidation with
dynamic parameters associated with water content and temperature. It also incorporates the reaction

of methanotrophic activity to changes in temperature and water content (Abichou et al., 2015b,c). The
LandSEM module encompasses four main modules as shown below (Abichou et al., 2015b,a).
• Climate module : It generates the minimum and maximum daily air temperature as well as daily
rainfall, contingent on the landﬁll site geographical reference.
• Soil property generation module: It generates soil properties like porosity and saturated hydraulic

• A core computational module: This part of the model essentially considers the landﬁll soil cover

CHAPTER 3
Solid Waste Management in Ghana
Solid waste management in Ghana, since time immemorial, continues to be a herculean task waiting
to be solved sometime in the life of the country. The gradual increase in population and resource con-
sumption over the years has put waste generation on the ascension (Amoah and Kosoe, 2014; Denutsui
et al., 2012; Amoah, 2010; Asase et al., 2009). The Kumasi metropolis stands as an example where
there are increased numbers of people due to child birth and high levels of urbanization. This has
resulted in over 2 million people living in the area and a yearly growth rate pegged at 5.4% (KMA,
2010b; Owusu-Sekyere et al., 2013). The more the number of people increase, the higher the amounts
of waste generated. Unfortunately, not a lot of ways or technologies for managing the waste generated
are available. It has therefore become a struggle for the country to properly dispose waste off. The
waste generated in the main cities are focused on in this chapter because it can be used as a worse case
scenario since the rural areas are expected to generate less waste.
In the history of Ghana, local authorities in the various cities (Kumasi Metropolitan Assembly (KMA)
and Accra Metropolitan Assembly (AMA) in the case of Kumasi and Accra respectively) were put
in charge of issues concerning waste management and were to render services to their residence and
industries. In the 1990s, this public sector led initiative saw a shift involving the private sector in an
Urban Environmental and Sanitation Project (UESP) funded by the World Bank in collaboration with
the government of Ghana (Owusu-Sekyere et al., 2015). The Public- Private Partnership (PPP) was to
improve the efﬁcacy of waste collection and service delivery, monitor performance, set realistic tariffs
and also to cut down the cost allocated to the government by making waste generators pay 10 to 20% of
the cost incurred (Owusu-Sekyere et al., 2015; Asare and Frimpong, 2013; Awortwi, 2004).
The public sector was to be responsible for the provision of services while the actual process of delivery
was undertaken by the private sector (Awortwi, 2004). This initiative brought about what is normally
referred to as “Pay- as- you- dump” (Oteng-Ababio, 2014). People were made to pay some token to
23
==PAGE:39==
dump their waste depending on the amount of waste dumped and the area of residence; be it a gated es-
tate or not. This caused people to reduce the amount of waste generated in their various homes (Amoah,
2010). Some of the private service providers that partnered the public sector are Zoomlion Limited, J.
Owusu Stanley Waste Management Limited and Freko Limited (Wemegah, 2015). Zoomlion Limited
being the main waste management group in Ghana commenced its partnership with the government in
January 2006 (Asare and Frimpong, 2013). This partnership tends to be concerned with the collection
of waste and its disposal in landﬁlls. Although there are a number of waste management technologies
being practiced all over the world, Ghana mainly landﬁlls all its solid waste with very few composting,
plastic and metallic scrap recycling or reuse.
3.1
Waste Types and Composition
Solid waste in Ghana generally includes household, institutional, industrial as well as construction and
demolition waste. The household and institutional waste mainly consist of organic (food) waste, garden
waste, sweepings, discarded electronic and electrical devices, ash and packaging materials (Miezah
et al., 2015; Anomanyo, 2004). Industrial waste, on the other hand, mostly comprises wood (shavings)
waste, metal scrapings, textiles, plastics, paper and waste from the food processing companies and
abattoirs (Osei, 2014). The organic or biodegradable portions of the waste have the highest fractions
and this may be attributed to the lifestyles of the citizens in that; the economy of Ghana depends on
the domestic use and export of agricultural products (Anomanyo, 2004; Asase et al., 2009). Hazardous
waste is generated but mostly in small quantities with the exception of some industries who generate a
little more but by the guidelines from the Ghana Environmental Protection Agency (EPA), control and
manage their own waste (Anomanyo, 2004). Construction and demolition waste are mostly reused at
the various sites and hence are rarely disposed of.

(Puopiel and Owusu-Ansah, 2014; Asase, 2011; Owusu-Ansah, 2008; Ketibuah et al., 2004). According

3.3
Solid Waste Collection and Handling
The two main ways of waste collection practiced in Ghana; especially Accra and Kumasi are the house-
to-house and communal modes of collection (Amoah and Kosoe, 2014; Oteng-Ababio, 2010; Asase
et al., 2009; Appiah Obeng et al., 2009). With the house-to- house practice, the households interested
are given plastic containers or bins with lids , in which they put all their mixed waste (Figure 3.2a). These
bins are picked weekly in front of their houses with monthly charges (Bonsu, 2013). It may be noted
that the areas that practice this form of collection are mostly the well planned residential communities
and gated estates; where the standard of living is relatively high.
Between the years 1999 and 2004, the house to house mode of collection increased from 2.1% to 20.8%
respectively leading to increased amounts of collected waste from 26.5% to 70% between 2001 and
2004 (Wemegah, 2015). The uncollected waste are usually indiscrimately dumped in gutters and water
bodies. Currently, some institutions (schools, hospitals and companies) have also been provided with
labeled bins as shown in Figure 3.2b to initiate some sort of sorting system.
The communal mode of collection, on the other hand, is when big metallic containers called skips are
placed at vantage points for the receiving of waste from the poor and low-income areas at no cost in
Accra and a little fee in Kumasi(Figure 3.2 c and d). Puopiel and Owusu-Ansah (2014) showed that
59.6% of low and high-class residence use skips and dustbins in Tamale. This gives a fair representation
of the large percentage of people who still use this mode of collection. These metallic containers are
averagely transported at a distance of about 18km by skip trucks to disposal sites when they are full and
are replaced as shown in Figure 3.2d , since there are no transfer stations (Oduro-Appiah et al., 2013).

Solid Waste Treatment and Disposal
Since Ghana does not engage in the sorting of waste or practicing of advanced waste management
technologies such as incineration and recycling, almost all the waste generated end up on landﬁlls.
Hence, there are almost no forms of waste treatment. The municipal solid waste, just as they are picked

for reuse on small scale. According to Addo et al. (2015), the scavenged recyclables form almost 20%
of the waste deposited. Scavenging for valuable material on landﬁll sites has become a business for
many people (Owusu-Sekyere et al., 2013).

3.5
Landﬁlling and Landﬁlls in Ghana
Landﬁlling in Ghana remains the most preferred option for waste disposal but most of the landﬁll sites
are not planned thereby leading to the creation of nuisance in the form of foul scent, ﬂies, ﬁre outbreak
and environmental degradation (Keelson, 2014; Owusu et al., 2014; Oteng-Ababio, 2011; Anaglate
et al., 2014; Owusu et al., 2012). Municipal solid waste with varied and mixed composition are de-
posited in two different types of landﬁlls in Ghana. The ﬁrst and most common type of disposal site
is the unengineered and unsanitary landﬁlls normally referred to as dumpsites (Figure 3.5a) (Nartey
et al., 2012). These sites are uncontrolled and hence have no leachate and gas collection systems. The
unengineered landﬁlls, normally found in open places, wetlands or lowlands (valleys) are positioned
considering the ease of vehicles accessing the area (Owusu-Sekyere et al., 2013). These landﬁll sites
receive 55% and more of the MSW collected in Accra and 80% in Kumasi (Nyame et al., 2012).
The second type of disposal site is the engineered landﬁll. These landﬁlls have been designed techni-
cally to be able to handle landﬁll emissions. With the aid of the World Bank and the Department for
International Development (DFID), four engineered landﬁlls were designed and developed in Kumasi,
Accra, Tamale and Sekondi-Takoradi in the year 2004 (Owusu-Sekyere et al., 2013).

3.5.1
Landﬁll Waste Composition
Unsorted MSW in the form of mixed waste are disposed of without any restrictions on the type of waste
any landﬁll is supposed to receive (Owusu-Sekyere et al., 2013). All types of waste, be it, household
organic waste, medical waste, hazardous waste, industrial and institutional waste and other bulk resi-
dential waste are allowed on landﬁlls. None of the waste is treated and hence, they are disposed of in
their raw state. Also, there are no speciﬁc requirements for waste received by any landﬁll. Since all
waste are landﬁlled and the organic waste has the highest amounts, the generation of GHG (methane)
cannot be overlooked.
3.6
Solid Waste Related Policies and Regulations
Over the years, there has been constant enactment of environmental regulation by governments around
the world. Where as the policies get stricter each day, non-compliance also arises, hence, the need
for enforcement (Heyes, 2000). The government of Ghana, also, has over the years put together Envi-
ronmental policies and guidelines which include the Local Government Act, 1993 (Act 462), National
Environmental Sanitation Policy (NESP,1999 and revised in 2009), Environmental Protection Agency
Act, 1994 (Act 490), Environmental Assessment Regulations, 1999 (L.I 1652 , Ghana Landﬁll Guide-
lines, 2002 and Manual for the Preparation of District Waste Management Plans in Ghana (Ackah, 2011;
Asase et al., 2009). The NESP formulated in 1999 was to serve as a guideline in protecting and main-
taining a conducive environment as well as the problems and limitations associated with environmental
31
==PAGE:47==
sanitation (Asase et al., 2009).
In Ghana, the waste management sector has been decentralized.The government has allocated respon-
sibilities to the Ministry of Local Government and Rural Development (MLGRD) to supervise the
Metropolitan, Municipal and District Assemblies (MMDAs) in mobilizing and disposing waste through
their waste management departments (Wemegah, 2015; Appiah Obeng et al., 2009). This was done
in accordance with the Local Government Act (1993), Act 462. Environmental policies are however
regulated by the Ministry of Environment and Science and Technology through the Environmental Pro-
tection Agency (EPA).The 1994 EPA Act 490 was passed to amend previous policies, consolidate laws
relating to environmental protection and to regulate the type , concentrations and disposal of waste into
the environment (Owusu-Sekyere et al., 2013). The country has since passed policies for assessing sani-
tation regulations and has frequently amended these policies to tighten loopholes (Owusu-Sekyere et al.,
2013).
Waste management and environmental health plans have been coordinated from various local govern-
ments and other agencies across the country by the National Environmental Sanitation Policy Coordi-
nation Council (NESPoCC ) in the quest to amend the NESP 1999 and add relevant legislations for the
control of waste. There has been inter-ministerial corporation between the Ministry of Health, Ministry
of Local Government and the Environment and Science Ministry to work towards constant amendment
of environmental regulations, yet, the area of solid waste management is still poses difﬁculties in the
country.
The EPA of Ghana endeavouring to promote and improve landﬁlling in Ghana developed the Landﬁll
Guidelines (2002) in order to tackle the issue of solid waste management. The document was made
serve as guidance in landﬁll site identiﬁcation and selection, design as well as operation and maintenance
(Owusu-Sekyere et al., 2013). The document also regulates the emissions from landﬁlls since the EPA is
the main regulator of emissions from landﬁlls. The EPA also put together the Manual for the Preparation
of District Waste Management Plans in Ghana to assist the district assemblies and other stakeholders in
the planning and management of waste.
The EPA of Ghana, which acts as the environmental policy enforcer is however faced with challenges of
not fully being able to decentralize its operations to the local level. Due to this, a part of the responsibility
32
==PAGE:48==
of the Ghana EPA is carried out by the waste management sector in the local assemblies. Most municipal
and district assemblies, thus, double as waste managers and waste policy enforcers. This has somehow
had consequences on the proper management of waste due to low adherence to policy resulting from
no or less supervision (Owusu-Sekyere et al., 2013). Another challenge as a developing nation comes
down to the cost of ensuring compliance with environmental regulations especially by foreign trade and
ﬁrms.
Moreover the relocation of most businesses that generate GHG into the country to take advantage of the
lax and seldomly enforced environmental regulations is also a problem (Rivera and Oh, 2013). This has
been a very critical and sensitive issue and has affected the strict compliance of environmental policies
in the country in order not to ward off foreign trade. There is room for further study on whether these
policies are effective or not, for which this study lacks the models to perform. Based on observation,
and the core principles on which these policies were established, it could be said that there have been
no improvements in the management of solid waste (Owusu-Sekyere et al., 2013; Owusu et al., 2012;
Ackah, 2011; Asase et al., 2009) .
3.7
Estimations and Modelling of GHG Emissions from Solid Waste
in Ghana
There is the need to quantify the GHGs emitted especially on landﬁlls in Ghana by using mathematical
estimations or modelling. Ghana being a party to the United Nations Framework Convention on Cli-
mate Change (UNFCCC) put together a national greenhouse inventory report for 1990 to 2006 as part
of the Second National Communication (Agyeman-Bonsu et al., 2011). Simple methods and default
values from the IPCC tier one methodology were used in generating the inventory. Some of the gases
whose GHG potentials were estimated in the inventory were carbon dioxide, methane and nitrous oxide
(Environmental Protection Agency, 2011). The total direct greenhouse gas emission in Ghana based
on carbon dioxide, methane, nitrous oxide and perﬂuorocarbons was estimated to be 12 MtCO2eq in
the year 2000 (Asante and Amuakwa-Mensah, 2014). This increased to 24 MtCO2eq in the year 2006
(Asante and Amuakwa-Mensah, 2014). This continuous increase in trend could be due to economic
growth (Asante and Amuakwa-Mensah, 2014; Agyeman-Bonsu et al., 2011).
33
==PAGE:49==
From the compilation, the waste sector was estimated to be the third largest contributor to national GHG
emissions with its main sources being solid waste disposal on dumpsites and wastewater. Between
the years 2000 and 2006, an average emission of 10% (Risoe, 2013; Agyeman-Bonsu et al., 2011;
Environmental Protection Agency, 2011). 23% of the methane generated in Ghana come from the waste
sector (Risoe, 2013).
Diawuo (2013), by analyzing the waste management practices estimated the waste contribution to GHG
emissions to be 3.8%. Keelson (2013) also used the Landgem ﬁrst order decay estimation tool to model
the methane gas emissions from two abandoned landﬁlls in Ghana namely Mallam No.1 and Oblogo
No.1. The Mallam No.1 which covers an estimated area of 8.66 hectares was opened in 1991 and
closed in 2001 whilst the Oblogo No.1 dumpsite of estimated area, 1.30 hectares was opened in 2002
and closed in 2007. The Mallam No.1 and Oblogo No.1 dumpsites both had depths and total waste
tonnage of 20.06m and 2,700,000 tonnes as well as 35.54m and 2,400,000 tonnes respectively. The
model showed generation peaks in 2002 and 2008 for the Mallam No.1 and Oblogo No.1 dumpsites
respectively and a total combined peak annual methane of 17,000 metric tonnes.
Several studies have looked and continue to look into the utilization of landﬁll gas especially methane
for the generation of energy in Ghana. In this case , installation of wells for gas extraction is ideal
(Shi et al., 2015). Unfortunately, no study has been undertaken to ﬁnd ways of mitigating landﬁll gas
emissions from old and small landﬁlls which may not be good enough for gas extraction or might still
have leakages even though gas is being extracted (Raco et al., 2010).
3.8
Drivers for Biocover Technology Implementation
A number of factors such as human safety, resource conservation and alleviation of environmental bur-
den of solid waste disposal have been the drivers pushing for an integrated municipal solid waste man-
agement practice (Asase et al., 2009). Similarly, the implementation of the biocover technology in
Ghana will require the identiﬁcation of drivers that could make it possible and achievable. Since the
implementation of the biocover technology will generate no income or resource substitutes as in the
case of gas extraction, the only driver could be the reduction of GHG emissions in the country and its
concomitant effect on climate change.

Climate change has the potential of threatening effective and sustainable development and poverty re-

in a number of sectors. The study reported expected high temperatures and less precipitation in the years

5.7.4
Limitations
Some of the challenges faced during the screening are;
• High temperature issues: From the manual of the TVA- 1000B gas analyzer, the instrument is
supposed to work within a temperature of 20◦C. A change in temperature higher than that causes
the instrument to warm up and exceed the optimized temperature. This warming was caused by
the increased temperature (above 33◦C ) of the surrounding air at the Ohwim landﬁll site. This
causes the ignition to be interrupted and cut off anytime it is re-initiated. The TVA-1000B is then
put off for the system to cool down which takes about two (2) hours to complete the process. Thus
monitoring methane gas emission in the afternoons was not an option due to the high temperatures
ranging between 30◦C and 40◦C. Measurements were therefore taken mostly at dawn or at sunset.
• Severe winds: The blowing of severe winds on the survey site affected the data collected in areas
that lie in the wind direction. When the wind blows from a more methane concentrated area to
a lesser one, there is a high probability that the less concentrated area may record high methane
values due to the movement of the winds. To curb this, some time was allowed for the passage of
the wind before measurements are taken. In some cases, the monitoring of such areas are retaken
the next day. This slows the work and leads to unwanted warm of the TVA-1000B (FID).

5.8.1
Estimation of Methane Generation-LandGEM
The LandGEM excel interface was initiated. In the user inputs worksheet, the landﬁll name and the

better the input data, the better the estimations made in the form of outputs. The model parameters k
and Lo were also chosen. In this case, the CAA conventional landﬁll default values were chosen since
there were no available data to estimate the parameters and the dump site used as the study site could
be considered conventional. The k used was 0.05 per year and Lo was 170 m3 per year. To make the
output of the model more reﬂective of a high organic waste landﬁll, a k of 0.3 per year was also used to
generate another scenario of methane generation.
0.3 yr1 was chosen because the ﬁeld value of k for food waste was proposed to be in the range of
0.289 to 0.688 yr1 (Sun et al., 2015). A group of scientist did a study on a Chinese landﬁll with 60%
organic content chose 0.3yr1. Since Ghana also has about 63% of organic waste, 0.3yr1 could be a
good k value to use. The already estimated yearly waste acceptance from the estimated waste density
and volume was then entered. After all the model inputs and parameters shown in Table 5.1 have been
entered, the estimated methane generation for k= 0.05 and k= 0.3 were reported in the results worksheet
as well as the graphs worksheet. From the methane generation per year, methane load per day and the

6.1
Moisture Content
Moisture content is one of the important factors that can determine if the soil cover can support oxidation
(Scheutz and Kjeldsen, 2004). Table 6.2 gives an overview of the estimated percentage moisture content
using four different samples. The values ranged from 21% to 28%. These values are quite similar to
percent moisture content values in previous studies that supported methane oxidation. Visvanathan
et al. (1999) reported that low moisture content could produce negligible oxidation whereas moisture
content between 15% and 20% could result in maximum oxidation rates. On the other hand, Faeiza et al.
(2015) assert thats the use of soil with moisture content of 28% could lead to signiﬁcantly low methane
oxidation efﬁciencies. Nonetheless, an addition of nutrients to a 30% moisture content soil led to an
increased methane oxidation efﬁciency whilst the addition to a 15% moisture content had a negative
effect on the methane oxidation (Albanna et al., 2007). This was attributed to the poor performance of
oxidation aiding bacteria due to microbial water stress or toxicity.
From these studies, it can be inferred that the suitable moisture content needed to aid oxidation would be
dependent on other factors as well. Hence, the Ohwim landﬁll site with the moisture content reported on
Table 6.2, may be good to support methane oxidation if nutrients in the landﬁll are not in excess to cause
toxicity or water stress. To be able to increase the efﬁcacy of the oxidation capacity, the topsoil should
have an optimal moisture content. Visvanathan et al. (1999) however indicates that the probability of
56
==PAGE:72==
getting a maximum methane oxidation in a tropical climate is high if the top soil keeps the right amount
of moisture. Since Ohwim is in the tropical forest and has a good moisture content range, it might be
able to support maximum methane oxidation.

CHAPTER 7
Conclusion and Recommendations
7.1
Conclusion
The study was set out to investigate the possibility of implementing the technological biocover solu-
tions in Ghana. Using the Ohwim landﬁll as a study site; which is representative of unengineered and
conventional landﬁlls in Ghana, a conceptual model of a biocover that could ﬁt the said landﬁll was
proposed. In order to achieve the objectives, the study sought to identify the types and amounts of solid
waste generated in Ghana and assess the possible emissions by probing the waste management system
in Ghana. In addition, the various landﬁlls types that receive the collected solid waste, were examined
to see if they are well structured to contain emissions.
To be able to predict whether Ghana would readily accept and implement the biocover technology even
though it has no monetary returns, the waste management as well as GHG policies and regulations were
studied. From the policies, possible drivers that could prompt the implementation of the technology in
Ghana were determined. The study tried to answer questions like;
• How is waste management in Ghana? Has the country moved from traditional waste management
practices to current ones?
• What are the types and amounts of solid waste generated and landﬁlled in Ghana?
• Are there any regulations on waste management in general, landﬁll designs and GHG emissions?
• How realistic is the implementation of the biocover technology?
The empirical ﬁndings were chapter speciﬁc, in that, most of the questions on solid waste management,
policies and drivers for the implementation of the biocover technology were addressed in chapter three
while the actual study on possible implementation using a study site was tackled in chapters four, ﬁve
and six. The description in chapter three revealed that the waste management practices in Ghana have not
77
==PAGE:93==
technically developed as expected since almost all the waste generated end up on landﬁlls. Moreover, the
same chapter identiﬁed the solid waste and GHG policies and the revisions made over the years as well
as the GHG emission estimates made. Furthermore, effects on climate change from GHG emissions
were recognised as the drivers that could lead to the execution of a full scale biocover project. One
particular program that points to climate change as the possible driver is the signing of the United
Nations Framework Convention of Climate Change (UNFCCC) and the drafting of the climate change
mitigation and adaptation strategy by Ghana.
In chapters four, ﬁve and six, ﬁeld monitoring and computer modelling were used to estimate the area of
the Ohwim landﬁll (study) site, volume and total amount of waste deposited there, organic and moisture
content of landﬁll soil, and the methane generated and oxidised. The area of the landﬁll was estimated
to be 12490 m2 using MapInfo GIS software. Based on availble geophysical waste thickness data for
the landﬁll, an estimated volume of 72540m3 was derived using the Geosoft software. With an assumed
waste density of 0.75tonnes/m3, the total amount of waste on the Ohwim landﬁll was estimated to be
54405 tonnes. Different soil samples from the landﬁll, used in the determination of organic and moisture
contents gave average estimates of 16.7% and 23.8% respectively.
Field screening of methane on the landﬁll with the aid of a TVA-1000B(FID) and an IR (biogas) detec-
tor reported methane concentrations on some parts of the landﬁll. The highest methane concentration
of over 1867ppmv were found at the slopy edges of the landﬁll. On the surface of the landﬁll very
low methane concentrations ranging between 0.1 and 1 ppmv were recorded. It is quite uncertain in
determining the reasons behind the variation in concentrations on the landﬁll surface and at the edges.
However, one reason could be the blockage of vertical transport of gases on the surface caused by plas-
tics bags in the waste and a high horizontal transport velocity. Also, the tendency of the landﬁll to
generate a natural biocover was considered. From the methane concentrations for each of the screen-
ing days, it was observed that days with rainfall had high methane concentrations. It can therefore
be inferred that the difference in methane concentrations for the various days might be inﬂuenced by
rainfall.
To estimate the methane generation on the Ohwim landﬁll, the ﬁrst order degradation model, LandGEM,
was used. Choosing the default k value for conventional landﬁlls and an estimated k value based on
the assumption that the landﬁll has high organic content, peak methane generation of 300Mg/yr and
78
==PAGE:94==
800Mg/yr were obtained for k=0.05 and k=0.3 respectively. The methane generated per year when
k=0.3 was higher than that of k=0.05 due to the assumed high organic content for k=0.3. From the peak
methane generation of 300Mg/yr, a methane load of 35g/m2/day is estimated and used in the LandSEM
model. The model showed the peaks of methane generation to be the year immediately after waste
disposal is stopped or the landﬁll closed. This is because the model assumes no lag phases and adds on
each years methane generation. Furthermore, the results from LandGEM emphasized the need to use
site-speciﬁc data and apply the right model parameters depending on the type of landﬁll and whether or
not it receives lots of organic material.
Finally, with the use of the LandSEM numerical model comprising a water balance, heat transport,
gas transport and oxidation modelling parts, the baseline and conceptual methane ﬂuxes and oxidation
efﬁciencies were simulated. The baseline model used a soil cover with less thickness (20cm) and or-
ganic content as compared to the conceptual model. The model also assumed a constant methane load
of 35g/m2/day being evenly distributed in the biocover proﬁle. Methane oxidation from the baseline
model was very low in months were soil moisture was less than 10% and temperature was high (about
40◦C). It was asserted that the low oxidation could be due to water stress on the part of the methan-
otrophs. On the other hand, increased oxidation was seen in months with optimal and moderate water
content and temperature. This showed that less moisture content and temperature cause a reduction or
even hinder oxidation; likewise excess moisture content and temperature. Hence, there is the need to
ﬁnd the optimal conditions when considering a biocover for a landﬁll. The oxidation efﬁciencies for
the baseline oxidation model were 12% and 22% for Vmax of 150nmols−1 g−1
drysoil and 300nmols−1
g−1
drysoil respectively.
The 1m biocover conceptual model with a high organic content, on the other hand , had methane oxida-
tion efﬁciencies of 32% and 52% for Vmax of 150nmols−1 g−1
drysoil and 300nmols−1 g−1
drysoil respec-
tively. In comparing the baseline and conceptual models, it was inferred that the thicker the biocover
layer and the higher the organic content of the biocover material, the greater the oxidation efﬁciencies.
In order to verify the increasing oxidation trend shown when an organic rich material is used, the or-
ganic content of the conceptual model was increased but the LandSEM model could not support the
simulation since it was not built for high organic content materials. To be able to achieve the same goal,
increasing Vmax values were used since it is highly correlated with the organic content of the biocover
79
==PAGE:95==
proﬁle and the bacteria population. The Vmax of 500, 700 and 1000 achieved oxidation efﬁciencies of
68%, 77% and 85% respectively. This trend meant the higher the Vmax, the higher the organic content
and the greater the oxidation efﬁciency.
From the study, a proposed biocover for the Ohwim landﬁl could be that which has an appropriate
(preferably high) organic content as part of the biocover material and also concentrates mainly on the
edges and sloped sides of the landﬁll. Using the Ohwim landﬁll as a model landﬁll for unengineered
and conventional landﬁlls in Ghana, it could be proposed that it is realistic and feasible to implement
the biocover technology in Ghana.
7.2
Recommendations
From the study, the recommendations below are made.
• Generation of a data bank for waste ﬂows(amounts and composition) and landﬁlls by the waste
management sector.
• Waste characterization of municipal solid waste currently generated and landﬁlled in Ghana.
• Longer duration of methane screening
• More estimates on landﬁll GHG emissions
• Improve adherence of solid waste and GHG policies and regulations
7.2.1
Future Research
For future studies, it is recommended that
• An evaluation of availability and accessibility of suitable biocover materials will be done.
• An extensive methane screening with more sensitive instruments will be done for a long period
of time. Also, a full scale research and implementation of a biocover on some landﬁlls in Ghana
aiming at higher oxidation efﬁciencies while using low technical solutions and materials is rec-
ommended.
