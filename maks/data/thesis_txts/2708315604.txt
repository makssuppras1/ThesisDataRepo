Abstract
Accurate and scalable shading modelling is important for standalone products with small
photovoltaic arrays, especially in urban environments that often feature complex nearby
shading obstructions. To model such obstructions, fisheye images are traditionally used,
but they are not conveniently scalable to support large quantities of shading assessments.
Meanwhile, area coverage and resolution of digital surface models have improved, in­
creasing the suitability of such geospatial data for shading assessments. An even more
detailed measure of local obstructions can be provided by 3D scanning using terrestrial
LiDAR. Shading modelling based on public DSM data and local LiDAR measurements, in
comparison to fisheye based methods, is assessed in this work.
At different locations in urban environments, it was found, that a DSM can conveniently
be used to model obstructions, with slightly lower, but competitive accuracy compared to
fisheye images. Its weaknesses are complex geometries such as trees and light poles,
leading to misrepresentation of both diffuse and direct irradiance. The results suggest,
that fully automated and remote shading modelling, based on DSMs, is already com­
petitive to contemporary fisheye based methods and can therefore help to scale up the
deployment of standalone PV products. Local LiDAR measurements represented com­
plex shapes much more accurately, and were therefore used to enhance details of DSM
data, leading to a decrease in residuals for direct irradiance modelling. For a complex en­
vironment dominated by trees, the LiDAR based shading modelling reduced the DSM’s
­23.44 W/m² long term mean bias error significantly to 1.54 W/m².

Introduction
Anthropogenic green house gas (GHG) emissions have led to an increase of global sur­
face temperatures. In fact, according to the Summary for Policymakers (SPM) 2021 report
by the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC), each of the last four decades
has been successively warmer than any decade before since 1850, while precipitation
over land increased and glaciers globally retreated, to name only a few climate change
consequences [1].
With a reported green house gas emissions share of over 70%, cities worldwide play a
major role in this man made phenomenon and therefore also bear great responsibility for
the decarbonization process. Figure 1.1 illustrates the 167 most polluting cities worldwide,
with emissions between 3.5 kt CO2­eq and 199.7 Mt CO2­eq. The largest polluting cities,
with regard to total green house gas (GHG) emissions, are located in Asian and European
countries [2]. However, the figure does not take population density into account, which,
especially in many cities in East Asia, explains high energy demand and subsequently
high GHG emissions.
For these cities the main contributing sectors to GHG emissions are related to industry,
waste and agriculture but also to energy supply for transportation, electricity generation
as well as commercial and residential buildings [3]. In an assessment of urban CO2 mit­
igation strategies in 124 European cities, the energy demand of the building sector was
categorized as the most relevant source of emissions, accounting for a total of 49%, and
suggesting it as the main focus of city action for emission reduction [3].
Figure 1.1: Total GHG emissions (Mt CO2­eq) in cities worldwide [2]
Therefore, advancing the decarbonization of these sectors goes a long way towards re­
ducing cities overall polluting impact. Electricity generation for commercial and residential
energy demand is where decentralized and sustainable energy sources come into play
to produce the required energy in small scale facilities and in proximity to where it is con­
sumed. Solar photovoltaic (PV) and solar thermal systems perfectly suit this cause and
can play a substantial role in the green transition within the residential and commercial
building sector by supplying emission­free electricity and heat.

Apart from the residential and commercial building sector, PV installations can power a
number of applications whose cumulative consumption can contribute greatly to a cities
overall emissions. As an example, the power supply for urban lighting has a large impact
on overall emissions, accounting for up to 40% of the municipalities’ electricity budget [4].
Changing the structure from grid dependent appliances to self­sufficient, self­powered
standalone products therefore has great potential to help cities further reduce their emis­
sions. It further helps to relieve local electricity grids, by reducing the overall capacity
needed to supply electricity demand. In the light of increasing share of renewable elec­
tricity sources, that lead to higher volatility of electricity production and increased stress
on the grid, this measured could help to reduce the need for grid expansions. This is
especially true for developing cities with in­stable grids.
Standalone products, whether they are city lights, traffic signs or other applications, often
utilize a solar panel to power themselves in combination with a battery to bridge time pe­
riods when there is not enough solar irradiance available to sustain operation. Generally,
self­sufficient products need to guarantee continuous and reliable operation in order to
fulfill their intended purpose. One example is a solar lighting solution, shown in Figure
1.2 which is used for public transit and promises to enhance passenger safety as well as
operator visibility [5].
Figure 1.2: PV Standalone street light for safety and visibility [6]
Standalone PV products have to be designed to last throughout the night, where the
absence of sunlight leaves them relying on their internal battery. Additionally, it has to
be ensured that even on winter days with the least sun hours or several consecutive
days without direct sunlight, the battery has enough capacity to fully supply the product’s
energy demand. In order to guarantee the functionality of these standalone products,
not only dimensioning of PV panel and battery are important, but also assessment of the
environment, and it’s potential to cast shadows on the panel throughout the year.
1.1
State­of­the­art in shading modelling
A key component of shading modelling is to model horizon obstructions around a location,
that can block sunlight from reaching the plane of array of a PV system. Throughout this
report, the term ’shading’ describes the reduction of solar irradiance at a specific location
2

==PAGE:13==
caused by obstacles. In current literature, four approaches for this can be identified: Using
3D modelling software, fisheye images in combination with image processing, DSM or Li­
DAR based modelling. It has to be mentioned that DSM and LiDAR can be closely related,
since DSMs are often derived from raw LiDAR data. However, as there are other sources
to create DSMs and LiDAR data can furthermore be used without being processed to a
digital elevation model, they are each treated individually within this section.
3D modelling software
A widely used approach to estimate shading losses for residential, commercial, or utility­
scale PV installations is to use manual 3D modelling software, such as Blender or Sketchup
to model the environment around a location of interest and base shading calculations on
that model. Certain solar PV­planning software applications, such as PV*SOL [7], can
import these prefabricated files and base their shading calculations on them when sim­
ulating system performance. Other software, for example System Advisor Model (SAM)
[8] or PVsyst [9], have a fully integrated option to create a model of the surrounding en­
vironment. SAM is an example of a free software that offers two ways of taking shading
losses into account. One way is to use shading information from on­site surveys and
measurements (see Section 2.3.1), whereas the other way calculates obstruction of di­
rect and diffuse sunlight based on the modelled 3D environment, the coordinates of the
location and relative sun position throughout the year. As a result of these calculations a
month­by­hour shading loss table is created, which is taken into account when simulating
the PV­plant’s yearly production and performance [10].
In general, software based 3­dimensional environment modelling is helpful and conve­
nient when performing a basic analysis of shading impacts of a few objects, without having
pre­existing location specific shading information available, access to sensors, cameras
or other tools for gathering on­site data. Also, if the accessibility to a location of interest
is restricted, a 3D model of shading objects can be a good workaround. A drawback of
3D modelling in these software applications is, that due to limited shape options and the
complex nature of certain objects such as trees, an accurate description of the horizon
obstruction might require sophisticated and complex effort.
These modelling tools are listed to ensure a more complete overview of the most com­
mon, state­of­the­art methods, however they are not going to be applied or investigated
throughout this work. Since the focus lies on shading modelling for PIPV deployed in ur­
ban environments, with typically complex surroundings, it is considered to be unfeasible
to use manual 3D modelling.
Hemispherical fisheye sky imaging
Fisheye cameras can acquire full 180° field of view images by means of wide angular
lenses. When these cameras are placed horizontally and facing upwards, they can take
full sky hemisphere images and from those, a representation of obstructing elements in
the surrounding area can be derived. Wide angular lenses distort the representation of
pictured elements following geometrical relations (also known as projection type) which
merely depends on their construction. However, if the projection type of the lens is known,
the image can be processed to obtain a characterization of the shading elements [11].
Afterwards, if that information is combined with the yearly sun path at a specific location,
a shading analysis can be performed.
Several authors have used fisheye images of the sky hemisphere to measure horizon ob­
struction at specific locations. Laungrungthip et al. developed a method to separate sky
from other objects using image processing [12]. Combining that with a sun path model,

they calculated the solar exposure of a designated location. Duluk et al. compared sev­
eral available commercial tools for shading estimation, including image­based methods,
with regard to their practical limitations, price, ease of use and recognized types of ob­
structions. While they found mayor differences between the solar access estimation of
these tools, they did not quantify their accuracy [13]. Isabella et al. used, among other
approaches, 3D CAD models, fisheye images or satellite based LiDAR data to capture
sky obstructions and model the performance of standalone PV products [14]. They report
an error of less than 1% of annual AC energy­yield, yet it is not specified which obstruction
characterization approach obtained such results. On the other hand, Alvarez et al. quan­
tified the accuracy of fisheye image based shading modelling using on­site measurement
validation [15]. The authors expose, that on short time scales, small errors in the horizon
line estimation can lead to high irradiance estimation errors, while these errors almost
average out for sampling periods over 15 minutes. They also found, that the horizon ob­
struction characterization of their fisheye­based methods tended to lead to overestimated
direct beam shading.
Generally, fisheye images are relatively familiar, easy to acquire and can provide accurate
information about horizon obstructions at a given location. Therefore, they are a popular
method for the estimation of shading losses. However, the method lacks scalability due
to the manual process of taking fisheye images on­site, which requires access to the
area or location of interest. Furthermore, for larger projects, featuring many locations,
acquiring the necessary horizon information with fisheye images becomes expensive and
time­consuming, making it unfeasible.
Digital Surface Models (DSM)
Digital Surface Models (DSM) are geospatial digitized maps representing a specified area
of the earth’s surface including all objects on it in a certain resolution. The Global Position­
ing System (GPS), digital photogrammetry, LiDAR and Advanced Space borne thermal
emission and reflection radiometry (ASTER) are among current technologies to acquire
these digital height maps [16].
It is important to mention that there is a variety of closely related data products that all
contain elevation information, but different types of features. Zhou states, that while there
are three commonly used terms for these digital elevation models, there is no universally
accepted definition for them [17]. The most widely used data products are named Digital
Elevation Model (DEM), Digital Terrain Model (DTM) and Digital Surface Model (DSM)
[18]. Throughout this report, the definition for these terms are: A DEM represents the
bare­earth surface, excluding any natural and built features. A DTM adds to a DEM by in­
corporating features of the natural terrain, such as rivers, water bodies and ridges, making
it more interesting for cartographic representations. Finally, the already mentioned DSM
captures all natural and built or artificial features of the environment such as vegetation,
houses, cars or power lines. In general, these data products are usually derived from the
same underlying measured data set. That is why, in the literature, DEM is often used
as an umbrella term for all of them, including for data sets mentioned in this report (i.e.
DK­DEM, SRTM DEM and TanDEM­x).
A comparison of the three types of elevation models is shown in Figure 1.3. In order to
give the maps more depth, a hill­shade effect has been applied to them, which helps to
enhance the three­dimensional appearance of the terrain by using patterns of light and
shadow.

As of today, DEMs are critical for land­use planing or flow­direction studies in case of
flooding for example, whereas the additional information in DSMs leads to different ap­
pliance fields such as forestry management or urban planning, where for example the
impact of proposed new buildings on the urban landscape can be investigated [18].
Digital surface models have already been utilized to model near and far horizon shading
on designated locations. For example, Nielsen et al. analyzed and quantified the shadow
cast on a Danish lake surface, based on a Danish DSM, by developing a Python­based
framework encompassing existing functions embedded in the open source QGIS platform
[19]. They concluded that the framework allows the assessment of potential shadows cast
on lakes and reservoirs, however it does not incorporate seasonal variations in vegetation
leaf coverage. Kodysh et al. presented a methodology for estimating solar potential on
multiple building rooftops for photovoltaic systems. The daily or monthly solar energy was
estimated using a DSM in combination with a geographic information system approach

[20].
A drawback of modelling shading obstructions based on digital elevation maps is, that it
is limited by the coverage and resolution of available data and therefore, might lead to
inaccurate and unreliable results. In general, when working with geospatial data, it has
to be made sure, that it is sufficiently up­to­date. Landscapes and urban environments
change over time, and elevation maps might not get updated often.
On the other hand, a major advantage of modelling shading using DSM data is, that it
can be completely automated, which allows for remote analysis without having access
to the locations of interest, therefore significantly reducing time spending. By increasing
data resolution of DSMs, the models based on them also become increasingly accurate
and reliable. Furthermore, coverage of high resolution DSM keeps growing worldwide,

LiDAR
LiDAR uses electromagnetic radiation ­ most commonly above the visible spectrum ­ to
compute the distance from the sensor to surrounding objects by measuring the time it
takes for reflected light to return to the sensor. Furthermore, the energy loss of the emitted
compared to the returning light pulse gives information about optical properties of the
surface that has been scanned. The fact that LiDAR is an active remote sensing system,
emitting energy through a transmitter, makes it independent of daytime as it can be used
in the absence of light. If the position of the sensor is accurately measured (e.g. through

1.2
Motivation
While for commercial or even residential PV plants, shading loss is a minor concern,
standalone products require high accuracy shading modelling. Urban environments often
feature significantly more shade from predominantly nearby shading objects. Since the
solar panel area on standalone products is typically small, shading can be one of the main
sources of energy yield loss, leading to unreliable functionality.
Companies that offer standalone products and their customers have an interest in knowing
the available irradiance for a specific area, which is an important input for the choice of
an appropriate deployment location or the correct sizing of relevant components such
as battery and solar panel. Accurate shading modelling can therefore help to ensure a
continuous and reliable functionality of standalone products.
The industry for standalone solar­powered products is small compared to PV rooftop in­
stallations and PV plants. Sources on market share and market development of stan­
dalone products are scarce. However, one small scale standalone application, where
market information was available, refers to solar street lighting. The 2019 global status
report on renewables in cities by the REN21 community, states, that the sales of solar
street lighting increased by more than 70% between 2013 and 2017 where the majority
was sold as standalone systems and only less than 25% of them were grid connected.
While the largest markets for these products are identified to be in Asia and the Pacific,
other areas such as the Middle East and Africa account for around one third of sales.
Furthermore, the potential of PV powered street lights in sub­Saharan Africa alone is
estimated at around 96­160 GW [31]. Based on this information, the market for all stan­
dalone products is expected to grow. In order to help develop this market, accurate and
conveniently scalable shading modelling can be a competitive advantage to install larger
amounts of products.
The most prevalent approach to model shading is to use fisheye images, which, while
being relatively accurate, is limited in terms of scalability, since a lot of manual input is
required when taking images for every single location of interest. Other methods using
geospatial data, such as digital surface models and 3D light detection and ranging data,
are promising to help overcome these shortcomings. Recent improvements in data quality
of digital surface models have increased the suitability for shading modelling applications:
data coverage has increased, while at the same time data comes at a higher resolution
[22], [32]. Thus, DSM­based methods could gain worldwide applicability and improve in
accuracy compared to other irradiance loss modelling methods, and therefore enhance
trust in stand­alone products and their adoption in urban environments.
One high resolution digital elevation model is the Danish DK­DEM, which covers the entire
country of Denmark. While it has been used by Nielsen et al. [19] to model shading
on a Danish lake, it has not been applied to perform shading analysis for PV purposes
yet. LiDAR technology opens a way to measure 3D information in greater detail than
in a DSM, because a DSM only reflects the maximum elevation of obstructions. LiDAR
can therefore be used to assess if geospatial data offers potential for higher accuracy in
shading modelling.

Methodology
This chapter describes the methodology of different shading modelling approaches, as
well as the tools and equipment used. First, information on how solar irradiance can be
understood from a modelling perspective is presented. Afterwards, it is explored, how
shading is modelled in various ways based on this understanding of irradiance.
2.1
Solar irradiance modelling
For modelling shaded irradiance at a given location, first the solar geometry and its com­
ponents have to be introduced. This allows for an accurate description of the solar position
and, later on, a distinguished consideration of the effect of shading obstructions on the so­
lar components. Additionally, the shading modelling’s underlying solar irradiance model
is introduced.
2.1.1
Solar geometry and solar irradiance components
In order to understand the principles of shading modelling for solar energy harvest, some
basic terms need to be defined. A comprehensive source used for the terminology here
are the Sandia National Laboratories [33]. An important aspect of solar irradiance mod­
elling is to describe the position of the sun at any given time. Its relative position to any
point on the earth’s surface can be described by the following angles: solar azimuth, solar
elevation or solar zenith. These angles are defined and illustrated in Figure 2.1:
• Azimuth angle describes the horizontal direction of the sun and is denoted γ. It
marks the angle between north and the horizontal position of the sun in a clockwise
rotation. Throughout this project, the azimuth angles are therefore defined as:
north = 0°, east = 90°, south = 180° and west = 270°
• Elevation angle describes the vertical angle between the horizon and the sun, and
is referred to as α. It is negative when the sun is below the horizon, otherwise it
ranges from 0° to 90°
• Zenith angle describes the angle between the vertical axis and the sun, denoted
θZ. It can also be calculated as 90° minus the elevation angle.
All angles mentioned above can be used to describe the position of other objects in the
sky, they are not limited to the sun.

Solar radiation, that travels towards the earths surface, is partially scattered by collision

For modelling purposes, irradiance needs to be defined into separate components, which

• Global Horizontal Irradiance (GHI) is described as ”the amount of terrestrial irra­

• Direct Normal Irradiance (DNI) ”is the amount of solar radiation received per unit

• Diffuse horizontal irradiance (DHI) ”is the terrestrial irradiance received by a hor­

The main principle when modelling irradiance falling on a specific surface, also called
plane of array (POA) irradiance for solar energy applications, is to split it into subcompo­

2.2
Shade characterization and irradiance loss modelling
The inputs to the shading model can be classified into two parts. First, the sun’s unshaded
irradiance and its position must be supplied to the model. Irradiance is needed in the form
of its components DNI and DHI, while the sun position can be characterized by the solar
elevation angle and solar azimuth angle. Second, horizon obstructions are needed in
order to assess irradiance loss caused by them.
An overview of the approach taken in this project is given in Figure 2.3. Four ways to
model horizon obstruction are explored in this project: fisheye images (from two different
cameras, Solmetric SunEye 210 and Ricoh Theta Z1), a DSM and 3D LiDAR measure­
ments. This obstruction information is then fed into a shaded irradiance model. All shading
modelling in this project is done for a virtual horizontal plane of array. Several locations

Horizon obstructions are measured at the virtual plane of array by one of the aforemen­

or horizon matrix (HM). A horizon line is mapping the maximum elevation angle of ob­
structions on the horizon around a location for each azimuth angle. In a horizon matrix
on the other hand, obstructions are mapped on an azimuth by elevation matrix containing
0 and 1, where 1 represents no shading and 0 stands for a blocked sky. Therefore, a
horizon matrix contains more information about obstructions below the maximum horizon
line. Here, a one degree resolution for both azimuth and elevation angle is used for both
horizon lines and horizon matrices.
When modelling shading, the effect of surrounding obstructions that block irradiance from
reaching a point of interest is modelled separately for the direct and diffuse irradiance
component, respectively.
The direct normal irradiance has two binary states: it is either totally blocked (0) or not
blocked (1). When modelling, the sun’s position at each time instance is compared to
the horizon line / matrix and incoming irradiance is either set to 0 if the sun is behind an
obstruction or remains unchanged if it is not. This way, a so­called shading vector (SV) is
calculated, which effectively maps the shading matrix to a time axis. This method lacks
the ability to allow a partial fraction of direct irradiance reaching a location, as would be
the case for transparent or semi­transparent objects such as glass. Future work could
explore this issue on cases with relevant obstruction.

proportion of the sky that is not blocked by any obstruction. The SVF is calculated from the

that diffuse light reaches the plane of array from all directions with equal intensity. Hence,

DSM visualization and processing
One of the objectives of this project is to determine how accurate shading on a standalone
PV­product in an urban environment can be modelled by digital elevation data. For this
purpose, the DK­DEM data set was selected, because it covers the entire area of Den­

Figure 2.8: Coverage of DK­DEM split in 100 km² tiles [45]
The DK­DEM is based on point cloud data from airborne LiDAR measurements taken by
up to four airplanes. To avoid mistakes in the measurements, the planes were scanning
the Danish landscape only on cloud free days within three seasons: autumn 2014 as
well as spring and autumn 2015. Around 415 billion data points were measured over the
entire area of Denmark. In order to provide a high resolution elevation map, scanning
was designed in a way that at least four points per square meter were mapped and stored
as a raster file with a cell edge size of 0.4 m [23]. Thereby, a horizontal resolution of
0.15 m and a vertical resolution of 0.05 m was achieved [32]. For better file handling,
the full DK­DEM is split into 10 km x 10 km tiles, which can be downloaded individually
for further processing [45]. The tile containing the relevant elevation information for this
report is marked yellow in Figure 2.8. In general, raster files are used to store, process
and display spatial data, such as height or albedo values, in a grid structure consisting
of rectangular shaped cells. Every cell contains an attribute value and a given location in
the grid. The size of the cells determine the resolution of the data that is displayed [46].
For visualization as well as editing purposes of the DSM the Geographical Information

The terrain analysis was done with the help of the r.horizon tool, which is located under

Figure 2.10: Flowchart describing the point mode of r.horizon
In order to get the horizon lines for the locations at Risø campus, the clipped DSM was
chosen as the input raster elevation map and the angle step size for the computation was
set to 1°. This was selected to get resulting horizon lines of the same azimuth resolution
as the other approaches in order to make them comparable. Furthermore, the coordinate
of the first location was input into r.horizon, as well as the desired output file name. Some
additional optional settings were adjusted to get the desired output file format: Write out­
put in degrees (default is radians); Write output in compass orientation, starting north = 0°
(default is counterclockwise, starting east = 0°). Moreover, a maximum distance to con­
sider when finding the horizon height (buffer) can be set. This buffer can greatly reduce
the computational time, if the input DSM covers a large area.
The rest of the settings were left blank or in default mode. After running the tool once, the
coordinates were changed to those of the second location and the process was repeated
until the horizon lines for every location were created and the outputs were stored in .csv
files for further processing.
The internal calculation method of the r.horizon tool is described in detail on the GRASS
website [49]. It starts computing in the eastern compass direction (E = 0°), seen from the
centre coordinates’ location, and rotates the azimuth angle counterclockwise in a defined
increment (set by the angle step input parameter: step) towards north (N = 90°), west (W
= 180°) and south (S = 270°) until all 360° have been covered. For each azimuth direction
the tool starts at a very shallow elevation angle and then follows the line of sight, checking
20 Assessment of shading modelling methods for solar powered products deployed in urban environments
==PAGE:31==
whether it hits an obstruction. When this happens, the elevation angle is increased to
pass just above that point and the process is repeated until either the line of sight reaches
a point higher than any point in the DSM or until the line of sight reaches the boarder
of the DSM region. Alternatively, the optionally set buffer limits this region. For either
scenario, the preceding obstructed elevation angle is stored in a .csv file along with its
respective azimuth angle. This way a full horizon line is created. It should be mentioned
that r.horizon takes the curvature of the earth into account for this calculation, making
remote features seem lower than their actual measured height. As mentioned earlier, the
output of the resulting horizon line can be altered to start at 0° = northern orientation and
rotate clockwise by changing an optional setting.
2.3.3
Measuring nearby obstructions using a 360° LiDAR sensor
There is a wide range of available LiDAR sensors that have different technical specifica­
tions and price points for all types of applications. They can for instance be short range or
very long range and can vary greatly in their horizontal and vertical resolution as well as
their field of view. For example, airborne LiDAR can be used for high resolution forest and
coastal mapping while shorter range LiDAR sensors might be used in autonomous vehi­
cles, driving assisted systems or for detailed terrestrial laser scanning. The latter refers
to a ground based method to acquire a highly dense and accurate 3D point cloud of the
surrounding environment. The objective of on­site LiDAR measurements for this project
is primarily the enhancement of the DSM raster.
LiDAR measurements
To measure LiDAR data points, Robosenses’ ”RS­Bpearl” was used, which is a sensor
intended to be used for autonomous vehicles [50]. It features a 90° vertical and 360° hori­
zontal field of view which makes it suitable for this project’s purpose. The full specifications
of the sensor are given in Appendix A.7.
The RS­Bpearl comes with a custom software provided by Robosense called Rsview. In
the software, a live view of the currently measured positional data can be displayed and
recorded as a .pcab file. In addition, a ’snapshot’ of the current measurement can be ob­
tained in .csv format. The sensor can be operated in 3 different return modes: strongest,
last and dual. In the strongest return mode, only the strongest reflected return from each
light pulse is output. Similarly, for the last return mode, only the last return value is output.
If set to dual, both are output simultaneously. For the present measurements, the dual
mode was used. The number of points is therefore doubled, leading to an enhancement
of the targets details [51].
To mount the LiDAR sensor, different camera tripods were investigated and the Manfrotto
475B was selected. The most important feature of the tripod for this purpose is a middle
column that can be extended separately. This way, multiple measurements from differ­
ent heights could be done, while ensuring, that nothing but the height changed between
measurements (i.e. azimuth and tilt of the sensor stayed constant).
An adapter was designed and 3D printed that allowed the mounting of the sensor to the
tripod. The sensor was attached with three M3 screws to the adapter, which has a 1/4
inch and a 3/8 inch thread. These are two industry standard sizes, making the adaptor
compatible with most standard tripods.
The sensor height above ground was measured and later used to find the relative position
of the LiDAR sensor from the modelled plane of array. The orientation of the sensor was
adjusted using a compass. In the manual, it is specified that the sensor’s Y­Axis is aligned

DSM enhancement with LiDAR data
The recorded and combined LiDAR data comes with some limitations: because the sensor
could not be mounted on ground level, (minimum height of the sensor on the tripod is

the elevation to which data is missing). In addition, when recording data, the laser turns

Consequently, it records data in 32 horizontal stripes with a certain angular difference (see
Chapter A.1 in Appendix for details). The LiDAR data therefore misses nearby low objects.
In addition, the size of the gaps between the horizontal stripes of data increases with
distance from the sensor. An example of LiDAR data with included distance information
is given in Appendix A.6.
Thus, generally speaking, the LiDAR data is best for nearby objects. As the sensor did
not move in space (apart from in the vertical axis) away from the location, the accuracy
and density of measured points decreases with distance from the location. Complemen­
tary, the DSM information is suitable to add detail for more distant objects, as the data is
conveniently available and the lower resolution matters less for objects that are far away
from the location or non­complex features, such as buildings.

Validation approach

The pyranometer is encased together with a battery and a Campbell Scientific CR300 Se­

be evaluated.

Special characteristics of the Smartladder
The Smartladder Location differs from the other locations in a few aspects. As mentioned
before, there are continuous shaded irradiance measurements available for a full year,
recorded by a pyranometer, that is part of the fixed installation. For this reason, that
pyranometer has not been placed next to the weather station, to identify its accuracy or
any tendency to over­ or underestimate the measured irradiance, as was done for the
portable pyranometer.
Other ways of investigating a possible bias were considered, such as doing a comparison
of the SL pyranometer and weather station measurements for solar noon of different days,
as there are no obstructions causing shading during that time of the day. Another idea was
to compare the portable with the SL pyranometer, and conclude on inaccuracies, based
on the deviation between the two. In the end, such validations were not performed here,
due to concerns with their accuracy and time constraints. The Smartladder pyranometer
is, however, of the same type as the portable pyranometer and is therefore expected to
behave similarly.
Additionally, because of the fixed setup of the Smartladder and pyranometer, shown in
Figure 3.11, the LiDAR measurement procedure had to be adapted. The camera tripod
had to be placed differently, and all measurements were taken at higher elevations above
ground, as the Smartladder construction was in the way of the tripod’s center column.
The measurement procedure itself was the same as described in Section 2.3.3, but the
minimum and maximum height above ground were 64 cm and 90 cm respectively.

graph, the data set has been split into two parts, based on a diffuse fraction (DF), seen

The figure shows systematic underestimation of GHI for azimuth angles under 200° and
overestimation for higher angles. It is believed that these underestimations for azimuth <
200° are caused by reflected irradiance from the facade of the very close building to the
west. When solar irradiance hits even surfaces such as windows or metal frames, it can
cause specular reflections, while the rough brick wall can cause diffuse reflections. These
effects can lead to higher measured irradiance at the pyranometer, when compared to the
predicted values, since the reflections are not accounted for in any of the models.
It is noticeable, that no effect from reflections seems to be seen in the results for Location
2, although it is only about 10­15 meters away from Location 1. This can be explained by
the fact, that a large part of the wall is made up of a bright colored brick, which causes
scattered reflections, that are of a diffuse nature. These reflections should therefore only
contribute to a small extent to irradiance further away from the walls, because the POA’s
viewfactor of the wall decreases with distance.
On the other hand, irradiance is systematically overestimated for azimuths > 200°. A po­
tential explanation for this are inaccuracies with the modelling approach, which is based
on the basic isotropic model. In this model, circumsolar irradiance is not explicitly sepa­
rated, but rather counted as a part of diffuse irradiance. As there is a building after 200°,
the models will block out only the components, that are attributed to direct sunlight. Be­
cause the circumsolar part is not counted as direct, it is not blocked, and the models are
therefore overestimating.

5
Conclusion
The main objective of this project was to evaluate, to which extent a digital surface model
and on­site LiDAR measurements can be used to estimate nearby and horizon shading
in urban environments, and how they compare to fisheye based approaches.
In this work, DSM data from the Danish DK­DEM was used to model obstructions. A
procedure to process the data and make it usable for shading modelling was developed.
This included selecting a part of the data, corresponding to the studied area, imposing
a coordinate reference system using QGIS, and calculating a horizon line from it with
GRASS GIS’s r.horizon function. The shading modelling results show, that the modelled
obstructions are generally realistic, although they lack detail of more complex shapes
such as trees or light poles. However, the accuracy of the DSM­derived model proved to
be competitive to fisheye images, for shaded irradiance modelling.
In addition, obstructions were modelled using local LiDAR measurements, conducted with
a height­adjustable, tripod­mounted LiDAR sensor with a 360° horizontal by 90° vertical
field of view. The captured 3D point cloud data was processed in Python to create a hori­
zon matrix mapping the obstructions. The LiDAR data showed a much greater level of
detail compared to the DSM for nearby trees, buildings and other features. However, the
LiDAR measurements taken were missing data for lower elevations, due to the minimum
height of the tripod. Further, more distant features are characterized in lower resolution
with larger gaps between data points. For these reasons, the LiDAR data were combined
with the DSM horizon line, using the LiDAR for closer features and DSM for further fea­
tures, e.g. using the strength of both methods. In the subsequent shading modelling,
it was proven, that the combination of LiDAR and DSM data into an enhanced horizon
matrix, creates competitive results, compared to fisheye and DSM based modelling.
The first sub objective was to evaluate the accuracy of DSM derived horizon shading data
compared to fisheye imaging using ground truth irradiance measurements.
The accuracy of horizon obstruction mapping based on the publicly available DSM was
evaluated for several locations resembling urban environments, that feature a variety of
different objects, including buildings, light poles, non­permanent objects and trees. The
assessment of the DSM’s accuracy was done by measuring on­site shaded irradiance for
each location with a portable pyranometer as ground truth. These observed irradiance
values were then compared to the modelled shaded irradiance to validate the obstruction
modelling of each approach.
It was found, that the DSM pictures objects with simple geometry, such as buildings, well.
However, more complex objects such as trees are displayed in a simplified shape, due
to the fact, that a DSM only contains maximum elevation information for each data point.
Therefore, the modelling of direct irradiance around edges of such shapes more frequently
leads to residuals.
At locations with less complex shaped obstructions, the DSM is therefore sufficient for
modelling shaded irradiance. This is because the SVF from DSM was found to be very
similar to the Fisheye methods. Predictions of the DSM model for overcast days, where
the DHI makes up the majority of the measured GHI, show a very close match to those
of the fisheye methods.

==PAGE:71==
On sunny days, with a high DNI component, the accuracy of the results from the DSM
model keeps up with the fisheye, showing better or similar RSME and MAE values than
fisheye. Here, the most decisive factor is the accurate detection and mapping of obstacles
within a location’s environment.
The second sub objective was to investigate, if the accuracy of horizon shading estimation
from DSM can be enhanced by local LiDAR measurements.
The same approach and locations were used, for assessing accuracy of the LiDAR en­
hanced DSM model. It was investigated, if the combination of on­site LiDAR measure­
ments with DSM data increased the level of detail and improved the accuracy of the shad­
ing modelling.
For specific cases that involve complex shapes, the LiDAR model led to a better map­
ping of the surrounding shading obstructions, and therefore a more accurate modelling of
shaded irradiance.
Location A, both during the summer and winter period, is exemplary for such an envi­
ronment, with multiple trees, deciduous and coniferous, and buildings surrounding the
location. The results from this study case show, that the additional 3D information pro­
vided by LiDAR, leads to a more accurate modelling of shaded irradiance, resulting in
the lowest error metrics. This is due to a much higher level of detail on the surrounding
environment, that a horizon matrix provides compared to a horizon line. For a location in
a park­like environment, low sun elevations (winter) might allow for a significant amount
of direct sunlight to reach the location, and a matrix has the potential to correctly model
this.
This could be a crucial factor to avoid over dimensioning of components in standalone
solar products, since winter is the worst case scenario with the lowest irradiance intensity
and the least sun hours.
An inaccuracy of the shading modelling approach was that it includes circumsolar irra­
diance in the diffuse instead of the direct component. LiDAR modelling results suffered
from this effect most, because they tended to have a higher SVF compared to the other
methods, due to the differences in SVF calculations between HL and HM. When the lo­
cation featured obstructions that blocked circumsolar irradiance, the LiDAR would there­
fore overestimate significantly more than the approaches with HM based SVF. A different
shading model, that handles circumsolar irradiance like direct irradiance, could be bene­
ficial when using HM. On days without direct irradiance, it could be shown, that the SVF
estimation resulting from a HM were generally more accurate.
The last sub objective was to evaluate the complexity and practical requirements of DSM
based shading modelling and LiDAR enhancement compared to fisheye modelling ap­
proaches.
The fisheye methods required manual steps in order to capture obstructions. At an on­site
visit, the cameras had to be positioned, and tilt angle and orientation had to be controlled.
Tilt and azimuth inaccuracies can be a main source of error, when creating horizon lines
from fisheye cameras. The SunEye provides an internal level and corrections, but the
Ricoh does not. While it is possible to correct for orientation faults in post­processing, it is
complicated to correct the tilt of an image without knowing the exact tilt­offset. Additionally,
fisheye approaches require images to be taken during the day, but even then, the sky
conditions can complicate the procedure. It is best to take images on overcast days,
however one might not always have a choice on when to acquire the images. Sunny
Assessment of shading modelling methods for solar powered products deployed in urban environments 61
==PAGE:72==
days can lead to sun glare in the images (as seen at Locations A ­ winter, SL and 1),
which might lead to building edges not being recognized correctly, while broken cloud
conditions might lead to clouds being detected as obstructions.
One of the upsides of shading modelling based on DSM data is, that once a workflow
is established, and sufficient data is available, it is a very convenient way to analyse
shading for a single location or even a bigger area. No measurement devices, manual
image taking or even access to a location of interest is required for this type of shading
assessment, making it very scalable and suitable to use, when multiple locations have
to be assessed. All required software is free, open­source based, and documentation as
well as examples of how to approach this topic are available online. Moreover, the DSM
approach can be automated using Python, further reducing effort and only requiring a
DSM and a location as input. With regard to accuracy, the nature of DSM based modelling
leads to some mistakes being easily avoided, as no manual measurements are required
that could introduce an error in tilt or orientation.
Some drawbacks of DSM based shading modelling, that became apparent during this
project, are the dependency on the DSM’s quality in terms of data resolution, coverage
and up­to­dateness. Even though data availability was not an issue for this work, it could
be for modelling in other parts of the world, where no or only low resolution data is avail­
able, which could lead to inaccurate shading modelling. The non­permanent obstruction
at location Smartladder shows the importance of the DSM being up­to­date, and how
the wrong positioning or detection of height of a feature in the surroundings can impact
modelling results.
Inaccuracy with the input coordinates, required for the GRASS GIS tool r.horizon, were
identified as a main source of error. They have to represent the location of interest very
accurately, as they strongly impact the outcome of the horizon line.
When the location’s true coordinates are even slightly misrepresented, objects in the sur­
rounding might appear distorted, or can be displayed with the wrong height or position.
The above­mentioned resolution of DSM is limiting, when it comes to modelling trees or
other complex shapes, such as street lights. Finally, the digital surface models do not
have the potential of being used for a horizon matrix, and therefore a more detailed shad­
ing modelling.
It was shown in this project that available LiDAR sensors provide sufficient range, field of
view, resolution and accuracy to be applicable for shading modelling purposes as shown
in this work. No conclusion can be drawn about the quantity of data points that is required
to obtain a reasonably good image of the environment, as this depends on many factors
such as the height­range of the tripod, the resolution and range of the sensor and distance
to surrounding objects. However, it is recommended for LiDAR measurements to use a
higher vertical range than the 26 cm used here, as the vertical resolution decreases with
increasing distance from the sensor.
Similar to taking fisheye images, LiDAR measurements required on­site visits. The equip­
ment for the setup used here was complex and required setting up a tripod and grid con­
nection, while the fisheye cameras are battery powered and only required a box to mount.
A main source of error for LiDAR measurements is to control azimuth, as is reflected in the
Smartladder results, where incorrect orientation of LiDAR lead to a high RMSE. However,
orientation can be corrected in post­processing and a future setup featuring an integrated
compass or GPS could reduce this uncertainty. Another source of error is, that the LiDAR
showed many gaps, especially for lower elevation angles and for very distant objects,
62 Assessment of shading modelling methods for solar powered products deployed in urban environments
==PAGE:73==
which needed to be filled. The gap filling algorithm could not distinguish between trees
with or without leafs, which leaves room for further investigations and improvements.
While the practical requirements show similar disadvantages as fisheye, the data acquired
with LiDAR can potentially lead to improvements over the fisheye methods. Distance
information from LiDAR measurements could be considered in future models to categorize
shading into near or far, and weighing the irradiance reduction based on that. Moreover,
LiDAR can potentially give information on the material of scanned surfaces. These two
additional inputs could be used for reflection modelling.
By creating a mobile terrestrial LiDAR platform featuring GPS, LiDAR could be used to
scan entire areas, which leads to higher flexibility compared to fisheye and the possibility
to analyse many locations, as the 3D environment could be used to create HM for multiple
coordinates (within this 3D environment). For instance, such a proposed mobile platform
could be used to enhance a DSM for areas with complex geometry within cities, such as
parks. This shows, that the LiDAR has a higher potential than what is represented in the
modelling approach here.
One practical downside of the LiDAR method is, that it requires the most expensive equip­
ment, including a tripod and the sensor itself. However, the cost do not significantly ex­
ceed those of the fisheye approaches.
5.1
Future work
Future research related to this project should use a model that treats circumsolar irra­
diance as direct instead of diffuse irradiance, such as the Perez model. In addition, a
horizon matrix should be used for more accurate SVF estimation. It is possible to calcu­
late a horizon matrix from both fisheye images and DSM, however, the DSM’s HM would
still only show maximum elevation and therefore not provide any additional information.
Future shading modelling should also account for reflections of surrounding surfaces.
Reflections could be modelled similar to the ground reflected component in the Liu Jordan
model (Equation 2.1). For example, the nearby housing facade at Location 2 could be
attributed an albedo value and a view factor. This would reduce modelling error, which
eventually allows for a better comparison between obstruction measurements.
In addition, the LiDAR data measurement procedure can be improved. The RS­Bpearl
supports external GPS receiver connections, and can therefore account for differences
in height, positioning or orientation, when moving around. Having a setup that can move
while recording, not only makes the measurements easier, but opens up a new way of
creating 3D environment models. A higher number of data points from different positions
would potentially allow creating a detailed 3D model, that is better than DSM data and
more flexible than fisheye images, as it would allow analysing multiple locations within
the environment model e.g. in a park. Eventually, if a higher field of view of the sensor
could capture the ground and low elevation angles too, the need to combine with a DSM
would be removed. Research on best practices and guidelines to automatize and optimize
this process are proposed as future work.
When the DSM horizon line is enhanced with LiDAR data, close by obstructions in the
DSM are currently removed in a manual step and replaced with a horizontal plane of
ground height of the location’s exact coordinates. Instead, the DEM could be used, to fill
up the removed areas, as it does not feature any vegetation or man made obstruction,
and would show the true ground heights.

==PAGE:74==
It should furthermore be investigated, if the DSM can be removed in a set radius around
a location, that is close enough, so LiDAR data is reliable and detailed. This would have
to be automatized and would remove the necessity to create an individual cut­out at each
location. Changing the procedure might make the results less accurate, but could save a
lot of time, when there are many locations to analyze.
Moreover, an advanced gap filling algorithm could be developed, that takes the nature of
each object into account before filling the stripes and holes. This way it could be possible
to detect for example a deciduous tree in winter time, and treat its gaps differently from
coniferous trees.
