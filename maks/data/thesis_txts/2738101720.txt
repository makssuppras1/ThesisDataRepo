Abstract 
Alzheimer disease (AD) is a debilitating neurodegenerative disorder characterized by 
progressive cognitive decline and impaired daily functioning. As the most common 
cause of dementia, AD affects millions of individuals worldwide, placing a tremendous 
emotional and financial burden on patients, their families and the healthcare system. 
Despite extensive research, current treatments for AD offer only symptomatic relief and 
fail to stop the disease progression, highlighting the urgent need for novel therapeutic 
strategies.  
One of the main challenges in developing effective AD treatment is the delivery of 
drugs to the brain. The blood-brain-barrier (BBB) is highly selective, preventing most 
drugs from reaching the central nervous system (CNS). An alternative approach could 
be by targeting the blood-cerebrospinal fluid barrier (BCSFB), which can regulate 
substance exchange but may be a more accessible target.  
This thesis explores the possibilities of developing novel PDC in the treatment of AD. 
These PDC are equipped with an acetylcholinesterase (AChE) cleavable linker, which 
is selectively cleaved in the brain to release a drug, but AChE is upregulated in patients 
with AD. An additional targeting effect would be achieved by utilizing the N-terminal 
of shuttle peptide on the surface of the Mumps virus, but recent studies performed in 
the groups of K. Qvortrup and M. Rosenkilde showed that it can facilitate transport 
through the BCSFB.  
The study successfully synthesized three distinct linker analogues, which were 
subsequently tested in release and inhibition studies. These studies revealed that the 
binding site of AChE is sensitive to steric bulk, indicating that further optimization is 
necessary to identify the most effective linker structure for the PDC.

1. Scientific background 
1.1. Alzheimer’s disease 
Alzheimer's disease (AD) is a progressive neurological disorder that affects memory- and 
thinking skills eventually affecting the ability to carry out simple day-to-day tasks [1]. AD 
typically affects individuals older than 65 but the changes that happen in the brain, often 
begin many years before any symptoms appear [2]. AD is the main cause of dementia, but 
AD describes the prolonged pathological process that unfolds over several years, whereas 
dementia refers to the late-stage tragic outcome of the disease [3]. It is estimated that 
approximately 50 million people worldwide are living with dementia [4]. However, recent 
advancements in medical science have increased the age average which consequently also 
leads to an increase in the number of people diagnosed with dementia [1].  
AD is devastating, but it is not only a personal tragedy but also a significant public health 
and social issue. It puts emotional and financial burdens on families and caregivers and the 
cost of care for AD patients is predicted to rise exponentially in the coming decades [5]. 
Therefore, a significant number of resources have been dedicated to understanding the 
cause and pathology of AD. Despite scientist’ extensive efforts, no cure has been found 
and the underlying cause is still not fully known. 
1.1.1. Alzheimer's disease pathogenesis 
Because of AD complexity and the lack of proper models to study the disease, the detailed 
pathogenesis of AD is not completely understood. Many hypotheses about AD have been 
developed and the two most known ones are the amyloid-beta hypothesis and the 
cholinergic hypothesis [1]. 
The Amyloid-beta hypothesis 
The amyloid-beta hypothesis is the most recognized among researchers and it states that 
the accumulation of β-amyloid (Aβ) peptides is a central event in the development of AD 
[6]. 
The amyloid precursor protein (APP) is a single-pass transmembrane protein that has high 
abundance in the central nervous system (CNS) [7]. APP can be processed in two types of 
pathways: the amyloidoegenetic pathway and the nonamyloidogenetic pathway (Figure 1). 
In the nonamyloidogenic pathway APP is first cleaved by the enzyme α-secretase into two 
extracellular fractions called sAPPα and C83. γ-Secretase subsequently cleaves C83, 
resulting in the release of the extracellular fraction P3 and the intracellular fraction AICD. 
Evidence suggests that sAPPα has neuroprotective properties and AICD has nuclear 
signalling functions [8]. 
In the amyloidogenic pathway, the cleavage of APP by β-secretase results in the 
extracellular sAPPβ fragment and the still membrane bound C99 fragment. C99 is further 
cleaved by γ-secretase into AICD and the “troublemaker” β-amyloid peptide [9].

The Aβ-peptide is mainly formed either in the 40 amino acid form (Aβ40) or the 42 amino 
acid form (Aβ42), but the length can vary between 39 to 43 residues. These Aβ monomers 
clump together and form insoluble amyloid fibrils or soluble amyloid oligomers. The 
amyloid fibrils can further aggregate into amyloid plaques while the oligomers might 
spread through the brain [10]. This eventually leads to neuronal cell death and 
neurodegeneration.  
The accumulation of Aβ is also thought to trigger a cascade of events that eventually lead 
to the formation of neurofibrillary tangles (NTS).  NTSs are intracellular aggregates of 
hyperphosphorylated tau protein, that normally support the microtubules in neurons. This 
function of tau is disrupted when it is hyperphosphorylated, resulting in cytoskeletal decline 
and eventually cell death [2]. 
The cholinergic hypothesis 
The cholinergic hypothesis was the first hypothesis proposed to explain the cause of AD 
and it is based on the observation that loss of cholinergic activity is normally observed in 
AD patients.  It states that AD is primarily caused by a deficiency in the production or 
function of the neurotransmitter acetylcholine (ACh) (See section 1.4) [11]. In the brain, 
ACh plays an important role in various physiological processes such as memory, sensory 
information and learning. 
Under normal conditions, cholinergic neurons in the brain, synthesize ACh from acetyl 
coenzyme A and choline, in a reaction catalysed by an enzyme called choline 
acetyltransferase (CAT). Upon arrival of neuronal impulse, ACh is released into the 
synaptic cleft where it interacts with ACh receptors located on the postsynaptic neurons

1.1.2. Current treatments on the market 
Despite the effort that has been put into AD research, current available therapeutic options 
are limited to drugs that only provide mild symptomatic benefits. The most common drugs 
found on the market today can be divided into two classes: Cholinesterase inhibitors and 
NMDA receptor antagonist.  
Cholinesterase inhibitors  
As discussed in the previous section, one of the causes of neurodegeneration in AD patients 
is the reduction in the production and uptake of ACh. Cholinesterase inhibitors (ChEI) 
alleviate the symptoms of AD by inhibiting the activity of AChE, hence increasing both 
the duration of action and level of ACh (Figure 2) [13].  
The three most common ChEIs are donepezil, rivastigmine and galantamine.  The first 
marketed drug for AD treatment, tacrine, was a nonselective, reversible ChEI, but its use 
was discontinued due to narrow therapeutic index and toxicity. The main drawback of 
ChEIs is their lack of specificity, which leads to adverse side effects [13]. 
NMDA receptor antagonist 
Aβ-peptides that accumulate in the brain of AD patients can cause abnormal rise in 
extrasynaptic glutamate levels by inhibiting glutamate uptake or triggering glutamate 
release from glial cells. The binding of glutamate to NMDA receptors results in an influx 
of extracellular calcium, which controls membrane excitability and synaptic transmission. 
Abnormally elevated glutamate levels lead to overstimulation of NMDA receptors resulting 
in excessive influx of calcium that ultimately leads to cell death [14]. 
Memantine is the only approved NMDA receptor antagonist, and it works by blocking the 
NMDA receptor and thus limiting calcium influx into the neurons (Figure 2) [13].

The newest additions to the AD treatment toolbox are the monoclonal antibodies 
Aducanumab and Lecanemab. Both work by binding to beta-amyloid plaques in the brain, 
thereby promoting their clearance. They show mixed clinical benefits and the high cost in 
addition to the accelerated approval has sparked some controversy in the scientific 
community [15].  
1.2. Drug delivery to the brain 
One of the biggest hurdles in the treatment of AD and other neurological disorders is the 
delivery of drugs to the brain. The brain is surrounded by multiple barriers that limit the 
entry of foreign molecules. This is a protective mechanism since the brain is one of the 
most important organs in the body. There are three principal blood-CNS barriers in the 
body, which are the blood-brain barrier (BBB), the blood-cerebrospinal fluid barrier 
(BCSFB) and the arachnoid barrier. Since the arachnoid barrier contribute insignificantly 
to the exchange between blood and brain compared to the other barriers, it will not be 
further discussed in this thesis [16].

1.2.1. The blood brain barrier 
The BBB is a selective semi-permeable cellular membrane between the interstitium of the 
brain and the blood. It plays a crucial role in maintaining homeostasis for optimal neural 
signalling, controlling molecular traffic and keeping harmful particles away from the brain 
[17]. It is composed of endothelial cells (ECs) that form the wall of the blood vessels, 
pericytes that partially cover the outside of the endothelial cells and astrocyte end feet that 
wrap around the vessels [18]. ECs are particularly crucial in maintaining the barrier 
functions of the BBB, employing various mechanisms for this purpose. Firstly, brain ECs 
are held together by tight junctions that serve as physical barriers and prevent movement 
of substances through the space between the cells. Secondly, they control the movement of 
ions and other molecules with specific transporters [17]. The efflux transporters are located 
on apical surface of the BBB and their role is to actively transport unwanted substances 
against their gradient out of the brain [19]. ECs also have transporters that move nutrients 
and other essentials down the concentration gradient and into the brain. Finally, ECs also 
have specialized enzymes that metabolize neurotransmitters, drugs and toxins that manage 
to get through the structural barrier[17].  
1.2.2. Crossing the BBB 
It should be evident by now that crossing the BBB is not easy. The drug penetration is 
limited to molecules with a very low molecular weight (400-500 Da) which means that 
only around 2% of small molecular drugs developed for brain disorders can pass through 
the barrier and no bigger biomolecules can enter [20]. These drugs are small, hydrophilic 
molecules that passively diffuse transcellularly across the BBB. To do that they must fulfil 
certain physicochemical properties that include size, lipophilicity, hydrogen bonding 
potential, polar surface and molecular charge. These kinds of molecules are sometimes said 
to follow the Lipinski’s rule of 5 [21].

There are some potential different routes that scientist could utilize to “smuggle” drugs into 
the brain. These routes can be split into two categories, the passive transport that is driven 
by gradient and the active transport that is energy dependent.  
Despite the tight junctions between ECs, paracellular diffusion could still occur. This type 
of transportation is mainly governed by the size of the substance, as only very small 
molecules can utilize this passage. If the properties mentioned above are considered in 
drug-design, they could potentially be manipulated at the molecular level to enhance CNS 
drug levels through passive transportation [21]. 
Some larger molecules can get through the BBB by energy-dependent mechanisms. Amino 
acids, vitamins and hormones reach the brain via carrier-mediated transport through carrier 
proteins while peptides and plasma proteins utilize receptor-mediated transport. These 
mechanisms could be explored in the search for new drug-delivery routes to the brain. As 
mentioned in section 1.2.1. efflux pumps are also energy dependent, but their job is to move 
unwanted molecules out of the brain [21]. This may cause many drugs to get expelled out 
of the brain again. Larger macromolecules use other transportation mechanism like 
adsorptive-mediated transcytosis and cell mediated transcytosis [22].

Other ways to get through the BBB have also been explored such as nanoparticles, 
antibody-drug conjugates and osmotic disruption of the BBB without much luck [24]. As 
of 2019 the FDA has not approved any biopharmaceutical, that can cross the BBB, in the 
treatment of neurological diseases [25]. There is therefore a dire need for new and

innovative ways to deliver drugs to the brain and one exciting possibility is to utilize the 
BCSFB. 
1.2.3. The blood-cerebrospinal fluid barrier 
As the name suggests, the BCSFB separates the blood and the cerebrospinal fluid (CSF). It 
is located in the ventricles of the brain and is established by choroid plexus (Figure 3). 
These specialized cells share the barrier function of the ECs in the BBB by forming tight 
junctions, while also having the unique role of secreting CSF [26]. The ECs in the BCSFB 
differ from those found in the BBB. They are fenestrated and therefore allow some free 
movement of molecules into the CSF. Ependymal cells mark the CSF-brain barrier [27]. 
The BSCFB is bi-directional, facilitating the transport of metabolic waste, proteins, and 
signalling molecules out of the brain into the CSF, while also allowing the passage of 
nutrients and other essential molecules into the brain [26]. 
1.2.4. Crossing the BCSFB 
Even though the BBB and the BCSFB share many similarities they also have some 
differences. The main difference being that the BCSFB is less restrictive than the BBB. 
This makes the BCSFB more accessible to foreign invaders than the BBB. However, 
despite this understanding, the BBB has received more attention in the efforts to deliver 
drugs to the CNS [28]. Many neurotropic viruses enter the CNS through the BCSFB. One 
of the pathogens that has utilized the BCSFB to enter the CNS is the Mumps virus (MuV).  
MuV is a single-stranded, negative-sensed RNA virus that is known to be the cause of 
Mumps. It is highly neurotropic with evidence of CNS entry in up to 50% of infection cases 
[29]. The exact mechanism of entry to the CNS is not known but experimental evidence 
suggests that the MuV enters via the BCSFB [29]. MuV expresses a small hydrophobic 
protein (SH) that is thought to be responsible for the delivery into the CNS. Recent studies 
by M. Rosenkilde and K. Qvortrup support this theory, as they discovered that that the N-
terminal of the MuV-SH is a ligand for GPR125; an adhesion G-protein coupled receptor 
expressed on the surface of the choroid plexus epithelium. In addition to that, it has been 
shown that activation of GPR125 induces endocytosis [30]. 
This discovery opens new possibilities for targeted drug delivery to the brain. By 
combining the N-terminal of MuV-SH with a therapeutic agent for a CNS disorder one 
might deliver the drug straight to the ventricles and therefore circumvent the BBB. In fact, 
M. Rosenkilde and K. Qvortrup showed that by combining the N-terminal of the MuV-SH 
with the appetite-regulating peptide drug exendin-4 increased delivery to the brain in vivo. 
These results are promising and highlight the potential of developing new site-specific CNS 
treatments.  
1.3. Protein-drug conjugates 
Targeted molecular/drug therapy is a type of personalised medical therapy mainly used for 
cancer patients. It aims to decrease the side effects of the treatment while simultaneously 
increasing the anticancer efficiency. One of the most common targeted drug therapies used 
today is antibody-drug conjugates (ADCs). ADCs contain a cytotoxic drug attached to cell-

targeting antibody via a linker. A second generation of targeted therapeutics has been 
developed, namely protein-drug conjugates (PDC) [31]. PDC are also made up of a 
cytotoxic payload and a synthetic linker but unlike ADC they utilize a targeting peptide 
instead of an antibody (Figure 5). 
 
Figure 5: A simple structure of a peptide-drug conjugate. Figure vreated with BioRender. 
An effective ADC/PDC should have certain characteristics. It should remain stable in 
circulation until the desired target is reached, where the cytotoxic payload should be 
efficiently released, hereby increasing the therapeutic effect while minimizing off-target 
effects. This can be achieved by a delivery antibody or peptide that is target specific and 
induce good cell entry, as well as a linker that is stable until it reaches the target, where it, 
upon encountering target specific conditions is efficiently cleaved to release the payload. 
The payload should only be toxic after its release [32]. Since the main focus of this thesis 
is the synthesis of new and innovative linkers, the author thinks it is worthwhile to dive a 
little deeper into current linker strategies.  
1.3.1. Linker strategies 
One of the most important things to consider when designing a PDC is the linker because 
its characteristics could impact the binding affinity and drug efficacy. As mentioned earlier, 
the linker needs to be stable until reaching its target to prevent untimely drug release 
resulting in off-target effect, but it also needs to release the payload effectively at its target 
[32]. Different linkers have been design based on the drug molecule used, handle for 
conjugation to protein/peptide, cleavability and more. These linkers can broadly be split 
into two categories, cleavable and non-cleavable [33]. 
Non-cleavable linkers 
Non-cleavable linkers are not activated through external stimulus but release the payload 
after metabolic degradation [34].  The non-cleavable linkers are relatively stable in plasma 
and show low off-site toxicity. Common non-cleavable linkers for PDC include oxime 
bonds and thioethers [34]. An example of a PDC that uses a non-cleavable linker, and its 
mechanism of action can be found in Figure 6.

Cleavable linkers 
Cleavable linkers are engineered to break down in response to certain conditions. They are 
roughly divided into two subclasses based on cleavage conditions; enzymatically cleavable 
linkers and chemically cleavable linkers [32].  
Chemically cleavable linkers include acid-cleavable linkers and reducible linkers. The 
former is cleaved under acidic conditions such as in the tumor microenvironment (pH 6.5-
6.9), endosomes (pH 5.5-6.2) and lysosomes (pH 4.5-5.0), while the latter is cleaved 
because of increased glutathione concentration in cancer cells[32], [33].  
An example of a protein-drug conjugate that utilizes acid-sensitive linkers is aldoxorubicin. 
It uses a hydrazone linker to connect the cytotoxic doxorubicin (Dox) and contains a 
maleimide handle for attachment to the protein. The Maleimide reacts with cysteine-34 of 
serum albumin to form a thioether bond and therefore a protein-drug conjugate is 
mobtained in vivo. This allows for site selective delivery in cancer treatments since albumin 
is a nutrient source for the growth of cancer cells. The Dox is only released in the acidic 
microenvironment of cancer cells after the hydrolysis of the hydrazone linker [36]. The 
mechanism of release for aldoxorubicin and its structure can be seen in Figure 7.

The most common type of reducible linker is the disulfide linker. The disulfide bond is 
usually cleaved by intracellular glutathione (GSH), which is a tripeptide that is found in 
greater concentrations in cancer cells compared to healthy cells. The fundamental concept 
behind the cleavage of these linkers is the difference in reduction potential between the 
intracellular compartment and the plasma. An advantage of the reducible linker over the 
acid-sensitive one is that it is relatively stable in circulation [37]. An example of a PDC 
with a disulfide as a linker can be seen in Figure 8.  
 
Figure 8: Example of a PDC with a disulphide linker and possible mechanism of release. Picture adapted 
from [38]. 
Enzymatically cleavable linkers exploit the high abundance of specific hydrolytic enzymes 
in cells to break down peptides and carbohydrates in a controlled manner. They can roughly 
be split into three categories that are peptide-based linkers, β-glucuronide linkers and 
phosphate-based linkers [37]. These linkers have mainly been used in the treatment of 
cancer.  
Peptide-based linkers have dipeptide bonds that are cleaved by proteases, thus leading to 
the release of a cytotoxic drug. Proteases are essential for life but despite that they can 
become dysregulated and therefore lead to diseases such as cancer and neurodegenerative 
diseases [39]. A common protease found in cancer environments is the 
matrixmetalloproteinase (MMPs) and therefore MMPs are a good stimulus to initiate the 
site-specific drug release. Other proteases that have been used as triggers are cathepsin B 
and legumain, which are overexpressed in certain cancers.  
Dipeptides are the most common peptide-based linkers for ADC and PDC [33]. The 
dipeptide PDC often include a spacer between the linker and the drug to speed up 
hydrolysis, since the bulkiness of the drug can sterically hinder the cleavage. The most used 
spacer is the p-aminobenzyl carbamate (PACB). There, a protease mediated cleavage leads 
to a 1,6-elimination that releases the cytotoxic payload, carbon dioxide and the remnants 
of the linker group (Figure 9) [39].

β-Glucuronidases are hydrolytic lysosomal enzymes that break down β-glucuronic acid

Additionally, β-glucuronidase is overexpressed in some tumour types, increasing the 
targeting effect of the PDC. Most of the drug conjugates that use β-glucuronide linkers

β-glucuronide followed by a 1,6-elimination that leads to the release of the drug in addition

design and mode of action of the targeted therapeutic. Cleavable linkers are generally 
preferred due to their enhanced drug efficiency and versatility[38]. In this thesis a cleavable 
linker cleaved by the brain-specific enzyme acetylcholinesterase will be exploited. 
1.4. Acetylcholinesterase  
As stated earlier, acetylcholinesterase is a cholinergic enzyme whose primary role is to 
break down ACh into acetic acid and choline (Figure 12) and thus terminate the impulse 
transmission between neurons. This is an extremely efficient and fast reaction as AChE 
degrades about 25000 molecules of ACh per second [40]. This predominantly takes place 
at postsynaptic neuromuscular junctions [40].

The active site of the enzyme is located 4 Ǻ from the bottom of a deep gorge and it is

a cation-π-interaction [41]. The esteratic subsite is formed by Ser203, His447 and Glu334, 
sometimes called the catalytic triad [42]. The gorge is also believed to contain additional 
subsites that serve to stabilize the transition state of the reaction or guide ACh into the 
active site (Figure 13) [42].

state of the His447. This leads to the formation of an acyl-enzyme intermediate, that is 
thought to be stabilized by Gly121, Gly122 and Ala201 residues in the oxyanion hole also 
called the acylation step. This is followed by the introduction of water into the active site

2. Project outline 
The aim of this project is to synthesize linkers that are selectively cleaved by AChE in the 
brain and incorporate them into a PDC. The PDCs would allow a brain-selective and 
efficient treatment of AD. The project is twofold. The first part focuses on the synthesis of 
various linker analogues containing different handles for attachment of the peptide in the 
PDC, while the second section is dedicated to developing a new linker design with a release 
mechanism distinct from those previously studied, allowing for a broader range of drugs to 
be released. Herein, project 1 will be referred to as P1 and project 2 will be referred to as 
P2.  
2.1. Project 1 
The purpose of this project is to synthesize different linkers, with various peptide handles. 
The objective will investigate how AChE tolerates the different handles and allow cleavage 
of the linker to release the drug. The peptide handles vary in both size and 
rigidity/flexibility. In this project a fluorophore will be installed as the model drug to allow 
easy analysis of cleavage properties. Previous results from the KQ group showed that the 
linker without the peptide handle can release the fluorophore in the presence of AChE. The 
previous studies also showed that the linkers were inhibitors of AChE, most likely due to 
the presence of the fluorophore. This characteristic could be leveraged for dual drug 
conjugates combining both AChE inhibition when the linker is attached to a drug, and after 
release, the free drug of choice can exert its therapeutic effect. To facilitate the delivery of 
PDC into the brain it is crucial to attach the SH-shuttle peptide. 
The general structure of the PDC can be seen in Figure 15. It is made up of three core 
components, the AChE cleavable linker, the amine-functionalized fluorophore and the 
peptide handle. Ideally, the final product will have an optimized peptide handle with the 
SH-shuttle peptide attached and a CNS-active drug installed instead of the fluorophore.

2.2. Project 2 
The purpose of this project is to synthesize different AChE cleavable linkers. As mentioned 
earlier, linkers previously studied in the KQ group showed inhibition properties, most likely 
because of the model drug aminocoumarin binding to the peripheral binding site of AChE. 
The objective of this new structural design is to determine if increasing the distance 
between the linker and the fluorophore minimizes inhibition, thereby proving that the linker 
itself it not an inhibitor. If so, the linker would be more versatile and allow attachment of a 
wider variety if drug structures to increase its application.  
This new linker design adds a spacer/cyclization linker that increases the distance between 
the AChE-recognizable moiety and the drug. The goal is to synthesise a couple of different 
analogues of this new linker design with the main difference between them being the 
substituent on the aromatic ring. It is hypothesized that the different electronic and steric 
effects of the substituents will affect the release rate and stability of the linker.

3.2. Synthetic strategy 
Before the synthesis could be started a detailed plan had to be made to ensure the most

The cyclic disilyl protection group is used to protect the amine instead of a more traditional

interfere with the synthesis of the Grignard reagent.  
The second step in the synthesis of 12C was a classic Grignard reaction. The freshly made 
Grignard reagent (12A) was reacted with compound 9 in situ to give 12B. Aqueous work-
up resulted in the loss of the cyclic disilyl PG. Isolation of 12B was attempted, but its 
hydrophilic nature made this process challenging. Therefore, 12B was Boc-protected

successfully be isolated in an overall yield of 28% over the three steps.  
The reaction mechanism for the second two steps of the reaction can be seen in figure 35.  
As previously mentioned, Grignard reagents are basic and nucleophilic, and therefore 
attack the electrophilic carbon of the aldehyde. This results in the breaking of the π-bond 
of the carbonyl and to form a tetrahedral alkoxide intermediate. The MgBr groups can 
coordinate with the negatively charged aldehyde oxygen and stabilize it. Water is then 
introduced to the system to protonate the oxygen and displace MgBr. This results in a newly 
formed C-C bond and a secondary alcohol, that will be used for drug attachment later on.  
 
Figure 35: Proposed mechanism for the formation of 12C. 
In the subsequent Boc-protection, the amine attacks a carbonyl site on Boc2O resulting in 
tert-butyl carbonate leaving as a leaving group (LG). NaHCO3 then deprotonates the amine 
to form the final product is. A base is not needed for the reaction to progress since tert-
butyl carbonate can also deprotect the amine, but it speeds up the reaction considerably and 
as a result a full conversion to the product was seen on the UPLC-MS after only 20 minutes.  
The t-butyloxycarbonyl (Boc) was chosen as the protecting group since it can selectively 
protect the amine in the presence of an alcohol, Boc-protected amines are relatively stable, 
and they can be easily deprotected under acidic conditions [50]

measure release by fluorimetry. In P1 7-amino-4-methyl coumarin (AMC) was chosen as

The final step in the synthesis of the AChE-cleavable moiety was the reduction of the

3.5.2. Synthesis of compound 28 
The first step in the synthesis of the cyclisation spacer involved the selective Boc-protection 
of one amine in N,N’-dimethyl-1,3-propandiamine so it could easily be attached to the 
AChE-cleavable linker. 
 
Figure 43: Reaction conditions for the synthesis of compound 29. 
To ensure that only one of the amines was protected, stoichiometry needed to be 
considered. Excess N,N’-dimethyl-1,3-propandiamine (3.1 eq.) was suspended in THF and 
the solution was cooled to 0 ̊ C before Boc2O was slowly added. This was done to slow 
down the reaction and minimize the risk of the di-protected product. This resulted in the 
desired product being formed in 53% yield. The reaction mechanism is similar to the one 
seen for product 12C, but in this case no base is used. Instead, the carbamate cation is 
deprotonated by a free amine (pKa ~ 9) rather than the tert-butyl carbonate (pKa ~ 4.5).  
 
Figure 44: Proposed reaction mechanism for product 28. 
 
3.5.3. Synthesis of compound 29a and 29b 
The next step in the cyclization spacer synthesis was to activate the unprotected amine for 
conjugation with the AChE-cleavable linker moiety. This was done by reacting 28 with 
N,N′-Carbonyl diimidazole (CDI) to form a carbamoylimidazole (29a), that is susceptible 
to nucleophilic attacks.  To enhance the electrophilic character, the carbamoylimidazole 
was transformed into its carbamoyl imidazolium salt (29b). This is a known way to activate 
poorly nucleophilic aliphatic amines and it has been used both in pharmaceutical and 
agricultural development [54]. 
==PAGE:45==
DTU Kemi 
KQ Group 
July 2024 
Page 35 of 105 
 
 
Figure 45: Reaction conditions for the synthesis of compounds 29a and 29b. 
The reaction is believed to initiate with the amine group attacking the CDI carbonyl carbon. 
This action displaces an imidazole anion, which can then deprotonate the 
carbamylimidazole nitrogen to form 29a. The methylation can then occur through a SN2 
reaction between 29a and methyl iodide, with the imidazole nitrogen that has lone pairs 
attacking MeI whith iodine acting as an excellent LG. This gives the positively charged 
product that is surprisingly stable but carbamoyl imidazolium salt have been shown to have 
superior storage properties compared to other N,N-disubstituted carbamoyl cation 
equivalents [54]. 
 
Figure 46: Proposed mechanism for the formation of 29a and 29b. 
The advantage of this approach is that is uses relatively mild reaction conditions and the 
formation of the activated product can be followed, unlike in P1. This approach can not be 
used in P1 to activate AMC since the nitrogen on AMC is not nucleophilic enough. 
Historically, phosgene has been used for the same purpose, but phosgene is a highly toxic 
gas that is both dangerous and difficult to handle. CDI is sensitive towards water so both 
reactions were carried out using dry solvents and under inert atmosphere.

The next step was to conjugate the AChE-cleavable moiety and the cyclization spacer.

3.5.7. Methylation 
The final step in the synthesis of all three analogues was the alkylation of the tertiary amine. 
This reaction yields a quaternary, charged ammonium group that is essential for enzymatic 
recognition. 
 
Figure 54: Simple schematic of the alkylation of the tertiary amine. 
The reaction proceeds according to a SN2 mechanism where the nucleophilic amine makes 
a back sided attack on the methyl iodide, resulting in a new C-N bond forming and C-I 
bond breaking. A similar mechanism can be seen in Figure 40. 
For the synthesis of the different linker analogues of this project it was important that this 
step was done in the end of the synthesis due to possible solubility issues that follow 
charged compounds in addition to the challenging purification of charged compounds. This 
step might need to be reconsidered in the future as the final therapeutic drug attached might 
have multiple nucleophilic sites that could react with methyl iodide.  
The reaction was relatively straightforward. After dissolving the synthesised reagent in 
methanol, methyl iodide was added in excess and the solution was left to stir at RT 
overnight. The crude was concentrated in vacuo, purified by reverse phase chromatography

3.6.3. Inhibition studies 
The inhibition assay is based on the Ellman method and was performed according to a 
protocol adapted from Sigma-Aldrich (MAK324, Sigma, USA). In this assay thiocholine 
is produced with AChE-mediated hydrolysis and when it reacts with 5,5’-dithiobis(2-
nitrobenzoic acid) (DTNB) it forms 5-thio-2-nitrobenzoic acid, which is yellow and 
absorbs at 412 nm. The intensity of the yellow color, measured at 412 nm, is directly 
proportional to the concentration of thiocholine, and thus to the activity of the 
cholinesterase enzyme [57]. A decrease in the intensity of the color indicates that the 
compound displaces the natural substrate (ACh) and might therefore be an inhibitor of 
AChE.

4. Future perspectives 
Due to time limitations, all the analogues were not synthesized. The next steps would 
therefore be to finish the synthesis of analogues 5, 6 and 7 in P2. Since the results of this 
thesis showed that a peptide handle is not tolerated the rest of the analogues in P1 would 
not be synthesized. Further studies are necessary to determine an effective method for 
attaching the peptide to the linker. Along the way, some synthetic steps could be optimized 
e.g. the fluorophore attachment. Based on the results from inhibition and release studies 
conducted after synthesizing and testing all analogues, an optimal structure may be 
identified, providing greater insight into the structure and mechanism of AChE.  
After optimization the final linker structure will be used to conjugate a small molecule drug 
to a shuttle peptide in a PDC. The peptide will be attached using either a CuAAC reaction 
for the peptide handle with the terminal alkyne or through peptide bond formation between 
the primary amine on the peptide handle and the carboxyl group at the peptide's C-terminus. 
The PK/PD (Pharmacokinetic/Pharmacodynamic) properties of the PDC need to be 
evaluated using both in vivo and in vitro models.

5. Conclusion 
The aim of the presented thesis was to develop, synthesize and test different AChE-clevable 
linkers that could facilitate drug delivery in the treatment of AD. The linker conjugates a 
shuttle peptide to a CNS-specific drug, thereby enhancing the specificity and effectiveness 
of the CNS treatment. Upon exposure to AChE in the brain, the linker is hydrolysed, and a 
spontaneous 1,6-elimination occurs that will either lead to a release of a drug or a further 
cyclization before the drug is release, depending on the linker design. 
Two different approaches were explored during this thesis. The first one was focused on 
making new handles for peptide conjugation, while the second one was about synthesizing 
new linker structure which allowed for more space between the AChE linker and the model-
drug to reduce inhibitor properties of the linker. Three different analogues were 
successfully synthesized and tested: two in P1 (2 and 3) and one in P2 (4). Synthetic 
procedures were started for other analogues but due to time constraints they were not 
finished. Optimization is also needed to increase the yield of some reactions, especially the 
fluorophore attachment. 
The inhibition and release profiles of all the synthesized analogues were tested using two 
methods: the adopted Ellman method for inhibition and fluorimetry for release. The studies 
were done under conditions mimicking the biological environment of the CSF. 
Additionally, compound I was included in the release studies of P1 and compounds 30 and 
II were included in the inhibition study in P2. The results from P1 showed that neither 2 or 
3 underwent enzymatic hydrolysis by AChE and neither of them was a potent inhibitor for 
the enzyme. Some hydrolysis was seen for 4, indicating that it is cleaved by AChE. 
Inhibition was also seen for compounds 4 and II, but it was not very potent. No inhibition 
was seen for compound 30. It is evident that more research needs to be done to optimize 
both linker structures for better inhibition and release rate. Once an optimized linker has 
been identified, it will revolutionize the treatment of AD and other neurological disorders 
by increasing selectivity of the drug-delivery and decreasing off target effects and therefore 
significantly improving the quality of life for both patients and their families.

performed using glass columns of varying sizes packed with Merck Geduran 60 Angstrom 
silica gel (40-64 μm particles) as the stationary phase. Eluents and volume ratios are 
specified under each reaction procedure. Solvent evaporation was done on a Heidolph 
laborota 4000 rotary evaporator efficient under reduced pressure (in vacuo). Solvent traces

B (0.1% FA in MeCN).

6.4.3. Inhibition studies 
A 0.02 M stock solution in DMSO was made for all compounds. The stock solution was 
then diluted with PBS buffer to a concentration of 1000 µM. A ten-fold serial dilution was 
performed using PBS buffer with 5% DMSO with concentrations raging from 0.00004 µM 
to 400 µM. A 0.04 µg/mL enzyme stock solution was made from AChE with a 
concentration of 250 µg/mL, using PBS as the diluting agent. The reaction mixture was 
prepared right before to give a final concentration of ATC of 3 mM and DTNB of 1mM 
using 400 mM ATC stock solution and 5mM DTNB stock solution.  
The measurement was done in a Costar 96 Flat Transparent plate and the absorbance was 
measured in a Tecan Spark® Multimode Microplate Reader. The assay was performed in 
triplicates. 50 µL of AChE was added to each well except the negative control with no 
enzyme, 50 µL of PBS was added to the well with no enzyme and then 50 µL of PBS with 
5% DMSO was added to the wells without the enzyme and an inhibitor. To the rest of the 
wells the inhibitors were added in different concentrations. The plate was then incubated at 
37 ̊ C for 10 min. 100 µL of the reaction mixture were quickly added to each well after the 
incubation using a multichannel pipette. The absorbance was measured after zero minuets 
and again after 10 minutes at 412 nm and at 37 ̊ C.  
The %inhibition was calculated with the following equation: 
%inhibition = (1-ΔATest cmp / ΔANo inhibitor) * 100% 
Where ΔATest cmp is the change in absorbance at 412 nm from time zero to 10 minutes for a 
particular compound, and ΔANo inhibitor is the change in absorbance at 412 nm from time 
zero to 10 minutes for the no-inhibitor control. The change in absorbance was calculated 
for each of the triplicates, and the average change was then used for the % inhibition 
calculations. This value was plotted up against the log([concentration]) in Prism, which 
also calculated the IC50 values.
