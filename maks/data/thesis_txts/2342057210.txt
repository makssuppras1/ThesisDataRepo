Dansk resume
Lasere der kan emittere synligt lys, har en bred vifte af anvendelser, f.eks. optisk data lagring
og læsning, kirurgi og i videnskab indenfor interferometri og ﬂuorescens mikroskopi.
Kun f˚a lasere kan dog emittere direkte i det synlige, eksempler p˚a disse er GaInP og GaN laser
dioder, helium-neon lasere og rubin lasere.
Et alternativ til disse, er ionen praseodym (Pr), der har grøn, orange, rød og dyb rød laser
overgange. De bedste resultater for laser operation af Pr, er opn˚aet med en frekvensfordoblet
optisk pumpet halvleder laser pumpet opstilling. Denne opstilling er dog ikke særlig kompakt i
forhold til en bl˚a laser diode pumpet opstilling. Derfor er det værd at undersøge bl˚a laser diode
pumpede opstillinger, selvom deres præstation ikke er p˚a niveau med de frekvensfordoblede optisk
pumpede halvleder laser pumpede opstillinger.
Det er netop en s˚adan en bl˚a laser diode pumped Pr:YLF faststof laser opstilling, der un-
dersøges og karakteriseres i denne opgave.
Lasing p˚a den røde (640 nm) 3 P0 −−→3 F2 overgang og de to dyb røde (670 nm) 3 P1 −−→3 F3
og (698 nm) 3 P0 −−→3 F3 overgange blev opn˚aet, med en rød laser eﬀekt p˚a 1.154W, der til denne
forfatters bedste viden er højere, end hvad der ellers er rapporteret i litteraturen for bl˚a laser diode
pumpede Pr:YLF faststof lasere.
Desuden blev den røde laser brugt til at generere ultraviolet laser lys i to ulineære krystaller
BBO og BiBO vha. frekvensfordobling.
Det blev undersøgt, hvad ˚arsagen var, til tærskler p˚a den røde laser, højere end hvad der ellers
er rapporteret i litteraturen og fraværet af lasing p˚a den grønne (525 nm) 3P1 + 1I6 −−→3H5
overgang. Dette førte bl.a. til m˚alinger af krystallens ﬂuorescence levetid og coating.
Ingen endelig ˚arsag p˚a ovenst˚aende problemer blev fundet, men det kunne konkluderes, at en
defekt i krystallen, der gav anledning til en høj termisk belastning, som s˚as udtrykt i en forværret
str˚ale kvalitet af den røde laser, højst sandsynligt var skyld i begge problemer.
Med den høje eﬀekt genereret p˚a den røde laser overgang, er det oplagt at fortsætte med
opstillingen, der har været brugt i denne opgave, for hvis en ny krystal anskaﬀes eller aktiv køling
anvendes p˚a den nuværende, kan det være resultater af samme slags kan opn˚as p˚a andre overgange.
I s˚a fald begynder den bl˚a laser diode pumpede opstillings præstation at kunne m˚ale sig med
den frekvensfordoblede optisk pumpede halvleder laser pumpede opstillings, og med sin kom-
pakthed, kan det være, at den bl˚a laser diode pumpede opstilling vil blive anvendt, hvis der skal
udvikles kommercielle Pr:YLF lasere til brug indenfor de mange anvendelser af synligt emitterende
lasere.

Abstract
A blue 443nm−444nm laser diode pumped Pr:YLF laser has been investigated and characterized.
Lasing on the red (640 nm) 3 P0 −−→3 F2 transition and deep red (670 nm) 3 P1 −−→3 F3 and
(698 nm) 3 P0 −−→3 F3 transitions were achieved, with a red output power of 1.154W, which to
this authors knowledge, is higher than what else have been stated in the literature for blue laser
diode pumped Pr:YLF lasers. The red laser output was frequency converted to the ultraviolet
through second harmonic generation in BBO and BiBO crystals in an extracavity conﬁguration
giving output powers of 51µW and 244µW respectively.
A higher than expected threshold on the red transition and problems generating lasing on the
green (525 nm) 3P1 + 1I6 −−→3H5 transition was also investigated, leading to measurement of the
crystal ﬂuorescence decay and crystal coating.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Lasers emitting visible light have found applications within many ﬁelds:
• Data storage and reading technology such as optical disc drives i.e. DVDs and Bluray dics
and bar code scanners.
• Display technology such as projectors and holograms.
• In medicine for surgery e.g. in ophthalmology for laser coagulation and also for cosmetic
applications such as removal of hair, stretch marks and tattoos.
• In science for spectroscopy, ﬂuorescence microscopy to excite dyes, interferometry and in
quantum optics experiments.
With so many applications, creation of lasers able to emit in the visible is a large research ﬁeld,
but few lasers exist that emit directly in the visible. Examples of these are:
• Semiconductor lasers - GaInP and GaN laser diodes (LDs) emitting in the red and blue
respectively. Unfortunately LDs has a tendency to suﬀer from poor beam quality.
• Gas lasers - Helium-neon and argon ion lasers are able to emit in the visible, but compared
to LDs they are large and bulky.
• Solid state lasers - Titanium-sapphire and ruby lasers that emit in the red and deep red
respectively.
Instead a diﬀerent approach, where light in the infrared is converted to the visible through fre-
quency conversion has been successfully employed to generate visible lasers, but these systems are
complex and have lower eﬃciency than directly emitting lasers.
An interesting alternative to the above mentioned approaches of making lasers emitting in the
visible is the praseodymium ion, emitting directly in the visible at green, orange, red and deep
red. Hence it covers a large part of the visible and it promises high eﬃciency and good beam
quality in the form of a solid state laser.
Laser operation of praseodymium is achieved by optical pumping in the blue spectral range of
440nm −480nm [1]. Early work on demonstrating the praseodymium ion’s lasing properties, was
done with ﬂash lamp and at cryogenic temperatures in 1962. Room temperature operation was
achieved with pulsed blue dye lasers in 1971 [2, 3]. As powerful continuous wave (cw) blue argon
ion and frequency doubled neodymium lasers became available in the 1990s the ﬁrst cw green,
orange, red and deep red praseodymium lasers were demonstrated [4].
In 2004 a frequency doubled optically pumped semiconductor laser (2ω-OPSL) at 480nm was
for the ﬁrst time used to pump praseodymium [5], and 4 years later, a maximum output power of
4
==PAGE:8==
4.3W green light was achieved [6]. The 2ω-OPSL scheme has demonstrated power outputs of 1W
and above for the green, orange, red and deep red transitions with eﬃciencies between 40 % and
75 % [7] and is to date, to this authors knowledge, the best performing scheme.
With the development of the blue GaN LD in 2000, the ﬁrst diode pumped scheme at 442nm
of praseodymium was demonstrated in 2004 [8]. Over the following years lasing on the green,
orange, red and deep red transitions were obtained [1, 9, 10]. Due to the power output limit of
blue LDs, the same high amount of power as for the 2ω-OPSL scheme has not been generated
on the transitions . The eﬃciency of the 2ω-OPSL scheme is also higher, due to the pump’s
better beam quality and higher Stokes factor. Yet the LD pumping scheme is worth pursuing as
it constitutes the most compact praseodymium laser to date [11].
In this thesis, a LD pumped conﬁguration of a Pr:YLF laser has been investigated and char-
acterized.
The basic theory of a laser, alongside the concept of modes are introduced in the theory section.
Here an introduction to the most basic LD theory is also given, and numerical calculations are
made of the overlap of the pump and resonator mode. The energy conﬁguration and cross sections
of Pr:YLF are discussed and a brief overview of the optical abilities of a Brewster plate and prism
are presented. Finally an introduction to nonlinear (NL) frequency conversion is given, with focus
on second harmonic generation (SHG) and how to achieve phasematch in an anisotropic medium.
Next is an overview of the laser setup, and the diﬀerent important parts of it, such as the
pump laser, focusing optics and crystal absorption and ﬂuorescence.
The results achieved on the red 640nm laser transition of praseodymium are presented, where
an output power of 1.154W is reported, the highest amount reported in the literature on this
transition for a LD pumped scheme to this authors knowledge.
Furthermore, results are presented, on achieving deep red lasing on the 670nm and 698nm
transition and dual wavelength operation on these in a Brewster plate conﬁguration.
After this follows an investigation into the thresholds of the red laser, higher than what else is
stated in the literature and the degradation of the red laser beam quality due to thermal eﬀects. It
is also investigated why green lasing could not be obtained within the Brewster plate conﬁguration.
Generation of ultraviolet (UV) laser light through SHG of the red laser has been achieved and
the results shown.
Finally comes a summary of the results obtained in the thesis ending with an outlook, con-
sidering the viability of the LD pumped Pr:YLF laser scheme compared to the 2ω-OPSL pumped
scheme as the choice of technology, should the Pr:YLF laser be commercially developed to be used
within the many ﬁelds of applications for lasers emitting in the visible.

Chapter 2
Theory
This chapter contains a theoretical introduction to many of the concepts discussed in the thesis,
its purpose to give the reader a suﬃcient background to understand the results presented later.
2.1
Laser theory
General laser theory concerning the basic operation principles of a laser, the use of a 4 level system
to achieve lasing and the characteristic form of the laser power output is covered in this section.
2.1.1
Basic principles
Basic laser principles are explained in many textbooks, this section based on the introductions
given in [12], [13] and [14].
A laser is essentially comprised of four elements:
• An optical ampliﬁer.
• An optical resonator.
• A pump source.
• An output coupling scheme.
The optical ampliﬁer consists of an active medium, such as a gas or a crystal, wherein light-matter
interaction takes place. The relevant light-matter interactions consist of the 3 processes:
1. Absorption - when an incident photon is absorbed by the active medium.
2. Stimulated emission - when an incident photon stimulates the active medium to emit a
replica of the photon.
3. Spontaneous emission - when the active medium without external inﬂuence emits a photon.
When the eﬀect of stimulated emission is to generate more photons in the resonator, than the
photons that are lost due to the collective losses of the resonator i.e. absorption, scattering, mirror
transmission, etc., the medium is said to exhibit gain and can in that case amplify radiation that
is within the gain bandwidth.
The optical resonator can as illustrated in ﬁg. 2.1 consist of two mirrors. It introduces a fre-
quency selective feedback into the laser system, reﬂecting the radiation back and forth, interacting
with the active medium on each pass.

population inversion, which with high enough pumping rate allows for more stimulated emission
than losses in the resonator i.e. the gain criteria.

2.1 be achieved by using a partially
transmitting mirror. The larger transmission, the larger resonator losses.

The laser system works, as spontaneous emission from the active medium is fed back by the
mirrors, hereby undergoing ampliﬁcation, due to the pump created population inversion. With
more passes, the laser radiation within the resonator is build up by the gain, until a saturation
point, where the gain exactly balances the losses in the resonator. Steady-state radiation is reached
and coupled out through the partial transmitting mirror.
2.1.2
4 level system
The active medium in a resonator can consist of atoms, ions or molecules, interacting with light
through their electronic energy levels. The active medium for the Pr:YLF laser is the Pr3+ ions,
which have 4-level system energy levels, that will be described referring to [13].
A schematic depiction of a 4-level system can be seen in ﬁg. 2.2.
An electron is raised from the ground level 0 to level 3 by the pump at a rate Wp. For an
eﬃcient 4-level system τ32 ≪τ31, τ30 i.e. the rapid non-radiative process transfers the electron to
level 2. In the ideal case τ10 ≪τ21 and the energy gap between the ground level and level 1 is so
large, that level 1 is not thermally populated i.e. its empty. With a long lifetime τ21, population
inversion on the lasing transition is then achieved for any given pumping rate and will be eﬃcient
if τ21 ≪τ20.

2.4.4
Numerical evaluation
The overlap coeﬃcient ηB in eq. 2.5 was evaluated numerically, the procedure described in the
following:
1. The numerical domain i.e. the crystal is deﬁned. It is divided into slices dz thick along the
z-axis. These slides are themselves divided into squares dx wide and dy high. The size of dx
and dy is chosen to properly resolve the ﬁelds, whereas the size of dz is taken as the size of
the smallest of the pump and resonator mode’s wavelength divided by an appropriate factor.
In appendix B, results for the convergence of the overlap when varying dx, dy and dz have
been investigated.
14
==PAGE:18==
2. For a given resonator the waist radius w0 of the resonator mode is calculated. The waist
radius and beam quality parameters in the x and y direction of the pump mode is deﬁned.
3. The total power of the pump and resonator mode in the slice at z = 0 is calculated. This
power should be conserved throughout the crystal.
4. The evolution of the beam widths w(z) along the z direction for the pump and resonator
mode is found through eg. 2.7. It is assumed, that if the beam width in the x or y direction
for the pump mode is lower than that of the resonator mode i.e. the pump mode is contained
within the resonator mode, that the overlap is unity.
5. If at a point along the z-direction, the beam widths of the pump mode in the x and y
direction should be larger than that of the resonator mode, then for this z-point and all
subsequent z-points, the ﬁelds are normalized and the ﬁeld products in eq. 2.13 evaluated.
The ﬁeld products are summed along the z-direction and the total power in each slice is
evaluated.
6. In the end ηB is evaluated as a product of the overlap found in the x and y direction i.e.
ηB = ηB,xηB,y.

In this thesis the studied medium for laser operation is a yttrium lithium ﬂuoride (YLF) host

In this section the energy level conﬁguration of Pr3+, the absorption and stimulated emission

2.5.1
The praseodymium ion
Pr3+ oﬀers ﬂuorescence on many visible transitions, seen in the energy scheme shown in ﬁg. 2.9.
Figure 2.9: Energy levels of the Pr3+ ion oﬀering lasing transitions in the visible, such as
green, red and orange. The pump scheme chosen in this thesis is the 3H4 −−→3 P2 transition.
Figure taken from [19].
Optical pumping is done from the 3H4 energy level to the 3PJ manifold, which is thermally
coupled to the 1I6 manifold [19]. From this manifold several transitions are possible, such as green
(525 nm, 545 nm) 3P1 + 1I6 −−→3H5, orange (607 nm, 604 nm) 3 P0 −−→3 H6, red (640 nm)
3 P0 −−→3 F2, deep red (670 nm, 695.8 nm and 698 nm) 3 P1 −−→3 F3 and 3 P0 −−→3 F3 and
far red (721 nm) 3 P0 −−→3 F4 [20].
Due to the small energetic distance between the 3HJ and 3FJ energy levels, rapid non-radiative
transfer occurs between these, restoring the electrons from the lower laser levels to the ground
16
==PAGE:20==
state 3H4, which as discussed in section 2.1.2 is an important property of a 4 level system. This
oﬀers two possible pump schemes 3H4 −−→3 P0 or, as chosen in this thesis, 3H4 −−→3 P2.
It has been observed [1, 7, 21], that the orange laser transition operates akin to a quasi 3-level
system, in the sense, that the transition suﬀers from reabsorption, not, as traditionally, due to
a thermally excited population of electrons in the 3 H6 energy level, but from the 3H4 −−→1D2

2.7
Nonlinear frequency conversion
NL frequency conversion is when light of one frequency is used to generate light at another
frequency through the interaction of the light ﬁeld with a NL medium. This concept has been
used in the work of the thesis to frequency convert red laser light at 640nm to UV.
In this section the general principle underlying the NL interaction is explained. The process
of SHG, its energy and phase match conditions are deﬁned and it is described how to achieve
phase match in an anisotropic media, and what this means for the angular acceptance bandwidth.
Finally an expression for the SHG power is introduced, alongside an explanation of how to evaluate
the expression.
2.7.1
The principle
Consider light incoming on a medium. The electric ﬁeld of the light interacts with the medium
creating a polarisation of the medium e.g. as the electronic cloud around atoms is distorted as
illustrated in ﬁg. 2.14.
Figure 2.14: It is illustrated how the E-ﬁeld of an external electromagnetic wave distorts
the electronic cloud of an atom setting up a dipole with dipole moment p. The sum of all
dipole moments gives a medium’s polarisation. Figure taken from [25].
If the response of the medium is linear, the polarisation oscillates at the same frequency as the
applied electric ﬁeld, but if the response is NL, which happens for high values of ﬁeld strength
i.e. high intensity of the light, the polarisation starts to oscillate at other frequencies. Since an
accelerated charge generates an electromagnetic wave, light at other frequencies than the incoming
light is generated, thereby leading to frequency conversion ([25], section 2.2.1).

Due to dispersion this phasematch condition is not automatically satisﬁed in any media. But in an

width. This sets a limit on the amount of incoming light that can be frequency converted, due to
the divergence of the beam.

4.4
Remarks
Two interesting observations for the red laser output were done:
1. The unusual high thresholds compared to the work of other people.
2. The degraded beam quality due to thermal eﬀects.
Considering the ﬁrst observation, eq.
2.3 shows that high thresholds can be caused by a
fast ﬂuorescence decay time τf or a low eﬃciency factor η. Looking at the eﬃciency factor as
introduced in eq. 2.5 it is equal to η = ηQηSηB.
The Stokes factor ηS is set by the wavelength of the pump and laser and thereby not diﬀerent
from what other people would have had. It can therefore be ruled out, as the reason for the
unusual high thresholds.
The calculations in section 3.4 show, that the conﬁguration of the laser setup leads to high
overlap coeﬃcients ηB and it can therefore also be ruled out.
Left is the quantum eﬃciency ηQ. Taking into account the second observation, it seems proba-
ble that the quantum eﬃciency of the crystal is low, since a low quantum eﬃciency leads to a high
thermal load, which would in turn cause thermal eﬀects in the crystal. Low quantum eﬃciency of
the crystal is therefore a very probable cause for the high thresholds.
Then what causes the low quantum eﬃciency? The unwanted crystal eﬀects introduced in
section 2.5.3 i.e. concentration quenching, color centres and dead sites are all probable causes, as
they absorb pump photons without generating lasing photons.
In section 6.1 it is investigated if concentration quenching could be the cause, by measuring the
lifetime of the crystal’s combined ﬂuorescence. Since it could be a combination of low quantum
eﬃciency and a fast ﬂuorescence decay causing the high thresholds, the lifetime measurement will
also uncover, if the decay time scale should be unusually fast.

Chapter 6
Investigation into high lasing thresholds
and absence of green lasing
After obtaining red and deep red lasing in chapter 4 and 5, the results lead to the consideration
of the following:
1. The unusual high thresholds for the red laser and the thermal eﬀects on its output.
2. The absence of green lasing in the Brewster plate conﬁguration.
The ﬁrst item lead to the conclusion, that a low quantum eﬃciency was probably the cause of
the high thresholds. In section 6.1 it is investigated, if concentration quenching is causing the
low quantum eﬃciency, as the lifetime of the crystal’s combined ﬂuorescence decay is measured.
The lifetime of the red 640nm and green 525nm ﬂuorescence decay is also measured, to see if the
transitions should behave much diﬀerently and therefore if it might be much more demanding to
achieve lasing on the green transition.
The second item lead to the conclusion, that a resonator made for the sole purpose of generating
green lasing should be made, and the coatings of the crystal and mirror measured. This is done
in section 6.2.
6.1
Fluorescence lifetime measurements
In [24] they investigate how concentration quenching eﬀects the lifetime of the 3PJ manifold, as
they measure the ﬂuorescence decay for two diﬀerent praseodymium ion concentrations 0.02 at-%
and 0.65 at-% and ﬁnd lifetimes 51µs and 36µs respectively. For the 0.65 at-% doped crystal they
ﬁnd a threshold of 53mW using an OC with R = 98%.
The laser crystal used for the results in chapter 4 has a concentration of 0.5 at-%, and gives a
threshold of 436mW for an OC with R = 98%.
Comparing the threshold at 0.65 at-% of 53mW with the threshold at 0.5 at-% of 436mW,
then should concentration quenching be the cause of the high threshold, the stated concentration
of the laser crystal must be wrong.
The lifetime measurement of the combined crystal ﬂuorescence, will therefore uncover the
following possibilities:
1. Should the lifetime measurement give a lifetime between 51µs and 36µs, the concentration
of the crystal is probably correct, and concentration quenching is not the cause of the low
quantum eﬃciency and in turn therefore not the cause of the high thresholds.
48
==PAGE:52==
2. Should the lifetime measurement be smaller than 36µs, the stated crystal concentration is
wrong and higher than 0.5 at-% and concentration quenching could therefore be the cause
of the low quantum eﬃciency and hence the high thresholds

6.3
Remarks
From the results of chapter 4 it was concluded that a low quantum eﬃciency was the reason
for the high thresholds of the red laser transition. With the investigation made in section 6.1
concentration quenching can atleast be ruled out, as the cause of the low quantum eﬃciency. Yet
the real reason is still not found.
It must simply be accepted that there is some defect within the laser crystal causing a low
quantum eﬃciency, leading to high thresholds and besides disables the crystals ability to generate
green lasing.
The low quantum eﬃciency leads to a thermal load of the crystal, which causes thermal eﬀects,
that have been observed in the degradation of the red laser output beam quality. It could be, that
the thermal load is so high, the lower levels of the laser transitions are thermally populated. The
4-level system then becomes non-ideal, and any given pump rate will no longer achieve population
inversion as described in section 2.1.2. Now there has to be excited more electrons into the upper
laser level than the amount there is thermally populated in the lower, before population inversion
can be achieved. This would explain the higher thresholds and why the green transition is more
aﬀected than the red, given its smaller energetic distance to the ground level.
Cooling the crystal would reduce the thermal load. Passive cooling is already employed in the
laser setup and in the literature it seems to be more than enough, to achieve lasing on the red,
orange and green transitions. Yet as the crystal has a defect, passive cooling might not be enough,
meaning active cooling should be employed. Unfortunately the equipment for active cooling was
not accessible in the lab.
In any case, it was decided to stop further investigations of the laser crystal, and instead utilize
the lasing results already obtained. Chapter 7 therefore contains the work of frequency doubling
the 640nm laser light to the UV.

Chapter 8
Summary and outlook
The following results have been obtained with the laser setup used in this thesis:
• Lasing on the red (640 nm) 3 P0 −−→3 F2 transition with an output power of 1.154W, higher
than what else has been stated in the literature of LD pumped Pr:YLF laser operation.
• Deep red lasing on the (670 nm) 3 P1 −−→3 F3 and (698 nm) 3 P1 −−→3 F3 transition using
a Brewster plate resonator conﬁguration. Dual-wavelength operation of the two wavelengths
was also achieved.
• Extracavity SHG of UV laser light at 320nm with output powers of 51µW and 244µW using
a BBO and BiBO NL crystal respectively.
Unusual large thresholds for the red laser, compared to the results of others, alongside thermal
eﬀects degrading the laser output beam quality were observed. It was also found, that green 525
nm lasing could not be achieved with the laser crystal. This lead to the following conclusion about
the laser crystal:
• Due to the crystal suﬀering from a low quantum eﬃciency, the lower laser levels become
thermally populated, meaning any given pumping rate no longer can achieve population
inversion, in eﬀect increasing the lasing threshold. As the green transition has a smaller
energetic distance to the ground state, it is aﬀected more.
It was investigated if concentration quenching could be the cause of the low quantum eﬃciency by
measuring the lifetime of the combined crystal ﬂuorescence decay. The result of 42µs compared
to the results obtained in the work of others, showed that the stated crystal doping concentration
0.5 at-% was correct. Hence this amount of doping, could not lead to concentration strong enough
to explain the low quantum eﬃciencies and in turn high thresholds and concentration quenching
was ruled out as the cause of the low quantum eﬃciency.
The lifetime of the red and green ﬂuorescence was also measured and found to be 45µs and
31µs respectively, i.e. no large diﬀerence to explain why one transition would lase and the other
not.
Since the laser setup has been used to achieve a higher red laser power than anywhere else stated
in the literature of LD pumped Pr:YLF laser operation, it seems worthwhile to keep investigating
the setup, as this type of result maybe could be achieved on other transitions. Therefore it could
be interesting in the future to look into:
• Actively cooling the laser crystal that has been used for the work presented here, to see if
this would make lasing on other transitions than the red attainable and what powers might
be generated on these.
62
==PAGE:66==
• Acquiring a new non-defective laser crystal, and as above, investigate lasing on other tran-
sitions and the powers that could be generated.
• Intracavity UV light generation.
Should it be shown that the LD pumped Pr:YLF laser scheme is able to generate powers on the
same magnitude as the 2ω-OPSL scheme, then with its better compactness it might become a
viable choice of technology, for developing commercially available Pr:YLF lasers to be used within
the many ﬁelds of applications for visible emitting lasers.

Determination of reflectivity (R) by transmission measurements
Optical properties of sputtered dielectric coatings
Sputtered optical coatings for the VIS and NIR exhibit extremely low scattered light and absorption losses (both in
the order of some 10-5... 10-4). This has been confirmed by direct measurements of scattered light and absorption as
well as by highly accurate reflectivity measurements (e.g. by cavity ring down spectroscopy). Knowing these very
small optical losses, the reflectivity of sputtered mirrors can be determined by measuring the transmission T and the
simple calculation R=100% - T - A
Transmission measurement vs. reflectivity measurement of optical coatings using spectrophotometry
In a normal spectrophotometer, the transmission can be measured with an accuracy of about 0.1...0.2% (depending
on the absolute value), whereas reflectivity measurements in spectrophotometers mostly have errors of about 0.5%.
Thus, the determination of the reflectivity of sputtered coatings in the VIS and NIR by transmission measurements
is much more accurate than direct reflectivity measurements.
Procedure of indirect determination of the reflectivity for any given AOI by transmission measurements
at AOI=0°
AOI (angle of incidence = angle between the incident beam and the surface normal of the substrate).
As explained above it is possible to determine the reflectivity on the base of transmission measurements at AOI=0°.
The procedure is the following:
First the transmission at 0° incidence angle is measured (dashed line in fig.1). Computer algorithms recalculate the
theoretical design to adapt to the measured spectrum until the theoretical transmission (continuous line in fig.1)
matches as good as possible to the measured spectral data.
Hence all errors which may occur during the coating process (e.g. slight deviation of the sputter rate with growing
number of layers, rising substrate temperature etc.) can be taken into account.
This procedure provides the real design of the coating enabling us to estimate many optical properties.
This method proved to be very accurate.
