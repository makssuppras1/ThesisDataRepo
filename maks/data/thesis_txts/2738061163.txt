Abstract
Ammonia is viewed as a potential substitute for diesel in the maritime industry, where it can
serve as a carbon-free fuel, decreasing both the emission of greenhouse gasses and the utiliza-
tion of fossil fuels. This thesis aims to investigate the challenges concerning the combustion
of ammonia, such as its poor combustion properties, and investigate the effect of additives in
the fuel mixture. Experiments are conducted in a laminar plug-flow reactor setup at reducing
and slightly oxidizing conditions in the temperature range 423 K-1023 K at 40 bar. Addition-
ally, the performed experiments are modeled in Chemkin to investigate any weak points in
the applied model.
The initial plan was to conduct experiments with H2 and NO as additives, but due to the
formation of ammonium nitrate when adding NO, no experiments with NO were conducted.
The performed experiments show that the addition of hydrogen has a positive effect on the
ammonia conversion for the oxidizing experiments, whereas it has a negative effect on the re-
ducing experiments. In the experiments with pure ammonia as fuel, conversion of ammonia
is detected despite the lack of an additive. Model predictions of the cases with pure ammo-
nia predict almost no conversion under the investigated conditions. Model predictions of the
oxidizing experiments are generally acceptable, and the precision increases as the hydrogen
fraction increases. In the modeling of the reducing experiments, the model is not sufficient,
and the large underprediction of the conversion of oxygen and hydrogen is especially note-
worthy. In conclusion, more work is still needed, specifically on ammonia oxidation at high
pressure and reducing conditions, as well as further investigation of the surface reactivity of
ammonia.

1.1
The background of ammonia
Ammonia combustion has been investigated since the 1950s, but before that, approximately
100 years ago, ammonia was already synthesized and utilized as fertilizer [1]. Current re-
search focuses on understanding the combustion properties of ammonia to utilize it as a green
carbon-free energy source.
In the maritime sector, the wish is to apply ammonia as a fuel instead of diesel. One benefit
of using ammonia as fuel is that it is a carbon-free molecule, which will result in a decrease in
greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions [2]. On the contrary, the combustion of ammonia can, under
specific conditions, result in the emission of NOx gasses. Therefore, it is highly relevant to
obtain precise models that can predict the behavior of ammonia combustion at a wide range of
conditions, especially at high pressures, to find the optimal operating conditions for a given
engine [2]. Such models exist but are not yet able to precisely predict the behavior of the
reactions, which is also a major motivation for this thesis.
1.1.1
Energy from ammonia
Ammonia stands as a competitor to hydrogen for energy storage. Ammonia can be produced
by the Haber-Bosch process, utilizing hydrogen produced by green energy and thereby storing
the green energy in ammonia. Many arguments exist for storing the energy in ammonia and
not keeping it in the already-produced hydrogen. Ammonia is easier and safer to transport
than hydrogen due to the very different properties of the two molecules. Ammonia is much
easier to liquify; therefore, the temperature and pressure under the transportation of ammonia
do not have to be as extreme as those of hydrogen. The explosion hazard of hydrogen is also
an important argument for transporting the energy as ammonia, as it is much less reactive
than hydrogen. Additionally, the volumetric energy density is higher in ammonia, and less
ammonia, on a volumetric basis, will have to be transported to move the same amount of
energy [3, 2, 4]. These differences between ammonia and hydrogen directly result from the
parameters shown in table 1. Ammonia is the second most distributed chemical worldwide

because it is transported widely to be used as fertilizer [2]. The infrastructure is, therefore,
already in place, and the safety hazards due to the toxicity of ammonia are already known
and managed [2].

1.1.2
Ammonia as fuel in the maritime sector
Ammonia is already utilized in combustion processes on an industrial scale but not as a direct
fuel. Instead, it is applied as a reducing agent in the deNOx process of fuel and biomass
combustion. This process limits the amount of NOx species emitted from the combustion [2].
This process will be described in more detail later (section 1.2.2).
Implementing ammonia as a fuel in diesel engines is not straightforward, as there are chal-
lenges due to the very different behavior and chemical nature of ammonia compared to diesel.
Difficulties with ignition and the emission of NOx are two challenges that will have to be
solved [2]. Both of these challenges will be easier to overcome when research of the processes
evolves, resulting in more experimental data and better models at engine-like conditions. This
will make it easier to find the correct solutions to obtain a fuel utilization that is stable and as
green as possible [2]. Motivation and incentive for substituting diesel fuel with a more green
fuel in the maritime sector is rapidly growing. This will lead to more experimental studies
Page 2 of 95
==PAGE:11==
concerning ammonia at high-pressure conditions, contributing to a better understanding of
the process.
1.1.3
Ammonia production
Today, Ammonia is mainly produced through the Haber-Bosch process. This process has
existed for many years, and the first plant producing ammonia with this process started oper-
ating in 1913 [3]. Since then, ammonia production has only been growing, and the worldwide
use is now around 150 million tons/year [2]. Due to the growing demand and the possibility
of utilizing ammonia as a green fuel, much research focuses on developing feasible green
ammonia processes.
The traditional Haber-Bosch process is relatively simple, following this reaction: N2 + 3H2 ⇌
2NH3. The challenge is that the process is very energy intensive, and when the H2 is produced
from steam reforming of natural gas, the carbon footprint becomes large [4]. Therefore, to
utilize ammonia as a green fuel, the production of ammonia has to change and come close
to being carbon neutral. There are different solutions to obtaining carbon-neutral ammonia
production, and MacFarlane et al. [5] have proposed a solution, incorporating three different
generations of ammonia production, illustrated in figure 1.
Figure 1: Illustration of the path to production of green ammonia, through three production generations, pro-
posed by MacFarlane et al. [5].
Page 3 of 95
==PAGE:12==
Generation 1 is the existing technology, where hydrogen is produced from natural gas, but
in addition, there is a carbon capture and storage (CCS) unit built in to capture the CO2 that
would otherwise be emitted under the production. The next generation, generation 2, will
utilize green hydrogen in the Haber-Bosch process. The final and third generation will use
electro-reduction of N2 and avoid using the Haber-Bosh process altogether. The challenges of
generations 1 and 2 are the economic viability, whereas for generation 3 is the technological
feasibility [2, 5].
1.1.4
Enhancing combustion properties of ammonia
As previously mentioned, the combustion properties of ammonia are poor. The flame speed
is slow, and the ignition energy is high, which are factors that negatively affect the perfor-
mance of ammonia as a fuel substitute for diesel. To overcome this challenge, many previous
studies have introduced other species with better combustion properties in a mixture with
ammonia [2].
Different types of species can work as an enhancing additive in ammonia combustion. Of-
ten, hydrocarbons, such as methanol or diesel, are utilized. Several studies have documented
the positive effects on the combustion properties when using these hydrocarbons as additives
under various conditions [2]. A different additive often implemented for enhancement of
the combustion properties, is hydrogen. This is a logical choice, as ammonia is produced
from hydrogen, and hydrogen can be reextracted by cracking of ammonia [2]. As described
previously, hydrogen is very reactive and, therefore, drastically improves the combustion
properties of ammonia without introducing carbon-containing species [2]. An interesting ad-
ditive whose effect on the combustion properties has not yet been thoroughly investigated is
NO. The interest in NO comes from the use of ammonia in Selective Non-Catalytic Reduc-
tion (SNCR) of NO, a process which will be described in a later section (section 1.2.2). A
hypothesis is that NO could promote reaction and thereby enhance the combustion properties.
Therefore, this thesis focuses on ammonia combustion, both with and without the addition of
H2 and NO.

1.2
Reaction mechanisms
Reaction mechanisms are often complex, and much research is required to get a coherent idea
of the critical reactions and how different conditions affect the selectivity of the mechanism.
This is also the case for the oxidation of ammonia. The following sections will review reac-
tions relevant to ammonia oxidation to obtain a stronger understanding of the behavior of the
sub-mechanisms controlling the overall reaction.
1.2.1
Oxidation of ammonia
Complete oxidation of ammonia follows the overall global reaction:
2NH3 +11
2O2 →N2 +3H2O
(1)
Whereas partial oxidation of ammonia follows the overall global reaction:
2NH3 +21
2O2 →2NO+3H2O
(2)
The desired process is complete oxidation. Harmful NO is emitted during partial oxidation,
and partial oxidation reduces the useful reaction heat. At standard conditions, the reduction
in the useful heat of the reaction is approximately 30 %, meaning 30 % less energy is released
as heat from the exothermic reaction [6, 7].
Ammonia oxidation is widely studied to understand the reaction mechanism and develop a
good model for reaction simulation. Such model development has been challenging due to
determining key reactions and their behavior under different conditions. Additionally, am-
monia is sensitive to different kinds of surfaces [8]. When modeling for small-scale exper-
imental purposes, where the surface-to-volume ratio is large, the effect of surface reactivity
can become a problematic factor to accommodate, as different materials are used in different
experiments. Before implementing any consideration of surface reactivity, a precise model
for the simulation of ammonia oxidation has to be established. This is still an ongoing task,
especially at high pressures. Nevertheless, it is important to consider surface reactivity when
Page 5 of 95
==PAGE:14==
comparing model predictions with experimental results [8, 9, 10].

These reactions between NH2 and NO significantly impact the consumption rate of ammo-
nia and the selectivity for producing NO and N2. They are also key reactions in the SNCR
process (see section 1.2.2). The NH2 and NO reactions are widely studied, and the reaction

1.2.2
Selective non-catalytic reduction
A widely applied process is the selective non-catalytic reduction (SNCR) of NO using NH3.
This reaction is applied in industry to decrease the amount of emitted NO. Therefore, the
process is also called thermal deNOx. As Glarborg et al. [8] describe, the process is only
effective under specific conditions: Oxygen must be present, the temperature must be above
1100 K, and below 1400 K. SNCR results from the study by Dagaut and Nicolle [11] are
illustrated in figure 3. This figure demonstrates that the highest NO reduction is achieved in
the aforementioned temperature gap between 1100 K and 1400 K.
Figure 3: SNCR results at lean conditions (φ = 0.1), compared to modeling predictions produced from the
model by Skreiberg et al. [12]. Experiments were carried out in a jet-stirred reactor. The initial conditions are
1000 ppm of NH3, 12500 ppm of O2, 500 ppm of NO (open symbols and dashed lines), or 1000 ppm of NO
(closed symbols and solid lines). Residence time: 100 ms. Data (symbols) are compared to the modeling results
(lines). Graph from the review by Dagaut and Nicolle [11].
The reactions of thermal deNOx are a part of the mechanisms described previously in section
1.2.1. The difference is that in thermal deNOx, the conditions are particular, pushing the
reaction pathway in a specific direction. Ammonia is first converted to NH2 by the O and H
radicals. The next step, which is the reaction between NH2 and NO, has two product channels
(reaction 6 and 7). An illustration of the reaction pathways of the thermal deNOx process is
found in figure 4.
Page 10 of 95
==PAGE:19==
Figure 4: Illustration of the thermal deNOx reaction paths, the dashed lines represent pathways only important
at high temperatures [8].
The branching fraction of these two reactions between NH2 and NO significantly impacts the
overall process. A change in the branching fraction of a few percentages affects the onset
temperature by up to 50 K, making modeling of thermal deNOx difficult. Therefore, it is very
challenging in the industry to predict the NO emission level precisely [8].
To obtain a sufficient deNOx, the branching fraction must be at least 25 %, or the overall
reaction becomes terminating, thereby shutting down the reaction. This can be obtained by
having a temperature above 1100 K because the branching fraction increases with tempera-
ture. In figure 5, the branching fraction is illustrated as a function of temperature. Looking
at the graph in figure 5, having a very high temperature for a more efficient deNOx would
make sense. However, this is not the case. At the high end of the SNCR temperature window,
the challenge is that NH3 is oxidized to NO rather than N2, antagonizing the objective of the
process. Therefore, the temperature must not exceed 1400K [8].

1.2.3
Formation of nitric oxide
As described in previous sections, NO is formed through minor pathways in NH3 combustion.
NO is formed through different mechanisms, and in high-temperature gas combustion, an im-
portant formation mechanism of NO is thermal NO formation. It is a well-studied mechanism
initiated by an oxygen atom attacking molecular nitrogen, forming NO and atomic nitrogen.
Afterward, the atomic nitrogen is oxidized by reaction with O2 or OH. The thermal NO for-
mation mechanism is shown in reactions 23 to 25 [8].
O+N2 ⇄NO+N
(23)
N+OH ⇄NO+H
(24)
N+O2 ⇄NO+O
(25)

Under ideal conditions, a flow reactor will utilize plug-flow to obtain agreement between
reaction time and reaction distance. In plug-flow, a cross-section inside the reactor will have

Only two studies of high-pressure oxidation of ammonia in a flow reactor, without adding

1.3.2
High-pressure experiments in shock tubes
A shock tube is a setup commonly used for high-temperature studies. It consists of a pipe
with a metal diaphragm inside, separating the test gas from the driver gas. The driver-gas
side is pressurized, and the diaphragm is burst, resulting in a shock wave moving through the
test-gas side. This shock wave increases the temperature and pressure in the test gas. As the
shock wave reflects from the end wall of the pipe, the gas is heated further. Depending on the
objective of the experiment, the reaction inside the pipe can be studied after the initial shock
wave or after the reflected shock wave has passed through the pipe and the gas has become
stagnant [22].
One method for determining the Ignition Delay Time (IDT), by the use of a shock tube, is
starting the time at the passage of the reflected shock wave, indicated by a pressure jump in
the signal. Ignition start time is then when the maximum slope of the OH∗signal intersects its
baseline. The time between these two events is the IDT [13, 15]. The method for determining
the IDT is illustrated in figure 10.

1.3.3
High-pressure experiments in rapid compression machines
The rapid compression machine (RCM) is utilized to simulate a compression stroke from an
engine. A reaction chamber is filled with the fuel mixture diluted with N2 or Ar, and a piston
is activated to apply high pressure and temperature extremely fast. Afterward, the piston is at
rest, and the subsequent pressure increase due to combustion inside the reactor chamber can
be monitored. This information can be used to analyze the autoignition characteristics of fuel
mixtures, including IDT and heat release rate [38]. When determining the IDT in an RCM,
the first point is at the end of the compression applied by the piston, and the next point is
at the steepest pressure increase measured, meaning at the faster pressure acceleration. The
time between these two events is the IDT [16]. In the following, two RCM studies of NH3
will be covered [16, 17].
IDT of ammonia was investigated by Dai et al. [16], using an RCM at equivalence ratios
0.5, 1.0, 2.0, and 3.0. The study was conducted at high pressure from 20 bar to 75 bar,
in a temperature range of 1040 K to 1210 K. A set of experiments, including the addition
of H2, was also performed to investigate the effect of H2 addition. A list of the performed
experiments is shown in Appendix A.1. He et al. [17] also investigated the IDT of ammonia
in an RCM at very similar conditions: Pressures from 20 bar to 60 bar, temperatures from
950 K to 1150 K, and equivalence ratios from 0.5 to 2.0. The study also investigated fuel
mixtures containing H2. An overview of the experiments from that study is also found in
Appendix A.1.
To explicitly showcase the effect of equivalence ratio, temperature, and pressure on the IDT,
Dai et al. [16] produced two figures. One being isobar (figure 14a), and one being isotherm
(figure 14b), both including three different equivalence ratios.

radical OH, which will promote the reaction. Additionally, this sensitivity analysis showcases
the large differences between the two models. He et al. [17] conclude that a great contribution
to the difference in the two models is the increased attention to the H2NO + O2 reaction in
the model by Glarborg et al. [8].

1.4
Sub-conclusion to literature study
Ammonia oxidation at high pressures is not well-researched because it has not been nec-
essary for the traditional application of the substance and because it is a difficult task. As
Page 35 of 95
==PAGE:44==
ammonia has become interesting as a carbon-free fuel, the behavior of combustion at high
pressure is now of high relevance. Research on the subject has begun, but there is still a long
way to reach a comprehensive understanding of the mechanism as conditions change. This
also affects the state of current modeling abilities, which are not yet able to perform precise
modeling predictions over a wide range of conditions. As seen in the studies covered in the
literature study, different models perform better under different conditions, and there is not
one model that can perform precise predictions over a broader range of conditions. Especially
the branching fraction of the reactions between NH2 and NO has a great impact on the quality
of the combustion. These reactions are currently well understood and studied, but mostly at
the very limited conditions of the SNCR reaction. At engine-like conditions, the selectivity
between these two reactions will have to be optimized to minimize emissions of NO and to
fully utilize the thermal energy in ammonia.
High-pressure flow reactor experiments are the type of experiments that come closest to sim-
ulating the ignition temperature range at engine conditions. Therefore, experiments utilizing
this experimental setup at high-pressure conditions can contribute to a better understanding of
how ammonia will behave as a fuel in the maritime sector. Only two studies of this type were
found in the literature. Unfortunately, the results from the two studies are not in agreement,
and neither are their modeling precision and exhaust gas composition. Research from shock
tubes and RCMs illustrates the difficulties with predicting the IDT of ammonia. Additionally,
they show the huge number of models developed for ammonia combustion and also the lack
of models that are able to perform good predictions under different conditions. The current
models found in the literature are accurate at different and very specific conditions, but none
of them are accurate over a wide range of conditions. From all the studies discussed in the
literature study, it is apparent that selectivity, IDT, and LBV are highly affected by pressure,
temperature, H2 addition, and equivalence ratio, making modeling very sensitive. It is of
high importance to get a better understanding of the mechanism so that ammonia can be used
to its full potential as a carbon-free fuel and energy carrier. The objective of this thesis is,
therefore, of high relevance and can hopefully contribute to a deeper understanding of the
ammonia oxidation mechanism at high-pressure conditions.

2
Experimental
To investigate the behavior of ammonia oxidation at high pressure, experiments are carried
out in a flow reactor under different conditions. Furthermore, NO and H2 are applied as ad-
ditives in various concentrations to investigate their effect on the reaction. The experimental
setup, procedure, and plan will be presented in the following. Additionally, all methods and
analytical apparatus will be introduced, and their uncertainties will discussed and quantified.
2.1
Experimental setup
The setup used for conducting the experiments is a laminar flow reactor that is able to run at
high pressure. The flow inside the reactor is assumed to be plug-flow under the conditions
applied in this study [44]. All experiments will be performed at 40 bar. At this pressure, the
maximum temperature in the setup is 1025 K. An illustration of the setup can be found in
figure 22. In appendix A.2.1, a picture of the setup can be seen.
Figure 22: Illustration of the experimental setup used in this thesis. Figure adapted and modified from the thesis
by Fabricius-Bjerre and Joensen [45].
Four different gasses can be introduced to the setup, consisting of a 155 cm long quartz
reactor placed inside a steel tube that works as a pressure shell. This pressure shell ensures
that the pressure inside and outside the quartz reactor is the same to prevent the reactor from
Page 37 of 95
==PAGE:46==
breaking due to pressure differences. The pressure inside the reactor and the pressure shell
are manually controlled in the setup software by typing in the desired setpoints. The system
regulates the pressure in the shell by introducing nitrogen and controlling the pressure with
two mass flow controllers. Inside the reactor, the pressure is regulated by a Baumann valve,
controlled by a pressure transmitter, illustrated by the dashed lines in figure 22.
Three heating elements are attached to the pressure shell. These regulate the temperature at
which the reaction occurs inside the quartz tube. These aim to create a long isothermal zone
inside the reactor. A thermocouple is placed inside a steel pocket located parallel to the reactor
in the pressure shell to investigate the length and the temperature of the isothermal zone. Extra
heat tracing is placed at the inlet and outlet of the reactor to elongate the isothermal zone.
Additionally, the piping introducing NO or H2 is wrapped in an extra heat tracing cable, and
the gas is introduced and mixed with the rest of the reactants right before the inlet zone.
These modifications are done so that the NO will first react as close to the isothermal zone as
possible. A picture of the additional heat tracing and the mixing point is found in appendix
A.2.1. If the NO were introduced with the other reactants further from the reactor inlet, all
NO would be depleted before reaching the inlet because NO reacts with O2 to form NO2.
The heat tracing at the outlet also heats the Baumann valve and ensures that condensation is
avoided.
When performing the experiments with H2 addition, the same configuration as in the NO
experiments is used, and the inlet that was used for introducing NO is instead used for in-
troducing H2. H2 is, therefore, heated before mixing with the other reactants close to the
inlet. This is unnecessary in the experiments with the addition of H2 but does not influence
the results either. The setup is kept the same for all experiments, both for practicality and to
maintain the same temperature profile for experiments with NO and H2.
The reactor’s outlet is connected to the apparatus responsible for analyzing the exhaust gas
composition. Section 2.3.4 and 2.3.5 will describe this in more detail.

2.2
Experimental procedure
The experiments are performed in a temperature range both at stoichiometric and reducing
conditions. High-pressure experiments in a flow reactor with the addition of H2 were not
found in the literature, and only one experiment with the addition of NO was found.
The initial conditions for each investigated case are determined before the actual experiments
are performed. The mass flow controllers responsible for introducing the desired amount of
each reactant are stable but not precise. Therefore, the correct setpoint for each mass flow
controller for each experimental case is found before starting the experiments.
The setpoints are determined by iteration. First, the setup is heated to a low temperature,
where no reaction occurs (determined by simulations). Next, the setup is pressurized, and the
Fourier Transform Infrared Spectrometer (FTIR) and Gas Chromatography (GC) are turned
on. The first tested setpoints of the mass flow controllers are based on calculations assuming
that the mass flow controllers are correctly calibrated. Then the GC and FTIR measurements
are utilized to calculate the equivalence ratio and the ratio between ammonia and additive.
The setpoints are then adjusted accordingly. This procedure is repeated until the desired
equivalence ratio and ammonia-additive ratio are achieved. The final initial conditions for
each experimental case are found in table 8.
The experimental procedure begins with heating the setup and turning on the GC and FTIR.
Once the setup is thoroughly heated and the heat tracing has had time to heat the Baumann
valve, the setup is pressurized to an absolute pressure of 40 bar. The pressurization itself is
performed with nitrogen to save the use of reactants. When the desired pressure is reached,
the reactants are introduced, corresponding to the case up for investigation. All investigated
cases will be presented in a later section (section 2.4).
When the measurements performed by the FTIR and GC are stable, an average of the stable
measurements is noted. The measurements are deemed stable when three GC measurements
are similar, and there is no increasing or decreasing tendency. The time to reach stabilization
of the system varies dramatically. In some cases, it can be as fast as 40 minutes, and in
other cases, it can take up to 2 hours. Generally, the stabilization time is longer for the
Page 39 of 95
==PAGE:48==
reducing conditions than for stoichiometric conditions. Additionally, changes in temperature
most times have a longer stabilization time than changes in the inlet composition. After the
stabilization, conditions are changed to the next case. The change can be a shift in temperature
or a change in the inlet composition of the gases. This cycle is repeated throughout the day
until it is time to shut down the setup.

The experiments employ different types of equipment and reactants. These include Gas Chro-

2.3.1
Calibration of heating elements
Three heating elements are employed along the reactor to control the reaction temperature.
To ensure an isothermal zone inside the reactor, the heating elements are calibrated by the
type K thermocouple, mentioned in section 2.1. This thermocouple is movable so that the
temperature can be measured along the whole reactor length.

reaction temperature. After completing the temperature profiles shown in figure 23, the data
points for each of the three heating elements are extrapolated to determine the setpoints for

Temperature uncertainties
Different aspects affect the temperature uncertainty. The thermocouple employed for mea-
suring the temperature inside the reactor is not placed inside the reactor. It is possible that
there is some varians between the temperature inside the reactor and the temperature where
the thermocouple is placed. Additionally, the thermocouple itself has an uncertainty. This
specific thermocouple has a relative uncertainty of ±0.75% and an absolute uncertainty of
Page 41 of 95
==PAGE:50==
±2.2 K. Due to the age of the thermocouple, this uncertainty could be higher.
When creating the temperature profiles, measurements are taken at two-centimeter intervals
using a ruler. Some uncertainty related to the measured step length can affect the isothermal
length. This uncertainty is only minor and will not have a large impact on the isothermal
length and the subsequent experimental results.
The isothermal zone is, in reality, not a perfect isothermal zone. As described previously, the
temperature can vary ±3 K, which can maybe affect some results if they are very temperature
dependent.
Some of the reactions are exothermic, and their heat release could also yield an uncertainty
in the temperature of the isothermal zone. The effect of the heat release is difficult to quan-
tify, as the heating elements and heat tracing of the setup will try to regulate this if the heat
release starts to affect the temperature. To obtain some knowledge of the extent of the heat
release, it is simulated in Chemkin. The simulation is performed for the stoichiometric and
reducing case with the highest Hydrogen content (RD80 and OX80 in table 8). The temper-
ature increase resulting from the exothermal reactions is plotted as a function of the isother-
mal temperature in figure 24. The figure shows a maximum temperature increase of 25 K
and indicates a trend of an increase in the temperature rise from the exothermic reactions
as the setpoint temperature becomes higher. This is expected as the conversion increases at
higher temperatures. In addition, the heat release is much higher in the oxidizing case. The
temperature increase could impact the actual temperature of the isothermal zone at higher
temperatures, which would be the most critical at the reducing conditions, as the conversion
of some reactants is very sensitive to temperature. This temperature sensitivity is greatest
around 800 K, where the heat release is relatively low, and the heat release at reducing con-
ditions is overall low. Therefore, the heat release is not expected to yield a lot of uncertainty
but will be considered.

2.3.4
Fourier Transform Infrared Spectrometer
After the Baumann valve, part of the flow goes to a Fourier Transform Infrared Spectrometer
(FTIR). This apparatus uses infrared light to identify covalently bonded molecules and their
fraction in a sample. Infrared light is absorbed by the molecules, resulting in an excitation
of the molecule [47]. The FTIR is utilized in this thesis to measure the NH3, H2O, and N2O
fractions. It also measures NO and NO2, but these are not detected in any of the experiments.
An illustration of how an FTIR works is found in figure 25.
Page 45 of 95
==PAGE:54==
Figure 25: Illustration of how an FTIR works. Picture from KEIT [48]
The FTIR consists of a source, a beam splitter, two mirrors, a laser, and a detector. First, the
IR source is split in two by the beam splitter. One part is sent to a moving mirror and one
to a fixed mirror. The two split beams are then recombined at the beamsplitter, resulting in
an interference pattern. This pattern is transmitted through the sample, where the absorbance
happens, and into the detector. The signal that reaches the detector is called an interferogram,
which has to be Fourier-transformed before an interpretable spectrum is generated.
FTIR uncertainties
The FTIR works excellent as a black box and is a very sensitive and accurate method overall.
Water is the species that is most uncertain, as cross-interaction of the water signal can happen
and thereby affect the measured water concentration. Investigating the water concentration
profiles, it seems that the behavior of the profiles has a clear tendency, but the actual absolute
values of the measurements are off. This suspicion is based on some water measurements
being negative. This is illustrated in figure 26, where the water profiles for all cases are
plotted. In the figure, it is seen how there is an apparent behavior of the water profiles, also
for the negative values, emphasizing that the water measurements are shifted due to cross

difficult to utilize the measurements as a quantitative measure and quantify the uncertainty

this, a mass balance analysis of the experimental cases is not feasible since the concentration

Gas Chromatography
Gas chromatography is utilized to measure the oxygen and hydrogen concentrations. The GS
employed for the experiments has a Flame Ionization detector (FID) and a Thermal conduc-
tivity detector (TCD). When measuring O2 and H2, FID is not feasible, as it utilizes these two
compounds in the detection method [50]. Therefore, the measurements from the GC in these
experiments will be performed with the TCD.
The GC works by utilizing differences in volatility in different compounds and can only be
used to measure compounds that are volatile enough to be vaporized. The machine has an
injection system, a column, and a detector. As mentioned previously, the detector utilized in
this case is a TCD. In figure 27, an illustration of the GC working principle is shown. A car-
rier gas, argon, is passed through the system with the sample, where it is heated. The affinity
differences towards the column material will result in a difference in the speed through the
column of the different compounds present [51]. The TCD can then detect the differences
in thermal conductivity, which is utilized to identify the different compounds [50]. A chro-
matogram of the sample is then visible in the GC software, where a calibration for H2 and O2
is utilized to detect which peak corresponds to which compound. An integration of the peaks
then reveals the corresponding concentration in ppm.

GC uncertainties
The following standard deviations are found by investigating each specific data point mea-
sured by the GC. The relative standard deviation of the oxygen measurements for measure-

trations under 300 ppm is 20 ppm. For hydrogen, the highest relative standard deviation for
measurements above 100 ppm is 7.1%, and the highest absolute standard deviation found at
concentrations under 100 ppm is 4.3 ppm. All measurements from the GC and their standard
deviation can be found in appendix A.2.3.
Even though the standard deviation of each data point is low, there are some other signifi-
cant problems with the GC measurements, which raises questions about their trustworthiness.
These problems will be described below.
As the GC measurements are obtained by integration of a chromatogram, some differences in
the obtained value for a specific measurement can occur when different people are integrating
the same peak. This is mostly relevant for the small O2 peaks, as the peak changes shape as the
concentration becomes smaller, which can be interpreted differently. Additionally, at these
low concentrations of O2, the percentage difference between different integration strategies is
larger than for the larger O2 peaks. A small O2 peak with two different integration strategies
can be seen in figure 28.

The GC columns gradually deteriorate with use, and the measurements are, therefore, gradu-
ally becoming more and more unprecise. There have not been performed repeated measure-
ments with a sufficiently long time in between to obtain knowledge of the behavior of the
gradual deterioration. This deterioration has also resulted in a baseline concentration of O2,
which was not present at the beginning of the experiments.
Some tests were performed to investigate the effect of these difficulties on the GC. First, a
test of the accuracy of the O2 concentration was performed to see how much the deviation is
between the actual concentration and the one measured by the GC. It was done by connecting
the gas bottle initially used for calibration of the GC, which is a flask containing 610 ppm of
O2, measurements were then performed by the GC, which resulted in a measured concentra-
tion of 455 ppm. This means that the O2 measurements performed by the GC are 25% to low.
This procedure was also performed with a H2 bottle with a concentration of 15000 ppm. Here,
the measured value of the H2 was 18885 ppm, meaning that the measured H2 concentration
is 26 % too high.
To further investigate, pure N2 was tested in the GC to determine the baseline concentrations.
Yet again, the size of these baselines has been smaller earlier in the completion of the experi-
ments, as this test is performed after the completion of all experiments and, therefore, at the
time when the deterioration of the GC columns must be at its worst. The baseline test showed
that when no O2 or H2 is present in the sample gas, the GC measures no H2, meaning that the
baseline for H2 is just 0. For O2, the baseline was determined to be 19.8 ppm.
Next, the columns are regenerated, and the test with pure N2 is again performed. The baseline
concentration for O2 is no longer present, meaning the column regeneration was successful.
Tests with the calibration gases are again performed to determine the effect of the column
regeneration. The results came out different than expected. Both the hydrogen peak and
the oxygen peak have become approximately 3 % larger. This is what is normally expected
from regeneration, but the effect was expected to be greater in this specific case due to the
large deviation found from the tests with the calibration gases. From this discovery, it is
Page 51 of 95
==PAGE:60==
concluded that something in the calibration setup of the GC went wrong in the experimental
preparation. The initial calibration measurements were conducted in a correct manner and
yielded the correct concentration. The problem occurred in the use of the calibration, from
the way it was set up in the software. Therefore, the measured data points have to be corrected
so that they match the correct calibration. This is performed by investigating the factors in
the GC data processing software that are applied to calculate the concentration of the species
in ppm from the area of the integration of the chromatogram peaks. This factor will be
referred to as the translation factor. The translation factor for hydrogen and oxygen from the
original correct calibration is found directly in the software. The translation factor for the
calibration gas measurements conducted before regenerating the columns is determined by
analyzing the peak areas in the software. However, instead of yielding a concentration of
455 ppm for oxygen, the factor is calculated so that the peak reflects the correct concentration
of 610 ppm. The difference between the two translation factors is utilized to correct the GC
measurements. Practically this results in the oxygen measurements being multiplied with
1.29, and the hydrogen measurements being multiplied with 0.79. This correction ensures
that the data points now correspond to the correct calibration.
The deviation in the data points, the baseline oxygen concentration, the sensitivity to the
integration strategy of the oxygen peak, and the gradual deterioration of the column contribute
to the uncertainty of the GC measurements.
Measurements and standard deviations from the GC are shown in appendix A.2.3.

reaction path diagrams can be used to describe the most significant reactions in all cases
according to the model. The reaction path illustrated in figure 34 is valid for cases without
hydrogen addition, and the reaction path in figure 35 is valid for cases with hydrogen addition.
This means that the presented reaction paths in this section are also valid for the reducing
conditions investigated in this thesis, which will be presented later (section 3.2).

Figure 34: Main reaction pathways for the oxidation of ammonia at 1025 K and 40 bar, when the hydrogen
fraction in the inlet is 0.
The reaction path diagram for the cases without hydrogen addition in figure 34 is conducted
at 1025K. The oxidation of ammonia is initiated by reaction with OH or HO2 to form NH2.
NH2 is then either converted back to ammonia, by reaction with HO2 or converted to N2H4
by reaction with NH2(+M). Next, N2H4 is converted to N2H3, by reaction with NH2, and
subsequently converted to tHNNH by interaction with M. tHNNH can then be converted to
cHNNH. cHNNH and tHNNH are converted to NNH, which decomposes to N2. Ammonia
can also be regenerated from the reaction between tHNNH and NH2. The reaction path is
not very complex, and it is seen how reaction with tHNNH determines if the conversion is
pushed towards the formation of N2, or to the regeneration of ammonia. The low conversion
predicted by the model in case RD00 could indicate that a significant amount of the formed
tHNNH is regenerated to NH3. In addition, less OH and HO2 will be available when no
hydrogen is introduced to the system, which might be the bottleneck in this path, as this is
the species responsible for the initial conversion of NH3 to NH2.

The reaction path diagram for the cases with hydrogen addition is illustrated in figure 35.

becomes more significant as the hydrogen ratio increases. The reaction pathway is initiated
by ammonia reacting with OH to form NH2. Next, NH2 has various possible reaction routes.

with NH2 in a terminating reaction forming N2 or react with HO2 to form NO2, which by
reaction with an additional NO2 molecule will form H2NO.

3.2.1
Model analysis: Reducing conditions
The reaction path diagrams presented in section 3.1.1 are also valid at reducing conditions;
new reaction paths are, therefore, not presented in this section. From the reaction paths in
figures 34 and 35, it is seen how OH and other oxygen-containing radicals are very important
in the conversion of ammonia, and naturally, when more oxygen is present, the amount of
these radicals will increase. The reaction between H2 and NH2, seen in figure 35, results
in the regeneration of ammonia, which could explain the low conversion of ammonia in the
reducing cases, as fewer OH radicals will be available to convert NH3, while plenty of H2 is
available to regenerate NH3. OH is the main radical responsible for converting NH3 to NH2,
also illustrated in figure 35.

In figure 43, the sensitivity of ammonia conversion in case RD40 is presented. The reaction
which has the highest sensitivity coefficient at 900 K is the promoting reaction:
H+O2(+M)⇌HO2(+M)
(43)
This reaction produces HO2, an important species in the conversion of ammonia, as HO2
in reaction with NO produces both NO2 and OH. As shown in figure 35, NO2 is utilized in
various reactions in the path. Additionally, OH is the main radical responsible for converting
NH3 to NH2. Reaction (43) is also part of the sensitivity analysis for hydrogen in figure 44,
where it has a small promoting effect. The promoting effect is, in this case, also due to the
subsequent formation of OH, as OH consumes H2.
The most promoting reaction for the ammonia conversion at 825 K is
NH3+H⇌NH2+H2
(44)
The reaction proceeds in the reverse direction and promotes the conversion of ammonia be-
cause of the production of H radicals. The promoting effect might sound counterintuitive, as
the reaction forms NH3. It is possible as the increase in H radicals outweighs the effect of
the formation of NH3. This also explains why this same reaction can be the most promoting
reaction in the H2 sensitivity analysis in figure 44, despite NH3 and H2 being on opposite
sides of the reaction.
The most inhibiting reactions in the ammonia sensitivity analysis are
NO2+H⇌NO+OH
(45)
H2NO+NH2⇌HNO+NH3
(46)
Reaction (45) is a chain propagating reaction, with radicals on both sides of the reaction.
The reaction produces OH and NO, which intuitively points to a promoting effect due to the
production of OH radicals. In the reaction path diagram (figure 35), it is seen how OH reacts
with NH3. The loss of NO2 must, therefore, lead to an inhibition of the ammonia conversion,
Page 78 of 95
==PAGE:87==
which exceeds the promoting effect of the formed OH radicals. From the reaction path (figure
35), it is seen how NO2 is responsible for many reactions, including the reactions that do not
regenerate NH3 from NH2, but instead converts NH2 to different intermediates. Additionally,
When more NO2 is utilized in the formation of NO, more NO is available in the terminating
reaction between NO and NH2. The reaction between NO and NH2 also has a branching
reaction route, but the overall reaction between NO and NH2 is terminating. The decreased
availability of NO2, and the increased availability of NO, therefore, result in the inhibition of
ammonia conversion.
Reaction (46) produces NH3, and the sensitivity is a direct effect of this. Reaction (46) is
also part of the sensitivity analysis for hydrogen, where it has an inhibiting effect on the
conversion. This is because hydrogen is mostly consumed by reaction with NH2 to form NH3
at reducing conditions. If the consumption of NH2 becomes more dominated by reaction with
H2NO, the conversion of H2 will decrease.
In figure 44, the reaction having the largest promoting effect on hydrogen conversion is reac-
tion (44), whereas the reaction with the largest inhibiting effect is
NH2+NO2⇌H2NO+NO.
(47)
This reaction produces H2NO and NO from NH2 and NO2, and was also a part of the sen-
sitivity analyses at oxidizing conditions in section 3.1.1. Under oxidizing conditions, the
reaction had a promoting effect on hydrogen conversion, opposite to the effect at reducing
conditions. To further explore this difference, the absolute rate of production of hydrogen
was investigated under different conditions. The investigation revealed that H2 is consumed
more rapidly by the OH radical under oxidizing conditions, while under reducing conditions,
NH2 is more predominant in converting H2. The reduction in available NH2 to convert H2 is
therefore inhibiting the conversion of hydrogen at reducing conditions. Meanwhile, the chain
propagating effect and subsequent production of OH radicals promote conversion under oxi-
dizing conditions. This explains why the sensitivity of hydrogen conversion to reaction (47)
is contradictory for reducing and oxidizing conditions. Reaction (47) is also part of the sen-
sitivity analysis for ammonia, where it has a promoting effect on the conversion due to its
Page 79 of 95
==PAGE:88==
chain propagating nature. The reaction between NH2 and NO2 also has a chain terminating
path producing N2O and water. This terminating reaction is also present in both sensitivity
analyses, inhibiting the conversion of ammonia and hydrogen, which was also seen in the
sensitivity analysis at oxidizing conditions.

3.4
Comparison and discussion of investigated cases
In the upcoming section, the findings from experiments and simulations will be compared
and discussed. This will lead to a comprehensive understanding of the data collected in this
thesis and the identification of potential areas for future research.
3.4.1
Differences between reducing and oxidizing experiments
The experimental results conducted at reducing and oxidizing conditions are very different.
As expected, the conversion of ammonia is significantly more pronounced under oxidizing
conditions. The initiation temperature is not as different between the two campaigns as could
be expected; the model predictions indicate a difference between 25-50 K between reducing
and oxidizing cases with the same hydrogen fraction, whereas the highest difference detected
in the experiments is 25 K. Additionally, in the cases RD10 and OX10, the initiation temper-
ature is actually 25 K lower in the reducing case, which is quite interesting as the initiation
temperature is expected to be the lowest in the oxidizing case for all tested hydrogen fractions.
In the oxidizing campaign, the increase in hydrogen concentration resulted in a higher conver-
sion of ammonia and a more pronounced formation of N2O. On the contrary, adding hydrogen
decreased the ammonia conversion in the reducing campaign. This is seen when comparing
case RD00 and case RD80. In case RD80, a slight initial decrease in the ammonia concentra-
tion is observed, but the concentration then increases and rises above the initially introduced
level. In case RD00, some continuous ammonia conversion is occurring, though it is slow
compared to the oxidizing campaign. In the cases, RD10 and RD40, where hydrogen is in-
troduced as fuel, the behavior of the ammonia conversation is similar to that of case RD80.
First, there is a small conversion of ammonia, after which the concentration increases. The
equivalence ratio is nearly constant in all cases of the reducing campaign. Only when hy-
drogen is added is an increase in ammonia concentration observed. Additionally, the overall
extent of the ammonia conversion decreases as the hydrogen fraction increases; this is illus-
trated in figure 47, where all the ammonia concentration profiles from the reducing campaign
are plotted. This suggests that adding hydrogen under highly reducing conditions may have
a negative impact on the conversion of ammonia.

3.4.2
Model performance
The main distinction in model performance between the two campaigns is that the model’s
predictions are much more accurate under oxidizing conditions compared to reducing con-
ditions. It is noteworthy that the predictions for the cases without hydrogen addition (case
RD00 and OX00) have similar poor performance.
In the reducing campaign, ammonia is the species whose conversion is predicted most accu-
rately, though the model does not accurately capture the behavior of the concentration pro-
file. The predictions for hydrogen and oxygen concentrations in the reducing campaign are
the least accurate, as their conversion is greatly underpredicted. Additionally, the initiation
temperature is captured more accurately in the oxidizing campaign, where the largest discrep-
ancy is 77 K, compared to a maximum discrepancy in the reducing campaign of 127 K. The
model’s performance improves in the oxidizing campaign as the hydrogen fraction increases,
both in the case of species concentrations and initiation temperature. Similar to the reducing
campaign, the ammonia concentrations are the most accurate predictions, while the hydrogen
and oxygen concentrations are the least accurate predictions.
Page 84 of 95
==PAGE:93==
The model’s poor performance under reducing conditions suggests that some rate constants in
the mechanism are inaccurate. According to sensitivity analyses under reducing conditions,
reactions involving radicals from the O/H/OH radical pool, as well as reaction pairs with chain
branching and chain terminating counterparts, are particularly sensitive. Additionally, the
consumption of hydrogen at reducing conditions was shown to be more dominated by reaction
with NH2, whereas under oxidizing conditions, it is dominated by reaction with OH. An error
in the rates of these reactions would, therefore, result in noteworthy model discrepancies.
The reaction rates of these reactions are known to be determined very accurately [54, 53].
Therefore, it is unlikely that the error stems from inaccuracies in these reaction rates.
Another possible explanation involves the formation of amines. The amines that are part of
the major reaction path under conditions without hydrogen addition are not part of the major
reaction paths when hydrogen is added. This is evident in the reaction path diagrams in fig-
ures 34 and 35. It is possible that amines play a more significant role in the reaction path, also
when hydrogen is added. Amines would hold onto a substantial amount of hydrogen atoms,
potentially explaining the rapid conversion of hydrogen. Alzueta et al. [54] also investigated
ammonia oxidation with the addition of hydrogen under reducing conditions, though at at-
mospheric pressure, and found the same discrepancy for hydrogen. In the study by Alzueta et
al. [54], one hypothesis for this discrepancy is a reaction between NH2 and diazene, forming
N3 and eventually N4 amines. In the study, it is clearly stated that enough work has not yet
been performed on this specific topic, but it is desired to investigate it further.
The discrepancy could be caused by some surface reactivity towards quartz. This was at-
tempted to investigate in this thesis. An extra set of experiments were performed, where
the surface area inside the reactor was increased. These experiments were conducted to in-
vestigate the effect of the surface reactivity of ammonia, and possibly hydrogen, as well as
investigating the overall effect on species concentrations and initiation temperatures. Due to
the increase of the surface inside the reactor, the residence time was decreased. This decrease
in the residence time could explain the changes in the experimental results from the original
case to the increased surface case. Therefore, the experiments were unable to provide any
meaningful insight into the surface reactivity. However, it would be interesting to conduct
Page 85 of 95
==PAGE:94==
further investigation using an experimental configuration that allows for easier control of sur-
face area increase and enables variation of the length of the isothermal zone. This would
enable maintaining a constant residence time despite the increase in surface area and would
be a more promising method for conducting qualitative data on the topic.
In the review by Glarborg et al. [8], it is mentioned how the H2NO subset in the mechanism is
not well established and primarily consists of estimated reaction rates. As seen in the reaction
pathway in figure 35, the H2NO intermediate is an impactful intermediate when hydrogen is
present in the fuel mixture. Therefore, this sub-mechanism is interesting to investigate further
in the search for an explanation for the model discrepancies at reducing conditions.
The outlined possible courses for the model discrepancies are all speculative, and the rea-
son for the poor model performance at reducing conditions is not known. Further research
and experimental studies dedicated to this specific problem will be necessary to address the
challenges with the model under reducing conditions.
3.4.3
Other findings
Experiments with NO as an additive were also planned to be a part of this thesis, but due
to challenges with ammonium nitrate, no actual experiments with NO were performed. The
specific reaction route leading to the formation is not known and were not further investigated.
Modifications were made to the setup in this thesis, to avoid pre-reaction of NO, but the
modifications showed not to be sufficient. The formation could possibly be avoided if NO
was introduced directly into the isothermal zone, but such modifications to the setup would
be very substantial, and it would not be obtainable in the timeframe of a master’s thesis.
Nonetheless, it is still an interesting hypothesis that NO could enhance ammonia conversion.
More research focussing on this is, therefore, of interest.
Different experimental studies were presented in the literature study section. These experi-
mental results were simulated using the model applied in this thesis. The simulation showed
that predictions of ammonia conversion under stoichiometric conditions and high pressure are
very accurate. Also, predictions of the ignition delay time showed to be satisfying, though
the applied model had trouble simulating the ignition delay time at low pressure.

4
Conclusion
Ammonia is an interesting compound as a carbon-free energy carrier. However, it has poor
combustion properties, which makes its application in engines more challenging. Further-
more, there are difficulties in modeling ammonia combustion at high pressures, also present-
ing a challenge in its use as a carbon-free fuel. In this thesis, the aim has been to investigate
ammonia oxidation at high pressure, both experimentally and from a modeling perspective,
with and without the addition of the additives NO and H2, in order to better understand the
challenges and explore the potential positive effect of those additives.
The experiments were performed in a laminar plug-flow reactor in a temperature range from
473 K to 1023 K. All experiments were performed at 40 bar. The model applied for the
simulations of the experiments is the model applied in the study by Jian et al. [53]. The
performed experiments consist of two experimental campaigns, one at reducing conditions
(φ ≈5.0) and one at slightly oxidizing conditions(φ ≈0.8). Furthermore, an additional set of
experiments were performed, where the surface area inside the reactor was increased. Within
the two experimental campaigns, the fraction of H2 in the inlet has been varied. Experiments
with NO have not been performed, as an initial test resulted in the formation of ammonium
nitrate as soon as NO came in contact with the other reactants. Modifications to the setup
were carried out to avoid the premature reaction of NO, but these changes were not sufficient.
The initiation temperatures in the two campaigns are quite similar. The biggest difference
observed in the initiation temperature for experiments with the same initial hydrogen fraction
was 25 K. When the hydrogen fraction in the fuel is about 10 %, the initiation temperature
for the reducing case is 25 K lower than that for the oxidizing case. At all other hydrogen
fractions, the lowest initiation temperature was found in the oxidizing case.
In the oxidizing campaign, an increase in the hydrogen fraction resulted in a higher conver-
sion of ammonia, whereas it had a slightly decreasing effect on ammonia conversion in the
reducing campaign. In cases where hydrogen was not added, there was still a significant con-
version of ammonia in both campaigns, despite the model predicting almost no conversion.
Generally, the model predictions were the most accurate at oxidizing conditions, improving
Page 87 of 95
==PAGE:96==
with increasing hydrogen fraction. The model is not sufficient in predicting the behavior at
reducing conditions, wherein especially hydrogen and oxygen conversion is underpredicted.
The predicted conversion of ammonia is within the experimentally determined range at re-
ducing conditions, but the overall tendency of the concentration profile is not captured.
4.1
Future work
For future work on this topic, experiments with NO are of interest. Experiments with NO
as an additive will require that the setup is significantly modified or that the experiments are
conducted using a setup where it is possible to introduce species directly into the isothermal
zone.
The surface reactivity of ammonia is also an area, which is relevant to investigate further.
Unfortunately, the experiment in this thesis that explored surface reactivity did not yield any
conclusive results on the topic. Conducting more research on this topic could enhance the
understanding of ammonia oxidation and the influence of surface reactivity. This could po-
tentially help explain some of the observed model disparities.
The most interesting aspect for future investigation is the discrepancies in the model under re-
ducing conditions. This topic requires extensive research and new experimental studies. Also,
further model analysis focussing on reducing conditions would be interesting to conduct. Cur-
rently, it is unknown what causes the discrepancy, and it would be extremely interesting to
find the cause.

A.4
Project plan
Title: High-pressure oxidation of ammonia in the presence of additives
MSc project, 35 ECTS points
Student: Anna Tveen Trap (s183874)
Supervisors: Peter Glarborg, Hamid Hashemi
Place: DTU Chemical Engineering.
Period: January 8th to July 8th 2024
Introduction
Background
Ammonia (NH3) is of interest as a non-carbon energy carrier. However, its use as a fuel in
diesel engines is challenging because it has poor combustion properties. It is essential to
develop reliable models for the combustion of ammonia at high pressure to facilitate the de-
sign and optimization of engines. Unfortunately, current models cannot capture accurately
the behavior under these conditions. In particular, the ignition delay is overpredicted. Small
amounts of nitric oxide (NO) can possibly promote reaction, but there are no reported exper-
imental investigations. Additionally, hydrogen (H2) is known to promote reaction.
Page A25 of A27
==PAGE:129==
Objectives
The project aims to investigate NH3 oxidation with/without NO and H2 addition at high
pressure through experimental and modeling approaches. The focus is on understanding the
impact of temperature, stoichiometry, and NO/H2 presence on ammonia oxidation at high
pressure.
Learning Objectives
Through experimental studies and Chemkin modeling, practical and theoretical insights into
NH3 oxidation at high pressure are gained. Additionally, experimental design and modeling
enhance technical skills. The project adopts a holistic approach, considering temperature,
stoichiometry, and the presence of NO and H2 in a complex combustion process. Precise
and unambiguous communication of findings is essential, promoting skill development in
conveying technical information. The project encourages independence and reflection on
learning, fostering technical problem-solving skills.
Methodology
Literature Study
Conduct an in-depth review of existing literature on ammonia combustion at high pressure,
NO interactions, and the effect of H2 addition. The literature study is performed to obtain an
understanding of the current state of knowledge comprehensively.
Experimental Study
Perform experiments in a flow reactor setup, allowing pressures up to 100 bar and tempera-
tures of 500-1000K. Explore ammonia oxidation as a function of temperature, stoichiometry,
and NO or H2 presence at high pressures.
Chemical Kinetic Modeling
Utilise Chemkin software to interpret the results using a detailed chemical kinetic model
based on experimental results obtained from earlier work at DTU Chemical Engineering.
Assessment
Evaluate the importance of NH3/NO/H2 interactions during the ignition of ammonia at high
pressure based on experimental findings and chemical kinetic modeling.

This project plan outlines a systematic approach to investigating NH3 oxidation. Progress

potential implications for future engine design.
