Abstract
Renewable energy sources, particularly solar photovoltaic (PV) systems, offer a promising alternative to
fossil fuels and play a critical role in mitigating climate change. However, their environmental benefits
depend on the degree to which they substitute for traditional energy sources. Changes in user behaviour,
following the adoption of solar panels, could reduce 28% of these environmental benefits. This is a
phenomenon known as rebound effects (RE). With Solar Home System (SHS) adoption growing rapidly,
especially in low- to middle-income countries (LMICs), it is crucial to understand how contextual dif-
ferences shape the occurrence of RE. This thesis aims to understand and prevent behavioural rebound
effects following the adoption of solar panels, focusing on the identification of contextual factors (CFs)
that influence them.
Using the Design Research Methodology, this research identifies behavioural rebound mechanisms (BRM)
and a set of 15 CFs through a systematic literature review and an analysis of an existing literature review.
To complement these findings, a survey of 37 households in high-income countries (HICs) provides
primary data on RE and examines the perceived influence of contextual factors following the adop-
tion of solar panels. These insights are synthesised into a design tool that supports SHS developers in
analysing the user context and identifying potential BRM during the design phase. The tool is evalu-
ated through critical incident identification, employing a think-aloud method and observations with
experts in behavioural science, RE, design engineering, the context of LMICs and companies in SHS
development. While the evaluations demonstrate the tools’ usability and usefulness, their practical appli-
cability is challenged by the low prioritisation of sustainability among SHS users and developers in LMICs.
This thesis contributes to the fields of sustainable design and behavioural science by offering an interdis-
ciplinary approach to understanding how contextual differences affect the behavioural responses of solar
interventions. Practical design strategies are introduced to mitigate rebound effects. Furthermore, the
work lays the groundwork for future research into context-specific behavioural responses of sustainable
interventions, helping to ensure that the environmental potential of solar technologies is fully realised in
diverse global settings.

Preface
The idea for this thesis originated from my interest in sustainable innovations, human behaviour and
rebound effects. As a design student passionate about human-centred design and environmental impact,
I wanted to explore how unintended user behaviour from sustainable design differs between contexts.
Throughout this project, I have engaged with theoretical frameworks, user perspectives, and design pre-
vention strategies, learning not only about rebound effects but also about the complexities of real-world
application.
This thesis was carried out from the 3rd of February to the 30th of June, 2025, as the final project (30
ECTS) for the MSc. Eng. Design and Innovation study line within the Department of Civil and Mechanical
Engineering at the Technical University of Denmark (DTU). The research was conducted under the
supervision of Prof. Daniela C. A. Pigosso, PhD. Imke van der Loo, and Marie Louise Pollmann-Larsen.
AI language models, ChatGPT (OpenAI, 2025), Gemini (Google, 2025), and Microsoft Copilot (Microsoft,
2025), were used to support the refinement and paraphrasing of text, as well as for assistance in Excel-
based coding. All AI-generated outputs were critically reviewed and adapted to ensure quality and
relevance.

Learning Objectives
The key learning objectives for this thesis are:
• Structuring and managing a research project based on the Design Research Methodology
• Evaluate existing literature on rebound effects and solar panel adoption to identify knowledge gaps
and limitations.
• Work closely with a specific case company to analyse the challenges and rebound effects in imple-
menting solar panels in low-income countries.
• Collaborating effectively with industry organisations and stakeholders to gather qualitative and
quantitative data on solar panel adoption.
• Evaluate the connections between stakeholders in the adoption of solar panels and map their
interactions to understand the responses affecting sustainability.
• Identify and analyse the contextual factors driving rebound effects of solar panels in low-income
and high-income countries.
• Investigate and map the behavioural mechanisms that lead to rebound effects in communities
adopting solar panels.
• Develop a design framework to prevent rebound effects caused by sustainable-oriented design.
• Test and validate the developed framework through a case study and real-world implementation to
assess its applicability and effectiveness.
• Assess the impact of the proposed design framework by identifying measurable outcomes.
• Critically reflect on the findings, limitations, and opportunities for improvement to advance the
understanding of rebound effects in sustainable design.

Definitions
Rebound effects
A rebound effect refers to the reduction in environmental benefits from a sustainable intervention, caused
by behavioural changes influenced by contextual factors, when the intervention is used as it is designed
for (adopted from Hertwich, 2005).
Mechanism
A structure of elements and activities that work together in a specific way to produce a particular outcome
through cause-and-effect relationships (Hedström and Ylikoski, 2010).
Behaviour
The actions or inactions of individuals or groups to internal and/or external stimuli (Davis et al., 2015).
Behavioural Rebound mechanism
Structures of causal processes that explain the behaviour leading to the occurrence of rebound effects.
Contextual factor
A contextual factor describes the external conditions that influence the strength and direction of a be-
havioural mechanism leading to rebound effects. They moderate the relationship between the design
intervention and user behaviour by strengthening or weakening its impact (adopted from the definition
of a moderator from Baron and Kenny (1986))
Mediator
The mediator explains the underlying psychological or contextual mechanisms through which an inde-
pendent variable influences a dependent variable (Baron and Kenny, 1986).
High-income countries
Countries where the gross national income per capita is higher than $14,005 (World Bank Group, n.d.).
Low- to middle income countries
Countries where the gross national income is lower than $4.515 per capita per year (World Bank Group,
n.d.).
Solar Home System
The system where solar energy is converted to electricity through a photovoltaic module, a converter, a
charge controller and a battery system. The system is applied in residential contexts (Salas, 2017).
Pro-environmental behaviour
A set of behaviours practised by individuals that seek to take measured actions to promote positive
changes in the environment and limit the effects of human negligence. (Kollmuss and Agyeman, 2002).

Introduction
Replacing fossil fuels with renewable energy sources is a promising option for mitigating climate change
(Arent et al., 2011). The successful reduction of carbon emissions with solar photovoltaic (PV) systems
depends on the ability of solar panels to substitute for another energy source, specifically grid-electricity
that is powered by fossil-fuel-based sources. The use of fossil fuels for electricity generation leads to
an increase in greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions, such as carbon dioxide, alongside other harmful air
pollutants, including nitrogen and sulphur dioxide particulates. In contrast, solar PV systems generate
electricity with virtually no operational emissions, thus directly reducing these greenhouse gases and
mitigating local air pollution (Qiu et al., 2019). However, these changes are not always straightforward
and may be limited or influenced by underlying mechanisms.
Solar PV technology operates by converting solar radiation into electrical energy. This process relies
on specialised cells, typically made from semiconducting materials like silicon, which release electrons
when exposed to sunlight. The movement of these electrons within an electric field initiates the gen-
eration of electricity (Huide et al., 2017). To harness this generated electricity, various components are
needed to create a solar home system (SHS), depending on the type of solar PV system: grid-connected
or stand-alone systems. Stand-alone systems, often referred to as off-grid PV systems, typically comprise
a PV generator, energy storage (batteries), charge controllers, and power conditioners (Rathore et al.,
2021). These off-grid systems are particularly well-suited for electrifying areas where population spread
makes access to centralised grid electricity challenging (Msarah, 2021). In contrast, grid-connected PV
systems feed directly into the regional electricity grid, often utilising "Net Metering" to trade excess
generated electricity back to utility companies, thus often negating the need for on-site battery storage
(Rathore et al., 2021). A key advantage of SHS is their lack of mechanically moving parts, resulting in
lower maintenance and operating costs. Furthermore, their flexibility in mounting, often on rooftops,
minimises impact on living conditions (Rathore et al., 2021), making SHS a robust and reliable solution
for gaining access to clean, decentralised energy (Metegam et al., 2025). The tangible environmental
benefits of SHS are evidenced by studies such as Mukisa et al. (2022), which found an annual household
GHG emission avoidance of 19.51-199.29 kg CO2eq in Sub-Saharan African countries.
The global expansion of solar energy capacity underscores its increasing importance in the energy tran-
sition (Figure 1.1) (IRENA, 2025). Countries with lower income levels, particularly across the African
continent, face heightened vulnerability concerning electricity access and supply reliability (Metegam
et al., 2025). This challenge is critical, as a country’s energy production is a crucial factor influencing
a country’s economic stability and environmental degradation (Metegam et al., 2025). Consequently,
SHS offer a robust solution for electrifying households currently reliant on unreliable electricity grids
(Faraj, 2023). This potential is supported by Rathore et al. (2021), who also identifies solar PV technology

The escalating demand for solar energy is further highlighted by the Africa Solar Industry Association

grid access (Mukisa et al., 2022). In the research of Mahn et al. (2024), the authors examined 43,467
households across 11 different countries in Africa, and found a strong correlation between solar adoption
and household assets, demonstrating that households with higher income are more likely to adopt solar
PV systems. These findings suggest that variations in geographical location, income level, and market
accessibility shape adoption patterns. This raises the question of whether these contextual factors also
influence behavioral responses after adoption, and how they might affect the type and extent of rebound
effects.

Rebound Effects
Various studies have examined rebound effects (RE) in different contexts and across a range of interven-
tions, each offering its own definition of the concept. Rebound effects are systematic and behavioural
responses to the implementation of sustainability-oriented interventions that hinder the potential sus-
tainability benefits (Van der Loo and Pigosso, 2024b) (Figure 1.2). Others define rebound effects (RE) as
the situation in which the actual energy savings or sustainability benefits achieved from an efficiency im-
provement or sustainable intervention are less than initially anticipated through engineering calculations
(Galvin, 2021). While the concept has been extensively studied, particularly within economics, a notable
gap remains in understanding the underlying behavioural mechanisms that drive these effects (Van der
Loo and Pigosso, 2024b). This gap is particularly pronounced in the context of low- and middle-income
countries (Andrew and Pigosso, 2024), where household consumption behaviours and electricity de-
mand patterns differ significantly from those in high-income nations (Nguyen et al., 2024). For instance,
Nguyen et al. (2024) identified a 16% to 28% rebound effect in households owning solar PV systems due
to behavioural changes in Vietnam. The undermining potential of RE is substantial, with some research
indicating that up to 47% of the sustainable gains could be hindered (Andrew and Pigosso, 2024).

Rebound effects can be measured on three levels: direct, indirect, and economy-wide (Brockway et al.,

2021). A direct rebound effect occurs when the increased efficiency or sustainability of a product or
service leads to higher consumption of that same product or service, partially or fully offsetting the
intended sustainable benefits (Exadaktylos and van den Bergh, 2021). In the context of SHS, this can
involve an increased use of "free" solar electricity to power additional appliances (Deng and Newton,
2017). An indirect rebound effect arises when the cost savings or other benefits from the intervention
lead to increased consumption of other goods or services, which potentially reduce the sustainability
benefits (Exadaktylos and van den Bergh, 2021). For SHS users, the savings of domestic energy bills could
be spent on other products or services, leading to new emissions (Deng and Newton, 2017). It is essential
to recognise that the direct and indirect rebound effects occur simultaneously and can collectively cause
economy-wide rebound effects (Brockway et al., 2021). To illustrate, solar panels can reduce fossil-fuel-
based electricity use at the household level but the increased economic activity and energy use elsewhere
in the economy can partly or fully offset the environmental savings, creating an economy-wide rebound
effect.
To understand how rebound effects occur, Van der Loo and Pigosso (2024b) have performed a systematic
literature review and identified and analysed 18 behavioural mechanisms. These mechanisms are cate-
gorised by moral licensing mechanisms, reappraisal of consequence mechanisms, motivational crowding
mechanisms, cognitive biases mechanisms, social mechanisms, and economic mechanisms (Table 1.2).
The structure of causal processes of the behavioural mechanisms explains the occurrence of rebound
effects (Hedström and Ylikoski, 2010). This overview of behavioural mechanisms serves as a framework
for understanding behavioural rebound effects associated with the adoption of solar panels in this study.

Research Gap and Relevance
While the environmental urgency for solar PV adoption is clear and its global uptake continues to accel-
erate across diverse income contexts, the potential for behavioural rebound effects to diminish these
benefits remains a critical and underexplored area. Specifically, there is a limited understanding of the
precise behavioural mechanisms that drive rebound effects in households following the adoption of solar
5
==PAGE:18==

PV systems, particularly when comparing the contextual differences between low- and middle-income
countries versus high-income countries. Understanding these differences is essential, as contextual
factors can vary greatly between settings, potentially triggering distinct behavioural mechanisms and
resulting in different rebound effects.
This research aims to bridge this gap of limited understanding of contextual factors that influence
behavioural mechanisms that cause rebound effects by employing the behavioural mechanisms frame-
work proposed by Van der Loo and Pigosso (2024b) to analyse and compare the contextual factors
driving rebound effects in households with solar PV systems in both low- to middle-income and high-
income country contexts. By explaining these mechanisms, this study will provide policymakers, energy
providers, researchers, and intervention designers with invaluable insights into possible behavioural
mechanisms across different contexts and design prevention strategies. A comprehensive understanding
of behavioural rebound effects is essential for developing more effective strategies and policies that
ensure the full environmental potential of solar PV systems is realised, thereby accelerating genuine
climate change mitigation efforts globally.

Methodology
The thesis aims to develop an in-depth understanding of the contextual factors (CFs) influencing the be-
havioural rebound effects of solar panels in LMICs. This understanding will be utilised to create a context-
specific design framework aimed at preventing behavioural rebound effects caused by sustainability-
focused design (Blessing and Chakrabarti, 2009). This framework will be based on the CFs identified that
influence the rebound effects following the adoption of solar panels in LMICs.
To support the process of this thesis, the Design Research Methodology (DRM) is utilised as a theoreti-
cal framework to structure the project. The methodology supports an effective and efficient approach
to design research by providing a structured, iterative framework that guides researchers through the
complex process of conducting design research (Blessing and Chakrabarti, 2009). According to the DRM,
the project is split into the following four phases (Figure 2.1). The research followed the chronological
sequence outlined below, though the Descriptive Study - I phase demanded considerably more time,
taking up about twice the duration of the other phases.

2. Descriptive Study – I (DS-I)

Rebound effects following solar PV adoption
The findings of the papers suggest that two types of rebound effects can occur after the adoption of
solar panels. The first rebound effect is the increase in energy consumption ((Deng and Newton, 2017),
(Frondel et al., 2023), (Vélez-Henao and García-Mazo, 2022), (Wang et al., 2024a), (Nguyen et al., 2024),
(McCarthy and Liu, 2022), (Horne and Kennedy, 2022), (Qiu et al., 2019), (Dütschke et al., 2021)). This
rebound effect arises from changes in household energy consumption behaviour following the instal-
lation of PV panels, which can undermine the environmental benefits of PV adoption by reducing the
extent to which electricity consumption from the public grid is offset (Frondel et al., 2023). Deng and
Newton (2017) studied the patterns of electricity use in households that adopted solar panels in Sydney
and presented that households generate up to 40% of their total electricity consumption with solar
energy. However, the total impact of solar energy is less, due to the operation of a rebound effect of
21%. Qiu et al. (2019) performed another study in the United States, utilising electricity data and survey
data and estimated an energy rebound effect of 18%. Additionally, the study from Nguyen et al. (2024)
found that households with solar PV systems initially rely less on grid electricity compared to non-PV
households. However, this reduction diminishes over time, indicating a gradual increase in grid electricity
consumption among solar PV-owning households following the post-installation period. This rebound
effect can be identified as a direct rebound effect, where the increased sustainability of energy leads to
higher electricity consumption. In the case of grid-connected solar PV systems, increased consumption
of self-generated solar energy by households reduces the amount of surplus energy fed back into the
grid. This, in turn, may increase the reliance on fossil fuel-based electricity to meet overall grid demand.
Therefore, higher energy consumption among off-grid solar PV households may lead to an increased
demand for supplementary fossil fuel-based energy sources, such as diesel or kerosene lamps (Galvin
et al., 2022).
The second rebound effect mentioned by the literature involves re-spending on additional products or
services following the adoption of solar PV ((Dütschke et al., 2021), (Wang et al., 2024c), (Galvin et al.,
2022)). In this indirect rebound effect, the perceived availability of "free" electricity from PV panels could
incentivise households to consume more electricity-consuming appliances. In parallel, the monetary
savings resulting from self-generated energy may create economic flexibility, enabling households to
spend more on other goods and services. The study from Dütschke et al. (2021) specifically highlights that
households often increase their investments in household technologies after installing solar PV. These
purchases can include water re-use systems, smart home technologies, or electric vehicles (EVs), which
can contribute to higher environmental impacts.

reassessing, identified as mediators in the literature review; perceived costs and effort (Hofstetter et al.,
2006), perceived level of efficiency (Dorner, 2019), perceived control and responsibility (Santarius, 2016).
Finally, several CFs were identified that do not directly influence the BRM but potentially influence the
CFs influencing the BRM. For example, political ideology is identified to influence social norms (Werfel,
2017) and past sustainable behaviour is identified to influence environmental self-identity (Lasarov et al.,
2022). These results show that there is complexity in how the CFs are influencing the BRM and potentially
influence each other.

3.3.1
Synthesising the Literature Reviews
A review of the SLR findings on rebound effects following solar panel adoption, along with the literature
from Van der Loo and Pigosso (2024b), reveals both overlapping and distinct influences between the
identified CFs and BRMs. In cases where a CF shows both strengthening and weakening influences on a
BRM, the direction reported most frequently is used. For instance, environmental self-identity is reported
as weakening the BRM in six instances and strengthening it in three; therefore, the weakening influence
is selected. If the number of strengthening and weakening instances is equal, the source offering the
most substantial evidence for the relationship is prioritised. For example, daily consumption metering is
identified by Dütschke et al. (2021) as weakening for mental energy accounting, while Qiu et al. (2019)
identified the relationship as strengthening. Since Qiu et al. provides the most evidence for the relation-
ship, a strengthening connection is chosen.
Figure 3.13 and Figure 3.14 visualise the BRMs with the identified CFs from both literature reviews. The
figures display the BRM itself, highlighted by the grey rectangle, and the CFs influencing the BRM by
blue arrows. The direction of influence is indicated using a "+" for a strengthening effect and a "−" for a
weakening effect. Mediators identified directly in the literature are highlighted in orange. In contrast,
those shown in dark grey are proposed by the author as potential mediators influenced by the solar PV
system. These were not directly identified in the reviewed studies, as such mediating effects were beyond
the primary focus of the original research. Overall, environmental self-identity is the most frequently
mentioned CF (12), followed by social norms (10), regulation (9), financial incentives (8), knowledge gaps
(7), cognitive capacity (5), and feed-in tariffs (5). The remaining CFs are mentioned three times or fewer.

3.4
Summary
This chapter examines the systematic literature review that contributed to the identification of rebound
effects resulting from the adoption of solar panels in HICs and LMICs. This review revealed two primary
types of rebound effects: increased energy consumption and re-spending on additional products or
services. Additionally, the literature further strengthened the understanding of the CFs and BRMs in the
specific setting of solar panel adoption. Loss aversion and mental energy accounting are behavioural
mechanisms that deviate from the BRMs identified by Van der Loo and Pigosso (2024b). Furthermore,
moral licensing, contribution ethics, time discounting, motivational crowding, behavioural income-effect,
and need satisfaction were identified in the literature as BRMs occurring following solar panel adoption.
Financial incentives, specifically feed-in tariffs, environmental self-identity, and regulations, were among
the most frequently mentioned factors influencing RE.
Next, the chapter conducts an analysis of the systematic literature review by Van der Loo and Pigosso
(2024b), to identify further potential CFs influencing BRM. Several key insights on the contextual factors
influencing the behavioural mechanisms were gained. Similar to the SLR, environmental self-identity
was frequently mentioned along with financial incentives. However, social norms appeared to be the
most frequently mentioned CF (9 times). Knowledge gaps emerged as a new CF, alongside NGO influence,
media influence, peer pressure, technological change, green advertisement, and cognitive capacity. The
results from both literature reviews resulted in a list of 15 CFs in total. Since a large number of studies
mention ’Moral licensing’ as a behavioural mechanism, most of the CFs are linked to this mechanism.
Furthermore, the chapter analyses the CFs in different contexts based on existing literature. First, the CFs
are analysed in HICs and LMICs in general. Then, the presence of CFs is explored in Denmark and Kenya,
proposing an initial evaluation of the differences between contexts in CFs and BRM. The evaluation of CFs
between HICs and LMICs suggests that financial incentives, feed-in tariffs, income level, environmental
self-identity, desire for comfort, and daily consumption metering are more present in HICs. Knowledge
gaps and cognitive capacity are likely to be higher in LMICs. Other contextual factors appear to be similar
in both LMICs and HICs or are more challenging to generalise, such as social norms, peer pressure,
regulations, media influence and electricity prices. Since many CFs are highly dependent on context and
cannot be generalised, a comparison is made between Denmark and Kenya. This comparison suggests
that electricity prices, income levels, social norms, peer pressure, environmental self-identity, media
influence, cognitive capacity, and daily consumption metering are more present in Denmark. Financial
incentives, desire for comfort, NGO influence, and knowledge gaps are more present in Kenya.
To further deepen the understanding of these contextual factors, a non-probability sampling survey was
conducted to collect primary data from solar panel users. The survey primarily collected respondents
from solar panel owners in mainly high-income countries, addressing the recognised gap in the literature.
The results identify the RE of an increase in energy consumption and the consumption of other (energy-
consuming) products. Additionally, a high level of environmental concern and awareness is detected,
suggesting a strong environmental self-identity among the participants. Furthermore, the influence
of NGOs and media appears to be less significant; electricity prices give the impression of weakening
potential rebound effects; social norms seem to place greater emphasis on environmental priorities; and
policies and regulations appear to influence adopters’ perceptions of solar PV systems.

This chapter focuses on the development of the Design Framework. It is divided into two main parts:
the first discusses an assessment tool that explores the relationship between contextual factors and
behavioural rebound mechanism, while the second part presents the Design Tool itself.
4.1
Understanding the Relationship Between the Contextual Factors and
Behavioural Rebound Mechanism
This section outlines the methodology used to develop a relationship matrix linking CFs and BRMs,
followed by a presentation of the matrix results and the identified relationships.
4.1.1
Method
To systematically analyse the influence of individual CFs on various BRMs and to understand how this
influences the occurrence of the mechanisms in different contexts, a relationship matrix was created as
a primary analytical tool. This matrix aims to display the differences in the likelihood of occurrence of
behavioural mechanisms between different contexts. The matrix is based on a qualitative assessment
of the CFs derived from both the systematic literature reviews and the analysis of the existing literature
review (Chapter 3).
The foundation for this assessment consists of 37 papers obtained from the literature reviews. The identi-
fied CFs from these papers were systematically integrated into the matrix. The decision for which CFs
were used in the framework is based on the decision tree in Figure 4.1. This decision tree helps to include
conceptually consistent factors, ensures non-redundancy and analytical clarity, and ensures practical
applicability. Each contextual factor in the matrix is considered as ’high presence’. For example, for social
norms, this includes a high presence of social norms regarding environmentally friendly behaviour.

Evaluation of Results and Limitations
To create a better understanding of the rebound effects and influencing contextual factors of solar panel
adoption, further primary data collection is needed. In particular, data focusing on the adoption of solar
panels in LMICs will help elucidate this matter. Once more evidence is collected on the contextual factors
and behavioural mechanisms, the likelihood that rebound effects occur in certain contexts can be revised
and further improved.
While the results provide an initial indication of CF-BRM relationship following solar panel adoption,
several limitations must be acknowledged. First, the current findings are constrained by the limited body
of literature specifically addressing behavioural rebound effects in the context of solar energy. As a result,
some relevant CFs may not have been captured in this study due to their absence in existing research.
Second, although the comparison between HICs and LMICs highlights contextual variation, it is equally
important to investigate intra-country differences. Factors such as peer pressure, social norms, and
comfort preferences can differ significantly across regions within the same country, and may influence
behavioural responses in distinct ways.
The comparative graphs offer a useful overview of the relative influence of CFs. However, they are based
on average influence scores across all BRMs. Since individual CFs can affect different BRMs in varying
and sometimes opposing directions, averaging these values may oversimplify the dynamics and should
therefore be interpreted with caution. It is also important to emphasise that the Influence score does not
confirm the occurrence of specific BRMs. Rather, they indicate the potential for CFs to shape behavioural
outcomes. To accurately assess the actual presence and activation of BRMs in various contexts, further
empirical research is necessary.

4.2.1
Method
Defining the Solution Space
Before starting the development phase, it was important to understand the aim of the design framework,
the Intended User and the design criteria. Therefore, a persona was developed to understand real-life
behaviours, values and needs of the Intended User (van Boeijen et al., 2013). The persona helped define
the problem definition and the design criteria in a later stage, and helped to define the Intended User. The
development of the persona and the list of requirements was performed based on the method description
of the Delft Design Guide (van Boeijen et al., 2013). The list of requirements was split into two sections:
the must-have criteria and the nice-to-have criteria. The first section outlines all the requirements that
the final Design Tool must meet to be considered functional, usable, and acceptable to users. The second
section includes all the features that are not strictly necessary for the product to function but significantly
enhance user satisfaction (van Boeijen et al., 2013).
Development
Throughout the development process, an iterative design approach was adopted (Figure 4.12). This
process involved the following sequential phases:
1. Initial Concept Generation and Evaluation. After the initial brainstorming session to explore
potential structures for the Design Tool, the preliminary concepts underwent a VALUE evaluation, a
structured method adapted from van Boeijen et al. (2013). This method allows for an initial review
and validation of ideas by articulating the Advantages, Limitations and Unique elements of each
concept. This assessment provided insights into valuable directions for solution finding.
2. Ideation and Refinement. A second ideation phase was conducted, employing both positive and
negative brainstorming techniques. The negative brainstorming session (DTU Skylab, 2025) (Ap-
pendix 9.9) supported the identification of critical success criteria and pitfalls for the Design Tool.
Following this brainstorming session, the Harris Profile method (van Boeijen et al., 2013) was
applied to evaluate the ideas and select the final concept based on the predefined design criteria.
3. Defining Prevention Strategy Objectives. The overarching goal for the Design Tool was to provide
design strategies to prevent rebound effects. Therefore, the overarching goal for each prevention
strategy per BRM was identified. This step ensured the subsequent design strategies were aligned
with specific BRM mitigation objectives.
4. Identification of Existing Design Strategies. A short desk research was performed to identify current
design strategies. The research focused on design strategies that specifically target the prevention
of rebound effects or support sustainable behavioural change.
5. Contextualising the Design Strategies. A final brainstorming session was conducted to iterate upon
the identified design strategies. This session included the rewriting to ensure their fit within the
specific context and understanding of the Intended User, enhancing their practical applicability.

Delivering the First Prototype
Throughout the conceptualisation of the tool, the eight golden design rules were kept in mind (Shneider-
man et al., 2009)
1. Consistency: The Design Tool keeps consistency in colours, font use, shapes, and elements reduces
cognitive overload and allows users to learn how to use the tool more quickly. By maintaining
uniformity in how actions are visualised, users can retain the knowledge they learned in one part of
the tool and apply it in other parts.
2. Design for new and experienced users: During the design of the tool, it is kept in mind that new and
experienced users will be using the tool. Therefore, it is essential to provide a clear explanation on
how to use the tool the first time, while also offering efficient options for experts.
3. Informative feedback: It is essential that every action the user takes provides clear information
on whether the action was successful or unsuccessful. In the Design Tool, the use of colours and
textual information highlights the actions, as Excel has limited capabilities to make this feedback
more visual.
4. Closure - activities into sequences: The Design Tool presents a clear order in the steps the user needs
to perform by indicating where in the process the user is. Additionally, the yellow button at the end
of each step gives an indication of the ending of the specific step. This helps users organise their
thoughts and limits confusion about unfinished processes.
5. Prevent errors: Locking the Excel sheets except for the cells that the users need to use, the Tool
prevents users from changing the model and creating unwanted errors or mistakes.
6. Allow undo: By allowing the users to go back in the process and change their input about the CF,
BRM or design strategies, users can correct errors.
7. Keep the user in control: Giving the user options to change their inputs, go back and forth, and
select design strategies they want to use as output of the tool, the Design Tool creates a sense of
control. It is essential that users do not feel trapped or feel like they are driven by the tool.
8. Reduce memory load: Using different sheets for the steps reduces information overload for the
user. Providing "pauses" in the process, such as changing sheets or scrolling down, allows users to
process the information sufficiently. Additionally, using highlighting colours and fonts will enable
users to focus on the critical information first without feeling overloaded.
After assessing and using the eight golden design rules in the Design Tool, the prototype was created.
During the development of the prototype, Gemini (Google, 2025) was used to provide suggestions for
formulas, and codes to enable the use of VBA’s in the tool. However, due to the limitations of Excel in UX
design possibilities, only the minimal viable product (MVP) processes were included to reduce complexity.
The first prototype, the Behavioural Rebound Evaluation Toolkit, presents a tool for solar innovators to
help assess the potential BRMs that can occur after the end-users adopt their SHS. The Excel tool has
six tabs including an introduction, a sheet to define the project scope, the contextual factor analysis, an
estimation of the potential likelihood of BRMs (from most to least likely), proposed design strategies, and
an overview of the design strategies, which can be exported as a PDF.

the matrix values as described in section 4.1.2. These values describe the strength and direction of CF
influencing the BRM and can therefore be used to calculate the likelihood of occurrence. Next, the user
can select a behavioural rebound mechanism to read more information about the BRM, how it applies in
the context, and decide whether they want to view design prevention strategies for each BRM. Step 4,
Design Prevention Strategies allows the user to view the suggested design strategies for each selected
BRM. This overview includes a list of the most influential CFs for each BRM, the name of the strategy,
and a description of the strategy. In the final step, 5. Export results, the user is able to select the desired
design prevention strategies and save them to a PDF. Figure 4.18 presents the different steps in the tool.

Descriptive Study - II
This section evaluates the proposed design tool based on the success criteria: usability, usefulness, and
applicability. The evaluation criteria propose a base to discuss the intended outcome of the tool: to
understand and prevent behavioural rebound effects of solar panel adoption.
5.1
Evaluation Method
This section introduces the methodology used to evaluate the Design Tool, including a description of the
evaluation aim, participants, and evaluation plan.
5.1.1
Evaluation aim
This evaluation aims to assess the usefulness, usability, and applicability of the proposed Design Tool
for preventing behavioural rebound effects following solar panel adoption. The usefulness refers to the
degree to which the user’s goals are satisfied with accuracy and completeness. This focuses mostly on the
actual task within the Design Tool that helps reach the desired outcome. Usability refers to the extent to
which users can effectively, efficiently, and satisfactorily use the Design Tool to achieve specified goals.
The usability is mostly focused on the user experience (UX) of the Design Tool. Applicability is defined as
the suitability of the Design Tool for use in the intended design context. This includes the relevance of
the design tool and feasibility (Blessing and Chakrabarti, 2009). The evaluation focuses on how the tool
supports the intended users in understanding the potential rebound effects and mitigating these effects
in various user contexts.
Therefore, the following research questions are defined:
• How usable, useful, and applicable is the Design Tool for its intended users and context?
• Which improvements are needed in the framework to enable adaptation to different geographical
contexts?
5.1.2
Methodology
A mixed methods approach was used to ensure both qualitative evaluation data and broader user percep-
tions. The evaluation consists of the following methods:
• A Critical Incident Identification (CII) was used in combination with the think-aloud method
(Hartson and Pyla, 2019). The Critical Incident Identification involves the designer of the tool

==PAGE:85==

observing the participants performing a task. Problems are observed and traced back to their cause
in the UX design and listed to be fixed in the following iterations. During the observation and
performance of the task (Appendix 9.12), participants were asked to verbalise their thoughts as they
exercised the design. This revealed otherwise hidden user experience data (UX data) (Hartson and
Pyla, 2019). During the observation, the designer of the tool observed the participants based on the
interaction with the interface, verbalised thoughts, and signs of frustration or delight.
• An observation sheet (Appendix 9.11) was used to document these observations. The verbal
expressions of the user were also recorded by the designer to prevent incomplete documentation
of the participant’s thoughts during the test.
• A post-evaluation survey (Appendix 9.10) was conducted in addition to the CII to collect quan-

usability, usefulness, and applicability after completing the requested task. The survey was split
into three sections based on the Evaluation Criteria. Each section contains statements based on
the Post-study System Usability Questionnaire of Sauro and Lewis (2012).
A Likert scale was used with five scale points to assess the participants’ perception of the Design
Tool. Finally, the survey included an open-ended question that allowed participants to share addi-
tional suggestions and feedback. The results of the survey can be found in Appendix 9.13.
5.1.3
Participants
For a comprehensive evaluation of the Design Tool different participant groups were established based
on the different areas addressed. The participants group consist of one user behaviour expert, one LMIC
context expert, four design engineers, two RE experts, and two intended users from different companies.
The first company included an installer and distributor of Solar Systems for residential and industrial
projects in Croatia. The other company develops and distributes solar home systems for off-grid commu-
nities in sub-Saharan Africa. The participants were contacted through e-mail and personal contacts. To
ensure that the most refined version of the Design Tool was evaluated by its intended users, participants
were tested in the order depicted in Figure 5.1. After each testing round, the results of the evaluation were
analysed and small improvements were made before the new testing round. These improvements were
selected based on the feasibility of the improvements within three dimensions:
• The improvements contributing directly to the core functionalities of the tool.
• The capability of the software of the tool, Excel, in implementing the improvements.
• The estimated time commitment required for the implementation of the improvements.

5.3
Analysing the Evaluation Findings
The evaluation of the Behavioural Rebound Evaluation Toolkit proved to be a successful method to collect
qualitative insights on the critical incidents and potential improvements of the tool. Evaluation with
different participant groups provided distinct insights, resulting in a more comprehensive understanding
of the tool’s impact. However, for further improvements and deeper insights, additional evaluations can
be conducted to assess the effectiveness of the questions in analysing the potential occurrence of BRMs.
Therefore, it is recommended to collect primary data on the occurrence of RE in different contexts, and
to evaluate the tool with relevant industry companies and stakeholders similar to the participants of
evaluation round 4.
Analysing the results from all the evaluation rounds, it is evident that the tool received a general consen-
sus regarding its usability. Specifically, the clarity of its steps, navigation, and instructions is perceived
as satisfactory. The improvements made throughout the development process involved clarifying the
instructions and specifying the expected outcome for each step. Each of the improvements contributed
to greater coherence across the different versions of the tool. However, further enhancements can be
made, particularly through the integration of more visual elements to support user comprehension and
engagement with the BRMs.
Regarding the usefulness of the tool, participants largely perceived it as valuable. However, there were
some disagreements concerning the tool’s expected functionalities and capabilities. In particular, uncer-
tainty was expressed about the steps involving the design strategies, which were occasionally perceived
as lacking sufficient guidance or clarity. Regarding the applicability of the Design Tool, feedback from
the intended users indicated that the tool is generally comprehensive and easy to use. Nevertheless, its
practical relevance may vary depending on the type of user. For instance, SHS developers are more likely
to benefit from the tool, as they possess the capacity to influence the design of the systems and their
components. In contrast, the tool’s applicability in LMICs was perceived as more limited. This perception
stems from the observation that companies operating in these regions tend to prioritise increasing user
comfort over sustainability goals. Additionally, users themselves may not yet view sustainable behaviour
as a primary concern.
The collected feedback, along with the challenges identified during the evaluation, has informed a final
round of revisions. These changes aim to enhance clarity, guidance, and adaptability, resulting in the
final concept of the Behavioural Rebound Evaluation Toolkit within the scope of this thesis.

Behavioural Rebound Evaluation Toolkit
This chapter presents the final concept of the Behavioural Rebound Evaluation Toolkit for solar develop-
ment. It describes the user relevance and practical applicability, the target user group, and explains the
navigation steps of the Design Tool. Finally, it provides suggestions for future development.
6.1
Tool Relevance and Practical Applicability
The Behavioural Rebound Evaluation Toolkit is designed to support the identification and analysis
of BRMs in the development of SHS across different contexts (Van der Loo and Aarsen, 2025). This is
critical, as behavioural responses of SHS users can significantly reduce the environmental benefits these
systems are intended to deliver. To capture these environmental benefits, it is essential to understand
the underlying causes of behavioural changes and how they may contribute to RE. Preventing these
behavioural changes requires early intervention, ideally before the adoption of SHS. Therefore, it is
necessary during the development phase to anticipate which BRMs are likely to occur and how they can
be addressed or mitigated
This Design Tool supports SHS developers and designers in this process by enabling structured analysis
of CFs that may influence user behaviour. Second, it allows users to identify and select targeted design
strategies to prevent RE. Altogether, the toolkit promotes early-stage consideration of context-specific
behavioural responses, helping integrate behavioural insights into sustainable product design. The tool
can be applied across various settings and used either individually or collaboratively in small teams. The
output of the tool supports both awareness and ideation of design strategies that align with the user’s
development context.
Given the limited awareness of RE in sustainable design, particularly in LMICs, this toolkit offers an acces-
sible entry point for recognising and addressing rebound effects. It bridges the gap between theoretical
research and practical implementation by translating academic insights into an actionable design tool.
Its usefulness, usability, and applicability were validated through expert feedback from ten professionals
across behavioural science, SHS development, design engineering, RE, and the context of designing in
LMICs.

6.2
Target User
The primary target user of the Design Tool is a design engineer involved in the development of SHS, specif-
ically within the context of sub-Saharan Africa, as illustrated in Figure 4.13. However, insights gathered
from the evaluation indicate that companies operating in LMICs may not prioritise sustainability to the
extent that they are actively motivated to address or prevent RE. As a result, the tool has been developed to
be applicable and useful for designers working in both LMICs and HICs. In HICs, sustainability tends to be
a more prominent design priority, which may lead to greater motivation among designers to understand
BRMs and incorporate strategies to mitigate them. The tool is specifically designed to support the needs
of these users by enabling them to:
1. Anticipate behavioural changes that may occur following the adoption of SHS.
2. Identify which contextual factors influence such behaviours.
3. Evaluate appropriate design strategies to prevent or reduce the likelihood of RE.
By facilitating this process, the Design Tool encourages context-specific, sustainability-focused design
decisions throughout the SHS development process.

understanding of the tool’s outcome and what is expected of them.

6.4
Future Improvements
Several future improvements can be made to enhance the impact and usability of the Behavioural Re-
bound Evaluation Toolkit. First, incorporating a feature that allows users to quantify potential rebound
effects and track progress over time could significantly increase the tool’s value. Second, awareness-
building efforts should enhance the tool’s implementation, particularly in LMICs, by highlighting the
importance of sustainable behaviour even in the context of ongoing social and economic development.
Third, to improve overall usability and accessibility, the tool could be developed into a web-based plat-
form, enabling users to access it from any location at any time. Additionally, integrating a feedback
mechanism that allows users to input their experiences and suggestions could support iterative improve-
ment of the tool and foster user engagement. Lastly, further development should focus on providing
more explicit guidance and practical examples to support users in effectively implementing the design
strategies.

Discussion
The main objective of this thesis was to create an understanding of the behavioural rebound effect of solar
panel adoption and which contextual factors influence these rebound effects. Therefore, the following
main research question was developed: What are the behavioural mechanisms and contextual factors
driving rebound effects in communities adopting solar panels in low- to middle-income countries,
and how can these rebound effects be prevented?. To answer this question, several other sub-research
questions were proposed, addressing different phases of the Design Research Methodology in the thesis
(Blessing and Chakrabarti, 2009). This methodology proved valuable in ensuring the reliability of the
results and served as an effective structure for organising the thesis and guiding the research process.
Below, the main findings and limitations from each phase are discussed in relation to the corresponding
project phase. The section concludes with future research suggestions.
Descriptive Study - I
The sub-research questions central to the Descriptive Study - I phase focus on understanding the be-
havioural mechanisms and rebound effects following solar panel adoption, as well as the contextual
factors in low- to middle-income countries compared to high-income countries. The first question, "What
are the behavioural mechanisms and rebound effects following solar panel adoption?", is answered by a
systematic literature review and a review of an existing systematic literature review by Van der Loo and
Pigosso (2024b). The SLR focusing on RE following solar panel adoption reveals two main rebound effects:
an increase in energy consumption and the consumption of other (energy-consuming) products. Second,
it identifies moral licensing, contribution ethics, time discounting, motivational crowding, mental energy
accounting, loss aversion, behavioural income effect, and need satisfaction as behavioural rebound
mechanisms. Aligning the insights with the results from the review of the existing SLR yields a list of
15 contextual factors, including an assessment of their direction and strength of influence. A context
comparison and a survey to collect primary data revealed that the factors indicated as the strongest influ-
encing CFs in HIC are financial incentives, environmental self-identity, desire for comfort, feed-in tariffs,
income level, and daily consumption metering. Other factors, such as knowledge gaps and cognitive
capacity, are identified as more present in LMICs. The results, however, are subject to several limitations:
• The existing literature on rebound effects following solar panel adoption is limited, particularly
regarding behavioural responses in users of SHS in LMICs. Many studies focus primarily on the
economic aspects of rebound effects, often neglecting behavioural aspects. As a result, more
evidence is needed to establish strong causal relationships, which limits the overall reliability of the
results and generalisability of the findings.
• Most of the literature focuses on the direct rebound effects of an increase in energy, without

investigating the long-term behavioural rebound effects. The study by Nguyen et al.(2024) also
highlights that RE can diminish over time. The analysed literature in this thesis captures mostly RE
and behaviour at a specific point in time, which does not fully reflect the evolving rebound effects
over time, potentially overlooking delayed behavioural responses.
• This thesis presents the relationship between the behavioural rebound mechanisms and CFs within
a static framework, where each CF is considered to independently influence the BRMs. However,
this approach does not account for the potential interdependencies or interactions between the
contextual factors themselves. In a real-world setting, CFs may influence each other; for example,
social norms may influence environmental self-identity. Treating CFs as isolated inputs, the
framework may oversimplify the complexity of user environments and overlook important dynamic
relationships that could change the BRM.
• The context comparison of CFs between LMIC and HIC may not be universally generalisable to all
regions. The analysis highlights the complexity of applying broad country classifications, as factors
such as social norms, energy infrastructure, and user expectations can vary significantly across
contexts. The literature review performed to understand these differences primarily draws on
individual sources and does not offer a comprehensive overview of individual CF across different
settings. This limits the depth of cross-contextual insights and transferability of the findings.
• The survey performed to collect primary data on user consumption behaviour and influencing CFs,
might be subject to data bias. The participant may have been influenced by social bias and filled in
the answer based on socially accepted responses. Additionally, the survey sample size is relatively
small, with the majority of participants based in the Netherlands. As a result, the data does not
represent users in LMICs, which limits the robustness and generalisability of the conclusions.
Prescriptive Study - I
The Prescriptive Study phase aimed to prevent the identified rebound effects. To answer the sub-research
question, "How can the identified rebound effects be prevented?", first, a matrix was developed to analyse
the relationship between CFs and BRM, including potential connections not yet identified in the existing
literature. Second, a Design Tool was developed to help guide SHS developers identify potential BRMs
and provide design prevention strategies based on the connections identified in the matrix. The tool
enables designers to assess the presence of CFs in the design context, analyse highly likely behavioural
rebound mechanisms and select design prevention strategies. The development of the Design Tool and
the outcome are subject to several limitations:
• The Design Tool operates under the assumption that BRMs always occur and are influenced by the
CFs. However, this may overlook the possibility that BRMs do not always arise independently of
the CFs. This means that even if the tool indicates a high likelihood of the BRM occurring, it may
still not arise. Further research is needed to investigate whether and to what extent BRMs actually
occur in different settings.
• The Design Tool focuses on the prevention of BRM, with the aim of reducing RE across HIC and
LMICs. It builds on the assumption that SHS developers are motivated to understand and prevent
RE. However, RE effects might not be undesirable in all contexts. Feedback from industry experts
suggests that specific rebound effects, such as increased energy consumption and re-spending of
saved resources, can positively contribute to economic and social development in LMICs. In these
settings, sustainability might not be the primary concern of SHS users. This highlights the need for
understanding the desirability of prevention RE, since it may conflict with broader development
goals.

• The tool operates on the identified CFs in the literature review, and potential other connections
between the CFs and BRM that are not identified in the literature. However, it does not account for
or can be adapted to different CFs that have not yet been identified. This limits the reliability of
the BRM analysis of the tool. Future improvements may include further exploration of CFs that
influence the BRM.
• The design prevention strategies included in the tool are grounded in insights from existing litera-
ture that support their potential effectiveness. However, the design strategies have been iteratively
adapted to align with the specific context of SHS. As a result, the practical applicability of the
strategies within this particular setting has not yet been empirically proven, which could limit their
effectiveness in real-world scenarios.
Descriptive Study - II
The Descriptive Study - Phase II consisted of evaluating the design tool. The sub-research questions
that this chapter tried to answer were "How effective is the developed framework in terms of usability,
applicability and usefulness for its intended users and contexts?" and "What specific improvements are
required in the concept development, implementation, realisation, and adaptation to different contexts?".
The evaluation was conducted with experts in RE, behavioural science, the context of LMICs, design
engineering, and two companies that operate in a real-world setting with SHS. The evaluation sessions
provided valuable suggestions for improvement, and throughout the evaluation process, the tool was
continually refined and improved. Overall, the participants had a positive perception of the usability and
usefulness. However, discussing the Design Tool with the companies highlighted that the applicability
proved to be a bit more challenging due to the intended users’ motivation to focus on reducing RE, as
well as their unfamiliarity with RE in general. All the suggestions combined resulted in the final concept
of the Design Tool, focused on SHS developers who want to understand BRMs and prevent RE.
• The validation of the tool is performed with experts of RE, behaviour sciences, design engineering,
and industry experts. While these evaluators provide a representation of the areas that the Design
Tool covers, it does not cover an in-depth analysis in a real-world setting with multiple industry
partners. This limits the evaluated applicability of the Design Tool and diversity of user perspectives,
necessitating further evaluation through real-world cases and an analysis of the tool in LMICs and
HICs.
• The evaluation method is subject to potential biases in participant responses. The method involved
a critical incident identification through observation and the thinking-aloud method. Given that
the designer of the tool also acted as the observer during the evaluation, the participants may
have felt more inclined to provide more favourable feedback. This could potentially obscure the
identification of challenges or limitations in the tool’s usability, usefulness and applicability.
• The Design Tool was tested in an academic setting, where participants were asked to assume the role
of a designer working on SHS in an LMIC. However, this artificial environment may not accurately
reflect the complexities of a real-world SHS design in low-resource contexts. Additionally, the
participants’ limited knowledge of the context-specific conditions in LMICs limits the evaluation of
the tool’s outcome; the representativeness of the BRM and effectiveness of the proposed design
strategies.
Thesis Relevance
This thesis makes a scientific contribution to the field of sustainable design in low-income countries by
presenting an analysis of contextual differences of behavioural rebound effects that may arise following
the adoption of sustainable technologies. Existing literature has paid limited attention to how sustainable

interventions can lead to various behavioural responses across different contexts, specifically in LMICs,
thereby hindering the potential environmental benefits. Therefore, this thesis bridges the gap between
behavioural science, sustainable design, and cultural innovation studies, contributing to an interdisci-
plinary approach and establishing a foundation for further research into contextual variations in the
rebound effects of sustainable technology interventions. Building on the behavioural rebound framework
proposed by Van der Loo and Pigosso (2024b), this research examines the behavioural mechanisms in
the context of solar PV systems in both LMICs and HICs. Notably, it identifies a previously unidentified
behavioural mechanism, loss aversion, as well as a sub-category of mental accounting, mental energy

Furthermore, this thesis also provides practical value by proposing design strategies aimed at mitigating
rebound effects. These strategies are implemented through a design tool developed for SHS designers.
However, the tool facilitates early-stage consideration of context-specific behavioural responses and
can be adapted across diverse settings. The tool can also support policymakers, NGOs, educational
institutions, and solar energy program implementers seeking to understand the potential behavioural re-
bound mechanisms and improve the environmental effectiveness of solar PV interventions. By increasing
awareness of behavioural rebound effects and offering actionable design strategies, this thesis supports
the creation of more context-sensitive and impactful sustainable energy interventions in LMICs.
Future work
This thesis reveals several promising directions for future work. First, a more in-depth understanding
of the CFs and their relationship to BRMs is essential to enhance the robustness of the findings. This
could be achieved through the collection of primary data on user behaviour following the adoption of
SHS in various LMICs and HICs. Having a deeper understanding of the behavioural responses allows
for more precise identification and categorisation of the behavioural rebound effects across diverse
socio-cultural and economic contexts. Second, further research is needed to understand the complex
relationships between the CFs and their influence on specific BRMs. Identifying additional CFs that
shape user behaviour would not only advance the theoretical understanding of RE, but also help refine
and validate the framework underlying the Design Tool.
Regarding the Design Tool itself, further evaluation is required to assess its effectiveness in various
contexts, particularly across diverse user groups. Iterative testing with designers, practitioners, and local
stakeholders could support improvements in both functionality and relevance. Additionally, the tool can
be expanded to support other types of sustainable interventions. This would require further analysis of
contextual factors independent of the sustainable intervention itself. To maintain the relevance of the
tools over time, their influence scoring system should be regularly updated to reflect emerging literature
and empirical findings on RE. Moreover, future iterations of the tool could include enhanced features
that distinguish between the likelihood of rebound occurrence and the influence of contextual factors.
This supports more nuanced assessments of the BRMs and decision-making during the design process.

Conclusion
This thesis focuses on understanding and preventing of behavioural rebound effects in both LMICs and
HICs following solar panel adoption. By exploring how CFs influence BRMs, this research adopts an
interdisciplinary approach to understand how contextual differences potentially limit the environmental
benefits from SHS. The literature review highlights the gap in the current research on the behavioural re-
sponses of SHS users following the adoption of the solar PV systems, specifically in LMICs. Building on this
gap, this thesis proposes a set of 15 contextual factors that may differently influence BRMs across contexts.
These insights are operationalised in the Behavioural Rebound Evaluation Toolkit, a practical design
tool aimed at supporting SHS developers in analysing the user contexts and anticipating potential BRMs.
The tool offers context-sensitive design prevention strategies to mitigate the BRMs and raise awareness
among SHS developers about RE. It also holds potential for use in academic environments, policymaking,
and NGO work with SHS. However, due to the limited and scarce empirical studies, the influence of CFs
on BRMs occurrence remains suggestive. Future work should deepen the analysis of these factors and
their complex interactions to validate and refine the tool’s predictive value.
This research contributes to sustainable design in LMICs by supporting early understanding of how sus-
tainable design can influence behavioural responses of users. It expands upon the existing behavioural
rebound framework of Van der Loo and Pigosso (2024b) by investigating the how the behavioural mech-
anisms manifest in the context of SHS, including the identification of context-specific behavioural
mechanisms.
By making the analysis of BRMs more accessible and practical, particularly in LMICs, this thesis offers a
first step toward integrating user behaviour into sustainable design processes. It calls on researchers, SHS
developers, and policymakers to consider the social, economic, and cultural dynamics that can amplify
or suppress rebound effects, ensuring that the intended environmental benefits of clean energy systems
are realised.
