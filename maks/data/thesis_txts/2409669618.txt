Summary (English)
Digital pathology is gaining ground in the cancer clinic and research. Simultaneously, the amount
of data is continuously increasing, creating a need for digital solutions. Image analysis algorithms
are used to analyse histopathology images to gain more information about e.g. diagnosis and
treatment of specic types of cancer. However, stain variations are observed in these images,
where the exact same tissue can have dierent colors and intensities, e.g. when processed at dif-
ferent laboratories. Image sets with stain variations make it dicult for image analysis algorithms
to give consistent results regarding e.g. object detection, segmentation, or tissue classication.
The major stain variations are observed in tissues stained with hematoxylin and eosin (H&E),
which also is the most common staining technique used in histopathology [46].
Therefore, the aim of this thesis is to develop a stain normalization algorithm to reduce these
stain variations. This is accomplished by implementing Inorm, which is the developed stain
normalization algorithm based on the approach presented in the article by B. Bejnordi et al.,
2016 [23].
Inorm normalizes histopathology images (referred to as source images) stained with H&E in
reference to a target image. This thesis demonstrates that after applying Inorm, the normalized
images are matched to the color and intensity information of the target image, and thereby the
stain variations are reduced. Normalizing images with Inorm is shown to signicantly increase
the performance of a developed nuclei detection algorithm (p < 0.05), but decrease classication
of tumor tissue by Convolutional Neural Networks. Furthermore, the thesis also demonstrates
that various target images in Inorm can be applied to obtain the desired color distributions of
H&E. When comparing Inorm to four existing stain normalization algorithms [19, 28, 32, 45],
visual dierences between the normalized images are observed. Besides that, signicant dier-
ences in the performance of the nuclei detection algorithm on the normalized images are observed
between Inorm (F1 score= 0.66) and the four normalization algorithms (F1 score= [0.55, 0.63]),
where the highest performance is observed for Inorm.
The thesis also describes the limitations behind the implementation of Inorm. It is found that
Inorm has diculties in normalizing images containing artefacts, such as ink artefacts, and has
problems in completely separating nuclei (representing hematoxylin) from eosinophilic tissue
(representing eosin). Considering its limitations, this thesis proposes to apply Inorm on H&E
stained images to increase the performance of image analysis tasks, and to obtain a consistent
H&E representation in all images.

Summary (Danish)
Digital patologi bruges oftere og oftere inden for kræftforskning og klinisk onkologi. Samtidigt
øges mængden af data konstant, hvilket skaber et behov for brugbare digitale løsninger. I dag n-
des og udvikles billedanalysealgoritmer til at analysere histopatologiske billeder, som kan bruges
til at opnå mere information om specikke kræftformer, f.eks. i forbindelse med diagnosticerings-
og behandlingsprocessen. Et stort problem er dog, at der opstår farvningsvariationer i de hi-
stopatologiske billeder. Det ses ved, at det præcist samme væv har forskelligt udseende på to
forskellige billeder (med samme farvning), når vævet f.eks. er blevet processeret på to forskellige
laboratorier. Billeder med farvningsvariationer gør det svært for billedanalysealgoritmer at give
ensartede resultater. Der er især problemer på billeder af væv farvet med hematoxylin og eosin
(H&E), da denne farvning skaber store farvningsvariationer. Dette er et problem, da H&E er
den hyppigst brugte farvningsteknik inden for histopatologi [46].
Formålet med dette projekt er derfor at udvikle en farvningsnormaliseringsalgoritme som kan
tage højde for og reducere disse farvningsvariationer. Vi har udviklet farvningsalgoritmen Inorm,
som er baseret på metoden præsenteret i en af de nyeste artikler omhandlende farvningsnorma-
lisering af B. Bejnordi et al., 2016 [23].
Inorm normaliserer således histopatologiske billeder farvet med H&E i forhold til et givent target
billede. Dette projekt illustrerer, at billeder normaliseret med Inorm matcher farve- og inten-
sitetsinformationen i det pågældende target billede, og dermed reduceres farvningsvariationen
på tværs af billederne. Vi nder, at en udviklet kernedetektionsalgoritme har signikant høje-
re performance på billeder normaliseret med Inorm end på billeder som ikke er normaliseret
(p < 0.05). Vi nder dog også, at klassicering af tumorvæv med Convolutional Neural Networks
falder efter normalisering med Inorm. Derudover nder vi, at forskellige target billeder kan bru-
ges i Inorm til at opnå den ønskede farvningsdistribution for H&E. Ved at sammenligne Inorm
med re andre farvningsnormaliseringsalgoritmer [19, 28, 32, 45], ses der visuelle forskelle mellem
H&E farverne på de normaliserede billeder på tværs af de re metoder. Derudover, nder vi en
signikant forskel på kernedetektionens performance på de normaliseret billeder fra Inorm (F1
score= 0.66) og fra de re andre metoder (F1 score= [0.55, 0.63]), med højest performance ved
Inorm.
Samtidigt nder vi, at Inorm har forskellige begrænsninger, som der skal tages højde for under
normalisering. Blandt andet har Inorm problemer med at normalisere billeder med artefakter,
såsom artefakter fra tuscher, og Inorm har også problemer med at adskille alle kerner (som
repræsenterer hematoxylin) fra eosinolt væv (som repræsenterer eosin). Taget disse begræns-
ninger i betragtning, foreslår dette projekt, at Inorm bør anvendes på H&E farvede billeder til
at øge performance af vilkårlige billedanalysealgoritmer samt til at ensarte H&E udtryk.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Motivation
Cancer is an enormous global health burden, aecting every region and socioeconomic group
[58]. Today, cancer accounts for about one in every seven deaths worldwide, which is more than
HIV/AIDS, tuberculosis, and malaria combined [58].
Hence, research is growing to prevent,
screen, early detect, diagnose and treat cancer. One of the most important tools in both cancer
research and clinic is histopathology [5]. Only at Rigshospitalet, the largest and most highly
specialized hospital in Denmark, 600-700 histopathology slides are processed daily [2].
Histopathology refers to the microscopic examination of histological tissues to study presence
and characteristics of disease, especially cancer [19]. Prior to the evaluation of the tissue, the
tissue is stained to visualize various cellular structures, most commonly with hematoxylin and
eosin (H&E).
Digital pathology is the process by which histological slides are digitized to produce high resolu-
tion images via digital scanners and afterwards analyzed with image analysis algorithms for tissue
detection, segmentation, and classication, among others [18]. Digital pathology can thereby of-
fer a fast and comprehensive analysis of tissue, providing an automatic, objective classication,
and quantication, which otherwise can be time consuming and subjective for pathologists [26].
As explained, there are major advantages in applying digital histopathology, however there are
also some diculties. The appearance of the images depends on the underlying tissue structure
and the processing of the tissue. However, undesirable variations in the images arise from dif-
ferent reasons, such as tissue preparation, type of xation, staining procedures, and the scanner
used to digitize the slides [18, 19, 23, 26]. These stain variations are observed as dierent colors
and intensities of the same tissues, e.g. eosinophilic tissue being bright pink in one image and
being purple in another image. These variations make it dicult for image analysis algorithms
to give accurate and consistent results. To improve the performance of the image analysis algo-
rithms, stain variations should be reduced by applying stain normalization algorithms.
Stain normalization algorithms deals with detecting the stains present in the image, and trans-
forming the color and intensity information of these stains to match the stains of predened
target distributions. The purpose of applying stain normalization algorithms is thus to obtain a
consistent representation of H&E across large-scale data sets.
Stain normalization oers a great potential for improving future digital analysis of histopathol-
ogy images. This is highly necessary as digitalization of stained tissue is increasingly gaining
ground. Furthermore, large-scale research studies generate millions of images, which is beyond
the capacity of manual analysis and has to rely on digital quantication [22].
==PAGE:12==
1.2 Aim of thesis
2
1.2
Aim of thesis
The aim of the thesis is to investigate state of the art of current stain normalization methods
applied on digital histopathology images, and to develop and implement the best possible stain
normalization algorithm. As the most used staining technique worldwide is H&E and the major
variations are observed using this technique, the thesis deals with implementing an algorithm
which normalizes H&E stained images.
The purpose is thereafter to examine if the performances of automated image analysis tasks are
better on the normalized images compared to the images without normalization.
1.3
Outline of thesis
The rest of the thesis is organized as follows. Chapter 2 presents an overall description of the
background theory, which will be useful to understand the further thesis, together with a revision
of the previous work in the eld. Chapter 3 describes the data and the preprocessing. Chapter 4
involves a thorough description of the theory and methods applied in this thesis. In chapter 5 the
steps of the implemented stain normalization algorithm will be presented. Chapter 6 evaluates
the implemented algorithm across two dierent data sets and compares the approach with other
state of the art algorithms. Chapter 7 contains the discussion. Finally, chapter 8 gives concluding
remarks and chapter 9 presents suggestions to future work.

Chapter 2
Background
2.1
Tissue preparation
The process of preparing tissue for histology to obtain a digital histopathology image is illustrated
in gure 2.1. The steps are the following [35, 42]:
(a) A biopsy sample is taken from the site of interest of the patient with a biopsy needle.
(b) The tissue is xed to preserve the tissue in its natural state. As tissue contains a high
amount of water, the tissue is dehydrated, such that water is removed. Thereafter the
tissue is embedded in a solid material, usually paran. Finally, a rm block surrounding
the tissue is obtained.
(c) The tissue block is sectioned into ultra-thin slices (mostly around 2-3 µm thick).
(d) Each tissue slice is mounted on a glass slide with a mounting medium.
(e) As most tissues are colorless when sectioned, the tissue is stained such that is possible to
see the tissue structures.
(f) Afterwards the stained tissue on the glass slide is scanned. The digital histopathology
image is nally obtained.
Figure 2.1: Tissue preparation process. (a) A biopsy is taken from the patient, (b) the tissue
is xed, dehydrated and embedded, (c) the block is sectioned into thin slices, (d) and afterwards
mounted on a glass slide. (e) The glass slide is stained and (f) nally scanned to obtain a digital
histopathology image [7, 10, 44, 53, 55].

2.2 Digital Pathology
4
2.2
Digital Pathology
Previosly, before digital pathology existed, the tissue was processed and afterwards the pathol-
ogists examined the tissue through a microscope. Today, some pathologists use digital solutions
in their everyday while others look at the digital solutions with skepticism [3].
Some of the benets of applying digital pathology are: 1) The possibility of sharing between
departments and colleagues without being in the same location, 2) being able to reproduce the
same results and reduce human errors, subjectivity and variability, 3) reduce time to examine
and analyse a tissue specimen, and 4) nally being able to learn from data and use computational
methods such as machine learning to obtain new information or measures that pathologists can
not give [3, 50]. Some of the limitations are: 1) Artefacts observed after scanning, and 2) lack of
standardization during the tissue processing, which results in stain variations mentioned earlier.
This thesis will focus on solving the second mentioned limitation, 'lack of standardization', thus
approaching stain variability.
2.3
Hematoxylin and eosin
H&E is the most common staining technique used in histopathology [46].
As described by
Martin Rasmussen [54], H&E is the workhorse at the hospitals. It gives a quick overview of the
morphology, is easy, and cheap to use. H&E is used every day in the department of pathology,
used before any other type of stain, and is the staining technique which basis for most diagnosis
at the hospitals in Denmark [54].
2.3.1
Hematoxylin and eosin staining
As most tissues are colorless and transparent when thinly sectioned, the tissue must be colored
with a stain that highlights tissue structures [35], e.g. with H&E. There are dierent forms of
H&E solutions that each produce dierent stain patterns and colors [40]. In general, the combi-
nation of H&E produces blue, purple, pink, and red colors.
H&E solutions have dierent dispositions to bind to dierent proteins in the tissue, also regarded
as anities. This anity primarily depends on charge forces. Therefore, especially the pH of
H&E is critical for an optimal staining, as the charge of the proteins will change when the pH
changes. Other features such as the chemical structure of the proteins can also have an eect on
the anity [40].
Hematoxylin has high anity for binding to chromatin, which is placed inside cell nuclei. In a
certain pH range, chromatin is negatively charged and hematoxylin is positively charged, and
charge forces make hematoxylin bind to chromatin. Thereby cell nuclei are colored blue/purple
by hematoxylin [40].
Eosin is used afterwards as a cytoplasmic counterstain. Eosin is in a certain pH range negatively
charged. Most proteins in the cytoplasm have positively charged amino acid chains, and again
charge forces make eosin bind to these proteins. Thereby proteins in the cytoplasm are colored
pink by eosin [40]. Other structures such as smooth muscle cells, stromal tissue, and erythrocytes
are also colored dierent ranges of pink by eosin.

2.4.1
Stain variation observed in ten Danish hospitals
To illustrate the large variations observed when staining with H&E, images from a study exam-
ining H&E variations in Danish hospitals are presented. The images are obtained from a study
which Martin Rasmussen [54] conducted. The study examined stain variation observed in ten
Danish hospitals, who are anonymous. A multi block consisting of 40 dierent tissue types was
sectioned into ten slices, as illustrated in gure A.1-A.3 in appendix A. All processing steps of

2.4.2.1
Stain variations with hematoxylin and eosin
The information given below is primarily based on information provided by Jane Andersen [2]
and Martin Rasmussen [54] when visiting Rigshospitalet and Odense University Hospital.
Today, there exists protocols for staining with H&E. The protocol applied at the Department
of Pathology, Rigshospitalet is shown in appendix B. However, not the exact same protocol is
applied in all other Danish hospitals. The protocol for staining with H&E at the hospital is
based on an agreement between the pathologists and the Medical Laboratory Technicians (Dan-
ish: bioanalytiker) at the given hospital. They decide the protocol to obtain a specic color and
intensity information for dierent tissues, and this protocol is applied when staining all tissues
at the hospital.
However, even at the same hospital, using the exact same protocol, variations are observed. Sev-
eral reasons can be considered as an explanation to these variations. Firstly, the primary method
of staining with H&E is by the 'dip and dunk' method. Some use autostainers where the racks
with glass slides are automatically moved from one container to another, while others are still
manually dipped into containers. When manually dipping, a stop watch is used to determine
the transition from one container to another, however variations in these timings occur, which
can cause the nal stain variations. Secondly, even if autostainers are used, variations can still
be observed, e.g. due to dilution and contamination, where tissue from one glass slide falls of
and moves to another glass slide. A reduced concentration occurs especially due to the 'dip and
dunk' method, where remains from the previous container (e.g. with alcohol or water) are being
transferred to the next container.
External quality assessment is performed on some immunohistochemical stains2, where certain
measures of stain quality are given. However, these external quality assessments are not applied
for H&E.
To sum up, large variations are observed when staining with H&E. Hence, stain normalization
is essential.

2.5
Stain Normalization
2.5.1
What is stain normalization?
Stain normalization refers to the application of image analysis algorithms which reduce the varia-
tions observed in histopathology images. The purpose of applying stain normalization algorithms
is to match all images to a target image and thereby obtain a consistent representation of H&E
across large-scale data sets.
Stain normalization is often implemented by rstly identifying a single image, the target image,
which represents the characteristics that the normalized images should match [18]. Thus, when
selecting a target image for H&E stained images, the target image should contain 'optimal' color
and intensity information for H&E. Thereafter, all other images to be normalized, the source
images, are transformed such that their color and intensity distributions match those of the
target image. The impact of the choice of target image will be shown later in section 6.1.5 and
discussed further in section 7.2.
2.5.2
State of the art
In the following, state of the art articles regarding current stain normalization algorithms will
be covered.
Note, when comparing the methods below, that dierent data sets has been applied for each
method. Two review articles, Onder et al. [26] and Sethi et al. [20], comment on the need for a
common digital pathology slide database to be used in stain normalization studies. Without hav-
ing this type of database, it is much more dicult to compare the performance of each method
with one another.
Furthermore, Onder et al. comment on the need of a method to evaluate the performance of
the normalization methods. Some of the studies below only present the images before and after
normalization, and validates the normalization visually. In other studies, dierent image analysis
techniques are applied before and after normalization, such as object detection, segmentation,
and tissue classication [18]. Onder et al. conclude that the best performance measure is the
comparison of image analysis algorithms before and after normalization [26].
In table 2.1 the essential articles covering stain normalization are presented. The table shows
the authors, publication dates, the overall method headlines, and the references to the articles
which describe or test the given article. The table also shows four of the common types of mea-
suring the performance of a normalization method: 1) the accuracy of a classication algorithm
(e.g. nuclei detection, tissue classication) which quanties the agreement between ground truth
(pathologist's annotation) and the algorithm, 2) the similarity between the normalized images
and a target image or and reference stain vectors3, and 3) visual appearance. Notice that some
of the methods below are described and evaluated in review articles [20, 26].

Histogram-based stain normalization
Histogram-based stain normalization represents one of the simplest methods of normalization
introduced by Jain et al. [45]. Using this approach, the RGB histogram of a source image is

image captures the exact same scene as the target image, except with a color change [22]. How-

scenes.

image, which is not always the case as illustrated in gure 2.4 and 2.5. In the gures, two examples

2.5.2.2
Color deconvolution
The method most frequently used for stain normalization is color deconvolution (CD) [18] to the
so-called optical density space. The basic concept behind color deconvolution is to separate each
individual stain and afterwards normalize each stain independently. In the case of using H&E,
the aim is to separate H&E, normalize each stain and afterwards recombine them to obtain a
normalized version of the original image [18].
To separate H&E in the RGB-space a non-linear model is required, as observed in image 2.7a.
However, in CD a simplied version of Beer-Lambert's law is used to simplify this separation by
calculating the optical densities [64]

The principle behind color deconvolution is summarized below:
• Separate the H&E stains by determining the stain vectors
• Estimate the contribution from each stain
• Normalize each stain vector independently
• Normalize all pixels as a linear combination of the two stain vectors

improve the original method proposed by Ruifrok and Johnston [57]. Consult table 2.1 for more
information.

surrounding tissue. The nuclei is dilated inside the nuclei possibility area, such that larger areas
of the weak nuclei can be found. Then the nuclei are separated based on distance and size.
Finally, all labels but nuclei are removed, such that a classied image with only one class is left.

The color information in an image is normally specied using the red-green-blue (RGB) color
model. In this model, a color is represented as a point inside a 3-dimensional cube, with the
three components being red, green, and blue [41].
When an image is captured of stained tissue, the resulting intensity in each (RGB) channel
depends on the absorption characteristics of the used stains and the amount in which those
stains are present. This is described by a simplied version of Lambert-Beers law [64]
Ich = I0,ch · e−S·cch,
(4.1)
where Ich is the intensity measured in channel ch, I0,ch is the intensity of channel ch when no
stain is present (i.e. background), and S represents the amount of stain with the absorption
coecients cch in channel ch.
The rst aim in Inorm is to separate the dierent stains present in an image, and classify each
pixel as belonging to a specic stain. The information useful for this classication is the absorp-
tion characteristic (cch), which dierentiates the stains from each other. Ideally all other factors
should be removed [64], and the stain separation should thus be independent of the amount of
stain. Thus, pixels showing the same color information but dierent intensity information, should
be classied as belonging to the same stain.
As the RGB space mixes the color and intensity information in the image, stain separation
results in complex 3D distributions (as illustrated in gure 2.7a). This makes it a dicult and
time-consuming task to recognize and separate the stains in the image [64]. Therefore, another
color model is proposed.

information. The chromatic information is described by the hue (dominant wavelength) and
saturation (purity of the color), where e.g. pink has a red hue and a low saturation [41]. The
intensity information is described using optical densities

4.2
Nuclei segmentation for hematoxylin sampling - Step 2
The aim with the nuclei segmentation is to nd representative samples from the hematoxylin
(H) class, which serve as ground truth for the subsequent classiers.
The review article by Onder et al., 2014 [26] states that no normalization method did take local
spatial features into account, and suggests this as an idea to future normalization methods.
Bejnordi et. al, 2016 [23] later develop a normalization method taking spatial information into
account. They use the spatial information regarding nuclei present in the tissue, as they assume
that the nuclei are round structures and are stained by hematoxylin. They use a variation of
Circular Hough Transform to detect the nuclei in the image.
However, the Circular Hough
Transform is restricted to look for circles and not e.g. elongated ellipses [43]. However, as the
nuclei in the data set have dierent shapes and sizes, this method is not suitable. Therefore,
another method is suggested.
4.2.1
Circularity measure
The proposed method is also based on detecting nuclei by searching for circular-like objects. The
roundness measure is dened by the circularity of an object
Circularity = 4πA
P 2 ,

where A is the area in pixels, and P is the perimeter [51]. The circularity is in the range [0; 1],
where a value close to 1 is a perfect circle and 0 is a line [51]. However, the circularity measure
is not only used to nd perfect circle-like nuclei with a circularity equal to 1. A low threshold is
selected such that dierent shapes of nuclei can be detected, but avoiding detecting any lines.

Two classiers are implemented and tested as classiers, K-Nearest Neighbour classier and
Articial Neural Network classier. K-Nearest Neighbour was selected as classier as it was
originally used in the paper by B. Bejnordi et al. [23]. The neural network was selected to try
out a more complex classier. Other classication algorithms could also be tested, and it is not
known if these classiers would perform better on this specic data.
4.3.1
K-Nearest Neighbour classier
The paper by B. Bejnordi [23] uses a K-Nearest Neighbour (k-NN) classier with 7 neighbours.
k-NN is one of the simplest classication algorithms. When using k-NN each data point is placed
in a d-dimensional space, where d is the number of features. K-NN is used to classify a new point
in feature space, however it does not 'learn' anything but solely relies on the distances in the
feature space [56]. Thus, to determine the classication of a point, k-NN combines the labels of
the K nearest points. It uses majority vote, where a point is assigned to the class most common
among its K nearest neighbours [67].
For this data set (cx, cy, D) are the features, i.e. each data point (or pixel) is a point in the
three-dimensional feature space, as illustrated in gure 4.6b. The Euclidean distance is chosen

as distance metric. The three features are standardized such that they have mean equal to 0 and
standard deviation equal to 1, which is important as dierent distance ranges will have a major
impact on the classication.
4.3.1.1
Number of neighbours
When classifying a new point in feature space, the number of neighbours, k, has a signicant
impact on this classication. As illustrated in image 4.7, a low k gives more curvature in the
decision boundaries, and the classication of a new point is determined by its local neighbours.
However, when applying low k the classier is more susceptible to overtting and can be inu-
enced by outliers. Higher values of k gives a smoother decision boundary, however, too high
values may lead to misclassications.

4.3.2
Neural Network
4.3.2.1
Neural Network as classier
Articial Neural Networks (ANN) can be much more exible than other classication methods
and can model nonlinearities in data [62]. In comparison with k-NN, ANN does 'learn' before
seeing a test point, which should be classied.
To create a neural network classier, it should rstly be trained to estimate the parameters of
the neural network, which are described later. The parameter estimation is performed using a
numerical optimization method. However, ANN does not always nd the optimal solution, and
is therefore trained multiple times with dierent random initializations, and the network with
best training error is chosen. Furthermore, the ANN tends to overt and therefore regularization
terms are applied to prevent this.
The neural network tools are obtained from ANN:DTU Toolbox [11], which holds a collection of
Articial Neural Networks (ANN) algorithms implemented for MatlabTM.

Naive Bayes classier - Step 6
A third classier is applied in one of the nal steps of Inorm, the Naive Bayes classier. This
classier is used to create certain weights, which are used to weight the nal chromatic and
density distributions. The purpose of applying weights is to account for the fact that the color in
a pixel is not only aected by one stain, i.e. the nal pixel color is determined by the absorption
of hematoxylin stain, eosin stain, and the background of the given pixel.
The classiers mentioned earlier (k-NN and NN) are used as hard classiers, as each pixel is
classied as belonging to a single class - either H, E or B [14]. The Naive Bayes classier can be
regarded as a soft classier, as each pixel is classied as belonging to more than one class with
a certain weight - for H, E, and B [14]. Besides that, the Naive Bayes takes prior information
into account when classifying a pixel.
The Naive Bayes classier is thus used after the 'hard classication' to calculate the posterior
probability of a given pixel belonging to each of the three classes (H, E, and B). The posterior
probabilities are thereafter used as weights.
E.g.
a nuclei pixel could be given the weights:
H = 0.87, E = 0.12, B = 0.1.

Stain Normalization Algorithm
5.1
Overview of Inorm
Inorm was implemented in MATLAB R2016, The MathWorks Inc., Natick, MA, 2000. During
the development of the algorithm, dierent goals are fullled:
• Take local morphological and spatial features into account.
• Create an automated framework with as little manual training as possible.
• Incorporate a quantitative performance measurement to validate Inorm.
The review article by Onder et al. 2014 [26] observes that all articles reviewed 'operated on indi-
vidual pixels without taking the local spatial dependency of structures in the histopathology slides
into account'[26], and suggest incorporating local spatial information to improve the current nor-
malization algorithms. For this reason, spatial information is also considered in the development
of Inorm.
The goal was also to implement a normalization algorithm with as little manual training or
manual framework as possible. A fully-automated framework is especially advantageous when
working with large-scale data set.
The development of Inorm is based on one of the newest implemented approaches from B.
Bejnordi et al., 2016 [23]. However, modications to this approach are made to create Inorm.
Two versions of Inorm are created; one for the colon tissue data and one for the breast tissue
data. However, the only dierence between the two versions is the threshold value applied when
segmenting in step 2 (see below).
5.1.1
Assumptions
Before explaining each step in Inorm, the assumptions in the model are presented:
(a) The staining has been performed with hematoxylin and eosin.
(b) The target image contains the 3 classes: hematoxylin (H), eosin (E), and background (B).
(c) The source image contains more than 1% tissue. Otherwise the image is not normalized.
(d) The source image (with more than 1% tissue) contains eosin (E) and background (B), and
can contain hematoxylin (H).
(e) Structures stained by hematoxylin are dierent from straight lines, but do not necessarily
have to be ellipsis or perfectly round.
(f) The source image contains no artefacts (e.g. ink artefacts) representing other classes than
H, E, or B.

1) Transform from RGB- to (cxcyD)-space by HSD transform

The rst step in Inorm is applying the HSD transform to transform the image from RGB-

Before calculating the cx, cy, ad D values, outliers from the density channels (Dr, Dg, and Db)
are removed. This is done by setting values above the 98th percentile equal to the 98th percentile.
Afterwards images with more that 99% background are removed by thresholding the D-channel.
2) Sampling from 3 classes (H, E, B)
Samples from the 3 classes; hematoxylin (H), eosin (E), and background (B) are obtained. H
samples are found by segmenting the nuclei or circle-like objects in the image. B samples are
afterwards obtained by thresholding the D-channels. Both the D, Dr, Dg, and Db channels are
thresholded, as low densities represent background pixels. Finally, samples from E are found by
removing the pixel candidates for either H or B.
500 samples are drawn from each of the three classes: H, E, and B, such that in total 1500
samples are obtained. These samples serve as ground truth data, and are used for training the
neural network classier in (3) and the Naive Bayes classier in (6).
Number of samples
The number of drawn samples (nsamples) was determined visually by comparing the appear-
ances of the normalized images for dierent nsamples (between 1-2000). It was observed that
nsamples above 500 did not not make visual dierence, and therefore nsamples = 500 was de-
termined. However, in images with few nuclei, it is not possible to obtain 500 samples to the H
class. Therefore nsamples is reduced automatically to the minimum number of samples available
of each of the three classes. The number of samples should always be equal for the three classes
to train the subsequent classiers.
If no nuclei and therefore no H is present, the number of classes are reduced to two. In that
case, the classication in step 3) is not performed.
3) Classify all pixels into 3 classes (H, E, B)
After obtaining the samples from the three classes, the neural network (NN) classier is trained.

the (cx, cy, D)-values for the H- and E-samples together with their corresponding class labels.
The NN is thereafter used to classify the pixels not being background or training samples by
using the remaining (cx, cy, D)-values as input features.

To validate the normalization by Inorm, the performance of the nuclei detection algorithm is
evaluated. Its performance is compared before and after normalization by calculating the preci-
sion, recall, and F1 score.
Two versions of the nuclei detection algorithm are developed. The rst version is tuned to 10
training images without normalization, and the second version is tuned to the same 10 training
images with normalization. Hence, all steps of the two algorithms apart from the thresholds are
the same.
The normalization is performed, applying the target image in gure 6.1 on the 60 validation
images. The precision, recall, and F1 score is calculated for each image, and the mean values of
these calculations are shown in table 6.1. The performance measures are calculated image-wise
such that each image has the same impact on the overall score, independent on the number of
nuclei in the image.
Table 6.1 shows the performance results. It illustrated, that the nuclei detection algorithm has
higher precision but lower recall on normalized images than images without normalization. Thus,
fewer false positive nuclei are present but having a higher number of false negative nuclei.
The overall measure taking both the precision and recall into account is the F1 score, which is
higher for the normalized images (0.66) than for the non-normalized images (0.61). A paired
t-test is applied to test the null hypothesis, that there are no dierences between the means of
the two groups (before and after normalization). The paired t-test rejects the null hypothesis
(p < 0.05), i.e. showing that there is a signicant dierence between the means of the F1 scores.
This indicates that normalization improves the performance of image analysis algorithms such

Chapter 7
Discussion
7.1
Low F1 scores for the nuclei detection algorithm
When evaluating the performance of the nuclei detection algorithm, the F1 score is regarded as
the most important validation measure. However, when evaluating the results of the F1 scores
on the validation data set, the F1 score never exceeds 0.66 - neither before or after normalization.
Ideally the F1 score should approach 1.
Why is the F1 score so low?
1. It is dicult to detect nuclei in images where the nuclei are "smeared out", which is
illustrated in gure 7.1. Besides that, it is dicult for the pathologist to manually mark
the nuclei centers in these images, which also could give rise to a low F1 score. If these
types of images were removed, the F1 score would increase, but to avoid modifying the
data, these results are not presented.
2. The F1 score tends to be very low in images containing few nuclei. E.g. if the image
contains 20 nuclei and 5 of them are wrongly classied, the F1 score is very low.
3. Finally, the F1 score may be low because of the limitations in obtaining samples from H,
E, and B, which will be explained in section 7.4.

7.2
Choice of target image
The impact of choosing dierent target images was presented in section 6.1.5. It was shown, that
the performance of the nuclei detection algorithm was altered after normalization (F1 score =
0.64  0.66) when comparing to before normalization (F1 score = 0.61).
However, how should the target image be selected, and what is a good target image?
This is much discussed in articles regarding stain normalization [19, 20, 23, 26]. According to Jane
Andersen [2] and Martin Rasmussen [54], the most important property of a good target image,
is that it represents a good contrast between the structures, which are wished to be examined.
In regard to applying image analysis algorithms, the target image should be selected to optimize
the performance of the specic task, e.g. if the image analysis algorithm should detect nuclei,
the target image should represent good contrast between nuclei and other structures.
7.3
Appearance of stain variations
The purpose of Inorm is to remove the stain variations observed within H, E, and B. However,
it is dicult to identify the underlying causes behind these stain variations, and therefore also
dicult to know what type of information Inorm actually removes. Does Inorm remove variations
caused by staining, scanning, sectioning, or caused by actual biological dierences?
This is
dicult to determine visually by looking at the images. Therefore, studies should be conducted
varying the xation type, thickness, staining procedure, scanning, etc. separately, and thus gain
more knowledge about these stain variations.
7.4
Obtaining ground truth samples for H, E, and B
7.4.1
Thresholding DR-channel
As explained in section 4.2, samples representing the hematoxylin class (H) are obtained by
detecting circular-like structures, i.e. nuclei. The circular-like structures are found after thresh-
olding the DR-channel, to remove eosin and background samples. However, it was problematic to
nd a xed threshold value which could be applied across the DR-channel in all images. There-
fore, a fairly low threshold value was applied, which would increase if above 30 % of the pixels
where classied as nuclei.
Applying automatic thresholds, such as percentile thresholding or thresholding by Otsu's method
was tested instead of applying xed thresholds, but showed bad results. Percentile thresholding
was problematic due to the fact that each image in the data set has a dierent proportion of each
class (H, E, and B), e.g. if one image has a high number of background pixels (implying a low
threshold) and another has a high number of eosin pixels (implying a higher threshold). Thus,
the threshold value obtained from calculating a certain percentile would be highly dependent on
the information in the image.

Thresholding by Otsu's method1 was problematic when e.g. having tissues with purple cyto-
plasm showing little contrast between nuclei and cytoplasm, which led to an overestimation of
the number of hematoxylin pixels.
For the breast cancer data set, a xed threshold was tested across multiple patches. Multiple
patches were obtained from the same whole slide image, and thereby it was tested to select a
threshold based on the median values of the patches belonging to the same whole slide image.
However, as stain variations are observed in the same whole slide image this did not increase the
performance either.
This implies that the problem of selecting a suitable threshold is complicated and needs to be in-
vestigated further. This threshold is highly important as the samples are found after thresholding.
Besides that, the DR-channel is not necessarily the best channel to threshold to detect nuclei.
The density channel was applied in the article by B. Bejnordi et al. [23], however other channels
could also be examined, e.g. cx and cy. This will be left for future work.

Chapter 8
Conclusion
The thesis deals with reducing stain variations which arise e.g. due to variations in tissue prepa-
ration, staining procedures, and scanning procedures. These variations are especially observed in
histopathology images stained with hematoxylin and eosin (H&E). To deal with stain variations
the stain normalization algorithm, Inorm, is proposed. Inorm is based on one of the newest
state of the art papers by B. Bejnordi et al., 2016 [23]. Inorm was applied on two data sets; one
consisting of colon tissue data with manually annotated nuclei centers by a pathologist, and an-
other consisting of breast tissue data with known tumor regions, both tissues stained with (H&E).
It was visually observed that stain variations were reduced after applying Inorm, and the contrast
between hematoxylin and eosin was enhanced. It was also observed, that the colors of the nuclei
(representing hematoxylin) and eosinophilic tissue (representing eosin) approached the colors of
the same structures in the target image.
In addition, the performance of a nuclei detection algorithm was investigated on the colon tissue
images before and after normalization.
It was observed that the performance increased sig-
nicantly on the normalized images, as the agreement with a pathologists nuclei annotations
increased. Also, another image analysis algorithm was tested which classied tumor in breast
tissue using Convolutional Neural Networks. However, the accuracy of the tumor classier de-
creased after normalization with Inorm.
Furthermore, it was shown, that Inorm could normalize images to dierent target images.
Lastly, Inorm was compared to four state of the art stain normalization algorithms: RGB His-
togram Specication [45], Reinhard et al. [28], Macenko et al. [32], and Khan et al. [19]. A
signicant dierence was observed between the performance of the nuclei detection algorithm
using the four stain normalization algorithms and Inorm, where images normalized by Inorm
gave the best performance.
Several limitations of the algorithm were identied. Inorm had diculties in normalizing im-
ages containing artefacts (e.g. ink artefacts), and had diculties when obtaining samples from
hematoxylin and eosin as discussed in section 7.4.
To sum up, Inorm is suggested to be used for H&E stained histopathology images before applying
image analysis algorithms, as it provides better results than existing stain normalization algo-
rithms. Thereby it would be possible to obtain fully automatic image analysis algorithms, and
avoid manual calibration steps, when analyzing histopathology images. However, the limitations
presented in this thesis should be examined to further improve the normalization results.

Chapter 9
Future Work
This chapter describes the work that is left out of this thesis, and which would be the natural
steps to pursue in the future work of Inorm.
• Changes to Inorm
 Add eosin (E) classier: Samples from the eosin (E) class are obtained by removing
candidate samples from the hematoxylin (H) and background (B) class. However, no
'specic' detection of samples belonging to E is applied. Thereby E can contain noisy
samples from e.g. ink artefacts. To avoid this, a classier detecting E samples should
be implemented.
 Detect samples in cxcy-space: Samples from H and E serve as ground truth data
for training the classiers in Inorm. It should be examined if identifying them in the
(cxcy)-space would give better results, as H and E are organized in clusters in this
space.
 Change weigths: The Naive Bayes classier is used to weight the contribution of
each stain type in each pixel. Instead of using these weights, it should be examined if
the weights calculated by the neural network gives a better result.
 Optimize execution time: Future work could also be to optimize the execution
time of Inorm. The slowest step in Inorm is the piecewise linear scaling, and it is
therefore suggested to optimize this step rstly.
• Incorporate a distance measure between two distributions in 3D color space:
To examine if the H, E, and B color distributions in the normalized images approach the
color distributions of the same classes in the target image, a distance measure should be
incorporated. The most common distance measure used among stain normalization algo-
rithms is the standard deviation of the normalized median intensity (NMI). However, using
this measure, the intensities are compared, but the color information is not. Therefore,
another distance measure taking both the color and intensity information into account
should be estimated. This measure must compare the H, E, and B distributions in the
3D-space (e.g. (cxcyD)-space, HSD-space, HSI-space, or RGB-space) in the original images
and normalized images.
• Apply Inorm to more data and test other image analysis algorithms: Inorm
should be tested on other data sets of tissues stained with H&E. Preferably data sets with
tissues from dierent organs, and processed at dierent laboratories presenting dierent
variations. After normalizing, the performance of other image analysis algorithms should
be tested, e.g. tissue classication, nuclei segmentation, etc.
