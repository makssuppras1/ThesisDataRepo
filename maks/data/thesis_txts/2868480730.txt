Abstract
Purpose: This thesis aimed to evaluate the potential of Diffusion Model (DM) for tumor
segmentation in 3D PET imaging, specifically focusing on the head and neck area, which
presents unique challenges for accurate cancer delineation. The study sought to com­
pare the performance of DMs to the widely­used nnU­Net framework while identifying the
challenges and limitations of diffusion­based methods in segmentation tasks.
Methods: This research involved implementing both a Latent Diffusion Model (LDM) and
a Denoising Diffusion Probabilistic Model (DDPM) pipeline. In the LDM pipeline, 3D PET
and mask data were autoencoded into a latent space before undergoing the diffusion pro­
cess. Multiple configurations for autoencoding, including VAE­GAN, VAE, and AE, were
explored alongside simple synthetic data generation to expand the dataset and assess
its impact on performance. Additionally, the performance of the LDM was systematically
compared against the DDPM to evaluate their respective effectiveness in this context.
Results: The findings demonstrated that while nnU­Net achieved superior segmentation
performance, the LDM pipeline exhibited potential due to its modularity and ability to in­
tegrate multimodal data. However, significant information loss during the autoencoding
stage was identified as a key limitation, negatively affecting both reconstruction quality
and subsequent diffusion. Tripling the dataset size through simple synthetic data genera­
tion led to significant improvements in Gross Tumor Volume (GTV) autoencoding results,
highlighting the importance of dataset size. While the final LDM output did not achieve
a high Dice Similarity Coefficient (DSC), it demonstrated the model’s ability to accurately
identify tumor regions. Additionally, the implementation of a standard DDPM using only
PET data yielded promising results, achieving a DSC of 0.668 across all test samples
(comparing to nnUnet DSC=0.80) and 0.814 for larger tumors.
Conclusion: Although the current implementation of LDM and DDPM did not outperform
nnU­Net, the findings indicate that diffusion models are a promising avenue for future
research in segmentation tasks. Addressing limitations such as information loss, refining
synthetic data generation techniques, and incorporating additional data modalities could
enhance the performance and robustness of diffusion models, ultimately unlocking their
full potential.

Introduction
Cancer remains one of the leading causes of morbidity and mortality worldwide [Wor22;
Rit19]. It is characterized by uncontrolled cell proliferation, which can invade surrounding
tissues and metastasize to distant organs [SMJ20]. The heterogeneity and unpredictabil­
ity of cancer cell formation present significant challenges not only in diagnosing the dis­
ease but also in planning effective treatments and providing accurate prognoses. Despite
rapid technological advancements, cancer treatment remains a complex and difficult is­
sue in many aspects. Medical imaging plays a crucial role as the primary method for
tumor localization, enabling the detection, characterization, and monitoring of cancerous
changes, which are essential for guiding clinical decision­making.
Head and neck cancers (HNCs) encompass a diverse group of malignancies arising from
the oral cavity, pharynx, larynx, nasal cavity, and paranasal sinuses [16]. Predominantly
head and neck squamous cell carcinomas are associated with risk factors such as to­
bacco use, alcohol consumption, and human papillomavirus (HPV) infection [16]. These
cancers are notorious for their aggressive nature, propensity for early metastasis and sig­
nificant impact on vital functions such as speech and swallowing. Accurate delineation
of tumor boundaries plays a critical role in understanding the size and progression of the
tumor within the body. It is particularly important in treatment planning, where precision
is essential to maximize tumor control while minimizing exposure of healthy tissues to
radiation [03].
Positron Emission Tomography (PET) combined with Computed Tomography (CT) is a
pivotal tool in oncological diagnostics. It synergistically merges metabolic information
from PET, which uses radiotracers such as 18F­FDG to highlight areas of high glycolytic
activity characteristic of tumors, with detailed anatomical data provided by CT scans. This
hybrid approach improves tumor visualization and accurate staging. Moreover, by defin­
ing tumor volumes more precisely and facilitating dose escalation to metabolically active
regions, PET/CT enables highly targeted radiotherapy while minimizing damage to sur­
rounding healthy tissues [Boe+14a]. However, the intricate anatomy of the head and neck
region and the variable physiological uptake in structures such as salivary glands, mus­
cles, and lymphoid tissues present significant challenges for accurately segmenting tumor
tissue from surrounding healthy structures. An additional challenge is separating tumor
cells from those that are reactive to treatment in patients observed at different stages of
therapy. The manual segmentation of PET/CT images is labour­intensive and subject
to inter­observer variability, highlighting the need for automated, accurate segmentation
methods [Hat+11; Kov+24].
Traditional image analysis techniques for PET/CT segmentation have relied on threshold­
based methods, region growing, and edge detection algorithms [Mit+21]. While these
methods offer simplicity, they often fail to account for the complex heterogeneity and 3D
characteristics in PET/CT images. The advent of machine learning ML has revolutionized
medical image analysis, with algorithms capable of learning from data to make predic­
tions [Est+19]. More recently, deep learning (DL), a subset of ML involving neural net­
works with multiple layers, has shown remarkable success in image segmentation tasks
[Lit+17a; Kov+24]. Convolutional Neural Networks CNNs have been particularly effective,
learning hierarchical features directly from the image data [RFB15a]. Among these, Ko­
vacs et al. [Kov+24] demonstrated state­of­the­art nnU­Net implementation for PET/CT
segmentation, achieving a performance Dice Similarity Coefficient (DSC) of 0.80, which

is comparable to expert delineations. This work aligns with the HECKTOR challenge
[And+22], an international benchmark competition that has driven innovation in HNC seg­
mentation by encouraging participants to evaluate and refine their approaches on a shared
dataset. However, models like nnU­Net cannot incorporate clinical context, such as prior
treatments or referral reasons, which are critical in real­world clinical decision­making.
Diffusion­based models DM present a promising alternative to overcome nnUnet’s limita­
tions. Unlike CNNs, diffusion models offer a more natural mechanism to integrate condi­
tional information, such as clinical history, directly into the segmentation process. Their
flexible architecture and multimodality allow them to integrate and adapt to various types
of medical imaging, such as PET/CT, MRI, ultrasound and clinical textual data [Fen24;
Kaz+23]. All of this promises to create personalized segmentation models tailored to in­
dividual patient characteristics [Wol+21].
Inspired by the physical process of diffusion, Diffusion Models have gained prominence
in generative modeling and image synthesis by learning to reverse a noising process,
gradually refining simple distributions into complex data representations [HJA20]. While
their generative capabilities have been widely explored, their effectiveness in medical im­
age segmentation remains to be systematically evaluated. Recent studies have demon­
strated the potential of diffusion models in medical image segmentation, offering robust­
ness against noise, better handling of uncertainty and the ability to capture intricate data
structures [Wol+21]. They can capture subtle variations and fine­grained details in tumor
boundaries [Kaz+23] and also manifest strong generalization capabilities across different
imaging modalities and datasets, reducing the risk of overfitting to specific data charac­
teristics [Son+21a]. Therefore, a key objective is to determine whether diffusion­based
models can achieve segmentation performance comparable to, or better than, state­of­
the­art CNN­based approaches such as nnU­Net [Kov+24].
1.1
Aim
This project aims to develop and evaluate diffusion­enhanced segmentation methods for
PET/CT images in the context of Head and Neck Cancer. To address the challenges
posed by large medical images, I will train a Latent Diffusion Model (LDM) in latent spaces
and a Denoising Diffusion Probabilistic Model (DDPM) in image space for comparison.
To assess performance, I will use the Dice Similarity Coefficient (DSC) as a key met­
ric, providing a comprehensive evaluation of the diffusion models’ capabilities relative to
previously implemented state­of­the­art techniques. This investigation will contribute to
determining the suitability of diffusion models for enhancing automated segmentation in
oncology imaging.

Background
2.1
PET/CT
Positron Emission Tomography (PET) is a powerful molecular imaging modality that plays
a crucial role in the diagnosis, staging, and treatment monitoring of various cancers [DHF10].
By utilizing radiotracers such as Fluorodeoxyglucose (FDG), PET captures the metabolic
activity of tissues, allowing for the visualization of hypermetabolic regions [ZLS11]. When
the radiotracer accumulates in tissues with high metabolic activity, such as cancerous
cells, it undergoes positron emission. The emitted positron travels a short distance within
the tissue before colliding with an electron, a process known as annihilation, which pro­
duces a pair of 511 keV gamma photons traveling in nearly opposite directions [SK06].
These photons are detected by a ring of scintillation detectors surrounding the patient,
as shown in Figure 2.1. The precise detection of these photons, combined with Time­
of­Flight (TOF) technology, improves spatial resolution and sensitivity by narrowing the
possible location of the annihilation event. PET scanners often employ sophisticated iter­
ative reconstruction algorithms, such as Maximum­Likelihood Expectation Maximization
(MLEM) or Ordered Subsets Expectation Maximization (OSEM), to create detailed three­
dimensional metabolic activity maps.
Figure 2.1: Illustration of the PET imaging process. Figure from [Key17].
The CT component of PET/CT serves as a crucial anatomical reference, providing high­
resolution structural images that facilitate the accurate localization of metabolic activity.
PET/CT system integrates functional and anatomical information by assessing the spatial
relationships between tumors and surrounding tissues.
2.1.1
SUV normalization
Standard Uptake Value (SUV) normalization is a fundamental normalization method in
18F­FDG PET imaging, facilitating the standardized assessment of radiotracer uptake
across different patients and imaging sessions [Boe+14b]. PET measurements are typi­
cally expressed in units of becquerels per millilitre (Bq/mL); however, SUV normalization
involves calculating the ratio of the tissue concentration of the radiotracer to the injected
dose normalized by body weight, resulting in values expressed in SUV units [Wah+09].
This normalization process enables the comparison of metabolic activity between lesions
and aids in differentiating malignant from benign tissues [Mol+24]. SUV is computed using
the formula:

2.2
Convolutional Neural Networks
Convolutional Neural Networks (CNN) have revolutionized the field of deep learning by
handling the complexities of image data. Their success stems from the ability to auto­
matically learn and extract hierarchical feature representations through layers of convo­
lutional filters, which capture spatial and temporal dependencies in images [LBH15]. The
core technological idea behind CNNs involves using convolutional layers to detect local
patterns, pooling layers to reduce dimensionality, and fully connected layers to perform
high­level reasoning. Additionally, advanced techniques such as batch normalization and
dropout are often integrated to enhance training stability and prevent overfitting [IS15].
The use of activation functions such as ReLU, Leaky ReLU, and ELU contributes to non­
linear feature extraction, enabling the network to model complex relationships within the
data [NH10; Kla+17]. This architecture enables CNNs to achieve remarkable accuracy
in tasks such as image classification, object detection, and segmentation by leveraging
large datasets and powerful computational resources [KSH12]. In the context of image
segmentation, CNNs are particularly valuable due to their capability to accurately delin­
eate structures and identify regions [Lit+17b].
2.2.1
U­Net
The U­Net architecture is distinguished by its symmetrical encoder­decoder structure,
which facilitates the efficient extraction and reconstruction of image features. The encoder
path, also known as the contracting path, consists of a series of convolutional and pool­
ing layers that progressively capture high­level semantic features while reducing spatial
dimensions. Conversely, the decoder path employs upsampling and convolutional layers
to restore the spatial resolutions.
One of the most innovative aspects of U­Net is the incorporation of skip connections be­
tween corresponding layers of the encoder and decoder. These connections allow the
network to retain and utilize fine­grained spatial information that might otherwise be lost
during the downsampling process. By concatenating feature maps from the encoder to
the decoder, U­Net combines contextual information with precise localization, enhancing
the accuracy of the segmentation boundaries.
The original 2D U­Net [RFB15b], presented in Figure 2.2, has become a foundational
model in image segmentation, specifically designed for processing two­dimensional im­
ages. Grey arrows indicate skip connections, where feature maps from the contracting
path are copied and concatenated with the corresponding layers in the expanding path.
The number of channels increases during downsampling (e.g., from 64 to 512) and de­
creases during upsampling, facilitating a more detailed segmentation output. Notably,
max pooling reduces spatial resolution, while up­convolutions restore it, leading to an
output segmentation map with 2 channels.
Building upon this, 3D U­Net [Cic+16], extends the architectures to handle three­dimensional
volumetric data. The transition to 3D allows the network to leverage contextual information
across adjacent slices, leading to more coherent and accurate segmentation of complex
anatomical structures. Additionally, 3D U­Net can exploit the inherent spatial continuity in
volumetric data, improving the consistency of results across different slices.

2.3
Variational Autoencoders
Autoencoders (AE) are a type of neural network designed to learn efficient, low­dimensional
representations of data by encoding input data into a latent space and subsequently re­
constructing it through a decoder, minimizing the reconstruction error. While AE operates
with a deterministic latent space, Variational Autoencoder (VAE) extends this framework
by introducing a probabilistic approach to the latent space, enabling the generation of
new data samples that closely resemble the training data. Introduced by Kingma and
Welling [KW13], VAEs blend the principles of variational inference with the traditional au­
toencoder architecture, offering a more versatile framework for generative modelling.
The VAE architecture comprises two main components:
1. Encoder (Recognition Model): Maps input data x to the parameters of the approx­
imate posterior distribution qϕ(z|x). Typically parameterized as a Gaussian distribu­
tion with mean and variance outputted by neural networks.
2. Decoder (Generative Model): Reconstructs data x from the latent variables z,
modeled by pθ(x|z). This component is also usually implemented using neural net­
works, enabling flexibility in modeling complex data distributions.
At the core of VAEs lies the concept of learning a latent variable model for data generation.
Unlike traditional autoencoders that learn deterministic mappings, VAEs assume that the
data is generated from a set of latent variables z through a probabilistic decoder pθ(x|z).
A critical aspect of VAEs (Figure 2.3) is the sampling process from the latent space,
which facilitates the generation of new data points. To enable efficient and differentiable
sampling, VAEs employ the reparameterization trick [KW13]. Instead of directly sampling
z from qϕ(z|x), the model samples an auxiliary noise variable ϵ from a simple distribution
(typically a standard normal distribution) and expresses z as a deterministic function of ϵ
and the encoder’s parameters:
z = µϕ(x) + σϕ(x) ⊙ϵ,
ϵ ∼N(0, I)

Moreover, the sampling strategy in VAEs plays a crucial role in the quality of generated

data points in the latent space, enabling smooth transitions and the generation of novel
data instances that lie within the learned manifold [Søn+16]. Techniques such as impor­

of the latent space, allowing for more accurate and diverse data generation.

distribution [KW13].
2.4
Diffusion Models
Diffusion models are a class of probabilistic generative models that generate data by iter­
atively reversing a noise­adding diffusion process. These models initiate the generation
process with pure Gaussian noise and progressively denoise it through a series of learned
transformations to produce high­fidelity data samples [Soh+15; HJA20]. The fundamen­
tal principle involves defining a forward process that gradually adds Gaussian noise to
the data over multiple time steps and a reverse process that learns to remove this noise

modality of data by capturing multiple modes of data distribution through their stochastic
generative process. This capability allows them to model complex and diverse data pat­
terns, ensuring that the generated segmentations are both varied and representative of
the underlying data distribution [Son+22].

2.5
Latent Diffusion Models
Figure 2.5: Pixel space to pixel space pipeline with latent space transformation in be­

Latent diffusion models (LDMs) [Rom+22] perform denoising diffusion in a compressed
latent space rather than at the pixel level. This design leverages an autoencoder (Sec­
tion 2.3) to map high­dimensional inputs x to latent codes z. Once images are encoded,
the forward and reverse diffusion steps (Section 2.4) operate on z, significantly reducing
computational overhead. The dimensionality reduction often preserves essential seman­
tic features, enabling high­fidelity reconstructions when the decoder is sufficiently expres­
sive [HJA20].
In a typical LDM pipeline, the network architecture includes a pre­trained or jointly trained
autoencoder and a U­Net­based diffusion backbone. The latter is configured to input
noisy latent codes zt at time t and output either the predicted noise or the denoised latent.
The noise scheduling mechanism ({βt} or {αt}) is analogous to pixel­space diffusion, but
applied to the dimensionally­reduced latent domain. This approach allows processing
of higher resolution images with fewer resources, making LDMs scalable to tasks like
volumetric data generation [Pin+22] or text­to­image synthesis [Sah+22; Rom+22].
2.5.1
Latent Diffusion Models Training
Training LDMs typically proceeds in two main stages: autoencoder training and diffusion
training in latent space. The first stage involves learning an encoder–decoder pair that
compresses images into latent representations. The second stage trains the diffusion
process on the latent codes. Below is an outline of the typical procedure:
Stage 1: Autoencoder Training.
The training of Latent Diffusion Models begins with
configuring the autoencoder to efficiently encode and reconstruct the input data. The en­
coder, denoted as Eϕ, employs convolutional and optional attention layers to down­sample
the high­resolution input x into a compressed latent representation z. The decoder, Dψ,
is designed to perform the inverse operation, reconstructing the input image from the la­
tent code to minimize the reconstruction loss Lrecon(x, ˆx). This loss typically includes a
term for Kullback­Leibler divergence to ensure the latent space adheres to a predefined
10
Diffusion­Enhanced PET/CT Segmentation for Head/Neck Cancer
==PAGE:21==
distribution, enhancing the generative capability of the model [Søn+16; KW13]. The re­
sulting encoder and decoder setup is optimized not only for semantic accuracy but also
for computational efficiency, balancing the latent space dimensions to manage resource
use effectively [Rom+22].
Stage 2: Latent Diffusion Training.
With the autoencoder framework established, train­
ing progresses to the diffusion model, where a U­Net is trained to model the noise charac­
teristics at various diffusion timesteps t, learning to invert the noise addition process and
retrieve the clean latent code z0 from its noisy counterpart zt. The primary training goal in
the context of segmentation is to enable the model to predict structured noise patterns that
correspond to semantic regions in the latent space, ensuring that the reverse diffusion pro­
cess refines noisy latent codes into meaningful segmentations. During training, the noise
prediction loss, typically a mean­squared error between the predicted noise and the true
noise, is computed and used to backpropagate gradients through the network [HJA20].
These gradients update the weights of the U­Net, enabling it to iteratively improve its abil­
ity to recover precise segmentations from corrupted latent codes. The diffusion model
architecture includes advanced features such as residual blocks, skip connections, and
attention mechanisms, which are particularly beneficial for capturing spatial relationships
critical for segmentation tasks. Time­dependent embeddings are incorporated to track
the progression through the diffusion timesteps, ensuring the model adapts to varying
noise levels effectively. Regular validation with segmentation­specific metrics, such as
Dice Similarity Coefficient (DSC) and Intersection­over­Union (IoU), ensures the model is
optimizing for accurate and clinically meaningful segmentation results.
This two­stage training approach allows Latent Diffusion Models to manage the detailed
reconstruction of images from a compressed latent space, facilitating their application
in fields requiring high­resolution outputs from computationally restrictive environments,
such as medical imaging and generative art [Sah+22; Men+22].
2.6
Training Guidance
Training guidance refers to the strategies and mechanisms implemented to direct the
learning process of latent diffusion models towards achieving more accurate and reliable
predictions. This involves integrating additional information, such as conditional inputs,
which help the model adhere to specific spatial and semantic constraints [Rom+22]. By
enforcing training with guidance methods, the model is better equipped to capture com­
plex spatial relationships and adapt to diverse data distributions, thereby improving overall
performance and robustness.
2.6.1
Conditional Guidance
Latent Diffusion Models support conditional guidance by incorporating various types of ex­
ternal information, such as anatomical landmarks, segmentation masks, keypoints, depth
maps, and text. These conditional inputs guide the generation process to produce im­
ages that meet specific structural or semantic requirements [Rom+22]. According to the
state­of­art LDM model presented in Figure 2.5, conditional guidance in LDMs could be
implemented using two primary techniques:
• Concatenation: In this approach, the model concatenates the conditional data
with the latent representation before the denoising steps. Typically, the conditional
input (e.g., segmentation masks or text embeddings) is transformed into a lower­
dimensional vector, which is concatenated with the encoded latent variables of the
input image. This concatenation happens at different layers of the U­Net architec­
Diffusion­Enhanced PET/CT Segmentation for Head/Neck Cancer
11
==PAGE:22==
ture, including the bottleneck layer, allowing the network to integrate both image
data and condition throughout the denoising process.
• Cross­Attention Mechanisms: Cross­attention enables the model to focus on spe­
cific regions or features of the conditional input. The mechanism works by computing
attention scores between the latent features of the image and the conditional input,
such as text or segmentation maps. These attention scores determine which parts
of the condition are most relevant to each latent feature, guiding the generation pro­
cess accordingly. Cross­attention layers are integrated into the U­Net architecture,
allowing the model to efficiently incorporate high­dimensional conditional inputs at
multiple stages of the denoising process. This approach is particularly effective
for handling complex conditions, such as long text descriptions or highly detailed
segmentation masks, where direct concatenation may not capture all the relevant
information [Rom+22; Vas+17].
During training, the model receives paired data consisting of images and corresponding
conditional inputs. Conditional data is either encoded via dedicated encoders (for text or
high­dimensional conditions) or applied directly when it consists of simpler forms (e.g.,
binary masks). The model learns to minimize a combination of reconstruction loss and
guidance consistency loss, ensuring the generated images faithfully adhere to the pro­
vided condition [Iso+18].
2.7
GAN
Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) are a class of Deep learning models introduced
by Goodfellow et al. [Goo+14] that consist of two neural networks, the Generator (G)
and the Discriminator (D), which are trained simultaneously in a game­theoretic frame­
work. The Generator aims to produce realistic synthetic data that resembles the true
data distribution, while the Discriminator seeks to distinguish between real data and the
data generated by the Generator. This adversarial training approach has proven to be
highly effective in a variety of applications, including data generation, augmentation, and
validation.
2.7.1

sample G(z). The Discriminator, on the other hand, evaluates whether a given sample

tion pg(x). The adversarial training process involves optimizing the following objective

3.3
Data Preprocessing
The preprocessing pipeline included three main stages: downsampling and voxel rescal­

each step of the process.
3.3.1
Downsampling and Voxel Resizing
All images originally had voxel dimensions of (1, 1, 2), with spatial dimensions of (512, 512, z),
where z represents the number of slices, varying between scans but generally ranging
from 117 to Z with a median of 200. To reduce computational complexity and memory
requirements while maintaining anatomical detail, these images were downsampled to di­
mensions of (256, 256, z) with voxel sizes of (2, 2, 2). The binary segmentation masks were
downsampled using nearest­neighbor interpolation to preserve their discrete binary val­
ues, while PET images were downsampled using B­spline interpolation to ensure smooth
and continuous voxel intensity transitions.
3.3.2
Cropping
After downsampling, the images were cropped to eliminate extraneous regions and focus
on areas of interest. This process was guided by tumor location coordinates provided
in the training dataset to ensure adequate coverage of tumor regions while minimizing
irrelevant areas. The resulting cropped images had dimensions of (196, 196, z), where z
represents the size of the third dimension, with max(z, 196). The test dataset was cropped
in exactly the same way as the training dataset to maintain consistency and ensure com­
parable preprocessing across datasets.

sions of 3, 4, 8 and 16.

KL Divergence Step Size
The KL divergence step size, which balances reconstruction
accuracy and latent space regularization, was carefully adjusted by tuning the weight
of the KL term in the loss function. To ensure meaningful latent representations, the
adjustment focused on maintaining a trade­off where the latent space captured sufficient
structure from the data without becoming overly constrained. The measure was defined as
the ability to reconstruct input data with high fidelity while preserving a smooth, continuous
latent space, as evaluated by reconstruction loss and the distribution alignment with a
Gaussian prior. We set up KL term weight to 50.
Optimization Settings:
The models were trained for a maximum of 400 epochs, with
an early stopping mechanism employing a patience of 10 epochs to prevent overfitting
once the loss metrics ceased to decrease significantly. Various learning rate strategies
were also investigated, including fixed learning rates of 1 × 10−3, 1 × 10−4, and 1 × 10−5,
as well as dynamic learning rate schedules. The dynamic schedules tested included a
cosine annealing strategy, starting at 1 × 10−3 and gradually decaying to 1 × 10−5, and a
linear decay schedule with the same initial and final learning rates.
3.4.2
Latent Diffusion Component
This section describes a tailored latent diffusion component of the LDM model. Here, the
model generates segmentation masks based on input PET images and corresponding
binary masks. The approach integrates components from PyTorch Lightning and MONAI
[MON24a], with architectural designs, hyperparameter choices, and training strategies
refined through systematic experimentation. A customized 3D U­Net, the rationale be­
hind hyperparameter tuning, and the comprehensive training and evaluation pipeline are
presented below.
The LDM pipeline begins with preprocessing the 3D PET images and binary Gross Tumor
Volume using pre­trained autoencoders, which compress the high­dimensional data into
latent representations. These representations are concatenated, and noise is systemati­
cally injected into the latent segmentation space. A diffusion model is trained to iteratively
denoise the latent segmentation representations, effectively learning to reconstruct ac­
curate segmentation masks. The denoised latent representation is then converted back
to image space using the pretrained decoder. The pipeline culminates in evaluating the
model’s performance using the Dice Similarity Coefficient and other metrics on a valida­
tion set.
3.4.2.1
Architecture and Hyperparameters
The implementation utilizes input channels derived from the concatenated latent spaces
of PET images and binary segmentation masks, while leveraging 3D convolutions to pre­
serve spatial context across the volumetric data. The network produces output channels
that align with the latent space, enabling the reconstruction of accurate segmentation
masks.
Batch Size:
For training with full volumes, a batch size of 14 per GPU was used. In
the patch­based approach, the batch size was increased to 24 per GPU, leveraging the
reduced memory requirements of smaller input dimensions.
Scale Factor:
During the training process, scale factors were applied to the latent rep­
resentations of PET and GTV images to ensure that the standard deviation of the latent
space approximates a Gaussian distribution, as described by Rombach et al. [Rom+22]
This step was motivated for stabilizing the training process and maintaining consistent la­
Diffusion­Enhanced PET/CT Segmentation for Head/Neck Cancer
23
==PAGE:34==
tent representations, which facilitated effective interaction between the modalities during
the diffusion process.
Network Configuration:
An initial configuration of the U­Net featured channel dimen­
sions of (32, 64, 64) with a single residual block per level. To explore a spectrum of model
capacities, experiments were conducted with intermediate channel dimension values be­
tween (32, 64, 64) and (128, 256, 512).
Attention Mechanisms:
Attention mechanisms were integrated to improve feature rep­
resentation in deeper layers. Attention was applied only in the two deepest layers, as
specified by setting attention_levels=(False, True, True). This configuration con­
centrated computational effort on abstract features.
Additionally, num_head_channels
was set, meaning that the shallowest level omitted attention heads while the deeper lev­
els employed a certain number channels per head, respectively. These settings were
established based on experiments balancing model expressiveness with computational
efficiency.
Normalization Settings:
The normalization settings were chosen to ensure stable gra­
dient flow and smooth convergence, particularly in the deeper layers of the network.
Specifically, norm_num_groups=32 and norm_eps=1e-06 were selected, informed by the
recommendations of Rombach et al. [Rom+22], which highlight the role of effective nor­
malization in diffusion models. The suitability of these settings was assessed by observing
the gradient magnitudes during backpropagation and analyzing the convergence patterns
of the loss function across training iterations.
Diffusion Process:
For the diffusion component, the DDPMScheduler was employed
with parameters num_train_timesteps=1000, beta_start=0.0015, and beta_end=0.0195.
These values were determined in state­of­art implementation published in MONAI repos­
itory. Further enhancements included enabling upcast_attention=True to increase the
precision of attention operations and setting resblock_updown=True to improve multi­
scale feature learning. Both choices were validated by experimental results.
Optimization Settings:
The Adam optimizer was selected, and multiple learning rates,
including 2.5e-5, 1e-5, and 5e-6, were tested during training to analyze their effects on
model performance and convergence. Training was conducted for a maximum of 2000
epochs, with early stopping applied if no improvement in loss was observed for 20 con­
secutive epochs.
3.4.2.2
Training and Evaluation
The training was conducted on multi­GPU clusters to efficiently process the computational
demands of 3D volumetric data. The distributed training leveraged data parallelism, en­
suring optimal utilization of hardware resources while maintaining synchronization across
GPUs for gradient updates.
During the final LDM evaluation, each validation image was processed through the in­
ference pipeline, which included encoding PET images into latent space, concatenating
them with random noise in the form of a mask, and subsequently denoising to reconstruct
segmentation masks. An ensemble approach was employed to enhance the robustness
of predictions, wherein 3 outputs were generated for each test image by varying the noise
realizations during inference. The ensemble outputs were aggregated by calculating their
voxel­wise mean, providing a probabilistic segmentation map. Those maps delivered

results.

Discussion
This thesis investigates the potential of 3D Diffusion Models for head and neck cancer
segmentation, aiming to evaluate their performance relative to the nnU­Net framework
under identical data conditions [Kov+24]. The choice of DMs was driven by their demon­
strated effectiveness in prior work, where they proved capable of generating probabilistic
refinements and quantifying uncertainty—critical aspects for addressing challenges such
as tumor boundary variability in clinical applications [Wol+21; DG24]. Moreover, a key
advantage of DMs lies in their ability to assimilate multimodal data (e.g., PET, CT, MRI,
and text), thereby offering a richer characterization of tumor regions. DMs benefit also
from a stochastic sampling process that enables the generation of multiple segmentation
masks from the same input, enhancing accuracy through ensemble predictions. Wolleb et
al. [Wol+21] demonstrated this advantage by showing that ensembling five segmentation
masks increased the DSC from 0.866 to 0.881.
Dong and Gong [DG24] investigated the usefulness of DM ­ specifically Denoising Dif­
fusion Probabilistic Model for HNC segmentation using the HECKTOR dataset[And+22]
and also compared it with nnUnet. Their research was published near the completion of
this thesis, but the findings presented by them are highly relevant to this project. The
results obtained by them underscored the impact of DDPM’s multimodality, achieving a
Dice Similarity Coefficient (DSC) of 0.739 with dual PET­CT inputs—significantly surpass­
ing single­modality PET (0.570) and CT (0.352). Notably, their 3D Diffusion approach
(DSC = 0.739) also outperformed a 2D variant (DSC = 0.669) and exceeded the nnU­Net
baseline in their specific setup.
Despite these demonstrated strengths, high memory demands often compel 3D DDPM to
rely on patch­based processing, risking a loss of global context. Consequently, this thesis
explored for HNC segmentation both classical DDPM and Latent Diffusion Model (LDM)
[Rom+22] as evolution one that could address memory constraints by operating in a com­
pressed latent space—thereby retaining critical volumetric information while enhancing
computational feasibility.
My experiments revealed that, under identical data and resource conditions, 3D LDM
didn’t give results least comparable to those obtained using nnUnet, and significant losses
in processing quality occurred in both the autoencoding and diffusion stages. In turn,
conventional 3D DDPM (using only PET inputs) achieved better PET­only performance
than reported by Dong and Gong [DG24], attaining a DSC of 0.668 across all test volumes
and 0.814 on larger volumes, compared to their 0.570 for PET­only. Nonetheless, my
segmentation results were suboptimal compared to nnU­Net (DSC = 0.80)[Kov+24].
Since LDM offers computational efficiency that would allow not only full­resolution data
processing but also computationally efficient multimodal implementation, most of the ex­
periments performed were focused on investigating this model. As the key element of this
model is the autoencoding of data to and from the latent space, a significant portion of
the research focused on optimizing autoencoders for both PET and mask data, with par­
ticular emphasis on segmentation masks. The primary goal at this stage was to minimize
the loss of tumor volume during autoencoding. Initial experiments using state­of­the­art
VAE­GAN implementations [MON24a; Rom+22] yielded unsatisfactory autoencoding re­
sults. This led to an exploration of different data loading strategies, including training on
3D full­resolution volumes, 3D patch­based approaches, and 2D slices for comparison.
Diffusion­Enhanced PET/CT Segmentation for Head/Neck Cancer
35
==PAGE:46==
For segmentation mask autoencoding, neither 3D full­resolution volumes nor 3D patch­
based methods achieved DSC higher than 0.88 for latent space dimensions of 3 and 4, as
inspired by configurations in state­of­the­art MONAI implementations [MON24b]. The 2D
slice­based approach [MON22] significantly improved mask autoencoding performance
(to DSC = 0.946) but resulted in reduced accuracy for PET images. This suggests that
while 2D models simplify spatial representations for binary images, they may sacrifice
more crucial for non­binary images autoencoding 3D contexts, such as volumetric tumor
consistency and spatial relationships across slices. To further analyze how the generative
component and latent space regularization influence representation quality, additional ex­
periments focused solely on the generator, comparing Variational Autoencoders (VAEs)
and standard Autoencoders (AEs). For PET images, the VAE­GAN consistently produced
the best visual performance, confirming the effectiveness of adversarial loss in capturing
more sophisticated 3D structures, where spatial information is critical for context under­
standing. However, for binary masks, excluding the GAN component and also the KL
divergence loss significantly improved performance. This finding suggests that the binary
mask data lacks sufficient variability or distributional complexity to benefit from the regu­
larization imposed by KL divergence. Moreover, the KL loss encourages the latent space
to follow a normal distribution, which may not align well with the inherently discrete and
non­Gaussian nature of binary masks. Removing the KL loss allowed the autoencoder
to focus on preserving detailed mask contours better, directly enhancing segmentation
performance.
Despite exhaustive efforts to optimize the mask autoencoder—including hyperparame­
ter tuning, network size adjustments, and other improvements detailed in Section3 —
the current setup failed to achieve better results for smaller latent space dimensions(3
and 4). This prompted an exploration of whether increasing the latent space size could
enhance representation accuracy. Even with four­fold and eight­fold increases in latent
space size, 3D VAE performance plateaued at a Dice Similarity Coefficient of 0.92, in­
dicating that 8% of segmentation mask data was still lost during encoding and decod­
ing. While this larger latent space marginally improved results, it significantly increased
memory demands, raising questions about its practicality. Beyond hyperparameter op­
timization, the small training dataset emerged as another potential limitation in training
the diffusion model from scratch. To address this, a simple synthetic data generation was
proposed to assess the impact of increased tumor representations on mask autoencoder
performance. By augmenting the dataset through morphological transformations of exist­
ing tumors and repositioning them within the body, the number of tumor instances tripled.
This augmentation led to an improvement in the DSC of almost 9 percentage points for
segmentation masks, suggesting that increasing tumor diversity is a promising avenue for
autoencoding model enhancement. However, it is important to note that morphological
transformations may not introduce entirely new patterns, serving only as a stopgap mea­
sure. For a more generalizable improvement, the dataset would need to be augmented
with more sophisticated synthetic images or additional real samples.
The next phase of the project involved training the 3D LDM model using latent represen­
tations with dimensions 3 and 4. A quantitative analysis of the decoder outputs revealed
that training the LDM model introduced information losses exceeding 10%, primarily due
to the limitations of the decoder for masks. Additional data loss occurred during the au­
toencoding of PET images, which served as the foundation for generating segmentation
masks. Specifically, tumor regions in the reconstructed PET images sometimes exhibited
reduced activity compared to the original images, while activity was falsely detected in
areas outside the masks (Figure 4.2, Figure 4.3). It is worth noting that due to this incon­
36
Diffusion­Enhanced PET/CT Segmentation for Head/Neck Cancer
==PAGE:47==
sistency between the input and the output for the autoencoder, we backpropagate wrong
values, and thus confuse the model. Despite multiple attempts to tune the model, the
LDM achieved very low DSC and faced significant challenges with thresholding the de­
noised outputs. As shown in Figures 4.4 and B.12, ensemble analysis indicated that the
model could detect tumor regions and distinguish them from the background. However,
converting the denoised signals into binarized masks proved difficult, as the outputs, while
capturing approximate tumor boundaries, were inconsistent and unreliable for accurate
segmentation.
To address the poor performance of the LDM, I also implemented a vanilla 3D DDPM
model from the MONAI library[MON23]. This approach achieved a DSC of 0.67, which,
while an improvement, was still inferior to nnU­Net performance. These results suggest
that the challenges are not solely due to the autoencoder but also influenced by the dif­
fusion process in our setup. This opens up a discussion on how much information is
lost during autoencoding and how much – during the diffusion process and whether the
reasons for losing this information for the first and second stages of the process are the
same, such as too little data, unrepresentative data, too few computational resources.
An analysis of our setup highlights the high quality of the input data, which surpasses
those used in similar challenges such as HECKTOR[And+22] or BRATS[Men+15] in terms
of resolution, precision of annotations and diversity of intensity values. However, high­
quality data does not necessarily correlate with ease of learning for the model and can
make the autoencoding process more challenging. Our results indicate that additional
data, greater computational resources, and later more extensive network architectures
are likely needed to address the challenges. A key weakness of the LDM model lies in its
cascading error mechanism: inaccuracies introduced at the autoencoder stage propagate
and amplify during the diffusion process, leading to further degradation in performance.
A number of limitations hinder satisfactory segmentation results with LDMs and warrant
further consideration. One major challenge is the probabilistic nature of diffusion models
and their reliance on latent space representations, which complicates result interpreta­
tion. Computational demands also pose a significant limitation, as even LDM processing
was constrained to small batch sizes, with only 4 full­resolution 3D images or 5 patches
per batch on a GPU. This leads to high batch­to­batch variability, suboptimal gradient up­
dates, and training instability, which could be mitigated through techniques like gradient
accumulation or the use of instance or group normalization instead of batch normaliza­
tion. Furthermore, the PET dataset used in this study is inherently more challenging to
analyze as it relies on single­channel intensity values that reflect metabolic activity, un­
like MRI data, which provides higher resolution and structural detail. This results in less
well­defined boundaries and increased complexity in segmentation tasks. Additionally,
the significant variability in tumor size and shape within the dataset (see Figure 4.1) fur­
ther complicates generalization, particularly for irregular or atypical cases. Lastly, medical
data­specific challenges exacerbate these issues, including the sensitivity and inaccessi­
bility of PET images and annotations, which are prone to human error and hardware de­
pendencies. The limited availability of pre­trained weights for PET/GTV imaging restricts
the use of transfer learning, while the unique nature of those data makes generating real­
istic synthetic samples particularly challenging. Addressing these limitations will require
innovations in both computational strategies and data augmentation tailored specifically
for PET/GTV imaging.
Future work could explore several promising directions to improve LDM­based segmen­
tation models. One avenue involves designing individualized architectures for the GTV
Diffusion­Enhanced PET/CT Segmentation for Head/Neck Cancer
37
==PAGE:48==
and PET autoencoders, as well as the diffusion model itself, tailored to the specific char­
acteristics of each modality. For instance, while VAE­GAN excels at encoding PET im­
ages, a standard autoencoder (AE) is better suited for GTV segmentation, suggesting
that modality­specific refinements could significantly enhance model performance. An­
other direction is the incorporation of additional conditional modalities, such as computed
tomography (CT) data or clinical textual inputs, to provide richer contextual information
and improve segmentation accuracy by leveraging multimodal data. Expanding the latent
space of the LDM is also worth exploring, as finding an optimal balance between richness
and computational feasibility could enhance both fidelity and efficiency. Finally, gener­
ating high­quality synthetic data to address data scarcity and improve model robustness
could diversify training examples, standardize dataset quality, and enable better general­
ization by capturing a broader range of tumor shapes, sizes, and activity patterns.

6
Conclusions
This thesis investigated the implementation of Diffusion Model (DM)s for tumor segmen­
tation in PET images, comparing their performance to nnU­Net under identical conditions.
While nnU­Net demonstrated superior results overall, this study highlighted critical chal­
lenges and opportunities for diffusion­based methods. Significant information loss during
the autoencoding stage of LDMs negatively impacted reconstruction quality and the diffu­
sion process, emphasizing the need for improved latent representations. A key finding for
LDMs was that for both PET and Gross Tumor Volume (GTV), the modality had a critical
impact on autoencoding results, as demonstrated in experiments with VAE­GAN, VAE,
and AE. Another key finding was the improved GTV autoencoding results after tripling the
dataset size through simple synthetic data generation, underscoring the importance of
data availability. Notably, for Denoising Diffusion Probabilistic Model (DDPM), results on
larger samples were comparable to nnU­Net, demonstrating their capability to compete
under specific conditions. However, diffusion models exhibit strong potential due to their
modularity, scalability, and adaptability, particularly in tasks involving multimodal data in­
tegration. While nnU­Net outperformed DMs in this study, diffusion models represent a
promising avenue for future research, with the potential to rival or surpass traditional seg­
mentation methods by addressing current limitations, such as mitigating information loss
and leveraging larger, high­quality datasets.
